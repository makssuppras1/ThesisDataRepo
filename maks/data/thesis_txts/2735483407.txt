Abstract
This thesis explores the frontier of mental health care by developing an AI-based therapeutic as-
sistant, leveraging the advanced capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Retrieval-
Augmented Generation (RAG) to provide empathetic, informed, and contextually relevant sup-
port to individuals seeking psychological assistance. This project aims to use advanced AI
technology to mimic the intricate and complex aspects of human therapy sessions, aiming to
make mental health support more accessible and personalized.
The research delves into the intricacies of designing an AI system that understands and pro-
cesses human emotions, queries, and narratives through the utilization of a diverse corpus of
therapeutic dialogues and psychological frameworks. By harnessing the power of RAG, the AI
therapist dynamically retrieves and generates responses that not only resonate with the users’
emotional states but also guide them towards introspection and self-help strategies. Further-
more, this study investigates the ethical implications, challenges, and potential biases involved
in automating such deeply personal interactions and ensuring that the system’s recommenda-
tions are grounded in psychological best practices.
This project lays the groundwork for future research into AI-assisted therapy, emphasizing the
potential of AI to complement traditional therapeutic practices by offering scalable, immediate,
and stigma-free support.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In an era where mental health is escalating into a crisis - the World Health Organization (WHO)
has strongly signalled towards an impending pandemic of psychological disorders. The stark
reality is that mental health conditions have surged globally - with an estimated 1 billion people
living with a diagnosable mental health disorder which is almost 12% of the current world
population [1]. The World Health Organization also reports that from 1990 to 2013, there was
a nearly 50% increase in the number of people suffering from depression and/or anxiety, rising
from 416 million to 615 million [2]. The economic consequences of these health losses are
equally large: a recent study estimated that the cumulative global impact of mental disorders in
terms of lost economic output will amount to US$ 16.3 trillion between 2011 and 2030.[3]
The situation is no less dire in Denmark. Though often cited as one of the happiest countries in
the world, it is still not immune to the widespread mental health challenges rampaging across
the society - particularly in the young adults. A study by the Danish health Authority [4] found
that by the age of 18, 15% of young adults in Denmark have been diagnosed with a mental disor-
der. COVID-19 pandemic in 2020-21 has only intensified these figures - with major depressive
symptoms skyrocketing during the pandemic’s first year [5]. This local challenge is nothing but a
strong reflection of the long brewing global crisis and indicates towards the disparity between the
growing demand for therapeutic services and their scarce availability characterized by lengthy
waiting periods and substantial costs for consultations. This disparity is even more pronounced
in low and middle-income countries, where mental health services are often scarce or non-
existent. A study conducted over a 15-year period from 2016 to 2030 across 36 countries of
varying income levels [6] analyzed the economic implications of enhancing mental health treat-
ment, specifically through psychosocial counseling and the use of antidepressant medications.
The findings estimated the total cost of this expansion at approximately $147 billion. However,
the economic return significantly surpasses the investment, with a projected $399 billion gain
attributed to a 5% improvement in workforce participation and productivity, and an additional
$310 billion benefit stemming from improved health outcomes.
It is against this backdrop that this thesis proposes the creation of an AI Therapist. This project
merges the capabilities of latest Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) with the advanced
processing power of Large Language Models (LLMs) to deliver empathetic, informed, and con-
textually relevant support to individuals seeking psychological assistance. The ambition is to
disrupt the geographical, social, economic barriers in mental health care by leveraging AI tech-
nology to emulate the complex dynamics of human therapy sessions - making mental health
support more accessible, personalized, and devoid of stigma. The deployment of LLMs and
RAG technologies is poised to create a therapeutic tool that not only understands and processes
emotional and cognitive expressions but also generates responses that promote introspection
and self-help strategies.

1.2
Related Work
1.2.1
Existing Solutions in AI Therapy
The intersection of artificial intelligence (AI) and therapy has yielded a new frontier in mental
health care, marked by the advent of AI-driven therapeutic agents. The current state-of-the-art
in AI therapy is represented by intelligent systems that offer conversational support, emulate
empathetic responses, and provide cognitive-behavioral interventions. These systems, often
referred to as therapeutic chatbots or virtual agents, are grounded in robust psychological the-
ories and are designed to replicate aspects of the therapeutic process.

Fine-tuning for Specificity
To ensure LLMs perform well on specific tasks, fine-tuning of its knowledge is employed to train
it for a specific use-case - similar to what has been done during building this AI Therapist tool.
This fine-tuning process involves additional training using a task-specific dataset, which helps
the pre-trained model adapt and optimize its parameters to the particularities of the desired
task. The benefits of fine-tuning are substantial as it enables the model to learn task-specific
patterns and nuances, thus improving effectiveness and accuracy in task-specific responses.
However, this process typically requires high-processing GPUs due to the size of the models.
One of the emerging techniques in fine-tuning is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), which works un-
der the umbrella of Parameter Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT). LoRA technique involves injecting
trainable rank decomposition matrices into each layer of the transformer architecture, reducing
the dimensionality of the features. This allows for fine-tuning large models by significantly de-
creasing the number of trainable parameters. Such advancements are paving the way for more
efficient use of computational resources while maintaining model performance.
Fine-tuning pre-trained language models for specific tasks presents a significant computational
challenge due to the vast number of parameters. LoRA, or Low-Rank Adaptation, offers a math-
ematically elegant solution to this problem [27], targeting efficient fine-tuning while preserving
the rich linguistic capabilities of models such as GPT-3. LoRA operates on the principle that
instead of updating the entirety of a model’s weight matrix, which is prohibitively expensive in
terms of computation, one can introduce a low-rank matrix that encapsulates the necessary
adaptations. This matrix, denoted as ∆W, has the same dimensions as the original weight ma-
trix W0 of the transformer model but is decomposed into two smaller matrices, A and B. These
matrices have dimensions d × r and r × d respectively, where r is the rank and significantly
smaller than d, the original dimensionality. The adapted output h for an input vector x is then
computed by summing the product of the input with the original weight matrix and the product of
the input with the new low-rank matrices, mathematically expressed as h = (W0 +ABT )x. This
addition of low-rank modifications effectively retains the core strengths of the pre-trained model,
while imparting new knowledge or adjusting to new tasks through a vastly reduced parameter
space.
The advantage of LoRA lies in its balance of efficiency and performance. By manipulating
only a small subset of parameters, LoRA ensures that the depth of knowledge and nuanced
understanding ingrained in the original model is not lost. Instead, it is subtly steered to accom-
modate the peculiarities of the new domain or task at hand. The rank parameter r is exclusively
important in this context, as it determines the extent of adaptation to the used-case and the
computational load that the LLM needs to get tuned. A smaller r leads to quicker training times
but must be chosen judiciously to avoid undermining performance.

Input Layer :Tokenization, Embedding and Encoders
Tokenization is the foundational process in NLP that involves breaking down text into smaller
units, called tokens. These tokens can be words, subwords, or even characters [22]. Techni-
cally, tokenization is the first step in transforming text into a format that can be interpreted by
machine learning models. The complexity of tokenization varies with the language and the
model’s purpose. For instance, models like BERT use WordPiece tokenization [29], which
splits words into commonly occurring subwords or symbols, allowing for efficient processing
of language by capturing the meaning of prefixes and suffixes. This method helps in handling
out-of-vocabulary words and reduces the size of the vocabulary needed by the model. Each
token is then converted into a numerical representation, known as a token ID, which is used in
subsequent stages of the model, such as embedding and encoding. Next up, the embedding
layer maps these discrete tokens into continuous, high-dimensional numerical vectors, which
allows the model to interpret the tokens in a numerical form that captures semantic meaning.
This transformation is facilitated by an embedding matrix, which acts as a lookup table. Each
unique token is associated with a vector in this matrix. [22] The process is not merely a transla-
tion but also captures the semantic meaning of the words. For instance, the BERT embedding
algorithm uses bidirectional processing to generate context-dependent embeddings, providing
nuanced representations of language. Similarly, OpenAI’s algorithm leverages a transformer
architecture to understand complex contextual relationships between words.

Positional encoding is then added to these embeddings to provide the model with the se-
quence information of tokens, as the model itself does not inherently understand order. Lan-
guage is not just about the words used, but also about their sequence. Without the knowledge
of order, sentences become jumbled and incomprehensible. Transformers unlike their RNN
counterparts, do not have an intrinsic sense of word order. Positional encodings are the solu-
tion to this, providing the model with information about the relative or absolute position of the
tokens in the sequence. The combination of embeddings and positional encodings ensures
that the model comprehends both the meaning of individual words and their context within the
sentence.
The Core Architecture : Series of Transformer Blocks
At the core of the transformer model are the transformer blocks, each a mini-factory of un-
derstanding that processes input text. It is basically a sequential stack of several transformer
blocks, each containing an attention mechanism and a feedforward neural network. The atten-
tion mechanism performs a key function by enabling the model to ’attend’ to different parts of
the input sequence when processing a particular word. For example, in a sentence like ”The
cat sat on the mat,” when processing the word ”sat,” the attention mechanism can focus more
on ”cat” and ”mat” due to their direct relevance to the action of sitting. It does this by assigning
higher ’attention scores’ to these related words, creating a weighted representation of the in-
put. There are different attention strategies [23] that refines the model’s focus and processing
capabilities : Self-Attention operates within a single block, using its own output as queries,
keys, and values to understand internal relationships. In contrast, Cross Attention bridges
encoder and decoder in sequence-to-sequence tasks, where the decoder’s output informs the
encoder’s focus. Sparse Attention, recognizing the quadratic complexity of traditional self-
attention, opts for a sliding window approach to efficiently handle longer sequences. Lastly,
Flash Attention addresses the hardware bottleneck of memory access on GPUs. It opti-
mizes attention calculation by reducing memory transactions, leveraging input tiling to stream-
line interactions between GPU memory and SRAM, improving computational efficiency. These
strategies collectively enhance the operational agility of LLMs, allowing them to manage longer
sequences and more complex tasks with greater computational economy. The output of the
attention mechanism is then passed through a feedforward neural network within the same
transformer block. This network consists of layers of neurons that apply further transformations
to the weighted representations. Each layer in the feedforward network applies a set of learned
weights to the input it receives, adding non-linearity and allowing the model to learn complex
mappings from inputs to outputs. Together these mechanisms enable the transformer to pro-
cess input data in a context-aware manner, capturing intricate dependencies regardless of their
position in the input sequence.
The Figure 2.3 shows a transformer model at the centre, having multiple attention and feedfor-
ward blocks in series—stacked sequentially—allows the model to develop a deeper and more
nuanced understanding of the input data. Each layer can capture different levels of abstrac-
tion: the first might pick up on basic syntactic structures, while deeper layers might understand
complex semantics or context beyond immediate word pairs. This layering effectively creates a
hierarchy of features, where higher levels of the architecture can recombine and reinterpret the
outputs of lower levels to form more sophisticated representations of the input data. This hier-
archical processing is analogous to how humans process information - from simple recognition
to complex reasoning.

Output Layer : Softmax function
After processing through the layers of attention and feed-forward networks, the transformer
model arrives at the final stage: predicting the next word in the sequence. It does so by as-
signing scores to all possible next words, with the softmax layer stepping in to convert these
scores into probabilities. The softmax ensures that these probabilities sum up to one, enabling
the model to make a probabilistic prediction about what comes next. The word with the highest
probability score becomes the model’s prediction, culminating the intricate process of under-
standing and generating language.
2.1.3
Benefits and Limitations of pre-trained LLMs
Large Language Models (LLM’s) offer transformative advantages across various domains, en-
hancing how we interact with technology and process information. Their benefits extend far
beyond mere text generation, touching upon aspects of innovation, learning, and application
diversity.
OpenAI’s LLM’s like GPT-3, GPT-4, Mistral, Google’s Gemini exhibit remarkable versatility,
being deployed across a spectrum of tasks including language translation, content generation,
sentiment analysis, and even intricate problem-solving in domains such as healthcare and law.
Their design enables continuous self-improvement, where exposure to new data refines their
capabilities in text understanding and production. Notably, their rapid learning proficiency,
highlighted by in-context learning, allows them to adapt to new tasks with minimal examples,
bypassing the need for laborious retraining. This synthesis of adaptability, learning efficiency,
and broad application potential underscores the transformative impact of LLMs in both research
and practical applications.
Despite their impressive capabilities, LLM’s are not without their limitations and challenges They
can generate information without regard to its veracity, leading to ”hallucinations” of false con-
tent. While adept at generalizing, they often lack the deep, specialized understanding necessary
for certain domains. Their knowledge, fixed at the last training, may not include the latest in-
formation, posing a challenge for tasks requiring up-to-date data. Security risks and inherent
biases from their training data are also critical issues, necessitating robust measures to ensure
ethical use and prevent misuse. These challenges highlight the need for vigilant development
and responsible deployment of LLMs in various applications.

2.2
Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is an advanced framework that significantly enhances
the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) by integrating external knowledge sources
into the generative process. RAG aims to overcome common issues in LLMs, such as producing
inaccurate content or hallucinations, by allowing the model to pull in up-to-date and relevant
data from a vast pool of information. [33] This mechanism is especially beneficial because
it enriches the model’s responses with the most current information available, as LLMs often
present incorrect information, especially when faced with questions that fall outside their training
data. [34] This can manifest as outdated or overly general responses that don’t align with the
specific and current information sought by users. Moreover, LLMs may source answers from
unverified materials, and their training on varied datasets can lead to confusion when the same
term is used in multiple contexts. [34] For instance, consider an LLM as a voracious reader
who has memorized vast amounts of literature but hasn’t updated their knowledge with recent
publications. When asked about a newly released scientific study, the model might provide
an eloquent but outdated summary based on older studies, failing to satisfy a user’s need for
the latest information. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses these challenges by
guiding LLMs to fetch up-to-date information from external databases to enhance the relevance
and accuracy of their outputs. Think of RAG as a real-time research assistant that helps the LLM
to check the most recent and authoritative sources before answering, akin to providing the LLM
with a live feed of current events and expert analysis. This not only improves the reliability of
the responses but also fosters user trust by allowing the verification of sources, thus enhancing
the model’s transparency and accountability.

2.3
Vector Databases
Vector databases are specialized systems designed to manage and efficiently retrieve vector
data which are essentially arrays of numbers that represent complex information (such as the
high-dimensional vector embeddings) used in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) models.
These databases are essential in the functioning of RAG models because they can swiftly per-
form similarity searches, which are crucial for identifying relevant documents or data that the AI
model can use to generate accurate and contextually appropriate responses.
Role of vector databases in RAG
Figure 2.8: Vector Database Search Process
This figure represents the process flow from raw text to vectorized data storage and similarity search in a vector database, highlighting the transition through an embedding
model to the ADX vector storage system.
In a RAG system, when a user inputs a query, the system must quickly sift through a vast
amount of potential data to find the most relevant information. This is where vector databases
shine: they are engineered for high-speed similarity searches that compare the query’s vec-
tor representation with a pre-indexed database of vectors corresponding to different pieces of
knowledge. [36] This process is made possible through techniques like k-nearest neighbor (k-
NN) searches, where the database finds the ’k’ most similar vectors to the input query vector.
(typically value of k ranges from 1-10 to get the most accurate results for the RAG). But the
search capabilities of vector databases are not just about speed; they are also about precision.
Through methods like cosine similarity [Fig. 2.9], which measures the cosine of the angle be-
tween two vectors, or Euclidean distance, which calculates the straight-line distance between
two points in vector space, [37] vector databases can discern which documents are most likely
to be relevant to the user’s request. This level of precision is critical for RAG models to pro-
vide responses that are not only quick but also meaningful and accurate. Moreover, vector
databases enable dynamic updating of their indices which means that as new information be-
comes available, it can be added to the database without the need for a complete rebuild. This
feature is particularly useful in RAG models, which require access to the latest information to
remain relevant and accurate.

(LLMs) by incorporating advanced retrieval strategies and chaining mechanisms. The frame-
work provides a suite of tools that allow developers to create sophisticated AI applications that

actions or processing chained commands. Langchain can be imagined as a ’wrapper’ that wraps
around the RAG at a higher level to facilitate easy development and deployment of applications
which contains diverse API endpoints.

The Company Therapist Dataset
The dataset utilized for the AI Therapist project comprises a comprehensive collection of 769
transcripts sourced from therapy sessions hosted on therapist.com, involving 34 patients,
showcasing a broad spectrum of mental health issues. These transcripts originate from Dr.
Balis’ (the therapist) extensive records [40], encompassing therapy sessions, patient journals,
doodles, official records, phone conversations, and a variety of written and visual materials.
Dr. Charles Balis was a psychiatrist who completed his residency at Columbia Presbyterian
Hospital in New York City and later established a solo practice in San Francisco, contracting
with CalaCare, Inc., an HMO, and providing mental health services for Silicon Impressions Inc.
(SII), a large computer hardware and software company in the area. This diverse collection
provides a deep dive into the personal journeys of Dr. Balis’ patients, offering a window into
their lives and struggles. It includes a detailed list of patients, documenting their initial and most
recent sessions with Dr. Balis. Every patient’s profile is linked to all related materials, presenting
an exhaustive view of their therapeutic journey.
The range of diagnosed conditions spans from Avoidant Personality Disorder to Bipolar Dis-
order, Depression, Delusional Disorder, and Panic Disorder, among others, highlighting the
dataset’s capacity to mirror a wide array of psychological experiences and conditions. This
diversity is pivotal for crafting a fine-tuned LLM and an external database which capable of
understanding and interacting with a broad spectrum of user mental states and needs. It’s im-
portant to note that the dataset did not come pre-annotated with these diagnoses; they were
individually identified and collated from individual patient summaries available on the dedicated
website http://www.thetherapist.com/Introduction.html. A word cloud in Fig.3.1 visualizing the
variety of mental health conditions addressed within this dataset underscores its comprehensive
nature and relevance to developing nuanced AI-driven therapeutic applications.

processing in the RAG framework.
Ease of Use and Maintenance: Opting for Pinecone also hinged on its status as a fully-

burden, sparing developers from the intricacies of infrastructure maintenance, such as apply-
ing software updates or security patches. The advantage here is twofold: it streamlines the
development process and significantly lowers the entry barrier for those less acquainted with
the intricacies of vector database management. Another key aspect to consider was the dif-
ference in hosting between ChromaDB and Pinecone. ChromaDB operates as a local server,
while Pinecone is a cloud-based service. This means that running ChromaDB locally would re-

by leveraging the transformative power of contextual embeddings from BERT (Bidirectional En-
coder Representations from Transformers). It deviates from the traditional static word embed-
dings by harnessing the dynamic nature of models like BERT to produce unique, context-aware
representations for each word occurrence. This innovation is significant because it enables the
same word to yield distinct embeddings influenced by its immediate textual environment, which
leads to a more nuanced and accurate semantic interpretation.
As shown in the Figure 4.20, the BERT Score works by embedding both the candidate text
(what the model generates) and the reference text (the ground truth) into a high-dimensional
space. It then computes the cosine similarity between each token pair from the candidate and
reference. This metric effectively measures the semantic proximity of words, factoring in their
contextual roles within their respective sentences. By doing so, it aligns with the intuitive un-
derstanding that words carry different meanings in different contexts. Moreover, BERT Score
goes beyond just mere similarity measurement, by integrating an importance weighting system
which preferentially weights rarer words that often carry more significance in conveying seman-
tic content. This ensures that key words, which are crucial to the meaning of a sentence, are
given due attention in the overall score. To facilitate better interpretability of the results, BERT
Score also incorporates baseline rescaling, adjusting the raw scores to a more understandable
range, while maintaining the scores’ ability to accurately rank text generation outputs.
BERT Score is a well-established metric used for various NLP tasks, including machine trans-
lation and summarization. This allows for easier comparison of results across different models
and datasets.
Metrics of BERT Score
The BERT score metric is broken down into three components:
• Precision: This measures how much of the generated response is reflected in the refer-
ence text, focusing on the details and content present in the response.
• Recall: This evaluates the extent to which the generated response captures the essence
of the reference text, showing the completeness of the response.
• F1 Score: This is the harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, providing a single score
that balances both aspects to reflect the overall quality of the generated response.
For this used case, BERT Scores play a pivotal role in assessing the effectiveness of the AI’s
communication. High precision in the BERT Score suggests that the AI’s responses closely
match the relevant information from the reference texts, which in this case would be the re-
sponses or information provided by a qualified therapist. This means the RAG pipeline is pro-
viding accurate information, but not necessarily capturing the full scope of the therapeutic dia-
logue. On the other hand, High Recall indicates that the pipeline is capturing a large amount
of the reference text’s content within its responses. For an AI therapist, this means it is not only
giving correct answers but is also addressing a wide range of issues expressed by the patient,
showing a comprehensive understanding of the patient’s problems. The F1 Score, being the
harmonic mean of precision and recall, gives a balanced view of the AI’s performance, com-
bining both the accuracy and completeness of its responses. In a therapeutic context, a RAG
pipeline with a high F1 Score would be adept at providing relevant and thorough responses,
which could be crucial for building trust and rapport with patients.

RAGAS (RAG Assessment Score)
RAGAS (Retrieval-Augmented Generation Assessment Score) is a comprehensive framework
designed solely to assess the performance of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) models
for specific used-cases. It focuses on evaluating the model’s ability to accurately retrieve and
generate responses based on given queries. The framework incorporates a variety of met-
rics that collectively offer a deep insight into the RAG model’s effectiveness, coherence, and
relevance. The evaluation with RAGAS is conducted through the following criteria and metrics:
Answer Similarity
Definition: This metric evaluates the degree of semantic resemblance between the RAG-
generated response and a set of reference answers (ground truth) deemed correct or ideal.
Values: Higher values indicate a closer match and better alignment to the expected responses,
suggesting that the RAG’s output is aligned with therapeutic standards or goals.
Significance: In an RAG Therapist application, high answer similarity ensures that the RAG’s
responses are consistent with therapeutic practices, enhancing the reliability of the therapy pro-
vided.
Answer Relevancy
Definition: Measures how relevant the RAG’s responses are to the user’s queries or state-
ments. Relevance of an answer is determined by its ability to directly address the original
question accurately and appropriately. It’s important to note that this assessment of relevance
prioritizes directness and appropriateness rather than focusing solely on factual accuracy. An-
swers may be considered irrelevant if they lack completeness or contain unnecessary details,
regardless of their factual accuracy.
Values: A higher score indicates that the RAG is effectively addressing the user’s concerns or
questions, without deviating into unrelated topics.
Significance: This metric is paramount in a therapeutic context. Users seeking emotional sup-
port or guidance need responses that are truly helpful and address their specific concerns. High
answer similarity alone isn’t enough; the response needs to be informative and relevant to the
user’s situation.
Faithfulness
Definition: Assesses whether the RAG’s responses are accurate and truthful to the information
available, without introducing fabrications or hallucinations.
Values: Higher faithfulness scores indicate that the RAG is not misleading the user or creating
false narratives.
Significance: In therapy, it is vital that the information and support provided are grounded in
truth and evidence-based practices, making faithfulness a critical metric for AI Therapists.
Context Precision
Definition: Assesses the precision of the retrieved context, giving a clarity on whether the
information pulled from the knowledge base is directly relevant to the query. It primarily focuses
on the proportion of retrieved contexts that are relevant to the query.
Values: Higher precision values suggest that the RAG is capable of pinpointing and addressing
specific aspects of the user’s statements or questions accurately.
Significance: Precision in understanding and responding to the user’s context is essential for
personalized and effective therapy. Having access to all the relevant information is helpful, but
in a therapeutic context - prioritizing emotional intelligence and active listening might be more
important than achieving perfect recall of every detail from the conversation history.

Context Recall
Definition: Measures the RAG’s ability to recall and integrate relevant information from the
user’s previous inputs or the ongoing conversation.
Values: Higher recall scores indicate that the RAG effectively utilizes the context of the con-
versation in its responses.
Significance: For an AI Therapist, being able to remember and coherently integrate past con-
versation elements is crucial for building a continuous and evolving therapeutic relationship.
Answer Correctness
Definition: Determines the accuracy of the information provided by the RAG in its responses.
Answer correctness comprises two essential components: semantic similarity between the gen-
erated response and the reference text, along with factual accuracy. These elements are inte-
grated using a weighted system to determine the answer correctness score.
Values: Higher correctness scores reflect the RAG’s ability to offer responses that are factually
accurate and therapeutically appropriate.
Significance: This metric is also important, but with some nuances. While factual accuracy is
desirable, sometimes a perfectly correct answer might not be the most therapeutic approach. A
therapist might use techniques like reframing or open ended questions that wouldn’t be scored
as ”correct” by traditional metrics, but are still valuable for the conversation.
Harmfulness
Definition: Identifies any potential harm in the RAG’s responses, including but not limited
to, promoting negative behaviors, providing incorrect medical advice, or using insensitive lan-
guage.
Values: Lower scores are desired, indicating fewer or no harmful elements in the responses.
Significance: Minimizing harm is essential in any therapeutic setting to protect the user’s well-
being. For an AI Therapist, it is critical to avoid causing any potential harm to the user. Thera-
peutic conversations can deal with sensitive topics and the application should minimize the risk
of providing misleading information.
The evaluation of the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) model through RAGAS is a pro-
cess which begins with the RAG model generating answers for a predetermined set of input
queries, utilizing its external knowledge base to inform its responses. These responses are
then meticulously evaluated using a suite of critical RAGAS metrics, which includes (but not
limited to) context precision and recall, answer relevancy, faithfulness etc. They assess the
responses’ alignment with the context of the query, their relevance to the posed questions, and
their adherence to factual accuracy. Finally, the effectiveness of the RAG model is determined
by comparing these generated responses to a set of ground truths. This comparison provides
a clear measure of the model’s performance, highlighting its capability to deliver responses that
are both accurate and relevant to the user’s needs.
Holistic Significance of the Quantitative Metrics
RAGAS complements other evaluation methods like RAGxplorer and BERT Score by providing
a multifaceted evaluation framework that not only assesses semantic similarity (as BERT Score
does) but also evaluates the model’s retrieval effectiveness and the factual accuracy of its re-
sponses. While RAGxplorer offers insights into the document retrieval process and the seman-
tic relationships within the knowledge base, RAGAS focuses on the end-to-end performance
of the RAG model, from retrieval to response generation. Together, these methods provide a
holistic view of the RAG model’s capabilities, identifying areas of strength and opportunities for
improvement.

In the evolving landscape of Artificial Intelligence, ensuring data compliance and mitigating risk

of the EU AI Act, the associated risks of deploying AI in the therapeutic domain are identified,

6.1
EU AI Law
The European Union’s regulatory framework for Artificial Intelligence (AI), predominantly drafted
in the EU AI Act, represents a significant effort to manage the complex implications of AI tech-
nologies. The Act stipulates a set of organized rules for AI systems within the EU market,
focusing on high-risk applications and establishing requirements for transparency, accountabil-
ity, and safeguarding fundamental rights. As the EU AI Act delineates [55], high-risk AI systems
are subject to rigorous conformity assessments, adherence to quality and data management
standards, and continuous post-market monitoring, as stated in Articles 6, 9, and 10. [55]
’Providers’ like such AI therapist application are mandated to conform to these regulations, en-
suring that their systems are developed and deployed in a manner that respects the privacy,
safety, and rights of individuals. This includes the application of subliminal techniques or the
exploitation of vulnerabilities that could distort behavior or decision-making processes, as pro-
hibited by Article 5 of the Act.
6.2
Risk Assessment
The risk assessment for the AI therapist application [56] involves a thorough analysis of potential
risks and devising strategies to mitigate them. Identifying risks involves considering data privacy
breaches, biases in decision-making, and misuse of sensitive information. Mitigation strategies
[56] focus on implementing strong data encryption, conducting regular algorithm audits to elim-
inate biases, and ensuring transparency in data use. These strategies aim to safeguard user
data, promote unbiased therapeutic advice, and maintain the application’s integrity, aligning
with the EU AI Act’s requirements for risk management and data quality.
6.2.1

tem providers determine their obligations under the EU AI Act. By inputting specific information

their system, including relevant article numbers. The AI Act Compliance Checker [57] reveals
that the AI therapist application, ’Dr. Nina’, is classified as a ’provider’. This classification trig-
gers specific obligations under the EU AI Act, including establishing risk management processes
(Article 9), utilizing high-quality data (Article 10), and maintaining thorough documentation and
transparency (Articles 11-15). Additionally, the application must incorporate human oversight

7.1
Development Phases Overview
The evolution of the AI therapist, utilizing Large Language Models (LLMs) and Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG), progressed through a series of carefully designed phases. Each phase had

the objectives, activities, and outcomes that contributed to the development of the AI therapist.
7.1.1
Initial Concept and Design Phase
Objective: The primary goal was to establish the foundational framework for employing Large
Language Models (LLMs) in the creation of an AI therapist, with a focus on incorporating Retrieval-
Augmented Generation (RAG) to enrich the therapeutic interaction dynamics, striving for a re-
sponsive and context-sensitive system.
Activities: Conducting an extensive literature review on existing AI therapy models to com-
prehend the field’s current state was a pivotal aspect of this phase. It included the selection
of the appropriate LLM and RAG components and the commencement of the system’s design,
concentrating on the synergistic integration of these technologies.
Outcome: The result was a conceptual blueprint for the AI therapist that integrated RAG, equip-
ping the system with the capability to offer dynamic, context-aware therapeutic responses. This
blueprint laid the groundwork for the subsequent development and operationalization of the AI
therapist.

7.3
Challenges and Limitations
7.3.1
Data-Related Challenges
In contrast to typical master’s thesis where a pre-annotated dataset is often provided, this project
required the identification and procurement of a unique dataset fitting the development of an
AI therapist. The primary challenge was sourcing a dataset that contained detailed patient-
therapist dialogues suitable for training an AI to simulate therapeutic conversations. Eventu-
ally, a suitable dataset was identified within a subreddit, offering raw dialogues that closely
matched the project’s requirements. The scarcity of publicly available, detailed datasets con-
taining patient-therapy dialogues presented a significant hurdle. The necessity to turn to un-
conventional sources like subreddits [58] underscored the challenges in data acquisition for
specialized AI applications. Transforming the raw data from the subreddit into a structured,
usable format involved a comprehensive process of scraping, cleaning, processing, and struc-
turing. This process was not only time-consuming but also technically demanding, requiring
meticulous attention to detail to ensure data quality and usability. Although the dataset pro-
vided detailed descriptions of patient profiles, possible diagnoses, and conversations, it was
inherently limited to certain mental health conditions. This limitation posed challenges in devel-
oping a universally applicable AI therapist capable of handling a broader range of conditions.
The task of preparing the data for the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework was
particularly laborious. Given the project’s ambitious goal and the lack of readily available anno-
tated data, this step became a crucial yet time-consuming phase of the research.
The extensive effort required in data preparation significantly extended the project’s timeline,
affecting overall research efficiency and delaying subsequent phases of model development and
testing. The focus on specific mental health conditions dictated by the available data, limited
the AI therapist’s applicability to a broader spectrum of psychological issues. This limitation

7.3.2
Methodological and Technical Challenges
Methodological Challenges
The adoption of state-of-the-art components within the rapidly evolving field of AI, particu-
larly Large Language Models (LLMs) and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), presented a
unique set of methodological challenges. The core of these challenges was the dynamic nature
of technological advancements, which often introduced new or improved methodologies at a
pace that could potentially outdate selected components before the completion of the project.
Specifically, the project aimed to utilize the RAG framework to enhance the AI therapist’s ability
to provide contextually relevant and accurate responses. However, the continuous emergence
of new RAG paradigms and improvements in LLM capabilities posed a dilemma: settling on
specific components could limit the project’s potential to achieve the highest possible accuracy
and performance.
Technical Challenges
On the technical front, the project navigated through several hurdles. Firstly, the implementation
of the RAG framework required meticulous integration of various components, including data
preprocessing, embedding models, and the Pinecone vector database, orchestrated through
the LangChain framework. The selection of components such as the OpenAI text-embedding
models underwent revisions to incorporate more efficient and cost-effective versions, reflecting
the project’s responsiveness to technological updates. Additionally, computational resources
emerged as a significant concern. Despite the feasibility of cloud-based APIs and database
solutions to circumvent hardware limitations, the financial cost associated with training GPT
models and making repeated API calls for evaluation became prohibitive, amounting to approx-
imately 1500 DKK. This financial burden underscored the importance of university funding for
the project’s viability, highlighting the need of balance between technical feasibility and resource
availability.
Impact on Research Outcomes
The methodological and technical challenges, particularly the rapid pace of advancements in
AI technologies, imposed constraints on the project. The commitment to specific RAG com-
ponents early in the research phase meant that subsequent developments like for example
SelfRAG [59], could not be incorporated without significant revisions - potentially limiting the
cutting-edge nature of the final AI therapist application. Moreover, the financial and computa-
tional constraints limited the scope of testing and fine-tuning, which could impact the robustness
and generalizability of the research findings. The fluctuating landscape of AI technologies ne-
cessitates a flexible and adaptive research approach, balancing the depth of exploration within
chosen methodologies against the breadth of technological advancements. Future research
might benefit from establishing mechanisms to incorporate emergent technologies more flu-
idly, ensuring that projects remain at the forefront of the field. Additionally, securing adequate
funding and computational resources emerges as a critical factor in supporting the iterative
development and comprehensive evaluation necessary for AI-driven projects.

within these states is essential for providing targeted support and interventions, necessitating
the design of specific interaction patterns and prompts aligned with CBT principles.
The implementation of real-time feedback loops within the state machine framework will be
critical for adjusting therapy sessions based on user responses, ensuring the therapy’s rele-

This thesis study represents a significant leap forward in the integration of Artificial Intelligence with
therapeutic practices, particularly focusing on the development of an AI Therapist utilizing Retrieval-
Augmented Generation (RAG) and Large Language Models (LLMs). Through meticulous research
and innovative methodology, this work delves into the complexities of simulating therapeutic inter-
actions, aiming to enhance accessibility and effectiveness in mental health care.
At the heart of this exploration is the ambition to democratize mental health support, bridging the gap
between cutting-edge technology and the nuanced needs of individuals seeking psychological help.
This document outlines the challenges, findings, and potential pathways forward, shedding light on
the intersection of technology, empathy, and care.
Technical University of Denmark has been instrumental in providing the resources and environment
conducive to such pioneering research. As we stand on the brink of a new era in mental health
support, this thesis not only contributes to academic knowledge but also opens up new avenues for
practical application and future exploration in AI-driven therapy.
