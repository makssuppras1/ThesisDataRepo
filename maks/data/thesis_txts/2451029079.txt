Abstract
A customer looking to buy a loudspeaker is generally given little info about the actual sound of
the product. This is because it is hard to quantify good sound. However, there is one attribute in
particular which very strongly correlates with preference: bass depth. This is generally the only
attribute of sound which the customer is given any sort of measure of in the loudspeakers technical
data, through the lower cut-oﬀfrequency.
Yet, an experiment by SenseLab found that there was no relationship between the listeners experi-
enced bass depth and the technical data for lower cut-oﬀfrequency. This gave rise to an experiment
to investigate how diﬀerent loudspeaker roll-oﬀcharacteristics inﬂuence the perception of bass depth.
The experimental systems consisted of ﬁve typically used bass reﬂex alignments, a reference system
and an anchor system. The samples were 20 second outtakes from four musical pieces, two of which
was used in SenseLab’s experiment.
The results of this experiment showed three diﬀerent contradictory trends across the four samples,
leading to the conclusion that the loudspeaker’s roll-oﬀcharacteristics’ inﬂuence on the perception
of bass depth is highly dependent on the source material. Through additional data from an accom-
panying focus section form, ﬁlled out by every assessor, it was found that listeners tended to focus
on the highest bass power sections of the sample when assessing the attribute.
A model was created with the goal of predicting the measured bass depth ratings. Correlation
between model and measured results were great for three out of the four samples making overall
performance fairly good. With some improvements the model might be able to guide future loud-
speaker design by predicting which box design will result in the greatest perceived bass depth across
a large library of stimuli.

Introduction
When buying loudspeakers many aspects of the product is taken into account: price, size, shape,
and hopefully sound quality. While the ﬁrst three are easily evaluated the sound quality is not. The
loudspeaker manufacturer will typically only provide a frequency range for the loudspeaker and in
some cases, typically for more expensive products, the loudspeakers frequency and phase response
curves will be plotted. Reviewers and Hi-Fi magazines will use subjective wording to describe the
sound of the product.
This wording often depends heavily on the product’s price and thus, is
mainly a descriptor of its performance among similar products. The question remains: "does this
loudspeaker sound good?".
This thesis aims to gather new information about the perceptual aspects of the electrodynamic
loudspeaker response that may be able to answer at least a part of this question. To achieve this
we must ﬁnd an objective way of assessing the quality of sound, understand what is detectable by
the human ear and how it inﬂuences the perception of sound.
Many studies have been conducted with regards to attributes of sound. One of the more investi-
gated aspects are frequency response preferences. In 2012, [1] looked at the relationship between
measurement and perceptual aspects of headphone sound quality for a number of attributes. The
attribute "good spectral balance" was shown to have the highest correlation with preference, indi-
cating that an overall ﬂat frequency response is valueable. [2] carried out experiments to ﬁnd the
audibility of frequency response irregularities. It was found that sharp peaks with heights of 6 dB
was correctly identiﬁed by 60 % of subjects in musical stimuli. It was also found that sharp peaks
in the response were both easier to detect and more degrading to overall sound quality than equally
sized response dips.
Bang & Olufsen did experiments in 2001 to understand the inﬂuence of lower cutoﬀfrequency and
frequency response ripple on the perception of lower and upper bass when using musical stimuli
[3]. It was found that lower cut-oﬀfrequency had a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the perceived level of lower
bass, granted that the presentation level was above the hearing threshold for the relevant frequency
range. Furthermore, it was found that amplitude ripples of ± 3 dB were needed to cause audible
change in the perceived level of lower bass independent of reproduction level. This eﬀect, however,
was shown to be very stimuli dependent.
In 1977 Blauert and Laws measured typical group delay distortions in headphones and carried out
experiments to determine the threshold of audibility by using all pass ﬁlters capable of creating sharp
symmetric peaks of varying strength in the group delay response [4]. However, the frequency range
used throughout their experiments were limited to between 500 and 8000 Hz. It was concluded that
natural phase variations in most playback equipment need not be corrected in the tested frequency
range. Their experiments with trained subjects resulted in a threshold of audibility around a couple
of milliseconds, raising towards lower frequencies. In similar fashion, [5] discussed the importance
of group delay at lower frequencies in relation to phase distortion introduced in the recording and
1
==PAGE:12==
reproduction chain. The conclusion was that group delay at low frequencies causes clearly audible
changes to sound quality, and it was stated that more work was needed to establish the perceptual
thresholds for group delay distortion at low frequencies.
A department of the Danish company: FORCE, former DELTA, known as SenseLab, have for years
been working with subjective testing in the ﬁeld of audio, speciﬁcally towards evaluation of sound
quality. SenseLab uses a group of trained listeners that are performance monitored to achieve the
most accurate results possible. Throughout their work, they have developed the "sound wheel" [6],
which is a collection of carefully chosen and deﬁned attributes of sound, which together encompass
all aspects of perceived sound from a loudspeaker. This replaces the diverse, subjective vocabulary
that is commonly used to describe sound character and allows for objective judgment and discussion
of any loudspeaker.
In eﬀorts to model sound quality and loudspeaker preference, SenseLab has performed numerous
experiments on attributes relating to timbre, using their sound wheel for guidance with regards
to attribute deﬁnitions and selection. During a particular experiment focused on medium priced
compact loudspeakers a quite peculiar set of data emerged. There seemed to be no relationship
between perceived bass depth and the lower cut-oﬀfrequency as speciﬁed by the manufacturer,
even for loudspeakers from the same manufacturer [7].
In conjunction with another ﬁnding of
SenseLab’s, that preference is strongly correlated with bass depth; one can conclude that this is
deﬁnitely worth researching.
Looking at the results from their experiment, it is clear that the objective measure given for bass
depth by the manufacturer is not satisfactory in this instance. Something about the loudspeakers
response has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the perception on its sound, and what causes this disparity
is unknown. A size diﬀerence in two of the loudspeakers, which exhibited this diﬀerence, points
towards the disparity originating in enclosure design, which would aﬀect the loudspeakers roll-oﬀ
characteristics.
With this information in mind the thesis goal can be formulated:
This thesis attempts to investigating how diﬀerent loudspeaker roll-
oﬀcharacteristics are perceived and attempts to model the observed
behavior.
To achieve this an experiment similar to SenseLab’s experiment will be set up, run and its results
analyzed. Afterwards, a model will be created to attempt to predict the measured results to give
insight into the underlying mechanisms and ﬁgure out why this disparity exists. Perhaps this insight
will be able to guide future loudspeaker design.

2.1
Signal Processing
From signal source to perception in the listeners brain, the path of the signal can be considered one
large chain of signal processing blocks. The pieces of this chain consist of tools applied to the signal
before it is sent to the loudspeaker, eﬀects caused by the propagation of the signal itself and the
processing of the signal in the ear and brain. This section will brieﬂy touch on the most important
signal processing tool used in the creation of systems in this thesis: ﬁlters.
2.1.1
Filters
A ﬁlter is a linear transformation which can suppress, retain and/or emphasize the signal amplitude
at certain frequencies of an input signal. The most basic ﬁlters, the high and low pass ﬁlters, selects
frequencies on one side of the cut-oﬀfrequency to suppress while the amplitude of the remaining
frequencies are maintained. Figure 2.1, seen below, shows the magnitude response of a 2. order low
pass ﬁlter. The cut-oﬀfrequency, commonly chosen as the half-power point, that is, the point from
which the output has fallen by 3 dB, is seen to be 100 Hz in the presented ﬁlter. For most types of
ﬁlter implementation, a phase change will also occur around the cut-oﬀfrequency.

2.2
The Electrodynamic Loudspeaker
An electrodynamic loudspeaker is a transducer for converting electrical energy to acoustic energy.
In an electrodynamic loudspeaker this is done by suspending a coil in a magnetic ﬁeld and applying
a current to the coil. This induces a magnetic ﬁeld in the coil that interacts with the air-gap ﬂux of
the permanent magnet to produce movement of the coil and attached diaphragm. The movement
of the diaphragm, or cone, displaces the air molecules on its surface, creating sound waves. A cross
sectional sketch of a typical electrodynamic loudspeaker can be seen below in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Cross sectional sketch of electrodynamic loudspeaker. Adapted from
[9].

2.2.1
Closed Box Loudspeakers
If a loudspeaker is radiating into free air, the bass response tends to be quite poor as the sound
waves radiates from the front and back side of the diaphragm out of phase and are likely to cancel
out. To prevent cancellation, the driver can be mounted in a baﬄe or enclosure, separating the
front and back waves. The simplest enclosure is the acoustic suspension system.
Acoustic suspension systems are closed box systems, on a wall of which the loudspeaker is mounted,
where the compliance of the air within the box dominates over the compliance of the loudspeaker
driver suspension. If the dimensions of the box are small compared to the wavelength, the volume
of air inside can be modelled purely as a compliance [10]. However, a physical box will exhibit a
complex impedance with compliant, resistive and mass components, dependant on the box and box
ﬁlling.

2.2.2

A vented box, or bass reﬂex enclosure, is a closed box in which an opening, a vent or port has been
created. When the loudspeaker diaphragm in such a box vibrates, part of the pressure wave created

closed box systems.

2.3
Psychoacoustics
As the signal leaves the loudspeaker, it will quickly arrive at the listener’s ear. From the pinna to
the inner ear there are four psychoacoustic phenomena which are important for this thesis: outer
and middle ear ﬁltering, loudness perception, frequency and intensity discrimination.
2.3.1
Outer and Middle Ear Filtering
Figure 2.6 provides an overview of the anatomy of the ear. The pinna is responsible the initial
ﬁltering as its complex shape spatially ﬁlters the incoming sound and contributes to our sense of
localization. When wearing headphones the inﬂuence of the pinna is greatly reduced. The ear canal
and its entrance, the concha, creates cavity resonances, further coloring the sound. The outer ear
11
==PAGE:22==
ﬁlters and channels the sound to the eardrum, which marks the boundary between the outer and
middle ear.
Figure 2.6: Anatomy of the ear [11].
Behind the eardrum the three ossicles: the mallus, the incus and the stapes transfer the vibrations
to the cochlea. Together the transfer functions of the outer and middle ear assume the shape of
a broad band pass ﬁlter, interrupted by the cavity resonances of the concha and ear canal, which
causes a peak around 4 kHz. These characteristics can be recognized in the hearing threshold curve,
seen in Figure 2.7, which closely resembles the inverted outer and middle ear transfer function
[11].
2.3.2
Loudness perception
When conducting hearing experiment focused at very low frequency characteristics, it is important
to keep in mind the assessors’ hearing thresholds. The equal loudness contours, as speciﬁed in the
ISO 226:2003 [12], are seen to rise quickly towards lower frequencies, reaching almost 80 dB SPL at
20 Hz for the absolute threshold curve (0 phon). As a result, hearing experiments will need to be
performed at presentation levels above this level if useful results are to be extracted in this range.
At the very least this should be kept in mind when discussing results later on.
The below ﬁgure presents four of the equal loudness contours in their phon values. The phon is
equal to the SPL at 1 kHz, and changes at the other frequencies such that all points along the
frequency axis have the same loudness. The 0 phon curve represents the hearing threshold, and
the contours ﬂatten out as loudness increases, causing lower frequencies to grow faster in loudness
12
==PAGE:23==
as sound pressure is increased. The equal loudness contours closely resemble the inverse shape of
the outer and middle ear transfer function. Note that while not included in the standard, the phon
cures raise quickly when approaching 20 kHz.
Figure 2.7: Four equal loudness contours adapted from ISO-226:2003 [12]
2.3.3
Frequency and Intensity Discrimination
The assessors’ frequency and intensity discrimination, that is, their thresholds for diﬀerentiating
small changes in frequency and intensity, can set expectations for what level of change the assessors
should be able to discern.
Frequency discrimination can be tested rather simply by presenting two tones to the assessor and
have them judge which had the higher pitch. Frequency discrimination seems to be dependent on
both frequency and level [13], where the ability to diﬀerentiate between similar frequencies decrease
with increasing frequency, and increases with increasing level. Figure 2.8 shows the pooled results
of several studies looking at frequency discrimination when a presentation level of 30 to 50 dB SL
was used, meaning 30 to 50 dB relative to the individual subjects’ absolute threshold.
13
==PAGE:24==
Figure 2.8: Frequency discrimination of pure tones at 30 to 50 dB SL [13].
While these studies only cover the standard audiometric frequencies, the ﬁtted line can be extrap-
olated to reach well under 0.5 Hz for lower bass frequencies.
Similarly, intensity discrimination also depends on level and stimulus type. For wide band stimuli,
the observed behavior across level satisﬁes Weber’s law [14], that is, the smallest detectable change
is constant, regardless of absolute level. This holds for presentation levels above 20 dB SL, and the
value is found to be in the range of 0.5 to 1 dB. For pure tone stimuli Weber’s law does not hold,
and intensity discrimination is easier with increasing level. Regardless, values around 1 dB can be
assumed for the purposes of this thesis.
2.4
Attribute Evaluation
When attempting to describe a loudspeaker’s sound, one question quickly appears; how can we
collect objective, high quality evaluations of diﬀerent attributes of sound without inﬂuence of the
assessor’s individual, subjective taste?
14
==PAGE:25==
Figure 2.9: The sound wheel, developed by SenseLab [15].
To guide this process, SenseLab has developed the sound wheel. The sound wheel, as speciﬁed
in [15], provides assessors with a collection of attributes to clearly deﬁne all aspects of sound,
allowing them to objectively describe their perception of audio. The attributes have been carefully
chosen and deﬁned to minimize inﬂuence from the assessor’s personal preferences and bias. For
any given listening experiment, the sound wheel can be used to select attributes relevant for the
experiment, in addition to providing clear deﬁnitions of the selected attributes to both test leaders
and assessors.
After the attributes for a listening test have been carefully chosen, someone needs to show up
to actually assess the experimental stimuli; enter the assessor.
Legarth and Zacharov [16] list
seven diﬀerent assessor categories. The seven categories are ranked after their members’ ability to
accurately assess the stimuli in a sensory test. The ﬁrst two categories are simply the "assessor",
any person taking part in the sensory test, and the "naive assessor", a person who does not meet
any particular criterion. The ﬁnal two categories being the "expert assessor" and the "specialised
expert assessor".
15
==PAGE:26==
An expert assessor is a person which has experience in sensory methodology, has high sensory
sensitivity and is able to make consistent and repeatable sensory evaluations of various products.
To become an expert assessor under SenseLab, the assessor must prove language proﬁciency, be
between 18 and 60 years of age, and undergo a series of tests to have their hearing and vision tested,
one of which is the standard pure tone audiometry to check the assessor’s hearing thresholds.
A specialised expert assessor is an expert assessor which has additional experience as a specialist
in the product and/or process which is being researched, and is at a much greater level able to
evaluate and predict smaller variations in the stimuli.
SenseLab maintains a panel of assessors that ﬁt into these two most advanced categories in the aptly
named Expert Assessor Panel (EAP). The members of this panel have their performance reviewed
after each test they partake in, and they are removed from the panel should their performance
deteriorate below the panel standards.

3.3
Conditions
A single condition, that the cut-oﬀfrequency of the main systems set be 50 Hz, were chosen. This
frequency is close to the cut-oﬀfrequencies in the speakers used during SenseLab’s experiment,
where cut-oﬀs ranged from 50 to 59 Hz, excluding a single outlier at 80 Hz. The condition should
be representative for a large array of loudspeakers, from compact speakers, as used in the SenseLab
experiment, to smaller ﬂoorstanding loudspeakers.
The number of judgments per assessor opened up for the possibility of several conditions, but it
was ultimately decided, due to the diﬃculty of the experiment, to keep to one condition for which
more data could be gathered.
3.4
Levels and Calibration
A presentation level of 70 dBa was chosen for the experiment.
This should emulate a typical,
comfortable listening level. Although little public data exists on the average listening level for home
use, Benjamin and Fielder [17] refers to results from an unpublished survey in which it was found
that the average preferred dialog level for home cinema listening was 69.5 dBa. This supports the
choice of 70 dBa as the experimental presentation level. This level was also used in SenseLab’s
experiment, therefore it is sensible to use this presentation level for the sake of consistency.
To make sure the diﬀerent samples are perceived to be equally loud, the reference ﬁltered samples
were loudness equalized using the ITU-R BS.1770-3 standard [18]. This will ensure, as far as the
model can predict, that the loudness of the diﬀerent samples are perceived to be equal despite their
spectral diversity. Since the loudness equalization ensures equal loudness only, the measured levels
might diﬀer slightly. Because of this, the level will be set such that the average a-weighted level
over the full 20 seconds of the four samples is 70 dB SPL.
When attempting to simulate loudspeaker roll oﬀ, it is important that the playback system itself
exhibits a ﬂat frequency response. To achieve this additional calibration ﬁltering is needed. The
setup for measuring the headphone transfer function is seen below in Figure 3.6.

4.1
Method
Based on the method used in SenseLab’s experiment it was decided that the test should be car-

Before performing the test all assessors were given an introduction to the experiment containing
general information about the experiment as well as a short instruction as to how the scales should
be used. It was stated that although the perceived diﬀerences might be small, the assessor should
attempt to use the scale without exaggeration.
If a diﬀerence could be perceived between two
systems, its magnitude should be clearly represented in their ratings. The experiment introduction
is included in Appendix A.
After reading through the instructions each assessor was also given a small training test, consisting
of only four systems. These were the reference and anchor and the most and least steep ﬁlters, the
synchronous and the 0.1 dB ripple Chebyshev. Only the JW and S samples were used. Through this
training test the assessor could get an idea of what to listen for to be able to discern the diﬀerent
systems, get familiar with the scales and overall setup without it aﬀecting the ﬁnal data.
During the experiment, the assessor, sitting in one of the isolated listening booths, interacted with
a computer interface, as seen in Figure 4.1, where the seven systems were presented for the current

Discussion
As seen in Table 4.1 the standard deviations for each assessor top out around 35 points for the
EM, S and ZC samples, while it is slightly lower for the JW sample. There is clear variation in the
scale usage between assessors, assessor A8 stands out, achieving the highest standard deviation in
three out of the four samples. On the other end of the scale, the ratings of assessor A6 exhibits
very small standard deviations of less than four points. Due to the scale step interval being 1.5,
this is equivalent to less than three interval steps on the sliding scale used to give the ratings, and
means, essentially, that this assessor has perceived the diﬀerent systems to be very similar for all
samples. After looking into this assessor’s data, it was found that the reference and anchor systems
were consistently rated above and below the other systems, and thus, there is no reason to consider
discarding the data from this assessor. Other assessors also show little scale usage for some samples,
though this behavior is seen mostly for the JW sample. This indicates this sample exhibited the
smallest audible diﬀerences between systems, and therefore it is natural to assume based on the
distribution of ratings that assessing this sample in particular carried the highest diﬃculty. This
was also conﬁrmed by several assessors which commented on the diﬃculty when rating this sample
in particular - this will be further discussed in section 6.
Upon visual inspection of the average scores plotted in Figure 4.2, three diﬀerent trends can be
seen in the data. For the EM sample, the less steep ﬁlters produced the greatest perception of bass
depth, while for the JW and ZC samples, the trend is exactly the opposite. In the S sample however,
the trend is almost ﬂat, a shallow inverted bell shape, possibly favoring the less steep ﬁlters ever so
slightly.
The signiﬁcance of these trends were challenged through a two way repeated measures ANOVA. The
analyses did not include the reference and anchor points, as these are added to provide end points
to the ratings. This means that it is expected that there will be a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the
reference and anchor, such that including it in the ANOVA would yield grossly exaggerated results.
Through the ANOVA main eﬀect of sample was found. Main eﬀect of system were not found, likely
due to the opposing trends seen across the diﬀerent samples, and additionally, signiﬁcant interaction
between sample and system was found. This essentially means that there is signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in the trends seen across samples, and that the average rating for any system changes with sample.

Intuitively one might assume that less steep ﬁlters would always produce the greatest perception
of bass depth for any sample, given that there is audible content below the cut-oﬀfrequency. This
should be the case here, as seen in the spectrograms in Figure 3.4. Yet, this prediction only ﬁrmly
holds for the EM sample, and thus, it becomes obvious that the expert assessors are not merely
assessing the very lowest audible frequency component present in the sample. As ratings for bass
depth and bass strength have seen strong correlation in earlier experiments [6], it is reasonable to
assume that the assessors are taking the total amount of bass content into account when attempting
to assess bass depth.
While the ratings for the anchor system is almost unchanged across samples, the reference ﬁlters
exhibit some variation. The reference ratings for EM, S and ZC are all relatively close to each
other, almost within ten points on the scale, but, the average rating for the JW reference system
is signiﬁcantly lower. This would indicate that the JW sample had overall less deep bass than the
other samples, which can indeed be seen in Figure 3.4, and could thus explain why it was perceived
to be more diﬃcult to rate than the other samples.
There is also some variation in distance from the ratings for the reference and anchor systems to the
main systems, especially for the S sample where the trend is ﬂat. One could say that for this sample
the cut-oﬀfrequency of the main systems are more important to the perception of bass depth than
their slopes. Furthermore, the distances gives some information about how eﬀective each system is
in reducing the perceived bass depth relative for each samples. Compare for example the distances
from the reference system ratings to the main system ratings for the EM and ZC samples. For the
EM sample, a larger distance from the reference down to the main systems is seen, meaning that
for this sample the system ﬁlters were more eﬀective in reducing the perceived bass depth. For the
ZC sample, the ﬁlters seem to have been less eﬀective in reducing the perceived bass depth, and
naturally there is also a larger distance down to the anchor rating, as these two samples’ reference
and anchor ratings are very similar. One could theorize that these diﬀerences are a result of the
samples’ frequency content; looking at the main system ﬁlters as a whole, their cut-oﬀfrequency

==PAGE:44==
seem to inﬂuence the perceived bass depth for the EM sample stronger than for the ZC sample,
where bass depth ratings suﬀer less overall. As such, one would assume there is a larger amount of
audible low frequency content in the reference ﬁltered EM sample than in the reference ﬁltered ZC
sample.
Circling back around to the standard deviation table (Table 4.1), it seems rather strange how the
trend for the S sample is ﬂat in the average score plot (Figure 4.2) when the standard deviation
for its ratings for each subject are on par, or even higher than that of the other samples. This
could simply be because assessors were in less agreement of the overall trend for the S sample, and
thus the average trend came out as more ﬂat. If this is the case however, one would think it would
come at the cost of a higher standard error, which is not seen. It could also be attributed to the
reference and anchor ratings being far removed from the main systems. Alas, the anomaly persists
when the data for the reference and anchor ﬁlters are removed from the calculation which ﬁnds
standard deviation of assessor ratings across systems. The deviations for the S sample is lowered
more than the others, but is not signiﬁcantly smaller than that of the other samples. In an attempt
to investigate the cause, the average data for all assessors was plotted together. By visual inspection
it does indeed seem like there is less agreement between assessors for the S sample, which intuitively
would come with increased standard errors relative to the other samples. However, it was found
that while assessors agree more on the overall trends of the other samples, there is in general little
agreement on the exact ratings, and thus, the ratings are spread across the scale which causes their
standard errors to be similar to that of the S sample.
Overall the results for the JW and ZC samples, which were the only samples used in SenseLab’s
experiment, seems to ﬁt well with SenseLab’s ﬁndings. They had found a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in bass depth between two speakers in particular, which due to their shared cut-oﬀfrequency and
loudspeakers size, was though to originate in the vented alignments used in these loudspeaker models.
The data collected here conﬁrms that this might have been part of the cause, as it was found that
diﬀerent vented alignments indeed result in signiﬁcantly diﬀerent bass depth ratings. It would have
been interesting to see both this experiment and SenseLab’s original experiment repeated with a
larger, shared sample library. This would be especially interesting for samples such as the EM
sample, which trend was found to be opposite to the trends of the JW and ZC samples.

5
Model
With the results of the listening test analyzed, we have a good idea of how assessors will perceive bass
depth for these four speciﬁc samples, dependent on the playback systems’ roll-oﬀcharacteristics. In
other words, we know the outputted bass depth ratings for these input samples and systems. We do
however not know how the stimuli is processed to create these outputs; the internal process is still
unknown. To understand this process, a model is created with the goal of recreating the measured
outputs. If the model creation succeeds, it might be able to predict perceived bass depth for any
input library, possibly aiding in loudspeaker design down the line.
When trying to create a model for perceived bass depth, there seems to be two big questions which
must be solved for the model to be able to adequately predict the observed behavior.
• Which sections of the sample are the listener likely to focus on when assessing bass depth?
• How does the auditory system interpret bass depth from those sections?
By looking at the focus section distributions, it can be seen that listeners are in general more likely
to focus on sections which contain a large amount of bass power. Furthermore, a bias towards
sections with lower frequency content is seen. Whether or not the assessor is able to judge bass
depth purely on the lowest frequency component in a given focus section is unknown, but it is safe
to assume that looking at total power within the section will result in a prediction relatively close
to the measured data. For this reason, the model will attempt to predict the measured results by
attempting to select appropriate focus sections and summing the total bass power of these sections
to be compared across the diﬀerent systems. The Matlab code created for the data analysis and
modelling is included in Appendix B.
5.1
Method
The model attempts to predict the assessors’ bass depth ratings by summing the total bass power in
the signal, weighted in time by the predicted focus sections. A block diagram of the model is shown
in Figure 5.1. The model imports the sample library, in this case, the unﬁltered 20 second snippets
of the four samples. The model then loops through each sample, where they are ﬁrst ﬁltered with
each of the system ﬁlters, reference and anchor included. Next the model enters a loop over the
seven ﬁltered snippets, and does a spectrogram calculation to receive a power spectrum estimate for
every 100 ms segment of the ﬁltered sample. This data is then sorted into a matrix, which in essence
is a lower resolution numerical equivalent of the spectrogram seen in Figure 3.4. Subsequently, the
matrix is band pass ﬁltered such that only information about the bass frequencies that are likely
to aﬀect the assessors’ ratings remain, controlled by the f_range parameter. The matrix is then
summed across frequency to obtain the ﬁltered samples’ power over time data.
37
==PAGE:48==
The power over time data is then multiplied with a logical vector, meaning its values are either 0 or
1, that aims to predict the focus sections of the assessor, keep those sections and discard everything
else. Alternatively, the measured focus distribution can be imported and used as a weighting where
the popularity of the diﬀerent sections are accounted for. Multiple approaches were implemented to
attempt to accurately predict the focus sections. The most simple strategy was to ﬁnd the average
power of the reference ﬁltered sample, and discard all data below a multiple of this average for
all systems. This method required some additional smoothing of the power over time data and
ﬁner adjustments of the discard multiplier. A percentile based approach was also attempted, where
only the highest x % power sections were kept. The most successful variant was a peak detection
strategy where the bass frequencies, 20 to 100 Hz, of the reference ﬁltered sample was scanned for
peaks, and a smaller interval around those peaks were kept while the rest was discarded. The time
interval length and maximum number of peaks could be varied, and the algorithm would try to
avoid selecting overlapping sections and would only keep peaks above a certain threshold.
Figure 5.1: Model block diagram.
38
==PAGE:49==
The resulting weighted power over time data is then summed across time to obtain total power in the
ﬁltered sample, and the system loop ends. End point normalization is applied on a per sample basis,
before the collection of rating predictions is given as output. Multiple normalization strategies were
implemented and tested. Among these were normalization based on reference power, total input
sample power, ﬁltered input sample power and rescaling based on the measured end points. To no
surprise the latter gave the better visual ﬁt.
The outputted rating predictions were sorted into a matrix, listing the prediction for all seven
systems and also included a simple measure of the slope of the predictions, excluding reference and
anchor, such that the overall trends could be quickly identiﬁed for a larger sample library.

behavior from the listening experiment, without the parameters seeming overly speciﬁc to the small
sample library. For all presented model predictions, the f_range parameter was set to the interval
20 to 100 Hz.

5.3
Discussion
The ﬁrst model results, with neither normalization nor time ﬁltering as seen in Figure 5.2, show
promising results just by summing the sound power from 20 to 100 Hz. While the normalization is
very clearly of by visual inspection, there seems to be a fairly good ﬁt at least for the ZC sample,
while the ﬁt for the S sample seems mediocre. Due to the diﬀerence in predicted reference ratings,
not much can be said about the EM and JW samples without zooming in.
In spite of lacking
normalization the r2 values presented in Table 5.1 shows great correlation for both the JW and ZC
sample, at 0.94 and 0.92 respectively. For the EM and S samples the model output and measured
data are seen to be almost fully uncorrelated, while the total r2 value comes in at an uneventful
0.42.
Multiple attempts were made to better normalize the results not reliant on measured ratings. Strate-
gies based on total input power, total input bass power, reference normalization and others were
attempted, none too successful. In the end it was decided to simply rescale the end points, reference
and anchor, to perfectly ﬁt the measured data, as this would not aﬀect the individual correlation
values, but make visual inspection of the results easier. The resulting data is plotted in Figure 5.3
and reﬂects the calculated correlation data, presented in Table 5.2, more clearly. The lower r2 values
of the EM and S sample now seem quite obvious, while the accuracy of the model for the JW and
ZC samples can be better appreciated.
When the measured focus distribution is introduced as time domain weighting, Figure 5.4 and
Table 5.3, drastic improvement is seen for the EM sample, which jumps from an r2 value of 0.01
to 0.89. However the correlation for the S sample falls to 0.01 from 0.05. The r2 value of JW
falls by a single point, while ZC stays at 0.92. Due to the drastic improvement for EM the total
model correlation grows as well, to a respectable 0.73. The correlation values are supported by
visual inspection of the plotted data, where quite a nice ﬁt is seen for EM, JW and ZC. While the
correlation value for the S sample is low, the trend of a much ﬂatter distribution is indeed present
in the predicted data. However the slight "u" or inverted bell shape is missing.
The results of the peak detection algorithm, aimed to predict focus sections is shown in Figure 5.5.
As seen the tallest peaks are correctly chosen as the only data to keep for the power summation.
Comparing with the measured focus distribution as seen in Figure 4.3, the predicted focus sections
only match well for the EM and JW samples, likely due to the nature of these samples’ power over
time curves, where there are fewer high power sections to choose between. In the plotted data, see
Figure 5.6, the ﬁt has indeed improved signiﬁcantly for the EM sample versus when no weighting was
43
==PAGE:54==
used, similarly to when the measured focus distribution is used as weighting. This is unsurprising as
the predicted focus was accurate for this sample. Furthermore, this sample’s individual correlation
using the predicted focus weighting is larger than for the measured focus weighting, up from 0.89
to 0.94. This is likely due to the fact that the measured focus sections overshoots slightly more
on both sides of the power peaks. The r2 values for the JW and ZC samples are unchanged, and
for the S sample it increases from 0.01 to 0.11. The total r2 however falls slightly, which may
seem counterintuitive. By visual comparison of Figure 5.4 and Figure 5.6, samples JW and ZC
are indeed very similar as hinted by their respective r2 values being equal, while there is a slightly
better visual ﬁt for the EM sample. Even though the correlation for the S sample is higher the ﬁt
actually seems to be worse, this was found to be due to the r value in fact being negative, meaning
there is negative correlation between model and measured ratings. This also explains the drop in
total correlation.
It is worth mentioning that even though correlation for the S sample was quite poor, the model did
not predict any large diﬀerences between its diﬀerent systems, excluding reference and anchor. In
fact the diﬀerences between the lowest and highest ratings for the measured and predicted ratings
are quite similar, and for the measured data they were found to not be statistically signiﬁcant. As
such, one could say that the r2 value is a bit strict of a measure for this sample; the trend is wrong,
but the model correctly predicted a lower variance between ratings across systems. An improved
focus section prediction algorithm might further improve results.
Taking everything into account, the model seems to conﬁrm that listeners will tend to focus on
sections with a larger amount of total bass power when asked to assess bass depth. Furthermore, it
is found that summing the bass power in these sections will give an adequate prediction of the relative
bass depth rating for most samples, such that the trend across diﬀerent roll-oﬀcharacteristics can
be found.

6
General Discussion
This section aims to further discuss the experiment and its results, the model and its results,
improvements that could have been made to either as well as further work.
During the experiment’s initial planning stage, musical stimuli was chosen to keep relevancy to
SenseLab’s experiment. A slightly expanded selection of musical tracks was prioritized over adding
multiple stimuli types to keep experiment duration low and thus, allow for more repetitions. How-
ever, it would have been interesting to redo the experiment with a larger selection of stimuli, both
a larger library of musical stimuli, as well as other stimuli types and investigate if the observed
patterns hold.
While the experiment was being held, most assessors commented on the diﬃculty of the test,
stressing that its diﬃculty was rather high, especially for the JW sample. In hindsight it might
have been a good idea to shorten the sample snippets down closer to ten seconds, as the employees
of SenseLab later pointed out this is a tool they use for the more advanced and/or harder test when
they wish to lower the tests overall diﬃculty, or help the assessors along. Multiple assessors also
expressed a want for the presentation level to be raised, as they felt it would make the test easier
and less frustrating. One can only speculate into whether or not lowering the diﬃculty using these
two methods would have resulted in a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent data set and/or stronger agreement
between assessors and/or between measured and predicted results.
The presentation level was chosen to imitate a normal listening experience, with levels set at around
70 dB SPL, a-weighted. While it was assumed that this would allow most of the very lowest fre-
quency components present in the samples to be audible by inspection of Figure 2.7, no measure-
ments were made to establish the actual limits. It became clear when a few subjects asked for
higher levels "to hear the bass better", that the level of some components might have been too
close to the threshold of hearing for some assessors. Especially for the S sample, the author was
surprised to see many subjects focus on the second, third or forth 50 Hz tones, when the low 30s
Hz tone just before it seemed like an obvious point of focus. However, as these other peaks also
contain signiﬁcantly more power, the assessors might have focused there either way. Regardless, it
would have been valuable to do a peak SPL measurement of all samples to compare with the zero
phon equal loudness contour. This way, which frequency components were likely inaudible to the
assessors would have been known.
When trying to model the measured results, normalization became an issue. While the individ-
ual correlation values were good, the spread of the diﬀerent systems or system diﬀerences across
samples could not be analyzed without some form of normalization. After numerous attempts at
normalization failed, the predicted results were simply rescaled to perfectly ﬁt between the measured
reference and anchor ratings for each sample. This is a problem as the model should preferably
be able to calculate and present this information for a large library of samples without relying on
its endpoints being rated beforehand. Towards the very end of the thesis however, an additional
45
==PAGE:56==
normalization strategy was found which showed promise. By normalizing by the bass power of the
signal present in the anchor system ﬁltered sample, and then taking the square root, a near perfect
normalization was achieved. When looking at correlation between the endpoints calculated through
this new normalization and the measured endpoints, an r2 value of 0.99 was found. Using this
normalization, all predicted anchor points will be given the same value. However, this should not
be an issue, as the measured anchor ratings were found to all be very similar. As this new strategy
was found so close to the thesis deadline, there was no time to properly implement this new strategy
into the model, create new ﬁgures and correlation tables and redo large sections of the modelling
section. Thus, this ﬁnding is only included here, as a bonus to indefatigable readers.
Through the focus section form, it was found that assessors would most often focus on the highest
power sections of the sample. Furthermore, a tendency to focus on sections with lower frequency
components was seen as well, regardless of these sections’ lower power.
However, as the focus
prediction part of the model was largely frequency invariant, and analyzed only at the tallest bass
power peaks, such sections were not included in the predicted focus sections. While this approach
resulted in suﬃcient focus predictions for three out of the four samples, it did not match well
for the S sample, were the ﬁrst lower power, lower 30s Hz bass note, saw large presence in the
measured focus sections, but was absent in the predicted one. By implementing additional ﬁltering
into the prediction algorithm or creating a more advanced prediction algorithm from scratch which
to a larger extent takes lower frequency power into account, correlation might be improved for
this sample. Brief testing was done using a custom focus prediction, consisting of only the ﬁrst,
lower frequency tone, and the third tone, which resulted in an r2 value of 0.66 for this sample.
This indicates that there is potential for a focus prediction algorithm which to a larger degree
takes frequency into account, however, such an algorithm might bias the focus sections of the other
samples in a way that makes overall correlation worse.
Earlier in the thesis a second, smaller experiment was also planned. In this experiment the model
would be ran for a new library of samples, which would then be rated by assessors using an in-
room setup, to see how well the model would work in a more natural listening environment. For
this purpose, a subwoofer enclosure was designed and built, using a SEAS XM001-04 subwoofer,
kindly lent from SEAS in Norway. The closed box subwoofer, with accompanying equalization ﬁlters
was designed to have a ﬂat in room frequency response down to 20 Hz, to provide an appropriate
playback chain. Sadly this second experiment did not see the light of day due to time constraints,
but it would certainly have been very interesting to see how the model would have performed in
"the real world". Perhaps an enthusiastic author will experiment with this in his spare time.
Overall, it seems that listeners tend to take the total bass power into account when assessing bass
depth, which as previously stated is strongly correlated with preference. Accordingly one could
say that, as long as the speaker is not spectrally unbalanced, a speaker with both louder bass and
lower cut-oﬀfrequency is likely to be preferred. Therefore, one could theorize that the best design
choice for a speaker, when looking purely at its frequency response, is whichever roll-oﬀfrequency

==PAGE:57==
and slope that results in the higher amount of bass power output. If the speaker has a low cut-oﬀ
frequency, that is below the lower frequency components of most musical pieces, an alignment with
a steeper roll-oﬀshould be chosen, such as the 0.1 dB ripple Chebyshev. Accordingly if the cut-oﬀ
frequency of the speaker is such that there is signiﬁcant content below it, a less steep roll-oﬀshould
be chosen, such as the Bessel or synchronous alignments. Of course the diﬀerent alignments and
their cut-oﬀs come with diﬀerent box size and loudspeaker driver limitations which must be included
in the design process.

7
Conclusion
An investigation regarding "Perceptual aspects of the electrodynamic loudspeaker response" has
concluded. After initial research the thesis set out to investigate how diﬀerent loudspeaker roll-oﬀ
characteristics are perceived, in addition to attempt to model the observed behavior. This was done
through the design and completion of a listening experiment, analysis of the experiment data and
subsequent modelling and analysis of model performance.
The idea for the listening experiment spawned from results published from one of SenseLab’s prior
experiments, where there was found to be no relationship between experienced bass depth and tech-
nical data for lower cut-oﬀfrequency. The experiment was designed to mimic SenseLab’s experi-
ment, utilizing the same facilities, experimental procedure and listening panel. Five experimental
systems were created to simulate the frequency roll-oﬀcharacteristics of diﬀerent, typically used,
vented loudspeaker designs, all with a shared cut-oﬀfrequency of 50 Hz. A reference and an anchor
system was also included in the experiment. The systems were applied to the two samples used
in SenseLab’s experiment in addition to two new samples, all of which were 20 second snippets of
musical pieces. During the experiment listeners were asked to rate the bass depth of each system
and sample combination. They were also asked to write down what sections of the sample they
focused on when assessing the attribute.
Between the 14 assessors which completed the listening test, 2268 individual data points were
collected, 81 for each of the 28 diﬀerent system/sample combinations. When plotted grouped by
sample, three diﬀerent trends were found in the ratings. For the EM sample, bass depth ratings
fell as the steepness of the system ﬁlters increased, for the S sample, the trend was ﬂat, and for the
JW and ZC samples, the samples used in SenseLab’s experiment, bass depth ratings increased with
increasing system ﬁlter steepness. Through a two way repeated measures ANOVA main eﬀect of
sample was found in addition to signiﬁcant interaction between sample and system. The focus forms
revealed that the assessors tended to focus on sections with higher total bass power. A bias towards
lower frequency sections was also seen. Due to these observations it was theorized that the behavior
could be model by summing bass power in the highest power sections of each sample.
Modelling eﬀorts resulted in very good r2 values for individual samples when focus predictions
were applied to the model. Overall the model could explain around 70 % of the variance found
in the measured data when additional normalization based on measured ratings was used. This
was achieved by summing the total bass power present in the samples, weighted in time by a focus
section prediction. Close to the thesis conclusion a new normalization method not reliant on prior
end point ratings was found. With some ﬁnal modiﬁcations, the model is likely to be able to make
competent predictions about the trend of bass depth ratings for any given sample and input ﬁlter
collection. Additionally it should also be able to predict with good accuracy the relative bass depth
ratings of diﬀerent samples. Thus, the model could be used as a tool to help choose appropriate
box designs.
49
==PAGE:60==
To summarize, the thesis has contributed with the following:
• Listening experiment to investigate how diﬀerent loudspeaker roll-oﬀcharacteristics are per-
ceived. Results show three diﬀerent trends which leads to the conclusion that perception of
bass depth is highly reliant on source material.
• Model that with some modiﬁcations can with acceptable accuracy predict the trends in per-
ceived bass depth for a any given ﬁlter set or sample library.
It is recommended that the model be tested for a larger sample library to further establish the
accuracy of its predictions.

Kære testdeltager 
I dette forsøg skal du lytte til syv forskellige simulerede højttalere, hvoraf 5 har små 
forskelle i bassen, de to andre er henholdsvis reference og anker – dvs. én som har 
markant større basdybde end de andre, og én som har markant mindre basdybde end de 
andre. Din opgave er at vurdere basdybden på alle 7 højttalere. 
Se figuren herunder, for testlayout og beskrivelse af attributten ”Basdybde”. Er du i tvivl 
om hvad attributten betyder, så spørg testlederen. Husk at læse attributdefinitionen 
grundigt! 
Det er vigtigt at du bruger skalaen, og sikrer dig at resultaterne ikke klumper sammen på 
midten/toppen/bunden, men undlad at overdrive dine resultater. Hører du en forskel i 
basdybden på ”A” og ”B”, er det vigtigt at den også reflekteres på skalaen. 
Forsøget indeholder 28 vurderinger pr. gentagelse, og du kommer til at foretage så mange 
gentagelser som du kan nå, indenfor de to timer testen tager. Hver af de tests, du er 
inviteret til, indeholder 3 gentagelser. Bliver du færdig med en gentagelse, skal du bare gå 
videre til den næste. Prioritér gode grundige vurderinger fremfor antallet af vurderinger, 
det er ikke vigtigt at du når igennem det hele, men det er vigtigt at du gør dig umage med 
vurderingerne. 
Pga. testens sværhedsgrad, kan det være en hjælp at zoome ind på mindre områder af 
lydeksemplerne, men du skal lytte lydeksemplerne igennem mindst én gang pr. gentagelse 
(Dvs. pr. 28 skærmbilleder!) Før du begynder at afgive vurderinger. Hvis du vælger at 
fokusere på et mindre område af en sample, bedes du markere, på side to i denne 
instruktion. 
Der vil være en kort træningstest, inden selve testen går i gang, brug træningen til at 
blive bekendt med testens format og layout, samt til at lytte på forskellene mellem 
lydeksemplerne. 
Hold gerne pauser, og spørg testinstruktøren hvis du er i tvivl om noget.
