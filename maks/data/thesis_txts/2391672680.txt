Abstract
The goal of this research was to propose a design of a tooling process chains for the paper bottle man-
ufacturing. In order to complete this task, a replication study has to be conducted. The replication
study investigated static and functional features. The investigated static feature, which has no direct
inﬂuence on the use of the tool was a tool’s surface roughness replication on the paper product. A series
of experiments was conducted with a use of four master plates. Those master plates were machined
with a die sinking EDM process to achieve diﬀerent surface roughness Sa values ranging from 0.22 µm
to 20 µm. Replications on the paper disc samples obtained from these experiments were analysed. The
functional features replication study investigated features with a direct inﬂuence on the use of the tool -
water removal channels. A master plate was prepared using a µED milling process with a diﬀerent sized
circular holes, ranging from diameter 35 µm to 1000 µm. After the experiments it was determined that
the biggest channel not giving an impression visible with a naked eye was a hole of a 200 µm diameter.
From both of these studies, a series of requirements and speciﬁcations was formulated for the design of
the tooling process chain. A research of possible manufacturing methods was conducted. Several designs
of tooling process chains were proposed with attempts of validation.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Nowadays, one of the most widely used material for any kind of packaging is plastic.
It has been
used already for a signiﬁcant amount of time. It is considered as a quite cheap and easy to manufacture
material. However, not everyone is aware of the environmental costs of using materials like plastic without
having a working recycling solutions in place. Plastic has a signiﬁcantly long period of degradation. A
plastic bottle thrown away today, could be still found 50 years later, being a constant threat to wildlife
and fauna. Plastic production depletes as well oil resources, which are not renewable.[1, 2]
Figure 1: Principles behind the Green Fiber Bottle Project. [3]
Therefore, a need for fully recyclable containers intended also for more advanced purposes has emerged
[4]. Extensive researches are being carried out in order for moulded pulp products (MPP) - products
moulded from paper pulp, to fulﬁll this need, as for instance sustainable containers for diﬀerent kinds of
refreshments, such as soda and beer [4]. The Danish Technical University (DTU) in a cooperation with
a major beer producer - Carlsberg and a moulded pulp products manufacturing company - EcoXpac, is
trying to develop a viable solution of ﬁnding an environmentally friendly material for bottle production,
and an alternative for plastic. From this cooperation a project was born to develope a feasible method
of producing a "Green Fiber Bottle" (GFB). Such a bottle would be able to degrade in the environment
in just seven weeks and be recycled as a paper product. [5] In order to achieve the best results of use of
paper ﬁbers, an eﬃcient and eﬀective production process has to be developed. A major impact on the
production process has a properly designed tool for the moulding process.

1.2
Scope of the Study
The scope of this study is to investigate the tool in the green ﬁber bottle production. A new, more
eﬃcient tool has to be developed, therefore a tooling process chain has to be designed. As a result a new
tool could be produced, that would improve the GFB production, not only in time, but in aesthetic and
surface quality as well.
In this thesis a series of connected studies were conducted to ﬁnd a valid constrains for the tooling process
chain. Both static features and functional features of the tool were considered. Static features having
an indirect inﬂuence on the ﬁnal product are less important for the functionality of the ﬁnal product,
but crucial for the aesthetics. The investigated static feature was the surface morphology of the tool and
the replication capabilities on the ﬁnal product. The functional features has a major inﬂuence on the
success of the process, without them the process would be impossible to produce a ﬁnal product. The
investigated functional features were the excess water removal channels and how they inﬂuence the water
removal rate, as well as the quality of the surface of the ﬁnal product.
The results of both of these studies were included in the constrain formulation for the tooling process
chain. Several tooling process chains were investigated, in order to manufacture a test disc to validate the
results of the previous studies. Test disc was a simpliﬁed model of the tool used for the GFB production.
However, the investigation of the tooling process chains was conducted while keeping in mind a more
complex geometry of the ﬁnal tool to produce a shape of the bottle.

2.2
Production Setup at EcoXpac
The existing bottle production setup at the EcoXpac facility is of a thermoformed (thin-walled) type,
using the pressure moulding principle, like in the Muller process. This setup is presented in the Figure 9.
Figure 9: Setup used for bottle production, with a drying mould (left) and the forming mould (right).
Production of the GFB can be divided into two stages. In the ﬁrst stage the shape of the bottle is formed
in the forming mould by supplying the pulp inside the mould and supplying the vacuum from outside
the mould in the vacuum chamber. Forming mould is lined inside with a wired mesh allowing the excess
water to be ﬂushed away while collecting the paper ﬁbres. Figure 10 shows the forming mould. After
this stage, the bottle has retained the shape of the mould, but is still containing water. This water has
to be removed in the drying stage, using a drying mould (made out of a porous aluminum to allow the
moisture escape, shown in the Figure 11). The drying mould is heated up to speed up the drying process.
A pressure is applied by an inﬂatable insert from the inside of the bottle, against the mould.
The process of producing a bottle follows the procedure:

1. Forming - the forming mould is tightly closed.
2. Pulp injection - pulp is injected from the constantly stirred container to the inside of the forming

3. Draining - while the injecting is still undergoing, the vacuum is applied from the vacuum chamber

heated. An inﬂatable insert is placed inside the bottle through the neck. The insert is inﬂated with
a desired pressure. The insert applies the pressure on the drying bottle from the inside and against
the mould.
7. Removing - after the bottle is dried, the insert is deﬂated and removed from the mould. The
mould opens and the ﬁnished bottle is collected.

2.3
Surface Metrology
To properly assess the results of the manufacturing processes, a suitable method of accurate measuring
has to be applied to achieve meaningful results. The discipline that deals with the quality of the surface
morphology is the surface metrology. The studies conducted in this research dealt mostly with a sub
millimeter dimensions, therefore an accurate method of measuring dimensions in micrometers had to
be applied. The traditional measuring devices, like calipers or contact CMMs (Coordinate Measuring
Machine) were not suitable for this task. A new improved measuring devices capable of measuring in
micro scale emerged - the optical microscopes. Those microscope are capable of acquiring an image of
the surface topography for further analysis. Two microscopes were used in this thesis to acquire images:
• Alicona InﬁniteFocus,
• Olympus LEXT OLS4000.
Alicona InﬁniteFocus is a focus variation microscope, shown in the Figure 13.
Alicona provides an
acquisition of a traceable measurements, with a high resolution, high repeatability and accuracy. [17]
Focus variation technique combines optiacl system’s shallow depth of focus and an ability of vertical
scanning. To acquire the image of the sample, an optimal objective position is selected on each layer of
the analysed surface. [18] The functional structure of a focus variation microscope is shown in the Figure

2.3.1
Surface Topography
The acquired images of the surface topography have to be described. The analysed characteristic in
this research regarding the surface topography is mainly the surface roughness.
Surface topography,
or surface geometry, can be broken down into more basic structures, based on their function. These
basic structures can diﬀer between each other in size, shape, location on the surface or height. All these
structures all together create the surface topography with no distinct reference to ease the measurement
and quantiﬁcation. In the most basic terms, the surface topography could be described as the deviations
from the ideal desired shape. But often it is not that easy. There are three major types of deviations:
[22]
• Roughness - the deviations directly on the surface, left as a result of the previous manufacturing
on the workpiece. Can reﬂect the shape of the used tool, or even the manufacturing process itself.
• Waviness - the deviations that have a longer wavelength, typically as a result of an improper or
faulty manufacturing process. Example could be the vibration of the tool during machining.
• Form Errors - deviations with a waves even longer than the waviness, diﬃcult to measure, caused
by the faulty slideways, rotating parts or thermal distortion.
The roughness and waviness is typically considered as the surface topography, or texture, shown in the
Figure 17. It is important to note that those deviations not always occur together. Therefore, measuring
of something that we do not know if it’s there or not can cause troubles or even errors in assessment.
Similarly, there is no clear solution whether to separate those deviations and evaluate only one of them
or bundle them together. [22]
The surface roughness is usually the most important and common to measure and describe, as it includes
the deviations left on the surface after the machining processes. Those deviations will always be on the
surface, even if the process is working perfectly. On the other hand, the waviness might not be present,

Replication Study
3.1
Scope of the Replication Study
As stated in the scope of the entire project, the goal is to develop a new more eﬃcient mould for the
Green Fiber Bottle (GFB) production. In order to succeed, a replication study has to be conducted.
There are two kinds of features that are regarded during the replication: Static Features and Functional
Features, as seen in the Figure 19.
Figure 19: Features analysed during the replication study.
Replication of both of those features depend on the replicated medium. In case of this project it is paper
pulp. Therefore, the selection of the features has to depend on the ﬁber properties. The static features
are the features that has an indirect inﬂuence on the manufacturing process of the paper bottles. The
static feature analysed in this thesis was the surface roughness. The surface roughness of the tool (mould)
used for the GFB production has no inﬂuence on the success of the process. However, using a various
surface roughness in the tool will result in a varied smoothness of the manufactured bottle. This could
be important from the aesthetic point of view. Usually, for the customer the nicer (smoother) the surface
the higher probability that the product will be bought. Green Fiber Bottles has to compete with the
already used plastic and glass bottles, which are smooth. On the other hand, producing a smooth tool
is more diﬃcult and more costly that producing a rougher one. Therefore, a limit has to be found. A
limit of a surface roughness that is imprinted in the paper, and while using a smoother tool, the surface
of the bottle is not getting smoother. The ﬁrst part of the study is focused on the roughness replication.
Second part deals with the functional features replication. In case of the GFB production, the functional
features in the used mould are the channels used for water excess removal. This has a direct inﬂuence on
the functionality of the mould. The more and the bigger are the channels the faster water can be removed
and in the result the bottle can be dried. However, those channels will be imprinted in the surface of the
manufactured bottle, and will distort its shape. What is required, are the channels for which the imprint
is not visible with a naked eye, but that will allow fast and eﬃcient water removal. The second part of
the replication study deals with the functional features.
From the replication study, a limit values for both the static and functional features will be known.
Surface roughness, and dimensions and shape of the channels. Those parameters can be then used as

4.2
Die Sinking EDM
Die sinking EDM is one of the most relevant variants of the electrical discharge machining (EDM). EDM
is a non-mechanical process of thermal shaping. Material of the workpiece is removed by the mean of
numerous consecutive electrical discharges between the said workpiece and a tool electrode. This process
is widely used for the mould and die manufacturing, and is emerging as a viable method for production
of ﬁnal parts also in the micro dimensions. The process is rather complicated with great interdependence
between the process parameters. At it’s most basic level the die sinking process can be described by three
elements: [26, 27]
• Tool Electrode - reproduces the shape in the workpiece by the electrical discharges,
• Spark - generated and controlled by the generator, is a mean by which the removal of the material
takes place,
• Workpiece - is the material which is being machined by the sparks in the shape of the tool electrode.
The graphical illustration can be seen in the Figure 21.
The characteristic of the die sinking EDM
process gives a ﬂexibility in the material selection for machining, as the hardness or the Young’s modulus

of the material has no inﬂuence on the results. However, there are some limitations. First of all, the
used material has to be electrically conductive, with a minimal conductivity of κ = 0.01S/cm. Also the
higher the homogeneity of the used material the lower the chance of selecting wrong parameters’ values,
as the material segregation can cause shape errors because of the diﬀerence in the material removal
rate and intensity. Other limitations include troubles in ﬂushing of the debris material in deep slots,
tongue formation in the long and thin necks, temperature variations during erosion can cause variations
in the linear dimensions. [27] In the EDM processes there is an occurrence of the thermal inﬂuence on
the surface of the machined workpiece. This thermal inﬂuence zone’s thickness is proportional to the
used pulse energy. To obtain low thermal stresses the pulse energy should be reduced, this however will
lower the speed of the machining. There is a need of maintaining an optimal compromise between the
process’ speed and resulting surface quality. [27] The three mainly used materials for the tool electrodes
are copper, tungsten and graphite. All of them has pros and cons. In this study, for the die sinking
EDM machining a copper electrodes were used. To produce the master plates a planetary motion of the
tool electrode was applied. It allows better ﬂushing of the removed debris and enhances the uniform
distribution of the wear, resulting in a longer life of the electrode. During this type of eroding, the tool
electrode moves relative to the workpiece about the axis perpendicular to the machined surface, as shown
in the Figure 22.
As shown in the Figure 21 the whole process takes place in the surrounding dielectric ﬂuid. The ﬂuid’s
functions are mainly: [26, 27]
• Isolation the tool electrode from the workpiece,
• Cooling down the tool electrode and the workpiece,
• Removal of the machined debris.

The three main process parameters in die sinking EDM are pulse length - T, idle voltage - U and pulse
intensity - I. The pulse length has an inﬂuence on the size of the gap between the tool electrode and the
workpiece. The longer the pulse is the bigger is the gap, also the removal rate and the wear of the tool
decreases, while the surface roughness increases. Otherwise, the shorter the pulse the better the surface
roughness. The idle voltage is used as the optimisation parameter as it has an inﬂuence of the gap size.
The last parameter I has the inﬂuence on the gap size as well, and on the resulting roughness. Other
important parameters to consider are timer - parameter that keeps the dielectric ﬂuid clean between the
tool electrode and the workpiece, pause (P) - describes the time between the subsequent pulses, and
compression - the distance between the tool electrode and the workpiece. [27]
The whole process of EDM machining can be simpliﬁed to a number of discharges between the tool
electrode and the workpiece. The single discharge can be divided into four phases:
1. Build-up phase,
2. Ignition phase,
3. Discharge phase,
4. Breakdown phase.
The phases are graphically presented in the Figure 23. In the build-up phase a voltage is applied, causing
the electrons to move and the current to start ﬂowing. Consequently, the dielectric ﬂuid is heated up above
the boiling temperature, it vaporizes and forms a plasma channel. In the ignition phase the electrons are
accelerated and cause ionization increasing the number of charge carriers. The strength of the dielectric
ﬂuid is exceeded. Also the change in the current and voltage occurs - voltage drops and current increases.

4.4
Observations after the Experiments
During the ﬁrst phase of the experiments several observations were noted and few mistakes were made.
To come up with a viable parameters for the moulding machine, few test runs were conducted. Those
test runs were initially conducted using a pulp mixture of an unreﬁned pulp. This pulp was deemed
unsuitable for the experiments as the water retention properties were too high for reasonable water
removal during the forming phase. The disks formed with this kind of pulp contained too much water
to obtain good impression of the insert in the moulding phase.
Even obtaining a uniform disk was
impossible. One possible solution to overcome this obstacle and still use the unreﬁned pulp would be
to extend the vacuum water removal step in the forming phase. However the available vacuum source
in a form of a vacuum cleaner could not handle an extended period of action as it could overheat. One
disk was tested with the vacuum step conducted for approximately 10 minutes (in comparison to the 25
seconds when using a diﬀerent pulp mixture), but the results were not satisfying and neither this disk
was moulded properly. After that a change to the pulp mixture of an unreﬁned pulp and ecoX-pulp
was made. Another notable point, was that the test runs and the actual experiments for the insert with
a surface roughness value Sa of 20 µm were conducted using a wired mesh as a backing plate. The
wired mesh provides excellent uniform water ﬂow through the micro holes and quick water removal, but
at the same time is not stiﬀand smooth enough. The resulting disk’s surface roughness was optically
more rough than the surface roughness of the disks made with the porous bronze backing plate. The
measured surface roughness was also proved to be higher. Therefore, a suspicion that this could inﬂuence
the impression of the insert’s surface quality on the disk aroused. It was decided that the experiments
with the insert with the surface roughness value Sa 20 µm have to be repeated with the porous bronze
backing plate, to keep all the conditions and parameters identical for all of the inserts. Few concerns
aroused in connection with the used pressure level. The moulding machine was capable of pressing with
a pressure level greatly exceeding the capabilities of the bottle making machine, therefore if using a high
pressure level the results could not be compared between the 2D disc results and potential experiments
on the bottle making machine. Also there was the aforementioned issue of the pressure drop and not
accurate peak pressure recognition. To counter those issues, a reasonably high pressure level was chosen,
that keeps the pressure even after the drop. It was decided that using a higher pressure will be beneﬁcial
for a good impression and the surface roughness value will never exceed the one made with high pressure
level while potentially using a lower pressure level in the future on a diﬀerent setup.

Functional Features Replication Study
The second part of the replication study deals with the replication of the functional features - shape and
dimensions of the water removal channels.
5.1
Feature Selection
As a water removal channels two basic shapes were regarded. A circular hole and a rectangular slot. A
circular hole gives a shape that can be easily manufactured, densely placed and the surface of the mould
in a uniform distance between each separate feature, and gives a uniform water removal. A rectangular
slot is more vaguely described as it have two major dimension, width and height, in contrast to only one
dimension in case of a hole - diameter. As described in the Figure 55.
Figure 55: Dimensions of the regarded water removal channels.
Those dimensions greatly depend on the ﬁber properties. The ﬁber length used for the experiments was
between 1.2 to 1.9 mm, while the width was between 25 to 35 µm. Depending on the chosen dimensions
the imprint mechanism can diﬀer. In case of a round hole, there are virtually no chances for a single ﬁber
to not be pressed, as the only possibility is for the single ﬁber to be placed upright vertically. In case of
a rectangular slot, the chances are higher, therefore, a square shape should be disregarded. A thin slot
with one dimension much bigger then the other should be used.
Two inserts were manufactured, one with a range of circular holes and one with a range of rectangular
slots. For the manufacturing of the circular holes a µEDM machine was used. For the rectangular slots
a die-sinking EDM process was used.

5.3
Electrical Discharge Machining
The general principle of the EDM process was already described in the previous chapter. Here a more
focus will be put on the µED milling and in part on wire EDM (WEDM). The µED milling is not
as commonly used as the die sinking EDM. In this process a rotating electrode is moved through the
workpiece on a deﬁned three-dimensional path. During this movement, the material of the workpiece is
removed by the continuous discharges. As a result a standard shaped electrode can be used to produce
various shapes without designing and manufacturing a special dedicated electrode for each operation.
The material used for the electrode has to have high strength, common used materials are:
• cemented carbide,
• tungsten,
• tungsten copper.
The µED is similar in it’s principle to the traditional milling. The rotating tool with a cutting edge is

produces a smaller diameter of the tool electrode. Thanks to this operation, only one size of electrodes

5.4
Pre-study of the EDM process
Diﬀerent hole sizes were selected to be analysed in the replication study.
The diameter to depth of
the holes ratio was decided to be kept 1:2. The reason for that was the easier manufacturing by EDM
methods and easier comparison of the same ratio of diﬀerent sized holes. The selected diameters and
depths are presented in the Table 13. The diameters varied from 35 to 1000 µm. Two methods of hole
manufacturing were investigated - pocketing and drilling. Pocketing is one of the most commonly used
operation in machining. In this operation the material is removed by a ﬁxed depth on the ﬂat surface, in
an area determined by a closed boundary. The tool in the pocketing operation is following an identical,
ﬁxed path on each depth layer. [35] In the case of this set of experiments, the boundary for the pocketing
operation was circular, with a diameter overlaying the channel diameter. The tool path started in the
center of the hole and moved outwards in a circular shape movement, as shown in the Figure 58.
Drilling, on the other hand, involves only rotation of the tool in a stationary position. Therefore, an
electrode has to be used with a diameter allowing the machined hole to be of the desired diameter. The
electrode starts above the center of the machined hole and moves downwards while removing the material.
The resulting hole has a diameter of the electrode plus the erosion of the material from the sides of the
used electrode.
A pre-study was conducted to determine which of these operations is more suitable for the given task.
A series of operations with diﬀerent channel sizes were machined using the pocketing operation. Then
the same dimensions were tried to be replicated by drilling. For all of the operations a 300 µm diameter
electrode was used. To achieve smaller diameter of the electrode, the electrode was dressed using the wire
EDM setup attached to the Sarix machine. Operations were performed on the test blocks made from the
same material as the actual inserts - STAVAX stainless steel.

5.4.2
Measurements
Measurements were conducted by acquiring the images of the manufactured holes using the Alicona
InﬁniteFocus microscope and then anaylsing those images using the SPIP software. Microscope Alicona
works in a similar manner to the previously used Olympus LEXT, regarding the handling of the image
acquisition. For the proper image acquisition few parameters had to be correctly set. Mainly the light
settings. Quality of the image greatly depended on the good light source, as the measured features were
micro holes with an diameter to depth ratio 1:2. ’Exposure time’ and ’Contrast’ were corresponding to
the light brightness and time of the image acquisition. Oversaturation with light resulted in oversaturated
pixels (presented with red colour) and spikes in the acquired topography, while the underlighted spots
resulted in the void pixels and holes in the acquired topography. Vertical and lateral resolution of the
acquired image could be modiﬁed for ﬁne tuning of the acquisition. However, generally the default values
gave satisfying results. To select the whole spectrum of the feature, a proper magniﬁcation lens had to
be manually selected, so that the hole is fully visible on the screen. After that a focus had to be set on
the surface of the workpiece using the focus knob. Then the focus was moved a bit up and the upper
Z position was selected. Then, focus was moved down until the bottom of the manufactured hole was
visible on the screen. Similarly, focus had to be moved a bit down from that position and the lower Z
position was selected. With such selected range the image acquisition could commence. Acquired images
could be saved and exported to the SPIP software.
In the SPIP software two dimensions had to be measured, the diameter and the depth of the hole. Before
the measurements plane correction was applied to remove the potential tilt of the sample, in the same
manner as in the case of the surface roughens analysis described in the previous chapter. For the diameter
measurements three approaches were tested. Those approaches were:
• Using the SPIP’s ’Particle and Pore Analysis’ module for detecting the hole and it’s diameter
(Figure 63),
• Using the ’Measure Shape’ function to draw a circle shape over the hole (Figure 64),
• Using the average proﬁle to mark the edges of the hole and note the distance between the mark-
ers(Figure 65).
What can be seen from these approaches, is that they give similar results. However, all of them have
some drawbacks. Method using the ’Particle and Pore Analysis’ should be the most accurate as it uses
an algorithm to ﬁnd and calculate the diameter. In most cases it works ﬂawlessly, but if the surface of
the workpiece around the manufactured hole is not ﬂat enough to distinguish from the hole area or there
are void pixels in the image, then the algorithm can not detect the hole properly. Example of a situation
like that, can be seen in the Figure 66.
When using the Measure Shape’ option, the accuracy of the measurement depends on the operator’s
accuracy to draw a circle over the hole. This can result in slight diﬀerences from the actual diameter, but
other than that, this method is probably the most reliable. Using the average proﬁle can be accurate,
but only in cases when the hole is uniformly round. Deviations from roundness can result in a diﬀerent

Results of Pocketing and Drilling
Comparison of the results of the holes manufactured by pocketing and drilling operation is presented in the

tries were taken, but the electrode was too thin after wire dressing to successfully complete the drilling
operation - it broke every time.
In the acquired image of the drilling operation of 50 µm using the
Sarix technology parameters it was impossible to measure the depth of the hole, as the bottom was not
recognized by the microscope. For the 200 µm pocketing operation two electrode sizes were used to
compare the results - 100 µm and 150 µm. The ﬁrst one resulted in a closer to the desired diameter, but
the operation took longer to complete, while the second electrode gave better completion of the depth
task. The drilling operation for 50 µm diameter was performed using two sets of parameters - ﬁrst one
taken from the parameter’s booklet included to the Sarix machine manual. The second set of parameters
was loaded from the Sarix parameters database. None of the results were satisfactory.
The most valuable observation from the obtained results was that the diameter is closer to desired
diameter after the pocketing operation. Depth is usually better after drilling (for lower desired depths),
however the diameter after drilling is not in the acceptable closeness to the desired one. The reason
for obtaining better diameter during pocketing is the process speciﬁcation itself. The tool moves in the
circular motion following a path and removes the material. This way there is a space for debris removal
by ﬂushing. In drilling there is no such space, as the used electrode is solid, ﬂushing of the debris can
only happen through a slight space around the electrode and the workpiece. The results were compared
with the conclusions from a study conducted by Yiquan et al. [38] That study concluded that a better
results were achieved by pocketing when the diameter to depth ratio was high comparing to drilled holes.
However the operation time was lower for the drilling operation. What could have an inﬂuence on both
the replication experiments and eventualy on the water removal is the smoothness of the channel entrance.
Yiquan et al. stated that the hole entrance is smoother after a drilling operation than after pocketing.
This however was not analysed in the comparison of this study, but could be of interest in the future
work.
In the end, pocketing was deemed a more viable solution for manufacturing the test holes for the replica-
tion study. However, the obtained depth was not close enough to the desired depth to use this operation.
To solve this obstruction, it was decided to investigate the process parameters of the pocketing operation
to achieve better depth results. The one parameter that could help was the adjustment factor.

5.4.4
Adjustment Factor during Pocketing
During the EDM process the material is removed by the electrical discharges between the workpiece
and the tool electrode. However, during this process also the material of the tool electrode is partially
removed. This is called tool wear and causes the change of dimensions of the electrode. This leads to the
miscalculations of the tool position by the EDM machine and results in a wrong depth level achieved after
the operation. To compensate the wear, the machine does two things. First, a number of control points
can be set during the whole operation. This control points happen at diﬀerent achieved depths. For
example, if a desired depth is 1 mm and the number of control points is 5, then when the machine thinks
that the depth is 1000
5
= 0.2mm the tool is removed from the hole and the length is checked in regard
to a known point. Then depending on the result of this checkup, the tool wear has to be compensated.
This compensation depends on the diﬀerence between the theoretical depth and the actual depth as well
as the set adjustment factor. The adjustment factor is a parameter that takes into consideration all the
factors that can inﬂuence the outcome of the happening erosion. It serves to manage and compensate
the tool electrode wear during the EDM operation. There are multiple factors that are included in the
adjustment factor like: [39]
• used material,
• diameter of the electrode,
• used dielectric ﬂuid,
• type of used wash,
• temperature,
• eroded shape.
If the set adjustment factor is not correct for the operation, then the machine after the control can over-
or under-compensate. To ﬁnd a correct values for the pocketing operation of the test hole manufacturing,
a short study was conducted. In this study a diﬀerent adjustment factors were investigated for diﬀerent
hole sizes. To reduce the number of required measurements the log ﬁles generated by the Sarix EDM
machine were used for selecting an appropriate adjustment factor value. A log ﬁle presents the results of
all the control operations executed by the program. An example of a log ﬁle can be seen in the Figure 70.
The initial adjustment factor value for this operation was 2.7. What can be read from the log ﬁle is the
real depth after each control sequence and the wear of the tool electrode. After the end of the operation
a new adjustment factor is presented, that, if diﬀerent from the initial adjustment factor, can be used to
improve the operation. This values from the log ﬁles were used to iteratively ﬁnd a correct adjustment
factor value.
To make sure that the values obtained from the log ﬁles are correct, before executing
the operations on the actual insert, manufactured holes were measured using the Alicona InﬁniteFocus
microscope.
5.4.5
Results of the Adjustment Factor Study
The results obtained from the images acquired by the Alicona microscope are presented in the Table

Tooling Process Chains
6.1
Introduction
The replication study done up to this point was necessary in order to design a tool for green ﬁber bottle
manufacturing. From the replication study it was concluded what speciﬁcations’ constrains has to be
applied for the tool to be eﬃcient in its function. Now it was the time to transfer this speciﬁcations into
a viable design of tool manufacturing. A tool is regarded as a part or a set of components forming a
part, that can be used to manufacture other parts. This manufacturing process can be singular, like a
sand mould in a casting process is used once and destroyed afterwards, or more desirable, multiple time,
like a mould in injection moulding can be used innumerable times to produce identical components until
it can be no longer used due to wear or other failures. In the case of this research an aim was to design
a multiple time use tool. However, as a validation of the chosen manufacturing process and selected
speciﬁcation, a simpler model was decided to be manufactured ﬁrst. A disc capable of being mounted
on the piston moulding setup. The design has a simple geometry, but can validate if the manufacturing
process is capable of producing the selected dimensions of channels and in the experiments if the water
removal rate is better then the previously used methods. However, it was always kept in mind that the
ﬁnal geometry is a rather complex 3D shape which also requires more complex manufacturing methods.
[26]
6.2
Design of Tooling Process Chains
By the term design of tooling process chain it means to propose a viable method and all the required
information to produce a fully functional tool. All the process steps has to be deﬁned from selecting the
material, through machining the material by the selected operations, quality assessment and validation
after the tool is ready, additional ﬁnishing if required, up to mounting the tool for its intended use. [26]
There are many various process that can be used to manufacture a ﬁnished tool, as well as combinations
of these processes. Sometimes the tool has to go through several steps or even assemblies until it becomes
ready to use. The processes can be divided into two group: additive and subtractive. The examples of
additive processes are: [26]
• electroforming,
• laser sintering,
• physical or chemical vapour deposition,
• printing (a broad term of polymer or metal additive manufacturing).
The examples of subtractive processes are as follows: [26]
• mechanical machining - cutting operations like milling or turning,
• electrical discharge machining (EDM),
• chemical etching,
• electrochemical machining (ECM),
• laser machining - ex. drilling.

Additionally a division can also be made regarding if the tool is obtained directly after the manufacturing
processes or indirectly if the additional step of removing a shape-giving substrate has to be removed to
obtain a tool. [26]
In case of this research a designed tool is a mould for paper bottle production, as presented in the
introduction. The described setup is located at the EcoXpac facility. The mould consists of two halves
and the intended use is for drying. The mould is going to be heated up. The function is to provide
channels for water removal. The water removal is conducted by applying pressure on the formed pulp
from the inside of the mould through an inﬂatable insert, against the mould, and by applying vacuum
from the outside of the mould. Heat acts as a catalyst to speed up the process. Currently the mould
is made from a porous material to provide the channels for water removal. However, to be able to fully
control the surface quality of the ﬁnal product and optimise the water removal rate a new tool has to be
designed. Therefore, from this summary of the manufacturing process of the paper bottles, a requirements
can be formulated: [7]
• Tool has to replicate the geometry of the bottle in the pulp,
• Tool has to produce by replication the desired surface quality of the ﬁnal product,
• Tool has to sustain large number of production cycles,
• Tool has to be corrosion resistant and neutral towards the pulp material and water,
• Tool has to provide water removal channels,
• Tool has to sustain temperatures above 100◦C, possibly up to 180◦C.
From the replication study some of the constrains were obtained. The surface quality greatly depends
on the customer requirements, but now the replication mechanism and what surface can be achieved by
using diﬀerent surface roughness of the tool is known. In the case of the functional features - the water
removal channels, the size and shape was experimentally chosen, so that it does not inﬂuence the surface
quality of the ﬁnal product. Circular channels of 200 µm diameter were decided on. However, this was
not enough for the tooling process chain design, as the distribution of the channels had to be chosen.
What pattern the channels has to be placed by and what the distance between the channels has to be. All
this was considered for the experimental disc, but keeping in mind that the ﬁnal goal is the bottle-shaped
mould.

6.3.1
Constrain Calculations
To recap, the main goal of this thesis was to propose a tooling process chain for a more eﬃcient water
removal from the formed pulp in the drying step of the manufacturing process. It was decided that an
experimental plate would be manufactured to examine the water removal in the 2D situation. Both full
3D mould and 2D experimental plate can use the same features for the water removal as the mechanism is
identical. The paper pulp is pressed from one side with a set pressure against a mould, while the vacuum
is applied from the other side of the mould to suck out the water excess. This can be greatly improved by
applying heat to transform the water into steam for easier removal. There is a need for channel features
in the mould to allow the water removal and provide space to escape from the paper pulp to the outside.
Currently, porous tools are used. However, the porous tools provide a random distribution of the escape
channels, and the water removal rate can vary between same tools made from porous material. Therefore,
a more controllable and more eﬃcient tools are needed. The parameter that can be used to describe and

6.4.4
Additive Manufacturing
Additive manufacturing, commonly known as 3D printing, is a broad term encompassing variety of
processes that create a product layer by layer either from polymer or from metal. For this study a light
polymerized processes were of importance. These processes use a liquid resin of a photosensitive polymer
that hardens in the presence of light. Using a light pattern layer by layer, a fully 3 dimensional object
can be produced. [64] Two main processes are distinguished:
• Stereolitography (SLA),
• Direct Light Processing (DLP).
Stereolitography is a laser based process.
The laser cures a liquid photopolymer held in a vat on a
movable platform. Initially the platform is held under the surface of the resin just so the ﬁrst layer of
the manufactured object could be cured. When the layer is completed, the platform moves down and the
next layer is being cured. When the whole object is cured, the platform raises and the object is removed.
After this process the product need subsequent cleaning and curing in the oven for full hardening. Figure
93 illustrates the stereolitography setup. [65]
Figure 93: Principle of the stereolitoghraphy process. [65]

than the SLA process because the idea is to use a white light with a LCD panel or a deformable mirror
device to apply a single layer structure in one pass. After the process is completed it also requires cleaning
and post-curing. Other advantages are less waste and lower operational costs. The Figure 94 illustrates

be the desired thickness. A combination of few processes might be required to fully use an etched product.
For example, a thin plate with desired channel structure might be placed on a thicker plate with bigger
sized channels.

6.4.6
Hybrid Processes
The alternative manufacturing method could be a hybrid process of additive manufacturing and electro-
forming. Instead of printing a plate with channels, a plate with pillars could be printed. Printing pillars
should be easier than printing "negative" spaces as it is easier to place the light spot and create a "solid"
space rather than around it. [67] The problematic part would be achieving a slender pillar structure of
0.2 mm diameter and 3 mm height. After obtaining a disc with pillars an indirect process chain could
be followed as proposed by Tosello et al. [70] Chain of coating the pillars by physical vapor deposition,
electroforming of, for example, nickel and copper, etching the printed structure, and ﬁnally machining
the back of the disc and cleaning from the unnecessary layers of materials used for electroforming. This
rather complex process chain might not be a best solution for the experimental disc manufacturing, but
might be viable for the mould production.
6.5
Conclusions
In this chapter several solutions for designing a tooling process chain were investigated. No clear conclu-
sions were drawn, as it would require more time allocated for validation with experiments. Only additive
manufacturing method was, in a small scale, tried to verify the capabilities of manufacturing desired
dimensions of channels. However, the results due to the misalignment of orientations was proven to be
worthless. From the research few manufacturing methods appear to be feasible. Micro milling or drilling
is capable of manufacturing micro sized channels, despite the length of the process of drilling each hole
separately. As well as µED milling, which was proven in the replication study. If the aspect ratio of
the desired channels would prove to be unattainable, a combination of parts might be used. An example
of that might be etching the desired channels in a thin metal sheet. That sheet then could be joined
with a more sturdy plate with bigger sized oriﬁces for the cost of loosing some free area on the disc.
This solution could be also applied for the more complex half bottle shape for the ultimate mould, as
the etched sheet could be formed into the desired shape and joined with a rest of the mould made by a
diﬀerent process with bigger sized features.

7. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
Master Thesis
7
Conclusions and Future Work
The goal of this research was to propose a design of a tooling process chain for a paper bottle production.
An extensive replication study was conducted to determine the requirement speciﬁcations for the tool.
The intended tool was a mould, which function was to allow the water removal during drying. To features
of the tool were analysed: surface roughness - a static feature, and water removal channels - a functional
feature. The static feature was analysed through experiments using master plates with diﬀerent surface
roughness. Those master plates were then used to produce paper discs. The replicated surface quality
was analysed. The functional features were also investigated experimentally, with a single master plate
on which diﬀerent sized holes were machined. The imprints of those holes on the paper disc samples
were analysed. The results of those experiments were used for formulation of requirements for the tooling
process chain design. Diﬀerent approaches for the tooling design were investigated and tooling process
chains were shown for the used master plates, as well as propositions for mould tooling process chains.
Main conclusions drawn from the study:
• Surface topography of the paper product depends on the surface topography of the used tool. It is
believed, and suggested by the results of the experiments, that there is a boundary of the surface
roughness of the tool. Below this boundary the paper used for manufacturing cannot be smothered
more and above the roughness of the paper product will be lower then the roughness og the used
tool.
• Investigation of the circular shaped features as a water removal channels showed that the biggest
feature not visible with a naked eye and one that is not clogged during operation is of a 200 µm
diameter. Also the smallest replicated feature visible with a microscope is of 50 µm diameter.
• Calculations of the density of water removal channels showed that to achieve a more eﬃcient water
removal than currently used, a pitch of 0.3 mm has to be used with a hexagonal channel pattern.
This results in a uniform spacing between channels, providing a free area for water removal uniformly
on the whole area of the tool.
• Manufacturing of the mould might be diﬃcult. There are however few viable solutions. Those
solutions include mechanical cutting processes, metal etching and electrical discharge machining.
Additive manufacturing process of direct light processing was investigated and could prove viable.
Unfortunately, a mistake in part orientation made the attempt worthless. Additionally, a material
used with AM processes might not be suitable for the intended use of the tool.
Future work might include more extensive surface replication study with more analysed values. The
method of analysis the surface replication could be improved by using random signal analysis or frequency
spectrum analysis. The functional feature replication study could be extended for other water removal
channels shapes, like for instance slots - master plate with slots was manufactured but the experiments
were not conducted in this research. Concrete tooling process chains should be validated by manufacturing
either a simpliﬁed model of the tool or the actual tool and then by measurements and experiments.
