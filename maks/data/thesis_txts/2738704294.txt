Abstract
Metabolic engineering has made significant strides in recent decades, yet optimizing mi­
crobial cell factories for value­added compound production remains a challenge. Yield
optimization requires meticulous management of carbon flux towards growth and produc­
tion, often compromising both. The motivation of this thesis is to explore quorum sensing
(QS) systems, particularly the tra system, as a strategy for autonomous dynamic regula­
tion of metabolic pathways in engineered microbial cell factories. Specifically, the work
conducted in this thesis characterizes the E. coli­adapted tra system and investigates the
growth effect of tra­controlled expression of enzymes related to the shikimate pathway.
The tra­induced pathway was compared with constitutive expression and the impact of dif­
ferential induction timing was explored. While QS systems have been extensively studied
in nature and several have been applied in synthetic biology, the tra system has, to the
best of our knowledge, never been used for autonomous dynamic regulation of genes in
the shikimate pathway. The results of this thesis demonstrate the potential growth impact
of overexpression of genes close to and in the central carbon metabolism. Specifically,
overexpression of tktA and ppsA resulted in a significant growth impediment of 34.3%
+/­36.2% and 74.5% +/­ 15.8% reductions in growth rate, respectively, compared to the
uninduced strain. Additionally, timing of induction was demonstrated to be important for
the growth impact: By delaying induction of Ptra until after entry into the exponential phase,
growth can be fully recovered. By leveraging QS systems for autonomous regulation, this
study expands the toolbox for more efficient and scalable microbial cell factories using
tra­based flux control in metabolic engineering. Future work could explore its application
in optimizing production of specific value­added compounds and investigate its efficacy
in other microbial systems.

Resumé
Optimering af mikrobielle cellefabrikker til produktion af værdifulde stoffer er fortsat en
udfordring til trods for, at der i de seneste årtier er gjort betydelige fremskridt indenfor
teknologi til at styre cellers metabolisme. Optimering af udbytte kræver en hårfin styring af
cellens carbonatomer for at balancere vækst og produktion. Desværre er denne balance
svær, og leder ofte til enten lav vækst eller lav produktion. Motivation for dette afslut­
tende projekt er at udforske quorum sensing (QS) systemer, specifikt tra­systemet, som
en strategi til autonom dynamisk regulering af metaboliske processer i mikrobielle celle­
fabrikker. Specifikt karakteriserer arbejdet i dette værk det Escherichia coli­tilpassede
tra­system og undersøger væksteffekten af tra­kontrolleret ekspression af enzymer fra
den metaboliske vej til shikimat. De tra­inducerede enzymer blev sammenlignet med
konstitutivt udtrykte enzymer, og effekten af forskellig induktionstiming blev undersøgt.
QS­systemer er blevet grundigt studeret i naturen og flere er blevet anvendt i syntetisk
biologi. tra­systemet er dog, så vidt det vides, aldrig blevet brugt til autonom dynamisk
regulering af gener i shikimatvejen. Resultaterne af dette speciale demonstrerer den po­
tentielle væksteffekt af overekspression af gener tæt på den centrale kulstofmetabolisme.
Specifikt resulterede overekspression af tktA og ppsA i en betydelig vækst reduktion på
henholdsvis 34.3% +/­36.2% og 74.5% +/­ 15.8% reduktion i væksthastighed sammen­
lignet med den ikke­inducerede stamme. Desuden vises det, at timingen af induktion er
vigtig for væksteffekten. Ved at forsinke induktionen af Ptra til efter starten af den ekspo­
nentielle vækstfase kan væksten genoprettes. Ved at udnytte QS­systemer til autonom
regulering udvider arbejdet i dette speciale værktøjskassen til at skabe mere effektive og
skalérbare mikrobielle cellefabrikker ved hjælp af tra­baseret fluxkontrol. Fremtidigt arbe­
jde kunne udforske tra­systemets anvendelse i optimering af produktionen af specifikke
værdifulde stoffer og undersøge systemets effektivitet i andre mikrobielle systemer.

1
Introduction
The production of value­added chemicals has been revolutionized by reprogramming of
cell factories through metabolic engineering, yet the challenge of balancing metabolic flux
still remains. There is a finite amount of resources (carbon, polymerases, ribosomes)
available to the cell, thus production usually comes at the expense of growth. This thesis
explores the potential of autonomous dynamic regulation through quorum­sensing (QS)
systems to address this challenge. By engineering QS circuits to dynamically regulate
metabolic flux towards products of interest at high cell densities, this work seeks to add to
the toolbox available for engineers when utilizing QS systems in synthetic biology. This
thesis aims to demonstrate the utility of the QS system tra in metabolic engineering by
focusing on the growth impact conferred by using QS systems in Escherichia coli. Genes
related to the shikimate pathway are used to illustrate the growth effect of tra­based reg­
ulation.
In this chapter, an overview of relevant literature on transcriptional regulation, QS, the
utilization of QS in engineering, and a brief introduction to the production of aromatic
compounds through the shikimate pathway is presented.
1.1
Dynamic regulation is pivotal for cell adaptability
The ability of a cell to dynamically change in response to changing environments is key
to its survival. Genomes contain a plethora of genes to accommodate varying conditions.
To survive and out­compete other species, or growing on a non­traditional carbon source,
the cell needs to quickly produce the proteins necessary [1]. It is, however, crucial for the
growth rate of the cell that energy and key metabolites are not spent carelessly. Conse­
quently, the cell needs to be capable of exerting transient control of resources to change
the level of transcription, translation and degradation of non­housekeeping genes and
their corresponding mRNA and proteins.
1.1.1
Metabolic flux is controlled through gene expression regulation
The cell can alter the metabolic flux of relevant pathways by exerting flux control. Flux
control refers to the change in the amount of metabolite that is processed by a catalytic
component of the system (typically an enzyme) caused by a change in either activity or
abundance of the catalytic component [2]. The abundance of enzymes depends on a mul­
titude of elements, ranging from frequency of transcription to rate of mRNA degradation
[3]. Fluxes are natively often controlled in large scale through regulatory networks, natively
controlled by stimulons responding to a physiological condition (e.g. protein degradation
due to heat shock). These stimulons control regulons, which in turn regulate targets.
Continuing the example of heat shock, the regulon could be HrcA targeting GroEL/ES or
DnaK/J [4].
In the pursuit of engineering efficient cell factories for production of value­added com­
pounds with high performance, the potential of mastering flux control in a production host
cannot be ignored.

such inducible system, researchers often use data showing the expression of a mea­

1.2
Types of regulation in prokaryotes
Regulation mechanisms exist throughout the central dogma, and each serve important
roles in the adaptation and survival of the cell. The first level of regulation is transcriptional
regulation, which describes a range of processes that affect the rate at which mRNA is
transcribed from the encoding DNA. Control of inducible promoters, an example of the dy­
namic systems described above, is a key element at this stage. Post­transcriptional regu­
lation describes the processes which regulate the amount of RNA in the cell. This includes
modifying mRNA by processes such as RNA splicing, RNA processing and RNA stabil­
ity. Translational regulation revolves around the control of protein synthesis. Prokaryotes
can exert control of the rate at which translation occurs through various means, exem­
plified by the variation in ribosome binding­site (RBS)/Shine­Dalgarno (SD) sequence.
Diversions from the consensus RBS sequence will lead to sub­optimal initiation of trans­
lation [7].
Post­translational regulation refers to the regulation of translated proteins.
This includes processes like proteolytic cleavage, phosphorylation, protein folding and
chaperone­mediated regulation. These signals can affect the localization, activity and
stability of the protein [4].
E. coli is the most commonly used model organism in biotechnology. Thus, it stands to
reason that the toolbox for engineering E. coli is the most well­equipped of all. The or­
ganism was one of the first studied intensely and thus ”domesticated” by the scientific
community. E. coli is robust and grows fast, both important features when conducting
experiments [7, 8]. Thus, E. coli is an obvious choice when, as in this study, exploring
new metabolic regulation mechanisms. Since prokaryotes, like E. coli, do not contain a
nucleus, mRNA can be translated as soon as it is transcribed, sometimes by multiple ribo­
somes at ones, in a complex known as the polysome. Further, transcriptional regulation
conserves cellular resources (polymerases, tRNA, ribosomes), freeing them for use else­
where in the cell. Thus, transcriptional regulation is the major prokaryotic lever of protein
expression [9].
1.3
Transcriptional regulation enables wide­spread dynamic
regulation
Transcription in E. coli requires a few key components; among the most important are
the template DNA, free nucleotides to make up the product, an RNA polymerase, and
a sigma factor. Contrary to eukaryotes, prokaryotes like E. coli contain only one RNA
polymerase, which is utilized for all transcription. To differentiate between promoters, E.
coli instead uses seven distinct RNA polymerase sigma subunits to initiate transcription.
Sigma70 (σ70) is used for transcribing the majority of genes related to cell maintenance
and growth, whereas, for instance, σ54 is responsible for regulating gene expression for
nitrogen metabolism. σ70, encoded by the rpoD gene, is the primary sigma factor used
for transcription of most genes expressed during the exponential growth phase of E. coli
[9]. 5 other sigma factors each regulate a subset of genes in the wild­type (WT) organism.
The sigma factor is responsible for recognizing the promoter sequence, and each sigma
factor has distinct recognition sequences. The consensus sequence of σ70 consists of
a (relative to the mRNA start position) ­35 sequence, which constitutes the initial point of
recognition for the housekeeping sigma factor, σ70. Additionally, a ­10 region, also known
as the Pribnow or TATA box, stabilizes the interaction between DNA and the holoenzyme.
Once stabilized, the double­stranded DNA will begin to melt and transcription will initiate.

Once the promoter is cleared by the holoenzyme, the sigma factor is released, leaving
the RNA polymerase to complete transcription [9, 10].
When expressing proteins, cells can transcribe the necessary mRNA constitutively or dy­
namically. Constitutive promoters are commonly found controlling housekeepimg genes,
and will vary in their resemblance to the consensus sequence of the E. coli RNA poly­
merase:σ70 holoenzyme [10]. More similar sequences constitutes stronger promoters,
whereas more different sequences will result in weaker promoters.
Dynamic control is utilized when an organism responds to stimuli ­ or the lack thereof. Two
distinct types of dynamic regulation are activation and repression: Dynamic promoters can
have sub­optimal binding of the holoenzyme, and even entirely lack the ­35 sequence.
In that case, an activator is required for facilitating transcription initiation in a process
known as activator regulation. Often, the activator acts as the recognition site for the
sigma factor, allowing recognition of the promoter by the holoenzyme. Alternatively, the
reason for dynamic regulation of transcription can be the presence of a repressor. In that
case, a repressor can be recruited to the operator site and physically block the promoter
region, preventing the RNA polymerase:sigma factor holoenzyme from interacting with
the promoter [1].
Nature has evolved to take advantage of an interesting artefact of some sub­optimal dy­
namic promoters: leaky expression. Leaky expression occurs when a dynamic promoter
is under imperfect transcriptional regulation, leading to low levels of basal transcription
from the promoter even in the absence of the corresponding transcriptional regulator.
Environmental regulation confers survival advantages
When responding to a change in the environment of the cell, leaky expression is especially
powerful. An example of environmental regulation aided by leaky expression occurs upon
the simultaneous removal of glucose and introduction of lactose as a carbon source for
E. coli. This change in environment leads to induction of the E. coli lac operon, which
allows the catabolism of lactose when glucose is exhausted. The repressor of the lac
operon primarily binds to the operator when lactose is absent. In the presence of lactose,
however, the repressor is released by binding of allolactose, allowing for transcription
of the genes required for catabolism of lactose. Leaky expression is important for the
adaptation of the cell, since some lactose­degrading enzymes are available to process
the lactose, allowing the cell to continue growing, albeit at a slower rate immediately after
glucose depletion [11].
1.4
Quorum sensing enables intra­ and interspecies
communication
Quorum sensing (QS) is a way for bacteria to communicate and regulate gene expres­
sion in response to density of cell­population in order to achieve a collectively shared
phenotype befitting of the status and environment of the population. According to Cao
et al. (2023) [12] any QS system consists of an autoinducer (the signal molecule), a sig­
nal transduction pathway and an activated or repressed promoter. Microorganisms with
QS systems produce a signal molecule (expanded below) and a transcriptional regulator,
which is the first part of a signal transduction pathway, both at a very low, basal level.
The signal molecule is exported from the cell, and will diffuse away from the cell at low
cell density. The concentration of signal molecule in the environment increases with cell
density, resulting in a high concentration of signal molecules in the immediate environ­
ment of the cell when cell density is high. The signal molecules is then brought into the
cell by both active and passive transport and the signal transduction pathway is triggered

==PAGE:19==
to either activate expression from a repressed promoter or repress expression from an
active promoter [3, 12–14].
Variation in any of these QS components is what differentiates QS systems, although the
role QS plays in gene expression can be shared across genera and species [15, 16].
1.4.1
QS enables diverse features in nature
The first reported quorum sensing system was the lux system from Vibrio fisheri, which
regulates bioluminescence in the marine bacteria [17]. Apart from bioluminescence, QS
is used for a variety of functions to regulate and coordinate the behaviour of complex
microbial communities [15]. QS can be used to discourage production of motility genes,
and instead increase production of genes involved in production of components of impor­
tance to biofilm formation. Other species instead repress biofilm formation in response
to increased concentration of the autoinducer [18]. QS is also involved in the induction
of virulence genes, for instance in the case of Pseudomonas aeruginosa in the lungs of
cystic fibrosis patients [15, 19]. QS has also been known to play a role in antimicrobial
resistance, exemplified by the upregulation of efflux pumps to expel antibiotic compounds
from the cell [12, 15, 20].
A common phenomenon in QS is the furthered activation of gene expression of the tran­
scriptional regulator and autoinducer synthase in response to increased levels of autoin­
ducer, resulting in a positive feedforward loop ensuring enhanced, coordinated and sus­
tained response across the population. This enables the use of QS for autonomous dy­
namic regulation, which is advantageous for meticulous control of metabolic flux [3, 15].
1.4.2
QS systems are diverse in nature
As mentioned above, QS systems often share some features between species. However,
there are multiple distinct mechanisms used by bacteria for QS. All QS is mediated by sig­
nal molecules, and the type of autoinducer (AI) helps divide QS systems into classes. The
most commonly discussed ­ and utilized for engineering purposes ­ are the acyl homoser­
ine lactone (AHL)­based systems, also know as AI­1, which is present in most Gram­
negative bacteria. AI­2 is another QS system: AI­2 is used and produced in many different
species (Gram­negative and Gram­positive species alike), allowing for intra­ and inter­
species communication. Autoinducing peptide (AIP)­based systems are found in Gram­
positive systems and works by recognition of the AI through membrane­bound receptors.
Other Gram­negative systems also exist; They include e.g. γ­butyrolactones (GBLs),
quiniones, as well as other signaling molecules [21].
1.4.3
Acyl homoserine lactone­based systems
Acyl homoserine lactones (AHLs) are small molecules used as signal molecules by Gram­
negative bacteria. AHL­based systems are diverse in nature, allowing for multiple homol­
ogous systems across species. The structural variety is however limited, since the AHLs
are commonly derived from the bacterial fatty acid synthesis [22]. The basic structure of
AHLs can be seen in Figure 1.2. The R­group of the AHL varies between species and
systems, and can range from C4 to C18 in length, and have a variety of modifications [14,
21, 23]. Some examples of AHL R­groups can be seen in Table 1.1.
There are two general types of receptors that are able to detect AHL autoinducers; tran­
scription factors present in the cytoplasm (e.g. Vibrio spp.) and transmembrane two­
component histidine sensor kinases (e.g. Pseudomonas spp.) [15]. The current study
will focus on the cytoplasmic transcription factors. The lux system from Vibrio fischeri
serves as an example of transcription factor­based promoter activation. The promoter
structure of the lux promoter (Plux) includes a repeated motif referred to as the lux­box
[15, 16].

Natural diversity brings opportunities and challenges
Quorum sensing systems vary dramatically in nature; from their substrate promiscuity to
their capability for activation. To better characterize QS systems, studies involving the dy­
namic and operational range are often beneficial. To this extent, Scott and Hasty (2016)
characterized multiple QS systems, including the WT TraR and engineered TraR(W) sys­
tems using the engineered Plux, with the tra­box replacing the lux­box, as described in
section 1.4.3.
A common occurrence when combining (the components of) several AHL­based systems
is cross­talk. Cross­talk describes the ability of the system components (transcriptional

1.5
Quorum sensing unlocks new tools in engineering
The need for optimized production of compounds (known or new­to­nature) for such di­
verse fields as the pharmaceutical, cosmetic, food, and chemical industries have long
been met with solutions based in metabolic engineering. Previously, static regulations
have been applied with great success to increase expression (constitutive over­expression)
or minimize competition from endogenous pathways (gene knock­outs). However, dis­
turbing the evolutionarily perfected resource balance by overexpression of endogenous
genes or the introduction of exogenous genes to produce new­to­system compounds, will
undeniably compete with endogenous resources to some degree. This may be detrimen­
tal to the functionality of such production system, as a system optimized for production
may result in stunted growth and ultimately sub­optimal yield [12, 15]. Yet, since QS sys­
tems are not essential for the central metabolism and thus growth of bacteria, the use of
QS systems does not immediately lead to strict selective pressure [18]. The Prather Lab at
Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), led by Professor Kristala L. J. Prather, has
throughout many years conducted extensive research on using QS for metabolic engi­
neering in the production of value­added chemicals through fermentation. Among the QS
systems developed in the Prather Lab is a circuit designed to control production of glucaric
acid in E. coli, a precursor for many compounds of interest, including nylons and deter­
gents. This was achieved by carefully timing the diversion of glucose­6­phosphate into an
exogenous production pathway through QS. In this way, the decision of when to switch
the focus of metabolism was autonomous [33]. In a study on naringenin production, the
lab engineered E. coli with two quorum­sensing systems from different bacterial species.
This allowed for multiple points of control for metabolic switches, preventing the diversion
of malonyl­CoA and delaying enzyme production to avoid accumulation of precursors.
This approach led to a tenfold increase in naringenin yield [34]. Collectively, the Prather
Lab has demonstrated the use of QS systems for synthetic switches in several metabolic
pathways, underlining the versatility of the use of QS in engineering. Furthermore, by
allowing autonomous control strategies, QS systems suggests potential for applications
in industrial scale, relieving the need for expensive and complex inducers.
Several other groups have contributed substantially to the adaptation and development of
QS systems for practical applications. Examples of the use of QS for engineering synthetic
systems to better our environment include environmental monitoring of harmful heavy
metal by biosensors amplified in response to cell population [35] and QS­induced biofilm
formation for bioremediation of industrial wastewater [36]. QS systems have also been
applied in the context of healthcare, with QS systems in E. coli designed to sense, target
and even cause cell lysis of Pseudomonas aeruginosa, the primary pathogenic organism
involved in colonizing the lungs of cystic fibrosis patients [19]. QS systems have also been
designed to sense pathogens and respond with production of antibiotic componds [20].
Lastly, QS systems have been applied to optimize bioproduction in several cases, and
thus increase profit margins [37, 38]. Apart from direct applications in industrial settings,
engineering of biological system using QS systems allows the design of more complex

technical systems. This is exemplified by the construction of a synchronized genetic clock

1.6.1
Chorismate is a value­added platform molecule
Chorismate is a key platform molecule used for production of many primary and sec­
ondary metabolites. The aromatic amino acids, tryptophan, tyrosine and phenylalanine
are derived from chorismate and are all essential for protein synthesis in E. coli, and in the
case of disruption of the shikimate pathway, these amino acids must be supplied. A mul­
titude of other aromatic compounds can be derived from chorismate, including vitamins,
polymers and pharmaceuticals [42, 43]. To the best of our knowledge, the production of
chorismate has not been regulated by the tra system until now.
1.6.2
Efforts to optimize yield of chorismate
Several groups have worked to optimize the yield of chorismate. Many varied strategies
and approaches have been explored. Several groups have increased precursor sup­
ply by downregulating or deleting competing pathways upstream chorismate (such as
the phosphotransferase system) and by overexpressing the enzymes (ppsA and tktA) re­
sponsible for producing the precursors (PEP and E4P) [42, 44]. Alternatively, if a specific
chorismate­derived compound is targeted, researchers can downregulating competing
pathways downstream chorismate e.g. by removing the ability of the cell to produce the
aromatic amino acids as was explored by Zomorrodi et al. (2022) [45]. Other strategies
not involving metabolic engineering include targeted protein engineering to optimize ef­
ficiency of enzymes and utilizing alternative fermentation strategies, e.g. by performing
fed­batch fermentation to constantly ensure excess levels of glucose [42, 45, 46]. It is
also viable overexpressing potential rate­limiting enzymes, and minimizing the effect of
system feedback as will be further described in the following section [47]. Lastly, as ex­
plored in this thesis, it is possible to dynamically regulating gene expression to optimize
chorismate production as shown by Dinh et al. (2019) and Ni et al. (2022) [34, 38].
1.7
The genes under investigations are of interest to
production through the shikimate pathway
To investigate the use of tra­based regulation in metabolic engineering it was decided, as
the basis of this thesis, to focus on overexpression of the shikimate pathway enzymes. All
enzymes in the shikimate pathway (Figure A.19) including the steps immediately before
the pathway (producing the precursors PEP and E4P) are potential targets. It is of interest
to target the rate controlling step(s), a subject which has been discussed in several studies
[44, 48–52]. In the following section, the targeted genes are presented. A schematic
overview of the reactions catalyzed by the targeted genes described below can be found
in Appendix A.1.
1.7.1
ppsA ­ 2.7.9.2
ppsA is the gene encoding the enzyme PpsA, which is part of glycolysis. It catalyzes the
hydrolysis of ATP, releasing the γ phosphate, and transferring the β phosphate to pyru­
vate in order to produce PEP. ppsA functions as a dimer, requiring Mg2+ as a cofactor.
Transcription of ppsA in the K12 strain is controlled by a wide range of regulatory mecha­
nisms responding to the carbon level in the cell. Optimizing production of PEP is expected
to allow more substrate to become available for AroG/F/H, the first step in the shikimate
pathway [28, 41, 52].
1.7.2
tktA ­ 2.2.1.1
tktA is the gene encoding TktA, a transketolase (I) that catalyzes the reaction of β­D­
fructofuranose 6­phosphate and D­glyceraldehyde 3­phosphate to E4P and D­xylulose
5­phosphate and the reverse reaction, as part of the PPP, and is thus important for the
redox­balance of the cell. tktA functions as a dimer, requiring Mg2+, thiamine diphosphate

==PAGE:28==
or Mn2+ as a cofactor. Optimizing production of E4P is expected to allow more substrate to
become available for AroG/F/H, the first step in the shikimate pathway [52]. Transcription
of tktA in the K12 strain has been shown to be redox­regulated [28, 41].
1.7.3
aroG ­ 2.5.1.54
aroG encodes the enzyme catalyzing the first step of the shikimate pathway. AroG is one
of three 3­deoxy­7­phosphoheptulonate (DAHP) synthases used to produce DAHP from
PEP and E4P, and in the process releasing phosphate. The three DAHP synthases differ
in their regulation, as the activity of each is downregulated in response to the presence of
one of the three aromatic amino acids, tyrosine (AroF), tryptophan (AroH) and phenylala­
nine (AroG). AroG acts as a tetramer in complex with the cofactor Fe2+. This is unique
for AroG as the other DAHP synthases acts as dimers with no co­factor required [28, 41].
Synthesis of DAHP is regarded as one of the primary rate­limiting steps in the shikimate
pathway [48, 52].
Feedback inhibition and resistant mutants
In the present study, a mutant of aroG, denoted aroG*, is used. This mutant is resistant
to feedback inhibition by phenylalanine, resulting in an enzyme that, in the context of this
study, is solely regulated through transcription regulation [47].
1.7.4
aroB ­ 4.2.3.4
aroB is the gene encoding AroB, a 3­dehydroquinate synthase catalyzing the reaction of
DAHP to 3­dehydroquinate (DHQ), releasing phosphate in the process. This is the sec­
ond step in the shikimate pathway. The enzyme is a metalloenzyme which as cofactor
utilizes either Co2+ or Zn2+. Further, NAD+ has proven to be an essential cofactor for
catalytic activity. AroB has proven to significantly improve flux through the shikimate path­
way in Corynebacterium glutamicum [52]. aroB has not been reported to be repressible
in response to any known compounds, and is constitutively expressed in the K12 strain
[28, 41].
1.7.5
aroL ­ 2.7.1.71
aroL and aroK encode isoenzymes of shikimate kinases (AroL and AroK) catalyzing the
fifth step of the shikimate pathway, the phosphorylation of shikimate to shikimate­3­phosphate
using ATP as the co­substrate. AroL exhibits a significantly higher affinity for shikimate
(100­fold), and has thus been selected as the target in the present study [51]. The reac­
tion requires Mg2+ as a cofactor. Ding et al. (2016), alongside other studies, identified
AroL as a key bottleneck enzyme in the E. coli production of chorismate derivatives [51,
52].

Gibson assembly primers
A primer used for Gibson assembly contains a tail which allows annealing to another
piece of DNA. In a primer pair used for Gibson assembly, the tails will match and ­ after
a 5’ exonuclease acts to chew back nucleotides to create a sticky end ­ allow annealing.
Thus, no specific DNA sequence is required to produce Gibson assembly primers, which
is advantageous for scarless cloning.
KLD primers
Primers used for KLD did not contain a designated specific sequence. As an example,
when generating primers for production of plasmids expressing GFP from constitutive
promoters, these promoters were each inserted as half the promoter on the two plasmid
tails when amplifying the template plasmid. The nature of KLD causes blunt­end ligation,
and the tails would thus ligate, creating the functional promoter of choice.
Sequencing primers
Sequencing primers contain only annealing regions, and are thus often shorter than other
primers used throughout this study. Sequencing primers were designed to anneal ap­
proximately 100 bp from engineered sites.
2.2.3
Production of chemically competent cells
The desired strain (Escherichia coli K­12 MG1655 for assembly of plasmids) was revived
from cryostock by inoculating in LB and growing overnight (O/N). The following day, the
culture was diluted 1:100 in 50 mL LB in a flask. The culture was grown to OD600=0.4­0.6,
transferred to a 50 mL Falcon tube and spun down at 3000rpm for 10 min at 4◦C using the
Beckman Coulter Avanti® J­E high­speed centrifuge. The supernatent was discarded, the
pellet gently resuspended in 7.5 mL complete minimal media, and the cells cooled on ice
for 10 min. The cells were spun down again, the supernatent then discarded and the pellet
resuspended in 1.8 mL. Cells were chilled on ice for 5 min, then 63 µL dimethylsulfoxide
(DMSO) was gently mixed into the culture. Cells were chilled on ice for 5 min, and another
63 µL DMSO was added. After a 5 min wait time, the 100 µl cells was aliquoted into 1.7
mL microcentrifuge tubes and stored at ­80◦C until needed.

• [AHL] is the concentration of inducer.
• EC50 is the inducer concentration at which Y = Ymax.
• n is the Hill coefficient.

4
Discussion
The toolbox available for metabolic engineers has expanded drastically over the last
decades. With the advent of smart, automated systems in most areas, the process of fer­
mentation to produce value­added compounds remains a tediously manual and resource­
dependent affair. This study’s investigations of tra­based expression of genes relevant
to the shikimate pathway seeks to add to this toolbox by demonstrating the differential
growth impact based on induction level of Ptra. The results of this study have several
interesting implications, and suggests the need for further research. In this section, some
of the issues, curiosities and potential future applications encountered during the work of
this thesis will be discussed.
4.1
Selecting the tra system for overexpressing endogenous
genes
The tra­system was selected for control of the shikimate pathway to increase flux towards
chorismate for future production of aromatic value­added compounds. For this purpose,
several other QS systems, and functional systems of E. coli were investigated for their
compatibility. Specifically for the purpose of this thesis (overexpression of endogenous
genes related to the shikimate pathway), it was relevant to select a QS system that had a
relatively high threshold for induction to prevent potentially disruptive premature expres­
sion. Further, the promoter should be relatively tight and ideally have a broad operational
range, allowing for previse control. Further, the tra system demonstrated a dynamic range
large enough to substantially increase enzyme production when activated. For these pur­
poses the tra system was preferred for the purpose of this study when compared to the
rpa and lux systems also tested (Figure 3.5).
4.2
Expression of GFP does not affect growth
The results of Figure 3.6 and Appendix Figure A.9 clearly show that Ptra­based expres­
sion of GFP does not affect growth, regardless of concentration of 3OC8. Expression
of GFP does not impact compounds of the central carbon metabolism, as opposed to
ppsA and tktA expressed from Ptra (Figure 3.8). This result demonstrates that the growth
effect of expression from an induced Ptra is clearly dependent on the nature of the prod­
uct rather than the concentration of AHL added. This effect is further supported by the
general relative lack of growth impact on uninduced strains, and the high growth impact
of overexpressing genes relevant to the central carbon metabolism seen throughout this
study.
4.3
Growth differences depend on the overexpressed gene
Overexpressing proteins in cells poses several risks, primarily due to the burden placed
on cellular machinery and resources (carbon, polymerases, tRNA, ribosomes, etc.), all of
which exists in a finite amount in the cell. Thus, overexpression of any enzyme theoreti­
cally has some impact on production. Kristala L. Jones Prather et al. (2000) demonstrated
the growth burden on cells in response to the overconsumption of metabolites involved
in the central carbon metabolism. By depleting substrates used in the central carbon
metabolism, other vital products may be produced less, leading to stunted growth and
ultimately lower production of the compound of choice [57]. This study reveals that Ptra­
based overexpression of PpsA and TktA cause the most significant growth impediment in
40

the current system (Figure 3.8). The difference of growth impact between the strains ex­
pressing ppsA and tktA is interesting, and an area that would be interesting to investigate
further, given more time.
PpsA is involved in the glycolytic pathway, and uses ATP to phosphorylate pyruvate into
PEP. Excessive activity of the enzyme might deplete ATP, the primary form of chemical
energy in the cell. By draining the cell of energy, growth impediment is expected. Further,
as part of glycolysis, overexpression might lead to changes in NADH/NAD+ ratios. Deplet­
ing pyruvate levels also risks affecting other pathways, like the TCA cycle, which requires
pyruvate [28, 41]. In the current study the expression of ppsA from a fully induced Ptra
was demonstrated to substantially delay the entry into the exponential phase. This can
be explained by the adaption of the cells to the initial energy and redox imbalances. How­
ever, the growth rate upon entry into the exponential phase is similar to that of the control
and uninduced strain. ppaA is thus an interesting candidate for further investigation.
TktA is part of the PPP, and increased flux through the PPP might lead to an overproduc­
tion of NADPH. This could result in redox imbalance, which would be detrimental to the or­
ganism, as transcription in the K12 strain has been shown to be redox­regulated. Further,
an increase in flux through the PPP might deplete the resources of other pathways (ma­
chinery and precursors) in the cell, which are supplied with glyceraldehyde 3­phosphate
partially from the PPP [28, 41]. In this study, overexpression of tktA from Ptra was shown
to have a large impact on the growth rate of the induced expressing population (Figure
3.8). A slower biomass accumulation is realistic due to less efficient energy production.
AroG*, involved in the shikimate pathway, is expected to significantly increase pathway
flux based on literature. The results of the initial experiment (Figure 3.8) show minimal
growth impediment, possibly due to an initial carbon bottleneck caused by PpsA and TktA.
Meanwhile, the results seen in Figure 3.10 and 3.11 shows significant impact on growth
due to induction of Ptra:aroG*. This latter result is more on par with expectations, since
several studies highlight the importance of overexpressing aroG* in production of aro­
matic compounds [42, 47, 48]. The experiments of this study needs to be repeated to
draw conclusions. Ding et al. (2016) demonstrated that overexpression of aroG* and
tktA, along with other genes, can boost tyrosine production [51]. If aroG* indeed encodes
a bottleneck enzyme, it is expected to see an impact on production, potentially reflected
in reduced growth, when overexpressing the enzyme ­ unless, of course, the product is
beneficial for growth like some amino acids. Based on these findings, it would be inter­
esting to investigate the simultaneous overexpression of ppsA, tktA and aroG*. Such a
strain is likely to have increased flux through the shikimate pathway, but will most likely
require careful monitoring and balancing to avoid growth inhibition. However, as seen in
Figure 3.11, the growth inhibition caused by overexpressing multiple genes related to the
shikimate pathway may not be accumulative.
The other genes investigated (aroL and aroB) do not seem to affect the metabolism of
the cell in the conducted experiment (Figure 3.8). This does not mean overexpression of
these genes is not toxic for the cell under other circumstances. Ratgher, it suggests that
the cell either 1) does not have sufficient flux through the shikimate pathway in the envi­
ronments tested to have a detrimental effect on the cell overall or 2) is not affected by the
overexpression of these genes even in the case of increased flux through the shikimate
pathway. To truly assess the growth impact of the genes, it is necessary to first upregulate
the production of a combination of precursors (most likely including the products of PpsA
and/or TktA). In the following section, potential effects of overexpression of the respective

4.3.1
The growth effect of expressing multiple genes does not seem
synergistic
The combined expression of aroG* and aroL from Ptra causes significant growth reduction
at 0 M C3O8, with an apparent slight recovery of growth upon induction (Figure 3.10).
This unexpected result requires further investigation, especially considering the potential
synergistic effects of overexpressing multiple genes in the shikimate pathway. Due to time
constraints, however, this experiment was not repeated. Interestingly, it seems growth of
strains expressing aroG* and tktA from Ptra (Figure 3.11) is entirely unaffected by the
addition of 3OC8. The strain shows growth comparable to the control in both induction
states, which is surprising given the apparent effect of aroG* in the same experiment.
This effect may be explained by the proximity of the two Ptra, which could cause divergent
homology, possibly physically hindering transcription from both promoters at the same
time.
If time had permitted, more experiments exploring the role of the genes causing a growth
defect (ppsA, tktA, aroG* and aroL) would have been conducted. As mentioned above,
the construction of multi­gene plasmids is interesting, but must be carefully designed

4.4
Expression and timing of induction of the tra system
largely determines the growth impact
Comparing expression of the same gene from a constitutive promoter (e.g. PBioFAB038)
and an inducible promoter (Ptra) can help explore the efficiency of using QS systems for
production [56]. The results of this thesis would suggest that expression of metabolically
relevant genes from constitutive promoters and expression from fully induced Ptra at in­
oculation confer a similar growth burden to the cell (Figure 3.12 and 3.13). Addition of
3OC8 has not been shown to affect growth in cells lacking expression of a metabolically
relevant gene from Ptra (Figure 3.6). However, the strains tested throughout this thesis do
not express traI, the gene encoding the AHL synthase responsible for production of the
3OC8. Integration of traI in the production host is necessary to evaluate important aspects
of the system and the potential benefits of gradual production changes, unlike the more
binary response to inducers like IPTG. Upon genome integration of traI, the growth effect
of Ptra­overexpressed metabolically relevant genes needs to be reevaluated. Transcrip­
tion of the synthase producing the QS signaling molecule has, in other lux­like systems,
been shown to add to the metabolic burden of the cell [59].
The timing of tra system induction also influences the growth burden. Although the tra
systen is not accurately described without the integration of traI, it is possible to mimic
the growth impact of varying concentrations of autoinducer at varying time points [29]. By
manually adding the AHL at predetermined time points corresponding to the lag phase,
the exponential phase and the stationary phase, the impact of timing can be investigated
(Figure 3.14, 3.15 and Appendix Figure A.12). The results of Figure 3.14 show that only
high concentrations (10−6 M) of 3OC8 impacts the growth of the population, and only at
induction (t = 0 hours) and after in the beginning of the exponential phase. These results
are ideal for optimal utility of the tra system, as this concentrations is unrealistic for a
traI­integrated system [31]. Upon traI integration, 3OC8 levels will increase with biomass,
delaying full induction until the exponential phase. After t = 4 hours (corresponding to
the beginning of the exponential phase in the strain carrying Ptra:tktA), induction with any
concentration of 3OC8 did not impact growth (Appendix Figure A.12). This is potentially
due to more late competition for the available carbon in the media. If so, the central
carbon metabolism will slow, leading to slower cell growth (late exponential phase and
stationary phase). In such a scenario, overexpression of tktA would potentially make no
difference to the cell as there is less flux through the PPP, and thus less substrate for TktA
to consume. However, the actual effects on growth would depend on many other factors,
including the overall metabolic state of the cell and the expression levels of other enzymes
[2, 57]. Yan et al. (2021) describes the growth­phase dependent effect of implementing
a heterologous QS system (lux) in Hafnia alvei. The study found significant differences
in the transcriptome of the same organism with/without an integrated heterologous QS
system, highlighting the burden of producing QS components on glycolysis [60].

4.5.2
Alternative cloning strategies
Throughout this thesis, Gibson assembly was used for assembling plasmids from two
fragments. While this process is definitely more specific than other cloning techniques,
a viable alternative could be Uracil­Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning, in which
overhands are ensured to be exactly compatible. Other cloning techniques in the Prather
Lab (all of which were tested for during this project to no avail) are: pJET cloning [61],
Golden Gate (using the restriction enzyme BsaI) [62] and Circular Polymerase Extension
Cloning (CPEC) [63].
4.5.3
Genome integration versus plasmid­based transcription
When engineering E. coli with the purpose of optimizing expression of a compound, sev­
eral decisions are required. First, a decision on the placement of the engineered DNA: is
it to be integrated on the chromosome of the bacteria, or produced transiently from a plas­
mid? There are advantages and disadvantages to both. Plasmid­based expression yields
higher (diversity in) copy­number, as opposed to chromosomal integration of one copy, in
which the gene is found in only the amount of times the chromosome is present (i.e. one
copy per cell). Plasmid­based expression, however, requires constant selection pressure
in order to not lose the plasmid, and thus the ability to express the GOI. This selection
usually requires the addition of an antibiotic to the media and the presence of the corre­
sponding resistance gene on the plasmid, continuously ensuring the only cells surviving
possess the plasmid. The production of the resistance marker imposes a burden on the
cell, leading to suboptimal growth relative to genome integration. Addition of antibiotics is
expensive, and thus unfavorable in industrial settings. Further, plasmids are commonly
used in proof­of­concept studies because genome optimization is especially time consum­
ing. Genome integration is more time consuming than plasmid assembly and transforma­
tion. In this thesis, only plasmid­based expression was investigated. However, upon
adaption for large­scale adaption, it would likely benefit growth to genome integrate all
QS components. The diversity in plasmid copy number is conferred by the origin of repli­
cation (ORI). Depending on the ORI, the plasmid is present in the cell at a specific count.
Types of origins can be compatible (controlled by separate factors) or non­compatible
(controlled by the same factors, and thus not necessarily divided equally between daugh­
ter cells). Evidently, if more plasmids are required in one engineered organism, the ORIs
must be compatible, leading to equal separation into the daughter cells of the engineered
organism. Further, each plasmid usually possess a unique selection marker, leading to
the death of the production organism upon loss of just one of the plasmids [8, 53]. The
ORI used in NMTK­plasmids was p15A, which was compatible with other ORIs possibly
utilized for further research. An engineer can include several copies of the transcriptional
unit, leading to higher expression of the gene ­ this can be done via genome integration
or plasmid­based expression. Jones Prather et al. (2000) demonstrated the importance
of balancing plasmid copy number to optimize production, as high­copy number confers
a growth burden on the cell. They argue that low­copy plasmids are especially useful
44

==PAGE:59==
in engineering when substrates are used in the central carbon metabolism, to prevent
overconsumption of substrates important to growth [57].
4.5.4
Transcriptional unit design considerations
Prokaryotic genetic architecture ­ and thus transcription ­ differs from eukaryotes in the
ability to express genes polycistronically in operons. When engineering E. coli, it was to be
decided if genes of multi­gene plasmids would be transcribed from operons or each gene
from an individual promoter. Selection of the operon structure leads to some constraints
in the number of genes expressed, since the chance of the RNA polymerase dissociating
from the DNA increases with each bp. Thus, by altering the order of genes, the rela­
tive rate of product can be controlled. If a protein is known to be the limiting step, the
corresponding gene should be placed in the beginning of the transcriptional unit, imme­
diately after the promoter [7, 10]. Due to time constraints, only two multi­gene plasmids
(pNMTK007 and pNMTK011, see Table 2.1) were assembled. pNMTK007 expressed the
GOIs from individual promoters (Ptra:aroG*+Ptra:tktA), while pNMTK011 expressed the
GOIs from the same Ptra (Ptra:aroG*:aroL). Without direct comparisons of plasmids con­
taining the same genes in different configurations, the impact of the plasmid architecture
in this system can unfortunately not be discussed.
pNMTK007 was designed with bi­directional Ptra. This was done to potentially take ad­
vantage of the dissociation of double­stranded DNA around promoters during transcription
initiation. The RNA polymerase needs to associate to the single­stranded DNA, so the
double­stranded DNA has to come apart in what is known as the transcription bubble. If
two promoters are placed back­to­back in a bi­directional manner, the transcription bubble
opened to allow transcription from one of the promoters might also ease transcription from
the other promoter. However, the distance between the bidirectional promoters is crucial
to this effect. If the promoters are too distant, the transcriptional bubble of one promoter
will not benefit the other. If the promoters are too close, any transcription factors required
to initiate transcription from one promoter might entirely repress transcription from the
others by effectively acting as a repressor physically blocking the RNA polymerase from
associating to the tra­box [9]. Without comparison of more plasmids containing the same
genes but in bi­directional versus uni­directional configuration, it is not possible to extract
any results from the experiments of this thesis regarding the optimal plasmid architecture.
4.6
tra­based production has potential in an industrial
settings
The knowledge on the tra system collected throughout this study, emphasizes the potential
of utilizing the tra system in industrial settings. Even though data on production of relevant
products related to the shikimate pathway was not obtained in this study due to a lack
of established method, we can hypothesize that strains showing growth impacts do so
because of detrimental effects caused by the actions of the respective enzymes. As seen
in Figure 3.6, the transcription of the non­metabolically relevant gene gfp did not lead to
impaired growth. In the same experiment, the ability to tune the promoter for controlled
expression was demonstrated. Abisado et al. (2018) describes the metabolic burden
conferred by production of QS components. To potentially negate this burden it would be
interesting to couple the use of QS in heterologous system directly to growth of the host
[32].
In an ideal situation, it would have been interesting to investigate how production of prod­
ucts relevant to the Ptra­overexpressed genes in this study effects the growth. Ptra has
been demonstrated to be a highly controllable system, unlike many other more binary
inducible systems. It is expected that the toggleability demonstrated by Ptra in the ex­

4.7
Future direction
Due to the time restricted nature of the work performed in this thesis, a plethora of ad­
ditional experiments would have been interesting to perform. Some of these have been
described in the sections above. In the following, a summary of future developments that
could potentially complement the findings of this thesis are described.
• Further plasmid construction: If time had permitted, a plasmid containing all genes
in the shikimate pathway shown to impact growth would have been incorporated
into the strain. To do this, it is relevant to first construct more multi­gene plasmids
in various plasmid architectures to determine the optimal configuration of genes.
From the results of this thesis, it seems the most important genes to include in such
a plasmid would be ppsA, tktA, aroG* and aroL. However, upon overexpression of
these, aroB might appear as the next bottleneck.

using QS systems instead of other dynamic promoters is, exactly, the autoinducing
character of the system allowing for optimal initial growth.
• Further investigating the growth impact of differential timing of induction: Additional
genes (and ideally plasmids containing more metabolically relevant genes) should
be investigated with regards to the growth effect of differential induction timing. In
particular, the pattern of growth impediment observed between the strains express­
ing ppsA and tktA should be elucidated. It is especially relevant to investigate these
effects after traI has been integrated.
• Establishing a method for production of chorismate: To truly characterize the poten­
tial benefit of Ptra­based overexpression of metabolically relevant genes, it is crucial
to be able to measure the production. Establishing a method (e.g. HPLC) for reli­

next step.

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, an E. coli­adapted tra QS system has been characterized in terms of dy­
namic and operational range. Ptra was selected for overexpression of genes related to the
shikimate pathway. It has been demonstrated that growth is impaired by overexpressing
selected genes (ppsA and tktA) related to the central carbon metabolism in E. coli. Genes
from the shikimate pathway, aroG* and aroL, also conferred a negative growth impact
upon overexpression, although additional research is needed to accurately describe the
effect on growth. The growth burden conferred by Ptra­based overexpression in response
to full induction with 3OC8 of strains expressing aroL and tktA was demonstrated to be
comparable to that of constitutive expression of the genes, but only when fully induced at
initial inoculation. However, the utilization of the tra system does not impact the growth of
the organism if a non­metabolically relevant protein, such as GFP, is expressed. By inves­
tigating the timing of induction of the tra­promoter, the vulnerability of a population in the
lag phase is revealed, as strains expressing metabolically relevant genes from Ptra show
only slight growth impediment when induced post­lag phase, and only at high concen­
trations of 3OC8. This indicates an advantageous match with the natural concentration
range of 3OC8 when produced by TraI. In order for the tra system to be truly autonomous
and applicable in an industrial system, traI encoding the 3OC8 synthase must be inte­
grated into the production system. The work conducted in this thesis demonstrates the
importance of characterizing dynamic systems, thus traI integration must be meticulously
designed to balance growth and production. In short, the tra system exhibits toggleabil­
ity, has low leakiness and later induction reduces the growth impact, making it an ideal
candidate for use in synthetic systems related to the central carbon metabolism.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit
def hill_equation(x, Vmax, K, n, basal):
"""Hill equation for dose-response curve with basal expression"""
return basal + (Vmax - basal) * (x**n) / (K**n + x**n)
