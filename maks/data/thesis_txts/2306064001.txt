Abstract
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) have shown great potential as an
improvement of the existing technology for ultrasonic medical imaging, which is traditionally
performed using piezoelectric transducers. The mechanical processing used for creating trans-
ducer arrays using piezoelectric transducers is inferior compared to the refined technology for
producing micron-sized features in MicroElectroMechanical Systems.
The fabrication of CMUTs with plate thickness in the range of 2µm is usually based on BESOI
wafers, which normally have sub-par device layer thickness tolerances of ±0.5 µm, which can
result in large deviations in critical CMUT parameters such as pull-in voltage and resonance
frequency. Thickness variation over a single wafer will affect the allowable operation bias Vop,
which will be limited by the cells with the lowest pull-in voltages.
No viable alternative to BESOI plates exist at the moment, as SIMOX SOI wafers are unavail-
able at thickness above ∼1.5µm. SOI wafers based on the commercial SmartCut™technology
promise adequate thickness tolerances, but are only available in large volume, making them
unsuitable in a university setting.
In this thesis, a process for producing CMUTs using a plate defined by a boron etch stop is
presented. The process was developed by optimising process parameters controlling surface
roughness and wafer curvature, leading to bondable boron doped wafers, ultimately resulting
in an obtained plate thickness of (2.02 ± 0.03) µm.
A functional CMUT with a boron etch stop plate was produced, and compared to existing de-
vices using BESOI plates. The boron etch stop based devices were found to have more uniform
plates, leading to more consistent correlation with a lumped element fit close to the pull-in
voltage, than what has previously been obtained on SOI based devices.
The findings of the work performed in this thesis contributed directly to the submission of two
conference abstracts; one to the 15th International Workshop on Micromachined Ultrasonic
Transducers (MUT), and one to the 2016 IEEE International Ultrasonics Symposium (IUS).

Introduction
The Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer (CMUT) is, as the name suggests, a
transducer which is constructed to transmit and receive ultrasonic pressure waves. This ability
facilitates an obvious application area in medical imaging [20], where ultrasonic imaging is tra-
ditionally performed using piezoelectric transducers. These types of transducers are produced
using traditional mechanical processing, which inherently limits the flexibility of the designs.
CMUTs, on the other hand, use clean-room processing techniques, which have been refined
over decades to easily provide sub-micron feature sizes.
In this chapter, the working principles of the CMUT will be outlined, and the physical models
used to describe the system will be described briefly. Afterwards, the challenges of producing
uniform plates will be addressed, and the current state-of-the-art will be summarised. Following
this, the aim of the thesis will be presented, and an outline of the thesis structure will be
provided.
1.1
Working principles
When the CMUT is used for imaging, an array of elements as depicted in Fig. 1.1 is often
used. Here, a 1-D array is illustrated, consisting of a number of elements that individually
have a large quantity of circular CMUT cells. A silicon plate with a thin Al layer covers each
element, such that all cells in each element can be activated by applying a bias between the
element and the bottom electrode contact. Each of the cells have a cross-sectional layout as
seen in Fig. 1.2. The important parameters here are the cell radius, a, the plate thickness, h,
the vacuum gap, gv, and finally the insulation oxide thickness, gi.
In its fundamental nature as a transducer, the CMUT has two operating modes, shown in
Fig. 1.3 and 1.4. In transmit mode, an AC signal is converted to mechanical movement of the
plate, which in turn creates pressure waves that propagate away from the device. The DC bias
has two functions: Firstly, it enables the plate to perform a full oscillation, as the electrostatic
forces are only attractive. Secondly, it provides the so-called spring-softening effect, lowering
the effective spring constant as the height of the vacuum gap is reduced. In receive mode,
incident pressure waves make the plate oscillate, resulting in an electrical signal that can be
registered using an amplifier.

1.2
State of the art
Generally, fusion-bonded CMUT devices have plate thickness on the order of microns, which
is usually fabricated using Silicon-On-Insulator (SOI) wafers. An overview of the process for
creating a thin plate on a CMUT device using such a process is shown in Fig. 1.5. First off, the
device layer of the SOI wafer is fusion bonded to a bottom wafer with cavities defined. Next,
the silicon handle layer is etched, preferentially using a process with a high selectivity to the
Buried OXide (BOX)-layer. Lastly, the BOX is etched, leaving only the device layer as a plate
on the bottom wafer.
Several different types of SOI wafers exist, each with their own applications and advantages.
The overall fabrication steps for three of the most prominent types are shown in Fig. 1.5.
The first technique, Bond and Etchback Silicon-On-Insulator (BESOI), involves bonding of an

±0.5µm pose significant alteration of critical parameters such as pull-in voltage and resonance
frequency.
Thus, another process to create plates with greater uniformity could be a significant advantage
for CMUT devices. In its essence, all that is needed for controllable plate creation is an well-
defined etch stop. When using SOI-wafers, the etch stop is the BOX-layer, with the requirement
of having a good Si/SiO2 selectivity, which is rather easily obtained. Two other widely employed
etch stop techniques are the boron etch stop and the electrochemical etch stop [4]. Of these
two, the most easily applied is the boron etch stop, which does not require biasing of the
sample during etching. The boron etch stop exploits the fact that several wet Si etchants have
a significant decline in etch rate above boron concentrations of around 5 × 1019 cm−3. Thus, a
process based on a boron etch stop can be summarised as seen in Fig. 1.7. First off, a high-
doped boron layer is created with the desired device-layer thickness. This layer is bonded to
the bottom wafer, and is thinned down using a KOH etchant that selectively stops on highly
doped boron layers (> 5×1019 cm−3). This leaves only the highly doped boron layer as a plate
on the bottom wafer.
This technique has been tried earlier at Technical University of Denmark (DTU), first in
2007 [12, 21], where a pressure sensor was designed using a similar approach. Here, surface
roughness problems was experienced, and the bonding had to be performed on an intermediate
BoroSilicate Glass (BSG)-layer. Another project was done in 2015, where it was attempted to
produce CMUTs using this technique [2]. Here, bonding to device wafers was never achieved
in practice.
The experienced difficulties can most likely be attributed to excessive surface
roughness or curvature, both of which are critical for successful bonding.

1.3. Aim of Thesis
1.3
Aim of Thesis
The aim of this project is to develop a process for producing CMUTs with 2 µm plates based
upon a boron etch-stop. The thesis will deal with the optimisations of parameters critical for
bonding, namely surface roughness and wafer curvature. It is not within the scope of this
thesis to perform optimisations of the bottom substrate, and as such already produced devices
will be used for the final fabrication steps.
The process should overcome the problems that prevented bonding in the earlier attempt at
DTU [2]. In order to obtain successful bonding of boron doped wafers, the following two criteria
should be met [27]:
• Surface roughness below 5 Å
• Curvature over wafer width below 25 µm
It is expected that CMUTs using boron etch stop based top plates will have several advantages
over CMUTs with SOI based top plates, due to higher plate thickness uniformity. This will
provide a sharper resonance peak, due to the smaller resonance frequency variation over the
elements. This should allow the devices to operate closer to the pull-in voltage, which should
in turn result in higher coupling coefficient, k2.
1.4
Thesis outline
The thesis is structured in 6 chapters, including the introduction chapter. In the following, a
brief overview will be given of the contents in each chapter.
Chapter 2 introduces the theory behind hydrophilic fusion bonding. Afterwards, different
methods for bond strength characterisation will be presented. This includes the design and
fabrication efforts that went into producing a bond strength test structure based on protruding
oxide mesas.
Chapter 3 explains the theory behind the boron etch stop for CMUT fabrication. This in-
cludes a brief overview of Fickian diffusion and the boron doping process. Afterwards, the
theory behind the KOH etch process will be presented, and the etch stop mechanism will be
accounted for. Subsequently, a process development section will describe the practical efforts
performed with regard to meeting the strict roughness and wafer bow tolerances required for
fusion bonding.
Chapter 4 describes the cleanroom fabrication of the CMUT with boron etch stop based plate.
First off, a general overview of the process for the bottom electrode will be given. Secondly,
the fabrication of the top electrode will be described in more detail, including the bonding and
etch back steps. Finally, the metallisation and electrode definition steps will be accounted for.
Chapter 5 presents the equipment used for characterisation of the CMUT devices. After-
wards, electrical and acoustical measurements of the finished devices will be presented, and a
comparison with the SOI based CMUTs will be given, and any differences will be discussed.

2
Fusion bonding
In this chapter, the theoretical concepts in fusion bonding will be treated, followed by different
qualitative and quantitative methods for characterising the bond strength and quality. Follow-
ing this, the process development performed with regard to a bond strength characterisation
method using protruding oxide mesas will be presented.
2.1
Theory of fusion bonding
Sufficiently flat silicon surfaces brought into intimate contact will bond spontaneously.
In
general, there are two different types of bonding mechanisms that can be used: Hydrophilic
and hydrophobic bonding. Hydrophobic bonding requires two silicon surfaces free of the 1-
2 nm native oxide that naturally forms on silicon surfaces. In this case, it is assumed that
the surfaces are hydrogen terminated. Upon contacting the wafers, Van der Waals bonds will
form between the hydrogen atoms on either side of the interface, forming the initial bond.
Hydrophilic bonding, on the other side, exploits the inherent hydrophilicity of the native oxide
on the silicon surfaces. Such surfaces are usually terminated in silanol groups as illustrated in
Fig. 2.1. Upon bringing such two specimens into contact with each other, the thin chemisorbed
water film that forms on either side of the interface will form hydrogen bonds to the opposite
side, forming the initial bond.
The bond strength obtainable without annealing is rather
small.
Values of 100 −150 mJ m−2 are reported for hydrophilic bonding, while somewhat
smaller values are seen for hydrophobic bonding, in the order 20 −30 mJ m−2 [27]. After the
bonding, a high temperature annealing step is usually employed to increase the bond strength
by more than an order of magnitude. In Fig. 2.2(a), experimental bond strength is illustrated
as a function of annealing temperature, both for hydrophobic and hydrophilic bonding. For
hydrophilic bonding, when increasing the temperature above the boiling point of water, the
trapped water molecules start evaporating from the surface, eventually allowing the terminating
silanol groups to contact each other. Here, a condensation reaction can occur, leaving stronger

An explanation for the apparently constant surface energy in the temperature range from
200−800 ◦C in Fig. 2.2(a), is that microgaps between wafers limit the area that can effectively
be bonded. When the temperature rises higher, viscous flow of the oxide can potentially fill
the microgaps, increasing the bonded area, and thus the observed surface energy [27].
Maszara et al. [17] reports that the mechanism of viscous flow is apparent at temperatures
of above 1000 ◦C, as is seen in Fig. 2.2(b). For lower temperatures, the surface energy does
not increase significantly over time after the initial bond has been formed. However, when the
temperature increases to 1000 −1100 ◦C, an increase in surface energy can be observed after
annealing for a long time > 1 hr. As the mechanism of viscous flow becomes more dominating
at 1200 ◦C, is is seen that the surface energy rises continually. In other words, if it is deemed
sufficient to anneal at a lower temperature - say - 900 ◦C, the anneal time can be made rather
short without compromising the obtainable bond strength at that particular temperature.
2.2
Bond characterisation
As the bonding steps in MicroElectroMechanical Systems (MEMS) device fabrication constitute
some of the most critical steps, reliable characterisation of the attained bond quality is vital.
Several methods are discussed in the literature, and the subject is reviewed in great detail
in [42]. Basically, the methods can be divided into non-destructive and destructive methods.
The destructive methods are most often designed to give a quantitative figure for the bond
strength, by directly testing the mechanical properties of the bond interface. While potentially
accurate, the destructive nature of the methods limit the practical applicability of the methods
in a production setting. The non-destructive methods are often aimed at giving an overview

necessary to investigate other options for quantitative bond strength analysis.

IR Transmission
By placing the bonded wafer pair in a measurement setup as the one illustrated in Fig. 2.5,
inhomogeneities across the wafer can be registered. The polychromatic spectrum emitted by
the white light lamp is filtered, so the light that reaches the sample is primarily in the near
infrared range. Upon interaction with the sample, the light scatter on impurities and lattice
imperfections, which in principle can be visualised given good enough imaging resolution.
Another phenomenon that can be seen is concentric interference rings, known as Newton’s
rings.
These rings occur when a gap between two surfaces vary in size on a length scale
comparable to the wavelength of the illuminating light, over the course of a greater lateral
distance. This means than there will be alternating fringes with constructive and destructive
interference between the transmitted and reflected light.
In practice, this allows for quick inspection of bonded wafers with great contrast between
bonded and non-bonded areas. When using a set-up as the one described here, the imaging
resolution depends mostly on the camera utilised. In the case of a CCD camera, the sensitivity
drops off around 1100 nm, which is also the wavelength where the absorption of silicon de-
creases. This means that the measurement regime is on the edge of what is physically possible,
resulting in a higher required gain to obtain the desired signal level, leading to more visible
noise in the images due to dark current. To mitigate this, one is left with the option of cooling
the CCD, or using a photo-sensor based on InGaAs which is often sensitive in a spectrum up
to 1700 nm.

by Middleman et al.[19]. In gas-source doping, a boron containing gas is fed into the furnace
tube, where it reacts with oxygen to form boron trioxide. In the case of diborane as the source
gas, the reaction is as follows
B2H6 + 3 O2 −−→B2O3 + 3 H2O.
(3.5)
The major issue with a gas source, is the susceptibility to both axial and radial source gas
depletion. Thus, if the process parameters are not well controlled, this approach could yield
great variations in doping concentrations. Using solid source boron doping, the axial depletion
is less of a concern, as described in the following section.

3.2
Doping characterisation
When utilising impurity doping in a process, proper characterisation of the resulting depth
profiles and altered electrical properties is an essential tool. Depending on the specific re-
quirements, multiple approaches can be used. A very direct and accurate technique is SIMS,
where an ion beam is used to sputter surface atoms off the sample, the macroscopic atomic
composition of which can then be obtained using the count rates from a mass spectrometer.
Owing to the relative complexity and high time consumption of the above method, another
simpler method can be used for quick characterisation of the doping level.
As the doping
process in its nature increases the quantity of free charge carriers, electrical sheet resistance
can be used to measure the doping level. Both of these methods will be discussed in more
detail in the following.

effective surface area available for reaction. A surfactant covering a (110) surface may strongly
influence this mechanism.
An interesting phenomenon can be experienced when adding IPA to a KOH etchant, namely
formation of pyramidal hillocks on the etched surface, as seen in Fig. 3.11. The occurrence of
these pyramids is explained by Peeters [26], who argues that because IPA decreases the ratio
between the lateral <110> etch rate and the <100> rate, any pyramidal structure that is
initiated will grow larger as the etch progresses. Without IPA, any such initiated pyramid will
be underetched away due to the higher <110> etch rate. The initiation is mostly attributed to
contaminants in the etching solution, and Peeters [26] showed that the resistivity of the used
De-Ionised (DI) water has paramount influence on the amount of created surface damage, as
any contaminant that places itself on the surface will mask off that area, causing the growth
of a pyramid to begin. It was also found, that agitation can decrease the amount of damage
caused.

in the trials at lower oxygen concentration.

3.4.4

3.5. Summary
3.5
Summary
In this chapter, the basic theory of boron doping from planar solid sources has been introduced.
Following this, theory of KOH etching was introduced, and the mechanisms behind the etch
stop, as well as the addition of IPA was discussed. Following this, the current state of boron
doping was investigated, with a focus on determining whether a standard procedure would
yield surfaces with sufficiently low surface roughness and curvature to allow fusion bonding.
This was found to not be the case, which necessitated two sections on process development,
each focusing on controlling one of these bonding-inhibiting factors. Following this, different
anneal temperatures were tested, and it was found to be sufficient to anneal the bonded wafer
pairs at 950 ◦C. This was shown to result in a plate thickness of (2.02 ± 0.03) µm, very close to
the desired 2 µm. Having developed a suitable process for fabricating bondable boron doped
surfaces with a controllable junction depth, the foudation is laid for treating the actual CMUT
fabrication in the next chapter.

CMUT Fabrication

In this chapter, the entire fabrication process will be discussed, making clear where the top

The process for fabricating the bottom electrode will be described briefly, in order to give the

4.1
Overview of process

Experimentally, bottom substrates were

4. The entire oxide and nitride is stripped
5. A thin insulation oxide is grown, and SiN4 is deposited
6. The nitride layer is patterned to define the cavities
7. A second LOCOS oxidation creates the post oxide to form the cavities
8. The top electrode is fusion bonded to the bottom substrate
9. The top electrode is thinned down
10. Opening to bottom electrode and definition of top electrodes in aluminium

4.3
Bonding and finalisation
4.3.1
RCA Cleaning
The top electrodes are RCA cleaned using a slightly different approach than the one discussed
earlier. The steps performed are as follows:
• RCA-2 (5:1:1 H2O:HCL:H2O2) 5 minutes
• RCA-1 (5:1:1 H2O:NH4OH:H2O2) 10 minutes
• 5 % HF 15 seconds
• RCA-2 (5:1:1 H2O:HCL:H2O2) 10 minutes
Between all steps above, wafers are rinsed in DI water as usual. The first change is the inclusion
of the RCA-2 step prior to the RCA-1 step, which is traditionally performed first. This is done
because the NH4OH in the RCA-1 solution will attack the silicon surface, thus decreasing the
thickness of the boron doped etch-stop layer. By using the less aggressive RCA-2 solution first,
a thin protective oxide layer is grown, protecting the silicon during the subsequent RCA-1
treatment. Another difference is the reduction of the HF bath submersion time from 30 s to
15 s, as well as the omission of the second HF step. This is generally done within the MEMS
Applied Sensors group, in order to preserve step heights on the surface. This is especially
important when the process incorporates silicon nitride diffusion masking, as a 30 s HF dip
will easily reduce a cavity depth by more than 10 µm. In this simplified process without any
structures, the importance of this is less pronounced. One argument could be to avoid potential
surface roughness increase by the HF etch.
The bottom electrodes are also RCA cleaned, with the second HF dip omitted, and the first
one reduced to 15 s.

Conclusion
In this project, a successful process for fabricating 2 µm plates for CMUTs using a boron etch
stop process was developed. The process was tested by bonding top wafers to 1-D CMUT
bottom substrates, which yielded fully functional CMUT devices, living up to the expectations
with regard to improved performance over SOI based devices.
The theory of hydrophilic fusion bonding was presented, accounting for the reactions taking
place during the pre-bond and annealing steps. The traditional Maszara method for charac-
terising bond strength quantitatively was described, and found to be largely inapplicable for
samples annealed at temperatures over 700 ◦C due to the imminent risk of brittle fracture dur-
ing blade insertion. Another method explored in literature based on protruding oxide mesas
was presented, and a process and mask set for fabricating these structures was developed. The
characterisation of these devices did not yield bond-strength data, probably due to limited
imaging resolution. The PL Mapper method, not used before in the MEMS Applied sensors
group, was discovered as an alternative to IR Transmission imaging for qualitative bond qual-
ity characterisation. It was found to produce images of bonded wafers with better contrast
to un-bonded areas, less noise, as well as the practical ability to characterise an entire wafer
in one image. Also, the characterisation using the PL Mapper is largely independent on the
operator, yielding more consistent and comparable data.
The theory behind solid source boron doping was described, and an analytical doping model
based on Fickian diffusion was presented. This model was compared to more elaborate sim-
ulations performed using the Athena process simulator, and the simple diffusion model was
found to be insufficient at the very high concentrations considered in relation to etch stops
(> 1019 cm−3). Following this, the theory of KOH etching was described, accounting for the
surface reactions involved in the process. A possible mechanism for the varying etch rates of
the different crystal planes in silicon was described, and dramatic lowering of the etch rate
in areas with high concentrations of p-type dopants was discussed. Finally, the influence of
the addition of IPA as a surfactant with regard to obtaining p/p++ selectivity in excess of
1 : 100 was described. Afterwards, a standard process for boron doping was investigated, and
it was found to produce boron doped wafers with surface roughness Ra > 5 µm, along with
wafer bows in excess of 70 µm, both of which are unacceptable for fusion bonding. It was
found that a doubled O2 flow of 0.4 SLM would decrease the surface roughness by as much as
96%, reaching best-case values of Ra = 1.64 Å. It was further found that a process based on
double-side doping of the wafers could reduce the curvature to below 30 µm, which was found
to be sufficiently low for successful fusion bonding. SEM plate thickness measurements of the
fabricated plates showed a thickness over the central 60 mm of (2.02 ± 0.03) µm, very close to
the desired thickness of 2 µm, in sharp contrast to the standard SOI wafers with device layer
thickness of (2 ± 0.5) µm normally used in the group.
The devices fabricated were characterised using measurement equipment at DTU Nanotech.
First off, impedance measurements were performed on a single element, and compared to

equivalent devices with SOI based plates. Generally, the boron etch stop devices were found to
have higher pull-in voltage than the equivalent SOI based devices. It was found that the fitting
error of a lumped element fit is generally lower for the boron etch stop based devices than the
SOI based devices, and that this error remains consistently low until very close to pull-in for
the boron etch stop devices. For the SOI based devices, the fit quality starts to degrade further

boron etch stop based devices, leading to CMUT cells with coinciding resonance frequencies
and pull-in voltages.
Measurements on every 10th element was performed over most of an array produced with
boron etch stop. Here, the resonance frequency was found to be fres = 10.9 MHz ± 3.8% at
Vbias = 250 V, where the uniformity can be related directly to a small plate thickness variation
over the array. Lastly, brief acoustical measurements were performed, where the ability of the
CMUTs with boron etch stop based plate to produce ultrasonic pressure waves was confirmed.
6.1
Outlook
Although CMUT devices with boron etch stop have successfully been fabricated during the
course of this project, many optimisation tasks and interesting prospects remain to be explored.
Process-wise, a large problem encountered was related to the formation of pyramidal hillocks
on the surface during KOH/IPA etching, limiting the etchant solution to the processing of
a single wafer before it needs to be discarded, if an unacceptable amount of hillocks is not
to be produced. The influence of parameters such as KOH concentration, temperature and
agitation could be explored. Also, the introduction of a N2 ambient above the etchant solution
as discussed by Peeters [26] could be tested, with the prospect of reducing CO2 absorption
from the etchant/air interface.
Another issue that could be explored is the non-uniformity in doping level observed using the
process developed in this project. A correlation between the oxygen flow and achieved surface
roughness was found, at the expense of reduced doping uniformity.
It may be possible to
find a more optimal set of parameters, where the doping uniformity is increased, while still
maintaining acceptably low surface roughness.
Another problem encountered was related to the wafer curvature. It was found that the wafer
bow on new wafers were largely varying, but generally on the order of 30 µm. In order to
avoid the screening procedure to find the wafers with the least curvature, wafers could possibly
be ordered with a better tolerance. If the problems related to different doping levels in the
asymmetrically placed slots during boron doping could be solved, a very simple double-side
doping process with no screening necessary could be used.
Device-wise, the boron etch stop plates could also be tested on the larger 2-D CMUT arrays,
where the improved plate uniformity could possible yield even greater improvements of the
uniformity of parameters across the arrays.

D.2. Procedure: Manual bonding
Side view
Front view
Figure D.6: Illustration of the manual bonding concept. To the right, a front view of the wafers is
seen. The arrows indicate the points where the wafers are held with one hand. To the left, a side view

D.2
Procedure: Manual bonding
In earlier CMUT devices fabricated within the group, the fusion bonding step has been per-
formed using the EVG NIL bonding machine. This equipment allows for low pressure bonding
p < 1 × 10−3 mbar, which is thought to be beneficial for the operation of the CMUT devices.
However, as this machine was unavailable when this experiment was carried out, an approach
with manual bonding by hand was tested. This method was used by [2], and has also been
recommended by DTU Danchip employee, ph.D. Karen Birkelund. The concept is illustrated
in Figure D.6.
The concept is in its nature very simple, but there are some crucial points that must be followed
in order to get successful bonding. First of all, new gloves is to be put on before handling the
wafers.
The wafers should be handled as little as possible, and the box should be closed
immediately after taking wafers out. Second of all, a special set of tweezers, dedicated to this
use is recommended. It is also advisable to do the procedure in an area without other people,
preferably right below a HEPA filter.
When taking the wafers out of the box, the bottom wafer is held in one hand, only handled by
its side edges, as illustrated. Next, the top wafer is taken out, and held in the same position.
Then, the two wafers are moved carefully together, so that only 2-3 mm of space remains
between them. It is important that the wafers are not handled by their top/bottom, as this
will increase the risk of particle deposition on the surface. When the wafers are in position,
pressure is applied between the thumb and index finger on the other hand, in the centre of the
wafer stack. The wafers should bond immediately, but pressure is applied for 20 s to be on
the safe side. A practical note here, is that the gloves can get stuck in the gaps at the edges,
between the two wafers. This is thought to be beneficial when bonding a concave top wafer to
a flat bottom wafer, as this distance will make sure that the applied force will preferentially
make the wafers contact in a centre point.

Fabrication of CMUTs using a boron etch-stop method
Søren Elmin Diederichsen, Filip Sandborg-Olsen, Mathias Engholm,
Kim Christiansen, Anders Lei, Jørgen Arendt Jensen, and Erik Vilain Thomsen
Technical University of Denmark, 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark
seldi@nanotech.dtu.dk
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) fabricated using the direct sili-
con fusion bonding technique can be prone to large thickness variations of the ﬂexible plate,
e.g. due to the chemical-mechanical polishing process used to deﬁne the device layer of cer-
tain silicon-on-insulator wafers. For silicon plates in the few-micrometer thickness regime this
means that the relative thickness variation becomes considerably large, typically 2 ± 0.5 µm
(± 25%), which translates to a relative deviation of up to 40% in pull-in voltage and 25% in
resonant frequency.
Thus, being able to predict and control the thickness variation of the plate is of great impor-
tance in order to fabricate devices with a predictable and uniform performance. The objective
is therefore to demonstrate the fabrication of CMUTs with silicon plates using a low-cost, bond
and boron etch-stop technique.
This work presents a bond and boron etch-stop scheme for fabricating a 2 µm thick plate of a
CMUT device. A standard 4" N-type silicon wafer is highly doped with boron to a concentration
of >1020 atoms/cm3 using a solid planar diffusion predeposition at 1125 ◦C for 60 min. The
doped wafer is fusion bonded to a silicon dioxide surface and thinned down using an 80 ◦C,
20 wt% KOH solution with isopropyl alcohol added to increase the selectivity between the highly
doped and undoped regions to approx. 1:100.
The surface roughness of the doped silicon surface is, with an atomic force microscope,
measured to Ra = 2 Å. The bow of the doped wafer before the fusion bonding step is <30 µm
across the wafer. The sheet resistance of the boron-doped layer is 3 Ω/2, which makes it
suitable as a top electrode. Scanning electron micrographs of cross-sections near the bonding
interface reveal a silicon plate mean thickness of 2.01 ± 0.06 µm (± 3%) over a distance of 6 cm
around the wafer center, as shown on the ﬁgure. This corresponds to a relative deviation of up
to 5% in pull-in voltage and 3% in resonant frequency.
The proposed method enables outstanding control of the plate thickness variation and is
considered a low-cost alternative to other methods prone to a large plate thickness variation.
Furthermore, this wet etch procedure allows for batch processing, making it a suitable method
for large-scale production of highly doped ﬂexible plates used as top electrodes in CMUTs.

Background, Motivation and Objective 
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) fabricated using the direct silicon fusion bonding technique can be prone 
to large thickness variations of the flexible plate, e.g. due to the chemical-mechanical polishing process used to define the device layer 
of certain silicon-on-insulator wafers. For silicon plates in the few-micrometer thickness regime this means that the relative thickness 
variation becomes considerably large, typically 2 ± 0.5 μm (± 25%), which translates to a relative deviation of up to 40% in pull-in 
voltage and 25% in resonance frequency. 
Thus, being able to predict and control the thickness variation of the plate is of great importance in order to fabricate devices with a 
predictable and uniform performance. The objective is therefore to demonstrate the fabrication of CMUTs with silicon plates using a 
low-cost, bond and boron etch-stop technique. 
Statement of Contribution/Methods 
This work presents a bond and boron etch-stop scheme for fabricating a 2 μm thick plate of a CMUT device. A standard 4" N-type 
silicon wafer is highly doped with boron to a concentration of > 1020 atoms/cm3 using a solid planar diffusion predeposition at 1125 
°C for 60 min. The doped wafer is fusion bonded to a silicon dioxide surface and thinned down using an 80 °C, 20%w KOH solution 
with isopropyl alcohol added to increase the selectivity between the highly doped and undoped regions to approx. 1:100.

The surface roughness of the doped silicon surface is measured with an atomic force microscope to be Ra = 2 Å. The wafer bow of the 
doped wafer before the fusion bonding step is < 30 μm across the wafer. The sheet resistance of the boron-doped layer is 3 Ω/sq.,

deviation of up to 5% in pull-in voltage and 3% in resonance frequency.  
The proposed method enables outstanding control of the plate thickness variation and is considered a low-cost alternative to other 
methods prone to a large plate thickness variation. Furthermore, this wet etch procedure allows for batch processing, making it a 
suitable method for large-scale production of highly doped flexible plates used as top electrodes in CMUTs.
