Abstract 
 
This study investigated the physiological response of human subjects to non-uniform indoor 
environment generated by chilled ceiling combined with localized chilled beam (CCLCB). The 
indoor environment generated by chilled ceiling combined with mixing ventilation (CCMV) was 
used as reference. 
The main objective was to study the influence of thermal environment generated by CCLCB on 
human physiological factors and compare it to the influence of thermal environment generated by 
CCMV. The physiological measurements included skin temperature measurement, which is 
currently considered a good indicator of thermal comfort, and indicators of the influence of 
ventilation systems on eye symptoms, i.e. blinking frequency and tear film quality. Other 
physiological factors which can potentially be related to thermal comfort were also investigated, 
i.e. blood pressure, forced vital capacity of lungs, heart rate and interbeat interval. 
24 subjects (12 male and 12 female) were exposed to the environment generated by the two 
systems under two background room air temperatures of 26 oC and 28 oC (summer conditions). 
The experiment was performed in an experimental chamber with dimensions of 4.2 m x 5.4 m x 
3.1 m. The chamber was equipped as one man office with two work stations with one laptop at 
each. Direct solar heat gains from the window were simulated by five heated radiant water panels 
on one wall and heated floor in half of the room. The main work station (WS1) was situated by the 
window and the other one (WS2) – on the opposite side of the room. The LCB was mounted above 
WS1 to create a cooler zone for the occupant sitting at the main work station. During the exposure 
the subjects had control over the primary air flow rate supplied by the LCB. Depending on the 
condition, the room temperature was kept at 26 oC or 28 oC and the supply air provided by mixing 
ventilation or the LCB was kept at 10 L/s or 13 L/s. The total exposure time was 1.5 hours, with 
additional 0.5 hour of acclimatization before the experiment. Subjects spent three sets of 30-
minute exposure: at WS1, WS2 and then back at WS1 again. 
The study showed that the direct convective cooling from the LCB was able to compensate for the 
radiant heating from the simulated window. The LCB succeeded in creating a cooler zone for the 
occupant at WS1. This resulted in certain physiological responses, like lower skin temperature and 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
higher blood pressure. The systems performed equally well at WS2, away from the window and 
LCB, since the chilled ceiling provided more or less the same conditions for the background 
environment in both tested systems: CCLCB and CCMV. The subjects were able to control the 
primary air flow from the chilled beam, which affected their blinking rate. The direct convective 
cooling from the LCB lowered the thermal symmetry caused by the radiant heating from the 
simulated window. 
The study showed that the systems affected neither tear film quality nor heart rate and interbeat 
interval. The study suggested that the system with CCLCB can be unhealthier for people with high 
blood pressure. Due to lower skin temperatures caused by the direct convective cooling from the 
LCB, the vasoconstriction of veins was higher in order to reduce heat exchange, which resulted in 
higher blood pressure. Results of forced vital capacity test suggested that the direct convective 
cooling from the LCB may cause more shallow breathing 
More studies should be performed to ascertain these factors and their influence on human well-
being. 
 
Keywords: Localized chilled beam, Chilled ceiling, Micro-environment, User control, Physiological 
responds, Thermal comfort

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Indoor environment 
The main purpose of heating and air-conditioning in buildings is to provide an acceptable 
environment, which does not have a negative impact on human health and performance. Comfort 
problems or benefits caused by factors like air velocity, humidity and thermal comfort are included 
in international standards such as ISO 7730 (2005) and ASHRAE 55 (2004). The standards specify 
the combination of those factors that will create indoor environment acceptable to the majority of 
the occupants in an occupied space. 
Today’s focus on human health and performance due to indoor environment leads to interest in 
optimizing the building systems, which would provide comfortable indoor conditions. It gives 
opportunities to develop new types of ventilation. This study investigates a novel system of 
ventilation and air-conditioning, which creates a personalized micro-environment for every 
occupant at their work stations with a non-uniform environment in the background. Performance 
of the new approach will be investigated by the physiological response of people exposed to the 
generated environment. 
1.1.1. 
Comfort 
1.1.1.1. 
Thermal comfort 
Thermal sensation of a human refers to the thermal balance of the whole body. Several factors 
have impact on this balance. Besides physical activity and clothing, there are influential 
parameters of the indoor environment: air temperature, humidity, velocity and mean radiant 
temperature. After estimating those factors, the predicted mean vote (PMV) (Fanger, 1972) can be 
calculated for prediction of the thermal sensation. The predicted percentage of dissatisfied (PPD) 
can be calculated from PMV. The index gives information on the percentage of people who feel 
discomfort or dissatisfaction in the given indoor conditions. (DS EN ISO 7730: 2005).  
Criteria for general thermal comfort and local discomfort can be found in standards such as ISO 
7730 (2005) and ASHRAE 55 (2004). The standards specify conditions of indoor environment which 
are acceptable for at least 80% of occupants. It is difficult to create conditions, which would satisfy 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
all occupants of the space, since requirements for comfort are not the same for everyone due to 
large physiological and psychological variations among people. (ANSI/ASHRAE 55, 2004). 
Local thermal discomfort can be a result of unwanted cooling or heating of a part of the body. It 
can be caused by differences of temperature in the room, like radiant temperature asymmetry, 
vertical temperature difference, cold or warm floors; and also draught due to air movement 
cooling the body locally (DS EN ISO 7730: 2005). 
 
1.1.1.2. 
Perceived air quality 
The quality of indoor air has influence on occupants' comfort as well as health and performance 
and is determined by two factors. Chemical composition of the indoor air with concentration of its 
components makes the first factor. The second one is the human subjective perception, which can 
be expressed by air acceptability, air freshness, irritation, etc. Air quality is perceived as good 
when the indoor air is evaluated as fresh and pleasant. Perceived Air Quality (PAQ) is important for 
assessing indoor environment, however not always useful for evaluating health risks of people 
breathing that air (Melikov & Kaczmarczyk, 2012).  
Standard EN 15251 (2007) defines four categories (I, II, III, IV) of indoor environment based on the 
level of dissatisfaction. The first three categories correspond to categories A, B, C defined in CR 
1752 (1998). The categories correspond to a maximum of 15, 20 and 30% of dissatisfied 
occupants. Category IV in EN 15251 (2007) corresponds to more than 30% of periodically 
dissatisfied. 
Pollution in indoor air stems from occupants, building materials, furnishing and the HVAC system 
itself (Fanger et al., 1988). Standards for indoor air quality define required level of ventilation for 
different types of space. In CR 1752 (1998) the required minimum ventilation rate is a sum of flow 
rate per person and per square meter of the floor area. The ventilation rate per person 
corresponds to pollution (odor) emitted from the person and the one per square meter – to 
emissions from the building, furniture, HVAC system, etc. (Olesen, 2004).

1.1.2. 
Influence on human health and performance 
1.1.2.1. 
Building-associated illnesses 
The influence of indoor environment in artificially air-conditioned buildings on human health and 
well-being has been a theme of research area since 1970 (WHO, 2000). Poor indoor air quality can 
have a significant impact on human health in occupied places like office buildings, schools, 
hospitals, commercial centers or means of transport (Costa and Costa, 2006). 
Any illness caused by indoor environment factors belongs to the group of building-associated 
illnesses. There are two categories of building-associated illnesses: sick building syndrome (SBS) 
and building-related illness (BRI). These categories exclude long latency illnesses such as lung 
cancer. 
BRI relates to health effects and disorders, which can be easily linked to environmental factors in a 
specific building. It frequently involves skin and respiratory illnesses due to easy contact of 
environmental contaminants with these tissues (Aditama and Andarini, 2002). Such illnesses 
include rhinitis and asthma, which can be caused by exposure to allergenic chemicals, moulds or 
spores. Certain irritant exposures, including allergens and fibrous glass, can cause skin disorders, 
conjunctivitis and/or rhinitis. Infectious diseases are also known to be resulting from the indoor 
environment exposure, such as legionnaires’ disease, viral infections and tuberculosis. A single 
type of exposure can cause several types of responses (Redlich et al., 1997). Therefore, BRI 
includes symptoms characteristic due to exposure to certain chemicals like carbon monoxide or 
formaldehyde, and diseased caused by bacteria, viruses or fungi. Those symptoms often do not 
disappear after leaving the place and not all of the occupants are necessarily affected by the 
exposure (Costa and Costa, 2006). 
SBS has to be distinguished from BRI. The term SBS refers to non-specific complaints, which are 
usually related to a specific building by their temporal pattern of occurrence. According to the 
World Health Organization in 1982, more than 20% of the occupants of artificially air-conditioned 
buildings who reported symptoms that disappear, or persist, when they were away from the 
place, were classified as sick building syndrome (WHO, 1983). The symptoms include headache, 
fatigue, dizziness, respiratory complaints such as chest tightness, coughing, and shortness of 
breath, rash, mucous membrane irritation (burning eyes, nose and/or throat), etc. Types and 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
intensity of the symptoms are different for occupants staying in the same building, what may be 
caused by different microenvironments and host factors. Some people may not be influenced at 
all, while others may have unpleasant symptoms, which can be disruptive for work productivity. 
Generally the symptoms improve if the person leaves the building or if improvements are made in 
the quality of indoor air, but the time course for different people may vary. SBS is a multifactorial 
matter which includes physical (such as temperature, relative humidity, noise and lightning), 
chemical, biological and psychological factors (Redlich et al., 1997; Aditama and Andarini, 2002). 
The phenomenon of SBS gained attention in the 1970s due to development of new buildings, 
which are more energy-efficient (air-tight buildings) and depend on mechanical ventilation. 
Modern building constructions increased the use of synthetic materials. That, as well as, the 
increased amount of workers in offices (increased occupation rate per square meter), automation 
of office work and stress, impact on the prevalence of SBS. From the various factors that cause 
SBS, two of them are major: air contaminants and ventilation system. 
Common sources of air contaminants stem from materials such as paints, carpets, floor, 
insulation, cleaning products, etc. Some materials can be a source of molds and bacterial growth in 
the case of water leaks or high humidity. They can also absorb organic compounds and release 
them subsequently. Common indoor contaminants are shown in Table 1.

In order to achieve good quality of indoor air, the ventilation system has to provide enough fresh 
air to all occupied spaces in the building and to reduce the concentration of air contaminants by 
removing used air. However, inadequate functioning of HVAC systems can cause many problems, 
which can stem from bad location of the air supplies (near a source of pollutants), delivery, the 
processes of heating or cooling the air and removal of used air. A wrong design of ventilation 
system may result in the fresh air getting to the return ducts bypassing people’s breathing zone. 
The system itself can be a source of contaminants, releasing some materials into the air from 
unsealed fiberglass or other insulation materials. If such materials get wet, they become a good 
environment for growth of microorganisms, well-hidden from sight. 
Duration of work in the place of exposure plays an important role. Other factors associated with 
the risk of SBS are for example job satisfaction, stress and low job status. In some studies women 
seem to be at higher risk than men. 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
Even when a specific factor has not been found for SBS, actions should be taken to reduce air 
contamination and improve ventilation, since it is almost always a factor contributing significantly 
to SBS (Redlich et al., 1997). 
The first approach to a occupant’s concerns about potential environmentally caused disease is to 
exclude other possible reasons of the problem, for example inadequate tear film production and 
stability, which could stem from other diseases. Next physiological mechanism should be 
identified and organ function quantified. Problems like chest tightness or wheezing need 
documentation of changes in airways before and after work with the use of peak expiratory flow 
tracings or spirometry. If there are no abnormalities, the discomfort does not represent a 
measurable disease. In that case, the discomfort is related to SBS. Efforts to explain that 
discomfort suggest that there exist recognizable physiological responses to the “sick building” 
environment (Aditama and Andarini, 2002). 
 
1.1.2.2. 
Performance 
SBS cause difficulties in work as well as absence due to health issues. When the symptoms become 
very disruptive, some costly responses may be required, such as renovating the building (e.g. 
removing wall coverings in order to get rid of molds) and changing ventilation system. However, 
small decreases in productivity contribute dominantly to the total SBS cost (Fisk, 2000). 
In a New England survey (US Environ. Prot. Agency, 1989) on self-reported productivity due to 
poor indoor air quality, the average productivity loss amounted to 3%. In a study of U.K. office 
workers (Raw et al, 1990) there was about 4% average self-reported productivity loss for all 
workers (with or without SBS symptoms). A study of workers provided with individually controlled 
ventilation system (Menzies at al, 1997) showed that occupants reported fewer SBS symptoms 
and increase in productivity by 11%. Another study on office workers’ environment (Kroner and 
Stark-Martin, 1992) indicated 2% increased productivity with provision of individual temperature 
control. 
Measured data on the link between SBS and work performance given by Nunes et al (1993) show 
that time of response for workers who reported SBS symptoms was 7% longer in one 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
computerized neurobehavioral test, and the error rate was 30% higher in the second test. 
Research on Norwegian classrooms (Myhrvold et al, 1996) indicated that higher concentrations of 
carbon dioxide (caused by lower ventilation rates) resulted in the increase of SBS and poorer 
performance by increasing the reaction time in a computerized test. Reduced SBS and 
improvement of performance in the reaction time test by 5,3% was noted after renovating the 
classrooms with poor indoor environment, compared to the ones without renovation. Another 
research (Wargocki, 1998) investigated the influence of presence or absence of a 20-year old 
carpet, not visible to the participants, on SBS and work performance. The major outcomes of 
removing the carpet were following: minor decreases in selected pollutants; better PAQ; decrease 
of some SBS symptoms, especially headache and dizziness; increase in the simulated office work 
(amount of typed text) by 6,5%; increases in performance in two additional tests by 2,5% and 
3,8%; increases in logical reasoning by 3,4% and reaction time by 3,1%, but also a decrease in code 
substitution test by 2%. 
The objective data suggests that certain aspects of performance can be decreased due to SBS by 
3% to 5%, however they cannot be clearly related to the magnitude of overall performance 
decrease from SBS. The subjective data from self-reports indicate average decrease of productivity 
caused by poor indoor air quality by 4%. 
Indoor environment can also have a direct impact on work performance, without causing health 
problems. Many studies indicate that performance in some tasks, such as typewriting, factory 
work, recognizing and responding to signals, reading, learning and memorizing, is significantly 
influenced by changes in temperature of a few degrees within the range of 18 °C to 30 °C. Other 
studies however, find no such linkage. Optimal thermal comfort may overlap with optimal 
productivity in the case of complex or creative mental work, whereas for some types of mental 
work performance may be increased by slight thermal discomfort, like slightly cool temperatures 
(Fisk, 2000).

1.2. 
Ventilation and air-conditioning 
There are three ways of heat exchange: conduction (through direct contact of two objects), which 
can be generally neglected in the field of indoor climate; convection (fluid or air movement around 
an object) and radiation: electromagnetic wave emission from an object. 
The most common ventilation type is total volume ventilation (TVV) which aims to create a 
uniform and comfortable indoor environment. Most popular types of ventilation and air-
conditioning are described below. 
 
1.2.1. 
Mixing ventilation 
The aim of mixing ventilation is to mix the supplied fresh, cold air with the warm polluted room air 
in order to dilute the pollution generated in the room. If the air is completely mixed in the room, 
the vertical distribution of concentration (temperature) is linear (Figure 1). Mixing ventilation has 
relatively low expenses, but warm and humid outdoor air may cause high energy consumption 
when cooling and dehumidifying the supplied air. 
 
Figure 1. Mixing ventilation principle 
The air is usually supplied from the ceiling or a wall, close to the ceiling. Air movement in the room 
depends on the location of supply and exhaust diffusers (Figure 2). In case A the cold air falls down 
the room where it is heated by sources like occupants and equipment, then circulates up to the 
exhaust diffusers. In case B the air movement is random because of stronger mixing due to high 
momentum of the supply air and thermal flows from heat sources.

21 
 
 
Figure 2. Air movement due to diffusers location A) wall B) ceiling 
 
1.2.2. 
Displacement ventilation 
Displacement ventilation aims to create an upward air movement. Supply air enters the room 
close to the floor and then rises after meeting heat sources. This results in a stratification pattern 
and rise of pollution concentration along with height, towards the ceiling, and establishing 
stratification zone. Above the stratification zone the displacement ventilation pattern disappears 
and mixing is observed. The inhaled air is therefore of better quality provided the stratification 
zone is above the breathing zone of the occupant (Figure 3). However, due to typically low air 
velocity, the occupants’ movement can destroy the displacement pattern and result in 
environment similar to the one with mixing ventilation. There are also other drawbacks of 
displacement ventilation, such as expensive investment cost due to large ducting. The system can 
be used only in cooling mode, because hot air rises upwards and would not spread along the floor. 
Displacement ventilation creates a risk of draught at the feet level because of the location of 
diffusers.

Both types of total volume ventilation (TVV), mixing as well as displacement, do not take into 
account occupants’ different behavior or thermal preferences and differences. Hence 
dissatisfaction may occur with the generated indoor environment. Dissatisfaction can also stem 
from local thermal discomfort caused by draught and vertical temperature differences 
(Kaczmarczyk et al., 2004). 
 
1.2.3. 
Personalized ventilation 
Personalized ventilation (PV) provides fresh air for every occupant directly into their breathing 
zone. There are various strategies, for example personal air terminal device with or without 
conditioning of supply air (Figure 4). The occupant can control the flow rate, direction of the 
supply air and sometimes also temperature. The adjustments of these factors to personal 
preferences as well as fresh air supplied to the breathing zone, result in higher potential to 
improve the air quality in case of PV than TVV. 
Research proved that PAQ is higher and SBS symptoms decrease in the case of PV in comparison 
with mixing ventilation. Improvement of PAQ depends on the room temperature as well as the 
difference between room and personalized air. Improvement is bigger when the personalized air 
has lower temperature than room air (Kaczmarczyk et al., 2004).

1.3. 
Cooling 
Modern offices create environment with increasing amount of heat generating equipment. Along 
with heat from occupants, they create large heat loads, hard to deal with using only ventilation 
systems. To overcome this problem, radiant water cooling techniques have been developed to use 
in rooms. Those water cooling systems bring advantages such as energy savings and better 
thermal comfort. For proper functioning however, the cooling systems must be used in 
conjunction with the ventilation system. There are three main types of ceiling mounted cooling 
systems: ceiling panels, exchanging heat by radiation; passive convection (passive chilled beams) 
and active convection chilled beams, exchanging heat by convection. The active chilled beam 
combines two functions – it also has an inlet diffuser for supplying ventilation air (Fredriksson et 
al., 2001). 
 
1.3.1. 
Chilled ceiling 
Chilled ceiling generates operative temperature lower than the indoor air temperature and 
exchanges heat by radiation. Since it cannot supply fresh air and dilute the pollution in the room, it 
has to be combined with mechanical (mixing or displacement) ventilation. The supply air should 
meet requirements for indoor air quality set in standard EN15251 (2007), while the chilled ceiling 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
cools the air, providing acceptable thermal conditions. The need for cooling the supply air can 
therefore be reduced especially in places where the ventilation is driven by the heat loads. 
The chilled ceiling consists of panels which are typically cooled by chilled water. The panels absorb 
radiant heat from occupants and warmer objects’ surfaces, and use natural and forced convection 
to cool the air near the ceiling. The panels consist of highly conductive material (like graphite) with 
a serpentine pipe placed in it, a frame and a high emissivity material on the face side towards the 
room (Figure 5). The pipe can be made of copper or polyethylene (PE). The water circulating inside 
the panel provides an equal temperature over the board of high conductivity material and the 
absorbed heat from the room is removed with the return water. The ceiling panels can also be 
used for heating the room temperature. 
 
 
Figure 5. Uponor comfort panel 
 
1.3.2. 
Chilled beam 
Chilled beam systems are installed in office buildings and spaces where good thermal environment 
is needed. There are two different types of chilled beams in use: active (closed or open) and 
passive (Figure 6). An active chilled beam provides both supply air and air conditioning. The room 
air is entrained by the supplied air inside the beam and led through a heat exchanger where it gets 
cooled. Next it is mixed with fresh air supplied to the beam and redistributed back into the room 
already cooled. In the case of passive chilled beam, a cooling coil cools the air passing through, 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
which is led into the room by natural convection (caused by air temperature differences). There is 
no forced convection involved. The passive chilled beams are typically combined with mixing 
ventilation and can be used only in cooling mode. 
 
Figure 6. Chilled beam types (REHVA guidebook No 5, 2007) 
 
A chilled beam with a special design, which aims to create micro-environment for every occupant 
at their work station, with possibility to control the primary air supply within a certain range, is 
named a “localized chilled beam” (Nygaard and Uth, 2014). The air is supplied directly over the 
work station, creating a zone separated from the background environment (Figure 7). The 
generated micro-environment is characterized by lower temperature than the surrounding air. The 
system with localized chilled beams gives the opportunity for personally ventilated spaces within a 
room with no need for separating with walls or partitions, however the thermal micro-
environments may affect the background environment and also neighboring stations, depending 
on the distance between work stations as well as the primary air flow rate and air temperature. To 
help achieving the background temperature, LCB can be combined with chilled ceiling, especially if 
the workers spend time away from their work stations. This project investigates the performance 
of a single LCB combined with chilled ceiling.

1.4. 
Human physiological response to ventilation and air-conditioning 
1.4.1. 
Blinking frequency and tear film quality 
Eye irritation is a common SBS symptom. Eyes complaints such as “dry eyes” are related to factors 
like indoor air pollution and office equipment. Eye irritation can be caused by alteration of the 
precorneal tear film. The tear film (TF) protects the eye from external factors. Indoor pollutants as 
well as disadvantageous thermal factors such as high room temperature and low relative humidity 
(RH) can result in altering the TF – thinning and breaking up. Tear film stability depends also on 
individual physiological traits. (Wolkoff, 2005). Tear secretion and blinking have major influence on 
a good quality TF, since blinking refreshes and moistens the eye. 
Blinking frequency (BF) depends on an individual. Average frequency varies from less than 2-3 up 
to 20-30 blinks per minute (Wolkoff, 2005). Typical BF for an occupant at rest in neutral indoor 
conditions is around 12-20 blinks per minute. BF is influenced by factors like attention, gaze 
direction or task complexity. Visual tasks lower the BF; high air velocity during resting conditions 
(>1 m/s but possibly also lower) on the other hand, increases it. It has been observed that high RH 
decreases BF and increased ambient temperature – increases it. Low BF increases water 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
evaporation from the ocular surface. Visual work and playing video games not only result in lower 
BF, but also enlarges the exposed area of eyes, causing a larger water loss (Wolkoff, 2005). 
Dehydration at room temperature of body fluids like aqueous humor, tear fluid, saliva and cervical 
mucus on microscope slides produce ferning patterns in the shape of dendritic structures. The 
process of ferning is caused by interaction of electrolytes with macromolecules, like mucous 
polysaccharides or proteins. Different ratios between them and various salt concentrations result 
in different patterns of ferning. Out of many methods of performing the ocular ferning test, 
collection of tear fluid with a glass rod is the easiest one. The tear fluid is dropped on a microscope 
slide and dried at room temperature to produce ferning patterns. The process depends on 
environmental factors such as temperature and humidity. 
Rolando (1984) defined four classes of tear film ferning patterns according to closeness and 
branching. Type I presents uniform structures; type II shows small spaces. Type III presents big 
spaces and poorer branching. Type IV shows no ferning pattern. Type III and IV are considered 
poor quality and tend to appear more often for people with dry-eye condition than for people 
with normal tear function (Horwath et al., 2001). Subjective grading is based on an examiner 
grading a particular conditions due to a fixed scale or standard. 
The Rolando scale of grading became the main method used in work on tear film quality. 
However, it was not originally developed as a repeatable, standardized grading tool. The main 
difficulty with using the traditional Rolando scale comes from  the lack of protocol for accurate 
sample preparation, the categorization of ferning patterns which restricts sensitivity (large 
variances especially around types I and II) and that not all ferning patterns seem to be represented 
by the scale. It is important to develop a suitable grading scale if the tear ferning test is meant to 
become a routine clinical examination. Masmali et al. (2013) suggested a new 7-point grading 
scale. In contrast to Rolando scale, it was based on observation of an image library with a wide 
selection of ferning patterns. Due to an overlap between grades 2 and 3 and between grades 6 
and 7, statistical analysis allowed to collapse the scale down to five grades, in order to create an 
acceptable working scale. The new 5-point grading scale was classified from 0 to 4 (Figure 8). 
 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
 
Figure 8. Baseline images of the new 5-point grading scale (Masmali et al., 2013). 
 
The final 5-point grading scale proved to be more reliable than the previous Rolando scale. Larger 
increment steps promote better repeatability and reproducibility, making the ferning test an easy 
method for tear film pattern classification (Masmali et al., 2013). 
 
1.4.2. 
Skin temperature 
Most studies on thermal comfort focus on subjective perception of the generated environment. 
Thermal sensation originates in skin and core temperature receptors, therefore they present an 
important aspect of study concerning thermal sensation and comfort. The overall thermal 
sensation of human body is affected differently by cooling certain body parts. Some body parts, 
such as back, chest or pelvis, have a big impact on overall thermal sensation, whereas others, like 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
hands and feet, have much less influence on overall sensation (Huizenga et al., 2005; Arens et al., 
2006). 
Research (Huizenga et al., 2004) show that in neutral conditions average skin temperature quickly 
becomes stable (after 10 minutes) and fluctuations are very small - within 0.1 °C. It is worth 
mentioning that in neutral conditions skin temperatures of hands and fingers have bigger 
variations. Apparently this phenomenon is caused by active hand vasodilation and constriction for 
regulation of heat loss up to the neutral point. These fluctuations are not subjectively perceived. In 
conditions of warmer room temperature, average skin temperature increases and in colder it 
decreases. The core temperature always responds in the opposite direction of the skin 
temperature (Huizenga et al., 2005; Arens et al., 2006). 
The thermal state of the whole body influences the rate at which hand skin temperature recovers 
after local cooling. Quick recovery takes place when the body is warm and hence the hand 
vasodilates quickly. Slow recovery happens when the body is neutral or cool because of no need 
for pumping the blood to the hand for releasing heat after cooling is removed. In cold 
environments of temperature around 16-19 °C muscular activity of one hand and its lack in the 
other (constrained on the mouse while doing computer work) can cause temperature differences 
between hands up to 3  °C (Huizenga et al., 2005; Arens et al., 2006). 
 
1.4.3. Potential physiological factors 
Thermal comfort is today an important subject on investigating HVAC systems. Most of the 
research however, focuses on subjective thermal sensations, which cannot explain how the 
environmental factors influence thermal comfort or why people may perceive the same conditions 
differently. This problem is complicated. The studies should not be narrowed down to HVAC areas, 
but also include physiological investigation. Skin temperature is currently considered a good 
indicator of physiological indicator of thermal comfort and therefore appears in many studies. 
However, human thermal sensation depends on many complicated changes in a body other than 
skin temperature. Therefore, studies begin to emerge on other physiological factors that can 
potentially be related to thermal comfort, such as heart rate variability or electrocardiograph (Yao 
et al., 2009). There is a need to perform more studies in order to understand the complex 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
physiological response of a human body to thermal conditions and its impact on thermal 
sensation. In this study, besides skin temperature, blinking frequency and tear film quality, other 
physiological factors are investigated: blood pressure, forced vital capacity of lungs (FVC), heart 
rate (HR) and interbeat interval (IBI). 
FVC is the amount of air which can be forcibly exhaled from the lungs after taking the deepest 
breath possible. The FVC test is measured by a spirometer. It is used to determine many lungs 
diseases. A normal adult vital capacity is between 3 and 5 liters and depends on age, gender, 
height, mass and ethnicity. 
Heart rate is the heartbeat speed, usually measured in beats per minute (bpm). HR can vary due to 
physical needs like the need to absorb oxygen. HR depends on factors like physical activity, stress, 
anxiety, sleep, drugs or illnesses. Normal HR of an adult ranges from 60 to 100 bpm. 
IBI is the time between individual beats of the heart and is usually measured in milliseconds. IBI 
varies from beat to beat in a normal heart function. This natural variation is known as heart rate 
variability. Some cardiac conditions like periods of exercise, as well as certain illnesses, can cause 
the IBI values to become nearly constant. There is yet no specific application of the IBI data for 
heart conditions. 
 
2. Justification 
The present ventilation strategies are ineffective with regard to energy efficiency, since the whole 
space is conditioned, even though most of it is rarely occupied by people. Advanced solutions for 
preserving energy are needed. Reducing the air flow is not a good solution, since it affects the 
occupants’ PAQ, comfort and health. Water based air conditioning systems directly in the room, 
i.e. chilled ceiling and chilled beams is much more efficient in removing the generated heat from 
the room than air (for the same volume of air and water). A system with localized chilled beams 
provides people with more comfortable micro-environment at their work stations, however when 
the workers move outside of the cooler zone created by the system, the elevated room 
temperature causes discomfort. Hence combining the LCB with chilled ceiling can be a solution to 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
lower the temperature difference between the local cooler zone and background environment. 
The chilled ceiling will slowly cool the surroundings by radiation, while the LCB will provide fast 
response cooling due to the convective heat exchange. 
Previous study (Nygaard and Uth, 2014) investigated performance of a LCB in comparison to CCMV 
in the same facilities. In conditions of 28 °C the LCB performed worse than CCMV which created 
more uniform environment. This was because of the highly elevated background air temperatures 
– up to 30 oC. Moreover, CCMV provided more cooling to the whole body and higher indoor 
quality. In conditions of 26 °C the subjects assessed PAQ better with LCB, however performance 
was proven to be higher for the system with CCMV. 
For improving the performance of the LCB system, a combination with chilled ceiling was 
recommended. The environment would still remain non-uniform, however the chilled ceiling 
would provide cooling for occupants away from their work stations. 
As research showed, the subjective human response may differ from actual performance and 
health impact due to psychological effects. Therefore physiological response to thermal conditions 
needs to be studied. 
The novel system of chilled ceiling combined with a localized chilled beam (CCLCB) creates a 
micro-environment for every occupant at their work station through individual control. The CC 
system is used to keep the background conditions at the maximum allowed by the standard air 
temperature level (EN 15251-2007). As there is almost no air movement outside of the WS (where 
the LCB is installed) some non-uniformity in the generated conditions may be expected. The 
magnitude of this non-uniformity should be studied and compared to more uniform conditions, 
like chilled ceiling combined with mixing ventilation (CCMV) in human subject experiment 
experiments. 
The project of human subject experiment was a part of collaborative research between DTU Byg, 
HALTON and UPONOR. 
 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
3. Objectives 
The objectives of the proposed research are:  
 To study and compare human physiological response to the thermal environment under 
conditions generated by radiant and convective cooling provided by CCLCB and radiant and 
convective cooling provided by CCMV systems under 26 and 28 oC room air temperature 
(summer conditions); 
 To study the impact of CCLCB on skin temperature of selected body locations (forehead, 
neck and hands) and to compare it to the impact of CCMV system; 
 To study the impact of CCLCB on objectively measured eye comfort - blinking frequency 
and tear film quality, and to compare it with the impact of CCMV system; 
 To investigate the impact on heart rate and interbeat interval by the two ventilation 
systems under summer conditions;  
 To investigate the impact on blood pressure by the two ventilation systems under summer 
conditions; 
 To investigate the impact on lungs vital capacity by the two ventilation systems under 
summer conditions.

Climate chambers 
The experiment was held at the International Centre for Indoor Environment and Energy (ICIEE) at 
DTU in chambers 5 and 6 used for ventilation and indoor climate experiments, Figure 9. Exposure 
to the tested systems was held in chamber 5 with dimensions of 4.2 m (L) x 5.4 m (W) x 3.1 m (H). 
The chamber has a HVAC system which can supply from 12 L/s to 170 L/s, and additionally up to 
170 L/s of outside air by a booster fan, which gives the maximum flow rate of 340 L/s. The 
temperature in the chamber can range between 10-40°C and the relative humidity between 10-
90%. Chamber 6, adjacent to chamber 5, was used as acclimatization room before the exposure.

In one of the walls in chamber 5 (adjacent to the control room) there was a simulated window. 
The window was simulated by five identical radiant panels (from Uponor) placed beside each

polyethylene serpentine pipes inside. A metal plate, painted in grey for maximum emissivity, was

4.5. Experimental procedure 
4.5.1. 
Introductory session 
Before the experiment several introductory sessions were held in order to explain the procedure, 
show the experimental facilities to the participants and teach them how to fill in the 
questionnaires, take the tear film samples and the FVC test. From those people, 24 final 
participants were selected and consensus agreements were signed. 
 
4.5.2. 
Experimental session 
The session began with acclimatization in chamber 6, where the subject spent 30 minutes. The 
temperature in the acclimatization room was kept at 26 °C or 28 °C, depending on the case. 
Immediately after entering the acclimatization chamber, the subject assessed the perceived air 
quality (PAQ) by answering the first paper questionnaire. After about 5 minutes, the second paper 
questionnaire was filled. The information content of the questionnaires is set in Table 5. The 
subjects received a bottle of water. They were allowed to change their clothing throughout the 
whole experiment. After answering the questionnaires, physiological measurements were taken. 
Firstly, the subject’s blood pressure was measured. Next, the subject took the first tear film 
sample and performed the forced vital capacity test (FVC test). The subject was asked to put on a 
heart belt. Four iButtons were attached to the subject’s skin with a medical tape, to measure the 
temperature in four points: forehead, hands and back of the neck. About 5 minutes before the end 
of the acclimatization phase, the subject filled in the third paper questionnaire about PAQ and 
overall thermal sensation. When the acclimatization phase was over, the subject entered chamber 
5 and the experimental exposure began. 
Immediately upon entering chamber 5, the fourth paper questionnaire, on PAQ, was filled. Before 
the first subject for the day entered the chamber, the heat load from one person was simulated by 
a heated dummy (100 W). It was not used in between the sessions. Once a test subject left 
chamber 5 after one and a half hour stay, the next one entered 30 minutes later. Thus four 
subjects were tested per day as already mentioned. The subject sat at the first work station (WS1), 
by the simulated window, where the subject’s response was studied to the exposure of the 
generated environment via the computerized questionnaire. While at WS1, the subject was under 
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
steady-state work activity on the computer. Questions were answered right after sitting and then 
again after 25 minutes. Between the questions, the subjects played simple computer games, such 
as Tetris or Snake. The computerized questionnaires were based on PAQ, whole body thermal 
sensation, local thermal sensation of selected part and local air movement acceptance as well as 
general SBS symptoms. After 30 minutes at WS1, the subject moved to the second work station 
(WS2) at the opposite side of the chamber (Figure 9), where the procedure from WS1 was 
repeated. Depending on the case with the LCB (i.e. case 1 or 2 or 3), upon leaving WS1, the 
primary air flow from the chilled beam was set to minimum (10 L/s) or maximum (13L/s). After 30 
minutes at WS2, the subject moved back to WS1, repeating the same routine as in the first 30 min. 
At the end of the time spent in chamber 5, the subject’s blood pressure was measured once more. 
Then the subject took a tear film sample and made the FVC test for the second time. Finally, the 
subject removed the heart belt and iButtons and the session was over. The time procedure is 
shown in Table 7.

4.6. Data analyses 
For statistical analysis of the data a computer program - Statistica 10 was used. 
Firstly the data distribution was tested with the Shapiro-Wilcoxon test. If the resultant p-value was 
higher than 0.05 the distribution was normal and if lower – not normal. 
For normally distributed data a parametric test - repeated measure analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
was performed to see whether the investigated factor had influence (if p-value was lower than 
0.05). Next the post-hoc Newman-Keuls test was performed which compared all the conditions 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
with each other and determined whether the differences were significant (p-value lower than 
0.05). 
For not normally distributed data a non-parametric test – Friedman ANOVA was performed to 
compare multiple dependant variables, since participants were the same in all conditions. If the p-
value showed significant differences (p<0.05), Wilcoxon matched pairs test was performed to 
compare two dependent variables. Significance level of p≤0.05 rejects the zero hypothesis that the 
results are similar (H0). To determine whether the conditions had different influence on male and 
female participants (significance level of p<0.05), the Mann-Whitney U test was performed for 
comparing two independent variables.

Blood pressure

The subjects had their blood pressure measured at the start and end of the experiment. The first

bike (often in a hurry), and the measurement in the acclimatization chamber was taken only a few 
minutes after their physical effort.

6. Discussion 
The thesis investigates changes in human physiological parameters in response to thermal 
environment generated by radiant and convective cooling provided by CCLCB and CCMV under 
summer conditions (26 °C and 28 °C  room temperature) and comparison of the system’s influence 
on these factors. 
 
6.1. 
Tear film and blinking frequency 
As shown in Figures 29 and 30, most of the collected tear film samples (94%) were of good quality. 
The number of samples with improvement, regression and no change of TF quality were similar in 
all the studied conditions. The number of samples with improvement was also similar to the ones 
with regression in all the cases, Figure 31. Therefore the investigated systems showed no influence 
on the TF quality. 
Statiscal analysis showed that the tested conditions did not affect significantly the blinking 
frequency (p>0.05). The average BF did not exceed typical BF at rest in neutral indoor conditions, 
i.e. around 12-20 blinks per minute (Wolkoff, 2005). A tendency to increase the BF was noted 
between start and end of exposure at each work station, Figure 32. The changes were significant 
(p<0.05) in all tested conditions at the first stay at WS1 (I WS1) and not significant (p>0.05) at 
WS2. At the second stay at WS1 (II WS1) the changes were significant only in cases with higher air 
temperature (28 °C) and air flow (13 L/s) – both CCLCB and CCMV. In cases with CCLCB the BF was 
influenced by individually controlled primary supply air of the LCB. The BF increased along with 
increased air flow. As per other studies (Wolkoff, 2005), it was observed that the BF increases with 
ambient temperature and air velocity. 
 
6.2. 
Blood pressure 
The results presented in Figures 27 and 28 indicate that in the case of CCLCB at the lower 
temperature (26 °C), the diastolic blood pressure is higher than in the case with CCMV at the same 
temperature, as well as in the case with CCLCB at higher temperature (28 °C). This could be due to 
==PAGE:84==
84 
 
the fact that in the case with CCLCB at 26 °C the veins were more vasoconstricted in order to 
reduce the heat exchange, which resulted in higher blood pressure. Compared to CCMV at the 
same temperature, this could be due to the fact that in the case of CCLCB the body was more 
directly cooled by convective air. This in turn indicates that for people with high blood pressure 
direct convective cooling on the body (LCB) is unhealthier than radiant cooling, as well as 
conditions with lower room temperature. As for the change in time of the systolic blood pressure, 
the use of the CCMV can have an influence on its significant decrease, but only at high 
temperature (28 °C). This could be caused by lesser vasoconstriction due to higher temperature 
and the lack of direct convective cooling, which resulted in lower blood pressure. However, the 
actual impact of the cooling systems cannot be explicitly defined, due to many confounding 
factors, which may influence the blood pressure, such as physical activity, stress and also alcohol 
consumption. It seems blood pressure could an indicator of influence of ventilation systems on 
human thermal comfort and health; however more studies should be performed with the use of 
this physiological factor. 
 
6.3. 
Skin temperature 
Skin temperature depended on thermal conditions – it was lower at room temperature of 26 °C 
and higher at room temperature of 28 °C. 
Skin temperature changed depending on time and location of the subjects in the room, i.e. when 
they were at WS1 or WS2. Just like previous research on skin temperature (Huizenga et al., 2004) 
showed, the temperature became stable in a short time, up to 10 minutes after moving from one 
work station, with certain local thermal conditions, to another. As shown in Figures 37-48, in 
conditions with CCLCB the skin temperature was lower at WS1 than WS2 due to direct convective 
cooling from the LCB. In conditions with CCMV the skin temperature was higher at WS1 than WS2 
since the main influence on the temperature was caused by radiant heat from the simulated 
window. These results show that the convective cooling of the flow from the chilled beam was 
able to compensate for the radiant heating from the window. The changes in skin temperature 
when the subjects moved between the work stations are most expressly visible for the forehead 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
(Figures 37 and 38), because the temperature sensor was attached to the front of the body which 
was directly exposed to air movement from the ventilation system. 
In conditions with the same room temperature (26 °C or 28 °C) the skin temperature at WS1 was 
higher in the case with CCMV than with CCLCB. The reason for the lower skin temperature in cases 
with CCLCB was the localized convective cooling of the flow from the chilled beam. At WS2 the 
temperatures were similar in all the tested cases. This result shows that both systems provided 
similar flow conditions in the occupied zone away from WS1. The radiant cooling from the chilled 
ceiling was more or less the same for the studied cases with chilled beam (CCLCB) and mixing 
ventilation (CCMV). At WS1 the skin temperature was similar in conditions with CCLCB at room 
temperature of 28 °C and in conditions with CCMV at room temperature of 26 °C. It shows that 
with regard to an occupant’s work station, the same skin temperature can be achieved with 
decreasing room temperature by 2 °C when using the LCB.  
Results for blood pressure at the end of exposure (Figure 27 b)) showed that in conditions with 
room temperature of 26 °C the diastolic blood pressure was significantly higher in case 1 (CCLCB) 
than in case 5 (CCMV). Skin temperature of all four investigated body parts (hands, forehead and 
neck) at II WS1 (by the end of exposure) was visibly lower in case 1 than in case 5. In conditions 
with room temperature of 28 °C significant decrease of systolic blood pressure between start and 
end of exposure (Figure 28) was noted in case 4 (CCMV), while there were no changes in cases 2 
and 3 (CCLCB). Skin temperature of all four investigated body parts, at II WS1, was higher in case 4 
than in cases 2 and 3. Generally, when the skin temperature decreased, the blood pressure was 
higher and when skin temperature increased, the blood pressure was lower. It can be explained by 
vasoconstriction of veins due to lower temperatures in order to reduce heat exchange, which 
resulted in higher blood pressure. 
Females had higher neck skin temperature than males (Figure 42), which could be caused by long 
hair insulating the back of the neck from the cooling systems. Males had higher skin temperature 
of hands than females, Figures 45 and 48.  As shown in Figure 49, there was a thermal asymmetry 
at WS1; the left hand temperature was significantly (p<0.05) higher than right hand due to radiant 
heating from the left side. Research (Huizenga et al., 2004) suggests that thermal asymmetry 
between hands may occur due to muscular activity of one hand and its lack in the other 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
(constrained on the mouse while doing computer work). Since the computer mouse was 
controlled by the right hand by all the participants, it could lower that hand’s temperature in 
comparison to the left hand, which could contribute to the thermal asymmetry.  It can be seen in 
Figure 49 that the thermal asymmetry at WS1 was higher in cases with CCMV (cases 4 and 5) than 
with CCLCB (cases 1, 2 and 3). The LCB reduced the thermal asymmetry caused by the radiant 
heating from the window due to local convective cooling. 
 
6.4. 
Heart measurements 
The heart rate significantly decreased in time in all the tested conditions, Figures 50-51. This was 
probably due to the lasting time of no physical activity. The subjects were more and more relaxed 
during the exposure. As heart rate stands for heartbeat speed (beats per minute); the interbeat 
interval, as time between individual beats (milliseconds), should be in inverse ratio to HR. Indeed, 
the results presented in Figures 52-53 showed the tendency of the IBI to increase in time in all 
cases but 4 (CCMV 28 °C). Statistical analysis showed that the conditions affected neither the HR 
nor the IBI. 
 
6.5. 
Forced Vital Capacity 
Average lung capacity was around 5 liters for male and below 3.5 liters for female participants in 
all tested conditions, Figure 36. The results were found about 0.5 liter lower than in a study in the 
United States  (Stanojevic S, Wade A, Stocks J et al., 2008) for males and females in the age 
between 20 and 30. Statistical analysis showed that the conditions didn’t affect males differently 
than females. 
Differences in the FVC test between start and end of the exposures were not significant (p>0.05) in 
all tested cases, Figure 35. The lack of abnormalities rules out measurable diseases and indicates 
that any possible respiratory discomfort that could occur, such as chest tightness, is related to SBS 
symptoms (Aditama and Andarini, 2002). Results of FVC test after exposure to the condition with 
CCMV 26 °C were significantly higher than after all 3 conditions with CCLCB (Figures 33 and 34). 
This could suggest that direct convective cooling from the chilled beam caused more shallow 
==PAGE:87==
87 
 
breathing in order to reduce heat exchange, which resulted in a more shallow forcibly exhaled 
breath after the exposure. More studies should be performed to ascertain this factor and its 
influence on human well-being. 
 
7. Conclusions 
The general conclusion from the results is that in conditions generated by chilled ceiling with 
localized chilled beam, the direct convective cooling of the flow from the LCB was able to 
compensate for the radiant heating from the simulated window and reduce thermal asymmetry in 
comparison to the more uniform environment generated by chilled ceiling with mixing ventilation. 
More specific conclusions are following: 
 All five tested conditions under two background room air temperatures of 26 and 28 oC with 
the two systems, CCLCB and CCMV, had no influence on the tear film quality. Most of the tear 
film samples (94 %) had good quality. Amount of samples with improvement, regression and 
no change of the quality remained the same in all conditions; 
 There was a tendency to increase blinking frequency (BF) between start and end of exposure 
at every work station, in all the tested conditions under two background room air 
temperatures of 26 and 28 oC with the two systems CCLCB and CCMV. There were no 
differences of BF between the conditions; 
 Forced vital capacity of lungs was higher for males than females in all the tested conditions 
under two background room air temperatures of 26 and 28 oC with the two systems CCLCB and 
CCMV, however conditions did not affect them differently; 
 Heart rate decreased and interbeat interval increased in time in all the tested conditions under 
two background room air temperatures of 26 and 28 oC with the two systems CCLCB and 
CCMV. There were no differences between conditions; 
==PAGE:88==
88 
 
 Females have higher skin temperature of the neck and lower skin temperature of hands than 
males in all the tested conditions under two background room air temperatures of 26 and 28 
oC with the two systems CCLCB and CCMV; 
 In conditions of the same room temperature (26 °C or 28 °C) blood pressure was higher in 
cases with CCLCB than with CCMV; 
 There was a tendency in the tested conditions to increase blood pressure with decrease of skin 
temperature and conversely; 
 In cases with CCLCB skin temperature was lower at WS1 than at WS2 and conversely in cases 
with CCMV; 
 In conditions of the same room temperature (26 °C or 28 °C) skin temperature at WS1 was 
higher in cases with CCMV than with CCLCB. There were no differences at WS2; 
 Thermal asymmetry between hands was bigger in cases with CCMV than with CCLCB; 
 In cases with CCLCB blinking frequency increased along with primary air flow from the chilled 
beam.
