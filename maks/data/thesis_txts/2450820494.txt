Abstract
Increased smartphone usage potentially keeps people awake and impairs their sleep,
which could have harmful effects on their health. This study analyses individuals’
overnight app usage using a large-scale, multicultural dataset comprising data of over
12 million nights gathered from over 100,000 users. Furthermore, it examines the
quantity of overnight app usage, characterises people demonstrating excessive use
and analyses whether their overnight app usage changes over time. In this research,
we ascertained that individuals show substantial overnight app usage; approximately,
12% of nights are affected by overnight app use, while 77% of users with more than
90 nights registered demonstrated overnight app activities. In addition, the results
revealed that younger users, users with shorter average sleep duration and those who
went to bed late are associated with more excessive overnight app use.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The smartphone is not merely a cellphone any more; it has become a tool to achieve
so much more. People use phones to browse the internet, play games, watch videos,
communicate through social media, navigate, send emails and listen to good music.
They can be addicted to their smartphones, and some are more exposed to it than
others. Smartphone addiction and excessive phone use are associated with symptoms
such as functional impairment, loss of control, withdrawal and compulsive behaviour
[Lop17; Lin+16; Tao+10].
Smartphones can easily be taken into the bedroom, and many studies have exam-
ined the effects of smartphone usage before bedtime. These studies indicate that ex-
cessive smartphone use is associated with short total sleep duration, delayed bedtime
and sleep disturbances [CG10; THH11]. Interruption by smartphone use overnight
can have adverse effects. Continuous interruptions of sleep might cause insufficient
deep sleep and hamper biological restitution and recovery with increased risk of var-
ious diseases [Rod+10; Cap+10].
Despite the gravity of the issue, there exists a
paucity of researches regarding smartphone usage during people’s sleep. The reason
for the lack of studies in this field could perhaps be because proper data is not publicly
available. The studies that exist and cover phone use overnight are predominantly
conducted with self-reported sleep data and have a limited number of participants
and monitor solely certain activities on the smartphone.
This study is conducted in collaboration with Sony Mobile, which provided a large-
scale dataset with information regarding people’s sleep and app usage. Sony Mobile
is the creator of the app Lifelog and the wrist-actigraphy Smartband. Through the
Smartband and the app, they have collected a unique dataset regarding users’ sleep,
motions, locations, app usage and more. We take advantage of the dataset to examine
the interplay between smartphones and sleep for over 100 thousands users. We make
use of the collected information to identify and quantify smartphone use overnight.
By using sleep data from the Smartband, we are able to identify with more precision
if a particular app use occurs between sleep onset and offset compared to relying
on self-reported data. We aim to identify which apps are being used overnight, and
when during the night the app use occurs. Furthermore, we attempt to characterise
people with excessive overnight app use to understand which group of people are
most vulnerable to using smartphones during the night. Moreover, we aim to analyse
whether overnight smartphone use is increasing, decreasing or not changing over time.

Research questions
In this thesis, we aim to answer the following research questions:
• How often and how much does overnight app usage occur?
• When does overnight app usage occur, and which type of apps are people using?
• Which groups of people have excessive overnight app use?
• Is overnight app usage increasing, decreasing or not changing over time?
These questions observe overnight app usage from different angles in order to under-
stand the scale of interrupted sleep that is caused by app use overnight.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
This chapter reviews the main findings and theoretical background on apps and sleep.
The chapter contains three sections.
Section 2.1 introduces findings and theories
about sleep. Second section, 2.2, covers studies on apps and mobile phone use. Finally,
section, 2.3, reviews studies on app usage around sleep.
2.1
Sleep
This chapter discusses the importance of sleep and the correlation between sleep
quality and health, as well as other theories regarding sleep. The chapter is divided
into three sections. Section 2.1.1 introduces the different stages of sleep, section 2.1.2
introduces and explains circadian rhythms, and section 2.1.3 reviews the correlation
between sleep and health.
2.1.1
Stages of sleep: REM and NREM
Sleep consists of two states, the REM (rapid eye movement) state and the NREM
(non-REM) state. A night comprises of multiple sleep cycles, which are on average
90 to 110 minutes long. Each cycle starts in the NREM state, in which a person
progresses through four NREM stages before ending in the REM state, often called
the dream state. The four NREM stages are often referred to as N1, N2, N3 and N4,
with the N1 and N2 stages consisting of lighter sleep and stages N3 and N4 consisting
of deeper sleep. Throughout a typical night, a person has four full sleep cycles before
waking up in the fifth cycle. Deep sleep (N3 and N4) is relatively long in the first
sleep cycles but decreases as sleep progresses. Light sleep (REM, N1 and N2 stages),
however, is shorter in the first sleep cycle but becomes longer as sleep progresses,
during which it is easier for a person to wake up [C+05].
2.1.2
Circadian rhythm
A circadian rhythm is a 24-hour sleep–wake cycle, which affects physiological be-
haviour and controls when we become tired.
Not only humans have a circadian
rhythm, but most other organisms as well [A+06]. People can differ in their sleep-
wake cycle, which is often referenced as having different chronotypes. The circadian

clock can be affected by light, advancing or delaying the circadian clock. The light
can come from devices such as TVs, smartphones and tablets, or the environment
[Roe+07]. Insufficient sleep can also affect the circadian clock. It can delay the cir-
cadian melatonin phase and advance the circadian phase of wake up time [Mar+13].
2.1.3
Sleep and health
2.1.3.1
Sleep loss and diseases
Studies have found that insufficient sleep can increase the risk of various diseases and
other health problems.
A study from 2006, which explored the relationship between the development of
diabetes (type 2) and sleep duration, found that men with less than 6 hours of self-
reported sleep per night were twice as likely to have diabetes compared to those who
reported 7 hours of sleep per night. In addition, the study showed a strong correlation
between long sleep duration (more than 8 hours per night) and diabetes [YAM06].
Multiple studies have shown that a short sleep duration increases the risk of obesity
[Cap+08]. Furthermore, a study from 2013 showed that insufficient sleep increases
energy intake considerably, and the risk of weight gain increases [Mar+13].
Moreover, a study from 2003 shows that short and long sleep duration are as-
sociated with coronary heart disease.
In a study conducted in 1986, over 70.000
females answered a questionnaire regarding their sleep duration. The subjects were
then monitored for the next 10 years. In total, 934 coronary incidents were registered,
and subjects with less than 7 hours or more than 9 hours of self-reported sleep had
a modestly increased risk of coronary incidents [Aya+03]. Additionally, studies have
shown a significantly increased risk of hypertension in people with less than 7 hours
of sleep and particularly for people with an extremely short sleep duration (<6 hours
per night) [Gan+06; Got+06].
Impaired sleep can also affect mental health since studies have found a close asso-
ciation between depression and impaired sleep [SCW93; Arm07].
2.2
Apps and mobile phone use
This chapter is divided into two sections. The first section, 2.2.1, reviews how phone
usage is associated with habits that might lead to addiction. The section also reviews
health effects and traits related to mobile phone usage. The second section, 2.2.2,
examines the literature in terms of different apps’ popularity and the usage within
apps.
2.2.1
Habits, addiction, health effects and satisfaction
The literature suggests that smartphones create habits of frequent phone use as dy-
namic content is quickly accessible through the device. This habit is sometimes called

a checking habit [Oul+12]. Sometimes, habits can lead to excessive smartphone us-
age, which might lead to addiction [BP05; TTK09]. There is a gender difference in
terms of addictions, where males are more likely to have higher smartphone addic-
tion compared to females [LK18] while females use the smartphone more frequently
[KLC15].
Subsections 2.2.1.1-2.2.1.3 illustrate the results from different researches that have
explored smartphone habits, addictions and health effects.
2.2.1.1
A study exploring smartphones’ checking behaviours
This section reviews the results from a study that explored smartphones’ checking
behaviours. Among other things, the study investigated three other studies in the field
and analysed the data from these studies to answer the question of how prominent
the habit factor is in mobile phone use. The first study which they investigated was
conducted in 2009 with 136 participants from various age groups. The participants
were existing owner of the Android smartphone G1, which contained software to track
users’ inputs and screen state. Smartphone use was compared to the usage patterns
of laptop users (N = 160). The second study was conducted in 2005 with three users
groups (N = 5+4+6), which permitted data collections of smartphone usage over 2
to 4 months period. The third study was conducted in 2010, where 12 students in
Helsinki were given mobile phone (Nokia N97), and they kept a diary of their usage
the next two weeks after receiving the smartphones. The participants described what
the smartphone was used for in different time slots during the day and the emotions
and feeling linked to the mobile use [Oul+12].
All of the three studies showed that brief usage sessions were common. The first
study showed that 18% of usage sessions were short, with a focus on one application.
The second study showed that 35% of usage sessions contained viewing the home
screen for about one second.
The studies also showed that people with a habit
of short and frequent ”checks” on the smartphone had longer overall smartphone
use.
The third study, the diary study, showed that participants experienced the
continuous short ”checks” on the smartphone as an annoyance rather than as an
addiction. Participants mentioned that the reason for the frequent brief ”checks” was
mostly related to time-killing and entertainment. However, the small sample size is a
limitation of the diary study and might have inhibited investigations on smartphone
addiction [Oul+12].
2.2.1.2
Studies on problematic phone use and health affects
Some phone usage can be problematic, sometimes it is thought to be inappropriate,
compulsive usage or becomes an addiction where people have a hard time controlling
the usage [BP05; TTK09].
We review results from five studies which investigate
problematic phone usage in terms of excessive use, addiction, habits and health effects.
The first study we will review was conducted in 2005, before the age of smart-
phones.
The study measured phone use in terms of problematic use, habits and

addiction, by using a questionnaire scale. The results showed that young people have
particularly more mobile use and problematic use. Furthermore, the study showed
that low self-esteem and extraversion are important factors when predicting users
with higher mobile use and problematic mobile use [BP05].
The other study was conducted in 2009 and explored the correlation between
personality characteristics outlined in addiction literature and mobile phone use. The
study was conducted by sending out questionnaires where 444 people between 18 to
39 years old participate.
The study showed a correlation between loneliness and
overall phone use but not a correlation between loneliness and problematic mobile
phone use. The gender appeared to have a weak correlation to overall phone use and
problematic use. Moreover, the study showed that high self-monitoring people are
more likely to have problematic phone use [TTK09]. The literature also suggests that
people with high self-monitoring trait, are also extroverts, which are more likely to
have problematic phone use and addictive behaviours. [BCB80; BP05].
The third study was conducted in 2008 and investigated phone use from 595
participants. Users with potentially excessive phone use reported they had problems
in controlling the usage. The same user group was also associated with higher anxiety,
lower self-esteem and more depression [Ha+08].
The fourth study was conducted in 2014 and explored the correlation between
compulsive smartphone use, and stress as well as psychological traits from 325 par-
ticipants. The study showed a positive relationship between compulsive usage and
technostress. Moreover, overdependence on smartphones can increase users’ stress
and compulsive usage. Furthermore, the research shows that compulsive use of smart-
phones is correlated to materialism and locus of control [Lee+14].
The fifth study was conducted in 2011 and investigated the relation between mo-
bile phone usage and adverse health effects of young adults.
In a total of 4,156
participants answered a questionnaire at the beginning of the study, and a follow-
up questionnaire one year later. The study showed that frequent mobile phone us-
age increase the risk for mental health symptoms and users experienced more stress
[THH11].
2.2.1.3
Gratification and Satisfaction
The literature suggests that there is a relationship between satisfaction and continued
usage of social apps. One study from 2016 found out that the continued usage of
social apps is driven by habitual use, tight connection with others and satisfaction
[HCT16].
Another study from 2010, focuses on gratifications of the social media
platform, Facebook (both the Facebook website and the smartphone app). In the
study, data was collected from students through 21 interviews and 77 surveys. The
study concluded that the two main gratifications to use Facebook were to kill time
and entertainment [QY10].

2.2.2
Usage of apps and apps’ popularity
There is a paucity of researches regarding both what people do with apps and what
apps people mostly use.
Most of the information which is known about what apps people use comes from
the Pew Research Center. They send out questionnaires with a focus on social media
app usage.
Their research has shown that YouTube and Facebook are the most
popular social media apps, but the usage is very different between age groups. The
young generation, for example, uses Instagram and Snapchat considerably more than
the older generation. They also report that 78% of users in the age group of 18-24
years old use Snapchat, while only 54% of users in the age group of 25-29 years old
use the app [Cen18].
A study from 2015 explored what type of content, users were sending through
the Snapchat app, and carried out a comparison between Facebook and Snapchat
usage. The study showed that Snapchat users were mostly sending contents about
themselves, what they were doing, or things they regarded as funny. The study also
found that Facebook was the leading social media app for keeping in touch with family
and friends, while flirting was one of the primary motives for Snapchat use. Further,
the study showed that Snapchat usage enhances more jealousy than Facebook usage
[UMK15].
Studies which have investigated daily app usage are up in years. Those studies
show that the average daily app usage is around 70-100 minutes per day [Fal+10;
SKH11]. A study has shown that there is considerable diversity in app usage, finding
that users have on average 10-200 interactions per day and an average interaction
length of 10-250 seconds [Fal+10].
2.3
Sleep and Apps
Many previous studies show a negative effect of device use on sleep, where device
use is related to shorter sleep duration and delayed bedtime [CG10; THH11]. This
chapter reviews studies which have explored how device and app usage affect sleep.
The first section, 2.3.1, describes how light exposure and smartphone usage before
bedtime affect sleep. The second section, 2.3.2, illustrates what has been researched
in the field of smartphone usage overnight. Finally, the third section 2.3.3 reviews
the accuracy of actigraphs used to track people’s sleep.
2.3.1
Light exposure and smartphone usage prior to sleep
Studies have investigated smartphone usage before sleep and have found that it can
delay the time people fall asleep and decrease sleep quality. Artificial lights from
smartphones decrease the release of the soporific hormone melatonin, which reduces
sleepiness [Cze13]. A study from 2016, which recorded smartphone screen-time and
sleep habits for 653 participants, showed that long screen-time is correlated with
==PAGE:22==
8
2 Literature review
worse sleep quality and shorter sleep. The same study also found that long screen-
time around bedtime is associated with delayed sleep onset [Chr+16]. The term sleep
onset is used for the time people fall asleep while the term sleep offset is used for the
time people wake up.
Several other studies which have investigated device usage around bedtime in ado-
lescence show similar findings that device usage before bedtime affects sleep quality
and sleep length [Mun+11; Hys+15; Van07].
2.3.2
Overnight smartphone usage
The research field of smartphone usage overnight is not well researched. The studies
which exist, are mainly based on questionnaires and self-reported sleep or app usage.
One study found out through a questionnaire that 11% men wake up a few times per
month by their smartphone, and 45% reported that they wake up only occasionally.
The same study found out that 14% of women wake up a few times per month, and
46% wake up only occasionally by their smartphone [THH11].
Another study which quantifies overnight app usage using data from the Copen-
hagen Networks Study is reviewed below.
2.3.2.1
A study of overnight app usage
A recent study from 2018 was conducted on overnight app usage. The goal of the
study was to quantify overnight phone smartphone usage and explore possible cor-
relation with sleep loss and physical and mental health symptoms. The study used
data from 815 participants in the Copenhagen Networks Study (CNS). Students from
the Technical University of Denmark participated in that study, and were handed a
new smartphone which monitored their smartphone usage, location and other be-
haviours for over two years. Furthermore, they answered a questionnaire (Karolin-
ska Sleep Questionnaire) at the beginning of the CNS study, which asked about,
among other things, their mental and physical health, sleep duration and sleep qual-
ity. The recorded smartphone usage were limited to Call-activity, SMS-activity and
Facebook-activity. A SMS-activity was registered when a text message was sent, and
Facebook-activity was registered when a participant liked a post or uploaded a status
on Facebook [Rod+18].
The study conducted on overnight app usage, explored data from the first 4 weeks
after the CNS study began in September 2013. Only data from workdays were used.
They saw high smartphone usage in the hour before sleep, and the smartphone usage
decreased the first 4-5 hours after self-reported bedtime and then raised again before
the participants woke up. One-third of the participants had smartphone usage within
the first 2 hours of the sleep duration. The study also shows that approximately 12-
15% had smartphone usage in the middle of the night (3-5 hours after bedtime).
The majority of the recorded smartphone activities were outgoing text messages or
76.5%, while Call-activities were 21.7% and Facebook-activities only 1.8%. Out of the
==PAGE:23==
2.4 Summary
9
815 participants, ∼41% had one or more nights interrupted by smartphone usage
during the 4 weeks. Out of the approximately forty-one percent, 36% occasionally had
overnight smartphone usage (defined as 1-3 interrupted nights) while 6% had frequent
overnight smartphone usage (defined as 4 or more interrupted nights). Out of the
participants who had frequent overnight smartphone usage, there were proportionally
slightly more women than men. In addition, the participants that had frequently
interrupted sleep, reported shorter sleep duration, or 7.0 hours, compared to the rest
(7.8 hours). Besides, users with frequently interrupted sleep had higher body mass
index (BMI). The study saw no clear difference between the participants with and
without overnight smartphone usage, in terms of mental health indicators [Rod+18].
The limitation of this study is that the recorded smartphone usage is only limited
to certain apps and actions on the phone (call, SMS and Facebook activities). In
addition, the sleep onset and offset are self-reported where the participants report
what is the average bedtime and wake-up time, meaning when smartphone usage
occurs, it is not known with great certainty whether it occurs during the night or
not (between sleep onset and offset). Furthermore, the participants were of similar
age (mean: 21 years), and all students were in the Technical University of Denmark
[Rod+18].
2.3.3
Using actigraphs for sleep measurement
The last years, actigraphs have grown in popularity. It is a wrist-worn device which
measures people’s sleep and activities. The device uses accelerometers and scoring
algorithms to detect awake and sleep cycles [Sad11; Gir+12].
Studies have shown that actigraphs are reasonable accurate at distinguishing
between awake and sleep phases and estimating sleep patterns for normal sleepers
[Anc+03; Sad11].
However, actigraphs have more difficulty in detecting sleep for
older adults, people with a sleep disorder, and detecting wake phases between sleep
periods [Sad11].
2.4
Summary
There is a strong correlation between sleep quality and health. People with impaired,
too short or too long sleep are more likely to deal with health problems like diabetes,
coronary heart disease, hypertension and depression. Light has a significant impact
on sleep. People who are exposed to much light before sleep have more difficulty to
fall asleep, and light can shift the circadian clock. Furthermore, studies show that
longer screen-time before sleep is associated with decreased sleep quality, shorter sleep
and delayed sleep onset.
Some mobile use is driven by habits, and some people suffer from mobile addiction.
Excessive smartphone usage is associated with adverse health traits such as loneliness,
anxiety and depression. Studies have found that younger people have more phone
usage and problematic use.

Only a few studies have investigated app use overnight, and they are mainly based
on questionnaires and self-reported sleep. However, those studies which exist show
more overnight app use for younger people and those who have a higher body mass
index. Researches on the quality of sleep data collected by actigraphs show that the
devices are reasonably accurate at detecting people sleep time for normal sleepers,
but it is more difficult to detect sleep for older adults, people with a sleep disorder,
and detecting wake phases between sleep periods.

Data collection and storage

3.1.1
Data collection
The dataset contains data, collected from the Android mobile app, Lifelog, which
was originally launched in April 2014. Lifelog is an activity tracker app, from Sony
Mobile Communications, which tracks people’s movement and app use. The Lifelog
app shows, among other things, how many steps the user has taken throughout the
day, calories burnt, sleep duration, walking time, running time and app usage. The
app is free and open for anyone to download via the Google Play Store.
Part of the data comes through an actigraph from Sony, called Smartband. The
Smartband is an additional device to the Lifelog app, which users can buy.
The
Smartband collects data about users’ heart rate, sleep and movements. The data are
transmitted from the Smartband to the Lifelog app. There exists three types of the
Sony’s Smartband, they are SWR10, SWR12 and SWR30. Data from the SWR10
Smartband was excluded in this research due to uncertainties of the wristband’s
accuracy [Ost17].
3.1.2
Data storage
After every month, the collected data from the Lifelog app during that month is put
into permanent storage at Sony. The data are stored in an S3 bucket at Amason Web
Services (AWS). Amazon S3 is a scalable file storage solution which supports large-
scale data with easy access [Bra+08]. In the S3 buckets, the data are stored in files

when dealing with large datasets and use JSON for schema definitions [Mae12].

4.1.1
Python and PySpark
We did most of the coding in the programming language Python, which has rich sup-
port for data processing and machine learning through various third-party packages
[Ped+11]. One of them is PySpark; it is an open-source Python package for Apache
Spark, which is a large-scale analytics engine for data analysis and processing. Apache
Spark and PySpark have the advantage of being able to run tasks parallelly on dif-
ferent machines or nodes in a cluster, implying that execution for data processing
can be distributed on multiple machines which is helpful for massive executions on a
large-scale dataset [Zah+10]. The PySpark uses a dataframe as a data-structure to
work with the data. Dataframes are immutable and can handle a tremendous amount

4.3.1
Quantifying overnight app usage
When quantifying the amount of overnight app usage for a user, we mainly use two
different units. The first unit is a fraction of nights with overnight app usage inter-
ruption. With other words, it is the percentage of nights (from the total number of
nights the user has) that include some app usage independent of how long the app
usage duration is. The second unit is how long the app usage interruption is over a
single night. When average interruption length is calculated and presented in chapter
5, it is the average interruption length only for the nights that have some overnight
app usage interruption. Meaning that it solely takes into account the nights that
include at least one overnight app session and none of the nights which do not have
any overnight app usage.
The latter unit, interruption length, which is the total app usage duration over
a single night, can then be divided into two other measurements which describe the
interruption in more details and were occasionally used. They are; the number of app
sessions over a single night and the duration of those sessions.
It is worth to mention, that when the term interruption is used, it is solely refer-
encing to an interruption by app usage overnight. Thus, interruption length is the
==PAGE:48==
34
4 Methodology
duration of the overnight app usage.
Moreover, a fraction of interruptions is the
percentage of nights, which include some overnight app usage.
When quantifying interruptions by overnight app usage, we often calculated the
distribution and different averages for different groups. What we also need to know,
is the error, if the calculated mean is the correct mean. We decided to calculate
the SEM, which gives an interval for the mean with a certain confidence. With that
information, it is possible to notice if there is a significant difference between two or
more means. The SEM shows how precise the sample mean is, and is affected by
both the sample size and the standard deviation (SD). SEM is calculated using the
formula 4.2 [AB05].
SEM =
SD
√sample size
(4.2)
To calculate the standard error, we calculated the variance of the sample as early as
appropriate and propagated it through our calculations. For example, calculating the
variance of sleep duration for each user and then propagate it when grouping users
together. When doing the error propagation, we followed the rules in the book An
introduction to Error Analysis [Tay97]. After the error propagation, we calculated
the standard deviation by taking the square root of the variance and then applied
formula 4.2 to get the SEM. Finally, we multiplied the outcome with the constant
1.96 to get 95% confidence interval. The figures in chapter 5 that have error bars,
have that in common that they represent SEM with 95% confidence interval. By
looking at error bars between two or more averages, it is possible to notice whether
there is a significant difference or not with a 95% confidence interval.

A short introduction is now

regression and random forest, and can run parallelly on multiple nodes in a cluster

data with the MLlib package and since the magnitude of our data is relatively small,

random forest model, we tried the XGBoost algorithm to see if the outcome would
improve.

4.3.2.3
Training, optimisation and evaluation
Before training the data, it is randomised and then split into 90/10 where 10% of
the data is called test set and is kept entirely clean for testing on the final models.
The 90% of the data is split again into 80/20 where 80% is used for training (called
training set) and 20% used for evaluation of the trained model and to evaluate when
optimising hyper-parameters (called evaluation set).
A 10-fold cross-validation was used to train the logistic regression models to avoid
overfitting, and we thought were suitable given the data size we had. All the training
==PAGE:50==
36
4 Methodology
set was used for the 10-fold cross-validation, and the evaluation set was used to
evaluate the models afterwards.
Next, we optimised hyperparameters by using the training set and evaluate us-
ing the evaluation set.
For logistic regression, we tried to optimise the following
parameters: C and penalty. For the random forest, we tried to optimise the following
parameters: min_samples_split, max_depth and n_estimators.
When evaluating, we looked at the accuracy, precision, recall and f1-score, but put
the most focus on the accuracy and the f1-score as we determined the cost of false
positive and false negative to be equal. The definitions of these numbers are following
where TP stands for true positives, TN stands for true negatives, FP stands for false
positives and FN stands for false negatives [DG06]:
• Accuracy = (TP+TN)/(TP+FN+FP+TN)
• Precision = TP/(TP+FP)
• Recall = TP/(TP+FN)
• f1-score = 2×(Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)
From the results, we also looked at the feature importance and the coefficients from
the logistic regression to get a better understanding of how the features affect the
models. Finally, we used the untouched and fully clean data, the test set, to evaluate
the final model to avoid overfitting.

5.1.7
Summary of quantifying overnight app usage
The analysis of quantifying overnight app usage has covered some of the fundamental
questions of this research. We quantified how much people use apps overnight, which
groups of people have the most overnight smartphone use and which apps people are
using during the night. The main findings are the following:
• On average, users have 12.24% of nights affected by overnight app usage.
• When users start using apps during the night, they stay on average for 12.25
minutes on their smartphones.
• Users who go on average late to sleep have slightly but significantly more
overnight app usage. But many previous studies have shown that people with
a lot of device use before bedtime are also associated with late bedtime [CG10;
THH11; Chr+16].
• Overnight app usage is considerably more likely to occur during weekends than
on workdays.
• Users who sleep less on average have substantially longer overnight app usage,
but the sleep duration for the nights with app usage is almost 1 hour longer

users with shorter sleep on average can be expected as the literature shows that

models created, we experimented by using countries instead of continents as a feature,
without better results.

the lowest overnight app usage were selected to use for the modelling, in total 8,028

without app usage but 15.74 minutes for nights with app usage. That tells us that
when people start using their smartphones during the night, they end up being awake
three times longer than otherwise. The results have some limitations in terms of the
Smartband’s possible inaccuracy of detecting wake-ups.
5.6
Summary for all results
We described and examined overnight smartphone use from different angles. The
average user has 12.24% of nights affected by overnight app use, and they stay on
average for 12.25 minutes on their smartphones. Younger users, users who have a
shorter sleep or go late to bed have higher overnight smartphone use than others.
The nights where users have overnight smartphone usage are around 1 hour longer
compared to nights without smartphone usage. People from Asia have considerably
more nights interrupted compared to people from Europe. During nights, users are
mostly communicating with each other, using social media apps, reading news and
watching videos.
We build models to predict if a user has a high or low overnight app use. The

This chapter summarises and discusses the results of the research and puts it into a
wider context. Sections 6.1-6.5 discuss different part of the study and finally section
6.6, discusses strengths and limitations of the research.
6.1
Discussion of quantifying overnight app use
Our results showed that the average person has 12.24% of nights affected by overnight
app use of some degree, and the average interruption length per night is 12.25 minutes.
It was striking that most users, or 77.33%, who have 90 nights or more registered
in the dataset, had at least one night affected by overnight app use. Another study
covering overnight app use over 4 weeks, showed that around 45-50% users had at
least one night with overnight app use in the first 5 hours after bedtime [Rod+18].
Our results show a similar number or around 55% of users with 28-30 nights registered
in the dataset, had at least one night with overnight app use. However, the study
used different methods to track app use and sleep, making a comparison to our results
more tricky [Rod+18].
When looking at the distribution of interruptions by app use, for users with more
than 90 nights registered, we saw that most users have relatively few nights inter-
rupted and most users have a quite short interruption length. However, it is shocking
that plenty of users have more than 20% of nights interrupted by overnight app usage
and are using apps for around 15-40 minutes on average each night.
It was not surprising that overnight app use was significantly more during week-
ends (Fridays and Saturdays) as people most often have a day off, the day after. We
also found out that users with high overnight app use, fall asleep later on average.
Many previous studies have shown that device use before bedtime and exposure of
artificial lights from smartphones delays bedtime [CG10; THH11; Chr+16].
We found that users with short average sleep duration had more prolonged inter-
ruptions by app use compared to those who sleep longer. That is expected compared
to the literature, which shows that device usage and smartphone usage before bed-
time is related to shorter sleep as well [CG10; THH11; Chr+16]. However, it was
surprising that the nights which are affected by app use are considerably longer. A
possible reason for that, is people need longer sleep duration as those nights contain
sleep disorder by smartphone use.

There was little difference between gender in terms of overnight app use. It was
however interesting that females have fewer nights affected by app usage, but they stay
longer using the smartphone when they pick it up during the night. That is converse
to what other study showed. That study found that females use the smartphone more
frequently than men, but the study was not limited to app use overnight [KLC15]. We
saw that males had slightly more frequent app use overnight, which can be related
that males are more likely to have higher smartphone addiction [LK18]. However,
the difference detected between genders were small and not significant based on 95%

We found that overnight app use was significantly higher for younger people. The
relative difference for a fraction of nights with an interruption between the youngest
and the oldest age group is quite enormous, around 112%.
That is expected as
other studies have shown that young people have particularly more mobile use and
problematic use [BP05].
When comparing overnight app use for different BMI categories, we saw small and
almost no significant difference, especially after normalising the data by age, so each
age contributes equally towards each BMI category.
Analysis of geographical factors was partly limited as the dataset is highly unbal-
anced regarding which countries and continents people live in where most of the users
are from Asia and Europe. People in Asia have substantially and significantly more
overnight app usage compared to people in Europe. There is also a vast difference
between some countries. The Netherlands has many nights interrupted by app usage
compared to Spain, where the relative difference is 114.8%. Some of the difference
between continents and countries might be associated with cultural differences, but
we did not investigate that in further details.
When comparing our results to another study which investigated overnight smart-
phone use, we see some similar results, such as people with overnight app use have
shorter average sleep duration. However, the study did not contain any information
on sleep duration for the nights, which were affected by app use. That study also
found out there was a correlation between overnight smartphone use and BMI, con-
versely to our findings, which show rather small and almost not noticeable difference
between BMI and overnight app use. Besides, that study analysed the relation be-
tween overnight app use and mental health without finding a clear correlation. Our
study did not analyse mental health indicators as the dataset did not contain any
such information [Rod+18].
Our study did not cover the reason for why the user started using the phone during
the night, whether the user was awake and consequently grabs the phone or if the
user waked up because of light or sound from the smartphone. Regardless, this app
use overnight deserves public attention, especially for the group of people who have
very frequent and prolonged app use during the night as impaired sleep has adverse
effects on health [YAM06; Cap+08; Aya+03; Arm07].
Our results showed the overnight app use is mostly around communicating with
others, using social media apps, reading news and watching videos. However, the most
used app during night is the Chrome web browser. We can not know what people are

Discussion of predicting low and high overnight
app use
We trained classification model to predict if users have very high or very low overnight
app use. The accuracy was 74%, and f1-score was between 72-77%, which is consid-
erably better compared to random guessing (50% accuracy). The feature importance
and the coefficients for the logistic regression model were mostly expected compared
to many of our results of quantifying overnight app use for different groups of people.
The most prominent feature we added to the model, was the average app duration
during the day. The more you use apps during the day, the more you use apps during
the night. These results can be connected to literature which shows that smartphones
create habits of frequently checking the phone, and excessive smartphone usage might
lead to addiction [Oul+12; BP05; TTK09].
From the coefficients of the logistic regression model, we saw younger users with
high app usage during the day and go late to sleep with short average sleep duration
from either Asia or North-America are likely to have high overnight app usage.
6.3
Discussion of time of night when app use occur
A large part of overnight app use occurs right after sleep onset and just before sleep
offset.
That is similar results compared to results from another study exploring
overnight app usage [Rod+18]. This frequent use before waking up and shortly after
sleep onset raises questions whether part of this use might be false data due to possible
imprecision of the Smartband. Whether only small or more extensive part of the
smartphone use during the first hours after sleep onset is false data, this frequent
use deserves attention regarding public health. According to the literature, people
spend more time in deep sleep the first hours of the night, and the frequent app usage
in the first part of the night can cause continuous disruptions of deep sleep which
can hamper biological restitution and recovery with increased risk of various diseases
[C+05; Rod+10; Cap+10].
The frequent wake up before sleep offset, might be explained by the fact that
people are less in deep sleep the last part of the night where it is easier for a person
to wake up, for example, from sound or light from the smartphone [C+05].

Discussion of how overnight app use is changing
over time
We saw a slight increase over time for average overnight app use length. We expected
more increase, but it turns out that some people are increasing while others are
decreasing quite much in terms of interruption length. However, we had limited users
to analyse on an individual level as few users have a lot of continues data for the

We trained a logistic regression model to predict which users are increasing a lot
over time, and which users are decreasing a lot, in terms of interruption length by
overnight app use. The model was slightly better than random guessing but was still
quite poor at classifying, but we only had 402 users to train and evaluate. Meaning we
were not successful at identifying which groups of users are increasing or decreasing
over time.
Three of the features used for the modelling were average app duration during the
day, average sleep duration and average sleep onset time. It might improve the model
by using instead the delta value of those features, something we did not manage to
look deeper into. With that, it is possible to see if a change over time regarding sleep
onset time, sleep duration and app usage during the day is associated with a change
in terms of overnight app use.

use
There is a statistically significant difference in awake time for nights with app use
and without. Users are on average awake for 5.06 minutes per night for the nights
without app use but three times longer, or 15.74 minutes, for nights with app usage.
These results tell us that users are not only killing time by browsing the smartphone
when they are awake but extend the awake time. The results are expected as artificial
lights from smartphones reduces sleepiness [Cze13]. The results have some limitations
in terms of the Smartband’s inaccuracy of detecting wake-ups. A study found that
actigraphs have more difficulty in detecting wake phases between sleep periods, such
as wake-ups during night [Sad11].

Sections 6.6.1-6.6.5 discusses different limitations of the research and their possible
impacts on the results, while section 6.6.6 discusses the strengths of the research.

6.6.1
Incorrect phone usage and self-reported data
It is unknown to us whether more than one person uses the smartphone. After a
user goes to sleep, for example, a spouse who is still awake could be using the phone,
which could result in false data.
Besides, not all users are likely to provide correct information about themselves
(for example, gender, birth date, height and weight) when they sign up to the Lifelog
app, making some data in the dataset incorrect. We do not know the amount of
erroneous data entered. Nevertheless, we observe an extraordinary number of people
registered using the default data value when they sign up (for example, birth date
and gender).
6.6.2
Distribution of people in the dataset
The distribution of people in the dataset is unbalanced. Therefore, the results could
be biased. According to the dataset, 66% of the users are registered as male, while
only 31% are female. The number of young and older adults is limited, and most users
are aged between 30 to 45 years. Over 93% of the users are from Europe or Asia, and
less than 7% are from other continents. User distribution by country within Europe
and Asia is also uneven, but 20% of all users are from Japan. This geographical
imbalance could mean that cultural differences may have a more substantial impact
on the results than otherwise.
As the Lifelog app is only available to smartphones running the Android operating
system, the dataset does not include, for example, iPhone or Windows phone owners.
Furthermore, the people in the dataset should be able to afford the Smartband or get
it as a gift, making the group in our dataset homogeneous to a certain extent. People
who have the Smartband might be healthier people or persons who think more about
health.
6.6.3
Dataset’s size
Although the whole Lifelog dataset contains data for almost 11 million users, only
181,689 users have sleep data. After the pre-processing was done, the number of users
decreased down to 124,522 users. Furthermore, a lot of those users are missing some
data attributes such as age, gender, country and BMI. It was therefore only possible
to use a subset of the users for certain analysis. This was especially true for doing
the machine learning part, where all user attributes needed to be registered and with
a minimum number of nights. Around half of the users have less than 30 registered
nights in the dataset.
6.6.4
The Smartband
The imprecision of the Smartband provides a possible limitation to this research. The
accuracy of the Smartband’s detection for sleep duration has been validated, but less
==PAGE:96==

is known about the Smartband’s accuracy at detecting deep sleep and wake ups in
the middle of the night. The validations showed that the Smartband (except the
SWR10 type) was rather accurate in detecting the correct sleep duration. However,
the validations are somewhat limited by the number of users analysed [Ost17; Ste18].
Besides, studies have shown that actigraphs are reasonable accurate to distinguish
between awake and sleep phases for normal sleepers [Anc+03; Sad11].
However,
actigraphs have more difficulty in detecting wake phases between sleep periods [Sad11].
Also, people who have been working with the Smartband and the dataset informed
us that the Smartband may have difficulty detecting when people are awake while
lying still in bed. This gives rise to some concern that users might be trying to fall
asleep without success even while the Smartband states they are asleep. If the user
gives up trying to fall asleep and starts browsing the Internet on their smartphone or
watching YouTube, then the registered data of overnight app usage will be false, as
the user did not fall asleep that night. However, we applied various methods during
the pre-processing intending to reduce possible false data.
Another possible limitation regarding the Smartband is that it may be irritating
to wear it while trying to fall asleep. This is especially true for the first nights before
the person has got used to the wristband; this is also our personal experience. In such
cases, the sleep quality can be affected, and the user might wake up more often than
otherwise and have different sleep duration. This can be a concern as some users only
have few nights registered.
The third factor regarding the Smartband which should be mentioned is that
people might take off their Smartband for specific activities such as running, meaning
less distance will then be registered than the user ran.
6.6.5
App usage not taken into account
As mentioned in 4.2.2.3, a possible background service app activity was removed.
Therefore app usage during the night can be more than the results in this research
indicates.
Today some of the users’ interactions go solely through a background
service without having to launch the app into foreground. An example of that is
push notification with action buttons. The user can read an incoming message from
the phone’s lock screen during the night after waking up from the notification’s sound
without having to open up and launch the app into foreground. Furthermore, the
user can interact with the notification, during the night, through action buttons. An
example of that is a push notification from a messenger app called Messenger where
user can reply with a like through an action button.
Apps can allow many different interactions through notifications’ action buttons
such as archiving or deleting an email or reply to a message directly from the noti-
fication. These kinds of interactions, both reading messages from push notifications
on the lock screen and interact with the push notifications through action buttons
such as reply and archive, has most likely increased with increasing distribution of
the Android versions which support these kinds of interactions. With Android 5.0,

released in late 2014, notifications could appear on the lock screen and with Android
7.0, released in late 2016, it was possible to reply to messages directly through a
notification action button [Pro17].
6.6.6
Strengths
This study has several strengths. Even it was mention that we were only able to use
a subset of all the users in the dataset, the study still contained considerably many
users (N = 124,522). A portion of the users had data for a long time, and a small
subset contained data for over two years. The study was conducted with collected
data from the Lifelog app and the Smartband and did not rely on self-reported data.
Previous studies covering overnight app use, rely on self-reported sleep data where
the user reports average sleep onset and offset time.
With collected data from a
device, sleep onset and offset is measured per night. Furthermore, the research is a
multi-cultural study, covering data from over 200 countries, even most users are from
Europe and Asia.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Smartphone usage can keep people awake and impair their sleep, with adverse effects
on health [Cze13; Rod+10; Cap+10]. In this thesis, we described overnight smart-
phone use to quantify the usage for different demographics and characterise users
with excessive overnight app use with possible adverse health effects in mind. We
found substantial overnight app use, where over 12% of an average person’s nights
are affected by overnight smartphone use; the average interruption length per night
is over 12 minutes. Furthermore, over 77% of users in the study, with registered data
of at least 90 nights, had overnight app use.
We found that overnight smartphone activity is associated with age, bedtime, ge-
ographical factors and sleep duration. Young people use smartphones substantially
more during the night, up to twice as much as compared to older adults.
Night
owls who go to bed late on average have slightly but significantly higher overnight
app usage compared to those who go to bed early. Furthermore, people from Asia
have, relatively, over 30% more nights affected by overnight smartphone use com-
pared to Europe. We also found that people with short average sleep duration have
substantially more overnight smartphone use. However, nights where users perform
smartphone activities, are almost 1 hour longer compared to nights without smart-
phone use.
Our analysis showed that people are considerably more likely to use their phones
overnight during weekends and the average use is longer as well. We documented that
in users’ overnight smartphone usage, they mostly use communication apps, social
apps, news apps and watch videos.
We trained classification models to predict those with excessive overnight app use
and those without, with around 74% accuracy. The most important feature was app
duration during the day, but other important features affecting the model were: age,
average sleep duration and average sleep onset time. The coefficients from the logistic
regression model showed that users from either Asia or North America, who are young,
with high app use during the day and who go to bed late with short average sleep
duration are more likely to have excessive overnight app use.
On looking into the time of night when app usage occurs, we found that most usage
is at the beginning of the night, shortly after sleep onset, and just before people start
to wake up. Conversely, there is proportionally low app use in the middle of the night.
People prefer to use video apps early in the night, and communication, social and
browsing apps in the morning before sleep offset.
==PAGE:100==
86
7 Conclusion
The analysis on change over time in terms of overnight smartphone usage showed
that the usage length per night seems to be slightly increasing but is, however, not
entirely clear. When looking on an individual level, some users are increasing, and
some users are decreasing their overnight app use length per night. We trained a
logistic regression model to predict whether a user is increasing or decreasing in
terms of interruption length, without much success.
We analysed wake time for nights with and without overnight smartphone use,
where nights without app use had about 5 minutes wake time on average, while
nights with app use had about three times longer wake time or 15 minutes.
This overnight smartphone use, especially excessive use for specific demographics,
creates concerns about adverse effects on public health.
With the dataset used in this study, we cannot know why the user started using
the phone during the night, whether the user was awake and used the device as
entertainment or was woken up by light or sound from the smartphone. For future
work, we suggest expanding the research to include what triggers app use overnight
and whether the use started from a received push notification or was self-driven.
