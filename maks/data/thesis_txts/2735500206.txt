Abstract
Mobile health (mHealth) solutions are at the forefront of revolutionizing healthcare delivery for in­
dividuals suffering from chronic lifestyle­related diseases. By leveraging digital coaching, mHealth
platforms offer scalable interventions aimed at facilitating significant changes in lifestyle behaviors
crucial for health improvement. Despite their proven potential, sustaining user adherence poses a
substantial challenge. While numerous studies have validated the effectiveness of electronic health
(eHealth) programs in enhancing health outcomes, there exists a notable gap in understanding how
the level of user engagement directly influences behavior change.
This thesis aims to bridge this gap by dissecting the drivers and dynamics of engagement patterns
among participants engaged in LIVA’s health program. By deploying an advanced data pipeline and
employing Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), this study meticulously identifies distinct clusters of user
engagement. Analysis of data from 1777 participants enrolled in the National Diabetes Prevention
Program reveals three distinct engagement clusters, exhibiting remarkable consistency throughout
the program’s 9­month duration. Furthermore, the investigation highlights three unique user profiles at
the program’s onset, laying the groundwork for a comprehensive understanding of initial engagement
impacts.
Crucially, our findings demonstrate a linear relationship between the level of user engagement and
the degree of weight loss, providing empirical evidence of engagement’s pivotal role in facilitating
health outcomes. Moreover, the application of Random Forest models to predict program dropout
risks achieved an accuracy of 80%, underscoring the predictive models’ efficacy in identifying at­risk
participants early on.
The culmination of this research underscores the significance of self­monitoring behaviors as key
predictors of program adherence, emphasizing the necessity of self­motivation in recognizing and
committing to behavior change. This thesis not only contributes to the existing body of knowledge on
mHealth interventions but also underscores the critical role of user engagement and self­monitoring
in achieving successful health behavior change.

Introduction
The digital revolution has entailed an endless stream of possibilities within the healthcare sector,
particularly in the field of mobile health (mHealth) solutions. These innovative solutions have shown
great promise in delivering scalable interventions aimed at altering health behaviors to populations
battling chronic lifestyle­related diseases. Such conditions, which include type 2 diabetes, obesity,
cardiovascular diseases, and certain types of cancer, represent a significant burden, being leading
causes of mortality and disability globally. The escalating prevalence of these diseases underscores
the urgent need for cost­effective, scalable strategies that encourage healthful behavioral changes
[1, 2, 3].
Digital health coaching has emerged as a powerful tool in this context, offering accessible, affordable
programs that support individuals in adopting healthier lifestyles. Leveraging asynchronous interven­
tions through mHealth solutions, this approach has proven effective in promoting dietary improve­
ments, weight management, and increased physical activity, all while delivering care and treatment
in a manner that is both efficient and economical.
Despite the potential advantages of these digital platforms, ensuring users adherence, especially
during the crucial initial weeks, remains a significant challenge. Early engagement is crucial, as it
is frequently associated with improved health outcomes and significantly influences user behavior
within the mHealth app. Yet, the dynamics between user engagement patterns and health outcomes
still needs to be further explored in the existing research [4].
This master thesis aims to investigate the drivers and dynamics of engagement patterns over time
among patients with chronic lifestyle­related diseases using LIVA—a Danish eHealth coaching plat­
form designed to mitigate long­term complications [5]. This research focus on a case study involving
a group of users who have embarked on a comprehensive 9­month program offered by LIVA, which
is meticulously aligned with the principles of the National Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP). The
DPP’s core mission is to prevent the onset of diabetes through a concerted effort focused on lifestyle
modifications. By considering its evidence, LIVA’s program is engineered to instigate significant be­
havioral changes among its participants, thereby reducing their risk of developing diabetes in the
long­term.
Building on the exploration of mHealth solutions and the pivotal role of LIVA in supporting individuals
through lifestyle modifications, this thesis underscores the significance of user engagement as a
cornerstone for the success of such programs. Recognizing the critical impact of how users interact
with digital health platforms, it becomes essential to understand user engagement evolution over
time, and assess its influence on program adherence and success. Thereby, the research questions
proposed are as follows:
1. What types of user engagement exists in the LIVA program, and how do they relate to
health outcomes? This question seeks to categorize the diverse ways users engage with
the app, recognizing that engagement extends beyond simple frequency of use to cover the
depth and quality of interaction. Identifying distinct patterns of engagement could offer insights
into how different approaches to the app correlate with improvements or regressions in health
metrics.
2. How does engagement change over the 9­month National Diabetes Prevention Program?
Understanding that user engagement is dynamic, this question attempt to explore how en­
gagement trajectories change over time. It considers whether engagement tends to increase,
decrease, or fluctuate in patterns that could be indicative of future adherence.
3. Can early engagement patterns predict program dropout risks? This question delves into
the predictive potential of early engagement patterns. By identifying features of engagement
that are strongly associated with dropout risk, the research aims to enable the early tailoring of
interventions.

Following the research questions, the methodology of this thesis begins with the employment of un­
supervised learning techniques to identify and categorize clusters based on data collected from the
initial two weeks of engagement within the program. This phase aims to address the first two research
questions by categorizing users based on patient characteristics, early behavioral tendencies, and
subsequent health outcomes. By identifying distinct clusters of engagement, this approach seeks to
uncover the diverse ways users engage with the app and how these engagement patterns evolve
over the course of the 9­month National Diabetes Prevention Program. Such analysis is critical for
understanding the depth and quality of user interaction, providing insights into how variations in en­
gagement correlate with health outcomes and adherence levels over time.
Transitioning from the foundational understanding gained in the initial phase, the methodology ad­
vances to a supervised learning paradigm. This shift is designed to tackle the third research ques­
tion, focusing on the predictive potential of early engagement patterns in identifying users at risk of
program dropout. By constructing a predictive model based on user behavioural data, this approach
aims to identify specific engagement features that indicate a higher likelihood of discontinuing the pro­
gram. This predictive model represents a crucial tool for personalizing interventions early in the user’s
journey, aiming to enhance engagement and reduce dropout rates, thereby optimizing the likelihood
of achieving beneficial long­term health outcomes for the users.
Concluding this introduction, the significance of this research extends beyond the academic field,
addressing a critical gap in the current understanding of digital health interventions. By systematically
exploring the intricate relationship between user engagement and health outcomes within the LIVA
platform, this thesis aims to contribute valuable insights to the field of mHealth. Furthermore, this
thesis highlights the power of data­driven approaches in enhancing the efficacy and reach of health
interventions in the digital age.
This report starts with an overview of eHealth, user engagement models, and the specific context
of Liva Healthcare. Following this foundational section, the report transitions to a detailed review of
related work in the domain of digital health interventions, with a particular focus on user engagement
patterns. This chapter also includes prior research findings specific to Liva Healthcare.
The next methodology section is dedicated to the the research design and the analytical techniques
employed in this study. It provides a detailed description of the data collection process, the unsu­
pervised and supervised learning methods used for data analysis, and the rationale behind choosing
these specific approaches. The results chapter follows, describing the actual findings of the research
and addressing the research questions posed earlier. It presents the user engagement patterns
identified, their evolution over the 9­month period, and their implications for program adherence and
dropout risks.
The reports culminates into a thorough discussion of these findings and weave them into the broader
domain of digital health research. The conclusion serves as the culmination of the report, encapsu­
lating the key points derived from this research.

Background
In this section, the background context necessary to better comprehend the scope and significance
of the research conducted is presented. It outlines the fundamental concepts relevant to electronic
Health (eHealth) in chronic diseases, emphasizing the role of user engagement and its influence on
health outcomes. Additionally, the chapter discusses the concept of churn rate as a pivotal metric
for gauging user engagement on digital health platforms. This is followed by introducing the case
study of LIVA Healthcare, a Danish eHealth coaching platform aimed at managing lifestyle choices
and long­term complications associated with chronic conditions, thereby highlighting the research
question.
2.1
eHealth possibilities
Recent decades have witnessed a remarkable surge in digital interventions aimed at mitigating inef­
ficiencies within the healthcare system through enhanced quality and effectiveness of care. Particu­
larly, mobile health (mHealth) technologies have revolutionized the way information is shared between
healthcare providers and patients, facilitating immediate communication. This aspect is vitally impor­
tant for managing chronic diseases, allowing healthcare professionals to deliver care asynchronously,
thus fostering ongoing adherence to preventive lifestyle measures.
Chronic conditions, including type 2 diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular diseases, are major con­
tributors to morbidity and mortality, accounting for over two­thirds of all deaths and consuming 75% of
healthcare budgets in Europe [6]. This underscores the urgent need for more scalable and resource­
efficient treatment modalities. The potential of mHealth and eHealth to transform healthcare is signif­
icant, especially if leveraged appropriately. Their capacity to provide personalized, accessible, and
efficient healthcare services is paramount in addressing the escalating challenges posed by chronic
diseases. Furthermore, it is projected that the burden of these conditions and the associated health­
care expenditures are expected to rise in both developed and developing countries, not only due to
aging but also because of lifestyle and societal changes [7, 8]. This projection emphasizes the critical
need for the strategic deployment of mHealth and eHealth solutions to not only manage but potentially
reverse this trend, offering long­term efficacy and sustainability in healthcare delivery.
Several studies have explored various aspects of this potential, from chronic care management and
integrated frameworks for prevention and treatment to the implementation of mHealth­based ap­
proaches in clinical psychology and medicine. For instance, Castelnuovo et al. (2015) demonstrate
how eHealth and mHealth platforms can significantly enhance chronic care management for obesity,
highlighting the potential of these technologies to facilitate weight loss, improve adherence, and re­
duce long­term complications, thereby offering a cost­effective solution to traditional care challenges
[9]. Similarly, Dietz et al. (2015) advocate for an integrated framework that combines clinical and
community resources to effectively combat obesity and its related diseases, emphasizing the crucial
role of dietary and physical activity interventions enabled by mHealth technologies [10]. Hamine et
al. (2015) contribute to this narrative by systematically reviewing the effectiveness of mHealth in­
terventions in chronic disease management, particularly highlighting the positive impact on glycemic
control and adherence in diabetes care [11]. Lastly, Agnihothri et al. (2020) present a comprehen­
sive analysis of mHealth’s value in managing chronic conditions such as diabetes and hypertension,
underscoring the improvement in health outcomes and efficiency in healthcare delivery [12].
Collectively, these studies provide insights into the effectiveness of mHealth interventions, including
their impact on treatment adherence, patient outcomes, and the overall management of chronic dis­
eases [13]. As literature suggests, the effectiveness of these solutions is intrinsically linked to their
ability to incorporate behavioral change techniques (BCTs) and personalized feedback mechanisms
[14]. This highlights the need for further exploration and integration of mHealth technologies into
healthcare systems.

2.2. User Engagement
2.2
User Engagement
User engagement in eHealth refers to the extent and manner in which individuals interact with digital
health platforms and services. This interaction is not just quantifiable in terms of frequency and
duration but also qualitatively assessed by the meaningfulness, personal relevance, and impact of
the technology on the user’s health behavior and outcomes [15]. When there exists a desire to use a
digital platform consistently and repeatedly, a user is said to be engaged, indicating that the platform
is not merely used, but the user is actively engaged with it.
This engagement is a multidimensional construct that encompasses the emotional, cognitive, and
behavioral experience of a user with a technology resource, both at any given point in time and over
time [16]. Thus, being dissected into this three core components:
• Behavioural: This involves the actions the user take when interacting within the eHealth plat­
form, such as logging in, inputting data, completing tasks, and utilizing resources. This is often
the most visible aspect of engagement, including both the frequency and types of interactions.
• Emotional: This refers to users’ feelings and attitudes towards the eHealth platform. It encom­
passes user satisfaction, trust in the platform, and the emotional connection users feel with the
service. Positive emotional engagement has been seen to be crucial for long­term platform use
and adherence to health interventions.
• Cognitive: This relates to the mental effort and attention users invest in eHealth platforms.
This includes understanding health information presented, making decisions based on platform
advice, and reflecting on personal health data.
Understanding the role of user engagement in eHealth is critical for the success of digital health
interventions. A large number of studies have shown that engaged users are more likely to exhibit
behaviors that contribute to positive health outcomes, making engagement a key factor in the efficacy
of eHealth solutions [17, 18, 19].
Firstly, engagement has been seen to directly influence adherence to digital health programs. Users
who are actively engaged—emotionally, cognitively, and behaviorally—are more likely to follow through
with prescribed interventions and health­related activities. This adherence is crucial for chronic dis­
ease management, where consistent behavior changes and monitoring are essential for managing
conditions effectively.
Secondly, user engagement enhances the personalisation of health interventions. As users interact
with eHealth platforms, they generate data that can be used to tailor interventions to their specific
needs and preferences. This personalisation is only possible through sustained engagement, where
ongoing user interactions provide the necessary insights to refine and adjust health recommendations.
On the other hand, user engagement is influenced by a myriad of factors ranging from the design of
the intervention to the personal motivations and barriers faced by users:
• User­Centric Design: This results essential for fostering engagement. Designing with the user
in mind ensures that eHealth platforms are intuitive, accessible, and relevant to the target pop­
ulation. Incorporating user feedback during the development phase can significantly enhance
the usability and appeal of digital health interventions.
• Motivational Factors: This refers to personal health goals, perceived benefits of the interven­
tion, and intrinsic motivation play a crucial role in user engagement. Gamification elements and
reward systems can also boost motivation by making the health intervention more engaging and
enjoyable [20].
• Barriers to Engagement: This include technological challenges faced by users, lack of digital
literacy, privacy concerns, and the perceived relevance of the content [21].
Thereby, tailored strategies that address barriers and leverage facilitators are essential for enhancing
user engagement and, consequently, the efficacy of eHealth interventions.

Theoretical Frameworks
Behavioral change theories are psychosocial theories that provide a foundational framework for un­
derstanding the complex process by which individuals modify their health behaviors in response to
eHealth interventions [22]. Among the most influential ones is the Health Belief Model (HBM) [23],
which states that a person’s belief in a personal threat of an illness or disease, coupled with their
belief in the effectiveness of the recommended health behavior, can predict the likelihood of adopting
healthy behaviors.
Similarly, the model of the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) suggests that an individual’s health
behavior is determined by their intention to perform the behavior, which is itself influenced by attitudes
toward the behavior, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. Thus, the more favorable
the attitude with respect to a behavior and the greater the perceived behavioral control, the stronger
an individual’s intention to perform the behavior should be [24].
The Transtheoretical Model (TTM) provides another critical perspective by delineating the stages
through which individuals pass in the process of behavior change, from precontemplation to main­
tenance [25]. The TTM model proposes that behavior change is a process that occurs in five main
stages: precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action and maintenance, ending in the sixth
stage of termination, where the individual has no desire to return to their unhealthy behaviors. It
emphasizes that behavior change is a personal and nonlinear process, allowing for tailored interven­
tions that match an individual’s current stage of change and it has been applied to a wide variety of
behaviors including dietary changes.

customer retention but also serves as an indicator of the value and effectiveness of the eHealth ser­

2.2. User Engagement
Factors contributing to churn in eHealth platforms are multifaceted, involving both service­related
attributes and user engagement dynamics. A significant driver of churn is poor user experience,
including difficult navigation, lack of content, or technical challenges. These issues can cause user
frustration, leading to decreased engagement and increased likelihood of leaving the platform. Thus,
service quality attributes such as usability and reliability are crucial for customer loyalty.
Users may churn when eHealth platforms fail to meet their expectations regarding health outcomes,
support, or personalized experiences. This highlights the importance of aligning service attributes
with customer expectations. Similarity, the perception of insufficient value is another critical factor
driving churn [28].
The presence of alternative or competing services can further exacerbate churn rates, particularly
if these alternatives offer superior features, more attractive pricing, or better health outcomes. This
suggests the need for eHealth platforms to continuously innovate and improve their offerings to retain
users in the long­term. Strategies aimed at improving usability, offering personalized and valuable
health solutions are essential for reducing churn and fostering user loyalty.

In this section, Liva Healthcare is introduced by describing the eHealth platform and their intervention

2.3.1
LIVA platform
Liva Healthcare is a health tech company that specializes in digital therapeutics and behavior change
to prevent, manage, and treat chronic diseases like type 2 diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular
diseases. Established in 2015 in Denmark, it focuses on integrating personalized, one­on­one lifestyle
coaching with a digital­first approach. Their platform, called LIVA, is designed to support patients in
their journey towards healthier lifestyles through extensive evidence­based programs that have been
validated by clinical studies [29, 30, 31, 32, 33].
The LIVA platform acts as a mHealth tool that supports behavioral changes for their members by help­
ing them manage their disease through live video sessions, weekly coaching, personalized advice,
goal tracking, and group support (Figure 2.2). Within this framework, it facilitates initial goal­setting
interactions with health coaches, enabling patients to set, record, and pursue their health objectives
such as steps taken, weight management, and dietary habits. In addition to goal monitoring and dia­
logues with health coaches through messaging and advice, patients can participate in online forums
for community support.
Health coaches use the platform through an internet browser, offering proactive guidance based on
the patient’s activity and data entered into the platform on a weekly­to­monthly basis. The intervention
program, which spans 3 to 12 months, collects personal data and health information from patients to
deliver treatment services and support research initiatives. LIVA’s approach has been demonstrated
to be successful in managing chronic diseases through digital health interventions.

LIVA offers different type of programs for a range of health interventions. This includes support for

Additionally, LIVA plays an active role in national diabetes prevention initiatives, supplying resources
and coaching to those at risk to prevent the onset of type 2 diabetes.
The National Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP), where LIVA is involved, consists on different ini­
tiatives typically following evidence­based guidelines that have been proven to delay or prevent the
onset of type 2 diabetes. This includes the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC’s) DPP,
which is a well­researched model showing that making modest lifestyle changes can significantly
reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes by as much as 58% for adults with prediabetes [34].
The LIVA program begins with a referral, where participants are recommended by their healthcare
providers to join. Once signed up, they create a profile on the LIVA platform and schedule an initial 45­
minute video consultation with a chosen coach. This session is critical for establishing a personalized
plan and setting specific health and lifestyle objectives in areas such as weight, diet, and exercise.
A key aspect of the LIVA approach is the collaborative decision­making between the healthcare prac­
titioner (HCP) and the member, which has been found to be highly effective in promoting adherence
and successful long­term weight management. Thus, the strength of the program lies in the devel­
opment of a robust relationship between the HCP and the member, facilitated by their interactions on
the platform.
Communication between the coach and the member is exclusively conducted asynchronous online
via LIVA. Initially, interactions occur weekly for the first three months, transitioning to biweekly and
eventually to an as­needed (ad­hoc) basis. During the intervention, members regularly log various
health metrics and personal data into LIVA, contributing to a comprehensive data profile that supports
their progress tracking.
Following the onboarding session, the coach monitors the member’s development through the plat­
form, offering tailored guidance and encouragement. This regular contact, primarily through video
messages and text, helps the coach stay informed about the member’s progress and challenges,
enabling personalized support throughout the journey.

3.1
Digital Health on Weight Loss
The domain of digital health, particularly in weight management and diabetes prevention, has gained
prominence, as evidenced by the study of SL Painter et al., ”Drivers of weight loss in a CDC­recognized
digital diabetes prevention program.” [36]. This work, analyzing the experiences of 2,0000 partici­
pants over a period of 12 month, offers valuable insights into the effectiveness of digital interventions
on weight loss. It emphasizes the influence of self­monitoring behaviors and the role of coaching
interactions. The analysis revealed that self­monitoring behaviors, specifically food logging, were
significantly correlated with weight loss. Moreover, participants who were more engaged with the
program, often through increased coaching, showed a greater propensity for weight loss, demon­
strating the importance of personalized support in digital health programs.
This is further illustrated by the systemic review by Van Rhoon et al. (2020) [37]. This comprehen­
sive review aimed to identify which technology­driven diabetes prevention interventions were most
effective in producing clinically significant weight loss. By analyzing 19 studies, Van Rhoon et al.
discovered that interventions successful in the short term (achieving ≥3% weight loss at ≤6 months)
employed an average of 5.6 more behavior change techniques than those that were not effective.
Similarly, long­term effective interventions (achieving ≥5% weight loss at ≥12 months) utilized 3.7
more behavior change techniques than their less effective counterparts.
The review highlighted several key behavior change techniques prevalent in over 90% of effective
interventions, including social support, goal setting, feedback on behavior, and self­monitoring of out­
comes of behavior. Notably, interventions that incorporated digital features facilitating health and
lifestyle education, behavior/outcome tracking, and online health coaching were among the most ef­
fective. This underlines the pivotal role of digital features in enhancing the effectiveness of digital
health interventions, aligning with the findings of Painter et al. regarding the importance of engage­
ment and personalized support.
3.2
Engagement Patterns through Clustering
In the field of digital health, understanding user engagement patterns is crucial for tailoring interven­
tions to individual needs. Several studies highlight the potential use of employing clustering analysis
to elucidate how different groups interact with digital health platforms and tools.
The study ”Outpatient portal use among pregnant individuals: Cross­sectional, temporal, and cluster
analysis of use” by Morgan et al. [38], aimed to characterize outpatient portal use among pregnant
individuals, identifying clusters based on cross­sectional and temporal patterns of engagement. Em­
ploying hierarchical agglomerative clustering at the patient level, the analysis revealed four distinct
clusters: schedulers, resulters, intense digital engagers, and average users. This classification un­
derscores the varied nature of portal use, suggesting that engagement strategies should be diversified
to meet the specific preferences and needs of each group. For instance, schedulers might benefit
from enhanced appointment scheduling functionalities, while resulters may prefer more immediate
access to test results and interpretations.

3.2. Engagement Patterns through Clustering
Another study conducted by M. Chia et al. [39], delves into the daily screen and non­media habits
and sleep patterns of preschool children across multiple countries. Utilizing an unsupervised clus­
tering approach, where the optimal number of clusters was determined by the McClain index, the
research identified seven emergent clusters that delineate distinct media consumption and sleep pat­
terns among preschool­aged children. This segmentation provides crucial insights for developing
more targeted approaches to manage screen time and promote healthier media habits and improved
sleep quality among young children.
Furthermore, the study ”Who uses Mhealth? User archetypes for physical and mental health apps”
by M. Aziz et al. explores the diverse user archetypes of health apps, focusing on both physical
and mental health applications [40]. Through K­prototypes clustering analysis, this research delin­
eates between two main clusters for mental health app users: help­seeking users and maintenance
users. For physical health apps, three clusters were identified: happy conscious occasional users,
happy neurotic occasional users, and unhappy neurotic frequent users, revealing various levels of
contentment and engagement frequency with the apps.
Among others, these studies illustrate the nuanced landscape of digital health engagement. By em­
ploying clustering analysis, researchers can unveil distinct patterns of use and engagement.
3.2.1
Types of Models
Employing the right clustering model is crucial for accurately capturing the nuances of user behav­
iors and exploring health engagement patterns. There exists different clustering algorithms classified
as: (1) Hierarchical­based, (2) Partitioning­based, (3) Density­based, (4) Grid­based, (5) Distribution­
Based, (6) Model­based, and (7) Spectral Clustering. However, it was found that the majority of seg­
mentation applications for user profiling either used hierarchical or non­hierarchical (i.e., partitioning)
methods [41].
The choice of model significantly impacts the ability to distinguish different groups, especially given the
diverse nature of user interaction data, which may include a mix of binary and continuous variables, as
well as varying data distributions. This subsection discusses models that have been used particularly
at handling these complexities in unsupervised clustering contexts for developing robust behavioural
profiles.
On one hand, the complexity of user engagement data often lies in its composition of both binary
(e.g., has/has not completed an action) and continuous variables (e.g., time spent on a platform)
[42]. K­medoids, a partitioning­based clustering model, has been highlighted for their versatility in
scenarios involving mixed data types. Shamsuddin et at. [43] presented K­medoids’ suitability for
mixed­type variables, showing its advantage over K­means when dealing with a mix of different types
of variables. Hierarchical clustering is another versatile method for analyzing mixed datasets. Hsu et
al. [44] proposed a distance hierarchy method for mixed data clustering, facilitating the expression
of similarity between categorical values and unifying the distance measurement for numerical and
categorical data within a hierarchical framework, thereby enhancing the clustering quality for mixed
datasets.
While mixed data types pose a significant challenge in clustering user engagement data, another crit­
ical aspect is data distribution. Although user behavioral data tends to be predominantly continuous,
the distribution of this data can vary widely, impacting the performance of clustering models too. For
instance, Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), a type of model­based clustering, assume that the data
is generated from a mixture of several Gaussian distributions. This assumption makes GMMs really
powerful when the data adheres to these conditions, as it can accommodate mixed distributions and
model the covariance structure within each cluster. However, its effectiveness diminishes when the
data significantly deviates from Gaussian distributions [45].
GMMs are also particularly valuable for clustering user engagement data, capable of modeling com­
plex behaviors as mixtures of multiple Gaussian distributions. This attribute allows GMMs to capture
the nuanced engagement patterns unique to each user, facilitating the generation of synthetic data
that mirrors real­world patterns.

Previous studies of Liva Healthcare
This part aims to synthesize the outcomes observed from past studies of Liva Healthcare for a deeper

One of the first studies by Liva Healthcare was titled ”Electronic Health Lifestyle Coaching Among
Diabetes Patients in a Real­Life Municipality Setting: Observational Study.” (2019) [33]. This focuses
on the effectiveness of their eHealth lifestyle coaching intervention among diabetes patients in Danish
municipalities, conducted between 2016 and 2018. Observing obese diabetes patients participating
in the intervention from 3 to 12 months, the study found significant weight reductions, with an average
loss of 4.3% of initial body mass or 4.8 kg over a mean period of 7.3 months. Those engaged in the
intervention for over 9 months experienced even more pronounced weight loss. This investigation
offers evidence of the positive impact of eHealth lifestyle interventions on diabetes patients’ lifestyles
in a municipal setting.
Another pivotal study ”Predicting Dropouts From an Electronic Health Platform for Lifestyle Interven­
tions: Analysis of Methods and Predictors” (2019) [29], addresses the reasons behind patient attrition,
assesses the possibility of predicting and preventing such dropouts, and identifies high­risk patients
using predictive factors. Various statistical methods, including t­tests, simple regression, logistic re­
gression, decision trees, and random forest models, were utilized to identify potential predictors of
dropout. Results indicated that dropout could be predicted with an 89% precision using a random
forest model with 11 predictor variables. Significant predictors included the intervention provider, two
weeks of inactivity, and the number of advice received from health coaches. It was also found that en­
gagement significantly dropped before dropout and that patients were at the highest risk of dropping
out at the beginning of the intervention. However, it suggested that time series analysis of activity
patterns could provide a more robust basis for risk models.
Liva Healthcare presented also a 2­year randomized controlled trial to assess the clinical and eco­
nomic effectiveness of its LIVA eHealth lifestyle coaching program for obese individuals, with and
without type 2 diabetes, across various Danish municipalities [30]. This study, featuring a 1­year
intervention and a subsequent 1­year retention analysis, compared outcomes between participants
receiving LIVA’s program alongside standard municipal preventive measures and a control group re­
ceiving only the standard care. Weight loss, improvements in HbA1c levels, reduced use of glucose­
lowering medication, and improved quality of life were observed in both groups but more significantly
in the intervention group.
Following studies were conducted to emphasize the critical role of behavior change techniques as
foundational elements of successful health interventions, stating that solutions that integrate feed­
back and coaching, especially those delivered by health coaches, have been identified as markedly
effective [31, 32].

program, with each participant having a unique set of dates reflecting their personal journey through
the expected 9­month duration of the program, with the exception of any early dropouts. Finally, the
last data splits considers interactions after the program’s closing date for each participant.

4.5
Unsupervised Learning for Clustering
Given our first research question ”What types of user engagement exist in the LIVA program, and
how do they relate to health outcomes?”, this section aims to employ unsupervised learning to identify
different patterns of engagement among LIVA program users. Understanding these patterns is pivotal
for assessing the program’s effectiveness and its influence on health outcomes.
Preliminary exploratory analysis of the dataset revealed that the continuous features predominantly
exhibit Gaussian distributions (Appendix A), suggesting that user engagement patterns might be simi­
larly complex and varied. This observation led to the selection of the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMMs)
as the most appropriate unsupervised clustering model for our dataset. GMMs stands out for its flex­
ibility in modeling complex data distributions, making it an ideal choice for capturing the intricate en­
gagement patterns. The decision to utilize GMMs was further strengthened by its capacity to accom­
modate the diverse nature of user engagement, thereby facilitating a more granular understanding of
how engagement types relate to health outcomes [50, 51].
Here, we did not directly use the engineered features. Instead, we employed principal components
as the input data to reduce the dimensionality of our dataset.
4.5.1
Gaussian Mixture Models
For all clustering GMMs models that were trained, it was crucial first to identify the optimal number of
clusters for our analysis. Proper initialization can lead to more accurate clustering and convergence
to better local optima, whereas poor initialization might result in sub­optimal clustering solutions. To
inform this decision, we utilized hierarchical clustering and K­Means to estimate the potential cluster
count [52, 53].
For the K­Means clustering approach, we employed the elbow method to determine the optimal num­
ber of clusters. This method involves plotting the within­cluster sum of squares (WCSS) against the
number of clusters. The WCSS measures the variance within each cluster, and as the number of
clusters increases, the WCSS typically decreases because the clusters are smaller and more tightly
grouped. The ”elbow” point on the plot, where the rate of decrease sharply changes, indicates the
optimal number of clusters. On the other hand, for hierarchical clustering, we determined the optimal
number of clusters by analyzing the dendrogram, a tree­like diagram that represents the hierarchy
of clusters formed by the algorithm. Specifically, we looked for the largest distance that we could
vertically cut through the dendrogram without intersecting too many cluster links.
Thus, both techniques—K­Means with the elbow method and hierarchical clustering with the den­
drogram analysis—provided complementary insights into the data’s structure, guiding us toward an
informed decision on the optimal number of clusters to use for our GMMs clustering models.
After determining the optimal number of clusters, we initiated the GMMs by setting the centroids—
defined as the mean positions for each cluster—using a stochastic approach. The centroids, derived
from the K­Means clustering, served as the starting points for the GMMs’ means, providing a robust
foundation for the Expectation­Maximization (EM) algorithm to converge. The EM algorithm iteratively
refines the model parameters parameters—the means (µ), covariances (Σ), and mixture weights (π)—
to maximize to maximize the likelihood of the observed data.
These parameters are crucial for defining the characteristics of the GMM:
1. Means (µ): The centers of the Gaussian distributions in the mixture, indicating where each
distribution is centered.
2. Covariances (Σ): The shape and orientation of each Gaussian distribution, determined by the
covariance matrices that describe how spread out the distributions are.
3. Mixture Weights (π): The probabilities representing the mixing proportions of the Gaussian
distributions, indicating the likelihood that a data point comes from each distribution.
This process involves two main steps: the Expectation step (E­step) and the Maximization step (M­
step).

6
Discussion
This study embarked on an exploratory journey to unravel the drivers and dynamics of engagement
patterns among patients with chronic lifestyle­related diseases participating in a comprehensive 9­
month program facilitated by LIVA, adhering to the principles of the National Diabetes Prevention
Program.
Indeed, Liva Healthcare’s extensive research has already demonstrated the efficacy of their programs
in facilitating weight loss among participants [30, 31, 32]. Furthermore, they have addressed the issue
of patient dropout by developing various predictive models to identify potential dropouts early in the
program [29]. Despite these significant contributions, there remained a gap in the literature regarding
the detailed examination of user engagement over time. It was this gap that led us to frame our
research questions as stated in the introduction. It also prompted us to develop a comprehensive
data pipeline, spanning from data collection to model selection and evaluation. This pipeline was
meticulously designed to address the complexities of measuring engagement over time, enabling us
to capture and analyze the evolving nature of user interactions with the LIVA platform.
In addressing our first research question, ”What types of user engagement exist in the LIVA program,
and how do they relate to health outcomes?”, our analysis revealed significant insights into the vari­
ability of user interaction within the app both before the program officially commenced and during its
run.
Within the preliminary two­week phase, our research delineated three distinct clusters, capturing the
diversity of user engagement prior to the commencement of the program. These were: Consis­
tent Engagers, who demonstrated significant and sustained interaction, characterized by numerous
sessions and prolonged engagement, hinting at a robust enthusiasm for the program; Considered
Starters, marked by shorter, albeit more focused, app interactions and a cautious, reflective approach
to participation, evidenced by fewer registrations; and Quick Testers, distinguished by minimal ses­
sion counts and ephemeral engagement, indicative of cursory experimentation with the program’s
offerings.
As the program progressed, our analysis, informed by the extensive amount of data collected during
the actual duration of the program and organized into biweekly periods, led to the identification of three
distinguishable clusters in the first biweekly period: High­Engaged Users, demonstrating regular,
intensive interactions and proactive communications; Moderate Users, with notable but balanced
engagement, underscored by a moderate messaging activity; and Low Engagers, characterized by
subdued utilization of the program’s sessions and messaging features, suggesting either integration
challenges or varying degrees of commitment.
Furthermore, a longitudinal analysis—spanning the entire duration of the program—was conducted
to identify any potential new patterns of engagement. Interestingly, the findings revealed no emer­
gence of new engagement types; instead, the clusters identified within the initial two weeks persisted
throughout the program. This outcome not only answers our second research question, ”How does
engagement change over the 9­month National Diabetes Prevention Program?”, but also emphasizes
the enduring nature of initial engagement patterns.
This analysis was further enriched by the creation of Sankey diagrams (Figure 5.12), which allowed
us to visualize the transition of users among different engagement groups. Although individuals clas­
sified as Consistent Engagers prior to the program demonstrated only a slightly reduced probability of
later becoming Low Engagers compared to Quick Testers and Considered Starters, our observations
underscore a critical insight: user behavior before starting the program does not significantly affect
user engagement during the program.
From this analysis, we were able to compute probabilities for switching groups and completing the
program. We observed that High and Moderate Engagers were more likely to finish the program,
while any transition to Low Engagement significantly increased the probability of quitting.

Interestingly, Moderate Users emerged as the most stable in terms of maintaining their engagement
levels, indicating a consistent interaction with the program’s components. In contrast, High­Engagers
showed a greater propensity for changing their engagement levels, suggesting that their initially in­
tense interaction might not always sustain over the long term. Crucially, being categorized as a Low
Engager three times notably increased the probability of dropping out of the program. This pattern
highlights a critical threshold effect, where prolonged periods of low engagement correlate strongly
with disengagement from the program objectives and ultimate withdrawal. This finding points to the
importance of early and continuous intervention strategies aimed at re­engaging those at risk of falling
into or remaining within the Low Engagement category.
Thus, given the distinct engagement clusters identified within the LIVA program, it becomes impera­
tive to devise targeted intervention strategies that cater to the unique needs and behaviors of each
user type. Considering user profiles before starting the program, for Consistent Engagers, strategies
might focus on further enhancing their experience and sustaining their high level of motivation through
advanced personalized content and more in­depth health tracking features. For Considered Starters,
interventions could aim to gradually increase engagement through motivational messaging, empha­
sizing the program’s benefits and milestones. Meanwhile, Quick Testers present a critical challenge
but also an opportunity for significant impact during the synchronous live session with the coach be­
fore starting the program. Strategies for these groups could involve personalized outreach efforts to
understand their barriers to engagement, coupled with customized engagement plans that offer flex­
ible, less time­intensive interactions to build their interest and commitment gradually. In light of the
engagement clusters identified during the LIVA program—High­Engaged Users, Moderate Users, and
Low Engagers—our findings suggest interventions tailored to each group’s specific needs. For High­
Engaged Users, maintaining their interest and leveraging their active participation could involve intro­
ducing more challenging health goals or advanced modules to deepen their commitment and provide
new learning opportunities. Moderate Users, exhibiting a balanced approach to engagement, might
benefit from personalized feedback and encouragement. The strategy for Low Engagers, however,
might require a more foundational approach aimed at rekindling their interest and motivation. This
foundational approach for Low Engagers could include arranging an additional synchronous call with
their coach, specifically designed to explore and address the barriers to engagement or challenges
they face in changing their behaviors. This call could be an opportunity to reassess and realign the
program’s objectives and expectations with the participant’s current situation, ensuring that goals are
both realistic and achievable.
Expanding upon these findings, our exploration into the first research question reveals the substan­
tial impact of the level of user engagement on health­related outcomes within the program. The ob­
served correlation between the percentage of weight loss and engagement levels among participants
at the program’s end underscores a significant finding: there is a direct influence of user engagement
on health outcomes, evidenced by a positive linear relationship between engagement intensity and
weight loss achievement (Figure 5.18). This statistically significant correlation strengthens the argu­
ment that consistent and active involvement in the program’s activities significantly correlates with
notable health improvements, especially in terms of weight loss. While Liva Healthcare has exten­
sively studied the effectiveness of their program on weight loss, the dimension of the level of user
engagement as a determinant of health outcomes has not been explored in their research until now.
Our investigation fills this critical gap by highlighting the role of engagement level in the program’s
success. The positive linear relationship between engagement levels and weight loss provides new
insights into how user interaction with the program can directly influence health results.
Our findings align with research conducted outside of Liva, such as the study by Morgan et al.(2022)
published in Digital Health [38]. This study contributes to the broader discourse on the importance of
user engagement in digital health interventions, emphasizing the need for a nuanced understanding
of how different patterns of engagement over time can impact health outcomes.
Addressing our third and final research question, ”Can early engagement patterns predict program
dropout risks?”, we utilized Random Forest models, enhanced through double cross­validation for
both model selection and validation. The process of hyperparameter tuning and feature selection
played a pivotal role in augmenting model performance, leading to significant findings regarding the
predictability of program dropout.

providing insights into health progress; and the total amount of manual registrations, further empha­
sizing proactive health management. These identified features underscore the importance of active
and consistent user engagement and self­monitoring in predicting program adherence. The promi­
nence of such features suggests that fostering an environment that encourages regular interaction
and personal investment in health management can be key in reducing dropout rates.
In relating our findings to broader discussions on digital health interventions and behavior change,
the analysis of self­monitoring behaviors and their predictive value in program adherence resonates
strongly with existing literature. Specifically, the study by Painter et al.(2020) provides valuable con­

loss, underscoring the effectiveness of self­monitoring as a mechanism to drive health­related behav­
ior changes. This aligns with the features we identified as crucial in predicting program dropout, which
collectively highlight the role of active engagement and self­monitoring.
This connection further corroborates the theoretical framework presented in Section 3.2.1 of our
background, the Transtheoretical Model of Behavior Change. According to this model, individuals
progress through various stages of readiness to change, with self­motivation playing a key role in
moving towards and maintaining healthier behaviors. Our findings suggest that digital health pro­
grams that facilitate and encourage self­monitoring can significantly impact users’ ability to progress
through these stages. By actively engaging with the program’s features users demonstrate a level
of self­motivation and commitment that is crucial for behavioral change and, ultimately, for achieving
sustainable health outcomes. Moreover, the emphasis on self­monitoring highlights its importance
not just for weight loss but as a fundamental component of behavior change that can prevent relapse
into unhealthy behaviors.
Building on the established relationship between self­monitoring and the Transtheoretical Model of
Behavior Change, it’s essential to recognize that engagement within digital health programs extends
beyond mere behavioral aspects. Our current investigation has predominantly focused on behav­
ioral engagement, assessing how users interact with the program’s features and their commitment
to self­monitoring activities. However, engagement is a multifaceted concept that also encompasses
emotional and cognitive dimensions (Section 2.2), which are critical to the success of eHealth pro­
grams. The omission of these dimensions from our analysis represents a limitation and an area for
future exploration.

6.1. Limitations and Future Work
6.1
Limitations and Future Work
This study, while providing significant insights into engagement patterns within the LIVA program, is
subject to certain limitations that warrant consideration together with future research.
One of the primary methodological constraints arises from our choice of the GMMs for clustering. The
decision to employ GMMs was predicated on its probabilistic nature, offering a flexible approach to
modeling data that can accommodate the presence of sub­populations within our dataset. However,
a foundational assumption of the GMMs is the expectation that the data it models are drawn from
Gaussian distributions. We employed a log transformation aimed at normalizing data that exhibited
right­skewness. Despite this, the Shapiro­Wilk test yielded some p­values below the threshold of
0.05, indicating that the assumption of normality could not be conclusively accepted for all of the
features. Although the transformed data more closely approximated a normal distribution, it was not
perfect.
Another significant limitation pertains to the scope of our investigation. The study was conducted
within the context of a single program facilitated by LIVA, which may constrain the generalizability of
our findings. Although our group of participants in the program of study was quite large, engagement
patterns identified and analyzed in this setting might not be directly applicable to other populations
or digital health programs without further validation. This limitation is further compounded by the
high dropout rate observed in our participant dataset, which could influence the engagement patterns
identified. Future work could explore the application of GMMs to other programs facilitated by LIVA
to validate if the engagement behaviors observed can be generalized across different settings.
Additionally, our analysis did not account for the influence of external factors such as barriers to en­
gagement, motivational factors, or the efficacy of user­centric design in fostering engagement (Sec­
tion 2.2). While LIVA includes elements of motivation by incorporating health goals, assuming a
design focused on user engagement, we did not explore if there were particular obstacles that par­
ticipants encountered which may have impacted their engagement. Future studies should consider
these external factors by incorporating qualitative research methods, such as interviews or surveys,
to capture the nuanced barriers and motivators affecting user engagement. Understanding these
elements could lead to better strategies to enhance user interaction with digital health programs.
For the predictive analysis utilizing Random Forest models, future work could explore alternative pre­
dictive models, such as neural networks, to potentially improve the accuracy of dropout predictions.
While their potential superior performance, it is important to acknowledge their ”black box” nature,
which could limit interpretability and explainability. Thus, subsequent research could seek to bal­
ance the trade­off between model performance and transparency. Furthermore, future investigations
should also consider exploring methods that more effectively incorporate past features, aiming to en­
hance the model’s ability to leverage historical data for improved prediction accuracy. Such methods
could include time series analysis or advanced feature engineering techniques designed to capture
temporal dynamics more accurately.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this master thesis contributes to the understanding of the drivers and dynamics of en­
gagement patterns over time among participants with chronic lifestyle­related diseases in a health
program by LIVA. It reveals novel insights into how user interactions unfold and influence health out­
comes. Our findings significantly enhance the digital health literature by identifying distinct engage­
ment patterns, understanding their persistence over time, and linking engagement levels to tangible
health improvements, particularly in terms of weight loss.
Through a meticulous data pipeline and the use of GMMs, we identified three primary user engage­
ment clusters: High­Engagers, Moderate Users, and Low­Engaged Users. These clusters demon­
strated remarkable consistency across the 9­month program, underscoring the enduring nature of
initial engagement behaviors. Additionally, we discerned three distinct user profiles before the pro­
gram’s commencement, which intriguingly showed no significant direct influence on the engagement
profile during the program. This revelation adds a new dimension to our understanding of engagement
in digital health programs.
While numerous studies have validated the effectiveness of eHealth programs, our research fills a
critical gap by linking the effectiveness of these programs to user engagement and subsequent health
outcomes. This aspect of our study underscores the importance of engagement as a key determinant
of program success, particularly in terms of achieving significant weight loss. As participants’ engage­
ment with the LIVA program increases, so does the effectiveness of their weight loss outcomes.
The predictive modeling aspect of our research, utilizing Random Forest models, demonstrated the
feasibility of early engagement patterns to forecast program dropout risks with considerable accuracy
of 80% across all periods of the program. This finding highlights the potential of predictive analytics
in identifying at­risk participants early in the program, thereby enabling targeted interventions.
A pivotal conclusion of our study is the identification of self­monitoring behaviors as crucial predictors
of program adherence. This finding accentuates the importance of individual motivation and the
active role of self­monitoring in sustaining engagement and minimizing dropout rates. Our research
suggests that digital health programs that encourage and facilitate self­monitoring can significantly
strengthen user engagement and, by extension, enhance health outcomes.

This appendix provides a comprehensive visualization of some of the feature distributions within our

normality of feature distributions and the effectiveness of transformation techniques.

the program, as examples. The initial histogram shows the raw distribution of each selected feature,
providing a visual account of their inherent skewness. Subsequently, log transformations were em­
ployed to address right­skewed distributions, an approach to align them more closely with a normal
distribution.
To complement these visualizations, we utilized Quantile­Quantile (Q­Q) plots. By comparing the
quantiles of our feature distributions to those of a standard normal distribution—both pre­ and post­
transformation—we could visually assessed how closely the data follow a normal distribution.
Moreover, the Shapiro­Wilk test was applied to quantify the departure from normality, with results dis­
played alongside the corresponding plots. Post­log transformation, it is noteworthy that the features
were standardized, and there was a marked improvement in the Shapiro­Wilk test values. Although
the p­value remains below the 0.05 threshold—implying that we cannot conclusively state the data
are from a normally distributed population—the data now exhibit a distribution that is more closely
approximating normality. This was a way to further validate the effect of the log transformations.
