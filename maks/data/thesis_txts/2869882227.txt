Abstract
The increasing demand for renewable energy, coupled with ambitious targets for offshore
wind energy, highlights the need for innovative strategies to optimize wind farm planning and
decision-making. This research investigates the application of real options analysis (ROA) to
optimize overplanting strategies in the development of offshore wind farms under uncertainty.
Utilizing a case study of the North Sea I A2 lease area in the Danish offshore wind tender,
this study assesses the economic and operational trade-offs of overplanting in the context of
uncertain grid interconnection expansion risks.
A structured methodological framework is applied, which integrates real options thinking with
optimization modeling to assess the feasibility and economic impacts of various overplanting
strategies. The analysis includes several scenarios, such as (1) a base case with a fixed grid
capacity of 1 GW and an evaluation of overplanting, and (2) scenarios that consider a future
grid interconnection expansion. The study utilizes decision trees and Monte Carlo simulations
to capture probabilistic uncertainties and their influence on optimal investment strategies.
The findings indicate that while overplanting can increase gross annual energy production, it
also leads to higher curtailment losses under fixed grid capacity constraints, thereby diminishing
its economic viability. In scenarios involving uncertain grid interconnection expansion, the
study demonstrates that a phased investment strategy — delaying turbine procurement until
the grid capacity is expanded — consistently leads to lower levelized cost of electricity (LCOE)
compared to an immediate overplanting approach. Additionally, the study suggests that flexible
procurement strategies, which capitalize on economies of scale and technological advancements,
serve as a risk mitigation mechanism for developers confronted with uncertain infrastructure
timelines.
This thesis contributes to bridging the gap between theoretical real-options applications and
practical decision-making in offshore wind energy projects. The insights derived from this study
provide valuable guidance for policymakers and developers in designing adaptive investment
strategies that enhance financial resilience and operational efficiency in offshore wind tenders.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In the face of escalating climate extremes and the energy crisis, one path to a solution is
clear: accelerating the transition to renewable energy is essential to secure Europe’s energy
independence and tackle global warming.
Greenhouse gas emissions from human activities have been identified as the main cause of global
warming. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) highlighted in its latest
report, that global greenhouse gas emissions have continued to increase in the period 2010-2019,
mainly from sectors such as fossil fuel use, agriculture, and consumption and production habits.
In all parts of the world, this man-made climate change is already significantly impacting many
weather and climate phenomena [48]. At the same time, Europe faced an energy crisis in 2021
and 2022 due to its dependence on fossil fuel imports for its energy supply. To reduce this
dependence and meet its climate change targets, Europe has invested heavily in renewable
energy development - a structural transformation of the energy system [49].
And it is not just Europe’s energy system that is changing - this fundamental shift from fossil
fuels to renewables, driven by the urgent need to tackle climate change, is happening worldwide.
In this transition, offshore wind energy has become a cornerstone, offering huge potential for
scalable and sustainable power generation. In 2023, 10.8 GW of new offshore wind capacity
was added globally, an increase of 24 % compared to the prior year. At the same time, Europe
experienced a record year and significantly contributed to the global expansion with 3.8 GW
from 11 newly constructed wind farms in seven different markets [58].
Despite this upwind, the wind industry faces growing challenges impacting its growth and
development. Escalating raw material costs, price inflation, rising interest rates, and ongoing
supply chain issues have had a significant impact on the profitability of offshore wind developers.
According to McKinsey’s recent analysis, the project costs of many developers have risen
significantly since the start of the decade, with the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) increasing
from 40 % to 60 % compared to 2020 [93]. This highlights the urgent need to develop innovative
strategies and optimize planned wind farms to maximize the use of offshore wind farm capacity
and to ensure economically viable projects in the face of increasing costs and increasing LCOE.
These challenges are highly relevant to Denmark and its efforts to achieve its own climate
goals. In 2020, Denmark published the Climate Act (Danish: Lov om klima), which should
lead the country to fulfill the Paris Agreement. Denmark set itself the goal of a greenhouse
gas emission reduction of 70 % by 2030 compared to 1990 as well as to have a climate-neutral
society by 2050 [24]. As one of the global frontrunners in wind energy, a pivotal part of this
transition is offshore wind energy. In order to achieve these goals, the Danish government

launched an offshore wind energy tender for 6 GW of offshore wind capacity in April 2024,
marking the biggest tender in the history of the country. The tender design further allowed for
so-called overplanting design, which enables successful bidders to develop projects exceeding
the minimum capacity of each site [3].
The concept of overplanting has emerged as a promising approach for optimizing the design of
a wind farm and its performance by optimizing the capacity between the installed generation
and the transmission system. This strategy has the potential to increase energy production
during periods of low wind speeds; however, it also introduces complexities related to energy
curtailment and economic optimization [98].
This thesis aims to address these complexities through the lens of real-options analysis (ROA).
By incorporating different uncertainties, this study evaluates how overplanting strategies can be
optimized to minimize the LCOE. This research adopts the Danish North Sea tender mentioned
before as a case study. The motivation for this research lies in its potential to contribute
to the growing research on offshore wind optimization, providing insights for developers and
policymakers. As the global demand for renewable energy continues to grow, the findings of
this thesis can serve as a guide for future offshore wind projects and design decisions under
uncertainty - for future successful projects in Denmark and around the world.
1.2
Research Questions and Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the application of ROA to optimize overplanting
strategies in the development of offshore wind farms under uncertainty. The primary focus of
the research lies in minimizing the LCOE while taking into account significant challenges in

2.1
Overplanting in Offshore Wind Farms
The concept of overplanting in wind farms describes the strategy of installing more wind turbine
generation capacity than the transmission system can handle, also called capacity optimization.
The main incentive behind this approach is to improve the performance and profitability of
the wind farm itself by maximizing energy production during low-wind periods. Despite some
energy curtailment during high wind periods, overplanting can lead to more efficient utilization
of the transmission system infrastructure and, ultimately, to higher annual energy production
(AEP) for the wind farm system [98].
Figure 2.2 illustrates the concept of overplanting and was presented by Wolter et al. (2020)
as part of their study on overplanting under different regulatory schemes [98]. The figure
shows the influence of the increased generation capacity on the overall wind farm power curve.
In addition to the total power curve of the wind farm, the graph also includes the export
capacity of the transmission system and an exemplary representation of the probability of wind
speeds in the form of a Weibull curve. It can be seen that the additional installed capacity

the potential unavailability of individual wind turbines, which represents the steeper power
curve below the export capacity of the transmission system (dark red). At the same time, the
maximum deliverable power is capped so that energy must be curtailed in times of strong winds
and high turbine availability. Furthermore, the Weibull distribution illustrates that the wind
climate generally has an average wind speed that is below the nominal power wind speed of
today’s offshore wind turbines. This means that additional power can be generated during a
considerable part of the operating time. Consequently, the available transmission capacity can
be used more effectively, while only a limited amount of generation is curtailed throughout the

Moreover, Wolter et al. (2020) highlighted in their study the significant challenges and trade-offs

overplanting strategy by offering flexible subsidy structures, whereas the previous Danish tender
design discouraged these strategies due to state subsidies, which were bounded to certain energy
outputs. In addition, they found that overplanting itself becomes more attractive to a developer
when fixed turbine costs are reduced compared to transmission costs. This is, for example, the
case for project areas that are far offshore and therefore require an increased investment in the
transmission system. In general, the study highlights the potential of overplanting as a viable
design concept to optimize the utilization of available transmission infrastructure. However,
it also points out that the economic incentives of this strategy are significantly dependent on
different regulatory schemes [98].
However, the role of overplanting in reducing the LCOE for wind power generation has not
yet been fully explored. This is primarily due to the fact that the ideal balance between the
installed generation capacity and the transmission capacity is mainly determined by the initial

a significant portion of the total costs, the motivation to overplant is reduced. For instance,
early offshore wind projects, which were located nearshore and had relatively low transmission
costs, demonstrated limited consideration of overplanting in their initial stages [98]. Therefore,
several studies have examined the relationship between overplanting and other factors, including
dynamic thermal rating of the cable infrastructure and energy storage ([33],[18]), and seabed

Faye-Bédrin et al. (2023) study focuses on an overplanted offshore wind farm and the opti-
mization of the energy management strategy. By integrating dynamic thermal rating of the
cable infrastructure and energy storage, they managed to improve the profitability of the plant
and reduce curtailment. The researchers used stochastic dynamic programming to manage the
complex interactions between power production, thermal constraints, and storage capacity of
the system. The findings show that combining overplanting with dynamic thermal cable rating
significantly increases the power export by utilizing cable capacity beyond static limits, while
stochastic dynamic programming outperforms heuristic methods in minimizing the operational
costs of the plant. They proved that this approach maximizes the utilization of the existing
infrastructure and supports more efficient renewable energy generation [33]. Adding to this,
Daminov et al. (2023) demonstrate that combining overplanting with a dynamic thermal rating
can boost offshore wind farm revenue by up to 204 % and reduce LCOE by €1/MWh. However,
the profitability highly depends on the market conditions, with significant increases under high
electricity prices [18].
Another study by Fernández et al. (2023) evaluates the profitability of overplanting with a
focus on wind farms in Spain specifically. The researchers analyzed how a generation capacity
exceeding the transmission system capacity impacts the overall energy output and economic
returns of the wind farm. They have demonstrated that overplanting can enhance the AEP by
approximately 3.87 GWh per additional turbine by simulating the performance using PyWake.
This is despite the potential curtailment of 1.69 GWh due to overloading of the transmission grid.
Further, the analysis reveals that overplanting becomes economically viable when electricity
prices exceed a threshold of €70/MWh, highlighting the potential of this strategy to improve
profitability by maximizing the utilization of the transmission infrastructure under certain
market conditions. Nevertheless, the study points out the need to balance curtailment losses
with the electricity market and suggests further research on the integration of energy storage
and grid optimization to improve the overall efficiency [69].
In a further study done by McInerney and Bunn (2017), it was analyzed how the economic via-
bility of overplanting changes under different support schemes in the British market. According
to their findings, an overplanting of up to 8 % of the maximum capacity under fixed feed-in
tariffs (FITs) increases the project’s Net Present Value (NPV) by 2.3 %. Even greater financial
results are to be expected under market-based support schemes due to reduced opportunity
costs during high wind conditions. The research further highlights the profitability of increased
generation capacity especially at low wind speeds combined with premium-to-price subsidies
and an improved utilization of the existing transmission grid system. At high wind speeds the
overall profitability decreases due to the curtailment risk [61].

2.2
Levelized Cost of Energy
The concept of Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) is a metric used to describe the average
cost per unit of energy produced for a given system, such as a power plant, over its lifetime.
This metric is particularly useful for comparing the economics of different power generation
methods and different projects within the same energy source (e.g. offshore wind). Today, this
metric is commonly used to compare the cost of generating electricity from renewable energy
technologies with conventional technologies. To make this comparison, the standard LCOE
measure incorporates various factors such as generation volume, investment costs, potential
fuel costs, operation and maintenance (O&M) costs, and the life of the plant or project into a
single cost per unit of electricity [57],[53].
Following the definition of LCOE as a concept, this chapter will first provide an overview
of recent developments in LCOE across various energy sources, highlighting trends in cost
competitiveness. Subsequently, it will focus on the methodological framework for calculating
LCOE and its application in the planning and development of offshore wind farms. A recent
overview of the different LCOE values for each energy source for 2024 in Germany is shown in
Figure 2.3.

2.2.1

Following the discussion of recent international developments in LCOE for various energy sources,
the focus now shifts to the methodological foundations of LCOE. This segment undertakes a
comprehensive evaluation of the current methodologies employed in LCOE calculation while
offering a detailed analysis of the underlying parameters and assumptions. Furthermore, it will
highlight the various applications of LCOE in the literature, with a particular focus on its use
in the assessment and decision-making process for offshore wind projects.
The mathematical formulation of LCOE varies based on the stakeholder (e.g. researchers,
policymakers, project developers) and the specific application focus. For project developers,

case, electricity. With this definition in mind, the simplified form of LCOE is calculated by

2.2.2
LCOE Studies with focus on Wind Energy
Given the relative ease with which the LCOE metric can be applied, the metric has gained
widespread use among researchers, practitioners, policymakers, and other interested parties.
As the initial Google Scholar search demonstrated, a substantial number of publications are
available that focus on LCOE. A simple search for "Levelized Cost of Energy" yielded 32,800
results, of which approximately 83% were related to wind energy (search operator: "Levelized
Cost of Energy" AND "Wind") (as of 09/01/2025). A comprehensive evaluation of this extensive
collection of publications is beyond the scope of this paper.
Consequently, the following
examples are provided to illustrate select areas of application. As previously described, the
LCOE metric is primarily used to compare the cost efficiency between different projects and
energy generation technologies to help stakeholders determine the most economically attractive
option for energy generation. However, the question now arises as to whether this is the only
area of application for this metric, or whether LCOE can also be considered for other areas
of application. A study by de Simon-Martin et al. (2022) explored the economic efficiency
of various microgrid scenarios, utilizing the LCOE metric to analyze the results. The study
concluded that integrated microgrids generally have lower LCOE compared to standalone
technologies, a result primarily attributed to economies of scale [84].
Mehta et al. (2024) conducted a study with a focus on the optimization of wind turbine size
for offshore wind farms. LCOE was used to identify the most important factors for technical
optimization. The LCOE metric was integrated into a multidisciplinary optimization framework
to minimize the cost of the wind turbine design. The study’s findings indicated that the optimal
turbine size for the North Sea, the area under consideration, is 16 MW with a rotor diameter
of 236m [62].
Apart from its implementation in the context of wind energy and electricity generation, LCOE
variants find application in energy storage systems (Levelized Cost of Energy Storage), the
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Optimization Under Uncertainty
12
assessment of the economic viability of hydrogen production (Levelized Cost of Hydrogen), and
carbon capture technologies (Levelized Cost of Carbon), amongst others. These metrics are
crucial for evaluating the overall costs of these systems, which is essential for the successful
integration of renewable energies into the existing grid infrastructure. These metrics have
thus evolved into a cross-industry metric, supporting a wide range of stakeholders in making
strategic decisions by offering standardized comparisons. Consequently, they serve not only to
evaluate the cost efficiency of energy systems but also of newly emerging sectors [36].
Although LCOE is widely used to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of different energy projects, the
metric also has several limitations in capturing real-world economic factors due to its simplicity.
The mathematical formulation of LCOE does not take into account factors such as flexibility
in terms of price and grid availability, seasonal fluctuations, or the market value of electricity,
which can influence the economic performance of the various generation technologies [53],
[30]. Furthermore, the simplicity of this metric can sometimes ignore regional socioeconomic
factors, project-specific risks such as policy changes and financial considerations, and potential
supply chain disruptions, which could lead to uncertainties in its application [10]. Furthermore,
differences in discount rates and tax incentives across different regions and countries can lead
to inconsistent LCOE comparisons [30], [5]. Whilst the present study is principally focused on
the LCOE metric, it is noteworthy that other studies have proposed new metrics to address
the shortcomings of LCOE. There would be the Value-Adjusted LCOE, or Levelized Avoided
Cost of Energy and Cost of Valued Energy (COVE), to name a few [83]. The concept of COVE
in particular is worth investigating further, as it incorporates both, the costs and revenue of
energy projects. This concept was first proposed by a study from Dykes (2020) from DTU and
considers the value of the produced energy in the market. This includes factors such as the
timing of production, price fluctuations, and grid demand. By capturing these in the metric,
COVE can be used to design and optimize energy projects for their profitability and not just
for their cost-efficiency. The study concludes that future energy project designs should include
both, LCOE and value-based objectives such as COVE to ensure long-term competitiveness in
the energy market [29].
In summary, LCOE serves as a critical metric for assessing the economic viability of diverse
energy production technologies, thereby offering a standardized measure for comparing costs
across projects and energy sources. Recent developments reveal a marked improvement in the
competitiveness of renewable energies, which are increasingly able to contend with conventional
energy production in terms of cost, and in some cases, prove more economically attractive. While
LCOE provides valuable insights, it is important to acknowledge its limitations, including its lack
of consideration of market dynamics, price volatility, and grid flexibility. This underscores the
necessity for complementary metrics. Approaches such as the COVE address these limitations by
incorporating revenue generation and market conditions, thus providing a more comprehensive
perspective on project profitability. While challenges persist, LCOE remains a widely used
tool in academia and industry to support investment decisions, strategy development, and the
optimization of offshore wind projects.
2.3
Optimization Under Uncertainty
The development of offshore wind farms involves significant uncertainties, ranging from wind
resource variability and market price fluctuations to supply chain risks and regulatory changes.
==PAGE:24==
2.3 Optimization Under Uncertainty
13
Traditional deterministic optimization approaches may fail to capture these complexities, and
therefore potentially lead to suboptimal investment decisions. The concept of optimization
under uncertainty (OUU) offers a framework, that includes probabilistic elements into the
decision-making process, thus enabling developers to assess risk-adjusted strategies and im-
prove the economic resilience of their offshore wind projects. This section explores existing
methodologies in OUU within the context of offshore wind energy, aiming to highlight their
relevance for overplanting strategies, LCOE minimization, and investment flexibility under
uncertain dynamics.
First, it is important to clarify the definition of uncertainty. According to Clifton et al. (2016),
uncertainty can be defined as a "measure of the random fluctuations of a repeated measurement,
or the variability of the difference between predictions and observations of a process around the
mean of those measurements." In this context, it is essential to distinguish uncertainty from
measurement bias. The latter refers to the difference between an average of many measurements
and the actual value, and it can be eliminated by calibration or adjustments to the process [13].
In the context of wind energy, uncertainty arises due to several different factors, one being
the long- and short-term variations in the wind resource (e.g. wind characteristics, wind
speed and direction, or extreme weather) which have a direct impact on both the energy
output and infrastructure costs [4, 64]. Given this context, classical deterministic optimization
methods reach their limits in that they do not take into account the different variants of
uncertainties. Consequently, using these methods to solve an optimization problem can lead to
suboptimal or unreliable solutions. These optimization problems are called optimization under
uncertainty, and various models have been developed to address the weaknesses of deterministic
models by incorporating the natural uncertainties of these problems. These include, among
others, stochastic optimization robust optimization, reliability-based optimization (RBO), and
distributionally robust optimization (DRO) [76, 77, 64, 59].
2.3.1
Theoretical Approaches to OUU
The following section outlines selected fundamental methodologies within OUU and computa-
tional techniques to solve these, before providing examples of how these approaches can be used
to analyze wind energy systems. In particular, stochastic programming, robust optimization,
and probabilistic modeling techniques have been widely applied in the energy sector to account
for all kinds of different uncertainties.

Three of the most significant approaches are two-stage stochastic programming, chance-

decisions are divided into a first stage, which occurs before the realization of uncertainty, and a

sets a production plan for its product before the actual demand is known, and then adjusts the
stock levels of the product to the actual demand. In contrast, chance-constrained optimization
prioritizes the feasibility of constraints with high probability. This approach finds application
in critical areas such as the reliability of the power grid and the management of supply chains
in companies. Lastly, expectation-based models are utilized to minimize the expected loss
function over possible uncertainty realizations. These models are most commonly employed
in the financial sector and in risk mitigation for investment portfolios under changing market
conditions [77].

ν →∞. Another solution method is provided by so-called decomposition techniques, which
primarily solve large stochastic optimization problems iteratively by breaking them down into
smaller sub-problems [77].
Stochastic optimization is a widely utilized concept in various fields, including finance, logistics,
and energy systems. [77]. For instance, Hua et al. (2024) employed scenario-based stochastic
programming to optimize the component design and operation plan of integrated energy systems,
taking into account solar and wind variability. The study used Monte Carlo sampling and
scenario clustering to generate realistic uncertainty scenarios, and by developing a multi-stage
optimization framework, both the optimal system sizing and the optimal operational planning
could be determined robustly and efficiently [44].
A study by Quick et al. (2023) presented the significance and implementation of a method
known as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in the context of wind energy. This study applied
the method to optimize the layout of wind farms, thereby demonstrating an improvement in
the calculation efficiency in comparison to other deterministic methods. The gradient of the
AEP of the wind farm was estimated using a Monte Carlo simulation, which made it possible to
consider a substantial amount of atmospheric conditions as part of the optimization. The study
demonstrated that this method frequently yielded higher AEP solutions in a notably shorter

span of time compared to deterministic approaches, particularly as the number of wind turbines
increased. The findings suggest that dynamic optimization of wind turbine placements while
accounting for uncertainties through the use of SGD, can enhance the efficiency of planning
wind farms [74].
Two further examples of the application of stochastic programming in the context of OUU and
with the focus on wind energy are provided by Quick et al. (2020) and Quick et al. (2017).
These two studies focus on the optimization of wake control strategies in individual wind
farms, taking into account various uncertainties (e.g., wind direction and speed, the intensity of
turbulence, or the different yaw positions of the individual wind turbines). The 2020 study
implemented a polynomial chaos expansion model to predict wake behavior under uncertainty,
revealing that wind direction is the predominant factor influencing the wake deflection. This
approach resulted in a reduction of extreme yaw offsets of the turbines, which led to an energy
production improvement of 0.48% for the wind farm when compared to deterministic methods
[73]. The second study, published in 2017, incorporated OUU to address the uncertainties
in the yaw alignment of wind turbines at a specific wind farm. The findings indicated that,
depending on the wind direction, this can result in an increase in energy production of up to
1.4%. Furthermore, the study showed that the average AEP could be enhanced by 0.2%, while
also reducing extreme yaw events [72].
Robust optimization
is defined as a deterministic approach to decision-making under uncer-
tainty. In contrast to stochastic programming, these models assume that uncertain parameters
belong to a limited uncertainty set and, under this assumption, search for solutions that remain
feasible even under the worst-case uncertainty scenarios. Further, constraints of an optimization
problem are reformulated in such a way that they apply under all possible realizations of
uncertainties. This method thus ensures that the solutions remain reliable and robust. Within
robust optimization, a distinction is made between different uncertainty sets (e.g., interval-based,
polyhedral, and norm-based definitions). These uncertainty sets differ primarily in their degree
of conservatism and comprehensibility. A notable disadvantage of this method is its strong
focus on achieving feasibility, which can result in excessively risk-averse solutions. This concern
can be mitigated by adjusting the robustness level. It is employed in contexts where the
consideration of uncertainties related to demand, costs, and resource availability is crucial for
making long-term strategic decisions, which include, but are not limited to, facility location
decisions, management of supply chains, and the energy system [38].
Mittal & Mitra’s (2019) study demonstrated the potential application of robust optimization
with a focus on wind energy. The researchers used the robust optimization method to design
different wind farm layouts that remain effective even with long-term wind uncertainties, which
they defined as the speed and direction of the wind. To achieve a balance between maximizing
wind farm performance and minimizing the costs, they developed a two-stage optimization
framework. The approach taken by the researchers ensured that the method for determining
the optimal solutions remains feasible across a range of wind scenarios. The study’s findings
indicated that robustly optimized layouts not only guarantee an increased minimum output
but also mitigate the risk of overestimating energy production [64].
Reliability-based optimization (RBO)
can be defined as an advanced optimization approach
that includes uncertainty in system performance and ensures that solutions meet a predefined

level. It can be distinguished from traditional deterministic optimization, which can be risky
at times, by its integration of probabilistic constraints on errors in the optimization process.
RBO is typically solved using first- and second-order reliability methods. However, the use of
additional strategies, such as double-loop, single-loop, and uni-level methods, has also been
demonstrated to improve the efficiency of the calculation and analysis process. The primary
application of this method is in civil engineering and aerospace, two fields in which uncertainties
in loads, material characteristics, and environmental factors can have a substantial effect on
the reliability of the solution [59].
Nonetheless, the method can also be applied in the context of renewable energy systems. In
particular, RBO has been shown to play a crucial role in strengthening the resilience and cost-
effectiveness of wind energy systems. By incorporating the assessment of failure probabilities
into the optimization process, RBO offers a distinct advantage over traditional approaches. A
study by Clark et al. (2021) highlights the potential of reliability-based layout optimization
and its capacity to mitigate the impact of wake effects on turbine failure rates. As a result, this
approach has the potential to reduce operating and maintenance costs while maintaining the
same energy output [12]. Another example of the application of RBO modeled in the offshore
wind sector is the study by Pérez-Rúa et al. (2021). In this study, the researchers applied
the RBO method to analyze offshore wind farm collection systems, and they optimized the
configuration of the cable design by using stochastic mixed-integer linear programming models
[70]. These studies demonstrate that optimization strategies based on reliability information
can improve efficiency, minimize maintenance costs, and enhance the operational sustainability
of wind farms.
Distributionally robust optimization (DRO)
is an optimization framework that extends the
robust optimization from before by including ambiguity sets of probability distributions instead
of assuming fixed uncertainty sets. An ambiguity set describes a set of possible probability
distributions and there are different approaches to achieve this (e.g. moment-based sets or
discrepancy-based sets). This methodology is designed to minimize worst-case expected costs
across a range of realistic distributions, thereby ensuring the robustness of the decision-making
process in the face of distributional uncertainty. In contrast to traditional robust optimization
methods, DRO avoids overly conservative outcomes while providing out-of-sample guarantees,
making it a highly applicable approach in different fields (e.g. wind energy systems), where
uncertainties can have a substantial impact on decision-making processes [76].
A study by Agra & Cerveira (2023) used this method to account for the uncertainty in wind
direction and speed during the design optimization of the wind farm cable network. These
uncertainties are especially essential in the evaluation of energy losses within the wind farm.
Under the assumption that the precise probability distribution is not known but can be
regarded as an ambiguity set, a two-stage mixed-integer model was proposed in this study and
subsequently solved through a decomposition algorithm [4].
Huang et al. (2025) and Xiong & Singh (2017) present two further studies on the application
possibilities of DRO to improve the reliability and efficiency of energy systems under uncertainty.
The former developed an opportunity-constrained optimal power flow model that accounts
for dependent wind energy uncertainty using ambiguity sets, which was able to significantly
reduce conservativeness and computational complexity by using decomposition [45]. Xiong &

2.4
Real-Options Analysis in Wind Energy
Investment decisions under uncertainty have been a long-standing subject of research, particu-
larly in the areas of finance and management. Some of conventional valuation methods, such as
NPV, have been found to lack the required flexibility to address the needs of decision-makers in
the presence of uncertainty. It is in this context that real options analysis (ROA) has emerged
as a crucial framework, extending the principles of traditional option theory from the financial
sector to the evaluation of real-world assets. By offering a dynamic approach to investment
decision-making, ROA provides a valuable tool for assessing a wide range of investment options
involving irreversible and uncertain elements [27]. The fundamental principles of this method
and its application in the area of renewable energies will be discussed in greater detail in the
following section.
In 1973, Black and Scholes published a significant paper on options in finance. In this paper, the
two scientists developed a model for pricing financial options based on arbitrage-free principles
[9]. Building upon this model, Cox, Ross & Rubinstein (1979) expanded it with a binomial
model for option pricing, thereby simplifying the calculation of financial options [17]. In 1977,
Myers was among the first to apply these concepts to real investments. He defined real options
as "discretionary future investments" that offer strategic flexibility to the companies involved.
These options refer to real assets, such as infrastructure projects or investments in renewable
energies, while traditional financial options focus on the exchange of securities in various markets
[65].
Trigeorgis (2017) offers a further definition, characterizing real options as those in which
the decision-makers have the right to act strategically in order to adapt to evolving market
conditions, though they are not obligated to do so. This flexibility has the potential to increase
the value of investment options, particularly in capital-intensive industries with uncertain
market developments [90].
In the context of ROA, there are several types of real options, each of which offers unique
strategic advantages for incorporating uncertainty into the decision-making process. For the
sake of completeness, a few of these are mentioned here. One is the deferral option, which
allows a decision-maker to postpone an investment until market conditions improve in its
favor. Under certain market conditions, this can even lead to a doubling of the value of the
==PAGE:29==
2.4 Real-Options Analysis in Wind Energy
18
investment [60]. The idea of growth options, on the other hand, describes the potential for an
investment to increase further as a result of beneficial market conditions [15]. Then there are
divestment options, also called abandonment options, which give the investor the flexibility to
exit an investment at any time if it becomes unprofitable. [63]. Another possibility is offered by
so-called switching options: here, the decision maker is given the opportunity to adjust various
inputs or outputs of the project according to the current dynamics of the markets[28]. Finally,
there is the option of staging investments. This option is particularly interesting if the decision
maker wants to reduce certain risks associated with uncertain political support and volatility
in the supply chain because it provides the decision maker with the flexibility to divide the
investment into phases [14].
When comparing ROA and the net present value method, the main difference is that ROA
explicitly focuses on possible uncertainties and the implementation of flexibility in the decision-
making process to compensate for these uncertainties. The NPV method, on the other hand,
calculates the present value of expected cash flows by discounting them with a risk-adjusted
interest rate. However, this method assumes that investment decisions are taken definitively
and does not take into account the possibility that strategies may also adapt over time in
response to changing external factors. By focusing on uncertainty and flexibility, ROA considers
the value of waiting to make a decision and also allows for the possibility that certain decisions
may be adjusted based on new information [27].
This is confirmed by McDonald & Siegel (1986), who found in their study of deferral options
that they have a significant value, especially when there is a high level of uncertainty associated
with the investment. They concluded that companies should not simply invest when the net
present value is positive, but rather wait until the expected benefits significantly exceed the
costs [60].
Now that the main differences and advantages of ROA have been explained, the next question
is how to model these real options. Many methods have been developed to do this, so only
three selected approaches will be discussed in more detail below. All three methods allow for a
robust investment strategy.
Firstly, the method of binomial lattices and decision trees is presented. This approach allows
investment decisions to be modeled in sequences and incorporates step-by-step developments.
This is accomplished by dividing a given time period into discrete intervals, which allows asset
developments to be captured using an iterative process. The model assumes that the value
of an asset can change by a factor u or d over a given time step. The current value of a
project, represented by S, is multiplied by the corresponding factor. The probabilities of an
increase or decrease in value are denoted by p and (1 −p), respectively. This relationship can

This method of evaluating investment decisions under uncertainty has proven to be particularly
useful in the context of decisions involving three specific options: deferral, expansion, and
abandonment. This approach enables the decision-maker to reevaluate the potential outcomes
at each node and, based on these assessments, identify and implement the most valuable courses
of action [17].
Stochastic dynamic programming (SDP) is another method that is mainly used to optimize large-
scale projects under uncertainty. This method also uses a multi-stage process approach to solve
a decision problem. It uses the so-called Bellman principle of optimality, which can essentially
be summarized as an approach in which the best course of action for each phase of the decision
problem is determined recursively on the basis of possible future states and the associated
returns [104]. By incorporating such flexibility, the SDP offers decision-makers a dynamic tool
for variably adapting their strategies, in contrast to conventional static evaluation approaches
[11]. In the context of wind energy investments, Vogstad & Kristoffersen (2010) applied the
SDP to evaluate the profitability and timing of various wind farm projects. Their study
demonstrated that the SDP method enables investors to consider uncertain market conditions
and political incentives, allowing them to optimize investment decisions by postponing, resizing,
or terminating their own projects in response to various uncertainties (e.g., future electricity
price fluctuations and changes in government subsidies) [92].
The two previously mentioned methods are frequently accompanied by the Monte Carlo
simulation, which is a statistical method for evaluating the performance of an investment
strategy. One of the main advantages of using Monte Carlo simulation to solve ROA problems
is that it can model various uncertainties and provide probabilistic investment assessments,
rather than relying on single-point estimates. In doing so, the Monte Carlo simulation provides
a distribution of possible outcomes by running thousands of simulations. This allows decision-
makers to evaluate risk-adjusted investment strategies and identify optimal decision points
under uncertainty. Consequently, this approach leads to increased flexibility in decision-making
and a significant contribution to strategic planning [99].

2.5
Cost Scaling in Offshore Wind Farms:
Economies of Scale
As the offshore wind energy industry continues to expand, it is becoming increasingly important
to understand potential cost scaling mechanisms in order to optimize project feasibility and
financial viability. The concept of economies and diseconomies of scale plays a significant role
in determining the costs of a wind farm as they constantly increase in size. The purpose of
this chapter is to provide a comprehensive overview of these economic effects and to serve as a
theoretical background for the chosen approach in this analysis.
First of all, it is important to understand what kind of costs occur when designing a wind farm
project. As previously mentioned, the LCOE metric mainly incorporates project investment costs
and O&M costs. Project investment costs are also referred to as capital expenditures (CAPEX)
and are a one-time investment in order to set up the wind farm, while O&M costs are part of
operational expenditures (OPEX). The CAPEX refers to the upfront investment that is needed
to develop, procure, and install an offshore wind farm. These costs are usually the largest portion
of the total project expenses and can be described in three main categories: Development and
consenting (e.g. project management, engineering, surveys, or legal authorization), production
and procurement (e.g. turbines, foundations, and the transmission system), and installation
and commissioning (e.g. component installation, insurance, and harbor costs). OPEX, on the
other hand, refers to the costs which occur during the operational life of the wind farm. These
costs include but are not limited to, potential seabed rental fees, transmission charges, and
different forms of maintenance campaigns (corrective, proactive, and indirect) which include
==PAGE:33==

22
personnel costs for monitoring and technicians, the logistical infrastructure (e.g. hiring of
supply vessels), spare parts, and special equipment [79].
When it comes to the latest cost developments, most studies focus on the overall cost trends
in the whole offshore wind value chain, highlighting the significant decline in LCOE over the
last years. The main drivers for this development are the already mentioned economies of scale
during production, standardization, increasing size and capacity of wind turbines, competitive
supply chains as well as technical improvements and innovations of advanced technology. These
projections provide valuable insights into the economic feasibility of offshore wind, supporting
informed investment decisions and guiding research and development efforts [51, 46, 81].
The Danish Energy Agency’s technical energy catalog predicts future price developments for
offshore wind farms by applying a learning curve method. This method demonstrates the
economic effects of an increase in learning in the context of technology production. It assumes
that the production of a unit increases the knowledge about it and thus enables a cost reduction
in the production of future units of the same technology. The method also utilizes effects that
are caused by economies of scale. Furthermore, the success of learning and the associated
learning rate are influenced by the maturity of the technology itself. Technologies in their early
stages of development are characterized by a high degree of uncertainty and significant cost
reduction potential, while more mature technologies, such as offshore wind turbines, have a
comparatively moderate development potential [21]. This learning curve method is a widely
used approach and helps to predict the future development of wind energy costs (see also [81,
71, 95]). However, this method does not indicate how the project costs of an individual plant
would be affected by changing the quantity, in other words up or down-scaling the overall
capacity of an individual project.
Therefore this analysis introduces an approach to model economies and diseconomies of scale
within an individual wind farm power plant. Economies of scale have been widely studied in
different industries and can be defined using a decreasing average cost function with increasing
production output [86]. Other studies also examined the presence of economies of scale in the
power generation industry specifically, confirming the existence of these effects in this sector.
Hereby are cost reductions mainly achieved through operational synergies, procurement, and
bulk purchasing strategies, the utilization of shared infrastructure, as well as increased capital
utilization. The studies both indicate that smaller utilities benefit more from the economies of
scale effects by scaling up, while larger utilities can experience diminishing returns [34, 35].
A market report by Wiser et al. (2011) provided some insights on economies of scale for
wind power specifically. They investigated the state of the wind power industry in the United
States and found that onshore wind farms do experience certain economies of scale effects,
especially for wind power plants with a total capacity smaller than 20 MW [96]. A subsequent
study by Junginger et al. (2004) examined the potential for cost reduction in offshore wind
farms. The study concluded that, while the industry as a whole possesses considerable cost
reduction potential through the scaling up of turbine sizes, the mass production of components,
and standardization, certain economies of scale exist at the plant level. Specifically, larger
offshore wind farms benefit from economies of scale, as fixed costs are distributed over a greater
production volume. The study further found that orders of 100 or more identical turbine
types can achieve price reductions up to 45% compared to smaller order quantities, thereby

underlining the effect of bulk purchasing strategies. Furthermore, technological improvements in

The Danish developer Ørsted released a report about the development of the offshore wind

2.6
Research Gap
This literature review has demonstrated that there is already a substantial amount of research
on the optimization of offshore wind energy, covering a wide range of focus areas. However, it
has also revealed some significant gaps in the existing literature. As the analysis of publication
trends presented earlier in this chapter shows, while the literature on overplanting strategies is
expanding, it remains comparatively unexplored in comparison to other areas such as LCOE
modeling and the real options analysis. The existing literature has primarily focused on the
theoretical advantages of overplanting strategies with regard to a more efficient utilization of
the existing transmission system. However, it has neglected to address the regulatory and
economic challenges in its consideration. Furthermore, numerous studies have examined the
LCOE as a measure for cost assessment. Yet, only a few of these studies have integrated it
into real options approaches to consider the flexibility of investments under uncertainty and its

energy has been highlighted, it is clear that its application with a focus on the overplanting of

integrates overplanting strategies and LCOE modeling. This approach will provide a com-

projects. Additionally, it offers new insights into the economics of offshore wind farms in the

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
The following chapter aims to provide a more thorough description of the methodology and
quantitative framework employed in this study. First, the research approach is described in
more detail, followed by a comprehensive description of the case study, which, among other
things, describes the auction itself and presents the specific site conditions. This is followed by
a detailed description of the valuation criteria used and their calculation, as well as further
assumptions and background to the analysis. Finally, a focused explanation of the real options
analysis and the scenarios designed within this framework are being described, thereby linking
the theoretical framework with the practical applications.
3.1
Research Approach
The following research adopts a quantitative approach in order to analyze the economic and
technical feasibility of overplanting strategies in offshore wind farms with the use of ROA. The
study integrates elements of stochastic optimization, techno-economic modeling, and sensitivity
analysis to evaluate how developers can strategically leverage the overplanting of wind farms
under uncertainty. The analysis framework is divided into several phases, each of which focuses
on different aspects of optimizing the design of the wind farm and the associated investment
decision under uncertainty.
The study begins with a deterministic base case that serves as a benchmark for evaluating
alternative strategies. In this base case, a fixed grid feed-in capacity and a minimum number of
wind turbines are assumed in order to evaluate the LCOE under the specific site conditions,
without considering future uncertainties. The findings from this case form the basis for the
expansion of the model into a stochastic optimization framework.
Building on this foundation, an OUU framework is implemented to capture the stochastic
effects of key external factors. These factors are then examined using a scenario-based analysis.
The work identifies and focuses on the scenario with the uncertainties related to a potential
grid interconnection expansion and whether additional capacity will be provided after a certain
period of time. In order to model these uncertainties, this analysis implements a Monte Carlo
simulation approach to generate probabilistic scenarios. The expected LCOE is statistically
calculated, taking into account various probabilities for the grid interconnection expansion and
its timing. This provides a framework for risk-adjusted decision-making.
Subsequently, the ROA is introduced as a tool for advanced investment evaluation, with the
objective of identifying the most suitable timing for overplanting in the scenario. As previously
discussed, ROA, in contrast to traditional discounted cash flow methodologies, considers the
flexibility of the developers in adjusting wind farm designs and strategies over time as a reaction
to changing circumstances. Decision trees as well as probability models are used to compare

various overplanting strategies (e.g., investing in additional turbines in advance or waiting
for market conditions to develop) to determine which approach leads to the most favorable
economic outcome.
By following this approach, the present research integrates technical optimization with an
economic analysis and uncertainty modeling to provide a comprehensive review of different
overplanting strategies in the context of offshore wind farm development. The study hereby
aims to provide a new perspective on investment flexibility and to address the challenges faced
by today’s developers in an evolving energy market.
3.2
Case Study: Danish Offshore Wind Tender
Denmark serves as a prime example of a nation well-suited for the development of offshore
wind energy. Its geographical location, surrounded by the North and Baltic Seas, positions it
favorably for the utilization of robust and consistent winds (see Figure 7.3). The country’s
commitment to this field dates back to 1991, marked by the installation of the world’s first
offshore wind farm in Vindeby [106] The Danish focus on offshore wind energy can be attributed
to a combination of necessity and favorable framework conditions. Following decades of reliance
on onshore wind power, which has resulted in the occupation of significant land areas, Denmark
is now turning its attention to its extensive maritime sector to expand its wind energy capacities
(see Figure 7.4 and Figure 7.5).
As demonstrated in Figure 3.1, the majority of newly installed Danish wind turbines have
been situated offshore in recent years, accounting for slightly over a third of Denmark’s total
wind power capacity of over 7 GW. Collectively, offshore and onshore wind energy currently
contributes in excess of 50 % of Denmark’s electricity supply, with onshore turbines accounting
for just under 56 % and offshore turbines for the remaining 44 %.
Looking into the future, Denmark plans to further add offshore wind generation capacity.

3.3.2.1
Energy Losses
In the absence of energy losses, which are defined as the energy that would theoretically be
available but cannot be supplied due to technical or other reasons, an inaccurate AEP estimate
would be calculated. Such losses indicate the discrepancy between the gross and net energy
produced. Data from a study conducted by the National Laboratory of Renewable Energies
(NREL) is being used to incorporate various losses in energy production [13].
The objective of said study is to identify the causes of losses and uncertainties in the estimation
of the energy yield of wind turbines. To this end, the research focuses on the process of
energy estimation prior to the construction of the wind farm, where the energy production
of wind turbines can be predicted with an uncertainty distribution (see Figure 7.9 in the
appendix). It appears that, despite the model’s comprehensive consideration of extensive loss
parameters, the actual energy production from the wind farm remains below the modeled value.
The research has also identified the primary causes of energy losses in wind farms, which are
primarily attributed to wake effects (0-10%), availability (2-4.5%), electrical losses (1-3.5%),
environmental losses (1-5%), and curtailment (<4%) (see Figure 7.10 in the appendix) [13].
A more thorough explanation of these different loss categories is provided in the following
paragraph. Losses within the availability category can be attributed to various factors, including
operating conditions at the site and the age of the wind turbines. Losses resulting from wake
effects include those caused by the reduction of the wind resource within a turbine wake. The
modeling of these losses can be achieved through the implementation of various wake models.
To mitigate these losses, various optimization options exist in the context of site planning and
control strategies. Electrical losses within a wind farm derive from two primary sources: the
transmission of energy through collection cables, transformers, and other systems, and the
consumption of energy by the wind turbines themselves (e.g. rotor alignment). The extent
of these losses is dependent on various factors, including site design, cable routing, and the
electrical design and efficiency of the components. The losses associated with wind turbine
performance are defined as the difference between the energy generated by the turbine and the
energy predicted by the OEM power curve at a given wind speed. These losses are contingent
on the specific operating conditions at the turbine’s location. Environmental losses, on the
other hand, are attributed to factors such as icing, soiling, and erosion. These losses, like some
of the other categories mentioned, are influenced by the unique characteristics of the specific
site. Finally, losses due to curtailment are largely driven by grid restrictions; however, they can
also be attributed to environmental impacts or requirements related to licensing, such as the
prevention of bird mortality or shadow impact [13].
In this research, the AEP calculation assumes losses resulting from wake effects (lwake), avail-
ability losses (lava), electrical losses (lel), wind turbine performance (lperf), and losses caused by
environmental factors (lenv). The energy losses resulting from curtailment (lcurt) in this analysis
are exclusively focused on grid restrictions and are dynamically calculated. Curtailment occurs

3.3.3
Cost Modeling
Any economic optimization aimed at finding an optimal trade-off between wind farm production,
operating costs, and financial costs requires the use of cost models. A relatively simple model
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Model Evaluation Parameters
36
is used here due to the overall and more general nature of this thesis, with its focus on real
options analysis. The cost assumptions and model used are described in the following section.
To ensure the model accurately reflected the most current costs, the most recent technology
data catalog for electricity and District Heating generation from the Danish Energy Agency was
utilized. The most current version of the catalog was published in February 2025. The data for
offshore wind energy is based on the latest offshore wind project in the Danish North Sea, the
Thor project [21]. As this wind farm is located in the immediate region of the areas considered
in this analysis, the same framework conditions (e.g., seabed conditions, water depth) are
assumed. This enables the direct utilization of the cost assumptions outlined in the catalog.
The cost analysis is based on two primary categories: CAPEX and OPEX. CAPEX refers to
expenditures incurred during the investment phase of a project, including costs for technical
equipment such as turbines and their foundations, installation of these components, and all
costs associated with project development. It also covers transmission infrastructure expenses,
particularly array cables, export cables, and substations. The cost data in the technology
catalog do not reflect costs incurred for possible land leases, unforeseen events, financial costs,
or the cost of decommissioning. Therefore, these costs are not included in this analysis. The
OPEX cost data is divided into two subcategories: fixed and variable costs. Fixed OPEX costs
are not influenced by the fluctuating production of the wind farm. These costs include personnel
costs for wind turbine operation, insurance, and specific equipment and vessels. Additionally,
there are variable OPEX costs that depend on the volume of electricity produced. These
include all kinds of spare parts, lubricants, and other repair costs (e.g., technical personnel,
and logistical measures such as ships or helicopters).

3.3.3.1
Economies of Scale
In addition, this model introduces a cost model in order to account for economies and dis-
economies of scale. The model assumes, that these effects have a certain influence on the
individual cost components needed for the design of the wind farm. The initial Literature
Review has shown, that certain scaling effects can be observed within a wind farm, however,
some studies showed no or minor evidence of it. A detailed analysis of actual data would be
required to determine its exact magnitude and impact, which was outside of the scope of this
study. Therefore, the simple model presented in this study is based on certain assumptions
regarding the scaling factors and their impact on cost modeling. These assumptions are further
elaborated upon in the following sections.
Consequently, for a smaller number of wind turbines, a significant portion of the substation costs
is borne by each turbine. Conversely, as the number of turbines increases, the proportion of
substation investment per turbine decreases, resulting in a relative proportion that is significantly
lower with a larger number of turbines. In contrast, the economic principle of diseconomies of
scale predicts an increase in costs with an escalating number of products. These effects have
been incorporated into the cost model and are apparent, for instance, in the cable costs to reflect
longer cable distances and higher capacity equipment as part of the additional transmission
infrastructure needed with the increasing number of turbines.
The approach to reflect these economic effects within the costs in this analysis makes use of
power-law scaling, which can be described using Equation 3.12. For each cost component, a
scaling factor is applied using a logarithmic relationship, where economies of scale reduce per-
unit costs (e.g., turbine procurement), while diseconomies increase costs for certain components.
This approach was chosen since it is a proven way to reflect real-world cost variations, which
follow a power-law distribution [85].

economies of scale and require careful optimization to balance benefits and costs.

Statements
As mentioned earlier, the present study uses the ROA to strategically evaluate the technical
design and related investment decisions in the development of offshore wind farms under
==PAGE:57==

uncertainty. As previously discussed, the ROA incorporates future events and their probability
into the optimization framework, thereby reflecting flexibility in decision-making. This gives
the method a decisive advantage in strategic evaluation compared to conventional deterministic
models. The objective of this study is to determine how various overplanting strategies, in
combination with the corresponding procurement decisions and a possible expansion of the
transmission grid feed-in capacity, affect the economic feasibility of the wind farm. This is
assessed using the LCOE, which has previously been described in detail.
In order to integrate this method into the analysis in a structured way, a scenario-based stochastic
optimization approach has been developed. In this approach, the various uncertainties are
explicitly modeled, and a Monte Carlo simulation is used to evaluate the expected LCOE by
integrating probability distributions of the most important uncertainties. This allows for a
solid comparison of different strategic options.
Furthermore, a decision tree was developed in the context of this study, which maps the
developers’ decisions, uncertainties, and possible outcomes for each scenario. The tree visually
represents the natural order of the strategic decisions, thus enabling quantitative modeling using
the corresponding optimization results. The mathematical formulation of the optimization
problems is based on a stochastic optimization framework as described in the literature review,
which ensures that both the flexibility in the timing of investments and the scaling of the
infrastructure and overplanting strategies are considered in the decision-making process.
Finally, the application of ROA to the strategic evaluation of offshore wind farm development
leads to an adaptive decision-making process in which both risk and potential return are
optimally balanced. The results of this study aim to provide new insights for developers and
to support investment strategies in the long term that are influenced by uncertain future
conditions.

3.4.4.2
Optimization Statement
Due to the associated uncertainty, the optimization problem to minimize the LCOE is modeled
as an optimization problem under uncertainty (OUU). In this scenario, the expected LCOE is
calculated as a probability-weighted mean of the two results previously mentioned: (1) grid
expansion occurs with probability π and (2) grid expansion does not occur with probability
(1 −π). This uncertainty is addressed by applying a Monte Carlo simulation approach, which
generates a range of probability-weighted results for the LCOE. To formulate the mathematical
optimization problem, the problem definition was initially presented in the form of a Word
problem:
Word-Problem:
A developer is planning to build a wind farm system in the North Sea, which
currently has a planned maximum grid feed-in capacity of 1 GW. The developer plans to use 15
MW wind turbines. To achieve an economically feasible business case, the developer is planning
to optimize the number of turbines so that the LCOE is minimized. As part of the tender
design, the grid operator is open to negotiating a feed-in extension after X years of operation.
The developer would like to explore this option further and analyze how it can be utilized for a
potentially better business case and how it would affect the initial project design idea.
In order to solve this OUU problem, the method of stochastic optimization is utilized, as it
==PAGE:64==

53
was described in Section 2.3. This was done by using the general description of the method in

4.2.1
Fixed Grid Extension (Deterministic timing)
The ROA in this study is undertaken using a structured approach, which involves the devel-
opment of four sub-scenarios that gradually increase the complexity and uncertainty of the
decision-making process for turbine procurement and the potential grid expansion. The initial
step involves a deterministic investigation of the grid expansion. Under the assumption that the
expansion of the feed-in capacity is guaranteed (π = 100%), the analysis focuses on the point
in time when the capacity is expanded. Furthermore, this analysis assumes that if oversizing
of the capacity with additional turbines is recommended, these must be procured in year 0,
i.e. at the start of the project. The interconnection capacity is increased from 1 GW to 2 GW
in a given year, denoted by X. The specific year, X, varies depending on the particular case
under consideration (e.g., X = 3, 5, 7, 10 years). This approach is used to assess how different
expansion times affect the optimal number of turbines and the resulting LCOE. From the
perspective of a developer, this analysis can provide early indications of the optimal turbine
purchase quantity at present and future times, when the grid expansion is guaranteed at the
time of the project bid. Since an early grid expansion allows for greater utilization of the
installed turbines, the LCOE is expected to decrease more when X is small while reflecting
a higher number of turbines. This deterministic approach provides a baseline against which
stochastic expansion scenarios can be compared.
The initial analysis was conducted on the basis of the real wind climate in the North Sea
(NEWA). This resulted in the following observation, as illustrated in Figure 4.6.

4.2.2

uncertainty into the expansion timing and integrates it into the model. The objective is to
optimize the procurement timing of turbines while taking into account the expected LCOE at
different points in time. In this stochastic grid expansion, the analysis continues to assume that
the expansion of the feed-in capacity is guaranteed (π = 100%), but that the point in time X
is uncertain. To model this, a probability distribution was introduced instead of a fixed year

financial, or technical constraints. The model runs a large number of simulations with different
expansion years and thus evaluates whether it is advisable to purchase additional turbines
today or to take a conservative approach and wait since a delay in the capacity expansion is to
be expected. By incorporating a probability-weighted expected value approach, this analysis
provides a more realistic estimate of the LCOE when the transmission system development

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
Building upon the findings of the previous chapter, this chapter offers a critical interpretation
of the results and presents an analysis of how overplanting strategies and decision-making
approaches influence the economic and operational performance of offshore wind farms over
time. The discussion compares the results of the modeled scenarios and evaluates the individual
trade-offs between cost efficiency, flexibility, and risk reduction.
5.1
Key Findings
The present study has examined the influence of ROA on the design and decision-making
process for offshore wind projects. The study focuses on possible strategies for oversizing
the capacity of a wind power plant while taking into account an uncertain expansion of the
grid feed-in capacity. The results provide insights into how strategic decisions affect economic
valuation criteria, such as the LCOE. They also highlight the importance of decision flexibility
in the face of uncertain external events and suggest that this should be included in future
decision-making processes.
The initial step in this analysis involved the formulation of a baseline scenario, which highlights
the fundamental characteristics of the site under consideration and provides an initial financial
estimate. In this case, no increase of the grid feed-in capacity was assumed. The analysis
used the prevailing wind conditions for the site based on the data provided by the NEWA
and demonstrated that, under the assumptions made here, an optimal LCOE value of 61.11
EUR/MWh is achieved when no overplanting takes place. For the IEA 15 MW reference plant,
this translates to a wind farm consisting of 67 turbines, with low curtailment losses (about 0.3%),
which indicates an already efficient utilization of the available grid capacity under these wind
conditions. In a subsequent analysis it was further demonstrated that overplanting results in
an increase in gross annual electricity generation, but concurrently leads to significantly higher
curtailment losses. A marginal rise in net electricity generation was observed, indicating a more
efficient use of the existing transmission grid at lower wind speeds. However, these additional
export profits are insufficient compared to the increasing capital expenditures, ultimately
resulting in a rising LCOE value. The analysis reveals that the highest investigated overplanting
level of 40% results in curtailment losses of approximately 21% and an LCOE value of 72.67
EUR/MWh, thereby substantially diminishing the project’s profitability. Moreover, a basic
analysis of varying wind turbine sizes reveals that, given the present state of technology, the
IEA 15 MW reference plant achieves the most advantageous economic and operational key
figures for the prevailing conditions in the Danish North Sea, thereby confirming the findings
from existing literature on the optimal turbine size.
The present simulation further examined the influence of different wind climates on the feasibility
of overplanting strategies. The measured wind climate, characterized by high wind speeds,

turbines would rapidly lead to increased curtailment losses. However, under a distinct wind

characterized by suboptimal wind conditions can reduce the costs, when taking into account
the effects of economies of scale. However, the general feasibility of the project is constrained

The study continued with examining how potential grid feed-in expansion scenarios could
influence the investment strategies for offshore wind farms. For this purpose, both deterministic
and stochastic investigations were carried out. The deterministic scenario for expanding the
grid feed-in capacity demonstrated that an increased capacity of 2 GW would enable a further
reduction in LCOE values due to the higher number of wind turbines. This is attributable
to the simple modeling of economies of scale presented in this study, which can also have a
decisive impact within a wind farm project or for an individual developer (e.g. bulk purchasing
for multiple projects). At the same time, the study shows that the timing of the expansion can
have a significant impact on the economic viability of the project. Earlier expansion, specifically
within the first 3 to 5 years, results in LCOE values comparable to those associated with a
feed-in expansion at the start of the project. On the other hand, later expansion timings, in
this analysis exceeding 7 years, lead to LCOE values that approach those of the base scenario.
This tendency can be explained by extended periods of significant curtailment losses. Based
on these findings, a stochastic analysis was performed to assess the impact of the uncertain
timing of expansion on the LCOE values. The Monte Carlo simulations revealed that procuring
additional wind turbines in phases as the project progresses results in consistently lower LCOE
values compared to procuring all turbines at the initial stage of the project. This strategy
leverages the effects of CAPEX discounting and technological learning effects, which were
introduced in this study, leading to reduced future investment costs.
The real options analysis applied in this context demonstrated that the flexibility of the decision-
making process for different procurement strategies can play a decisive role in optimizing
the profitability of offshore wind farms. In the present analysis, preventive overplanting in
expectation of the expansion at the start of the project would only make economic sense if
the expected expansion is implemented with a high probability and at an early stage. It
has been demonstrated that decision-makers who adopt a dynamic strategy, aligned with
the uncertain market developments, can achieve a substantial reduction in financial risks.
This recommendation is further strengthened by the consideration of both the uncertainty
surrounding the expansion timing and the uncertainty of the expansion itself.
Here, the
strategy of procuring additional turbines only after the expansion is implemented results in
more favorable LCOE values. Adopting this dynamic decision-making approach allows decision-
makers to avoid the need for additional investments in turbines that would otherwise be
underutilized due to feed-in limits or face increased curtailment losses during the project’s entire
operational life if the expansion is not realized at all. This analysis ultimately demonstrates
that the implementation of real options strategies to strategically defer procurement decisions
can strengthen the financial resilience of the project.
Moreover, this study provides implications for the various stakeholders involved in the de-
velopment of offshore wind projects. For example, a developer can use the approach of a

phased investment strategy presented in this study to mitigate various risks associated with the

the hydrogen infrastructure or by formulating a comprehensive plan for long-term demand
growth by promoting the electrification of various sectors. These measures would likely increase
the attractiveness of these projects to developers.
This study has demonstrated the value of real options analysis as a decision-making tool for
the development of offshore wind farms. The study highlights the method’s ability to minimize
financial risks and improve the adaptability in uncertain market conditions. The findings
further show that flexibility in procurement and phased deployment can result in a more robust
investment strategy compared to static approaches.
5.2
Limitations of the Study
This chapter addresses the limitations of the research, including model assumptions, data
availability, and simplifications in the optimization and real options framework. It hereby
discusses how these limitations may affect the generalization of the results. The present analysis
is subject to certain limitations in the area of economic modeling, including the assumption
regarding the cost components used here as part of CAPEX and OPEX. The Danish Energy
Agency published an updated report on these assumptions in early 2025, and these assumptions
are based on data from the most recent offshore wind project in the Danish North Sea, which
indicates a well-founded assessment. However, projections of future cost developments are
subject to considerable uncertainties that are not reflected in the present analysis. A fluctuation
in these assumptions would directly influence the results of this analysis regarding the phased
turbine procurement, since future investments are based on these assumptions. In addition, a
detailed cost modeling of the individual components could provide a more realistic assumption
and better assess the impact of the individual components on the overall results. Furthermore,
it is important to note that potential disruptions in the supply chain, such as material shortages,
have the capacity to substantially influence the cost structures of these components.
In this context, it is important to mention the model that has been introduced for the purpose
of modeling economies of scale in the procurement of components. The aim of this model is
to map possible cost effects depending on the number of products. However, since a detailed
economies of scale model does not exist to the authors knowledge, the model that has been used
==PAGE:89==
5.2 Limitations of the Study
78
here was developed based on a literature review. The individual scaling factors are estimated
due to a lack of data on this topic. Consequently, the model’s assumptions are subject to a
degree of uncertainty, which is also transferred to the LCOE modeling. For future applications,
the development of a more advanced model, accounting for sensitivities, is recommended.
Furthermore, the analysis for the economic evaluation of the scenarios focuses on the LCOE
method, which, although it is widely used to evaluate energy projects, is not without its
limitations. For example, all parameters that would allow a detailed consideration of the
project’s financing are neglected here. In addition, it is known that LCOE modeling does not
take into account the time-varying value of electricity, which essentially refers to the temporal
effects associated with varying electricity generation and demand. Consequently, alternative
methods (e.g., COVE) that can account for the actual value of electricity might be more
applicable. Moreover, the assumption regarding electricity prices is based on historical data
from 2018 to 2022. However, given the volatility of these prices and their sensitivity to political
and geopolitical factors, stochastic modeling of future price developments, which could include
predicted changes in demand, could improve the robustness of the assumptions.
In addition, the present analysis examines a limited range of wind climates. Although the
study considers two wind climate scenarios (NEWA and an artificial wind climate), seasonal
fluctuations or long-term climate changes lead to a more complex variability of the wind
resource. This variability can directly influence the various strategies and their feasibility, as
demonstrated. It is important to mention that, although the results obtained are consistent
with the findings from the existing literature, a validation of the model using real wind farm
data has not been carried out. Additionally, the overplanting approach is still relatively new and
unexplored in the field of offshore wind energy, which is why there is little evidence available
for a long-term assessment.
In the context of the real options analysis, the present study is subject to further limitations. For
example, the model does not include an economic valuation that would positively influence the
early procurement strategy of wind turbines. A developer might prefer the early procurement
of turbines in order to secure the required number of wind turbines and thus prevent possible
bottlenecks in the market. The study focuses on the timing and occurence of a potential grid
interconnection expansion as a flexible decision, though other real options (e.g., the integration
battery storage) could offer additional strategic value in the decision-making process, when
combined with the present assessment. Furthermore, the study modeled the timing of grid
expansion as a probabilistic event using a Weibull distribution. However, it is important to
note that the occurrence of this event is subject to negotiations between the developer and the
grid operator, as well as other factors such as financial constraints or political commitments,
all of which are challenging to incorporate into a probabilistic distribution.
Finally, it should be acknowledged that this analysis is based on the assumption of a stable
political environment. Future policy changes could significantly influence the feasibility of the
strategies examined in this study, but these changes are not reflected in the present framework.
==PAGE:90==
5.3 Recommendations for future research
79
5.3
Recommendations for future research
Several recommendations for future research can be derived from the results obtained in this
study and the limitations identified. The potential for future research on this subject could
include the numerous uncertainties present within the design and planning process of offshore
wind energy projects. The comprehensive review of the existing literature in the beginning
has revealed that research is already being carried out on the various uncertainties within
a wind farm project, thus future research could implement this with a particular focus on
overplanting strategies and the ROA. For instance, a comprehensive analysis of the variability
in wind resources could offer insights into how overplanting strategies might be influenced by
seasonal or long-term changes in the wind climate. Moreover, a fundamental investigation of
the Weibull parameter uncertainty in regards to overplanting could provide further insights
about its impact.
As previously mentioned, the results obtained are subject to uncertainties in the cost modeling
that have a direct influence on strategic decisions. A comprehensive analysis of the uncertainty
surrounding future price fluctuations could significantly help to understand the impact on the
outcomes of this study and, consequently, the strategic investment decision of a developer.
Additionally, the development of a detailed model to capture economies of scale within a wind
farm is recommended, given the current limited research availability on this subject.
This analysis could be built upon by other works to vary the economic methodologies used to
evaluate the strategic decisions, for example by exploring whether the strategic recommendations
hold if the optimization is not focused on minimizing the LCOE, but instead on maximizing
the NPV or minimizing the COVE of the project. Furthermore, improved modeling of volatile
market conditions, for example by developing stochastic electricity price models, would be
beneficial. Subsequent research could also focus on the expansion of the integrated optimization
problem under uncertainty by implementing further design variables. These variables could
include the optimization of the overplanting strategy as well as a layout optimization, which
could then be extended to include the modeling of wake effects or an in-depth cost modeling
approach.
Alternatively, a multi-objective optimization approach could be developed for
the overplanting of offshore wind farms, including various parameters and resulting in the
development of optimal overplanting strategies.
In the context of the ROA carried out here, scenarios were also identified that include the
integration of Power-to-X systems or battery storage in connection with overplanting strategies.
Future work could build on this research by investigating the economic efficiency of hydrogen
production as a risk mitigation strategy against curtailment losses or by exploring the potential
of storing excess generation from overplanting in batteries and feeding it into the grid when
electricity prices are high. Furthermore, the development of a multi-level real options model
could provide a framework for assessing flexible decision-making processes across various project
phases, thus including multiple uncertain scenarios. For a more detailed investment analysis,
which could be of particular interest to developers and financial stakeholders, future research
could also involve a comparison of real options with traditional discounted cash flow approaches.
Such a comparison could serve to more clearly highlight the value of flexibility in offshore wind
investments.
==PAGE:91==
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis has explored the application of Real-Options Analysis (ROA) as a framework
for improving the decision-making and design process of offshore wind energy projects under
uncertainty. By focusing on overplanting strategies within the context of the North Sea I A2
lease area, the study provides a structured assessment of how flexible investment decisions
impact project feasibility, particularly in relation to grid expansion and economic uncertainties.
The findings demonstrate that overplanting under fixed grid constraints results in a diminishing
economic return due to increased curtailment losses. In the base case, a 1 GW capacity limit is
optimally utilized with the minimum number of turbines leading to minimal curtailment, and ad-
ditional capacity leads to higher LCOE values, ultimately reducing project profitability. However,
under a stochastic grid expansion scenario, a phased turbine procurement approach—delaying
additional investments until the grid interconnection expansion is realized—proves to be the
more economically viable strategy. This approach utilizes the benefits of CAPEX discounting,
technological learning effects, and flexible adaptation to market uncertainties.
From the perspectives of policy and industry, the results underline the importance of regulatory
certainty in interconnection expansion to enable optimized wind farm investments. Developers
can benefit from real-options thinking by incorporating dynamic procurement strategies rather
than committing to fixed investment plans. Additionally, policymakers should consider mecha-
nisms that provide clearer timelines for grid interconnection expansions and market conditions,
thereby reducing investment uncertainty.
In conclusion, this research demonstrates the critical role of ROA in offshore wind planning,
offering a structured approach to managing uncertainties while optimizing cost efficiency and
project feasibility. The insights provided in this thesis serve as a foundation for future research
on real-options applications in renewable energy investments and contribute to the broader
discourse on optimizing offshore wind farm design strategies in uncertain policy and market
environments.
