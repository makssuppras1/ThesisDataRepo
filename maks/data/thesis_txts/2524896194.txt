Abstract  
This project is a master thesis in Chemical and Biochemical engineering at Sino-Danish Center and the 
Tehcnical University of Denmark. The project focuses on treatment of ballast water. The International 
maritime organization has introduced a set of regulations in regards to the amount of living organisms 
present in the ballast water when it is to be released from a vessels ballast tanks. The project is written in 
collaboration with the company BAWAT which has developed a ballast water treatment system. The Ballast 
water treatment system partly works by pasteurizing the ballast water and partly by removing oxygen 
present in the water, which means that the organisms present in the water either dies due to the thermal 
treatment or are unable to multiply because of the lack of oxygen present in the water. In this project it has 
been attempted to create a model which can be used to describe the oxygen stripping process tanking 
place within BAWAT’s test setup located at Hundested Harbor. This was done in order to describe and 
optimize BAWAT’s ballast water treatment technology thus reducing the needed treatment time and heat 
in order to reduce costs in connection to using the system. 
The test setup used in order to create this report consists of an L-shaped tank which can be separated into 
various numbers of compartments. Experimental data was collected from the setup in nine different 
configurations, which then has been compared to a model for ideal reactors which was made in matlab. 
Nine different cases were tested with different amounts of compartments as well as different sizes in 
openings between these compartments. In order to fit the model to the experimental data from the various 
cases the model has been made more complex and a back mixing flow was introduced, where water moves 
in the opposite direction of the main flow. The back mixing was introduced in order to explain the slower 
drop in oxygen concentration compared to the expectations if the water moved in the same direction.   It 
was possible to create models for all the cases which more or less fitted the experimental data. It could be 
seen that it was possible to model the first compartments in the various setup correctly while it was harder 
to get the model to fit with the experimental data from the last compartments in the various setups.

1. Introduction 
This thesis is written as a part of the SDC-program in Chemical and Biochemical engineering which is taught 
by professors, PhD.’s and other lectures from The Technical University of Denmark, Kgs. Lyngby and 
Institute of Process Engineering at University of Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing. Furthermore has the 
report been written in collaboration with the Danish company BAWAT A/S which focuses on ballast water 
treatment.  
The focus of the thesis is about modeling various setups, created in BAWAT’s test tank located in 
Hundested Harbor. By using the test setup BAWAT is able to create various configurations simulating ships 
ballast tanks so that they can investigate how various configurations of restrictions present in the ships 
ballast tanks will affect the ballast water treatment process. Together with BAWAT the problem statement 
presented below in “2. Problem Statement” has been decided. The problem statement is followed by a 
Problem Analysis where the background for the need for ballast water treatment will be explained, as well 
as the currently existing ballast water treatment setups will be investigated.  
2. Problem Statement 
In this report it will be attempted to model the performance of a setup from BAWAT in regards to the 
reduction percentage of organisms assumed to be present in the ballast water. The reduction percentage 
will be determined as a function of circulations through the BAWAT-system. Furthermore various sizes of 
openings between compartments and thus the possible backflow of ballast water will be taken into account 
as well as the Nitrogen flow required to kill microorganism which survive the pasteurization. Furthermore 
the model uncertainty will be taken in to account in order to insure the robustness of the model and that it 
is compliance with rules and regulations.  
In order to understand the process BAWAT has made a pilot test system where it is possible to follow the 
oxygen levels in the various compartments as the treatment proceeds.  
Since the water which has been pasteurized and thus contain no living organisms also contain a low level of 
oxygen due to the injection of Nitrogen it is assumed that it is the reduction in oxygen and the reduction of 
organisms are following each other so that when it is possible to measure a reduction in oxygen present in 
the water the amount of living organisms in the water is also being reduced. This is however of cause 
dependent on that both the heat treatment and injection of nitrogen is running at the same time.   
The reason for the measurements of oxygen levels rather than presence of organisms directly is that there 
currently does not exist any in flow measurement probes which are able to determine the amount of living 
organisms present pr volume and thus the oxygen levels are used as an indicator instead. Therefore models 
of the oxygen levels needs to be created with which the treatment process can be described.

3. Problem Analysis  
In order to carry out the objectives stated in the problem statement a background knowledge is needed in 
regards to ballast water and ballast water treatment in general. This will be investigated in this section.  
Sea travel has been used for transporting large quantities of goods over long distances through human 
history. Over time trade and thus the need for transport has increased and as well has distances the ships 
travels. During travel the ships however is carrying various aquatic species with them and thus introducing 
them to new environment where they might become invasive species. The organisms might for example 
travel with the ships as fouling on the hull, in sea chests by being attached to anchors or other parts of the 
ship which gets in contact with the seawater, one of the largest problem however lies with the ships usage 
of ballast water, because of the large undistributed amounts of water present in ships ballast tanks. [1]  
Ballast water is used in order to stabilizing ships, carrying goods and is normally the same water as in which 
the ship is sailing. Ballast water is typically placed in ballast tanks between the inner and outer hull of the 
ship and is added when a ship unload its cargo and discharged when a ship is loading cargo in a new port.  
A problem with using water as ballast is, however, that when water is added to the ballast tank it is done 
quickly and without much filtration which means that local marine life is added together with the ballast 
water, as it can be seen below in Figure 4.  
3.1 Use of Ballast 
Freight and passenger ships are built in order to transport various goods oior people by sea and/or over 
fresh water bodies. When a ship is not completely loaded it is needed to add additional weight to 
compensate for the missing cargo which causes an increase in buoyancy, which again can cause:  
- 
A lack of propeller immersion into the water, 
- 
A lack of transversal slope which causes heeling,  which is that the ship lean to much back or 
forward, 
- 
A lack of longitudinal slope which causes a lack of trimming, that is that the ship lean too much to 
either left or right, 
- 
Static and dynamic stresses over the ship hull, which could be shear and torsion forces, bending 
and slamming, 
- 
Static and dynamic longitudinal or transversal instability, 
Thus it is needed to load extra weight onto the vessel in order to insure that they are seaworthy. This does 
not only applies to commercial vessels but also to other ships like navy vessels and large pleasure boats. All 
these ships needs to use ballast in order to stay seaworthy. [2] 
The material used to create the extra weight needed to keep the ships seaworthy is called ballast. 
Historically a lot of different solid materials such as sand, rocks, stone, cobble and iron has been used as 
ballast. After iron ships was introduced and replaced wood ships around the 1850’s new possibilities in 
regards to ballast appeared, and it was possible to start loading water, thus ballast water, into empty cargo 
holds or tanks, thus ballast tanks, on the ship. To use water as ballast proved to be easier and more 
efficient and thus this was adopted as the new practice and increased over the years. [3] 
==PAGE:9==

A ship which is not fully laden, is at a situation where it doesn’t use up its maximum allowed draught, which 
means that in regards to weight the ships carrying capacity has not been fully exploited. This is normally the 
situation when ships are at port and are either unloading or loading cargo. Furthermore the ship might not 
use up its maximum capacity if there is no cargo available to transport, but it needs to move on to a new 
port for its next charter or if the cargo transport is so light that the ships max volume is too low to be fully 
loaded in terms of weight and thus the volume of the ship is the limiting factor. [2] 
Furthermore even though that a ship might be fully loaded it might still require ballast water because the 
cargo the ship is holding might not be distributed equally at the ship and thus the ship might needed to be 
trimmed to one of the sides to stay stable within the water during its journey. Furthermore it might be 
needed to carry out various other ballast water operations during the rute depending on the weather and 
sea conditions during the ships route. These conditions could be shallow waters, an reduction in weight due 
to fuel and diesel consumption under the voyage. [3] 
Depending on which weather conditions it is expected in the area where a given vessel is sailing the ballast 
condition chosen by the vessel crew might also differ in the way that a maximum ballast water condition is 
chosen if bad weather and rough seas is expected. This is done in order to keep the ship stable while the 
bad weather is passing. If there is no expectations in regards to bad weather a light ballast water condition 
would be used in order to save fuel.  This is done by not filling the ballast tanks completely. This might also 
be the case when the ship is approaching its destination or sailing in inland channels/waterways. 
Furthermore ships would also reduce the present ballast from heavy ballast to light ballast conditions when 
sailing under safe conditions in regards to weather and sea or close to port, thus being able to reduce fuel 
consumption as well as getting ready for loading cargo. [3] 
When reaching shallow waters it might also be needed for the ship to discharge a part of its ballast in order 
to reduce the draught present, while if the ship needs to pass under a bridge it might need to increase the 
amount of ballast water in order to reduce the air draft.  
In regards to the consumption of fuel during a ships journey a average PANAMAX container vessel 
consumes around 100-180 tonnes of fuel per day, while in accordance with the International Convention 
for the safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS) from 1974 vessels needs to be trimmed in order to insure optimal 
hydrodynamic performance. Furthermore it is also needed to take the bridge visibility standards into 
account as well as the minimum draught needed for optimal propeller immersion. [3] 
For some vessel especially container vessels, passenger and cargo ferries (roll-on/roll-off)  which are 
loading cargo or passengers high above the waterline and for which ships the cargo often is not distributed 
equally specially ballast water tanks has also been installed so that it is possible to trim the ships in all kind 
of directions so that no matter how the weight has been distributed on board the ship it is possible to avoid 
any heeling in any direction. [3] 
This is especially important while the ships are in port and carrying out cargo operations. Normally vessels 
does not load nor discharge water in or from the anti-heeling tanks, instead a constant volume of water is 
present in these tanks and can then be pumped from one part of the ship to another depending on where 
extra weight might be needed. All this means that all kinds of commercial ships and vessels are basing their 
best operation practices and safety on usage of ballast water.  [3]

The ballast tanks present on a certain ship is hugely dependant on the type and size of the discussed vessel.

purposes as described in the previous section.  Ballast tanks can be situated in the vessels sides and are

part of the ship named Central Tanks (CT). Usually Forepeak tanks and after peak tanks are present in all 
type of vessels, though a few exceptions exist for example the Hamburg Express class container ships does

3.3 Transport of organisms with ballast water 
As long as water has been used for ballast it has been possible that various aquatic species would be 
pumped into the ballast tanks together with the water.  Since the ships often travel long distances the 
water, together with the aquatic species trapped in the tank, would be discharged at locations with 
different ecosystems and sea life than the locations where the water was collected. This means that it is a 
risk that new invasive species are introduced to ecosystems which in some cases are unable to cope with 
the new organisms introduced. The invasive species will spread quickly as they do not have any natural 
enemies in the new environment they have been introduced and as they are spread they will in danger the 
original sea life at the location. This is especially a problem when the ships are travelling between two very 
different environments, like if the ship is travelling from sea water to fresh water or from a cold water 
environment to warm water environment or the other way around. On Figure 4 it can seen how ballast 
water along with various aquatic organisms is loaded into a ships ballast tanks as the cargo hold is emptied, 
then the ship is sailing for its next destination where it is emptying the ballast tank while filling its cargo 
hold. This means that the ballast water and organisms present within the water is unloaded into a new 
environment and eco-system. Often the organisms present in the ballast water dies but sometimes they 
survive and spread. [4], [3]

in regards to the diversity of species in local ecosystems but is also creating massive economical problems 
around the world.  
3.3.1 Introduction of Zebra mussels to the great lakes  
In the late 1980’s the European zebra mussels, Dreissena polymorpha, were found within the Great Lakes.  
Since then the mussels has caused a lot of trouble and created a lot of expensive problems. The problem 
with the mussels is that they have no natural enemies in the Great Lakes and this has caused the 
population of mussels to explode. The mussel colonies in the Great Lakes are causing problems because 
they are blocking the pipes which are delivering water to cities and factories as well as the pipes which are 
providing cooling water for nuclear power stations as well as fossil fuel fired power plants. Besides these 
problems the mussel also attaches to ship and boat hulls as well as other marine structures and 
navigational buoys present in the water. Furthermore is dead mussels covering the beaches with sharp

mussel shells as well as rotting mussel flesh. The cost from the zebra mussel invasion has been estimated to 
be around 360 000 USD a year for the cities and industries affected, while it is estimated that the cost for 
the nuclear power stations affected is around 825 000 USD a year. The maximum cost for the problems 
caused by the mussels was reported in 1995 to be 600 000 USD for a shipping company 1.5 million USD for 
a single factory, 3.7 million USD for a water treatment facility and 6 million USD for a nuclear power station. 
The total cost over 10 years is estimated to be 3.1 billion USD for the power industry and 5 billion in total. 
Furthermore are the mussels destroying existing ecosystems as they filtrate the water so effectively for 
phytoplankton that the food source is removed from other organisms. By removing the phytoplankton 
present in the water the zebra mussels also increase the light levels and are thus promoting a nuisance 
bloom among algae. Furthermore they are also competing with native organisms about the surface where 
they grow and are thus reducing biodiversity by killing off local organisms such as clams and crayfish. The 
zebra mussels keep spreading and have spread over whole North America, from Canada to New Orleans 
and from Oklahoma to the Hudson River. [6] 
3.4 Solutions 
In order to stop the aquatic non-ingenious species (ANS) from spreading via ballast water the International 
Maritime Organization under the United Nations (IMO) has introduced the convention “The International 
Convention for the Control and Management of Ships’ Ballast Water and Sediments” in 2004, which is also 
referred to as the IMO Ballast water management convention. In the convention it was stated that it would 
go into effect a year after it had been ratified by at least 30 member states, with 35% of the global tonnage. 
This happened in September 2016. Besides the IMO BWM convention the US and more specifically the US 
Coastguard (USCG) has also introduced a new set of rules which are intended to be implemented in two

stricter set of guidelines at 2020. Currently, however, these plans has been postponed since it is impossible

3.4.1 Current Solutions 
Currently ships are using ballast water exchange (BWE) in order to reduce the risk of spreading ANS. BWE is 
the method used the most in order to reduce the risk of spreading ANS via ballast water. BWE is carried out 
by exchanging the ballast water taken in at port or in coastal areas with water from open sea. By doing so 
the change of transferring ANS is reduced, because only a few organisms from coastal regions are capable 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
of surviving at open and vice versa. Furthermore the amount of microorganism per m3 is lower at open sea 
than at shore and thus it is also possible to reduce the total amount of organisms present in the ballast 
tanks by carrying out BWE.   
3.4.2 Suggested solutions 
Besides BWE a range of new BWT systems has been created in recent years in order to comply with the 
IMO convention and the USCG specifications. These systems normally consist of multiple technologies 
which, when combined, are capable of reducing the amount of living organisms in the ballast water to a 
level below those required in the conventions.  
The treatment systems currently suggested has been made so they can be applied in all stages of the 
ballasting process, this could be while loading ballast water, in voyage, or at discharge of the ballast water. 
Currently the main technologies for ballast water treatment consist of a pretreatment process where the 
water is separated by filtration which might be aided by a coagulation- or flocculation-agent, after the pre 
treatment the ballast water undergoes either a physical treatment which could consist of UV radiation, 
deoxygenation, Inert gas or Nitrogen injection, ultrasound, fine filtration or heat treatment. Another 
possibility is that the ballast water is treated chemically by chlorination, electrochlorination, ozonation, 
chlorine dioxide, per acetic acid or another active substance. 
Hydro cyclone 
An easy and cheap way to separate larger particles and water from each other could be by using a hydro 
clone, the particles and water enters the hydro cyclone together and a circular flow is created this forces a 
separation based on density based on centrifugation and thus it would be possible to remove a large part of 
present particles before other treatment methods is introduced.    
Cyclonic separation has been proposed as a relatively simple and inexpensive way of removing larger 
particles and organisms from ballast water. Water and particles enter the hydrocyclone tangentially, thus 
setting up a circular fl ow. They are then drawn through tangential slots and are accelerated into the 
separation chamber. 
Centrifugal action tosses particles heavier than the water to the perimeter of the separation chamber. The 
solids gently drop along the perimeter and end up in the calm collection chamber of the separator. Solids 
may be periodically purged or continuously extracted from the separator. However, cyclonic separation of 
organisms with a specifi c gravity similar to that of water is limited which is valid for many plankton taxa. 
Therefore, some BWMS use the hydrocyclone as a preseparator which is followed by a fi ltration unit 
thereby enhancing the performance of the fi ltration unit. 
Filtration 
By intuition it might make the most sense to simply filter the ballast water before it enters the ballast tanks 
in order to insure no organisms are added to the tank in the first place and thus the problem with ANS in 
the ballast water would be solved. A problem to this approach is however the amount of ballast water 
needed and the speed with which it should be added to the ship and thus the needed flow through pose a 
problem to pure filtration. 
At current various filter method has been proposed and is being developed including disk filters, mesh and 
wedge-wine filters, while ultra filtration has not yet been introduced or tested with the huge volumes 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
required for it to work in regards to ballast water treatment. For the various filters the capability to remove 
particles larger than the filter pores lies at a level of 95-98% and furthermore a part of particles smaller 
than the mesh size might also be removed, which means the filters are highly effective. Some filtration 
systems use a combination of filters where larger particles are removed at coarse filter in order to increase 
the efficiency of the second and finer filter. It is however not assumed to be possible to reach the D-2 
standard with filtration alone why it needs to be combined with other technology. In regards to 
maintenance most filters are capable of carrying out an automatically backwash when needed in order to 
stay operational. As the filtration takes place at the location where the ballast water is loaded onto the ship 
it doesn’t matter that the collected organism is send back into the sea since the ship has not started its 
journey yet and thus is the water disposed into the same environment as where it was collected. [3] 
Ultraviolet Radiation 
Ultraviolet (UV) radiation is already at present day used in order to sterilize and purify water in a various 
range of applications. UV-radiation works by causing photochemical reactions in biological components 
such as nucleic acids (DNA, RNA) and proteins. The photochemical reaction makes the molecules 
degenerate and thus kills the organisms. The lower the UV-wavelengths are the more efficient is the 
treatment generally speaking, however it should be noticed that a problem with short wavelength is that 
their transmission through water is also reduced and that the performance might furthermore be affected 
of bubbles and various organic material present in the water. Furthermore does the effectiveness of the 
UV-treatment depends heavily on the pigmentation, size and morphology.  This means that while viruses 
and bacteria require small doses algae requires large doses due to their size. An disadvantage in relation to 
that could also be the possibility that small organisms would be capable to pass the UV-tube in the shadow 
of large organisms and thus would be able to survive the treatment, which might also be the case if the 
range of the UV-radiation decreases due to turbulence in the water passing the tube. Furthermore some 
organisms are able to repair themselves after exposure to the UV-light. A way to reduce these problems 
might however be to treat the water both while its added to the ballast tank and while it is discharged from 
the ballast tank. [3] Which is currently the method used by all commercial system using UV-radiation as a 
treatment method. Furthermore the effects of UV-treatment does not show immediately after treatment 
and thus it is hard to prove an effective system is installed and working when water is treated with UV-
radition at discharge of the ballast water [3].  
Deoxygenation 
Another way to reduce the amount of organism present in the ballast water is to reduce the oxygen 
concentration in the water. It is possible to reduce the amount oxygen in different ways. It is possible to 
reduce the oxygen content in water by adding various nutrients, glucose or a reducing agent which could 
be sulphide, another possibility could be a vacuum chamber or by adding an inert gas to the ballast water. 
[7] Studies however showed that adding nutrients or glucose facilitated an increased growth in bacteria

Electroclorination  
The use of electrochlorination as a means of preventing marine growth is well known. Electrochlorination is 
used on board so that the active substances are generated from the ballast water taken on board (no 
storage tank of chemicals) and this may either be done in a side stream or in the full ballast water stream of 
a vessel. Electrolyzers usually consist of a number of reactor cells arranged in series. 
A minimum salinity is needed for its effi cient use, in freshwater and lower brackish ballast water uptake 
zones marine water may be pumped into the line from a previously filled ballast tank to reach the required 
minimum salinity. 
 
3.2.3 Solution from BAWAT 
BAWAT’s ballast water treatment system is an in voyage system which means it doesn’t carry out the 
ballast water treatment as the tank is filled or emptied, but instead treats the ballast water while the ship is 
travelling between ports. The main treatment carried out is pasteurization. Which is currently applied using 
a temperature above 72OC and a holding time of 75 seconds, BAWAT however believes that it will be 
possible to reduce both holding time and temperature in the future.  
Depending on which temperature there are chosen for the pasteurization it is needed to heat the water for 
certain amounts of time until the amount of living organisms present in the water is assumed to be dead. 
After the pasteurization, nitrogen is added to the water in order to strip oxygen out and thus either 
eliminate the living organisms which did not react to the heat treatment or at least reduce their growth 
possibilities by restricting their access to oxygen. Furthermore is the addition of nitrogen gas to the water 
creating a cloud of bubbles when the water is reinjected into the tank which insures that the tank is well 
mixed. Furthermore is the gas bubbles which moves up through the tank also insuring that mixing takes 
place in the other compartment and thus reducing the risk of dead volumes.  
The system has been depicted at Figure 5 and is an in voyage system which means it is not possible to treat 
all the ballast water at the same time, instead water is pumped from the top of the ballast tank, down to 
the a heat exchanger where hot vapor or cooling liquid from the engine is it is used to heat the ballast 
water to a temperature above 68 OC.

5. Experimental  
In this section the preparations needed to be carried out in order to being able to collect data as well as the 
process in regards to collecting data will be described. 
5.1 Physical specifications of the pilot plant  
BAWATs pilot plant consists of a tank which is shaped as an L in order to create a situation similar to the 
one present within standard ballast tanks. The reason that ballast tanks is normally L-shaped is that it is 
standard practice to let the ballast tanks be situated outside the cargo hull, in order to being able to utilize 
the space which is present when a cargo ship has a double hull.  
On Figure 14 a cross section of a cargo ship can be seen, where the part of the ballast tanks that the 
BAWAT test system is simulating has been marked with red dots.    
 
Figure 14: Typical ballast tanks on ships, which the BAWAT test tank has been created to simulate. 
BAWATs ballast water treatment system consists, as earlier mentioned, of a pasteurization unit where 
water is pumped from the top of the tank into a heat exchanger where the water is first heated carrying 
out heat exchange by using the water leaving the pasteurization unit afterwards it is heated to the intended 
temperature by using of excess energy from the engine or the exhaust gas. Currently the water is heated till 
it reaches a temperature of 72O Celsius, when the water has reached 72o degrees Celsius it is hold in a tank 
at 75 seconds after which it is lead through the heat exchanger and thus cooled after which nitrogen is 
added in order to strip the water for oxygen. BAWAT is however continuously trying to reduce both holding 
time and pasteurization temperature in order to reduce the amount of energy used, the size for the 
treatment setup and the time needed to carry out the treatment. 
Furthermore it is possible to reduce the holding time or temperature by increasing the other. Thus if the 
temperature is increased the holding time can be reduced and if the holding time is increased it is possible 
to reduce the needed temperature.  
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
 
Figure 15: A schematic drawing of the BAWAT treatment system. Water is pumped from the top of the tank into a heat

water has reached 72o degrees Celsius it is hold in a tank at 75 seconds after which it is lead through the heat exchanger and 
thus cooled after which nitrogen is added in order to strip the water for oxygen. 
In the BAWAT test system a constant volumetric flow, v [m3 h-1], is pumped through the ballast tank system 
with a constant volume, V, [6 m3]. Typically ballast water tanks have a complex geometry with multiple 
compartments because of the structural setup between the two hulls where the ballast tank is situated. At 
the BAWAT test system it is possible to insert plates and thus creates up to 8 compartments in the tank.  
Furthermore it is also possible to increase or decrease the openings in these compartment separators so 
that it is possible to investigate both which effect various configurations of compartments has, but also 
what effect it might have to increase the openings in the compartments.  
Larger openings between the compartments are assumed to increase the amount of water which is running 
in the opposite direction of the main flow and thus the time needed to carry out a full treatment is 
increased.

5.2 Experimental Preparations 
Before experiments could be conducted as planed a range of preparations had to be made, these 
preparations will be described in this section.  
5.3 Experimental Plan 
In regards to BAWAT’s test plant there exist a wide range of parameters which can be altered including the 
N2-flow, water-flow, amount of compartments and the restriction between each of the compartments. In 
this report however the focus will be on the effect that the amount of compartments, as well as the 
restrictions between each of the compartments, has on the time it takes to reach complete treatment as 
well as on the volume needed to be treated to reach a satisfactory level in regards to organisms alive pr m3 
as well as in regards to oxygen concentration in the ballast water.   
In the experimental tests the N2 flow used has been set to approximate 7L/min while the liquid flow where 
initially set to be 6m3/h, but for some of the dataset it was reduced to be 5 m3/h because of a lack of 
energy in order to insure that the pasteurization process, where the water is needed to be kept above 60 
OC for 60 seconds, was carried out as intended and thus all living organisms present in the pasteurized 
water where eliminated. 
In order to determine the effect of various amounts of compartments, as well as the impact of different 
restriction levels, experimental tests were carried out. In the experiments the Nitrogen as well as the water 
flow was held constant while various configurations of compartments and restriction levels was 
investigated. It was choosen to look into configurations containing 3, 5 and 7 compartments while the 
restriction level between each of the compartments was changed between 96.5%, 93% and 89%. In total 
this provides 9 different setups which can be seen in Table  3.

5.4.1 Laboratory Calibration of HOBO Dissolved Oxygen Loggers 
The calibration of the HOBO oxygen loggers, seen on Figure 23 was carried out as described in user manual 
for the oxygen loggers “HOBO Dissolved Oxygen Logger (U26-001) Manual 15603-B MAN U26x”. 
Since the loggers will be used to measure oxygen in oxygen low environments it is needed to carry out a 
two step calibration, where the loggers are calibrated at a 100% saturated environment as well as in an 
oxygen depleted environment. 
For the saturated environment ordinary tap water was used, the room temperature in the laboratory was 
determined to be 22.3 degree Celsius and the tap water was heated till it reached the same temperature. 
After the tap water had reached room temperature it was used to wet a small sponge which was inserted 
into the calibration boot (Figure 23) after which the logger was inserted so the calibration boot was 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
covering the oxygen sensor and approximately 1 cm of the body of the logger. The logger was left in this 
position for approximately 15-20 minutes till it was no longer possible to observe any changes in the DO 
value given by the logger; this value was then saved and used to calibrate the logger.  
In order to create an oxygen depleted environment. A 0.002 M solution of sodium thiosulfate in water was 
made by mixing 10g ܰܽଶܵଶܱଷ in 2L water. When the solution reached room temperature the loggers 
where calibrated using the solution. 
This mixture did however not reach 0 in regards to dissolved oxygen concentration and thus the calibration 
had to be done again using sodium sulfite to create an oxygen depleted environment instead.  During the 
calibration the air pressure was determined to be 1033.1 hPa, by using the data provided by DMI for 
Jægersborg.  
For each calibration 20 minutes where used, at which it was seen that the DO values obtained from the 
loggers was no longer changing or that the change in values was so small that it was assumed it could be 
neglected.

5.5 Data Collection 
A typical day with data collection would start at around 07:00 in Hundested harbor where the system 
would be started up and configured. The compartment separators as well as the sensors would typically 
already be installed by the technical staff at the location. 
Sensors were placed within compartments by attaching them to the compartment separators. Thus has all 
the sensors been placed in the front of their respective compartments. In order to account for possible 
dead zones present within the compartment, the sensors were placed as far away from the openings in the 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
restrictions between the compartments as possible. Thus the sensors were placed at the locations assumed 
to be the ones where the lowest amount of mixing would take places within each of the compartments. 
The sensors in compartment 1 and the sensor DO-0 in the last compartment however diverge from this 
rule. The sensors in compartment 1 could not be placed in the front of the compartment as there was no 
possibility to attach them to anything. Thus the sensors were instead placed in the back of compartment 1. 
In regards to the sensor DO-0 it is not actually placed in the last compartment, but instead in the outlet 
from the compartment before the water undergoes treatment.  
At first water would be added to the ballast water test tank either from the harbor or from one of the 
Danish Hydraulic Institutes (DHI) test tanks or a mixture of water from the harbor and water from the DHI-
test tank. DHI-water is different from the water from the harbor because of an increased level of algae is 
present in this water and was typically used for the tests where pasteurization was included so that it was 
possible to detect a larger change in algae concentrations as a result of the treatment compared to if only 
the natural concentration had been present in the first place.  
While the tank was getting filled with water, the heating system for the pasteurization would be turned 
online and started to heat up. Furthermore it would be checked that it was possible to get a pressure of 
around 5 bar over the Nitrogen-membrane so that it was possible to gain a sufficient flow of nitrogen for 
the oxygen stripping process.  
After  these steps had been completed the water would be circulated in the tank with the pump in order to 
reach a similar oxygen level at the whole tank so that the various oxygen sensors would get a starting 
position as similar as possible to each other. After approximately 30 minutes of circulation the nitrogen 
flow would be turned on and data would be collected over the next 3-4 hours. 
Furthermore it was intended to run the pasteurization process along with the oxygen stripping process. 
Due to a failure of the heating source this was however not always possible. If the pasteurization process 
was running while collecting data, samples would acquired every 20 minutes and analyzed for the amount 
of algae present using 10Cells, while an experimental setup from BW Monitor would also be used to collect 
data in flow, in regards to the amount of chlorophyll present in the water.

7. Modeling  
By using the mass balances setup in models described in “4.2 Theory of Modeling” the following differential 
equations seen in Figure 34 was setup for the first obtained data series the 7 compartment, 96.5% 
restriction series seen in Figure 26.    
 
Figure 34: Initial differential equations used to create a model for oxygen removal within the different compartments. The 
equations denoted with dcl1-dcl7 is describing the oxygen concentration in the water phase while the equations denoted with 
dcg1-dcg7 is describing the oxygen concentration in the gas phase. ql is the flow through the compartments, cl1-cl6 is the 
concentration of oxygen in the respective compartments, while cl0 is the inlet concentration of oxygen in the water which is set 
to be 0. Vl is the volume of the compartments, while kla1-kla6 is the kla value used for the different compartments, generally for 
the different configurations the kla value is 6 h- in the first compartment and is then reduced to 0 in the last compartments. 
cl_star is the saturation level for oxygen. qg is the flow of gas through the system, cg1-cg7 is the oxygen concentration in the gas 
phase in the various compartments, while cg0 is the inlet concentration of gas which is set to be 0.5 vol %. Vg is the volume of 
gas set to be 1m3 in total and evenly divided out on the various compartments.  
From the differential equations seen in Figure 34 it is possible to obtain the model seen in Figure 35.  
==PAGE:52==

Figure 35: The actual data plotted vs. the simulated models for the data series with the configuration of 7 compartments, 96.5% 
restriction. The experimental data set is named Ci (i= compartment number) act and is drawn as continuous lines, while the 
simulated models is drawn with dotted lines and called Ci sim. 
On Figure 35 it can be seen that when back mixing is not taken into account the models fit very well at in 
the start for the first 3 compartments but quickly diverge from the actual data sets as the lines from the 
modeled data continuous to drop while the slope of the lines obtained experimentally starts to flatten. This 
means that the oxygen level is dropping quicker for the model data than in reality. The reason for this 
inconsistency between model and data is believed to be the back mixing which means that water from the 
later compartments return back to the prior compartments and thus increase the oxygen level in those 
compartments. Thus is a back mixing equation stage added to the differential equations seen in Figure 34 
and the equations displayed in Figure 36 is obtained.  
 
Figure 36: Differential equations used to create the model with added back mixing stage. bm2-bm7 is used to set a percentage of

8. Discussion 
8.1 Measurements in the Last Compartment 
From the results obtained with configurations of 3 and 5 compartments it can be seen that the DO-0 sensor 
always measured an oxygen level which was higher than the other sensors placed within in the last 
compartment.  
This might be caused by the fact that the sensor is simply placed in the pipes and thus not in the actual 
compartment as the other sensors. But the cause could also be that either the sensor malfunctioned or 
that, as earlier mentioned, a gas pocket in the pipes before the sensor was building up during the test 
series and when the oxygen level in the water got low was releasing oxygen back into the water.  
In order to test if the DO-0 sensor was behaving differently than the other sensors the DO-1, DO-2, DO-3 
and DO-0 sensors was placed within the same environment which was afterwards depleted of oxygen using 
sodium sulfite and it was observed that the DO-0 sensor was taking a longer time to reach 0 than the other 
three sensors, in an attempt to reach better results for the DO-0 sensor the DO-0 and DO-3 sensors was 
switched at the 4th of may. 
8.2 Measurements Temperature Dependencies 
As the data collected has been collected over a period of a month during the Danish spring, the water 
temperature of the water started at around 4 OC and ended at about 14 OC when the last experimental 
series was run. The amount of oxygen that water can store depends on temperature and this increase in 
temperature might have had a significant impact on the measurements carried out. Furthermore has some 
of the test series been carried out with heat, while some has been carried out without any heat due to that 
the heat source was mal functioning. The test series where heat was applied has most probably also 
behaved differently from the test series where no heat was applied and thus this might cause that it is not 
possible to compare the various test series.  
8.3 Mechanical force of the reinjection of the treated water 
The water together with the nitrogen is being reinjected into the system with huge pressure this creates a 
lot of mixing and makes, especially; the small compartments in the 7 compartment configuration behave as 
one compartment rather than as 2 or 3 separate compartments. When the setup is scaled up this effect will 
most probably disappear due to the larger volumes and distances in the actual ballast tanks where nozzles 
will be installed.   
8.4 Modeling  
It can be seen from the displayed graphs that it was possible to get the various configurations modeled so 
that the model and the experimental data was fitting each other. It can however be seen from the figures 
that the initial compartments fits much better to the model than the later compartments. This might be 
due to lagging understanding of what is actually going on within the pilot tank and how the geometry of the 
tank is affecting the back mixing. Furthermore it is possible to see that the mixing between compartment 1 
and compartment 2 for all models are above 40-50% of the main flow which indicates that these two 
compartments probably cannot be described as separate compartments, but instead should be considered 
one compartment. The reason for this is most probably that the nozzle injecting the water and gas into the 
==PAGE:61==
61 
 
system after treatment is doing that with such a force that does not only affect the behavior of the gas-
liquid mix in compartment 1 but also in compartment 2 and for the 7 compartments setup also 
compartment 3. Furthermore it might be interesting to explore the possibilities of combining the model 
without back mixing, which seemed to be the most accurate right after the treatment started, with the 
model including back mixing which made the best fit at the lower oxygen levels in the end.  
9. Conclusion 
It has been possible to obtain experimental data from BAWAT’s test setup and from these data create a 
model which was working well for the setups with 3 and 5 compartments and partly for the setups with 7 
compartments. It is possible to see that a rather large amount of back mixing took place between the 
compartments which most probably is because of the small size of the compartments together with the 
high pressure with which the water is returned back into the setup in compartment 1. 
Furthermore it was seen that while the model including back mixing was most accurate at low O2 
concentrations, the model which disregarded back mixing was more accurate initially at high O2 
concentrations. Thus it could be interesting trying to combine the two models so that initially back mixing is 
disregarded but as oxygen concentration falls back mixing is introduced to the model.  
10. Future Work  
It would be interesting to look into the temperature dependencies on the collected data. It would make 
sense to carry out all the data series displayed in this report with and without the pasteurization process 
running so that it would be possible to see the change in behavior of the slopes describing the oxygen level 
in the different compartments. Furthermore would it have been better for the data validity that the 
experiment had run over a period where the water temperature of the water in Hundested Habor did not 
change over time. As the data displayed in this report was collected during the Danish spring from the end 
of April and throughout May the water temperature was increasing from around 4OC in the beginning and 
for the last data series the temperature was 14OC. As the oxygen saturation level in water is dependent on 
the temperature this might have caused quite a decrease in the amount of oxygen present in the water and 
thus have affected the results.
