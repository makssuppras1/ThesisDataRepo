Abstract
Maritime inspection systems require robust automated methods for detecting corrosion
defects across diverse vessel environments and lighting conditions. While deep learning
approaches show promise for such applications, deploying models trained on synthetic
datasets to realôÙworld maritime scenarios presents substantial domain adaptation chalôÙ
lenges due to significant visual disparities between controlled synthetic environments and
actual inspection conditions.
This thesis develops and evaluates a domain adaptation framework for transferring corôÙ
rosion detection capabilities from synthetic maritime environments to realôÙworld ship inôÙ
spection scenarios.
A sevenôÙphase SelfôÙEnsembling Generative Adversarial Network
(SEôÙGAN) [1] inspired architecture is implemented with enhanced training strategies to
bridge the visual gap between synthetic corrosion datasets and authentic maritime inôÙ
spection imagery. The framework integrates style transfer networks, adversarial training,
and progressive selfôÙsupervised learning for domain alignment. Four comprehensive exôÙ
periments evaluate both 3ôÙclass fineôÙgrained corrosion classification (seam, edge, spot)
and binary corrosion detection tasks using VGG16ED and DeepLabV3 ResNet50 archiôÙ
tectures.
Results demonstrate significant performance variations based on task complexity and
dataset characteristics. Binary segmentation achieves substantially better domain adapôÙ
tation success, with mean Intersection over Union scores reaching 42.99% on realôÙworld
test data. In contrast, 3ôÙclass segmentation proves considerably more challenging, achievôÙ
ing 26.09% mIoU, highlighting the difficulty of fineôÙgrained corrosion type discrimination
across domains. The study verifies that extreme intraôÙdomain variability within the tarôÙ
get dataset, characterized by diverse lighting conditions, surface reflectivity, and imaging
parameters, significantly constrains adaptation effectiveness.
The research identifies critical limitations in current domain adaptation approaches when
applied to highly variable realôÙworld inspection scenarios. The multiôÙstage pipeline, while
theoretically sound, proves susceptible to error propagation across its phases, with subôÙ
stantial trainingôÙvalidation performance gaps (up to 34.6% mIoU) indicating insufficient
domain generalization. These findings underscore the need for more streamlined adapôÙ
tation methodologies and a more enriched real dataset that better captures realôÙworld
environmental variability.
This work contributes valuable insights into the practical challenges of deploying deep
learning models for maritime corrosion inspection, demonstrating that binary detection
approaches may be more suitable for immediate industrial deployment than fineôÙgrained
classification systems. The identified limitations provide a foundation for future research
directions, including simplified adaptation pipelines, domain stratification strategies, and
enhanced real data preôÙprocessing techniques to achieve more robust crossôÙdomain corôÙ
rosion detection capabilities.

Introduction
Corrosion constitutes a fundamental and economically critical challenge that significantly
impacts the maritime industry today.
The global economic ramifications of this pheôÙ
nomenon extend to numerous industries, with the maritime sector bearing a disproporôÙ
tionate burden due to the aggressive marine environment. Statistics for ship hulls show
that around 90% of ship failures are attributed to corrosion, including corrosion fatigue [2].
This underscores the critical importance of effective corrosion detection and management
strategies in maritime operations.
In marine vessels, particularly tanker ships and cargo vessels, corrosion poses critical
threats to structural integrity, operational safety, and regulatory compliance. The complex
geometry of ship structures, combined with varying environmental conditions and mateôÙ
rial compositions, creates a diverse landscape of corrosion manifestations that challenge
traditional inspection methodologies.
Traditional corrosion inspection in marine vessels relies heavily on manual visual assessôÙ
ment performed by trained surveyors. While these conventional approaches have served
the industry for decades, they suffer from inherent limitations including subjective interôÙ
pretation, limited accessibility to confined spaces such as ballast tanks, timeôÙintensive
procedures, and inconsistent coverage across vessel compartments [3]. Moreover, the
intermittent nature of scheduled inspections can allow significant corrosion progression
between assessment periods, potentially compromising vessel safety and operational efôÙ
ficiency.
The advent of deep learning and computer vision technologies has opened new possibilôÙ
ities for automated corrosion detection and assessment. Convolutional neural networks
(CNNs) and subsequently UôÙNets have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in image
segmentation tasks, enabling pixelôÙlevel classification of corrosion regions with unpreceôÙ
dented accuracy [4]. However, the application of these technologies to maritime corrosion
detection faces unique challenges related to domain variability, data scarcity, and the subôÙ
jective nature of corrosion classification.

This thesis addresses the potential for unsupervised domainôÙadapted semantic segmenôÙ

the SEGAN (SelfôÙEnsembling Generative Adversarial Network) model architecture. The

ãÂ MultiôÙClass to Binary Segmentation Analysis: Investigate the effectiveness of difôÙ

(seam, edge, and spot corrosion) and progressing to simplified binary classificaôÙ

Image Segmentation
Image segmentation is the process of dividing an image into smaller parts, or regions, that
are more meaningful and easier to analyze. These regions typically group together pixels
that share similar properties, such as color, intensity, or texture. The goal is to simplify the
image in a way that makes it easier for further tasks, such as object detection or scene
understanding.

There are several common approaches to segmentation, each based on different ideas:
ãÂ Thresholding: Separates pixels into regions by comparing their intensity values to
a threshold. This is one of the simplest and fastest methods.
ãÂ EdgeôÙbased methods: Detect places in the image where pixel values change

ãÂ RegionôÙbased methods: Start from seed points and grow regions by merging
neighboring pixels that are similar.
ãÂ ClusteringôÙbased methods: Group pixels into clusters based on similarity in feaôÙ

ãÂ Fuzzy methods: Allow pixels to belong to more than one region with varying deôÙ

ãÂ Neural networkãbased methods: Use models like Hopfield or Kohonen networks

As noted in [7], no single method works best for all types of images. The choice of segôÙ
mentation technique depends on the specific characteristics of the image and the goals
of the application.
2.2
Semantic Segmentation
Semantic segmentation is a fundamental task in computer vision that involves assignôÙ
ing a semantic label to each pixel in an image. Unlike object detection or classification,
which produce coarseôÙlevel outputs, semantic segmentation generates dense, pixelôÙwise
predictions, making it especially valuable for applications requiring precise spatial unôÙ
derstanding, such as autonomous driving, medical imaging, and scene interpretation [7].
Modern solutions often adopt encoderôÙdecoder architectures to effectively manage the
complexity of this task.

2.3
Transfer Learning
Transfer learning is a machine learning technique where a model is initially trained on
one dataset or task (the source domain) and then applied to another, related dataset or
task (the target domain). In this process, the model uses the knowledge gained from the
source domain to improve its performance on the target domain, even though the two
domains may differ in some way.
For example, a model trained to recognize objects in one type of image may be adapted to
classify objects in a different type of image, even though the underlying tasks are similar.
The main idea is that, although the source and target domains may not be identical, they
share enough similarities to allow knowledge transfer. [14]
Domain adaptation (DA) is a specific application of transfer learning where the model
is adapted to address differences in data distributions between the source and target
domains.
2.4
Domain Adaptation
Domain adaptation is a specific type of transfer learning that focuses on adapting a model
trained on one domain (the source domain) to work well on a related but different domain
(the target domain). In domain adaptation, the target task remains the same as the source
task, but the data distribution between the source and target domains differs. [14]
The classification of domain adaptation depends on the availability of labeled data. In unôÙ
supervised domain adaptation, the model has access to labeled data from the source
domain but only unlabeled data from the target domain. SemiôÙsupervised domain adapôÙ
tation uses a small set of labeled target data along with labeled source data. Supervised
domain adaptation, on the other hand, includes labeled data from both domains. Beyond
these, weakly supervised domain adaptation uses target domain data with limited or
imprecise annotations, such as imageôÙlevel labels or bounding boxes instead of full segôÙ
mentation masks. SourceôÙfree domain adaptation assumes access only to a trained
source model, without any source data, and adapts the model to the target domain using
unlabeled target data. Finally, continual and continuous domain adaptation handle
evolving domains over time, where the model must adapt to new target data incremenôÙ
tally, often without access to earlier source data. [15]
Domain adaptation can be further categorized based on the adaptation space in which
it operates. These spaces include the input space, the feature space (latent representaôÙ
tions within the network), and the output space. Additionally, some approaches integrate

multiple adaptation spaces, forming a category known as hybrid adaptation. This clasôÙ

Input space adaptation focuses on changing the input data to make the source and

appearance of images to reduce differences. Feature space adaptation concentrates
on modifying the latent representations that the network learns, ensuring that important
patterns remain consistent across domains. Output space adaptation improves the final
predictions of the model, often using techniques like adversarial training or selfôÙtraining to
refine results. Hybrid approaches leverage multiple adaptation spaces simultaneously to
enhance performance and robustness.

2.6.2
Training Challenges
Despite their success, GANs are notoriously difficult to train due to several fundamental
challenges:
ãÂ Mode Collapse: The generator may learn to produce only a limited set of outputs
regardless of the input noise, leading to poor sample diversity. This occurs when
the generator exploits weaknesses in the discriminator rather than capturing the full
data distribution.
ãÂ Gradient Vanishing or Exploding: In early GAN formulations, if the discriminaôÙ
tor becomes too accurate, the gradients used to update the generator may vanish,
stalling training. Conversely, unstable architectures may lead to exploding gradiôÙ
ents, especially in deep networks.
ãÂ NonôÙConvergence: Due to the adversarial nature of training, GANs may fail to
reach a stable equilibrium. Instead, the generator and discriminator enter oscilôÙ
latory dynamics, making convergence unpredictable or impossible without careful
regularization and tuning.

ãÂ Regularizing gradients: Adding penalties to the discriminatorãs gradient norm enôÙ
sures that gradients neither vanish nor explode, which stabilizes the training proôÙ

data.

and more complete latent spaces. Bidirectional GANs (BiGANs) [40] and Adversarially
Learned Inference (ALI) [41] introduce encoders to learn inverse mappings from data to
latent space, supporting more meaningful inference and sample quality. The Adversarial

Related Work in Unsupervised Domain
Adaptation
In Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA), approaches are often categorized based on
the adaptation space they target: input space, feature space, or output space. Each
space focuses on different aspects of the data to reduce the domain gap. This section
presents representative approaches for each category and discusses how they address
the challenges of domain adaptation. The methods selected for the experiments were
chosen based on the availability of implementation and the relevance of their underlying
methodologies.
3.1
Input Space Adaptation
Unsupervised domain adaptation in the input space mainly focuses on changing the viôÙ
sual style of images to reduce domain differences. Schwonberg et al. [15] identify three
main techniques used in this adaptation space: Style Transfer, Data Augmentation, and
Image Mixing, among others. These methods help models generalize better by making
the source and target domains more visually similar.
Chang et al.
(2019) [62] introduce the Domain Invariant Structure Extraction (DISE)
method, which aims to separate an imageãs structure from its texture. The idea is that
structural information is more important for segmentation, while texture varies between
domains. DISE learns to extract these components separately using a common encoder
for structure and domainôÙspecific encoders for texture. This allows images from one doôÙ
main to be restructured with the texture of another while keeping the original layout. The
method uses least squares GAN (LSGAN) [63] and domain adversarial training to make
structure features more invariant across domains.
Xu et al. (2023) [1] propose a selfôÙensembling approach called SEôÙGAN to improve doôÙ
main adaptation for semantic segmentation. Their method uses a CycleGAN [64] based
style transfer network to adjust sourceôÙdomain images so they look more like targetôÙ
domain images while keeping their original structure. The model is then trained on both
the original and styleôÙtransferred images to help it generalize better across domains. SEôÙ
GAN also includes a studentôÙteacher learning setup. The student network learns from
both real and styleôÙtransferred images, while the teacher network helps guide its predicôÙ
tions. A consistency loss ensures that the studentãs output remains close to the teacher's,
making adaptation smoother.
MelasôÙKyriazi and Manrai (2021) [65] propose PixMatch, which uses consistency training
and pseudoôÙlabeling to adapt models to a new domain. A key part of PixMatch is image
mixing, where regions from source images are cut and pasted into target images (CutMix
[66]). This helps the model learn from mixedôÙdomain features while maintaining segmenôÙ
tation consistency. Compared to adversarial methods, PixMatch is simpler to implement,
more stable, and computationally efficient.
Kim and Byun (2020) [67] propose a method for domain adaptation by altering the texôÙ
tures in the source domain images using the StyleôÙswap style transfer algorithm. This
technique replaces the synthetic textures with random textures, creating a more diverse
set of images. This variation encourages the model to focus on learning features based
on shape and structure rather than texture. By training the model on both the original and

transformed images, it becomes more robust to texture changes and better able to adapt

3.2
Feature Space Adaptation
Feature space domain adaptation helps improve performance when the source and target
domains have different distributions. Since the feature spaces (the data right before the
classifier) of the two domains are different, models trained on the source domain may not
perform well on the target domain. These methods work by aligning the feature distribuôÙ
tions of both domains, so that the model learns to map data from both domains to similar
points in the feature space, making it easier for the model to adapt to the target domain.
[15]
The methodology proposed by Chen et al. (2020) [68] for feature space domain adaptation
focuses on using a generative adversarial network (GAN) to align the feature distributions
between source and target domains. The approach consists of two main components: a
segmentation network and a classification constrained discriminator (C/D). The segmenôÙ
tation network is divided into a feature generator and a predictor. The feature generaôÙ
tor extracts features from both the source and target domains, while the discriminator is
tasked with distinguishing between the features from each domain. To prevent the model
from learning unclear features, the discriminator is provided with additional classification
information. This guides the model to focus on features that are consistent across doôÙ
mains and useful for segmentation. The adversarial loss encourages the generator to
produce features that are indistinguishable between the two domains, thereby minimizôÙ
ing domain discrepancies. An auxiliary segmentation loss is applied to the discriminator,
ensuring it captures relevant feature structures for segmentation tasks without backpropaôÙ
gating this loss to the feature generator. The overall objective is to train the segmentation
network to learn domainôÙinvariant features while optimizing the discriminator to enhance
adversarial learning.
Toldo et al. (2020) [69] propose a method for unsupervised domain adaptation in semanôÙ
tic segmentation that focuses on aligning feature representations from source and target
domains through clustering, orthogonality, and sparsity constraints. Their approach utiôÙ
lizes a clustering objective to group features of the same semantic class together while
pushing apart those of different classes. This helps reduce the domain gap by ensuring
that both source and target domain features are aligned in the feature space. Additionally,
an orthogonality constraint is introduced to enforce that features from different semantic
classes remain orthogonal, minimizing interference between them, while features from the
same class are similar. This is achieved by measuring the similarity between feature vecôÙ
tors and class centroids. To further refine the feature representation, a sparsity constraint
is applied, which reduces the number of active channels for each class. This promotes
more focused feature representations, improving the effectiveness of clustering and regôÙ
ularizing the feature space. By combining these constraints with a standard supervised
loss, the method enables better domain alignment, making the model more effective for
semantic segmentation tasks across both domains, even without labeled target data.
Chen et al. in [70] propose the ROAD (Reality Oriented Adaptation) framework. Their
approach consists of two key components: Target Guided Distillation and SpatialôÙAware
Adaptation. Target Guided Distillation aims to reduce the bias introduced by training on
synthetic images, which often causes convolutional filters to overfit to the synthetic style.
To address this, they use a pretrained model trained on real images as a reference. The
segmentation model processes both synthetic and real images, while the real images are
also passed through the pretrained model. A distillation loss encourages the segmentaôÙ

tion modelãs feature representations to mimic those of the pretrained model, guiding it to
learn more robust convolutional filters that generalize better to realôÙworld urban scenes.
SpatialôÙAware Adaptation enhances feature alignment between the source (synthetic) and
target (real) domains by leveraging the spatial structure of urban scenes. Instead of perôÙ
forming global feature alignment, which can be ineffective due to variations in object apôÙ
pearance and scale, they divide images into predefined spatial regions and apply domain
adaptation at the region level. Since objects of the same category tend to appear in similar
spatial positions (e.g., roads at the bottom, sky at the top), this spatiallyôÙaware approach
ensures that features corresponding to similar semantic content are better aligned. The
adaptation process uses adversarial training, where a domain classifier learns to distinôÙ
guish between source and target features while the segmentation model is optimized to
make these features domainôÙinvariant.
3.3
Output Space Adaptation
Output space adaptation methods focus on adjusting the modelãs pixelôÙwise predictions to
improve performance on the target domain. Common techniques include selfôÙtraining and
adversarial learning, with other approaches like entropyôÙbased and consistency learning
also used to enhance adaptation. [15]
Along with the input space adaptation method discussed in Section 3.1, Chang et al.
(2019) [62] extend their DISE method to output space adaptation. They introduce an adôÙ
versarial loss at the output of the common encoder to ensure that the structure extracted
from the source and target domains remains consistent. At this stage, a pixelôÙwise clasôÙ
sifier is employed to predict the segmentation map for both source and target domain
images. This classifier utilizes the shared structure features from the common encoder
to generate the segmentation predictions. The adversarial loss is applied to minimize the
discrepancy between the source and target predictions, thus encouraging the model to
generalize better on the target domain.
Building on their earlier approach 3.1, Chang et al.
(2019) [1] use selfôÙtraining with
teacherôÙgenerated pseudoôÙlabels for output space adaptation. Instead of picking only the
most confident predictions, they use all pseudoôÙlabels from the teacher network, which
is already stable and reliable. These labels, created from the teacherãs predictions, help
train the student network by guiding its learning through a crossôÙentropy loss, improving
segmentation on targetôÙdomain images.
The PixMatch method (MelasôÙKyriazi & Manrai, 2021) [65] focuses on improving domain
adaptation by ensuring the modelãs predictions are consistent across different versions
of the same target image. Initially, the model generates pseudoôÙlabels for target images,
which are then modified through various transformations, such as data augmentations,
style transfer, and FourierôÙbased techniques. These modified images are passed back
into the model, and a loss function is used to encourage consistent predictions, regardless
of the image changes. Since the underlying content of the image remains the same, this
consistency helps the model better generalize across domain shifts, making the training
process more robust.
Kim and Byun (2020) [67] propose an output space adaptation method that focuses on
learning textureôÙinvariant features for semantic segmentation. Their approach is based
on two key stages. In the first stage, they diversify the textures in the source domain by
applying style transfer techniques to create a range of texture variations. This helps the
model to learn representations that focus on shape and structure rather than relying on
specific textures. The model is trained using both the original and the transformed images,

to the target domainãs texture using a selfôÙtraining approach. After training the model with
the textureôÙdiversified images, the authors generate pseudoôÙlabels for the target domain
images. These pseudoôÙlabels are used to further fineôÙtune the model, helping it adapt to
the target domainãs texture and improve segmentation accuracy. By iterating this process,
the model becomes better suited to the specific characteristics of the target domain, while
maintaining its ability to generalize across various textures.
3.4
Comparative Analysis and Limitations
The reviewed domain adaptation methods show different strengths and performance charôÙ
acteristics across benchmark datasets. SEôÙGAN demonstrates strong performance by
combining GANôÙbased style transfer with selfôÙensembling learning [1], significantly boostôÙ
ing adversarial training performance while enhancing model stability compared to tradiôÙ
tional adversarial approaches.
PixMatch [65] achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks through its consisôÙ
tency training framework. The method is simpler to implement, more stable, and more
memoryôÙefficient during training compared to adversarial approaches. DISE [62] shows
effectiveness in disentangling structure from texture, with experiments verifying its supeôÙ
riority over several stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart approaches.
Feature space methods like CCD [68] and UDA Cluster [69] provide strong theoretical
foundations through adversarial training and clustering constraints respectively. ROAD
[70] addresses syntheticôÙtoôÙreal adaptation through spatialôÙaware alignment, showing parôÙ
ticular strength in urban scene understanding.
Implementation complexity differs notably across approaches. PixMatch [65] and LTIR
[67] offer more straightforward implementations, while methods like DISE [62] and SEôÙ
GAN [1] require sophisticated architectures with multiple encoders and loss components.
Several common challenges emerge across methods. Most approaches focus primarôÙ
ily on visual or stylistic differences rather than semantic shifts. Training stability remains
an issue for adversarial methods, which SEôÙGAN addresses through its selfôÙensembling
framework that provides more stable training dynamics. Additionally, methods often reôÙ
quire careful hyperparameter tuning and may need domainôÙspecific adjustments for optiôÙ
mal performance.

Base Architecture Selection
The segmentation networks in this work were initially derived from the BaseNet and DeepLabôÙ

introduced to improve performance and correct limitations and bugs present in the origiôÙ

by their proven effectiveness in semantic segmentation tasks, particularly their ability to
handle multiôÙscale features essential for detecting subtle corrosion patterns in maritime
environments.
VGG16ôÙBased Maritime BaseNet Enhancements
The customized BaseNet architecture leverages a VGG16 backbone, adapted specifically
for maritime semantic segmentation. The backbone utilizes a standard VGG16 modified
with dilated convolutions (dilation=2) to preserve spatial resolution while expanding the
receptive field for better contextual understanding. The original fully connected layers
(FC6 and FC7) are replaced with 3û3 dilated convolutions (dilation=4) for better contextual
learning without the spatial information loss associated with traditional fully connected
layers.
A critical enhancement was the introduction of an Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP)
block with dilation rates of 6, 12, 18, and 24 to handle scale variance and improve multiôÙ
scale context aggregation. This module proved essential for capturing the inherent variôÙ
ability in corrosion manifestation scales. The architecture also incorporates a custom
multiôÙscale summation strategy applied to enhance the extraction of maritime textures,
which often exhibit subtle and fineôÙgrained patterns that can be easily lost in standard
feature aggregation approaches. Additionally, corrections were applied to the classifierãs
forward pass to ensure proper propagation of activations, addressing bugs present in the
original implementation.
ResNet101ôÙBased DeepLabMulti Enhancements
The DeepLabMulti variant builds upon a dilated ResNet101 backbone and incorporates
several improvements for enhanced robustness in maritime environments. The backbone
employs ResNet101 with dilations applied to layers 3 and 4, preserving resolution while
expanding the receptive field to capture larger contextual information crucial for corrosion
detection. The architecture supports both singleôÙscale and multiôÙscale prediction heads,
allowing flexible experimentation and performance optimization across different scenarôÙ
ios.
The classifier is specifically adjusted for 3ôÙclass segmentation targeting the maritime corôÙ
rosion types: Seam, Edge, and Spot corrosion.
Critical implementation issues were
addressed, including errors related to missing tensor operations in the residual blocks
that were causing training instability and convergence problems. An optional supervision
branch from layer 5 is included to improve gradient flow during training, which proves parôÙ
ticularly beneficial for the deeper network architecture. The training strategy employs a
differential learning rate policy with a rate of 1û for the backbone and 10û for the clasôÙ
sifier layers to accelerate convergence while maintaining stable feature learning in the
pretrained backbone.

VGG16ED (Figure 5.1) uses a straightforward encoderôÙdecoder design tailored for seôÙ

Encoder (Backbone)
The encoder is based on a preôÙtrained VGG16 convolutional neural network that acts
as a feature extractor. Input images of size 672 û 376 pixels pass through VGG16, but
only the final feature maps from Block 5 (512 channels at 1/32 resolution) are fed into

convolutional layers. The VGG16 weights are initialized from ImageNet preôÙtraining and
fineôÙtuned during training to adapt to the corrosion segmentation task.
Decoder
The decoder consists of four decoder blocks that progressively reduce the number of
channels following the pattern 512 ã512 ã256 ã128 ã64. Each decoder block
consists of a 3û3 convolution, batch normalization, ReLU activation (configurable: ReLU,
LeakyReLU, or ELU), and dropout with progressive reduction (rates: 0.1, 0.1, 0.05, 0.03
for default dropout_rate=0.1) to avoid overôÙregularization in deeper layers.

A separate classification layer with a single 1û1 convolution maps the 64ôÙchannel decoder

Final Model Architecture
DeepLabV3ôÙResNet50 (Figure 5.3) employs an advanced encoderôÙdecoder architecture
with atrous spatial pyramid pooling (ASPP) and skip connections, specifically optimized for
corrosion defect segmentation. The ResNet50 backbone was chosen as it yielded better
segmentation results from the Resnet101, with less overfitting on the relatively specialôÙ
ized corrosion dataset, while also providing computational efficiency for betterôÙoptimized
models for the specific task of binary and multiôÙclass corrosion segmentation.
Encoder (Backbone)
The encoder utilizes a preôÙtrained ResNet50 convolutional neural network as the feature
extraction backbone. Input images of size 512 û 384 pixels pass through the ResNet50
architecture with the following key stages. The initial 7û7 convolution with stride 2 (Conv1)
reduces the resolution to H/2 û W/2, followed by the Res2_x block producing 256 chanôÙ
nels at H/4ûW/4 resolution. This is further downsampled through Res3_x to H/8ûW/8,
yielding 512 lowôÙlevel feature channels. Subsequent stages include Res4_x with 1024
channels at H/16 û W/16, and the final Res5_x block, which maintains the resolution but
expands to 2048 highôÙlevel feature channels. The ResNet50 weights are initialized from
ImageNet preôÙtraining with differential learning rates: backbone parameters use 2 û 10ã6
while decoder parameters use 2 û 10ã5, allowing fineôÙtuning of preôÙtrained features while
enabling faster adaptation of new components.
Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP)
The Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) module processes the highôÙlevel features
extracted from the Res5_x block to effectively capture multiôÙscale contextual information.
It consists of four parallel 3 û 3 atrous convolutions with dilation rates of 6, 12, and 18
(corresponding to an output stride of 16), enabling the model to perceive objects at differôÙ
ent scales. Additionally, imageôÙlevel context is incorporated via global average pooling,
followed by a 1 û 1 convolution. The outputs from all parallel branches are concatenated
and passed through a final 1 û 1 convolution to fuse the features into a 256ôÙchannel repôÙ
resentation. The final output maintains a spatial resolution of H/16ûW/16, enriched with

semantic information across multiple receptive fields.
Decoder with Skip Connections
The decoder is designed to recover spatial detail through skip connections and progresôÙ
sive channel reduction. Initially, lowôÙlevel features from the Res3_x block (with 512 chanôÙ
nels) are compressed to 48 channels using a 1 û 1 convolution followed by batch norôÙ
malization and ReLU activation. Simultaneously, the output of the ASPP module (256
channels) is upsampled by a factor of two to align its resolution with the lowôÙlevel features
at H/8ûW/8. These two feature maps are then concatenated to form a combined tensor
with 304 channels.
To refine these features, the decoder employs a series of three convolutional blocks that
systematically reduce the channel dimensionality. The first block (Dec_Conv1) reduces
the channels from 304 to 256, the second (Dec_Conv2) reduces 256 to 128, and the third
(Dec_Conv3) further reduces 128 to 64. Each block consists of a 3û3 convolution followed
by batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout. A progressive dropout strategy is
used, decreasing the dropout rates from 0.1 to 0.06 and finally to 0.03 across the three
stages, to balance regularization and feature retention.
Segmentation Head and Final Processing
At the final stage, a 1 û 1 convolutional layer projects the 64 refined features to 3 output
logits corresponding to the target segmentation classes. To restore the spatial resolution
of the original input image (512û384), a singleôÙstage bilinear upsampling with a scale factor
of 8 is applied. This design ensures efficient spatial recovery and maintains a smooth
feature refinement pathway, reducing the number of channels progressively from 304 to
3 without introducing bottlenecks.

5.3
Discriminator
The discriminator serves as the adversarial component in our GAN frameworks, tasked
with distinguishing between real and generated images. We implement a simplified PatchôÙ
GAN discriminator that operates on image patches rather than entire images, providing
more detailed feedback to the generator during training.
Our design philosophy prioritizes training stability over discriminator complexity.
The
architecture consists of only three convolutional layers with a reduced channel count
(ndf=32), making it deliberately weaker than typical discriminators. This design choice
prevents the discriminator from becoming too powerful too quickly, which can lead to
training instability where the generator fails to learn effectively.
The discriminator progressively downsamples the input image through strided convoluôÙ
tions (4û4 kernels with stride 2), reducing spatial dimensions from HûW to H/8ûW/8 while
increasing channel depth from 3 to 64. LeakyReLU activations are applied after the first
two convolutional layers to introduce nonôÙlinearity, while the final layer outputs raw logits
without activation. This patchôÙbased output means each value corresponds to the auôÙ
thenticity of a specific image region, providing spatiallyôÙaware feedback that helps the
generator produce more realistic local details.
The simplified architecture (Figure 5.7) proves particularly effective for maintaining generatorôÙ
discriminator balance during training, ensuring that neither component dominates the adôÙ
versarial process and allowing for stable convergence.

segmentation loss on both original and styleôÙtransferred source data, consistency loss beôÙ

Following Cubuk et al., the augmentation pipeline uses a twoôÙtier strategy: mild transôÙ
formations are applied to all samples to maintain realism, while stronger transformations
are applied selectively to samples containing underrepresented classes. This approach
increases diversity where needed without degrading overall image quality.
Progressive Parameters
The augmentation intensity is controlled using progressive parameters. The base level
for all classes uses elastic deformation with öÝ = 15, ü = 3, and distortion limits of 0.05
for both grid and optical effects. For minorityôÙclass samples, these values are increased
to öÝ = 25, ü = 5, and limits of 0.08, introducing more variation while preserving semantic
content.
To further support rare class learning, a targeted copyôÙpaste method was used:
1. Spot defect regions were identified and extracted.
2. Elastic and photometric transforms were applied to these patches.
3. Patches were pasted into lowôÙcoverage background areas in a contextôÙaware way.
This method produces synthetic but realistic defect examples while preserving label acôÙ
curacy.
Additionally, classôÙaware sampling was applied during training to ensure that images conôÙ
taining rare defects appear more frequently. This helps stabilize training and improves
recall on underrepresented categories.
All images were normalized to match pretrained model expectations. The complete pipeline
was implemented using the Albumentations library for efficient, reproducible augmentaôÙ
tion with reliable label handling.
This strategy led to better segmentation results, particularly for the minority class, reduced
overfitting, and improved robustness under varied inspection conditions, highlighting the
impact of tailored data augmentation in industrial vision tasks.

Conclusion and Future Work
This thesis explored the application of domain adaptation techniques for corrosion segôÙ
mentation, specifically examining the transition from synthetic maritime corrosion data to
realôÙworld inspection imagery. Through four comprehensive experiments (AôÙD), we sysôÙ
tematically evaluated the effectiveness of our proposed pipeline across both 3ôÙclass and
binary segmentation tasks using VGG16ED and DeepLabV3+ ResNet50 architectures.
While the overall architecture showed promise, the results of the 3ôÙclass segmentation
task were notably less successful compared to the 2ôÙclass setup. This discrepancy can
be primarily attributed to limitations in the dataset quality and distribution.
The Inspectrone dataset, although rich in highôÙresolution images, exhibited class imôÙ
balance and significant domain variability, especially across lighting conditions, image
scales, and perspectives. Additionally, a portion of the annotations, particularly in comôÙ
plex corrosion patterns, were sparse or inconsistent, which impacted the modelãs ability
to learn robust representations for all classes. The synthetic data, despite being wellôÙ
structured, introduced further domain shift, compounding the difficulty of generalizing in
the multiôÙclass setting.
Our results demonstrate a clear performance hierarchy based on task complexity. The 3ôÙ
class segmentation experiments (A and C) achieved limited success, with final validation
mIoU scores of 24.76% (VGG16ED) and 11% (ResNet50), respectively. In contrast, the
binary segmentation experiments (B and D) showed substantially improved performance,
reaching 40.39% mIoU (VGG16ED) and 47% mIoU (ResNet50). This performance gap
highlights the inherent difficulty of fineôÙgrained corrosion classification, where distinguishôÙ
ing between seam, edge, and spot corrosion types proves significantly more challenging
than binary corrosion detection.
Conversely, simplifying the task to a binary segmentation setting yielded more stable and
accurate results, likely because it mitigated ambiguity between closely related corrosion
classes. This suggests that fineôÙgrained classification in limited and noisy domains reôÙ
mains a major challenge. However, classôÙimbalance due to the prevalence of the backôÙ
ground class presented a significant obstacle for accurate corrosion detection.
Future Work
As part of future work, we propose exploring more streamlined architectures or pipelines
that are less convoluted, for example by using a simpler, endôÙtoôÙend semiôÙsupervised
framework, which may reduce error propagation. Additionally, curating a cleaner, betterôÙ
annotated dataset, or focusing on preôÙprocessing techniques to create a more robust
target dataset could significantly enhance segmentation performance across all classes.
Given the extreme variability within the target domain, future research should investiôÙ
gate comprehensive domain stratification and preprocessing strategies. The Inspectrone
datasetãs diverse imaging conditions, ranging from varying illumination levels to different
surface properties and environmental factorsëƒ suggest that treating the target domain as
a homogeneous entity may be fundamentally flawed. Future work could involve clusôÙ
tering target domain images based on quantifiable characteristics such as lighting conôÙ
ditions, surface types, imaging parameters, or vessel locations, followed by specialized
adaptation strategies tailored to each cluster. This domain stratification approach could

adaptation approaches when faced with highly variable realôÙworld datasets. The supeôÙ
