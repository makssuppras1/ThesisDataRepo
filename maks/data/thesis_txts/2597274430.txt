Abstract
Protection groups (PGs) are an important tool for the organic chemists during multi step synthesis
of complex molecules. The Carbonyl is one of the most important functional groups in organic
chemistry and due to its high electrophilicity it often requires protection. The current selection of
PGs for carbonyls are limited and the most applied PG today are the acid-labile acetal PG. The
small repertoire of PGs makes it diﬃcult to selectively do chemistry on one carbonyl group in a
complex molecule containing multiple carbonyl groups. In this thesis, a novel hydroxylamine func-
tionalized photoremovable protection group (PPG) for carbonyls is presented, which can be removed
in mild conditions. The PPG is based on an o-nitrobenzylic moiety and it can undergo two diﬀerent
reaction mechanisms during photolysis. The results of this project has shown that the ratio between
these two mechanisms is dependent on the solvent used in the reaction. The application of the PPG
has been demonstrated on 10 diﬀerent carbonyls by fast formation of oximes and in high yields. A
stability and orthogonal study has been performed showing that it is possible to remove a t-BuOCO
(Boc) group selectively by using triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA) in presence of the PPG without aﬀecting
it and also removing the PPG selectively in presence of the Boc group.

Introduction
Most organic molecules contain more than one functional group, and many diﬀerent functional
groups can react with the same type of reagent. This makes organic synthesis more diﬃcult and it
is therefore important for a chemist to be able to control which functional group will react, where
the functional group will react and how the functional group will react regarding stereochemistry?
These questions are about selectivity and there are three diﬀerent types of selectivity: Chemoselec-
tivity, regioselectivity and stereoselectivity.[1] Chemoselectivity is about the selective reactivity of
one functional group in the preference for others, regioselectivity is about where a reagent will react
and ﬁnally stereoselectivity is how the group reacts with regard to the stereochemistry of the ﬁnal
compound.[1][2][3][4][5]
One example of a chemoselective reaction, was made by chemists at the pharmaceutical company
Pﬁzer. They made use of the fact that ketones are more electrophilic than esters during synthesis
eﬀorts when developing anticonvulsants. Thereby, it was possible for them to chemoselectively react
lithium acetylide with a ketone in the presence of an ester (see ﬁgure 1.1).
Figure 1.1: Chemoselective addition of lithium acetylide to a ketone in presence of an ester.[1]
But what would the chemists have done if they wanted to do a nucleophilic addition to the ester
without aﬀecting the more reactive ketone? The most common way for making a functional group
react in the presence of a more reactive group is to use a protecting group (PG). The introduction of
PGs are a necessary evil whenever the chemoselectivity is disfavoured in a reaction with a starting
material containing multiple functional groups of similar reactivity.[1] The use of PGs complicates
the synthetic process by increasing the reaction sequence with two steps (introduction and removal
of the PG), which often results in lower yield and higher cost, but it is an eﬀective way to overcome
chemoselectivity problems.
The carbonyl is one of the most important functional groups in organic chemistry and due to its high
electrophilicity, it is often necessary to protect in multi step synthesis.[1][6] The current selection of

2
Protection Groups
PGs are an important tool for the organic chemists during multi step synthesis of complex molecules.
The correct choice of protecting groups is often decisive for the successful completion of the overall
synthetic procedure. There are many important criteria to be fulﬁlled in order for a protecting group
to ﬁnd wide application in organic synthesis. The PG should be introduced under mild conditions
in a selective manner; that is without aﬀecting other functional groups in the molecule. It should
be stable under all standard conditions used in organic synthesis; including conditions used in the
puriﬁcation procedure. The PG should be cleavable under mild conditions and in a highly selective
manner so that other protecting groups and functional groups present in the molecule stay unaﬀected.
It should be introduced and removed with readily available and cheap reagents. Furthermore, the
PG should have a positive- or a minimal negative impact on the physicochemical properties of the
molecule: It should be easily soluble and desirable it should be easily crystallizable. Only a few
protecting groups fulﬁll all the criteria and in most cases a compromise must be found. In most
cases the most diﬃcult requirement is to fulﬁll the requirement that the PG being stable and at the
same time, easy to remove.[6][7][8]
2.1
Orthogonal Stability and Modulated Lability
The use of multiple PGs in the synthesis of complex molecules introduces following two principles.[7]
• Orthogonally stable protecting groups
• Protecting groups with modulated lability
2.1.1
Orthogonal Stability
Orthogonality is the principle of selectively being able to remove each PG under speciﬁc reaction
conditions without aﬀecting the other PGs in a multiple protected molecule (illustrated in ﬁgure
2.1).[6][10]

Choosing a strategy based upon the principle of orthogonal stability guarantees a high degree of
ﬂexibility in the execution of a synthesis. Unfortunately, it can sometimes be impossible to ﬁnd and
use the required number of orthogonally stable PGs when synthesizing multifunctional molecules.
It can therefore be necessary to use PGs with modulated lability instead.[7]

2.3
An Overview of Protecting Groups
A wide range of PGs are currently available for the various functional groups and table 2.1 is an
overview of some commonly applied PGs in organic synthesis:[1][6][7] As can be seen in table 2.1,
many of the commonly applied PGs are removed by using acid as cleavage reagent. If the only PGs
developed for a certain functional group are acid-labile PGs then it creates the following limitations:
(i) It can be diﬃcult to implement an orthogonal PG strategy in the synthesis.
(ii) Acid catalysed reaction conditions cannot be applied when acid-labile PGs are used.
(iii) The molecule cannot contain any acid-labile functional groups; or these must be protected
during removal of the acid-labile PG.
In order to avoid orthogonal and lability problems in a synthesis, a functional group must have
multiple PGs with diﬀerent cleavage concepts.

2.4
Protecting Groups for Aldehydes/Ketones
Aldehydes and ketones are some of the most important functional groups in organic chemistry. The
high electrophilicity introduces challenges in a synthesis due to the high reactivity towards nucle-
ophiles. Protection of aldehydes/ketones are therefore often necessary during multistep synthesis.[1][6][12]
Aldehydes and ketones are usually protected as O,O-acetals/ketals; often as the cyclic analogs, e.g.
1,3-dioxolane or -dioxane formed by condensation with ethane-1,2-diol or propane-1,3-diol, respec-
tively (see table 2.2). Acyclic acetals/ketals are less stable than the cyclic acetals/ketals and therefore
only requires low concentration of acid for hydrolysis. [6][8]
Since the selection of PGs for carbonyls is limited, it can be diﬃcult to selectively react one carbonyl
group in a molecule containing multiple carbonyl groups. In addition, the removal of acetals/ketals
is done under acid conditions, which makes this type of PG incompatible with a molecule containing
acid-labile functional groups. Furthermore, the use of acid-catalysis is not possible with protected
aldehydes/ketones present in the molecule.
An alternative to the O,O-acetal/ketal is the sulphur analog which do not require acid for the de-
protection step but the S,S-acetals/ketals are rarely used due to following reasons.
Firstly, the
deprotection step often requires the use of heavy metals such as mercury, which then creates a toxic
waste disposal problem and serious health concerns. (see table 2.2).[6][13][14][15][16][17] Secondly,
most thiols and dithiols are unpleasant to work with due to their strong odors. The limited selection
of PGs for aldehydes and ketones put limitations on the synthesis of multifunctional molecules. It
is therefore of vital importance that new PGs for aldehydes/ketones, which can be removed under
mild conditions are being developed. The development of a PPG for carbonyls could be pivotal for
synthesis of more complex and ﬁne molecules.

Photoremovable Protection Groups
PPGs are protecting groups that can be removed by photo irradiation. PPGs consists of a chem-
ical stable chromophore, which can be selectively activated by irradiation with light at a speciﬁc
wavelength, leading to a chemical reaction and removal of the PPG.[7] Most of the known PPGs
are based on the rich photochemistry of chromophores, which are larger unsaturated systems, often
containing nitro, carbonyl, and aromatic groups.[18][19]
3.1
Excited State
Absorption of a photon from visible or ultraviolet light by a chromophore makes it possible for an
electron to move from the ground state to an excited state. In an electronically excited state, one
electron occupies a higher-energy orbital making the overall energy of the molecule higher than in
the ground state (see ﬁgure 3.1). The energy of light is given by E = hν, where h is Planck’s constant
and ν is the frequency of light (ν = c/λ, c = speed of light, λ = wavelength). Since the energy levels
of the molecular orbitals as shown in ﬁgure 3.1 are quantized, the amount of energy needed to move
an electron from a lower-energy orbital to a higher-energy orbital is a ﬁxed quantity. This means
that promotion of an electron will only happen when the frequency of the light corresponds to the

3.1.1
Types of Excitation
Upon excitation of a molecule, an electron is usually promoted to the lowest available vacant orbital,
but promotion to a higher orbital is also a possibility. The energetic ordering of the molecular orbitals
is: σ < π < n < π∗< σ∗.[22][23] The following molecular electronic transitions exist:
1. σ →σ∗. Excitation of alkanes, which have no n or π electrons.
2. n →σ∗. Molecules containing single bonded atoms with lone pairs can be excited this way.
3. π →π∗. This pathway is open to every molecules containing π electrons.
4. n →π∗. Every molecule containing double bonded atoms with lone pairs can undergo this
type of excitation.
The four possible excitation types shown above are listed in order of the amount of energy needed
to promote an electron. In order for an electron to move from σ →σ∗, light in the far-UV region is
needed, whereas ordinary UV light can make an electron move from n →π∗. The energy needed to
promote an electron from π to π∗is highly dependent on the degree of conjugation in the molecule.
The more conjugated a molecule is, the more displaced toward higher wavelengths the absorption
is. The amount of energy needed for promotion of an electron from π to π* is therefore lower in

3.2
Application of PPGs
PPGs are removed by light, avoiding the use of strong reagents such as acid or bases. PPGs are
therefore suitable for synthesis of sensitive molecules. [24] PPGs are also capable of caging biologi-
cally important molecules such as neurotransmitters, hormones and nucleic acids, which afterwards
can be released in a spatially and temporally controlled manner. These properties have made PPGs a
highly studied topic and ever since the ﬁrst reported use of a PPG back in 1962 by J.A Barltrop and
P. Schoﬁeld[25], numerous PPGs have been developed and utilized in a variety of applications from
protein science[26] and release of biochemical compounds in living tissue,[27][28] to light-controlled
release of pharmaceutical agents.[29][30][31][32] Other areas are in polymer science and solid phase
synthesis.[18] PPGs are often categorized by their major functional groups. Figure 3.2 shows some
of the categorized PPGs in the literature.[27][33][34][35]

The 2-nitrobenzyl Containing PPG
2-Nitrobenzyl (NB) derivatives are the most common photolabile protecting group.[6][24][27] Many
NB analogues have been developed for protection of various functional groups which makes the NB
family useful of releasing a wide range of groups, including phosphates, carboxylates, carbonates,
and carbamates, as well as thiolates, phenolates, and alkoxides. Structural modiﬁcations of the NB
group can be made in order to improve its photochemical properties. These modiﬁcations can be di-
vided into two main categories. The ﬁrst is modiﬁcation of the aromatic chromophore. This includes
expansion of the aromatic system as well as substitution at the phenyl ring.[18] An example of this
type of modiﬁcation is the o-nitroveratryl analog (see ﬁgure 4.1). This type of NB derivative has
undergone substitution at the phenyl ring by replacing Hydrogen atoms with two methoxy groups.
The methoxy groups have an impact on the bathochromic shift, resulting in a shift of the eﬀective
absorbance to longer wavelength.[18][24] The second modiﬁcation is substitution at the benzylic
carbon atom. NB derivatives containing two hydrogen atoms in the benzylic position produces a
nitrosobenzaldehyde through release of the leaving group. Nitrosobenzaldehyde has shown to cause
problem due to its more eﬃcient absorbance of irradiation in the region of λ > 300 nm compared to
the NB derivative. Furthermore, nitrosobenzaldehyde contains a reactive aldehyde and it is known
that this functional group can lower the eﬀectiveness of releasing amines and interfere with biological
systems through the formation of imines.[18] In order to overcome these limitations, it was found
beneﬁcial to introduce a substituent at the benzylic atom, e.g a CH3 group, which leads to the
production of a less reactive nitrosoketone as byproduct.

Nitration
The ﬁrst step in the synthesis of the hydroxylamine functionalized PPG is a nitration reaction. Con-

The ﬁrst step in the reaction mechanism is the formation of the highly electrophilic nitronium ion,
which happens by dehydration of nitric acid and thereby loss of H2O. The nucleophilic aromatic
ring attacks the nitronium ion, which leads to the formation of a tetrahedral carbon atom and a
cyclohexadienyl cation.[40] Final step is the restoration of the aromatic system. Nitration of 1α is a
regioselective reaction, resulting in a predominant attack from the aromatic ring at the only free para
position to one of the methoxy-groups on the nitronium ion. The reaction is controlled by electronic-
and steric eﬀects. 1α contains two electron donating groups (EDGs) and one electron withdrawing
group (EWG). EDGs are activating and ortho/para directing groups whereas EWGs are deactivating
and meta directing groups.[41] The nitro group ends up para to one of the methoxy groups because
the methoxy groups dominates electronically and because there is less steric hindrance in the para
position to the methoxy group, as compared to the ortho position.[1]

7.4.1
Amide Coupling
The amide coupling reaction has been optimized greatly in recent years in parallel with the develop-
ment of new amide coupling reagents.[44] One couplig reagent often used is PyBOP (benzotriazol-1-
yl-oxytripyrrolidinophosphonium hexaﬂuorophosphate). PyBOP is a derivative of BOP (benzotriazol-
1-yloxy)tris(dimethylamino)- phosphonium hexaﬂuorophosphate), which was developed to avoid the
generation of the carcinogenic byproducts that is seen with BOP.[44] PyBOP reacts with the car-
boxylic acid to form an active ester, which then react with the amine in an acylation reaction. The
use of PyBop requires the use of a base, where DIPEA (Diisopropylethylamine) is often used. The
reaction mechanism for an amide coupling using PyBOP and DIPEA is illustrated in ﬁgure 7.11.
First step in the mechanism is a deprotonation of the carboxylic acid forming the reactive carboxy-
late. Second step is a nucleophilic attack from the carboxylate against the electrophilic phosphor
atom resulting in the release of 1-Hydroxybenzotriazole. Third step is the breaking of the C=O
double bond forced by a nucleophilic attack from the oxygen atom in 1-Hydroxybenzotriazole. The
C=O double bond is then reformed, which results in the release of the phosphonium ion that forms
a strong P=O double bond, which drives the reaction. Fourth step is a nucleophilic attack from
the amine on the C=O double bond, resulting in the release of 1-Hydroxybenzotriazole and the
formation of the amide bond. Final step is a deprotonation by DIPEA. The reaction aﬀorded the
product in 81% yield.

Conclusion
8
Conclusion
In this project, a hydroxylamine functionalized PPG based on a nitrobenzylic moiety was succesfully
synthesised and conﬁrmed by NMR analysis. It has been demonstrated that the hydroxylamine
functionalized PPG can form oximes with diﬀerent types of carbonyls ranging from only consisting
of aliphatic substituents to only consisting of aromatic substituents.
Furthermore, it has been
shown that these oxime formations happens fast and in high yield.
A qualitative study of ﬁve
diﬀerent solvents impact on the ratio of compound X and Y was performed showing that THF was
the most ideal solvent to make use of in photolytic cleavage of compound W. The stability of the
hydroxylamine functionalized PPG has also been tested in an orthogonal study using TFA as cleavage
reagent, which shows that the PPG can withstand strong acid. The orthogonal study also showed
that it is possible to remove Boc and the PPG selectively without aﬀecting one another. The removal
of Boc in presence of the hydroxylamine functionalized PPG was conﬁrmed by obtaining UPLC-MS
and NMR analysis of compound 12B. A successfully removal of the hydroxylamine functionalized
PPG in presence of Boc was only conﬁrmed by UPLC-MS as a pure H-NMR spectrum of compound
13B was not obtained.
