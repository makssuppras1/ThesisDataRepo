Abstract
Ensuring environmental sustainability of a construction is not only dependent on the use
of materials but to a great extent the interaction of those materials, as the separation of
materials is essential for the recycling potential. The validation and quality assessment
of the design needs to be evidence-based in order to keep up with the growing demand
for documentation and legislation. In the design phase of a construction project, it is
important to be able to document and quantify the effects of design decisions. The
motivation to conduct this study comes from the desire to support design decisions with
evidence-based arguments.
The purpose of this project is to investigate existing methods and processes to compute
and model Design for Disassembly (DfD). Furthermore, the purpose of the project is to
implement DfD for the early design stages by coupling early stage design tools such as
Rhino and Grasshopper to DfD evaluation tools.
The primary design method used for this project is the double diamond method. The tool
development process follows an iterative method of implementation of functionalities.
As functionalities or components are developed, the key learnings from each implemen-
tation creates a foundation for the next cycle in the process. State of the art disassembly
methodologies where investigated to uncover an understanding of the indexes and terms
used to provide evidence for design for disassembly. The development of tools have
been achieved though the use of Rhino, Grasshopper, Python, and the Livestock3D
method. Graph theory has been implemented successfully in the evaluation of design
for disassembly plans.
A number of tools have been developed to evaluate assemblies in regard to disassem-
bleability. The developed tools support an automated interpretation of Rhino/Grasshop-
per geometry supporting efﬁcient and parametric evaluations during design phases.
Successful implementation of complete and selective disassembly evaluation has been
reached, and the implementation of weighted analysis have been achieved for weighted
parts. To support communication of disassembly plans, animation tools have been
developed. The developed tools have performed successfully for the evaluation of 2D
assemblies and are developed for easy extension to 3D evaluation.

Resume
En sikring af miljømæssig bæredydighed for konstruktioner afhænger ikke kun af de
brugte materialer, men i ligeså høj grad af disse materialers interaktion, da mulighe-
den for at separere materialerne er betydningsfuld for deres genanvendelsespotentiale.
Validering og kvalitetssikring af konstruktionernes design skal være evidensbaseret for
at overholde regler for dokumentation og lovgivning. I designfasen af et konstruktion-
sprojekt er det vigtigt at være i stand til at dokumentere og kvantiﬁcere effekten af
designbeslutninger med argumenter underbygget af evidens.
Formålet med dette projekt er at undersøge eksisterende metoder og processer for
at beregne og modellere Design for Disassembly (DfD). Formålet med projektet er
ydermere at implementere DfD i de tidlige designfaser ved at bruge tidlige designfa-
seværktøjer, som Rhino og Ghrasshopper, sammen med DfD-evalueringsværktøjer.
Den primære designmetode, som ligger til grund for dette projekt, er metoden Double
Diamond. Udarbejdelsen af værktøjet har været en iterativ proces med implementering
af funktionaliteter. Som funktionaliterne eller komponenterne er blevet udviklet, er
erfaringer fra hver iteration brugt som grundlag for den videre udvikling. Sate of the
art disassembly metodologier er undersøgt for at opbygge en forståelse for begreber og
indekser, der er brugt som evidens for DfD. Udviklingen af værktøjerne er foretaget i
Rhino, Grasshopper, Python og ved hjælp af Livestock3D-metoden. Grafteori er med
succes implementeret i evalueringen af design for disassembly planer.
En række værktøjer er udviklet for at evaluere assemblies i forhold for deres mulighed
for disassesembleablity. De udviklede værktøjer understøtter en automatiseret fortolkn-
ing af geometrien i Rhino og Grasshopper, og understøtter effektive og parametriske
evalueringer i designforløbet. Projektet har haft en succesfuld implementering af kom-
plet og selektiv disassembly evaluering, samt en implementering af vægtet analyse for
vægtede komponenter. For at understøtte kommunikationen af disassembly planer er der
udviklet animationsværktøjer. De udviklede værktøjer er i stand til at evaluere af 2D
assemblies og er udviklet med henblik på en nem udvidelse til 3D evaluering.

1. Introduction
The increasing focus on sustainability is creating a need for computational tools that
supports the design process of construction, for efﬁcient construction, maintenance, and
disassembly. Ensuring the environmental sustainability of a construction is not only
dependent on the use of materials but to a great extent the interaction of those materials,
as the separation of materials is essential for the recycling potential.
As designing for disassembly becomes increasingly important, the validation of such
designs becomes equally important. The validation and quality assessment of the
design needs to be evidence-based in order to keep up with the growing demand for
documentation and legislation. In the design phase of a construction project, it is
important to be able to document and quantify the effects of design decisions.
A study into the subject of Disassembly sequence generation and evaluation [Wang, 2014]
phrases the lack of tools in evaluating the disassembly performance of design:
"A few decades ago, the products were designed mainly to answer the customer
requirements and the possibilities of the manufacturers without taking into account the
environmental aspects and those of recycling during the design process. However, with
the implementation of new European and International standards and recommendations
of environmental legislation, the problem of dismantling and thereafter of products
recycling is increasingly important. The economical and environmental consequences of
products disassembling and re-using at the end of their life cycle are also to be taken into
account. Thus, today’s designers need new tools allowing them in particular generating
and evaluating disassembly operations." [Wang, 2014]
Environmental legislation and an increasing focus on sustainability in general drives a
need for development of methods that support sustainable architecture. The Report of the
World Commission on Environment and Development from 1987 deﬁnes sustainability
as:
"meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future genera-
tions to meet their own needs" [United Nations, 1987]
The introduction of the UN sustainability goals, and the update of the goals performed in
2015, has formalised the approach to sustainability by introducing 17 sustainable devel-
opment goals. The consumption of material and thereby the importance of designing for
disassembly, is associated with the 12th goal of responsible consumption and production.
Related to the goal the UN states the following:
1
==PAGE:10==
"Should the global population reach 9.6 billion by 2050, the equivalent of almost three
planets could be required to provide the natural resources needed to sustain current
lifestyles." [United Nations, 2015]
This means that to ensure a continued development of the modern society, we need to
change the way we handle the resources available to us. Reusing, re-manufacturing and
recycling materials will be essential for maintaining the current living standards in the
future.
In order to decrease the consummation of raw materials, disassembleability needs to be
a key factor for any construction. Ensuring that construction materials can be recycled
depends entirely on the ability to separate the materials from each other.
The tools at engineer’s disposal today are insufﬁcient when faced with the increasing
demand for evidence-based argumentation and documentation of sustainability perfor-
mance during the entire design process. The primary tool for assessing a construction’s
sustainability performance today is to perform a LCA (life cycle assessment) of the
construction. However, even if the LCA takes all materials of the construction into
account, the analysis does not provide information on dismantling and separation of the
materials.
"LCA has a powerful method to evaluate the environmental sustainability of products
and technologies, but only considers environmental items, and this aspect limits the
analysis potential of the method." [Favi and Germani, 2014]
The method for achieving a product that is designed in regard to the disassembly of
the product is, as of today mainly based on the designer’s abilities, in therms of spatial
understanding and awareness for all steps in the assembly and disassembly.
This particular issue was already speciﬁed in 2001 as part of a study into Recycling
Potential and Design for Disassembly in Buildings conducted by Catarina Thormark.
"The aspects of dismantling and recycling are rarely, if ever, included in the design
of joints and connections today. To dismantle modern constructions can therefore be
very time consuming, cause an unacceptable amount of damaged material, or simply be
impossible. It is, however, the knowledge of those joints and connections that architects
and engineers have in their toolbox when designing building constructions. To ﬁnd
joints that can be dismantled will therefore need some extra effort." [Thormark, 2001]
To claim that a product is designed for disassembly is very popular, but there does not
seem to be an effective method or tool for evaluating the quality of the design, leaving
the engineer with very weak arguments when making design decisions. The engineers
and designers are offered very little support from technology in analysing, and ensuring
the products performance in regard to the disassembly process. There is simply a lack
of methods and tools to assist the engineer in analysing and assessing the sustainability
of construction.
2
==PAGE:11==
A study into the subject of Automatic Generation of Constructable Brick Sculptures
conducted in 2013 [Testuz et al., 2013] explores the use of graph theory in conducting
efﬁcient and robust generation of the possible Lego R⃝block conﬁgurations, see Figure
1.1.
Figure 1.1: Two brick layouts (left), and their respective graph representations
(right). [Testuz et al., 2013]
The study into this subject is a step in the right direction in terms of assessing the
assembly and disassembly of constructions, but the study and the developed algorithms
are constrained by limits relevant to Lego R⃝blocks. The parts making up the assemblies
are limited to a speciﬁed range of sizes and designs, and the connection of the parts are
limited to connect only blocks stacked on top of each other, leaving parts side by side
unconnected to one another, see Figure 1.1. This premise is ﬁtting for the construction
of Lego R⃝ﬁgures, but the limitations are not suitable for general construction of archi-
tecture. On the other hand, the use of graph theory seems to be a rational and efﬁcient
way of evaluating assemblies, and is repeated in a number of studies into the subject of
design for disassembly.
The article Fabrication-aware Design with Intersecting Planar Pieces
[Schwartzburg and Pauly, 2013] also addresses the use of graph theory in the analy-
sis of assemble- and disassembleability. The article focus exclusively on assemblies
constructed of planar parts and with surface connections only, see Figure 1.2.
3
==PAGE:12==
Figure 1.2:
A 3D toy composed of orthogonal intersecting planar pieces.
The mesh of the dinosaur serves as a guiding volume for the design.
[Schwartzburg and Pauly, 2013]
The study has interesting investigations into the rigidity of surface connections, but the
limited part geometry and connection types does not apply well to general construction
joints.
In a similar study from 2017, Reconﬁgurable Interlocking Furniture [Song et al., 2017],
graph theory is utilised to evaluate assembleability and disassembleability.
The study is focused on assemblies that can be reconﬁgured to achieve multiple functions,
see Figure 1.3. The study is very interesting as it is focused on the reuse of parts more
than the recycling of the raw materials, which seems to be the most sustainable end-
of-life scenario. Again, the investigation is limited to a speciﬁc type of parts and
connections that does not translate to the vast variety of parts used in the construction of
permanent buildings.
4
==PAGE:13==
Figure 1.3: Reconﬁgurable assemblies of different functionality: ladder, stool, and
hand truck (left to right). [Song et al., 2017]
The studies performed on the subject of design for disassembly with digital tools are
intriguing in terms of utilising graph theory to arrange information of connectivity in
assemblies.
Design for disassembly is an approach often used in the design of temporary architec-
ture, but designing for disassembly is just as important to consider in relation to the
maintenance and the dismantling of permanent constructions. The design of sustainable
architecture is very closely linked to designing long lasting buildings. The maintenance
of the building is important to ensure the building has the longest possible lifespan and
that the building functions in the best possible manner during the use of the building.
The process of maintaining a building is connected to the process of disassembly, as the
removal or replacement of damaged components or components with a short lifespan is
essential in the maintenance the building.
1.1
Motivation and prerequisites
The motivation to conduct this study comes from the desire to support design decisions
with evidence-based arguments. The foundation of being an engineer is the ability to
provide the evidence needed to take responsibility. For most engineering disciplines,
the responsibility is in proportion to the evidence it is possible to produce, but for build-
ability and disassembly potential, engineers are mostly left with their own experience

The author’s personal requisites include experience with working with Rhino and
Grasshopper to generate and analyse digital construction models and programming
experience in the software MATLAB. Programming in Python is a skill acquired during
the project.
1.2
Problem statement
The purpose of this project is to investigate existing methods and processes to compute
and model Design for Disassembly (DfD). Furthermore, the purpose of the project is to
implement DfD in the early design stages by coupling early stage design tools such as
Rhino and Grasshopper to DfD evaluation tools.
The problem statements are:
• Which existing theories, methods, and processes for DfD are relevant to implement
in an early design stage?
• If existing tools for supporting the DfD methods exists, how do they compare and
can they be used in early design stages?
• If tools for supporting the DfD methods do not exist, which computational, mathe-
matical concepts can be utilized to perform DfD analysis?
• Based on above understanding of the current state of art; is it possible to implement
a proof of concept of DfD analysis tool to be used in an early design stage?

4.2
Disassembly methods
Different methods for disassembly has been described by various sources. The two
methods used in this project, complete disassembly and selective disassembly, are
adopted from a thesis Disassembly sequences generation and evaluation : Intregration
in virtual reality environment [Wang, 2014].
"Complete disassembly involves disassembling of all the components of an assembly,
but it is rarely the optimal solution owing to the high costs of the disassembly. Alterna-
tively, selective disassembly is usually more appropriate for manufacturing applications,
such as maintenance, repairing or recycling." [Wang, 2014]
Both the complete and the selective method are included in the development of digital
tools in this project.
C. Wang also deﬁnes two additional kinds of disassembly, which relates to the process
of the disassembly: linear disassembly and parallel disassembly. This project will
focus solely on linear disassembly. Meaning that only one part is moved at a time.
[Wang, 2014]
An article onDisassembly Sequence Planning for Products with Defective Parts in Prod-
uct Recovery [Gungor and Gupta, 1998] describes the method of DfD evaluation as
follows:
" The methodology involves the following steps:
• Generating the geometric-based precedence relationships among parts which are
represented in a matrix format denoted by DP (Disassembly Precedence).
• Generating an optimum disassembly sequence plans
• Carrying out disassembly process and resolution of uncertainty
[...] We deﬁne optimum disassembly sequence plan as an ordered set of parts"
[Gungor and Gupta, 1998]
Generating a disassembly sequence plan is the focus of this project and the ordered set of
parts is, as described above, the goal of the complete disassembly method. The complete
disassembly is considered a success when all components have been dismantled. In
other words, a complete disassembly is when all parts in the assembly are disassembled
from each other.
The selective disassembly method refers to removing a selected part from the as-
sembly. The article on A selective disassembly methodology for end-of-life products
[Kara et al., 2005] describes the following method for DfD evaluation:

"The steps are generating a liaison diagram, establishing precedence rules, generating
all possible disassembly sequences, and carrying out a winnowing process to select the
most feasible disassembly sequence(s)." [Kara et al., 2005]

5. Graph theory
The state of the art investigation has revealed graph theory to be a suitable approach when
evaluating design for disassembly [Wang et al., 2018, Kara et al., 2005, Wang, 2014,
Schwartzburg and Pauly, 2013, Song et al., 2017]. This chapter introduces the most im-
portant and relevant objectives of graph theory for this project.
A graph can be understood as a complex representation of elements (nodes) and the
relations between the elements (edges), see Figure 5.1. Each element can contain a col-
lection of information, which describes the element and the edges can likewise contain
information, which describes the content of the relation between two elements.
Figure 5.1: Colleagues as nodes of a graph
The easiest way to understand a graph is to consider a simple scenario where graph
theory can be applied. We can consider a group of colleagues as a group of nodes, with
each node containing information related to that particular person. The information on
one such person may in this case be their name, position, CV, hobbies, or information
on his or her family. The edges connecting each node, or the relationships connecting
the colleagues, might contain information on how long the two colleagues have been
working together, their common stories or experiences, or any other information relating
to the relationship between the two. You may also imagine not all nodes or not all
colleagues are connected by an edge or a relationship. All this information can be
arranged and illustrated in a graph.
22
==PAGE:31==
In a similar way, the relationship between components in a building can be arranged
in a graph and the mathematical advantages of graphs can be applied to evaluate and
optimise the construction. This section introduces the syntax and understanding of
graph theory forming the basis for present report. The syntax for this report is adapted
from Introduction to Averaging Dynamics over Networks [Fagnani and Frasca, 2018].
In graph theory a network consists of pieces or a sub-collection of information contained
in the elements referred to as points, vertices, or nodes, and the relationship between
these are contained in elements referred to as edges, lines, or arches.
5.1
Deﬁnition and syntax
A graph is denoted G = (V,E) consisting of a ﬁnite set of vertices, V = {vi,vj,...,vn}
and a set of edges consisting of subsets of V, E = {{vi,vj},...}.
Figure 5.2: Graph a), b), c), and d)
Figure 5.2 shows four graphs illustrated graphically. The speciﬁcations of each graph
will be further addressed in the following sections.
5.1.1
Order of graphs
The cardinality of vertices deﬁnes the order of the graph, meaning that for a graph where
the number of vertices is three, |V| = 3, the order of the graph is 3. Figure 5.2 a), c) and
d) shows graphical illustrations of graphs of the order 3.
5.1.2
Directional edges and neighbours
Edges of a graph can be described as directional and will in this case be indicated
graphically by an arrow. For the edge (u,v) ∈E, the start node of the arrow is denoted u
and the end or arrow head of the edge is denoted v. In other words, the edges (u,v) is
said to go out of u and into v. Two nodes that are in some way connected by an edge
are set to be neighbours. If two nodes (u,v) are connected by an edge from u to v, v is
called the out-neighbour of u and u is the in-neighbour of v.

5.2
Graph types and families
The present section describes a number of different types or families of graph that are
important to the understanding of the mathematical handling of graphs.
5.2.1
Symmetric graphs
As described in Section 5.1, an edge (u,v) ∈E is a directional connection between two
vertices starting at u and ending in v. The reverse edge (v,u) ∈E is the edge starting in
v and ending in u. The reverse graph of G is the graph that is obtained by reversing all
edges. The graph for which rev(G) = G is true is known as a symmetric graph. For this
graph, all edges have an opposite edge contained within the set E. Symmetric graphs
or undirected graphs often illustrate edges as lines or arches without arrow heads. The
degree of vertices in a symmetric graph refers to the number of edges connected to the
vertex. The degree is then denoted d abandoning the in/out labels of the directional
graph. In Figure 5.2 graph a) ,b), and c) are symmetric graphs, whereas d) is not.
For the algorithms developed in this project, both directional and symmetric graphs are
used.
5.2.2
Loops
Loops or self-loops are the edge (u,u) ∈E describing an edge connecting a vertex to
itself. Loops can be either allowed or disallowed. Simple graphs often disallow loops.
When allowed, graphs are often referred to as multi-graphs. For graphs where loops
are disallowed, the adjacency matrix will always contain only 0’s in the main diagonal.
In Figure 5.2 graph a) and d) show two 3-order graphs. a) disallows loops, and the
adjacency matrix Aa in Equation 5.2 contain only 0’s in the main diagonal in comparison
to the adjacency matrix Ad in Equation 5.2 belonging to graph d), of Figure 5.2, that
allows loops. In Figure 5.2, graph d) features a loop from v1 back into v1.
5.2.3
Isomorphic
Two isomorphic graphs are in essence the same graph, but the two graphs may differ in
the labeling of the vertices, meaning that even if two graphs have a different naming of
the vertices, they can still be mathematically identical.
25
==PAGE:34==
Figure 5.3: Isomorphic graphs a), b), and c)
Mathematically isomorphic graphs are deﬁned to be two graphs G = (V,E) and G′ =
(V ′,E′) where there exists bijection between V and V ′ and E and E′, and thereby also
between G and G′. In Figure 5.3, all three graphs shown are isomorphic. Graph a) is
a variation of b) where nodes are labeled differently, and c) is a variation of b) that is
graphically rearranged.
Isomorphic graphs introduces a key point to this project, as the naming of components
are not to effect the evaluation of disassembly.
5.2.4
Regular and complete graphs
An empty or trivial graph En = (V, /0) is a graph consisting of only vertices. On the other
end of the scale the complete graph KV = (V,(u,v) : u ̸= v) (here excluding loops) is the
graph where all vertices are adjacent to all other vertices. In other words, all possible
edges (excluding loops) are represented in the graph. Figure 5.2 a) shows an example of
a complete graph K3. The number 3 refers to the number of vertices or the order of the
graph. As two complete graphs of order n is per deﬁnition isomorphic, the notation Kn
is allowed for complete graphs. Regular graphs are deﬁned as graphs where all vertices
have the same degree. The graph shown in Figure 5.2 a) is a 2-regular graph. Complete
graphs are always regular as all vertices of a complete graph are of (|V|−1)-degree.
26
==PAGE:35==
5.2.5
Sub-graphs
Sub-graphs are a selection of nodes from another graph, including the edges between
the selected nodes.
Figure 5.4: Assembly to two sub-assemblies
Sub-graphs are used in the algorithms developed in this section as some disassembly
methods produces subassemblies. Generating two sub-graphs and comparing their
collected list of edges to the total list of edges in the original graph can uncover the con-
nections or edges, that must be broken during the disassembly in to two subassemblies.
5.2.6
Paths and circuits
A path within a graph G = (V,E) is deﬁned as a collection of vertices u,v,a that are
connected by edges in a sequence or a path of length l ranging from u to v within G via
an ordered list of nodes (w0,...,wl). The path must be constructed so that
• w0 = u and wl = v
• (wi,Wi+1) ∈E for every i ∈0,...,l −1
Figure 5.2 c) shows an example of a path ranging from v1 to v3 via the ordered list
(v1,v2,v3) making v3 reachable from v1. This particular graph also features the opposite
path from v to u, making v1 reachable from v3. For that reason, v1 and v3 is said to be
communicating.
A circuit or closed path is a branch of paths that have the same start and end vertex.
Figure 5.2 a) illustrates a cycle graph.

6.1
Animation and visualisation
The animation of disassembly procedures are at the moment the most efﬁcient way
to communicate the result of the investigation, and for this reason the functionality of
animation is one of the most important. Not only does the animation allow for easy
communication, but it helps understanding the results during the development of the
tool. The animation and visualisation of results are therefore introduced early in the
development process.
6.2
Automated geometry recognition
Being able to evaluate geometric restrictions directly based on the raw geometry without
relying on the human interpretation of the geometry acts not only as a more efﬁcient
evaluation, but it makes it possible to evaluate geometries of a higher complexity. On
the other hand the geometries needs to be modelled in detail, and all components must
be included for an accurate evaluation.
In the preliminary investigation a mesh based interpretation of geometries is developed.
Knowledge from the mesh based interpretation led to the method developed and utilised
for the last branch of development. The last branch of tool development bases geometry
interpretation on a ﬁnite grid of points.
6.3
Weighted analysis
The function of adding weight or value to paths and connections is essential for the
practical application of the tool. There are a vast number of aspects that effects the
optimal solution for a design. Being able to apply these aspects to the analysis is
necessary for the evaluation to be acute and essentially useful in real life projects. As an
engineer, a number of aspects supporting the decision to use one design over another
are used and all these aspects are necessary to include in the computational evaluation
for the result to be satisfactory. On the other hand having these aspects formalised and
documented enables more complex evaluations and more uniform evaluations of the
quality of designs.
6.4
2D and 3D
To ease evaluation and communication of the disassembly process, the addition of
the third dimension is not included. The third dimension is essential for practical
use of the tool, but as the procedure for adding another dimension is the same as the

==PAGE:38==
procedure applied for the ﬁrst two directions, the knowledge acquired from adding other
functionalities are valued over adding the third dimension.
6.5
Tolerances for equipment
In practice, dismantling a construction is not only restricted by having space to move
components that make up the construction, but also to provide space for the tools and
the people necessary when performing the disassembly. For example the space in front
of a screw needs to accommodate a screwdriver and a hand in order for the screw to be
dismantled.
6.6
Restricted disassembly directions
In some cases, it might not be possible to remove components in speciﬁc directions. For
example the replacement of windows in a high-rise building might be preferred to be
performed from inside the building to avoid the introduction of a lift or crane. In other
cases, the windows could be to large to handle inside and it is necessary to ensure that
the window can be removed from the outside of the building.
6.7
Stationary structural parts and structural integrity
Ensuring the structural integrity of a building during the disassembly process is im-
portant for obvious reasons. A tool to assist the evaluation of DfD should be able to
consider the structural integrity at every step of disassembly to ensure that the disas-
sembly is performed safely. Developing the functionalities to asses stability is in it self
comprehensive and due to the extend of this project the structural integrity will not be
included in the tools developed.
6.8
Translocation and rotation
Translocation is the term used for moving geometries along a vector and is with the
term rotation a fundamental geometric operation for movement. The combination of
translocation on rotation can be utilised to describe any movement in space. In other
words the process of moving a component requires either translocation or rotation, or a
combination of the two.

focus is on translocation of parts. The rotation is omitted to simplify the procedures and
allow time to test the inclusion of other functionalities.
6.9
Complete disassembly evaluation
The complete disassembly evaluations, where the all parts are separated, mainly relates
to the construction and end-of-life dismantling of the construction.
6.10
Selective disassembly evaluation
The partial or selective disassembly is an important feature when assessing the design in
regard to maintenance. The functionality is driven by the selection of a speciﬁc part and
the method of dismantling it.

9. Disassembly methods
The methods for disassembly utilised by the component described in Section 8 are
described in this chapter. As described in Section 4.2 there are a multiple methods and
approaches to disassembly. For this project, only the two methods described in this
section are used.
9.1
Method one: Complete disassembly function
This function evaluates and returns the complete disassembly order and directions. The
function is the main evaluation algorithm for the ﬁrst method, complete disassembly.
The function evaluates the assembly one part at the time and moves the parts in small
steps according to the selected ﬁneness of the point grid. For each step in the disassembly
process the part is evaluated based on the ﬁnite-graph. As each step is performed the
graph is updated in accordance with the new location of the part. The outline of the
process for the function is illustrated in Figure 9.1. The ﬁgure shows the part under
investigation is located in the graph and the related nodes are evaluated to return one of
the results; the part is free from the assembly, the part is stuck and can not be moved
at this point, and the part is able to move. The two ﬁrst outcomes will result in an
investigation of the next part in the assembly. The last outcome will initialise the
movement of the part. The investigation of a part continues until one of the two ﬁrst
outcomes are reached and the evaluation is complete when all part are freed from the
assembly, or the remaining parts are unable to be disassembled.

10. Weighted selective disassembly analysis
The functionality of weighting parts and connections is essential for developing a tool for
practical application. The weighting enables the differentiation of parts and connections
to achieve the optimum disassembly procedure. For example it is easier to dismantle a
bolt-connection rather than a weld-connection and a tool for practical application must
be able to distinguish between the two to evaluate the optimum solution.
The weighted selective disassembly analysis is a further development of the selective
disassembly method presented in Section 9.2. The weighted analysis is developed
to accommodate user inputs that differentiate various components and connection of
the assembly. The weighted analysis is only signiﬁcant when performing a selective
disassembly, as that method involves the selection of one disassembly procedure over
another. If for example a part is fragile it is preferable not to disturb or dismantle this
part during maintenance of another part.
The ﬁrst step of the weighted analysis component is the generation and visualisation
of part graphs. The visualisation prompts the user to generate the data needed for the
weighted analysis. The user is prompted to generate values for both part and connection
characteristics, see Section 10.2. A number of characteristics could be deemed signiﬁcant
for a speciﬁc project, but the characteristics might differ greatly form one project to
another. For example, one analysis might prefer to remove parts produced by a speciﬁc
company. Another analysis might focus on the swiftness of part replacement during
maintenance.
For this investigation, four weighted characteristics are selected. Additional character-
istics are possible to add as the tool reaches a practical application stage and speciﬁc
needs are uncovered. The characteristics selected to be included in this investigation is
elaborated in Section 10.2.
The second step of the weighted analysis is the disassembly evaluation. The evaluation
includes the user input of weighted characteristics.

The values are structured to increase with the difﬁculty of moving the part. The scoring

Figure 10.2. The domain of the values are simpliﬁed to 1, 2, and 3 for the development
of the tool, but the evaluation of the optimal path for selective disassembly is calculated
based on a lowest summarised value. The values can for this reason be extended to any
selected domain. The part lifetime can for example be introduced as number of years
instead. The ﬁxed domain is chosen for the development process as this means the values
are equally calibrated. The characteristics can be expanded, subdivided, or ignored as
needed for a speciﬁc project. The characteristics described are the ones selected for
developing the functionality.
Part moveability
This characteristic refers to the difﬁculty of moving a speciﬁc part. It is a sum of the
attributes that makes moving the part easy or difﬁcult. The value correlated to attributes
such as the cost/risk/difﬁculty of moving the part. Information such as if a part is fragile
65
==PAGE:74==
or not is included in this category, but other aspects like the physical weight of the part
might affect the value for moving the part. Likewise, a bulky part might not be desirable
to move. The characteristic might take into account if a crane or lift is needed to move
the part.
Catarina Thormark propose a number of categories in a similar way [Thormark, 2001],
see Figure 10.2. The assessed parameter described as "Damage to the material caused
by disassembly" is included in this characteristic. Other parameters of the proposal falls
into other categories.
Part lifetime
This characteristic is simply the lifetime of the part, as this might effect the order desired
for the disassembly. For example it would be preferable to be able to replace part of a
short lifetime without having to remove and risk damage to parts of a longer lifetime.
Connection type
The type of connection is an important value as the joints chosen for a construction has
a great impact on the ability to undo or disconnect that particular connection. This issue
has also been addressed by Thormark [Thormark, 2001].
"The aspects of dismantling and recycling are rarely, if ever, included in the design
of joints and connections today. To dismantle modern constructions can therefore be
very time consuming, cause an unacceptable amount of damaged material, or simply be
impossible. It is, however, the knowledge of those joints and connections that architects
and engineers have in their toolbox when designing building constructions. To ﬁnd
joints that can be dismantled will therefore need some extra effort." [Thormark, 2001]
Connections such as bolt and nut connections are considered good in regard to disas-
sembleability, as the connections can be done and undone a number of times without
causing damage to the connected parts or the connector (bolt and nut). Connections
done with glue or mortar on the other hand might make the connection difﬁcult to break.
It might also introduce the need for chemical procedures or hazardous materials to break
the connection.
Disconnection time
This characteristic collects information that might effect the time it takes to break a
connection. This is to some degree related to the type of connection, but two connections
of the same type might have different disconnection times. For example, two bolt
connections might be assigned the same value for the characteristic "connection type",
but the number of bolts included in the connection could greatly effect the time needed

to disconnect the joints. The category also cover aspects such as the accessibility of the
joint. For example, if a screw joint needs to be undone it effects the time if a electric
tool is not able to access the joint and hand tools are the only option.

A large portion of the parameters used to assess reuse-ability by Thormark, see Figure
10.2, are in included in this characteristic. The "Time requirement" is an obvious one, but
also the "Access to joints", "Tolls/equipment", and "Risks in the working environment"

12. Conclusion
The initial literature review revealed a number of studies into DfD analysis with digital
tools, but all studies introduces different restrictions to the analysis to simplify the
evaluation. The restrictions limits the application to permanent architecture.
The many approaches to DfD, establish a baseline for the algorithms necessary for
DfD evaluations, but the many different approaches and methods underline the lack of
guidelines for the format and extent of evidence needed.
The study also showed that the evaluation and assessment of DfD is very complex and a
lot more work needs to be put into developing not only digital tools but also guidelines
for the evidence that the evaluations should provide.
The development undertaken in this project resulted in a successful implementation of
automated geometry interpretation. Rhino or Grasshopper geometry can be interpreted
automatically and evaluated to produce complete and selective disassembly plans.
The evaluation of disassembly procedures have been enabled by the implementation of
graph theory. The use of graph theory to describe components and their interrelationships
have been found to be efﬁcient and versatile. Graph theory has also been found to be
capable of handling data in a more diverse ways. The investigations performed in this
project showed an improvement in structured data evaluations when using graph theory
instead of the currently preferred 2D structures used in for example spreadsheets.
The development of the tool performed in this project has resulted in a successful
application of weighted parts, which enables the differentiation of parts based on selected
characteristic. The application of weights drives the disassembly evaluation to produce
the optimum disassembly plan based on the selected characteristics and applied weights.
The functionality of distinguishing between connections needs further development to
show the full potential of the DfD evaluation tools.
The developed tools have performed successfully in the evacuation of 2D assemblies
and are developed for easy extension to 3D evacuation.
This work shows that applying the Livestock3D method to the evaluation enables a more
diverse and robust evaluation engine. The link between the geometry in Rhino/Grasshop-
per and the sturdy Python language enables DfD evaluation, but also for other complex
evaluations of geometry.

13. Discussion and further work
It is recognised that the effort to achieve a sustainable construction is a complex process,
which includes all aspects of the design, including insight into structural engineering,
indoor climate, LCA, user experience, ﬂexible design, economy, and more. And that it
is necessary for some sort of guideline to bee formed before it is possible develop digital
tools that provide the support needed in an effortless manner.
As DfD is still mostly addressed in regard to temporary architecture, the ﬁnancial gain of
documenting a disassembly plan is less than if it was demanded for permanent structures.
The legislation on sustainability is swiftly developing and it might be this development
that creates the market for digital tools to support designing for disassembly.
The timing of this investigation might be of as the demand for such tools are not yet
established in the construction industry. Swift and efﬁcient construction is an upfront
ﬁnancial gain. Maintenance and dismantling of constructions on the other hand are a
gradual or far away ﬁnancial gain, and for this reason less comprehensible.
13.1
Further development
The amount of further development needed for assessing DfD is staggering. Ensuring
a evaluation method that account for the entire disassembly process and all possible
outcomes, will be essential in producing a digital tool that mimics the evaluating abilities
of experienced engineers.
The amount of data generated by experience encourages the use of artiﬁcial intelligence
based digital tools.
Developing algorithms that can handle geometrically ﬂexible components, such as
membranes and rubber ﬁllets is another major aspect to tackle. Many more algorithms
for various instances are needed in order for the digital tool to provide efﬁcient and
robust evidence.
