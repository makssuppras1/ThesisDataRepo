Abstract

Abstract
The current presence of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) in nature repre-
sents a major environmental challenge. Their application in a wide range of products
has entailed an increased focus on these substances following the findings that some
of them can cause adverse effects in humans, and a number of them have been re-
stricted.
Although the toxicity of some PFASs has been well studied, knowledge
on the potential adverse environmental effects of fluorinated degradation products
that may emerge following the breakdown of mother PFAS compounds such as the
pesticide diflufenican (DFF), and the pharmaceutical fluoxetine (FLX) is limited.
This study investigates the aquatic ecotoxicity of three fluorinated degradation
products of pesticides and pharmaceuticals; 3-(trifluoromethyl)phenol (3-TFMP), 4-
(trifluoromethyl)phenol (4-TFMP), and trifluoroacetic acid (TFA). To achieve this,
the toxic effects of these substances toward algae (R. subcapitata), invertebrates (D.
magna), and fish (D. rerio) are evaluated by conducting tests following OECD Test
Guidelines 201, 202, 211 and 236, obtaining both short-term and long-term ecotoxicity
data for the base set for aquatic freshwater organisms.
3-TFMP was found to induce chronic toxic effects in freshwater organisms at
a concentration of 0.9 mg/L, qualifying for the CLP classification aquatic chronic
2. No aquatic hazard classification was assigned to 4-TFMP and TFA even though
long-term toxicity was observed for TFA; this was seen at 8 mg/L. None of the test
substances are observed to be toxic (T) under the PBMT framework. Acute toxicity
was observed for all substances in the mg/L range, and aquatic toxicity ranking of
3-TFMP > 4-TFMP > TFA can be established for R. subcapitata and D. magna, and
4-TFMP > 3-TFMP > TFA for D. rerio.
PNECfws were estimated from the long-term ecotoxicity results to be 0.006, 0.13,
and 0.56 mg/L for 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, and TFA, respectively, the latter of which
was already reported to ECHA before this study was conducted. Following ECHA
guidelines for the derivation of water quality criteria, AA-EQSfws are suggested to be
equal to these values. Based on the short-term ecotoxicity results, MAC-EQSfws of
0.041, 0.118 and 1.36 mg/L were suggested for 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA.
With the suggested freshwater quality criteria, this study provides the basis for
an environmental risk assessment of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, and TFA with respect to
aquatic freshwater ecotoxicity. The results further indicate that the MAC-EQS for
TFA set by the Danish EPA at 2.37 mg/L may be underestimated. Given the limited
data on fluorinated degradation products, it is recommended to conduct persistence
and mobility studies for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP to conclude whether these substances
pose a risk to the aquatic environment.

CHAPTER 1
Background & Aim
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) make up a category of chemicals that
are used in several industrial activities due to their extreme stability and unique
properties to repel water, fat, and dirt (Glüge et al. 2020). Because of these properties
and their wide applicability in industry, they were regarded as miracle compounds
for several decades. However, their growing presence in the environment has led to
an increased focus on their potential to degrade, bioaccumulate, and induce toxic
effects on various organisms (OECD & UNEP 2015). Since PFASs are exceptionally
durable under environmental conditions, they do not degrade in nature, and several
long-chain PFASs (LC-PFASs) have been found to bioaccumulate and cause adverse
effects in humans (Kjøholt et al. 2015).
As a result, the use of some LC-PFASs
such as perfluorooctanesulfonic acid (PFOS) and perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA) has
been restricted. The restriction of LC-PFASs has caused the industrial sector to shift
toward the use of PFAS free alternatives but also short-chain PFASs (SC-PFASs)
due to the demand for substances with similar properties. Uses of SC-PFASs include
the production of PFAS containing pesticides and pharmaceuticals to which they add
desired properties, such as increased stability, which prolongs the half-life of these
products.
An example of a SC-PFAS is trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), which is a fluorinated
degradation product of both the herbicide diflufenican (DFF), and the pharmaceuti-
cal fluoxetine (FLX). TFA is also used in a variety of industrial process, especially
for the synthesis of organic chemicals (López and Salazar 2013). TFA is well mon-
itored, and its extensive use has resulted in its presence in virtually every type of
aquatic environmental compartment, since a shorter carbon chain length increases
the hydrophilicity and thus the mobility of SC-PFASs (Kjøholt et al. 2015). The
widespread presence of TFA has entailed that it is relatively well studied in terms of
environmental behavior and toxic effects.
In contrast to TFA, knowledge on other fluorinated degradation products such
as 3-(trifluoromethyl)phenol (3-TFMP) and 4-(trifluoromethyl)phenol (4-TFMP) is
limited. 3-TFMP is both used in the manufacturing of DFF, but can also be formed
from it when the herbicide degrades, and it can further degrade to TFA (Rouchaud et
al. 1990). Since DFF is dispersed directly in nature through its use in the agricultural
sector, 3-TFMP is likely present in the aquatic environment. 4-TFMP is used for the
synthesis of FLX. FLX has been found to degrade to 4-TFMP during photolysis, and
4-TFMP has also been detected in the urine of users of this drug (Urichuk et al. 1997;
Tisler and Zwiener 2021). Therefore, it is possible that the aquatic environment is
exposed to 4-TFMP substance through wastewater discharge. Like 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP

is also a precursor of the SC-PFAS TFA.
Initially SC-PFASs were believed to be relatively harmless compared to LC-PFASs,
in part due to their limited bioaccumulation in organisms (Falkenberg et al. 2016).
However, this assumption has been disproved, raising concerns about the presence
of perfluorinated degradation products like SC-PFASs in the environment (Arp et al.
2024). This is especially true for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP for which there are almost no
aquatic ecotoxicity data. Furthermore, although the ecotoxicity of some SC-PFASs
such as TFA is relatively well studied, data are still limited for these compared to
LC-PFASs. Adding to this, it is highly probable that 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP will be
found if searched for in surface water samples since PFASs are inherently persistent.
If toxic, they may pose an environmental hazard to aquatic organisms.
Under the Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals
(REACH), the environmental hazard of a compound can be determined according to
Annex XIII, for example by assessing if a substance is a substance of very high concern
(SVHC) due to PBT (persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic) or vPvB (very persistent
and very bioaccumulative) properties (Davies 2006; Rudin et al. 2023).
However,
concerns have been raised in the scientific community about mobile substances such as
PFASs, as they may go unnoticed since many PFASs are not bioaccumulative (Rudin
et al. 2023; Arp et al. 2024). Therefore, calls have been made for the regulatory
introduction of PMT (persistent, mobile, and toxic) or vPvM (very persistent and
very mobile) criteria with the purpose of rectifying this regulatory shortcoming.
The presence of SC-PFASs such as TFA in the aquatic environment is increas-
ing, incentivizing research on the ecotoxicity of fluorinated degradation products.
Although the framework for assessing the hazard of these substances exists, data
on the ecotoxicity of most fluorinated degradation products are remarkably scarce.
Therefore, the aim of this study is to investigate the aquatic ecotoxicity of 3-TFMP,
4-TFMP and TFA on three trophic levels; algae (R. subcapitata), invertebrates (D.
magna), and fish (D. rerio). Based on ecotoxicity data for this complete base set
of aquatic organisms freshwater environmental quality standards (EQSs) can be sug-
gested for each of the fluorinated degradation products assessed. The study will be
conducted as follows:
To understand which substances make up the PFAS family and why they are so widely
used, the history and development of their definition will be described. Subsequently,
the most important regulations and directives relevant for PFASs will be described
with the industrial applications of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA to explain why the
environmental occurrence of fluorinated degradation products is increasing. Their
environmental behavior will then be presented in light of their environmental profiles
to describe their dispersion in the environment. To assess the aquatic ecotoxicity of
3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA, experiments will be carried out according to OECD Test
Guidelines (TGs) 201, 202, 211 and 236. Based on the results, EQSs for freshwater
will be estimated for each substance to provide suggestions for surface water quality
criteria in terms of both short-term and long-term aquatic toxicity.

2.1
Per- and polyfluorinated substances: History,
definition and applications
PFASs is a group of synthetically produced compounds widely used in modern society
across a broad range of consumer products and industrial goods (ECHA 2023a). The
first PFASs were invented in the late 1930’s, and in the 1940’s and 1950’s PFASs
gained a significant foothold on the global market (Brennan et al. 2021). Since then,
numerous more PFASs have been invented.
The definition of PFASs has changed throughout the decades in which they have
been applied. Until the 21st century, PFASs were mostly considered to be hydrocar-
bons in which all H atoms were substituted by F atoms, and these were referred to as
perfluorinated compounds (Banks et al. 2013). In 2011, Buck et al. (2011) suggested
the definition of PFASs to encompass aliphatic substances in which all H atoms of
the hydrocarbon molecule from which it is derived are replaced by F atoms (perflu-
oroalkylated substances), or where at least one C atom in the carbon chain (but not
all) is saturated with F atoms (polyfluoroalkylated substances).
The Global PFC Group under the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and
Development (OECD) and United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) found
that the definition by Buck et al. had a shortcoming, namely that fluoroalkylated
substances with multiple functional groups, such as perfluoroalkyldicarboxylic acids
were not included. They therefore decided to map out the total number of PFASs
on the global market in 2018, defined as substances containing a -CnF2- (n ≥3) or
-CnF2nOCmF2m- (n and m ≥1) moiety (OECD 2018). Under this definition, the
Global PFC Group identified approximately 4700 substances. Following this, several
reports have stated that 4700 PFASs exist, thereby implicitly using this categorization
by the Global PFC Group as a definition of PFASs (Pilli et al. 2021; ECCC 2023).
Subsequently, it was suggested that an oﬀicial amalgamation of the definition by
Buck et al. and the PFAS identification approach by the OECD/UNEP be carried
out (Z Wang, Buser, et al. 2021). In 2021, the following definition for PFASs was
thus proposed by the OECD and is of today widely used when referring to PFASs
(OECD 2021):
”Any substance that contains at least one fully fluorinated methyl (CF3-) or
methylene (-CF2-) carbon atom (without any H/Cl/Br/I attached to it).”
==PAGE:16==
2.1 Per- and polyfluorinated substances: History, definition and applications
4
Later, the US Congress defined PFASs as “man-made chemicals with at least one
fully fluorinated carbon atom” (Williams et al. 2022). It should be noted that few
exceptions irrelevant to this report apply to the OECD definition and will therefore
not be described in further detail (OECD 2021).
A crucial and important shift in the perception of what a PFAS is that the above
definition is not exclusively restricted to aliphatic substances. The shift from the
PFAS definition suggested by Banks et al. and OECD/UNEP in 2018 to that of
OECD in 2021 implies that many more PFASs are included in the PFAS family than
before. According to the COMPTOX database administered by the United States
Environmental Protection Agency (U.S. EPA), the entire group of known PFASs
under the OECD definition comprised of more than 14,000 chemicals as of January
2024 (this number varies depending on which PFAS definition is applied) (Williams
et al. 2022; Grunfeld et al. 2024; U.S. EPA 2021)
Products in which PFASs can be found include (but are not limited to) water
and fat repellent surfactants, firefighting foams, non-stick cookware, textiles, pes-
ticides and pharmaceuticals (Buck et al. 2011; Glüge et al. 2020; Wee and Aris
2023). The widespread use of PFASs is due to their unique physicochemical proper-
ties; the molecules themselves contain a non-polar end containing the carbon-fluorine
group (the ”tail”), and another end containing a polar functional group (the ”head”).
This entails that PFASs are both hydrophobic (fat-repellent) and lipophobic (water-
repellent), as well as oleophobic (dirt repellent) (Kjøholt et al. 2015; Glüge et al.
2020).
The C-F bond is the strongest single bond in organic chemistry, meaning that
PFASs are extremely durable under environmental conditions (Gaines et al. 2023).
Namely, defluorination through processes such as photolysis, hydrolysis and oxida-
tion/reduction of PFASs, are not known to occur in nature (ECHA 2024c). In addi-
tion, only isolated strains of bacteria and fungi have been observed to degrade PFASs,
while no naturally occurring organisms have been observed to perform complete de-
fluorination (Shahsavari et al. 2021). PFASs are thus generally said to be resistant to
both abiotic and biotic degradation. However, some polyfluoroalkylated substances
such as 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, and fluorotelomer alcohols (FTOHs) can undergo bio-
transformation, but only to perfluoroalkylated molecules such as TFA, which is the
endpoint in the natural degradation chain (Zhang et al. 2020; Bhardwaj et al. 2024).
PFASs can be divided into two different groups depending on their carbon chain
length. LC-PFASs contain a carbon chain of 7 or more per- and/or polyfluoroalky-
lated carbon atoms, while SC-PFAS contain 6 or less (Buck et al. 2011).
In the
literature, PFASs that contain 3 or less per- and/or polyfluoroalkylated C atoms are
sometimes referred to as ultra short-chain PFASs. However, in the current study,
PFASs containing carbon chains of 6 or fewer C atoms will simply be referred to as
SC-PFASs. The properties of LC-PFASs and SC-PFASs can vary greatly from sub-
stance to substance, and therefore they cannot always be regarded as a single group
with the same physicochemical properties although all share the same property of
being persistent (Baun et al. 2025).
Another aspect that affects the physicochemical properties of a PFAS is whether
==PAGE:17==

5
it is neutral or ionic. Ionic PFASs contain an acid group, e.g. perfluoroalkyl carboxyl
acids (PFCAs) and perfluoroalkyl sulfonic acids (PFSAs). On the other hand, the
category of neutral PFASs comprises of perfluoroalkylated substances containing hy-
droxyl and sulfonamide functional groups (Bossi and Ellermann 2023). Data suggest
that fluorosulfonamides are more toxic than sulphonic acids, which, in turn, are more
toxic than carboxylic acids. This rule of thumb seems to apply to both long- and
short-chain PFASs (Kjøholt et al. 2015; Leuthner et al. 2024).
2.2
Regulatory framework for PFAS in the EU
Public authorities around the world have been concerned about the presence of PFASs
in the environment since the 1990s, when it became publicly known that some exert
toxic effects on organisms that ingest them (Gaber et al. 2023). The most common
(and some of the most well-studied) examples of toxic perfluoroalkylated species are
PFOS, and PFOA. As a result of the increased attention on this issue in the scien-
tific community and the public, restrictions have been placed on the use of PFOS
and PFOA via the Stockholm Convention in 2009 and 2019 respectively (Vorst et
al. 2021; Brennan et al. 2021). Additionally, perfluorohexanoic acid (PFHxA), and
long-chain perfluorocarboxylic acids (LC-PFCAs) that contain carbon chains of 9-21
carbon atoms, their salts and related compounds have been adopted into the Stock-
holm Convention. It should be noted that while industrial nations have succeeded in
imposing restrictions on the use PFOS and PFOA, these substances are still widely
found in nature, and used in some developing and underdeveloped countries (Wee
and Aris 2023). For example, they have been found in samples of imported products
in the US (Rodgers et al. 2022).
The restriction of PFASs has encouraged the innovation of PFAS-free alternatives
that have similar properties (Figuière et al. 2025). However, for many applications,
PFASs are still the optimal option on the market, and therefore the issue of environ-
mental exposure to these substances has persisted; regulatory action is slow on the
PFAS issue, since substances the potential restriction of substances is a demanding
and time consuming process, often leading to the restriction of one or a few substances
at the time. Due to the sheer number of PFASs, industry has therefore been able
to shift to other substances of the PFAS family in a cascade-like trend following the
ban on a few target substances. An example of this is the use of hexafluoropropylene
oxide dimer acid (HFPO-DA) as an alternative to PFOA, or perfluorobutanesulfonic
acid (PFBS) as a replacement for PFOS (Gaballah et al. 2020; U.S.
EPA 2024).
HFPO-DA and PFBS have since been placed on the Candidate List of Substances of
Very High Concern in the EU due to their persistence, mobility and toxicity (Hale
et al. 2020).
In an effort to avoid this development, and since PFASs have now found in virtually
any environmental compartment on Earth due to their high mobility and persistence,
the scientific community and public authorities are discussing whether the continuous
release of PFASs as a whole is attainable. In this regard, a restriction proposal for all

PFASs falling under the 2021 OECD PFAS definition has been put forward by the
five EU Member States Germany, Denmark, the Netherlands, Sweden and Norway

2.2.1
Pre-market regulations
In the European Union, all chemicals that are produced and/or imported in quanti-
ties of >1 t per year must be registered under the REACH regulation of 2006.The
registration process includes data collection on physicochemical properties, as well as
information on the persistence of the substance in the environment, bioaccumulation
potential, and toxicity. The specific data collection requirements vary depending on
the tonnage band in which a given substance is found (EC 2006). For substances
imported or produced in the EU in quantities of >10 t/yr, it is mandatory that a
Chemical Safety Assessment (CSA) be carried out and reported in a Chemical Safety
Report (CSR). The purpose of the CSA is to assess whether a given substance is
harmful and poses a risk to human health and the environment (ECHA n.d.[a]). Ac-
cording to REACH, a substance can be classified as an SVHC and be subject to
restriction by being placed on the Candidate List of SVHCs if it is found to pose a
risk to human health and the environment. The criteria for qualifying as an SVHC
are stated in Article 57 and are further specified in Annex XIII in REACH (EC 2006).
These criteria can be seen in Table 2.1 and are essential for the environmental hazard
assessment of chemicals.
Since the 2000s, calls have been made for determining the environmental risk
of persistent, hydrophilic compounds (Steinhäuser et al. 2004). Vidal et al. (2009)
pointed out this shortcoming in EU environmental legislation due to the lack of
monitoring of mobile substances e.g. transformation products of pesticides and phar-
maceuticals. Since then, the discussion on whether to implement legislation related
to the mobility of a substance has been gaining traction, only accelerated by the
increased attention on PFASs in the public debate after environmental issues such as
the introduction of HFPO-DA. Under REACH, substances such as PFOA and PFOS
have been deemed SVHCs on the basis of their persistence, mobility, and toxicity
with reference to the equivalent level of concern (ELoC) principle presented in Article
57(f) in REACH (EC 2006; Hale et al. 2020). However, this has only been done on
a case-by-case basis, and currently the mobility of a substance is not applied in the
hazard assessment of chemicals under REACH.
In addition to REACH, the Classification, Labelling and Packaging (CLP) which
was put in force in 2008 is a substantial part of the chemicals legislation in the EU,
separate from REACH. The CLP is meant to enforce the United Nation’s Globally
Harmonized System (GHS) framework in the industrial sector of the EU with the

goal of providing transparency and communication about all substances and/or mix-
tures (ECHA n.d.[a]).
Regardless of the volume in which a substance/mixture is
produced or imported, it must be labeled if it is hazardous to ensure the safety of
humans and the environment. Producers or importers of a given substance must also
report any hazard information to the C&L Inventory under ECHA (ECHA n.d.[a]).
Some hazard classifications that exist in the CLP are derived from the two outdated
pieces legislation, the Dangerous Substances Directive (DSD), and the Dangerous
Preparations Directive (DPD), which are not included in the GHS. In 2023, a CLP
amendment was put into force which integrates the environmental hazard criteria
of PBT substance from Annex XIII in REACH into the CLP hazard classification
system. Moreover, two new hazard classifications were introduced and allow the clas-
sification of a substance as persistent, mobile and toxic (PMT), or very persistent and
very mobile (vPvM) which is crucial for the potential classification of PFASs due to
their inherent phyiscochemical properties (see Table 2.1.
The specific criteria for a substance to be classified as PMT or vPvM are shown in
Table 2.1. The criteria for a substance to be deemed M or vM are based on the Koc
of said substance. Several test guidelines and other tools for estimating the Koc of
a substance are suggested by ECHA in accordance with the CLP legislation (ECHA
2024b). However, when it comes to PFASs, this must be carried out with caution
with reference to their unique surface-active properties (Hidalgo and Mora-Diez 2015;
Xiang et al. 2018).
In addition to the PBMT framework under the CLP, the hazard of a substance
can also be assessed through the CLP aquatic toxicity classification system. This
classification system applies specifically to the environmental hazard to aquatic or-
ganisms. Depending on the ecotoxicity and persistence data for a given substance,
an aquatic hazard classification can be assigned.
For a substance to qualify for the labeling aquatic acute 1 (A1), an acute EC50/LC50
of ≤1 mg/L must be observed - this applies to all substances regardless of their
degradability. Four chronic toxicity categories exist (aquatic chronic 1-4 (C1-4)) and
apply differently depending on the degradability of the substance and the amount
of available chronic toxicity data (EC 2016). For substances such as PFASs which
are not rapidly degradable (e.g., not ready biodegradable), the classifications aquatic
chronic 1 (C1) and aquatic chronic 2 (C2) can be assigned if chronic toxicity data
exist for three trophic levels (ECHA 2023c). If chronic toxicity data in the form of
ECx or NOEC are ≤0.1 mg/L, C1 applies, and C2 applies if these data are ≤1
mg/L. In cases where there are insuﬀicient chronic toxicity data available, Aquatic
chronic 3 (C3) can be assigned if the substance is not rapidly degradable and acute
EC50/LC50 values are > 10 to ≤100 mg/L. The implications of this in terms of
aquatic ecotoxicity are that if the short-term EC50/LC50 of a substance is found to
be > 100 mg/L, it is not required for the labeling of aquatic toxicity under the CLP,
which is also true for substances if long-term ECx/NOEC data are > 1 mg/L (EC
2016).

In the year 2000 the Water Framework Directive (WFD) was implemented in the
European Union to ensure that water bodies in various environmental compartments
met the needs of ecosystem functionality and human use (EC 2023b). In short, the
main purpose of this legislation is to obtain good status (meaning both good ecological
and good chemical status) in various surface waters as well as groundwater in the
EU as outlined in Article II of the regulation. In Article IV of the legislation, the
specific actions required from each member state to meet these needs are described

and include encouragement for cooperation between Member States within the union,
as these often share parts of the same bodies of water (European Council 2000). The
goal of the WFD was to reach good status in all water bodies in 2015, with 2027 being

2.2.3
Establishing surface freshwater quality standards
The Environmental Quality Standards Directive (EQSD) was introduced in 2008 and
entered into force in the EU in 2010 as a replacement for existing guidelines on
environmental limit values from the 1980s (European Council 2008).
The EQSD
acts as an addition to the WFD as a measure to reach the aforementioned goals of
good status in European bodies of water. The EQSD framework specifically outlines
environmental quality standards for priority substances which are laid out in the WFD
to which the Member States must adhere to reach the water quality goals of the WFD.
Under the EQSD, two categories of EQSs exist:
Annual average value EQS
(AA-EQS), and maximum allowed concentration EQS (MAC-EQS).
An AA-EQS
describes the average concentration of a substance during a measuring period of one
year at which no environmental hazard is expected. The AA-EQS of a substance
is closely related to the Predicted No-Effect Concentration (PNEC) of a substance
which describes the predicted concentration at which no adverse effects are expected
in a given environmental compartment. Under REACH it is a mandatory requirement
to estimate the PNEC for any given substance. The PNEC is based on ecotoxicity
data and can be used as a tool to provide water quality criteria such as EQSs for use
in environmental legislation. This is crucial for the environmental protection of any
recipient from exposure to a given substance.
Specifically in the case of surface freshwater environmental compartments, the
method for deriving the AA-EQSfw is the same as for deriving the PNEC. Thus,
the approximation AA-EQSfw ≈PNECfw can be made for all substances (EC 2018).
On the other hand, a MAC-EQS is the maximum concentration allowed for a single
measurement of a priority substance. The purpose of the MAC-EQS is to provide a
maximum tolerable environmental concentration of a substance in the case of pulse-
like emissions into the environment such as accidental spills or discharge of heavily
polluted water. Unlike the AA-EQS, the MAC-EQS is derived by the lowest short-
term EC50/LC50 value at hand, and not on the PNEC.
Note that AA-EQSs and MAC-EQSs differ for a substance depending on whether
it is measured in fresh water or salt water. Currently, EQSs for PFASs are being

discussed within the EU. In 2022, the Scientific Committee on Health, Environmental
and Emerging Risks (SCHEER) proposed that these be based on relative potency

2.2.4
Additional methods for establishing European water quality
criteria
The PNEC approach is related to direct ecotoxicity and has the function of predicting
the potential environmental hazard of a substance. However, for a complete risk as-
sessment of a substance with the aim of establishing water quality criteria, the PNEC
is not necessarily suﬀicient on its own, depending on the compound’s physicochemical
properties and ecotoxicity data. Specifically, in addition to PNEC based EQSs two
other methods can be applied to establish water quality criteria for a given substance;
secondary poisoning, and human toxicity.
Secondary poisoning refers to the hazard that a substance poses to top predatory
organisms (such as humans) by ingestion of contaminated prey. With this method,
quality criteria are made based on toxicity data for mammals and birds (EC 2018).
Secondary poisoning assessments are required for substances with a logKOW ≥3
whose bioconcentration factor (BCF) has been estimated to be ≥100 L kg−1 and
where there is evidence of long-term mammalian toxicity (EMA 2024). From a given
intake rate described by the no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) of the sub-
stance, or No Observed Effect Concentration (NOEC) data, quality standards can
then be derived.
A human toxicity assessment is required for compounds that are suspected of
being 1) carcinogenic, mutagenic or reprotoxic (CMR), 2) able to cause irreversible
effects, or 3) bioaccumulative according to the same criteria as for substances subject
to secondary poisoning assessment (EC 2018). The environmental quality standard
can then be derived from the NOAEL or NOEC for the given substance which are
based on the mammalian toxicity of the chemical combined with the expected daily
intake of fish and shellfish. In this regard, environmental quality standards can also
be established based on human exposure to contaminated drinking water (EC 2018).

2.3
Environmental occurrence of PFASs
Of the 850,000 t PFASs that are used annually in the EU alone, an estimated 75,000
t end up in the environment during the use phase, excluding emissions during the
waste phase (ECHA 2023a). The waste phase includes processes such as landfilling,
which are suspected to act as PFAS sources due to leaching and may therefore pose
a significant problem for predicting and mitigation of PFAS emissions in the future
(Busch et al. 2010; Knutsen et al. 2019; Capozzi et al. 2023). The complexity of this
problem increases further when degradation products from fluorinated precursors are
kept in mind.
Owing to their inability to degrade under natural conditions, PFASs that get
released into the environment will remain there indefinitely and spread to different
spheres of the environment, which has given rise to their colloquial branding as ”for-
ever chemicals” (Ahrens 2011). LC-PFASs tend to adsorb to soil, while SC-PFASs
are more mobile and can travel long distances using aqueous media (Brendel et al.
2018). In 2019, ECHA reported the presence of HFPO-DA which is also known as
GenX when it is on its ammonium salt form, in the North Sea over 1700 km from
Dordrecht, where its production site was believed to be located (ECHA 2019b).
Water is not the only media through which PFASs can spread. For example, fluor
gases used in refrigerators and the air conditioning of cars that get released through
leakage can travel thousands of kilometers by air before degrading into PFASs such
as TFA and precipitating (Adlunger et al. 2021). As such, various PFASs such as
PFOS and PFOA have been found in rainwater in Tibet and Antarctica.
PFOA
concentrations in these areas were found to exceed the oﬀicial advisory guideline
values of the US EPA for drinking water (Cousins, Johansson, et al. 2022).
Following their increasing presence in the biosphere, PFASs have been found to
be ingested by a broad range of biota, from bacteria and fungi to plants and animals
including humans (Rumsby and Manning 2018; Sunderland et al. 2019; Wang et al.
2020; Golovko et al. 2022). This uptake is either a result of direct exposure (biocon-
centration) or indirect exposure (biomagnification). As for bioconcentration, PFASs
can enter plants by active nutrient uptake via their roots (Wang et al. 2020). The
presence of PFASs in smaller aquatic organisms is mostly due to exposure to water
polluted by PFASs (Rumsby and Manning 2018). For humans, a significant direct ex-
posure route is drinking water and food containing mother compounds of PFASs and
PFAS degradation products (Adlunger et al. 2021; Falkenberg et al. 2016). As these
substances move up the food chain, biomagnification can lead to progressively higher
concentrations in the tissues of top predators — including humans — through their
diet (Kärrman et al. 2019). For example, PFASs have been identified in vegetables
and crops for human consumption, and high concentrations of PFASs were found in
the predatory grey seal in Sweden in 2012 (Boisvert et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2020).

2.3.1
Emerging concerns over fluorinated degradation products
During recent years, the industry has experienced a shift from LC-PFASs towards
short-chain ones, in part due to regulations and customer demands. In this section,
the reason for this change is described along with new potential issues that have
arisen from this development due to the fact that regulations on PFASs have been
based primarily on LC-PFASs as exemplified by Section 2.1. The substances chosen
for the ecotoxicity tests to be conducted in this study are also described.
LC-PFASs were the first ones to be commercialized and used, and they have
been used for several decades (Brennan et al. 2021). Due to the high lipophilicity of
LC-PFASs relative to short-chain ones, BCF values tend to correlate positively with
the C-length of PFASs, resulting in the potential for LC-PFASss to bioaccumulate (Pi
et al. 2017). LC-PFASs have been found to accumulate in the liver, kidney, and blood
by binding to proteins found in these compartments such as liver fatty acid binding
protein (L-FABP) and human serum albumin (HSA) (Brunn et al. 2023; Vujic et al.
2024). The fact that several of them induce toxic effects in humans is well documented
(Wee and Aris 2023; Leuthner et al. 2024).
Specifically, PFOS and PFOA have
been observed to induce reprotoxic, carcinogenic, and mutagenic effects in mammals,
immunotoxic effects in humans, and they affect aquatic organisms adversely in a wide
range of types of toxicity (Boudreau et al. 2003; Ding et al. 2013; Yang et al. 2019;
Mao et al. 2023; Ma et al. 2023; Bline et al. 2024). Immunotoxic effects in mice have
also been observed for perfluorononanoic acid (PFNA) (Rockwell et al. 2013).
As mentioned above, the industrial sector has turned away from using LC-PFASs
following the realization that several LC-PFASs are hazardous to humans and the
aforementioned restrictions imposed on some of the widely used ones. In their place,
short-chain analogues that have similar properties, or PFAS free alternatives are
now being used (Wang et al. 2015; Falkenberg et al. 2016). The transition toward
SC-PFASs is possible because they are less restricted then LC-PFASs, since they have
been assumed to be less bioaccumulative and toxic than long-chain ones (Cousins et al.
2020). However, while SC-PFASs may be less toxic than their long-chain equivalents,
they are not necessarily harmless, as examplified by the aforementioned case with
the substitution of PFOA and PFOS by the SC-PFASs HFPO-DA, and PFBS. This
is likely also the case for fluorinated degradation products that do not necessarily
fall under the definition of SC-PFASs but may have similar properties. Furthermore,
increasing toxicity with shorter chain-lengths of PFCAs has been observed in the
rotifer Brachionus calyciflorus, while the reverse pattern has been observed in other
organisms such as D. magna. Moreover, some species such as the sludge worm Tubifex
tubifex even show random fluctuations in toxicity over an increasing carbon chain-
length for PFCAs (Tichý et al. 2010). This shows that predicting the toxicity of
PFASs is diﬀicult, and that generalizations on this matter should be made with
caution (Wang et al. 2014).
Generally, knowledge about the ecotoxicity of fluorinated degradation products is
extremely limited, while SC-PFASs are widely used, which means that the normally
applied precautionary principle is largely being disregarded in this process (Cousins et

2.3 Environmental occurrence of PFASs
13
al. 2016; Ateia et al. 2019; Dimitrakopoulou et al. 2024). Evidence that SC-PFASs
appear increasingly in human blood and urine samples has further exacerbated con-
cerns (Zheng et al. 2023).
Adding to the concerns that degradation products of fluorinted substances such
as SC-PFASs may pose risks to human health and the environment, many of them
have also been found to be extremely mobile in water, as mentioned in Section 2.3
due to high hydrophilicity due to low log Koc values (Kjøholt et al. 2015; Falkenberg
et al. 2016; Brendel et al. 2018; R Li and Gibson 2022). This has led scientists to
advise against letting the release of SC-PFASs and their precursors go unnoticed and
unregulated, as they possess the potential to accumulate in the water cycle as a whole,
exposing humans and other organisms to them (Scheurer et al. 2017; Arp et al. 2024).
2.3.2
PFAS containing pesticides
Until November 2023, 37 PFAS containing pesticides were approved for use in the
EU (EC n.d.). 14 of these were used in Denmark in 2023 (DK EPA 2023). Since
then, the approval period for 20 of the PFAS containing pesticides has ended, but
14 of them have been re-approved, implying that 31 PFAS containing pesticides were
approved in the EU as of April 2025.
The most widely used PFAS pesticide in Denmark is DFF which is a PFAS under
the OECD definition because it contains a trifluoromethylphenyl group from the
addition of the PFAS 3-TFMP (Fujiwara and O’Hagan 2014). DFF was introduced in
Denmark in 1999, and the active substance is marketed both in products containing
only the pure substance, and as part of a mixture with other compounds such as
Mateno Duo (DK EPA 2024a; SEGES 2025). The herbicide is used in wheat, barley,
rye, and triticale fields, fruit and Christmas tree plantations, and in grass, vegetable,
and berry production to inhibit the growth of weeds such as garden speedwell, ragwort,
waterhemp and palmer amaranth by phytoene desaturase (PDS) inhibition (Svendsen
et al. 2020; DK EPA 2024a; Soltani et al. 2024).
The advantage of fluorination of pesticides is that it increases the stability of the
active substance and degradation products that may also have biocidal properties
(Donley et al. 2024). In addition, fluorination increases the lipophilicity of the sub-
stances and allows the synthesizing of compounds with a specific stereochemistry for
high-precision targeting of unwanted species (Alexandrino et al. 2022).
DFF is listed on the Danish candidate list for substitution of pesticides and has
been observed to show moderate to high persistency in aerobic soil and medium to
very high persistency under anaerobic conditions (EFSA 2007). 2-[3-(trifluoromethyl)phenoxy]pyrid
3-carboxamide and 2-[3-(trifluoromethyl)phenoxy]pyridine-3-carboxylic acid have been
identified as the only major degradation products of diflufenican in persistence ex-
periments, indicating that degradation products such as 3-TFMP which is a likely
degradation product of DFF, are not well studied.
Nonetheless, DFF is still being used to a vast extent; DFF sales in Denmark more
than tripled from 27 t in 2013 to 95 t in 2022 where it ranked 6th in the sale of all

14
pesticide active substances (DK EPA 2024a). Data on global DFF sales are relatively
scarce, although it has been estimated that 2,332 t PFAS containing pesticides were
sold in France in 2021, 561 t of which were DFF (PAN Europe 2023). The use of DFF
has been approved in the EU, UK, Australia, South America, and the US, although
more detailed data on the consumption herein are not available (PMRA 2023; Soltani
et al. 2024). Sales figures for DFF in the UK in 2020 was 376 t (Ridley et al. 2020).
Picolinafen is another PFAS containing pesticide that is approved for use in the
EU and contains a trifluoromethylphenyl group and may degrade to 3-TFMP, but
sales information is limited. It can be assumed that it is only used to a limited degree
in Denmark; it has not been purchased in Denmark since 2013 (DK EPA 2024a).
2.3.3
PFAS pharmaceuticals
The use of fluorination of active substances to increase stability is not restricted
to biocides.
For example, the selective serotonine reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) FLX
contains a trifluoromethylphenyl group for this exact purpose (Inkielewicz-Stêpniak
2011). 4-TFMP crucial for the synthesis of FLX. FLX, often marketed as ProzacTM
(Urichuk et al. 1997; Wawryniuk et al. 2018).
It is used for treatment of mental
illnesses such as depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and obsessive
compulsive disorder (OCD) (Sohel et al. 2024).
FLX was 5th most used antidepressant in the US in 2022 after sertraline, esci-
talopram, trazodone, and bupropion. According to the ClinCalc database driven by
the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ), 24 million prescriptions
were made that year (AHRQ 2025). In Europe, other SSRIs appear to dominate
the market, but FLX is still prescribed, with a higher market share in Spain than in
Northern Europe according to Forns et al. (2019). The exact mass of FLX produced
and consumed annually is not clear, as prescription data does not mention the dose
of the substance.
2.4
Environmental profiling of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA
As showcased in Section 2.3.1, concerns about the release and exposure to PFASs
have until recently been voiced with the primary focus being on LC-PFASs. Since
the regulatory spotlight has not been directed at SC-PFASs or degradation products
of fluorinated substances, the international community now faces a global burden
of dealing with these substances whose potential environmental hazard may have
been overlooked in terms of persistence, environmental distribution, and ecotoxicity.
The fact that data on the ecotoxicity of these substances are limited, while their
use is increasing, highlights the demand for knowledge on this topic. The fluorinated
degradation products of DFF and FLX are understudied in terms of the environmental
parameters mentioned above. This is especially interesting because 3-TFMP and 4-
TFMP are degradation products of DFF and FLX respectively, and these can further
degrade to the SC-PFASs TFA.
==PAGE:27==
2.4 Environmental profiling of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA

To assess whether 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA pose a risk to the environment,
they have been selected for investigation of their freshwater ecotoxicity in this study.
The reason for the selection of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP is that they are similar struc-
turally, and TFA is a degradation product of them both. Furthermore, TFA is stud-
ied to a much higher degree than 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP and can therefore act as an
excellent reference substance for comparative purposes. While TFA is well studied
compared to 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP, its aquatic toxicity has nonetheless been inves-
tigated significantly less than many LC-PFASs, and further ecotoxicity data on it is
therefore welcome.
3-TFMP and 4-TFMP are further interesting because they only became defined
as PFASs according to the OECD following the introduction of the revised PFAS
definition put forward by the OECD in 2021 as mentioned in Section 2.1. A brief
description of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, and TFA and their environmental profiles based on
available data on them a priori are presented in the following sections. The ecotoxicity
data presented in the tables only describe the effects observed in the test organisms
that will be exposed in the current study, namely R. subcapitata, D. magna, and D.
rerio. Note also that no distinction between short- and long-term ecotoxicity data
for the R. subcapitata is made in the environmental profiles. EC10 and NOEC values
from 72 h algal tests therefore refer to chronic toxicity endpoints, whereas EC50 values
refer to acute endpoints as reported to ECHA (2022). In the context of R. subcapitata,
no distinction will be made between the effect concentrations denoted ECx and ErCx
(representing the inhibition of growth rate as specifical endpoint).
2.4.1
Environmental profile of 3-(trifluoromethyl)phenol
3-TFMP, or m-(trifluoromethyl)phenol, is used for the synthesizing of DFF and is
also a degradation product of it (Rouchaud et al. 1990; Fujiwara and O’Hagan 2014).
3-TFMP is similarly used in the synthesis of the herbicide picolinafen which is ap-
plied against broadleaf weeds in line with DFF (L Li et al. 2016; Park et al. 2023).
Due to the chemically similar structure of DFF and picolinafen, 3-TFMP is also a
likely degradation product of the latter. The tonnage band of 3-TFMP has not been
reported to ECHA (ECHA 2019a).
It has been recognized that TFA is formed from DFF, and given the structure of
3-TFMP, it is a likely intermediate substance in the process of this degradation chain
(Bhat et al. 2022; Joerss et al. 2024; Albers 2024). Specifically, it is possible that
TFA is formed from 3-TFMP via aromatic oxidation (Leeuwen and Vermeire 2007;
Tisler and Zwiener 2021).
Currently, the only data on the ecotoxicity of 3-TFMP is a short-term EC50 of 11
mg/L toward D. magna (ECHA 2019a). The exact environmental distribution of the
substance is unknown, as current regulations, for example in the EU, do not enforce
monitoring of it, but DFF may serve as a diffuse source of 3-TFMP emissions to the
environment. quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) and quantitative
structure-property relationship (QSPR) modeling data suggest that 3-TFMP is not

environments are exposed to 3-TFMP through direct dispersion of DFF in agricultural
areas. Humans are also likely to be exposed to 3-TFMP as a residue of DFF by

2.4.2
Environmental profile of 4-(trifluoromethyl)phenol
The SC-PFAS 4-TFMP is also known as p-(trifluoromethyl)phenol is used in the
production of fluorinated monomers and polymers (Sigma Aldrich n.d.[b]). It is also
a degradation product of FLX.
FLX has been proven to undergo degradation to
4-TFMP by O-dealkylation of the intermediate Norfluoxetine (NFLX) (Tisler et al.
2019, Liu et al. 2002). No tonnage band of 4-TFMP has been reported to ECHA,
and 4-TFMP does not exist in the ECHA CHEM database (ECHA n.d.[b]; ECHA
2024a).
4-TFMP is not bioaccumulative according to the CLP criteria (see Table 2.3),
and it has been detected in the urine of FLX consumers (Urichuk et al. 1997). In
this regard, wastewater is a possible source of 4-TFMP emissions to the environment.

simulated sunlight, possibly via aromatic oxidation like 3-TFMP (Tisler and Zwiener
2021; Guo et al. 2025). The specific rate at which it was degraded was not reported.

2.4.3
Environmental profile of trifluoroacetic acid
TFA is a SC-PFAS which is studied to a far greater extent than many other SC-PFASs.
In terms of mass, its presence in the environment surpasses all other PFASs (Arp et
al. 2024). It is disputed whether TFA occurs naturally due to the mining of fluoride-
containing minerals such as fluorspar and thermal vent emissions, but it is certain
that TFA is produced synthetically and released to the environment as a consequence
thereof (Joudan et al. 2021; Adlunger et al. 2021).
TFA is used directly for the
synthesis of organic compounds as both solvent, reagent, and catalyst, but it is also a
degradation product of a myriad of PFAS mother compounds and thus has multiple
pathways to the environment (Adlunger et al. 2021).
TFA has been reported to be manufactured and/or imported to the EU in a vol-
ume of 100-1000 t annually (ECHA 2022). However, estimating the exact annual
production volume and emissions of TFA is challenging when considering potential
precursors to the substance. Lopez and Salazar (2013) reported the production vol-
ume of TFA in the US alone to be > 0.45-4.5 million kg in 2002. More recent figures
==PAGE:34==
2.4 Environmental profiling of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA
22
on the exact mass of TFA in the environment are scarce, however PFAS containing
pesticides alone have been estimated to have an emission potential of 3,200 t TFA per
year, of which 627 t are derived from DFF (Joerss et al. 2024). Other sources of TFA
include pharmaceuticals and hydrofluorocarbon (HFC)s (F-gases) that since the in-
troduction of the Montreal Protocol have been substituting chlorofluorocarbon (CFC)
gases (Velders et al. 2022). The use of the latter was estimated to contribute to TFA
emissions in Europe by 14,650 t TFA in 2020, and the same study hypothesized total
TFA emissions from HFCs to be 1,384,018 t in the period 2000-2050 (Behringer et al.
2021). With TFA being the final degradation product in the degradation chain of
many fluorinated compounds, the direct and indirect emission of TFA may pose as
an irreversible accumulation of this substance in the environment (Arp et al. 2024).
The environmental distribution of TFA generally seems to be contained to aque-
ous compartments in the environment due to its extreme hydrophilicity, which implies
that it does not accumulate in soil. Indeed, TFA has been detected in virtually all
types of aquatic environment (Adlunger et al. 2021). Because of its hydrophilicity it
has been suggested for use as a reference substance for the determination of ground-
water age (Albers and Sültenfuss 2024). In a study conducted by the Pesticide Action
Network (PAN) Europe, 26 surface water samples from surface waters across 10 Euro-
pean countries, and 6 groundwater samples from 4 European countries were analyzed
for the presence of TFA, and TFA was present in all samples with average concen-
trations of 1,2 and 1,0 µg/L in each compartment respectively (PAN Europe 2024).
In Denmark, TFA was detected in 40% of drinking water wells from a sample of 778
wells and 90% of groundwater samples under the Danish Groundwater Monitoring
Initiative (GRUMO) in concentrations of 0.1-2 µg/L with a detection limit of 0,05
µg/L (Thorling et al. 2023; Albers 2024).
Other European environmental agencies have reported widespread contamination
of TFA in both surface- and groundwater as well (Adlunger et al. 2021; Bänninger et al.
2024; Joerss et al. 2024). Although TFA has been observed in surface waters across
Europe, no oﬀicial, EU-wide EQSs have been established.
However, in Denmark
freshwater EQSs have been determined based on aquatic ecotoxicity data reported
to ECHA resulting in an AA-EQS of 0.56 mg/L and a MAC-EQS of 2.37 mg/L (DK
EPA 2024b).
TFA has been observed to enter the stems and leaves of plants via the xylem
meaning that there is a significant potential for TFA uptake from plants in humans
and plant-eating animals (Rollins et al. 1989; Boutonnet et al. 1999; Hees et al. 2024).
Residual TFA has also been found in tea and beer, which has been coupled with
said uptake in plants (Adlunger et al. 2021). Mammalian toxicity studies have not
concluded TFA to be toxic or bioaccumulative. However, calls have been made for
toxicity studies specifically on humans. It has been argued that while TFA does not
bioaccumulate, the background concentration of it in nature will increase as long
as it is emitted/produced from mother compounds, thereby increasing the constant
exposure to any organism that is in contact with water contaminated by TFA (Zheng
et al. 2023).
Ecotoxicity assays have indicated both short-term and long-term toxic effects

Chemicals and stocks

3.2
Methods
3.2.1
Choice of OECD Test Guidelines
To obtain toxicity results for the ”base set” for freshwater organisms covering three
trophic levels (algae, invertebrates, and fish), OECD TGs 201, 202, 211 and 236 were
chosen to be conducted (OECD 2011; OECD 2004; OECD 2012; OECD 2013b). This
choice was also based on the desire to follow the respective TGs that are oﬀicially
recommended in EU regulations for ecotoxicity testing. Furthermore, the choice of
tests renders an estimation of EQSs possible with respect to freshwater ecotoxicity.
It should be emphasized that all tests were conducted with the intention of perform-
ing chemical analyses on the test concentrations in each test that was performed.
However, time constraints did not allow for the execution of this.
3.2.2
Reference tests
Reference tests were performed for TGs 201, 202 and 236 to ensure proper execution
of the test when examining the target PFASs. 3,5-dichlorophenol (3,5-DCP) was used
as reference substance for long-term toxicity toward R. subcapitata and short-term
toxicity toward D. magna, following OECD TGs 201 and 202. 3,4-dichloroaniline (3,4-
DCA) was applied as reference substance on D. rerio embryos according to OECD
TG 236.
3.2.3
Range finding tests
For OECD TGs 201 and 202, range-finding experiments were carried out for 3-TFMP
and 4-TFMP. This was carried out to identify the approximate concentration at
which a response could be observed by applying the test procedures described below
with concentrations differing by 1 order of magnitude. Subsequently, the tests were
conducted with concentrations differing from each other by a specified factor. Ideally,
this would have been conducted on D. rerio as well; however time did not allow for
this.
3.2.4
Preparation of stocks and general test procedures
In each of the tests conducted below, the experiments were carried out identically
for each substance, diverging only in the concentrations to which the organism were
exposed. The specific concentrations applied in OECD TGs 201, 202, 236 and 211 can
be seen in Appendix A.3, A.4, A.5 and A.11 respectively. The stock solution used
for the concentration series was produced from the pure substances i.e. reference
substances 3,5-DCP and 3,4-DCA, and test substances 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, and TFA,
and Milli-Q (MQ) water so that the same stock solution could be used for both algae,
daphnia, and fish embryo tests.

3.2.5
Freshwater Algal Growth Inhibition Test
The long-term algal test was carried out with R. subcapitata according to OECD
TG 201 with the exception of the recommended concentration difference factor of 2.2
being changed to 2.5 for TFA and 4-TFMP, and 10 for 3-TFMP (OECD 2011). The
exposure method was static, as the media was not changed during the test.
A concentration series was prepared for each substance in 25 mL measuring flasks
with stock solution and MQ water. These were mixed thoroughly and transferred
to measuring beakers. Subsequently, three replicates of each test concentration were
made by transferring 4 mL from the measuring beakers to 20 mL vials. 5 negative
control vials containing algal TG Medium were also prepared.
The cell density of the algal stock was measured using a MultisizerTM 3 Coulter
Counter Analyzer (Beckman Coulter, Indianapolis, USA). The volume needed for
an initial concentration of 20.000 algae/mL was then calculated, and the algae were
transferred from the algal stock to each glass vial. The glass vials were closed with
screw caps allowing for CO2 exchange with the atmosphere.
The glass vials were placed in clamps in plastic cylinders on an orbital shaker
table (IKA ®KS 260) with 200 RPM in a climate chamber at 22.6°C. The vials
were exposed to light emitted from fluorescent tubes (30 W/33; Philips Amsterdam,
The Netherlands) with a light intensity of 115.5 µmol m−2 s1, measured using an
LI-189 Quantum/Radiometer/Photometer (LI-COR, Nebraska, USA). 0.4 mL was
transferred from each vial to a test tube along with 1.6 mL acetone on the first day
of the test, and again after 24, 48, and 72 h and placed in the dark. This was also
done with 5 blind test tubes containing only TG Medium. Between 16 and 24 h after
the last extraction, the amount of chlorophyll present was measured using a Hitachi
F-7000 Fluorescence Spectrophotometer (Hitachi High-Technologies, Tokyo, Japan)
at respective excitation and emission wavelengths of 420 and 672 nm. The relative
growth inhibition was computed from the fluorescence observed.
3.2.6
D. magna Acute Immobilization Test
The short-term toxicity test for D. magna was conducted in line with OECD TG 202,
although the geometric concentration series was altered from the recommendation of
2.2 to 5 for TFA and 3-TFMP, and 2.5 for 4-TFMP (OECD 2004). Furthermore, the
dissolved oxygen level was not measured before, during or after the test, as experience
at the laboratory at DTU says that complying with dissolved oxygen content demand
is generally not an issue.
For each exposure concentration, 4 replicate beakers were filled up to 20 mL with
M7 and stock solution to obtain the desired concentration. 5 control beakers were
prepared with only M7. 5 daphnia were then added to each beaker and kept in the
dark for the entire experiment with no change of media, resulting in a static exposure
method. The number of mobile daphnia were counted after 24 and 48 h, and the
immobilization ratio was calculated expressed as EC50 values.

3.2.7
Fish Embryo Acute Toxicity (FET) Test
Test setup
The FET Test was carried out in line with OECD TG 236 with minor alterations
following a static test setup since the medium was not renewed throughout the test.
The wells were not preconditioned as recommended by OECD TG 236 due to the
high test concentrations and the high water solubility of both 3,4-DCA and the 3
PFASs (Crossland 1990).
The concentrations assessed for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP
were chosen to be 1, 5, 10, 50, and 100 mg/L based on the toxicity responses observed
during OECD TGs 201 and 202 in the current study. The maximum concentration
was set at 100 mg/L due to CLP labeling criteria for aquatic toxicity mentioned in
Section 2.2.1. As a consequence, the test did not adhere to the recommendation of
the guideline which states that a geometric dilution series should be applied with a
maximum factor 2.2 between each concentration. A single limit test was conducted
for TFA with a concentration of 100 mg/L.
Preparation of zebrafish medium
Zebrafish (ZF) Medium was prepared at DHI (Hørsholm, Denmark), whereby a pH
of 7.4 was observed.
The oxygen content was measured to be 8.4 mg/L at 22°C,
corresponding to 96% O2 saturation (Clesceri et al. 1998). The conductivity of the
water was measured to be 669 µS cm−1 and 22.3°C. Stock solutions of each of the
PFASs were prepared by from the substances and MQ water in measuring flasks by
magnetic stirring for 1 h. 3,4-DCA was prepared using an ultrasonic water bath for
20 minutes followed by 1 h of magnetic stirring. The pH of all stocks was measured
and adjusted using NaOH and HCl, until a pH of 7.4-7.5 was reached. The pH of the
medium was measured again and its pH was adjusted further, reaching a pH of 7.0.
Preparation of dilution series
A concentration series with was made in volumes of 400 mL in a total of 13 0.5 L
blue cap flasks (1 for the negative control, 1 for 3,4-DCA, 1 for TFA, and 5 for each
of the concentrations of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP). The pH was measured in all of these
to ensure a pH of 7-8. A subsample of the 13 blue cap flasks was then made by
transferring 0.2 L from the 0.5 L flasks to another set of 0.5 L blue cap flasks. The
original blue cap set remained at DTU for collection on the first day of the test, and
the subsample set was transported to the laboratory at DHI for use in the embryo
wells.
Embryo collection and bulk exposure
At the Parasitology and Aquatic Pathobiology section of the Department of Veterinary
and Animal Sciences at the University of Copenhagen (KU), female zebrafish were
observed to lay eggs which were fertilized by males over the course of 45 minutes. 23

zebrafish pairs, a total of 46 individuals, were estimated to participate in the mating.
The embryos were collected in Petri dishes containing E3 (ZF Medium containing

then sorted coarsely under a stereo microscope. Embryos that appeared to develop

temperature of approximately 28°C, to start the bulk exposure. Thus, a total of 312
randomized and fertilized embryos were used during the test.
Individual exposure
The blue cap flasks containing the embryos were transported for 30 minutes to DHI in
a polystyrene box to ensure a stable temperature. The content of the blue cap flasks
used for bulk exposure including embryos were carefully transferred to large crystal-
lization dishes, and embryos were observed under a stereo microscope to ensure that
all embryos used in the test were fertilized and developing properly. 13 NunclonTM

L blue cap flasks previously brought to DHI according to OECD TG 236. pH, O2
saturation, and conductivity of the water were measured in each blue cap flask to
ensure compliance with the guideline criteria prior to the transfer to the exposure
plates. The hardness of the dilution series was measured with LCK 327 Cuvettes in a
DR3900 Spectrophotometer (Hach, Loveland, USA). Healthy embryos from the large
petri dishes was placed in each of the wells in each plate. In instances where some
eggs had already coagulated after the bulk exposure, healthy eggs were prioritized
(13 of the 24 embryos exposed to 50 mg 4-TFMP/L, and all embryos exposed to 100
mg 4-TFMP/L were coagulated upon arrival to DHI). These were still placed in one
of the plates. Excess embryos (both dead and alive) were counted and then discarded.
All plates were placed in a room with a mean temperature of 25.9 ± 0.6°C measured
with a Eupry water temperature logger and a 14:10 light/dark cycle.
Observation procedure and conclusion of test
Every 24 h (varying from 23-26 h) the embryos were observed.
The four apical
endpoints described in OECD TG 236 were observed using a stereo microscope; 1)
coagulation of the embryo; 2) lack of somite formation; 3) nondetachment of the tail;
and 4) lack of heartbeat. The hatching of embryos was also noted when observed.

3.2.8
D. magna reproduction test
The long-term ecotoxicity test on D. magna was performed according to OECD TG
211 (OECD 2012).
Only one concentration of TFA was assessed (8 mg TFA/L),
whereas three concentrations of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP were tested in concentrations
of 0.091, 0.3, and 0.91 mg 3-TFMP/L, and 0.1, 0.33, and 1 mg 4-TFMP respectively.
10 beakers were prepared per substance per concentration along with 10 control
beakers per batch of tests initiated. Each beaker contained a total of 20 mL of M7
and substance depending on the exposure concentration assessed. Feed for the daph-
nia was prepared by concentrating algae by centrifugation of 45 mL algal stock in
a ROTANTA 460 R (Andreas Hettich GmbH, Tuttlingen, Germany) at 5000 RPM
and 23 deg. C for 10 minutes in 50 mL falcon tubes. Excess water was subsequently
removed to reach a final volume of approximately 5 mL containing the algae. The
algal cell density was then measured in a MultisizerTM 3 Coulter Counter Analyzer
(Beckman Coulter, Indianapolis, USA). The volume needed to reach 107 algal cells
per daphnia was calculated and transferred to all beakers. One daphnia of an age of
< 24 h was added to each beaker and put in a room of 18-20°C on a 16:8 light/dark
cycle for 21 d. Every 24 h, algae were added by the same approach as described
above. The exposure method was that of a semi-static one: Every three days the
test medium was renewed to prevent substance concentrations from decreasing more
than from the initial concentration 20%. Three endpoint parameters were observed
in each concentration of substance: 1) the cumulative number of offspring, 2) the
time to first offspring, and 3) the size of the adult female D. magna after 21 d. If the
death of an adult female was observed, it was noted including the cause of death if
this was known.
3.2.9
Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was conducted on data generated in the OECD TGs 201, 202 and
211 using R with the code seen in Appendix A.7. Since a dose-response curve is
S-shaped (i.e., it is non-linear), the drm() function was applied with an LN.2() model
which requires data input that follows a sigmoidal trend (Meddings et al. 1989; Ritz
et al. 2015). The drm() command is generally used to predict a non-linear response

from a dataset that exerts a non-linear tendency. The predict() function was then
applied to predict 95% confidence intervals from the standard errors of the data.
A one-tailed Student’s t-test was performed on the long-term toxicity data that
was generated for D. magna to assess the data for NOEC/LOEC values. This was
done under the following hypothesis test for the number of offspring produced and
the size of the adult female D. magna after 21 d:
H0 : µControl = µPFAS
(3.1)
HA : µControl > µPFAS
(3.2)
On the other hand, the following hypothesis test for the time to first offspring
under the assumption that the development of the daphnia might be haltered by the
exposure to a given PFAS was:
H0 : µControl = µPFAS
(3.3)
HA : µControl < µPFAS
(3.4)
The t-test was carried out in Excel using the T.TEST() function, and the p-value
generated from it was double checked by conducting a two-sample t-test assuming
equal variance from the Data Analysis ToolPak, comparing the concentration of each
PFAS to the control. The standard deviation was calculated, from which the 95%
CIs were computed.
When using the Student’s t-test for comparing the mean of two samples, the
samples should follow a normal distribution (King and Eckersley 2019). To check
whether the data followed a normal distribution, a Shapiro-Wilk test was conducted
in R on all of the endpoint result data (i.e., number of offspring, time to first offspring,
and size of the adult female D. magna) as recommended in OECD TG 211, since this
test is applicable for sample sizes where n < 50 (OECD 2012; Souza et al. 2023). This
was cross-checked with an Anderson-Darling test for normality which can be applied
for small sample sizes as well (D’Agostino and Stephens 1986). This was carried out
in R using the ad.test() code applicable for data arrays (i.e., sample sizes) of > 7
(Gross and Ligges 2003).
When ambiguity was observed between results, e.g. when comparing effect con-
centrations between two substances, a two-sample z-test was performed with a 95%
CI under the following hypothesis:
H0 : EC50,PFASi = EC50,PFASj

4.1
Reference experiments
Reference experiments were carried out to ensure the validity of the results of the
acute toxicity experiments. 3,5-dichlorphenol was used as reference substance on R.
subcapitata and D. magna. In the algal test, an EC50 of 2.4 mg/L [2.1, 2.7]95%CI was

D. magna, aligning with what was found by Skjolding et al. (2021) (see Appendix
A.4). Immobilization was observed in less than 10% of the daphnia in the control
group, whereby this validity criterion was fulfilled (OECD 2004). For OECD TG 236,
3,4-dichloroaniline was used as a reference substance at 4 mg/L by which a mortality
of 70% was observed as seen in Appendix A.9, thus fulfilling the validity criterion for
the positive control (EC 2014; OECD 2013b).

observed in the 4-TFMP stock solution on day 18.

Discussion
5.1
Data quality and reliability considerations
5.1.1
Choice of tests for aquatic ecotoxicity
OECD TGs 201, 202 and 211 are mentioned as valid options for quantifying the
toxicity of a substance towards algae, invertebrates and fish in the endpoint specific
guidance document on the CSA (ECHA 2023b). However, it must be noted that
OECD TG 236 (Fish Embryo Acute Toxicity (FET) Test) is currently not a test
recommendation under in the CSA guidelines. Nonetheless, calls have been made in
recent years to incorporate OECD TG 236 into the set of options for recommended
tests for the aquatic toxicity of chemicals induced on fish. For example, Sobanska et
al. (2018) have argued that while OECD TG 236 does possess limitations in terms of
predictive power due to a low number of studies using this test, studies have shown a
notable correlation between the OECD TGs 236 and 203 (Fish, Acute Toxicity Test),
the latter of which is currently recommended in the only OECD TG for acute toxicity
in fish (ECHA 2023b).
One of the main concerns for the introduction of OECD TG 236 as a valid test for
ecotoxicity to D. rerio is that the metabolic aspect in embryo development is not taken
into account (Sobanska et al. 2018). However, this would be relevant mainly in cases
where the production of reactive degradation products occurs. This would require the
test substances to be relatively unstable. Since PFASs are inherently persistent, this
can be assumed to be irrelevant for the current study. On the contrary, OECD TG
236 may be an optimal and cost-effective method to assess the ecotoxicity of PFASs
for persistent compounds.
5.1.2
Lack of chemical analysis
The fact that time constraints hindered the execution of chemical analyses in the
tests means that the results shown are based on the assumption that the actual
concentrations throughout the tests correspond to the nominal concentrations. This
is an uncertainty that must be taken into account in the interpretation of the results.
Although degradation of the test substances is unlikely due to their persistence,
adsorption of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP, or TFA to beakers, blue cap flasks, test organisms,
or in the case of OECD TG 211, the algae used as feed for the daphnia could have
occurred. As stated in their environmental profiles, a significant rate of volatilization
==PAGE:56==
5.1 Data quality and reliability considerations
44
is expected for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP, and due to this, a further reduction in test
concentrations for these substances may have taken place despite the short time
span of the tests (or frequent renewal in the case of OECD TG 211).
Assuming
that no human errors were made with respect to preparation of stock solutions and
transfer of these to concentration series, it is therefore possible that the estimated
effect concentrations are higher than the actual concentrations. On the other hand, if
water has evaporated from beakers, vials and plates and the substances predominantly
remained in the water of the test media, the actual test concentrations could be higher
than the nominal ones. This would result in higher effect concentrations than has
been estimated.
The lack of chemical analyses notably reduces the robustness of the effect concen-
trations. The results of the chemical analyses that should ideally have been performed
would make an estimation of a mass balance of the test setups possible, reducing this
uncertainty. This would especially be of relevance in OECD TG 236, since it rec-
ommends preconditioning of the wells for the embryos, which was not carried out as
mentioned in Section 3.2.7.
5.1.3
Deviations from OECD Test Guidelines
Although the validity criteria were met regarding algal growth, daphnia immobiliza-
tion, mortality of adult female D. magna, and the multiple criteria for the D. rerio
embryos in the respective tests, some deviations occurred.
Firstly, the use of geometric concentrations were not met with respect to the
OECD TGs 201, 202 and 236 which state that a maximum factor of 2.2 should be ap-
plied between each test concentration. Even though range finding tests were carried
out for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP in OECD TGs 201 and 202, exceeding the recom-
mended gap factor in test concentration may still be pragmatic, since range finding
results only provide a coarse estimate of the order of magnitude of the effect concen-
trations. This is especially relevant taking into account time limitations of the project.
Fortunately, dose-response curves were established for each substance in OECD TGs
201, 202 and 236.
This means that the decision to exceed the concentration gap
factor of 2.2 was a valid choice, since the aim of this study was to suggest EQSs for
which effect concentrations are required.
In OECD TG 201, the pH had reduced by more than 1.5 compared to initially
in some test concentrations (see Appendix A.3). This suggests that it was not due
to the metabolism of the algae, since they use CO2 during photosynthesis, which
should shift the carbonate system towards a reduction in the number of protons in
the medium, resulting in an increase in pH. A possible explanation is that the pH
may not have been completely stabilized at the start of the experiment. Since both
3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA are all acids, dissociation may have occurred during the
experiment.
This development is surprising in the case of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP
whose pKas are > 8. In the case of TFA, which is a strong acid, it would be expected
to dissociate rapidly. The change in pH may indicate that the buffer capacity of the
==PAGE:57==
5.1 Data quality and reliability considerations
45
TG Medium has not been suﬀicient.
As mentioned in Section 4.5, the controls did not reach the 60 offspring which
is otherwise expected from TG 211. However, since this was the case for none of
the controls and that the survival ratio criterion for adult female D. magna was still
met, it is possible that the specific strain of D. magna applied does not reproduce
at this rate. Furthermore, OECD TG 211 says that the renewal of the test medium
should occur at least three times per week. Instead, this was carried out once every
3 days throughout the test. It is possible that a higher renewal frequency would have
increased the overall survival ratio; two out of the eight adult female daphnia that died
during the test perished because they could not swim properly due to algae sticking
to their antennae. Conversely, this would increase the handling and thereby risk of
accidental deaths of adult females, since two of them were crushed by the filter upon
transfer to new medium. Notwithstanding, the TG primarily describes the need for
renewal as a means to maintain constant test concentrations. It is unlikely that any
of the SC-PFASs that were assessed were degraded due to their chemical structure,
so if a reduction in test concentrations was observed, it is likely due to adsorption
and/or volatilization of the substances as described in Section 5.1.2. In TG 211, the
time to first offspring observed for 3-TFMP seemed to correlate positively with the
test concentration. This tendency is not seen for 4-TFMP for which the results seem
more ambiguous. A possible explanation for this is the low observation frequency
which may entail that the time at which the first offspring is highly dependent on the
exact time at which the daphnia were observed in the 24 h intervals. An increased
observation frequency could reduce the uncertainty of the results for this endpoint.
Another observed deviation from a validity criterion was the hatching ratio of the
embryos in the negative control in OECD TG 236. As mentioned, a 79.2% hatching
ratio was observed. In a study conducted by Busquet et al. ((2014)) in which data on
numerous substances were assessed with OECD TG 236 in a number of laboratories,
this was observed in 3 out of 153 assays, meaning that this observation is possible but
not normal. A possible explanation for this development could be the temperature
fluctuations measured in the room even though it stayed within the recommended
interval (see the SI). Furthermore, the negative control was the first plate to be
observed on the last day of the study. An additional observation of the negative plate
could have been carried out, for example 1 hour later, at which point a hatching ratio
of ≥80% may have been observed. If the test were to be repeated, multiple negative
controls could be prepared to mitigate this uncertainty.
As seen in Appendix A.14, the wells that were most affected by loss of water from
48 h to 72 h into the test experienced a lower overall hatching ratio than less affected
wells. Since the salts of the ZF Medium and the test substances are not volatile,
an increase in the concentration of these are likely to have occurred in the affected
wells. This may have caused the growth conditions to be suboptimal for the embryos.
Nonetheless, a definitive correlation between loss of water and lack of hatching cannot
be concluded when looking at all affected wells. A repetition of the experiment using
parafilm to cover the plate lids completely on all days should rule out this uncertainty.

5.1.4
Computed growth inhibition of R. subcapitata
At the highest concentrations applied where no algal growth was observed, an inhi-
bition of > 100% was calculated which is not theoretically possible. This is likely
due to variance in the observed algal concentration at t = 0 h, as this is found at
approximately the same order of magnitude as the algal concentration at t = 72 h if
no growth has been observed. This means that the inhibition results become sensitive
to fluctuations in observations from t = 0 h, rendering negative growth rates after
72 h possible. Due to the formula used to calculate the inhibition, the inhibition
can thus reach a value above 100% (see the OECD TG 201). Note again, that in
the estimation of the effect concentrations, inhibitions above 100% were corrected to
exactly 100%.
Across all three test substances, algal growth inhibition was observed to be nega-
tive in the case of some concentrations, indicating the possibility of growth stimulation
(hormesis). However, hormesis was not considered a reason for this, as the observed
negative inhibition was not extreme and the absorbance data seemed to fluctuate to
some degree for test concentrations in which no algal growth inhibition was observed.
A hormesis model was therefore not applied (OECD 2011).
5.1.5
Lack of response for TFA in OECD TGs 202 and 236
As described in Sections 4.3 and 4.4, the R code used for determining ECx values could
not provide an effect concentration for TFA in OECD TGs 202 and 236. The reason
for this is that the LN.2() model only allows data input that follows a log-logistic
trend which is graphically represented by a sigmoidal curve as described in Section
??. Therefore, when no response is observed, the data do not allow the computation
of effect concentrations. In that regard, it can be concluded that when the R script
is unable to run, it indicates that no toxic effects are observed in any of the applied
test concentrations.
5.1.6
Ambiguity of normality testing in OECD TG 211
In the normality tests conducted on data acquired from OECD TG 211 regarding the
time to first offspring and the size of female adult D. magna after 21 d, the results
of the Shapiro-Wilk and Anderson-Darling tests were inconclusive. This is possibly
due to the relatively small sample size (n = 8-10 animals depending on whether any
adult female had died), which makes normality testing highly dependent on individual
results of the test.
5.1.7
Formation of particles in 4-TFMP stock in OECD TG 211
As mentioned in Section 4.5.2, colorless particles were observed in the 4-TFMP stock
solution on day 18 of the D. magna reproduction test. Whether this affected 4-TFMP
==PAGE:59==

47
concentration during the final days of the test is uncertain and could have been
investigated through chemical analysis of the samples that were extracted during the
test. It could be hypothesized that adsorption of 4-TFMP on these particles could
have occurred, lowering the test concentrations.
The reason behind the formation of these particles may have been precipitation or
bacterial activity. However, since the results do not indicate that the reproduction of
D. magna exposed to 4-TFMP was statistically different from the negative control of
the test, and since no unusual number of adult female D. magna deaths were observed,
it could be inferred that bacterial activity, if it occurred, was at least not pathogenic
(see SI).
5.1.8
Confidence intervals for 4-TFMP and TFA in OECD TG 201
As seen in Tables 4.2 and 4.3 as well as Figures A.1 b) and c), the confidence intervals
around the EC50 of 4-TFMP and TFA are estimated to be extremely wide. For 4-
TFMP, this is likely due to the great variability in the observed absorbance at t =
72 h, for example seen at 16 mg 4-TFMP/L (see Appendix A.3). The same is true
for TFA, for which inconsistent absorbance levels were measured at concentrations
below 200 mg TFA/L.
This degree of data variability implies that the estimated EC50 for these substances
are highly uncertain, although it is likely that the EC50 lies between 16 and 40 mg/L
for 4-TFMP and between 80 and 200 for TFA. Nevertheless, this uncertainty must
be considered when interpreting these effect concentrations, and the results suggest
that the test should be repeated using test concentrations within the intervals where
the EC50 of the substances lies to increase the accuracy of the effect concentration
estimates.
5.1.9
Confidence intervals for 3-TFMP in OECD TGs 202 and 236
As exemplified by the results in Table 4.1 and Figure A.1, the CIs estimated by the
R code are extremely narrow in the case of 3-TFMP from the results generated in
OECD TGs 202 and 236. This is likely due to the low variability of the responses,
which is usually not observed to this degree in ecotoxicity studies. In OECD TG
236, the only response observed between 0 and 100% in all plates with 3-TFMP test
concentrations is 10% at 10 mg/L. The drm() function applied in R thus predicts
normally distributed CIs with extremely low variability in the prediction data, which
may not be representative of results generated upon repetition of the experiment.
This becomes clear when the CIs of 3-TFMP are compared to the significantly wider
ones observed for 4-TFMP; the CIs of 4-TFMP are likely more realistic and more
accurate, as these are based on significant embryo mortality observed at multiple test
concentrations.
The ultimate consequence of the fact that the LC50 of 3-TFMP is based on a sin-
gle response observed in the concentration series is that the LC50 estimate is highly

uncertain. It can be inferred from the results that the LC50 of 3-TFMP for D. re-

5.2
Comparative toxicity of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA
Upon comparison of the effect concentrations from the various TGs, the following
toxicity ranking can be made based on EC50/LC50 values: 3-TFMP > 4-TFMP >
TFA for R. subcapitata and D. magna, and 4-TFMP > 3-TFMP > TFA for D. rerio.
For 3-TFMP, the most sensitive organism is R. subcapitata followed by D. magna,
and then D. rerio, whereas the most sensitive organism to 4-TFMP is R. subcapitata
followed by D. rerio, and lastly D. magna.
It must be underlined that the placement in the ranking of toxicity for D. rerio
towards each substance is not definitive; a two-sample z-test shows that the LC50
observed for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP are not significantly different (p > 0.05, see
Appendix A.8. This calls for additional testing to reduce this uncertainty.
5.3
Aquatic toxicity profiles for 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA
5.3.1
3-TFMP
3-TFMP is the substance that exhibited the highest toxicity in OECD TGs 201, 202,
and 211. Significant effects were also observed in OECD TG 236. With 3-TFMP
not being rapidly degradable and having a chronic NOEC of 0.9 mg/L (< 1 mg/L),
3-TFMP can be classified as aquatic chronic 2 (EC 2016).
With the current, data 3-TFMP does not fulfill the criterion of being labeled T
under the PBPMT framework of the CLP, since its lowest observed long-term effect
concentration is the NOEC of 0.9 mg/L (> 0.1 mg/L). The short-term EC50 of 7.5
mg/L observed for D. magna in the current study is of the same order of magnitude
as the only available aquatic ecotoxicity data point reported to ECHA of EC50 of 11
mg/L (see Table A.1).
Although 3-TFMP is not T, it may prove to be vMvP due to its assumed low
logKoc, however in the current study this logKoc is based on data of 4-TFMP, so to be
certain about the mobility of 3-TFMP, a test should be carried out to obtain a logKoc
specifically for this substance, e.g. by means suggested by ECHA, for example OECD
TG 106 (ECHA 2024b; ECHA 2023b). As of now, data suggest that 3-TFMP is not
ready biodegradable according what has been reported to ECHA (2019a). However,

modeling, and it is recommended that persistence is evaluated by supplementary data
if QSAR/QSPR data are used (ECHA 2023c). This calls for experimental data on
the persistence of 3-TFMP to be generated, e.g. by OECD TGs 301, 302 and/or
309 in order to directly conclude whether or not 3-TFMP is persistent and can be
classified as vMvP (ECHA 2023c).
5.3.2
4-TFMP
4-TFMP indicates lower toxicity than 3-TFMP towards R. subcapitata and D. magna,
but higher toxicity towards D. rerio. In relation to CIs in OECD TG 236, it also be
noted that even though a 5% response was observed for 1 mg 4-TFMP/L, the lower
boundary of the CI in that data point was negative, indicating that it cannot be
concluded whether this effect was due to the exposure to 4-TFMP (Hespanhol et al.
2019).
If 4-TFMP is assumed to have similar properties to 3-TFMP in terms of degrad-
ability, the data generated in this study, an acute EC50 of 19.3 mg/L for D. magna
and an acute LC50 of 11.8 mg/L for D. rerio would result in the classification of
4-TFMP as aquatic chronic 3 under CLP criteria. However, the assumption that 4-
TFMP is persistent is only based on the structural similarity of 4-TFMP and 3-TFMP,
and therefore this classification has not been assigned to 4-TFMP in its revised en-

be investigated, ideally by experimental data from OECD TGs 301, 302, or 309, or
alternatively through the use of QSAR/QSPR modeling.
The lowest effect concentration observed for 4-TFMP was a chronic NOEC of 6.4
mg/L in R. subcapitata. Therefore, 4-TFMP does not meet the T criterion of the CLP.
On the other hand, 4-TFMP indicate higher aquatic more toxic than TFA, judging

be reached with certainty for the NOEC/LOEC data from OECD TG 211. The short-

(Nałęcz-Jawecki 2008). While this is not a robust comparison due to different test
setups, test durations, and organisms, this may indicate that effect concentrations
could be expected in the mg/L range for crustaeceans.
There is a potential for 4-TFMP to be vMvP because of its logKoc being < 2.
However, as described in 2.4 this value is based on multiple empirical formulas. All
of these are influenced by a degree of uncertainty, and therefore this value should be
used with a grain of salt. As for 3-TFMP, this calls for an oﬀicial, direct estimation
of the logKoc of 4-TFMP via e.g. experiments recommended by ECHA (2024b). The
same goes for its persistence, for which no data exist as of now; multiple OECD TGs
are recommended for this as mentioned earlier (2024b).

5.3.3
TFA
The observations made in the current study indicate that TFA is less toxic than both
3-TFMP and 4-TFMP for all the organisms studied. Upon comparison of the results
acquired in this study with existing data, there are both similarities and differences.
First, the acute EC50 for D. magna of > 500 mg/L is in line with what is reported

obtained for D. rerio exposed to 100 mg TFA/L, as an LC50 of ≥999 mg/L has been
reported to ECHA (2022).
In contrast, an acute EC50 for algae has been estimated at 237 mg/L in a study
using OECD TG 201 as reported in the registration dossier for TFA (ECHA 2022).
Comparing this to the observed EC50 for algae of 136.4 mg/L in this study, a higher
toxicity toward R. subcapitata has been observed than what has been reported to
ECHA. However, the CI displayed also indicates a high uncertainty with respect to
the observed chronic EC50 value of the current study (see Figure A.1).
In addition, the chronic NOEC value of /≤8.0 mg/L observed for D. magna
in this study is notably lower than the NOEC of ≥25 mg/L reported in the TFA
registration dossier. However, this does not change the current classification of TFA
under the PMT or the aquatic toxicity classifications; it stays C3. Nonetheless, the
final decision on labeling TFA a PMT/vPvM is pending as mentioned in Section
2.4.3.

5.4.1
Derivation of Predicted No-Effect Concentrations
As a result of the ecotoxicity data that have been generated for the fluorinated degra-
dation products in the current study, freshwater PNECs can be derived for each sub-
stance from the lowest chronic effect concentration observed. These are the chronic
NOECs of 0.9, 6.4, and 80 mg/L all observed in TG 201 for 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and
TFA respectively. An assessment factor of 50 can be applied for these substances, as
chronic ecotoxicity data have been generated for two trophic levels with algae and
invertebrates, which results in PNECfws of 0.006 mg/L for 3-TFMP, 0.13 mg/L for
4-TFMP and 1.6 mg/L for TFA based solely on data from this study.
The PNECfws cannot be established without involving already existing chronic
freshwater ecotoxicity data. This did not exist for 3-TFMP or 4-TFMP prior to this
study. However, this is not the case for TFA for which ecotoxicity data for TFA are
more accessible. As seen in Table A.3, the lowest chronic effect concentration derived
under guidelines that are recognized by ECHA is an EC10 of 5.6 mg/L derived from
OECD TG 201, which had already reported to ECHA prior to the current study. As
described in Section 2.4.3, an AF of 10 has already been applied for the derivation of a
PNECfw for TFA of 0.56 mg/L. Therefore, the PNECfw for TFA remains unchanged
when all data are taken into account.

a more robust estimate of the PNECfws (OECD 2013a).
5.4.2
Derivation of Environmental Quality Standards
The PNECs estimated above can be utilized to calculate the concentrations at which
there is not expected an effect on the aquatic freshwater environments in both short-
term and long-term, following the EQSD legal framework.
Freshwater EQSs for long-term effects can be established for the test substances
based on their respective PNECfws, because AA-EQSfw ≈PNECfw as mentioned in
Section 2.2.3 (EC 2018). The resulting AA-EQSs can be seen in Table 5.1.
As for short-term effects, freshwater MAC-EQSs can be derived using an AF of
100 for each substance since acute toxicity data have been generated at three trophic
levels (EC 2018). Since the MAC-EQSfw is based on the lowest acute toxicity data
available, a MAC-EQSfw of 0.041 mg/L for 3-TFMP is computed from the acute
EC50 of 4.1 mg/L toward R. subcapitata. For 4-TFMP, the MAC-EQSfw is based on
the LC50 of 11.8 mg/L toward D. rerio and is therefore 0.118 mg/L. In principle, the
MAC-EQSfw for TFA could be based on the observed LC50 of > 100 mg/L, but this
estimate would be uncertain in a practical context, such as the determination of a
MAC-EQSfw in legislation. For TFA, this value is therefore based on the EC50 of
136.4 mg/L observed toward R. subcapitata, yielding a MAC-EQSfw of 1.36 mg/L.
Interestingly, the estimated MAC-EQSfw for TFA of 1.36 mg/L is lower than the
current MAC-EQSfw established by the Danish EPA of 2.37 mg/L, indicating that the
estimated environmental risk of TFA may be underestimated (DK EPA 2024b). This
observation must be made considering the data uncertainties pointed out in Section
5.1. In addition, the measurements of TFA performed in European surface waters
indicate that TFA levels are currently far from this threshold.

5.4 Hazard assessment of 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA

5.4.3
Limitations of derived EQSs
It is important to underline that the current study only provides data for the sugges-
tion of EQSs for 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA. Indications for the hazard that these
substances pose to the environment can only be interpreted in light of freshwater
environments as shown in Section 5.4.4. Marine ecotoxicity data still do not exist for
3-TFMP and 4-TFMP and are scarcer for TFA compared to freshwater data (ECHA
2022). Conducting tests on ecotoxicity toward marine organisms may be relevant
for TFA due to the emissions which occur annually and marine concentrations being
measurable for this substance (Freeling and Mangels 2024). Conversely, it may be
less relevant for 3-TFMP and 4-TMFP due to the lower emissions of these relatively
to TFA in combination with the dilution that would occur if transported to the ocean.
As described in Section 2.2.4, substances with a logKow ≥3 are subjected to
further investigation by which secondary poisoning and/or human toxicity is taken
into account for the establishment of EQSs.
While the logKow of the substances
assessed in the current study does not indicate bioaccumulation, it is plausible that
humans are exposed to both 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP in addition to TFA. For 3-TFMP,
this assumption is based on the agricultural DFF emissions which subsequently could
lead to residual 3-TFMP being present in food products. Data are scarce on whether
4-TFMP is produced from FLX/NFLX in the human body or if this transformation
occurs after excretion of these compounds. Nonetheless mammals or humans may be
exposed to 4-TFMP following the emission of FLX/NFLX and subsequently 4-TFMP
to the environment.
As of now, data on secondary poisoning and human toxicity toward TFA is ex-
tremely limited, and they are non-existent for 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP. Therefore, sec-
ondary poisoning and human toxicity assessments could be undertaken to investigate
possible adverse effects toward predatory recipients.
5.4.4
Challenges for risk characterization of fluorinated
degradation products
As seen above, the data that have been generated in this study can be applied to
suggest EQSs for the protection of surface freshwater environments and lays the
groundwork for the aspect of risk assessment related to aquatic ecotoxicity. However,
in order to conclude whether 3-TFMP, 4-TFMP and TFA currently pose a risk to
these environments, predicted environmental concentrations (PECs) are needed. No
PEC has been reported to ECHA for either of these substances (ECHA 2019a; ECHA
n.d.[b]; ECHA 2022).
As mentioned in Section 2.4.3, TFA concentrations have been detected in Eu-
ropean surface water measurements showing an average concentration of 1.2 µg/L
(Albers 2024; PAN Europe 2024). Using this value as its PECsw, an RCRsw of 2.1−3
would be obtained. Based on this initial result, TFA does not pose a risk to surface

53
freshwater, but ideally an oﬀicially reported PECsw supported by solid monitoring
data should be used for this estimate.
The risk to which the environment is exposed by 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP cannot
be quantified with an RCRsw, as these substances are not monitored under current
practices of public authorities and PECs are unavailable. An estimate of the formation
of 3-TFMP from the degradation of DFF in the environment could be made based on
the sale figures of DFF published by the Danish EPA whereby a worst-case emission
potential of 37.4 t could be established for the year 2022 in Denmark when the
formation of 3-TFMP is based on a the 0.41:1 molar mass ratio between 3-TFMP
and DFF. This estimate assumes that all DFF sold in 2022 was used that year and
that a 100% degradation ratio from DFF to 3-TFMP is valid. However, there may
be other sources of 3-TFMP, and therefore a reliable RCR cannot be established for
this substance as no PEC is available. Hypothetical estimates could also be made for
4-TFMP, but with high uncertainty as well.
5.5
Future prospects of freshwater contamination by
fluorinated degradation products
Current trends in the contamination of freshwater environments by fluorinated degra-
dation products points toward increasing levels of these substances. For example,
Albers and Sültenfuss (2024) have pointed out that the concentration of TFA in pre-
cipitation has increased by a factor of 2-4 in Europe from the 1990s to 2020.
A
study conducted by Cahill (2022) displayed that TFA concentrations had increased
by an average of 6 times in California during the years 1998-2021 with median con-
centrations being 180 ng/L in surface water. Furthermore, Thorling et al. (2023)
found an inverse correlation between TFA levels and groundwater age in Denmark,
supporting concerns regarding the increasing presence of TFA in the environment
(Arp et al. 2024). The increase in TFA concentrations further seems to follow an
exponential trend, so the rate of increase in annual environmental concentration is
likely to accelerate (Albers and Sültenfuss 2024).
Given that pesticides are assumed to be a substantial source for TFA contami-
nation, it is highly probable that this trend also applies to its precursors, such as
3-TFMP, combined with increasing sales figures seen for DFF in recent years as men-
tioned in Section 2.3.2. This may also be true for 4-TFMP. 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP
are not monitored to the same degree as TFA, and this may prove detrimental to the
environment as exemplified by the toxicity ranking between the substances assessed
in the current study. While the PECs of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP are likely lower than
that of TFA, other degradation products such as SC-PFAS that are PMT or vPvM
could go undetected in the environment. For 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP, this is at least
true until their eventual transformation into TFA. This reinforces the incentive for
the proposal of restricting the use of all substances that fall under the current OECD
definition of PFAS put forward by the Member States earlier mentioned.
==PAGE:66==
5.5 Future prospects of freshwater contamination by fluorinated degradation products

Since the findings of the current study show that 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP are more
toxic to aquatic organisms than TFA, it begs the question whether public authorities
are looking for those which pose the greatest risk to the environment. In this regard,
it could be debated whether current PFAS monitoring programs should be revised.
It would be especially concerning if the ECxs of unassessed fluorinated degradation
products proved to be of lower orders of magnitude than observed in this study. How-
ever, an extensive analysis program would be required to identify other fluorinated
degradation products SC-PFASs that may exist in the environment, after which a
prioritization of which SC-PFASs to monitor should be made.

6 Conclusion
55
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Fluorinated degradation products formed by PFAS precursors, such as pesticides
and pharmaceuticals like DFF and FLX, present a major environmental challenge in
terms of surface water quality. Due to their inherent properties of being mobile and
persistent in the environment, they are likely found if searched in surface water anal-
yses. Their widespread and increasing presence in aquatic environments, combined
with the lack of ecotoxicity data on them incentivizes the generation of data on their
potential adverse effects toward aquatic organisms substantially.
The aim of the current study was to close this knowledge gap for 3-TFMP and
4-TFMP as well as providing additional ecotoxicity data for TFA. By conducting
OECD TGs 201, 202, 211 and 236, aquatic ecotoxicity data was generated for the
base set of aquatic organisms; algae (R. subcapitata), invertebrates (D. magna) and
fish (D. rerio). In the case of R. subcapitata and D. magna, both short-term and
long-term data were produced.
3-TFMP was found to cause long-term toxic effects toward aquatic organisms
at concentrations of 0.9 mg/L. Given its persistence, it therefore qualified for the
aquatic environmental hazard classification aquatic chronic 2 under CLP criteria.
The observed toxicity ranking of the test substances was established to be 3-TFMP >
4-TFMP > TFA for R. subcapitata and D. magna, while it was 4-TFMP > 3-TFMP
> TFA for D. rerio. No toxicity classification was assigned to 4-TFMP and TFA
based on the observations of this study, but they may prove to be vPvM according
to CLP criteria, which may also apply to 3-TFMP.
Taking into account aquatic ecotoxicity data from this and other studies, PNECfws
and thereby AA-EQSfws were suggested at 0.006, 0.13 and 0.56 mg/L for 3-TFMP,
4-TFMP and TFA respectively. Meanwhile, the respective MAC-EQSfws for 3-TFMP,
4-TFMP and TFA were determined to be 0.041, 0.118, and 1.36 mg/L.
These findings provide the basis for an environmental risk assessment of 3-TFMP
and 4-TFMP and suggest that the MAC-EQS of TFA set by the Danish EPA may be
an underestimation. However, there are still major knowledge gaps on the experimen-
tal side for both the persistence and mobility of 3-TFMP and 4-TFMP, encouraging
further studies on these parameters in line with OECD TGs.
Since many other fluorinated degradation products with properties similar to those
tested this study may be present in aquatic environments, it could be argued that
environmentally problematic substances may go under the radar under current mon-
itoring procedures. This reinforces the necessity for further studies on environmental
behavior and ecotoxicity for other fluorinated degradation products, as knowledge
about these are scarce in general.
