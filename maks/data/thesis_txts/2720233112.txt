Sb2S3 solar cells - Master thesis
Abstract
This project investigates the effect of annealing on the crystal growth and orientation of Sb2S3
absorbers, a novel quasi 1D-material for photovoltaic applications. The absorbers were grown
on two types of substrates, Mo-coated (Mo-substrate) and sulfurized Mo-coated (MoS2/Mo,
MoS2-substrate) soda-lime glass, by sputter deposition of SbS target. The precursors were
annealed in different temperatures from 300◦C to 450◦C for minutes under N2 atmosphere.
All absorbers were characterized by Raman spectroscopy, X-ray diffraction analysis (XRD),
scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and current density-voltage (J-V) measurements. From
Raman and XRD spectra, Sb2S3 phase is observed in all absorbers, while those deposited on
Mo-substrate, revealed Sb10S15 and Sb2(SO4) phases with annealing temperature increase.
The absorbers grown on MoS2-substrate show an enhancement of [hk1] orientation, when
annealing takes place below 350◦C, and a constraint of [hk0] orientation.
SEM images
show that higher crystallinity of Sb2S3 absorbers is obtained at lower temperatures, below
350◦C, especially with the selection of MoS2-substrate, while annealing temperature increase
leads to degradation of the surface. Finally, fabrication of functional Sb2S3 solar cells with
Zn(1−x)MgxO (ZMO) as n-type layer was unsuccessful, as devices did not present photovoltaic
behaviour.

Introduction
Nations strive to deal with the escalating pressure on the energy system worldwide and
reduce the CO2 emissions, constraining the temperature increase up to 1.5◦C. As a result,
policy initiatives and scenarios have been adopted in order to achieve the net zero emissions
targets of more than 50 countries, including the European Union, and the rapid promotion
of clean energy based on renewable energy systems and eco-fuels.[1] In 2021, CO2 emissions
reached 36.6 Gt, a record increase of 1.9 Gt due to the accelerated post-pandemic economic
growth and the risen demand in coal.[2] The primary world energy consumption leveled up
to almost 600 EJ, while 82% of primary energy use was based on fossil fuels.[3] In order to
reduce the need of fossil fuels, dilute CO2 emissions and still have the ability to cover the
electricity demand, renewable energy systems should expand, with photovoltaic and wind
systems to be the main leaders, mostly due to low costs and availability.[4]
The PV industry is growing rapidly, especially the last decade, with a capacity of 225 GW in
2015 and the additions reaching 90 GW in 2016-2019, while it provided around 3% of global
electricity generation in 2021, with a record PV capacity increase of 150 GW.[2],[5],[6],[7]
Silicon (Si) solar cells own the highest market share of PV production that has reached 95%
in 2021.[2] There are two major types, monocrystalline and poly-crystalline Si solar cells,
with efficiencies of 26.7% and 24.4%, respectively.[8] Even though earth-abundant materials
are used for their fabrication and a relatively high efficiency is achieved, silicon has an indi-
rect bandgap and thicker Si layers are required for sufficient solar absorption, with optimal
thickness to be around 100 µm for crystalline Si.[9],[10] This use of more material results in
a higher energy payback time that elevates the total PV module cost.[11]
On the other hand, PV systems based on thin-film solar cells have half energy payback
time comparing to Si ones, while they can be used on flexible substrates that will help
the development of other solar applications, such as PV building integration.[11],[12] They
had the remaining market share of 5% solar industry, in 2021, and different types of solar
cells have been commercialized.[2] CdTe and CuIn(Ga)(Se)2 (CIGS) solar cells are the most
widespread with efficiencies of 21% and 23.35%, respectively.[8] Other thin film technologies
are based on GaAs solar cells (29.1%) and organic/inorganic perovskites (23.7%).[8] How-
ever, the scarcity of some elements, such as indium and gallium, and the toxicity of others,
such as selenium, that hide environmental hazards, has led to the investigation of alternative
materials, that are non-toxic and abundant.[13]
Sb2S3 solar cells have gained noticeable attention for their properties.
Apart from the
environmental-friendly and eco-rich elements that they contain, they may be also grown
with a preferential orientation (quasi-one-dimensional or quasi-1D) to allow the efficient
extraction of photogenerated carriers and overcome grain boundaries, that reduce the de-
vice performance.[14] The highest efficiency that has been recorded so far is 10.5%.[15] The
detailed description of the material properties and solar cell structure is given in Chapter 2.1.
The efficiency of Sb2S3 solar cells is not competitive to their commercialized alternatives,

the preferentially oriented Sb2S3 thin-films, should be exploited, in order to improve the

Theory
2.1
Sb2S3 solar cells
As mentioned in Introduction, this project investigates the growth of Sb2S3 thin films, the
effect of annealing on composition and orientation, as well as the performance of cadmium-
free Sb2S3 solar cells. Firstly, a short description of the material with developments in its
deposition are presented, while after the operation of Sb2S3 solar cells and already researched
cell structures are discussed.
2.1.1
Antimony sulfide (Sb2S3)
Sb2S3, or else antimony sulfide, is known for its favourable optoelectronic properties and is
a candidate for substitution of toxic and scarce thin film solar cells (CIGS, CdTe), while
quite a few research is help for the investigation of its growth and characteristics. It consists
of antimony (Sb) and sulfur (S), two earth-abundant and eco-friendly elements. It presents
energy bandgap of 1.7 eV and relatively high absorption coefficient of 1.8×105 cm−1 at 450
nm.[16] It is a binary compound with an orthorhombic crystal structure (see Figure 1) and
forms a single stable phase, eliminating the probability to present secondary phases. It can
be deposited and crystallized at low temperatures (<350◦C), due to its low melting point
at 550◦C.[17] Sb2S3 forms a needle-like crystal, that consists of infinite 1D Sb4S6 ribbons,
bound with Van den Waals forces. The lattice constants of the crystal are a=11.3107 Å,
b=3.8367 Å and c=11.2285 Å. In Figure 2, three different orientations [hk0], [hk1] and [001]
are shown.[16]

As 1D material, Sb2S3 is anisotropic in connection with electrical and optical properties,
while defect formation also presents anisotropy along the different orientation of the crystal.[18]
As a result, the performance of Sb2S3 solar cells depends on the preferred orientation of the
crystal. Zhou et al. applied VASP code simulations in Sb2Se3 crystals (isostructural to Sb2S3
crystals) to investigate the different electronic properties in various crystal orientations. They
reported that [001] had no dangling bonds and (010), (110) and (120) planes, parallel to for-
mer direction, indicate lower formation energies that lead to surface passivation, elimination
of grain boundaries at the edges of ribbons and decrease in recombination centers. However,
this contrasts with other photovoltaic materials, such as Si or CdTe (see Figure 3), that
present defects at grain boundaries and lead to recombination of photo-generated carriers,
due to existence of unsaturated bonds.[19],[20] Deng et al. developed Sb2S3 absorbers with

quasiepitaxial growth that promotes [hk1] orientation and increases the mobility of carries

The most new and advanced techniques for Sb2S3 deposition are rapid thermal evapora-
tion (RTE, 4.17%[24]), hydrothermal (6.84% [25]), sputtering and annealing (2.41%[26])
and closed space sublimation (CSS, 4.45% [27]). In this project, RF magnetron sputtering
and annealing is used for the deposition and crystallization of Sb2S3 layers. Medina-Montes
et al. reported that absorbers presented p-type conductivity with high absorption (>104
cm−1) and improved crystallinity after annealing at 300◦C.[28] The same group reported
crystallization of Sb2S3 precursors at 300◦C annealing temperature.[29] Luo et al. applied
post-sulfurization and post-selenization processes in sputtered Sb2S3 samples, in which the
latter presented higher crystal quality and less recombination losses.[30] Gao et al. investi-
gated the properties of Sb2S3 precursors as for annealing temperature and gas atmosphere
(pure S, pure Sb2S3 and both), obtaining better crystallinity at 350◦C in pure S-atmosphere
and reporting the highest efficiency with this method (2.41%).[26]

2.1.2
Sb2S3 solar cell operation
The operation of Sb2S3 solar cells is based on the p-n junction general principles, with the
p-type material to be Sb2S3, while different n-type materials have been investigated and
some of them are referred below. P-type materials have excess holes (acceptors), while n-
type have excess electrons (donors). A p-n junction is formed when a p-type and a n-type
layer are linked. Electrons diffuse from the n-type to the p-type area and holes from p-type
to n-type area, where they recombine. The area near the contact of the p-n junction, that
lacks no free carriers exist, is called the depletion region.The ionized donors and acceptors
in the depletion region, the former closer to the n-type and the latter to the p-type, create
Page 5 of 39
==PAGE:13==

a potential, that is called ’build-in or diffusion voltage’. This build-in voltage introduces a
barrier to the free carriers, that disrupts their tendency to move on the other side conductive
area.
In Figure 5, the p-n junction is under no bias and the depletion region is wide, while the
energy bands bend and the build-in voltage resists to the movement of the carries. If an
external voltage is applied and is positive in connection with the potential of the p-type area,
the diffuse voltage will decrease and electrons can move freely to the p-type area. It should
be noted that the Fermi energy (the energy level with probability of finding an electron to
be 50%) under no bias is constant and closer to the valence band in the p-type material and
to the conduction band in the n-type material, while under forward bias the Fermi energy
breaks in the depletion region.[33]
Figure 5: P-n junction (a) under zero bias and (b) under forward bias.[33]
When the p-n junction is under illumination, which is the case for solar cells, the absorption of
photons with ≥Eg (Eg is the band gap of the semiconductor) results in additional generation
of electron-hole pairs. Minority carriers, which are electrons for the p-type region and holes
for the n-type region, rise and, as a result, a flow of electrons from the p-type to the n-type
and vice versa for holes initiates. This flow is called photo-generated current, that adds up
to thermal-generated current. Light-generated carriers forward-bias the solar cell and the
barrier height is lowered as described for p-n junctions previously.[34] Illumination of the
solar cell shifts down the characteristic I-V curve of the p-n junction to the fourth quadrant.
Figure 6 shows this shift with the equivalent circuit to the right. The diode law, that a solar
cell is subject to, is given by the equation:

2.1.3
Sb2S3 solar cell structure
There are various materials that are used as n-type (buffer) layers in Sb2S3 solar cells. The
buffer layer is in contact with absorber and creates the p-n junction, while it plays a vi-
tal role in collection and extraction of photo-generated carries. TiO2 is a common buffer
layer that is used for the fabrication of Sb2S3 solar cells. Doping and treatment of TiO2
is reported to result in enhanced electron transport.[16] Han et al. post-treated TiO2 with
inorganic salt zinc halide (ZnCl2, ZnBr2, ZnI2) and reach an efficiency of 7.08%, while there
was also an improvement in the crystallinity of Sb2S3 absorbers.[36] Cadmium sulfide (CdS)
is another typical buffer layer with higher electron mobility (350 cm2V−1s−1) that TiO2 (6.3
cm2V−1s−1) and still the only buffer layer used in substrate configuration.[16] Post-treatment
methods have been tried in order to increase the overall performance of the solar cell, such
as Zeng et al. that achieved an efficiency of 4.73% applying annealing of CdS at 400◦C for
5 minutes.[37] Wang et al. observed the highest efficiency of 10.5%, depositing Sb2S3 with
hydrothermal process and the addition of ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) for higher
crystal quality, using CdS as buffer layer.[15] However, Gao et al. reported lattice mismatch
between Sb2S3 and Cds layers, that lead to interface defects and recombination losses.[26]
Zinc oxide (ZnO) is an alternative buffer layer, also with higher electron mobility than that
of TiO2 (200 cm2V−1s−1).[16] Liu et al. achieved one of the highest efficiencies of 2.9% with
applied ZnO nano-arrays.[23]
In this project, magnesium zinc oxide (Zn(1−x)MgxO) or ZMO is used as buffer layer, in
order to fabricate Cd-free solar cells. However, no literature was found with the fabrication
of Sb2S3 solar cells with ZMO, so a brief description of the material and its properties is
presented.
ZMO is originated when ZnO is alloyed with MgO and doped with Mg ions. The bandgap
of ZMO varies according to Mg concentration and takes values from 3.3 to 4 eV.[38] Rajb-
handari et al. reported a linear relation between Mg concentration (%) and ZMO energy

Losses on Sb2S3 solar cells
As shown in Introduction and section 2.1.3, Sb2S3 solar cells present relatively low efficiency
in comparison to other commercialized thin-films such as CdTe and CIGS. This is mainly

due to recombination losses that result from bulk and interface defects and decline solar cell
performance parameters such as open-circuit voltage (VOC) and fill factor (FF). Some of
these defects are listed below[16]:
• At the interface of Sb2S3 absorber with the n-type layer, defects are formed due to
unsaturated bonds and lattice mismatch that create recombination centres.
• Introduction of contamination at the Sb2S3/n-type layer interface may also result in
losses from carrier recombination.
• Sulfur loss in Sb2S3 absorbers lead to S-vacancies in the surface of ribbons and grain
boundaries, resulting in recombination losses and mismatching with n-type layer.
• Other types of defects that lead to carrier loss are Sb-vacancies, antisites of S and Sb,
as well as S interstitials.
Even though recombination losses are known for their detrimental effect on the performance
of thin film solar cells, the factors that constrain the efficiency of Sb2S3 solar cells and their
transport mechanisms are quite unexplored.[39] Courel et al. conducted a theoretical in-
vestigation of these limitations and claimed that, apart from Sb2S3 bulk and Sb2S3/n-type
interface defects, also series and shunt resistance may affect the performance negatively.[39]
However, more experimental research in needed in order to understand in depth the mecha-
nisms that lead to lower Sb2S3 solar cell performance.
2.2
Magnetron sputtering as fabrication method
For this project, magnetron sputtering method was used for the deposition of Sb2S3 precur-
sors and the basic principles of the method are presented below.
Sputter deposition is a physical vapor deposition (PVD) method and is used to deposit
thin films of a variety of materials (metals, semiconductors, insulators) onto substrates. It is
based on sputtering process, in which material atoms or molecules are ejected from a target
that is bombarded by high-energy particles, and reach the substrate, so as to form a film.
Magnetron sputtering is a high-rate vacuum deposition technique that lies on the same prin-
ciples as the former, with the addition of a specifically-shaped magnetic field applied to the
target. The target bombardment takes place by positive ions that resulted from an electrical
gas discharge, called plasma. The process of plasma generation initiates with the emission
of electrons from the cathode, that are accelerated to the anode and ionize the gas atoms.
Ions transfer part of their momentum to target atoms, which in turn strike to other target
atoms, forming an avalanche process. In this way, certain surface atoms gain the necessary
momentum and are ejected out of the target. Also, secondary electrons are formed and leave
the target with the sputtered particles. With the application of magnetic field on the surface
of the target, while the cathode is placed inside the area where magnetic field has a high
effect, electrons (primary and secondary) are confined locally. As a consequence, the chance
of the ionization of a gas atom in this region rises, the plasma density increases and, as a
result, the erosion rate is higher. A typical magnetron sputtering layout is shown in Figure
9.

Magnetron sputtering is always conducted under vacuum, where pressure of 10−2 mbar or

Magnetron sputtering consists of various types, such as DC or RF magnetron sputtering.
In former, DC power is applied straight to the target and is used for deposition of metals.
However, DC magnetrons are not suitable for insulators or dielectric materials as targets,
as there is no current flow through them. RF sputtering is used for the deposition of semi-
conductors, operating with an alternating current at high frequencies (∼13.56 MHz). In
general, magnetron sputtering offers several advantages, such as high-rate deposition, high
film adhesion and simple usage, and exhibits various applications, from microelectronics and
optics to the field of decoration.

2.3.1
Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a scattering characterization technique that provides information of
the vibrational and other low-frequency modes of a material and can be also used as iden-
tification of different molecules. It is based on Raman effect, in which a small fraction of
incident to the sample radiation is scattered inelastically due to vibrations of atoms inside
the material. As a result, the scattered photons have slightly different frequencies than of
initial light. Raman scattering consists of Stokes and Anti-Stokes scattering, whether the
scattered frequency is lower or higher than the incident one, respectively (see Figure 11).
In Raman spectroscopy, a monochromatic laser beam is directed to a beam splitter and
after to an objective lens that focuses light onto the specimen. After, scattered light is di-
rected to a spectrometer through the beam splitter and a focusing lens, while there is also a
Rayleigh filter that cuts out the laser wavelength as well as wavelengths below. Stokes bands
are more intense than Anti-Stokes ones due to the transition to higher vibrational levels and,
as a result, measurements are based on these, while Anti-Stokes ones are eliminated by the
Rayleigh filter.

2.3.2
X-Ray diffraction analysis (XRD)
X-Ray diffraction is an analytical characterization method that is used for phase identifica-
tion, investigation of crystal structure or size, and determination of crystallographic preferred
orientation. It is based on the diffraction of X-rays (10−10 m) from the crystal planes of spec-
imen under research.
X-rays are scattered, due to interactions with electrons, from the atoms lying on the different
crystal planes and will interfere constructively or destructively, depending on the incident an-
gle and the distance between the planes. For specific angles, X-rays will result in constructive
interference that is described by Bragg’s law (see Figure 13a):
nλ = 2dsinθ
(3)
where n is an integer, λ is the wavelength of the X-rays, d is the distance between crystal
planes and θ is the diffraction angle in degrees.
The most common XRD geometry that laboratories apply is called Bragg-Brentano geome-
try, where the X-ray source and detector are facing the sample (see Figure 13b). The main

inciding to the specimen), the sample holder, the secondary optics (collect the diffracted

2.3.3
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)
Scanning electron microscopy is a characterization method that provides information about
topography, composition and crystallography of a sample, as well as other physical proper-
ties, such as magnetic areas and micro-structural electrical fields. An electron beam scans
the surface of the specimen and the ejected electrons create a magnified image of the sample.
There are two types of SEM images: the secondary electron and the back-scattered electron
image that are based on the working principle of SEM set up.
SEM consists of an electron gun and the generated electrons (typical energy from 0.1 to
30 keV) are focused on the specimen through various electromagnetic lenses (see Figure
14a). The lens system contains a condenser lens, that adjusts the intensity of the electron
beam on the sample, an objective lens, that focuses the beam on the specimen and electro-
magnetic deflection coils, that bend the beam and scan the whole surface. The interaction of
the e-beam with matter results in two main types of ejected electrons: (a) the back-scattered
electrons (BSEs), that are scattered electrons of primary beam by the atoms of the sample
and a large amount of their initial energy is maintained, and (b) secondary electrons (SEs),
that are ejected electrons from the surface with low kinetic energies (0-50 eV) and originate
Page 13 of 39

back-scattered electron and secondary electron detector, respectively. There are, of course,
other interactions with the material, such as X-Ray and bremsstrahlung radiation, Auger
electrons and cathodoluminescence, that use respective detectors, however, are out of scope
of this thesis and are not discussed further. From the origin of the BSEs, it is clear that
they provide information from below the surface (up to 40% of top region), while SEs offer
knowledge only close to surface. It should be noted that the whole system operates under

Both annealing
temperature and substrate have an effect in Sb2S3 absorber crystallization and orientation.
For both Mo and MoS2 series, 300◦C present more favourable [hk1] orientation, while with
annealing temperature increase, [hk0]-oriented absorbers are more probable to form. Luo
et al. confirms the opposite, that higher annealing temperature leads to promotion of [hk1]
orientation in Sb2S3 absorbers.[30] Gao et al. suggests that orientation of Sb2S3 samples
was not affected by annealing temperature, as all presented [hk0] orientation.[26] As for use
of different substrates, sulfurization of Mo-substrate has led to elimination of monoclinic
Sb10S15, as well as Sb2(SO4) at 350◦C, 400◦C and 450◦C. In addition, a clearer enhancement
of [hk1] orientation is implied for lower annealing temperature (300◦C and 350◦C). Liu et al.
reported similar results with the application of selenization of Mo-substrate, forming a MoSe2
layer. The [hk1] orientation of Sb2S3 samples was enhanced, while [hk0] was constrained.
MoSe2 increases the roughness of the substrate and the nucleation sites for growth of Sb4S6
ribbons.[23]

thickness of the deposited film.[57] Investigation of deposition time of ZMO should be
conducted, so as to find the optimal ZMO thickness for Sb2S3 solar cell fabrication and
the successful extraction of photo-generated carriers.

5
Conclusion and future work
In this study, the effect of annealing temperature and substrate selection on Sb2S3 crys-
tal growth and orientation is exploited. The precursors were deposited via sputtering of
SbS target, on Mo- and MoS2- substrates, followed by annealing at various temperatures
(300◦C, 350◦C, 400◦C and 450◦C) for 15 minutes. All absorbers reveal the crystallization
of precursors in Sb2S3 phase, while for those on top of Mo-substrate, Sb10S15 and Sb2(SO4)
phases appear from 350◦C and above. Absorbers at lower annealing temperatures (<350◦C)
show an enhancement of [hk1] orientation and a suppress of [hk0], especially those on MoS2-
substrate, based on calculated intensity ratios. In addition, they obtain better crystallinity,
with large grains and no voids or pinholes, while grain size is reduced with annealing tem-
perature increase, that reaches up to 50% for absorbers on Mo-substrate. Degradation of
Sb2S3 is promoted at higher temperatures, with less intensity to be at absorbers with MoS2-
substrate. Finally, fabricated Sb2S3 solar cells with Zn(1−x)MgxO (ZMO) as n-type layer
were not functional, as they did not operate based on photovoltaic effect.
Since Sb2S3 is a new under research material at Photovoltaic Materials and Systems group
at DTU Fotonik, quite a few questions are still unanswered and extensive research is needed
in order to understand in depth its growth mechanisms. Research that could be beneficial
for our better understanding is:
• characterization and optimization of MoS2-substrate, as Sb2S3 absorbers present better
properties when deposited on sulfurized Mo-substrate.
• EDX analysis of Sb2S3 absorbers, in order to verify the existence of Sb10S15 or Sb2(SO4)
phases on surface (white crystals in SEM images).
• detailed analysis of XRD patterns, that unfortunately was not conducted due to limited
time frame, for the accurate determination of texture orientation of Sb2S3 absorbers
and maybe a ’sweet point’ on annealing temperature that favours [hk1] direction.
• characterization of all Sb2S3 devices, such as SEM imaging, and research of ZMO
deposition parameters, or even exploration of alternative Cd-free n-type materials, as
these are the first Sb2S3 solar cells fabricated in the group and a better understanding
of this device failure is necessary.
