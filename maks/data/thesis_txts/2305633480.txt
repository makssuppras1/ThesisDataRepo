Abstract
As global warming caused by emission of greenhouse gasses causes severe climate
changes, the urge to ﬁnd environmental friendly alternatives to services such as
heating and transportation increases. In the future power system, these services
are predicted to be electriﬁed through distributed energy resources (DERs) such
as heat pumps (HPs) and electric vehicles (EVs), which aﬀect the loading level of
distribution networks.
A key challenge of the future is to ﬁnd eﬃcient methods of operating distribution
networks with a large number of DERs implemented. In recent research, the day-
ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is considered a way of activating the distribution
network to alleviate congestion from DERs.
In this thesis, the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is evaluated
through oﬄine and online testing, which reveals limitations and application pos-
sibilities of the method. The method’s dependency on predicted data describing
DER operation is addressed and a method of solving this issue is proposed through
model predictive control (MPC).
From the evaluation of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in an emulation of
a real power system, this thesis concludes that the method, in its current formula-
tion, cannot be used as a congestion management method in the northern Rønne
distribution network. As reactive power ﬂows are not considered in the DCOPF
formulated optimization model of this method. These reactive power ﬂows cause
additional loading of distribution network transmission lines, which exceed their
rated current. Furthermore, some of the transformers in the network are subject
to overloading in scenarios where the distribution network is highly loaded. From
a voltage stability perspective, the voltage magnitude at all distribution network
buses are kept within operational limits.
To increase the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod as a congestion
management method, an ACOPF formulation of the DSO optimization model could
be used instead of the DCOPF formulation to include more power system stability
constraints and phenomenons. Another approach is to describe the power transfer
capability in the DCOPF optimization model by a percentage margin lower than
the actual rated power transfer. This allows capacity in the transmission lines for
transmission losses as well as reactive power ﬂows.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
As global warming, caused by an increase in greenhouse gas emissions such as
CO2, has lead to an increase in the temperature of the planet [1], climate changes
such as storms, ﬂoods and landslides are predicted to occur more often in the
future [2]. To address the environmental issues from global warming, the global
electricity sector moves towards a fossil free future and the Danish government has
published an ambitious plan for Denmark to become independent of fossil fuels by
the year 2050 [3]. The consumption of fossil fuels used in the transportation and
heating services in residencies are responsible for contributing to the emission of
CO2. Therefore a key challenge of the future is to replace these services with more
environmental friendly alternatives [2]. The Danish government predicts that by
the year 2050, services such as heating and transportation will mainly be powered
by electricity through heat pumps (HPs) and electric vehicles (EVs), respectively
[3]. When electrifying services using distributed energy resources (DERs) such as
HPs and EVs, the consumers’ dependency on the reliability of the power system
increases [4].
In the energy strategy of 2050 it is an important objective to maintain a high
reliability of the electricity supply while converting to green energy. Security of
supply is a key future challenge as electricity demand will grow together with the
population and the economy in Denmark [3]. A further increase in electricity de-
mand is expected due to electriﬁcation of services and implementation of DERs.
Electrical appliances including DERs are designed to operate with a voltage sup-
ply of certain magnitude and frequency. Distribution system operators (DSOs) are
obligated to provide power that satisﬁes the consumers’ requirements at all times.
1/146
==PAGE:30==
1.2. Thesis Objective
It is therefore necessary that the DSOs ensure the reliability of the power supply
in the future distribution network and allow an integration of a large number of
DERs [4].
Today distribution networks are considered passive and designed to allow cos-
tumers to consume electricity whenever they see ﬁt [2]. This means that they are
designed to transfer power that meets demand from residential and industrial loads
only, without DERs. As DERs increases the demand at residential loads, conges-
tion issues could arise in the future distribution network [2]. If congestion issues
cause overloading of power system equipment such as transmission lines, the sys-
tem reliability decreases and aﬀects the end-user by limiting their behaviour. In
today’s distribution network, indications of congestion issues are dealt with by re-
inforcement or replacement of network equipment [4]. This approach is expensive,
which lowers the social welfare of the system [4]. It is diﬃcult to predict the exact
distribution of DERs in the future which complicates the investment process of grid
reinforcement [2].
In the Ideal Grid for All (IDE4L) project, the concept of an active distribution
network is used to deﬁne a system that utilizes control mechanisms and monitoring
of the network to predict and deal with congestion issues. An active distribution
network utilizes the ﬂexible nature of DERs. DERs can be used for ancillary ser-
vices as their consumption can be controlled to alleviate congestion issues without
substantially aﬀecting the end-user. By activating the distribution network, the
DSOs could potentially extend the lifetime of the current distribution network and
prevent a decrease in power system reliability [2].
In recent research the above mentioned concept of an active distribution network
has been utilized to develop control methods that can alleviate congestion issues.
These control methods are considered as suitable tools for congestion management
with respect to the implementation of DERs in a passive distribution network [5].
The main focus of this thesis is to investigate the aforementioned control methods
and evaluate the eﬃciency of one of the methods through oﬄine and online testing.
1.2
Thesis Objective
The overall objective of this thesis is to evaluate the eﬃciency of the day-ahead
dynamic tariﬀmethod as a congestion management method through oﬄine and on-
line simulations, with respect to power system stability and to address the method’s
dependency on predicted data. The overall objective is achieved by performing three
main tasks:
• Evaluate the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in an oﬄine

1.3
Methodology
To establish the real time digital testing platform, the northern Rønne MV
distribution model used by the Bornholm DSO Østkraft is used as a real life repre-
sentation of the actual grid [6]. From the model of the distribution network, data
concerning the power system equipment is extracted and used to represent the dis-
tribution network in the RTDS. The communication protocol server is created using
MatrikonOPC software described in [7]. Base demand data is found in [8] and [9]
as historical data and used as a representation of predicted base consumption. The
predicted base prices are based on historical data found in [10].
The day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is implemented in the software tools Mat-
lab and GAMS based on the theory described in [11]. The consumption patterns
of EVs are based on the statistical data of consumer usage of conventional vehi-
cles described in [12]. The HPs are modelled using heat ﬂows of a house with the
approach described in [13].
The power system stability evaluation of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is
done by monitoring the results from the MV distribution network simulated on the
RTDS. The analytical tools used to determine the stability of the power supply to
the consumers is described in [4] where the voltage magnitude constraint of ±10%
of rated bus voltage at network buses and overloading of transmission lines and
transformers are the only indications of stability considered. The usage of direct

investigated by determining the network stability with the power transfer capability

1.4
Scope and Limitations
In this thesis the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is evaluated as a tool for al-
leviating congestion issues in the northern Rønne distribution network. Therefore
the results and ﬁndings can only be used as an indication and a guideline for diﬀer-
ent distribution networks. The eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
is evaluated based on limitations that are apparent from oﬄine and online simula-
tions. Furthermore the eﬃciency of the method is based on the method’s ability
to alleviate congestion while satisfying voltage magnitude constraints at network
busses and loading constraints of transmission lines and transformers. While eval-
uating the eﬃciency of the method, a number of assumptions and limitations are
introduced where the primary limitations are brieﬂy addressed in the following:
The distribution network used in this thesis is only simulated in a steady state
operation with all loads being balanced three phase loads. This means that no fault
scenarios are investigated in this thesis.
The voltage stability of the power system is analysed with respect to voltage
magnitude only.
Short term voltage variations caused by disturbances such as
transients are ignored. The concept of voltage frequency stability is omitted for the
present thesis as it is controlled at the transmission network level. The frequency
of the system is assumed constant at 50 Hz throughout the simulations.
To evaluate the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, the number of
DERs in the system are implemented as future scenarios where a certain percentage
of the residents at each load point have electriﬁed their transportation and heating
services.
When simulating the power system in oﬄine and online environments, recon-
ﬁguration and reactive power control actions are assumed already performed as a
method of alleviating congestion issues.

Chapter 2
Congestion Management Methods
In extension to the introduction and motivation of the present thesis described
in Chapter 1, the electriﬁcation of services such as heating and transportation
can have an extensive impact on the future distribution network stability. In this
chapter, an introduction to congestion management methods is given.
Each of
the congestion management solutions are brieﬂy described by means of function,
action takers and their impact on the social welfare. In the end a comparison of
the methods is described with respect to congestion management in a future active
distribution network.
Congestion management can be characterized as a control method that prevents
overloading of power system components such as transmission lines and transform-
ers [15]. There exist several possible methods of performing congestion management
in the distribution network, where major techniques and theories are presented in
this thesis. The investigated solutions to congestion issues are listed in Table 2.1.
The easiest way of alleviating congestion is to simply shred active power consump-
tion that exceeds the congestion constraints of the network equipment [5]. How-
ever, this heavily aﬀects the freedom of end-users, therefore the active power control
method is not considered as a method of congestion management in this thesis. The
possible congestion management methods are characterized by two major categories
Network or Non-network solutions.

2.1
Network solutions
The concept of network solutions to congestion issues are characterized as actions
taken by the DSO to alleviate congestion by adding or replacing network equipment
within the distribution network [4]. The four network solutions shown in Table 2.1
all represent actions that require investment in the grid which goes through a long
term planning process.
The planning of investing in the grid topology starts 3-5 years before the invest-
ment is done. The planning process includes several steps where short and long
term demand forecasts are investigated. Based on these forecasts, risks of conges-
tion are identiﬁed and diﬀerent solutions are proposed. The optimal solution is
chosen from a technical and economical perspective [4].
In order to alleviate congestion within the power system, the reinforcement plan-
ners are required to accurately predict the future distribution of power consuming
loads as the planning of investment begins years in advance. As services are pre-
dicted electriﬁed in the future power system, larger individual power consuming
units are distributed in the distribution network and the exact distribution is dif-
ﬁcult to predict which can implicate the planning process of investments in the
distribution network [2]. The investment in the distribution grid can be carried
out through investing in diﬀerent network equipment as shown in Table 2.1. In the
following the network solutions are presented and their impact on the social welfare
of the system is discussed.
Social welfare of a system can be characterized as a concept describing the overall
satisfaction and well-being of the various groups in a market in terms of economics.
From a power system viewpoint, the transmission system operator (TSO) is a non-
proﬁt organization while the power generating companies, retailers and consumers
8/146
==PAGE:37==
2.1. Network solutions
aims at increasing proﬁt or decreasing costs. To maximize the well-being of all the
participants in the power system, it is of interest to minimize the costs of operating
the distribution network [16].
2.1.1
Transmission Lines
The addition or replacement of transmission lines in the distribution network can
be a solution to congestion issues when the bottleneck is an existing transmission
line. In today’s distribution network, the transmission lines are designed and scaled
to deal with the maximum possible expected demand from loads [4]. As described in
Chapter 1, the overall demand from loads in the distribution network is predicted
to increase and additional demand is expected from implementing DERs in the
network [2].
Therefore congestion issues in transmission lines pose a threat to
the reliability of the future distribution network, and reinforcements are predicted
necessary. Reinforcement of transmission lines requires an investment in the system
from the DSOs, which means that the operation of the distribution network costs
increase which lowers the social welfare in the system [4].
2.1.2
Transformers
Transformers are designed to shift the voltage level between two parts of a
network. An example is between the MV and the low voltage (LV) distribution
networks. In order to function, these transformers have a certain rated apparent
power level which indicates its operational limit. If the loading of the transformer
exceeds the operational limit of the transformer, its temperature increases and
faults can occur [14]. The system reliability depends highly on the safe operation
of transformers, and the predicted increase in load demand could cause severe
problems in the future distribution network [2]. As is the case for transmission line
reinforcement, transformer reinforcement has to go through the planning process
described earlier.
The cost of adding or replacing a transformer in the system
increases the DSO costs which also decreases the social welfare of the system [4].
2.1.3
Distributed generation
Distributed generation (DG) units can be used to alleviate congestion issues in
the distribution network. DG can be characterized as small generating units placed
in the distribution network and capable of delivering active power to the grid.
Examples of DGs are residential photovoltaic (PV) systems, small onshore wind
turbines and micro combined heating and power (CHP) units. DGs can alleviate
congestion by generating active power close to the load points which decreases the
9/146
==PAGE:38==
2.2. Non-network solutions
active power ﬂows in the distribution network. From a social welfare perspective,
the investment in DG units is relatively high and depends highly on governmental
reforms and subsidies, but the operation of most DGs is almost cost free. The
cost of active power generation of DGs is compensated by the DSO, who has to
purchase less power from the spot market when DGs are operating. Therefore the
social welfare is decreased slightly when considering DG investment and operational
costs [4].
2.1.4
Distributed storage
Distributed storage (DS) units can be characterized as battery storage units at
residents or at distribution network buses, fuel cells or EVs. These units are all
capable of consuming power from the distribution network and store the energy. In
recent studies, the usage of large battery storage stations in the distribution network
to alleviate congestion issues is presented [17]. In the distribution network, batteries
can be distributed to load connection points where congestion issues could pose a
problem during peak load hours. If high congestion occurs, the DS units can release
their energy to the grid and supply power to local loads [4]. In recent research the
concept of vehicle to grid (V2G) is developed which enables EVs to deliver power
to the grid when needed [18]. From a social welfare perspective, the DS units are
comparable to DG units. Social welfare decreases as investment prices are relatively
high, but increases as the operation costs are small and because DS units can be
utilized to store cheap power from renewable energy sources (RES). The EVs as DS
units are interesting as their main function is to serve as transport for the end-user
and the investment costs can therefore be subtracted from the power system social
welfare equation.
2.2
Non-network solutions
Instead of reinforcing the grid, non-network solutions could be a possible tool
for alleviating congestion as they are characterized as solutions that utilizes the
present distribution network and optimize its operation while satisfying power sys-
tem stability constraints [5].
The non-network solutions can be divided into two groups as shown in Table 2.1.
Direct control methods are characterized as congestion management that changes
the operation of the present grid equipment to alleviate congestion issues. The
indirect control methods refers to congestion management that utilizes market con-
trols. The market controls are used to alleviate congestion by changing the price of
operation in hours of congestion and thereby shift DER operation in the distribu-
10/146
==PAGE:39==
2.2. Non-network solutions
tion network [5]. The indirect control methods activates the distribution network
by changing the consumption within the network when congestion is a risk [2]
The direct congestion management methods are describe next followed by a
description of the indirect congestion management methods.
2.2.1
Reconﬁguration
As a direct control method, the DSO can utilize the power system equipment by
opening and closing switches and thereby change the grid topology to alleviate con-
gestion issues. When load points at diﬀerent locations in the system have diﬀerent
demand, the DSO can change the topology of the network to supply power to high
demand loads through the path of low demand loads. In terms of social welfare im-
pact, the reconﬁguration of the distribution network is cost free for the consumers.
However, the reconﬁguration might cause higher transmission losses as the optimal
load ﬂow conﬁguration is no longer in operation [19]. The reconﬁguration can be
done using diﬀerent optimization models where the losses are minimized while alle-
viating congestion in the distribution network [20–22]. The increase in transmission
losses cause the operational costs of the DSO to increase slightly which lowers the
social welfare of the system.
2.2.2
Reactive Power Control
Reactive power control can change the voltage level at the network busses and
thereby change the ﬂow of reactive power in the network. Reactive power control is
performed by utilizing power system equipment such as tap-changing transformers,
static var compensation units (SVC) etc. As the transfer of reactive power through
transmission lines increases the transmission losses and current level, the utilization
of local reactive power control close to inductive or capacitive loads is an important
feature of the power system as they can change the power factor of the loads in the
network [4]. Besides the traditional power system components capable of reactive
power control, batteries and converters from DERs and DGs can change their power
factor and change the local voltage level [23]. The costs of reactive power control
are very low, as only the loss eﬀects are considered. Therefore the social welfare of
the system only decreases marginally.
2.2.3
Control of household appliances
The DSO could potentially control common household appliances to alleviate
congestion during peak load hours. Appliances as refrigerators and freezers are
11/146
==PAGE:40==
2.2. Non-network solutions
common in most residencies and can be considered as thermal energy storage units.
These units can be characterized as thermostatically controllable loads (TCLs) and
function by consuming power which is used to cool the content of the containers.
As the lower temperature limit is reached, the TCLs turn oﬀand temperature
increases. In this way, HPs can be characterized as TCLs that produces heat. If
the DSO can control the consumption of these units to shut oﬀbefore the lower
temperature limit is reached, the overall consumption of the distribution network
is decreased for a short time period. As a large number of TCLs are distributed
within the network, the combined power reserve is considerably high [24]. The social
welfare is not aﬀected by the control of TCLs as long as the TCL temperature limits
are satisﬁed.
Besides TCLs, the residents in the distribution network contain deferrable loads
(DLs). These loads are washing machines, dryers and dishwashers which can be
controlled to consume power during oﬀpeak load hours instead of exactly when
they are ready to start. The control of DLs can be compared to the control of EVs
to alleviate congestion by decreasing the overall demand when required. As long as
end-user requirements are satisﬁed, the social welfare is not aﬀected by this control
method as was the case of controlling TCLs [24]. The consumption from DLs and
TCLs can be controlled by price signals as is the case for DERs which are described
by indirect control methods presented in the following.
2.2.4
Day-ahead Dynamic Tariﬀ
In the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, the ﬂexibility and price-sensitive na-
ture of the DERs in the system is utilized to alleviate congestion. The DSO can
change the dynamic tariﬀ(DT) at diﬀerent load points in the distribution network
which are at risk of congestion issues. The tariﬀof loads connected to transmis-
sion lines with high congestion is increased. The aggregators that control DERs
experiences an increased tariﬀand by optimizing the operational cost, they can
shift consumption of DERs to hours with less congestion. A key advantage of this
method is that it is performed in a decentralized manner [25]. This means that
the DSO holds all information about the grid and optimizes the predicted energy
plan of DERs in the network and calculates the distributed DTs. Afterwards it
communicates the DT to the aggregators which also calculate energy plans of the
DERs based on predicted data. In recent research the optimal solution to the DSO
and aggregator optimization models are found to be equal if a feasible solution
exists, meaning that DSO and aggregator calculated energy plans are equal [11].
After the energy plans are calculated by the aggregators, they participate in the
day-ahead electricity market and are obligated to follow the posted energy plans in
the following day at each load point in the network. From a social welfare impact
12/146
==PAGE:41==
2.2. Non-network solutions
point of view, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod does not change the consumers
base load costs as the dynamic tariﬀonly applies to DERs. As the DSO strive to
ﬁnd the lowest tariﬀthat can alleviate congestion issues, the impact on the social
welfare is at its minimum when satisfying the stability constraints of the power
system [11].
2.2.5
Distribution Grid Capacity Market
The grid capacity market is composed by an iterative process where the DSO
starts before the day-ahead market is cleared, by sending the predicted electricity
prices to the aggregators. The aggregators calculate DER consumption patterns
based on predicted data and sends it to the DSO. The DSO evaluates the congestion
of the distribution network with the DER consumption patterns included. If the
DSO predicts congestion issues, the energy price is increased and the aggregators
calculates a new energy plan by minimizing the operational costs of the DERs.
This iterative process continues until the DSO is certain that congestion issues
are not a risk. The ﬁnal energy plans of DERs in the network are posted in the
day-ahead spot market by the aggregators [26, 27]. As was the case for the day-
ahead dynamic tariﬀ, this method minimizes the impacts on social welfare while
alleviating congestion.
2.2.6
Intra-day Shadow Price
In the two former mentioned indirect control methods, the congestion manage-
ment is done in the day-ahead market, where the intra-day shadow price method is
done on the intra-day market. In this method, the aggregators and DSO calculate
optimal energy plans which satisfy congestion constraints by following an iterative
process as in the distribution grid capacity market. This method begins when the
day starts and as the day progresses the aggregators aim to meet their energy plans
by controlling consumption of the DERs. If the real demand of one aggregator is
diﬀerent from its submitted energy plan, the aggregator can utilize the ﬂexible de-
mand of DERs to change the consumption of its subscribed DERs, if excess energy
capacity is available. Therefore new energy plans are calculated for each of the sub-
scribed DERs. The new energy plan is more precise because it is optimized closer
to the point in time of consumption. This method assumes that there are several
aggregators within the distribution network and that they can have diﬀerent needs
of ﬂexible demand. If the energy capacity of an aggregator’s subscribed DERs is
insuﬃcient, the aggregator can exchange ﬂexible demand with other aggregators in
the system through an intra-day market controlled by the DSO. The price of the
exchange in ﬂexible demand is characterized by the shadow price in the hour of
13/146
==PAGE:42==
2.3. Comparison
operation. The shadow price is calculated during the day-ahead market as the cost
of changing the consumption by one unit of power [28]. The social welfare of the
system is marginally aﬀected as it is only the shadow prices and regulation to meet
original energy plans that increases the costs of operation .
2.2.7
Flexibility Service Market
This method of congestion management diﬀers from the former mentioned in-
direct control methods.
Instead of planning the consumption from DERs with
respect to congestion issues in the distribution network, the aggregators plan their
consumption by minimizing the operational costs using the base electricity prices.
In this method, it is the DSO’s job to make sure that the congestion constraints of
the distribution network are satisﬁed. By buying demand reduction from DERs in
the distribution network on a ﬂexible service clearing house (FLECH) market, the
DSO can alleviate congestion. The role of the aggregators is to estimate ﬂexible
demand capable of reducing consumption based on end-user historical data and
interaction. In the FLECH market, the DSO relies heavily on the consumers to
decrease their demand during peak load hours. The DSO buys the demand reduc-
tion from the aggregators by setting a price of the services. As this market can be
characterized as a sellers market, the price depends on the number of DERs capa-
ble of reducing demand in the distribution network. If the number of DERs in the
distribution network is limited the price of congestion management could be very
high, therefore the DSO has to decide whether to reinforce the grid instead [29].
This method aﬀects the social welfare of the system by increasing the operational
costs of the DSO but at the same time it decreases the costs of the consumers.
2.3
Comparison
To summarize on the diﬀerent congestion management methods, the network
solutions have the highest impact on the social welfare, followed by the in-direct
control methods. The solutions aﬀecting the social welfare the least are the direct
control methods. Therefore it is recommended to primarily deal with congestion
issues through direct control non-network congestion management methods.
If
congestion issues are still present in the distribution network, the in-direct control
methods can be utilized. If there still exists a risk of congestion issues, the DSO is
required to use network solutions and reinforce the distribution network to alleviate
congestion [5]. In this thesis, all reconﬁguration and reactive power control actions
are assumed already performed in the distribution network. Therefore, the indirect
control methods are compared to determine the most suitable method of alleviating

==PAGE:43==
2.3. Comparison
congestion.
Either a single or multiple indirect control methods can be used to alleviate
congestion. When looking at the indirect control methods, the usage of the ﬂexible
service market is limited as the method requires a high penetration of DERs in
the distribution network [29]. The intra-day shadow price method can be used to
alleviate congestion when there is a mismatch between predicted and actual con-
sumption from DERs in the system. However, this method requires a number of
aggregators in the system in order to function reliably. Furthermore it is worth
noticing that the Flexibility Service Market and the intra-day shadow price meth-
ods use an iterative communication process to alleviate congestion which is time
consuming [28].
The distribution grid capacity market and day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethods
are similar in their impact on the social welfare. As the latter is a decentralized
congestion management method, there is no iterative communication method be-
tween the DSO and aggregators [25]. This means that the time consumption of
ﬁnding the optimal energy plan of DERs is more comprehensive for the former of
the two methods, and that the latter mentioned method relies less on a reliable
communication channel. Therefore the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is consid-
ered the most promising congestion management method in this project and the
rest of the present thesis is used to evaluate the eﬃciency of this method in terms
of alleviating congestion and satisfying power system stability constraints.

In extension to the congestion management methods described in Chapter 2,
the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is described in further detail in this chapter.
First of all, the concept of DTs is described in terms of function and calculation.
Afterwards the mathematical models used by the DSO and aggregators in the day-

3.1
Dynamic TariﬀConcept
In subsection 2.2.4, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is brieﬂy introduced.
The day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is composed by three participants, a DSO,
aggregators and the day-ahead electricity market. The objective of the DSO in the
day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is to secure a safe operation of the distribution
network when implementing DERs. The safe operation of the distribution network
with DERs implemented is controlled by changing the otherwise constant tariﬀat
diﬀerent load points in the network. This makes the tariﬀdynamic [25].
By changing the DT at diﬀerent load points in the network, the DSO allows
more DERs to be implemented in the system compared to a case where congestion
constraints are not respected.
If DERs are allowed to optimize their consump-
tion without any control from the DSO, the majority of the ﬂexible demand from
DERs will happen in the hour with the lowest electricity price, which can lead to
overloading of power system equipment [25].
Therefore, the DSO determines the risk of congestion and increases the DT
17/146
==PAGE:46==
3.1. Dynamic TariﬀConcept
The Day-ahead Dynamic TariﬀMethod
at load points in hours where congestion is a risk through an optimization model.
The optimization model utilizes predicted consumption from base load demand and
ﬂexible demand to identify congestion issues. By increasing the DT of a certain
hour and network bus, the DERs connected to this bus experience a higher price
of operation in this hour. The increased prices shift part of the ﬂexible demand to
other hours where the cost of operation is lower. Base load demand is not aﬀected
by the DTs in the network which means end-user freedom in terms of electricity
costs of basic household appliances are unchanged when implementing the day-
ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod [25].
The DTs in the distribution network are calculated from the diﬀerence between
the distributed locational marginal prices (DLMP) and the base electricity prices
in each hour as shown in Equation 3.1 [25].
DTt,n = DLMPt,n −ct
(3.1)
The base electricity prices for each hour ct are predicted in the day-ahead dy-

[25].
The objective of the day-ahead electricity market is to determine the base price
of electricity in each hour by calculating the amount of active power production
from generating units in the power system that meets the predicted consumption
in each hour of the next day.
This is called clearing the day-ahead electricity
market [30]. The day-ahead electricity market is cleared at midday before the day
of operation and can be described illustratively using Figure 3.1 [31].

3.1. Dynamic TariﬀConcept
The x-axis in Figure 3.1 is the amount of active power demand and generation
in the system in a single hour of operation. The y-axis is the price per unit of active
power. The purple line in Figure 3.1 represents the demand cost function in the
system. The demand cost function is generated by DSOs and major loads, such
as aggregators, bidding into the day-ahead electricity market. Each participant
determines the highest price of electricity they are willing to pay and how much
power they require and informs the day-ahead electricity market accordingly. The
participant willing to pay the most is used to deﬁne the left most section of the
demand curve. As the price of electricity decreases, more participants are willing
to pay for consuming active power and the accumulated power demand increases.
This method of ranking demand participants is called the merit order, which also
applies to the power generating units [30].
The blue curve in Figure 3.1 represents the generation cost function. Generating
units bid into the day-ahead electricity market by estimating the costs of operation
and the amount of power production they are capable of during the hour of oper-
ation. Generating units that utilize RES such as wind farms and PV power plants
usually have the lowest operational costs and are put in the left most section of the
cost function. As the power production increases, the price of generation increases
as well. The conventional power plants powered by coal and gas has high opera-
tional costs which places them higher up the cost function than the RES generating
units [30].
The intersection between the two cost functions in Figure 3.1 is called the equilib-
rium point which characterizes the amount of power that is generated and consumed
in the power system as well as the price per unit of active power. The electricity
price at the equilibrium point is the price paid to each of the generating units that
are planned to produce power in the power system. The price of electricity is also
the same for all demand units that are willing to consume power after the electric-
ity market is cleared [30]. The electricity price is characterized only by generation
costs and taxes are not included.
Besides the base electricity prices, the DT in Equation 3.1 is found from the
hourly DLMPt,n for each load point in the network [25]. The concept of DLMP in
the distribution network can be characterized as marginal costs of increasing the
consumption at diﬀerent load points in the network which are composed by the
base electricity price and the Lagrange multipliers of operational constraints [32].
The operational constraints are deﬁned by the representation of the power system
used in the optimization model [19].
When optimizing the operation of the distribution network from the DSO per-
spective, the objective is to minimize the costs of operation by solving the optimal
power ﬂow (OPF), while satisfying network stability constraints. These constraints

==PAGE:48==

The Day-ahead Dynamic TariﬀMethod
are deﬁned by the type of OPF used by the DSO optimization method. In power
system operation theory, two types of OPF methods are deﬁned, DCOPF and al-
ternating current OPF (ACOPF). In the DCOPF method, only the active power
ﬂows are considered, in the ACOPF method, both active and reactive power ﬂows
are considered as well as voltage magnitude and angle constraints. The precision
and computation time depends highly on the number of parameters and constraints
considered in the OPF method. More constraints increase the calculation time and
the precision [19].
In the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, the DCOPF method is used in the
optimization model to deﬁne the power system operation because of its fast calcu-
lation time [33]. The Lagrange multipliers of the optimization model are therefore
only based on active power ﬂows in transmission lines. Each transmission line has
a rated active power transfer limit. The Lagrange multiplier of each transmission
line is calculated based on whether the transmission line is operated close to the
active power limit or not [25].
If the loading of a transmission line is very low, the Lagrange multiplier is equal
to zero and the DLMP is equal to the base electricity price. If a transmission line
is loaded close to the active power limit, an increase in line loading can force the
loading limit to be exceeded. The line loading limit must be respected in each
hour and the additional consumption is therefore shifted to another hour where
the base price is higher but line loading is lower. This means that the Lagrange
multiplier will be a positive number and deﬁnes the marginal costs of shifting the
increased consumption to an hour with higher base electricity price [25]. The DTs
in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod can therefore be deﬁned as the Lagrange
multiplier of the line loading constraint and the congestion marginal prices.
As described in subsection 2.2.4, the DSO calculated DT in the network are sent
to the aggregators. The objective of the aggregators in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀ
method is to plan the operation of subscribed DERs while minimizing the opera-
tional costs. The aggregators are assumed rational and they consider the predicted
base electricity prices and posted DTs to plan DER operation when calculating the
energy plans of subscribed DERs based on predicted ﬂexible consumption. When
experiencing non-zero DTs in the network, the aggregators shifts the consumption
of DERs that exceeds line loading limits without knowing the line loading limits
[11].

following steps:
1. Data is predicted:
• The DSO predicts base demand from historical data of residential and
industrial load consumption patterns.
• The DSO predicts hourly base electricity prices from historical data of
the day-ahead electricity market.
• The DSO determines the price sensitivity coeﬃcients from the predicted
generating unit cost function in the day-ahead electricity market.
• The aggregators predict outside temperature from historical data that
aﬀects the operation of HPs in the system.
• The aggregators predict EV driving patterns from historical data.
2. The DSO performs the optimization described in section 3.2 using the pre-
dicted data and information about the distribution network. The distribution
network data needed is the:
• Grid topology used to ﬁnd the PTDF of the network load busses and
transmission lines.
• Loading limits of transmission line within the distribution network.
3. The DSO calculates the DTs of the system from the optimal solution to the
optimization problem using Equation 3.14 and the Lagrange multiplier λ of
the line loading constraint.
4. The calculated DTs are send to the aggregators in the distribution network.
5. The aggregators utilize the predicted data and the calculated DTs to solve
the optimization model shown in section 3.2.
6. From the solution to the optimization problem, the aggregators extract the
energy plans of the subscribed individual DERs, and calculate the combined
energy plan for each bus in each hour of operation.
7. The aggregators post bids into day-ahead electricity market by calculating
the total active power consumption from subscribed DERs in each hour of
operation.
In the ﬁrst step of the algorithm ﬂow, the required data is predicted. In this
thesis it is assumed that each parameter is only predicted by either the DSO or
the aggregators and these predictions are then shared between the participants in
the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod. If each parameter was predicted by both

In extension to the presentation and in-depth description of the day-ahead dy-
namic tariﬀmethod in Chapter 3, this chapter is used to simulate the method with
diﬀerent objectives. The ﬁrst objective is to theoretically show how the day-ahead
dynamic tariﬀmethod works in terms of alleviating congestion within a distribution
network. The second objective is to ﬁnd the theoretical maximal electriﬁcation per-
centage of the northern Rønne distribution network used in this thesis. The third
objective is to identify and describe any limitations that aﬀect the eﬃciency of the
day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod.
The oﬄine testing environment is created in a Matlab and GAMS platform,
where Matlab is responsible for constructing the database ﬁles used to describe
the predicted data in the GAMS optimization software. The GAMS software is
responsible for solving optimization models described in Chapter 3.
The oﬄine platform is started by Matlab, which obtains predicted data based
on historical data as well as grid related data and stores the information within
INC ﬁles visible for the GAMS software. The GAMS software reads the INC ﬁles
and performs the DSO optimization to calculate DTs as described in section 3.2.
After the DTs are calculated in GAMS, the aggregators optimization is performed
to calculate optimal energy plans within the same GAMS script. This means that
the DTs are send directly inside the GAMS software. The results from DSO and
aggregator optimization are send to Matlab through GDX ﬁles.

In the DSO optimization model presented in section 3.2, the line loading con-
straint is described in Equation 3.4 by the rated active power transfer capability
of the transmission lines in the distribution network. In the 10kV distribution net-

factor is the thermal limits of the conducting material [14].
When transmission lines carries current, they produce heat as transmission
losses.
The thermal limit of transmission lines depends on the material and is
used as a representation of how much current the conducting material can carry
before it degrade. Therefore the transmission lines can be characterized by their
rated current. The rated current Ir and the rated voltage level Vr is known by the
DSO, and the rated power transfer capability of transmission lines Pr can be found
through Equation 4.1 [14].
Pr = Ir · Vr
(4.1)

is calculated by the line current limit and the line to line voltage level. The line

Summary and limitations
By analysing the results from the test cases conducted, diﬀerent applications
and limitations of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod become apparent. First of
all, when the penetration level of DERs in the distribution network is increased,
the costs of operating the DERs increases due to an increased DT at diﬀerent load
points. At a low penetration level, the costs of operating DERs are only slightly
increased, which means that the social welfare of the system is decreased slightly as
seen in Figure 4.3. If the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is not used to alleviate
congestion, the lines would become overloaded and the eﬀects on end-users would
be more critical than the decreased social welfare.
At a certain penetration level, the transmission lines are loaded enough to force
HPs to change their consumption patterns in order to store energy up to an hour
of high DT as seen in Table 4.3 and Figure 4.9. At high penetration levels, the cost
of operating the DERs increase greatly. With a high penetration level, the day-
ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod notably decreases the social welfare in the system.
However, while increasing the costs of operation, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀ
method ensures that no transmission lines are overloaded according to the results
from oﬄine environment testing. At these high penetration levels, the DSO has to
determine whether reinforcing the distribution network would increase the social
welfare compared to using the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod for alleviating
congestion.
From the line loading results in the 25% DER penetration case, line L5 is oper-
ated at rated active power transfer level in hour 4 where the base electricity price is
lowest as seen in Figure 4.2. This means that the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
optimizes the DER operation to be as high as possible in an hour with lowest price.
From an environment perspective, this can be utilized to consume more energy
from RES as when wind is blowing hard, the prices decrease in the hour with most
wind [30].
This means that the consumption from DERs increase in this hour.
Therefore, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod helps consuming cheap and envi-
ronmental friendly energy from wind farms and less wind power is exported which
increases the social welfare in the system.
From a congestion point of view, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is able to
alleviate congestion when electrifying up till 79% of the residents in the northern
Rønne distribution network tested in this thesis. Beyond this level of penetration,
the DSO is required to utilize other congestion management methods to alleviate
congestion explained in Chapter 2. One way of allowing additional DERs in the
distribution network is to reinforce the distribution network.
From a planning
point of view, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod can be used to identify which

and their accuracy when evaluating the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀ.
Therefore the following chapter focuses on how the predicted data presented in
section 4.1 can aﬀect the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, and how
to make sure aggregators follows posted energy plans when subject to inaccurate
predictions.

Chapter 5
Inaccurate Predictions
In order to make the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod function eﬃciently, the
DSO and aggregators are required to accurately predict base prices, price sensitivity
coeﬃcients, base demand and DER operation. In predicting DER operation, the
HPs depend on the accuracy of outside temperature predictions utilizing the HP
model described in section B.2 in the appendix and the EVs in the system depends
on end-user driving patterns described in section B.1 in the appendix.
In the
following, a description of each of the predicted values and the consequences of
inaccurate predictions is described.
An imperfect prediction of the base prices from the day-ahead electricity market
leads to diﬀerent operational costs of operating DERs in the system which depends
on the degree of the inaccuracy. If prices are predicted to be very low in an hour
during the next day but in fact are substantially higher, the actual price paid by
the end-user is higher and the social welfare in the system is decreased. If prices
are predicted greater than in reality, the costs of operating DERs decreases.
As the price sensitivity coeﬃcients are part of the quadratic term in the objective
functions for DSO and aggregator optimization, an inaccurate prediction aﬀects the
costs of operating the system. A predicted value lower than the actual value means
that the cost of operating DERs increases and vice versa.
If the base demand is badly predicted, the overall capacity in the transmission
lines left for DER operation is changed. The impact of an imprecise base demand
forecast can be described from Figure 4.5 where a higher base demand in hour 4
means that more power consumption from DERs connected to bus B5 has to be
shifted away from this hour. To shift more power away from hour 4, the DSO
has to further increase the DT for this hour and bus. A lower base demand than

By predicting the outside temperature during the next day, the HP operation
is determined. The HPs in the network consume more power if the temperature is

impact of imperfect temperature forecasts can be described from Figure 4.6 where

If the inside

that the energy plan posted by the aggregators can be violated and the actual
consumption can be diﬀerent from the planned consumption.
As in the case of imperfect outside temperature predictions, the predicted op-

the stability of the distribution network.
In predicting EV operation, diﬀerent
forms of end-user behaviour can implicate the consumption and availability of EVs.
End-user behaviour aﬀecting the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
includes forgetting to connect the EV when parked at home, arrival and departure
hours on diﬀerent days and changes in the distance driven in the day. The irrational
behaviour of end-users aﬀects the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
which becomes apparent when looking at Figure 4.7. If the EV is unavailable in the
hour where it is planned to charge, the EV must consume power later during the
day in order to satisfy end-user constraints. If the EV discharges more during the
day as a result of longer driving distance, the EV is required to charge additional
power from the grid compared to the energy plan. If the end-user drives less during
a day, the EV battery SOC could reach its maximum SOC level and limit further
consumption. All these factors aﬀect how well the EVs can follow the energy plan
posted by the aggregator.

the ﬂexibility and energy capacity of the DERs at each load point as well as their
individual energy plan calculated using the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod. The

5.1
Dealing with imperfect DER operational predic-
tions
In Chapter 2, the Intra-day shadow price congestion management method is
described. This method could be used to change the energy plan of DERs during
the day to ensure satisfaction of the energy plan at each load point. However, the
market controlled by the DSO uses a centralized algorithm where the aggregators
will propose a new optimal energy plan which alleviates the mismatch and the DSO
evaluates the new energy plans taking line loading constraints into consideration.
The DSO then informs the aggregators about congestion issues by increasing elec-
tricity prices. This iterative algorithm continues until the mismatch is minimized
and congestion constraints are satisﬁed [28]. If this method is implemented in the
day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, the beneﬁts of the decentralized method are an-
nulled as two way communication between aggregator and DSO is required in the
intra-day shadow price method.
Therefore a new method of alleviating mismatch between posted energy plan
and actual consumption in distribution network busses is proposed in this thesis.
The new method utilizes MPC to minimize the mismatch. MPC is a optimization
method that tracks the diﬀerence between a plan created in advance and a forecast
of actual values performed close to the hour of operation. A MPC model predicts
the mismatch and optimizes control actions that can balance the mismatch dur-
ing the prediction horizon of the model. In MPC models, the control actions are
limited to time periods within the modiﬁcation horizon. In short, a MPC model
investigates the mismatch in the prediction horizon and performs control actions
in the modiﬁcation horizon to even out the mismatch [38].

with the modiﬁcation of the EV charging implemented as the sum of the individual
energy plan and the change in EV consumption. Equation 5.7 can be interpreted
as a modiﬁcation of Equation 3.11 where the consumption from HPs is substituted
by the individual HP energy plan and change in consumption in each hour.
The MPC model is implemented in GAMS. The MPC model is intended to run
right before each hour of operation during next day where the energy plans are
calculated using the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod. The algorithm ﬂow of the
MPC model is shown in Figure 5.1.
The MPC model is initialized before each hour of operation by the aggrega-
tors which load the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod results and sets the load
point energy plans as the trajectories desired to follow.
After the energy plans
are loaded, the aggregators deﬁne the prediction and modiﬁcation horizon for the
control actions performed by the MPC model. For the proposed MPC model, the
modiﬁcation horizon is chosen as 8 hours which means that the MPC model can
control the change in DER consumption during the hour of operation and the fol-

in hours within the modiﬁcation horizon. By revealing the prediction errors in the

each bus. The parameters aﬀecting DER operation are predicted closer to the hour
of operation, and therefore more precise compared to the predicted values from the
day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod which are performed half a day or more before
the hour of operation.
The new temperature prediction is done 8 hours in advance which means that
any inaccuracy will be visible within the modiﬁcation horizon of the MPC model.
For EV operation, the visibility of new predictions of driving distance and avail-
ability depends on the hour of which the inaccurate prediction is present. If the
inaccuracy is happening within the modiﬁcation horizon set by the MPC model
before the EV leaves in the morning, the inaccuracy is assumed visible inside the
modiﬁcation horizon. If the inaccuracy is predicted after the EV leaves home, the
prediction is assumed visible when at least one hour of EV availability is present
inside the modiﬁcation horizon. This assumption is included to ensure that the
MPC model will have a feasible solution when including the next day SOC level
constraint in Equation 3.7. In this formulation of the MPC model, the inaccurate
predictions are made visible for the aggregators as described to give a theoretical
example of how the method can be formulated and used to solve the problem of
inaccurate predictions, in reality the aggregator might realize the change in EV
consumption at a diﬀerent moment in time than assumed here.
As shown in Figure 5.1, when all required data are obtained, the MPC optimiza-
tion model is solved and the results are interpreted. The MPC model returns the
energy capacity parameters of the DERs deﬁned as the SOC of the EV batteries
and the inside air and structure temperature of the houses in the system as well as
the control actions performed in the modiﬁcation horizon which are added to the
originally posted energy plan and results in a new energy plan for each DER. The
results regarding stored energy in DERs are used as initial values for the following
hour of operation. In this way, the MPC model becomes aware of past control
actions which aﬀects the operation of DERs in the system.
The new energy plans calculated from the MPC model results deﬁne the oper-
ation of each DER in the system in the modiﬁcation horizon and are utilized as
the new trajectory that is desired to follow by the MPC model. By calculating the
combined consumption from DERs at each load point from the new energy plans,
the aggregator can investigate whether a mismatch between the original and new
energy plans is present. A mismatch can be present if there is insuﬃcient energy
capacity available in the modiﬁcation horizon to balance the mismatch.
If a mismatch between the posted and the new energy plan is present at a
load point during the hour of operation, the aggregator informs the DSO about
the mismatch. A mismatch means that the DSO is required to buy up- or down-
regulating power at the regulatory market which can be expensive depending on
55/146
==PAGE:84==
5.3. Simulations and Results
the nature of the regulation needed [30]. Besides regulating the required power
in the distribution network, the DSO needs to investigate whether the mismatch
results in congestion within the distribution network.
The DSO compares the new consumption level at each load point to the distribu-
tion network loading limits. If lines are overloaded, the DSO is required to perform
regulating actions which prevents the congestion. These regulation actions can be
load or production shedding of base load and DGs, respectively, or the release of
stored energy in batteries if present.
As a mismatch between the posted and the new energy plan results in control
actions taken by the DSO, the price of operating the distribution network increases.
These additional costs are thought distributed to aggregators responsible for the
mismatch.
The reason why the aggregators have to cover the expense of DSO
regulation is that in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, they are responsible for
predicting the operation of subscribed DERs. If the mismatch is a result of end-
user irrational behaviour, the aggregator can punish the end-user for not informing
the aggregator about their expected consumption. From a economic perspective,
the precise prediction of DER operation is important for all participants in the
day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, which motivates the participants to give precise
information that helps predicting the operation of DERs.
A way of increasing
the accuracy of EVs driving and availability could be by allowing EV end-users
to inform its aggregator about changes in EV utilization. If the EV owner knows
that on one day he or she will arrive home later than normal, the aggregator can
be informed that the availability will be diﬀerent than normally. In this way, the
aggregator is able to predict end-user behaviour better with the help of end-users.
In order to allow end-users to help aggregators in predicting EV consumption, a
communication platform has to be established between the two participants. Such a
platform could be proposed as a website where end-users inform aggregators about
expected driving patterns.
After the new initial values are stored, the MPC model waits until the next hour
of operation before repeating the steps in Figure 5.1. In the following section, the
MPC model is tested with diﬀerent prediction errors.

Summary and limitations of MPC simulations
The results from testing the eﬃciency of the MPC model show that when the
temperature is higher than predicted, the MPC model is capable of distributing the
excess power consumption to EVs as long as they do not reach their SOC capacity
limit as shown in Figure 5.3. The MPC model was able to balance a mismatch in
the morning hours where the actual consumption was up to 28.2 kW lower than the
planned consumption. When the temperature is lower than predicted, the MPC
model tries to balance the mismatch by distributing HP consumption to hours
before the inaccurate prediction as shown in Figure 5.5. In this way, the maximum
mismatch during the day is lower than in a situation where the MPC model is not
used. It is important to notice that all the test cases used to show the eﬃciency of
the MPC model are theoretical. In the inaccurate temperature prediction cases, the
temperature prediction error is assumed visible and accurate 8 hours in advance.
This might not be the case in reality but shows how the MPC model can utilize HP
and EV energy storage capability to even out possible mismatches due to inaccurate
predictions.

RTDS. The RTDS is a powerful hardware tool capable of calculating power system
parameters in real time. When simulating the network in real time, the digital

real time, 1 hour of simulation takes 1 hour to calculate [40].

6.1
Distribution Network model in RSCAD
The implementation of the MV distribution network of Rønne is done in this
thesis by modelling transmission lines, external grid, transformers and loads. Im-
plementing transmission lines is done through pi equivalent circuits, as transmis-
sion lines shorter than 15 km cannot be implemented as transmission line models
in RSCAD [40]. The pi equivalent circuit representation of transmission lines is
widely used in power system studies and is valid when the system is balanced and
the phase voltages and currents are shifted by ±120°, which is the case in this
thesis as only steady state operation of the distribution network is considered [19].
Real transmission lines can be characterized by distributed resistances in series
with inductances and in parallel with a shunt capacitances per unit length.
In
the pi equivalent circuit these parameters are lumped over the entire length of the
transmission line which represents each transmission line by a single resistance R,
a single inductance XL and a shunt capacitance XC/2 at each terminal.
In the distribution network model in [6], each connection between buses is rep-
resented by one or more transmission lines in series. If all these transmission lines
are implemented in the RSCAD software, the number of electrical nodes in the
network would be several hundreds. In RSCAD, there is a limit of 72 electrical
nodes that can be implemented in a subsystem [41]. By modelling all transmission
lines as pi equivalent circuits, the RSCAD model is limited to a single subsystem,
which means the implementation of the Rønne distribution network is limited to
only 72 nodes.

the connections between buses represented by more than 1 transmission lines is
reduced through the Kron reduction method. In power system ﬂow studies, the

The real time digital testing platform created in this thesis contains a distribu-

and a GAMS script.
The OPC server, DSO client and aggregator clients are all implemented on a
single computer, but all communication between clients is done through the OPC
server to emulate how the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀwould work if it was imple-
mented in a real power system. Ideally, the DSO and each aggregator should be
implemented on separate computers to emulate the implementation of the day-
ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in a real power system. However, as the day-ahead
dynamic tariﬀmethod is a decentralized method, only one way communication is
performed and the usage of a single computer does not corrupt the results. The
communication links inside the real time digital testing platform are shown in Fig-

6.2.1
Open Platform Communication Server
In Figure 6.5 the OPC server is placed in the middle of the digital real time
testing platform. The task of the OPC server is to enable and allow communication
of data between clients in the testing platform. An OPC server is a powerful tool
capable of communicating across diﬀerent protocols. The OPC standard on which
the server is based is used in various industries to enable transfer of data between
diﬀerent devices and databases [43]. As shown in Figure 6.5, the OPC server can
translate the OPC protocol data from the DSO and aggregators into the DNP3
protocol and vice versa.
The communication between the clients across the OPC server is enabled by
utilizing the MatrikonOPC software.
The software package includes the server
itself and an explorer tool which can be used to monitor the server data. The Ma-
trikonOPC software assigns data to server tags. Each server tag represent a certain
data object and its value. When a device has to read or write a value to a data
object, it connects to the server and speciﬁes the required server tag in order to
assure that the data is stored in the correct location [7]. The RTDS client in this
project communicates with the OPC server by receiving power consumption data
assigned to control signals of the loads as shown in Figure 6.4. The communica-
tion of power consumption data requires certain server tags, these server tags are
explained in depth in subsection 6.2.4.
The MatrikonOPC DNP3 server is created on the computer and connected to
the RTDS through the method described in [44].
First step in the creation of
the OPC communication server is to deﬁne a new server conﬁguration. The new
server is conﬁgured as a network channel named "RTDS Network Channel" using
the UDH protocol. The UDH protocol is part of the internet protocol suite and
allows communication to and from the OPC server through the internet [44].
Next, a network host is created in the network channel.
The network host
is named "RTDS Network Host" and is connected to a RTDS rack containing a
GTNETx2 card by deﬁning the host IP address as the IP address of the GTNETx2
card [44]. A GTNETx2 card can be implemented in a RTDS rack which enables the
communication to and from the RTDS rack across the internet [41]. The network
host is further deﬁned by a port number which speciﬁes the internet connection
between the RTDS and the OPC server. For DNP3 communication, port 20000 is
registered as the common communication port [44].
By deﬁning the DNP3 communication port for the network host, a DNP3 unit
can be deﬁned on the network host. The DNP3 unit is named "RTDS DNP 3". By
following these steps described in [44], a communication link across the internet is
established between the OPC server and the RTDS.

the OPC server and allows reading and writing of the OPC server from the Matlab
session [45].
6.2.2
Distribution System Operator
The DSO is implemented on the computer and represented by a Matlab and a
GAMS software session. The purpose of the DSO client in the real time digital
testing platform is to calculate the DTs in the distribution network and inform
the aggregators about these DTs. The DSO predicts price and base load demand
data and obtains DER operational data from databases as shown in Figure 6.1.
These data are required in order to run the optimization model as explained in
section 3.3. The predicted data, distribution network topology and line loading

network are deﬁned in Matlab and forwarded to the GAMS session in INC-ﬁles.
After storing data in GAMS INC-ﬁles, the DSO solves the optimization problem
deﬁned in Equation 3.2 and Equations (3.4) to (3.13) using the GAMS session. After
an optimal solution to the optimization problem is found, the DSO calculates the
DTs in GAMS and sends the DTs to the Matlab session through a GDX ﬁle.
In Matlab, the DSO client connects to the OPC server and stores the DTs on

transmission lines and transformers [46].
The active power losses in transmission lines are called copper losses and are
referred to as I2R losses, where I is the total current lagging the voltage a shown
in Figure 6.8. As the current increases, the active power losses in transmission
lines increases by the square of the current.
In transformers, the active power
losses comes from core losses as well as copper losses in the transformer windings.
The core losses are made up by eddy current losses and hysteresis losses in the
transformer core that comes from magnetically induced currents in the core and
less than perfect permeability of the core material respectively [46]. In hour 4 the
active power losses in the network are 35.9kW and 37.6kW for the network with
and without the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod implemented, respectively. As
the network with the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod deactivated imports and
consumes more power in hour 4, more current ﬂows in the system, which results in
higher active power losses.

6.3. Simulations and Results
6.3.4
Summary and Limitations
In the online environment testing done through the utilization of the real time
digital testing platform, additional limitations of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀ
method becomes apparent. First of all, the data transfer of calculated DTs is sub-
ject to rounding errors. These rounding errors aﬀect the aggregator calculation of
the DER energy plans as the DTs are calculated with a very high precision and even
a small error can aﬀect the aggregators optimal solution as shown in Figure 6.13.
Besides the technical limitation of the real time digital testing platform, power
system stability issues are apparent from the online environment testing. The three
power system stability parameters investigated in this thesis are the line loading,
voltage magnitudes at buses and transformer loadings.
From all three test cases, at least one line is overloaded in at least one hour
of operation even with the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod activated to alleviate
congestion. This means that the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
is somewhat lowered as it is unable to alleviate congestion in a real power system.
The reason why the lines are overloaded is that the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
calculates the DTs using a DCOPF representation of the distribution network and
therefore does not consider losses in power system equipment and the increased
current in the lines due to transfer of reactive power in the system.
In order to solve these problems, diﬀerent approaches are considered. First of
all, an easy solution to the problem would be to decrease the active power transfer
limits in the DSO optimization shown in Table 4.1 for the Rønne grid by a certain
percentage that allows reactive power ﬂows and transfer losses in the cables. The
percentage should be determined by investigating the power factor of loads in the
network and thereby determine the absolute current in the network.
By decreasing the overall power transfer capacity of all transmission lines in the
network by a certain percentage, the theoretical maximum implementation level of
DERs in the network is decreased and the price of operating DERs increases as less
DERs can consume power in the hour where the base price is lowest. By decreasing
the power transfer limit of transmission lines when using the day-ahead dynamic
tariﬀmethod, the inaccurate predictions has less eﬀect on the power system stability
which helps the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod.
A second solution could be to calculate the DTs while including reactive power
ﬂows. This could be done by using an ACOPF power ﬂow representation of the
distribution network instead of a DCOPF representation. This would increase the
calculation time of the DSO in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod as more power
system parameters would be included as variables.
107/146
==PAGE:136==
6.3. Simulations and Results
A third solution could be to investigate reactive power control that can help
the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod eﬃciency as introduced in Chapter 2. By
performing reactive power control in the system through shunt capacitors or DER
power electronics, the transfer of reactive power in the distribution network is
decreased. By decreasing the reactive power ﬂow in the system, the current in the
lines decreases which decreases both active and reactive power losses in the system.
Besides the line loading limitation, the voltage magnitude of distribution net-
work buses are investigated in this thesis to evaluate the voltage stability of the
distribution network. From all the three test cases, the voltage magnitude of all
distribution network buses is decreased but all of them are satisfying the voltage
stability constraint when the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod activated. Without
the congestion management method activated, bus B5 is operated at a lower voltage
magnitude than the voltage stability limit when the network is highly penetrated
by DERs.
The last power system stability parameter investigated in this thesis is the load-
ing of the transformers. In the test case with 25% penetration of DERs, none of
the transformers are overloaded. However, when increasing the DER penetration to
50% and 75%, some of the transformers are overloaded in both scenarios with and
without the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod activated. In the 50% penetration
of DERs test case, the transformer at bus B25 is the limiting factor and not the
active power transfer capacity of transmission line L26. In order to deal with this
problem, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod could include transformer loading
in the DSO optimization model.
Besides showing the limitations in power system stability parameters and trans-
fer of data across the OPC server in the real time digital testing platform, the
online simulations shows the importance of recognizing other power system param-
eters in the formulation of the congestion management method. In the distribution
network implemented in the real time digital testing platform, the reactive power
ﬂows aﬀects the results already with a penetration level of only 25% DERs. As the
penetration level increases the transformers rating of apparent power can become
the limiting factor. Furthermore, with a high level of penetration of DERs in the
network, the active and reactive power losses have a high impact on the loading of
the cables in the network.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this thesis, the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod developed
in [11] is evaluated by testing the congestion management method in both oﬄine
and online testing environments. In the evaluation of the method, a number of
limitations and assumptions have been included. In this chapter, some of these
limitations and assumptions are addressed and their impact on the results are
discussed.
In this thesis, the distribution network is only simulated in a steady state situ-
ation with balanced three phase loads. If fault scenarios where tested, i.e. loss of
transmission lines or reduced capacity of transformers, the calculated energy plans
could potentially not be followed as the distribution network data are changed in
fault situations. In extend to the fault scenarios, only over- and undervoltages are
investigated. If faults happens in the network, transients could aﬀect the voltage
and current in the network, which further implicates the use of the day-ahead dy-
namic tariﬀmethod, as DERs are encouraged to increase the line loading up to the
limit. If a fault happens on a heavily loaded line, many end-users are aﬀected.
In this thesis the distribution of DERs is assumed uniform among the load
connection points in the network.
In the oﬄine tests, the line L5 limited the
implementation of DERs to 79%. Additional DERs could be implemented in the
other network buses without causing infeasible solutions to the optimization model
of the DSO.
In the online simulations, the recognition of reactive power ﬂows and the result-
ing inductive current overload the transmission lines. In this thesis, any reactive
power control and reconﬁguration in the network are assumed already performed.
If the network was tested with reactive power control capable of changing the power
109/146
==PAGE:138==
Discussion
factor of the loads to become closer to unity, less reactive power would ﬂow in the
network, and overloading of transmission lines could potentially be avoided.
A
power factor closer to unity would also decrease the loading of the transformers in
the network.
In the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod, all aggregators are assumed rational.
This means that they are modelled to calculate DER energy plans that minimize
the cost of operation. If irrational aggregators were implemented, the eﬃciency of
the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod would decrease as it relies on aggregators to
follow DSO calculated DTs to alleviate congestion within the distribution network.
The predicted data used in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod are assumed
predicted by only one participant. If both the DSO and aggregators did the pre-
dictions, there is a possibility of unequal predicted data which aﬀects the optimal
solutions of the two participants to become diﬀerent.
The northern Rønne distribution network is assumed connected to a strong
external grid capable of maintaining constant power by delivering the required
power that matches the demand throughout the day. If the external grid is weak,
the system frequency in the distribution network could be aﬀected as the demand
changes throughout the day.
In the distribution network, all loads are assumed residential to emulate a worst
case scenario as only residential units utilizes both HPs and EVs. If industrial loads
were implemented, the base load demand curve would be diﬀerent which could aﬀect
the implementation of DERs in the network.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The need of an eﬃcient congestion management method that safely allows im-
plementation of a large number of DERs in the distribution network is apparent
as heating and transportation services are predicted to be electriﬁed in the future.
The day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod that can activate the distribution network
is evaluated in this thesis in terms of its congestion management capability and its
eﬀects on the secure operation the future distribution network in the northern part
of Rønne.
The day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is initially tested in an oﬄine testing en-
vironment where only active power transfer capability of the distribution network
is considered as the power system constraint. In this thesis, the grid related data
of the northern Rønne distribution network is implemented together with a rep-
resentation of predicted DER operation due to outside temperature, EV driving
distance and EV availability.
The oﬄine tests show that the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod allows a uniform
distributed implementation of up to 79% DERs in the network. From the oﬄine
tests, the social welfare of the system decreases slightly when the DER penetration
level doesn’t causes changes in HP optimal operation plans. When the implemented
DERs exceed a certain penetration level, the DERs’ cost of operation increases
heavily as HPs operation is changed due to congestion in peak base load demand
hours. In this thesis, the high increase in DERs’ operational cost happen when
having a DER penetration level of 75% and 79%.
From a planning perspective, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod can be tested
in an oﬄine environment to identify bottlenecks in the distribution network when
increasing the penetration of DERs in the network. This means the distribution
111/146
==PAGE:140==
Conclusion
network planners can analyse diﬀerent load forecast scenarios and determine where
to reinforce the distribution network in order to allow further implementation of
DERs in the distribution network. In this thesis, oﬄine simulation results shows
that bus B5 in the northern Rønne distribution network is the limiting transmission
line as it is the only line being operated at maximum in almost all of the day when
having a penetration level of 79% DERs in the network.
The oﬄine tests with a low penetration level of DERs shows that most of the
EV consumption happens in the hours with the lowest base electricity prices. As
RES electricity is much cheaper than electricity from fossil fuels, the day-ahead
dynamic tariﬀcan increase the social welfare by planning DER consumption when
high RES electricity is produced. This means the power system can export less of
the cheap electricity and produce less fossil fuel based electricity.
In the oﬄine tests conducted in this thesis, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
is found to heavily depend on accurate predictions of DER operation. In this thesis,
a new method utilizing MPC is proposed which can help aggregators to follow cal-
culated energy plans by utilizing additional energy capacity in the distribution net-
work when inaccurate predictions are used in the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod.
From testing the proposed MPC method, higher than predicted outside tem-
perature is dealt with as EVs can consume the excess HP power. With a lower
outside temperature than predicted, the MPC model has diﬃculties as EVs cannot
decrease consumption in hours where charging is not planned. For the test with a
shorter driving distance, the MPC model allows a storage of excess energy in both
house air, structure and EV batteries. With a longer driving distance, the MPC
model controls the HPs to change their operation which allows EVs to increase
their charging to cover additional consumption from driving. The last test case of
the MPC model with inaccurate EV availability, the MPC model recognizes the
inaccurate predictions and changes the EVs consumption plans by controlling the
HPs consumption.
For the higher than predicted outside temperature test, longer than predicted
driving distance and earlier departure time of EVs test, the MPC model is found
incapable of completely balance the mismatch. However, the MPC model shows
promise as it satisﬁes end-user constraints and decreases the mismatch when inac-
curate predictions are present.
Besides the oﬄine testing performed in this thesis, a real time digital simula-
tion platform is created with the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod implemented
which allows testing of the method in an emulation of a real distribution network.
The online tests are conducted and the results are analysed in terms of line and
transformer loading as well as under- or overvoltages at distribution network buses.

Conclusion
Online simulation results show that the transfer of data across the OPC server
and between software sessions in the real time digital testing platform causes a
rounding error in the precisely calculated DTs, which forces the DSO and aggregator
optimal solutions to be diﬀerent.
From the online tests, the eﬃciency of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
is aﬀected by the calculation of DTs through a DCOPF representation of the dis-
tribution network. As power losses and reactive power ﬂows in the network are
ignored in calculating the DTs, the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod is unable to
alleviate congestion as lines are overloaded due to inductive currents transferring
the reactive power in the network. When increasing the DER penetration percent-
age, simulation results show that transmission losses in the distribution network
increases and therefore have a higher aﬀect on the loading of the transmission lines
in the network.
The transformers in the distribution network are only designed to cover base
load demand. Results from online simulations in this thesis show that additional
consumption from DERs forces the transformers to overload when the DER pene-
tration level causes maximum active power transfer in the transmission lines. As
DERs are controlled to consume power when the price is lowest, high loading of
the transformers happens in the hours with the lowest price. Not all transformers
in the network are overloaded. In feeder 2, the transformers are designed with a
apparent power rating that exceeds the active power transfer in the transmission
lines, which means these transformers are not overloaded when using the day-ahead
dynamic tariﬀmethod.
The voltage magnitude of all buses in all the online test cases performed in this
thesis is within the voltage stability limit of the rated voltage ±10%. Results from
simulating the real time digital testing platform show that voltage instability is not
an issue when implementing the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in the northern
Rønne distribution network. However, results show that without the congestion
management method implemented, the LV buses in the distribution network can
violate the voltage stability constraint.
By summarizing simulation results, this thesis concludes that the eﬃciency of
the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in its current formulation as a congestion
management method is concluded very low. The use of a DCOPF formulated opti-
mization model ignores transmission losses, reactive power ﬂows and transformers’
MVA rating, which causes transmission lines and transformers to overload when
implementing the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod in an emulation of a real power
system. The eﬃciency of the method could be increased by utilizing ACOPF rep-
resentation of the network instead of DCOPF in the optimization model, or by
implementing reactive power control that can control load power factor at unity. If

the DSO wishes to use a DCOPF formulation of the optimization model, the load-
ing of transmission lines could be decreased by a certain percentage margin to allow
reactive power ﬂows as well as transmission losses. If the DSO keeps the DCOPF
method, it is very important that the MVA rating of transformers in the network is
evaluated to determine whether additional constraints should be included to avoid
overloading the transformers.
8.1
Future Work
Through this thesis, the following subjects of future work are identiﬁed as in-
teresting research topics:
• Further testing of the MPC model eﬃciency in terms of controlling aggrega-
tors to follow the calculated energy plans when subject to inaccurate predic-
tions. It would be interesting to see how V2G technology can increase the
eﬃciency of the proposed MPC model.
• An economical and technical investigation of the required reactive power con-
trol in the distribution network that can decrease the reactive power ﬂow in
the network and thereby decrease the line loading in the transmission lines.
• An eﬃciency comparison between an ACOPF formulated day-ahead dynamic
tariﬀmethod and a DCOPF formulated method with transformer loading
constraints and a percentage decrease in active power transfer capability used
in the optimization model.

B.2
HP data
In section 3.2 the operation constraints of the HP are presented. The objective
of the HP in a household is to keep the inside temperature between an upper and
a lower temperature limit. The HP functions by turning on and oﬀbased on the
temperature inside the house. Normally the HP is only controlled by the inside
temperature and increases the temperature until it reaches the upper limit. After
which the HP is turned oﬀand the temperature decreases to the lower limit which
causes the HP to turn back on.
In order to model the HP operation in heating the inside air of the house,
characteristic data of the house and the HP as well as a prediction of meteorological
data are required. The modelling of the HP operation is done by analysing the heat
ﬂows within a house, as shown in Figure B.2.
Figure B.2: Heat ﬂows aﬀecting the inside air temperature of the house.
In Figure B.2 the air inside the house is shown as having a heat gain from the
HP, and heat losses from conduction through walls and roof to the outside air and
convection to the structure of the house. Heat conduction is the rate of heat that
goes through an element such as a wall that separates two mediums with diﬀerent
temperatures. In this project the heat is assumed to be conducted through the
house walls and roof only. Heat convection is the transfer of heat between a surface
and a ﬂuid or gas such as the intersection between a house wall and the air inside
the house [13]. In a real house there are additional heat ﬂows such as heat gains
from solar irradiation through windows and heat losses through the ventilation

Appendix C
Oﬄine Simulation Results with
50% DER Penetration
In this case study, the penetration level of DERs in the distribution network is set
to 50%, which means half of the houses connected to each bus have electriﬁed both
heating and transportation services. The number of houses electriﬁed in this case
scenario is equal to 2370, which means 2370 EVs and 2370 HPs are implemented
in the distribution network.
By applying the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod
to alleviate congestion and simulate the system, the objective value of the DSO
optimization problem is found as 13,614 DKK. By comparing the objective value
of the DSO to the objective value of the aggregators, the eﬀect of the DT can be
found. For aggregator 1, the objective value is found through simulation as 9,070.7
DKK. The objective value of aggregator 2 is found as 4,630.6 DKK. The objective
values found for each aggregator are summed and compared to the objective value
of the DSO optimization. The total diﬀerence in objective value is 86.9 DKK. The
diﬀerence means that the use of the day-ahead dynamic tariﬀmethod to alleviate
congestion increases the total price of operating DERs in the system by only 86.9
DKK.
The eﬀect of the DTs in the distribution network can be seen in Table C.1 where
the base prices and the DTs are shown for busses B1 to B14. For all other busses,
the DT is found equal to zero as no lines are congested.

between B1 to B14. For bus B5 and B6, the DT is increased in hours between
hour 1 and hour 6 and in hour 24. From Table C.1, the DLMP at busses B5 and
B6 is nearly constant to alleviate congestion on the lines of feeder 2. As seen in the
increased costs of operating DERs in the system due to the increased congestion
