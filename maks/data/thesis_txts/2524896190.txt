Abstract  
The identification of the modal parameters of structural systems using the responses under 
operational or forced excitations is referred with the term Operational Modal Analysis (OMA), 
The current OMA methods have been developed on the basis of two basic though, restrictive 
assumptions: (i) linearity and (ii) stationarity. However, in most practical cases, the systems 
are altered due to the friction induced nonlinearities, and the robustness and efficiency of the 
OMA methods are questioned. The current study examines the effect of a friction  providing 
mechanism to an experimental set up consisting of a pair of reduced scale (1:100) offshore 
platform models that were constructed for the scope of this study. Numerical simulations were 
also performed in order to reinforce the results of the experimental study, while both time-
domain and frequency-domain methods were used in order to identify the modal 
characteristics. The findings of this study is not to optimize the OMA algorithms, but to reveal 
that the existing OMA algorithms can be successfully employed to identify the dynamic 
characteristics of engineering systems, even though the involved nonlinearities alter the 
assumptions of linearity and stationarity.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Objective 
The identification of the modal properties of a structural system, has been a continuous study 
especially through the last decades. The reason for this, is that this problem is existing 
permanently in structural monitoring, and control in general. In this paper an experimental 
modal testing of off-shore platform models, due to random loading and friction effects is 
presented.   
The basic experimental setup, is consisted of two reduced scale (1:100) off-shore platform 
models. The platforms are one-story structures, composed of a steel rigid diaphragm and four 
square profiled steel columns. Operational Modal Analysis techniques, require the tested 
structures to be excited by a kind of excitation that has broadband characteristics, similar to 
white noise. The structures were excited with random vibrations by a pneumatic system able 
to oscillate the models through compressed air flow. It is important that all the modes of 
interest have to be adequately excited so that their contributions will be able to be captured by 
the measurements, and that the response data have the necessary quality [1]. For that reason, 
each platform was connected with 8 strain gauges and 3 accelerometers. The accelerometers 
are placed in order to measure the responses of the models due to the effects of random 
loading and friction, at a later stage.  The control and the monitoring of the experiments were 
succeeded with the use of NI LabVIEW 2016 software. The obtained data, measured from the 
model responses at the laboratory, are used to estimate the parameters of models that 
describe the dynamic behavior of the system. The identification is performed through time-
domain and frequency-domain methods.  
The results are compared with the analysis of a finite element model in ANSYS software. The 
structural matrices have been extracted, and simulations have been performed. The data have 
been processed using Operational Modal Analysis, and the modal parameters of the structures 
were estimated using time domain and frequency domain techniques.  
After the modal testing of the basic setup, a reduced scale model of a bridge has been placed 
between the platforms, in order to create effects of friction-type damping on the existing 
structural system. Except the bridge itself, a number of different experimental cases were 
examined. The decision of selecting the bridge was not random or coincident. Off shore 
platforms are rarely isolated in the sea. In most cases, two or more platforms are connected in 
real conditions. It is already proved that all linear models have dynamic properties, which can 
be identified with OMA techniques. The same steps of analysis are followed along with the 
introduction of the friction-type damping. The analysis of the new system will enable some 
further conclusions about the question if the same identification technique, can be efficiently 
used for estimation of the modal parameters for systems that are responding slightly non-
linearly (Brincker & Ventura, 2015).

2. Experimental setup for linear experiments 
The basic model that was set for the experiments is consisted by two, one storey, 
steel platforms. Each one of these structures is composed by one steel plate, 
which is supported by four steel columns. The model platforms are designed as 
simplified scaled models of real, existing platforms in a scale of 1:100. The two 
steel diaphragms, have different dimensions. The areas of both diaphragms are 
square, thus the dimension of each side, for the bigger and the smaller platform, 
is 0.4 and 0.3 meters respectively. The thickness of the steel plates is equal to 5 
millimeters, for each of both plates. A top view of the examined platforms is 
illustrated in Figure 2.1 below. 
 
Figure 2.1: Platform models geometry- top view 
To continue with, the columns of the structures are identical, and have a square 
area profile. The side of every column profile, has a dimension of 0.08 meters. All 
the columns are vertical to the steel plates on the top, and the ground at the 
bottom, and share the same height, which is equal to 0.6 meters. The four 
columns at the ground, form a square, 0.2 m by 0.2 m, for the small, and 0,3x0.3 
for the big platform respectively. The whole model, has the same total height, 
including the deck. This can be explained by the fact that the steel plates have 
been drilled so that holes of 6 mm radius exist in the nodes of a 6x6 and a 8x8 
grid, for the big and the small platform respectively. The columns are clamped 
both, in the steel plates on the top, and in the basis of the model setup, which is 
considered as the “ground”. It is needed to be mentioned that for the needs of this 
study, the connection between the slab and the columns was altered, but this is a 
case that will be further described, in another part of this report (Elshafey, 
Haddara, & Marzouk, 2009). 
The distance between the edges of the sides of the two close platforms is kept 
equal to 30 centimeters. The distance was chosen to be such a small distance 
because of the idea to study additional cases of random loading, enhanced with 
mechanisms that would provide different excitation conditions.

Measuring the vibrations and the responses of the platforms due to random 
loading, is a process that needed the appropriate number of seismic 
accelerometers, connected on the structures. In this case, the two platforms were 
instrumented with a total number of 6 accelerometers. The examined system of 
the two platforms needed at least six degrees of freedom to be recorded in order 
be analyzed thoroughly. Additionally, a number of 16 strain gauges were 
mounted in both platforms, in the specific locations on the top of the steel 
columns, and right below the steel diaphragms. This was decided because that 
are shown in Figure 2.2 The position of the accelerometers is indicated in Figure 
2.3. Samples were gained, with a sampling rate of 2560 samples per second for 
the preliminary tests, and 1651,7 samples per second for the bridge experiments. 
The deformation and acceleration data were recorded at the 39 channels using a 
multiple-channel acquisition system converting the data from analog to digital 
format. The software that was used to control the experimental process was NI 
LABView 2016. 16 channels corresponded to the raw data, another 16 to the 
filtered data, 6 channels to the acceleration measurements, and the first channel 
of course indicating the time step. The data were recorded in channels, which are 
included in a set of measurements that cover a time duration of 2 and 10 
minutes, depending on the needs of each test. Measurements from 2 minutes 
duration tests were executed for the preliminary tests, the parametric analysis 
tests and the intensity level configuration, while 10 minutes test were performed 
for the bridge test cases (Brincker, Andersen, Martinez, & Tallavo , 1996).

3. Configuration of test settings 
There is a number of preliminary steps that need to be completed before the performance of 
Operational Modal Analysis: 
1. The data need to be filtered 
2. The data need to be decimated 
Before the performance of modal analysis through the system response measurements, an 
analytical model was developed. The analytical model was constructed for two main reasons. 
The first reason was the necessity of estimating the natural frequencies of the small and the 
big platform for filtering and decimating the data. The data were decimated with a decimating 
factor that was equal to 20, meaning that the data points for each channel were 20 times less. 
So finally the decimated acceleration data for each channel were reduced from 991000 to 
49550. The Nyquist frequency was reduced to 41.3 Hz. The pneumatic system was used to 
excite the model through compressed air. The air was programmed to flow within a frequency 
range from 2 to 20 Hz.  
3.1 Measurements 
The measurements is definitely one of the most important fields of OMA. A complete and 
cautious Operational Modal Analysis would majorly depend on the quality of the obtained 
data. It should be empasized that if this of the whole study would be performed cautiously and 
with providing the necessary quality in each step, then it would be safe to state that all the 
following steps could be relatively easier and more secure. The most important factors that 
should be satisfied in order for good quality data to be obtained are as follows: 
• The tests should have been carefully planned and performed. 
• Acceptable quality signals should have been recorded for the tested model setup. 
3.1.1 Sampling rates 
Sampling rate sets the upper boundary of the frequency band, used for the analysis of the 
received signals. The sampling rate is the number of data samples recorded per unit of time. 
Sampling rate is commonly described and referred as “samples-per-second” (sps), or “sampling 
frequency.” The upper limit of the frequency band is provided by the Nyquist frequency.

3.1.2 Frequency Ranges 
The frequency range for each measurement, is the frequencies that exist between the 
maximum and minimum frequencies that will be analyzed. The choice of the maximum 
frequency is probably the most challenging part of this task. It usually has the greatest impact 
on the total data or system bandwidth and on the various instruments of the measurement 
system. The maximum frequency is usually selected to equal the highest of the following: 
• The maximum significant frequency of the operating response of the structure. 
• A standard frequency for the data acquisition system, for instance, systems for testing of 
large structures are often having a standard Nyquist frequency of 50 or 100 Hz (Brincker & 
Ventura, 2015).

4. Intensity levels configuration 
After the construction of the model platforms, the connection of the necessary strain gauges 
and accelerometers, and the configuration of the experiment handling setup, a parametric 
study was held in the laboratory. The scope of this study was the configuration of a number of 
different intensity levels that would be useful in the next steps of this project. The next aim of 
this study, as it is already referred, was mainly an experimental study after the 
implementation, of a friction type, damping mechanism in the direction of the first and fourth 
degree of freedom. The main concern in this study was to achieve an acceptable value of 
accelerations and deformations of the structures that would be measured at the locations were 
we had introduced the sensors. That was the goal because it was expected that after the 
implementation of the friction providing mechanism, which is the bridge in this case, the 
responses and the deformations would be reduced .An empirical boundary that was set, was to 
achieve from each test, at least, a maximum value of 150 micro-strains for both platforms, and 
accelerations close to 1g. 
The first parameter that was altered through the different tests, were the distance of the 
nozzle hole to the side of the platform that was receiving the compressed air. To continue with, 
the second basic parameter that was tested, was the diameter of the nozzle hole. Another 
parameter that was examined, was the tightening of the bolts at the points were the columns 
of the platforms meet the ¨ground¨ of the setup. The nodes that form the steel plates with the 
steel columns were fully clamped and remained at the same condition, through the series of 
the experiments.  
All the experiments that will be described further in this chapter, were performed with the 
same settings except the parameter that was changed for the needs of each test. The system 
settings that remained stable were: 
 
The sampling rate was set to 2560 samples per second 
 
The frequency range of the of opening the valves was ranged between 2  and 20 Hz 
 
The duration of every test was decided to be equal to 120 minutes. 
4.1 Distance 
The first parameter that was studied, was the distance between the nozzle holes and the 
platforms. Six different distances were set, due to the capability of the constructed system, to 
change the distance easily, without affecting the safety and the quality of the setup. While the 
effects of altering the distance was checked, the nozzle hole diameter was selected to be equal 
to 2 mm.

4.3 Support conditions 
After the completion of examining the results of changing nozzle sizes and distances, a new 
study was decided to take place in the laboratory. An analysis about the change of the modal 
parameters due to the change of stiffness of: a) the nodes between the slab and the columns, 
and b) the nodes with the deck took place. The first question was to decide if the one case was 
about to remain stable and change the conditions in the other, or if it should be a combination 
of both of them. Finally, it was decided that a significant change in the stiffness of the 
connection of the steel plates with the steel columns could be very dangerous in terms of 
human and material safety but also in terms of risking to be out of subject, meaning that the 
assumption, that the rigid diaphragm is fully clamped on the columns would collapse. 
Consequently, only the tightening of the column connections with the ground was analyzed.

4.5 High Configuration 
In the same case study, where the considered as ‘high configuration’ settings were applied, the 
first basic difference, in comparison with the low intensity, are the values in y axis. In the 
high configuration, it is observed that the measured deformations and accelerations, meet the 
requirements that had set before. These requirements, were :  
 
Max deformation should be higher than 150 micro-strains per test for each one of both 
two platforms, recorded at least from one strain gauge.   
 
Record at least 200 micro-strains from at least 1 case that was tested.  
These requirements were set because of the aim of this project, to deal with friction effects. 
The general rule of thumbs that was considered, was to achieve close to 5 times higher 
deformations in the high configuration, than in the lower one. The explanation was, that after 
the implementation of the friction type damper, the system probably could only represent the 
¨sticking¨ and not the ¨sliding¨ type of friction if there would not be achieved such a high level 
of excitation, described in terms of deformations. That means that the bridge that would be 
installed could create a damping condition over the critical one, and the structural system 
probably could not oscillate properly.

5. Non-linear simulations with friction 
A friction mechanism is a damping provider, meaning, that at any given time, it could have two 
possible motion states:  
a) A stick state 
b) A slip state 
 The motion of the damper varies between these two possible states. A friction damper has the 
property of dissipating energy from dynamic loads only when it is the slip state. In terms of 
kinematic conditions, a damping mechanism belongs in the stick state if there is no relative 
motion between the damper and the damping interface, in any other case it belongs in the slip 
state. While being in the stick state, the friction force that generates, is equal to the loading 
force, otherwise it is equal to the constant slip force. The state of the mechanism, is affected by 
the mechanical properties, and also determined by the response of the structural system where 
the damper is placed. Due to the fact that the two states are completely different, the dynamic 
behavior of the systems that contains the damper, is nonlinear.  
A dynamic analysis of a system which contains stationary states is complex. A stick–slip model 
is adopted for the friction elements. Without sophisticated hysteretic equations, these numerical 
methods directly use the stick–slip force condition from Coulomb’s friction theorem to derive 
numerical solutions for structural response.  The state-space approach is a widely used analysis 
tool in the modern theory of dynamics and control (Lu, Chung, Wu, & Lin, 2006). 
An important benefit of selecting a state-space algorithm for non-linear simulations with 
friction, is that the only assumption that is needed to be considered, is an external excitation, 
defined with a stable time-step, and that the damping can be either proportional or modal (Lu, 
Chung, Wu, & Lin, 2006). 
As it is stated the experimental study of the non-linear system would include both the two model 
platforms, connected with the bridge components. However the non-linear simulations, 
regarding the case of this study have been operated concerning the one of the two platforms, 
specifically the large platform enhanced with friction forces at the direction where the degree of 
freedom number 4, was set. The reason for that, was that creating a FE Model with exactly the 
same support conditions in the structural nodes, and the same load cases was a complicated and 
time consuming effort. Additionally, the main aim of these simulations was not to achieve a 
perfect correlation between the experimental models and the numerical simulations, but to test 
if the simulation results, including the identification of the model parameters of the examined 
system would match with the estimated modal parameters of the finite element model for the 
same system. This was an opportunity to test the OMA algorithms in non-linear cases, with the 
responses obtained from the FE model, before analyzing the measurements from the physical 
model. The non-linear simulations, were operated and commented by the author with the 
collaboration of Tobias Friis, PhD student of the section of Structural Engineering, in terms of 
providing the model for the simulations from the FE model outputs. The performance, analysis 
and comments of the simulations, also the Friction force – time representation, and some 
alterations in the plots  were a responsibility of the author.

6. Non-linear experiments 
The dynamic response of a structural system enhanced with friction-type damping providers is 
non-linear. This nonlinear response could be explained by the fact that the friction mechanism 
introduces two types of motion. These states of motion are referred to as stick and slip states. 
The essential force and kinematic conditions of a friction damper, in these two states, are 
different (Lu, Chung, Wu, & Lin, 2006). In this experimental study, a bridge structure, was 
added in the existed linear system of the two model platforms. The stick and slip conditions of 
the friction affected system were introduced experimentally, with the introduction of a number 
of different cases for examination. To create the stick condition, the system was chosen to be 
excited through the lowest possible intensity level from all possible 8, described in chapter 4, 
towards with the addition of the bridge on the decks of the two platforms. For the 
representation of the slip condition, the system was excited with the highest intensity level, 
from those who were described at the same chapter. The main idea behind this configuration, 
was a step by step approach of the two motion states, towards a number of different 
experiments, in terms of system alterations, such as the total weight of the bridge.

The purpose of this study, was the construction of a reduced scale model of offshore platforms, 
to enhance it with friction-type damping mechanism which corresponds in real needs and 
conditions, and to evaluate if the existing OMA algorithms are capable of being used in order 
to estimate the modal parameters of systems that respond i9n the non-elastic regime. The 
experimental cases that were defined, were tested and it was revealed that the OMA 
techniques, are capable of identifying the number of the physical modes that exist when a 
system alters due to the implementation of friction forces, or variation of the existing friction 
forces. Additionally except of the recognition of the number of the modes of oscillation, the 
modal parameters can be identified with a high level of precision The results of the test cases 
that were performed, indicated, that the examined non-linear system while it was strongly 
damped due to friction, it appeared like a linear system. The case of stationarity in 
combination with linearity of the responses, in my perspective, should be handled by setting a 
relatively large duration of the experiments, and a relatively high sampling rate in order for 
the friction states to be recorded thoroughly, and a sufficient number of response data to be 
recorded. In terms of identification and analysis, the relatively large amount of data could be 
balanced with decimating the responses to an acceptable number of data points. An also very 
important factor that influences the experiments, is the experiment conditions. The cables 
should be handled in a way that they would not cause additional damping that actually is not 
caused due to mechanical characteristics of the examined system.
