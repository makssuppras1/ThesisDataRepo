Abstract
Predicting the horizontal stiffness of large timber frames is a critical aspect of early-stage
design and presents a complex challenge. This prediction typically requires finite element
analysis due to numerous interacting parameters and the influence of semi-rigid connec-
tions. This thesis introduces an integrated parametric and machine learning framework
to investigate the horizontal stiffness of these structures and identify key design drivers.
The methodology combines parametric modeling in Grasshopper, using the FEM-Design
API for finite element analysis, to generate a large dataset of frame geometries and their
structural responses to wind loading. This high-dimensional data is then analyzed us-
ing three different regression techniques in Python: linear, nonlinear, and artificial neural
networks, to model the relationships between design parameters and horizontal deflection.
Results show that cross-bracing and the rotational stiffness of column-base connections
are the most influential factors for controlling horizontal deflection. The framework was
successfully validated on a simplified beam case, yielding accurate predictions allowing for
the creation of design tools and a simplified expression. However, the models developed
for the more complex frame showed significant variance and were not sufficiently accurate
to produce generalized design tools.
In conclusion, this study establishes a robust integrated framework that successfully iden-
tifies the primary drivers of horizontal stiffness in large timber frames. While the complex-
ity of the full frame model prevented the creation of additional design tools, the developed
methodology lays a solid foundation for future advancements and can be adapted to other
structural timber challenges, ultimately promoting more efficient and reliable early-phase
design practices.

Introduction
The demand for timber in construction is steadily increasing, particularly in Denmark,
where national regulations are pushing the construction industry toward more sustainable
practices. These regulations aim to limit the environmental impact of new buildings, mea-
sured in CO2-equivalents per square meter of floor area per year, based on the building’s
expected lifespan of 50 years.
In Denmark since early 2023, newly constructed buildings larger than 1000 m2 have
been required to comply with a limit of 12 CO2-eq./m2/yr (Tozan et al., 2023, p. 8).
As of July 1, 2025, this threshold will be reduced to 7.1 and will apply to nearly all new
building types. Further reductions are scheduled, lowering the limit to 6.4 in mid-2027
and 5.8 in mid-2029 (SM, 2024, Tab. 2).
Studies consistently highlight timber’s potential to significantly lower the environmen-
tal footprint of buildings. Research by Træ i Byggeriet (Steffen and Stistrup, 2024), for
instance, investigated replacing steel and cement-based materials with timber in vari-
ous building components. This study revealed that substituting the primary structural
system yielded the largest CO2-eq. reductions, ranging from 7% to 31% (0.3 to 1.9 CO2-
eq./m2/yr) across five out of six considered building types, with industrial buildings show-
ing the most substantial decrease. The study also found that timber-based alternatives
are generally cost-effective.
These findings are further supported by another study that specifically assessed portal
frames made from steel, reinforced concrete, and timber. It considered the main mem-
bers, connections, and foundations, concluding that timber has the lowest environmental
impact for both 10- and 25-meter spans (Hegeir et al., 2022).
The growing use of timber in construction has increased demand for efficient and reli-
able design tools. Timber’s low stiffness often makes serviceability and deflections the
limiting design criteria rather than strength (Kliger et al., 2022, p. 183). While vertical
deflections can be estimated with simplified formulas, predicting horizontal displacements
under lateral loads such as wind or seismic actions typically requires detailed finite element
analysis, which is often impractical in early design stages.

State-of-the-art
Connection behaviour adds further complexity; Timber joints are commonly idealized
as fully pinned or fixed, but often exhibit semi-rigid behaviour due to material deforma-
bility. This can significantly affect structural response, a study on three-pinned portal
frames found that incorporating realistic joint stiffness of the corner increased mid-span
deflections by 91% compared to idealized fixed joints (Fabriciuss and Ozola, 2019).
To address these challenges, this thesis focuses on heavy timber frames with large
interior spans. A parametric design framework will be developed using Grasshopper to
generate varied frame geometries, which will then be analyzed under horizontal wind loads
using FEM software. The resulting dataset will be examined using machine learning tech-
niques, such as nonlinear regression and artificial neural networks, to identify relationships
between structural parameters and horizontal deflections.
1.1
State-of-the-art
In recent years, the application of machine learning (ML) in structural engineering has
grown rapidly. According to recent surveys (Thai, 2022; Ocak et al., 2024), the number
of related publications nearly doubled from 70 in 2020 to 130 in 2021. Artificial neural
networks dominate the field, used in approximately 47% of studies, with linear regression
in 4%. The primary focus is on predicting load-bearing capacity and material strength,
representing 67% of papers collectively, with only 10% address full structural analysis
or design, typically concerning trusses or tall buildings under seismic loads (Ocak et al.,
2024). All considered publications focus exclusively on steel or concrete-based materials.
Despite this growth, machine learning applications in timber structures remain limited
and are not covered in major reviews. A few recent studies have examined the lateral
performance of tall mass timber buildings, using nonlinear regression (Kiær and Olesen,
2024) and artificial neural networks (Hegeir et al., 2024). This reveals a clear gap and the
potential for broader use of machine learning in early-phase timber design.
This study aims to address this gap by investigating the horizontal stability of large
timber frames. Figure 1.1 shows three examples of such recently constructed structures.

1.2
Project objectives and research questions
The main goal of this thesis is to support early-phase design of large timber frames
by developing an integrated parametric and machine learning framework. This aim is
addressed through the following specific objectives:
•
Develop design tools and recommendations to support early-phase design of large
timber frames by creating a parametric framework that enables systematic analysis
using machine learning methods.
•
Present relevant theory and design principles in accordance with the governing Eu-
rocodes, including the draft prEN 1995-1-1, to ensure practical relevance and im-
prove accuracy by avoiding unrealistic structural configurations.
•
Analytically investigate a simplified structural case to verify the developed para-
metric framework and provide a trial case for the machine learning methods.
These objectives can also be framed as the following research questions:
•
Which structural parameters most significantly influence the horizontal deflection
of large timber frames?
•
What are the strengths and limitations of different machine learning methods when
applied to this structural context?

2.1.
Wood as a construction material
Most glulam factories are capable of producing members up to 40 m in length; however,
practical limits are often set by transportation logistics. When delivered by truck, the
total vehicle length is typically restricted to 24–30 m without a special permit (Crocetti
et al., 2024, pp. 21–25).
Stock range
Standardized dimensions for ST are defined in DS 146, where planed planks typically
have a width of 45 mm and heights from 90 mm to 215 mm in 25 mm increments. These
planks are also used as lamellas in GL members, making the GL width 45 mm and the
height a multiple thereof. The minimum GL height is 90 mm (two lamellas), while the
maximum is typically limited to around 2 m by manufacturing constraints.
Two common types of GL are homogeneous and combined. Homogeneous GL, typically
used for posts, consists entirely of T22 lamellas—where T22 denotes the strength class
based on tension tests. These posts are square with side lengths ranging from 90 to 165
mm. Combined GL, generally used for beams, has above 17% of the top and bottom
lamellas as T22, while the middle lamellas are of lower strength class T15, optimizing
material use.
Most dimensions are commonly in stock with a strength class of GL30c and GL30h.
A complete list can be found in Gross, 2024, Tab. 4, p. 25.

2.1.
Wood as a construction material
The principal material directions in timber are denoted as L (longitudinal), T (tan-
gential), and R (radial). In most engineering applications, the distinction between the
transverse and radial directions is negligible. As a result, timber is commonly modeled

2.2
Serviceability limit state
The serviceability limit state (SLS) ensures timber structures limit deformations to pre-
vent damage, maintain usability, and preserve appearance. Due to timber’s anisotropic
and viscoelastic nature, long-term effects like creep and moisture-induced movements
significantly influence performance.
SLS criteria are crucial, especially for slender or
lightweight timber elements, to ensure comfort and avoid damage to non-structural com-
ponents. (Kliger et al., 2022, p. 183).

Beam and column systems are one of the most common frame types, suitable for

Three-pin trusses are ideal for large-span buildings where beam-column systems
are insufficient. They consist of two inclined beams connected at the ridge and supported
by columns fixed to the foundation, with a steel tension member at their base.
The
beams are commonly prismatic, but can be tapered. This system is structurally efficient
and economical for spanning wide areas, though it requires careful detailing of joints and
tension elements.
Portal frames are often used in buildings like sports halls, warehouses, and indus-
trial facilities where full headroom and efficient use of internal space are priorities. They
typically feature three-hinged arches with curved or sharp-cornered haunches. Although
they may use more material than parabolic or circular arches, portal frames are especially
suitable for poor soil conditions, since the column-base joint is pinned.
Generally for frames with a hinged joint in the apex/ridge share a minimum recommended
roof pitch of 14◦, which aims to limit both vertical and horizontal deflection due to verti-
cal loading (Crocetti et al., 2024, p. 131). Furthermore, these structures are favorable as
they are statically determinate, meaning internal force distribution remains unaffected by
uneven settlement, moisture-induced movement, or joint deformation (Kliger et al., 2022,
p. 84). For instance, if the ridge joint in a three-pin portal frame were rigid, seasonal
moisture changes would induce unwanted stresses in the members.

Beam/truss and column systems are stable because rotation is restrained at the column
base through rigid or semi-rigid connections, while portal frames achieve stability by
restraining rotation at the corner joints, respectively corresponding to bottom left and

In addition to these mechanisms, stability can also be ensured with diagonal braces,
commonly made from either steel ties or square timber members. Steel ties resist only ten-
sion, while timber braces resist both tension and compression. However, the compressive
stiffness of timber braces may be significantly reduced by flexural buckling, depending
on their length, covered subsection A.2.1. Furthermore, the stiffness of braces may be
affected by connection slip, this is discussed in subsection 2.4.1. Lastly, stability can also
be achieved using shear walls, often constructed from plywood or OSB sheathing panels
secured with fasteners such as nails. However, this type will not be covered in the present
study.
The stiffening effect of cross bracing is significantly greater than that of fixed joints.
For example, a study of a square glulam frame with a 6 m side length and 200×300 mm
cantilevered columns subjected to a point load at the beam showed that adding a Ø10
mm steel tie increased the initial stiffness by a factor of 7. Replacing it with a Ø20 mm
tie raised the stiffness by a factor of 25 (Kliger et al., 2022, Tab. 6.1).
In large timber buildings, the horizontal stiffness of individual frames may be insuffi-
cient to meet SLS deflection limits. To address this, cross bracing or shear walls are often
installed in the end walls (gables) and connected to the intermediate frames through lon-
gitudinal bracing. This approach also enables the use of beam and column systems with
pinned connections to the foundations.

Timber joints
This section covers the most commonly used connection types in timber frames, cate-
gorized by their configuration and assumed mechanical behavior, typically classified as
either pinned or rigid. It also includes an overview of the stiffness properties of individ-
ual fasteners, as well as calculation methods for determining the rotational stiffness of
connections.
Column-base connections
Starting with the connections between the column and the foundation seen in Figure 2.12,
where the possible support reactions are given for each one.
Figure 2.12: Common column base connections with blue arrows indicating forces trans-
ferred to the foundation. (Inspired by Crocetti et al., 2024, Fig. 14.10/13/14/17)
The left-most connection consists of externally mounted steel plates that are cast into
the foundation and secured to the timber using fasteners, such as nails or screws. When
the plates are oriented parallel to the strong z-axis of the cross-section, the connection is
considered nominally pinned. If instead the plates are oriented parallel to the weak y-axis,
the connection is considered nominally rigid. This distinction arises because, in both cases,
the applied moment ME is resisted by a force couple: tension in one of the steel plates
and compression in the timber in contact with the foundation. The rotational stiffness of
the joint depends heavily on the distance between these opposing forces. In the pinned
configuration, this lever arm is small, resulting in low rotational stiffness, whereas in the
22
==PAGE:31==
2.4.
Timber joints
rigid configuration, the increased distance between tensile and compressive forces leads to
significantly higher rotational stiffness. This will be covered in detail in subsection 2.4.2.
The second type of connection consists of slotted-in steel plates joined to the timber
using dowels or bolts. The pinned and rigid configurations correspond to examples 2 and
4, respectively, in Figure 2.12. In the latter, the force couple is formed either between the
two steel plates or through contact in cases where the plate is slender enough to allow
buckling.
For both connection types, vertical tensile forces are transferred to the steel plates via
the fasteners, while vertical compressive forces are transmitted directly through timber-
foundation contact. In pinned configurations, horizontal loads are carried by the fasteners.
In rigid configurations, horizontal forces are resisted primarily by compression perpendic-
ular to the grain combined with a plastic bending mechanism in the steel plates (Crocetti
et al., 2024, 14.3.2).

Input parameters and collection of objects
The first step is the definition of X, which is tailored to the structural problem of interest.
This process typically begins with a maximalistic identification of all potentially relevant
input parameters. The corresponding parameter ranges are established based on a com-
bination of literature review, engineering judgment, physical limitations, and practical
considerations, with the primary goal of covering all values likely to be encountered by
future users.
The next step depends on the number of identified parameters.
If the number of
parameters is relatively low, typically fewer than six, a discrete set of values is manually
defined for each parameter.
These values are chosen to achieve a good resolution of
the output. Importantly, they may not be uniformly spaced; in cases where the output is
highly sensitive to changes at low values and stabilizes at higher values, a denser sampling
at the low end may be preferable.
A common choice is between 4 and 7 values per
parameter. The total number of observations is then computed as N = QM
j=1 nj, where M
is the number of parameters and ni is the number of values for parameter j. For example,
if M = 6 and each parameter has 5 distinct values, the total number of combinations
becomes N = 56 = 15625, which is already substantial. As the number of parameters
increases, N can quickly become computationally intractable.
In high-dimensional problems, i.e., many parameters, a different strategy is adopted.
Instead of using discrete values, probability distributions are defined for each parameter,
allowing the user to specify a total number of samples N directly. The procedure for
defining the distributions is similar to the earlier process, but sampling is performed
probabilistically rather than deterministically. Examples of both approaches are presented
in the beam and frame case studies, respectively.
Creating structural model and loading
Using the input parameters, a single object i is selected and used to generate the corre-
sponding FEM-Design model. This is achieved through the FEM-Design API components
in Grasshopper, the process can be divided into loading and structure.
Loading is modeled in three steps: defining the load case, assigning one or more load
types (e.g., line load), and creating the load combination.
Defining structural elements typically begins with specifying the geometry.

connectivity to adjacent members, followed by the assignment of cross-sectional properties
at each end. Since the probabilistic approach can yield non-standard material properties,
a custom component was developed to define the material properties accordingly.

pering that may violate ULS criteria. Although these may not significantly affect model
performance, their inclusion is inefficient, as designers are unlikely to be interested in their
stiffness. One possible solution is to sample member dimensions from a normal distribu-
tion centered on ULS-based design recommendations, depending on the frame geometry
(Crocetti et al., 2024, Tab. 3.1).
Lastly, some frames include intermediate columns placed so closely that they overlap
with the main columns, which is physically impossible. These configurations are also
unrealistic, as designers typically aim to optimize bracing by maintaining 45◦< αbr < 60◦
(Crocetti et al., 2024, p. 180). Although the bias for nim in Table 6.1 was selected to
prevent the occurrence of such cases. These cases reinforce the need for filtering unrealistic
geometries before FEA in the parametric framework.

7
Conclusion
This study successfully developed and demonstrated an integrated parametric and ma-
chine learning framework designed to aid the early-phase design of large timber frames.
The framework provides valuable insights into the relative impact of various structural
parameters on horizontal stiffness, a key factor for satisfying serviceability requirements.
The findings clearly highlight the dominant stiffening role of cross-bracing and the greater
effectiveness of enhancing rotational stiffness at the column-base joint compared to the
corner joint. Despite these advances, the complexity of the frame model with 14 param-
eters limited the ability of machine learning methods to generate sufficiently accurate
regression models for practical design tools.
The implementation and accuracy of the numerical structural analysis using FEM-
Design within Grasshopper were successfully validated by analyzing a single-span pris-
matic beam with semi-rigid supports under a uniformly distributed load and comparing
the results to theoretical expressions. The machine learning framework was also vali-
dated using this simplified structure. In this case, it was possible to derive a simplified
expression for flexibility using nonlinear regression, achieving an average prediction error
of 1.48 mm/(kN/m) corresponding to R2 = 0.99 Furthermore, by optimizing the artificial
neural network architecture, a near-perfect prediction model was achieved, enabling the
development of visual design aids for trade-off analysis.
For structural problems with high dimensionality, systematically covering the design
space becomes infeasible. A probabilistic sampling approach was therefore proposed. Its
application to the frame model highlighted the need for thorough definition of param-
eters and preliminary filtering to exclude unrealistic configurations. Reducing problem
complexity by subdividing the dataset into specific frame types, such as portal frames or
simple beam-and-column systems, was found to improve regression model performance
and is recommended for future work.
The two cases reveal key strengths and limitations of regression methods. Structural
problems are usually nonlinear, making nonlinear regression effective, but it depends
heavily on prior knowledge of the specific problem. For highly complex problems, ANNs
excel because they capture nonlinear relationships flexibly without manual guidance.

A
Ultimate limit state design of
timber elements
This appendix covers the relevant ultimate limit state design requirements needed to
validate a proposed frame and to explain the structural reasoning behind the common
frame types examined. Aspects specific to glulam structures and semi-rigid connections
are included, in addition to the rules given in Section 8 of prEN 1995-1-1:2023.
These ULS requirements are ultimately related to the collapse of the structure as a
result of the following causes: rupture of cross-sections due to excessive loading, transfor-
mation into a mechanism due to instability, loss of equilibrium, and failure due to fatigue
(pr1995-1-1:23, 4.2.1). The first two are the primary focus. In essence, verification is
based on ensuring that the effect does not exceed the resistance, Ed ≤Rd, under design
conditions that account for uncertainties, such as those related to material properties, see
subsection 2.1.3.
A.1
Structural modeling approach
Multiple approaches are permitted for determining the effects of actions. The approach
adopted here is geometrically linear and based on the initial geometry, meaning that the
stiffness matrix K is defined for the undeformed structure.
The material is assumed
to be linear elastic, which corresponds to first-order analysis. This method is allowed
if imperfections and deformations do not increase the effects of actions by more than
10 percent, which is the initial assumption here.
Otherwise, second-order analysis is
required, where the same principles are applied to the deformed geometry. Lastly, the kc
and km method is applied, meaning that the effects of local and global imperfections and
deformations are implicitly accounted for (pr1995-1-1:23, 7.2.1).

B.2

The study presents a closed-form solution for a prismatic beam with semi-rigid joints,
derived using Timoshenko-Ehrenfest beam theory. This solution is achieved by combining
the classical stiffness method with Green’s functions, where the complete beam response
is decomposed into homogeneous and fixed components. Unlike the Euler-Bernoulli (EB)

UDL. An example involving a single-span, one-story frame is used to demonstrate the
approach, comparing it to traditional FEM. The results highlight the advantages of the
proposed method, particularly in terms of accuracy and the ability to avoid discontinuities
in the solution.
