Abstract
A key aspect of transitioning to weather-driven energy sources is utilising the flexibility
of energy consumption to meet the weather-driven time of production.
An important
example of such flexibility is the charging of electric vehicles (EVs). EV charging can
often be postponed or advanced to times when the grid is in plenty, for example, overnight.
This process is popularly called Smart Charging. This thesis provides models, methods
and code for solving the Smart Charging problem of minimising costs.
The thesis demonstrates that the Smart Charging problem can be solved by a simple linear
program which is very fast to solve. For controlling the charging optimally over time,
Economic Model Predictive Control (EMPC) is suggested for solving the linear program
with a receding horizon of up to six days of length. The models are developed and tested
on real EV behavioural data.
For comparison of various models, a novel measure is proposed: Relative Total Cost, which
defines the relative difference from the total cost of charging having Perfect Foresight
relative to Dumb Charging (as opposed to Smart Charging). This provides a framework
for assessing all Smart Charging algorithms in a 0 to 100 % range from the perfect solution.
Both deterministic and stochastic EMPCs are developed. The deterministic models treat
the price forecasts as the ground truth. The forecasts prove to fool the model into taking
worse decisions than the Day-Ahead model, which only acts on the known prices 11-35
hours ahead.
To model the inherent uncertainty of the price forecasts, the Stochastic Multi-Day Ahead
model is developed. It is based on building a probabilistic forecast on top of the point
forecast, from which statistical scenarios of the price residuals can be sampled. By defining
the two-stage stochastic program of the problem, the model learns that forecasts are
inherently uncertain and proves to improve performance relative to the deterministic Multi-
Day Ahead model on the training set. A test set is obtained to estimate the out-of-sample
performance, on which the stochastic model degrades in performance, calling for adaptive
estimation of the covariance structure in the residuals.
Ultimately, the Day-Ahead Model provides the best results on both training and test set.
The model is found to exploit the majority of the value within EV flexibility. Suggestions
for additional enhancements are provided. The average savings per vehicle is estimated
to be 1337 DKK (28 %) per year.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The future energy sources are primarily weather driven. The greatest challenge of tran-
sitioning to weather driven energy sources is the inherent stochasticity of the weather.
Unlike fossil energy sources, which are deterministic in their power production as deter-
mined by the calorific content of the fuel, renewable energy sources vary with the weather.
Because energy storage is not feasible on a large scale shortly, the energy sector needs to,
to some extent, be flipped upside down. Where traditionally, the production was adjusted
to meet the demand; with stochastic production, the demand needs to be adjusted to meet
the production. This is done by enabling and utilising the flexibility in energy consumption.
Flexibility is the possibility of postponing or advancing energy consumption.
One of
such examples is the charging of an electric vehicle. The vehicle does not have to charge
immediately when plugged in overnight as long as it is charged and ready the next morning.
The utilisation of electric vehicle flexibility is popularly called Smart Charging. In a fossil
fueled world, the price of power is often rather constant, and in that case, the flexibility of
consumption is not of importance. However, with recent highly fluctuating energy prices,
there is value to gain from utilising the flexibility by moving the energy demand to periods
where the market is in plenty.
Electric vehicles possess a major portion of the private flexibility and are projected to
increase towards and particularly after 2035, when the sale of new fossil-fueled vehicles
will be prohibited in the EU. The question of inquiry in this thesis is
Research question
How can the charging costs of electric vehicles by minimised for the end-user
by utilising their flexibility?
1.1
Challenges
A key component to unlocking flexibility is incentives.
Barriers to such have recently
been removed such that electric vehicle owners are rewarded for providing their flexibility.
Dynamic retail power prices and dynamic tariffs are examples of such incentives. Conse-
quently, Smart Charging has gained a lot of attention recently, both commercially and
academically.
A main obstacle to people embarking on the modelling journey is the lack of data. Data
on plug-in hours and state-of-charge of the vehicle is needed. State-of-charge (SOC) refers
to the amount of electricity on the battery. Particularly this information is diﬀicult to

1.2
Literature review
A lot of research has been conducted recently on utilising the flexibility of various energy
systems. Smart Charging EVs [1][2], Smart PV [3], Smart Buildings [4][5], Smart Heating
[6], Smart Cooling [7], Smart Home [8], etc. However, when it comes to Smart Charging
EVs, little research has been conducted with real behavioural data.
All smart systems involve optimally controlling a unit by minimising some notion of cost
while adhering to the natural constraints of the problem, including potential user input.
Recently, Economic Model Predictive Control (EMPC) has gained a lot of attention in
these areas. Many of the applications of EMPC are based on deterministic, point-estimate
forecasts. However, in this thesis, Stochastic EMPC is proposed. This entails a proba-
bilistic extension of the point forecasts and statistical scenario generation as inspired by
Pierre Pinson et al. [9], thereby enabling a stochastic programming setting from which
the model ideally learns the inherent uncertainties of the price forecasts.
More on electric vehicles
Wang et al. [1] distinguish between three different perspectives on Smart Charging: Smart
grid oriented, aggregator-oriented and customer-oriented. The first revolves around peak
load flattening (peak shaving), frequency regulation and voltage regulation in a centralised
fashion for the benefit of grid stability. Within this work lies the idea of using the fleet of
EVs as a big battery for providing ancillary services to the grid, a related model predictive
control problem [10]. The second perspective focuses on semi-centralised utility- and cost
optimisation for the main benefit of the aggregator. The third perspective is a completely
decentralised strategy of cost or CO2 minimisation from the perspective of individual EV
owners. This thesis is a contribution to the research on the latter.
Halvgaard et al. [2] implement an EMPC for customer-oriented Smart Charging but only
with perfect information on electricity prices and calls for future work to be done on mod-
elling the stochasticity of electricity prices. This thesis answers to that call. Additionally, a
vacuum exists in the literature on Smart Charging models tested on real EV behavioural
data. This thesis strives to fill that gap and provides models and implementations for
decentralised, customer-oriented Smart Charging.

Chapter 2
Theory
Smart Charging of EVs or any other electric device is inherently an Optimal Control
Problem. This means controlling the charging such that some notion of cost is minimised
while adhering to the inherent constraints of the problem.
Traditionally, control theory has been applied in industrial processes and has focused
on maintaining a specific set-point value for a given variable and making adjustments
according to the measured process variable. The most common example of such a controller
is the proportional-integal-derivative controller.
However, this is a feedback controller,
meaning it is purely reactive to feedback from the difference between measured and set-
point values. A feedforward controller acts on forecasts without measuring the process
on-line [11].
Recently, with the rising of more stochastic energy sources, the focus on feedforward control
has increased. For optimally charging an EV, both on-line measurements of the system and
forecasts of the system’s future behaviour are needed. Model Predictive Control combines
the two.
2.1
Model Predictive Control
Model Predictive Control (MPC) solves a finite-horizon constrained optimisation problem
at each time step t, implements only the first decision at t and then solves the problem at
new in the next time step t + 1. This strategy is also called receding horizon control. The
concept is illustrated in Figure 2.1.

Metrics

Chapter 3
Background
3.1
The Electricity Market
The Danish electricity market, like the markets in most European countries, is divided
into three main markets: Day Ahead Market, Intraday Market and Balancing Market as
shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: The architecture of the Danish electricity market consisting of three markets:
Day Ahead Market, Intraday Market and Balancing Market [16].
The end-consumer purchases electricity from a retailer, who procures it from one of the
markets mentioned above. Most electricity, over 70%, is traded in the Day Ahead Market
[17]. The prices at the Day Ahead Market are called the spot prices. It is established
through a clearing price mechanism, in which all producers are paid the marginal cut-off
price between supply and demand. The spot price is determined independently for each
hour of the day. The Intraday Market offers a venue for suppliers and demanders to trade
energy to align with their established targets from the day before. As the supply and
demand for electricity must be in perfect equilibrium at all times, given that electricity
must be consumed immediately upon generation, the balancing market serves to rectify
discrepancies in supply and demand. Entities that exceed or fall short of their consump-
tion levels are penalised or rewarded by the balancing price, which is determined in the
frequency regulation market, governed by the Transmission System Operator (TSO). To
stabilise the grid, the TSO facilitates procurement of up-regulation purchased as auxiliary
services.

4.1
EV behaviour
Behavioural data of EV users has been provided by Monta. Monta is a Danish scale-up
company aspiring to become a global player in EV charging. A user can charge their EV
in numerous ways, for example, on public charge points as we know it from fossil-fueled
vehicles. However, with electric vehicles, it is like having a gas station at home, allowing
users to charge their EV without intermediaries. This typically results in lower energy
costs.

4.1.2
Lack of data
Monta’s Smart Charging is not designed to utilise users’ flexibility properly. The current
state is that users charge to full, which is essentially both the minimum and maximum
state-of-charge specified for a planned pick-up time. Full can be any percentage such as
80 %, 90 % or 99 % in order to avoid battery degradation. A general rule of thumb for
extending the battery lifetime of most batteries is to avoid charging above 80 % unless
used shortly after.
However, commuters can often do with much less and ideally, the
Smart Charging algorithm charges the vehicle to full on cheap days and just charges the
necessary on expensive days. What should be included then as user input is minimum
state-of-charge along with the planned pick-up time, where minimum state-of-charge could
be any user-friendly unit such as percent or kilometres and then converted internally to
kWhs.
Because the algorithm currently charges to full, people generally only plug in when they
need to charge. This does not utilise the full flexibility of the users. Since it requires only
seconds to re-plug in the car when returning home, this thesis assesses the full flexibility,
where users plug in every night, and the model then decides whether or not to charge. For
determining reasonable estimates of such times, it is clear from Figure 4.3 that
1. Users mostly plug in at 22.00 or before
2. Users mostly plan to plug out at 6.00 or later

made with a safe margin such that the flexibility is not over-exploited. The assumptions

4.1.3
Data Quality
As with most real data, flaws and lack of quality is present and handling these has been
a major time consumer in this work. Examples of these are:
1. The amount of electricity leaving the charger differs from the amount entering the
battery’s state-of-charge.
2. State-of-charge can increase in a session even though no charging was performed.
3. State-of-charge can decrease in a session even though charging was performed.
4. The timestamps of the charging points relate to several weeks before or after the
charge session for which the car was plugged in.
Particularly the first one, of which the two following items are variants, is present and
requires handling.
Vehicles with the last case are disregarded from the data set.
To
handle the first one, an eﬀiciency parameter rt is introduced, describing the ratio of the
energy charged that actually enters the battery at the charge session at t. For the same
vehicle, rt varies greatly, often between 40 % and 120 %. The latter meaning that 120 %
of the energy leaving the charger enters the battery. The eﬀiciencies of vehicle 10885 is
depicted in Figure C.2 in Appendix C. In extreme cases, rt is down to -1000 % and up to
700 %. Clearly, the lack of ”eﬀiciency” is not only due to heat loss in the cables but also
simply lack of data quality.
To mitigate this issue, a global eﬀiciency parameter r is introduced for each vehicle. This
thesis uses the median value of rt as an estimator for r. The median eﬀiciencies r across
the 100 vehicles are described by quantiles in Table 4.3.

1. Padding forecasts to a fixed length of t = 0..200 hours with M = 25000

5.2
Stochastic Models
The models discussed thus far have been based on a deterministic approach, disregarding
the inherent uncertainty in price forecasts. In order to account for the uncertainty, this
report explores the application of stochastic programming. Stochastic programming allows
the model to learn that the prices are not deterministically known, that they are uncertain,
and that the uncertainty generally increases as the prediction horizon extends.
The EMPC problem at hand is a multistage stochastic programming problem. At each
hour t+k, there is a recourse option to charge more provided that the vehicle is plugged-in.
So the model may be looking up to 6 days ahead (or further if price forecasts are available),
but at each new hour, too little charging can be recoursed. However, treating the problem
as a 144-stage problem becomes computationally intractable, but the value of treating it as
such would also be extremely limited. Essentially, the price is deterministically known 11
to 35 hours ahead (and often closer to the 35, since the Day-Ahead prices have been settled
when people plug-in in the evening), so the recourse possibility lies in acting differently
on potentially different forecasts far into the future. It seems reasonable to assume that
the decision will not change much in each iteration. The big change in information occurs
when the Day-Ahead prices are revealed. So for these reasons, a two-stage approximation

6.3
Forecasting usage
When designing a controller which considers forecasts of future prices multiple days ahead,
the controller requires an estimate of the expected future usage. Since usage is only mea-
sured as a difference between the last plug-out at the time of re-plug-in, the signal is
spike-and-slab distributed, i.e. sparse and zero-inflated until a sudden spike is measured.
This makes the signal extremely diﬀicult to predict accurately. Consequently, rather sim-
ple low-variance techniques are applied in this thesis, i.e. there exists room for improve-
ments in applying more sophisticated methods. As described in Section 4.1.1, the use is
backwards-averaged until the last plug-out. Since this information is extracted from linear

Chapter 8
Discussion
Various models have been established and tested for solving the Smart Charging prob-
lem. This chapter critically evaluates the work conducted, proposes directions for further
improvement, and situates the solutions within their broader context.
8.1
Points of critique
8.1.1
Different data input design
A valid point of critique of the work in this thesis is assuming known plug-in hours zt and
using a very simple forecast model for ut. This does not reflect the modelling situations
of today.
The reasoning behind the choice is that a re-vision of Smart Charging has
been proposed. Instead of always charging to full, a minimum state-of-charge bmin
t
was
introduced as user input which allows the user to specify time and minimum desired state-
of-charge for the day. This design exploits the flexibility of the user to a much larger
extent. As seen in the results, this allows the optimisation model to automatically decide
whether or not tonight is a good time to charge - rather than always charging upon plug-
in. Of course, it requires the user to plug in most nights, or the app could notify the
user returning home if it expects to charge tonight. Additionally, it will solve most of the
infeasibility issues in the results, where a sudden spike in usage demand was insuﬀiciently
planned for.
In this design, expected plug-in hours and minimum state-of-charge are provided by the
user, which makes an accurate forecast of usage unimportant. Instead of trying to predict
when users are embarking on a long journey, which is extremely diﬀicult to predict, they
will just tell the controller by setting the bmin
t
to 80 % or 100 % for time t. The earlier
they do so, the better the model can plan for it. Ideally, this should free the user from
keeping an eye on the prices each night because the model automatically does it.
8.1.2
Simplifications
The presented models assume a constant charge speed at the capacity xcap (e.g. 11 kW).
However, the charging is not fully linear with time. The charging speed depends, to some
extent, on the state-of-charge.
This relationship can easily be added to the model by
introducing a maximum charge speed being some function of the state-of-charge mapping
to a proportion of the capacity xcap
xmax = f(xcap, SOC)
∈[0, 1] · xcap
However, the non-linearities are mainly found to be a decreasing charge speed when state-
of-charge is above 80 % [23]. For state-of-charge between 20 % and 80 % the difference
Smart Charging Electric Vehicles
51
==PAGE:59==
is neglectable, and thus the constant charge speed is a reasonable approximation in most
cases.
Similarly, the results are based on a median eﬀiciency r when in fact the eﬀiciency varies
greatly as rt. Other estimators than the median can be used, or one can try to model the
heat loss part of rt by a function of the outdoor temperature, battery temperature and
the state-of-charge. However, the varying eﬀiciency rt will remain an issue native to the
Smart Charging problem. A small comfort lies in the fact that when the vehicle is actually
charging, the algorithm can adapt to the rt by stopping the charging when a measured
certain minimum or maximum state-of-charge is reached.
8.1.3
Adaptive estimation
When testing the stochastic models on out-of-sample test data, a performance drop was
measured. One explanation for this is that the covariance matrix, despite regularisation,
was overfitted to the training data. Another explanation is that the model lacked adap-
tation to the new variance-covariance structure in the forecast residuals of the test set.
When implementing this in production, one would instead update the covariance matrix
recursively and adaptively. This is computationally feasible because the covariance matrix
of the forecast residuals is shared among all vehicles.
Similarly, the terminal cost ˜c should be adaptively estimated instead of finding a single
value for ˜c on the training set and using that rigidly on the test set. This is particularly
important if the prices generally change because the terminal cost is essential to the
decision of whether or not to charge.

By doing so, one would have to be really careful with overfitting, which might already be

In this work, the usage ut has been forecasted by a simple point-estimate forecast. Since
the usage is inherently stochastic, an immediate enhancement is also to model ut probabilis-

statistical scenarios of usage can only come closer to but never better than having perfect
foresight of usage, and since it was shown that perfect foresight of usage did not improve
performances considerably, this approach is only expected to give a few extra percentages

program increases exponentially in size for scenarios of both ct and ut.
8.2.1
Additional potential enhancements
1. Other covariance shrinkage techniques which preserve the true covariance.
2. Other scenario reduction techniques which preserve the variance.
3. Box-Cox transformation or similar to further Gaussianize the data, particularly to
handle the fat tails at the longer horizons.
4. Probabilistic chance-constraints of the wait-and-see user input constraints to provide
further flexibility.
5. Acquire better spot price forecasts.
6. If the user is risk averse, one could find a good trade-off at the eﬀicient frontier,
which also minimise the variance of the expected total cost.
8.3
Perspective and context
This thesis has provided models, methods and code which is readily implementable for
solving the Smart Charging problem. However, the approach is only viable in the near fu-
ture when the amount of electric vehicles is fairly low. Although Smart Charging of electric
vehicles is intuitively a good idea, it also possess a great challenge: The synchronisation
of consumption. As more and more people start to synchronise in charging behaviour,
since the cheap hours of electricity are the same for everyone, consequently, synchronised
pressure is put on the grid. This poses a challenge for three entities.
Firstly, it puts pressure on the local grid governed by the Distribution Service Operator
(DSO). Since the local grid was originally not built for home-charging of electric vehicles,
the local grid can simply not withstand too many homes charging their EV at the same
time. With the increasing electrification of society, the local power grid eventually needs
to be expanded, but this is a very time-consuming and costly affair, and since the power
demand is expected to more than double towards 2040 [24], a more immediate solution is
necessary as well.
Secondly, it creates disturbances for the country-wide grid governed by the Transmission
System Operator (TSO). With more irregular and yet synchronised demand, the power
grid is harder to keep stable.
Thirdly, as the demand for electricity becomes harder to predict, it becomes harder for
electricity retailers to purchase the right amount of electricity at the right time in advance.

Since most electricity is traded in the Day-Ahead market, electricity retailers have to
forecast the synchronised demand behaviour of the EV fleet.
The solution to address these challenges has to include a revision of the market structure.
The Smart Charging problem will change its form accordingly. As the emergence of electric
vehicles is creating the problems, luckily, in many cases, they can also be a key part of
the solution. Electric vehicles can help load balancing from both the TSO and the DSO
perspective (either by direct load control or indirectly through a dynamic pricing scheme),
and a simple price signal transmitted from the electricity retailer to the vehicle can aid
the retailer in minimising their imbalances of procured and consumed electricity of their
customers.
Additionally, as the peak hour tariffs have increased substantially in Denmark in 2023,
Vehicle-2-Home is becoming more and more attractive; Using the electricity in the battery
when the grid is strained and the price is high, and re-charging it during the night when
the grid is unstrained, and the price is low. This would also benefit the non-EV-owners
who have little flexibility by putting less pressure on the grid. In this case, one has to be
careful with battery degradation. Charging the battery and using the energy flow both
ways is expected to negatively impact the battery. However, the proportionality of the
degradation is still uncertain and debated in the literature [25].
The integration of electric vehicles into our energy systems is certainly emerging, and the
challenges it comes with will hopefully be met with smart solutions.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This thesis has provided models, methods and code which is readily implementable for
solving the Smart Charging problem. The results are based on minimising economic costs,
but the models and methods apply equally well for minimising any other notion of costs,
such as CO2 or even a weighted average between different cost measures. This chapter
concludes on the research question:
Research question
How can the costs of charging an electric vehicle be minimised for the end-user
by utilising their flexibility?
Various models have been established and tested to answer this question of solving the
Smart Charging problem. The Perfect Foresight model, the Dumb Charging model and
the Relative Total Cost measure establish a framework for assessing the performance of
any Smart Charging algorithm. For such algorithms, both deterministic and stochastic
EMPCs have been proposed.
The deterministic, Multi-Day Ahead model seeks to exploit spot price forecasts for a
horizon of up to 6 days. However, the deterministic Multi-Day Ahead model is fooled by
the price forecasts being different than promised, since the model performs inferior to the
simpler Day-Ahead model, which considers only the known prices 11-35 hours ahead.
To treat the inherent stochasticity of the forecasts, the Stochastic Multi-Day Ahead model
was established. The developed method is based on modelling the forecast residuals with a
multivariate Gaussian distribution for the horizon length, from which statistical scenarios
of the forecast errors can be sampled. The full set of scenarios constitutes a discretised
representation of the stochasticity in the price forecasts, where each scenario represents a
path which the forecast residuals can take. Defining the deterministic equivalent of the
two-stage stochastic program allowed for these scenarios to feed directly into a similar
deterministic optimisation. Covariance shrinkage and scenario reduction were imposed
to obtain good, realistic scenarios. The Stochastic Multi-Day Ahead model was found
to improve the performance relative to the deterministic version on the training set but
proved inferior on the test set, possibly due to a lack of adaptation to the new variance-
covariance structure in the forecast residuals.
In the end, the simple Day-Ahead model provided the best results on both training and
test set.
For a large, complex model to be preferred, the performance would have to
be not just equal to that of the simpler model but significantly better. Thus it can be
concluded without a doubt that the Day-Ahead Smart Charging model is the best among

the proposed ones. The model proved to exploit the majority of the value within EV
flexibility. Thus the Smart Charging problem is reasonably solved, but there is always
room for improvement, and suggestions for such have been discussed.
The implementation of the Day-Ahead model is estimated to save the average end-user
1337 DKK (28 %) per year in raw electricity prices. By changing their data design and
implementing the Day-Ahead model, the average Monta-user is estimated to save 919 DKK
(19 %) per year compared to the current Smart Charging algorithm. The assumptions
behind these estimates are stated in Table 4.2 and mainly contain a nightly plug-in.
In conclusion, the Day-Ahead Smart Charging model, among others, is a readily imple-
mentable model for minimising the cost of charging an electric vehicle for the end-user by
utilising their flexibility.
