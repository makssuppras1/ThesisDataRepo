Abstract
Introduction: One of the limiting factors of magnectic resonance spectroscopy imaging (MRSI)
is the poor sensitivity to nuclei of interest. However, hyperpolarized 13C spectroscopy have
shown increases in signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) > 10.000. [1-13C] pyruvate spectroscopy is used
for analyzing the current metabolic state, since the concentration of the end-products helps
clinicians detect abnormalities, like increased lactate production in malign tumors. The down-
side of the hyperpolarized [1-13C] pyruvate is that its longitudinal signal quickly decays, on a
T1 scale and the transversal signal, on a T ∗
2 scale. For this reason, a fast spectroscopic sequence
is necessary to utilize the hyperpolarized signal, before it returns to equilibrium.
Methods: An attenuated concentric rings trajectory is developed, where a Gaussian weight-
ing function is used to distribute the radii of the rings in the spatial dimension. The rings
are attenuated with time to approximate a Gaussian distribution in the spectral dimension.
The trajectories were reconstructed using non-uniform fast Fourier transform and a iterative
conjugate gradient (CG) sensitivity encoding (SENSE) method. The sequence is simulated
in JEMRIS and implemented on a clinical MR scanner. Signal-to-noise calculation and point
spread functions (PSF), based on simulations of five different variations in trajectory, are done
in MATLAB.
Results: The regridding of the simulation in JEMRIS and the data from the scanner show
bad spatial localization, but easily identifiable spectral peaks. With CG SENSE the spatial
localization is improved and noise is reduced in the spectral dimension. The amplitude of the
spatial PSF’s side lobes increased, with the increase in attenuation of the trajectories. The
spectral PSF is the same for all five simulated trajectories. With multiple simulations the noise
converges upon the same value for all five trajectories.
Conclusion: The sequence was successfully developed, simulated in JEMRIS, implemented on
a clinical MR scanner and reconstructed using CG SENSE. The amplitude of the spatial PSF’s
side lobes increased, with increasing attenuation of the trajectories, but no change in the SNR,
which was unexpected. Further work is needed to optimize the sequence and test it using13C.

3
Introduction
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopic Imaging (MRSI) is the technique of combining the imaging
capabilities from MR and the analytical capabilities from nuclear magnetic resonance spec-
troscopy, allowing monitoring of metabolite distributions within the body. MRSI is used clin-
ically for quantifying metabolic abnormalities in the brain, prostate, breast and other organs
[1].
A limiting factor for MRSI is the sensitivity, since the polarization of nuclei of interest is only
a couple of parts per million (ppm), which gives poor signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and increases
scan times of patients. This can be changed by hyperpolarization, which is a technique that
produces nuclei that are highly polarized. By polarizing 13C the SNR have been shown to
increase > 10.000 [2]. Currently, when using dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization, only
pyruvate is approved for clinical trials [3].
One of the challenges of hyperpolarization is that once the hyperpolarized sample is restored
to room temperature, the hyperpolarized signal decays back to thermal equilibrium on a time
scale of T1. This means that the polarizer needs to be in close proximity to the MR scanner,
and once the sample has been injected intravenously into the patient, the MRSI sequence needs
to be executed fast to utilize the hyperpolarized signal, before it disappears. There are three
major categories of fast imaging of hyperpolarized signals; metabolite specific imaging, model-
based imaging and fast spectroscopic imaging. They all have different trade-offs but all of them
try to improve SNR, coverage, resolution and scan time in different ways [4]. This thesis will
focus on fast spectroscopic imaging.
To reduce scan time, an improvement in SNR and the spectral-spatial point spread function
(PSF) is sought to be achieved. Instead of reshaping the PSF by post acquisition filtering, it
has been shown that by matching the sampling density in k-t-space to a desired filter in both
the spatial and spectral dimension, the SNR and spectral-spatial PSF could be improved [5] [6]
[7].
The increased SNR can then be used to reduce the amount of necessary sampling in k-t-space,
when reconstructing with iterative conjugate gradient (CG) sensitivity encoding (SENSE).
The iterative CG SENSE method is able to reconstruct an arbitrarily under-sampled k-t-space.

Theory
4.1
Chemical shift
Nuclei with an odd number of protons have inherently a spin angular momentum [8]. This
effectively makes the nuclei magnetic dipoles, which is essential for MRI to work. When the
nuclei are exposed to a strong static magnetic field, B0, in the scanner, the dipoles will tend to
align slightly along the field, creating a net magnetization, M, pointing in the direction of the
applied field. If the nuclei are subjected to a secondary field, B1, in a different direction than
B0, the net magnetization will begin to precess around B0. The precession frequency is called
the Larmor frequency, which is given by the Larmor equation
f = γ · B0
(1)
where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio, (given in MHz/T) of the nucleus and B0 is the strength of
the applied static magnetic field in tesla. Because of the abundance of water and fat in the
body, 1H is widely used in the clinical setting, where the gyromagnetic ratio is 42.58 MHz/T
[9]. The gyromagnectic ratio for 1-13C is about 4 times lower than 1H, at 10.705 Mhz/T. [10]
If all nuclei in the body were precessing at the same frequency, then spectroscopic imaging
would not give additional information since the spectrum would have a single peak. The nuclei
in the body are, however, not precessing at exactly the same frequency. This is due to an effect
called chemical shift, causing the nuclei in different local environments to precess at different
frequencies. The effect arises from different covalent electron structures surrounding nuclei and
hence having a “shielding” effect from the magnetic field. Looking at the Larmor equation,
eq. (1), it can be seen that changes in B0 will change the precession frequency of the individual
nucleus. The effective field experienced by the nucleus is given by
Beff = B0 −B0σ = B0 · (1 −σ)
(2)
where σ is the shielding constant, which depends on several factors in the local chemical en-

The B0 field is more practical to increase when working in a clinical setting. Human-sized

in the spectra, but will require better gradient hardware with increased slew rate to cover the
required frequency spectrum [14].
At a static magnetic field of 9.4 T and temperature of 298 K, eq. (6) calculates the nuclear
polarization to 32 parts per million (ppm) for 1H and 8.1 ppm for13C [11]. This shows that
even when using very high B0 fields, the polarization is still in the ppm scale.
A different approach to gaining better sensitivity was discovered in the 1950’s by Overhauser,
Carver and Slichter [15] [16]. The sample would be cooled down, which enhances the difference
between the energy states, hence breaking the Boltzmann thermal equilibrium. Enhancing the
difference between the energy states gives a greater polarization and hence better sensitivity.
The methods that achieve non-equilibrium in the spin distribution are called hyperpolarization
techniques. One of the techniques is called Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (DNP), which utilizes
the high polarization of unpaired electron spins compared to the nuclear spins. Irradiation with
microwaves close to the Larmor frequency of the electrons will cause a polarization transfer from
the electron spins to the nuclei. Dissolution-DNP was invented in 2003 by Ardenkjaer-Larsen
et al. [2], and they produced an increase in SNR > 10.000 times in liquid-state NMR.

4.3
Acquisition schemes
When imaging anatomical structures with MRI only k-space is required. K-space is used to
describe the spatial frequency domain, where the raw data is sampled. Every position in k-
space corresponds to a unique phase roll based on its wave length and direction. The phase
roll can be mathematically described as exp(−i2πk · r). The phase rolls are created using the
three orthogonal gradients in different combinations. The signal is sampled either during or
after the use of gradients. The strength of the signal describes how well a particular phase roll
matches the scanned object. If k-space has been filled critically according to the Nyquist sam-
pling theorem and in a Cartesian manner, then k-space can be Fourier transformed to get the
reconstructed image. The reconstructed image will then be a weighted sum of the individual
phase rolls in k-space.
For MRSI, multiple samples are required from the same point in k-space, but along the en-
tire free induction decay (FID). A new axis with time is therefore introduced and the formalism
is to call it k-t-space. For a 2D MRSI, which is mainly covered in this report, the k-t-space
would contain a kx, ky and t axis. If sampled critically according to the Nyquist theorem and in

4.3.1
Point Spread Function
The PSF is formally defined as the response of an imaging system to a point source. Information
about the PSF allow prediction of how an object will be imaged, since an object can be seen
as a collection of point sources. The PSF can be found by Fourier transforming the weighting
function of k-t-space. Some of the standard Fourier transforms can be seen in fig. 5. It can
be seen that a uniform sampling over a rectangular area in k-space would have a sinc PSF.
This would give unwanted signal in neighbouring voxels. During post-processing a filter can be
applied to the rectangular coverage of k-space with e.g. a Hanning window. This would reduce
side lobes, but it also suppresses some of the signal sampled in the outer edges of k-space. This
sampling time could have been used in the middle of k-space to average out some of the noise,
and hence improve SNR. It therefore improves SNR when matching the sampling density to a
desired filter, rather than applying a filter in post-processing.

4.3.2
Reconstruction of k-t-space
Depending on the trajectory of choice, reconstruction of k-t-space can be simple. If sampled
on a Cartesian grid, a 3D FFT produces a spatial image with a spectral dimension. However,
if the trajectory is not sampled on a Cartesian grid, the reconstruction becomes more complex.
In an effort to reduce total acquisition time, parallel imaging has been commercially used since

the beginning of the 2000s [21]. When not adhering to the critical Nyquist sampling rate, the
reconstructed image will be aliased, but by using multiple receiver coils and parallel imaging
algorithms, the image can be reconstructed. One of the algorithms which use the aliased images
for the reconstruction is called sensitivity encoding (SENSE) [22]. The SENSE algorithms were
designed for Cartesian trajectories and are numerically very demanding for arbitrary k-space

of the iterative image reconstruction is shown below in fig. 6. The method utilizes the individ-

preconditioning. This is done by using intensity and density correction in the reconstruction
process, like seen in fig. 6. When the approximation is sufficiently accurate the image is in-
tensity corrected one last time and a k-space filter is applied. The filter is necessary when the
trajectory does not cover the rectangular k-space, which is wanted for the Cartesian image grid.

Methods
The k-t-space trajectories and coils have been designed and simulated in JEMRIS [24], an open-
source multi-purpose MRI simulator. The k-t-space trajectories have also been designed and
reconstructed in MATLAB [25]. The Michigan Image Reconstruction Toolbox, a collection of
open-source image reconstruction algorithms designed by Jeff Fessler and his group, was used to
reconstruct the data with non-uniform FFT (NUFFT) and the CG SENSE method[26][27][28].
The sequence was tested on a clinical 3T GE SIGNA Premier MR scanner at Rigshospitalet,
Copenhagen.

Discussion
The amplitude of the 1D spatial PSF were as expected, considering the same sampling time
and sampling points were used in all of them. Chiew et al. [6] showed that using a sampling
density in k-space approximating a hamming window would reduce side lobes. The side lobes in
fig. 20 increased in amplitude, when the attenuation increased in the temporal dimension, and
they increased when σ decreased. The weighting function is not a hamming window, but the
expectation was that a Gaussian weighting function would reduce side lobes as well. However,
a possibility is that σ is too small, since the Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian with
the inverse standard deviation [30]. Another possibility is that, because of the attenuation in
the temporal dimension and the density weighting function in the spatial dimension, the high
spatial frequencies are not covered very much in the trajectories, which might lead to increased
amplitude in the side lobes.
The 1D spectral PSF’s were exactly the same for all five trajectories. This was not expected,
since the attenuation of the temporal dimension should have worked as a filter. The confusing
thing is that they are completely the same, which suggest another explanation than the filtering,
since the big difference in filtering should have changed something.
The regridding of the Shepp-Logan phantom, from the JEMRIS simulation, shows that the
NUFFT operator correctly re-grids the data onto a Cartesian grid and transforms it into the
image domain. Usage of the developed sensitivity maps in the reconstruction with CG SENSE
shows the potential for improving, especially, the spatial localization.
The implementation of the same sequence on the clinical MR scanner was done successfully.
The regridding and CG SENSE reconstruction showed results similar to the simulation in JEM-
RIS. The regridding had bad spatial localization, but the spectral dimension clearly showed the
two peaks. After CG SENSE the spatial localization became much better, the spectral peaks
got more narrow and the noise was reduced.
The maximum signal in the simulations was the same for the spectral and spatial 1D PSF,
for all five trajectories. This is again due to the trajectories having the same sampling time and
July 1, 2022
32
==PAGE:39==
Implementation of fast spectroscopic imaging on a clinical MR scanner
sampling points. Calculating the SNR between the trajectories then becomes only a matter
of comparing the noise, since the signal cancels out. The noise for all five trajectories was,
however converging upon the same value (see table 4). This does not support that multiple
samples in the same part of k-t-space should average out the noise, and hence reduce noise.
Even the CRA1−0.233 trajectory, where there is a quick reduction in ring radius, and every ring
is attenuated to kx = ky = 0 at tend, there is not seen any reduction in noise compared to CR.
To ensure that the simulations were done linearly and no scaling was done, the noise and signal
was reconstructed together and separately to ensure that the outcome was the same either way.
It was. However, fig. 23 shows that the fluctuations seen, when simulating once, might be due
to spatially correlated noise in the CRA1−0.233 image, which is not seen in the CR noise image.
This might stem from spatially low-frequency noise, due to the quicker narrowing of the rings
radii and attenuation over time.
The readout period of the implemented sequences is 160 ms per ring. With 38 rings, the
total acquisition time is 6080 ms. Using SENSE Pruessmann et al. [22] [23] have shown recon-
struction of data with a reduction factor of data of up to 6. High reduction factors will, however,
cause low SNR and noise augmentation, when using CG SENSE. From Pruessmann’s work it
seems that a reduction factor of 4 is the maximum without deteriorating the reconstructed
image. This might indicate that the number of rings could be reduced by 4, effectively reducing
the acquisition time by 4. The reduction factor could be increased if SNR was gained elsewhere.
Future Perspectives
In this thesis the T1, which is 30-40s [20], of hyperpolarized 1-13C]Pyruvate was not considered.
During the imaging the total signal will decay exponentially on a timescale of T1, which will
also cause a weighting of the rings in k-t-space. Similarly, every excitation will remove some of
the longitudinal magnetization. When combining both the T1 effect and the flip angle it has a
substantial effect on the signal over time, which should be investigated.
The sequence should be implemented on a clinical scanner with hyperpolarized 13C, to ensure
its feasibility.

8
Conclusion
A CRA spectroscopic sequence was successfully developed and simulated in JEMRIS. The simu-
lation was reconstructed using sensitivity maps, NUFFT and the iterative CG SENSE method.
The sequence was subsequently developed, using MATLAB toolboxes, for implementation on a
SIGNA Primier 3 T MR scanner at Rigshospitalet, using the MNS Research Pack. The recon-
structed simulated data from JEMRIS, and the data from the scanner showed similar features.
Regridding of the data showed bad spatial localization, but the spectral peaks were easily iden-
tified. When reconstructed iteratively with CG SENSE the spatial localization improved and
the noise was reduced in the spectral dimension.
Using the iterative CG SENSE reconstruction method, a reduction factor of 4 might be achiev-
able, which would significantly reduce acquisition time.
The 1D spectral PSF were identical for all simulated trajectories. The amplitude of the 1D
spatial PSF’s side lobes increased, the more the trajectory was attenuated. The amplitude of
the side lobes of CRA1.0.233 were the largest compared to the other trajectories. This was likely
due to the low coverage of high frequencies in the CRA trajectories. An increase in SNR for
the CRA trajectories was, however expected, but not seen in the noise simulation. The issue
does not lie in non-linearity or scaling in the reconstruction process, and therefore likely causes
needs to be further investigated.
Further studies should test the sequence on 13C, instead of water, and test the optimal re-
duction factor for CG SENSE. The effects on the hyperpolarized signal from T1 and the flip
angle should be implemented in the sequence design.
