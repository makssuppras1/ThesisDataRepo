Abstract
A novel numerical framework for investigating self-similar indentation problems in 2D and 3D
for elastic-plastic material is developed and demonstrated in the present work. The framework
is built around solving this type of problem by applying a spatial integration technique and thus
eliminating the issues associated with the traditional Lagrangian procedure. The history depen-
dence of the problem is obtained through performing the spatial integration along streamlines.
The main focus in the presented work is to expand the current state-of-the-art by allowing the
framework to be performed on an unstructured mesh. The method behind the newly developed
framework is ﬁrst veriﬁed on a structured mesh in 2D by comparing it to existing numerical
studies. Afterward, the framework is tested on an unstructured mesh yielding the same results
as the structured mesh. A detailed numerical parameter study is provided to reveal how the
relationship between the mesh discretization and the streamline resolution inﬂuences the analy-
sis. In particular, its inﬂuence on the stability of the iterative scheme is presented. In addition,
the accuracy of the analysis was found to be closely related to the structure of the mesh. As
a proof of concept and to advance the ﬁeld of steady-state frameworks in 3D, stress ﬁeld plots
are provided for three of the most common indenters, namely the conical indenter, the Vickers
indenter, and the Berkovich indenter.

Introduction
The task of engineers has always been to look for ways to put scientiﬁc discoveries into appli-
cation. This often involves describing the physical world through numerical tools. The ﬁnite
element tool has been prevalent for mechanical engineers when working with solid mechanics.
However, solving plasticity problems can be challenging due to the history dependency involved.
The most common approach to such problems is the Lagrangian incremental scheme, which is
computationally demanding. In this thesis, a steady-state framework is explained and refor-
mulated to solve a self-similar indentation problem in 2D and 3D for an unstructured mesh.
The new method is computationally faster and avoids numerical instabilities associated with
the Lagrangian incremental approach.
Dean and Hutchinson (1980) proposed a new method for solving problems characterized by
having a steady-state solution. This method is called the spatial integration method and was
originally applied to crack growth in a homogeneous solid, which was an alternative to the
general Lagrangian incremental approach. For a crack tip propagation, the diﬀerence between
the concepts of the two methods can be understood as follows: For the general Lagrangian
method, the crack tip will, to an observer, grow through the material in time and thus the stress-
and strain ﬁelds are time-dependent. However, if the observer were to travel along with the crack
tip, the observer would no longer experience any change in the stress- and strain ﬁeld, and with
this, the solution would be time-independent due to the observer never experiencing any change
in the ﬁeld. The introduction of this moving reference frame is the main idea behind the spatial
integration technique. In order to introduce this moving reference frame, the variables related
to the Lagrangian incremental method have to be converted into spatial derivatives associated
with the moving reference frame. Unlike the Lagrangian incremental schemes where integration
is done with respect to time, the steady-state framework obtains the history through performing
a spatial integration along speciﬁc lines. These lines are called streamlines and represent the
material ﬂow, such that the material passing through the domain is described by the spatial
integration method. Besides the computational speed, one of the most signiﬁcant advantages of
the method is that the domain will remain the same throughout the entire analysis.
Since its publication in 1980, many researchers have used the steady-state framework as a
strong tool to examine crack growths. Some of the subjects that have been investigated are:
Crack velocity dependent toughness in rate dependent materials (Landis et al., 2000), the strain
gradient eﬀects for rate-sensitive materials (Nielsen et al., 2012), ductile crack growth along a
laser weld (Niordson, 2001), crack growth characterized by strain gradient plasticity (Wei and
Hutchinson, 1997) and 3D-modeling of crack growth in a thin ductile plate under small-scale
yielding conditions (Sobotka and Dodds, 2011).
The method is capable of solving any steady-state problem, which is widely seen in manufac-
turing processes such as wire drawing, welding, and rolling. The potential of the method is
enormous due to the computational speed it provides. Up until now, the framework has been
very problem-speciﬁc since it would have to involve a speciﬁc mesh generated for the particular
problem to be solved. However, if the framework could be reformulated to a more general state,
the potential it could oﬀer commercially is immense, with the possibility to integrate simulations
of actual manufacturing processes on site.
Another framework that exploits the steady-state formulation was presented by Balagangad-
har et al. (1999), Balagangadhar and Tortorelli (2000), and Yu (2005).
Their method is a
displacement-based reference formulation where both Eulerian and Lagrangian characteristics
are used. The method relies on a mixed formulation where the history-dependent response ﬁelds

One of the biggest advantages of the framework is that convergence is guaranteed as opposed to

The steady-state framework by Dean and Hutchinson (1980) has later been expanded to non

This includes results for single-crystal

problems of single crystalline monazites (Juul et al., 2019). Using the steady-state framework for
self-similar problems is still a relatively unexplored subject. Examples where the self-similarity
exists are in particular scratch testing, void growth problems, stationary crack problems and
indentation problems with the latter one being the subject of this thesis.
The self-similar indentation problem is very similar to the steady-state problem. Instead of the
observer moving along a reference frame, the origin of the reference frame is kept constant. As
the indenter starts pushing deeper, the ﬁelds of stresses and strains will expand, but the shape
of the ﬁelds will remain the same. By exploiting the maintained shape of the ﬁelds, one can
imagine that if we were to zoom out at the exact same rate that the ﬁelds of stresses and strains
were expanding, the ﬁelds would become stationary. This would remove the time dependency of
the analysis, and this is the main idea behind the framework for solving self-similar indentation
problems. For self-similar problems, the history is also obtained through a spatial integration
of its streamlines. These streamlines will always emerge from a single point, which is from the
indenter tip for indentation problems.
The current state-of-the-art method dictates that the streamlines have to align with the Gauss
points of the mesh. A big drawback to this requirement is the limitation of the mesh, which
up until now has restricted the analysis to a radial mesh. In addition, another drawback is
that the placement of the Gauss points dictates the resolution of the analysis. This means if
another region of interest on the mesh is to be examined, it would require a new mesh with
new locations of Gauss points. Imagine that the dependence between streamlines and Gauss
points could be completely disregarded.
This would mean the analysis could be performed
independently of how the mesh was constructed. Moreover, this would entail that new regions
of interest could easily be examined on the same mesh and that the framework could easily
be implemented in commercial FEA software as a strong numerical tool for solving indentation
problems. This reformulation of the current state-of-the-art is the backbone of this thesis, and
Figure 1 illustrates the reformulation against the old way of solving indentation problems in 2D.
Figure 1a shows the old way of solving indentation problems where Gauss points and streamlines
have to align such that the mesh has to be radial. This method will throughout the thesis be
referred to as the Gauss-Method. Figure 1b illustrated the new method where streamlines can
be placed independently of the Gauss points. The need for a radial mesh is no longer necessary,
and it is possible to achieve the same results on an unstructured mesh. Even though this is quite
useful in 2D, the actual beneﬁts lie in the 3D analysis, where a 3D radial mesh is very undesired
due to its complexity. As of now, the framework for indentation problems in 3D has not yet
been achieved. In this work, the new reformulated method is shown to work with both great
precision and robustness in 2D. In 3D, the reformulation is found to be a very strong numerical

2.1
Self-similarity
The concept of self-similarity is used within many ﬁelds often related to describing fractals, such
as the ”koch snowﬂake”. In this thesis, the concept of self-similarity relates to the shape of the
solution for a given physical ﬁeld. A self-similar problem means that the shape of the solution for
a given physical ﬁeld remains constant with the deformation while the size of the physical ﬁelds
changes with the deformation. Two conditions must be respected for a self-similarity to exist.
The ﬁrst condition is that only one independent length parameter can exist, which is the contact
length between the indenter and the surface of the domain. This means the self-similarity only
exists for indenters with straight edges such as wedge indenters in 2D and conical indenters,
Vickers indenters, Berkovich indenters, etc. in 3D. The second conditions is that the expansion
of the physical ﬁelds has to be constant. This requires that the indentation rate is constant.

2.2
Spatial integration
The next step is to describe how the spatial integration is performed to obtain the history
dependence of the system. The solution of the self-similar problem is characterized by a scaling
of the physical ﬁeld where its shape is unchanged. The solution can be represented by straight
lines emerging from the indenter tip since the origin of the self-similar solution is located at this
point. These lines are called the streamlines, and the idea behind the streamlines are illustrated
in Figure 4 where a material point is tracked in time. The material point is marked by a plus
sign at three diﬀerent time frames. As illustrated in Figure 4 the physical ﬁeld will change in size
over time. Even though the location of the material point is unchanged, the relative position of
the material point inside the physical ﬁeld will have changed entirely. For the material point at
time t = 0 that is located at the edge of the physical ﬁeld, this same material point will lie in a
whole other position at time t = t1 and t = t2, and thus the physical quantity of the material
point must have changed in time. However, for the material point located at the edge of the
physical ﬁeld at time t = 0 there must exist a corresponding material point at the edge of the
physical ﬁeld at time t = t1 and t = t2 (marked with circles in Figure 4) lying on the same
streamline. These material points will have the same physical quantity as the material point at
time t = 0 but with diﬀerent locations on the streamline.
For an expanding physical ﬁeld, a material point must be getting relatively closer to the origin

2.3
Principle of virtual work
Between each integration point, a number of subpoints are added. These are chosen adaptive
based on the gradient found between the two integration points as a stabilizer for regions with
large stress gradients. For large stress gradients, many subpoints are added to ensure a more
accurate evolution of ﬁeld quantities is found. The diﬀerence between integration points and
subpoints is that for subpoints, the ﬁeld quantities are based on the already found strain gradient
between the integration points.
The idea is that it has a lower computational cost, and if
a suﬃcient number of integration points are chosen, the subpoints can be used with a good
approximation. For subpoints located outside the plastic zone, these are obviously redundant due
to the linear relation between the stress and strains. However, for the case with one integration
point inside the plastic region and one in the elastic region, the subpoints are of great importance
since they will catch the transition between the two zones more accurately and thus the history-
dependence. Inside the plastic region, the subpoints will inﬂuence the tangent modulus and will
thus provide a better precision at a lower computational cost. Another reason for the added
subpoints is that the integration path for each streamline is individual, and for easy bookkeeping
of the solution, it is, in general, a good idea to have the same amount of integration points in
each streamline, which is obtained by instead letting the subpoints be adaptive.

3.2.1
Newton Raphson scheme
The Newton Raphson scheme works in the following way. First, a starting guess of the natural
coordinates is required (ξ0 and η0). From the shape function, the new ”guessed” global coordi-
nate (P0) is found from the natural coordinates. If the diﬀerence of the ”guessed” coordinates
(P0) and the actual coordinates (P) are not below a threshold ϵN = 10−12, then a new guess
must be used:
|P −P0| ≤ϵN
(25)
The updated guess for ξ0 and η0 in the next iteration are found by subtracting the inverse of
the Jacobian matrix times the diﬀerence of the two coordinates:
ξ0 = ξ0 −J−1(P0 −P)
η0 = η0 −J−1(P0 −P)
(26)
From these new natural coordinates, P0 is updated, and Equation (25) are once again evaluated.
If the criterion is still not met, the natural coordinates are updated once more according to
Equation (26). This scheme continues until the convergence criterion given in Equation (25)
is fulﬁlled. The scheme is very eﬃcient and requires only a few iterations to ﬁnd the natural
coordinates.
3.2.2
Pitfalls
It should be noted that only four areas have been evaluated in Equation (24), which is not strictly
correct since the quadratic elements used has eight nodes, and hence eight areas ”should” have
been evaluated. However, even if all eight nodes had been evaluated, there is no guarantee that
this method will ﬁnd the correct element since the lines between two nodes are not straight for a
quadratic element. Figure 8 shows an element with a point seemingly lying within the element.
The dotted line marks the actual borders of the element, and this is one of the cases where a
”wrong” element is found with the area method presented in Section 3.2.

return a value for either ξ or η outside the interval [-1,1]. This means that every time the area
method ﬁnds an incorrect element (around 1% of the elements for a radial mesh), the correct
element must be a neighboring element.

3.3
Spacing of integration points and streamlines
In order to achieve accurate results with the fewest possible streamlines and integration points,
the placement of streamlines and integration points were investigated. Figure 9 illustrates a
linear and logarithmic spacing of the integration points. The linear spacing worked for the most
part, but a very ﬁne resolution was needed to catch the plasticity close to the indenter tip. The
logarithmic spacing had the advantage of a very large spacing at the outer elements where only
elastic strains are present, and close to the indenter tip, a very small spacing was present such
that the plastic zone could be calculated more accurately. For this reason, a logarithmic spacing
was chosen and is used throughout the rest of the report.
Figure 9:
Schematic of the indenter tip with a linear spaced streamline in blue to the left and a
logarithmic spaced streamline in red to the right.
The logarithmic spacing is performed in an adaptable fashion such that it is possible to control
how big the diﬀerence in the spacing is between the inner points and the outer points. This is
done by a shift in the logarithmic function. The integration points of the streamlines do not
start at the very center of the indenter tip (0, 0) since this would create singularities in the stress
ﬁeld for the elements in contact with the indenter. On the other hand, the distance from the
indenter tip should not surpass the distance to the ﬁrst Gauss point, or else the mapping from the
streamline to the Gauss point is not possible, which is elaborated in Section 3.4. Therefore, the
distance to the integration point furthest downstream is computed as half the distance between
the indenter tip and the Gauss point radial closest to the indenter tip.
Another important aspect to consider was the placement of the streamlines. With no apparent
advantages of the placements, the streamlines were equally distributed with the same angle
between each streamline. However, a problem to consider was the ﬁrst and the last streamline.
If these streamlines were oriented horizontally, the same pitfall as described in Section 3.2.2 was
encountered due to the nature of the shape function of the quadratic elements. To prevent this,
these two streamlines are angled such that they are not aligning with the borders of the top
elements and are not angled so low that they are below any Gauss points since the values of the
stresses in the Gauss points needs to be interpolated from the integration points.

streamlines are evenly distributed with the same angle between each streamline, adding another
streamline moves every streamline in the mesh. If stress gradients are circumferentially large,
this movement of streamlines will considerably inﬂuence the mapping scheme and thus provide
radically diﬀerent stress results. As the number of streamlines is increased, this eﬀect becomes
minor since the movement of the streamlines becomes smaller and smaller.
For both resolutions of integration points, a low number of streamlines causes big errors in
stresses.
This is due to multiple Gauss points encapsulating the same triangles used in the
mapping scheme. Having multiple Gauss points being based on the same linear interpolation
heavily decreases the accuracy of stresses. This also explains why the error of stresses is so
large compared to the error in displacement for a low number of streamlines. For less than 50
streamlines, the convergence criterion in Equation (29) is for most of the cases never reached,
and the iterations are stopped at 1000. Most of the solutions obtained for such low streamlines

4.1.4
Concluding remarks
The eﬀect of both the number of streamlines and integration points has been outlined, and
the analyses were seen to provide excellent results with as few as 200 streamlines and 300
integration points. However, what has not been considered is the mesh. The mesh is a crucial
factor in the parameter study, and therefore, it can not be stressed enough that the following
parameter study is only applicable for the exact mesh where it was performed. Choosing the right
amount of streamlines and integration points can not be generalized but needs to be considered
individually for each mesh to be examined. If the mesh is very coarse, it is inappropriate to
choose a large number of streamlines and integration points since these parameters will only
upscale the calculation time and provide just as poor results as the mesh dictates. On the other
hand, choosing an inappropriate small number of streamlines and integration points for a very
ﬁne mesh will also provide poor results, as was shown in both Sections 4.1.2 and 4.1.3.

contact length without rerunning the overlay. However, if another mesh is to be used or another
resolution of the streamlines is to be used, a new overlay must be rerun.

Discussion
It was found that the method of placing streamlines independent of the Gauss points provided
excellent results on a radial mesh in 2D. For a rectangular mesh, the stresses of the elements
around the indenter were found to be inaccurate.
The inaccuracy is assumed to be caused
by a too coarse discretization of the elements around the indenter. Another possibility is the
resolution of the mesh. It was concluded from the parameter study that 500 streamlines and 1000
integration points would be suﬃcient for the forthcoming 2D analysis. However, the parameter
study was performed on the radial mesh, and thus there is no guarantee that this choice of
streamlines and integration points were suﬃcient for the rectangular mesh. Lastly, the mapping
scheme was chosen to be based on neighboring streamlines. This scheme is seen to provide good
results when both the resolution of streamlines and integration points are chosen suﬃciently
high. However, a weak spot of the method is if the ratio between streamlines and integration
points is high. This would cause the ratio between the side lengths of the interpolation triangles
in the mapping scheme to become high as well. This is undesired since the problem has large
stress gradients both radially and circumferential. The accuracy of the analysis is thus dependent
on the analyst to pick reasonable sizes of both parameters. Therefore, it could be considered
changing the mapping scheme.
A new way of mapping could be to do it across the three
integration points closest to the Gauss point instead of the premade triangles.
An important aspect of the analysis is the placement of both the streamlines and the integration
points. This project used a logarithmic distribution for the integration points, which was based
on the distribution of Gauss points for the 2D radial mesh.
Ideally, the integration points’
distribution should be chosen according to the mesh. As discussed previously, the integration
points are highly connected to the Gauss points of the elements. One can not provide accurate
results without the other. This is also one of the drawbacks upon the method. The method can
only provide accurate results if a good mesh is provided. The current implementation with the
linear placement of streamlines is a little unambitious. It is known that some areas of the stress
ﬁeld have much larger stress gradients circumferential than others. For this reason, these areas
should be implemented to have a higher density of streamlines. This could easily be implemented
by placing the streamlines more adaptive. However, as this project is the ﬁrst study to apply
the method of independent streamlines, the aim has been to prove the validity of the method.
The method of placing streamlines independent of the Gauss points in 3D was shown to work
but also subjected to several limitations. First, the method’s eﬀectiveness got lost due to the
very time-consuming mapping scheme in the overlay. Furthermore, the eﬃciency of the iterative
scheme was lost due to the solving of the linear system [K]{U} = {R}. It is possible to optimize
the eﬀectiveness for both the overlay and the linear solver. For the mapping scheme, a new
search algorithm needs to be implemented.
Searching blindly through the whole domain is
very ineﬃcient. Extending the mapping scheme such that a qualiﬁed guess is used to ﬁnd the
hexahedron where each Gauss point is located could optimize the overlay enormously. Likewise,
solving the linear system could be parallelized to speed up the process. This would give access
to creating much ﬁner discretizations in 3D and thus understanding the details of the indenter
types to a greater extent. However, neither of these tasks was within the scope of the project.
The scope was to generalize the approach to work even on an unstructured mesh in 3D, which
succeeded.

Conclusion
In this work, an extension of the steady-state framework by Dean and Hutchinson (1980) is
developed to solve self-similar indentation problems in 2D and 3D for an unstructured mesh. The
framework uses a spatial integration technique that relies on an iterative scheme to determine the
solution and therefore oﬀers a signiﬁcant advantage over the traditional incremental Lagrangian
solver. The spatial integration technique obtains the history along streamlines. The main focus
in the presented work is to verify and develop the framework on an unstructured mesh. The
veriﬁcation is performed by comparing a 2D structured mesh to existing numerical results to
validate the method. Afterward, a 2D unstructured mesh has been validated by comparison
to existing numerical results.
It was found that the accuracy of the analysis was dictated
by the number of streamlines, the discretization of the mesh, and the structure of the mesh.
A structured radial mesh in 2D and a spherical mesh in 3D were found to provide the best
accuracy to element ratio. This was found to be caused by the streamlines of the self-similarity
emanating radially in 2D and spherically in 3D. The relationship between the streamlines and
the discretization of the mesh were investigated and showed a close dependency. Increasing the
number of streamlines without increasing the discretization of the mesh would not yield more
accurate results, and increasing the discretization of the mesh without increasing the number
of streamlines would make the iterative scheme unstable. In general, it was found that a poor
choice of mesh discretization would make the streamline integration overshoot.
A full 3D simulation of the self-similar indentation problem was enabled by generalizing the
streamline integration to an unstructured mesh. This has up until this point not been possible,
and the results are groundbreaking. The stability of the iterative streamline integration scheme
was investigated, and the stability was seen to be tightly coupled to the relation between the
discretization and the streamline resolution in the same manner as for the 2D problem. The
full 3D simulations have been performed for a conical indent, a Vickers indent, and a Berkovich
indent showing great results and advancing the state-of-the-art within this ﬁeld.
7.1
Future work
With the full 3D simulations working, the method’s potential is enormous.
Multiple exten-
sions can be added to the code for further state-of-the-art research. The obvious extension to
the working 3D code is to implement the ﬁnite strain formulation, such that the code is not
limited to shallow indentations. Another interesting implementation would be new material
models. Previously crystal plasticity has been modeled in 2D, making the extension to 3D a
straightforward choice. In addition, other material models such as the hyperelastic model or the
hypoelastic model are great possibilities.
Some of the more mundane tasks to be solved, but of no less importance, are to increase the
algorithm’s eﬀectiveness. As discussed in the thesis, the streamlines are distributed with equal
spacing across the domain. Implementing an adaptive scheme that could place more streamlines
where stress gradients are large circumferential would improve the accuracy at these regions
and speed up the program by providing fewer streamlines in areas of small gradients. Another
task that would speed up the program is parallelizing the solver and the mapping scheme.
Furthermore, implementing a search algorithm for the mapping scheme (instead of searching
the whole domain blindly) could heavily reduce the computational time of the overlay. Lastly,
an easy ﬁx for getting a smoother stress ﬁeld plot is to create a routine that saves the nodal
solution of the stresses and hereby allows a smoothing of the stress ﬁeld plots.
