Abstract
Error correction codes are crucial for ensuring robust and reliable transmission of data
in communication systems and traditional error correction techniques often struggle with
distributing data though multiple channels. This motivated the design of a new class of
codes, called Fountain codes, and later refined into Raptor codes.
This paper presents a design and FPGA implementation of the inner Raptor code and
provides insight to numerous design considerations. In order to verify the implementation
of the inner Raptor code, a series of simulations and hardware tests are run. The test
results are evaluated against the theory of Raptor codes to evaluate the correctness of
the design. Additionally, the design analyses two methods of structuring and generating
encoded symbols and compares their impact on the decoders ability to recover the original
data. The throughput of the design is found and put in relation to the degree of which the
decoder is parallelised. Lastly the size and throughput of the implementation is compared
to LDPC codes.
It was found that the implementation of the decoder allows for parallelisation of 160, which
allows for a throughput of at least 43.67Mbps. Tests showed that the initial structure of
the encoded symbols performed poorly and that the second structure fared far better. The
comparison with the LDPCs revealed that the throughput of the design was only one-tenth
the LDPCs, while using a larger area. However, it was argued that the code is better at
handling transmission to multiple receivers with different channel conditions.
The implementation presented can be optimised further and through optimisation the de-
sign could improve its throughput considerably, but likely not to the point of the LDPCs.

Introduction
Reliable and efficient transmission of data over the internet has become a crucial part
of today’s society. And an ever increasing number of key applications and services is
becoming part of the internet. It is therefore no surprise that different methods of providing
a reliable transmission have been developed. One well known method is the TCP protocol
which, achieves its reliability by facilitating retransmission of lost data. However TCP
has been shown to not perform well, in channels exhibiting a large degree of loss, when
transmission time is high. Furthermore, TCP also struggles during broadcast/multicast.
These issues is mostly due to TCP being an ack-based protocol. Another method of
improving the reliability is through the use of traditional erasure codes. However these
codes often have to make a trade-off between rate and erasure correcting capabilities,
and therefore lack the ability to adapt to changes in the channel.
Fountain codes are a class of codes, which is well suited to provide reliable transmission
of data to multiple receivers over poor channels. Fountain code’s name stem from the en-
coders ability to generate an infinite stream of encoded symbols, which is often depicted
as a fountain. The decoder collects a fixed number of encoded symbols to successfully
recover the original data, often depicted as a bucket. The stream of potentially infinite
symbols and the decoder’s ability to use any subset of these, gives Fountain codes the
ability to adapt to changing channel conditions. This structure also works well in broad-
cast/multicast systems.
The first realisation of Fountain codes were LT codes, wherein a process of encoding
using a degree distribution, called Robust Soliton Distribution, and a random selection
of input symbols, was introduced [1]. LT codes also introduces a method of decoding,
wherein a graph is slowly deconstructed to recover the data. Late Raptor codes was
introduced which improved on the cost of the LT code by concatenating it with an erasure
pre-code. The addition of the pre-code allows the LT code to only recover a constant
fraction of the original data, leaving the remaining data to the pre-code. An example of
Raptor codes being used is in the ATSC 3.0 standard part A/331 [2], where it is used
in broadcasting of video streams and file delivery. Additionally [3], specifies that Raptor
codes can be used when implementing Multimedia Broadcast/Multicast Service(MBMS)
in 5G and LTE.
This thesis implements the inner Raptor code, called the LT code, in an FPGA. The perfor-
mance and correctness of the implementation is analysed and contrasted with other FEC
code, like the LDPC. The first chapter goes through the theory behind Fountain codes and
LT codes, with a quick introduction of how a raptor degree distribution can be found. The
second chapter goes through the choices made when designing the LT code, as well as
how the LT code is implemented in hardware. The testing and the results are provided in
chapter four. Lastly the challenges and imperfection in the implementation is discussed
in chapter six.

Fountain codes
Fountain codes, also known as rateless erasure codes, is a class of codes with a range
of coding techniques. This paper will focus on Fountain codes in the context of LT and
Raptor codes which utilise irregular sparse graphs for the encoding and decoding algo-
rithm. The use of sparse-graphs can also be found in LDPC codes[4]. Furthermore, the
focus will be on symbols and operations over the field F2, as it lends itself well to hardware
implementations.
Fountain codes aim to improve the reliability and robustness of transmissions over fixed
rate erasure codes, by adapting to any erasure probability p < 1 [5]. This is known as
a rate-less code. They are designed for transmission of symbols over a binary erasure
channel(BEC), characterised by the probability p that an erasure occurs. The capacity of
the BEC is related to the probability p that an erasure happens, which has been shown
by Peter Elias to be 1 −p [5]. Fountain codes achieve this without retransmission by,
streaming an potentially infinite stream of encoded symbols.
To reliably transmit data over a BEC with p < 1, without retransmission, the encoder must
have the capacity to encode an infinite amount of encoded symbols from the same set of
k input symbols. The receiver should then be able to reconstruct the original k symbols
from any subset of n = (1 + ϵ)k encoded symbols [4, 5], ϵ is the overhead. Additionally
the overhead, in relation to the codes ability to recover the original symbols, is often used
to evaluate the code’s performance. This is unlike fixed rate codes where the SNR curve
is often used.
The most essential part of Fountain codes is the degree distribution D, which allows for the
constructing of more compact graphs by organising the degrees of the encoded symbols.
To encode a single symbol a degree must first be obtained, which is done by sampling
from a degree distribution. This degree then determines the number of input symbols
used in the calculation of the encoded symbol [5]. From this it can be seen that the
degree distribution plays a large part in determining the encoding and decoding cost of a
Fountain code. The parameters of a Fountain code often include the degree distribution
together with the number of input symbols k, as (k, D)

how to design a degree distribution, which can cover all k input symbols with a practical
number of encoded symbols. LT codes creates a sparse tanner graph during encoding by
repeating alg. 1 to produce a potentially infinite stream of encoded symbols from k input
symbols. LT codes are able to encode an infinite stream of encoded symbols, from which
the LT decoder can recover the k input symbols in a BEC with a probability of erasure of
p < 1 [5].

LT decoding is done by constructing a tanner graph from n encoded symbols and then by
deconstructing the graph over multiple iterations, as described in alg. 2. The LT decoder
can successfully recover the k input symbols, from any of the n = (1 + ϵ)k encoded
symbols, with probability at least 1 −δ [1]. Notice that alg. 1 lacks the edges connecting
the encoded symbol to the input symbols, which is required to construct the tanner graph
at the decoder. How the edges are communicated to the decoder, is decided by individual
implementation.
Deconstructing the graph to recover the k input symbols has three steps. First, an en-
coded symbol with a degree of 1 is selected. Notice that when d = 1 in alg. 1 no XOR
operation are done, and the input and encoded symbols is therefore identical.
All encoded symbols, connected to the input symbol, are then XOR’ed with the value of
the input symbol. Suppose that the decoder knows the initial values of c1 and c2, as well
as their constituent symbols, the values of which are not yet known:
c1 = m1
c2 = m1 ⊕m2
c1 is known to have a degree of one and is therefore XOR’ed with all other encoded
symbols with a connection to m1:
c1 ⊕c2 = m1 ⊕m1 ⊕m2
= m2
The results is that the encoded symbol c2’s degree becomes one, and now contains the
value of the input symbol m2.
As the last step, the degree of the encoded symbols is reduced by one and the edges to
the input symbol are deleted. This process repeats until all input symbols are recovered
or no encoded symbols of degree one are left, in which case a decoding failure occurs.
Figure 2.3 shows the decoding of the graph constructed in fig. 2.2, and the entire process
is described by alg. 2.

Raptor
Raptor codes are a concatenated code, consisting of an erasure correcting precode and
an LT code. The motivation behind Raptor codes is to reduce the average symbol cost,
from O(ln(k)) to constant cost, by reducing the amount of symbols which the LT code must
recover to a constant fraction. In [5], the author shows that the presented implementation
result in a linear number of edges in the graph, compared with the non-linear k · ln(k)
edges when all input symbols are recoverable using LT coding. The input symbols not
recovered by the LT code must then be recovered by the precode.

Hardware design of LT code
This section provides insight into how the LT code has been implemented in an FPGA, with
descriptions of the different choices made during the design of the encoder and decoder.
LT codes are heavily dependent on the degree distribution and random selection of sym-
bols to perform the encoding of the encoded symbols. The decoder must then use the
encoded symbol and the knowledge of its neighbours to succeed in decoding. How the
neighbours are communicated to the decoder is not specified by the LT code, and the
design must therefore establish a means to express the neighbours ID to the decoder.
Here the ID is the memory address of the neighbour.
A simple way of communicating the neighbours would be to add a header with all the
addresses of the original symbols, which was used to calculate the encoded symbol,
directly. However this comes at the cost of a large per-symbol overhead, with an average
of a = 5.87 addresses used in each encoded symbol. The largest overhead would be
66 · 16 bits, which makes the use of smaller symbols sizes impractical.
The average overhead of each encoded symbol when using this method is:
aPerNode = ⌈log2(k)⌉· a
(3.1)
Rather than transmitting the individual addresses, a seed-based address compression ap-
proach can be used. This reduces the overhead down to a single seed and degree, where
the degree communicates the number of neighbours contained in the encoded symbol,
which is not communicated by the seed. Seeding a random generator and appending
the seed along with the degree ensures that, the decoder can determine the neighbours
of the encoded symbol. The decoder determine the neighbours by, seeding an identical
random generator and, sequencing it d times. The seed-based method improves on the
overhead, when compared with previous method, but has the downside of increasing the
complexity as a greater level of control is required.
The overhead of each symbols depends on the total number of input symbols k and the
largest degree possible ΩD and, can be expressed:
aPerNode = ⌈log2(k)⌉+ ⌈log2(ΩD)⌉
(3.2)
The overhead of each encoded symbol can be improved further by using existing infor-
mation contained in the data flow, such as sequence numbers, packet headers, when a
symbol was received, or implicit information e.g. the position relative to other symbols
[1]. This implies that the overhead of each symbols can essentially be reduced to zero.
However, using existing or implicit information can be very dependent on the system in
which the LT code is implemented and, would most likely require a more complex system
to generate the addresses at the encoder and decoder.
In the design, seed-based address compression has been chosen to facilitate the commu-
nicate of the composition of encoded symbols. It was chosen because of the substantial
reduction in the overhead. Especially, when compared with the first option of directly
adding the addresses to the header. Additionally, seeding and controlling a random gen-
erator, such as a PRBS, is a simple task in comparison to using implicit or external infor-
mation as, described in the third option.

The design realises the seed-based address compression, by using PRBS to generate

A second PRBS is used in the design to generate degrees with the desired degree dis-
tribution. This is done by matching the random value from the PRBS with a series of
thresholds. Using a PRBS is possible because, it has a uniform distribution with the range

once it will repeat the same sequence again, and that make the PRBS deterministic and

The design uses a k of 65535 with the degree distribution given in table 2.1 and, encoded

This encourages the use of a large symbol size to obtain a smaller overhead, which could
potentially increase the performance of the design. One way of accomplishing this in
practice would be to apply LT coding to k packets and, when this is done to an Ethernet
packet the smallest possible symbol size becomes 64 bytes. Increasing the symbol size
costs memory and, the encoder would need to store k-times the symbol size and the
decoder (1 + ϵ)k–times the symbol size. When using large symbols sized a problem can
arise where not enough data is present to be split into k input symbols and padding must
be added, which reduces the performance of the design.
The system has therefore been designed to use a symbol size of 512 bits, the minimum
size of an Ethernet packet, to examining the performance of the design when coding entire
packets. The memory used by the encoder must therefore store k-times the symbols size,
and with k = 65536 the total memory usage becomes 512bits · k = 33.554.432bits or just
over 4MB.

The DDR4 memory imposes some limits and challenges on the design and, one obvious
limit imposed by the DDR4 is its data rate. And while boards with faster memory exist,
such as the High Bandwidth Memory(HBM), they are not used due to their increased
implementation complexity. DDR4 performance is very dependent on the how the read
and write addresses being sequenced and, performs best during sequential reads and
writes and worst during random reads and writes, see table 3.2. Due to the structure of
a LT codes the addresses received by the DDR4 is decided randomly and this results
in disjoint addressing, which decreases the DDR4 bus efficiency to about 24% for most
of its operation[6]. The efficiency might be increased with clever use of address storage
and sorting, however this is beyond the scope of the design. The performance hit of
random reads and writes is part of the reason why the symbol size of 512 bits was chosen,
as it maximises the number of random reads and writes. This is because any symbols
above 512 bits would be stored on more than 1 address, which increases the number of
sequential read and writes.
Reading data from the DDR4 is done by applying the enable (app_en) and command
(app_cmd), as seen in fig. 3.3. The response from the DDR4 is then, with some variation,
received later and in the same order as requested, see bottom of fig. 3.3. Notice that the
DDR4 does not respond with what address the read data belongs to. The design must
therefore store the addresses of the outgoing requests until the DDR4 responds. The
design manages this by writing the addresses of all read requests into a FIFO, which at
the decoder is used to write updated symbols back into memory, and at the encoder it is
used to select the seed.
A major challenge of using DDR4 is the app_rdy signal, which is used to signal if the
DDR4 is ready to receive user input, which is only accepted when the signal is high,
see fig. 3.4. This might not appear as much of a challenge, being reminiscent of the
full/empty signal in a FIFO interface and, while the DDR4 do behave like this to some

Controller
The design implements a controller to manage the DDR4 reads and writes correctly, leav-

from the DDR4 until, the app_rdy signal becomes zero. The controller then transition to
the WaitReady state waiting for the DDR4 to become ready, it then returns to the read or
write state. Immediately after receiving the last input symbols the controller transitions to
the Wait empty state, where it waits for the DDR4 write FIFO to be emptied.

fig. 3.13.

signal as an enable signal. Additionally a counter based on the input degree is used, to

3.3
Header Decoder
All header information, the seed and degree, received by the decoder is routed to the
header decoder component. Here it is stored inside RAM block(s) and processed in ac-
cordance with the Raptor/LT decoding procedure mention in section 2.1. At the top of
the header decoder is an FSM controlling one or many RAM controllers components, the
amount of RAM controllers being dependent on the degree of parallelisation. Each of
the RAM controllers are also connected to an address generator. The RAM controller(s)
is controlled using an FSM and together with the address generator, runs the Raptor/LT
decoding algorithm. The RAM controller communicates with the top FSM to synchronise
the RAM controllers to ensure that the decoding is done correctly.
The state diagram of the FSM, seen fig. 3.15, has a total of 4 states. The initial state
is the Write Input state where the headers of the packets received from the encoder are
loaded into the RAM. Selecting which RAM the input is routed to is determined by the
⌈log2(NumbofRAM)⌉MSB of the write counter, transitioning to the next state once these
bits are equal to the number of RAM blocks. Thereafter the FSM alternates between three
states until decoding fails or succeeds. First the FSM signal the ram controllers to find
a node of degree 1 until one of the controllers return the valid seed of a degree one. In
the Generate state it transmits the seed to all RAM controllers and signals them to start
generating addresses, using the gen_start signal, it then enters the Wait State next. Once
all RAM controllers have finished updating their internal RAM, the process starts over.

3.3.1
RAM controller
While the Header Controller is responsible for coordinating the RAM controllers, the RAM
controllers themselves are where Raptor/LT decoding is performed. Figure 3.16 shows
a block diagram of the RAM controller, with a general overview of the how the design
functions. As data is received by the RAM controllers a mask is appended to the seed and
degree before being stored in RAM, this mask is a 66 binary mask. The mask identifies
which of the 66 addresses that can be generated from the seed are valid addresses. In
other words the mask is used to identify the edges removed in the previous iterations.
The decoding process has a total of 3 steps, first a degree of 1 is found. this is done in
the Find Node State by running through the entire RAM block until a node with degree 1 is
found. If a degree one node was found, the FSM moves to the Gen NodeOne State next.
In this state the address generator, seen in fig. 3.14, runs a PRBS until a valid address
has been generated. The address found is then communicated to the Header Decoder
using the seed_valid signal.
If at any point the gen_start is received, the RAM controller must then move towards the
Generate State. Note the gen_start signal means that the address of a degree one node
has been found, this address is transmitted along side the gen_start signal. However
when in the Gen NodeOne State it must wait for the address generator to finish, due to
the component being shared by both the Generate and Gen NodeOne State. The address
generator uses gen_last to signal that it is done, seen in the state diagram in fig. 3.17,
where it either changes state to the Wait GenLast State, or if the gen_start and gen_last
is 1 simultaneously, the Generate State.
The RAM controller then remains in the Generate state to remove all addresses matching
the address, received with the gen_start signal, from the RAM. Removing all matching
addresses is done by running all RAM entries through the address generator. Each time
a match is found within an entry it has the degree decreased and the corresponding mask
bit flipped to a zero. Once the address generator is done with an entry it is written back
into RAM with an updated degree and mask. In the bottom right corner of fig. 3.16, it can
be seen how the degree is updated by subtracting one from each matching address.
Updating the mask, seen in the bottom center of fig. 3.16, is done be addressing a ROM
with a counter. This counter is ticks each time the address generator produces a valid
output, and the counter is set to zero when a new entry is provide to the address generator.

3.5
Decoder component
The Data decoder components contain 512 registers set to the data of the degree one
node, found by the Header Decoder, the ensuing data is XOR’ed with the registers and
then routed to the DDR4 Controller.
As an example, when the degree one node in fig. 3.20 is found by the Header Decoder,
it is read from the DDR4 into the Data Decoder which is then stored in the registers. The
Header Controller then locates all the nodes connected to the top left node in fig. 3.20, and
sends the address to the DDR4 Controller. This is then received by the Data Decoder and
XOR’ed with the first node before being sent to the DDR4 Controller, which then updates
the data of the top left node, stored in the DDR4. What is effectively done is, the degree
one symbol is read and then all other symbols with and edge to it are updated and written
back into memory. The output of the decoder is the data of the degree one node and its
address in the encoder.

Tests and Results
Tests have been performed to both examine and verify that the components operate as
expected. The simulation tests use the tool Open Source VHDL Verification Methodol-
ogy(OSVVM), as using the OSVVM simplifies and speeds up the design of tests.
OSVVM is an open source verification tool aiming to improve upon verification by provid-
ing a number of functionalities such as, random values, scoreboard and coverage tools.
Together with tools for reporting test conditions, results and errors, in a readable manner.
To use OSVVM effectively VHDL-2008 must be supported, as it relies on structures not
supported in previous versions, with some newer functionally locked behind VHDL-2019
support. OSVVM provides most of its functionality through functions and procedures, and
it is through these, along with the OSVVM Testbench Framework that the simulation tests
are performed. An example of the OSVVM testbench framework can be seen in fig. 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Example of the OSVVM testbench framework, from [10].
The OSVVM framework consist of three components, the test controller, verification com-
ponent(s) and the device under test(DUT). The test controller manages the verification
components through the use of transaction records, transaction records is a VHDL record
containing parameters, options, data signals and operations. The operations is a type of
command sent by the test controller to the verification component, which then drives and
wiggles the DUT interface. The simulation using OSVVM uses the Model Independent
Transactions(MIT) this is a ready made transaction record containing a host of proce-
dures, functions and operations, reducing the time to develop the verification components.
The verification component can contain a range of complex or simple operations. The data
used by the operation is provided by the test controller through the transaction record. The
advantage of using OSVVM is a reusable structure between tests of different components.

Discussion
The LT decoding has been tested using two different procedures in constructing the tanner
graph, in the first procedure the graph is constructed by sequencing the degree PRBS
after the seed PRBS had generated d addresses, seen in fig. 5.2. The second procedure
involved sequencing the two PRBSs together, with each output being the seed of an
encoded symbol. Where the addresses within the symbols are the seed and d sequences
of a identical PRBS, seen in fig. 5.1. Examining the result points to the success of the
Header Decoder being dependent on how the Graph Generator constructs the encoded
symbols.
The first procedure is the one implemented in the encoder and the second procedures is
the one used to debug and verify the Header Decoder.
The tests indicates, that the second procedure performs the best, as it generally recovers
the most input symbols. However this approach reproduces the same encoded symbols
with a period p, equal to the period of the PRBSs, as seen in fig. 5.1. And while increasing
the size of the seed PRBS increased the period, it came with the cost of the encoder not
being able to confidently cover all input symbols.
Furthermore, in section 2, it is implied that n encoded symbols must cover all input sym-
bols with a probability of at least 1 −δ. And while there might exist seeds, which possibly
could cover all input symbols, non was found during testing. Using a ”good” seed is not
enough to ensure a high decoding probability, as the initial seed and period becomes less
defined once part of the symbols is erased. Suppose the use of a good initial seed, where
the first quarter of the period is erased and the rest is received without erasure. This is
roughly the same as the initial seed being the value of the PRBS, at the time when the
erasures stopped.

Conclusion
This thesis has investigated how the inner code of a Raptor code with a large symbol size,
can be implemented inside an FPGA. Furthermore the speed and size of the encoder and
decoder has been analysed.
To support large symbol sizes, a choice was made to use the available DDR4 memory
which, because of the code structure, had a low efficiency of 24% limiting the data rate
of the encoder to one-fourth the DDR4 rate. The DDR4 bottlenecked the encoder which
resulted in an estimated encoding speed of around 34Gbps. However in the total system
the decoder was seen to have the lowest data rate, with the limiting factor being the
number of reads the Header Decoder could perform in a single clock cycle. This resulted
in an estimated average rate of 43.67Mbps, when configuring the Header Decoder to be
parallelised by 160. The result of the decoder has been compared to two LDPC codes,
which performed better in both speed and size, and it was explained how the LDPC are
better suited to high speed applications. And it was argued that Raptor codes are better
suited for broadcast and multicast, since they do not need to adapt to the channel condition
of multiple receives.
The graph created by the encoder was observed to not recover all of the encoded symbols
with the limited number of seeds provided. It was thought that the structure of this graph,
could be the cause of the failure, and to investigate if the failure was due to the imple-
mentation or graph structure, a software test was developed. The result of the software
test indicated that the implementation was correct, as both the software and hardware
implementation failed at the exact same point. And it was therefore speculated that, the
reason behind the decoding failures would most likely be the graph structure.
To further investigate the failure, a second graph structure was constructed by connecting
the Graph Generator to the Header Decoder. This resulted in a graph structure where the
constituents of the encoder symbols had a high degree of overlap. Additionally, the graph
had the drawback of repeating the same encoded symbols with the same period as the
two PRBSs. However, the Header Decoder was able to successfully decode the graph for
all of the seeds tested, which indicated further that the fault was with the graph structure
created by the encoder.
The impact of the PRBS choice was investigated through a range of PRBS types, which
indicated that the seed generation should use a PRBS operating in the range of k. As
oversizing the PRBS by just one caused the coverage to decrease to 85.93% over a
period of 81920. The impact of PRBSs on the degree distribution was tested and the
results showed that, using an oversized PRBS caused a deviation from the actual degree
distribution. It was also argued that a leap-ahead PRBS structure should be used, as the
tests provided a degree output which appeared more random. And it was observed that
it reduced the number of identical degrees bunched together, which was attributed to the
increased randomness.

Future work
The LT encoders simple design leaves a design with one possible bottleneck, the DDR4
memory, and any future work should be focused on either replacing it with better per-
forming memory such as HBM or improving the read efficiency. Achieving better read
efficiency requires that, as many reads as possible is done sequentially. A potential im-
provement could be to store and sort the address, together with an identifier in memory
before any read request is sent to the DDR4. Then, at the output another round of stor-
age and sorting to ensure that data is received correctly at the encoder components input,
or instead, by using enough encoder components to handle all of the output nodes, this
would also require some added routing logic.
It might also be possible to improve the resource utilisation of the encoder by removing the
FIFO, by modifying the encoder. Removing the FIFO at the DDR4 module would require
a second seed/address generator synchronised to the same seed as the one used by the
graph generator. However, this could make the previously mentioned DDR4 efficiency
improvement harder to implement.
An improvement to the decoder could be to create a tree structure in the Header Decoder
to improve timing and allow for higher degrees of parallelisation. This would introduce a
bit of latency, but increase the throughput.
To further investigate the correctness of the PRBS configuration, which was able recover
all of the input symbols, should be done by implementing more thorough tests. If the
results of the test invalidates the PRBS setup, then another way of generating encoded
symbols must be designed.
Investigating the viability and potential performance increase of a decoder, that can de-
code while encoded symbols are received. This would most likely require a queuing sys-
tem.
