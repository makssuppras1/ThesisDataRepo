Abstract
Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) remains a critical challenge in biometric security,
particularly for face recognition systems vulnerable to presentation attacks. This theВӯ
sis builds upon the current state of the art, which relies solely on the vision transformer
while discarding the text transformer, by investigating the integration of soft biometricsвҖ”
specifically facial attributesвҖ”into vision language models to improve PAD performance.
A variety of models and experiments are conducted, including zeroВӯshot PAD, zeroВӯshot
PAD using attributes, feature extraction, fineВӯtuning of vision transformers, fineВӯtuning of
vision and text transformers with attributes, guided fineВӯtuning using attributes, and model
fusion. Facial attributes are incorporated into the text encoder, while the foundation modВӯ
els weights are based on FaRLвҖ”which is pretrained on significantly less data than the
foundation models used in the state of the artвҖ”are employed with the aim of boosting
performance. LoRA layers are added during training to facilitate efficient fineВӯtuning. The
results demonstrate that combining facial attributes with FaRL can potentially enhance the
robustness and adaptability of PAD models, with the greatest improvements observed in
zeroВӯshot scenarios, having an increase in AUC of 3.3 percentage points and a decrease
in HTER of 4.6 percentage points. Although none of the models surpass the state of the
art, many achieve competitive results despite requiring far fewer training epochs and using
foundation model weights trained on substantially less data. Model fusion yields the highВӯ
est overall accuracy. These findings highlight the potential of integrating soft biometrics
with domain specific foundation models to strengthen biometric security.

Introduction
Biometrics is the automated recognition of individuals based on their biological and beВӯ
havioural characteristics [1]. Biometric characteristics are biological attributes of an inВӯ
dividual from which distinctive and repeatable values can be extracted for the purpose
of automated recognition [2], examples of this are fingerprints, iris, ear shape and face
topography.
Biometric systems, while widely used for secure identification Вӯ ranging from unlocking
consumer devices to serving as a step in multiВӯfactor authentication (MFA) Вӯ face a critiВӯ
cal limitation: вҖқbiometric characteristics are not secretsвҖқ [3]. Unlike passwords or tokens,
faces, fingerprints, and other biometric traits are inherently exposed. The same qualiВӯ
ties that make facial recognition attractive for identificationвҖ”such as the ability to capture
images from a distance and without direct interactionвҖ”also render these systems vulВӯ
nerable to exploitation by malicious actors. Given the ease with which facial images can
be obtained, they present an ideal target for attackers aiming to impersonate legitimate
users.
Biometric systems have several attack points [4], as illustrated in Figure 1.1:
1. Attack Presentation: The attacker presents a spoofed biometric trait (e.g., fake
fingerprint, face image) to the sensor in an attempt to impersonate a genuine user.
2. Modify Biometric Sample: The attacker alters the raw biometric signal (e.g., finВӯ
gerprint image, voice recording) before it is processed into a digital format.
3. Override Signal Processor: The attacker bypasses or manipulates the hardware
or software that processes the raw biometric input into a feature template.
4. Modify Probe: The attacker tampers with the probe template (i.e., the biometric
data captured during the current session) before it is passed to the matcher.
5. Override Comparator: The attacker bypasses, disables, or replaces the matching
module responsible for comparing the probe to the reference template.
6. Modify Biometric Reference: The attacker alters the stored reference template
used for comparison, potentially inserting a forged or stolen identity.
7. Override or Modify Database: The attacker compromises the biometric database
to access, delete, replace, or inject stored biometric templates.
8. Modify Score: The attacker tampers with the similarity score resulting from the
matching process to falsely indicate a successful or failed match.
9. Override Decision: The attacker manipulates or bypasses the decision logic to
force a system response (e.g., granting access) regardless of the actual matching
result.

Face recognition systems are particularly vulnerable to Presentation Attacks (PA), which
attempt to deceive the system by presenting counterfeit biometric data [3]. Such methВӯ
ods exploit weaknesses in traditional biometric detection, enabling attackers to bypass
security measures and gain unauthorized access to systems.
To counter these threats, presentation attack detection (PAD) techniques are essential
to distinguishing between bona fide (genuine, true) and fraudulent biometrics. PAD has
seen an explosive growth in interest over the last decade, with the amount of publications
nearly doubling in the last 5 years, as seen in Figure 1.2. PAD approaches can be broadly
categorized based on the amount of data available for training the detection model [5]:
вҖў ZeroВӯshot training allows a system to detect presentation attacks without requiring
prior training on specific attack examples. This approach leverages the modelвҖҷs genВӯ
eralization ability to recognize new, unseen attack types, making it highly adaptable
to novel threats.
вҖў OneВӯshot training involves training the system with only a single example of an
attack from a particular category.
вҖў FewВӯshot training requires training the system with only a few samples from each
attack class.
ZeroВӯshot PAD will function as a baseline for performance across the experiments preВӯ
sented in this thesis, while using facial attributes to guide the training process will be the
primary focus of this thesis.
One key limitation of stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart presentation attack detection (PAD) methods is their
limited ability to generalize, primarily due to the scarcity of available training data. HowВӯ
ever, the emergence of foundation models (FMs), such as VisionВӯLanguage Models (VLMs)
[6], has demonstrated impressive capabilities in image understanding, enabled by their
vast and diverse training datasets. These models open up promising new avenues for
PAD, offering the potential for more adaptable and robust biometric security systems.

2.1
VisionВӯLanguage Models
VisionВӯLanguage Models (VLMs) [6] can loosely be defined as models that can process
sequences of both textual inputs l вҲҲLвүӨn and visual inputs i вҲҲIвүӨm. Here, L represents
a finite alphabet, with LвүӨn denoting strings of length up to n, while I refers to the space
of 2D RGB images, and IвүӨm encompasses sequences of images with a maximum length
of m [9].
2.1.1
Families of VisionВӯLanguage Models
VisionВӯLanguage Models [6] can generally be categorized into the following 4 families [10]:
вҖў Contrastive models [10]
вҖў Masking models [10]
вҖў Generative models [10]
вҖў Pretrained backbones [10]
It should be noted, that the categories are not mutually exclusive.
Masking VLMs enhances multimodal understanding by requiring the model to reconstruct
missing information. In Masked Image Modeling (MIM), parts of an image are hidden, and
the model learns to predict the missing patches using an accompanying text caption. In
contrast, in Masked Language Modeling (MLM), certain words in a caption are masked,
and the model must infer them based on the corresponding image [10].
This approach encourages the model to develop fineВӯgrained connections between vision
and language, improving its robustness and generalization [10]. Many modern VLMs
leverage masking, including BEiT [11], M3AE[12], FLAVA[13], MaskVLM[14] and BLIP
[15], which use masked prediction to learn rich multimodal representations.
Generative VLMs are designed to generate new content, such as images from text deВӯ
scriptions or text from images, rather than just matching or retrieving existing content.
These models learn the joint distribution of both modalities and use this knowledge to
produce novel outputs that are coherent across both vision and language [10].
For instance, a textВӯtoВӯimage generative model takes a textual description as input and
generates a corresponding image, while a imageВӯtoВӯtext model can generate a descriptive
caption for a given image [10].
Examples include autoregressive models [16], variational autoencoders (VAEs) [17], or
diffusion models [18].
Pretrained backbones VLMs based VLMs often use openВӯsource large language models
(LLMs), like Llama [19], to learn the mapping between a pretrained image encoder and
the language model [10]. Examples include BLIPВӯ2[20] and MISTRAL [21].

Contrastive VLMs learns to align images and text by mapping them into a shared emВӯ
bedding space. Using contrastive learning, it pulls correct imageВӯtext pairs closer while
pushing incorrect ones apart. These models rely on separate vision and language enВӯ
coders, such as CNNs[22] or Transformers [23], and are trained on largeВӯscale datasets
without explicit labels [10].
E.g., an image of a dog will be closer related to the text вҖқdogвҖқ then to the text вҖқcatвҖқ.
Notable examples include CLIP [24], ALIGN [25], and LiT [26].
This thesis aims to utilize this zeroВӯshot capability and ability to generalize on unforeseen
objects/attributes, therefore focusing on contrastive VLMs. The VisionВӯLanguage Model
selected for this thesis is CLIP [24], chosen for its strong generalization capabilities, which
stem from being trained on a vast and diverse dataset.
2.1.2
Contrastive VLMs
The core goal of contrastive VLMs remains the same for all models, but how the architecВӯ
ture is setup varies. This section will focus on how the family of CLIP [24] models works
and achieves its goal.
Figure 2.1: Architecture of CLIP, image from [24]
The contrastive pretraining, also called ImageВӯText Contrastive Learning (ITC), as shown
in Fig. 2.1, works by utilizing a dataset of correct textВӯimage pairs, where the text describes
the corresponding image. These pairs are then fed into a text encoder (e.g., a Transformer
[23]) and an image encoder (e.g., a Vision Transformer (ViT) [27]).
The objective is to maximize the similarity of the diagonal elements (correct pairs) and
minimize the similarity of the offВӯdiagonal elements (incorrect pairs) during training. The
similarity function, typically cosine similarity, is denoted as s [24].
Diagonal: The diagonal elements represent the correct pairs:
s(Ii, Ti)
for i = 1, 2, ..., N
OffВӯDiagonal: The offВӯdiagonal elements represent the incorrect pairs:
s(Ii, Tj)
for i Мё= j
To achieve this objective of maximizing the similarity of the diagonal and minimizing the
similarity of the offВӯdiagonal elements, the InfoNCE loss [28] is typically used.

CLIPвҖҷs [24] zeroВӯshot capability is powerful because it is trained on a massive and diВӯ

2.1.3
Facial Representation Learning
CLIP [24] is renowned for its strong generalization capabilities. However, since this thesis
focuses on zeroВӯshot facial presentation attack detection, a model specifically trained on
facial data may yield better performance. Several models have been proposed in this
area [30][31], with FaRL for Facial Representation Learning (FaRL) [31] standing out as
the one achieving the best results.
FaRL is a preВӯtraining framework designed to learn a universal facial representation transВӯ
ferable to various face analysis tasks such as face parsing, alignment, and attribute recogВӯ
nition. It combines the contrastive learning from imageВӯtext pairs (ITC), inspired by CLIP
[24], for highВӯlevel semantic understanding, and Masked Image Modeling (MIM), inspired
by BEiT [11], for lowВӯlevel visual detail capture. PreВӯtraining is conducted on LAIONВӯFACE
[32], a dataset containing 50 million face imageВӯtext pairs extracted from LAIONВӯ400M[33],
with a 20 million subset available for fast evaluation.
FaRL can be adapted as FaceCLIP [31] , leveraging its ViTВӯB/16 architecture and ITC preВӯ
training. FaRLвҖҷs[31] weights can be loaded into the standard CLIP framework, enabling
zeroВӯshot faceВӯrelated tasks.

As illustrated in figure 2.3, an image is divided into patches, which are then flattened and
linearly projected into the transformer encoder block. To help the transformer aggregate
information from all patches, a CLS token is prepended to the sequence of patch embedВӯ
dings. This special learnable token serves as a global representation of the entire image
[27].
The transformer encoders multiВӯhead attention, as can be seen in figure 2.2, is a mechaВӯ
nism that enables the model to focus on different parts of the input sequence simultaneВӯ
ously. It allows the model to learn different relationships between the tokens (or patches,
in the case of Vision Transformers) by applying attention multiple times in parallel. This
means that the model can capture diverse contextual information from various parts of
the sequence, enabling it to build a more comprehensive understanding of the input data
[27].
The attention mechanism uses a query (Q), key (K), and value (V) for each token, and
computes attention scores that determine how much each token should focus on othВӯ
ers. The model computes attention multiple times (heads), each time learning different
relationships, and then combines the results[27].
вҖў Q (Query): The query represents the token that is asking for information. It is used
to search for relevant tokens in the sequence (or image patches, in ViT) [27].
вҖў K (Key): The key represents the token that will be compared against the query. ItвҖҷs
like a label for the token that the query is asking about [27].
вҖў V (Value): The value represents the information that is carried by the token. ItвҖҷs the
actual content or data that the model wants to retrieve once the attention score is
calculated [27].
вҖў O (Output): The output is the result of the attention mechanism. ItвҖҷs a weighted sum
of the values (V), where the weights come from the attention scores between the
query (Q) and the key (K). This output is a representation of the input that highlights
the most relevant information based on the attention scores [27].
The MLP transforms the output of the attention layers into a form that is suitable for the
downstream task [27].
The MLP head is responsible for producing the final output of the model. It typically conВӯ
sists of one or more layers that map the processed token representations (including the
CLS token) into the final prediction [27].
The transformer encoder blocks are stacked sequentially, where the output of one block
becomes the input to the next. ViTВӯBase (ViTВӯB) consists of 12 layers of transformer
encoder blocks, ViTВӯLarge (ViTВӯL) has 24 layers, and ViTВӯHuge (ViTВӯH) contains 32 layers
[27].

2.3
Presentation Attack Instruments
While there are conceptually unlimited ways to perform presentation attacks, they can be
broadly categorized into the following four types of
Presentation Attack Instruments (PAI) [3][37]:
вҖў Print attacks work by printing out or displaying an image of the attacked identity
towards the sensor, in order to gain access. While This kind of attack is the simplest,
it is conversely also the most important to protect against [3]. A way to circumvent
PAD systems using print attacks is to print out the face and cut out the face (paper
cut [3]) and either present it or wear it as a mask, potentially with holes in the eyes,
mouth, and nose. Wearing it as a mask with holes where the facial features has the
most movement is a simple yet effective way to both present depth and liveness of
the presentation attack instrument (PAI) [38], while research has shown that many
systems are still vulnerable to this type of attack [39][40][41].
8

вҖў Replay attacks works by displaying a video of the targeted identity towards the
sensor, in order to gain access. While it is not significantly more difficult to create
than other PAI, it is often more effective as it incorporates the liveness features of
the targeted individual [3]. Especially with the rise of new AI models, that can easily
translate static images into dynamic lively videos[42][43][44].
вҖў 3D Mask works by creating a 3D model of the face of the attacked identity and
showing it to the sensor, in order to gain access. The complexity of this method
can range from something simple as printing out the face and plastering it on top
of a plastic bag, to recreate the depth of the image. More advanced 3D mask PAI
includes recreating a hyper realistic mask through the use of a mold of the attacked
identityвҖҷs face and either presenting it or wearing it as a mask [3][45].
вҖў Other attacks can involve the use of makeup or plastic surgery to physically mimic
the targeted identity [3]. Alternatively, DeepFakes [46] can digitally alter a personвҖҷs
face in realВӯtime to resemble the targeted identity.
These PAIs align with two main attacker intentions: (a) impersonating another individualвҖҷs
biometric traits to gain unauthorized access, and (b) concealing their own identity to evade
detection by the biometric system.
2.4
Datasets
To evaluate presentation attack detection (PAD), including zeroВӯshot PAD, for facial recogВӯ
nition, a variety of publicly available datasets will be used. These datasets provide a
comprehensive collection of both bona fide and presentation attack samples, enabling
the assessment of the modelвҖҷs ability to detect attacks from previously unseen attack
types. The following datasets are commonly used in the evaluation of PAD systems:
вҖў Idiap replayВӯattack[47] dataset includes videos of both bona fide and presentation
attacks from 50 individuals, recorded under controlled and adverse conditions. It
contains 200 bona fide videos and 1000 attack videos, with 200 print attacks and
800 replay attacks, divided equally between 400 phoneВӯbased and 400 tabletВӯbased
replay attacks [47].
вҖў CASIAВӯFASD[48] dataset consists of 50 subjects with genuine face videos recorded
in natural environments, including blinking behavior.
Fake face attacks include
warped photos, cut photos (with eye regions removed), and video attacks displayed
on an iPad. Videos are captured in low, normal, and highВӯquality resolutions usВӯ

2.5.1
Area Under the Curve
The True Positive Rate (TPR), also known as recall or sensitivity, measures the proporВӯ
tion of actual positives (e.g., bona fide samples) that are correctly identified by the model.
TPR =
TP
TP + FN
Where TP is the number of true positives and FN is the number of false negatives.
The False Positive Rate (FPR) measures the proportion of actual negatives (e.g., preВӯ
sentation attacks) that are incorrectly classified as bona fide samples by the model.
FPR =
FP
FP + TN
Where FP is the number of false positives and TN is the number of true negatives.
The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve [54], plots the True Positive Rate
(range is 0Вӯ1) against the False Positive Rate (range is 0Вӯ1). ROC visually represents how
well a classification model separates two classesвҖ”bona fide samples and presentation
attacksвҖ”by plotting the False Positive Rate on the xВӯaxis and the True Positive Rate
on the yВӯaxis across different classification thresholds (see figure 2.4). Each point on
the curve corresponds to a specific threshold, showing the balance between correctly
detecting bona fide samples and wrongly classifying attacks as bona fide. A curve closer
to the topВӯleft corner signifies that the model achieves high detection of bonafide samples
with low misclassifications of attacks, while a diagonal line indicates random guessing
[54](see figure 2.4).
Often a scalar value is preferred over a graph when evaluating and comparing perforВӯ
mance. The Area Under the (ROC) Curve (AUC) [54] provides a summary of the modelвҖҷs
ability to correctly identify bonafide samples versus presentation attacks, regardless of
the chosen decision threshold (see figure 2.4). An AUC of 1.0 represents perfect clasВӯ
sification, while an AUC of 0.5 suggests no discriminative power, equivalent to random
guessing[54].

2.5.2
Half Total Error Rate
The error metrics False Acceptance Rate (FAR) and the False Rejection Rate (FRR),
while similar to the FPR and the False Negative Rate (FNR), are more precise and domain
specific to biometrics. These rates measure the systemвҖҷs susceptibility to errors during
user verification and identification processes [55].
FAR is the probability that an unauthorized individual is incorrectly granted access. It is
expressed as:
FAR =
Number of False Acceptances
Total Number of Imposter Attempts
A high FAR indicates that the system is too lenient, allowing unauthorized access.
FPR is the probability that an authorized individual is incorrectly denied access. It is
calculated as:
FRR =
Number of False Rejections
Total Number of Genuine Attempts
A high FRR means the system is too strict, causing inconvenience to legitimate users.
FAR and FRR are inversely relatedНҫ decreasing one often increases the other. The sysВӯ
temвҖҷs threshold determines this balance. Adjusting the threshold can make the system
more or less strict, leading to a tradeВӯoff between security (low FAR) and usability (low
FRR), see figure 2.5. The Detection Error Tradeoff (DET) plots the FAR against the
FRR.
The Equal Error Rate (EER) represents the point where FAR equals FRR, offering a
balanced performance.
The evaluation metric used for face antiВӯspoofing is often the Half Total Error Rate
(HTER)[56]:
HTER = FAR + FRR
2
HTER is typically evaluated at the EER threshold, as its value is dependent on the chosen
threshold [55] (see how the HTER values differs for different thresholds in figure 2.5).
In contrast, AUC represents the overall ranking quality across all thresholds, making it
thresholdВӯindependent.
An HTER value of 0.5 represents random guessing, with 0.0 being a perfect system.
2.5.3
APCER and BPCER
In addition to the aforementioned FRR and FARвҖ”which quantify errors at the biometВӯ
ric matching levelвҖ”BPCER (Bona Fide Presentation Classification Error Rate) [57] and
APCER (Attack Presentation Classification Error Rate)[57] are metrics specific to PAD
systems. While FRR and FAR assess whether genuine users are correctly matched or
impostors are incorrectly accepted, BPCER and APCER evaluate the PAD modelsвҖҷ ability
to distinguish Bonafide Presentation (BP) from Attack Presentation (AP).
вҖў BPCER measures how often a genuine (bona fide) presentation is incorrectly clasВӯ
sified as an attack. Low BPCER indicates high usability.
вҖў APCER measures how often a presentation attack is incorrectly classified as genВӯ
uine. Low APCER indicates high security.

FoundPAD

FoundPAD [60] utilizes only the CLIP[24] image encoder, while the text encoder is disВӯ
carded.
The image encoder is based on a Vision Transformer (ViT) [27] architecture (see section
2.1.4), which processes face images into feature representations. The image encoder
from CLIP [24]is employed as the feature extractor for face images. It is initialized with preВӯ
trained weights from CLIP to leverage the generalization capabilities obtained from largeВӯ
scale imageВӯtext preВӯtraining. The encoder processes face images, which are tokenized
into patches and passed through a series of transformer blocks. FoundPAD [60] fineВӯ
tunes the query (Q) and value (V) projection matrices (Orange modules in figure 2.6) in
the multiВӯhead selfВӯattention (MSA) layers of the image encoder using rsLoRA [36] (see
section 2.2. An additional fully connected (FC) classification layer is added on top of the
adapted image encoder [60].
This layer takes the feature representation output by the encoder and outputs a binary
prediction Лңy: bona fide or presentation attack. Binary crossentropy loss is then used to
compare Лңy with the ground truth, y, to update the modelвҖҷs trainable parameters:
LBCE = вҲ’(y log(Лңy) + (1 вҲ’y) log(1 вҲ’Лңy))
(2.3)
During inference, the fineВӯtuned image encoder extracts features from face images. The
classification layer predicts the class label based on the extracted features, with the highВӯ
est output score being chosen. A visualization of this pipeline can be seen in figure 2.6.

Facial Biometrics
In the context of facial recognition, the terms soft biometrics and hard biometrics are
used to differentiate between distinct types of facial characteristics used for identification:
Hard Biometrics (Facial Features)
These are unique, physiological traits of the face that can be measured and used to definiВӯ
tively identify an individual. Examples include:
Vision Language Models for Presentation Attack Detection
15
==PAGE:24==
вҖў Facial landmarks such as the relative positions of the eyes, nose, and mouth.
вҖў Facial geometry, including the proportions and structure of the face (e.g., distance
between eyes, shape of the jawline).
These features are generally stable over time and unique to each person, making them
ideal for accurate face recognition and identification[80].
Soft Biometrics (Facial Attributes)
These refer to more generalized, nonВӯunique characteristics of the face that provide adВӯ
ditional context but are not individually definitive for identification. Examples include:
вҖў Age estimation based on facial appearance.
вҖў Gender, such as distinguishing between male or female.
вҖў Ethnicity based on observable facial traits.
вҖў Facial expression (e.g., smiling, frowning, neutral).
вҖў Facial hair presence or absence (e.g., beard or mustache).
Soft biometrics offer valuable supplementary information for narrowing down potential
candidates in face recognition tasks, but they cannot be solely relied upon for definitive
identification like hard biometrics can. [80]
Facial attributes are numerous and which ones that are the most important differ widely
from study to study[81][82] [83].
Biometric Characteristic Extraction: Some typical ways to gather these biometrics
are through holistic approaches: PCA [84], texture analysis [85], or through more landВӯ
mark oriented approaches: Geometric landmarks [86], Elastic Face Bunch Graph [87] or
through hybrid approaches or using deep learning approaches [88].
Challenges: Multiple attributes, such as attractiveness and confidence can be very soВӯ
ciocultural, making it highly influenced by bias. Additionally, some attributes may induce
bias, such as lipstick and beard w.r.t. gender bias [89]. Some other challenges include
Pose, Illumination, Expression and aging (PIE), where the same person may not present
as the same person depending on the angle, lighting, aging or expression, or the general
quality (focus, sharpness, brightness, contrast or fidelity) of the image [81].
Grouping: Facial attributes are typically grouped based upon the domain that they are
used in, and differ widely [81][82] [83]. Therefore there doesnвҖҷt appear to exist a general
consensus on how to group facial attributes, and many groupings are themselves to an
extent vague.
Some have suggested to group them by how fineВӯgrained the attribute is (e.g., age, ethВӯ
nicity are fineВӯgrained while gender, mask and glasses are binary), and if the attribute is
global (e.g., Age, expression, attractiveness) or local (e.g., Hair style, mask) [90].
I have decided to group facial attributes in figure 2.7 based upon its general temporal
stability, where something like the demographic attributes change very little if anything
throughout a year, and expressions can change many times in a couple of seconds.

5.6
Guided Finetuning using Attributes
The experiments methodology is described in 4.2.8, where a BCE loss is combined with
the contrastive loss to help guide the training process towards the correct class, as it
seems that training based solely upon attributes pushes the classification towards the
wrong class in some instances.
The results of this experiment are shown in table 5.15. When compared with the results
in table 5.6 for the same modelВӯfinetuning approach combination but is not guided, this
approach decreases the AUC score by 3.88 percentage points on average, which is surВӯ
prising and quite a bit worse. Although a good idea in theory, guiding the finetuning is not
effective. An hypothesis of why this is the case is that contrastive loss can act as a form
of regularization by encouraging the model to focus on the relative distances among samВӯ
ples, not just raw classification, and this may be especially important with such varying
facial attribute prompts. Although BCE loss is a good idea when not using text prompts,
when using text prompts the focus should generally be solely contrastive loss.

5.9
General Discussion
An important consideration is that all the datasets used consist exclusively of individuals
of Asian ethnicity. As a result, applying PAD to datasets with a more ethnically diverse
population may lead to reduced performance.
When it comes to using FaRL as a foundation model, it seems that the less data used
to finetune on, the better FaRL does when compared with SOTA. Its utility when comВӯ
pared diminishes signficantly on larger datasets. The reason for this may be that FaRL
is already tuned to the facial domain, and therefore does not get the same improvement
from finetuning. This is shown when comparing the feature extractor to finetuning the
model, where the feature extractor im many cases had close to identical performance to
finetuning.
In most cases, using FaRL as a foundation model rivaled the stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart (SOTA)
but did not quantitatively outperform it. However, it is important to note that FaRL was

pretrained on only a 1/20th subset of the LAION400M dataset used by the SOTA model.
From this perspective, FaRL performed very well for PAD. One reason for the superior
performance of the stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart model trained on LAION400m may be that training on
larger datasets helps the model capture complex patterns, even if those patterns are not
directly related to the target domain.
Many of the experiments throughout this thesis only did validation every 4th Вӯ 8th epoch,
which may contribute to a lower than possible performance, as the best epoch may have
been an epoch where validation was not run.
To further support this hypothesis, it was shown that the most impactful epochs were the
first 8 epochs, where beyond the first 8 epochs performance slowly started to diminishes,
possibly due to overfitting.
In general, it seems that using finetuning approach 2 (All attributes) and evaluation apВӯ
proach 1 (All attributes) led to the best performance. In general, it seems that the more
facial attributes, the better performance.
In general, it seems that the vision transformer plays a greater role when it comes to PAD,
and it may not be worth it to train the text transformer, even when using contrastive loss.
For model fusion, combining two individually strong models that perform differently across
datasets leads to significant performance improvements. In this thesis, the most effective
fusion approach was mean soft voting.
For finetuning, the best LoRA parameters found was Alpha 8, Rank 8, Dropout 0.2. AlВӯ
tough some of the experiments attempted so many variable combinations, that the inВӯ
creased performance may simply be attributed to noise.
There were many combinations and permutations of different model approaches that were
considered, but due to the sheer computational requirements of finetuning, even reducing
the amount of parameters with LoRA, it was not feasible to do that.
FineВӯtuning the ViTВӯBВӯ16 model typically took around 20 minutes per epoch, totaling apВӯ
proximately 13 hours for 40 epochs. Since training was repeated four times for each
crossВӯsource combination, a single experiment took about two days to complete. In conВӯ
trast, the ViTВӯLВӯ14 model required twice as long per epoch, making it impractical to conduct
the same number of experiments with itвҖ”despite its use in stateВӯofВӯtheВӯart approaches.
5.9.1
Bias and Faireness
While facial attributes such as gender, age, and ethnicity have shown potential to improve
presentation attack detection (PAD) performance in some instances, their use raises imВӯ
portant concerns regarding bias and fairness. Demographic attributes may introduce unВӯ
wanted correlations that reflect societal or dataset biases rather than true indicators of
presentation attack detection. For instance, if a training dataset over represents certain
ethnicities or age groups in either the bonafide or presentation attack classes, the model
may inadvertently learn these unwanted biases. This could result in disproportionate false
rejection or false acceptance rates across demographic groups. Although this thesis does
not explicitly explore fairness across the datasets, the use of soft biometrics requires cauВӯ
tion and highlights the importance of balanced datasets and demographicВӯaware evaluaВӯ
tion protocols in future work.

Presentation Attack Detection is a critical component of secure biometric authentication
systems. The methods explored in this thesis, particularly those that incorporate facial

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate several key research questions related to improving preВӯ
sentation attack detection (PAD) through facial attributes and model strategies.
Research Question 1: How can facial attributes improve presentation attack detecВӯ
tion?
Using facial attributes generally achieved results comparable to models without them, sugВӯ
gesting that facial attributes alone are not the most critical factor for high PAD accuracy.
However, in the context of zeroВӯshot presentation attack detection, incorporating facial
attributes significantly improved performance, with an increase in AUC of 3.3 percentВӯ
age points and a decrease in HTER of 4.6 percentage points in the bestВӯcase scenario.
Highlighting their value in scenarios with limited training data.
Research Question 2: Which combination of facial attributes has the largest impact
on classification?
For zeroВӯshot PAD, the presence of the glasses attribute had the greatest positive impact
on detection rates. Conversely, when fineВӯtuning models with facial attributes, increasing
the number of attributes generally improved performance. Interestingly, including the atВӯ
tack type as an attribute did not enhance resultsвҖ”in fact, it led to diminished performance.
Research Question 3: How does the fusion of different visualВӯlanguage models afВӯ
fect performance?
Fusing multiple models, each specialized in different aspects of the domain, led to a sigВӯ
nificant boost in overall performance, surpassing what each model achieved individually.
This confirms the effectiveness of leveraging complementary strengths in model fusion.
Research Question 4: How does using FaRL as a foundation model affect perforВӯ
mance?
FaRL demonstrated strong potential, requiring minimal fineВӯtuning to achieve excellent
resultsвҖ”especially when facial attributes were integrated during fineВӯtuning or in zeroВӯ
shot PAD settings.
In summary, this work shows the nuanced role of facial attributes in PAD and highlights the
benefits of combining specialized models and leveraging advanced foundation models like
FaRL. While facial attributes alone may not guarantee superior performance, their strateВӯ
gic use, particularly in zeroВӯshot scenarios, and model fusion techniques open promising
avenues for future research and practical applications in presentation attack detection.
Future Work Experimentation was an organic process, with the setup evolving dynamiВӯ
cally in response to the results obtained. Each experiment required substantial compuВӯ
tational resources and therefore had to be run somewhat in parallel. As a result, some
