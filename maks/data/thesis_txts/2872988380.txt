Abstract
This thesis presents the development of a multifunctional Mn–CeO2/Silk methacrylate hy-
drogel patch designed for chronic wound management. The system integrates Mn-doped
cerium oxide nanozymes into a photo-crosslinked SilMA hydrogel, aiming to simultane-
ously scavenge reactive oxygen species and enable real-time ultrasound monitoring. The
Mn:CeO2 nanozymes exhibited dual superoxide dismutase- and catalase-like activity, val-
idated through screening and kinetic studies, with balanced antioxidant performance and
good colloidal stability. The nanozyme-loaded hydrogels generated oxygen bubbles in re-
sponse to hydrogen peroxide, producing ultrasound contrast in situ, an approach that al-
lows non-invasive wound monitoring. Mechanical characterization revealed that nanopar-
ticle loading modulates gel stiffness and extensibility, with higher loadings enhancing me-
chanical strength towards physiologically relevant ranges. Ultrasound imaging demon-
strated a dose-dependent enhancement of echogenicity. This platform shows promise as
a theranostic wound dressing, offering both therapeutic and diagnostic functionalities.

Introduction
Chronic and complex skin wounds impose a heavy global health and economic burden
[1]. In the United States, for example, an analysis found ￿8.2 million Medicare patients
with acute or chronic wounds in 2014, with annual treatment costs on the order of $28–96
billion [2]. Many of the most expensive cases involve surgical and diabetic ulcers. In Eu-
rope, around 3–4 per 1000 people (roughly 1.5–2.0 million of the EU-27 population) have
active wounds at any given time, with prevalence rising sharply in aging and comorbid
populations [3]. These wounds (e.g. pressure ulcers, venous or diabetic leg ulcers) often
require long-term care, repeated debridement, dressings and antibiotics, and they signif-
icantly reduce quality of life. As populations age and chronic diseases like diabetes and
obesity grow, the incidence of non-healing wounds, and associated costs, is expected to
increase even further.
Current wound management typically relies on periodic clinical inspection or laboratory
tests, which can miss early warning signs. Infection is a common and dangerous com-
plication: microbial colonization dramatically amplifies inflammation and impedes tissue
repair [4]. Yet by the time overt infection or delayed healing is noted, significant tissue
damage may have occurred. Recent efforts in smart wound care have focused on inte-
grating biosensors to track parameters such as pH, temperature, or metabolic markers,
aiming to support earlier and more personalized interventions [5].
A critical yet often under-monitored factor in impaired healing is oxidative stress. Re-
active oxygen species (ROS), such as superoxide anion (O2•￿) and hydrogen peroxide
(H2O2), are integral to the wound healing cascade [6]. At physiological levels, they support
hemostasis, antimicrobial defense, and signaling pathways that regulate angiogenesis
and tissue remodeling. However, sustained inflammation or infection can trigger exces-
sive ROS production by neutrophils and macrophages, overwhelming the tissue’s antiox-
idant capacity. This imbalance leads to oxidative damage to lipids, proteins, and nucleic
acids, promoting chronic inflammation, apoptosis, and extracellular matrix degradation
- hallmarks of non-healing wounds [7]. ROS levels are also directly linked to delayed
epithelialization and impaired granulation tissue formation.
The dynamic and localized nature of oxidative stress necessitates real-time, in situ moni-
toring for accurate clinical insight. However, most existing diagnostics (e.g., swab cul-
tures, redox assays) are invasive, slow, and fail to capture spatiotemporal variations
in wound biochemistry. Recent technological advances have spurred interest in “thera-
nostic” wound dressings that combine therapeutic functions with non-invasive diagnostic
imaging [8, 9]. In this context, nanozymes-nanomaterials with enzyme-mimetic activity-
offer a promising platform for responsive wound care. In particular, manganese-doped
cerium oxide (Mn-CeO2) nanozymes mimic both catalase and superoxide dismutase ac-
tivity, breaking down H2O2 and O•−
2
while releasing O2 as a byproduct [10, 11, 12]. Impor-
tantly, the generated oxygen microbubbles are echogenic, allowing for ultrasound-based
monitoring of catalytic activity and oxidative stress in situ [13].
This project develops a smart hydrogel wound patch that integrates Mn–CeO2 nanozymes
into a methacrylated silk fibroin (SilMA) matrix. The system is designed to: (i) scavenge
ROS, reducing inflammation and promoting healing, and (ii) enable real-time ultrasound
monitoring of redox status through O2 microbubble generation. This dual-function, redox-
responsive platform offers a novel approach to personalized, image-guided wound care.

Clinical Background
2.1
Anatomy of Skin
The skin is the most prominent and functionally diverse component of the integumentary
system. It covers an area of approximately 1.5–2.0 square meters in an average adult
and weighs about 4–5 kilograms [14]. Structurally, the skin is composed of three prin-
cipal layers - the epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis (subcutaneous tissue) - each with
distinct anatomy and functions (Figure 2.1. These layers work together to form a multi-
functional barrier that protects the body from the external environment, provides immune
surveillance, and houses the sensory apparatus for touch, pain, and temperature. Struc-
turally, the cutaneous membrane consists of three distinct layers: the epidermis, dermis
and hypodermis (subcutaneous tissue), each with distinct anatomy and functions.
Figure 2.1: Skin layers illustration [15]
The epidermis, a stratified squamous epithelium, provides a physical and permeabil-
ity barrier that prevents water loss and microbial invasion [16]. Its outermost stratum
corneum consists of terminally differentiated keratinocytes (corneocytes) embedded in a
lipid matrix, forming the main barrier against environmental damage [17]. The basal layer
contains proliferative keratinocytes, melanocytes that produce photoprotective melanin,
and immune-active Langerhans cells [18].
The underlying dermis is a dense connective tissue layer providing tensile strength and
elasticity, largely due to its collagen and elastic fiber content [16]. It houses fibroblasts,
immune cells, vascular networks, and nerve endings, serving critical roles in nutrient de-
livery, immune surveillance, and wound repair [19]. The dermal vasculature supports
angiogenesis during tissue regeneration.
The hypodermis, composed mainly of adipose tissue, provides cushioning, insulation, and
serves as an energy reserve [14]. Beyond its structural role, adipose tissue is metaboli-

2.2
Wound Healing Stages
Wound healing is a tightly regulated, multistep physiological process by which the body
restores tissue integrity following injury. This process unfolds through four canonical and
overlapping stages - hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling - each
characterized by distinct cellular events and molecular mediators that must occur in a co-
ordinated and timely manner for effective repair [20, 21]. Although traditionally described
in sequence, these phases are highly interdependent, with significant temporal and func-
tional overlap mediated by complex signaling networks (Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Wound healing stages and ROS activity [7].
Immediately upon injury, vascular damage triggers vasoconstriction and platelet aggre-
gation, culminating in the formation of a fibrin clot that serves both as a hemostatic plug
and as a provisional extracellular matrix (ECM). This clot provides a scaffold for cellular
migration and acts as a reservoir for cytokines, chemokines, and growth factors, including
transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β), platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), and vascu-
lar endothelial growth factor (VEGF), which initiate and regulate the healing cascade [21].
Within hours, the wound environment shifts into the inflammatory phase. Blood vessels di-
late and become permeable under the influence of vasoactive mediators, allowing plasma
proteins and leukocytes to infiltrate the wound bed. Neutrophils are the first responders,
executing phagocytic functions and releasing reactive oxygen species (ROS) and prote-

==PAGE:13==
olytic enzymes essential for microbial killing and debris clearance [14]. ROS produced
during this phase serve not only as antimicrobial agents but also as important secondary
messengers in signaling pathways that regulate inflammation and tissue repair.
As inflammation proceeds, monocytes are recruited and differentiate into macrophages -
key orchestrators of wound healing. These cells exhibit phenotypic plasticity, transition-
ing from a pro-inflammatory (M1-like) state to a pro-healing (M2-like) phenotype, enabling
them to resolve inflammation, clear apoptotic cells, and secrete growth factors that stim-
ulate fibroblast activity, angiogenesis, and re-epithelialization.
The proliferative phase, typically commencing days after injury, is marked by fibroblast
proliferation, ECM deposition, and angiogenesis. Fibroblasts infiltrate the wound, pro-
ducing type III collagen, fibronectin, and glycosaminoglycans to establish granulation tis-
sue that supports neovascularization and further cellular recruitment. Simultaneously,
keratinocytes at the wound margins proliferate and migrate across the wound bed, re-
establishing the epidermal barrier. This phase is highly dependent on the balance of
ROS, which at physiological levels promote angiogenesis and fibroblast activation, but
when in excess can impede collagen synthesis and cellular viability.
The final remodeling phase extends over weeks to months, during which the provisional
ECM undergoes maturation. Type III collagen is replaced by type I collagen, fibers are
realigned along mechanical stress lines, and myofibroblasts mediate wound contraction.
The vascular density of the scar decreases as unnecessary capillaries regress, and the
tensile strength of the repaired tissue progressively increases. Throughout this phase,
ROS continue to influence tissue remodeling processes, with dysregulated ROS levels
linked to pathological scarring or chronic wound formation.
To summarise, the wound healing process represents a dynamic interplay of cellular and
molecular events, tightly modulated by growth factors, cytokines, and oxidative media-
tors. While controlled ROS production is indispensable for normal healing, sustained or
excessive ROS generation disrupts this balance, highlighting the potential of targeted
ROS-modulating therapies in wound management.
2.3
ROS in wound healing: their origin, role and scavenging
2.3.1
Origin of ROS in Wound Healing
Wounding induces a rapid and localized increase in reactive oxygen species (ROS) at the
injury site. These ROS originate primarily from two cellular sources: the activity of NADPH
oxidase (NOX) enzymes and mitochondrial oxidative metabolism (Figure 2.3. During the
inflammatory phase, activated neutrophils and macrophages mount an oxidative burst via
NOX2, producing large amounts of superoxide (O•−
2 ) within phagosomes to combat invad-
ing pathogens [22, 23, 24]. Some superoxide escapes into the extracellular environment,
where it may undergo further reactions or dismutation. In addition, resident cells at the
wound margin - including keratinocytes, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells - express NOX
isoforms such as NOX1 and NOX4, and dual oxidases (DUOX), generating ROS that
act as intracellular and paracrine signaling molecules [22]. Mitochondria also contribute
significantly to ROS production, particularly under hypoxic or inflammatory conditions.
Electron leakage during oxidative phosphorylation, mainly at complexes I and III of the
electron transport chain, leads to superoxide formation in the mitochondrial matrix [24].
This mitochondrial superoxide is subsequently converted to hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) by
manganese superoxide dismutase (MnSOD). Consequently, the principal ROS species
in the wound environment are superoxide and hydrogen peroxide, with H2O2 serving as a
relatively stable and diffusible signaling molecule [7]. Secondary ROS, such as peroxyni-

2.3.2
Dual Role of ROS: Physiological versus Pathological Effects
ROS play a dual role in wound healing - acting as essential signaling mediators at phys-
iological levels while exerting cytotoxic effects when produced in excess. During the in-
flammatory phase, neutrophils and macrophages employ ROS for antimicrobial defense.
The conversion of superoxide to hydrogen peroxide facilitates the generation of potent
oxidants, such as hypochlorous acid via myeloperoxidase, critical for pathogen clearance
[26, 24]. Beyond host defense, ROS serve as signaling molecules that regulate key wound
healing processes. Hydrogen peroxide acts as a chemoattractant, promoting immune cell
recruitment to the wound site [23]. ROS also modulate platelet activation and coagula-
tion, further supporting hemostasis [26]. In the proliferative phase, sustained low levels
of ROS influence cellular behavior by activating redox-sensitive transcription factors like
NF-κB, AP-1, and HIF-1α. These transcriptional regulators enhance the expression of
growth factors and cytokines, promoting angiogenesis, fibroblast proliferation, and ker-
atinocyte migration - all essential for granulation tissue formation and re-epithelialization
[7, 27]. For example, ROS-mediated stabilization of HIF-1α enhances VEGF produc-
tion, driving neovascularization in the wound bed. However, when ROS production is
excessive or prolonged, it results in oxidative stress, leading to lipid peroxidation, protein
oxidation, DNA damage, and ultimately impaired cell viability [7, 27]. Persistently ele-
vated ROS levels sustain a pro-inflammatory state, disrupt growth factor activity, degrade
the extracellular matrix, and hinder wound progression - features characteristic of chronic
wounds such as diabetic foot ulcers and venous leg ulcers [26]. Excessive ROS also
facilitate biofilm formation and prolonged protease activity, further inhibiting healing pro-
cesses like angiogenesis and re-epithelialization [7]. Thus, ROS act as a double-edged
sword in wound healing: indispensable for defense and signaling at controlled levels, yet
detrimental when unchecked. The ability of the wound environment to maintain redox
homeostasis is crucial for the successful resolution of injury.

The regulation of ROS levels during wound healing relies on enzymatic antioxidants,
primarily superoxide dismutase (SOD) and catalase (CAT), which neutralize harmful

Catalase, predominantly peroxisomal, limits hydrogen peroxide accumulation and thus
prevents hydroxyl radical generation via the Fenton reaction [28]. However, unlike SOD,
catalase expression is not significantly upregulated after injury. Its mRNA levels remain
largely unchanged, and protein expression may even decline as healing progresses [30,
32]. This reduction likely facilitates the persistence of low-level H2O2 required for redox
signaling during proliferation and angiogenesis. The compartmentalization of catalase
restricts its access to cytosolic and extracellular H2O2, roles likely filled by other perox-
idases such as glutathione peroxidase and peroxiredoxins. Evidence from Roy et al.
demonstrated that catalase overexpression impaired wound healing in mice, suggesting
that excessive scavenging of H2O2 disrupts beneficial redox signaling essential for tissue
repair [32].
The coordinated activity of SOD and CAT is finely tuned across wound healing phases.
Inflammation demands robust superoxide clearance by SOD, while catalase activity is
restrained to allow signaling-level H2O2 to accumulate. As healing transitions to prolif-
eration and remodeling, antioxidant systems adapt to reduce ROS levels progressively,
promoting inflammation resolution and tissue maturation.
The balance is therefore dynamic: during the inflammation phase, robust SOD activity
is crucial to prevent runaway radical damage, whereas catalase activity is restrained to
permit beneficial H2O2-mediated signalling. Later, as the proliferative phase progresses,
antioxidant defences are adjusted to gradually tip the balance toward reduction of ROS,
aiding the resolution of inflammation and onset of tissue remodelling. This regulated in-
terplay ensures that each phase of wound healing has an appropriate redox environment.
In summary, superoxide dismutase and catalase are key regulators of the wound redox
milieu; together they scavenge excess ROS (thereby protecting tissues from oxidative
stress and chronic wound pathogenesis) while still allowing the ROS-mediated signalling
necessary for normal inflammation and proliferation to occur. Their activity and expres-
sion are under tight biological control during wound repair, underlining the importance of
redox homeostasis in successful healing.

2.4
Ultrasound Imaging and Microbubble Contrast
2.4.1
Principles of Ultrasound and B-Mode Imaging
Ultrasound imaging operates by transmitting high-frequency sound waves into tissues
and detecting the echoes reflected from interfaces between materials of differing acoustic
impedance [13]. Acoustic impedance (Z = ρc), defined as the product of tissue den-
sity (ρ) and sound speed (c), governs how much of the incident wave is reflected at a
boundary. The greater the impedance mismatch, the stronger the reflected echo. Soft
tissues and fluids typically have acoustic impedances around 1.5 MRayl, while gases
possess much lower impedance due to their low density and high compressibility. As a
result, even microscopic gas bubbles within a liquid present a significant acoustic mis-
match, producing strong reflections and making them highly echogenic on ultrasound
[33]. B-mode (brightness-mode) imaging forms the basis of clinical ultrasound, displaying
a two-dimensional grayscale image where the brightness of each pixel corresponds to the
amplitude of the received echo. This modality enables real-time anatomical visualization
of soft tissues (Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Block diagram of B-mode US imaging [34].
2.4.2
Microbubbles: Acoustic Properties and Contrast Mechanisms
Microbubbles enhance ultrasound imaging by exploiting their unique acoustic behavior.
Unlike rigid particles, gas-filled microbubbles are compressible and oscillate volumetri-
cally in response to the alternating pressure of ultrasound waves. This oscillatory motion
dramatically increases their acoustic scattering compared to non-compressible tissue par-
ticles of similar size [35]. Microbubble resonance-the frequency at which oscillation am-
plitude is maximized-depends on bubble size. Fortunately, microbubbles small enough to
traverse human capillaries (<8 µm) resonate within the 1–10 MHz range, aligning with typ-
ical clinical ultrasound frequencies [35]. At resonance, microbubbles behave as efficient
point scatterers, generating echoes disproportionate to their physical size.
Critically, microbubbles exhibit non-linear oscillations even under modest ultrasound in-

tensities. This non-linearity leads to asymmetrical expansion and contraction, resulting
in the emission of harmonic frequencies (multiples of the transmitted frequency) [33].
Tissues generally respond linearly, reflecting only the fundamental frequency.
Ultra-
sound systems exploit this difference using harmonic imaging techniques-such as pulse
inversion-to isolate bubble-specific echoes, thereby enhancing contrast and reducing tis-

bles can be detected within the imaging field.
2.4.3
Cavitation Dynamics and Imaging Relevance
Under ultrasound, microbubbles may enter different cavitation regimes based on the
acoustic pressure applied. At low mechanical indices, stable cavitation occurs, where
microbubbles oscillate rhythmically about their equilibrium diameter. This stable oscilla-
tion enhances imaging contrast and may induce local microstreaming-small fluid move-
ments around the bubble-that can influence the surrounding environment [36].
At higher acoustic pressures, bubbles can undergo inertial cavitation, characterized by
rapid expansion followed by violent collapse. This collapse can generate localized high
temperatures, shockwaves, and fragment the bubble into smaller nuclei [36]. While iner-
tial cavitation is exploited therapeutically (e.g., for tissue ablation or enhanced drug deliv-
ery), diagnostic imaging typically maintains acoustic pressures within the stable cavitation
range to preserve bubble integrity and ensure consistent echo enhancement.
2.4.4
In Situ Generation of Oxygen Microbubbles via Hydrogen Peroxide
Decomposition
Beyond commercial contrast agents, microbubbles can also be generated in situ through
chemical reactions. As previously discussed in the context of wound oxidative stress,
H2O2 is rapidly decomposed by catalase into water and oxygen gas. This catalytic reaction-
already detailed in section 2.2-can be leveraged to generate microbubbles in situ within
tissues or wound cavities.
The resultant oxygen microbubbles display the same acoustic properties as pre-formed
contrast agents-primarily due to the significant acoustic impedance mismatch and com-
pressibility of the gas core. Unlike commercial lipid- or protein-shelled microbubbles, oxy-
gen bubbles generated in situ are transient, lacking stabilizing shells and dissolving rapidly
in tissue fluids. Nonetheless, when continuously generated (e.g., via sustained H2O2 ex-
posure), they can transiently enhance ultrasound contrast.
Clinically, this reaction has been harnessed in applications such as contrast-enhanced
ultrasonography of fistulas or abscesses, where H2O2 instillation delineates cavities and
tracts by creating echogenic microbubble clouds [37]. The capacity to generate contrast
on-demand at the site of interest-without systemic administration-makes this approach
particularly attractive for wound assessment and localized imaging applications.
Another important aspect of wound monitoring is assessing the biological activity within
the wound - essentially, is the wound healing or is it inflamed/infected? Here, the H2O2-
responsive microbubble approach offers a unique functional assessment. A chronic, non-
healing wound often has elevated levels of reactive oxygen species like H2O2 due to sus-
tained inflammation and oxidative stress. By employing catalase-based contrast agents
or even simply applying a low concentration of H2O2, one could non-invasively test the
wound’s chemistry.
If a burst of microbubble echoes is observed, it indicates the presence of catalytically
active compounds (enzymes or microbes producing catalase) and peroxide. In practical
terms, a strong bubbling response could signify areas of active inflammation or bacterial

==PAGE:18==
infection (many pathogenic bacteria, such as Staphylococcus, produce catalase). Con-
versely, a weak or no bubble response might indicate low peroxide levels, which could
correlate with a less inflamed environment or, if H2O2 was injected, possibly poor perfu-

It is important, of course, to interpret such results in context: a very dry or necrotic wound
might not distribute the H2O2 well even if bacteria are present, and some bacteria (like

trast. We are not just seeing the shape of the wound but potentially gauging its metabolic
or microbiological state through the lens of bubble generation.
In summary, using ultrasound with oxygen microbubbles (from H2O2 decomposition) pro-
vides a powerful and multifaceted tool for wound evaluation. The underlying physical
principles-acoustic impedance mismatch, enhanced scattering from oscillating microbub-
bles, and the unique non-linear cavitation effects-explain why even a small amount of
gas in a wound can produce a big jump in ultrasound signal. The integration of the H2O2
chemical reaction means that this gas can be generated on demand, at the specific site of
interest, and even tied to pathophysiology (since wounds themselves generate peroxide).
This background establishes the scientific basis for the thesis work: it justifies explor-
ing H2O2-induced oxygen microbubbles as contrast agents for wound imaging and moti-
vates the methodology. By leveraging well-understood acoustic phenomena and a bit of
clever chemistry, we aim to improve wound monitoring - making it more informative (both
structurally and functionally) while maintaining the safety and bedside convenience that
ultrasound is known for. The following sections of the thesis will build on this foundation,
detailing how we implement this idea and evaluate its effectiveness, but without delving
into a full literature review here, we have set the stage with the essential principles and
rationale for our approach.

3
State of the art
As outlined in the previous section, wound healing is a complex process that is further ex-
acerbated in chronic wounds by persistent infection, elevated oxidative stress, and local
hypoxia [38, 39]. In response to these multifactorial challenges, advanced biomaterials
such as nanoparticle-loaded hydrogels have emerged as promising alternatives to tradi-
tional dressings such as dry gauzes and occlusive films [5].
Hydrogels, composed of three-dimensional networks of hydrophilic polymers, are widely
used in wound care for their high water content, excellent biocompatibility, and ability
to maintain a moist healing environment. These matrices not only absorb exudate and
conform to irregular wound geometries, but also adhere gently to tissue without causing
trauma upon removal, thereby minimizing pain and secondary injury [40]. When NPs are
incorporated, these systems become multifunctional “smart” hydrogels that combine the
passive protective role of the polymer network with the bioactivity and responsiveness
of embedded nanomaterials. This synergy enables tailored therapeutic actions, including
antimicrobial activity, ROS scavenging, oxygen generation, or controlled delivery of bioac-
tive molecules [27]. Moreover, the modular design of these composites, ranging from nat-
ural to synthetic polymers and from physically to chemically crosslinked networks, allows
fine-tuning of mechanical properties, degradation kinetics, and biological interactions to
suit diverse wound types [41].
This section reviews the current state of hydrogel-nanoparticle systems for wound heal-
ing, where emerging systems are thus not only therapeutic but increasingly diagnostic,
capable of providing real-time feedback on the biochemical status of the wound, marking
a paradigm shift toward interactive and responsive wound management.
3.1
Hydrogel-nanoparticle composites for wound healing
Silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) are well-known antimicrobial agents; incorporating AgNPs
into hydrogels has shown enhanced antibacterial efficacy and improved wound closure in
vivo. Liu et al. (2021) reported a photocrosslinked silk fibroin-aloe vera hydrogel contain-
ing AgNPs that significantly accelerated full-thickness wound healing, promoting angio-

metal-oxide NPs like zinc oxide and titanium dioxide have also been explored for their an-

developed a dual-network silk fibroin/ carboxymethylcellulose hydrogel loaded with ul-

This ROS-scavenging and oxygen-generating dressing dramatically improved healing of
diabetic wounds, achieving 100% closure in 14 days versus <80% for a standard dress-
ing. Such results underscore the potential of nanogels to actively modulate the wound
microenvironment, accelerating tissue regeneration.

while useful, carbon-based antioxidants are often combined with other agents for a more
comprehensive ROS-scavenging strategy.

Underscored by the previous chapter is the prominence gained my cerium oxide nanopar-

repeatedly donate and accept oxygen [74, 75]. This redox couple allows CeO2 to mimic
antioxidant enzymes such as SOD and CAT, catalytically neutralizing ROS like superoxide
radicals and hydrogen peroxide in a regenerative manner [76].

chronic wound environments, where prolonged ROS scavenging is essential [82].

Hydrogel properties
SilMA is prepared by covalently grafting methacrylate groups onto silk fibroin (SF) side
chains, a structural protein derived from Bombyx mori silkworm silk [84]. SF is an attrac-
tive biomaterial for tissue repair due to its exceptional mechanical strength, biocompati-
bility, and tunable biodegradability. Its native toughness arises from a hierarchical struc-
ture: the fibroin heavy chain (∼390 kDa) features repetitive hydrophobic domains (rich in
Gly-Ala sequences) that self-assemble into crystalline β-sheet microdomains, embedded
within an amorphous hydrophilic matrix [85, 86]. The dominant β-sheet polymorph (“Silk
II”) imparts mechanical resilience and enzymatic stability to the network.
In hydrogels, the β-sheet content acts as a physical crosslinking mechanism, modulat-
ing both stiffness and degradation [84]. Processing methods such as sonication, alcohol
treatment, or thermal cycling can control β-sheet formation, enabling customization from
soft, fast-degrading gels to stiff, long-lasting scaffolds.
To produce SilMA, degummed SF is dissolved and reacted with glycidyl methacrylate
(GMA) or methacrylic anhydride (MA) under mildly basic conditions.
GMA reacts via

5.6
Ultrasound image acquisition and processing
Ultrasound imaging was employed to assess the generation of oxygen microbubbles re-
sulting from the catalytic decomposition of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) by nanoparticle-
loaded hydrogels. The experimental setup consisted of a water bath placed inside a
plastic container, serving as the acoustic medium. A soft silicone tube was affixed to the
inner wall of the container and filled with a test solution composed of H2O2 and nanoparti-
cles. The tube was sealed at both ends to maintain a closed environment under vacuum,
thereby preventing ambient air interference and allowing isolated monitoring of gas evolu-
tion. Silicone was specifically chosen as the tubing material due to its acoustic impedance
being close to that of water, which minimizes ultrasound wave reflection at the interface
and enables effective transmission into and out of the tube. This material property en-
sured that the interior of the tube was clearly visualized in the acquired images, allowing
accurate detection of contrast signal changes resulting from microbubble formation. A
handheld ultrasound probe was positioned above the silicone tube, immersed in the wa-
ter bath. The probe was connected to an iPhone via a dedicated mobile application, which
allowed real-time image acquisition and automated cloud-based synchronization with a
laptop for further image processing and analysis. Imaging parameters, including gain,
depth, and frequency, were kept constant across all samples.
The solutions tested included a range of H2O2 concentrations. A 1 M stock solution of
H2O2 was used to prepare samples at concentrations of 5, 10, 20, and 50 mM. To explore
lower concentration ranges, typical of in vitro studies, a 0.1 M intermediate stock was
used to prepare additional samples at 0.01, 0.05, and 0.1 mM. Each sample was freshly
prepared prior to imaging and loaded into individual silicone tubes.
Image processing was performed using custom Python scripts. The workflow began with
an interactive routine for selecting the region of interest (ROI), which allowed for the
manual isolation of the silicone tube area within each image. All pixel values outside
the selected ROI were set to zero in order to eliminate background contributions. Initial
mode analysis within the ROI revealed a dominant pixel intensity value of 15.4, indicating
the presence of low-level noise or isolated bright specks. To mitigate this, a threshold
mask was applied in which all pixel values below 16 were reassigned to zero. Follow-
ing denoising, gamma correction was applied to enhance contrast, and several contrast-
enhancement masks were tested to identify the most effective image representation. The
mask providing optimal visual clarity and histogram separation was selected for further
analysis.
Then quantitative analysis was performed on the processed images. Key metrics included
the mean pixel intensity within the ROI, the mode intensity value and its standard devia-
tion, and the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), calculated as the ratio of mean signal intensity to
the standard deviation of background intensity. These parameters were used to compare
the ultrasound contrast behavior of different nanoparticle formulations across a range of
H2O2 concentrations, thereby providing insight into their oxygen and contrast-generating
capacity under ultrasound monitoring.

Based on the combined enzymatic performance, particle stability, and synthetic repro-

CeMg, while exhibiting some CAT activity, showed limited superoxide neutralization and
highly variable particle dispersion. Thus, CeMn emerged as the most balanced and sta-

in biological systems. The superior behavior of CeMn is attributed to the effects of Mn

distinct pale blue colour after aging, consistent with the presence of Ce3+ ions and oxy-

the lead nanozyme aligns with the requirements of the hydrogel-based sensing platform
under development. A high CAT-like activity is essential for generating oxygen bubbles
upon contact with H2O2, serving as the acoustic contrast mechanism for US detection. At
the same time, sufficient SOD activity is needed to prevent accumulation of superoxide
radicals, ensuring comprehensive ROS neutralization in the wound environment.

in both the bulk lattice and surface chemistry of CeO2 nanoparticles.

this method is sensitive to measurement error in v, which appears on both axes. In high-
variability systems such as 15:1, this can obscure trends and increase regression uncer-
tainty. The Hanes-Woolf plot, which rearranges MM into [S]/v vs [S], is often considered
more robust when v is noisy, as it minimizes reciprocal terms. However, it is susceptible
to skewing from low substrate concentrations. In this study, HW analysis of the 15:1 data
returned the highestKm value (20.11 mM), diverging significantly from the other methods.
This inflation is consistent with the system’s failure to achieve saturation, and suggests
that HW may overestimate Km under such conditions.
Collectively, these alternative analyses underscore that in non-ideal enzymatic systems
- where saturation is partial or kinetic parameters are obscured by batch variability - no
single method is definitive. Instead, cross-validation using multiple fitting strategies pro-
vides a clearer understanding of catalytic behavior and its limitations. The 15:1 Mn:CeO2
system presents the most divergent behavior across methods. While EH produced a
Vmax of 1.26 µmol∙L−1∙s−1 and Km = 10.37 mM, the HW method resulted in Vmax = 1.54
µmol∙L−1∙s−1, with a Km of 20.11 mM. The spread inKm values is greater than for the
other two systems, and the lack of clear saturation in the original MM plots suggests that
these parameters should be interpreted with caution. The EH plot (see attached) exhibits
high dispersion, with v appearing nearly flat across a broad v/[S] range. The HW plot, by
contrast, shows a clean fit at high [S] but suffers from disproportionate leverage by the
highest substrate points (100 mM), skewing the slope and overestimating Km.
This discrepancy illustrates a known issue in HW plots: they are highly sensitive to sys-
tematic error in high-[S] conditions, and because [S]/v is plotted against [S], experimen-
tal error in v only appears on one axis, leading to misleading linearity in noisy systems.
The EH method, while theoretically more balanced, places v on both axes and amplifies
variability when v fluctuates - as seen in the wide SD bars of this system. While MM
and LB remain primary tools, the inclusion of EH and HW plots is valuable in systems
like 15:1 where saturation is not reached and replicate variability is high. However, the
discrepancies between EH and HW parameters for 15:1 reinforce the need for cautious
interpretation when linearization assumptions are not fully met. For the 5:1 nanozyme,
consistency across all fitting methods - nonlinear and linear - confirms its suitability as the
most catalytically efficient and kinetically robust system among those tested.

0.05%, and 0.1% w/v (see Figure 6.20 ). For each group, four independent replicates

stress-strain curves up to approximately 15% strain, within the physiologically relevant
deformation range for skin.
Figure 6.20: Compression curves of SilMA hydrogels with increasing CeMn NP content.
Published data on unfilled SilMA hydrogels report relatively low compressive strength/-
modulus. For example, Lin et al. measured a compressive stress of ∼70.1 kPa at 80%
strain (10 mm/min), while other studies reported moduli as low as ∼20 kPa for 10% (w/v)
SilMA tested at 50% strain [90, 98]. More broadly, SF gels are generally classified as “soft”
when their compressive modulus is below 29 kPa, and “tough” when it exceeds 1-2 MPa
[99]. Our pristine SilMA samples, showing a compressive modulus in the range of ∼129-
215 kPa, lie well above the soft regime and are consistent with reinforced or moderately
crosslinked systems, yet still well below the “tough” range. Notably, our SilMA controls
displayed a significantly higher modulus than commonly reported for unfilled SilMA gels.
This discrepancy may stem from our optimized photopolymerization protocol. In partic-
ular, the concentration of the photoinitiator LAP is known to directly influence crosslink-
ing density and mechanical stiffness. Yeh et al. (2025) demonstrated that increasing
LAP concentration in SilMA formulations led to a marked rise in compressive modulus-
from ∼15 kPa to over 40 kPa-by enhancing network formation during UV curing [100].
Thus, our relatively high modulus values may be partially attributed to enhanced poly-
merization efficiency under our specific LAP and UV exposure conditions. A comparative
analysis of the tested systems revealed that the incorporation of CeMn NPs significantly
modulated the mechanical response of the hydrogels. Pristine SilMA exhibited a com-
pressive modulus of approximately 168 kPa. Upon addition of 0.025 wt% CeMn, the
modulus increased slightly to 198 kPa, while 0.05 wt% loading yielded a more substantial
enhancement to 405 kPa, suggesting progressive matrix reinforcement due to the inclu-
sion of rigid nanozymes. Interestingly, at 0.1 wt% loading, the compressive modulus rose
sharply to 1220 kPa, representing a nearly sevenfold increase over the unfilled control.
The modulus slightly plateaued or decreased in some replicates, likely due to nanopar-
ticle agglomeration disrupting uniform stress distribution. This trend aligns with previous

This study examined the effect of Mn-doped CeO2 NP incorporation on the viscoelas-

across different NP-loading concentrations. Frequency sweep measurements revealed
that doping SilMA with Mn:CeO2 nanoparticles modulates hydrogel stiffness, though not
in a straightforward, dose-dependent manner. G′ increased upon nanoparticle addition
compared to the undoped control. Among the tested concentrations (0.025 wt%, 0.05
wt% mg, and 0.1 wt% per sample), the 0.025 wt% group displayed the highest G′ across
the entire frequency range, followed by 0.1 wt%, control, and finally the 0.05 wt%.

6.6.6
Benchmarking against literature
To contextualize our findings, the catalytic performance of the SilMA-loaded CeMn 5:1
hydrogels was benchmarked against prominent CeO2 and CeMn-based nanozymes re-
ported in the literature. Li et al. (2025)developed porous CeO2 nanozymes that exhibited a
maximum velocity of approximately 1.17 µM∙s−1 and Km of 4.69 mM under dark conditions
[72]. Their activity was significantly enhanced under light stimulation, relying on ligand-to-
metal charge transfer mechanisms. However, such systems require photonic activation
and intricate synthetic strategies, limiting their applicability for physiological wound envi-
ronments.
Yeh et al. (2025) reported a Mn–Ce dual–atom catalyst supported on carbon matrices,
achieving a Vmax of 0.321 µM∙s−1 and a Km of 27.7 mM [100]. Although mechanistically
sophisticated, this system displayed lower catalytic rates and required complex structural
control. In comparison, our SilMA–CeMn hydrogels demonstrated competitive or superior
catalytic performance directly within a wound-compatible hydrogel matrix. The 0.10 wt%
NP-loaded SilMA hydrogel achieved a Vmax of 2.375 µM∙s−1 with a Km of 3.79 mM, out-
performing both literature references either in maximum catalytic rate or substrate affinity.
Lower NP concentrations (0.025–0.05 wt%) also yielded effective catalysis, with Vmax val-
ues ranging from approximately 1.7 to 3.3 µM∙s−1 and Km between 6.67 and 8.44 mM.
These results highlight the advantages of the SilMA–CeMn system: it delivers high cat-
alytic efficiency without the need for external activation, elaborate post-synthetic modi-
fication, or hybridization with carbon scaffolds. Furthermore, its simple co-precipitation
synthesis ensures reproducibility and scalability, making it a strong candidate for clinical
deployment in wound monitoring and therapy.

Limitations
While this work establishes a promising foundation for a multifunctional ROS-scavenging
and ultrasound-responsive hydrogel, several limitations remain that must be acknowl-
edged. First, the mechanical response of the hydrogel upon nanoparticle incorporation
displayed a non-monotonic trend, with low nanoparticle concentrations either softening
the gel or decreasing its tensile strength. This suggests that nanoparticle loading can dis-
rupt the polymer network, potentially by interfering with the crosslinking process or causing
heterogeneous clustering within the matrix. The absence of a clear dose-dependent me-
chanical enhancement indicates that optimization of nanoparticle dispersion and loading
protocols remains necessary. A related issue is the lack of direct assessment of nanopar-
ticle distribution within the hydrogel bulk. Although target weight percentages were used
during fabrication, no post-synthesis quantification or imaging was performed to verify ho-
mogeneous dispersion or actual retained loading. This introduces uncertainty regarding
the reproducibility of both enzymatic activity and mechanical reinforcement across sam-
ples. Enzymatic kinetics analysis, though rigorously performed using multiple models,
yielded data sets with considerable variability and occasionally high standard deviations.
These fluctuations may stem from batch-to-batch differences in nanoparticle synthesis,
variations in assay conditions, or intrinsic heterogeneity in the nanozyme activity itself.
This variability highlights a need for improved standardization in synthesis and assay pro-
tocols to ensure reliable performance data. Furthermore, while the biocompatibility of
individual components such as SilMA and Mn-Ce2 is supported by existing literature, no
direct cytotoxicity or cell culture studies were conducted in this work to validate the safety
of the composite hydrogel. This omission is significant given the intended biomedical
application, especially since the photoinitiator LAP, though generally considered low in
toxicity, has not been tested here for residual effects post-crosslinking in the presence of
nanozymes.
Another critical aspect not addressed is the hydrogel’s adhesion properties to biological
tissues. Adhesion is a key functional requirement for wound dressings, influencing their
stability, sealing capacity, and integration with host tissue. The lack of both mechanical
adhesion testing and biological adhesion assessment represents a gap that must be filled
before considering clinical application.
Additionally, the porosity and microstructure of the hydrogel were not characterized be-
yond mechanical testing. Porosity is known to directly influence nutrient diffusion, cell
infiltration, and tissue integration, especially in regenerative medicine contexts. Without
benchmarking the pore architecture—either via SEM or porosimetry—it remains uncertain
whether the gel’s microstructure would support cellular colonization or vascular ingrowth,
both critical for wound healing.
Another key limitation is the absence of crystallographic analysis to confirm the incorpo-
ration of manganese into the Ce)2 lattice structure. While the enhanced catalytic activity
observed in Mn-doped samples suggests successful doping and increased oxygen va-
cancy concentration, no X-ray diffraction (XRD) studies were performed to substantiate
lattice distortion or phase modification. XRD analysis could have provided direct evidence
of Mn substitution within the ceria fluorite lattice, typically observable as peak shifts or
broadening in characteristic diffraction patterns. The lack of such structural validation
leaves the precise mechanism of activity enhancement speculative. Without confirmation

of doping at the crystallographic level, it remains uncertain whether the catalytic improve-
ment is due solely to lattice engineering or possibly attributable to surface adsorption
or secondary phase formation. Future work should include systematic XRD character-
ization, coupled with techniques like XPS or TEM, to elucidate the structural basis of
the nanozyme’s enzymatic properties. Finally, while the ultrasound response was suc-
cessfully demonstrated ex vivo, no long-term stability studies were performed to assess
whether the ROS-scavenging capacity and bubble generation persist under physiological
conditions, nor was the ultrasound signal quantified in biologically relevant settings such
as simulated wound fluid or tissue models. This limits the immediate translatability of the
system to in vivo applications.
Overall, these limitations underscore the need for further systematic investigation into the
biological, mechanical, and functional properties of the Mn–Ce2/SilMA hydrogel to ensure
its suitability as a clinically viable wound management platform.

Conclusion and Future Perspectives
This thesis established a Mn–CeO2-loaded SilMa hydrogel as a multifunctional platform
for chronic wound management, integrating ROS scavenging with ultrasound-based sens-
ing via catalytic oxygen bubble generation. Among the tested formulations, Mn–CeO2
nanoparticles at a 5:1 doping ratio offered the best compromise between catalytic activ-
ity, colloidal stability, and reproducibility. When incorporated into SilMA hydrogels, these
nanoparticles conferred both superoxide dismutase- and catalase-like activities, enabling
effective ROS neutralization within physiological ranges.
Mechanical testing revealed that nanoparticle loading influenced tensile properties in a
non-linear fashion. While 0.10 wt% loading enhanced modulus and strength toward skin-
mimetic values, it compromised extensibility and introduced aggregation risks. The 0.05 wt%
Mn–CeO2 formulation emerged as the optimal compromise: providing balanced catalytic
performance, mechanical compliance, and structural homogeneity, thereby representing
the most promising candidate for further development.
The system’s ROS-scavenging kinetics compared favorably with literature-reported nanozymes,
particularly given its simple synthesis and lack of dependence on external stimuli. How-
ever, observed variability in enzymatic assays underscores the need for tighter control
over nanoparticle dispersion and gel homogeneity.
Future work should address the structural confirmation of Mn doping (e.g., via XRD),
systematic biological validation, and detailed characterization of adhesion and porosity.
Optimization of nanoparticle loading will also be crucial to maximize therapeutic potential
without sacrificing mechanical integrity.
Overall, the Mn–CeO2/SilMA composite hydrogel presents a compelling foundation for a
theranostic wound dressing—combining antioxidant activity with bioresponsive imaging—
and merits further exploration toward clinical translation.
