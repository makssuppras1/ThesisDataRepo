M.SC. THESIS
Abstract
This thesis investigates the speed-power exponent which is often said to be at least three. Based
on this speed-power relationship, slow steaming is feasible in order to minimise fuel consump-
tion and emissions. In novel literature the speed-power exponent of three has been shown to
be an invalid assumption at low vessel speeds.
The project is based on operational data from a shipping company consisting of noon reports
for 88 tankers divided into 15 sister ship groups. The tankers are between 35,000 and 110,000
DWT and there are up to four years of noon reports for some of the vessels. In order to study
the speed-power relationship, a piecewise linear regression taking draught and different speed
intervals into account is introduced. Thereby the speed-power exponent at different draught
conditions and speed intervals are determined.
It has been shown that the speed-power exponent is signiﬁcantly lower than three at speed
intervals below the design speed. It has also been shown that it is difﬁcult to prove the speed-
power exponent of at least three at high speed intervals since the speeds actually sailed by the
vessels are very often lower than the speed intervals from the model test curves. The model
test curves have though been proven to have a speed-power exponent of at least three for all
draughts.
In order to do vessel power prediction and performance monitoring it is necessary to have
accurate benchmarks which is often based on the model test curves. By knowing that the
exponent is decreasing with decreasing speed, a better benchmark at low speed intervals can
be deﬁned. The signiﬁcantly lower exponent at low speed intervals is very important in the
slow steaming debate since slow steaming will not be as good as often stated if the exponent
is not constant. A constant exponent will lead to underestimation of the needed power at low
speeds and thereby the effect of slow steaming is overestimated.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Fuel consumption is a very essential part of shipping today since a big part of the proﬁt de-
pends on minimisation of this consumption. But even more important is the minimisation of
emissions in order to make the shipping industry greener and more sustainable. The emissions
of most concern are CO2, SOx, NOx and particular matters (IMO, 2014). This has lead to vessel
performance monitoring in order to control the fuel consumption by intervention before the
performance is too low and therefore unsustainable and unproﬁtable. The biggest factor of
decreasing performance over time is biofouling which is growth of micro organisms, algae and
plants on the hull sides and bottom as well as on the propeller. This means increased hull side
roughness and increased frictional resistance of the ship when moving through water which is
equal to increased fuel consumption and emissions.
In the beginning of the 2000’s the International Maritime Organization (IMO) prohibited use of
the very efﬁcient anti-fouling paints containing organotin compounds such as tributyltin (TBT)
which were harmful for the maritime environment (IMO, 2002). A full prohibition of these
coatings were in force from 2008 which meant that all coatings now has to be sustainable for
the maritime environment. Fouling has therefore had an increased focus for the last 20 years
since sustainability has gained high importance in society and the new coatings are less efﬁ-
cient than the toxic ones previously used.
Vessel performance monitoring began around 2008-2009 after the complete prohibition of the
TBT coatings. Maersk started their vessel performance department in 20091 and other shipping
companies did the same around that time. Today vessel performance is often outsourced from
the shipping companies which means there now are many different companies offering ves-
sel performance monitoring e.g. COACH Solutions, Vessel Performance Solutions, Propulsion
Dynamics, GreenSteam and FORCE Technology just to name a few of the Danish companies.
The essential part of vessel performance monitoring is to compare the current performance for
a given vessel against a benchmark. The benchmark is often estimated by model test done in
a towing tank but can also be done with computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) or with a sea
trial where there is corrected for the inﬂuence of current, wind and waves. The benchmark
is power or fuel consumption as a function of speed at different draught conditions and this
benchmark is describing the newbuilding performance of the vessel which in the theory is the
best the vessel can perform. These benchmark curves which will be called speed-power curves
are the focus of this study, especially the curves at lower speed intervals.
1.1
Background
The focus on fuel consumption and vessel performance driven by the present green and sus-
tainable mindset has lead to many initiatives in order to minimise emissions. One of them is
slow-steaming which is signiﬁcant reduction of the engine load even down to 10-20% maxi-
mum continuous rating (MCR) according to (MAN Energy Solutions, 2012) and (Psaraftis and
Kontovas, 2014), which is achieved by reducing the speed of the vessel. As there is a simple lin-

Furthermore sea trials and model test are often done around the design speed and therefore at
fairly high speed intervals. This has been observed as part of working as a student assistant at
COACH Solutions who provides vessel performance monitoring and voyage optimisation to
shipping companies. Therefore COACH Solutions wants better estimation of the speed-power
curves at lower vessel speed intervals where it is not just done by extrapolating the sea trials
or model curves but by investigating data for many vessels and vessel groups. The problem
can be seen in Figure 1 where noon report data is plotted. The power is corrected for added

Previous work
Three articles were the basis for this project and especially (Adland et al., 2020) which started
the idea for the project. All the articles are from 2020 which shows the relevance for this study.

(Adland et al., 2020) - Optimal ship speed and the cubic law revisited: Empirical evi-

This article is the starting point for this thesis and how the derived models are approached. The
study is based on more than 11,000 noon reports from 6 Suezmax and 10 Aframax tankers from
January 2013 to December 2016. The Suezmax tankers have a deadweight of around 160,000

1.2.2
(Taskar and Andersen, 2020) - Beneﬁt of speed reduction for ships in different weather
conditions
This article investigates fuel savings due to speed reductions in different weather conditions
where voyages at many different speeds are simulated instead of using noon reports. Six differ-
ent vessels have been investigated - three different container ships, two different bulk carriers
and one tanker. It is concluded that assuming a cubic speed-power relation can cause a signiﬁ-
cant error when calculating the effect of speed reduction. At the same time it is also concluded
that fuel savings from speed reduction are very dependent of the weather conditions. This
means that the effect of speed reduction is much smaller in bad weather than in good weather.
1.2.3
(Tillig et al., 2020) - Reduced environmental impact of marine transport through speed
reduction and wind assisted propulsion
This article proposes a model for improved prediction of fuel consumption for generic ships
where main dimensions are given. Furthermore this model is coupled with an economic model
such that the costs and income of a journey can be optimised together with the fuel consump-
tion.
1.3
Data
Data for this project was kindly provided by COACH Solutions. The data set is containing
noon reports, vessel data and speed-power curves from model test for 15 sister ship groups
each including between 2 and 13 ships, meaning 88 ships in total. There are up to four years
of noon reports from some of the vessels and the total number of noon reports is 51,826 after
ﬁltering. All the ships are tankers and all the data is from the same shipping company. The
data for the project will be described in-depth in Section 3.
1.4
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the speed-power exponent (elasticity) which is often
said to be at least three. This is done by analysing operational data consisting of noon reports
from tankers through econometric models based on linear regression and least squares. At the
same time both the operational data and speed-power models based on econometric models
are compared with speed-power curves from model test for validation of both. The thesis
originates from the fact that these model tests are very often done at speed intervals higher
than where the vessels operate. Therefore ways of extrapolating the model tests down to lower
speed intervals are needed when doing vessel performance analysis where speed-power curves
are used as benchmark.

2
Theory
In this section the theory that is essential for the project is described. This is primarily the two
topics: power prediction and linear regression. The theory for power prediction is from the
International Towing Tank Conference (ITTC) (1978 ITTC Performance Prediction Method (ITTC,
2017a) if otherwise is not stated. This theory is also found in the ISO 19030 (ISO, 2016a), (ISO,
2016b) and (ISO, 2016c). The theory for linear regression is from STAT 501: Regression Methods
(Pardoe et al., 2021) which is the online material for a graduate statistics course at The Pennsyl-
vania State University based on the book Applied linear regression models (Neter et al., 2003).
2.1
Resistance and power prediction
The total resistance on a vessel is determined by summing all the resistance components:
Rt = Rsw + Rwind + Rwaves + Rswell
(2.1)
Where Rsw is the stillwater resistance, Rwind is the added resistance due to wind, Rwaves is the
added resistance due to wind generated waves and Rswell is the added resistance due to swell.
From the total resistance the effective power Pe is determined:
Pe = Rt V
(2.2)
This is the power needed to propel the vessel forward at the given speed V. But due to loss
induced by efﬁciencies the delivered power Pd from the engine is:
Pd = Pe
ηt
(2.3)
Where ηt is the total efﬁciency which is a product of all the efﬁciencies in the system: the hull
efﬁciency ηh, propeller efﬁciency ηp, relative rotative efﬁciency ηrr and mechanical efﬁciency
ηm:
ηt = ηh + ηp + ηrr + ηm
(2.4)
The fuel consumption is calculated from the delivered power via the SFOC curve which de-
scribes the fuel consumption at different loads. The SFOC curve is engine dependent but can
generally be described as a U-shaped curve with the lowest value at 75-85% MCR and the curve
can be approximated with a second order polynomial, (IMO, 2014, p. 110).

The stillwater resistance is determined by:

Where ρw is the water density, V the vessel speed, Sw the wetted surface area of the vessel and
Ct the total resistance coefﬁcient which is the sum of all the related resistance coefﬁcients:
Ct = (1 + k) Cf s + Cr + ∆Cf + Ca + Caas + Cas
(2.6)

2.5

The speed-power relationship can be approximated with P ≈V3 as described in Section 1.1
which is often used. This is based on the stillwater resistance in Equation (2.5) and the relation

2.7
Linear regression
Linear regression is a basic approach to modelling the relationship between an independent
variable xest (predictor) and a dependent variable yest (response) as a linear function:
yest = β0 + β1 xest
(2.17)
Where β0 and β1 are the regression coefﬁcients describing the intercept and slope respectively.
This simple linear model is by deﬁnition used to describe the observed data (xobs, yobs):
yobs = β0 + β1 xobs + ϵ
(2.18)
Where ϵ are the residuals and deﬁned as the difference between the observed response yobs and
estimated response yest:
ϵ = yobs −yest
(2.19)
The residuals are zero if the data is completely linear but otherwise there will be some devi-
ation between the observed and estimated response seen as scatter around the estimated ﬁt
when plotting the observed data together with the linear model.
In order to rely on simple linear regression four assumptions should be met:
• Linear dependency between predictor x and response y. This is best shown by plotting
the observed data together with the linear model.
• Independent residuals such that the residuals are time independent and independent
from each other.
• Normal distributed residuals.
• Equal variance at all values of x.

Ship-Desmo is an excel tool made by Hans Otto Kristensen and DTU (Kristensen, 2020) which
can be used to determine principal dimensions as well as predict power requirements and emis-
sions. It is based on empirical and semi-empirical equations, e.g. the Guldhammer-Harvald
and Holtrop-Mennen methods, (Pedersen et al., 2010) and (Holtrop and Mennen, 1982). It can
therefore give estimates on required power based on principal dimensions which is necessary
for this project since exact hull form, number of propeller blades, propeller diameter and sim-
ilar important variables in ship design are not known. The DTU programme I-Ship4 was also
considered to be used in this project since it can also do power prediction but as many of the
propeller variables would have to be guessed, Ship-Desmo was more suitable. Futhermore
Ship-Desmo has vessel type speciﬁc excel sheets, therefore only the tanker version of the Ship-
Desmo excel sheet was used in this project.
In the ”Ship data” tab in the excel sheet all known vessel data was used as input. There-
fore, the ”Alternative 1” column was used as more input is possible compared to the ”Default
values” column. See Appendix A.2 to see screenshots of how some of the vessel groups are
deﬁned in Ship-Desmo. Hereby the wanted vessel parameters seen in Table 1, 2, 3 and 4 are
obtained. It is though noted that the displacement is in [tons] in Ship-Desmo and in [m3] in
the four tables with vessel data from COACH Solutions, but they do agree with ∇s [tons] =
∇s [m3] · 1.025 [tons/m3].
The speed-power curves are generated by using the tab ”PAS2” which is power prediction for
service condition for main dimensions for ”Alternative 1”. The draught condition is deﬁned
in ”Ship data” by the capacity utilisation where 100% is the design condition. The capacity %
therefore has been found so the actual draughts in Ship-Desmo agree with the draught con-
ditions for the model curves for each vessel group. The capacity % for each vessel group can
be seen in Table 6. In the ”Ship data” tab the ”Service speed at actual draught” is set to speed

4
Methods
In this section the theory from Section 2 is coupled to the project and it is shown how the theory
will later be used in the analysis. Therefore, a simple example of a piecewise linear regression
model is described in-depth.
4.1
Simple model
The starting point for the model generation is the most simple power law model describing the
relationship between power P and speed V reported in the noon reports:
P = x1 Vx2
(4.1)
Where x1 and x2 are the unknown variables. Here the exponent x2 is the speed-power elasticity

The simple model is the basis for the extended model that take the draught and speed intervals

4.2.1
Draught
One of the most important parameters regarding the speed-power relationship is the draught.
As seen in the model curves and the Ship-Desmo curves in the previous section, the ballast

curve and scantling curve should differ quite a bit which should also be covered by the model.
At ﬁrst there was built different models for different draughts by grouping the noon reports
to different bins, e.g. ballast, design and scantling draught groups. Hereby the data was seg-
mented and the models were only affected by data with around the same draught. This worked
well but in order to secure consistency, full use of all the data and only working with one model,
a model taking care of the draught by itself was built:
ln(P) = ln(x1) + x2 ln(V) + x3 T
(4.6)
Where T is the mean of the reported for and aft draught. This extended model with draught
in the model means that speed-power curves at different draught will be parallel but shifted
when studied in a log-log plot. This is called an additive effect5. In order to take into account
that the model curves are not parallel in log-log domain, an interaction term is introduced since
the exponent x2 seems to be dependent of the draught.
ln(P) = ln(x1) + x2 ln(V) + x3 T + x4 ln(V) T
(4.7)
Hereby the slope for the linear equation is dependent on the draught:
ln(P) = ln(x1) + (x2 + x4 T) ln(V) + x3 T
(4.8)
When transformed back into the nonlinear form the speed-power relationship becomes:
P = x1 V(x2+x4 T) exp(x3 T)
(4.9)
This will later be used as the most basic speed-power model as the draught is necessary to
describe the speed-power relationship. The further extensions of the model will therefore be
added to this model in Equation (4.7) and (4.9).

and 14 knots and afterwards optimised with different number of breakpoints and their loca-
tions. This is done by minimising the sum of squared errors (SSE), Equation (2.34).

where the mean slope changes the most abruptly according to the speciﬁed minimum thresh-
old6. Hereby the index is found and the speed from the associated noon report is then the
breakpoint. This will be shown in the analysis in Section 6.

4.2.3
Other predictor variables
Other predictor variables can be added in a similar way as draught and speed to the speed-
power regression model. It would be obvious to include weather data as a predictor variable,
which can be added as an additive effect by deﬁning it in an extra column in the A-matrix.
Each weather variable could then be included as a column. In this project the weather vari-
ables are relative wind direction, relative wind speed, wave height, relative wave direction,
wave period, swell height, relative swell direction and swell period. All the directions are in
0-360 degrees, it would therefore be beneﬁcial to transform this to 0-180 as e.g. wind from 10
degrees and 350 degrees would have the same effect on the vessel. This can be achieved in
MATLAB by abs(wrapTo180(dir)).
It would also be relevant to include time as a variable since the vessel performance will decrease
over time when the hull gets fouled. To implement this it would be necessary to distinguish
between different vessels as the performance over time would be individual for each vessel.
By distinguishing between vessels the model would thereby capture vessel speciﬁc variations
which can be caused by different crew, variations in hull construction and more. This would
be implemented with a categorical predictor similar to the dummy variables introduced in the
23

piecewise linear regression. There would then be a column in the A-matrix for each vessel and
this column would be 1 for every data point (noon report) associated with the speciﬁc vessel
and 0 for all other vessels. Thereby a vessel speciﬁc predictor variable xi is introduced in the
model.
Other variables that could be included in the regression are e.g. trim, water salinity, water
temperature and water depth. It is though important to keep in mind that the correct variables
are included in the model such that it is correctly speciﬁed. Otherwise it will be underspeciﬁed,
contain extraneous variables or be overspeciﬁed8:
• Underspeciﬁed: The model will not capture essential relationships in the data. In this
case it could be to exclude draught as an interaction effect such that the speed-power
exponent is the same regardless of draught.
• Extraneous variables: The model includes variables with no inﬂuence on the response or
the other predictor variables.
• Overspeciﬁed: The model will then contain redundant variables e.g. the predictor vari-
ables are correlated which can lead to problems with multicollinearity.

5.1
Filtering
Filtering of the data is necessary both in order to remove outliers that makes no physical sense
but also in order to get the linear regression to work. Linear regression in MATLAB cannot
work with NaN when the robust built-in functions are not used. This means noon reports con-
sisting of NaN in columns of interest for the regression have to be removed. But it is though
still wanted to have as much data as possible for the regression which means that the ﬁlters are
not aggressive. This is possible because COACH Solutions has a lot of validations when the
data is reported and the data has also been processed by COACH Solutions which means that
the data is not completely raw. (Adland et al., 2020) only study data in the 6-16 [kn] interval
in order to remove outliers but in this study there is only data from around 5 [kn] to 16 [kn]
without speed ﬁltering, see the ﬁgures with noon report vs speed-power curves in Section 3.3.
The ﬁlters applied are:
• Mean draught > Tb −1 [m] where Tb is mean of Tb,a f t and Tb,f or in Table 1, 2, 3 and 4.
• Mean draught < Ts + 1 [m].
• Corrected speed > 0 [kn].
• Power corrected > 0 [kW].
• Power corrected < 1.1 · MCR [kW].
• Hindcast data ̸= NaN.

5.3
Power corrections
In the noon reports there are both power based on fuel consumption and corrected power
where wind and waves are accounted for. Therefore it is shown how the corrected power is
obtained even though it is not the exact same method as COACH Solutions. The theory from
Section 2.3 and 2.4 is used to correct the power.
The wind corrections are determined with Equation (2.13) and Figure 2. In order to estimate
the wind coefﬁcient, laden is deﬁned as draughts above (Td −2 [m]). Since the transverse area
is only known for one draught, this is also accounted for with change in draught multiplied
by the breadth. The noon reports contain relative wind direction but otherwise the relative
direction had to be determined from the vessel heading and true wind direction in an earth
coordinate system, see (ITTC, 2017a).
The wave corrections are done with the DTU Added Resistance Tool, (Nielsen, 2015) and (Mar-
tinsen, 2016), used on hindcast data for wind wave data and swell data separately. The noon
reports have wave direction given with 0 degree as headwind but the DTU Added Resistance
Tool calculates with 180 degree as headwind, which therefore is accounted for. The DTU Added
Resistance Tool can only determine added resistance due to waves at headwind (180 degrees)
to beam wind (90 degrees). Therefore the added resistance due to waves is 0 when the wave
direction is below 90 degrees. This is a big source of error when comparing with COACH
Solutions´ corrected power where added resistance due to waves is calculated for all wave di-
rections.
When wind and wave corrections are subtracted from the resistance based on fuel consumption
calculated with Equation (2.2), the stillwater resistance can be determined and the corrected
power afterwards. In Figure 11 the corrected power from this project is plotted against the
corrected power from COACH Solutions. It is seen that the corrected power agree to some
extent but it can also be seen that the corrections in this project are missing some essential
corrections since the COACH Solutions corrected power is always equal or lower than the

vessels and (Td −3 [m]) for the LR2 vessels. This is done based on the draught distributions
seen in Figure 13, 16 and in Appendix B.1. For T050-09 the separation draught would be at
T = 9 [m] which is the green part in Figure 13 and at a ”valley” in the draught distribution
for all vessels in Figure 12. The same draught separation apply to T050-12 and can be seen in
Figure 16 and 15.
From the descriptive statistics it can be seen that the mean speed and draught are relatively sim-
ilar for vessels within the same deadweight segments. This especially apply to the MR2 vessels
(see Table 8 and 9) and LR1 vessels (see Table 12) since the vessels in those vessel groups al-
most have the same dimensions and deadweight. But it is clear that the power differ within the
deadweight segments. T050-09 and T053-13 in the MR2 segment both use much more power
at the same average speed and draught than the 3 other vessels in the segment which all are
around 3150 [kW] (mean across all vessels and all draughts). T050-09 has a mean power of
3961 [kW] for all draughts and T053-13 has 4301 [kW] for all draughts. This is contrary to the
expectations from the previous comparison of T050-09 and T050-12. In Table 10 it is seen that
the average speed for T050-09 and T050-12 are 12.2 [kn] and the average draught is 9.7 [m] for
both vessel groups. But there are 734 [kW] difference in average power which is about 20%.
The statistics are based on 6759 and 5359 noon reports respectively.
There is seen a signiﬁcant variation in the LR1 segment in Table 12 where the power vary be-
tween 3870 [kW] and 5026 [kW] for all draughts for the 6 vessel groups. It can be concluded
that T074-02 and T075-08 are similar, T074-04, T075-02 and T075-05 are similar and T075-05 is
by itself in the middle with 4430 [kW] (all draughts).
There is in general larger speed and power in laden than in ballast which apply to almost all
vessels - only 12 out of 88 vessels have larger speed in ballast than in laden. Only Vessel 1 in
T050-09 has larger power in ballast than in laden but it is based on only 30 noon reports as de-
scribed in Section 5.4.1. The larger speed in laden condition agrees with the fact that time con-
straints and contracts have to be complied with when sailing with cargo (Adland et al., 2020).
The larger power in the laden condition agrees with the larger speed for the laden condition
but also the general fact that draught increase power. This can be seen from the speed-power
curves from model test.

5.5
Speed-power curve comparison
In order to spot obvious differences between vessel groups in the same deadweight segments
(independent of the previous descriptive statistics), the speed-power curves from model tests
are compared. The vessel groups would be expected to have relatively similar speed-power
curves at the same draughts for vessel groups in the same deadweight segment. The four
deadweight segments can be seen in Figure 18, 19, 20 and 21. For the the MR1 segment it
would be expected that the speed-power curves for T035-03 are below the curves for T039-06
since the only big difference in vessel parameters between the two vessel groups is a 14 [m]
difference in Lpp, see Table 1. As T035-03 has higher curves than T039-06, this must then be
due to the 10 year difference in year of construction. The same apply to the MR2 vessel groups
where T053-13 differs from the other vessel groups in Figure 19 and the vessels are at least
four years older than the vessels in the other vessel groups. In the descriptive statistics in the
previous section it was clear that T053-13 differs from the other MR2 vessel groups but here
it is evident at the design state. It is though more difﬁcult to see how much T050-09 differs
from the other MR2 vessel groups including T050-12 as the speed-power curves look similar
especially at 12-13 [kn] where the T050-09 vessels primarily sail according to the descriptive
statistics. But in the descriptive statistics T050-09 was closer to T053-13 regarding mean power.
The speed-power curves for T050-09 look very similar to the other MR2 vessel groups at bal-
last and design condition but the scantling curve is clearly different from the other MR2 vessel
groups scantling curves in Figure 19. But T050-09 is not older than T050-10 and T050-12 so the
difference in speed-power curves and mean power in the descriptive statistics is not due to the
vessel group being signiﬁcantly older like T053-13.
This tendency of older vessels with higher speed-power curves is not as easy to see for the
LR1 vessels in Figure 20 even though there is a 10 year difference between T074-02, T075-04
and T075-08 compared to T074-04, T075-02 and T075-05. The newest vessel groups have lower
curves at the scantling draughts but the differences are less at the ballast draughts. However,
the differences are more apparent in the descriptive statistics where T074-02, T075-04 and T075-
08 are the vessel groups with the highest mean power. For the LR2 vessels in Figure 21 there is
again a 10 year difference which means that bigger vessel group T110-06 has lower curves than
T105-02 which is similar to the MR1 vessels.
Something worth noting is that the speed-power curves for the newer vessels are covering a
larger speed interval and the speed interval is often increased downwards. This means that the
lowest speed for the speed-power curves is substantially reduced, e.g. from 13 [kn] to 10 [kn].
This is easily seen in Figure 20 and 21. Here the newer vessels have presumably been designed
to also perform well at lower speeds since the model test cover down to 10 [kn].

In this section the results from the project are presented where the focus is on the speed depen-

6.1
Simple model for each vessel and vessel group
The simple model extended with the draught as additive and interaction effect, see Equa-
tion (4.7), is used for all the vessels in all the vessel groups in order to determine the speed-
power exponent when not considered as speed dependent. At the same time this analysis is
done to determine the draught dependency. In order to determine the draught dependent ex-
ponent the ballast, design and scantling draught is inserted afterwards in the estimated model
which has the form seen in Equation (4.9). Therefore the result is three speed-power curves.
In Table 13 the exponents can be seen for all vessels at ballast, design and scantling draught,
where the draughts are from Table 1, 2, 3 and 4. Ballast draught in the models is the mean of
Tb,a f t and Tb,f or as seen in the vessel data. The speed-power exponent for each vessel group is
also determined where the model is considering noon reports for all vessels in a given vessel
group. This means that the exponent for the vessel groups are not an average of the vessel
speciﬁc exponent but independently modelled on all data at the same time. The speed-power
exponents can be seen in Table 13 and in Figure 22. It is clearly seen that the exponent is not
three or above for the simple regression model as would have been expected. This result agrees
with (Adland et al., 2020). It is also seen that with increasing draught the speed-power expo-
nent is in general decreasing which is also seen in (Adland et al., 2020). Hereby the exponent is
dependent of draught but for some of the vessel groups there are less dependency e.g. T050-12.
It is clearly seen in Table 13 that some vessels are outliers due to the exponent being based on
too few noon reports, e.g. Vessel 01 in T050-09 which has 30 noon reports and is the vessel with
the highest exponent (3.46 in ballast). It is also seen that Vessel 01 in T105-02, which only has
two ballast noon reports, has a negative speed-power exponent (-2.21 in ballast) in Table 13.

6.3
Speed dependent speed-power models for all vessel groups
The extended speed-power model is the speed dependent model, see Equation (4.18), where
a given number of breakpoints is added to the model depending on the vessel group. Op-
timally the speed intervals would be found automatically based on optimisation, similar to
(Adland et al., 2020) where the squared errors are minimised. As described in Section 4.2.2
findchangepts is used in MATLAB on the corrected power in the noon reports that are sorted
by speed. The noon reports are sorted in MATLAB by sortrows(NR, ’SpeedCorrected’,
’ascend’) where NR is the table with noon reports as rows. A minimum threshold is then
set manually for each vessel group by looking at plots produced by findchangepts and assess
if it looks reasonable.
6.3.1

a minimum threshold of 2. The function has actually found 7 breakpoints but some of them are

a speed interval in this project have to consist of at least 150 noon reports, similar to (Adland
et al., 2020), the ﬁrst breakpoint is removed. The threshold is set manually because the function
findchangepts otherwise will ﬁnd too few or too many intervals which are not distinctive
speed intervals. The function deﬁnes the intervals by change in slope and mean which can
clearly be seen differ between the intervals in Figure 28 by the red lines. The variation in
power, which makes it look like a very noisy signal, is showing the scatter that have been

DISCUSSION
8
Discussion
In this section the results are discussed and a broader view of the results from this project is
introduced, e.g. the results can be used in the slow steaming debate. Furthermore it is discussed
how to improve this kind of study of the speed-power exponent.
8.1
Data
It is clear that more data would be preferable for this kind of study and especially data for the
same vessel group. It is also preferable to have data for the same vessels and then have less
vessels to consider in order to minimise variables. This could be achieved through continuous
monitoring instead of noon reports. Noon reports are fundamentally based on averages over 24
hours which means that the data will be describing how the vessels sail throughout most of the
time during those 24 hours. Even though COACH Solutions uses weighted averages of data
by use of AIS-data which improves the data, the data is still being deﬁned by the speed that
the vessel sails at most of the time. This means that the higher speed intervals which the vessel
rarely sails within will be more difﬁcult to capture, if the vessel does not sail at high speeds in
the majority of the 24 hours. Thereby a noon report, where a vessel sails relatively fast in a good
amount of time, will still be a lower speed data point if the vessel also sails at a lower speed in
a good amount of time. By using the continuous monitoring data, the shorter periods where
the vessel sails fast will be captured. This is e.g. seen in A comparative study on ship performance
assessment based on noon report and continuous monitoring system datasets (Themelis et al., 2018)
where noon reports and continuous monitoring data are compared for the same time periods.
Here it is seen that the continuous monitoring data include data at higher and lower vessel
speeds than the noon reports even though the data sets are for the same time period.
In Uncertainty analysis in ship performance monitoring (Aldous et al., 2015) noon reports and con-
tinuous monitoring data are compared, where it is shown that the uncertainty of a 90 day
continuous monitoring data set is similar to a 270 day noon reports data set. When the scope
of this projects is considered, there would be more data for a 90 day continuous monitoring
data set than 270 noon reports. It means that data for a much shorter time period could be used
for this project and thereby the effect of time dependent variables could be minimised in the
regression. This would in itself minimise uncertainty because there are data for up to 4 years
for some of the vessels in this project.
Another crucial aspect of the data is the corrected speed and power which is used throughout
this project for the regression models and descriptive statistics. It is important that the correc-
tions are accurate and based on correct hindcast data as all corrections in this project are based
on hindcast data. This could also be based on measurements onboard the vessels. This is done
to minimise human error and calibration errors which can happen to speed logs but then trust
and uncertainty in hindcast data from a third party are introduced. In Section 5.3 it was shown
that power corrections could be done with basic corrections for added resistance due to wind
and waves and obtain similar results as COACH Solutions but with signiﬁcant deviance. In
order to have a better foundation for the regression, it was chosen to use the more compre-
hensible corrections provided by COACH Solutions. In order to remove uncertainty from the

From the descriptive statistics in Section 5.4 it is clear that all the vessels primarily sail around

8.2
Comparison with (Adland et al., 2020)
In (Adland et al., 2020) there are 7600 noon reports for the aframax tankers and 4325 noon re-
ports for the suezmax tankers which means 11,925 noon reports in total. Therefore the 51,826
noon reports in this project seems like a lot, but when it is divided between 15 vessel groups
and half of the vessel groups only consists of 2000 or less noon reports, the vessel groups with
more data are comparable with the vessel groups in (Adland et al., 2020). If it was possible
to combine the similar vessel groups and see it as one group, there would be a lot more data
since there are many similar vessel groups in the MR2 and LR1 deadweight segments. The
initial thought was to combine similar vessel groups but as has been shown in the descriptive
statistics and analysis there can be a lot of variation between vessel groups that look similar on
paper. This can also apply to vessels within the same vessel group but in order to have enough
data for the regression, vessels where not removed from vessel groups. As seen in Section 6.5
three MR2 vessel groups were combined in order to have 10,562 noon reports in total but the
speed dependent model did not change much from the three vessel group speciﬁc models.
This project differs from (Adland et al., 2020) in regards to tanker sizes where the vessels are
signiﬁcantly smaller in this project. The regression model are also deﬁned different where
corrected power and corrected speed are used in this project but uncorrected power together
with weather variables describing current, wind and waves are used in (Adland et al., 2020).
Regarding loading conditions (Adland et al., 2020) is only considering ballast and laden condi-
tions and deﬁning models for each loading condition resulting in different speed breakpoints
for each loading condition. In this project the data is divided into ballast, design and scantling
draught when plotting, but the model is using all data where draught is deﬁned as both an
additive and interactive term in the model deﬁnition. Thereby the regression is based on more
data in this project and is at the same time taking the exponent as function of draught into
account. In (Adland et al., 2020) the model is only exploiting the ballast noon reports when
the exponent for the ballast draught is determined which means only a part of the data set is
included instead of all noon reports.

8.3
Improvement of the regression models
The regression models are kept as simple as possible in this project for clarity and simplicity.
At the same time it is easier to base it on physics. There could easily be added many extensive
additive and interactive terms in the regression model which has also been tried in this project,
but then it is easy to loose perspective and difﬁcult to see which variables are important. This
will also lead to extraneous variables and multicollinearity. It is a better starting point for this
kind of study to begin with the most simple model and if the results look close to what is ex-
pected then the model can be extended further for even better results.
It would though be relevant to extend the model from this project further by implementing
e.g. predictor variables describing the time dependency in the data. Hereby the decreasing
vessel performance due to fouling would be taken into account and also if the vessel has just
been drydocked. One of the assumptions described in Section 2.7 is that the residuals have to
be independent of time which they are not completely considered in this project. This would
therefore be the ﬁrst step to do in order to improve the regression model.
Another improvement to the resulting speed-power curves would be to implement smooth
transitions between the speed intervals. This will make them more physical with a gradual
transition of the exponent between the intervals and better describe the data.
8.4
Use of the speed dependent models for performance prediction
In a situation of performance prediction, it is assumed that the vessel performance benchmark
has to be determined from model test curves without having any noon reports. In this project
the speed-power exponent for the model test curves was determined for all vessel groups at
all draughts from the model test and it was seen that the exponents were at least three for
the high speed intervals the model tests were conducted at. The model test curves were then
extrapolated down to low speeds by keeping the exponent constant and it was seen that this
did not agree well with the noon reports. Therefore, it would be necessary to extrapolate the
model test curves down to lower speed intervals with a decreasing exponent. A further study
would then have to determine a general relationship between speed and speed-power expo-
nent. This could e.g. be a non-dimensionless study where the exponent and speed intervals are
determined based on Froude number.
8.5
Slow steaming debate
It is clear based on the results from this project that the speed-power exponent is below three
at lower speeds but it has been more difﬁcult to show that the exponent is much above three
at higher speeds. Therefore it will deﬁnitely be overestimation of the effect of slow steam-
ing if an exponent of 3-4 is assumed at lower speed intervals which means underestimation
of the needed power and thereby fuel. This means that slow steaming at even lower speeds is
necessary to achieve the desired minimisation of fuel. This is relevant for the slow steaming de-
bate which can be described as speed optimisation vs speed reduction (Psaraftis, 2019). In this
project the speed-power exponent is also draught dependent which means the exponent for
scantling draught is signiﬁcantly lower than for the ballast draught. Together with the speed

dependent speed-power exponent this makes it more feasible to slow steam on a ballast voyage
than a laden voyage. There is therefore better potential for slow steaming at ballast draughts
than for laden draught conditions where speed optimisation would be a better alternative.
This study is only for tanker vessels and the results will therefore not apply to all vessel types
but as bulk carriers are relatively similar to tankers, similar conclusions would possibly also
be drawn from a study with data from bulk carriers. As container ships differ a lot in hull
shape, vessel parameters and speed intervals, the conclusion will not apply to these. It is of-
ten said that the speed-power exponent is higher for container vessels, according to (Psaraftis
and Kontovas, 2013) and (Kristensen, 2010) it is 4-5 and could be even higher. But seen in the
light of this study it is possible that the speed-power exponent for container vessels are just as
dependent of speed as has been shown here for tankers. This would be relevant to study with
operational data. In (Kristensen, 2010) this been studied based on empirical formulas (early
work of Ship-Desmo (Kristensen, 2020)) and an exponent below three has not been shown, not
even at low speeds.
It is important to note that the fuel consumption, emissions and power will always be reduced
when reducing the speed. So even if the speed-power exponent is lower than expected at lower
speeds, the fuel consumption will be lower if the transport work is kept constant. But it will
just not be as rewarding when the increased sailing time is taken into account compared to an
exponent above three. Therefore speed optimisation could be a better solution than just blindly
implementing speed reduction in order to decrease emissions.

CONCLUSION
9
Conclusion
It can be concluded from this study that the speed-power exponent is signiﬁcantly lower than
three at speed intervals below the design speed for tankers of between 35,000 and 110,000
DWT. This has been shown for all the studied vessel groups based on a speed dependent lin-
ear regression model working on speed-power data from noon reports but also when a simple
regression model is applied. At the same time it can be concluded that a draught and speed
dependent regression model can be used to describe the speed-power relationship based on
noon reports if there are an adequate amount of data and the quality of data is sufﬁcient. An
adequate amount of data is needed in order to have data covering the whole speed interval of
interest. Sufﬁcient data quality is needed in order to minimise scatter in the reported power. In
order to have enough data, vessel groups consisting of sister vessels have been studied instead
of individual vessels. This is done based on descriptive statistics where the operational proﬁle
regarding draught, speed and power have been compared between sister vessels. For vessel
groups with adequate data quality and data amount it has been shown that the regression
model is coinciding and in good agreement with the reported data. For these vessel groups
the model test curves have been proven to agree relatively well with both the regression model
and the reported data. This has especially been seen for the ballast loading condition where
there have been a lot of data covering a wide speed interval.
The study has shown that it is more difﬁcult to show an exponent of at least three at high speed
intervals than to show that the exponent is signiﬁcantly less than three at low speed intervals.
This is argued to be caused by relatively few data points at high speeds because all the vessels
sail signiﬁcantly slower than the speeds the model tests have been performed at.
The regression model in this study is kept as simple as possible in order to keep it physical and
therefore only draught and speed are included. Because relatively good agreement is seen with
the major part of the data, the most simple speed dependent model is a good basis to further
extend the model in a future study. In order to use the results in vessel performance monitoring
it is needed to make a model that extrapolates the model test curves down to speeds below the
design speed and incorporate a decreasing exponent with decreasing speed. Thereby a speed-
power curve can be estimated without noon reports but only based on model test curves.
A speed-power relationship with a constant speed-power exponent will clearly underestimate
the needed power when slow steaming. This means the effect of slow steaming with regards to
fuel consumption is overestimated. It is therefore not as good an option to slow steam as some-
times stated in the literature. Furthermore, the exponent is almost always stated in literature
to be at least three and even up to six. This gives a good reason for slow steaming when the
fuel consumption wants to be decreased signiﬁcantly. If the constant speed-power exponent
is not valid, speed optimisation is a better alternative to speed reduction in order to sail more
sustainable.
