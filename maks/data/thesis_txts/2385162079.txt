Abstract
This Master Thesis presents the response analysis of the DTU 10MW Wind
Turbine mounted over a ﬂoating support structure. The support structure is
a semi submersible designed by the company Dr.techn.Olav Olsen AS, which
has been upscaled to host a 10MW wind turbine. The mentioned company
together with DTU and other industry and research partners, take part in the
EU Horizon2020 Project LIFES50+ on the design and optimization of ﬂoating
oﬀshore wind turbines.
The numerical model of the ﬂoating wind turbine is implemented in HAWC2
to handle the aero-hydro-servo-elastic response and coupled with WAMIT to
calculate the hydrodynamic loads. The natural frequencies of the structure are
then identiﬁed via free decay tests, the system is veriﬁed, the hydrodynamic
damping is calibrated and the controller was adjusted.
The response of the ﬂoating wind turbine is evaluated by simulating some of
the typical ultimate design load cases (DLCs) for onshore and bottom ﬁxed
oﬀshore wind turbines. The setup of the simulations adapts the DTU Wind
Energy Oﬀshore Design Load Basis (DLB) framework and the LIFES50+ Project
Design Basis considering the most relevant design standards and engineering
practices. It takes into consideration the lower natural frequencies of the ﬂoating
platform to deﬁne the duration of the simulations and events such as gusts.
As oﬀshore substructure design is site speciﬁc, the simulations were run with
realistic met-ocean parameters from a site located in the Gulf of Maine (US)
that was considered in the LIFES50+ Project.
Due to the slow controller response to avoid negative instabilities, the largest
loads were registered for the cases with extreme turbulence and for the ones under
the appearance of gusts. In those cases the response of the ﬂoating platform
matches the expected performance of the system.
==PAGE:4==
ii
The accidental limit state consisting in the break of the upwind mooring line
is also simulated, as the system is redundant, both during normal operation
and storm conditions. This leads to a large surge and yaw motion of the ﬂoater
which may compromise the integrity of the electrical cables. During and after
the transient there is negative thrust due to the magnitude and direction of the
aerodynamic forces, lift and drag, in the blades which are in feather position. In
those cases there are also blade edgewise instabilities, specially increased for 10
degrees incoming wind direction, with this speciﬁc case leading to the largest
blade edgewise and tower bottom and tower top fore-aft bending moments.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This introductory chapter starts giving a general picture of renewable energy
trends and its importance in society. Afterwards, the focus is on oﬀshore wind
energy, its advantages and drawbacks compared to onshore wind and its market
situation. The main substructure concepts are covered, both ﬁxed bottom and
ﬂoating, and their main characteristics are presented. Finally, the LIFES50+
Project is introduced and the motivation, objectives and approach taken in this
Thesis are stated.
1.1
Policies for Renewable Energy
Energy is a fundamental resource in the economical and social development of
communities, supporting mobility, industrial growth and social welfare. Neverthe-
less, it has a great impact in the environment from climate change and pollution
to land or water use and modiﬁcations of ecosystems. These environmental
concerns are clearly spreading and inﬂuencing policymakers.
The Paris Agreement reached at COP21 in December 2015 was a major mile-
stone in the ﬁght against climate change, establishing a goal to limit global
temperature rise in this century of 2◦C above pre-industrial levels. As energy
related emissions account around two thirds of global CO2 emissions, considering
emissions related to power generation, transportation and consumption in build-
ings and industries[1], a shift paradigm towards a renewable and sustainable
energy system is happening right now.

However, this is not only due to environmental conservation eﬀorts as renewable
energy technologies are continuously dropping its costs thanks to technological,
logistics and/or ﬁnancial advances. This surge of renewables, mainly wind and
solar as can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1, is also leading to a shift in the job market as, for
example, in 2016 solar employed more people in the U.S. electricity generation
than oil, coal and gas combined[2].
While previously renewable energy targets were around 5 to 10% among the
most developed countries, nowadays one can ﬁnd goals such as the ones of the
European Union to have 20% of renewable energy in the global energy supply
by 2020[3]. And this won’t stop there as many countries, such as Denmark [4],
plan to completely decarbonise their energy sector by 2050.

1.2
Oﬀshore Wind Energy
The ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm was Vindeby, which was built in Denmark in 1991
and started to be decommissioned in March 2017. It comprised 11 wind turbines
with a capacity of 450kW, giving a total of 4.95MW, each made by former Bonus
Energy now Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy[7]. Denmark was also the place
to build the ﬁrst utility scale oﬀshore wind farm with Horns Rev I built by
DONG Energy[8] in 2002, where 80 2MW Vestas [9] wind turbines where erected
to give a total capacity of 160MW.
From that time forward, technological development has brought bigger wind
turbines, larger wind farms and greater water depths. An example of the ﬁrst
of this trends is Burbo Bank Extension an oﬀshore wind farm built by DONG
Energy oﬀthe coast of Liverpool (UK) where Vestas’ V164-8.0MW has been
used. It is important to notice that just one of this wind turbines has a higher
power output than the whole Vindeby oﬀshore wind farm named before.
Despite this expansion, oﬀshore wind energy is still more expensive than onshore
wind due to the following reasons:
• Construction of a substructure so as to link the wind turbine to the
seabed. In general, these substructures are very heavy, built at the port
using construction methods from shipbuilding or oil and gas and imply
thousands of hours of welding, steel cutting...
• Higher and more complex loading. The stochastic nature of wind loads is
combined with the stochastic sea states and marine currents, introducing
broader load combinations and higher mechanical stresses which have to
be considered during the design phase. It is specially important to consider
fatigue loads due to periodic wave loading. In the end, this forces lead to
the usage of more steel, larger substructures, more complex transportation
and higher costs.
• Use of expensive and specialist vessels during installation due to the size
and weight of the parts and structures.
• Corrosion from marine environment.
• Expensive electrical connection due to the requirement of submarine cables,
energy losses with increasing distance from shore and reliability diﬃculties.
• Inﬂuence on marine mammals and birds has not been studied enough.
• Land use displacing others activities such as ﬁshing, recreational or military
activities.

• Necessity of remote monitoring systems so as to assess performance or
possible faults as in situ inspection requires helicopters or boats. Moreover,
there is a strong dependence on weather windows to perform maintenance
activities.
Nevertheless, the key factors which overcome those diﬃculties explaining the

1.3
Oﬀshore Concepts
There are diﬀerent types of foundations from ﬁxed substructures rooted to the
seabed to ﬂoating platforms anchored by tendons or mooring lines. The main
parameter which determines whether to choose one or another is the cost which
in the end will depend on the water depth. By May 2017 all the commercial
oﬀshore wind farms, excluding some pilot projects and demonstrators, make use
==PAGE:26==
6
Introduction
of ﬁxed foundations as they are placed in water depths below 70 meters. All these
projects have been built in places with good wind conditions and reduced water
depths so as to achieve the lowest Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE) possible.
However, there are big markets which have few shallow water sites available such
as U.S. (West Coast mainly), Japan and even France. Therefore, the goal of
ﬂoating foundations is not being installed further oﬀshore as this would mean
uneconomical electrical transportation costs, it is exploiting good wind resources
in places close to population centers, eliminating water depth as a constraint.
1.3.1
Fixed Foundations
There are multiple types of ﬁxed foundations but the focus will be on the most
extended these days:
• Monopiles: cylindrical steel piles which are driven up to 30 meters into the
seabed. They have been the most popular due to the ease of construction
and installation in shallow water depths up to 45 meters. The increase
in turbine mass, dynamic loading and stiﬀness requirements has lead to
greater wall thicknesses and diameters.
• Jackets: three or four legged space frame structure applicable at larger
water depths up to 70 meters due to its higher stiﬀness. Normally, they are
piled to the seabed and they are more expensive than monopiles as they
require more working hours per foundation as being more complex. They
are also more vulnerable to fatigue because of the multiple welded joints.
• Gravity based: conical or ﬂat high mass concrete structures used for shallow
water depths below 25 meters. Can be built onshore and ballasted in situ.
The absence of penetration into the seabed permits the usage in rocky
seabeds and minimize environmental impact as there is no piling noise.
On the other hand, as was noted before, ﬂoating foundations have only been
used in demonstrators or pilot projects. However, it is expected an important
rise in the coming decades, in fact, IRENA (International Renewable Energy
Agency) has identiﬁed ﬂoating foundations as a "game changing" solution in
oﬀshore wind energy in terms of reduction of the LCOE and spreading of oﬀshore
wind farms[14]. As with ﬁxed foundations there are many diﬀerent designs, still
there are three main concepts as presented in ﬁgure 1.4:
• Spar buoy: ballasted cylinder with catenary or taut spread mooring lines
with drag or suction anchors. Between its advantages are the tendency for

Overall, the main advantage against ﬁxed foundations is the greater standard-

in foundation design, hence leading to cost reductions. Whereas with ﬁxed
foundations each foundation of the wind farm is designed independently as the
water depths and soil characteristics vary. Furthermore, the maximization of
onshore activity reducing the dependence on weather windows and the use of
lower cost vessels are to be considered.
There is also an important technology transfer from oil and gas as ﬂoating
oil platforms have existed for decades and, indeed, the names of the technical
concepts referred above come directly from oil and gas tradition. Nevertheless,
there are many diﬀerences as for ﬂoating wind turbines more dynamics are
playing due to the distinct mass distribution and loading. Finally, a wind farm
require hundreds of units to build making the costs and times of installation and
manufacturing even more critical, while normally an oil reservoir requires only
one drilling platform.
The diverse experiences with pilot projects and prototypes has shown the impor-
tance of the assembly line approach versus the oil and gas fabrication methods.

1.5
Motivation
Floating wind energy represents a promising energy source providing utility scale
electricity to populated coastal areas, removing water depth as a constraint for
oﬀshore wind farm construction. But ﬂoating wind energy will only be signiﬁcant
in the future energy mix if there is a substantial cost reduction.
This cost decrease can be achieved by improving the design of ﬂoating foundations
so as to enhance the dynamic performance of the wind turbine while reducing
the weight and materials, easing manufacturing, transport and installation. This
optimization of the design, which can be seen as a reduction in safety factors,
requires a better knowledge of the physical behaviour of the system and external
interactions.
Wave tank tests of scale models and extensive computational
simulations and analysis, which are tackled in this Thesis, have to be done to
accomplish this objective.
In fact, the analysis of a ﬂoating wind turbine is a very complex problem due
to the interaction between aerodynamics, structural dynamics, hydrodynamics
and control. This numerical problem requires aero-hydro-servo-elastic numerical
codes, whose results have to be validated against experimental results.
Finally, the simulation of the design load cases (DLCs) permits to check the
dynamical response of the whole ﬂoating wind turbine and its diﬀerent parts
against the diverse environmental conditions which it will be exposed to. It
also allows to simulate faults and accidental states, studying the diﬀerent eﬀects
separately. In all these cases it is possible to study those discrete situations, but
what is more important, the accumulated eﬀect of all those situations over the
life of the structure.
In the end, this enables a reduction in the design’s uncertainty and risks, which is
critical considering the importance of reliability in the harsh marine environment
and the diﬃculties for the access in maintenance operations.

Numerical Models and
Tools
Two softwares were employed to perform the simulation of the ﬂoating wind
turbine: HAWC2 and WAMIT. HAWC2 was used to calculate the aeroelastic
response of the wind turbine, while WAMIT was adopted to analyze the wave
interactions. Furthermore, diverse international standards, oﬀshore standards
and recommended practices were considered in order to develop the mentioned
numerical models, deﬁne the simulation parameters and post process the results.
This chapter will introduce the main characteristics of the mentioned softwares,
how is the simulation setup and which engineering standards had to be considered
in the analyses.
2.1
HAWC2
HAWC2[24] is the acronym of "Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code
2nd generation", it is an aeroelastic code with the purpose to calculate wind
turbine response in time domain. It is developed and distributed by DTU Wind
Energy and used both in industry and academia internationally, being also
validated and compared to other numerical codes through numerous research
projects.
It comprises diﬀerent modules from structural dynamics, aerodynamics, hydro-
dynamics or water kinematics to control and wind turbulence and wake models.
==PAGE:34==
14
Numerical Models and Tools
The structural formulation is based on multibody dynamics with the turbine
divided in bodies, each with its own coordinate system. Those bodies are pined
together by algebraic constraints and composed by Timoshenko beam elements
with six degrees of freedom. The aerodynamic model is blade element momentum
(BEM) theory, being extended to handle dynamic inﬂow, tip loss, shear eﬀect
and other phenomena.
The hydrodynamic loads are computed by means of the known Morison equation,
presented and discussed in Section 3.3.4, extensively utilized to calculate wave
loads for oﬀshore structures from oil and gas platforms to oﬀshore wind turbines.
It is a semi-empirical model applicable to hydrodynamic transparent structures
and compact bodies with a ratio between the characteristic length D and the
wave length λ: D/λ < 0.2, i.e. bodies which not alter signiﬁcantly the incoming
wave ﬁeld.
On the other hand, so as to deal with large volume structures an interface has
been developed in HAWC2 to couple the calculations with WAMIT.
2.2
WAMIT
WAMIT[25] is a panel method numerical code to compute loads and motions of
oﬀshore structures subject to waves, it was ﬁrst developed by Prof. J. Nicholas
Newman’s group at M.I.T. (Massachusetts Institute of Technology) and then at
WAMIT Inc. It considers linear or second order potential ﬂow theory and the
velocity potential is solved by the boundary element integration method. Due
to the extensive simulation campaign linear theory was applied to reduce the
simulation time, therefore linear airy waves were considered.
Large volume structures or non transparent with λ: D/λ > 0.2 modify the
incident wave ﬁeld, hence wave energy is being transmitted to the structure
generating diﬀraction waves radiated from the body. WAMIT takes into account
these diﬀraction forces and inertia forces too, nevertheless viscous eﬀects need
to be incorporated and these was done by using the Morison equation through
HAWC2 without considering the inertia terms. A thorough explanation and
justiﬁcation of the chosen hydrodynamic model, the diﬀerent forces and the
setup is provided in Section 3.3.4.
Finally, it was required to make a CAD panel mesh of the ﬂoating platform
on one of the formats which WAMIT is able to read. In such a way, Multisurf
software developed by the company AeroHydro[26] was chosen to generate the
geometry and the mesh.

2.3 Relevant Engineering Standards

2.3
Relevant Engineering Standards
Engineering standards and recommended practices are documents elaborated in
cooperation between classiﬁcation societies or commissions and companies. They
are voluntary and serve as guidelines. Numerous were considered, as was noted
before, to build the numerical models, select the range of parameters which drive
the simulations and post-process and analyze the obtained results.
All these standards had to be taken into account to design the ﬂoating wind
turbine since, although they focus more in certain aspects, they cover the
structural, mechanical, electrical and control design and also the assembly,
installation, commissioning and maintenance. Therefore, a brief description and
the direct inﬂuence on this Master Thesis is exposed below.
1. IEC 61400-1 Wind turbines - Part 1 Design Requirements[27].
It provides design requirements to guarantee the integrity of the wind
turbine against the diverse external hazards. It speciﬁes the design load
cases (DLC), the design situations which the wind turbine is expected to
withstand along its lifetime. Provides the basic parameters for deﬁning
wind turbine classes such as reference wind speed and reference turbulence
intensity. It also indicates numerical expressions for wind proﬁles, turbu-
lence models and gust deﬁnitions, which are required as an input for the
DLB simulations. A more detailed description about the DLB, the chosen
DLCs and the input parameters is provided in Chapter 4.
2. IEC 61400-3 Wind Turbines - Part 3 Design Requirements for
Oﬀshore Wind Turbines[28]. It focuses on assessing the external condi-
tions of an oﬀshore wind site ensuring the integrity of the turbine. Evaluates
marine conditions from waves and currents to water levels, tides, sea ice
or marine growth. And deﬁnes sea states with its parameters and joint
probability distributions. It also gives partial safety factors for the extreme
loads and fatigue assessment and speciﬁes how to post-process the results
considering the diﬀerent stochastic realizations. As it is clearly stated in
this standard, the design requirements exhibited are not necessarily enough
to guarantee the integrity of a ﬂoating wind turbine. For those reasons, an
extension of this standard, IEC 61400-3-2, was created to address ﬂoating
wind turbines’ design.
3. IEC 61400-3-2 Wind Turbines - Part 3-2 Design Requirements
for Floating Oﬀshore Wind Turbines[29]. This extension of the IEC
standard, which is still a draft, focuses on station keeping, design of the
support structure and the deﬁnition of limit states. Furthermore, there is
a modiﬁcation in the duration of some of the gust events so as to take into
==PAGE:36==
16
Numerical Models and Tools
account natural frequencies platform motions, as the natural frequencies are
signiﬁcantly lower than ﬁxed platforms. Finally, new DLCs are speciﬁcally
provided for ﬂoating wind turbines to assess mooring line break transient
events and redundancy check conditions, in case the mooring system is
redundant.
4. DNV-OS-J101 Design of Oﬀshore Wind Turbine Structures[30].
Addresses site conditions, loads and load eﬀects and proposes more DLCs
for the analysis.
5. DNV-OS-J103 Design of Floating Wind Turbine Structures[31].
The targets are design, construction and inspection of ﬂoating wind turbine
structures both support structures and station keeping systems. It also
establishes that in order to capture non linear eﬀects, second order eﬀects
and slow varying motions, the simulation length has to be extended from
the standard ten minutes to three to six hours.
6. DNV-RP-C205 Environmental Conditions and Environmental
Loads[32]. Deals with the model analysis and prediction of environmental
conditions (wind, wave and currents) acting on structures. It speciﬁes
which models are valid to calculate hydrodynamics loads depending on
the structure and the sea state. Finally, it establishes the panel mesh
requirements, convergence tests and frequency discretizations to model the
hydrodynamic loads for large submerged bodies. This principles where
taken into account to make the panel mesh for the ﬂoater as is explained
in Section 3.3.4.
7. Design Load Basis for Oﬀshore Wind Turbines[33]. This report
describes the DLB used at DTU Wind Energy for oﬀshore wind turbines.
It is based in IEC 61400-3 and extensively describes the diﬀerent DLCs,
specifying their duration, operational wind speeds, fault conditions, gust
types, number of stochastic realizations and post processing methods. This
report was the foundation and main guidance for the DLB deﬁnition and
was adapted appropriately for ﬂoating wind turbines.

3.3.2
Mooring System
The purpose of the mooring system is to maintain the position of the structure
and prevent excessive motion. It comprises three chains as catenary lines with
120 degrees of separation. Each of those chains has two segments with a clump
weight of 50t in between acting as ballast. Consequently, the restoring force
comes from lifting and lowering the mooring line and clump weight. The existence
of the clump weights permits to reduce the length of the mooring lines in around
150m each, if the equivalent weight of the chain per length in water, shown
in table 3.4, is taken into account. Then, seabed alteration and marine area
occupation is minimized.
The required dimensions and characteristics to model the mooring system are
presented in table 3.3 and the distributed properties of the chains are given in
table 3.4.
The computational model used for the moorings is dynamic, based on a cable ele-
ment with hydrodynamic drag, added mass and buoyancy forces. Its complexity
made the simulations signiﬁcantly slower than for ﬁxed bottom wind turbines.
It also required a ﬁner time step for the simulations with upwind mooring line
break and even after the time step was reﬁned there were convergence issues
with some simulations. A closer look to the mooring line model is provided in
[39] and a view of the mooring line layout is given in ﬁgure 3.4.

Hydrodynamic Properties
If the equation of motion, Eq.3.1, is recalled but only considering the ﬂoating
substructure:
(M + A) ¨x + B ˙x + Cx = Fhydrodynamic + Fmooring
(3.2)
The selected hydrodynamic model will deﬁne the values of A, B and Fhydro, the
hydrodynamic added mass matrix, the hydrodynamic damping matrix and the
hydrodynamic forces, respectively. Depending on the geometry of the body and
the incident wave ﬁeld, diﬀerent forces and phenomena appear and certain types
of loads dominate, as shown in ﬁgure 3.6.
Figure 3.6: Wave force regimes. D=characteristic dimension, H = wave height,
λ = wave length. [40]
In such a way, slender bodies can be analyzed by only considering drag and
inertia forces, while for large bodies diﬀraction and radiation forces should be
taken into account. If the dimensions of the Olav Olsen OO-Star ﬂoater are
considered diﬀraction forces cannot be neglected, however there would be certain
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Olav Olsen OO-STAR Model
27
parts of the platform such as the pontoons, the central shaft and the columns
where drag is signiﬁcant.
Consequently, all those phenomena are taken into account by coupling the
diﬀraction, radiation and added mass forces from WAMIT to the drag forces
calculated with HAWC2, at every time step. WAMIT is run only once giving
those matrices in frequency domain and the problem is transformed to time
domain through the Cummins equation[41] by HAWC2, but for this reason the
speed of the calculations was signiﬁcantly reduced.
The drag forces per unit length from the Morison equation are calculated with
HAWC2 as:
fdrag = 1
2Cd ρ Du |u|
(3.3)
with Cd drag coeﬃcient, retrieved from [32], D the characteristic dimension in
the direction of the ﬂow, ρ water density and u the relative velocity of the water
particles. Nevertheless, as the drag coeﬃcient depends on the Reynolds number
and hence on the ﬂuid velocity, it shouldn’t be constant. Although due to time
constraints and the great number of simulations to be performed, a constant
value was considered for each section of the ﬂoater.
Otherwise, WAMIT was used to gather A, B, both corresponding to the radiation
forces, and the diﬀraction forces. In short, diﬀraction forces are wave propagation
due to sheltering eﬀects from an object and radiation are radiated waves at the
frequency of body oscillations. As stated in Section 2.2, WAMIT is a panel
method which performs a frequency domain analysis of the ﬂoating platform
against linear waves and with a CAD panel mesh with the geometry as input.
This panel mesh was made with Multisurf as seen in ﬁgure 3.7.

3.3.5
Controller Properties
Neither the standard onshore controller nor the one for oﬀshore bottom-ﬁxed
wind turbines, are applicable for ﬂoating wind turbines due to negative damping
instabilities. This negative damping appears because of variations in the relative
wind speed as a result of ﬂoater motions.
If the thrust curve is considered there are two diﬀerent zones, one below the
rated wind speed where an increase in the wind speed translates into an increase
in the thrust and the other one above the rated, where the increase in the wind
speed leads to a reduction in the thrust. This increase or reduction in the thrust
==PAGE:51==
3.3 Olav Olsen OO-STAR Model
31
with the variation of the wind speed is due to the blade pitch control, as for
every wind speed the blades are pitched conveniently to provide the thrust force
required by the thrust curve.
Below the rated wind speed, there is positive damping as when the turbine pitches
to the front the relative velocity increases and so does the thrust, damping that
frontward motion. Conversely, when the turbine pitches back the relatively
velocity is reduced and so does the thrust damping that backward motion.
Nevertheless, for wind speeds higher than the rated when the turbine pitches to
the front with the consequent increase in the wind speed, the thrust is reduced
increasing the forward motion. And when it pitches back with the diminution in
the wind speed the thrust grows amplifying the motion.
Therefore, a diﬀerent controller is needed with lower gain and slower response to
avoid those negative damping issues. The standard DTU Wind Energy controller
was used with the parameters tuned to achieve this behaviour. A comparison
between the standard onshore controller and the one applied for the ﬂoating
wind turbine is shown for diﬀerent turbine and ﬂoater parameters in ﬁgures 3.10
and 3.11, respectively. There, 1m/s wind steps with 50s of duration are used
as input. It can be appreciated that for wind speeds over the rated, the pitch
platform oscillations are ampliﬁed by the onshore controller. The impact of this
phenomena is particularly critical for the platform pitch and the aerodynamic
rotor power.
From the other hand, a slower control system has a more diﬃcult performance on
high turbulence scenarios and under gust events leading to more spiky loads and
to more extreme values due to the later response. Still, the ambiance turbulence
is lower in oﬀshore environments due to lower roughness of the terrain as there
are not sheltering objects and the ground surface is smoother. However, the
turbulence due to wake interactions is larger than onshore as wind turbine
clusters are normally bigger and the lower ambiance turbulence leads to slower
wake recovery and hence longer wakes.
Finally, a constant power control strategy was chosen rather than constant torque
so as to better the electric power, having a more constant power output. It was
also necessary to make use of a low pass ﬁlter due to high frequency oscillations
which appeared when the controller was used for the ﬁrst time.

3.3.6
System Identiﬁcation and Calibration
At last, the system identiﬁcation and calibration was performed so as to match
the real behaviour of the ﬂoating wind turbine and increase the accuracy of the
numerical model. Therefore, the mass of the numerical model was corrected to
get the designed draft of 22m, leading to a mass of 22360270kg and a relative
change from the previous mass of 3%.
On the other hand, the hydrodynamic damping of the ﬂoating platform was also
corrected to get damping ratios ξ close to 10% for the translational degrees of
freedom and 5% for the rotational degrees of freedom.
In such a way, free decay tests were carried out and e.g. in the case of the
translational degrees of freedom unidirectional forces close to the rated thrust
were applied on the ﬂoater. After the ﬂoater had reached the equilibrium under
the action of the force, the force was released with the ﬂoater reaching the
repose position through a free decay motion at the natural frequency. Then, the
damping factors were computed and modiﬁed to get the desired ones as if the
system was lineal.
Afterwards, the simulations were repeated, but as the system is in fact non
linear several iterations were required. The explanation of the procedure for the
rotational degrees of freedom is similar to the one just mentioned, but applying
torques close to the tower tilt moment due to the rated thrust. All simulations
had no wind nor waves.
In such a way, the necessary added hydrodynamic damping was identiﬁed for
every degree of freedom and is shown in table 3.5. As it can be seen in the
mentioned table, it was not necessary to add any hydrodynamic damping for
the yaw degree of freedom. Furthermore, an example of a free decay test for the

4.1
Design load cases
In order to ensure the structural integrity of any wind turbine and its substructure,
no matter if it is situated onshore or oﬀshore, it is necessary to consider a design
load basis. That is to deﬁne certain design load cases or DLCs which represent
the most characteristic situations that the wind turbine will experience during
its lifetime. It covers from normal power production to fault situations such as
grid loss, pitch runaway, large yaw error or extreme weather conditions, and
some of those will be explained more thoroughly afterwards.
==PAGE:58==
38
Design Load Basis Deﬁnition
For the certiﬁcation of an oﬀshore wind farm it is required to perform the
DLCs as stated in [30]. Although performing those DLCs is not compulsory,
the certiﬁcation of these projects permits to derisk them and gives investors
certainty that the design has been analyzed according to industry standard
procedures. Some of these standards are so extensively used in industry that a
design will only be able to compete in the market if it follows them. Reducing
the risk and increasing the trust of external investors is particularly critical
with innovative concepts such as ﬂoating wind turbines. Therefore, it is more
necessary to extensively perform this kind of analyses.
Furthermore, due to the broad range of situations which are analyzed, it is
possible to assess the eﬀect of factors which may not have been contemplated
along the previous design phases. This may lead to further and more speciﬁc
studies or tune certain parameters such as the controller behaviour to cope with
those situations.
The study of extreme and fatigue loads is the tangible outcome from the DLCs
analysis and post processing. From the one hand, extreme load analysis permits
to distinguish which situations drive the highest loads in the diﬀerent parts of
the wind turbine and the substructure. This may conduce to ﬁnite element
analyses using those registered loads as an input to further study the load eﬀects
and internal stresses in the (sub)structure.
On the other hand, fatigue loads are the result of the accumulated action over
time of the diﬀerent external cyclic forces, with a major contribution of waves
rather than wind. Calculating the lifetime damage of the substructure is essential
to ensure that it will withstand the expected lifespan of the project. Those DLCs
take into account the probability of occurrence of situations such as normal
power production, extreme turbulence, start-up, shut down or parked conditions.
In this Thesis the focus is on ultimate load analyses of the Olav Olsen OO-Star
ﬂoater and a further fatigue analysis would be an imperative future extension of
this work.
This design load basis deﬁnition is an adaptation of DTU’s "Design Load Basis for
Oﬀshore Wind Turbines"[33] for ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines. This adaptation
covers a diﬀerent deﬁnition of certain gust events by taking into account some
of the natural frequencies of the ﬂoater and accidental states consisting in one
mooring line break under power production and storm conditions. Moreover, the
time duration of the simulations is evaluated and extended conveniently due to
the large natural periods of the ﬂoater which lead to longer transients. It is also
an extension of the LIFES50+ "Design Basis"[42], covering more simulations to
total around 4000 cases.

4.1.1
Design Load Cases for Onshore and Oﬀshore Wind
Turbines
The typical design load cases considered for onshore and oﬀshore wind turbines
and substructures comprise diverse operational states for the wind turbine such
as:
• Normal power production.
• Start up and shut down.
• Control failure: pitch runaway, extreme yaw error or one blade stuck at
ﬁne pitch.
• Grid loss leading to a parked or idling state.
Then, distinct wind conditions and parameters are taken into account:
• Extreme wind.
• Wind directions.
• Turbulence.
• Wind shear.
• Dynamic events such as gusts.
The sea state conditions are also speciﬁed by selecting the wave spectrum which
better ﬁts the measurements recorded at the site and selecting the correspondent
signiﬁcant wave heights and peak periods. Wave headings can also be chosen
permitting to simulate situations with wind and wave misalignment. Those
situations are particularly interesting for ﬂoating wind turbines as in those cases
wave induced motions are not reduced by the aerodynamic damping.
Moreover, wave currents are also deﬁned by specifying velocities and proﬁles.
Lastly, seabed conditions need to be taken into account.
The result of the design load cases are characteristic loads which, according
to the approach adopted in this Thesis, after being multiplied by the partial
safety factor lead to the design loads. Those design loads are the main results

a dynamic and/or a non linear analysis those design loads would lead to the

on performing the dynamic and/or non linear analysis commented before with
the characteristic load leading to the characteristic load eﬀect. This characteristic

other case the characteristic value is the mean of the maximum values of the
diﬀerent realizations for the worst case. Although, certain design load cases
(DLC2.1, DLC2.2 and DLC5.1) deﬁne the characteristic value as the mean of

As it was noted before some of the modiﬁcations from the standard oﬀshore
design load basis are related to the deﬁnition of gust events, which now need to
acknowledge the lower natural frequencies of the ﬂoater. In such a way, longer
gust events need to be taken into account to consider possible resonances or
interactions.
For extreme operating gusts or EOG the time period of the gust leads to up
to seven diﬀerent cases (each of the natural periods and the 10.5s standard
duration) as the duration of each of the natural periods is multiplied by 1.5.
Due to the disparity between gust duration the amplitude cannot be assumed to
be constant with time and hence is retrieved from the Equation (28) from IEC
61400-3-2[29] instead of Equation (17) from IEC 61400-1[27].
From the other hand, for extreme direction change (EDC) and extreme coherent
gust with direction change (ECD) time duration coinciding with the yaw natural
period of the ﬂoater are regarded. This recommendations were followed on the
correspondent design load cases.
Apart from this, in all the previously pointed and the next simulations the yaw
bearing is locked so as to get the desired yaw error.
4.1.2
Speciﬁc Design Load Cases for Floating Oﬀshore
Wind Turbines
Besides the modiﬁcations of the time duration of the simulations and the gust
deﬁnitions, there are speciﬁc accidental situations which need to be recognized
when analyzing ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines. Some of those situations com-
prise one mooring line break, only in redundant systems, under normal power
production or with the turbine parked under storm conditions. Others study
leakage in the ﬂoater in both scenarios. All of those are speciﬁed in "Table 1 -
FOWT speciﬁc design load cases" in [29].
The structural safety of the whole structure and, in this case, the station keeping
system is ensured by means of a safety class methodology. The classiﬁcation
of the structure in those safety classes contemplates the failure consequences,
which can be risks to human life or economical or environmental nature. It is
considered that ﬂoating wind turbines will be unmanned during severe met-ocean
conditions, then permitting to assign a "normal safety class". Nevertheless, non
redundant systems must be designed to one safety class higher than redundant
systems, thus to "high safety class". Those safety classes are deﬁned in terms of
safety levels which are materialized into annual probability of failure, according
to [29] [31].

is the risk of collision with adjacent units if they were part of a wind farm.

Chapter 5
Discussion of the Results
The analyses of the results of the design load cases is presented, ﬁrst of all
by displaying the design load values for diﬀerent parts of the ﬂoating wind
turbine. Therefore, the design loads for the tower bottom and tower top bending
moments, both fore-aft and side-side are given, together with the extreme tower
top accelerations. Furthermore, the blade root bending moments and the forces
at the fairleads of the three mooring lines are also shown. In addition, some
load cases are studied more thoroughly to demonstrate certain phenomena or
show trends. Finally, the negative thrust appearance during mooring line break
simulations is explained along with the blade edgewise instabilities for 10 degrees
incoming wind direction direction.
The chapter starts by stating the limitations and inaccuracies in the deﬁnitions of
the simulations. In such a way, the correct interpretation of the results requires
to consider this aspects and the speciﬁcations of the design load cases given in
table 4.1.
5.1
Inaccuracies and Computational Limitations
in the Extension and Deﬁnition of the Simu-
lations
The correct interpretation of the results and its validity depends on the assump-
tions and choices made when building the numerical models and the selected
==PAGE:76==
56
Discussion of the Results
parameters to run the simulations. The limitations or issues related to both
aspects will be discussed below. While some of them are related to the scope of
the Thesis, others are due to computational constraints when running and post
processing the simulations or inaccuracies introduced in the model.
First of all, the inaccuracies introduced in the model which were noticed at a
too late stage in the Thesis to be rectiﬁed are:
• No-shear simulations: preliminary simulations during model setup were
performed without considering wind shear. Unfortunately, this setting
remained unchanged until it was noticed 14 days before submitting the
Thesis. The majority of the simulations could be re-done, post processed
and analyzed conveniently. Except DLCs 1.3, 9.1 and 9.2, which still don’t
account wind shear.
Few sample simulations were run with wind shear to evaluate the eﬀect of
this condition, revealing that the main parameter which is aﬀected by this
issue is the tower top fore-aft bending moment. This moment is due to
the load contributions of the three blades. In this way, when the blades
pass through the upper half of the rotor swept area the contribution to
this moment is positive, whereas it is negative when they pass through the
lower half.
If the shear is considered, the wind speeds seen by the blade when passing
through the upper half are higher than the ones when passing through the
lower half. This higher wind speeds drive a larger bending moment, thus
the mean values of the bending moment are positively shifted compared
to the case without shear. Generally, all cases without shear had negative
extreme fore-aft bending moments, whereas with shear they were positive.
Although this was the most clear implication, every force, moment, dis-
placement or acceleration which oscillates around zero is susceptible to
change from one situation to the other. But as it has been explained the
greatest changes were for the tower top fore-aft bending moment.
• Non symmetry of the WAMIT model of the ﬂoater: as it is mentioned in
3.3.4 the WAMIT panel model of the Olav Olsen ﬂoater is not perfectly
symmetrical due to the coarse panel mesh used. This was because of
computational constraints. The shift in the center of buoyancy of the
ﬂoater is only 2.58cm, but still lead to the appearance of three terms in
the hydrostatic matrix 3.4. The implications of this aspect are commented
in 3.3.4 and are not signiﬁcant.
Then, some computational limitations are pointed:
• Number of simulations: ideally turbulent wind simulations with diﬀerent
==PAGE:77==

Deﬁnition of the Simulations

wind seeds would also consider distinct wave seeds, however due to the
increase in the number of cases and the time constraints in the project
this recommendation from [33] couldn’t be followed. As an example, three
wave seeds should have been used for DLCs 1.3, 1.6 and 6.1. For those
cases 1944, 702 and 18 simulations were run, respectively. If three wave
seeds would have been considered they would have turned into 5832, 2106
and 54 simulations, respectively.
• Duration of the simulations: sea states are normally deﬁned for a period
of three hours and therefore the simulations representing normal operating
conditions or stationary fault situations should have been performed for
those three hours. This would have been the case for DLCs 1.3, 1.6, 6.1,
9.2 and 10.2.
Nevertheless, there was a limitation when saving those long simulations
in the Gorm cluster. Shorter ﬁles could be saved in binary format, while
longer ones had to be saved in ASCII, with the same simulation requiring
roughly ten times more storage space when being saved in ASCII.
In this case, shorter and longer ﬁles were also determined by the time
step of the simulation, so when the time step was reduced to cope with
storm conditions, DLC 6.1, or mooring line break, DLCs 9s and 10s, ASCII
format had to be used. This lead to results ﬁles with of more than 27GB
each. Hence, reading them using Pdap was not possible and they couldn’t
be read either with MATLAB in a reasonable amount of time.
• Performance of the simulations: due to the dynamic mooring line model
the simulations were comparatively much slower than for a bottom ﬁxed
wind turbine. This issue was expanded with the reﬁnement of the time
step when performing simulations under storm conditions or mooring line
break. The time step had also to be reﬁned because of convergence issues.
Otherwise, a closer look can be taken to the numerical models which were used:
• Wave models: irregular linear waves were considered in all simulations as
WAMIT’s model is linear. However, large waves simulated during storm
conditions are realistically non-linear. This issue could be solved by using
computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD), but then it wouldn’t be feasible to
perform so many simulations.
• Aerodynamic models: the aerodynamics of the ﬂoating wind turbine are
more complicated than for ﬁxed bottom wind turbines due to the back
and forth movement because of the waves. This leads to unsteady ﬂow
over the rotor, particularly when the blades are pitched to feather position
during storm conditions, which could be better understood by using CFD.
The appearance of blade edgewise vibrations for 10 degrees incoming wind
==PAGE:78==
58
Discussion of the Results
direction, in DLCs 10.1 and 10.2, could also be inspected by means of CFD,
this issue is discussed in 5.4.2.
Finally, some aspects regarding the diﬃculties to specify the parameters of the
simulations and analyze the results are reﬂected:
• Coupled nature of the system: the highly coupled nature of the system be-
tween the aerodynamics, structural dynamics, hydrodynamics and controls,
makes it complicated to clearly identify and separate causes from eﬀects
during the simulations. This was deﬁnitely experienced when negative
thrust appeared during mooring line break simulations. The explanation
of this circumstance is provided in 5.4.1.
• Deﬁnition of the gust events: the response of the turbine under gust events
in start-up or shut-down situations is highly dependant on the time lag
between both events. That’s why it would be necessary to dedicate more
time to identify what are the combinations which lead to the ultimate
loads. Assessing this circumstance would require more simulations and
was outside the scope of the Thesis.
• Large number of parameters involved, considering both inputs to the system
to be deﬁned and outputs which can be evaluated. In general, there are a
lot of parameters to be speciﬁed when running the simulations, increasing
the probability to misunderstand or miss some of them.
Moreover, that great amount of parameters implies that there are lots of
possible combinations when trying to separate causes from eﬀects by doing
diﬀerent simulations. For example, it was necessary to perform numerous
simulations when assessing the negative thrust during mooring line break
conditions. In some of those cases, the rotor was completely ﬁxed, in others
the wind was steady or turbulent and there were or there weren’t waves.
• Distribution and schedule of the work: time wise a signiﬁcant part of this
Thesis was devoted, ﬁrst to build the numerical models both in WAMIT
and HAWC2 and verify them and also to get those numerical models
adapted to deal with a large number of ﬂoating wind turbine simulations in
the Gorm cluster. Thus, leaving less time to further analyze the mooring
line break simulations,the edgewise instabilities in the blade or extend the
work to e.g address fatigue.

The ultimate loads are used to verify the tower, blades and moorings design.
The blade root moments, tower bottom bending moments, tower top bending
moments, tower top accelerations and mooring lines forces at the fairleads are
displayed in ﬁgures 5.1, 5.2, 5.4, 5.5 and 5.7, respectively. The tables gathering
those exact values are given in Annex B, with blade bending moments, the tower
bending moments, tower top accelerations and mooring forces in tables B.1, B.2,
B.3 and B.4, respectively.
As can be seen for the tower and blade bending moments, the ultimate loads from
the accidental states under storm conditions 10.1 and 10.2 are always among the
highest. However, it is important to remark that due to computational and time
constraints during this Thesis only nine simulations were performed for each of
those cases. So for each yaw error one wind seed was contemplated for three
diﬀerent sea states. Those three diﬀerent sea states were deﬁned as the 50 year
reference sea states in table 4.9, each with a signiﬁcant wave height Hs of 10.9m
and a peak period Tp of 9.00, 12.50 or 16.00s.
Therefore, as there weren’t diﬀerent realizations for the turbulent wind speed
the characteristic value was directly the extreme value from all those simula-
tions, instead of taking the mean of the diﬀerent realizations as the standards
recommend. Still, the number of simulations performed is the same as the ones
deﬁned in the LIFES50+ design load basis [45].
Furthermore, as later on will be explained, those accidental simulations under
storm conditions experienced negative thrust and edgewise vibrations on the
tip of the blade with frequencies of 0.826Hz. This was conﬁrmed by getting
the mode shapes of the ﬂoating wind turbine with HAWC2, which revealed this
edgewise blade motion for that named frequency. Those excitations happened
for all the cases under storm conditions, including 6.1 where the mooring lines
were intact.
Blade edgewise vibrations are also reported in [36] for the DTU 10MW Wind
Turbine, for operational and stand still conditions, when the incoming wind
has 30 or -30 degrees.
One of the solutions given in the mentioned report
is changing the structural pitch which would imply a redesign of the blades.
Another proposal, which is reported to work properly, is modifying the pitch
sequence, that is changing the pitch of the blade which is in the upward position.
A possible extension of this Thesis would be to apply this last solution, see if it
works for this case and try to reduce the vibrations.
Nevertheless, all cases in DLC 10.1 and 10.2 where the incoming wind had a

DLC 1.4 - Power production in extreme coherent

ﬂoater. If the power spectral density (PSD) of the yaw time series is seen at the

was presented in table 3.6 and it is fyaw = 0.01026Hz.
On the other hand, if the platform pitch is contemplated in the middle left
sub ﬁgure, it can be seen that for the pitch rotation the oscillations are also
undamped. There are clearly two main frequencies in those oscillations which
correspond to the yaw natural frequency commented before and to the pitch
natural frequency, which was fpitch = 0.03097Hz, as seen in the ﬁgure on the
left.
Finally, it is possible to check the development of the platform’s surge and sway
motions in the last row of the ﬁgure. In such a way, it can be appreciated that
there is a decrease in the surge of the ﬂoater. This is because the wind speed
in the rotor’s direction after the gust is over the rated, hence leading to this
diminution. If the sway is considered it can be seen that there is a motion in the
direction of the gust, so the platform is displaced getting a new mean steady
position. Those PSDs can be checked in the left, appreciating that the surge
motion is heavily inﬂuenced by the pitch and the yaw rotations. This is not
the case for the sway motion whose frequency is not any of the ﬂoater’s natural
frequencies.
Lastly, as a remark this behavior should be addressed by modifying the controller
and by adding damping to the yaw degree of freedom, as this response is not
realistic at all.

DLC 2.1 - Power production with grid loss
This DLC shows what happens if there is a grid loss event during normal power

the thrust, rotor torque, blade pitch angle, tower top fore-aft acceleration and

In this paper some of these fault

and spar ﬂoaters.
Firstly, if the thrust time series is considered it can be appreciated that after the
grid loss there is an aggressive negative thrust, taking around 4s to the negative
peak, and after a short transient of around thirty seconds the thrust is stabilized
close to zero. Something similar happens with the rotor torque. Both signals
exactly represent the expected transient behaviour.
Furthermore, this negative thrust leads to a negative fore-aft acceleration in
the tower with a peak of −1.159m/s2 and the side-side, which is not shown
here, peaking at −6.075m/s2. The magnitude of this acceleration is 6.1720m/s2,
which is above the safety threshold of 2.943m/s2.
The negative thrust also provokes a fast platform pitching action so that the
rotor is pulled back into the wind. That’s why considering HAWC2’s coordinate
reference system the pitch motion is negative. After this initial pitch rotation
there is a decay motion with a frequency close to the pitch natural frequency as
seen in the PSD signal. It can also be seen that the pitch angle of the blades
goes to feather position, which is 82 degrees as speciﬁed by the controller.
The highest loads for this fault condition are experienced for wind speeds close
to the rated as the thrust is the largest then. The behaviour introduced in ﬁgure
5.9 is similar to the one for DLCs 4.2 and 5.1, which correspond to shut-down
during extreme operating gust (EOG) and emergency shut-down, respectively.
In the case of DLC 4.2 the response is increased if the shut-down happens close
to the peak of the gust, as the slow controller response leads to a higher thrust
before the shut-down.

5.4.1
Floater Motion and Negative Thrust
The analyses of the results of the simulations which involved the upwind mooring
line break revealed the appearance of negative thrust. Even though there was a
signiﬁcant yaw motion after the mooring line break, the ﬂoating wind turbine was
never in downwind position. Lastly, it was clear that the negative thrust could
be explained by considering the aerodynamics of the blade in feather position.
First of all, it is important to contemplate the aerodynamics of a section of
the blade in normal operating conditions as it can be seen in the ﬁrst row of
ﬁgure 5.11. There, it can be seen how the relative wind speed Vrel is a result of
the incoming wind speed V0 modiﬁed by the normal induction factor a and the
contribution of the rotational speed of the blade ωr(1 + a′). With the rotational
speed of the rotor as ω, the radius of the section of the blade as r and a′ as
the tangential induction factor. This relative wind speed gives the direction
of the drag force d and the lift force l. The sum of those two forces gives a
resultant force R over the section of the blade, which can be separated in two
contributions; one normal to the rotor plane PN and one tangential to it PT .
Finally, the thrust is a result of the integration of PN over the diﬀerent sections
of the three blades, whereas the rotor torque is the analogue but considering PT
and ω.
So as to get the direction of the lift force it is possible to consider the variation
of the lift coeﬃcient cl with the angle of attack α, as shown in ﬁgure 5.12 for the
DTU 10MW wind turbine for diﬀerent blade sections. Therefore, for positive
angles of attack below 50 degrees the lift coeﬃcient is positive, which means that
the direction of the lift force is the one from the pressure side to the suction side
of the blade. Nevertheless, if the lift coeﬃcient is negative the direction of the
force is the opposite. This lift force will always be perpendicular to the relative
wind speed. A more complete insight into rotor aerodynamics and the equations
behind can be experienced in [47].
After the mooring line break the controller was adjusted to pitch the blades to
feather position so as to reduce the forces in the blades. This initial situation
would correspond to the sub ﬁgures of the second and third rows of ﬁgure 5.11,
where the wind speed in the sub ﬁgure of the second row has an angle of 10
degrees with the rotor plane and for the one on the third row the angle is 350
degrees, following the coordinate reference system of HAWC2.
In each of those rows it can be seen that at the start the lift force is negative
==PAGE:95==

turbine will modify the magnitude of lift and drag forces. Hence, if the projection
of the lift force over the normal direction of the rotor plane is higher than the
one of the drag force, PN and the thrust force will be negative.

the incoming wind with 10 degrees has a negative Vx so the sway displacement
is negative and the opposite takes place for 350 degrees. Again, the situation for
0 and 350 degrees is similar.
This sway displacement leads to diﬀerent forces in the remaining mooring lines.
Thus, the mooring line which is in the opposite direction of the sway displacement
presents the highest forces at the fairlead. Both mooring lines rotate along the
anchor until the new equilibrium position is reached. During these rotation the
end fraction of both mooring lines are always touching the seabed. Indeed, more
than 200m of mooring line are always touching the seabed.
Finally, it can be seen that for all cases the thrust starts being positive and
during the transient goes to negative because of the commented negative lift due
to the variation of the angle of attack. The remaining yaw error after the yaw
motion referred before leads to the persistence of the negative thrust for 10 and
350 degrees incoming wind speeds.
During all this accidental simulations and also in 6.1, the rotor is not locked.
Even if the blades are completely pitched, due to the tangential aerodynamic
forces, the blades slowly rotate with a mean of 2rpm.
A deﬁnite proof that the negative thrust is due to the negative lift can be seen
in ﬁgure 5.16 where the force in the local x and y directions of each blade is
integrated over all blade and gathered, projected in the thrust direction and
summed for all blades. This corresponds to one of the simulations with a wind
direction of 350 degrees and the yaw motion is also displayed.

possible to further inspect this issue. The analysis is not straight forward as
there are large yaw motions for all cases, varying the angle of attack of the blade
which also changes because of the turbulent wind speed.

Chapter 6
Conclusions and Future
Work
From an economical and market analysis point of view, it is reasonable to
see ﬂoating wind energy as a promising energy source providing electricity to
populated coastal areas, removing water depth as a constraint for oﬀshore wind
farm construction. However, this will only happen if substantial cost reductions
are achieved. Although those cost reductions are just about to come with market
volume, renewable energy additions in a scenery without almost any subsidies
require new players to already have competitive costs before entering the market.
The greater standardization in manufacturing as water depth and soil type might
not have impact in foundation design can be one of the driver of this cost decrease.
But it can also be accomplished by improving the design of ﬂoating foundations
by enhancing the operating performance or easing transport, manufacturing or
installation. The work in this project was related to the operating performance
as it involved analyzing the response both in normal operating conditions, typical
fault situations and accidental states involving the break of the upwind mooring
line.
The coupled nature of the system hampered the analysis and the accurate
identiﬁcation of causes and eﬀects. Furthermore, there were computational
limitations, mainly because of the dynamic mooring line model requiring ﬁner
time steps and thus longer simulations. This together with the necessity of
extending the simulations to correctly capture the low natural frequency motions
of the ﬂoater, lead to large and highly time consuming simulations to run, store
and post process. This gives an authentic experience of the complex trade oﬀ
==PAGE:106==
86
Conclusions and Future Work
between the accuracy of the results, the complexity and computational cost of
the numerical models.
Otherwise, the simulations of the design load cases revealed the fundamental
importance of the controller. In such a way, the slower response so as to avoid
negative damping instabilities due to the back and forth motion of the ﬂoater,
leads to higher loads under gusts and extreme turbulence scenarios. Those higher
loads mainly aﬀected the tower bottom fore-aft bending moments, blade root
ﬂapwise bending moments and tower top side-side bending moments.
It is essential to highlight that the deﬁnition of the gusts is complicated, because
of the great number of possible combinations regarding both duration and time
lag. Then, it cannot be assured if the highest possible loads were registered. But
what is more important, this doesn’t give an idea about how realistic are those
situations. Thus, a probabilistic approach would be worth to be explored.
On the other hand, the upwind mooring line break simulations revealed unex-
pectedly large yaw and surge motions, which should be studied more thoroughly.
The extent surge motion also compromises the integrity of the electrical cables
of the ﬂoater. Hence, design modiﬁcations either in the mooring line system to
reduce the motions or in the electrical cables should be considered. It would also
be imperative to increase the amount of simulations of this situation to better
understand this issue.
In this respect, the appearance of blade edgewise instabilities lead to the highest
edgewise bending moments. This were mainly transmitted to the fore-aft bending
moments, both tower base and tower top, due to the feather position of the
blades. This drove the largest loads for those mentioned parts of the wind turbine.
Nevertheless, those high excitations only happened when the incoming wind had
10 degrees and not for 0 or 350 degrees. A further study of this circumstance
should be performed, probably by means of computational ﬂuid dynamics.
Finally, the presence of negative thrust and the identiﬁcation of the causes
required to perform an extent number of simulations and careful analyses. It
was realized that this was due to blade aerodynamics, the relative orientation
and magnitude of lift and drag forces during the yaw motion.
At this point, it can be useful to recognize possible extensions and future work,
which either couldn’t be covered because of time constraints or were completely
outside the scope of this Thesis. They would serve to clarify some issues discussed
along this Thesis or complement the insights.
• Fully perform the ultimate design load cases accounting for great mis-
alignment between wind and waves in operating conditions, 2.2y, or fault
==PAGE:107==
87
situations, 6.2 and 6.3. This could also help to understand the blade edge-
wise instabilities issue. Moreover, the realization of the ultimate design
load cases involving the malfunction of the controller, 2.2p and 2.2b, would
also be a compulsory extension.
• Apply the recommendation provided in [36] to see if the edgewise vibrations
are reduced by modifying the blade pitch angle sequence.
• Run the fatigue design loads cases, they are required by the standards
and should be relevant because of the higher motions of the ﬂoater and
wave motion interactions. In such a way, the design load cases would be
completed.
• Extend the duration of the simulations to comply with the three hour
requirement, only for the cases speciﬁed by the standards.
• Extend the accidental simulations, both in number of cases and length of
the simulations, to understand the interactions and truly get the ultimate
loads. A further study could be to perform computational ﬂuid dynamics
simulations in this situation. The study could also involve the usage of a
diﬀerent aero-elastic tool e.g. FAST to compare the results and evaluate
the divergences.
• Modiﬁcation of the WAMIT panel mesh to avoid the shift in the centre of
buoyancy, the appearance of the non diagonal entries in the hydrostatic
matrix and comparison of the obtained results.
• Modify the design of the ﬂoater, its moorings or the electrical cables to
cope with the large motions after the mooring line break.
• More detailed study of the gust interactions, evaluating more combinations
and their likeliness.
• Techno-economical assessment of the ﬂoater, logistics, construction meth-
ods, operation and maintenance or environmental impacts. These implica-
tions could also be extended when building a real scale wind farm with
hundreds of ﬂoating foundations.
• Non linear waves simulations for the storm conditions, although the use of
WAMIT would then be compromised.
