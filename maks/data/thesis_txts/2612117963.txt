Abstract
As the world’s population continues to grow, much more eﬀort and innovation are urgently needed
in order to sustainably expand agricultural production, while reserving the world’s limited water
resources. The misuse of water due to insuﬃcient knowledge of the actual crop water requirements,
or inappropriate irrigation techniques, can lead to loss of water, higher production costs for the
crop producers, and as a result negative environmental impact. Sugarcane crops are one of the
most water-demanding crops, and the world’s largest by production quantity. Hence, immediate
action for their sustainable agricultural management is required. In order to sustainably monitor
water resources, water supply must precisely match water demand. Key solutions in that direction
are water footprint (WF) and water use eﬃciency (WUE) estimates and evapotranspiration (ET)
measuring, as they provide accurate and much needed information about the actual water crop
requirements.
Traditional methods for water management and other agriculture practices are costly, time con-
suming, and provide low-resolution data. A great alternative is the cutting-edge technology known
as Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS). Precision agriculture can greatly beneﬁt from the very high
spatial and temporal resolution (VHR) maps of Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) and ET, that
these systems provide. The latter are extremely useful in better estimating WUE and WF. The
main goal of this thesis was to display the value of remote sensing based models, in order to assess
WUE and WF for a commercial sugarcane farm in El Viejo, Costa Rica.
In order to achieve the aforementioned goal, we further improve the remote based ET and GPP
Priestley–Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory (PT-JPL) model, by replacing the albedo equation
and optimizing the maximum light use eﬃciency parameter, (εmax). A validation of the model
then took place, using UAS images and insitu Eddy Covariance (EC) data for the period of 2016-
2018. The model performed well for assessing both types of data. More speciﬁcally, on the UAS
case and the instantaneous time-scale, the model could simulate the variation of GPP and ET
with RMSE=3.37 gC/m2s, MAPE=14% and RMSE=39.52 W/m2, MAPE=7% respectively. On
the daily time-scale, the model managed to estimate GPP and ET with RMSE=0.91 gC/m2d,
MAPE= 7.8%, and RMSE=27.27W/m2, MAPE=23.7% respectively. Finally, the joint model eﬃ-
ciently predicted GPP and ET, even during the long-term assessment. The pooled standard error
for the daily GPP and ET estimates, was equal to Perror=0.36 gC/m2d and Perror=3.84 W/m2
respectively. For the instantaneous time-scale, the pooled standard error was equal to Perror=0.42
gC/m2s and Perror=8.22 W/m2 for the GPP and ET respectively.
Additionally, WF and WUE parameters were also rigorously assessed in this study. The joint
PT-JPL suﬃciently simulated the daily variations of WUE with RMSE=0.42 gC/kg of H2O and
MAPE=21.64%. Additionally, the remote sensing based WF=152.94 m3/t was closer to the bench-
mark WF=171.23 m3/t from EC ﬂux tower than the WF=210.62 m3/t based on (Hoekstra et al.,
2011), which overestimated WF by 23%. This proved that to get more precise WF measurements,
empirical remote sensing based estimates of crop evapotranspiration (ETc) are essential. The reso-
lution of these ETc measurements can be also increased by the use of the UAS, which also enables
new scales of measurements and hence new understandings of ET.
Finally, our results demonstrate the applicability of the joint PT-JPL ET and LUE GPP model to
eﬀectively predict GPP, ET, WUE and WF estimates. In addition, the ﬁndings of this study prove
that UAS are like to generate precise estimations of crop water requirements and crop evapotran-
spiration at high spatial resolution, further improving sustainable water resources management
techniques. Concluding, for the global water crisis, no single measure is a remedy. There is a need
for a combination of technical, behavioral and policy instruments and it is in that direction that
we need to work in.

Introduction
Water is the lifeblood of ecosystems, the connecting link between all communities, vital to human
health and to all living creatures, a necessary tool for well-being and a precondition for economic
prosperity [11]. However, nowadays almost all freshwater resources are under pressure due to cli-
mate change, the constantly increasing population and fast growing economic activities [12],[13].
Additionally, changes in our planet’s temperature and precipitation patterns, the average surface
temperature increasing by 0.8 oC since the early 20’ century [14] and the decreasing precipitation
frequency during summertime [15], are severely aﬀecting the availability of fresh water in the world
[16]. As a consequence, food security is also threatened.
Agriculture is the main water consumer, and as such is severely aﬀected by climate change [17],
[18]. From all the world’s freshwater, a staggering 70% is currently used to irrigate crops [19],
[20]. As the world’s population grows and water scarcity increases, the question of how we feed
the world with ﬁnite resources becomes more urgent. It is estimated that a global population of 9
billion [21] by 2050 will require a 50% increase in food production [22], therefore increasing the de-
mand of water and land. To sustainably increase food production, will require new sustainable and
advanced ways for monitoring and managing agricultural water use, whilst achieving Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs). An additional eﬀect of climate change is aridiﬁcation and degrada-
tion of the surface due to warmer temperatures [23]. Warmer temperatures increase the water’s
evaporation rate into the atmosphere [24],[25], leaving less water for plants and therefore, increas-
ing plant stress. This risk of aridiﬁcation and degradation in the world’s croplands due to warmer
temperatures further threatens the world’s food security in addition to the threat agriculture faces.
In order to support mitigation strategies and inform water management decisions, it is impor-
tant to closely monitor the ﬂuctuations in the hydrological cycle components [23]. This can be
achieved by using Earth Observation (EO) data to calculate rates of evapotranspiration (ET), en-
ergy, and CO2 exchanges between land surface and atmosphere, at both high spatial and temporal
resolution and provide near real-time maps of these data to all interested parties [26].
Studies on global, regional and farm scale show that Earth Observation (EO) data can be of
critical importance for agriculture. "Due to its capacity to regulate information relevant for farm-
ing, remote sensing can become the global source of future, information driven global agriculture"
[27]. When integrated into water and environmental management models, these EO data can sup-
port food security and ensure sustainable and eﬃcient agriculture and water management through
veriﬁable rules and regulations [28].
As already mentioned above, high spatial and temporal resolution maps of land surface-atmosphere
energy, water and CO2 ﬂux exchanges, such as evapotranspiration (ET) and gross primary pro-
ductivity (GPP), are crucial for sustainable and precise agriculture as well as water resources
management [9].
Photosynthesis is the biochemical process with which plants absorb sunlight to convert CO2 and
water into carbohydrates. It is the most vital process that regulates the whole global carbon cycle.
Closely linked with the latter, Gross primary productivity (GPP) is deﬁned as the total amount of
organic matter produced by plants through photosynthesis at the ecosystem level [29],[30]. GPP
is a crucial component of an ecosystem’s carbon ﬂuxes and the carbon balance between biosphere
and atmosphere. Hence, accurate estimates of GPP are essential for quantifying plant production,
understanding the global carbon cycle, enhancing vegetation yields and coping with climate change
[29], [31], [32], [33]. Remote sensing techniques based on reﬂectance-based vegetation indices (VIs)
are often used to estimate GPP at the diﬀerent scales [29].
Evapotranspiration (ET) is the transfer of water, from the surface to the atmosphere.
ET is
determined by the air’s water capacity, solar radiation, air temperature, wind speed and the soil
moisture. In addition, ET is a keystone, multifaceted climate variable that uniquely links water
(evaporation), energy (latent heat ﬂux) and carbon cycle (transpiration-photosynthesis trade-oﬀ)
[34], [35],[23]. It is the most important climatic predictor of biodiversity, a predominant variable
needed for water management in the agriculture sector and ﬁnally, a signiﬁcant parameter for

An Introduction to Remote Sensing and Earth Observation Concept
Remote Sensing or Earth Observation (EO) can be broadly deﬁned as the technique of observing
and analyzing objects from a distance without being in direct contact with them. A more precise
deﬁnition is, the practice of monitoring and analyzing information about the physical character-
istics of the Earth’s surface, by measuring their reﬂected and emitted electromagnetic radiation
at a distance [47], [48], [49], [50]. This reﬂected and emitted electromagnetic radiation is then
collected by the remote sensors. These remote sensors can be mounted on satellites, aircrafts or
on unmanned areal systems (UAS), and they can be either passive or active. The latter catego-
rization is based on whether these sensors respond or not to external stimuli. More speciﬁcally,
passive sensors are those which respond to external stimuli and record the natural energy that is
reﬂected or emitted from the Earth’s surface. The most common source of radiation detected by
these sensors is reﬂected sunlight. A typical example of a passive sensor is the camera, as it records
electromagnetic radiation reﬂected from an existing source, the sun. On the other hand, active
sensors, are sensors that have their own internal source of illumination (i.e. ﬂash) [51], [52].
Moreover, these remote sensors can measure diﬀerent spectral wavelengths, from reﬂected visi-
ble light, to ultraviolet (UV), infrared (IR) and ﬁnally microwave radiation. The fundamental
physical principle that EO relies on, is that diﬀerent features of the Earth’s surface interact dif-
ferently, and each one of these features have a speciﬁc "spectral signature", which makes them
distinguishable from each other [53]. More analytically, "spectral signature" is the intensity of
light reﬂected from a surface divided by the intensity of incident light [54]. A good example that
illustrates the aforementioned deﬁnition is depicted in the ﬁgures below (Fig. 2 and 3).

to green vegetation and water. Knowing the typical spectral response characteristics of the Earth’s
main surfaces, it is possible for someone to identify each of the Earth’s surfaces with a remotely
sensed imagery, and thus evaluate and estimate their condition.

From Satellites to Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS): A New Remote
Sensing Approach
It was in the early ’60s when the ﬁrst satellite was launched, signaling a new era in terms of remote
sensing monitoring. As of 2008, more than 150 satellites were in orbit, measuring and recording dif-
ferent sections of the electromagnetic spectrum [55]. Today, with the most advanced technological
development, the science of earth observation (EO) has beneﬁted greatly. It was only late 2015’s
when a new satellite called Copernicus Sentinel-2 was launched, which monitors the variability of
surface conditions [56]. As can be seen in ﬁgure (Fig. 4), the resolution of images produced by the
diﬀerent remote sensing platforms, has been increasing drastically during recent years.
Figure 4: Evolution of the Earth Observation (EO) systems (Figure was derived from [4]).
However, satellite based remote sensing, despite its high spatial resolution, is not suitable for preci-
sion agriculture, due to low pixel resolution. To solve this problem, a new remote sensing platform
has been introduced, Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS). UAS have become increasingly popular
and almost ubiquitously used by the remote sensing community due to the low cost, ﬂexible revisit
times, simplicity of operation and image processing, low altitude ﬂights and multiple camera pay-
loads, ranging from visible, near and thermal infrared spectral ranges [57], [58]. When compared
to satellites, UAS provides opportunities to acquire very high temporal and spatial resolution (<
1 m) data, under diﬀerent weather conditions [26], [59], [60], [61], [62].
Additionally, there is a hypothesis that in the near future, UAS data will be able to substitute
satellite images and when combined with existing remote based models, will be used to generate
spatially detailed heat ﬂux maps that provide insight into diﬀerent evaporation rates and plant
stress at the decimetre scale [60]. Thus, UAS data, with their very high spatial resolution, are
particularly important for optimizing food production and precision agriculture [9].

1.3
Micrometeorological Methods for Estimating Energy and Water Fluxes
Exchanges
One of the most well-known micrometeorological methods for estimating CO2, water and energy
exchanges between the atmosphere and the terrestrial ecosystems, is the Eddy Covariance (EC)
technique [63]. EC technique is at the core of the global atmospheric-terrestrial monitoring network
[64]. It estimates turbulent ﬂux by calculating the covariance of ﬂuctuations in the vertical wind
velocity [65]. This technique is also used for validation for remote sensing based climate models.
Some disadvantages of the EC technique can be spotted when it is applied on tall, heterogeneous
canopies, sloping surfaces, and unstable atmospheric conditions [66]. Also, this technique requires a
continuous energy supply, has high construction costs and is highly dependent on assumptions [67].
For all the aforementioned reasons, it is an imperative need to close the gap between coarse spatial
resolution images from satellite platforms, and topical, narrow data from EC ﬂux towers, and ﬁll it
with more accurate estimations of global energy ﬂuxes. One way to achieve this, is to develop GPP
and ET remote sensing models further, so they can analyze very high resolution images (VHR)
and data obtained by UAS.

Basic Theory of Evapotranspiration
Evapotranspiration (ET) can be deﬁned as the transfer of water from the surface to the atmo-
sphere. Evaporation occurs when a moist surface is exposed to dry air. This exposure causes the
water from the surface to gradually evaporate into the atmosphere, thus increasing the amount of
water vapor in the surrounding air. Transpiration on the other hand, is the evaporation of water
from plant leaves. Water moves from the soil through the roots, into the plant and out to the
atmosphere through its leaves. When the surrounding air is saturated with water vapor, evapora-
tion as well as transpiration stop [68]. The rate of evapotranspiration depends on the ecosystem’s
available energy for evaporating water and the air’s ability to hold water [68].
There are two terms related to ET in need of further clariﬁcation.
The ﬁrst one is potential
evapotranspiration (PET), and the second one is the actual evapotranspiration (AE). The diﬀer-
ence between these two terms, is that the former is deﬁned as the amount of evaporation that
would occur if suﬃcient water was available [68]. On the other hand, (AE) is the amount of water
that is actually removed from the surface due to evapotranspiration processes.
One of the most common equations for calculating evapotranspiration from vegetated surfaces
is the Penman-Monteith equation. The Penman-Monteith equation presents the thermodynamic,
aerodynamic and biological processes controlling evapotranspiration (Eq. 4):

1.5
Vegetation Indices (VIs)
In the earth observation (EO) and remote sensing science, vegetation indices (VIs), which were
developed in the 1970s, are widely used and have beneﬁted numerous societal and scientiﬁc appli-
cations. Some of these applications are for example, the assessment of vegetation’s health, natural
resources monitoring, CO2 and H2O resource management, agricultural eﬃciency and early warn-
ing systems [72], [73].
In general, the main goal of all these applications, is to evaluate the condition of a vegetation
present in a scene. The amount of light reﬂected from a surface, depends on the amount of sun-
light energy reaching the surface as well as the surface’s speciﬁc properties. Since sunlight energy
varies and depends on time and atmospheric conditions, it is insuﬃcient to simply measure the
reﬂected light from a surface and characterize its properties [72]. This can be overcome by comb-
ing more than one spectral bands to form new indicators, known as VIs. There are several ways
for calculating VIs [74]. VIs can be estimated by "rationing, diﬀerencing, sums and by forming
linear combinations of spectral band data" [72]. The main purpose of using VIs is to enhance the
vegetation’s signal, while at the same time decreasing the surface’s background eﬀects [72], [73].
In general, VIs are associated with various properties and characteristics of the plants. However,
there are notable diﬀerences in how they describe the plants’ actual status. Thus, multiple VIs
can be used at the same time to complement each other [75].
VIs are aﬀected by various parameters. Some of these parameters are for example the solar zenith,
viewing angles and the soil’s background properties (i.e. soil moisture). Finally, VIs estimated
based on "data obtained over the same target, but with diﬀerent sensors, may not be the same
because of detector and ﬁlter characteristics of the instruments" [72]. The nature of these problems
should be carefully considered when someone is analyzing and interpreting VIs.
Plants consist of four pigment groups: 1) chlorophylls (Chl), 2) carotenoids, 3) ﬂavonoids, and
4) betalains [76]. These pigments aﬀect and regulate basic biological functions. Changes in pig-
ment content and composition can be used as markers of the vegetation status [76]. When sunlight
strikes on a vegetation’s surface, the majority of the light in the near-infrared (NIR) region is
reﬂected, while visible wavelengths (red) are absorbed by the chlorophyll pigment for use in pho-
tosynthesis. The greater the amount of vigor of green vegetation present, the larger the contrast
between NIR (0.7–1.3 mm) and red (0.6–0.7 mm) reﬂectances. Higher contrast between NIR/red
reﬂectances also means healthier vegetation. On the contrary, lower spectral contrast usually cor-
responds to vegetation under stress or lower vegetation cover. VIs are a reliable tool and provide
a consistent and suﬃcient data of the canopy’s photosynthetic capacity [72].
One of the most widely used vegetation indices in remote sensing is the Normalized Diﬀerence
Vegetation Index (NDVI). The Normalized Diﬀerence Vegetation Index (NDVI) is a simple indica-
tor of the photosynthetically active biomass. In other words, NDVI is a reliable tool for suﬃciently
estimating the vegetation’s health and density. It also allows to visualize vegetated areas in a map,
as well as to detect abnormal changes in the growth process [77]. In order to estimate NDVI,
visible and near-infrared spectral wavelengths are used (Eq. 9), where ρNIR and ρRED represent
the reﬂectance values of the near-infrared (NIR) and the red (visible) bands respectively [78], [77].
NDVI = ρNIR −ρRED

and the fraction of the absorbed photosynthetically active radiation (fAPAR) [81]. Furthermore,
eﬀorts in order to correlate NDVI with temperature and land surface temperature (LST) have
also been conducted [82],[83]. Additionally, other studies tried to explore NDVI’s relationship with
precipitation, since precipitation inﬂuences NDVI through its eﬀect on soil moisture [84]. However,
even though NDVI is the most used VI, it also has some limitations. For example, it is highly
sensitive to soil’s optical properties and saturates at higher ranges of LAI [80]. Therefore, in order
to minimize these limitations, other VIs can be applied (i.e. EVI, OSAVI etc), which generate
similar results.
In the Results chapter, and more speciﬁcally in chapter (Ch. 4.2.2), a detailed presentation of
the correlation between the camera (UAS) and EC ﬂux tower derived NDVI is presented.

1.6
Justiﬁcation
As the world’s population continues to grow, much more eﬀort and innovation are urgently needed
in order to sustainably expand agricultural production, while reserving the world’s limited water
resources. This idea is also strongly supported by the United Nations with the establishment of
sustainable development goals (SDGs). More speciﬁcally, SDGs 2 "Zero hunger" and 6 "Ensure
availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all", should have been achieved
by 2030. In order to ensure sustainable water resources management, especially in the agriculture
sector and in water limited regions, an extensive identiﬁcation, and quantiﬁcation of the under-
lying ecophysiological processes within the surface and atmosphere continuum, are required. In
particular, quantiﬁcation of the GPP can be used in order to estimate crop yields, while ET, WF,
and WUE estimates can be used for minimizing water consumption and improving current water
management techniques.
More speciﬁcally, ET is a key parameter for sustainable water resources management. ET rep-
resents the actual water requirements of a given crop. Underestimations or overestimations of
crop water requirements are likely to cause higher production costs and have negative environ-
mental impact. Thus, accurate and precise knowledge of the exact water loss through actual ET,
as well as the WF of a crop, are critical for increasing water use eﬃciency. Improvements in ET
estimates will improve crop yields and at the same time, will reduce the amount of water used
[85]. These aforementioned eﬀorts can be supported with the help of remote sensing UAS products.
UAS are a new and leading edge technology, which provides new scales of very high spatial and
temporal resolution (<1m) estimates. These estimates enable new understandings into critical
parameters and pave the way for more improved monitoring.
1.6.1
Project Objectives
The broader objective of this study is to demonstrate the value of remote sensing based models,
in order to estimate WUE, WFs and ET from a commercial sugarcane farm, in El Viejo, Costa Rica.
The more speciﬁc objectives of this study, are divided into three diﬀerent areas.
The ﬁrst, is
to assess the applicability of the joint Priestley - Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory ET (PT-JPL)
and LUE GPP model for estimating GPP and ET. In addition, this study aims to improve the
existing model, either by applying diﬀerent equations to estimate important and vital model pa-
rameters (i.e. surface albedo), or by calibrating and optimizing other parameters (i.e. longwave
incoming radiation (LWin) and sugarcane maximum light use eﬃciency (εmax)). Also, it seeks to
validate the model after its improvement. Through very high resolution (VHR) data obtained from
UAS remote sensing and direct EC data, it will evaluate the model’s accuracy and robustness. In
addition, the model’s eﬃciency in capturing variations in diﬀerent temporal scales (instantaneous
and daily) will also be evaluated for the period 2016-2018.
The second objective of the thesis is to estimate the WUE and WF parameters and its three
diﬀerent components (WFGreen, WFBlue and WFGrey), based on remote estimates of ET and GPP.
Finally, the last objective is to assess the advantages of remote sensing based WF, when compared
to the WF based on the standard methodology proposed by (Hoekstra et al., 2011) [86], and using
Eddy Covariance WF as the benchmark.
The outcomes of this dissertation will help illustrate which indicators are best for assessing crop
status. Furthermore, WF and WUE estimates, can be useful for crop producers because it can help
them revise their existing irrigation strategies and techniques, in order to minimize water misuses
(i.e. from full to deﬁcit irrigation strategy). In conclusion, this thesis may be another reason to
reconsider our currently used practices and dietary habits, and shift towards techniques and food
with low water requirements.

3.1
Land Surface Net Radiation Budget Theory
Land surface energy balance is critical in order to describe and evaluate the land surface ecological,
hydrological and biogeochemical processes. Land surface energy balance can be expressed by the
following equation (Eq. 10), where Rn is net radiation, G is soil heat ﬂux, H is sensible heat ﬂux
and ﬁnally LE is latent heat ﬂux [101].
Rn = G + H + LE
(10)
Furthermore, the land surface net radiation budget is characterized by Rn, which expresses the
balance between the incoming radiation from the atmosphere and outgoing longwave radiation
from the Earth’s surface. Rn is the total energy that is available to inﬂuence the climate. More
speciﬁcally, Rn is calculated based on the equation (Eq. 11):
Rn = (SWin −SWout) + (LWin −LWout)
(11)
SWin is incoming shortwave radiation, which is the radiation received directly or indirectly from
the sun. A signiﬁcant part of SWin is reﬂected back to the atmosphere by the surface. This is the
outgoing shortwave radiation, SWout, and it is highly dependent on the surface albedo. LWin and
LWout, are incoming and outgoing longwave radiation respectively. They are electromagnetic radi-
ations emitted by the surface and the atmosphere within the range of infrared spectral wavelength
region. All these aforementioned types of radiation, are calculated in W/m2. Surface longwave
radiation is a very important parameter, which highly inﬂuences the Earth’s climate system. More
speciﬁcally, LWin, which is the direct measure of radiative heating of the atmosphere to the sur-
face, plays an important role in the estimation of evapotranspiration. Therefore, knowledge of the
spatial and temporal variability of the LWin on local, regional and global scales is very valuable
and important [101].
As brieﬂy mentioned, net radiation (Rn) is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by albedo (Eq.
12).
Albedo
is the amount of solar radiation reﬂected by a surface. Albedo varies between 0 and 1. A value
equal to 0 means the surface acts as a "perfect absorber". This means that the reference surface
absorbs all incoming radiation. On the contrary, a value equal to 1 means the surface is a "perfect
reﬂector", and that reﬂects all incoming energy. For example, oceans appear to be black bodies
because water absorbs almost all the radiation it receives. On the other hand, snow reﬂects almost
all the radiation it receives. Forests, ﬁelds and deserts reﬂect diﬀerent amounts of incident radia-
tion, depending on their characteristics [10].
Surface albedo varies by location, season and land cover type.
A possible increase in albedo,
is equivalent to a signiﬁcant loss of the radiative energy absorbed by the surface and a possible
reduction in the frequency and duration of the precipitation events [102]. Evaporation may also
decrease, further inhibiting precipitation. More speciﬁcally, an increase of 0.1 units, in regional
albedo corresponds to a 20% reduction in rainfall events [103]. The most noticeable changes in
albedo occur in regions experiencing desertiﬁcation and deforestation. It has been investigated that
reductions in vegetative land cover may trigger albedo to increase by 0.2 units. In addition, since
the majority of the available forests have been replaced by agricultural crops, it has been observed
that the albedo of extensive regions of the middle latitudes has altered [103]. Furthermore, it has
been well recognized and accepted, that surface albedo is one of the most radiative uncertainties in
the current climate modeling. The majority of global circulation models, are still using prescribed
ﬁelds of surface albedo, which often generate 5 ± 15% error estimations from place to place, and
time to time [102]. Thus, it is imperative to ﬁnd reliable methods for estimating surface albedo
over large areas.
Rn = SWin · (1 −albedo) + LWin −Lwout
(12)

3.2
Joint Priestley - Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory ET Model (PT-
JPL) and LUE GPP Model
Generally, the remote sensing joint ET and GPP models, which incorporate satellite or UAS data
into the modeling process, can be classiﬁed into two main categories. The 1) "Top-down" and
2) "Bottom-up" [9], [106]. The joint Priestley - Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory (PT-JPL) ET
model and LUE GPP model, used in this research, belong to the ﬁrst category. They are parsi-
monious models, capable of performing calculations both on in situ point data and on remotely
sensed rasters of any extent, from topical, regional and even global. The "Top-down" models try to
represent and plot the ecological behavior of the canopy as a whole, by using various environmental
constraints to estimate the actual status of the ecosystem functioning [9], [106]. The joint (PT-
JPL) ET and LUE GPP model, uses the same environmental constraints and has proved capable
to successfully simulate ET and GPP [9], [106]. Additionally, (PT-JPL) model has the highest
correlation with ﬂux tower observations, it does not require a lot of data and has consistent and
reliable performance over a range of land surface conditions [107].
More analytically, the ET part of this joint model derives from the Priestley and Taylor (1972)
equation for potential ET, and incorporates various environmental and biological variables to down-
regulate the potential value to the actual one [9]. This equation relates evapotranspiration to the
available energy, given by net radiation [68]. The model partitions latent heat ﬂux (LE) into three
components: 1) wet surface evaporation (Ei), 2) soil evaporation (Es) and 3) canopy transpira-
tion (Ec). The Priestley-Taylor evapotranspiration equation is being used in this joint (PT-JPL)
model, since it overcomes all the parameterization problems that Penman-Monteith equation has
(i.e. "challenge to characterize the spatial and temporal variation in surface conductances to water
vapor, without using ﬁeld calibration and the aerodynamic terms" [108]). The Priestley-Taylor
equation replaces the surface and aerodynamic resistances with an empirical multiplier aPT [108],
which most of the time equals to aPT=1.26. This multiplier is an empirical ratio of potential
evapotranspiration, to equilibrium potential evapotranspiration and it is dimensionless. Although
many studies have shown that the Priestley and Taylor approach leads to underestimations of the
ET in many locations, the structure of the equation, which is simple and not data demanding, is
a good alternative of the Penman-Monteith ET approach. [109], [110].

3.4
Model Calibration and Assessment
Calibration is essential for achieving consistency in an estimation. Regularly, calibration involves
establishing a relationship between instrument response and one or more reference values. Linear
regression is one of the most frequently used statistical techniques for calibration. Once the re-
lationship between an input value and the response value is established, (most of the times it is
represented by a straight line), the calibration model is then used backwards, in order to predict
a value from an instrument response.
In this study, linear regression was applied to predict and estimate longwave incoming radia-
tion (LWin), as a signiﬁcant gap of insitu LWin was observed for a long period of time in the
EC meteorological ﬁeld’s data. In addition, another reason why a linear regression calibration
method was applied in this study, was because high negative BIAS was spotted between calcu-
lated and Observed/Insitu LWin from the EC meteorological data. In statistics, the term BIAS of
an estimator, corresponds to the diﬀerence between this estimator’s expected value and the true
value of the parameter estimated. High negative BIAS means that the prediction is inaccurate and
tends to underestimate the parameter. More speciﬁcally, in order to apply the linear regression
method to the dataset of this study, a random sampling of 70% of the total model calculated LWin
measurements, were selected. The remaining 30% was used for validation. Moreover, the model’s
accuracy was assessed both on half-hourly (instantaneous) and on a daily time scale. Regarding
the daily time scale, average values were estimated if no more than 20% of the half-hourly values
were missing within one day. If a day had less observations than the required, then that day’s
values were excluded from the calculations.

Incorporating New Albedo Formula into the Model
Surface albedo is one of the most important properties of the underlying surface, which directly
aﬀects the earth’s climate [114]. Albedo is the reﬂection of the incoming energy back into outer
space, and with an increase in albedo, a higher proportion of the solar energy is reﬂected back to
space. Figure (Fig. 13), depicts and analyses what happens to the incoming energy ﬂuxes (radia-
tion) when they enter the Earth. Approximately 30% of the incoming energy ﬂuxes (radiation), is
reﬂected back into outer space, through three main processes: 1) Backscattering, the interaction
of the incoming energy/light with the gas molecules in the air, 2) Reﬂection from clouds, from the
water content (droplets) that is stored inside the clouds), and 3) Reﬂection from the Earth’s surface.

clouds, and reﬂection from the Earth’s surface. (Figure was derived from [10])

and modifying the albedo calculation formula, is likely to generate more accurate and robust ET

3.5.2
Parameter Optimization
Light use eﬃciency (LUE) is a key parameter in estimating gross primary productivity (GPP)
based on remote sensing data and model calculations. It describes the eﬃciency of the canopies
for absorbing solar energy [116].
However, maximum LUE (εmax) is speciﬁc for each type of
vegetation and varies seasonally. There are diﬀerent factors inﬂuencing LUE. At the leaf scale,
studies have found that the εmax is mainly related to chlorophyll content and leaf age [116]. At
the canopy scale, factors such as LAI and canopy structure are likely to inﬂuence εmax [116]. In
addition, a lot of studies have tried to calculate and deﬁne a speciﬁc maximum LUE (εmax) value
for the diﬀerent types of vegetation, but even for the same type of vegetation, many studies have
calculated diﬀerent values. Some characteristic cases are, for example, the case of soybean where
in [30] a value of 1.42-1.45 gC/MJ is given and in [43], for the same species, the εmax is equal
to 0.65-0.90 gC/MJ, almost 50% lower. Another example is, wheat vegetation, where in [43] it is
equal to 0.85 gC/MJ and in [117], it is 2.86 gC/MJ. In addition, for conifer forests, an εmax value
of 1.8 gC/MJ is suggested by [42], while in [46] for the same vegetation, a value of 2.453-2.553
gC/MJ is recommended. Other studies, such as [9] and [106], suggest a common and optimized
εmax value of 2.97 gC/MJ, for diﬀerent vegetation species (a willow bioenergy ﬁeld and a high
latitude temperate deciduous forest respectively).
Regarding maximum light use eﬃciency εmax for the sugarcane crops, [118] suggests a value of 2.28
gC/MJ. Other studies [119], estimate a range for possible εmax values ranging from 1.63 to 2.09
gC/MJ and 0.71 to 1.03 gC/MJ, under irrigated and rain-fed conditions respectively. Finally, [120]
and [121] propose a value of 1.7 gC/MJ and 2.0 gC/MJ respectively. These variations of εmax,
even for the same vegetation type, is an issue found very often within the scientiﬁc community.
For the above reasons, maximum light use eﬃciency εmax, will be further evaluated and optimized
in order to match the speciﬁc sugarcane vegetation characteristics of this study’s ﬁelds.
The Monte-Carlo simulation method will be used in order to optimize the maximum light use
eﬃciency εmax, with the root mean square error (RMSE) operating as an objective function. The
basis of the Monte-Carlo simulation is that the probability of diﬀerent outcomes cannot be resolved,
due to random variable interference. Hence, the Monte-Carlo simulation operates by constantly
applying and repeating random samples, in order to achieve the best possible result. A Monte
Carlo simulation takes a parameter (in this case εmax) and assigns random values to it. The GPP
model is then run, and the results of the objective function (RMSE) are provided. The process
is repeated again and again, assigning to εmax diﬀerent values. Then the εmax that generates
the lowest RMSE is selected. The (εmax) values in this study, were sampled 100,000 times with
uniform distribution within their corresponding range (1.63-4.9 gC/m2MJ) based on the above
studies. In addition, 70% of the total observations were used for calibration, while the remaining
30% for validation. The best ﬁt parameter value was then chosen.
Finally, in this study, the optimized maximum light use eﬃciency εmax was calculated for four
diﬀerent cases. The ﬁrst three cases tried to ﬁnd a speciﬁc and suitable εmax value, in order to ﬁt
each year (i.e. 2016, 2017, and 2018) separately. The fourth case takes into account only the years
2017 and 2018, as those are the only years that have observations available for the entire year.
For that, the 70% of the total observations were used for calibration, while the remaining 30% for
validation. 2016 was only used for validation for the fourth case, as it has very limited observations.
More speciﬁcally, data were only available for the time period 9/12/2016-31/12/2016.

3.6
Water Footprint
Freshwater resources are limited, and their demand is constantly growing. To get a better overview
of the environmental aspects of each water-consuming operation and to see how much water is al-
located to each of them, [122] introduced a new quantitative and qualitative indicator, namely
"Water Footprint". Water footprint is the total amount of freshwater resources needed for produc-
ing a ﬁnal product (i.e. sugar), considering both direct and indirect water consumption [122]. An
accepted method for estimating water footprint, is the method that divides the water-consuming
operation into 1) Blue, 2) Green and 3) Grey water footprint components. The "Blue" water foot-
print component refers to the amount of water (surface and groundwater) used in the production
process. At the same time, "Green" water footprint is the amount of water available by precip-
itation and soil moisture. Lastly, "Grey" water footprint component is the volume of freshwater
required to absorb a speciﬁc pollutant load, based on existing freshwater quality standards and
given natural background environmental concentrations [123].
Water footprint for any kind of
product or operation is always expressed as water volume per product unit (m3/t).
Eﬀorts of measuring water consumption per major crops (i.e sugarcane), especially in regions
facing potential severe droughts due to climate change (i.e. Costa-Rica), are necessary in order to
assess agricultural water use dynamics in light of the increasing water conﬂicts [124]. The main
objective of the water footprint assessment is that with this method is possible for someone to
understand how human activities are related to water scarcity and pollution issues. In addition,
water footprint provides the basic information necessary, in order to make an operation of any kind
more sustainable. [86]. In addition, WF is an indicator that contributes to the achievement of
SDG6 for sustainable water resources management, as it provides accurate knowledge about water
consumption, as well as its competing demands [125].
Furthermore, it raises awareness of the
scarce water resources and promotes and enhances water-related policies. Also, WF strengthens
and helps the creation of more resilient farming systems that achieve maximum water use eﬃciency.
Unfortunately, it is a very common phenomenon for farmers to use more water than what the crops
really need. WF assessment helps farmers understand what the actual crop water use is, and what
measures they can take to further improve and minimize water consumption (i.e. improving crop
irrigation techniques, organic mulching, etc). Finally, it is worth mentioning that WF is also an
economic water productivity indicator, as it is the reverse of water productivity [124]. Thus, higher
water consumption volumes correspond to lower proﬁts for the interested parties.
Water footprint of a crop describes the total water volume needed (i.e crop evapotranspiration
(ET)) in order to produce 1 ton of crop yield [124]. Sugarcane has generally larger WF than beet
sugar, and approximately 210 liters of water are needed in order to produce 1 kg of sugarcane
[126]. Most of the time, as also in this case, the sugarcane plantation is irrigated. The "Blue" WF
of sugarcane plantations, accounts for about 27% of the total crop water uses, which is a lot higher
than other crops [126]. Regarding Costa Rica, the total WF for the whole country is estimated to
be equal to 5900 m3/year, while the WF per capita is equal to 4100 litre/day [127]. Speciﬁcally
for the Guanacaste province, the water footprint for the sugarcane crops is equal to 139 m3/t for
the "Green", 57 m3/t for the "Blue", and 13 m3/t for the "Grey" components respectively. The
total WF of the sugarcane is estimated to be equal to 210 m3/t [128]. Similarly, other studies
focused on irrigated conditions found the average "Blue" WF for sugarcane in Tamazula (Mexico),
to be 104.9 m3/t. Generally, sugarcane WF can range between 120-410 m3/t [129], [123], [128],
depending on climatological and meteorological conditions. Finally, another reason that causes
variability in WF estimates, is the diﬀerences in the modeling approach, and the equations used
for estimating critical parameters (i.e. eﬀective precipitation, crop evapotranspiration, etc).

4.1.1
Model Calibration Results
With reference to chapter 3.4, linear regression calibration was applied to longwave incoming ra-
diation (LWin) of the year 2018, in order to increase the accuracy of the ET and GPP model,
by minimizing BIAS, RMSE and MAE. In the following ﬁgures and tables, the results before and
after the calibration are displayed.
As illustrated in ﬁgure (Fig.14), a linear regression equation was established between LWin ob-
served/insitu from the E.C. ﬂux tower and LWin calculated based on the Prata ([105]) approach.
This linear correlation equation was estimated based on a random sample of 70% of the total
observations. The equation is y=1.378x-108.25. The other 30% of the total observations was used
for validation. As can be seen from the tables (Tab. 4 - Tab. 5), the calibration managed to
decrease BIAS and RMSE from -52.20 W/m2 and 57.69 W/m2 to -13.53 W/m2 and 27.06 W/m2
respectively. This corresponds to a 74.08% decrease in BIAS and 53.09% in RMSE. MAE similarly
followed the same declining pattern. The initial value was 52.20, and it decreased by approximately
67.39 %. It is worth mentioning that while BIAS, MAE, and RMSE were signiﬁcantly lower after
the implementation of the linear regression calibration, the standard deviation of the calculated
LWin was slightly increased, from 18.65 to 25.57, which is approximately a 37% increase. This
means that the values are slightly more dispersed after the calibration, when compared to the
initial model.

As can be observed, the correlation of the daily observations is higher compared to instantaneous
observations by approximately 20%. RMSE, BIAS and MAE statistics (Tab. 15), for both instan-
taneous and daily observations are very low, indicating that both NDVI datasets are robust and
reliable.
The diﬀerence between daily and instantaneous NDVI correlation, is possibly linked to sensor’s
(UAS vs. EC) speciﬁc characteristics. Furthermore, other factors that may have contributed to
this outcome, are the surface’s speciﬁc characteristics, observation times and solar azimuth angle
position. These factors can increase the randomness and uncertainty of the NDVI measurements
[155].
Based on the above, and taking into account, the homogeneous conditions of the surface in which
the six ﬂight campaigns took place, observation time and solar azimuth angle position, we can
safely deduce that it was the sensor’s speciﬁc characteristics that aﬀected the correlation between
the instantaneous NDVI observations the most.
Although the sensor’s speciﬁc characteristics could have strongly inﬂuenced the instantaneous
data, the same may not true for the daily NDVI data. It is likely that the averaging process of the
daily NDVI data minimized the sensor’s eﬀects.

Long Term Model Analysis.
Validation on a Daily and Instanta-
neous Time Scales for the Years 2016, 2017 and 2018
4.3.1
Daily Time Scale
The outputs of ET (W/m2) and GPP (gC/m2d) of the joint Priestley - Taylor Jet Propulsion Lab-
oratory ET (PT-JPL) and LUE GPP model, were validated on both the daily, and instantaneous
time-scales, for the years 2016, 2017 and 2018. It is worth mentioning that for the El Viejo site
and for year 2016, the installed PAR sensor, turned out to be wrong sensor and was removed from
the site. For this reason, short-wave incoming (SWin) radiation was used for calculating PAR for
2016. In addition, only December was available for analysis from 2016’s dataset. Furthermore, for
summer 2017, a re-calibration of the PAR sensor took place, since considerable underestimations
were detected. An analysis for 2015 was not possible, as necessary data such as NDVI were missing
from the dataset.
Plots and scatter plots of the calculated times series and their validation for the daily time scale,
are illustrated in the following ﬁgures (Fig. 26 - 29). In addition, in tables (Tab.16 - 17), ﬁt-
ting statistics are accompanying the aforementioned plots. The mean absolute percentage error
(MAPE) for the daily GPP (gC/m2d) for years 2016, 2017 and 2018 was equal to 32%, 28% and
20% respectively. At the same time MAPE for the daily ET (W/m2) for years 2016, 2017 and
2018, was equal to 29%, 37% and 22% respectively. The above statistics indicate that the joint
Priestley-Taylor ET and LUE GPP model performs satisfactory when calculating GPP and ET,
while capturing suﬃcient variations over a longer period of time.
Figure (Fig.
26), and more speciﬁcally graph A, presents GPP validation for December 2016.
The graph indicates that the joint model for this year, seems to accurately identify and record
most variations. Nevertheless, in order to make a reliable comparison between the calculated and
observed GPP values, as well as, to draw a ﬁnal conclusion for 2016, year-round recordings are
necessary. Also, it is important to highlight that in the period 2015-2016, Costa Rica experienced
a severe drought, due to El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) weather pattern. Actually, the
2015-2016 El Niño weather event, was one of the strongest on record, with a 3.4 Ocean Niño In-
dex (oC). This weather event, is likely to have caused a decrease in the overall GPP, due to the
reduced precipitation and high temperatures (oC). However, since no complete picture is available
for the whole 2016, it is not possible to detect a clear GPP pattern, neither to conclude something
about the speciﬁc year. Graph B corresponds to year 2017. Also for this year, the model seems
to suﬃciently illustrate GPP variations. The time gaps for this year, are due to sensor’s recording
failure. Finally graph C, corresponds to 2018. As can be seen, in the period 1/1/2018-8/2/2018,
there is a noticeable underestimation in the model’s performance. This underestimation is caused
from the limited available data, due to the malfunction of the insitu sensors.
Furthermore, both 2017 and 2018 have higher GPP values during the period May-July.

As mentioned above, a lot of studies suggest that εmax is speciﬁc for each type of vegetation. From
the above analysis, it turns out that even though the study was carried out in the same sugarcane
ﬁeld, εmax did not remain constant for all three years. On contrary, for the years 2018, 2017
and 2016 the Monte-Carlo simulation estimated an optimized value equal to εmax=4.2140 gC/MJ,
εmax= 3.4216 gC/MJ and εmax= 2.999 gC/MJ. In order to investigate this notable diﬀerentiation
in εmax, it was decided to assess two diﬀerent cases. The ﬁrst case includes the years 2016, 2017,
and 2018 only for the period 9/12 till 31/12, since that was the only time-period that data were
available for 2016. The second case includes the entire years 2017 and 2018.
For the ﬁrst case, in the ﬁgure (Fig. 37), it can be seen that 2016 has in general higher GPP
values compared to 2017 and 2018. At the same time, according to [158], the month of December
for the years 2016, 2017, and 2018 had 20, 28, and 30 sunny days and was approximately 20%,
12% and 6% cloudy respectively. Hence, a pattern is observed. When there is less sunshine and
a higher percentage of cloudiness, higher GPP values are detected. This observation also come to
an agreement with what [106] and [159] have also observed.
Figure 37: Comparison of the GPP (gC/m2d) for the three years 2016, 2017, and 2018 and the
speciﬁc period (9/12-31/12).
More speciﬁcally, under cloudy days an increase in the plant’s carbon absorption rates is de-
tected. This increase is inextricably linked with diﬀuse photosynthetic active radiation (PAR).
On cloudy days, there is an increase in longwave radiation emissions, due to the backscattering
eﬀects. Backscattering is deﬁned as the phenomenon that occurs when radiation is scattered from
72
==PAGE:74==
Towards Sustainable Farming
Technical University of Denmark
the original direction of motion, at angles greater than 90◦. This type of radiation (diﬀPAR) "pen-
etrates deeper into the canopies than direct PAR, thus the vertical distribution of PAR is more
homogeneous throughout the sugarcane canopies" [106]. In addition, under sunny days usually
direct PAR occurs. As a result, the leaves at the top of the canopy directly exposed to sun, to
be saturated, whereas the photosynthetic capacity of the shaded leaves to be constrained, due
to limited intercepted radiation. On the contrary under cloudy conditions, the aforementioned
saturation eﬀects of the sunlit leaves are less pronounced. Thus, the eﬃciency of photosynthesis,
GPP, and LUE are likely to be increased under cloudy conditions [106], [159].
In the second case, the annual comparison between 2017 and 2018, a similar pattern with the
above case is also observed. In ﬁgure (Fig. 38), the GPP of the year 2018 is higher compared
to 2017. At the same time, Figures A and B (Fig. 39) show that the precipitation of 2018 was
higher and more intense for almost the whole year compared to 2017. Also, the vapor pressure
deﬁcit (VPD) of 2017 is greater than 2018 for approximately the whole year. Studies [106], [159],
and [160] have reported that intensive stomatal control due to higher levels of VPD can lead to a
decreasing GPP and LUE. In addition, precipitation was more frequent in 2018 than 2017. This
most likely means that the cloud cover of 2018, should be larger than the 2017 one. Thus, both
precipitation and VPD explain why εmax value for the year 2018 is higher compared to 2017.
The increase of diﬀuse PAR (diﬀPAR) due to cloudiness and low VPD levels, has been reported to
signiﬁcantly aﬀect LUE and carbon absorption in terrestrial ecosystems.

Water Footprint (WF) for the El Viejo Sugarcane Commercial Farm
As mentioned in the Materials and Methods chapter (Ch. 3.6) the water footprint (WF) indicator
is a new and accurate way for assessing global water scarcity. Figure (Fig. 40) and the tables (Tab.
25-27) present the estimated WF for three diﬀerent cases. In the ﬁrst case, crop evapotranspiration
(ETc) was calculated based on the FAO56 Penman-Monteith method (Eq. 55), a recommended
approach for calculating ETc based on [86]. In the second case, ETc was calculated based on the
joint PT-JPL ET and LUE GPP model. Finally, the third case uses ETc, which was measured from
a state-of-art E.C. ﬂux tower. It was observed that in the ﬁrst case, and for all the three methods
used for estimating eﬀective precipitation (Peﬀ), the ﬁnal WF was noticeable larger compared to
the other two cases, by 37% for the second (PT-JPL model) and by 22% for the third (Insitu E.C)
case respectively. It is worth mentioning that despite this aforementioned diﬀerence between the
calculated WF, all WF come to an agreement with other similar sugarcane studies [123], [129], and
[128].
Furthermore, for all three cases the "Blue" WF component prevails over "Green", with a per-
centage ranging between 54-65% for "Βlue" and 35-46% for "Green" component respectively (Fig.
40). More analytically, the "Blue" WF component ranges between 83.04-134.37 m3/t, while the
"Green" ranges between 55.97-83.41 m3/t. This is something to be expected, given that the ma-
jority of sugarcane crops in the Guanacaste region use mainly "blue" water based on the irrigation
systems from the Tempisque River and the Arenal-Tempisque irrigation district [161]. In addition,
the El Viejo sugarcane farm uses irrigation throughout the year to maintain the necessary moisture
and water demands for the growth of the sugarcane crops. Also, sugarcane crops have one of the
longest growing periods and their growth can stop completely if there is a shortage of water. Thus,
the ﬁeld could become unharvestable, which can also cause food shortages and consequently aﬀect
the local economy.
Furthermore, sugarcane crops require approximately 1500-3000 liter of water in order to produce
one kilo of sugarcane [162]. In addition, as sugarcane crops rely mainly on "Blue" water, their
water consumption is possible to trigger water conﬂicts between other sectors and ecosystems that
also rely on "Blue" water during the dry season. In combination with the frequent El Niño weather
events and the fact that the current farm is located in one of the hottest regions of Costa Rica
[88], makes the WF estimations necessary for these sugarcane ﬁelds.

As already mentioned above, the Penman-Monteith ET case generates higher values of "Blue",
"Green" and "Total" WF compared to the other two cases. This may be due to the fact that, for
calculating ETc in the case of Penman-Monteith, a simple multiplication between reference evapo-
transpiration (ETo) and crop coeﬃcient (Kc) is performed. This process may not be representative
of the existing conditions. Τhis is because ETo is a variable that represents the evaporation rates of
a hypothetical small, green grass surface. Meanwhile, Kc, whose values derive from FAO look-up
tables, is trying to up-scale these hypothetical conditions into actual values with a simple and
rough multiplication. Moreover, even if these Kc values are generally representative of sugarcane
crops, they may not be representative of the speciﬁc sugarcane cultivars. In addition, Kc values are
aﬀected by additional local factors, which are not taken into account in these rough Kc estimates.
Kc depends on the applied irrigation techniques and their frequency, the climate and weather con-
ditions, the soil’s characteristics as well as the agricultural and agronomic practices (i.e. fertilizer
management, preparation of seeds for sowing, planting and harvesting, etc) [163]. In addition,
the fact that diﬀerent Kc values have been set very broadly for each growing stage, may also not
be representative of the actual evapotranspiration conditions of these stages. Hence, Kc values
derived from FAO look-up tables, as well as from other literature resources, can diﬀer signiﬁcantly
from the actual ones, if the local climate conditions and management practices, vary from those
that these "look-up" Kc values were obtained from [163]. On the contrary, in the other two ET
cases, for all the methods used to estimate eﬀective precipitation (Peﬀ), the ﬁnal WF estimates
was approximately the same.
Furthermore, it is worth mentioning that the "Green" WF values for the ﬁrst method (Inclination
method), used for calculating Peﬀ(Eq. 59 -60) and for all the three ET cases, are always lower
compared to the other two methods (USDA-SCS and CROPWAT-FAO). For the ﬁrst ET case
(Penman-Monteith), when we compared the ﬁrst Peﬀmethod with the other two, an overestima-
tion by 5% was observed for the "Blue" component. On the contrary, a noticeable underestimation
is observed in the "Green" component for the ﬁrst method when compared to other two Peﬀmeth-
ods, by 9.4% and 8% respectively.
The same patterns are also observed in the other two ET
cases. More analytically, for the 2nd ET case (Priestley-Taylor), the "Blue" component of the
ﬁrst Peﬀmethod (Inclination method) was overestimated by 7% and 12% against the other two
methods respectively. Similarly, the "Green" component was underestimated by 11.83% and 20%
compared to 2nd (USDA-SCS) and 3rd (CROPWAT-FAO) method respectively. Finally, for the
third ET case (E.C. ﬂux tower), the "Blue" WF component, of the 1st (Inclination method) was
overestimated by 7% and 10% compared to 2nd (USDA-SCS) and 3rd (CROPWAT-FAO) method
respectively. This diﬀerentiation between the Peﬀmethods is due to the fact that in the 1st Peﬀ
method (Inclination method), (Brouwer and Heibloem et al, 1986), only the monthly precipitation
was taken into account. Also, this method is applicable only when the total slope of the location
is not greater than 5%. On the contrary, the other two Peﬀmethods take into account additional
basic parameters for determining Peﬀ. These parameters are for example ET, irrigation needs, soil
properties, and consumptive water use. Therefore, seeing how Peﬀaﬀects the two WF components,
in order to have more accurate WF estimates, the Peﬀequation should be adapted to the local
conditions each time [138]. The following ﬁgure illustrates all WF components, for all three ET

In addition to the above estimates, another parameter that was examined in this study was the
water use eﬃciency (WUE). WUE as mentioned above, is the ratio of the crop (biomass) produced
to the total actual evapotranspiration. It worth mentioning that both WUE and WF are two
indicators, that assess the broader water use eﬃciency concept in two diﬀerent ways. WUE is an
indicator that usually assesses the eﬀectiveness of crop water use in a unit of agricultural produc-
tion, without taking into account the eﬀects of this water use on the environment [38]. On the
contrary, WF assesses not only the use of water required for the production of a particular product,
but also examines the environmental impacts associated with the entire agricultural production
systems [38].
Moreover, it is important to highlight that WF is calculated based on the crop yield. Thus, a
direct comparison cannot be made between WUE and WF in this study.
Figure (Fig. 41) shows the WUE indicator, based on the GPP and ET values calculated from
the joint PT-JPL model, as well as the WUE calculated from the insitu EC ﬂux tower, for the year
2018. As can be seen, both cases are well matched with the coeﬃcient of determination equal to
R2=0.50. Also, BIAS and RMSE are equal to 0.11 and 0.42 gC/kg of H2O respectively.
In this study, WUE ranges between 0.35-3.33 gC/kg of H2O. Other studies conducted on irri-
gated sugarcane ﬁelds, estimated WUE to range between 1.2-3.6 gC/kg of H2O [164], [165] and
[166]. At the same time [9] and [106] mentioned that WUE is mainly aﬀected by GPP. From this
analysis, it is concluded that WUE in this study has great potential for improvement. This im-
provement can be achieved either by optimizing the used irrigation systems or by selecting speciﬁc
sugarcane cultivars that regulate more eﬀectively the ET.

Furthermore, a lot of WF studies exclude the grey water footprint component from their esti-
mations.
This is due to a lack of quality measurement standards in the speciﬁc regions.
In
addition, "Grey" WF is excluded due to diﬃculties to fully understand the environment’s ability
to assimilate a certain quantity of pollution load, and ﬁnally due to diﬃculties to acquire actual

be a remarkably high amount considering that only nitrate fertilizers were included in the corre-
sponding estimations.
Finally, with the increasing demand for sustainable water resources management and agricultural
productivity, WF and WUE are pivotal water indicators, since they provide a better picture of
where and when the current levels of water-use are likely to cause water shortages and ecological
harm in the region. Reliable information for the crop growth, WUE and WF are necessary in order
to optimize the currently used irrigation systems and irrigation scheduling throughout the growing
cycles.

5
Conclusions
The main objective of this thesis was to demonstrate the value of remote sensing based models when
estimating water footprint (WF) and water use eﬃciency (WUE) for a commercial sugarcane farm
in El Viejo, Costa Rica. A remote based evapotranspiration (ET) and gross primary productivity
(GPP) model was used, the Priestley-Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory (PT-JPL) model. This
model was improved by introducing a new albedo formula and calibrating the longwave incoming
radiation (LWin) parameter. In addition, an optimization was carried out for the maximum light
use eﬃciency parameter (εmax). The model was validated using both UAS images, as well as,
micrometeorological data derived from the insitu Eddy Covariance (EC) ﬂux tower for the period
2016-2018.
The model’s performance for assessing both type’s of data was high.
For the remote sensed
UAS images on the instantaneous time-scale, GPP was ranging between GPP=11-40 gC/m2s,
with RMSE=3.37 gC/m2s and MAPE=14%. The instantaneous ET ranged between ET=178-
418 W/m2, with RMSE=39.52 W/m2 and MAPE=7%.
On the daily time-scale, GPP varied
between GPP=4.87-8.39 gC/m2d, with RMSE=0.91 gC/m2d and MAPE=7.8%, while ET was
ranged between ET=53.75-96.66 W/m2, with RMSE=27.27 W/m2 and MAPE=23.7%. As far as
the long-term analysis was concerned, the model performed satisfactory in predicting GPP and
ET. In the daily time-scale, the GPP pooled standard error was equal to Perror=0.36 gC/m2d,
while the RMSEs ranged between RMSE=1.52-2.06 gC/m2d, with the lowest value spotted on
2018. The daily ET pooled standard error was equal to Perror=3.84 W/m2 and the RMSEs varied
between RMSE=26.17-46 W/m2. On the instantaneous long-term time scale, the model showed
improved performance compared to daily time-scale. More speciﬁcally, for instantaneous GPP,
the pooled standard error was equal to Perror=0.42 gC/m2s, and RMSEs was between 4.44-6.14
gC/m2s. The instantaneous ET had a pooled standard error equal to Perror= 8.22 W/m2 and
the RMSEs ranged between 90.59-141.99 W/m2. The higher RMSEs in the instantaneous ET
measurements were due to greater variations of the parameter’s values during the diurnal 24 hours
cycle. The improved model’s performance in this time-scale, can further support precision manage-
ment practices, since these estimates give real-time information about the crop’s requirements for
water and nutrients. In addition, the linear regression calibration applied on the LWin parameter,
managed to decrease BIAS and RMSE by 74.08% and 53.09% respectively. The optimization of
the εmax parameter, signiﬁcantly aﬀected the GPP model’s performance, by reducing the RMSE
by 64%, 28% and 1.31% for 2016, 2017 and 2018 respectively. This led to the conclusion that even
though εmax is speciﬁc for each type of vegetation, it is also aﬀected by other environmental and
biophysical factors. One of these factors is diﬀuse PAR. Diﬀuse PAR and increased aerosols, proved
to have a positive eﬀect on GPP and εmax. On the other hand, increasing vapor pressure deﬁcit
(VPD) is likely to hinder the GPP and LUE model’s performance due to intensive stomatal control.
Another major aspect of this thesis, was the assessment of WF and WUE indicators, calcu-
lated from remote estimates of GPP and ET. WF in this study were estimated for three diﬀerent
cases. In the ﬁrst case, crop evapotranspiration (ETc) was calculated based on the FAO Penman-
Monteith method. In the second, ETc was calculated from the joint (PT-JPL) model. Finally,
in the third case, ETc was calculated from the insitu EC ﬂux tower data. The remote sensing
based WF=152.94 m3/t was closer to the benchmark WF=171.23 m3/t from EC ﬂux tower, when
compared to the WF=210.62 m3/t based on (Hoekstra et al., 2011), which overestimated WF
by 23%. These ﬁndings pinpoint that although the methodology recommended by (Hoekstra et
al., 2011) [86] gives a rough WF estimate of a speciﬁc area, in order to have more accurate and
realistic WF measurements, empirical crop ETc estimates are required. Additionally, the joint
(PT-JPL) model suﬃciently represented the daily variations of WUE, with RMSE=0.42 gC/kg of
H2O and MAPE=21.64%. The annual average WUE ranged between 1.70-1.80 gC/kg of H2O year.
Concluding, the above results demonstrate that the improved remote sensing based ET and LUE
GPP model, was able to accurately predict GPP, ET, WF and WUE at the farm level. The model
was validated and proved capable for capturing both long-term and instantaneous variations. Fi-
nally, the accuracy of these measurements can be further improved using UAS. The use of UAS,
could prove an extremely valuable tool for improved and sustainable water resources management,
as they provide very precise estimates at very high resolution.

Future Work and Recommendations
Based on the above analysis and guided by the need for more accurate results, an additional recom-
mendation for improvement of the joint Priestley-Taylor Jet Propulsion Laboratory ET (PT-JPL)
and LUE GPP model, is the cloudiness index (CI). The CI is also recommended by [106]. This
index is a good proxy of diﬀuse PAR in order to assess the impacts of diﬀuse PAR on the GPP
and ET.
Another possible improvement to the current model is the replacement of the current Leaf Area
Index (LAI) equation, which is currently based on the NDVI, with an improved one based on
reﬂectances derived from the green and red-edge regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. This
is also recommended by [167] and [168], since the NDVI is insensitive to high values of LAI. On
the other hand, by using reﬂectances from the new recommended spectral regions, the saturation
problem of NDVI can be solved. In these spectral regions, the absorption coeﬃcients of chlorophyll
are much smaller compared to the red region.
Finally, regarding WF, in order to have more accurate and representative results from the speciﬁc
sugarcane ﬁelds, it is important to know the exact crop yields, as well as, the amount and type
of fertilizers used. Concluding, when calculating the WF indicator, it is very important to adapt
eﬀective precipitation (Peﬀ), as well as ETc equations to existing environmental conditions.
