Abstract  
This project started because Børneriget wanted to look closer into which logistic options there 
are available to make the carts that supports the medical staff to treat the patient at the new 
children hospital BørneRiget lean.  
BørneRiget is being taken into service in 2024, and due to this the workflows and logistic 
decision are still on a vague level; therefore, this thesis became more a proof of concept rather 
than giving a concrete answer.   
In this thesis, there is first investigated what there exist of current literature within logistic, 
lean and simulation in healthcare and the area in-between them. There was here discovered 
that the keywords themselves didn’t reveal many articles related to the issue in this thesis, but 
the area in-between consisted of several articles that where beneficial.  Then a computer 
simulation software “AnyLogic” has been used to get a flexible decision system, that takes the 
current patient distribution into consideration and the medicine burden and output the 
number of carts they need to service these patients. The simulation is based on discrete event 
modelling, and an aggregated flow of the patient flow. In the thesis there are presented three 
different approach to the problem where the third solution is implemented. The solution is 
using a reverse analogy where carts, modelled as liquid tanks, increases when a patient get in 
contact with a doctor. The project ended well, with tons of possibilities for further work with 
the simulation. One reflection is that it would have been beneficial for the project if there had 
been weekly status meetings with the BørneRiget department.

Introduction 
 
 
This report together with a simulation is the product of a collaboration between a student 
supervised by an associate professor from the Management Department, and Rigshospitalet in 
shape of the Department of Quality and Data at Rigshospitalet (FA) and BørneRiget. This thesis 
is taking a closer look on the minimum number of medical carts needed in relation to the new 
children hospital that is set to be finished in 2024.  
This report is based on the following parts:  
• Theory and method section 
Here the current theory behind three essential subjects relating to this thesis is being 
investigated. These subjects are simulation, logistic and lean with a healthcare perspective. The 
process of procuring the simulation and report will also be briefly discussed in the methods 
section.   
• Analysis 
In the analysis section the product is being made. It is presented how data is gathered and 
processed, as well as how it was possible to make a working simulation of the outpatient clinic.  
• Results, discussion and conclusion  
In these summarizing chapters, the findings of the analysis are presented and discussed. The 
simulation flexibility, assumptions and limitations are discussed. Then there is an extended 
perspectivation chapter, which reveals the different options for further work and the actions 
necessary before the model can be used in the real world. These options are ranked according 
to how important they are, and how easy they will be to implement.   
While this thesis is relevant for Rigshospitalet to decide how many carts there are needed in 
relation to the distribution of medicine, it is not its only use. The report and simulation is a 
guideline for how other optimization flows deciding on equipment needed can be determined. 
The thesis contains a simulation model that uses techniques within the software “AnyLogic”. 
The knowledge behind this software is limited, but since it is a free simulation software this 
project also becomes relevant in terms of simulation use for universities. This simulation uses 
two of the libraries that are available in AnyLogic, and while the process library is the most 
commonly used, the use of the fluid library is a bit unorthodox and reveals a new way of using 
this library. The use of AnyLogic will be described further in the method and analysis chapter.  
The following chapters will go in depth with the project background and describe how this 
results in a problem statement.

Organisation description 
Rigshospitalet (RH), is Denmark’s’ leading hospital for patients who needs highly specialised 
treatment. It is placed in the centre of Copenhagen, surrounded by The University of 
Copenhagen, Fælledparken and Panum. In this thesis, the collaboration partners from RH are 
the BørneRiget department (BD) and Department of Quality and Data (FA).  
Juliane Marie Centeret (JMC), is the current building which operates as the children hospital. It 
has some of the leading specialist within children treatment but are currently limited by 
physical space for further development. Therefore, RH has decided to create a new children’s 
hospital called BørneRiget. They aim to build a hospital that equally focuses on treatment, 
education and research, as well as maintaining that they are state of the art. The need for 
patient treatment can change over time, which is why it is important that the project contains 
a high precision in relation to patient treatment, while still maintaining a high flexibility in the 
project. RH vision for the BørneRiget building project includes the following statement which 
reflects the focus on the project: “We want to invent and introduce new patient care. We want 
the specialist to come to the patient instead of the other way around. We want to introduce 
the hospital to the world.” (Rigshospitalet, Vision og Principper for Verdens Bedste 
Børnehospital, 2018)   
The action plan that the BD formulated to achieve this vision is where this thesis is introduced. 
To achieve the vision, BD want to rethink the patient process flow, and what the patient 
distribution looks like. One of the areas that are affected by this is the medical carts the 
doctors use to distribute the medicine between the patients in the different rooms on the 
different floors, and therefore the BD want to investigate how the cart relates to the patient 
distribution.  
 
The Department of Quality and Data (FA), works with process optimization and Lean at 
Rigshospitalet. It supports the clinical staff at Rigshospitalet and its primary work is to ensure 
quality, patient safety, avoid waste of any kind, optimize work processes and ensure 
consistency across the hospital, in a way such as the patient experience a coherent process 
(Rigshospitalet, n.d.).   
 
The activity in the outpatient clinic is constantly growing because it is expensive to hospitalize 
people, and the government has a strong focus on not bedding the patients. This is most likely 
one of the reasons there are a focus on making the flow as smooth as possible as the patient

Problem area 
BørneRiget are going to be built with five specific design principals which are as follows: 
Integrated play; Designed for everyday life; See me, ask me, let me; The good journey; Clearly 
defined zones. One of these principals, “The good journey”, that like the other initiatives 
require some changes in the logistics to create as smooth patient journey as possible, and 
where this thesis is interesting.  
This change of logistics, includes a major change where the outpatient clinics will be shared 
between departments. This also means that there are change of practise, where instead of the 
patient going to the health-care personnel, the health-care personnel are going to the patient. 
This behaviour RH choose to call pitstops. It will change the patient distribution, and hereby 
also the medicine flow and the patient flow.  
With the thought of a pitstop, Rigshospitalet lunched a project considering the number of carts 
there are needed to treat a given patient distribution. Not necessarily to solve the issues that 
arise when considering pitstop, but to create a simulation that considers the connection 
between patient, doctors, carts and medicine use for further implementation of the pitstop 
thought.  
Each doctor is presumed to visit the patient with a cart. BD would like to create a universal cart 
that can be used by all specialists. Each specialist is presumed to have one out of five drawers 
for special professional use and one drawer all the specialist takes from. With an analysis of the 
patient data the flow of the workload on the different specialist will emerge. Based on 
interviews with BD, assumptions on how much medical equipment is needed for each patient 
has been made. With this information, an aggregated simulation of the everyday at the 
outpatient clinic will be made to make it possible for the user to gain more insight in how many 
medical carts there are needed. The solution needs to be precise, but as the rest of BørneRiget, 
it also needs to be flexible for changes in the patient distribution and treatment, for instance 
the thought of pitstop. The following problem statement emerged from this.

Problem statement 
How can simulation be applied to determine the minimum need for medical carts at an 
outpatient clinic for treating incoming patients with alternating needs? 
 
 Assumptions and limitations  
Simulation will always be a model of the reality and therefore by definition never 100 percent 
true to reality. Since the new building that the children’s hospital is moving into is not yet 
functional, the results from this thesis cannot be tested in real life and therefore the results can 
only be used as indications. To solve this assignment, the first issue is to scope it, which is done 
by creating assumptions.  
 
• In the current flow at the outpatient clinic, about 80 % of the medicaments and utensils 
are one-time use (Bille, 2018). For the simplicity of the project, it is decided to model as 
if everything that is available at the cart is one-time use. If the solution presented in this 
thesis is implemented it is relevant to consider adding a line from Sterilization Center to 
the central storage at the outpatient clinic. This would not however impact more on the 
process flow at BørneRiget, and does not impact the medicine use either.   
 
• In this assignment, the number of patients pr. drawer will be determined the following 
way: The workload of the different doctor’s specializing’s medicine use will be based on 
interview with BD where they will give a number from 1-3, where 3 is the heaviest 
workload. The cart can contain X number of patients based on this workload. This 
assumption will do that the precise number of carts needed cannot be calculated. 
Rigshospitalet does not have precise data on their medicine need pr. patient. In the 
discussion section it is debated how one can gain this knowledge without counting for 
every single patient. This discussion includes an investigation into the American 
hospitals and if this would have been beneficial for the project. BD was consulted when 
the assumptions were made, and are satisfied with this assumption.  
 
• The medicine/medical equipment is always available at the central storage at the floor. 
Given the project limits to the outpatient clinic flow, it will be presumed that the supply 
chain from supplier up until the outpatient floor works well and there is always the 
stock needed to fill the carts.  
==PAGE:13==
Page 12 of 76 
 
 
•  The outpatient clinic can be used efficient seven hours pr. day, 245 days a year 
(Rigshospitalet, Vision og Principper for Verdens Bedste Børnehospital, 2018). This is 
normal outpatient clinic opening hours.  
 
• While the outpatient clinic consists of many departments, this thesis will only be looking 
at three departments which will make the basis of how to handle the rest. The goal with 
this thesis is to find out how what the number of carts are based on, and not the exact 
number of carts needed.   
 
• Since BørneRiget is not able to provide specific patient data, there will later in the thesis 
be developed presumed arrival patterns. These arrival patterns, are discussed with 
Christian Sørup to ensure the assumption is not too far away from reality and are based 
on the aggregated patient data given.   
 
• Most of the resources in this project are assumed in this project. This includes number 
of doctors, nurses, carts available, number of operation rooms available, number of 
seats in reception. This is because BørneRiget is still in the early stages of their process. 
The first sod is yet to be made, and BørneRiget does not have a lot of decisions 
determined yet.  
 
• The process blocks are assumed from an aggregated patient flow.  
 
• The process times are also assumed, based on the knowledge that a patient visit takes 
approximately 45 minutes.  
 
It is important to understand that the case treated in this thesis is a proof of concept, and the 
development of Børneriget is in an early stage and BD can therefore not give an exact answer 
to much of the flow. The goal is therefore not to provide a number for them, but to provide a 
“plug and play” solution, where they afterwards, when they have the exact numbers, can plug 
it in and get a result.

Simulation modelling 
To solve the case given by Rigshospitalet, a model was needed. A model is an imitation of the 
real world. It is important to have in mind that a simulation is never 100 % true to the real 
world, but it can give indications on how the processes can work. Even with the most 
complicated model, it can never be more complex than the real life, since factors that are 
considered irrelevant for the system is chosen not to be included in the model. Choosing 
simulation over experimenting with real objects is often done because it can be too expensive 
or impossible to carry out the real-life experiment. The last argument is one of the reasons in 
our case with BørneRiget since the building yet not stand. Once the model is build, there comes 
an understanding of the original system, and one can test how the system acts under various 
conditions and the system can be optimized (Borshchev, 2013). Simulation is often done by one 
of the two following methods: 
 
• One of the most popular simulation software’s in companies is Excel. It is based on 
formulas and scripts and with different add-ons allow you to perform Monte Carlo or 
optimization experiments. This has its advantages in being simple and less time-
consuming compared to other programmes. However, it comes short, when handling 
time and causal dependencies, large number of parameters, non-intuitive influences 
between variables, or non-linear behaviour. 
Monte Carlo Simulation is a mathematical technique that generate random variables for 
uncertainty in a system. It is never deterministic, but can give an approximation of 
reality (The Economic Times, n.d.).  
An example where Monte Carlo can be used is within gambling; roulette for instance. 
Watching from the casinos perspective, they would like to know how likely people are 
to win. With 100 spins, it is clear there is a lot of variance, and the model cannot give a 
clear answer. Using the Monte Carlo method, it is possible to make e.g. a million 
permutations/simulations, after which the results become much clearer.  
 
==PAGE:27==
Page 26 of 76 
 
• Another modelling type is simulation modelling. Simulation modelling is done with 
software tools that typically require some learning, but is then easier to use than for 
instance creating a model in Excel. Simulation models are also a better tool than an 
Excel spreadsheet when it comes to convincing people about the result, because people 
can relate to the models more easily.  
 
Since BørneRiget is not yet build, it is presumed to work with the current processes there are 
available at the outpatient clinic at the children hospital, and the data the simulation can be 
built on is historical. The model build in this project is using three different patient 
distributions, it consists of large number of parameters, and stochastic variable is introduced to 
create variability in the medicine flow. Because of the large number of parameters, and the 
fact that it is important there is an alignment on the hospital flow, simulation modelling is the 
right choice in this assignment. It is easier for the hospital to build further upon a model 
created in simulation than a model created in excel.  
 
There are many kinds of simulation software tools out there. One of these tools are AnyLogic, 
which has the advantage of having tools that are free to use. It also has an extra focus on 
healthcare, which in our case is highly relevant. It is based on Java, and if the simulation lacks 
options, a Java code can often be implemented in the software as workaround.  
To make the simulation, it was first decided which kind of simulation method there was 
needed.

System dynamics 
System dynamics is a method created in the 1950s by MIT professor Jay Forrester (Tang & 
Vijay, 2001). System dynamics are mainly based on feedback loops, also known as circular 
causality, in the system. The range of systems this fits includes business, urban, social and 
ecological types of systems. System dynamic has, as seen in the diagram, a relative high 
abstraction level, which is expressed in the programming language of the method. The 
language is primitive compared to the other methods, but is full of notions that does not have 
measurable equivalents in the world. For instance, if one where to consider the impact of 
advertising in business system. This makes it far more difficult to thinking straightforward task 
as process flowchart and agent behaviour. The level of detail is usually not high, but the focus is 
instead on the system relation. There has been made a simple example of the use of system 
dynamics is in figure 9. The population size of deer is decided by a birth fraction and a lifespan. 
These objects are decided by a couple of equations. Given a start population of 100, a birth

Agent based modelling 
Agent based modelling is a relatively ned method, that before the early 2000s was an academic 
topic. The adoption of the modelling method by simulation practitioners were mainly due to 
advances in the object-oriented modelling, UML and state charts development (Borshchev, 
2013). The rapid growth of CPU power and memory were also a reason, because agent based 
models are more demanding in terms of computer power.

With agent based modelling, one might not know how the system elements interact with each 
other, but has information about the objects in the system behave individually. An agent can 
be many things; vehicles, people, organization, non-material things etc. The global behaviour of 
the system then emerges out of many concurrent individual behaviours.

Discrete event 
In discrete event modelling, the system is being modelled as a process, a sequence of 
operations being performed across entities (Borshchev, 2013). Flowcharts usually begin with a 
source that generates entities in flow, and ends with a sink block that remove the entities from

system. In-between, a set of operations is being made on the entities. These operations could 
be queueing, splitting, combining, service of resources etc. When entities must compete for 
resources, i.e. there are fewer resources than entities, a que is automatically made. Entities 
and service times are often stochastics drawn from a probability distribution and therefore, 
most discrete models are stochastic themselves. The output of these models could for instance 
be within utilization, bottlenecks and system throughput.  
An example of a discrete event can be seen below where a simple version of a submarket 
grocery shopping trip is shown. In these simple cases, if the resource they need is grabbed by 
another customer they que and wait for it to return. The same happens at the cashier; they 
make a que. In real-life, it would make sense to make a workaround the first que, and to 
minimize the second bottleneck.

However, it is much more straightforward to use discrete event simulation since we know the

AnyLogic 
AnyLogic is a tool that can handle multimethod, tackling complex challenges and gain a deeper 
insight into the independent processes that operates within (AnyLogic, n.d.)  
The software origins in 2000 and is based on an object-oriented approach, elements of UML 
standard, GUI and Java. It includes a graphical interface, and to some extend Java-code can be 
implemented directly. It is based on different libraries, that for instance includes a process 
model library, Pedestrian library, Rail library and fluid library.  
AnyLogic has a large library that supports discrete events, and metrics such as utilization rates, 
the time patients are in the clinic, and waiting-time. It also has a user-friendly interface, 
allowing people with no experience in the program to experiment and change input 
parameters in the model. It also has other libraries that can interact with the discrete library, 
making the item utilization within the medicine cart even more clear. The downside of

Evaluation on simulation modelling and drawbacks   
As mentioned in the problem statement, Rigshospitalet wanted to enlighten the minimal 
number of medical carts needed. With this simulation one can chose how the cart should be 
filled with medicine. One can see at a given time how many carts is needed to treat a number 
of patients based on the previous chosen allocation. Furthermore, one can see how utilization 
on the cart and the medical personal change through the simulation. To provide more details 
on change routine and the number of carts they need to buy, one would need more 
information on the space available for carts. In the optimal solution, there is space enough on 
the carts to treat all patients on a given day, allowing the carts to be refilled at night, and 
hereby the logistic burden does not affect the daily work.    
In sense of flexibility, the number of patients seems to influence our model significantly. If the 
patient flow increases by 33%, it will, in the current simulation model, max out many of the 
resources, which makes the model not very flexible with that in mind. This is also due to there 
was very little information available containing resources and the model made is therefore 
tailored to the specific patient flow. If BørneRiget decides to run on minimum number of carts, 
they will have to remember that they cannot have fewer carts than doctors, since one doctor 
require one cart. If the medicine use is heightening, either because the approximation was 
wrong or because the doctors begin to use more medicine on patients, the number of carts 
needed over a week is greatly increased, but does not change on other parameters.

should be swapped with real numbers to provide the number of carts they need.  
Discussion   
In this chapter, the assumptions made in this project, the process in the project, as well as the 
results of the analysis will be discussed.   
One of the downsides to simulation is that there always will be some sort of limitation on the 
complexity that is gathered.  In this project there was a lot of assumptions and limitations that 
created a limited model to work with.  
To limit the model to only include one-time use utensils and medicaments did not have a big 
impact. It is equal to the stopping the modelling at the central storage at the floor, simply a 
limitation of the model size and does not impact the direct flow. 
The limitation with the hospital is only open 9 hours a day impact the utilization on the 
resources. Since Rigshospitalet is providing a service, and is not a production company it is not 
likely they are going to treat patient at night, even though it would reduce waste. Instead, 
Rigshospitalet should investigate if the resources can be maintained the remaining hours of the 
day. In the optimal flow, the carts only need a refill once a day after closing hours, meaning 
that the porter or a nurse can refill the carts in piece. If there are not enough room for the 
number of carts needed in one day the task become a bit more complex, meaning that the 
hospital requires many resources to ensure the doctor always a filled cart during the day. It 
would be waste of doctor’s time and therefore not Lean to have the doctor to refill the cart. 
The idea with one universal cart that can be used by everybody is good. The complexity of the 
system is lowered, and therefore the flow can run easier. With 22 departments, they should 
make some considerations if there are overlaps and any departments can be gathered in 
groups, or the shelves can be divided further. The cart can most likely not contain 22 shelves 
and still be used by a doctor due to the size becoming an issue.  
Another problem is related to the carts medicine use and how the medicine use is determined. 
During the last presentation at BørneRiget, it was discussed how it was possible to best asses 
how much medicine the doctors would use within their specialities. The hospital management 
has an aggregated number of what they buy in, but it is difficult to specify it within the 
departments. It could also be done by interviews with the doctors, but they would possibly 
==PAGE:56==
Page 55 of 76 
 
have difficulties remembering this and their judgement would be a subjective point of view. 
Another way to tackle the issue is to write to an American hospital, where everything is billed 
to the patient. They might not have the exact same departments, or use of medicine, but it is a 
start. A combination of an all three could also be a solution, which would minimize mistakes 
and biases.  
Rigshospitalet does have information on the specific patient flow, and with the model it is 
simple to input and remove the assumption made on this. Rigshospitalet also contain more 
information about the time that the patient spends at the hospital and a “time spend”-
distribution is also easy to input in the current model.  
To use this simulation for more than the issues presented in problem statement, the process 
flow of the doctor, patient and nurses needs to be specified more in relation to what one 
wishes to illustrate.  
In relation to the simulation method chosen in this thesis, it could have made sense to use 
Monte Carlo simulation if one were short on time. AnyLogics discrete event model does 
provide more than just an answer to the problem statement, though. It opens a world of 
possibilities within simulation on the hospital, and it is easier to agree with healthcare personal 
on the flow behind the simulation with AnyLogic than using Monte Carlo simulation.  
Because the patient flow was so aggregated it was decided not to make a value stream 
mapping on the processes and other lean tools. To get the flow at hospital on paper it would 
make sense to create a workshop with some of the healthcare personnel making a value 
stream model over their flow to identify the processes that are value adding for the patient. 
Then the 6S technique presented in the theory section can be used to removing the waste from 
their processes and then the new flow could make sense to implement in the simulation. Then 
the simulation can be used not only to find the minimal number of carts, but also identify 
waste components, focusing the simulation on lean.  It is, however, also relevant to discuss if 
BørneRiget is interested in a flow that is 100 % lean. If the simulation was extended to include 
the central storage for instance, and everything must arrive just-in-time (JIT), there will be no 
buffer in case of a breakdown in the chain and a sending is delayed. If there is no storage as 
Lean prescribes, the hospital will in such a case stop working as the doctor cannot treat 
patients without medicine. In the following perspectivation chapter the further work 
possibilities are discussed and ranked according to importance.

Perspectivation/ Further work 
In this thesis, the perspectivation will focus on the possibilities for further work with the model.   
The possibilities for further work made on the model made in this thesis is endless. It is, 
however, not all work that is relevant to our problem statement, and the further work is 
therefore ranked 1-3, where rank 1 is highest priority. The ranking is based on both importance 
and how big a task it is.  
 
• Decision on space allocation within cart. Possible with optimization experiment – Rank 
1 
One of the issues with the model as it is right now, is to set the cart allocation. With the current 
model, the cart allocation is made on best guess, but it is impossible for the human eye to 
consider all patient flows, when allocating medicine. There is also a stochastic variable that 
influences the medicine use within the different specialities. A solution for this issue could be 
to create an optimization experiment. An optimization experiments is where the model is run, 
keeping most of the parameters fixed, but allowing a few parameters to be variable. In other 
words, one can watch the system under certain condition, to improve the decision on the 
parameters.  The variables are changing a tiny bit, from each iteration always looking for the 
one that created the best possible solution. In our case, the goal is to minimize the number of 
carts used over week. This optimization experiment can be done manually running 25+ 
iterations of the simulation changing the parameters slightly but AnyLogic also offers a 
solution. AnyLogic solution is based on OptQuest engine from Opttek changing the question 
from “what if” to “what’s best” (AnyLogic, n.d.). Using AnyLogics automatic logarithms it is also 
easy to simulate over 500 runs. It would be a relevant implementation and an important push 
forward into the direction of being functional tool for Rigshospitalet.   
 
• Analysis of new patient distribution with pitstop thought – Rank 3 
One of the wishes from BørneRiget was to implement the new patient flow in the simulation. 
However, they did not think about the fact that the pitstop thought with gathering the patient 
visit changes the distribution. To analyse the impact of pitstop implementation is an 
opportunity for a new thesis, but implementing the patient distribution that emerge in the 
current simulation is straightforward.  
 
 
==PAGE:58==

• Decision on physical space for carts at BørneRiget - Rank 2  
BørneRiget needs to decide on how much space there is reserved to the carts at the new 
hospital. Then it can be calculated the number of carts that are possible to have based on cart 
size and square meters available, which then can be used to determine the carts fill-up cycle. If 
the space that is allocated allow the carts to be refilled at night, the extra flow is relatively easy 
to implement in the model.  During the process of building BørneRiget this question is 
answered, and can then be implemented in the simulation model easily.  
 
• Gain a deeper understanding of the medicine use – Rank 2 
Another aspect that needs to be considered when deciding on the carts that are needed is the 
amount of medicine that are used. During the literature search nothing about medicine use 
came up, which indicates that either the wrong keywords where used or there is not written 
any articles that consider the medicine use at hospitals. As mentioned earlier, BørneRiget did 
not have any record of the specific need and contacting the industry for help might be 
necessary. It might be possible to connect with an American hospital, that as a private hospital 
is presumed to bill the customer down to the last pipette.  
 
• A more detailed patient distribution – Rank 1 
The more precise a patient distribution the simulation model can get, the more precise the 
output is. The hourly data regarding patient distribution is there somewhere, and it just need 
to be implemented into the model.  
 
• Not everything in the outpatient is single time use – Rank 2  
The flow to and from sterile centre could be implemented. It was during an interview with the 
interest group at BørneRiget that Kristian Bille mentioned he did not believe in keeping very 
many items reusable, since the production now a days cost so little. The extra process 
implementation is easy, but not so relevant for the problem statement.   
 
• Our supply chain stops at the central storage rank - 3 
Within the central storage, everything is presumed to be in stock. To be Lean the whole supply 
chain must be considered, and from the Rigshopistalets side implement just-in-time thinking so 
that the inventory stock is gone or at least minimal. It is important to consider the risks of

sometimes having items out of stock. It is very relevant for the hospital to consider the whole 
supply, and this would also make an interesting subject for a new thesis.    
 
•  Include all departments - 1 
In this thesis, the model is only simulating three departments. The goal would be to simulation 
with all the departments. Due to the limitation on mixTank it is not so easy to implement in 
AnyLogic to simulate all department at once, but it is possible to simulate three departments at 
a time.  
 
• Include the doctors, nurses and the people who work at the outpatient clinic – rank 3  
It would create refined flow, and give a sense of ownership to the people whom the model is 
affecting. It would probably also make these people more susceptible towards any changes 
that might occur in their work at the hospital, if they had had an influence on the model.  
 
If the decision on which projects to work further with gets too difficult it could relevant to 
make an analytical hierarchy process(AHP) (Saaty, 1990). AHP is a multi-criteria decision 
method to help decide between alternatives. The final ranking is based on a pairwise 
evaluation of all criteria’s. The reason it is not chosen to go further with this, is that many of 
the alternatives are prerequisites for the model to work. For instance, the patient distribution 
and a number on the medicine use. When these are in place, the model can be expanded, 
possible decided with the AHP. It would make sense to create the optimization experiment in 
order to create the highest utilization of the cart, and make it as lean as possible, without 
taking it the step further and use everything on the cart before refill.

Conclusion  
To conclude on this project, one must revisit the problem statement:  
How can simulation be applied to determine the minimum need for medical carts at an 
outpatient clinic for treating incoming patients with alternating needs? 
To answer this statement, AnyLogic was applied to setup an outpatient clinic, with a link to an 
analogy on the medicine use. To determine the precise number of minimum carts needed one 
require information on the resource utilization, a given patient flow and a given medicine need 
which were not possible to acquire at this point. However, this simulation model is a proof of 
concept and gives the relation between patient distribution, medicine need and carts, which 
where the goal at the beginning.  
Another important issue for BørneRiget was a flexible model, because many things can change 
in the BørneRiget project. While the simulation itself is not so flexible towards changes as seen 
in the scenario testing, the groundwork for the model is now made and can be changed 
without big knowledge in AnyLogic. It is small, simple changes that needs to happen now to 
fulfil their needs and it is very flexible compared to a model made with the Monte Carlo 
simulation. It was necessary to make a lot of assumptions in this thesis, and it was therefore 
possible to create a simulation that fit with high utilization. The reason no further 
implementation was done, was due to time limit, but also the fact that BørneRiget needs to 
make more decision before it can be implemented. Some of these questions include, but is not 
limited to, how the flow exactly is going to be, how is the layout of building and how much 
physical space they plan to allocate to carts. In the end it can be concluded that this has been a 
==PAGE:61==
Page 60 of 76 
 
successful project, and a proof of concept has taken place. It has plenty of rooms for further 
implementations, and a strict guideline to which information they need to acquire in order to 
achieve a working model they can use to plan their cart purchase.

Introduction 
 
In this report, there will be an investigation of how technology is used in the health care sector. 
Technology is becoming a bigger part of the medical industry. Technology is used as an assisting 
tool and in some cases replacing human jobs. Technology can be found in all parts of the 
human sector. By making a quick google search, tons of different solutions for the use of 
technology appears. Below are some of the examples of how technology is implemented and 
used. 
 
• 
Artificial intelligence can be used to analyse molecular structures and design medicine 
to treat viruses. 
 
• 
Healthcare trackers are used in the elderly industry for the doctor to see that the elderly 
is doing fine at home. 
 
• 
3D printing can be used to create limbs, prosthetics, medicine, operation equipment etc. 
and has a great potential in third world countries where cheap medical equipment is 
essential. 
 
• 
Virtual Reality is one of the newest forms of technology, and so far only a fraction of its 
use has been shown. It can be used for young doctors to put themselves in the patients’ 
place and prepare the health care staff for surgeons. 
 
• 
Robots that acts instead of nurses, transport people, medicine locker, 
etc. (Medicalfuturist, u.d.) 
 
 
This report is made as product of the special course “Which technical solution is there 
 
available today to support nurses in dispensing medicine?”, as a front runner for a master thesis. 
The topic will cover one of three theory pillars (technology, simulation, logistics) in the thesis. 
The thesis will be covering the logistic optimization, and the different possibilities for dispensing 
medicine will be part of this. 
 
Technology in the health care sector can be used for both storage, transport and dispensing 
and all three aspects will be investigated in this report. 
 
 
Research question 
 
Based upon the description above, the following research question emerged: 
 
“Which technical solution is available today to support nurses in dispensing medicine?”

Methodology 
 
In order to make a literature review, one must be structured and go through an iterative process 
where discovering the right keywords are the target. To answer the given research question 
there will be a quantitative analysis making an overview of the search, followed by a short 
qualitative analysis, taking a closer look into some of the research. 
 
Quantitative analysis is a systematic, empirical approach that uses statistical or 
mathematical methods to process data. It is a helpful tool in the process of making sense of 
large amounts of data, in order to discover a trend within said data. The quantitative analysis 
needs all the data it can get, however, the data has to be relevant to the topic. In order to access 
the scope of the research area a search string strategy has been proposed. In this report 
searches through databases as Scholar.google.com, Findit.dtu.dk and Scopus will be used. 
 
• 
Google Scholar is a database which will represent a more general use of the words and 
will not only contain articles. Google scholar lets one search through all scholarly 
literature, from articles, theses, books, abstracts to professional societies, universities etc. 
 
• 
Scopus, which is a multidisciplinary database that contains bibliographic information, 
reference list from indexed publications. It contains over 50 million records including 
journal titles, books, book series conference papers and patents. Contrary to 
findit.dtu.dk, Scopus also allows the searching to run through abstract and keywords, 
where findit.dtu.dk is limited to searching through headlines. 
 
• 
Findit.dtu.dk is also a search database, which includes some of the searches from scopus, 
but reaches a bit broader than scopus. It covers the research areas of the university. It 
covers scientific journals, subject database, e-books and DTU library’s printed books. (DTU, 
u.d.) 
 
All three databases are valid scientific databases, which will represent the available data on 
a given topic.

Three logistic solutions 
 
 
 
For this more qualitative approach, the searches have been made for the three logistic solutions 
mentioned above. There will be a short introduction, a search of the three databases and in the 
end a matrix comparison of the logistic solutions. 
 
 
Horizontal carousels 
 
Horizontal carousels are a set of bins that revolve around a track and can transport the medicine 
and medical equipment around in the hospital. 
 
Horizontal carousels generally outperform robotic system for a fraction of the cost, but as it 
requires a lot of space it is often a solution that is available when the layout is built from 
the beginning. There is an expensive first-time investment; however, the running cost is low 
afterwards. 
 
As for limitations, horizontal carousels have a maximum size it can carry around, and larger 
equipment must use another solution. As the solution mainly is relevant with new buildings, 
their use is limited. The size of the items it can carry relates directly to the size of the carousels.

Conclusion 
 
Looking at technology AND hospital AND pharmacy shows that it is a topic of growing interest. The 
majority of the articles are from the United States, where almost 50 % of the articles are from. The 
top 10 keywords do not show that logistic solutions are in close relation the search. 
 
Linking keywords as transport, dispensing and storage still gave many articles that did not have 
any relation to logistic, but also a few articles surrounding centralized and decentralized 
dispensing, and how automation can be a core competence in hospital pharmacy. There was 
still too many hits to get a clear view and it was decided to look closer into three specific logistic 
solutions, to see what their advantage/disadvantage are. 
 
Looking closer into AGV, tube and horizontal carousels show that horizontal carousels has some 
advantage in cost, while tube system is the fastest system and AGV is the most flexible. The 
research also shows that literature that surround logistic solutions in health care are limited and 
a master thesis going in depth in the subject will increase the knowledge on this topic.
