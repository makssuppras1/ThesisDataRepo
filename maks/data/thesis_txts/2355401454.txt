Abstract
In MAN Diesel & Turbo 5 and 6 cylinder engines represent the majority of 2-stroke marine
diesel engines sold and are typically designed with a shafting system resulting in a torsional
resonance in the middle of the operating range, resulting in stress amplitudes exceeding the
fatigue endurance limit of the shaft. As a consequence a RPM range around the resonance,
called a Barred Speed Range (BSR), is not allowed for continuous operation and must be passed
quickly to avoid fatigue failure of the shaft.
Furthermore, in the ongoing strive to improve fuel eﬃciency problems with acceptable engine
accelerations have occurred more frequently.
For certain bulkers and tankers with a direct
coupled Fixed Pitch Propeller (FPP), this combination of a main critical torsional resonance
and poor acceleration performance means increased number of stress cycles in the shafting,
which may result in an unacceptable fatigue life time of the propulsion shaft and it is therefore
of great interest to assess these acceleration issues.
Traditionally torsional vibration calculations have been performed in steady state in the fre-
quency domain. However for prediction of passage time of the barred speed range and resulting
stress amplitudes, calculations in the time domain are required due to the transient condition
of accelerations.
This thesis investigates the above mentioned acceleration issues by employing a numerical model
developed in the time domain and by using high resolution measurements obtained from existing
vessels to assess the torsional vibrations of the shaft system. The acquired results give insight
into the dynamics of vessels containing a barred speed range and shows how gas excitations and
propeller damping interact and how the resulting accelerations aﬀect the torsional stresses and
fatigue endurance lifetime of the shafting.
Keywords
Barred Speed Range · Acceleration issues · Propeller curve · Resonance · Torsional
stress · Fatigue endurance · Gas-excitations · Transient vibrations · Time domain · Frequency
domain · 2-stroke marine diesel engines · Fixed Pitch Propeller

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In MAN Diesel & Turbo 5 and 6 cylinder engines represent the majority of 2-stroke marine
diesel engines sold and are typically designed with a shafting system resulting in a torsional
resonance in the middle of the operating range. This resonance is not possible to avoid due to
other design considerations. Typically the resonance results in stress amplitudes exceeding the
fatigue endurance limit of the shaft.1 As a consequence a RPM range around the resonance is
not allowed for continuous operation and must be passed quickly to avoid fatigue failure of the
shaft. The region for such a RPM range is called a Barred Speed Range (BSR), see Figure 1.1.
The upper limit in the plot is deﬁning the maximum peak stress allowed and is called the
transient limit (τ2), whereas the limit below deﬁning the maximum continuously stress is called
the continuous limit (τ1).
Figure 1.1: Consequence of RPM range around resonance showing the 1-node main critical resonance mode for
torsional vibrations on 5 & 6 cylinder engines and indicating the torsional stress amplitude in the intermediate
shaft with a barred speed range (BSR).
The ongoing strive to improve the fuel eﬃciency of the propulsion system has resulted in larger
propellers operating at lower RPM and reduced engine power. As a result problems with ac-
ceptable engine acceleration have occurred more frequently. Low acceleration performance of
the propulsion system results in reduced maneuverability, but also increased number of stress
cycles within the barred speed range, which may result in an unacceptable fatigue life time of
the propulsion shaft.
Traditionally shaft calculations are performed in the frequency domain allowing for fast com-
putations considering steady state only. Therefore a large body of experience from frequency
simulations and measurements have been built up over decades. However for prediction of pas-
sage times of the barred speed range and resulting stress amplitudes, calculations in the time
domain are required due to the transient condition of accelerations. For a general introduction
to torsional vibration calculations see [1].
It should be made clear that these acceleration issues are only relevant for certain bulkers and
tankers equipped with 5 or 6 cylinder 2-stroke diesel engines with direct coupled ﬁxed pitch

propellers. Whereas container vessels are not relevant as they typically have their main engine
midships giving very long shafts.
1.2
Motivation
Poor engine acceleration is giving rise to new problems in the BSR and has to a large extent not
been encountered in older vessel designs. The lack of detailed transient calculations and lack of
experience makes this a very interesting ﬁeld of research, as extended knowledge is needed in
order to remedy these problems.
The maritime industry is regulated and controlled by classiﬁcation societies issuing class rules
to ensure shipping safety.2 Due to the trend of poor engine acceleration the classiﬁcation soci-
eties have introduced rules demanding a transient time domain simulation of the passage of the
barred speed range to ensure acceptable fatigue life time of the propulsion shafting, see extract
of rules from: "DNV GL section 2.4.3 Relevant cases for simulation" below:
Passing through a barred speed range
Simulation of ﬁxed pitch propeller plants shall take into account the most important properties
of the propulsion, the ship mass and resistance (fully loaded) and the RPM control.
The result of transient vibration documentation shall contain the peak vibration level and an
estimation of the equivalent number of cycles. The acceptance criterion is the peak torque (or
stress) and the corresponding equivalent number of cycles that shall be used for the shaft calcu-
lations.
A typical result from such a transient vibration simulation can be seen in Figure 1.2. Here
it is possible to see the trend while passing through a BSR. It is evident that the maximum
amplitude stress occurs at around 4 seconds which corresponds to the main critical resonance
followed by a fast decrease in stress levels. Notice the mean stress has been ﬁltered out.
Figure 1.2: Example of stress amplitudes during a quick passage of the barred speed range (BSR).
There is therefore a high motivation to develop a model in the time domain, which precisely
describes and predicts these key features of the propulsion system.

1.3
Project Description
The scope of this study, is to obtain insight into the transient torsional vibrations of certain bulk-
ers and tankers with poor engine acceleration, equipped with 5-6 cylinder 2-stroke, crosshead,
marine diesel engines with a direct coupled ﬁxed pitch propeller. These engines have a 1-node
main critical resonance in the middle of the operating range implying a BSR restriction. The
acceleration issues observed and resulting reduced endurance fatigue lifetime of the shafting is
investigated by developing a model in the time domain performing Torsional Vibration Calcula-
tions (TVC) and by using high resolution measurements obtained from existing vessels. Hereby
making it possible to assess the acceleration performance and the torsional stress amplitudes
experienced in the shafting system. Accordingly the endurance fatigue lifetime of the shafting
can be estimated.

2
Numerical model of propulsion system
The target is to make a model of the propulsion system in the time domain, which for torsional
calculation purposes, produce accurate results. Throughout decades TVC’s have applied lumped
mass models3 in the frequency domain and it has become industry standard. Also at MAN
Diesel & Turbo a lumped mass model in the frequency domain exists, called GTORSI. The
accumulated experiences over the years from simulations and measurements from vessels have
built up a large body of knowledge making it possible to improve GTORSI. Further GTORSI
has proven to match experience very well and verifying the mandatory vibration measurements
obtained at sea trial. The inﬂuence of discretization of the continuous shaft system including
engine and propeller is described in [1] and it is found that even systems with few elements
(10-20) give accurate results in terms of natural frequencies and corresponding modeshapes.
Alternatives such as the Finite Element Method (FEM) are therefore generally not used for
TVC’s for modeling purposes, as it is not necessary to employ a large FEM model.
Also
one might experience additional problems with a ﬁnite element model, when solving it in the
transient time domain.
E.g.
the ﬁne discretization means internal waves will be present in
the structure, therefore the timestep must be suﬃcient small to overcome this issue and obtain
proper results, making the model very time consuming.
On the contrary lumped mass models are not consuming much computer time and provide
fast results. The short computation time is an important feature, as simulations in the time
domain require many iterations, especially when performing frequency sweeps. Moreover some
of the model parameters from GTORSI can be reused, though some modiﬁcations must be done.
Finally GTORSI can be used to validate the model in the time domain in steady state.
Therefore a lumped mass model is chosen as it provides a general and robust simulation tool
there is easy to alter, due to its simple structure, while being fast to use and remaining accurate.

Below follows some explanation to the model. The system is modeled as a straight shaft and
crank angles corresponding to the layout of the crankshaft are then added for the cylinders. The
external loads from gas pressures and oscillating masses act on the pistons and hydrodynamic
loads act on the propeller, which also provides extensive damping. The structural damping
consists of both relative and absolute damping. This damping is modeled using a combination
of physical damping and damping ratios achieved from the frequency domain. The damping can
be used directly in the frequency domain, however in order to obtain results in the time domain
the damping must be approximated.
The long ﬂexible shaft connecting the engine with the propeller typically consists of two parts;
the intermediate shaft and the propeller shaft, these are mounted together via a bolted ﬂange
connection, see Figure 2.2. Also, the greatest stress amplitudes occur in the intermediate shaft
for 5 and 6 cylinder engines.

and propeller shaft, when the ship is in dry dock for maintenance.
Any moment compensators present in the shaft system are connected to the main shaft by
means of a chain drive. The moment compensators are thus physically not positioned on the
main drive line, but are mounted on separate shafts and rotate at twice the engine speed and in

moments, see Figure 2.3.

enables eﬃcient matrix solving. However this advantage is ﬁrst truly exploited for large systems,
like ﬁnite element models having a large number of equations. This is not the case here, as
lumped mass models only have few equations.

2.4
External loads on crankshaft
The 2 loads acting on the crankshaft come from the gas pressure and the oscillating masses.
Figure 2.6 shows the gas pressure acting on the piston as function of the crank angle. The
compression pressure pcomp is the ﬁrst peak at 0 Crank Angle Degree (CAD), i.e. at TDC and
the second peak is the max pressure pmax obtained after combustion. Gas pressure is either
found from simulations or measured using pressure transducers and the piston force is then

Propeller
There are in general two diﬀerent types of propellers; ﬁxed pitch propellers (FPP) and control-
lable pitch propellers (CPP). With a CPP it is possible to pitch the blades through a hydraulic
system, however FPP’s are simpler, cheaper, more eﬃcient and have more reliable designs and
are therefore far more common. Furthermore a CPP is normally not a required feature for large
vessels. Finally a vessel equipped with a CPP does not have acceleration problems in terms of
passing the BSR, as it can simply pitch the propeller blades. Therefore only cases using single
FPP systems will be considered. A FPP always rotates clockwise seen from aft9, when sailing
ahead.

Estimating mean velocity when accelerating
There are some complications as a precise estimate of the mean velocity is now needed for
computing the absolute damping for every step in the time marching scheme. For steady state
conditions, this is simple as the mean angular velocity remains the same. However when accel-

5.4
Solver: Newmark scheme
The Newmark integration scheme is an implicit solver with higher order accuracy.[7] This time
integration method is in particular useful when solving transient problems, as it has very nice
properties, i.e. it can be made unconditionally stable.[8] The stability and accuracy character-
istics depends on the two parameters γ and β presented in the scheme. When γ = 1/2 and
β = 1/4 the scheme corresponds to the constant-average-acceleration method, which makes the
scheme unconditionally stable with second order accuracy. Unconditionally stable means the
calculations will not blow up, regardless of the time step, ∆t chosen. I.e. very large ∆t can be
used and the solution goes neither towards plus nor minus inﬁnity. However, though numerically
stable, a poor choice of ∆t will still yield non-physical results, rendering the computations use-
less. The implicit method solves the equation at the "new" time level. The equation is implicit
in the sense that the unknown θ cannot be isolated and expressed explicitly by the known θ’s
(these θ’s are not known either). This is diﬀerent from an explicit solver where it is possible to
isolate the unknown θ.[9] An explicit method relies on information from the "old" time level and
is typically more simple mathematically, this makes it faster to perform the computations for
a single step, however with the drawback that the calculations are not stable for all values of
the time step, ∆t. Though the implicit Newmark scheme is not as fast as an explicit scheme at
performing the computations for a single step, the Newmark scheme has the advantage of using
larger time steps bringing down the total number of computations.

Program Structure
6.1
Flow chart
Figure 6.1 depicts a ﬂow chart containing an overview over the essential design of the program
in sequential order.
The main script executes the Torsional Vibration Calculations (TVC)
consisting of 3 sections. In section 1 an initialization procedure reads in data and the user sets
up the study and analysis. In section 2 a controller ﬁnds the solver for the chosen analysis
and solves the system. Finally the system is post-processed in section 3 and relevant plots are
created.
Figure 6.1: Flow chart
The features of the individual sections are described in greater detail below:
In Section 1 all system data is imported which includes stiﬀness, mass inertia, relative and ab-
solute structural damping, propeller damping, number of cylinders, ﬁring order, total oscillating
masses, MCR power of system, diameters, engine speciﬁc gas harmonics, use of tuning wheel,
use of torsional vibration damper etc. This data is all found in the inputﬁle and gas harmonic
ﬁle. For an example see Appendix. Moreover the external loads are mapped into lookup tables.
Utilizing the periodic nature of the loads i.e. f(θ) = f(θ + 2π) it is only necessary to use crank
angles for 1 revolution.
In section 2 bilinear interpolation schemes are used extensively, when the system is solved re-
peatedly.
While time-marching through the simulation the external loads, consisting of gas
excitations and oscillating masses are updated for each step employing the created lookup ta-
bles. This ensures fast computation while maintaining high accuracy.
Finally in section 3 the results are post-processed, hence ﬁnding torques and stresses from the
calculated angular deﬂections and estimating endurance fatigue lifetime of the shaft.

The program is general and capable of processing diﬀerent engines from the database using key
data for the engine speciﬁed. It is also possible to change various settings in the program, but
they are not included in the ﬂow chart. E.g. in the preprocessor measured transient data from
the combustion process can be imported instead of the steady state harmonic gas excitations.
Also other studies can be performed and in general one can choose among 3 diﬀerent studies;
• Eigenvalue analysis computing eigenvalues and corresponding modeshapes.
• Sweep
• Transient analysis
The normal case is the transient analysis, however also a steady state frequency sweep can be

Hydrodynamic characteristics of propellers
The limitations of the transient time domain model are mainly due to simpliﬁcations introduced
in the modeling of the propeller. Therefore a more sophisticated propeller model is now investi-
gated. This propeller model depends not only on the engine speed, but also on the vessel speed.
As such the system is expanded by adding the dynamics of the vessel, hereby introducing an
extra state to the model.
A propeller’s hydrodynamic characteristics describes its thrust, torque and eﬃciency.[13],[14]

Kq. These are non-dimensional coeﬃcients, established by applying dimensional analysis to the
propeller thrust force Ft and the propeller torque Tp, which can be represented by the following
functions:

Tp = f2(ρ, D, v, g, np, p, µ)

9.1
Ship resistance
The total ship resistance consists of many diﬀerent sources, which can generally be split into 3
main categories[16]:
1) Frictional resistance, 90%
2) Residual resistance, 8%
3) Air resistance, 2%
Where the ship’s total resistance in per cent for low speed vessels is approximately, as stated
above. The residual resistance comprises wave resistance and eddy resistance. In general all
the resistances are proportional to the square of the speed, but for higher vessel speeds the
wave resistance increases much faster. E.g. in a speciﬁc case a ship designed for a speed of 15
knots was required to be increased to 17.6 knots, however this would lead to a relatively high
wave resistance, requiring a doubling of the necessary propulsion power, as most of the power
57
==PAGE:68==
would be converted into wave energy. However not considering high vessel speeds, the total ship
resistance denoted the towing resistance Fr can be given as:
Fr =
1
2ρCrAwv2
(1 −t)
(9.8)
where Cr is the resistance coeﬃcient, Aw is the wetted area and t is the thrust deduction set
to 0.2. This is of a quadratic form more in-depths models with the same overall quadratic form
exist, allowing for additional prediction factors and coeﬃcients, when extensive knowledge of the
vessel is available, see [17]. However it is not the scope of this text to attempt an in-depth study
of the resistance models. In general the towing resistance is strongly aﬀected by the cleanliness
of the hull and throughout the lifetime of a ship a fouled hull may increase the resistance by
25%-50%. Moreover the sea conditions in terms of, waves, current and wind also aﬀect the
resistance to a large extent.
9.2
Vessel model
In order to describe the interactions between the shafting system and the ship motion a two
state model for a single mass is set up according to Newton’s second law.[18] The model consists
of 2 diﬀerential equations. The ﬁrst describes the torque acting in the shafting from the engine
to the propeller and the second describes the forces exerted on the ship, i.e. the thrust force and
the resistance force. In the diﬀerential system below the two states are deﬁned as the rotational
speed of the engine ω and the velocity of the vessel v respectively:
I ˙ω = Te(ω) −Tp(ω, v)
m˙v = Ft(ω, v) −Fr(v)
(9.9)
with initial conditions: ω(0) = ω0 and v(0) = v0. Where I is the total mass moment of inertia
of the shafting system. That is the combined engine and propeller inertia, where the entrained
water of the propeller is taken into account and m is the total mass of the ship incl. cargo (the
displacement volume times the seawater density). Also an added mass of 5% of the ship mass is
accounted for in m, making the total mass larger. The added mass is due to the inertia added
to the system as the accelerating vessel must move the surrounding ﬂuid. Te is the torque of
the engine which under acceleration is following a heavy propeller curve, though limited by the
torque limiter, see Figure 9.2 and Tp is the propeller torque, deﬁned in equation (9.2). Finally
Ft is the thrust force from the propeller and Fr is the total towing resistance acting on the ship.

10
Conclusion
A model in the time domain has been developed to investigate the transient torsional vibrations
of certain 5-6 cylinder 2-stroke diesel engines with direct coupled FPP and poor engine acceler-
ation. These engines are operating with increasing larger propellers at lower engine speeds and
reduced power, while having a shaft system with a 1-node main critical resonance in the middle
of the operating range implying a BSR restriction.
The time domain model was initially veriﬁed in steady state conditions against the independent
frequency domain model, GTORSI from MAN Diesel & Turbo. In this validation process the
time domain model has shown fully capable of reproducing correct results.
Hereafter the transient time domain model has provided simulation results containing the peak
vibration levels and corresponding number of cycles under dynamic operation. These estimated
results have been used to determine endurance fatigue life time of the shafting, by employing
Miner’s sum evaluation. When compared with high resolution measurements the simulated re-
sults showed similar trends, however with some discrepancy.
The diﬀerences in the peak stresses and number of cycles were found mainly to be due to in-
accurate modeling of the propeller. The results strongly indicate that the propeller torque and
propeller damping under transient conditions a more complicated than in steady state condi-
tions. Therefore it has been hard to obtain precise results of the peak stresses and corresponding
number of cycles and hence also the fatigue life time. Especially correct peak stresses are im-
portant as they strongly inﬂuence the resulting fatigue life time.
To overcome the issues of the propeller torque an additional propeller model has been proposed.
A simple vessel model has been carried out in order to enable the use of a more sophisticated
propeller model, which takes into account the inﬂuence of the advance number and utilizes the
hydrodynamic propeller coeﬃcients.
The response of the vessel model has shown promising
results yielding similar trends to those found in the measurements.
In order to exploit the newly proposed propeller model it must be implemented in the existing
time domain model. However combining the two models and obtaining new results has not yet
been done. Furthermore similar modiﬁcations of the propeller damping may be introduced.
The most interesting ﬁndings have come from comparing the simulated transient results with
the measured results.
It is evident that better acceleration in some cases result in reduced
stresses and improved passage times. Accordingly greatly improving the fatigue life time of the
shafting. This result is not trivial, because in order to obtain better acceleration the engine
torque limiter is increased and thereby also the gas excitations, hence the stress levels are at
ﬁrst thought to increase. The actual reduction in stress levels observed, is usually explained
by the time it takes to build up vibrations.
However the parametric study as well as the
measurements strongly indicate that the stresses are not only aﬀected by the passage times,
but also by varying propeller damping.
Suggesting a more complex nature of the propeller
damping exists for transient conditions.
Finally for long passage times the vibrations have
already becomes fully developed and hence the vibrations are no longer aﬀected by the passage
time. Therefore the stress reduction in this case can only be explained by varying propeller
damping.

10.1
Further Work
Suggestions for further work is to ﬁrst and foremost implement the more sophisticated pro-
peller model in the time domain model. Also modiﬁcations of the propeller damping may be
introduced as mentioned above. Also a governor controller could be implemented to simulate
adjusted torque limits. Transient simulated gas excitations could be introduced. A ﬁrst im-
provement would be to include turbo lag eﬀects from the turbochargers.
Other interesting improvements of the model could be to introduce varying mass moments of
inertia for the cylinders, instead of equivalent constant mass moments of inertia. Also a varying
expression for the mass moment of inertia at the propeller could be obtained, taking into account
the variations from the entrained water. The kinematics of the crank-slider mechanism could
be improved by not assuming constant angular velocity when deriving the expression. Finally
the model could also be used for other cases like simulating the event of an ice block in the
propeller, which is relevant for ice classes.
