Abstract
Decoupling is a means of reducing the inter-dependency of components in the structure of
a program.
This process is often used in large-scale, commercial program design, as it offers
greater flexibility as well as makes the program easier to maintain and develop. Parallelization
also necessitates some form of decoupling, which is a cornerstone in program efficiency. This thesis
explores the applicability of decoupling on graphical workbenches with the aim of accomplishing
the goals specific of such a program. We implement a decoupling into GEL’s MeshEdit program,
and analyze the differences it made with respect to the goals and the development cost. Then
we explore different ways to leverage this decoupled design, and compare these implementations.
The project found that the decoupling of the workbench made the development of new features
significantly easier, and that the act of decoupling is especially applicable in graphical workbenches.
We also found that the decoupled design allows for much wider range of parallelization, as well as
integration into other programs and workflows. Finally, we determined that separating into two
independent processes with inter-process communication is not only feasible, but the performance
costs of doing so is negligible compared to the remainder of the program.

Introduction
Software tools for geometry processing and visualization are fundamental for a wide range of topics,
ranging from product development, to simulations, to terrain mapping, and medical purposes. These
processes have the potential to be highly specialized for specific topics or domains, offering technical
functionality that could in some cases be deemed a necessity for work in that particular field.
In the field of graphics research, a graphical workbench in which you can manipulate and display
objects in a step-wise and experimental manner is an example of such a tool. As opposed to com-
mercial applications, these kinds of in-house tools can get away with being much more rudimentary
and without polish, as they are primarily designed for expert users and meant for internal use. This
brings the advantage of minimizing development overhead on top of making the tool potentially more
straight-to-the-point when used for its specialized purpose.
However as such, these kinds of programs often end up tightly-coupled.
While this may be good
enough in the short term, it can have strong ramifications towards long-term viability of the tool.
Tight-coupledness makes code harder to read and greatly increases maintenance and further devel-
opment requirements, and also strongly limits or completely removes opportunity for parallelization
and modularity. On a usability standpoint, the lack of such features could have real-world impacts on
work efficiency, as certain actions could render the whole program unresponsive, or the program may
be stuck with sub-optimal implementations that are very hard to replace.
Decoupling is the act of taking a tightly-coupled process and refactoring it to be loosely-coupled.
This involves decomposing it into smaller self-contained components, and establishing the minimally
necessary communication between them. Doing so enables stronger modularity and increased readabil-
ity, making the program more flexible and easier to maintain. This is widely used in more commercial
operations, such as high-profile 3D programs, web browsers, and operating systems, where there is a
much more strict focus on scalability. Through additional generalization of communication, the pro-
gram can also become much more multi-purpose, opening up potential upgrade paths that were not
possible before.
In this thesis, we will explore the applicability of decoupling on a graphical workbench for research,
and analyze the benefits and opportunities it may provide. More specifically, we will apply decoupling
to the geometry processing and visualization components of such a tool, with the aim of improving its
long-term scalability. This will be done with a focus on parallelization, and modularity opportunities
in regards to the visualization and user interface aspects of the tool. We will also compare different
ways of communicating between the two components, both within a single process, and across multiple
independent processes.
To accomplish this task, we will work with an example case of a 3D Geometry Processing and
Visualization tool, GEL, developed and maintained by the supervisor of this project, Jakob Andreas
Bærentzen. Here, we will compare the specifics of the implementations, and also measure their per-
formance impact on the program as a whole. We will also implement proof-of-concept examples of
opportunities provided by this accomplishment. In the end, we will conclude on the results of the
analysis and generalize our findings to a broader understanding of what a graphical workbench is.

2
Background
In this chapter, we will go through the theoretical background of this project. Notably, this includes
the definition of what a graphical workbench really is, as well as an analysis on the end-users of such
a tool, and their needs. It also includes definitions and related terminology surrounding tightly- and
loosely-coupled processes, as well as parallelization concepts. These will become a central focus point
for the project as a whole. We will then talk about GEL as a graphical platform, and introduce its
purpose, features, and limitations, with insight from its developer.
In the second half of this chapter, we will cover theoretical background for interaction between sep-
arate processes and relevant concepts. This will also contain a section on several different available
mechanisms for establishing communication between two separate processes.
2.1
Graphical Workbenches
A Graphical Workbench is in this thesis defined as a software program that provides functionality to
visualize and manipulate 3D geometric data in an iterative manner. Its purpose is primarily to facili-
tate experimentation; that is, operations are often executed in steps by the user, sometimes repeated,
where by each step, the result is rendered to that user. An important aspect in the visualization, is
that this is an interactive visualization that can be used to inspect the data fully. At minimum, this
means that the view can be zoomed, translated, and for domains in 3D-space, additionally be rotated.
The fundamental structure of the visualization aspect in the tool is often entirely unrelated to the
remainder of the tool, with the exception of the domain-specific rendering methods that may be em-
ployed. This remains the case, even when the program is tightly-coupled. The visualization process
may need to have an understanding of the data it needs to visualize, but on a theoretical level, it
does not care neither how nor when the data it receives was generated. When tightly-coupled with
the remainder of the program anyway, it can lose all interactivity while the tool is busy processing.
This may not matter for short operations, however especially in the field of graphical research, it is not
uncommon to have operations that take a lengthy amount of time. If the same graphical interface is
also responsible for user input, then this could halt the user’s work altogether.
In addition to this, it also becomes significantly harder to maintain or replace parts of the tool. This is
especially notable if the render performance has a significant impact on the usability. The program may
reach a point in which its frameworks or implementations become outdated, but it is not very feasible
to adapt or upgrade. This can render the program stuck as ”dead weight”, wherein the only choices
are to continue using it despite its weaknesses, harming the work efficiency of the user in the long term.
It is because of these usability concerns, that it becomes a relevant matter to decouple the graphical
interface entirely from the data processing component of the workbench. This would allow paralleliza-
tion of the two processes, effectively letting the user ”work ahead” of the machine, either preparing the
next step or simply just continue inspecting the mesh while waiting. Additionally, if the decoupling
results in a one-directional dependency between the graphical interface and the rest of the tool, it
could also open up the possibility of replacing the visualization component altogether; for example,
for newer and more efficient graphical frameworks.
2.1.1
Target Audience
For graphical workbenches as defined in this thesis, the end-user is not the same as that of general 3D
graphical programs. With internal in-house tools like this, the end user is often an expert user; that is,
they often have a strong familiarity with the domain, and a high degree of technical expertise to adapt
the tool into their workflow. Furthermore, if the user is also the developer, they may have intricate
knowledge into the tool’s internal workings and know its strengths and limitations intimately. They
may also be able to modify and extend the code itself when their needs change.
Because of this difference in target audience, it is more important to emphasize the functionality
and flexibility than ease-of-use, particularly when considering user interfaces. There is also a reduced
6
==PAGE:7==
need for things such as help text or confirmation dialogues, which while may provide an extra layer of
security for users without close knowledge to the field, only goes to slow down expert users who know
exactly what they are doing.
In the cases of in-house tools where the end-user has access to the development of the program,
the extensibility of the code also becomes a large focus point. It may in some instances be a need
that the code is easily able to adapt to new workflows, or that new commands or actions are easy to
add, as the needs of the research progresses. Conversely, when the end-user and developer are one
and the same, reducing the development overhead becomes a balance point, as time would have to be
delegated between performing research and maintaining the tool that enables it. It is in direct conflict
with these needs to add too much that do not improve the usability of the tool to a notable degree.
2.1.2
User Interfaces
A strong consideration in graphical workbenches is the interface with which the user interacts.
It
should meet the minimal needs of the workflow, however in connection with the above, putting too
much time into making a sophisticated graphical UI (User Interface) may hinder or slow down work
efficiency more than it benefits. Expert users, especially those with high levels of familiarity, will be
able to use the tool even if the user interface is not of a high level of polish.
Additionally, many graphical UI’s are often static in nature.
What this means, is that graphical
elements such as buttons, dialogs, text fields, and more, take up an amount of space in the window, of
which only a fixed amount is available. This limits the extensibility of the tool, as at a certain level, it
becomes impossible to add any more elements without sacrificing on others. Too much clutter can also
lead to confusion. It is possible to dynamically populate areas using techniques such as scroll panels,
however these can be slow and tedious to navigate through, especially if the user already knows the
exact name and function of the command they wish to execute. On top of this, designing a graphical
UI takes large amounts of work, and is traditionally a manual effort.
To expert users, Console interfaces may become a very attractive option. Most graphics researchers
are already closely familiar with the terminal, and all major Operating Systems (OS) offer one out of
the box. Additionally, this built-in terminal comes with a range of expert-level functionality that may
greatly improve the efficiency of developer users. These include automatic clipboard support, com-
mand history, and the ability to automate via techniques such as batch scripts or other executables. It
also removes a point of maintenance, and can potentially be trivially implementable by simply reading
from the standard input.
Terminals and other Console-like interfaces are text-based which has its benefits and drawbacks. On
the benfits side, it suits command-based interaction systems, which offer high levels of extensibility
as opposed to graphical UI, since commands do not take up space on the screen. Command systems
are typically worse for average users as they need to familiarize themselves with possible commands,
however for expert users it can be a favorable system to interact with. It also makes it significantly
easier to add actions at a programmatic level, as the code necessary to implement a new function can
be contained within as little as a single statement.
On the drawback side, terminals offer little to no visualization support, which is required by the
definition of a graphical workbench. This forces the tool to be forced to implement the terminal and
the visualization window as two separate windows, or to re-invent the console directly within the
graphical visualization. The latter may make the use of the tool more streamlined, but basic features
such as clipboard and command history need to be manually recreated, and you lose all opportunity
for automation without significant development costs.

2.1.3
What makes a good graphical workbench for research?
Using the above analysis, we can compile a list of key points for the needs of a graphical workbench,
when specifically considering expert-level use and long-term viability:
• The program should specialize in a specific workflow or domain
• The program should be separated into clear, distinct modules
• The program should be able to have one or more of its modules changed, without affecting the
rest of the program to a notable degree
• The program should be easy to extend with additional commands and features
• The program should be able to continue being operated while it is busy
• The program code should be easy to maintain
• The user interface should be Console or Console-like
• The program should be responsive and reliable
These key points summarize the analysis into a set of goals, that a graphical workbench could poten-
tially aim to achieve, to increase its effectiveness with respect to expert-use. There may be some cases
in which some of these will not apply, or are of a lesser priority. Notably, Console interface is a design
choices and there may be other types of interfaces better suited for other domains. In many cases
however, actions can be captured in commands which makes Console interface particularly suited for
agile implementations.
The first point states that the program should specialize in a specific workflow or domain.
This
is integral to the concept of graphical workbenches, as it is what sets them apart from larger, more
commercial applications that may already exist. If the workbench does not provide any edge over a
commercial application, then it is often most cost-effective to go with the existing one, as it takes
responsibility of maintenance off your hands. Therefore the existence of graphical workbenches rely
on being able to specialize to a larger degree.
The subsequent points pertain to the maintainability of the program, and its long-term viability as an
ongoing tool for repeated consistent use. These summarize the desires that pertain to the benefits of
decoupling. Achieving these results in the program being much easier to read and maintain, thereby
prolonging its potential lifespan and usability in the long run. The final point retains that, while all
these changes may occur, the program should always remain responsive and reliable, so as to not slow
down work efficiency on a more general level.
These goals serve as the center focus of this project.
2.1.4
Other Expert-Level Features
In addition to the targets above, it is also possible to consider a range of further ideas that could act
as goalposts for the flexibility achieved by a modular design. These points could increase the flexibility
of a graphical workbench, and open up entirely new opportunities for developers or expert-level users
to achieve greater productivity.
(A) The ability to work with a range of standard formats
(B) The ability to be automated/controlled by another program
(C) The ability to be scripted for repeated complex workflows
(D) The ability to output data for another program to receive
(E) The ability to offload the workload to another computer, possibly across a network
8
==PAGE:9==
These are target points that we will also attempt to look into, but that will not be the main goals of
this project. Their benefits are niche and may only apply to certain scenarios, but they can provide
large productivity boosts in situations where they are applicable, and thus could be worth considering
depending on needs.
The ability to work with a range of standard formats could improve the program’s ability to be in-
corporated into a more complex workflow, featuring several independent tools in a way that essentially
achieves decoupling on a grander scale. Especially for expert users, the notion of standard formats
makes it particularly easy to implement cooperation between programs.
For expert users, automation can make a huge difference to the efficiency of their work. If the tool
is able to be automated in some shape or form, a lot of repeated workflows can be made to be much
simpler. It also enables dynamic processing, such as running a certain operation across a collection of
data all in one go, instead of manual labor. Automation can for example be achieved by reading from
the standard input just as described in Section 2.1.2, enabling it to be controlled by other programs
or to be scripted. It also inherently makes it possible to completely replace the interface; it could for
example be controlled by a program that is responsible for the interface, running the main workbench
as a headless background process. If the workbench is also able to output data through standard
output, then it could turn the entire workbench into a tool that can be used on a much higher level
across larger data-flow infrastructures.
Finally, it may prove beneficial if the work can be distributed to another system through some
cloud communication means. This could for example allow a supercomputer to do all the heavy lifting,
while the user’s system only needs to send, receive, and display the data through the visualization. In
cases where such supercomputers are available, it could substantially improve the raw speed of heavy
operations, which in turn would improve the efficiency of work. To perform this kind of communication
across a network of course introduces new concerns, and depending on the situation, it may not be as
feasible as it could be in others.
While none of these points are needs, they may serve as possible targets from which improvement
of a graphical workbench may be considered. At any given time, the development cost of implement-
ing such features should always be taken into consideration, when weighing the impact and how much
need there is for any given point. It will not always be necessary to find a solution that accomplishes
all these goals at once if the performance impact or ease-of-use drops significantly as a result. Many
of these may also be associated with tradeoffs, which could lead to some being more important than
others. The reliability of the tool also remains an upmost need.
Most of these points can be made possible through programmatic design, such as through the modu-
larity achieved by a loosely-coupled structure. By the use of abstraction, these may be interchangeable
with standard program operations, minimizing the development costs of their implementation. While
the points are not the main goals of this thesis, they will be used as hypothetical goalposts when con-
sidering the implementation of the project, so that the design principles discussed can lay groundwork
for potentially ambitious ideas like these.

2.2
Decoupling and Loosely-Coupled Processes
Decoupling is an act of taking a tightly-coupled process and transforming it to become loosely-coupled.
The ”coupling” of a program refers to the level of interdependency between its modules or components.
Decoupling is done through refactoring the code to reduce this interdependency, and comes with a range
of large benefits, both in the efficiency and usability of the program, but also more notably on the
development and maintenance. A decoupled program structure is widely considered higher-quality
software design[1][2].
2.2.1
Tightly-Coupled Processes
A process is tightly-coupled when it has a high level of interdependency between its parts. In systems
like these, the function of some or all components is highly dependent on other components. This makes
it difficult and confusing to understand the structure of the program, and thus makes it more challeng-
ing to change or maintain parts of the code, without needing to make changes in many other places.
Tightly-coupled processes are also more difficult to test, as it can be harder to isolate a single module
from the rest of the code. Finally, tightly-coupled processes are significantly less flexible and harder to
reuse, requiring much larger changes to be used in different contexts than it was originally designed for.
Examples of tightly-coupled systems can be if functions of components in code directly use the proper-
ties of another. This makes it subject to unforeseen effects if that object’s implementation is changed,
and will often send ripple effects into the rest of the system if it is not caught before compilation.
Another example could be cyclic dependencies: When one object stores a reference to another, which
in turn stores one back. This increases the interdependency, and makes each of these unable to exist
without the existence of the other. A third and final example could be when multiple different overall
functions are managed by a single class — This class could be said to tightly-couple these functional-
ities, as they all depend on this same class, which in turn depends on all of its functionalities.
Figure 1: Illustration of a tightly-coupled system.
Figure 1 illustrates a system with high coupling. In it, all components are dependent on each other
to a high degree, which makes data flow very complex and hard to follow. As can be seen on the
illustration, this can make it hard to gain an overview over the system as a whole. On a developmental
standpoint, this is reflected in how the code becomes significantly harder to read, and as a consequence,
makes maintaining the code more difficult.
2.2.2
Loosely-Coupled Processes
A process is loosely-coupled when it has a low degree of interdependency. This makes the structure
of those systems more modular, and reduces the risk of introducing bugs when changes are made[3].
Changes made to one object has minimal effect on the implementation of others. Low coupling also
makes the program more scalable and easier to maintain and extend over time. It also makes it more
modular and flexible, as well as makes the code more easily understandable to yourself (and others),
which has positive effects on long-term development. These directly correlate with a large portion of
the goals in 2.1.3.
10
==PAGE:11==
In systems like these, more of the logic of components is restrained to itself, and the necessary in-
teractions made with other components are therefore minimized. Examples of this can be designing
the structure more hierarchical, where low-level objects are provided with all the info they need from
higher-level objects, without themselves needing to fetch it from other components. Cyclic dependen-
cies would also be eliminated by such an approach. In addition, separating core functionalities of a
class into smaller subclasses with specific purposes could also reduce dependency, as each of these can
be isolated without the need of the others.
Figure 2: Illustration of a loosely-coupled system.
Figure 2 illustrates the same system as seen in Figure 1, but as a loosely-coupled system. Several of
the interdependencies have been removed through a hierarchical structure, in which the two central
objects manage and maintain their lower-level objects, which are all isolated components. The data
previously fetched directly across the components in the tightly-coupled system are instead passed
down from the parent, in a structural manner.
2.2.3
Cohesion
It is relevant to also discuss ”cohesion” when talking about decoupling. Cohesion is a term closely
correlated with coupling, which refers to the degree at which the modules or components of a system
are well-defined to a single purpose. Cohesion of a program is low, if the modules or components
contain many unrelated elements, or are responsible for multiple functions.
Coupling and cohesion are inversely correlated, in that increasing cohesion means restraining responsi-
bilities and functionalities of components to themselves, which in turn makes them less dependent on
other components, thus reducing coupling[4]. Similarly, a high degree of coupling could be sign of low
cohesion, as the elements necessary for each functionality are spread across the components which thus
rely on each other to perform the task. The example mentioned above of a single class tightly-coupling
multiple functionalities, is an example of low cohesion and a direct representation of the correlation
between these two.

Figure 3 illustrates the correlation between the two terms. In subfigure a), the modules (represented
by the bounding boxes) have high cohesion, as their functionality if self-contained and without any

Cohesion has the same effects on the readability of the code as coupling does.
When too many
unrelated elements exist within a single class in code, this class becomes increasingly harder to under-
stand and comprehend, and the nature of the code becomes more confusing. This results in making it
harder to maintain and develop the codebase, much the same way as a high degree of coupling. Since
these two are correlated, their effects are often compounded.
2.2.4
Decoupling Techniques
There are several different techniques that can help in the decoupling process, to create a more modular
program design from an existing tightly-coupled one[2]. Here we will briefly go through some of these
as examples, to give a more solid understanding of the processes involved.
Encapsulation is the act of hiding internal implementation details behind an interface or class.
This lessens interdependency as objects interact with well-defined transactions, rather than directly
accessing internal details.
Abstraction creates standardized ways for objects and components to interact, such that none of
then will need to be dependent on the direct implementation behind the interaction. This allows these
components to be more easily replaced or maintained, and increases modularity in the system.
Dependency Injection refers to the technique of injecting dependencies externally into an object,
rather than relying on the object’s internal implementation. An example of this is the use of higher-
order functions. This technique can reduce the need for objects to become needlessly dependent on
multiple things, if they only serve a purpose for an outer object.
Mediator Design Pattern is a design pattern in which mediator objects control the interaction
between components. This reduces the reliance between these components and allows their function
to be isolated, simplifying communication.
Dependency Inversion is a specific methodology for designing loosely-coupled software, which
states that high-level modules should depend on abstractions rather than concrete implementations.
It helps decouple the overall functionality from the implementation details, and allows these details to
be altered or changed, without affecting the rest of the application.
12
==PAGE:13==
There are many more techniques and design patterns for decreasing the coupling and increasing the
cohesion of a program, and this list is not exhaustive.
2.3
Parallelization
Parallelization is a cornerstone in efficient program design, and is a form of programming in which
various tasks are delegated across separate threads that can execute concurrently. Most modern day
computers have processors with multiple cores which can work simultaneously, and threading takes
advantage of this enabling theoretical increases to performance by a factor equal to the number of
threads in the CPU. Parallelization is not trivial however. Running asynchronous code can lead to
race conditions and require some sort of synchronization mechanism at the points of conjunction, to
avoid failures or unforeseen consequences in the execution of the program. A race condition is when
the system’s behavior is dependent on the exact timings of uncontrollable factors, such as internal
thread scheduling or CPU core clock frequency.
There are several synchronization mechanisms and techniques available.
Notably for this project,
Mutexes and Condition Variables are two such common objects. A Mutex is a form of lock that
prevents multiple threads from accessing the same resource at the same time. When acquiring the
lock, the thread will be blocked if another thread already holds that lock, until that thread releases it.
This can be used to ensure only one thread can access a certain resource at a time by encapsulating it
with mutex lock statements.
Condition Variables are also a form of mechanism that will block threads from proceeding until
certain events are triggered. When a thread is waiting on a condition variable, they are held until
another thread signals that condition variable to wake up one or all of the waiting threads. This can
be used to signal from one thread to another that data is available, ensuring this signal is not passed
until the data is fully written.
On practical terms, parallelization necessitates at least some level of decoupling. A tightly-coupled
system is much more difficult to parallelize, as the more interdependent the components of the system
are, the harder it will become to separate it into clear delegated tasks. In addition, a highly interde-
pendent system will have more points of conjunction, which requires extensive synchronization efforts,
to a point where it is often infeasible. This limits the effectiveness of the parallelization, and introduces
higher risk of race conditions. If it gets to the point where every thread is always waiting on the next,
parallelization loses all meaning. Efforts into parallelizing such systems often end up being equivalent
to a (potentially localized) decoupling effort.

GEL
In this project, we will be working with GEL as an example case of a graphical workbench.
GEL is short for GEometry and Linear Algebra, and is a C++ library developed and maintained by
Jakob Andreas Bærentzen, the supervisor of this project. The library contains mesh representations,
several spatial data structures, and functions for matrix-vector algebra for 2,3, and 4D dimensionality.
It also contains a series of utility classes for tasks such as parsing command line arguments[6]. GEL’s
mesh representation is based on the Half-Edge model. The library provides data structures to represent
these geometric objects, and a suite of functions to navigate and manipulate them.
Within the library exists the foundation for a graphical workbench named MeshEdit, which can be
compiled and ran as a standalone program. This uses custom-written rendering algorithms with the

els. MeshEdit comes with a built-in console-like overlay, which can be toggled on and off with the
Escape key, and allows the user to input commands that are registered in the MeshEdit Console, such
as saving and loading meshes, simplify or subdivide, and a suite of other operations and algorithms,
some specific to the half-edge model.

2.5
Multiple Processes and Inter-Process Communication (IPC)
When a system is sufficiently decoupled, it also opens up the opportunity to separate the different
modules into entirely independent executable processes. This isn’t always desired, but in some cases it
may open up interesting prospects for the usability of the program. For example, separating the two
processes would make them operate in each their own sandboxed address space, which could improve
security and robustness in their operation. This could also enable one half to continue running, even
if the other half crashes. For graphical workbenches, a primary idea could be to run the visualization
as its own process that runs permanently in the background, while the main tool is ran directly in the
OS’ terminal.
When a system like this is in a decoupled state, it is necessary to re-establish some sort of com-
munication between the modules to achieve the overall application functionality. For same-process
applications, this would be relatively trivial, being just a subset of function calls capable of passing
data between key components, perhaps through a controlling entity. However, when the application is
separated into multiple processes, they no longer share memory space, and thus cannot communicate
that way. Instead, they can use inter-process communication (IPC).
Inter-Process Communication are mechanisms offered by the Operating System that allow processes
to communicate with one another[9]. There are many different different types of IPC mechanisms, each
suited to different purposes, and each with their own strengths and weaknesses. Some rely on the kernel
to handle queues of messages, while others may use the file system as an intermediary. It is also possible
to provide access to a special section of memory, in which multiple processes can read and write data
simultaneously. Common for most of these, is that the IPC mechanism acts as a sort of middle-man,
handling the data between the two processes in its own way. It is therefore up to the operating system’s
implementations, how reliable or secure the transfer of data is and what limitations it may have.
2.5.1
Serialization
Before discussing mechanisms of IPC data transfer, we must discuss serialization and what it means
for programs hoping to take advantage of cross-process communication.
A caveat of communicating data between different memory spaces, is that the data objects will need
to be serialized. Serialization is the act of turning the state of a data object into a persistent form that
can be easily transported. Often this means systematically writing its properties and values down in a
pre-defined manner, such that there can be a mutual understanding between the two communicating
halves. The complimentary act of deserialization is the act of reverting that serialized form back into
(a copy of) the original object.
Common formats for serialization exists such as JSON or XML, but for internal communication that
doesn’t need to be human readable, the most efficient way is binary streams. This provides the most
densely-packed (and thus smallest) serialization forms without compression, as data can be directly
written without any conversion to higher-level representations such as strings, and without needing
to be encoded in standard character formats[10].
While all data is at its core binary, and thus it is theoretically possible to transport the repre-
sentation of the entire object, this is often bloated by additional fields and variables which may be
redundant or unimportant for the conveying of the object’s state. It also risks exposing potential
private implementation details, causing a big security risk. Additionally, as serialization must be ac-
companied by the complementary deserialization mechanism to be meaningful, deserialization of raw
data objects poses extreme security risks if a program sends faulty or even malicious data to attack a
device. Systematic serialization algorithms should always be a strict requirement. The optimization of
said algorithms also have a direct impact on the size of the serialized data, and hence, the performance
of the IPC mechanism.
GEL already comes with serialization- and deserialization systems for its mesh data structures. How-
ever as mentioned, it is only able to serialize directly into a file. Nevertheless, this could provide a good
starting point from which to generalize these systems, such that the serializer is agnostic towards what
16
==PAGE:17==
it outputs the data to. This could be seen as another act of decoupling, eliminating the dependency
of the serialization system to the file system through abstraction.
2.6
Common IPC Mechanisms
In the remainder of this chapter, we will go through several IPC mechanisms commonly offered by
most major operating systems. We will do some from the theoretical standpoint of how it could be
used with a decoupled graphical workbench, in which the visualization has been separated to an entirely
different process, which communicates with the main program through the IPC mechanism.
2.6.1
File System IPC
The File System is in itself technically an IPC mechanism, and is probably the most primitive. While
not specifically offered by the operating system in a manner intended for IPC, it does enable storing,
and thereby also sharing, of data between processes. All modern operating systems are file-based, and
most programming language implementations of the file system are completely portable and cross-
platform, making it one of the easiest methods to implement.
To use the file system for IPC, one or more processes must write files to a specific agreed upon
location or directory, while other process(es) read from it. A rudimentary way of knowing when such
a file has been written, would be to repeatedly check the file directory for changes with some interval.
However, both on Windows[11], Linux[12], and MacOS[13], the operating system offers some way of
receiving notifications when a file system event has occurred, including that of file creation or file
modify, within a specific location. This can be used so that when a process saves the data to a file,
the other process(es) will be immediately notified and can read the contents of the file.

restarts, and there is no functional requirement for the reader-process to be alive when the writer-

On top of that, since the act of reading and writing files is
such common practice, it would be possible to leverage this to allow a multitude of other programs to
interact with the outputted data, provided they can serialize the same way (such as by translating to
common file formats, albeit at a potential cost to efficiency).
2.6.2
Pipes
Pipes are another common form of IPC mechanism, and is probably the go-to for most standard IPC
situations. A pipe is a communication buffer established by the operating system, which provides a
means to read and write on either end of the pipe, or both if it is bi-directional. Within pipes there
are two types: Anonymous and Named.
Anonymous Pipes could be argued to be the most basic type of IPC mechanism. It can be created auto-
matically in a terminal by using the | symbol between two commands such as: "program1 | program2".
In this example, the pipe will be established directing the standard output of program1 to the standard
input of program2. Anonymous pipes can also be created directly within the code of a program when
spawning a child process, to work in a similar way, except the parent program has more control over
what data is sent to the standard input of the child program[16]. The parent program can also read
back the standard output of the child, and process it in whichever way it deems appropriate. This
essentially renders the child program a sort of tool used by the parent. In the duration of all of this,
the child process is entirely unaware of the parent.

Named Pipes are similar to anonymous pipes, except that they exist beyond the lifetime of the
processes at a system level, and are given a name. Other processes and applications can then connect
to the pipe through the operating system, by specifying the same name. The pipe itself is an abstrac-
tion of the file system, meaning it supports the same reading and writing operations, but this data
does not persist on the disk and exists instead a buffer inside the kernel[17]. When all connections to
a named pipe are disconnected, the buffer is removed.
By default, both reading and writing to a named pipe is blocking. This means that if a process
attempts to read data from a pipe that contains no data, it will block the process until data is available.
Similarly, if a process attempts to write while its buffer is full, it will be blocked until data has been
consumed. On the receiving side, this enables programs to effectively wait for data to be ready, taking
care of the notification problem automatically. However as a result of this property, it requires that
both programs are internally threaded and managed appropriately, so that this block doesn’t hinder
the rest of the program’s execution.

2.6.3
Sockets
Sockets are the basis of network programming, and allows the communication between two devices
across a network. Sockets can however also be leveraged on a local machine by using the localhost or
127.0.0.1 address. In both cases, the data sent and received through the sockets will transfer through
the kernel’s network layer, and thus always leaves the process. The socket receiving this data can just
as easily reside in another process on the local machine, as it could another machine across the network.
There are two main protocols in which sockets can communicate: TCP and UDP.
TCP, or Transmission Control Protocol, is a connection-based protocol, which establishes a con-
nection between client and server, ensuring reliable transfer of data. TCP connections are byte streams,
and thus there is no concept of message boundaries. When a data packet is sent, the reception is
confirmed with acknowledgement signals, and an error-checking algorithm ensures no data is lost or
corrupted. The TCP protocol also ensures packets are received in the order they are sent. These
features guarantee its reliability, but comes at the cost of extra overhead.
UDP, or User Datagram Protocol on the other hand, is a connection-less protocol that does min-
imal error checking. Across large networks, packets may arrive in the wrong order or even be entirely
lost. This is the case even on localhost. UDP is however faster, and makes for a suited protocol in
situations where speed is more important than reliability. It is also a message-oriented protocol, with
preserved message boundaries.
The usage of Sockets for local IPC generally adds more overhead than other forms of communica-
tion, but can be a very real consideration due to its wide support, and its ability to easily translate
into remote networked computation. This can be particularly useful if there is access to a supercom-
puter located somewhere within reach of the user, as described in the additional expert features in
Section 2.1.4. As the reliability of graphical workbenches is crucial, TCP appears as the only relevant
form to consider. Missing or incomplete data would require the user to redo and try again, defeating
the purpose of improving the usability of the tool.

track of the message’s boundary. This can complicate implementation, but ensures reliable transfer of
data of arbitrary size.

is also sometimes called an IPC Socket, as it runs locally on the computer only through internal
buffers[19]. It is interacted with in the exact same way as a non-Unix socket, however a particular
noteworthy difference is that the Unix Domain socket’s implementation of UDP guarantees reliabil-
ity[20]. This eliminates the weakness of said protocol, and could make it worth considering, if the
workbench is only intended to be run on the local system. UDP also supports broadcasting, which
means that the data processing engine (P2) could broadcast its data and any desiring visualizer could
receive it. This could for example trivially allow multiple visualizers or other services to be run side-
by-side.
2.6.4
Shared Memory
Shared Memory is a feature in operating systems that allows a process to allocate a section of memory,
that can also be accessed by other processes[21]. This section exists outside the address space of both
processes, and can be accessed using virtual pointers that map to that address in physical memory.
The benefit of shared memory is that it is one of the fastest IPC mechanisms, as it operates directly on
RAM much like the processes’ own memory. However it does not manage any type of synchronicity;
you must implement your own guards to protect against concurrent read/writes, which also needs to be
shared between the processes. Additionally, shared memory does not notify any processes of change,
and thus an alternate IPC mechanism must be used to signal the availability of the data, unless the
changes can be predicted or detected otherwise.
Shared memory can be accessed via a name, similarly to Named Pipes. As it is a raw section of
memory, it can store things directly without the need of serialization. It is also possible to allocate
an use the shared memory from the start, housing and manipulating the data the process wishes to
share inside the shared memory in the first place. This can completely remove redundancy, as the
20
==PAGE:21==
data is operated on directly in this memory, but will not work unless the data is stored contiguously,
such as with structs. Another drawback of shared memory is that the allocation size must be known
beforehand, and cannot be changed once it has been allocated.

Much like concurrent programming, using shared memory across multiple processes must be con-

corrupting the data. Such synchronization mechanisms must also be accessible to all processes. One
way to do this would be to embed it directly into the shared memory space that was allocated for the
data[22]. Another way could be to employ other IPC mechanisms, or if the operating system offers it,
named mutexes or other sharable synchronization mechanisms, to control access to the data.
For graphical workbenches, the nature of the data shared is typically dynamic in size, and thus the
requirement to pre-allocate shared memory with a definite size could be an obstacle. One simple way
to work around this, would be to determine an upper bound of memory size, and then allocate this
amount. However this will effectively waste system resources in a large portion of cases, and is also
not feasible for data that can be very, very large.
Another way is to stream the data, similar to TCP sockets, where the data is written to the shared
memory in chunks, and then read and compiled on the other end. This however eliminates the benefit
of avoiding redundant copies (as the whole dataset can no longer fit in the shared memory), and also
complicates things if there are more than 1 process reading from the memory. The last option is to
repeatedly allocate and de-allocate new shared memory, as the size demands change. This can be seen
as quite similar to dynamic allocation of in-process memory in C-based languages.
2.6.5
Other types of IPC
There exists more IPC mechanisms in the range of offerings by most operating systems, that we will
briefly mention here.
Events are named objects in the Windows, that can be fired by another process through the
same name[23]. Processes can wait on events to be fired to execute their logic. This can be useful
to coordinate efforts between different programs, but cannot transfer data. An equivalent of this on
other systems could be shared condition variables in the POSIX standard[24].
Signals are very similar to Events, except they target a specific thread. Signals are usually much
more low-level and are internally used by the operating system to trigger certain behaviors in threads
and processes, for examples to wake, or terminate said thread[25]. Programs may be able to leverage
custom signal handlers to achieve effects similar to Events, but without the ”broadcasting” nature of
Events.
Message Queues allow multiple processes to read and write to the a named queue in the operating
system’s kernel[26]. These are very similar to sockets, especially Unix Domain sockets with UDP, but
21
==PAGE:22==
sockets come with the benefit of being much more easily transferable to TCP/IP sockets, which extends
the flexibility of the program.
Memory-Mapped Files are a combination of Shared Memory and directly using the File System.
When a file is memory-mapped, either part or all of its contents are synchronized with a portion of
memory. Read and writing to this memory will modify said contents of the file. This is useful for
reducing RAM requirements of reading large files by reading it in windowed chunks, but can also be
used as an IPC mechanism, as these changes are reflected in any other process that maps to the same
file. The operating system will also hold these contents in its kernel buffers without flushing to disk
until necessary, potentially improving performance over naively writing and reading a file[27].
Shared Semaphores[28] and other synchronization mechanisms are also sometimes offered by some
operating systems, which while cannot be used to transfer data, can be useful when such data transfers
through other means require synchronization.

Methodology
The goal of this project, is to explore the applicability of decoupling on graphical workbenches, as a
means to achieve the goals laid out in Section 2.1.3. We also wish to along the way look into or reflect
on the viability of the additional ideas laid out in Section 2.1.4, as potential opportunities enabled by
this decoupling. Finally, we specifically want to focus on the potential of separating the visualization
from the processing into independent processes, and compare the different communication methods in
a direct comparison.
In order to achieve this goal, we will use GEL’s MeshEdit as our work case. We start by analyz-
ing the existing structure, in order to identify which parts constitute its tight coupling, and how it
affects the the usage and development of the tool. Then we will attempt to find the best approach to
perform decoupling, with emphasis on the geometry processing and visualization aspects of the tool.
This will be considered successful, when both of these can be ran as standalone programs, without
dependency on one another. The goal is then to implement several different Inter-Process Communica-
tion mechanisms into the two programs to allow them to communicate, and write down our learnings
along the way. Finally, we will measure the performance of each of these mechanisms, and use our
learnings to reflect on which method is most suited for both MeshEdit, and graphical workbenches
on a general level, as well as the overall results of the decoupling. The inter-process communication
methods will also be compared to traditional same-process parallelization.
The implementation will be made in C++, and will build directly upon the existing GEL library
as the foundation of our project. We will use the Visual Studio solution available inside GEL’s Github
repository[7] in Visual Studio 2022 on Windows 11. The implementation will consist of first modifying
and creating new code in the GEL library, and then using separate sub-projects inside the VS solution
to implement and compile the standalone separated processes. We will also implement automated
tests, to test the performance of the IPC systems.
The plan is to do all of this non-destructively; that is, we will make all modifications in new classes so
that they can be directly compared to the originals. We will employ agile development to explore this
decoupling process, and the final set of different IPC systems will be implemented as time allows.
The ultimate goal of this implementation is not to provide production-ready code, but rather to explore
opportunities and methods, and to lay foundational groundwork for some of these methods or more to
be implemented on a more robust level. Because of this, certain features of MeshEdit may be know-
ingly omitted from the new implementation, if they do not serve to further the goals of this project.
These will however be listed in a Limitations section at the end of the Implementation Chapter, and
suggestions on how to re-implement them will be provided.
3.1
Results Analysis
After the Implementation chapter, we will have a Results chapter, in which we will reflect upon the
goal list and how many of these were achieved, as well as to what degree.
We will also post the
results of the IPC performance measurements, and compare them to a range of other measurements
across other parts of the program. The performance measurements will be made by testing each of the
communication implementations across a range of test data. This will be measured as the additional
delay they will impart on the time between a command’s execution, and the mesh’s refreshing in the
Visualizer.
In the Discussion Chapter, we will discuss the features and opportunities with the implementation
of this project. Here, we will offer our thoughts on the applicability of decoupling on MeshEdit. This
will analyzed with respect to the goals, as well as the cost of development and projected mainte-
nance overhead. We will also consider the additional expert features laid out in Section 2.1.4, and
provide our input on the viability of achieving said features with our implementation as a basis. Fi-
nally, we will provide suggestions on how to take the provided implementations further, if one wishes to
make use of them, and generalize our analysis to a more broad understanding of a graphical workbench.

4
Analysis of MeshEdit’s Existing Structure
The first step in the implementation of this project, was to examine and analyze the existing structure
of GEL’s MeshEdit. In this chapter, we will go through the central components that pertains to the
usage of MeshEdit, as well as describe the connections between them and what role they serve. We
will also come to a conclusion as to its tight-coupled nature. This chapter is meant to give a good
understanding of the original structure, so that the implementation of the decoupling can be better
understood.
4.1
MeshEdit’s Central Structure
The MeshEdit program is primarily focused around three central classes; MeshEditor, Console, and
VisObj. All of these classes are responsible for the majority of the functionality of the workbench,
including both geometry processing and visualization features. Each of these depend on one another,
and their dependencies are deeply rooted in their implementation details.
4.1.1
MeshEditor
This class is responsible for housing all the data that is currently being worked on, as well as being
the primary entry point to the render that is displayed to the user. It contains an array of VisObj
instances, and a Console object it creates on initialization. This class provides all functionality of the
main UI window, including mouse and keyboard input, as well as responding to to resize events. In
addition, it also controls all of the meshes.
The MeshEditor class contains a large collection of command functions, which it can use with the
internal Console object. These are merely registered to the Console — The Console takes over any
responsibility of executing the functions. Thus, these solely exist in an anonymous namespace within
the MeshEditor source file. The functions however are unable to operate unless they are passed a
pointer to the MeshEditor, and so despite their execution being managed by the Console, they still
have to be registered through the MeshEditor.
4.1.2
VisObj
VisObj is another graphical class, that represents a geometric object in the editor, such as the Manifold
meshes.
It has its own display function, which renders the underlying Manifold, constructing a
ManifoldRenderer first if necessary. Many such Manifold Renderers exist, each correlating to a dif-
ferent render mode. Commands executed in the Console can cause this Manifold Renderer to be
reconstructed, to reflect changes in the mesh. The MeshEditor can also trigger a reconstruction, if
the render mode is changed via its hotkeys. The display method in is called from the MeshEditor’s
own display function, but also requires a reference to the Console object. The reason for this being
that the VisObj display logic also relies on the Console to control its behavior.
Only one VisObj is active in the editor at a time, and only this one is rendered.
4.1.3
Console
The Console class itself is also implemented with its own graphical features, and plays a part in the
UI presented to the user. It comes complete with a fully graphical text input, including data storing
the currently typed command, data controlling the caret, history of commands, and keyboard parsing
functions to manipulate these fields and trigger command executions. This can be seen in Figure 4
on the right. The keyboard input features rely on MeshEditor to retrieve them through the main UI
window, while MeshEditor rely on Console to handle them. The Console is also responsible for storing
and displaying any outputted text in its buffer, usually from commands printing outputs, which itself
must pass through MeshEditor to reach the Console.
The Console also allocates a map of Command objects, which maps strings to the registered com-
mand(s) by that name. Multiple commands may exist with the same name to account for overloaded
commands with different argument arity. The Console has the responsibility of parsing the string given
25
==PAGE:26==
as input into command name and arguments respectively, and also supports the ability to automati-
cally cast these arguments depending on the signature of the command. Despite it taking ownership
of the commands, these commands still depend on MeshEditor to access any meshes to process in the
first place.
4.2
Coupling Level
From a feature set standpoint, the overall tasks of the MeshEdit program is widely spread across all
these classes. These overall aspects consist of 1) Mesh Management, 2) Command System, 3) User
Input, and 4) Rendering.
Tracing these feature sets, we see that not one of them is isolated to its own module or compo-
nent, but rather all performed through part of multiple classes. Mesh Management is part VisObj
and part MeshEditor, Command System necessitates both MeshEditor’s registration implementa-
tion and Console’s input handling. User Input goes through two stages, firstly through MeshEditor
for hotkey functionalities, then passing the remaining keystrokes one-by-one for Console to handle,
rather than keeping all input logic to a single class which could render the others independent of this
functionality. Finally, Rendering goes through all classes, each of them having their own display
function which is eventually called from the render cycle root in MeshEditor.
All three classes are not only tightly-coupled to one another, but the feature set of visualization
and geometry processing are also very tightly-coupled, as their existence relies on the collaboration
of all three (or five, if counting Manifold and ManifoldRenderer) classes. The classes, in particular
MeshEditor, Console, and VisObj, are as a result also classes of very low coherence, as they do not
perform one well-defined function, but rather each plays a part of multiple independent tasks.

This tightly-coupled structure makes it difficult to maintain or modify the code, without needing
to go to all the other classes and react to the changes, causing further ripple effects all the way
through the chain. Replacing the rendering framework would not be easy, without tearing down all
of these classes and essentially rebuilding. Omitting any of them is also not possible, without making
the whole program functionless. Parallelization is also hard to do, as the Console runs synchronously
with the keyboard input functions, which is bound to the same render loop as the visualization. The
high interdependency also makes mesh modification hard to do concurrently.
One positive thing the current structure does, is that the Console’s reliance on MeshEditor for its
commands is handled externally through dependency injection. This is done by the Editor binding itself
to the function of the command using std::bind before passing the command on to the Console, who
does thus not in itself rely on MeshEditor. However, the mechanism for doing this is still dependent on
MeshEditor’s implementation of command registration, and so it could be argued that the commands
themselves still do not assert their own control over what dependencies they can inject.

5
Implementation
The goal of the implementation in this project was to recreate a decoupled variant of the original
MeshEdit, with the aim of accomplishing the goals laid out in Section 2.1.3. With the analysis of the
existing structure complete, we could move on to attempting to achieve this goal. The implementation
of the project can be seen as completing the following steps:
1. Split MeshEditor into two components; A Visualization component, and a Processing Engine
2. Transform mesh representation into a non-graphical component, that can be housed by the
Engine independent of visualization
3. Separate the built-in Console into its own standalone Command Interpreter class, and strip it of
its graphical representation
4. Enable Processing Engine to be operated standalone via standard input/console
5. Define communication needs between the Processing Engine and Visualizer
6. Implement Visualizer as standalone component that can link to the Engine
7. Modify Serialization system to suit IPC communication needs
8. Implement several different IPC mechanisms using Visualizer and Engine across separate pro-
cesses
The idea behind this plan was to focus first on decoupling MeshEdit by stripping it of its graphical
components, and enabling to run as a standalone Processing Engine. This could allow it to act as the
”core” of a workbench, around which a user interface could be wrapped. It would be responsible for
hosting the meshes, and provide the functionality to access and manipulate them, all while remaining
agnostic towards the manner of which the input is provided, and the way the output is used. To prove
this concept, we would use this design to implement a very basic standalone console application
running MeshEdit, in which the user is provided no graphical visualization at all, but still has complete
access to the entire MeshEdit command suite.
5.1
Mesh Editor Engine
To accomplish the goal of the console application, we first set out to create Mesh Editor Engine.
This class would take the mesh management responsibilities of the original MeshEditor, but without
the graphical aspect and input handling functions. This would be implemented in a new MeshEditorEngine
class, housed in the new Core namespace created for this project’s purpose. The class was built from
the ground up rather than being stripped down from a copy of the original MeshEditor, though many
of the implementations closely mimic the original function of that class.
MeshEditorEngine manages meshes in the same way as MeshEditor, but replaces the array of VisObjs
with a new non-graphical counterpart, named MeshObj. This new class contains the underlying Mani-
fold, and much like VisObj, is responsible for managing its state in the editor, such as storing backups
for undo functionality, and holding selection data. It however is stripped of all rendering functionality,
and solely manage the state of the mesh in the editor.
Meshes can be accessed from the owning Engine through the get_mesh and get_meshobj methods.
Rather than the original distinction between active- and index-based mesh accessors, the new im-
plementation boils this down to the two mentioned above, but overloaded such that if no index is
provided, it defaults to whichever is active.
To address the tight-coupledness of the original MeshEditor and Console classes, we chose to com-
pletely redesign the responsibilities of command execution and registration. We transformed the idea
of the Console into a simplified multi-purpose Command Interpreter, whose sole purpose was to pro-
vide command parsing utility, and return found commands when given an input. The responsibility of
28
==PAGE:29==
providing the input, as well as the responsibility of executing the output, then falls to whoever calls
upon it.
As a result of this responsibility shift, the MeshEditorEngine class was given the execute function,
which also serves as the main entry point for any user interface class to interact with the Engine.
This function simply takes an input, uses the internal Command Interpreter to parse and receive the
command, which the Engine then executes.
A notable difference between the command execution of MeshEditorEngine and the original Console’s
implementation, is the way errors are handled. In the original, when errors are encountered or com-
mands cannot be found, it will simply print an error message in the Console. This works for its use case,
but is not very flexible towards other kinds of user interfaces. As such, we chose to make execute throw
errors as an indication that something went wrong. This shifts the responsibility upwards, allowing
any class to determine itself how to respond to the error, rather than being bound to the MeshEditor’s
implementation of error handling. We designated Domain Errors to represent ”Command not found”,
and Invalid Argument errors represent syntax errors from the interpreter. Other exceptions indicate
an error in the execution of the command.
By not silently catching errors, we do not arbitrarily limit the options for outer classes on how they
wish to handle bad user input, especially when the intent of the MeshEditorEngine is to no longer be
both front- and backend, but rather isolated to a single backend-purpose. Should one wish to simplify
implementation, a shorthand helper function execute_s was also implemented, which simply catches
these errors and prints an error message much like the original MeshEdit program did.
Command print outputs is still a crucial part for the functionalities of the commands.
This was
implemented directly into MeshEditorEngine, as a callback function. Upon commands calling the
Engine’s print, the engine would internally call the function stored in its on_print field. This is by
default an empty function, but through adding a callback to this output, it allows the responsibility
of a user interface to be managed by an isolated frontend module, without adding a dependency from
MeshEditorEngine to it. A similar callback was implemented for on_command_executed. Internally,
this also allows the Engine to perform its own cleanups when commands have finished executing, if
needed.
During supervision, the developer of GEL expressed great interest in retaining the tab completion fea-
ture of the original Console. MeshEditorEngine provides support for this by exposing a tab_completion
function, which unlike the original Console’s variant, is a stateless function that instead takes a partial
input as argument, and returns the auto-completed input. This decouples this functionality from the
input system, making it far more modular and usable in many other contexts.
The remainder of the MeshEditorEngine, such as selection logic, closely follows that of the origi-
nal MeshEditor, and is not particularly relevant to discuss.
5.1.1
Command Interpreter
As mentioned before, the Console was replaced with a non-graphic Command Interpreter, whose sole
purpose was to store commands, and return pointers to them when the caller provides an input. This
class was also built from the ground up, but closely mimics the original Console’s structure in terms
of command object classes and storage data structures. It also retained all the functions that allow it
to parse an input string into a collection of arguments, including casting them appropriately based on
the type of command.
The CommandInterpreter’s main function parse_command takes an input string as well as an out-
put argument vector reference, and returns the command found by the first part of the input, along
with filling the argument vector with the remainder. It will return nullptr if no command can be
found. The Interpreter also comes with a iterate_commands function, which returns iterators for its
internal data structure. This is overloaded with a version taking a string argument, that will iterate
commands by a specific name. In addition, the tab_completion feature of the Engine is implemented
at the Interpreter level.
29
==PAGE:30==
The purpose of designing the class like this, was to further reduce overall coupling by enabling it
to be used as a multi-purpose interpreter, whereby the specific functionality of its use case can be
controlled by the outer class. While it is still only used in the MeshEditorEngine class as of this
project, it no longer enforces any functional dependency on the parent to which it belongs. Without
the responsibility of executing commands, the class doesn’t even rely on being bound to a Process-
ing Engine-type object at all; The CommandInterpreter simply provides isolated and self-contained
console-like input parsing to any class that may have such a need. To provide an example, it would
be possible to implement a remote console, that uses its own local Interpreter to parse and filter com-
mands, but never executes them; rather it communicates an ID of this command to a remote location
on which these IDs are synchronized, and the remote location executes them. It is also possible to
create an entirely new subclass of the underlying Command class, to allow for much more sophisticated
usage.
5.1.2
Simplification of Command Creation
Alongside the changes to the entire command system structure, we also simplified the process of
creating commands, to further decouple the dependency between the command system and Engine.
Previously as described in Section 4.1.1, commands existed as free functions in an anonymous names-
pace, which all required a pointer to the MeshEditor instance to perform their function. This used
dependency injection through std::bind to handle this dependency, but it still added a layer of
complexity as this dependency injection relied on an extra layer of functions found in the MeshEditor.
Commands were also themselves required to back up meshes before making changes, and forgetting
to do this would mean the loss of the undo functionality. Additionally, commands were also themselves
responsible for checking for the ”help” argument, and printing help text by themselves. These require-
ments both further decrease the coherence of all these systems combined, while needlessly complicating
the creation of commands, which goes against Goal 4 of 2.1.3.
To simplify this, we completely changed the structure of command registration and function bod-
ies. Commands could now be registered complete with any amount of dependency injection, with a
single function call directly to the Interpreter, anywhere in the Engine’s (or any other class’)
logic. This function would take a name, a help string, and then a lambda function, and can also au-
tomatically enforce both arity and argument types through its templated form. Lambdas are available
since C++ 11, and provides the opportunity to capture any desired variable in the scope of definition,
which functions similarly to std::bind but no longer forms a dependency on the Engine’s implemen-
tation to perform this. Commands can dictate themselves exactly what dependencies they want to
inject, at an individual level.
The screenshot below is a snapshot of the new Engine’s code, and demonstrates the increased readabil-
ity of this version’s implementation of command registration. The lambda in this example captures
the MeshEditorEngine through [this], providing the function implicit direct access to the Engine
without the need of a pointer.
Figure 11: Simplified command registration with the Command Interpreter.
It would also be possible with this for the lambda to capture any other external data without needing
to encapsulate or bind access to it through one of the other classes. This entirely decouples the Engine
30
==PAGE:31==
and Interpreter from the Commands in all situations. More examples can be found in the code inside
MeshEditorEngine.cpp.
In addition to the process of registering commands, this simplification also simplifies the responsi-
bilities of the command’s code. The command no longer has to check for the "help" argument, as
this is done at the Engine level, and will output the string passed as the help string on registration.
Additionally, commands were no longer responsible for telling the Engine to update when they had
made changes to a mesh. Instead, the command can declare intent to modify a mesh by using the
active_mesh_modify accessor function, which the Engine can use to track changes automatically,
giving the command no further responsibility other than to make its modifications and then exit.
The system behind active_mesh_modify also further decouples the command system from the en-
gine, allowing its implementation details to easily be changed without the need to update every single
command. For example, in the current version of this project, this function simply returns the active
mesh and sets a private flag inside the engine. However this could theoretically be changed to return
an object which inherits Manifold but may have a different function — such as to act as a ”sparse
changes” data structure that could be used in more sophisticated change-tracking systems. All of this
could be changed internally in MeshEditorEngine, without affecting any commands.

details of the lower-level ones. The design of the Interpreter decouples it from the editor, by allowing
it to act as a passive storage unit, agnostic to the manner in which objects returned by it are used,
thus removing the tight dependency between it and its parent class. Similarly, none of these classes
perform any user input retrieval at all, passing the responsibility of that to isolated modules specific
to this purpose.
This part of the project constitutes the decoupling of the geometry processing aspect of the origi-

5.3
Engine/Visualization Communication
With the current implementation, the Engine provides basic interface support through its input func-
tions, and its textual output functions. However it does however not have any visual outputs. Before
being able to build the graphical half of the decoupled MeshEdit, we first had to consider how the
communication would be done between the Engine and a Graphical Visualizer.
Our ultimate aim was to create an environment, in which the Engine exists passively without knowl-
edge of the outer process, but provide the ability for said process to hook into itself, in order to receive
updates whenever operations were completed which it can then use to display to the user however
it wants. This communication protocol was intended in such a way that a visualizer could be con-
nected to the Engine directly at first, but to facilitate ”middle-men” through the abstraction of the
communication layer. This would allow us to later mimic direct connections across separate processes
through the use of IPC mechanisms, which was a goal of this thesis. The Engine and Visualizer remain
completely unaware of the counterpart they are connected to.

The simpler this could be, the easier it would be to implement, and also the more flexibility it would

viable set of communication protocols that could accomplish the needs of an outer visualizing process.

• On Mesh Updated: Passes index and mesh
Called by the Engine whenever a mesh was modified in such a way, that it requires update to
the Visualizer.
This single callback-style communication signature made for the simplest implementation, with broad
space for the backend Engine to determine when and how to trigger updates in the frontend Visual-
34
==PAGE:35==
ization process. It uses the same dependency injection principle to avoid the coupling of the Engine
and its connected Visualizer. The index identifies the updated mesh by the index in which it is stored
in the Engine, which can be used to synchronize the meshes between the processes. The mesh itself is
the reference to the newly modified mesh.
With this, the Visualizer would be able to register a callback to this event, which would allow it to
simply await the Engine to notify of changes. This form of communication reduces coupling, and makes
it possible to change the logic of how the Visualizer reacts to changes without modifying the Engine.
Likewise, the Engine can also change how it tracks and handles changes, without the Visualizer being
affected. Both of these retain complete autonomy, and have no dependency on one another, with the
exception of this agreed-upon communication signature.
By additionally implementing this in an abstraction layer, it also becomes possible to completely
replace the Engine with other components implementing the same protocol. Figure 15(B) illustrates
the use of IPC mechanisms through a Server and Client object, which by the use of the same signature,
is able to intercept and handle the notifications in a manner that enables cross-process communication.
All without the need to implement IPC directly into the Visualizer or Engine, who remain indepen-
dent on these implementations. This design decision closely mimics that of the Dependency Inversion
principle, which is a known design pattern that help decouple systems (2.2.4).
5.4
Abstraction Implementation
We chose to make heavy use of abstract classes and inheritance in order to implement the commu-
nication layer for the reasons stated above, as well as to structure and organize the class hierarchy
and simplify development. Additionally, this design philosophy was also extended to a small range of
other components, to further improve the flexibility and open up more opportunity for fundamentally

always accompanied by a MeshEditorInterfaceExecutable — which is an unnecessary dependency.

5.5
Mesh Editor Visualizer
With the abstraction layers defined and implemented, we could now decouple the graphical half of the
MeshEdit program. The goal here was to reconstruct the same visualizer that was previously build
into MeshEditor, but as a standalone executable, and without its on-screen Console. As the developer
of GEL stated the interest in exploring the possibilities of completely replacing the rendering engine,
no effort was put into improving the implementation beyond its original state.
MeshEdit’s graphical capabilities rely on the OpenGL framework (through the use of the freeglut
toolkit), which is operated in C-style code. Thus, the original MeshEditor requires outside code to set
up the rendering environment, and to call its display and input functions.
We implemented the decoupled MeshEdit Visualizer in a very similar way, except all new classes
corresponding to this graphical half of MeshEdit were moved outside GEL and into the separate com-
piled program, just like the initialization code. In theory, doing this on a broader scale than done in
this project, could in conceivably eliminate all of GEL’s reliance on OpenGL entirely.

This class represents the graphical half of the original MeshEditor class. It contains all the same

already worked in a way very suited for the notification-style system used in the communication design.

it will update its pointer to, before flagging itself for re-render. As it doesn’t store meshes other than

5.5.2
ManifoldPointer
Through development, it became clear that there were two different types of Manifold references
passed through MeshEditorInterface: Managed and Unmanaged.
A managed mesh is when the
mesh exists in, and is managed by, the implementing interface class itself. A prominent example of
this is when that object is the Engine. To avoid redundant copies of potentially large data, we would
prefer for the Visualization process to simply access this mesh through a pointer to the managed mesh.
However this is not always possible if the Engine resides in a separate address space or might not
exist at all. For example, if a FileStreamClient loads a mesh from a file rather than an Engine, its
lifespan would only be for the duration of the scope in which the file is loaded. There are two ways
to solve this: One would be to require that all IPC-related communication clients must implement
their own Manifold storage, and more closely mimic the Engine. Another way would be to state that
meshes may come in two variants; either pointer form, or unique_ptr form, the latter of which would
indicate to the Visualizer, that they should take ownership of the mesh and handle its lifespan and
destruction itself.
For simplicity’s sake, we chose to make use of the second method, since the implemented MeshObjGL
class could easily be made to house the unique_ptr of a mesh, if needed. The ManifoldPointer
object was implemented to unify these two types, and is a struct with the following signature:

5.7
Parallelization Through Inter-Process Communication
The communication abstraction between the Engine and the Visualizer lays the groundwork for par-
allelization through the use of multiple processes, by implementing IPC systems in classes acting as
”middle men” between the two MeshEdit halves, just as seen in Figure 15. We will henceforth refer
to these as Communication Components. Communication components come in pairs, with one being
the Server sending data, and the other being the Client, receiving the data. These components make
use of the abstraction layer, representing the the functionality to receive meshes and to source meshes
respectively, and their use are tantamount to replacing the Visualizer and Engine components without
affecting the remainder of the system. The hook_interface function seen in the Visualizer could be
passed a Client component, as it also derives from MeshEditorInterface. In theory, these could also
be chained.
To implement a Server component: Hook into the Engine’s mesh update callback on initial-
ization, and then react to updates by serializing the mesh and transmitting it through a method of
your choosing.
41
==PAGE:42==
To implement a Client component: Derive from MeshEditorInterface and run a listening loop
in a thread upon construction, to listen for incoming messages from the Server. Whenever it detects
a message, read and deserialize the mesh from that message, and then fire the mesh_updated callback
function with the mesh. Care must be taken to implement the loop in such a way that it can be
gracefully stopped at any moment, when the Client is called for destruction. This should preferably
be done without any delay.
For the purposes of this project, we will use MeshEditConsole and MeshEditGLVisualizer as the two
separate processes for this part of the project. Servers will be implemented in the MeshEditConsole
program, and Clients in the MeshEditGLVisualizer program. The code of both of these programs
are changed, such that they create their respective components based on initial user input (or com-
mand line arguments) before running their main loop. MeshEditConsole is furthermore provided with
additional ”ipc” commands that change server implementation.
5.7.1
Serialization Changes
Through our analysis of MeshEdit’s original structure, we know that GEL already had a serialization
system for Manifolds. However, the Serialization class only creates an fstream, to which it writes
all the data. There is no functional dependency on this implementation, and thus this was needlessly
coupled. To leverage the existing system, we modified it such that the file stream was generalized
to a higher-level iostream instead.
fstream also derives from this, but this generalization allows
the serialization system to also support other streams, such as notably stringstream, without any
alternation to the rest of its code. Passing a iostream to its constructor would prompt it to use that
stream in its lifecycle. To provide backwards compatibility, we overloaded the constructor with the
original string& filename signature to automatically create an fstream.
stringstreams are useful for IPC components, as they allow the serialization to be written to an
in-memory object, whose bytes could be transmitted through other means such as sockets or named
pipes. Likewise, a message received from an IPC mechanism could be fed into a stringstream on the
receiving end, which the serialization system would now also be able to deserialize from.
This is the only change we made destructively; Making a new Serialization class would require deep-
sweeping changes to Manifold, ConnectivityKernel, ItemVector, and much more, which would all
be necessary to duplicate just to make them use the new Serialization class instead. In the interest
of time, we opted to simply modify Serialization itself instead.
5.7.2
Communication Component: File Stream
The File Stream components work with the idea of using the system’s file system to pass data be-
tween processes. The server receives mesh updates from the Engine, and stores the serialized data in a
file by a specific name. The client uses the operating system’s available notification features to detect
when a watched file is updated, and immediately loads and deserializes it. Both of these are connected
by instantializing them with the same file path.
Server Implementation: Whenever the Server receives an update, it creates an fstream at its
given path, which is set to overwrite any file that already exists there. It writes a processing flag of 0
to the first byte, then serializes the mesh into the file stream. When the serialization is complete, it
seeks back to the beginning of the first byte, and changes the flag to 1.
Client Implementation: The Client uses the Windows API ReadDirectoryChangesW feature, to
receive updates from the system whenever a file in its watch changes. To accomplish this, it parses the
given file path into the directory of the file, and retrieves a handle to this directory. An infinite loop runs
in a thread which calls ReadDirectoryChangesW with the handle and the FILE_NOTIFY_CHANGE_LAST_WRITE
flag and waits for a response. Whenever a change is detected through this, it compares the file name
with the targeted file, and if they match, it will proceed to the mesh loading step. In this step, the first
byte of the file is read to ensure that it is not currently being written to, and then the mesh is deserial-
ized from the remainder of the bytes, and a callback is fired to mesh_updated with an unmanaged mesh.
42
==PAGE:43==
Through development, it was found that the events notified by ReadDirectoryChangesW triggered
twice each time the file was written to by the Server. The first of these events would consistently occur
while the Server was still writing to the file. This is likely due to the truncation of the file that occurs
prior to writing, but this could not be distinguished in available API. Both of these modify events
would occur, regardless of flushing or closing the file. This created concurrency issues where the Client
would read incomplete or corrupted data, if it responded to the first notification.
This is the reason for the processing flag set in the first byte. When the Client received the first
update, if the server was not finished with the file, the first byte would always indicate 0. On the
second notification, this would be 1, indicating that the data is complete. In all the testing performed,
this last fact remained consistent on our system, however for small meshes, it was sometimes also 1
for the first update, and thus we cannot guarantee this reflects across all systems. This may not be
robust, but due to time constraints, we did not deem it priority to prove guarantee (See Section 7.0.2
in the Discussion chapter).
In earlier iterations, the Client relied on repeatedly retrying deserializing the mesh until it worked,
as a means to read the data as soon as it would be available. It also used timing-based discarding of
events that occurred too quick after each other, to avoid double-notifications. This was not a very
elegant solution, and also had the weakness wherein if the mesh deserialization took longer than the
tolerance level, the subsequent notification would be handled anyway, causing a double-loading of large
meshes.
The File Stream Client also implements the poll function, which allows it to check for the exis-
tence of a file, even if the change notification was missed, such as if the Client was only created after
this file was saved.

5.7.3
Communication Component: Named Pipe
The Named Pipe components work by registering a named pipe in the operating system, using a
pre-defined name shared between the Server and the Client in their source code. The Server sends
messages containing the serialized mesh data through the pipe, which the Client can read in a FIFO
queue style. Named pipes on Windows have the property that the creator must await the connection
of another process before the pipe can be used, and this operation is blocking. As such, the server
must thread this blocking connection and then use thread-safe synchronization to handle mesh updates.
Server Implementation: The Server creates an empty stringstream and launches a thread in
which it creates a named pipe and awaits client connections. Should any of this error, it will sleep for
1 second and then try again. Once a Client connects to the pipe, this loop exits, and the thread enters
the main loop. In this loop, the thread is put to sleep via a condition variable, until data is ready
to be sent.
When the Server receives a mesh update from the Engine, it will clear the currently pending
stringstream, and then serialize the new mesh into that. Then, it will signal the looping thread
through the condition variable to wake up, and this thread will write the contents of the stringstream
into the pipe it created. Mutexes are used to ensure the stringstream and condition variable remain
protected from race conditions.
Client Implementation: The Client launches a thread which enters a two-stage loop, that runs
as long as its running variable is true. The first of these stages attempts to connect to the named
pipe, if it exists. It does so repeatedly with 1-second sleeps, until it finally establishes connection. Then,
the second stage of the loop is run. In this stage, it repeatedly reads from the pipe, which blocks it
while there is no data available. When there is, it will receive and feed the data into a stringstream
until the message boundary is met. When the full message has been read, it creates a new unmanaged
Manifold, and deserializes the stringstream into it, and finally pass it to the mesh_updated callback.
Should the pipe ever become broken (such as when the Server shuts down), it will return to the
first stage of the loop. When running becomes false, this loop will exit, and the thread will gracefully
terminate. When the Client is called for destruction, it will set running to false and close the handle
to the pipe itself, causing the Client’s pipe handle to become broken, which will exit the currently
blocking pipe read, if there is any.

5.7.4
Communication Component: Socket
This Socket component is an implementation of the TCP/IP Socket protocol, capable of running
across a network. While in theory this should already be possible with the current code, we did not
have the means to test this across multiple systems, and so it has only been tested with the 127.0.0.1
(localhost) address.
The Socket components create a websocket through the operating system’s features, and uses those
to communicate the data. As mentioned with TCP, this data is in the shape of a byte stream with no
boundaries on the messages, and thus it is necessary to implement our own distinction. It is common
practice for these kinds of boundary-less messages to be lead by a length value, which is the method
we also employed for the Socket components.
Server Implementation: To create a TCP Socket Server, the component creates a listen socket
to listen for incoming connections.
This socket is created on initialization.
After initialization, a
thread is run in a loop which constantly awaits incoming connections on this socket. When a con-
nection is found and accepted, the resulting Client connection is stored in a client socket field in the
object.
45
==PAGE:46==
When the Server receives a mesh from the Engine, it will serialize the mesh to a stringstream
and retrieve the length of this stream in bytes. This value will be sent to the client socket, using a
pre-defined amount of bytes to store this value. In the current implementation, this is sizeof(size_t)
bytes. After this, the serialized data is sent to the socket. Both this and the connection acceptance
operations are protected by mutexes, to ensure the client socket is not replaced halfway through writing.
Client Implementation: The Client is constructed with an address and a port, with which it
will create a socket and attempt to connect to. This code runs in a thread, which loops infinitely with
1-second sleeps each time it fails to connect to a Server. When a Server finally accepts the connection,
it enters the second stage of its loop.
In the second stage, the Client will first read the same sizeof(size_t) bytes representing the
serialized size from the socket. Reading from a socket is a blocking operation, which holds the thread
asleep until data is available. Once it receives the size, it is capable of knowing how many bytes to
read for the subsequent message. This message is read in chunks, the size of which is determined
by the buffer the Client allocates (default 512 bytes, as defined by the DEFAULT_BUFLEN preprocessor
directive). On each chunk, the data is added to a stringstream and the amount read is subtracted
from the known size.
When this size reaches 0, the serialized data is completed. If any bytes were read, it creates a new
Manifold and deserializes the stringstream into this. The loop then goes back to the size reception
stage, until the connection is broken, at which point it goes back to the connect stage.
Currently, whenever a new Client attempts to connect to the server, it will overwrite the existing
client socket, resulting in the server only ever being able to manage 1 connection at a time. For the
purposes of this project, this was all we needed it to do, however should it be desired to allow multiple
connections and to broadcast mesh updates to them all, this could be done by replacing this with a
collection of client sockets, and iterating over them when sending the data.

5.7.5
Communication Component: Shared Memory
The Shared Memory components work by allocating an amount of memory through the operating
system’s Shared Memory support, equal to the serialized mesh’s size. As Shared Memory works by
name, by identifying the same name, the pointer received points to the same space in system memory.
To handle the synchronicity concerns of this mechanism, we make use of Named Mutexes offered by
the Windows API.
As shared memory is allocated with a specified size, it is not possible to allocate the memory once
and reuse it for all subsequent edits, without vastly overstating memory sizes to account for large
meshes. We therefore chose to go with the re-allocation system, in which the shared memory would be
re-allocated if the size demands increase above the current allocation, or falls below a tolerance. This
tolerance is configurable, and could be set to 0 to always re-allocate.
Since the system underlying shared memory acts just like raw memory, and therefore has no way
of notifying the waiting Client that new data is available, we used a Named Event to signal from the
Server process to the waiting Client process that data is available. There could be several other ways
to implement this, such as by the use of one of the other IPC mechanisms to send a message, but this
was chosen due to its suitability for this task and to cover a wider range of mechanisms.
Server Implementation: The Server initializes the Named Mutex and Named Event it uses for
communication with the Client. When it receives a mesh update, it will first create a stringstream
and serialize the mesh to it. It will then retrieve the size of this serialization.
If this size is more than the tolerance smaller than the last allocated size, or if the size is greater
than the last allocated size, the Server will close its handle on the shared memory, and allocate a new
one by the size of the serialized data. It will also allocate extra space to store the size of the serialized
mesh, similar to the socket. This is necessary as the Client cannot know ahead of time how many
bytes to read from the memory, especially not when its size doesn’t always match.
The size along with the serialized data is then copied over to this shared memory, and the named
event is signaled. All of this is protected by the named mutex, so that the Server does not write to the
memory while the Client is reading from it.
Client Implementation: The Client starts by creating two events; One named, being the one
shared with the Server, and another anonymous which it uses in its own logic. It then launches a
thread which runs an infinite loop.
In this thread, the Client awaits either of these two events. This is a blocking operation that will
only return when one of them is signaled. When this occurs, the Client checks if the signal came from
the update event. In this case, it opens up a view of the shared memory, and reads the size data
written to the first portion of the memory, then subsequently reads that amount of data afterwards
and writes it to a stringstream. Unlike the Server, the Client does not need to know the size of the
shared memory ahead of time, but it does need to know the boundary of the serialized data within,
especially if the data is smaller than the allocated size.
When this completes, it creates an unmanaged Manifold, and deserializes the stringstream into
that. When it’s done, it closes the view of the shared memory and releases the named mutex, causing
the Server to once again be the only entity with a handle to the memory. This design enables the
Server to control the de-allocation of the memory, as shared memory is only deallocated when all views
of it are closed.
The Shared Memory Client furthermore has all of this memory reading logic in its poll function.
This allows the receiving entity to request a manual update, such as if the shared memory is already
available and waiting, even if the signal was missed due to, for example, being initialized after the
Server’s event signal.

5.8
Parallelization in a Single Process
Up until now, we have focused a lot on decoupling for the purposes of separating two processes, and
different methods of establishing IPC. However, it is not the only way that the goals laid out for this
project can be achieved. While it may have its benefits to disconnect the two processes, in many cases
these benefits may simply not be necessary, in favor of the reduced complexity that comes with paral-
lelizing the program in a single process. So for completeness, we wish to also explore the opportunities
for single-process parallelization achieved by this decoupling.
The Communication Component system implemented for the IPC components has no functional limi-
tation to IPC and cross-process communication systems. In fact, there is no limitation for it to be used
in pairs at all. The abstraction enables the system to be used in a much wider range of applications
too, such as chaining components as ”building blocks” to process a signal between the source and its
destination. One notable such use would be to create internal parallelization.
To create a parallelization component, we used the existing system to implement a class which layers
on top of the Engine and receives the input from the interface in its place, and then executes on the
Engine using a parallel thread with appropriate thread-safety.
48
==PAGE:49==
Two such classes were implemented in this project: MultithreadedEngine and MultithreadedEngineQueue.
The MultithreadedEngine is capable of being constructed around a MeshEditorEngine, or able to
manage its own internal copy. It then starts a thread that runs an infinite loop which collects user
input from the command line, exactly the same as MeshEditConsole. Since this runs in a thread
separate from the main thread, its execution does not halt the interactivity of the visual window. This
class is more rudimentary, as it takes over responsibility of collecting user input through just using the
standard input, making it suitable for demonstration and testing purposes, but unsuitable for more
advanced control.
The MultithreadedEngineQueue is the opposite of this, in that it leaves control of its input up
to the owning class, allowing the same level of flexibility as the Engine itself.
It inherits from
MeshEditorInterfaceExecutable, and thus provides an execute function on par with the Engine.
When this execute is called, it pushes the given command into a queue and notifies a condition vari-
able, which is currently holding a thread created on initialization, waiting in an infinite loop. Once this
thread is woken up, it will pop commands off the queue sequentially, and execute them on its stored
Engine. When the queue is empty, it goes back to sleep.
This class’ implementation essentially means that when you execute on the Multithreaded Engine
in the main thread (or any other thread), it will pass the responsibility of actually performing the
execution to an internal worker thread, whenever it is available. While the prior Multithreaded Engine
class is used in the Visualizer demo program, the latter class is used in the performance tests, as it
allows input from any source, such as code itself.

These classes achieves many of the same decoupling benefits, without being multi-process or require

is no different from all of the IPC mechanisms.
5.9
Complete Visualization Replacement: Blender Integration
As mentioned in the original set of goals for this project, the idea of completely replacing the ren-
dering framework was something that was considered as part of the potential gains of decoupling,
and something we wished to explore further. With the structure that was developed throughout this
implementation, this quickly became very feasible, to a degree beyond our initial expectation.
As an additional experiment on top of the project’s original plans, we implemented a proof-of-concept
of entirely replacing the Visualization framework with another program, by implementing a MeshEdit
integration into Blender. Blender is a free and open source 3D modelling software, which comes with
powerful rendering capacities and is widely used for professional 3D applications[29]. Blender comes
with the ability to execute Python scripts, which served as the perfect way to implement a cross-
over integration as Python would be able to support many of the same IPC mechanisms we used in
MeshEditConsole.
To create the integration, we made a script inside Blender 3.6 using the Asyncio library to create
Sockets, allowing it to be used in conjunction with MeshEditConsole’s Socket Server.
By reverse-
engineering the deserialization system, we were able to read the bytestream in the correct manner to
retrieve the vertices and positions encoded into the serialization. This essentially gave us the com-
plete internal data of the transmitted Manifold, which we could use to translate into a format that
Blender could understand. Using this data and the internal Blender API, we could recreate the mesh
in Blender’s 3D scene, which would serve to visualize it. Just like the IPC components, this mesh
updates in real-time whenever changes are made in the MeshEditConsole program.
One implementation obstacle to note specific for this integration, is how Blender runs its scripts
synchronously, and any infinitely-running script would completely freeze the program. As the Asyncio
library is not actual parallelization but rather coroutines, this made it not possible run the event loop
responsible for managing Asyncio’s cycles. To circumvent this, we used Blender’s Modal Operators,
which is a form of event listener you can create in Blender.
This Modal Operator registers a timer for every 1 millisecond, which runs the Asyncio event loop
1 step, rather than running it until completion. This is done by calling event_loop.stop() right
before event_loop.run_forever(), as it will immediately be flagged to stop. By doing this, control
is relieved back to Blender between every cycle, and the program remains responsive.
Now, when launching MeshEditConsole and using the Socket Server on port 27015, running the script
inside Blender connects to the Engine, and meshes sent from there are reconstructed as Blender meshes
inside the scene. Using Window > Toggle System Console in Blender opens up a console that lets
you view the outputs from the script. While running, it will continuously await meshes until the user
cancels it. The Modal Operator was coded to respond to right-clicks or escape key presses to cancel
the loop. Modal Operators run entirely in the background, and thus this occurs when right-clicking
or pressing escape anywhere in the Blender window.

==PAGE:51==
Figure 26: MeshEditConsole connected to Blender via a Python script and Socket connection.

The small console on top of the Blender window shows it reading the data from the

The ability to completely visualization process is a particularly powerful one for graphical workbenches,
as it allows the developers of these in-house tools to save time and effort developing and maintaining
their own rendering implementations, while also potentially being even better. This allows them to
focus more on adding more features and functionality to the tools themselves, or to work on something
their time is better spent on. This script demonstrates the ability of using cross-process communica-
tion to enable bridging into scriptable programs such as Blender. There could possibly be very real
benefits to using a program like this, as it may simply be significantly faster in its render methods,
or may house a range of other powerful features that may be unfeasible to recreate in the graphical
workbench like MeshEdit.
There is also no reason why this should be limited to Sockets. Sockets were just chosen at random, as
it was already known that Python would support them, but it may be equally possible to use faster or
more robust IPC mechanisms provided Python can communicate with them too. Likewise, if another
program features another scripting language, the offerings of that language may dictate which IPC
mechanism will be put in use to communicate to it. This proof-of-concept demonstration also trans-
lates into other library integrations that don’t need to be communicated to using IPC in the first place.
It should be noted that this Blender script is a prototype only, and it is not suitable for real use.
Further work would be required to make this a robust Blender implementation. However it constitutes
a starting point, and a proof-of-concept that this is possible. The Blender script is saved directly in
the .blend file, which is included in the code for this project.

5.11
Limitations in the Current Implementation
The programs implemented in this project represent MeshEdit as a decoupled process, however it has
several limitations that were intended by design but not deemed a priority to implement, as well as
features that were lost from the original MeshEdit program. Most of these are conscious omissions
due to time constraints, which are not meant to reflect a final version. We will briefly touch upon
those here as an acknowledgement, and the provide our suggestion on how they would in theory be
implemented.
• Support for only 1 Manifold
The current implementation only supports 1 mesh through communication, despite the fact that
both MeshEditorEngine and ME_OpenGL have the ability to hold an array of meshes. This is
because all IPC components call update_mesh with index = 0. This is simply a placeholder. To
properly implement this, all communication components would need to add the index to their
message, such that it could be decoded alongside the serialized data. It may also be considered
whether to additionally add callback for on_active_mesh_changed to synchronize active mesh
too, or whether the active index in both processes should just be individual.
• No rendering-related console variables
As the Console was stripped off the Visualization, ME_OpenGL no longer has a way to change
its rendering-related console variables. For now, these have simply become regular fields in the
ME_OpenGL class that just never change. These includes things such as display shading and display
gamma, as well as notably active mesh index. The Visualizer can however still change render
modes with the numerical hotkeys. Our suggestion to re-implement these is to implement them
at a UI-level, by creating buttons or sliders, or alternatively using the suggestion to implement
CommandHouser directly into ME_OpenGL to give it access to local commands, which it could
change through its own command line.
• One-Way Communication
Currently, the MeshEditorInterface only allows for one-way communication. This could how-
ever be extended with for example a Execute Command: Passes string protocol which would
allow also passing commands back to the Engine, at the cost of complicating the necessary im-
plementations for the IPC components. This was deemed outside the scope of this project, but
the groundwork was laid out. Suggestions made elsewhere in the thesis to implement remote
command execution would necessitate this change.
• Reduced responsiveness of Visualization
It was observed that whenever MeshEditGLVisualizer was run with any threaded IPC Client,
the mouse drag responsiveness was jittery. This is theorized to be because the thread disturbs
the OpenGL main thread, and the solution would likely to be to integrate this directly into the
OpenGL main loop, but this was not deemed a priority for this project and never confirmed.

Results
In this Chapter, we will look back on the results of the project, and evaluate the accomplishments
of this process with respect to the goals laid out in Section 2.1.3. We will first walk through these
goals, and provide a brief evaluation of each based on the current version of the project. We will then
perform a Performance Measurement on each of the communication components across a range of test
meshes. At the end, we will measure the performance of MeshEdit as a whole, to provide comparison
to the rest of the program.
The subsequent Discussion Chapter will be used to discuss these results.
6.1
Goal Accomplishments
At the start of this thesis, we put forth an analysis of what makes a good graphical workbench for
research. In it, we laid out a series of goals, of which our objective was to figure out to what level the
act of decoupling the program would achieve. We now have our decoupled version of MeshEdit, and can
53
==PAGE:54==
evaluate based on those key points how much the program has changed. Below are the goals reiterated,
along with our evaluation on the difference in the accomplishment level of this goal compared to earlier
versions.
• The program should specialize in a specific workflow or domain
This goal has not changed, as it pertains to the basic foundations of a graphical workbench. The
decoupling of such a workbench does not in itself alter its function. It could however be argued
that the reduction in development complexity could lead to further optimizations. For MeshEdit,
the program already specialized in the half-edge model type, and this has not changed with our
implementation.
• The program should be separated into clear, distinct modules
Previously, MeshEdit comprised of three central classes that controlled almost every aspect of the
workbench. Now, the decoupling has enabled us to separate the processing and visualization parts
into distinct modules. In addition, the command system has also been separated into its own
module, now being capable of being plugged into many different use cases. Mesh representation
can also be said to be distinctly separated into a visualization representation, and an editing one.
• The program should be able to have one or more of its modules changed, without
affecting the rest of the program to a notable degree
This has been achieved as clearly demonstrated by the Blender integration. In the previous
MeshEdit, this would not be possible at all, without going through steps equivalent to decoupling.
• The program should be easy to extend with additional commands and features
The decoupling greatly optimized the command registration system, and enabled it to become
much simpler than it previously had been. This was technically already possible to adapt into
the non-decoupled version too, however the work required would be greater than it was on the
decoupled version, as it would require bigger changes to the help- and mesh update systems.
• The program should be able to continue being operated while it is busy
The previous MeshEdit became completely unresponsive when any command was executed in the
console, until it was completed. In the decoupled version, parallelization allows for the program
to continue being operated. All IPC components enable this, and the parallelization layer offered
by the MultithreadedEngine class also does.
• The program code should be easy to maintain
Through our qualitative assessment of the work required to implement changes in the early stages
of the project and the late stages, we would deem the maintenance costs to be significantly
reduced. This evaluation is based on the difficulty of implementation in the early stages of the
project compared to the late. Now, changing how the Engine handles mesh updates can be done
without altering every command, and likewise changing how the Command Interpreter parses or
stores its commands is able able to be changed without modification to any outer class. These
are merely some of the examples described in the report.
• The user interface should be Console or Console-like
Both the coupled and decoupled variant of MeshEdit have Console or Console-like user interface,
which both provided a command input format suited for expert users. However the decoupled
variant comes with the benefits of using the system terminal. This could theoretically be imple-
mented somewhat trivially into the coupled MeshEdit as well however, so it cannot be said that
the decoupling is what achieved this.
• The program should be responsive and reliable
Reliability remains consistent between both versions, and responsiveness is the same (with the
exception of the bug described in Section 5.11).
Reliability could be slightly reduced when
considering Sockets, however the use of the TCP protocol mitigates this to a large extent. We
would assess that the decoupling was made without sacrifices to either of these two key points.
54
==PAGE:55==
6.1.1
Additional Expert Features
On top of the basic goals laid out, we also put forth a range of additional expert features, which could
be looked upon as ”nice-to-haves” rather than needs. We will also briefly go through these, though in
this case with respect to the opportunity provided by the decoupling project, as actually implementing
them was outside the scope of the project.
• The ability to work with a range of standard formats
Both MeshEdit and the newly-decoupled MeshEditConsole can work on the same selection of
file formats: .obj, .off, .x3d, .bhm. More could be implemented, but the manner in which
these formats are saved to/loaded from is still coupled closely to the save_mesh and load_mesh
commands. Decoupling this into its own system of interchangeable format components could im-
prove the ability to add more, similar to the implementation of the Communication Components
in this project.
• The ability to be automated/controlled by another program
This is not achieved without the inclusion of two-way IPC communication that can allow the other
end to communicate commands backt to the Engine. If implementing this however, the Engine
could be controlled by any program using the same IPC methods, similar to how the Blender
script was achieved. A better way perhaps would be to implement the proposed Anonymous
Pipe Server, which would listen to standard input, and serialize to standard output. Then, other
programs could for example spawn "MeshEditBackend.exe" as a subprocess, and pipe inputs
into it.
• The ability to be scripted for repeated complex workflows
This could be achieved in the same manner as the above feature. Alternatively, PyGEL could
be extended to provide exposure to a ME_OpenGL instance, such that it could be used to script
inputs for this Visualizer. This would completely circumvent MeshEditorEngine.
• The ability to output data for another program to receive
This is achieved through using IPC to communicate this data, which can be received by any
other program, as demonstrated with both Blender and the decoupled MeshEditGLVisualizer
program. This could also be achieved with an Anonymous Pipe component as described above.
• The ability to offload the workload to another computer, possibly across a network
This is actually achieved by the Socket Server, if it can be verified to work across networks. In
this case however, it would find little use unless two-way communication is implemented.
6.2
Performance Measurement
A main goal of this project was to look into the feasibility of using cross-process communication in
terms of their impact on potential performance. In this section, we will look into the performance of
the IPC implementations, and compare them with the rest of the program.
To measure the performance of the different IPC and communication implementations, we created
an automated test bench which initializes an Engine and sequentially creates both a Server and a
Client. The Server is controlled via a thread that will send a specified command to the Engine, note
down the time, and then wait on a condition variable before proceeding again. The Client is hooked
up to a callback which will record the difference to the Server’s noted-down time, and then notify the
condition variable to run the command from the Server again.
This happens a certain number of iterations, across a certain collection of meshes, through a cer-
tain set of component tests. Before running the first iteration, the Server loads the mesh, causing an
update to the Client which is not recorded. This first iteration serves to both pre-load the mesh, as
well as to wait for the Client to connect before running the real tests, so that it does not skew the
results. This is more representative of real-world use, where the Client would be connected way prior
to actual mesh updates.
The number of iterations, selection of meshes, and command to execute are defined at the top of
55
==PAGE:56==
the source file for this compiled program. The interfaces to test are controlled down in the main func-
tion at the bottom, where they can be individually left in or commented out. Running the test will at
the end output a table-like display to console, stating the average latency across all record times, on
each mesh and each communication component.
The command we tested with was the debug_update_mesh command that was added during devel-
opment of the IPC components, which only calls active_mesh_modify without making any changes,
causing the engine to broadcast the mesh at the end (as well as copy the mesh to the internal undo
backup slot). Below each mesh is the serialized size of the mesh, which represents the total amount of
data each IPC mechanism had to transfer. The ”No decoupling” row measures solely the time spent
executing the test command.
These tests were ran on Windows 11, with an Intel i7-8700k processor and 32GB 3600MHz RAM.
The test program was compiled in Visual Studio’s Debug configuration, but was executed outside of
Visual Studio. The File Stream components were tested on 4 different drive types separately: NVMe
SSD with Operating System installed, NVMe SSD without OS, SATA SSD, and a SATA HDD at
7200RPM.

7
Discussion
Decoupling and loosely-coupled systems are particularly beneficial for the prospect of a graphical work-
bench. As these programs are experimental in nature and commonly subject to either continuous or
sporadic development, the ability to have a better overview and understanding of the code saves large
amounts of time in the longer run, compared to more static programs. Additionally, they often rely on
other technology or frameworks to ease the development requirements, allowing its developer to put
more time into the core functionality of the tool instead. This can range far wider than merely the
rendering framework. They will often use the best technology available at the time, but this can change
rapidly, especially as new frameworks emerge or the skills and knowledge of the developer expands.
As such, to have a more modular structure that allows these components to be updated, maintained,
or even replaced, with minimal effort to the rest of the system, is particularly useful when working on
an evolving system such as a research-oriented graphical workbench.
We have demonstrated through the implementation of this project that decoupling an existing sys-
tem of a graphical workbench has significant benefits to implementation and ability to be maintained
and further developed. We’ve shown through the implemented systems that the decoupling allows for
parallelization even up to the level of separate processes. The implementations in this project also
demonstrated the ability to completely replace the rendering component with a completely different
option, which is a notably big example of available technology that may rapidly evolve or become
outdated. In addition to the interfaces visible to the user, the internal decoupling of various compo-
nents in code was also the reason why systems such as command registration could be simplified. This
simplification then goes on to further accomplish the goals of a good graphical workbench.
However, when the development cost is a factor, it is always a balancing point around whether
you should put effort into making a code base more future proof, or just leave it as ”good enough”
on the premise that such maintenance might never be needed. Indeed, in the case of MeshEdit, it
took significant work to decouple the system from its coupled state — It could be argued that this
amount is equal or even higher than the amount of work saved in future developments. That said,
these benefits last long-term, and so we would assess that this restructuring is highly worth it, and
that the earlier it is done, the more benefits it will have.
To evaluate on the separation of the geometry processing and visualization processes in the tool,
the implementation of this has a lot of benefits, and can be said to be a prime target for decoupling
efforts. As described in the Background Chapter 2.1, the fundamental process of the visualization is
often entirely independent of the implementation of the remainder of the program. However when
tightly-coupled to the rest of the system, it becomes hard to distinguish it from the remainder of the
code, and thus harder to replace or update. By decoupling it from the processing, the independent
fundamental process is also separated into its own independent implementation. The isolation of this
feature not only accomplishes the benefits described earlier in this chapter, but also narrows its im-
plementation down to a single collection of files, making maintenance notably easier, or allowing you
to practically ignore it when working on other parts of the code. We found this separation to have
significant impacts on the development of the project.
This separation also allowed the two halves to be separated into individual independent programs.
One of the big questions of this project was whether or not this would impart a large impact on
performance, and how the impact compared to the remainder of the tool. Here, to what level modern
SSDs approach speeds perceptually equivalent to physical RAM was of particular interest. Evaluating
on the results of the IPC and Communication Component systems, the impact imparted by all IPC
mechanisms are significantly smaller than the cost of the remainder of the system, and in many cases
is completely negligible. Notably, using the File System on an NVMe SSD was the fastest out of
the group, and its impact was indeed imperceptible in our test use. It is an interesting discussion to
consider why this was faster than some of the other mechanisms that didn’t rely on a disk at all, but
we are unable to make any conclusions without looking into it further.
It still remains up for debate whether this effort of the implementation of the IPC systems is worth
it; These mechanisms required significant research and debugging to implement, and are not a very
portable solution as they depend closely on the OS that the program is running on. Notable effort
must be put in to make a portable, multi-platform solution. The decoupling of the system already
61
==PAGE:62==
accomplishes the large majority of the goals from 2.1.3 alone, and the additional costs of developing
cross-process communication must always be weighed against the needs.
If the purpose is just for limited use on a known set of platforms, it may however still be worth
considering. The benefits of separating the processes allows for things such as closing down the Engine
without losing the Visualization or vice-versa, and the opportunity to integrate with other programs
such as Blender might be a considerable benefit in few cases. But for most use cases, it might be the
best suited approach to simply employ the MultithreadedEngineQueue system. Unless separate pro-
gram integration is a necessity or direct upgrade, using single-process parallelization both accomplishes
the greatest resulting performance, and also imparts the least development cost on the implementation.
In the case that separate-program functionality is a considerable upgrade, it might also be more
interesting to look at the prospect of the Anonymous Pipe method, which was not implemented in this
project. By compiling a MeshEditEngine.exe program which simply reads and writes to the standard
inputs and outputs, it would be possible to spawn it as a subprocess rather than separate process (i.e.
owned by the parent process, and hidden from view) without compromising on its modularity and
flexibility. This could be done very akin to that of FFmpeg[30], which is a widely used media tool that
you can integrate into your code by launching it as a short-lived subprocess to accomplish a single
isolated task. Alternatively, it could be done with a longer internal lifespan, allowing the parent pro-
cess to continue communicating with it, to for example maintain meshes in its memory. This method
however would necessitate the parent process to collect user input.
Overall, the Communication Component system we implemented has a much wider range of flexibility
than was explored in this project. Conceivably, it is not limited to pairs or even IPC communication
at all. Using the standardized set of communication protocols, it would also be possible to implement
components to merely capture data and possibly make adjustments to it, before passing it on. This
could for example be a translator for another type of visualization. It would also be possible to gen-
erate serverless Clients, which generate their data, for example by sequentially going through files in
a folder, streaming from the internet, or constructing programmatically.
7.0.1
Latency
The latency measurements shows that modern-day computer IPC systems are more than fast enough
to where, when compared to the remainder of the process, makes very little difference to the overall
responsiveness of the graphical workbench. This is the case for all IPC mechanisms, with the arguable
exception of the TCP/IP Socket. While direct-memory may still have a little edge, the largest bottle-
neck in performance is by far the rendering system, and the serialization system.
The rendering system is really only solvable by replacing the rendering engine altogether; although
that was already a goal that started this exploration. This ability has been achieved, and the amount
of work needed to accomplish this goal is almost entirely just that of the work needed to implement the
rendering framework itself. The decoupling ensures that this would not need to be deeply integrated
into the rest of the system, as demonstrated by the MeshEditGLVisualizer program’s implementation.
The serialization system offers a more interesting discussion. The serialization system was only ever
used in the IPC mechanisms (outside of the ability to save .bhm files), and so it can be seen as being
part of the cost of implementing IPC. Thus, it could become the primary point of attack, when con-
sidering how to improve this implementation. In the MeshEditorEngine implementation section, we
described how the active_mesh_modify system could be used to implement more advanced change
trackers. An idea for this could be to use a subclass of Manifold, which offers all the same functions
to the remainder of the code, but internally merely logs these changes in an internal sparse structure,
with a reference to the original mesh. This new class could then be ”applied” to the original mesh upon
command exit. It might then be possible to transmit this change structure instead of the complete
mesh, potentially reducing the total size of data sent as well as removing the need for serialization.
Doing so would have the added benefits of preventing damage, if a command errors midway through
execution. The main drawback of this approach however, is be that the Engine and Visualizer would
be susceptible to de-synchronization, and might require occasional full-mesh synchronizations.
62
==PAGE:63==
Another thing worth looking into, would be whether Shared Memory can be sped up by serializ-
ing directly into it, rather than using stringstreams as a middle landing point. This would require
the serialization size to be deterministic, so that the memory required could be pre-allocated, but this
should be possible based on Manifold’s implementation. To do this, the Serialization class could
simply be extended to be able to write a char* pointer with an offset. This could perhaps allow Shared
Memory to have an isolated performance impact of near-zero.
Finally, it would also be possible to optimize the latency to reduce the impacts by optimizing the
serialization itself — That is, to make the serialization both smaller and faster. For example, GEL
serializes boolean vectors by writing each boolean into each their own byte. This leaves 7 out of every
8 bits in the data stream redundant, but is still necessary to deserialize the mesh. This could be
optimized by stacking 8 booleans into each byte, padding the final byte if necessary. Reductions like
this not only speed up the data writing, but also reduces the overall serialized size to a potentially
large degree, which in turn optimizes all IPC implementations too. Using compression does not seem
like the ideal approach, as the figures shown in Figure 28 shows that the largest bottleneck is the
serialization rather than the transmission; Compression would only sacrifice even worse serialization
for reduced transmission sizes. This may however be an interesting prospect to look into, if using
transmissions with limited bandwidth, or if disk space is limited.
7.0.2
File System & Persistence
The File Streaming IPC implementation offers some more interesting aspects worth discussing. It is
the only form of IPC that comes with automatic persistence. That means that it would be possible to,
for example, shut down the computer and restart it, and then seamlessly pick up on where it left off.
This kind of persistence would normally simply be accomplish with a manual save and load, but when
considering the goal of specialized workflows and work efficiency, the automation of this task may save
considerable time in the long run. Additionally, it would also ensure the work remains safe, even in
cases of crashes.
On top of that, the file system is also a very versatile system for IPC communication. For exam-
ple, a FileStreamClient does not even require a FileStreamServer — It would be entirely possible
to use the visualizer in conjunction with an entirely different program. Consider the example of using
a more powerful tool such as Blender, and by simply pressing Ctrl+S to save, the mesh instantly ap-
pears in the Visualizer running on the side. Of course, in the current instance it is limited to reading
only GEL serialized data, however if this system is leveraged to read other forms of files too, it could
enable deep integration into a workflow consisting of other tools, even if those tools do not offer direct
integration, as the vast majority do offer file saving.
Should this be desired however, it would necessitate the removal of the first byte flag system, in
place of another. This system makes the file unreadable to others, and was already not a very robust
guaranteed solution. It would be worth considering alternative ways of ensuring awaiting the complete
saving of a file through the file system itself instead.
7.0.3
PyGEL Integration
A final point of discussion, is that of PyGEL. The idea for PyGEL when used in a Python console,
to be able to create a MeshEdit window that would pop up, and then display meshes as they were
interacted with in the console, was an idea expressed by the supervisor early on in the project. There
was not time to explore this opportunity in this project, however we believe the decoupling also had
an effect on the prospect.
With the separation of ME_OpenGL from MeshEditorEngine, it might be possible to create the ME_OpenGL
instance from a Python binding, and pop up the window. This binding could furthermore create a
global container, such that when Manifolds are constructed in the Python environment, they are added
to this container which pushes updates to ME_OpenGL through the MeshEditorInterface inheritance.
A new function could be exposed on Manifolds, e.g update_visualization(), which could trigger
the container to send a notification for this mesh. A potential benefit of this approach is that several
modifications can be made in sequence, while only triggering a re-render at the very end.
63
==PAGE:64==
The integration of PyGEL would allow potential much more complicated commands and modification
instructions, than those found in MeshEdit. Python is also a scripting language widely used for data
science and research, and many existing packages may offer even further functionality with the need
to re-implement them. The integration would also be easily parallelizable, as the ME_OpenGL instance
could be ran in its own thread, enabled by the callback-style communication design we developed in
this project.
This would be a considerable suggestion to improving the usability of the graphical workbench, but
only applies to MeshEdit specifically. However in theory, other C-based programs can similarly expose
Python bindings to achieve similar results. Further research into this has been referenced in ”Future
Work” Chapter 9.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to explore the applicability of decoupling on a graphical workbench for
research, with the aim of improving its usability and efficiency, with respect to expert users and the
workbench’s development. To do this, we analyzed and decoupled MeshEdit, an existing graphical
workbench in the GEL library, which was a tightly-coupled program. We found that the decoupling
process greatly improved the readability and development opportunities of the program’s source code,
and that it enabled a simplification of multiple internal systems that significantly reduced maintenance
overhead. We also tested the ability to leverage this decoupled state to separate the geometry pro-
cessing and the visualization aspects of the tool, and compared different methods of communicating
between them using Inter-Process Communication, as well as single-process parallelization techniques.
We found that on modern computers, the performance of the available IPC mechanisms are fast enough
to where it makes very little difference when compared to the rendering time. We found that single-
process parallelization provided the fastest performance and the easiest implementation, but the IPC
systems allowed additional features such as persistence, cross-program integrations, and networked us-
age.
For the purposes of GEL’S MeshEdit, we conclude that the implemented MultithreadedEngine is
the best suited for its needs, as it solves the problem of parallelization and automatically comes with
integration into the system terminal, with the minimal development cost and the fastest performance.
On a broader scale, most graphical workbenches will also likely see the biggest benefits from inter-
nal single-process parallelization, which is made equally possible through the act of decoupling as all
the other IPC techniques are. It is also the only implementation that did not require any Operating
System-specific implementation, providing the maximal portability.
However, should the workbench developer desire persistence and/or the ability to use the visualization
with other tools, the File Streaming IPC mechanism is well-suited for this purpose, and also offers
the strongest performance of all cross-process IPC mechanisms according to our testing. We would
however propose a different means of ensuring synchronicity, than our implemented one. Should net-
worked usage and cloud computing be a valuable benefit, the TCP/IP Socket system also provides
for a reliable and still relatively efficient communication method. It may have the slowest performance
across the mechanisms tested, however if the performance increase from networked systems can offset
this cost, it can still serve as a worthy consideration. For maximal performance, the Shared Memory
mechanism has the potential to offer the fastest performance, if the program can be built around it
from the ground up to minimize copying and redundancy levels. For existing coupled systems where
this was not originally a consideration, this becomes less feasible. Named Pipes are hard to consider,
unless the simplicity of their implementation is desired, and the potential security risks of serializing
data to disk in an unprotected manner is too great.
Concluding on a broader scale, these learnings do not limit themselves to the case of GEL or the
domain of 3D meshes. The principles discussed in this thesis can equally apply to any sort of data
workbench, provided that the data can be serialized and transmitted. The performance measurements
of the IPC systems also do not account for the specifics of the serialized meshes, but rather generalizes
on the size of the data transmitted. Conceivably, similar systems could be applied to that of 2D texture
workbenches, or data science tools with generative diagram systems, and many more.
The hope is that this these learnings may help future developers of graphical workbenches pick
the most suitable approach to their situation, and inspire wider adoption in decoupling principles and
loosely-coupled design patterns, for the sake of reducing future problems and maintenance costs. This
can potentially save valuable time that can be better spent on other parts of the product.

Future Work
In this section, we will briefly discuss things that would be interesting to research further, or ways to
continue this project beyond the scope of this thesis. Some of these are loose ends mentioned earlier
in the report that warrants further analysis, and some are entirely new opportunities to take as this
investigation’s next steps.
One of these things, is to further investigate the applicability of the Socket system to translate the
graphical workbench into a cloud computation program. With access to a strong computer through a
networked connection, it would be interesting to see to what extent the performance gains from the
stronger computer counteracts the otherwise high latency of the Socket IPC implementation.
Another thing is to investigate the security of this implementation.
Throughout this project, no
consideration has been taken into data security, and it is a conceivable risk when exposing communi-
cation channels to outside processes, that it may result in interference or erroneous behavior. Further
investigation could either go into the different security options for some of the IPC types, or into data
validation and sanitation, to avoid catastrophic failures in the execution.
A big step forward would also be to look into the feasibility of some of the proposed serialization
changes from Section 7.0.1. It would be interesting to discover how big a difference the boolean vector
change makes to the mesh sizes, and what this means for both transmission latency and serialization
times. It would also be interesting to see if any sparse changes data structure could be implemented,
that could reduce the amount of communication needed by several orders of magnitude. This serial-
ization investigation could also attempt to look into why serialization fails for large meshes such as
bunny6, and if it is possible to do fragmented serialization.
The re-implementation of all limitations and features lost as described in Section 5.11 would also
be a possible way to continue this research. It would be interesting to find out to what extent the cur-
rent IPC solutions could handle two-way communication, and whether this limits the possible options
for some cases of graphical workbenches.
Finally, it would be interesting to see the remainder of the ideas proposed by this thesis to be imple-
mented and compared. These would include the IPC mechanisms we did not have time to implement,
notably the Anonymous Pipes and the Unix Domain Sockets, as well as a more robust File Streaming
system. The proposed PyGEL implementation from Section 7.0.3 would also be very interesting to
look into, as it has the potential to completely eliminate the need for MeshEdit as a whole, and provide
a much more flexible basis for a GEL Graphical Workbench.
