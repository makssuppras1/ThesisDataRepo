Abstract
Topological insulators (TI) are a class of materials that show promising properties for future
applications in spinctronics and quantum computing. Unique electronic and spin qualities arise at
their interfaces thanks to spin-orbit coupling and broken inversion symmetry. In particular, use
of III-V or II-V materials attracts great interest for device incorporation.
However, fabrication of complex circuits still requires a strong and precise technology for one
dimensional structures. The motivation of the Inspiring project has been led by the will of creating
a robust platform dedicated to nanoelectronics of materials from classes III-V and II-VI grown by
molecular beam epitaxy between CEA-Leti and IEMN.
The proposed work is a first step in this project. It focused on the study of the selective area
epitaxy of HgTe on lithographically masked CdTe substrates. The lowering of the growth rate
prevents the nucleation of the tellurium adatoms on the SiO2 mask so that growth only occurs on
the free part of the mask. The diffusion of the adatoms on the mask according to the shape of the
openings and the growth rate have been discussed. This effect is responsible for lateral overgrowth
of the structure and the formation of super-elevations at the edges of the grown patterns. Both
depend on the shape of the pattern. The height of the super-elevation is also influenced by the
growth rate while the lateral overgrowth is not. The width of the super-elevation is limited by
the diffusion of the Te adatoms on the top surface. This diffusion length is smaller than half of
the openings width so the peaks do not merge and top surfaces with low roughness (around one
monolayer) are observed. Mask removal tries are performed to allow for a cap layer.

Introduction
The past decades have seen the emergence of electronic devices in everyday life. The transis-
tors are at the core of these devices and huge progress have been made to develop and enhance
their properties. Particularly, the reduction of their size has been of main interest to increase
their density and so the efficiency of electronic devices. This evolution follows what is known as
the Moore’s law which states the number of transistors on a chip will double every two years.
Transistors with a channel length of tens of nanometers are now created. However at such small
scale, quantum phenomena appear (like leakage current) and decrease their efficiency while in-
creasing their energy consumption. Finding novel solutions to postpone the limits of the existing
technology is of great interest in both academic and industrial research.
In this quest for the future of electronics, a class of materials with peculiar properties has
emerged and show great potential for future application in spintronics or quantum computing.
These materials are known as topological insulators (TI) [1]. They have a bulk band gap like an
ordinary insulator but also protected conducting states on their surface. More, TI are character-
ized by a strong spin-orbit coupling (SOC) combined with broken inversion symmetry. This makes
possible the apparition of the spin-momentum locking property that is promising for real devices.
This effect, similar to the Rashba effect, allows for the manipulation of the electron spin without
magnetic gate which is at the core of the spintronics field [2]. Many innovative concepts relying
on the latter effect have already been demonstrated these last few years such as electrically gated
spinFET [3] or the quest for Majorana zero modes for robust Qubits [4].
Their use could lay the path towards development of robust circuits manipulating coherent
electron spin or topological quantum information. Among potential TI candidates, there are the
classes of III-V (InSb/GaSb quantum well) and II-VI (HgTe) materials whose properties have
been widely studied [3, 5]. Their strong spin-orit coupling as well as the layer quality one can
obtain during fabrication bring them among top materials for the application of the above men-
tioned effects. However, the manipulation of their properties in circuits requires solid nano-scale
technology that is still in need. Fabrication of one dimensional (1D) nanostructures is supposed to
reach the coherent regime and efficiently control the spin of electrons. Indeed, with the presence
of the bulk, HgTe TI are known to exhibit strong hybridization between the hole bulk bands and
the surface states. Therefore, the transport is non-ballistic and the local resistivity deviates from
perfect quantization [6].
The Inspiring project has then been imagined to address these issues. It aims at first the
realization of in-plane patterns (nanowire and Aharonov-Bohm ring) of InSb and HgTe using se-
lective molecular beam epitaxy. And then to evidence ballistic transport in such nanostructures.
The project would investigate both II-VI and III-V classes of material as well as mixed hetero-
structures thanks to their close lattice parameter. Such achievements could lead to the creation
of a state-of-art platform for nanoelectronics and quantum transport applications between the
CEA-Leti and the IEMN institute.
Therefore, this Master’s thesis focuses on the fabrication and characterization of in-plane
HgTe nanostructures on CdTe substrate grown by selective area molecular beam epitaxy. Selec-
tivity consists in finding growth conditions that favor epitaxy on the free parts of a lithographically

A very interesting characteristic of these materials for topological insulators physics is their
strong spin orbit coupling : ∆SO ≈1 eV. Also, HgTe presents an inverted band structure resulting
in a semi-metal electronic distribution. When grown on a substrate with a larger lattice constant
as CdTe, a bulk gap of a few tens of meV due to the tensile train is opened between the Γ8LH and
Γ8HH bands. This yields to the creation of an insulating bulk. These characteristics are necessary
conditions to be topological insulators. Strained HgTe has negligible bulk conduction and high
carrier mobility on the edges as long as the crystalline layer does not contain defects. High elec-
tron mobility (≈400 000 cm2/V−1.s−1) is expected to be measured in the nanowires [8]. However,
the tensile strain may lead to the apparition of structural defects. Their creation depends on
the film thickness. Here, the thickness of HgTe is lower than the experimental critical thickness
of relaxation, hc ≈140 nm above which defects could appear in the form of misfit dislocations.

Instrumentation for growth and characterization
This section presents the principle and the experimental setup of the techniques used during
the project. They have been employed to grow or characterize the HgTe/CdTe structures. First,
the working principle of Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) is described.
Then, information on
characterization techniques based on X-Rays are provided. Finally, Scanning Electron Microscopy
(SEM) and Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) techniques are presented.
2.1
Molecular Beam Epitaxy
The process relies on the creation of a crystalline film on top of a crystal substrate with atomic
precision by sending flux of atomic species onto the substrate. MBE growths are challenging as
they occur out of the thermodynamic equilibrium but the high precision and flexibility of the
growth parameters are main advantages for the realization of complex nanostructures with good
crystal structure. To achieve such precision an ultra-high vacuum environment (until 10−10 Torr)
is needed inside the chamber, preventing interaction between atomic fluxes. The latter are emitted
from effusion cells where the species undergo evaporation. The cells are precisely heated up by
coils, yielding to monitored fluxes of atoms. Effusion cell can be closed with shutters to grow layers
with different materials and improve the sharpness at interfaces. High quality growth is reached
thanks to adsorption of atoms on the heated substrate, called thereafter adatoms. They diffuse on
the surface and interact with each other. Their behavior is governed by these interactions. One
can distinguish three modes of crystal growth. The Van der Merwe mode, also know as layer-
by-layer mode, occurs when adatoms show high affinity with the substrate. In contrast, adatoms
may gather in clusters and nucleate in islands directly on the substrate, thus the island mode or
Volmer-Weber mode. The third growth mode corresponds to a mix on the two first modes: the
growth starts with layers but they become unfavorable and adatoms form islands on top.

The growth rate of HgTe is governed by the tellurium flux, i.e the sublimation temperature of

condense on the substrate while the mercury atoms are "filling the holes" creating II-VI bonds.
And the tellurium atoms being very mobile, they favor self organization. The growth occurs near
thermal equilibrium (200°C), the growth mode is therefore the Van der Merwe mode. The Hg
atoms can desorb easily hence the Hg/Te ratio is of the order of 1000/1 during the growth step
and the shutter is constantly open so the pressure is limited to 10−6 Torr in the chamber.
2.2
X-rays
X-Rays-based characterization techniques rely on the interaction between X-Rays waves and
the material. Investigations using X-Rays waves are performed to assess the structural character-
istics of a sample. There are non-destructive techniques offering a wide variety of parameters to
be probed: quality, roughness, thickness, strain ad more. It makes X-Ray techniques an essential
tool in characterization. X-Rays Diffraction (XRD) and X-Rays reflectivity are introduced in this
section. The X-Rays machine used during this study is a Bruker Discover 8.
To produce X-Rays, accelerated electrons (voltage scales with dozens of kV) are sent on a
copper anode where two phenomena lead to X-Rays emissions: the deceleration of the latter
electron (K-α energy) and the de-excitation of the anode electrons (K-β energy). Thus, waves at
different wavelengths are emitted. To be selective and keep only one specific wavelength, a four-
bounds monochromator is added at the end of the source and select the K-α1 wavelength of copper:
λKα1 = 1.54062 ˚A. Detection is performed thanks to a triple-axis detector with cancellation of
the diffracted beam divergence. Therefore, measurements are realised with a precision of 0.003°.
Different optic instruments (slits, collimators, mirrors, etc) are placed before and after the sample
to focus and collimate the beam.

2.2.2
Reflectivity
Reflectivity spectra reveal the interaction of incident radiations with the different layers of
a sample to provide information about the thickness, roughness or density of the layers. Snell-
Descartes law governs the reflectivity of the X-Rays waves on the different layers of a material:
n1 sin(θ1) = n sin(θ2)
Where n1 and n are the refractive indexes of the environment. It is 1 for air and for a material
lit with X-Rays:
n = 1 −δ + iβ
δ and β are respectively the dispersion and absorption coefficients, both are dependent on the
material density. The order of magnitude of δ is about 10−5, while β is even smaller. Hence, n
is slightly smaller than 1 and it exists a critical angle, θc below which total reflection takes place.
Then if the incident angle is larger than θc, X-Ray waves penetrate the material and are partially
reflected at each interface they encounter. It yields to constructive interference whose spectra is
related to the layer thickness, deduced from the periodicity of the oscillation. Figure 5 summarizes
all the information that a reflectivity spectra can provide.

2.3
Scanning Electron Microscope
Electron microscopy is one of the most used tools for morphological characterization. Its
high resolution, up to nanometer-scale, is obtained thanks to a focused electron beam sent to the
sample. The incident beam penetrates the sample according to its energy and the atomic number
of the specimen creating what is known as a primary excitation zone. On one hand, the more
energetic the incident electrons, the deeper they penetrate thus the bigger the excitation zone. On
the other hand, the larger the Z, the smaller the zone. The density of charge hinders penetration.
The size of the primary excitation zone will influence the signal acquisition, too large it could
yield to loss of details. The accelerating voltage has then to be carefully set. Typical energy level
of the incident electrons is 0.1-30 keV, emitted from a tungsten filament. To prevent scattering in
the column, a high-vacuum environment is needed.
The accelerated electron will mainly interact with the sample in two ways, inelastic or elastic
interactions, and provide information about the surface state of the sample.
The first type of interaction leads to the emission of secondary electrons (SE) from the sample.
Here, the incident electron transfers some of his energy to the sample, interacting with the electrons
or the atomic nucleus. SE are loosely bound electrons possessing energy less than 50 eV. They
are emitted from atoms at the surface of the sample (around a few nanometers deep) where the
incident beam penetrates. Hence they are widely used to study the topography of the analysed
specimen. SE are collected by the Everhart-Thornley detector (which attracts the latter thanks
to an applied bias (≈10 kV)) or by a so-called In-lens detector with higher surface sensitivity and

Elastic interactions occur when the incoming electron is scattered by the outer layer electrons
of the sample with similar energy (typically greater than 50 eV) or by the nucleus. The scattering
effect yields to high-angle deflections. If the electrons are deflected enough so that the can exit
the sample, they are called backscattered electrons (BSE) and can be used for sample analysis.
BSE provide information on both the composition and the morphology. In particular, the amount
of BSE depends on the atomic number Z: the higher, the more BSE are emitted and then the
brighter the element appears on the screen. They show a large energy so the region where they
are emitted from is larger than for SE, as they can not be absorbed by the sample. It yields to a
poorer resolution [13].
More interacting phenomena occur during the sample exposure to the electron beam, such as
characteristic X-ray or Auger emissions, but they are not discussed here.

Toward selectivity
Engineering of the material growth is very sensitive for InSb and HgTe. The difficulty is to
obtain 1D defect-free structures for longer coherence time and so facilitate the spin manipulation.
Regarding InSb, the first fabrication methods for nanowires or nanopillars focused on the
bottom-up process. Thermal chemical vapor deposition (CVD) [16], metal-organic CVD [17], or
molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) [18] are examples of techniques relying on the Vapor Liquid Solid
(VLS) mechanism using gold as a metal catalyst. However, the InSb nanostructures are not well
localized on the substrate and circuit fabrication seem prevented. In a different approach, the
work realised by IEMN demonstrated the realisation in-plane nanostructures of III-V materials
by selective area molecular beam epitaxy [19]. With high selectivity the mask can be removed
without damaging the nanostructures. Prominent group such as Microsoft SationQ at TU Delft
also work with selective are growth [20].
A huge difficulty for the realisation of complex patterns with HgTe is the presence of mercury.
It is indeed extremely sensitive to any external application due to its high volatility. Use of dry-
etching is hindered because it is known to introduce electrical defects. No process using common
techniques such as e-beam lithography have been developed so far. The tensions used lead to
thermal heating of the HgTe compound and so to mercury evaporation. Addition of chemical
etching steps have been studied to grow nanowires but the process lack of dimensional control
[21]. Realisation of nanowire with gold as a catalyst and using the VLS mechanisms have been
reported [22]. But it is not a tunable process and realisation of complex structure would not be
well controlled. In addition no convincing electronic transport data have been provided on low
dimensional HgTe/CdTe nanostructures thus far.
So choose of selectivity with MBE is a totally novel approach for realisation of in-plane HgTe
nanostructures. They latter would grow on patterned masked CdTe substrate thanks to the high
precision growth performed with molecular beam epitaxy. Selective area epitaxy of III-V materials
has already been proven with MBE (in the frame of the Inspiring project as well) and will serve as
a basis for the following investigation [19]. The achievement of such a novel growth process would
then be a first step towards the production of a robust platform for advanced electronic devices
based on the manipulation of coherent electron spin. The close lattice parameter of some III-V
and II-VI materials (CdTe, GgTe, INSb) is promising and allows for the realisation of hybride
nanostructures.
This section aims at presenting the different steps before the selective area growth experi-
ments. First, mask fabrication steps as well as the designed patterns are described. Then, the
realisation of 2D layers of HgTe on unmasked substrate in particular conditions is investigated to
assess the quality of the grown film.

3.2
First tries in a priori favorable conditions
From the studies performed at IEMN (Lille), the favorable conditions for selective epitaxy of
III-V materials are [19]:
— Low growth rate (GR): ≈0, 2 monolayer per second (ML/s)
— Low flux ratio
— High growth temperature
— Addition of a H flux
The low growth rate enhances the surface diffusion of the adatoms and favors re-evaporation
on the masked areas. Spontaneous nucleation on the mask is indeed less likely as the flux of
atoms reaching the sample is reduced. In these conditions, the sticking coefficient of the deposited
element on the silicon dioxide is lower and binding to the substrate becomes favorable. However,
this range of growth rate has not been experienced yet at CEA for HgTe. The growth of Te-based
compounds (HgTe, CdTe) usually take place around 2 ML/s. So there is no guarantee that the
process will behave similarly at lower growth rates.
Regarding the flux ratio, the high volatility of mercury prevents any change on this parameter.
The sticking coefficient of mercury is so low than it has to be sent in high excess.

Theoretical background
To serve as a basis for discussion of the results presented in the next section, the theory of
selective area growth (SAG) developed by A. Debald et al. is presented [26].
Due to the growth limiting parameter of tellurium, only tellurium fluxes are considered.
After being adsorbed on the mask, tellurium adatoms will diffuse on the surface for a specific
time τ which is the time corresponding to the fastest process between desorption, nucleation and
incorporation in the nanostructure. Therefore, a diffusion length can be defined as λ =
√
Dτ where
D is a diffusion constant. These phenomena are competitive so 1/τ is the sum of the inverse of their
characteristic time. With SAG one expects no nucleation on the hard mask neither incorporation
so that desoprtion is the fastest process. But long range surface diffusion can lead to an increase
of adatoms concentration at the mask transition, yielding to the formation of super-elevation
(adatoms diffuse to the top) or an increase in the nanostructure lateral overgrowth if the adatoms
are incorporated at the basis. The growth rate is locally increased.

length is higher because the purple line is shorter than the black line. It is the opposite for the
inner side of the ring. Thus a higher super-elevation is expected on the external side of the ring.
For a line shape (right hand side of the figure), the radius of curvature is infinite so the super-
elevation height is the same all along the line and theoretically smaller than the ring (radius of a
line ≫radius of a ring).
The width of the super-elevations depends on the diffusion of the tellurium adatoms on the
nanostructure. If the diffusion is long enough the two super-elevations can merge and give a flat
top surface.
The characteristics of these phenomena depend on the growth condition, the mask material
and the mask layout. Figure 18 summarizes the ideas described above.

Conclusion
Thanks to the low growth rate, the flux of adatoms on the mask is low so that nucleation is
unlikely and desorption is promoted: τdesorption is the shortest characteristic time. As the concen-
tration rises with a higher growth rate, the chances to observe nucleation are larger. Analysis of
the adhesion of tellurium atoms on the silicon dioxide layer is a good example of this phenomenon
even though the area covered by the nucleated island is still low (at most 2.5%). It can be in-
terpreted as the nucleation probability, and so desorption probability would be one minus the
adhesion coefficient.
Furthermore, the SEM images do not show any area where all atoms that would be adsorbed
in would diffuse towards the mask openings. Thus, it is suspected the mask diffusion is negligible
and the nucleation process is random and spontaneous which is not what the theory claims.
Nevertheless, it could explain why the lateral overgrowth of the structures is always the same.
If there was a sizeable surface diffusion area, with a higher concentration of adatoms, a bigger
lateral overgrowth would be observed and it is not the case here. Contrary, the formation of super-
elevation and the short lateral overgrowth observed are believed to be due to the concentration
of adatoms at the mask aperture. Adatoms in the vicinity of the apertures bind to the HgTe
in the openings. Super-elevations are formed and the structures grow a bit wider. Because the
super-elevations do not merge, the diffusion length of Te on HgTe seems rather small (FWHM of
the super-elevation may a good approximation so λHgTe ≈60 nm). But it is not understood why
all the adatoms are incorporated at the top and not the mask/structure transition where a bigger
lateral overgrowth would be observed. The discrepancies in the super-elevation height are most
likely due to the concentration of adatoms at the surface and are somehow related to the growth
rate.
Finally, considering the circle-shape structures, the difference in super-elevation height ac-
cording to the diffusion side is verified as well as the lateral overgrowth. Indeed, the peaks are
higher on the external side of the rings than for the inner side. The flux of adatoms (per length)
reaching the structure is therefore higher on the external side. According to the theory for surface
diffusion from the external side, the smaller the radius of curvature, the higher the height of the
super-elevation. Nevertheless, it is not what is observed here. The circle-shape structure are then
less favorable for shaped growth.

Further developments
5
Further developments
The project is only starting and many investigations still have to be performed.
The first step of the Inspiring project is the growth of HgTe nanostructures for ballistic
transport experiments. This requires the deposition of a CdTe cap layer on the structures to
prevent electron-hole recombination at the interfaces. But for that, the shape of the nanostructures
should not show such super-elevations. Also, the mask removal has to be enhanced because a cap
layer could not be deposited on such a rough surface. The main task is thus the improvement of
the growth process to remove or at least reduce the super-elevations.
To address these issues, growth runs at a higher substrate temperature (≈180 °C, above
Te could re-evaporate) should be done to promote self-organisation of HgTe. Thus, atoms in the
super-elevations could diffuse reducing their height and width. Growth runs at lower (in the order
of the mask thickness) and higher thickness are planned to study the evolution of the structure in
time. This could give some insights about when and how the super-elevations are formed. In the
meantime, growth runs with a varying growth rate will continue. A longer time of HF-etching or
HCl cleaning (for tellurium oxide removal and so the mask by lift-off) are considered to enhance
the surface state before the cap layer deposition. Repeatability tests will also be performed.
Finally, STEM observations are needed to study the arrangements of the HgTe crystals in
the opening and at the mask transition where lateral overgrowth start. Also, precise information
about the crystalline quality could be achieved because no XRD measurements could be performed
thus far. EDX experiments would give the chemical signatures of the sample.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this Master’s thesis was to experimentally demonstrate selective area molecular
beam epitaxy of HgTe on a masked CdTe substrate with Si02. It is the first step towards the
realisation of 1D nanostructures to study ballistic transport where the quantum coherence regime
should be reached.
In this work, favorable growth conditions for selectivity have been investigated. Starting from
a very low growth rate (0.26 ML/s) to more common growth rate (1 ML/s), selectivity of HgTe
on the silicon dioxide mask has been assessed and found to be efficient (at most the nucleated
islands cover 2.5% of a mask area).
Thanks to AFM scans, profiles of the grown nanostructures (lines and rings) from the mask
openings have been extracted. The formation of super-elevations has been reported at the surface
edges as well as lateral overgrowth. The height of the super-elevation is related to the growth
rate during the process. It seems that the faster it is, the higher the super-elevations. While
their FWHM is found to be constant (≈60 nm) and highlights that the diffusion length of the
adatoms in the super-elevation is limited. Considering the lateral overgrowth, it is found to be
approximately constant (≈75 nm) whatever the opening width.
The ring shape has shown that mask-originated adatoms contribution is more important from
the outside, so the external lateral overgrowth contribution is bigger than the inner side. Indeed,
the adatoms from the outside are concentrated on a smaller perimeter when they bind to the
nanostructure. It is the opposite in the inner side of the ring. Thus, higher super-elevations are
formed on the external edge. The super-elevation FWHM is the same as the one for the lines but
their height is more often lower. Nevertheless, the growth on the ring is not homogeneous due to
the small radius of curvature and the small aperture width so it is complicated to settle a typical
growth.
The height of the plateau in the center gives the proper deposited thickness as the super-
elevations do not merge. It is the same on most of the patterns so that the filling is efficient even
though discrepancies are observed among the three samples: from 30 to 35 nm. The roughness
of the top surface structure is of good quality (around one monolayer) and in line with what has
been previously achieved for 2D layers.
Mask removal for the subsequent deposition of cap layer has been investigated. After a pure
HF etching, silicon dioxide could still been seen on the AFM scans. These islands are believed to
be bound with the tellurium oxide. But the etching was inefficient on the HgTe structures so a
longer time of exposure can be envisaged. The mask thickness was found to be of 10 nm.
The perspectives developed in the previous section constitute challenging steps toward the
quantum transport experiments.
