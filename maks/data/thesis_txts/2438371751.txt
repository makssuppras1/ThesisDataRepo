Summary
Deep learning in context of autonomous driving covers a vast and very active research
area, namely convolutional neural networks and their applications. This thesis ex-
plores the current state-of-the-art model architectures and applies them to the prob-
lem of predicting the steering angle of a vehicle from a visual input in an end-to-end
manner. Mainly, the focus is on 2-D and 3-D convolutional models, which take a
single frame input or a video input respectively. Furthermore, the architectures are
evaluated on a synthetic dataset generated from a simulator, as well as on a publicly
available real-world dataset collected by human drivers in various road, lighting and
traﬃc conditions. The obtained results are compared to related literature and show
15%–40% reduction in RMSE of the steering wheel angle. Additionally, the best per-
forming model is able to run in real-time on consumer grade hardware.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Over the last few years autonomous and semi-autonomous driving has become more
prevalent. Companies such as Google, Tesla, Uber and traditional car manufactures
are competing to bring vehicles with various levels of autonomy to market. Signiﬁcant
amount of funds and research is put into development of self-driving systems, which
need to be safe and operate in real-time.
1.1
Autonomous driving in a nutshell
Driving is a complex task, therefore most of the companies working on autonomous ve-
hicles have broken down the self-driving system into several components and seem to
share a common array of sensors. Various camera systems are always presented (Mo-
bileye), supplemented by radar(s) (Tesla) and some use Light Detection and Ranging
device (LIDAR) to examine vehicle’s surroundings through light beams (Waymo, Audi).
Generally, the high level components consist of scene understanding/recognition, lo-
calization and path planning to determine the vehicle’s actions.
In order for such system to succeed, multidisciplinary collaboration is crucial. Relevant
ﬁelds include computer vision, deep learning, robotics and many others. Additionally,
the sensory hardware generates large quantities of data that need to synchronized,
processed and stored to further advance the development of autonomous systems. For
a more detail overview refer to Janai et al. 2017.
1.2
Problem formulation
As the ﬁeld of autonomous driving covers a wide research area of computer vision
with each topic being an active research question, it is natural to choose one task and
explore it thoroughly. For this reason, it was chosen to dive deep into end-to-end
models that make use of monocular vision. Currently, there are several proponents
of mainly using visual input in the ﬁeld, namely Tesla, Mobileye and Comma.ai. In
detail, the main task will be to predict the steering angle of a vehicle given a visual
input. This input can either be a single frame or a sequence of past frames (think
video), leading to questions such as
==PAGE:9==

1. Does using more than one input frame provide better performance? If yes, are
there any downsides or potential complications that should be taken into ac-
count? How to solve them?
2. What is the optimal way to formulate the problem of predicting the steering
angle? How well does it generalize?
3. Is it possible to exploit other data sources than the visual input?
It is important to note that this thesis in no sense tries to solve autonomous driving as
a whole, merely it explores and presents chosen sub-tasks of the overall self-driving

1.3
Thesis outline
Chapter 2 introduces necessary theoretical knowledge required to understand con-
cepts used throughout this thesis. Largely, the chapter is based on Nielsen 2015 I.
Goodfellow et al. 2016. Inner workings of artiﬁcial neural networks are presented
in Section 2.1. Next, an overview of convolutional neural networks is described in
Section 2.2 along with intuitive visual examples adapted from Dumoulin and Visin
2016. Section 2.3 lists several strategies helpful for overcoming commonly encoun-
tered problems when training neural networks. Lastly, Section 2.4 provides examples
and formulas for losses and metrics that will be used later in the Experiments chapter
(Chapter 4).
Moving on to Chapter 3, deep learning approaches for autonomous driving are pre-
sented, followed by a review of related work. Data collection process is introduced
in Section 3.2 along with a selection of popular dataset and simulators. Additionally,
pros and cons of respective datasets are provided. Section 3.3 bridges the gap between
theory and practise in context of using deep learning for self-driving cars, followed by
an overview of state-of-the-art network architectures. Next, approaches to modeling
temporal dynamics are introduced with relevant literature. To wrap up the chapter,
concepts such as multi-task learning and transfer learning are described.
Practical part of this thesis is described in Chapter 4, where the problem (re)formulation
is discussed as well as the carried out experiments are results are summarized. The
experiments are done on a real world dataset from Udacity and a dataset obtained
from the AirSim simulator, with ﬁndings presented in separate sections. Each of the
aforementioned sections also contains thorough discussion of obtained results, choices
behind model selection and failed attempts.
Finally, Chapter 5 summarizes all the ﬁndings and discussion along with proposed
future work.

Artificial Neural Network (ANN)
The study of neurological systems modelling gives rise to concept of (artiﬁcial) neural
networks, which inspired by how the brain works, are able to approximate some func-
tion, for example capturing the complex relationships between inputs and outputs for
regression or classiﬁcation tasks. The basic building block of these networks is an (arti-
ﬁcial) neuron. It is similar to a neuron in the brain, which receives input signals from
dendrites to produce a single output via an axon that in turn branches and connects
through synapses to dendrites of other neurons.
To model the aforementioned relationships mathematically, a weight (signal strength)
wj is assigned to each input xj of a neuron to produce a single output z by summing
over all the inputs:

Loss function
The loss function measures how wrong the model is, however not any function can be
chosen as some requirements need to be met ﬁrst (this will be clear once backpropa-

usually called the training of the network.

2.2 Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
10
A key diﬀerence between a fully connected artiﬁcial neural network and a convolu-
tional network is parameter sharing. Fully connected networks use each element of
the weight matrix once when computing the layer’s output, however the kernel in a
CNN “visits” parts of the input (depicted in Figure 2.1) while only one set of weights
is stored for all the visited positions. As a consequence, memory requirements for a
CNN are lower than for a traditional ANN. Taking a 200 × 200 black and white image
as the input, ANN’s neuron would have 40000 weights, while a 5×5 kernel would only
yield 25 weights in comparison.
The particular form of parameter sharing causes the convolutional layers to be equiv-
ariant to translation (I. Goodfellow et al. 2016, p. 334). In case of image inputs, convo-
lution creates a 2-D map of where certain features appear in the image. If the object in
the images was moved, the representation would shift the same amount in the output.
As an example, the ﬁrst layer of most convolutional neural networks learns to detect
edges and/or other low level features, the deeper layers with bigger receptive ﬁeld
(all input units that aﬀect output units) can learn to identify more abstract features
or patterns (Zeiler and Fergus 2013; Simonyan et al. 2013; Springenberg et al. 2014;
Karpathy et al. 2015).
2.2.1
Architecture
Although the ﬁrst publish CNN architecture LeNet dates back to nineties (LeCun et
al. 1998), the research and usage of (deep) CNNs did not become prominent until
AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al. 2012) won the ImageNet LSVRC (Deng et al. 2009, large
scale visual recognition challenge). AlexNet reached top 5 error of 16% compared to
runner-up with 26% error in it’s dominating victory. The architecture was based on
LeNet with more convolutional layers that were stacked on top of each other (hence the
name “deep” CNN). Ever since the 2012, the yearly ISVRC is driving the development
of deep, advanced and optimized CNN architectures such as GoogLeNet/Inception
(Szegedy et al. 2014; Szegedy et al. 2017, Inception v2-v4 and Inception-ResNet v1,
v2), ResNet (He et al. 2015a) and SENet (Hu et al. 2017).
The main building block of a CNN is the convolutional layer, usually this term refers to
convolution, followed by optional activation function, optional pooling and sometimes
zero-padding that can be either the ﬁnal or initial stage of the layer’s pipeline.
2.2.1.1
Convolutional stage
In context of CNNs, convolution and cross-correlation are used interchangeably due
to underlying implementations in deep learning libraries as discussed in 2.24. Con-
volution with a single kernel generates one kind of feature at diﬀerent locations, to
leverage computational power of GPUs multiple applications of convolution can be
performed in parallel (multichannel convolution), allowing the layer to learn more
features over the input space.

2.3
Overfitting and regularization
During training a neural network can ﬁnd such parameters that produce very little to
almost no loss, it that case the model suﬀers in generalizing when given previously un-
seen data. This phenomenon is called overﬁtting. Usually, before training the model
(in a supervised manner), the whole dataset is split into three parts: training, vali-
dation and testing. In order to achieve generalization, the validation loss should be
roughly the same or slightly higher than the (training) loss per epoch, however if
the model is overﬁtting, the loss keeps decreasing while the validation loss plateaus
or rises. So far the model has “learnt” from the training, but in order to evaluate
the model’s performance and the ability to generalize on unseen data, the withheld
testing split is used. Over the years, several methods have proven to be eﬀective in
preventing or reducing overﬁtting, these methods are referred to as regularization.

2.3.1
Dropout
Probably the most common method used in practice, dropout (Srivastava et al. 2014)
keeps a neuron active with some predeﬁned probability p, meaning that during the
training phase every epoch uses a sampled version of the network, however when
predicting there is no dropout applied. Despite the simplicity of this method, it has
proven to be very eﬀective and can be used in conjunction with other regularization
methods (Wager et al. 2013; I. Goodfellow et al. 2016).
2.3.2
Early stopping
As mentioned earlier, the undesired overﬁtting can be detected by monitoring the
loss and validation loss. Early stopping prevents overﬁtting (Caruana et al. 2001)
by terminating the training process once the validation loss starts increasing while the
loss continues to decrease. This is done retroactively with an option to specify after
how many epochs of non-decreasing validation loss should the training be stopped.
For implementation details and formal discussion about regularization eﬀects of early
stopping see I. Goodfellow et al. 2016, p. 241, sec. 7.8.
2.3.3
Batch normalization
A fairly new technique called batch normalization (Ioﬀe and Szegedy 2015) is a power-
ful and general regularization method that has been incorporated into state-of-the-art
models such as Inception and ResNet variants. The main idea is to normalize the in-
put to the layer by calculating the mean and variance for the current mini-batch. Both
mean and variance act as random values and ﬂuctuate as the batch samples are picked
randomly, which leads to every layer learning to be more robust to some variation in
its input (I. Goodfellow et al. 2016). Batch normalization also speeds up the training
as mentioned in the source paper (14 times fewer training steps while maintaining
the same accuracy in their experiments). The authors credit the aforementioned prop-
erties to so called internal covariate shift (see section 2 of the paper), however recent
research indicates that the gradient descent acceleration is observed even if no inter-
nal covariate shift is present (Santurkar et al. 2018; Kohler et al. 2018). To sum up the
recent ﬁndings, using adaptive optimizers is highly encouraged when including batch
normalization layers in the network as it makes the loss landscape smoother (the loss
function for each layer has “improved Lipschitzness” resulting in better gradient esti-
mates).

CHAPTER3
Autonomous driving
The work of C. Chen et al. 2015 categorizes the approaches used in the current au-
tonomous self-driving systems into two categories: mediated perception and behaviour
reﬂex.
The mediated perception approach breaks down the complex task of driving into
smaller sub-tasks or sub-components. The overall goal is to build an overview of the car’s
surroundings and other driving-relevant objects and make informed decisions about
controlling the car. Relevant sub-tasks include: lane, car, pedestrian and other obsta-
cle detection, interpretation of traﬃc signs and traﬃc lights. The complexity of the
aforementioned tasks cannot be underestimated as they are vital to a well functioning
self-driving system. It is important to note that even with the advances in computer
vision most of these tasks are still open research questions. Furthermore, scene un-
derstanding fundamentally requires mapping in three dimensional space and while
camera input can achieve decent results, more expensive sensors such as LIDAR or
traditional radar as well as external input like high-precision environmental and road
maps are often used in practice to achieve state-of-the-art results. Overall, a lot of in-
tricate components are necessary to perform the task of driving, which in some cases
can simply be viewed as controlling the velocity and steering of a vehicle.
The behavior reﬂex approach is more direct as it takes the inputs and produces a driv-
ing action directly. In practice, vast amount of collected data is required to produce
meaningful results. The data can be obtained by driving on real roads or from simu-
lator. Compared to mediated perception, the behaviour reﬂex approach fails to deal
with complicated driving scenarios as the representation of the scene state can be am-
biguous. One example is the variation of drivers’ actions, for example one may chose
to follow a vehicle or decide to overtake. Although many such cases could have been
captured, it is diﬃcult to obtain the full scene context from a still image. Moreover,
the model can struggle to learn if the inputs are roughly the same but the outcome
scenarios diﬀer (follow or overtake scenario). Since the inputs are directly mapped to
actions, the interpretability of the driving decisions vanishes into a model “black-box”.

3.1
Related work
Behaviour reﬂex
The simplicity of directly mapping the inputs to driving actions
dates back to Pomerleau 1989. The author of the ALVINN system adopts a neural
network to output steering angles in relatively simple terrain with very few obstacles.
The DARPA autonomous vehicle project (Muller et al. 2006, DAVE) has the same goal,
however it uses a convolutional neural network. Additionally, a larger dataset was
collected in variety of scenarios by a human driver.
A more recent work published by NVIDIA (Bojarski et al. 2016, PilotNet) also
utilizes a relatively simple CNN architecture along with a normalization layer and
fully connected layer (in total 250 000 parameters) to predict steering angles from
200 × 66, 3 channel image inputs. Compared to the previously mentioned works, the
model was trained on GPUs, which signiﬁcantly speeds up the training time compared
to using CPUs and enables the operation of the system at 30 FPS. The dataset used
consists of around 72 hours of driving in various weather, lighting and road conditions.
Furthermore, image augmentation was performed by slightly shifting and rotating the
image. Unfortunately, the authors only present “autonomy” as an evaluation metric,
penalizing the self-driving time when a human needs to intervene. Providing MSE or
MAE would have been beneﬁcial for reproducibility or comparison with other datasets.
A short section on visualizing the internal state of the CNN is provided with a follow-up
publication by Bojarski et al. 2017.
Direct perception
C. Chen et al. 2015 propose the combination of both self-driving
approaches to produce indicators (aﬀordance for driving actions) for task speciﬁc to
when the car is driving in lane or is on the lane markings (based on TORCS dataset).
Using a real world dataset (KITTI), where the lane marking might be missing, the au-
thors use two convolutional neural networks to predict 2-D coordinates of nearest car
in left, center and right area of the viewpoint. The ﬁrst CNN is responsible for handling
close ranges (using scaled down input) and the second CNN uses a centered crop of
the original input to cover the area of up to 55 meters ahead. The results have shown
that direct perception approach outperforms behaviour reﬂex in traﬃc, however when
the behaviour reﬂex approach has proven to be eﬀective on an empty track.
Similar approach is used in Chi and Mu 2017, however instead of hand-picked fea-
tures, the authors have designed a feature-extracting sub-network with a 128 dimen-
sional output trained real world driving data from Udacity. The sub-network’s input
is a sequence of RGB frames and the architecture makes use of spatiotemporal convo-
lution (Tran et al. 2015) together with convolutional LSTM layers (Shi et al. 2015).
The presented model was compared with popular CNN architectures for classiﬁcation
(AlexNet, VGG-16) and NVIDIA’s PilotNet. As expected, PilotNet, which has very few
parameters compared to likes of AlexNet and VGG-16, performed the worst, while
larger VGG-16 beat AlexNet but had 50 % worse RMSE than STConv + ConvLSTM +
LSTM. It is important to note that all pure CNN models used 1 frame input and did
not utilize information from the past. Moreover, several data augmentation strategies
were compared, concluding that temporal subsampling and cropping isn’t beneﬁcial

and spatial subsampling (from 640 × 480 to 320 × 240) barely aﬀects the loss while re-
ducing the memory requirements for training. The authors also propose training and

Privileged training
Synthesis of behaviour reﬂex and mediated perception is also ex-
plored in Xu et al. 2016. Application of privileged training (Sharmanska et al. 2013)
means that in addition to predicting the egomotion, semantic segmentation is taken
as a side-task during the training phase. The motivation behind this approach is that
while driving in traﬃc, small objects and less frequent scene phenomena can be cap-
tured with semantic segmentation and boost the model’s performance compared to
the behaviour reﬂex approach. The prediction of egomotion was formulated ﬁrst as a
discrete and later as a non-parametric “continuous” problem. In the former case four
states are used as output (left, right, straight and stop) whereas in the latter the steer-
ing angle and the vehicle speed are binned either linearly, logarithmically or data-wise
within a certain range. Therefore, both of the problem formulations are single-labeled
multi-class classiﬁcation problems. The architecture of the privileged multi-task model
consists of dilated fully connected convolutional layers (Yu and Koltun 2015) followed
by an LSTM layer with 64 hidden units (akin to Donahue et al. 2014). However, the
authors found out that normal convolutional layers perform slightly better when there
is no side-task. In both cases the CNN part (visual encoder) is adapted from AlexNet
and several other architectures for non-privileged training were compared. Tempo-
ral convolution with a 9 frame window achieved about the same accuracy (84 %) as
FCN/CNN+LSTM and 1 frame CNN had only 2 % lower accuracy than the previously
mentioned models. The dataset used for non-privileged training consists of around
27 000 video sequences with 36 s length and 640 × 360 resolution sampled at 3 FPS for
a total of roughly 2.9 million frames, which is almost 2.5 times more frames than 2012
ImageNet dataset. To prove the claim that privileged training improves performance
on limited amount of data the authors used 1000 video sequences (108 000 frames)
and discrete objective. The multi-task model beat the behaviour reﬂex approach 72 %
to 71 % in terms of accuracy, conﬁrming the authors’ hypothesis, albeit with a rather
small diﬀerence. Even though the margin was small on paper, inspecting real world
scenarios such as waiting on an intersection with red light showed that privileged train-
ing method infers the scene context and predicts high probability of stopping while
the behaviour reﬂex approach was uncertain and had almost equal probabilities for
continuing straight and stopping. In another scenario, when waiting behind a vehicle
whose driver just stepped oﬀthe brake, the behaviour reﬂex was more aggressive and
wanted to continue immediately in comparison to the privileged model that correctly
predicted to remain stopped for the time being.

3.2
Data collection
Collecting high quality driving and sensory data is expensive and requires signiﬁcant
technical overhead of synchronizing, cleaning, storing, oﬄoading and processing the
raw data. The collection process starts by equipping one or more vehicles with sensors
such as LIDAR, radar, GPS, various cameras and more as well as connecting to vehi-
cles’ Controller Area Network buses (CANbus), which utilize diﬀerent ports, message
formats to which the car manufactures don’t generally publicly release the speciﬁca-
tions. Secondly, driving in plethora of weather, lighting and road conditions takes a
lot of time and planning – ideally the drivers would cover urban as well as rural areas,
highways with varying amount of lanes and sceneries, diﬀerent types of intersections,
traﬃc lights, merging lanes and drive in uncommon situations such as roadworks, po-
licemen controlling the traﬃc ﬂow and so on. After collecting this data, it needs to
be oﬄoaded from the vehicle ﬂeet. Optimally this process would happen periodically
and would be done without any human interaction. Once the data comes in, checking
for anomalies or corruption is vital, after which it would be transferred, synchronized
and stored to enable further processing. In cases of object detection and semantic seg-
mentation tasks human annotators are required to obtain high quality labels, which
is time consuming and error prone, however crowdsourcing options became popular
with the rise of Amazon’s Mechanical Turk, but again, the annotation quality might
not reach the desired level and further error checking is advised. For more informa-
tion about how to conduct driving data collection see Fridman et al. 2017; Yu et al.
2018; Janai et al. 2017.

3.3
In context of Deep Learning
This section will explain why and how (deep) convolutional neural networks became
prevalent in autonomous driving related tasks, as well as explain several state-of-the-
art CNN architectures and compare their strengths and weaknesses.
Most people would agree that driving is largely based on the driver’s vision and previ-
ous experience. For example, a human driver perceives the lane markings and is able
to follow the road by projecting this visual information into steering angles. Of course
there are other factors at play when it comes to diﬀerent driving scenarios and being
on the road with other cars. However, it is reasonable to start exploring autonomous
driving from simpler conditions such as when there is no traﬃc or there is only a single
lane.
Previous work (Bojarski et al. 2016; Chi and Mu 2017; C. Chen et al. 2015; Xu et
al. 2016) suggests using convolutional neural networks to predict the steering angle
(among other state variables) given a visual input. This problem can be formulated
in several ways. Namely, the visual input can either be a single image or a sequence
of images sampled in time, while the output (prediction) can be a continuous or a
discrete value. The discrete value output is considered due to the fact that in reality
small perturbations of the steering wheel don’t aﬀect the heading of the vehicle.
Given the fast pace of development in CNN architectures, state-of-the-art designs will
be tested. Another factor that inﬂuenced this decision is the choice of older architec-
tures used in previous work (Xu et al. 2016; Chi and Mu 2017, both used AlexNet
for single frame input). These older architectures often contain more parameters, are
shallower, have higher memory requirements and higher number of ﬂoating point op-
erations, for a detail overview see Canziani et al. 2016.
3.3.1
Visual feature extraction
Any computer vision task that makes use of CNNs works in the following manner: ﬁrst,
the convolutional part of the neural network takes a high dimensional input (image)
and turns it into a same or lower dimensional representation (such as a 3-D tensor or
a vector) by means of convolutions, pooling and striding. Afterwards, this represen-
tation can be passed into subsequent parts of the model, for example in classiﬁcation
and regression problems, fully-connected layers with respective activation functions
are appended; for segmentation or image-to-image tasks some form of upsampling is
added.
To illustrate, NVIDIA’s PilotNet is presented. The network architecture consists of 5
convolutional layers and 3 fully-connected layers. The input (an RGB or YUV image
of 200 × 66 × 3) is passed through a batch-normalization layer, into the convolutional
layers with 5 × 5 (ﬁrst three) and 3 × 3 (last two) kernel sizes, 2 × 2 (ﬁrst three) or
==PAGE:32==
3.3 In context of Deep Learning
25
no strides (last two) and 24, 36, 48, 64 and 64 ﬁlters, after which a 18 × 1 × 64 tensor
remains that is ﬂattened into a 1152-D vector and passed into 3 fully-connected layers
of 100, 50 and 10 neurons. In the end a single value (steering angle) is predicted. It
is clear that the architecture depends on the input size, taking a larger input would
produce a signiﬁcantly larger ﬂattened vector, subsequently increasing the number of
parameters and “shifting” the complexity towards the fully-connected layers. However
there exist more general convolutional architectures that place fewer constraints on
the input shape (as will be shown later in Section 3.3.2).
The state-of-the-art convolutional neural networks, namely the winners of ImageNet,
have shown that deeper architectures tend to perform better than the shallow counter-
parts. For example NVIDIA’s PilotNet only contains 5 convolutional layers while some
deep CNNs go up to 150 layers.
3.3.2
Residual networks
Work of He et al. 2015a has introduced a novel idea of residual connections5 enabling
the rise of highly accurate, deep networks, which are easier to optimize and have
lower complexity than the previously used architectures. After the publication and
success in COCO and ILSVRC (ﬁrst place in classiﬁcation, detection and localization
track of the 2015 ImageNet competition) a lot of research has gone into residual net-
works, notably architectures such as ResNeXt (Xie et al. 2016), DenseNet (G. Huang et
al. 2016) and Inception-ResNet were introduced along with papers exploring interest-
ing properties of residual networks, for example the depth versus width performance
tradeoﬀ(Zagoruyko and Komodakis 2016, by placing importance on residual connec-
tions rather than the depth) or ensemble-like properties of residual connections (Veit
et al. 2016).
The motivation behind residual networks arises from the “degradation phenomenon”,
where constructing a deeper network does not reduce or maintain the training error,
however the training error begins to increase after certain depth. This points to an
optimization problem of vanishing/exploding gradient. Residual connections exploit
the fact that the representation of the underlying mapping H(x) of few stacked lay-
ers can be expressed by a residual F(x) as H(x) = F(x) + x, which as presented in
the source paper proves to be easier to learn. If the optimizer has trouble approximat-
ing the identity mapping in the traditional formulation, the residual formulation helps
the optimizer overcome this problem by simply zeroing out the weights of the residual.
On the other hand, if the function is close to the identity mapping, the residual for-
mulation helps the optimizer to ﬁnd the optimal representation more easily by ﬁnding
perturbations rather than learning the representation as a new mapping. The experi-
ments conducted in the source paper show that this preconditioning is reasonable as
the learned residual functions produce small responses.

3.3.4
Temporal aspect
Autonomous driving can be improved by accounting for previous states instead of pre-
dicting the future state from current information only. For example, speed prediction
naturally beneﬁts from a sequential input, in practise it is much easier to gradually
decrease the speed when the driver registers a car in front slowing down, while the
speed adjustment is very hard if not impossible to get right from a single frame.
To model such scenario the sequence of input frames is represented as a tensor of
shape (S, H, W, C) where S indicates the sequence length, H and W the dimensions
of a single frame and C is number of channels (3 for RGB, 2 for optical ﬂow). In
recent years several CNN architectures have been developed, starting with 2-D archi-
tectures such as frame fusion (Karpathy et al. 2014) or two-stream frame and motion
model, which takes a single frame as the input as well as a stack of optical ﬂows (Si-
monyan and Zisserman 2014). Later, the 2-D convolution was extended throughout
the sequence (time-distributed) and fed to a recurrent (LSTM) layer(s) (Donahue et
al. 2014), however the low-level motion information gets lost by ﬂattening the spatial
representation. To combat this problem, networks consisting of 3-D convolutional lay-
ers have been proposed but the higher dimension convolutional calculations are more
complex and time consuming making it hard to train a (deep) 3-D CNN from scratch
and as such Qiu et al. 2017 propose pseudo-3D approach combining the best of both
worlds. The architecture is inspired by ResNets and substitutes the 2-D convolution
with two consecutive 3-D convolutions with 1 × 3 × 3 and 3 × 1 × 1 kernels. On the
other hand Carreira and Zisserman 2017 propose to ease the training of deep 3-D
CNNs by ﬁrst bootstrapping the weights from established 2-D architectures (for exam-
ple Inception trained on ImageNet) by so called “boring-video ﬁxed point” procedure,
where the input image and ﬁlters are simply copied and rescaled across the temporal
dimension to preserve the original network response (see section 2.4 of the article).
3.3.5
Side tasks
While driving is mostly based on visual input, the scene understanding can be broken
down into several smaller subtasks, such as paying attention to the road signs, other
vehicles, surroundings or monitoring the current speed. In a purely behaviour reﬂex
==PAGE:36==
3.3 In context of Deep Learning
29
end-to-end manner, the model does not need to understand the subtasks themselves,
however it has been empirically proven that adding a side task with an objective func-
tion boosts model’s performance in the main task (Chi and Mu 2017; Xu et al. 2016),
eﬀectively turning the model into a multi-modal one.
The most basic side task is inclusion of IMU/state readings, for example given steer-
ing angle prediction as the main task, the side tasks can be predicting the speed. In
such case the model’s architecture remains unchanged but in more complicated side
tasks additional components can be incorporated, namely semantic segmentation is
described. Note that this task is an active ﬁeld of research in computer vision and only
a high level overview is presented.
3.3.5.1
Semantic segmentation
The goal of (pixel-level) semantic segmentation is to label all or some pixels of the in-
put image into categories. Popular architectures include residual connections, dilated
(atrous) convolution (Yu and Koltun 2015) and transposed convolution or upsampling
(encoder-decoder pattern, see U-Net by Ronneberger et al. 2015). Additionally, some
models are built on top of a residual network (backbone) with extra layers to produce
the segmentation maps from the spatially decimated input — for example DeepLab
(L.-C. Chen et al. 2017) and PSPNet (Zhao et al. 2016) make use of dilated convolu-
tion and pyramid-like representation to cover the whole receptive ﬁeld via increasing
dilations, followed by upsampling to segmentation maps.
3.3.6
Transfer learning
Given the rise of very deep convolutional neural networks and signiﬁcant advances
in deep learning speciﬁc hardware, it is substantially harder to train a model from
scratch on a regular desktop computer or a laptop. Take PilotNet and VGG-16 as an
example, the former has around quarter million parameters while the latter has over
hundred million, resulting in higher memory usage for just storing the model, hence
having to compromise with lower batch size for the larger models and slower training.
To alleviate the slow training without investing into new hardware, one can make use
of transfer learning.
The basic idea is that all convolutional architectures exhibit the same behaviour for
the ﬁrst couple of layers’ ﬁlters. They tend to learn the representation of low level
basic patterns such as colored blobs or textures/Gabor ﬁlters. This is further exploited
by reusing appropriate weights from an already trained model and get better perfor-
mance right of the bat compared to random initialization schemes. Most common
CNN architectures provide pretrained models on ImageNet data or the weights are
curated by respective deep learning frameworks.
If the problem’s domain is somewhat similar to ImageNet data (be it classiﬁcation,
==PAGE:37==
3.3 In context of Deep Learning
30
regression, segmentation, object detection, etc.) the pretrained (base) model can be
“frozen”, preventing all weight/bias updates, while additional (non-base) layers are
trained, for example on a new set of classes. Afterwards, the new model can be “ﬁne-
tuned” by unfreezing parts of the base model and training with a smaller learning rate,
potentially repeating this process several times. Even if the problem’s domain is not
similar to ImageNet, the ﬁrst couple of convolutional layers, which act as basic pattern
detectors, can be kept frozen while allowing the deeper layers of the base model to
be trained and initialized with better than random weights in order to achieve faster
training and convergence even if the training dataset is small.
Furthermore, if there are no available weights, the base model can be trained on a sim-
pler problem and reused as a “backbone”, i.e. a sub-part of a new model. Such training
scheme can be utilized for the aforementioned two-stream architectures, where the
sub-model of the single frame stream is pretrained before training the two-stream
model. Another example is a time-distributed CNN + RNN model where the convolu-
tional part is applied to every frame of the sequence and passed to a recurrent layer
such as LSTM or GRU, in which the recurrent part would be ﬁrst trained while freezing
the CNN part, followed by tine-tuning.

4.1
Dataset choices
Dataset from AirSim was chosen as the ﬁrst candidate due to its simplicity and exten-
sibility. Even though there are no other vehicles, the roads in the “landscape” environ-
ment are more diverse (uphill/downhill climbs, bridges, etc) compared to inner-city
or highway roads. Additionally, the dataset can be easily extended by collecting more
data through diﬀerent driving strategies from the simulator without much overhead,
that is the data is directly stored to disk, no need to oﬄoad the data from a vehicle.
Adapting the proposed models to a reinforcement learning environment would be a
possibility with a simulator. Overall the dataset consists of 46 688 RGB frames with
256 × 144 resolution.
4.2
Data augmentation
The augmentation strategies discussed in this section are dataset-agnostic, however
careful consideration is required when choosing what techniques should be applied. It
is always recommended to manually inspect the dataset before making any decisions.
4.2.1
Image augmentation
Several general image augmentation strategies are used. First, a sub-crop of the orig-
inal image is taken, removing unnecessary parts such as the skyline. Note that when
working with images that include traﬃc, cropping might reduce the ﬁeld of vision and
cut-oﬀparts of objects located close to the camera, for example back of a truck. Next,
brightness is randomly increased or decreased simulating lighter or darker lighting
conditions. Afterwards, the image and corresponding steering label is ﬂipped along
the y-axis with 50 % probability, eﬀectively doubling the size of the dataset or pro-
viding a mean to balance out skewed steering (Chi and Mu 2017, discourage image
mirroring as it produces unrealistic scenarios on real world roads). In the end, up to
90 % of the frames within a batch with 0 degree steering angle are dropped, this is
done because the drivers spend majority of the time going straight.
==PAGE:39==
4.2 Data augmentation
32
Heavy augmentation such as adding rain, fog, shadows, lens ﬂare and adjusting some
colors to mimic diﬀerent seasons (or even using style transfer) are possible but com-
putationally expensive, therefore these strategies aren’t used.
Optical ﬂow computed from two frames can also be considered when information
about motion is required. OpenCV provides several algorithms to calculate the optical
ﬂow but pretrained deep learning models are also available.
4.2.2
Label augmentation
A continuous target variable can be converted to a discrete one, changing the prob-
lem from regression to classiﬁcation. The transformation is problem dependant and
in some cases (such as discretizing the steering angle), the classes are very similar.
Several binning strategies are tried along with taking suggestions from the available
literature. Linear and logarithmic mapping oversaturate few bins around 0 degrees
while vastly under-representing extreme angles. Xu et al. 2016 conﬁrm that dividing
the steering angle range into linearly or logarithmically spaced bins isn’t as eﬀective as
looking at the angle distribution and adopting a “data-driven” strategy. This strategy
would ideally allow equal number of samples in each bin, however 0 degree steering
is so frequent that one class will simply be imbalanced. The (non-)uniformity of the
binning can be controlled by calculating S times number of desired classes percentiles
over the distribution, then discarding percentiles that are too close to each other (by
threshold T) and interpolating desired number of classes over the leftover percentiles
(see Figure 4.1). Pseudo code of this approach is listed in Algorithm 1.

Setup

4.4
Baseline
Establishing a good baseline is important for veriﬁcation of deep learning models. For
regression task, the most common or a random value is chosen as the baseline. When
it comes to classiﬁcation, the most common class or N most common classes with equal
probabilities will be used.
Furthermore, NVIDIA’s PilotNet model is also taken as a baseline to beat, mainly due
its simplicity and lower memory requirements. Input of 200 × 66 is used as mentioned
in the original article. The optimizer, learning rate and possibly dropout are chosen
based on hyperparameter tuning backed by HyperOpt (Bergstra et al. 2013).
Comparison between classiﬁcation and regression models is also made. For a classiﬁ-
cation model the output of the softmax layer y is mapped to a single value s (steering
angle) via bins b as s = ∑N
i=1 yibi or equivalently s = y·b and compared to the ground
truth through means of MSE and RMSE.
4.5
AirSim dataset
As mentioned in the beginning of this chapter, the AirSim dataset contains 46 688
samples of RGB images and steering, angle and throttle targets. Ten percent of the
data is withheld from the model during the training phase and marked as the test set.
The rest is divided and shuﬄed randomly into training set and validation set (overall
7 : 2 : 1 train, validation, test ratio).
4.5.1
Augmentation
The image augmentation described in Section 4.2.1 is applied along with randomly
ﬂipping the input frames and corresponding steering labels vertically. Next, the cate-
gorical versus regression mapping is explored as explained in Section 4.2.2. Mainly,
the goal is to ﬁnd whether discretizing the steering labels improves overall perfor-
mance. Lastly, inclusion of optical ﬂow as input is explored.
4.5.2
Model selection
To predict the steering various types of (deep) residual networks are tried, namely
ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. These networks can be modiﬁed by making them “wider”,
meaning that the number of ﬁlters per layer is multiplied by a width factor (Zagoruyko
and Komodakis 2016). Running optimal hyperparameter search for ResNet-34 re-
vealed that adding a 0.22 or a 0.33 dropout rate (in Keras’ terminology, the fraction of
inputs is randomly set to zero) after the global pooling layer leads to best performance,
therefore all residual networks include this modiﬁcation.
==PAGE:42==
4.5 AirSim dataset
35
Furthermore, the performance is compared to NVIDIA’s PilotNet and two-stream Pi-
lotNet, which takes an RGB frame as input to one stream and 10 past optical ﬂows
as input to the second stream (inspired by merging Simonyan and Zisserman 2014;
Bojarski et al. 2016).
4.5.3
Training and results
All of the models listed in Table 4.1 have been trained from scratch and use hyperbolic
tangent activation function in the last layer to limit the steering prediction within
[−1, 1] range.
The best results for PilotNet category of models are obtained with the Adam opti-
mizer and learning rate of 1×10−4, while reducing the learning rate by half when the
model’s validation loss plateaus and stopping the training if the validation loss does
not improve over 6 consecutive epochs.
Following the practice from ImageNet training on ResNets, the training’s dataset mean
for each channel is subtracted from the input. The categorical models’ loss function
is weighted cross entropy with the weights being determined from the binned distri-
bution. After lot of trial and error, the best performing model SE-ResNet-34 is taken
as the candidate for categorical mapping. Next, the number of bins and binning sen-
sitivity are varied to ﬁnd the best conﬁguration for the discretized model. Over the
course of the experiments it has been established that adding label smoothing via a
1-D Gaussian kernel improves the mapped MSE from any number of class labels.
As for the regression task, several residual architectures are tested. Namely, pure
ResNets, squeeze and excitation modiﬁcation of ResNets, and wide ResNets. Even
though these architectures are similar, their number of parameters and performance
diﬀers.
4.5.4
Discussion
Looking at the results it is clear that residual networks perform better than the base
PilotNet model. This is expected as the number of parameters is often more than 100
times higher in ResNets (see PilotNet vs ResNet-34 in Table 4.1) and ResNets have 17
and 33 convolutional layers compared to PilotNet’s 5. Wide ResNets-18 perform al-
most equally as plain ResNet-34 but at higher parameter cost. However wide ResNets-
34 have 28%–43% lower MSE than plain ResNet-34. Furthermore, the addition of
SE-blocks boosts performance by 3%–10%. Unfortunately, the bottleneck variants of
ResNets (-50, -101) turned out to be hard to train from scratch and produced worse
results even with transfer learning applied.

4.5.4.1
PilotNet architectures
The proposed two-stream PilotNet model has 30% lower MSE than the original imple-
mentation. Naturally, the gain is acquired by adding stack of optical ﬂows as input to
the second stream, which doubles the number of parameters but requires higher pre-
processing complexity due to several optical ﬂow calculations. Additionally, the train-
ing time is roughly double of PilotNet since the best results are obtained by ﬁrst train-
ing the original implementation from scratch and then training the full two-stream
model.
4.5.4.2
Label augmentation
The continuous to discrete mapping (i.e. turning a regression problem into classiﬁ-
cation) proved to be very dependent on number of bins and binning sensitivity. For
example looking at 80 bins and sensitivity of 50 and 100, the MSE is 4.6 · 10−3 and
3.0 · 10−3 respectively, suggesting that higher sensitivity might be better, however,
looking at 40 bins of 50 and 100 sensitivity, the MSEs are much closer, 2.9 · 10−3 for
the former and 3.2 · 10−3 for the latter, which contradicts the previous observation.

Overall, the mapping strategy is problem dependant and does not generalize well.
Furthermore, it introduces additional hyper-parameters to already complex models.
One upside is that the training is generally faster. Comparing it to the results of the
regression problem, even the simplest PilotNet model outperforms the best binning
model 2.0 · 10−3 to 2.9 · 10−3.

Failed attempts
Other than the listed architectures, other models were also tried but yielded poor to

els, combination of time-distributed 2-D CNN models and LSTM/GRU layer(s), either
trained from scratch or ﬁne-tuned also produced subpar results. Modifying PilotNet
with ConvLSTM (Shi et al. 2015) layers also proved futile.

4.6.1
Model selection
Since ResNets have performed so well on the AirSim dataset, they are also picked
here and compared to reported results from Chi and Mu 2017. Similar training pro-
cedure is adapted as the one described in the article, that is using the Adam optimizer
with learning rate 1 × 10−4 and halving the learning if the loss plateaus (via Keras’
ReduceLROnPlateau callback). Since the proposed ResNet model does not make use
of temporal information, the batch size is increased from 4 to 32.
To incorporate the temporal aspect, instead of a two-stream architecture the P3D and
I3D networks are chosen, mainly due to availability of a larger training dataset and
to see if state-of-the-art networks for action recognition can beat the “mix-all” archi-
tecture proposed in the article. Additionally, in case of training from scratch being
infeasible, it is possible to fall back to transfer learning and employ the strategies dis-
cussed in Section 3.3.4. The training procedure remains the same as mentioned before,
however due to increased memory requirements caused by the sequential nature of
the input, the batch size is set to 4.
The training is done on a single GPU (NVIDIA GeForce GTX 1080 Ti or Titan Xp)
compared to cluster of 6 Titan X GPUs mentioned in the article. Overall the training
takes from around 12 hours (ResNets) to up to 2 days (3D CNNs) in comparison to
the articles’ 4-5 days. One of the reasons for shorter training time is that the proposed
architectures are less complex than the Spation-temporal/LSTM/ConvLSTM network
used in the article.
4.6.2
Loss function
Following Chi and Mu 2017, the model produces a multi-modal output for steering,
speed and torque, hence the overall loss function C consists of three RMSE terms and
is deﬁned as
C = Csteering + α Cspeed + β Ctorque
(4.1)
where α = 1/10 and β = 1/10 to emphasize the steering loss term, however the
parameters can be freely adjusted.
For example if the model’s input was a sequence of frames, the speed loss could be
emphasized more as it is easier to predict the speed in that scenario compared to a
single frame input.

4.6.5
Discussion
While Chi and Mu 2017 provide a well structured overview of their work, there are a
few errors and inconsistencies regarding some model architectures. To address these
issues, the ﬁrst section of discussion covers the diﬀerences between the own imple-
mentation and the presented models in the aforementioned article.
4.6.5.1
Caveats
The Udacity challenge states that the model should only take one or more frames as
input, however in Chi and Mu 2017 it is mentioned that (previous) state vector input
is added in the recurrent part of the model. In all the presented models of this thesis,
the input is taken as a single frame or stack of frames, no state information is used.
Furthermore, Chi and Mu 2017 have incorrectly implemented PilotNet, they mention
frame size of 200 × 88, however the original article (Bojarski et al. 2016) uses 200 × 66,
this fact is especially visible in “Weight” column of table 2 (p. 7) where the reported
number of parameters is around 600 thousand but the original implementation has
only around 250 thousand (the increase comes from ﬂattening the output of the last
convolutional layer before the ﬁrst fully-connected layer, the output of the convolu-
tion is 4 × 18 × 64 instead of 1 × 18 × 64, therefore the ﬁrst fully-connected layer has
460 900 parameters instead of 115 300).
Due to storage and time constraints, only a subset of the Udacity dataset is used. Com-
pared to Chi and Mu 2017 who have used 404 916 frames for training, own models
have been trained on 103 833 frames (segments 09/29/2016, 10/03/2016 and CH2)
and managed to produce better results than the source paper.
4.6.5.2
Search for the best architecture
As expected, the newer (SE)-ResNet-34 architecture beats older AlexNet and VGG-
16 even when using half the spatial resolution (see Table 4.2). This again conﬁrms
that the residual architectures outperform older style of convolutional neural networks
and require less memory to store the model. Additionally, it is easily possible to train
the shallower variants of deep architectures such as ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 from
scratch.
The CNN + LSTM approach (Donahue et al. 2014) was superseded by 3-D CNNs as
transfer learning from the Udacity dataset on single-task steering angle regression as
well as adding a side-task of semantic segmentation and pre-training on Cityscapes
yielded very poor results, often worse than 2-D ResNets at the cost of higher training
time and numerical instabilities. The latter most likely due to LSTM layers and the
former due to time-distributed CNN base model as well as dilated convolutions for
semantic segmentation model backed by ResNet-34.
==PAGE:48==
4.6 Udacity dataset
41
However, training from scratch proved to be challenging for 3-D CNNs. The pseudo-3D
model could not converge, most likely due to the deep architecture of 63 layers (based
on 3-D ResNet-50) and with no publicly available weights to apply transfer learning
this architecture has not been further explored. Conversly, transfer learning is applied
to the inﬂated 3-D Inception model with great success. Carreira and Zisserman 2017
have provided pretrained weights on the DeepMind Kinetics dataset (Kay et al. 2017)
and the ImageNet dataset for both RGB and optical ﬂow inputs1.
To prepare the model for training, the ﬁrst two Inception blocks are frozen with pre-
trained weights (in terms of model nomenclature up to “Mixed_3c” layer) and the rest
of these blocks are initialized with pretrained weights but the layers themselves are
trainable. Since the original model is used for classiﬁcation, the “head”/classiﬁcation
block is discarded up to the global pooling layer and replaced with fully-connected
layers for steering, speed and torque output. Best results were achieved with Adam
and learning rate of 1 × 10−3 and gradient clipping as some training runs showed
gradient explosion. To mimic the same conditions as described in Chi and Mu 2017
the speed and torque losses are equally weighted with α = 1/10 and β = 1/10 to
emphasize the main task of predicting the steering angle. Additionally, denser and
larger temporal coverage produces better results, the best model of Chi and Mu 2017
uses 15 × 640 × 480 input in comparison to I3D’s 32 × 320 × 240 (both at 20 FPS).
Even though the source paper reports double the loss with temporal down-sampling,
I3D beats the best model even if the input sequence is taken as every fourth frame of
the original video. Better performance can be attributed to the deeper architecture
and preserving the spatio-temporal structure (i.e. omitting the recurrent layers or not
projecting the input to a lower dimensional vector directly). Moreover, Qiu et al. 2017
point out that the LRCN approach, which is partially used in Chi and Mu 2017, suﬀers
from neglecting the low-level motion information while 3-D convolution does not. All
in all, the ﬁne-tuned I3D model leads to 15%–40% reduction in RMSE compared to
the best result obtained by Chi and Mu 2017 while predicting the steering angle at
17 FPS on a 1080 Ti GPU, indicating that it would be possible to get predictions in
real time when using specialized hardware such as NVIDIA Drive boards or consumer
GPUs. Note that industry standard threshold for real time is established as reacting
within 100 ms, whereas fastest human reaction time is somewhere between 100 ms and
150 ms and typically, drivers take 600 ms to react when expecting an interruption (Lin
et al. 2018; Janai et al. 2017).

CHAPTER5
Conclusion
This thesis has explored the concepts of deep learning in the ﬁeld of autonomous driv-
ing, namely convolutional neural networks. As autonomous driving is a complex and
emerging ﬁeld in deep learning and makes use of multitude of sub-ﬁelds in computer
vision that are all active research areas, it is not possible to cover every aspect in this
thesis. Therefore, the prediction of the steering angle based on spatial and temporal
input is covered in the experimental part. The problem is modeled as regression ﬁrst,
then as classiﬁcation by discretizing the steering angles based on data-driven strategy,
which however did not prove to be eﬀective when comparing MSE to the regression
models. The model architectures were chosen from previous work but due to the rapid
development in computer vision they are shallow and relatively simple to today’s stan-
dards.
State-of-the-art deep networks are also explored for single frame steering angle pre-
diction. Speciﬁcally, residual networks with their wide and squeeze and excitation
variant. These models provide ﬂexible architecture, the ability to be easily trained
from scratch, and better performance, which is shown in the results.
The training was carried out on two datasets, one being generated from a photo-
realistic simulator (AirSim) and the other from real world driving data collected by
Udacity. In the AirSim experiments a well known, relatively shallow, convolutional
architecture consisting of roughly 250 thousand parameters created by NVIDIA called
PilotNet is compared to deep residual networks with more than 100 times the number
of parameters and up to 5 times the number of layers. As expected, the residual net-
works consistently beat PilotNet in terms of MSE with the best model having 4 times
lower error. A simple yet eﬀective adaptation of PilotNet is introduced by applying a
two-stream architecture with a single frame input and stack of past optical ﬂows. This
model performs 30% better than the original PilotNet at the cost of computing optical
ﬂows.
The focus for the Udacity dataset was to beat the best result of their competition as well
as results presented by an article after the competition has ended. In order to achieve
the best results, more complex models operating on sequence of frames have been
trained. The available literature points out that training of these 3-D CNNs is much
more diﬃcult than their 2-D counterparts, therefore concepts from transfer learning
==PAGE:51==
5 Conclusion
44
were applied to great success. In addition, multi-task learning (on vehicle state) was
introduced to further ease the training. Overall application of the aforementioned
approaches on the 3-D inﬂated Inception v1 model has lead to 40% reduction of the
RMSE compared to the best model published to date.
Furthermore, future work can dive deeper into 3-D and pseudo 3-D CNNs either by
acquiring more data for training models from scratch or creating a ﬂexible pipeline
for training various 3-D architectures where the models would be ﬁrst pretrained on
repeated ImageNet, followed by training on a true video dataset such as DeepMind’s
Kinetics and in the end trained on domain speciﬁc dataset leveraging transfer learning.
Training on multiple GPUs can speed up either of the aforementioned proposals and
allow more rigorous hyperparameter optimization of the I3D model in conjunction
with training an ensemble of models. Additionally, multi-task learning can be further
expanded by introducing a side-task that resembles driver’s attention such as object de-
tection or semantic segmentation if such data is available. Adding more input sources
is a possibility, but requires architectural changes to the proposed models. Popular
input sources include point cloud data from LIDAR or depth maps. Other formula-
tions of steering angle prediction can also be devised, for example parametrizing the
label distribution or other encoding functions that would emphasize spatial relations
between the mapped classes (Eraqi et al. 2017).
All in all, the carried out experiments show that spatio-temporal models are well suited
for predicting the steering angle. Considering that substantial amount of research is
invested into novel 3-D CNN architectures and strategies for easier training as well as
application of concepts learned from traditional CNNs, the results are likely to improve
greatly in the future and can possibly lead to manufactures incorporating more end-
to-end trained models into the self-driving pipeline of their autonomous vehicles.
