Abstract 
Early life events are considered of great importance in the development of mood disorders. 
Maternal stress during pregnancy has both short-term and long-term consequences in the 
brain development in children. These children have a higher incidence of neuropsychiatric 
disorders e.g. depression and anxiety. Furthermore, a sexual dimorphic pattern in the 
disorders exists. Women have a 50 % higher risk of developing depression compared to 
men suggesting that women are more sensitive to adverse stimulation. The stress-mediating 
brain circuitries are thought to be structural altered after prenatal stress. 
 The aim of this study was to investigate structural brain changes in female and male 
offspring at a young adult age after exposure to a variable prenatal stress paradigm between 
gestational day 13 and 21. Two stress-mediating brain circuitries were sought investigated; 
the hippocampus and the basolateral amygdala. The hippocampus was subdivided into the 
cornu ammonis 1 (CA1) and the dentate gyrus (DG) region of the hippocampus. The 
hippocampus was investigated in both the ventral and the dorsal part, since these two 
regions differ in functions during stressful events. To investigate the structural changes a 
free-floating fluorescent immunohistochemistry approach was utilized. The brain sections 
were stained with the γ-aminobutyric acid-ergic (GABAergic) interneuron parvalbumin 
(PV) and the astroglial marker, S100B.  
An increased S100B cell number in the DG region of the hippocampus was found in the 
prenatally stress offspring. The number was higher in males than females. In the BLA, a 
higher cell number was seen in males suggesting that a sex difference exists, but also a 
higher S100B cell number was seen in the prenatally stressed offspring compared to the 
counterpart female group. PV positive cells were only affected by prenatal stress in the 
BLA region with an interaction between treatment and sex. Sex differences were indicated 
in both prenatally stressed offspring and control offspring. The findings suggest that 
prenatal stress may affect structural brain changes. This study highlights the importance of 
subdividing the hippocampus in the ventral and the dorsal region, as well as the CA1 and 
DG region. Furthermore, the study confirms the importance of evaluating both female and 
male offspring.

1. Introduction 
Mood disorders have an extensive health and economic influence on the society today. 
It is indicated that early life events can contribute to later neurodevelopment outcomes 
e.g. depression and anxiety. Children born to women who were stressed during 
pregnancy are more prone to develop anxiety and depression-disorders (V Glover 2014; 
Richardson et al. 2006). Additionally, women have a 50 % higher risk of developing 
stress and depression-like disorders suggesting that women are more sensitive to 
adverse stimulation compared to men. It is unclear whether structural brain differences 
may contribute to the sex-differences.  
Maternal exposure to various stressors increases the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal 
(HPA) axis response, and thereby increases the glucocorticoid level in the placenta. The 
elevated glucocorticoid level can be fatal for the developing fetus. It is further shown 
that children exposed to prenatal stress have altered short and long-term physiological 
and behavioral abnormalities e.g. lower birth weight, locomotion and cognition 
problems, increased anxiety, and higher risks of developing depression (V Glover 
2014).  Structural brain changes in the prenatally stressed offspring may at least explain 
the physiological and behavioral changes.  
Prenatal stress is associated with structural brain changes and can vary dependent on 
sex, duration and severity of the stressors, and type of strain used in the animal studies 
(García-Cáceres et al. 2010). Prenatal stress is known to cause changes in among others 
the hippocampus, amygdala, and the prefrontal cortex. From animal studies it is known 
that the dorsal and the ventral hippocampus have distinct functions (Segal et al. 2010) 
and may therefore be affected differently by prenatal stress. One example is the 
glutamate release that has been found to be impaired specifically in the ventral 
hippocampus (Marrocco et al. 2012). Furthermore, it has been shown that the different 
amygdalar nuclei are affected differently after prenatal stress exposure (Charil et al. 
2010). Of the different amygdalar nuclei, the basolateral amygdala (BLA) has been 
linked to regulation of anxiety-like behavior (Cullen et al. 2013). However, it is still not

clear how the different brain structures in the stress-mediating brain circuitries are 
affected by prenatal stress. 
Cells that may be affected by prenatal stress are the astroglial-specific neurotrophic, 
S100B, and the calcium-binding protein parvalbumin (PV), expressed on γ-
aminobutyric acid-ergic (GABAergic) interneurons. S100B cells are expressed by a 
subtype of astrocytes and are considered important due to their crucial role in normal 
brain development. Furthermore, S100B cells have previously been found to be affected 
in the hippocampus of prenatally stressed offspring (Barros et al. 2006; D. Van den 
Hove et al. 2006). The PV inhibitory neurons undergo neurogenesis in late stage of fetal 
development. This is considered to be the most critical stage when exposed to prenatal 
stress and may be involved in the development of psychiatric disorders. For instance, 
PV positive cells have been shown to be decreased in medial prefrontal cortex and 
hippocampus in schizophrenic patients (Uchida et al. 2014). However, if the of the 
inhibitory neurons and the astroglial cells are affected by prenatal stress still remains 
unclear.  
The prenatal repeated variable stress model is used in this study to investigate structural 
changes in the offspring brain. The repeated variable stress model is used to avoid 
potential habituation as it is seen for repeated restraint stress models. The potential 
consequences of prenatal stress on structural brain changes will be examined by 
performing immunohistochemistry on free-floating sections for both offspring exposed 
to prenatal stress as fetus and non-stressed control offspring.

2. Aim of study 
To identify the underlying structural brain differences it is essential to investigate the long-
term effects of prenatal stress on synaptic transmission in both males and females. 
Therefore, a prenatal stress rodent model was established previous to this project in order to 
induce possible structural brain changes. The aim was to investigate the long-term effects 
of prenatal stress on brain structures involved in stress and mood disorders. This was done 
by employing double-staining fluorescent immunohistochemistry technique using the 
astrocytic marker, S100B, and the inhibitory neuronal marker, PV. The brain regions in 
focus were the dorsal and ventral hippocampus and the BLA.  
The following hypotheses are to be investigated:  
i. 
Males and females are expected to differ in brain structure independently of prenatal 
stress.    
ii. 
Prenatal stress is expected to cause structural brain changes in the hippocampus and 
in the BLA in a sub-region dependent manner. 
iii. 
Prenatal stress is hypothesized to be sex dependent regarding structural brain 
changes. 
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) studies have been performed in advance and are sought 
compared to the results obtained from the immunohistochemistry study.

3. Background 
3.1. Stress 
Stress is a normal response that is beneficial for the body to cope with emotional and 
physiological challenges. One can be exposed to various types of stressors and they can be 
either acute or chronic. Stress hormones are released after exposure to stressors and mediate 
both adaptive and maladaptive responses by interaction with different tissues and thus, 
exposure to stress not only affects the brain but also the immune, cardiovascular, and 
metabolic systems (Selye 1975; B. S. McEwen 2007; Hansen et al. 2009).  
The acute stress response involves activation of the autonomous nervous system and the 
HPA axis. Activation of the HPA axis results in a flight-or-fight response that makes it 
possible to respond to the stressor and thereby make it possible to take immediate action 
regarding the stressor (B. S. McEwen 2000; B. S. McEwen 2007).  The HPA axis is 
activated by external signals, e.g. stress (Herman et al. 2005). Activation of the HPA axis is 
a response from stressful stimuli, and is initiated in the medial parvocellular cells of the 
hypothalamic paraventricular nucleus (PVN). These neurons synthesize and secrete 
corticotropin-releasing hormones (CRH) and vasopressin. CRH travels to the adrenal 
pituitary in order to modulate the release of adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into the 
systemic circulation. When ACTH binds to the adrenal cortex glucocorticoids are 
synthesized and released. The released glucocorticoids then travels to either the brain or 
peripheral target tissue (Figure 1, Herman et al. 2005).

Additionally, the regulation of the HPA axis also implicates several brain regions, e.g. the 
hippocampus, amygdala, and prefrontal cortex. The hippocampus and prefrontal cortex are 
both involved in the inhibitory effect of HPA axis secretion whereas amygdala is 
implicated in the continued activation of the HPA-axis and thus glucocorticoid secretion 
(Herman et al. 2005). Amygdala is important in processing emotions and affective 
networks and is also linked to both fear and pleasure responses (Qiu et al. 2015). The 
hippocampus is a critical part of storing and declaring memory including spatial memory 
==PAGE:16==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis

where the prefrontal cortex is critical in cognitive control by organizing thoughts and action 
from other regions (M. Moser et al. 2015; Miller & Cohen 2001). 
Glucocorticoids, cortisol in humans and corticosterone in rodents, are important in the 
homeostasis of the body’s response to stressful events and act as key regulators of the HPA 
axis activity.  Furthermore, they are crucial in termination of the stress response by a 
negative feedback mechanism on HPA-axis. This mechanism is important in order to limit 
the exposure time to glucocorticoids in the different target tissues and thereby minimize the 
damage they can cause. The secretion of glucocorticoids in vertebrates is usually controlled 
by the circadian rhythm where it peaks in response to the initiation of the active phase 
(Herman et al. 2005). Glucocorticoids also target peripheral tissue, among others the 
cardiovascular system, adipose tissue, gastrointestinal system, immune system, the liver, 
and the brain (Reynolds 2013). Both mild and severe stressful events have been shown to 
have a direct adverse effect on multiple immunological mechanisms such as down 
regulation of cytokines, which are important for an adaptive immune response (Padgett & 
Glaser 2003).  
Two intracellular receptors for glucocorticoids are found in the brain, the glucocorticoid 
receptor (GR) and the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR, Tsigos & Chrousos 2002). GR is 
expressed in multiple brain regions. However, the binding affinity (KD) for this receptor is 
only 5-10 nM and thus binding of glucocorticoids will only appear under intermediate to 
high secretion rates of glucocorticoids. This is especially seen during circadian 
glucocorticoid rhythm and during stressful events. Supporting this, in a study by De Kloet 
et al (1998), it was shown that GR is a critical factor in the regulation of the HPA axis 
response as it acts on the negative feedback mechanism. On the other hand, the MR has a 5-
10 fold higher KD compared to GR resulting in binding of glucocorticoids during baseline 
secretion levels (Herman et al. 2005). The expression of this receptor is only found in the 
brain and is mostly expressed in hippocampus where the GR is more broadly distributed 
(Reul et al. 2000).  Additionally, the MR is a factor that regulates the normal function of 
HPA axis during low baseline levels of glucocorticoids (De Kloet 1998).

3.1.1. Prolonged stress 
If exposed to stressors for prolonged periods the body’s coping systems incl. the HPA axis 
responsiveness is impaired and can have a severe or even fatal influence on the homeostasis 
including body temperature, pH, and oxygen tension. If the body fails to adapt to the 
prolonged stressors the HPA axis activity will be increased, which is caused by an increase 
in ACTH and corticosterone secretion (B. McEwen 2001; B. S. McEwen 2007). Allostatic 
overload is a state where the response of the HPA axis is not turned off after exposure to 
stressors; it can be caused when the stressors become too severe or when they occur too 
frequently. This allostatic overload can cause changes in the response of the HPA axis and 
is depending on the ability to adapt to stress (Selye 1975). This can be explained by a 
desensitization of the glucocorticoid receptors causing an impaired negative feedback 
mechanism exposing the body for a prolonged time period of cortisol exposure that can be 
fatal for the body (Leonard 2005; B. S. McEwen 2004). Exposure to stressors for prolonged 
time has been associated with multiple diseases such as cancer, cardiovascular diseases, and 
psychiatric disorders (Salleh 2008).  
Exposure to stress during the pregnancy does not only affect the mother but also the fetus. 
Children exposed to stress during the fetal development have a higher risk of developing 
affective disorders later in life (Talge et al. 2007). 
 
3.2. 
Prenatal stress 
Prenatal stress can be caused by different types of stressful events during the pregnancy. 
These events can be war situations, earthquakes, and major life events in general including 
death in the family or problems at home (Zijlmans et al. 2015; Glynn et al. 2001). The 
exposures can vary from milder to severe stressors, which can be damaging to the fetus. 
Prenatal stress is associated with increased risk of different neurodevelopmental disorders 
in the child in both childhood and adolescence. Stressful events that occur during 
pregnancy can permanently change brain structure and impair behavior in the offspring 
(Reynaert et al. 2015). The effects of prenatal stress are thought to vary depending on the 
==PAGE:18==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 8  
 
time of stress exposure partly since neurogenesis occurs at different times in various brain 
regions during gestation.  
Human studies have shown that several health and developmental outcomes are associated 
with prenatal stress exposure in the offspring.  Maternal psychosocial stress has been shown 
to affect the gestation and birth outcome in relation to low birth weight and preterm birth 
(Entringer et al. 2010). Furthermore, children exposed to prenatal stress are more prone to 
have a physical handicap and suffer from hyperactivity (Ward & Stehm 1991).  
3.2.1. Mechanisms whereby stress can be transferred to the fetus 
During pregnancy the fetal development is very sensitive and can be impaired by small 
events. Changes in fetal development can be caused by factors acting directly on the fetus 
or indirectly via environmental factors acting on the mother. Several mechanisms have 
been suggested to be involved when the fetus is exposed to prenatal stress, however; more 
studies are needed to finally conclude on the involvement of e.g. cytokines, serotonin (5-
HT), and impaired cortisol levels (Entringer et al. 2008; Madhyastha 2008; V Glover 2014).    
In pregnant women the HPA axis will during pregnancy gradually be less responsive to 
stress in order to protect the fetus from too high levels of cortisol since increased cortisol 
level is inversely correlated with infant cognitive development (Austin, et al. 2005; 
Weinstock 2005) Animal studies have indicated that increased corticosterone level in 
pregnant dams is one of the underlying mechanisms that mediate a direct impairment of 
fetal development (Marta Weinstock 2005).  
Increased cortisol level in the mother following prenatal stress could increase in the cortisol 
level that crosses the placenta. The fetal exposure to cortisol is dependent on the placental 
enzyme, 11-β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase 2 (11β-HSD2), which metabolizes the active 
cortisol to the inactive cortisone and thereby protects the fetus from too high cortisol 
exposure. Both human and animal studies have shown that if the mother is exposed prenatal 
stress during pregnancy this may decrease the level of 11β-HSD2 in the placenta, which 
causes an increased fetal cortisol exposure (Figure 2, Charil et al. 2010; Mairesse et al. 
2007; Murphy et al. 2006).

Prenatal stress is also suggested to impact the serotonergic system of the brain, by inducing 
changes in the brain’s 5-HT neurons. Altered brain 5-HT is thought to partly induce the 
effects of prenatal stress on the offspring’s neurocognitive and behavioral development 
(Madhyastha 2008). Finally, epigenetic changes are being investigated as a mechanism by 
which prenatal stress impairs brain development and thus mediate alterations in offspring. 
A study investigating the effects of epigenetics modifications, found that the global DNA 
methylation was changed in offspring exposed to prenatal stress in both the hippocampus 
and frontal cortex in females and the frontal cortex in males (Mychasiuk et al. 2011).  
Furthermore, changes in methylation of the GPM6A genes in the hippocampus were found

Master’s Thesis

following prenatal stress. GPM6A encodes a neuronal glycoprotein involved in synapse 
formation (Monteleone et al. 2014; Sickmann et al. 2014). 
Neurogenesis is an important mechanism in development of normal brain function. The 
neurogenesis is the mechanism by which new neurons are generated from progenitor cells 
(P. E. S. E. Riksson et al. 1998). It is known that the neurogenesis is affected by exposure 
to prenatal stress. Prenatal stress induces abnormalities in the hippocampus and thus 
inhibits neurogenesis.  An explanation to this could be the increased activity of HPA axis 
following prenatal stress exposure since glucocorticoids inhibit hippocampal cell 
proliferation, and thus inhibit neurogenesis after prenatal stress (Gould et al. 1992; Lemaire 
et al. 2000; Maccari et al. 2003). It is still sought elucidated to find the cells that may be 
impaired after prenatal stress to better understand how the neurogenesis is affected.  
The brain is not the only part of the body that is affected in the offspring after prenatal 
stress exposure. The development of the fetal immune system is also affected. Many 
immune cells have receptors specific for hormones involved in the HPA axis (Entringer, 
Kumsta, et al. 2008; O’Rourke et al. 2014). Furthermore, there is an association between 
inflammatory responses and depression, and since prenatal stress causes higher risks of 
inflammation there may be a higher risk of developing depression later in life (Butkevich et 
al. 2009).  
 
3.3. 
Prenatal Stress Animal Models 
Repeated stress during the pregnancy is known for its impact on development of depression 
and anxiety in the offspring, however, the behavioral and neuroendocrine consequences are 
dependent on time and type of the stressor (Koenig et al. 2005). For instance, the effect is 
thought to be proportional with the severity (Wei Zhang et al. 2014). Furthermore, the 
frequency and the pattern of the stressor may also affect the outcome (Wei Zhang et al. 
2014; Richardson et al. 2006). Two types of prenatal stress animal models are 
conventionally used for experimental induction of gestational stress; the restraint stress

Page | 11  
 
model and the repeated variable stress model. The repeated variable prenatal stress model 
vary in the type and number of stressors during the pregnancy (Koenig et al. 2005).  
Most prenatal stress studies are based on stressing the pregnant female rats with the same 
stressor repeatedly. Restraint stress models can be performed in different ways e.g. restraint 
stress, restraint with bright lights, or saline injection (inducing physical discomfort) 
(Koenig et al. 2005; Barbazanges et al. 1996). The basics of these types of models are that 
the pregnant dams are repeatedly exposed to the same stressor e.g. three times a day for a 
limited time period. This model is known to have a direct impact on the mother and may 
therefore have an indirect impact on the fetus (Barbazanges et al. 1996; Ward & Weisz 
1984).  However, using the same type of stressor may cause the animal to adapt to the 
stressor and thus decrease the HPA axis response (homotypic habituation, Koenig et al. 
2005; Wei Zhang et al. 2014). Repeated exposure to different stressors, on the other hand, 
may elicit a stronger HPA axis response, and thereby achieving a more robust model for 
prediction of the effects of gestational stress on the human offspring (Koenig et al. 2005; 
Richardson et al. 2006).  
A study by Koenig et al. (2005), proposed a stress model using a repeated variable stress 
approach. This variable stress paradigm is based on multiple stressors with randomized 
exposure times in order to be able to compare the results to the type of stressors humans are 
exposed to in developed societies (Koenig et al. 2005). It is found that uncontrollable 
exposure to stressors may to a greater extent change the anxiety behavior as well as the 
weight of the adrenal gland (Wei Zhang et al. 2014).  The stressors commonly used for this 
type of animal model include forced swim test (FST), social stress, shift in light/dark phase, 
and restraint in a restrainer (Koenig et al. 2005). 
In order to apply the animal models to humans differences in gestation and brain 
development have to be considered. In rats, the gestation period is 22 days, where 
gestational days (GD) 14-22 are considered to be equivalent with second trimester of a 
human pregnancy (Kinnunen 2002).  However, compared to brain development in humans 
the fetal development of the rat brain is continued postnatal, which is important to consider 
when the effects of late stage stress are of interest (Dobbing & Sands 1979).

Very few studies have compared the correlation between repeated variable stress paradigms 
and repeated restraint stress paradigms, and it is still not clear, which model is the best 
when investigating prenatal stress. For instance, it is suggested that restraint stress 
compared to variable stressors shows different behavioral and neuroendocrine profiles in 
adolescence and adulthood. Thereby, the choice of model depends on what is sought to be 
investigated (Richardson et al. 2006). Animal models make it possible to understand the 
pathophysiology and the basal functions, since it is possible to better control the 
experimental conditions. Most important, the prenatal stress models enables structural and 
functional studies in the brain in order to reveal the changes prenatal stress has caused. 
 
3.4. 
Effects of Prenatal stress on offspring  
3.4.1. Behavioral alterations  
3.4.1.1. 
Human studies 
The effects of prenatal stress on behavior can emerge throughout life; in infancy, 
childhood, adolescence, and adulthood (V Glover 2014). Already in infancy behavioral 
impairments have been observed including temperamental variations (Buitelaar et al. 2003; 
Austin et al. 2005). Sleep problems are also observed in infants and toddlers in the age of 6 
months to 30 months (O’Connor et al. 2007). A prenatal stress study, in infants aged 14 to 
19 months has shown that their cognitive performance was lowered compared to a control 
group. Furthermore, an increased fearfulness was observed among the infants in this study 
(Bergman et al. 2007).  
Several studies, have investigated neurodevelopmental outcomes associated with prenatal 
stress in children aged 3 to 16. These studies have shown increased risk of emotional 
problems in the child with a special focus on anxiety and depression (O’Connor et al. 
2002). However, symptoms of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) and conduct 
disorder are also more frequent after prenatal stress exposure (Van den Bergh & Marcoen 
2004; Huizink et al. 2007; V Glover 2014). Depending on the stress exposure several 
outcomes have been observed. Studies have shown an increased risk of autism when 
==PAGE:23==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 13  
 
exposed to prenatal stress in mid- to late stage of pregnancy (Ronald et al. 2010). However, 
other studies have failed to find an association between prenatal stress and autism (J. Li et 
al. 2009). Schizophrenia has been shown to be associated with prenatal stress in the first 
trimester, while children with conduct disorders have been exposed to maternal stress in 
second or third trimester of the pregnancy (Khashan et al. 2015; van Os & Selten 1998; V 
Glover 2014). 
In adolescence and adulthood, there is an increased risk of dysregulation of physiological 
parameters leading to metabolic changes such as significant higher body mass index, 
hypertension, and primary insulin resistance that increases the risk of developing diabetes 
(Entringer et al. 2008; Entringer et al. 2010). Furthermore, sex differences were also 
observed in other studies, e.g. males have a higher incidence of anxiety-like behavior and 
ADHD compared to females. Females, however, are more prone to display depression-like 
behavior (P. Zhu et al. 2015; Wei Zhang et al. 2014).  
Studies have found a high incidence of a masculinization effect on females after exposure 
to prenatal stress. The prenatally stressed girls displayed a more male-typical behavior, 
which was indicated by their play, courtship, and social orientation compared to a control 
groups. In males a more infantilized behavior was observed. (Barrett et al. 2014).  
MRI studies have mostly been performed in humans. A study showed that a larger right 
amygdala volume was associated with affective problems in girls but not in boys after 
exposure to higher maternal cortisol concentration during pregnancy (Buss et al. 2012). 
3.4.1.2. 
Animal studies 
Several behavioral studies regarding prenatal stress have been performed such as 
exploration in novel environment, social and sexual behaviors, and behavior under stressful 
events (S. Maccari et al. 2003). The behavioral alterations in animal models are 
investigated in early life, adolescence, and adulthood. Early life studies have shown that the 
prenatally stressed animals have decreased locomotor activity, increased fear and anxiety-
like behavior, and are less explorative in their behavior  (A. C. Huizink et al. 2001; Poltyrev 
& Marta Weinstock 1997; Valle et al. 1997). However, a study by Deminiére et al. (1992) 
showed differing results with increased locomotor activity to novel conditions (Deminiére 
==PAGE:24==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 14  
 
et al. 1992). Prenatal stress also affects the ability to adapt to stressful conditions 
postnatally, which was shown in 14-day-old prenatally stressed offspring by a reduced 
vocalization (L. Takahashi et al. 1990; L. K. Takahashi 1994). The decreased vocalization 
is thought to be an expression of anxiety in pups (Gulia et al. 2014). 
Sex differences in behavior are observed in both human and animal prenatal stress studies 
(Van den Hove et al. 2013). Sex-dependent effects of prenatal stress is hypothesized be 
associated with sex-specific timing of important developmental processes during gestation, 
however the mechanism is not clear (Owen & Matthews 2003; Van den Hove et al. 2013). 
Depressive-like behavior was observed in both female and male rats indicated by an 
increased immobility in the FST (Alonso et al. 1991; Abe et al. 2007). However, another 
depression study indicated that prenatally stressed females had a longer length of 
immobility in the FST compared to the counterpart males, indicating that females show 
more depression-like behavior than males (Glover & Hill 2012). 
Furthermore, a study by Markham et al. (2010) showed that male offspring had a higher 
risk of cognitive impairments after prenatal stress exposure. Cognitive impairments are 
associated with both depressive-like behavior and schizophrenic symptoms, and may 
indicate that males are more prone to develop e.g. schizophrenia later in life (Markham et 
al. 2010). It is still debated what causes the sexual dimorphic behavior observed in both 
humans and rodents and it needs to be elucidated. It is, however, suggested that the brain 
structure in males and females are differently affected by prenatal stress (Barrett et al. 
2014). 
3.4.2. Structural brain changes induced by prenatal stress 
The prefrontal cortex, amygdala and the hippocampus, are all brain regions considered 
essential in mediating stress responses and the regions are critical for cognition, fear, 
learning, and memory formation (García-Cáceres et al. 2010). Long-term alterations caused 
by prenatal stress are also found to be sex-specific in brain regions involved in behavior (J. 
Bock et al. 2011; Murmu et al. 2006). Structural alterations in the brain may be associated 
with the development of ADHD, depression etc. when exposed to stress in critical periods 
of brain development during pregnancy (García-Cáceres et al. 2010).

Neurogenesis is not only occurring during brain development, but also postnatally (Dokter 
& Bohlen 2012). Adult neurogenesis is observed in some brain regions in among others the 
subgranular zone (SGZ) in the hippocampal region, in the subventricular zone (SVZ), the 
neocortex, the amygdala, and the striatum. However, in the amygdala, striatum, and 
neocortex the neurogenesis may occur at lower levels compared to the other regions 
(Dokter & Bohlen 2012). Prenatal stress is associated with differing consequences in 
different amygdala nuclei (BLA, central amygdalar nuclei, and lateral amygdala nuclei) and 
consequently the amygdalar nuclei should be investigated independently (Kraszpulski et al. 
2006).  
It is known, that prenatal stress has an effect on the dentate gyrus (DG) neurogenesis in 
offspring (Kawamura et al. 2006). Prenatal stress has been suggested to have an impact on 
cell proliferation at the embryonic stage in hippocampus, nucleus accumbens, and 
amygdala (Kawamura et al. 2006)  A significant decrease in the 5-bromo-2’-deoxyuridine 
(BrdU) density was observed in the hippocampus (60%) and in nucleus accumbens (40%). 
A trend was found for amygdala with a 30% decrease. Prenatal stress may have adverse 
effects on the development of limbic regions in the offspring, however, these effects are 
still unclear (Kawamura et al. 2006). Decreased cell proliferation in hippocampus has also 
been identified in prenatal stress models in juvenile, adult, and aged male rodents (Lemaire 
et al. 2000). Furthermore, a study by Belnoue et al. (2013) suggested that prenatal stress 
decreased the cell proliferation and neurogenesis on a long-term basis in the DG region of 
the hippocampus (Belnoue et al. 2013). Doublecortin staining in hippocampus suggested 
that the number of immature neurons in the hippocampus in prenatally stressed offspring 
was reduced. It was observed in both female and male offspring (Rayen et al. 2011).  
The ventral hippocampus is related to anxiety-related behavior where the dorsal part is 
more connected to spatial leaning (Kheirbek et al. 2014). It is hypothesized that during 
normal situations the hippocampal response is linked to other brain regions via the dorsal 
efferents. However, when exposed to stressful events it is suggested that the functioning of 
the hippocampus is altered. During the stressful events it was indicated that the ventral 
efferents were emphasized over the dorsal efferents. The ventral efferents synchronizes 
with more stress-related areas of the brain such as the BLA (Segal et al. 2010). The BLA 
==PAGE:26==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 16  
 
region have been suggested to be an important factor in the regulation and governance of 
anxiety-like behavior (Cullen et al. 2013). However, the BLA is not well studied in prenatal 
stress studies, and it remains uncertain if prenatal stress alters the BLA. Different studies 
have shown that after prenatal stress the dorsal and the ventral hippocampus are affected 
differently (Marrocco et al. 2012). Furthermore, it is still unclear whether the subregions of 
the hippocampus (i.e. DG, cornu ammonis (CA) 1 and 3) are functionally alike in the dorsal 
and ventral regions (Kheirbek et al. 2014). 
A study by Zhu et al (2004) has investigated neuronal changes in prenatally stressed rat 
offspring. They found that the number of hippocampal neurons is significantly decreased in 
female offspring but not in male offspring (Z. Zhu et al. 2004). Furthermore, there is a 
suggested correlation between decreased neurogenesis and reduction of granule cells in 
hippocampus after prenatal stress (Tamura et al. 2011). Additionally, it has been found that 
prenatally stressed females have a reduced number of granule cells compared to controls, 
confirming that  that neurogenesis may be affected as a consequence of prenatal stress 
(Schmitz et al. 2002).  
The effect of prenatal restraint stress on synaptic plasticity in hippocampus is affected in a 
sex- and region specific manner (P. Zhu et al. 2015). Expression of both phosphorylated 
extracellular signal-regulated kinase 1 and 2 (ERK1/2) are recruited when the brain 
undergoes synaptic plasticity and remodeling ERK1/2 is recruited. Females had increased 
ERK in CA1, CA2, and CA3 region of the hippocampus, where males only showed 
increased ERK expression in the CA3 region of the hippocampus after exposure to prenatal 
stress. (Z. Zhu et al. 2004). This suggests that subdividing the hippocampal region may 
elucidate other structural differences. Studies have indicated the importance of a region 
specific alteration after prenatal stress (Z. Zhu et al. 2004). 
S100B, an astroglial-derived neurotrophic factor, has an important role in normal brain 
development and is found to stimulate cell proliferation in glial cells (Selinfreund et al. 
1991). Elevated S100B levels are found in young adults with mood and psychiatric 
disorders after childhood psychological trauma. However, young adults with these 
disorders with no childhood trauma did not show significant elevated serum levels (Falcone 
et al. 2015). Furthermore, increased S100B levels may contribute to psychopathology of 
==PAGE:27==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 17  
 
different neuropsychiatric disorders, however, their specific role in these disorders still 
remains a question (Buschert et al. 2013). It is suggested that an elevated S100B makes 
young adults more susceptible to environmental stimuli; negative stimuli in adolescence 
increases the risks of psychiatric disorders (Buschert et al. 2013). 
In one study, the overall S100B level was reduced in one-day old offspring in the 
hippocampus after prenatal stress (D. Van den Hove et al. 2006). In females, the S100B 
level was positively correlated with fetal growth, where in males the hippocampal S100B 
level was negatively correlated with the level of corticostorone in plasma (Van den Hove et 
al. 2006). The morphological changes in the stratum radiatum region of the CA1 after 
prenatal stress exposure showed that there may be a long-lasting astroglial reaction. 
Furthermore, indications of changes in dendrites and synaptic loss in the adult offspring 
brain (90 days old offspring) were found. Glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) immune 
reactive astrocytes had an increased cell area with increased expression of S100B. (Barros 
et al. 2006). Additionally, a study by Garcia-Cáceres et al. (2010) also showed increased 
S100B level in prenatally stressed male offspring. However, stress in adulthood did not 
change the S100B level, suggesting that the astrocytes in the offspring brain were 
specifically affected by prenatal stress (García-Cáceres et al. 2010).  
All studies investigating changes in S100B after prenatal stress are either measuring serum 
level or using immunohistochemistry. However, it is not elucidated where in the 
hippocampus the changes have occurred. Furthermore, the S100B has not been investigated 
in the BLA that is known for being connected to the ventral hippocampus and may thus be 
affected by prenatal stress.  
GABAergic neurons are important in regulation of normal amygdala function hereunder 
processing of anxiety and fear behavior (Zuloaga et al. 2011). GABAergic neurons are 
classified depending on three calcium-binding proteins; calretinin, calbindin, and 
parvalbumin and are expressed in different regions of the amygdala (Zuloaga et al. 2011). 
After exposure to glucocorticoids under gestation, it was found that high levels of 
glucocorticoids induced an increased neuronal apoptosis in both a region- and sex-
dependent manner (Zuloaga et al. 2011). A part of these apoptotic cells in the amygdala 
were GABAergic calcium-binding proteins. However, this was primarily seen in infants

(Zuloaga et al. 2011). Furthermore, it has been found that prenatally stressed schizophrenic 
patients have decreased PV positive cells in the medial prefrontal cortex and hippocampus 
(Uchida et al. 2014).  
The glucocorticoids altered glutamatergic neurotransmission and in the ventral 
hippocampus in prenatal stressed offspring glutamate release was decreased, however, the 
GABA release was unaffected (Marrocco et al. 2012). This prenatal restraint stress study 
indicates an association between impairment in glutamate release and anxiety-like behavior 
(Marrocco et al. 2012).  
Not many studies have elucidated the role on PV positive cells in offspring at a young adult 
age after prenatal stress exposure. Studies have investigated the structural alteration in 
relation to PV, since it is known to be a contributing factor to different psychiatric 
disorders. Subsequently, it still remains unclear if altered PV expression is affected in a 
sex- and region dependent manner after prenatal stress.

Animals  
Sexually experienced male Sprague-Dawley rats and virgin female Sprague-Dawley rats

4.4. 
Perfusion and sectioning procedures 
Offspring were transcardially perfusion fixed between PND75 and PND80, i.e. at a young 
adult age. The perfusion fixation of the rat was performed by perfusing phosphate buffered 
saline (PBS, pH = 7.4) into the rat until the liver turned whitish (30 ml/min for 5 min).  
Afterwards, 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) in PBS was perfused until the claws turned stiff 
(30 ml/min for 10 min). Subsequently, the brain was removed from the rat. The brains were 
stored overnight in 1 % PFA before transferring the brains to PBS containing sodium azide 
until use for MRI imaging and subsequently immunohistochemistry. The brains were stored 
for more than a year before conducting immunohistochemistry. All immunohistochemistry 
studies and analyzes were conducted in a blinded manner. 
The perfusion fixed brain tissue was prepared for sectioning by transferring the brains to a 
30% sucrose solution. The sucrose solution works as cryoprotectant by preventing ice 
crystals from forming when the tissue is frozen. After adding the 30% sucrose the brains 
were floating in the sucrose solution. The brains were placed in a 4oC cold room with 
gently shaking for approx. 48 hours or until the brains had dropped to the bottom of the 
container, equivalent to mature tissue. The brains were then ready for cryostat sectioning. 
The flow chart is visualized for frozen sectioning in Figure 5.

4.5. 
Immunohistochemistry 
The sectioned tissue was used for free-floating immunohistochemistry by performing an 
indirect fluorescent staining using a primary antibody followed by a fluorescently labelled 
secondary antibody. The approach was performed by double-staining the tissue sections. 
The experiment was divided into 3 batches; i) batch 1 consisted of 8 rat brains, ii) batch 2 
consisted of 9, and finally iii) batch 3 consisted of 10 rat brains.  
All procedures were conducted at room temperature unless another temperature is 
specified.  
First, the free-floating sections were washed 2-3 times with KPBS to remove excessive 
sodium azide. When the excessive sodium azide was removed, KPBS was added to each 
container and left over shake for 10 min (see Figure 7). This washing step was performed 3 
times. Afterwards, the sections were incubated with 1% hydrogen peroxide (H2O2; blocks 
endogenous peroxidase activity) in KPBS for 10 min followed by a washing step with 
==PAGE:36==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 26  
 
KPBS. An additional blocking step was applied. The blocking step consisted of 5% Normal 
Swine Serum (NSS, Jackson Immunoresearch) in a KPBS, BSA, and Triton X-100 (Sigma) 
solution (blocking buffer). The blocking solution incubated for at least 20 min while 
shaking (maximum 5 hours of incubation) in order to avoid unspecific binding of 
antibodies to antigens on the tissue sections. The blocking step was followed by a double-
staining with the primary antibodies to protein PV (Sigma-Aldrich) and to protein S100B 
(Abcam). S100B (1:100) and PV (1:200) were diluted in blocking buffer and NSS and left 
overnight at 4oC while shaking.  
The following day the sections that were incubated with primary antibodies were left to 
temperate to room temperature (approx. 30 min) before discarding the primary antibody 
solution. The primary antibody solution was discarded and the sections were washed 3 
times for 10 min/wash (shaking) with KPBS. Meanwhile, the secondary antibody solution 
was made. The solution consisted of blocking buffer, donkey anti-rabbit Alexa Fluor 488 
(1:400, Life Technologies) against S100B, goat anti-mouse Alexa Fluor 568 (1:400, Life 
Technologies) against PV, and Hoechst Stain (0.6 µl/ml blocking buffer). The secondary 
antibody solution was added and incubated for minimum 60 min while covered. 
Subsequently, the sections were washed 3 times with KPBS for 10 min/wash.  A 0.5% 
gelatine solution was prepared and utilized to mount the sections onto Superfrost cover 
slides (Thermo Scientific).  
After the last wash, the KPBS waste was discarded and the sections were added to the 
gelatine solution in a petri dish. The sections were orderly sorted depending on the brain 
regions making it easier to identify regions of interest later on. After sorting, slices were 
mounted on Superfrost slides and left to dry covered for 1 to 2 hours. When the slices were 
completely dry, cover slides were mounted with DAKO Fluoromount. The slides were 
covered with 2 carton slide trays and stored at 4oC until use.

4.7. 
Cell quantification 
The software ImageJ 1.48s was used for cell quantification. A script was developed for 
automatic cell count for each cell marker in the preliminary studies. However, when the 
macros were applied to the primary studies the intensity and quality of the images varied 
too much for the macros to properly detect the cells (alignment between manual and 
automatic cell count were lower than 90%). Therefore, a manual cell count was performed 
for each cell marker.  
Some considerations regarding the cell count were made in order to reduce variances in 
included cells between each image. An important consideration for cell number was the 
color intensity of the cells due to the 40µm thick slices, which caused the cells to appear in 
multiple layers. If the cells were too weak in intensity and the cells were too big and too 
blurry they were considered to be in a lower layer of the brain slice and were excluded from 
the count.

4.8. 
Statistical analysis  
The statistical analyzes were performed using the software SigmaPlot version 11 and all 
graphing was conducted in Excel. The statistical procedure for each of the markers of 
interest was as follows: 
First, it was checked if the data for each marker could be described as being normally 
distributed. This was done by performing a Shapiro-Wilk test for each marker. 
Furthermore, by plotting the data in a histogram data was also visually inspected. When the 
Shapiro-Wilk test gave a p-value above 0.05 (𝑝> 0.05) the data was considered to be 
normal distributed and the variable was then further analyzed as parametric. In cases where 
data failed the normality test, the dataset was transformed using a simple 𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠(𝑦)- 
transformation. Afterwards, a Shapiro-Wilk test was performed again to check if the 
transformed variable was normal distributed. Furthermore, the homogeneity of variance 
was tested. The data-set was considered homogenous if the p-value was above 0.05. 
All the variables were normal distributed or could be transformed into being normal 
distributed. Also, the test for homogeneity of variance passed and thus all variables were 
analyzed utilizing analysis of variance (ANOVA).  A Two-Way ANOVA was performed 
on the dependent variable and compared to the two independent variables denoted sex and 
condition, respectively. When the ANOVA showed a significant difference (𝑝< 0.05), a 
Post Hoc test was performed by using the pairwise multiple comparison Tukey test.

5. Results 
Prenatal stress is associated with behavior alterations in the offspring. These alterations 
may at least partly be due to structural changes in the brain. To investigate if prenatal stress 
leads to structural changes in the adult offspring brain two immunohistochemistry markers 
were used; S100B and PV.  S100B is a glial-specific calcium-binding protein and is 
expressed on a subtype of mature astrocytes. S100B is a neutrophic factor in the developing 
brain and furthermore it induces cell proliferation of glial cells (Buschert et al. 2013). The 
mean number of S100B positive cells is indicative of how many astroglial cells are present 
in the BLA and ventral and dorsal hippocampus in control and prenatally stressed offspring 
PV is expressed on GABAergic interneurons; these PV proteins are important regulators of 
intracellular calcium dynamics in neurons, which could cause changes in the brain that 
could facilitate e.g. depression (Caillard et al. 2000).  PV positive cells and S100B cells are 
expressed in both the CA1 region and DG region of the hippocampus and in the BLA. 
Images of PV positive cells and astrocytes expressing S100B were obtained from BLA, 
CA1 from both ventral and dorsal hippocampus, and DG from both dorsal and ventral 
hippocampus. The ventral and the dorsal hippocampus were examined individually based 
on the knowledge that these two regions have distinct functions in the brain.  
The experiments included a total number of 27 young adult rats.

6. Discussion 
The purpose of this study was to investigate morphological changes in inhibitory neurons 
and astrocytes following exposure to prenatal stress in young adult male and female rat 
offspring. Specifically with the focus on identifying sex differences after prenatal stress 
exposure and investigate the consequences in the ventral and dorsal region of the 
hippocampus and in the BLA region. These two brain regions were chosen as they are 
known to be impacted by stress and mood disorders. The structural brain changes were 
evaluated by quantitative measures of the number of S100B and PV positive cells in the 
BLA, the CA1 and the DG regions of the dorsal hippocampus, as well as the CA1 and the 
DG regions of the ventral hippocampus.  Neuronal changes were observed in the number of 
PV cells in both the BLA and in the dorsal CA1 region in a sex-specific manner. 
Furthermore, the astroglial S100B cell marker was found to be higher in male offspring 
compared to females. Finally, S100B positive cells in the ventral DG region were shown to 
be both sex- and treatment dependent. 
 
6.1. The effect of stress on pregnant dams 
In the current study, the corticosterone level in the stressed pregnant dams was shown to be 
increased compared to control dams (Sickmann et al. submitted), indicating that the HPA 
axis was activated.  Furthermore, this suggests that the stressed mothers were affected by 
the stress paradigm. Another indication that the stress paradigm impacted the dams was a 
smaller weight gain in the stressed mothers during gestation compared to the control 
mothers (H. Sickmann et al. 2013). Altogether, this suggests that the offspring have been 
indirectly exposed to and potentially affected by stress during gestation. Other studies have 
also confirmed fetal similarities (Wainstock et al. 2015; Storey et al. 2006).  
 
 
==PAGE:54==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 44  
 
6.2. The structure of the dorsal and ventral hippocampus were differently 
affected by prenatal stress 
It is relevant to analyze the ventral and dorsal hippocampus independently for several 
reasons. First, the two regions of the hippocampus have functional differences. The ventral 
hippocampus is implicated in the process of emotions while the dorsal hippocampus 
primarily is associated with memory and spatial learning ( Van den Hove et al. 2006). Also, 
in prenatally stressed animals glutamate release was found only to be decreased in the 
ventral hippocampus and not in the dorsal hippocampus (Marrocco et al. 2012).  
6.2.1. S100B positive cells were altered in the ventral hippocampus 
Some affective disorders are associated with glial pathology (Sanacora & Banasr 2013). In 
this study, the astrocytic distribution of S100B in the dorsal hippocampus was not affected 
by prenatal stress. However, the ventral DG in the hippocampal region suggested a prenatal 
stress effect in a sex-specific manner.  
In contrast to our findings, a study by Van den Hove et al. (2006) found that the S100B 
level in one-day-old PS offspring showed a decrease in S100B in the hippocampus (D. Van 
den Hove et al. 2006). However, in adult offspring (90 day old) there was an increase in the 
stratum radiatum of the CA1 region in the numbers of S100B positive cells (Barros et al. 
2006). In the present study, no significant differences were found in either the dorsal or 
ventral CA1 region in the young adult offspring. The differing results for the S100B 
expression could be due to the expression are dependent on the age of the offspring. The 
neurotrophic factor S100B acts in different brain regions in different time periods during 
childhood and adolescence. It was found that the concentration of S100B serum was 
changed in the first 20 years of life. However, in adults the S100B serum concentration was 
found to be constant (Briefs et al. 2002). The age of the offspring in the different studies 
may be one explanation for the variation between studies of prenatally stressed offspring in 
astroglial cells in the hippocampus. However, for this to be a reasonable, a correlation 
between S100B serum concentration and S100B expression in the brain should exist.  
The study by Barros et al. (2006) used a restrainer as the only type of stressor with three 
exposure times a day from GD14 to GD21 (Barros et al. 2006). However, in the present 
==PAGE:55==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 45  
 
study a repeated variable stress paradigm was used, which could indicate that the utilized 
prenatal stress paradigm may affect the outcome. Barros et al. did not distinguish between 
the ventral and dorsal CA1 region of the hippocampus, which could further explain the 
differences in the current study and the study by Barros et al. (2006). 
S100B is released in response to activation of the serotonin 5HT1A receptor and this 
receptor has been shown to be decreased in four-week-old offspring after exposure to 
prenatal stress in the ventral hippocampus only (Pawluski et al. 2009). In the present study, 
a significant difference was found in the ventral DG region between the prenatally stressed 
offspring and the control offspring, suggesting that the S100B cell number is elevated in 
prenatally stressed offspring. (Pawluski et al. 2009; Barros et al. 2006; Van den Hove et al. 
2006). The impaired 5HT1A receptor level in the ventral hippocampus may be related to the 
increased number of S100B positive cells in the prenatally stressed offspring, found in this 
study. This further highlights the importance of subdividing the hippocampus in ventral and 
dorsal hippocampus as well as CA1 and DG.  
There is debate about how S100B contributes to health and disease. Elevated S100B serum 
level is associated with both negative and positive consequences (Hohoff et al. 2010; 
Buschert et al. 2013; Rothermundt et al. 2003). For instance, S100B has a neuroprotective 
role by stimulating neural outgrowth, neuronal survival, and supporting the serotonergic 
system  (Rothermundt et al. 2003; Buschert et al. 2013). However, elevated S100B have 
also been associated with increased risk of development of psychological disorders and 
mood disorders (Hohoff et al. 2010; Buschert et al. 2013). A study by Buschert et al. (2013) 
indicated that emotional behavior and neuroplasticity could be affected by an interaction 
between elevated S100B levels and environmental stimuli (Buschert et al. 2013).  The 
S100B cell number was higher in the prenatally stressed offspring in the ventral DG region 
compared to the control offspring. The increased cell number could indicate that prenatally 
stressed offspring may be more susceptible to changes in the emotional processing after 
exposure to e.g. stress.

6.2.2. The number of PV cells was not affected by prenatal stress 
The pregnant dams in this study were exposed to prenatal stress in the last week of 
gestation. The GABAergic system is developed in the last stage of the pregnancy and may 
thus be affected by prenatal stress (Fine et al. 2014). Thereby, suggesting that prenatal 
stress may affect the inhibitory system in relation to cell proliferation, differentiation, and 
cell survival. There were no changes in the number of PV positive inhibitory neurons after 
prenatal stress exposure in either dorsal or ventral hippocampus. However, a sex difference 
in the dorsal CA1 region was found with an increased number of PV positive cells in 
control females compared to control males. This indicated that prenatal stress does not 
impact the development of the number of PV positive cells in the hippocampus. However, 
it may be suggested that of other consequences on the GABAergic system after prenatal 
stress may exist.   
Very few studies have investigated the expression of PV cells in the hippocampus. 
However, in a restraint stress study by Marrocco et al. (2012) an investigation of the 
glutamate and GABA release in the dorsal and ventral hippocampus was performed. 
Glutamate release was found to be decreased in prenatally stressed offspring specifically in 
the ventral hippocampus, while GABA release showed no significant change (Marrocco et 
al. 2012). The GABA release may not be affected because the neuron density remains the 
same. Another explanation may be that the efficiency of the release is either up- or down-
regulated in order to maintain the overall release at the same level. This may be in 
correlation with the fact that the present study did not show a difference in the number of 
PV positive cells expressed on GABAergic interneurons. This may lead to the suggestion 
that the PV cell number found in the hippocampus is not the reason for altered behavior in 
offspring. However, other functional effects of prenatal stress on PV interneurons may be 
the reason.  
Several studies have investigated the functional effect of PV in stress related situations. The 
inhibitory neurons, like GABAergic interneurons, are fundamentally altered in some 
psychiatric disorders (Fine et al. 2014). In the present study, the PV positive cell number 
was not altered in either the dorsal or the ventral hippocampus in the prenatally stressed 
offspring. This may suggest that it is not the PV cell density that is affected but rather a 
==PAGE:57==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 47  
 
function deficit. Glutamate decarboxylase (GAD) enzymes are involved in the function of 
inhibitory neurons by catalyzing a decarboxylation of glutamate into GABA  (Fine et al. 
2014; Barker et al. 1998). GAD65 and GAD67 (the two isoforms of GAD) are important 
enzymes during brain development and GABA cells are dependent on the level of these 
enzymes. The level of these enzymes was shown to be altered by prenatal stress (Barker et 
al. 1998). However, if the altered level is because of altered density of GABA cells or 
because of an altered efficiency of GABA cells is still not known. Brain-derived 
neurotrophic factor (BDNF) is designated to be involved in the regulation of nitric oxide-
synthesizing neurons, which is a subtype of GABAergic interneurons. As a consequence of 
prenatal stress the expression of mature BDNF was found to be long-term altered, which 
caused a decreased neuronal density in the hippocampus in stressed individuals (Grigoryan 
& Segal 2013). This suggests that inhibitory interneurons may be affected by prenatal 
stress. 
During stressful events MRs modulation of synaptic transmission in the ventral 
hippocampus is considered important to maintain the functionality in this region (Maggio 
& Segal 2009).  MRs were suggested to be important in the dendritic maturation of granule 
cells (Tamura et al. 2011). The granule cells plays are an essential role in associative 
memory. Furthermore, impaired granule cells are associated with some affective disorders 
(Tamura et al. 2011; Maggio & Segal 2009). A study indicated that prenatal stress impaired 
the expression of the MR  and thus affected the granule cell maturation (Grigoryan & Segal 
2013; Tamura et al. 2011; Maggio & Segal 2009).  
These findings indicate that it is important to divide the hippocampus not only into dorsal 
and ventral hippocampus, but also subdivide these regions further. Furthermore, the 
underlying mechanisms e.g. enzymatic reactions and down-regulation of receptors affected 
by prenatal stress should be better understood.

6.3. Altered astrocytic and neuronal numbers in BLA  
Mood disorders are often associated with structural changes in the BLA region in relation 
to size, shape, and functional changes (Hammack et al. 2012) The BLA region is a part of 
the stress system and is therefore considered to be affected by prenatal stress. In this study, 
the number of S100B positive cells was affected in a sex but not treatment-dependent 
manner. The number of cells was greater in male offspring compared to female offspring. 
Whether this may partly explain why females have a higher susceptibility to develop mood 
disorders relative to men remains to be established.  
Stress-released glucocorticoids modulate fear memory by binding to MRs and GRs in the 
BLA nuclei of the amygdala, which could increase the emotional-vulnerability (Montero-
Pedrazuela et al. 2011). After exposure to stress the MRs and GRs are thought to have 
opposite roles in regulation of synaptic plasticity (Tamura et al. 2011; Grigoryan & Segal 
2013). An imbalance in the two receptors can be affected by events in critical phases of the 
developing brain, e.g. by maternal stress during pregnancy. This imbalance could be fatal in 
relation to a dysregulation in the activation of the HPA axis (Juruena et al. 2015). Stress 
during pregnancy may increase the glucocorticoid level in the mother, which may 
contribute to a higher exposure of glucocorticoids in the fetus. This could indicate that 
prenatal stress may induce alterations in BLA development. However, based on this study, 
the elevated level of glucocorticoids in the pregnant dams from prenatal stress exposure did 
not induce alterations in the number of S100B positive cells at young adult age in the BLA. 
Not only elevated glucocorticoid level may affect the emotional-vulnerability but also 
among others malnutrition, and alcohol and nicotine exposure.  
Interactions between sex and prenatal treatment existed for the number of PV positive cells. 
This indicated that prenatal stress affects the offspring differently in males and females. 
Generally, studies show that stress exposure affects the GABAergic system in the BLA 
(Hadad-Ophir et al. 2014; Martijena et al. 2002; Giachero et al. 2015). A study by 
Martijena et al. (2002), indicated that the inhibitory control by the GABAergic system in 
the prefrontal cortex and the BLA could be contributing to the onset of fear and anxiety-
==PAGE:59==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 49  
 
like behavior (Martijena et al. 2002). However, the consequences of prenatal stress on the 
inhibitory interneurons are still not clear. 
Our results suggest that the number of PV cells was reduced in prenatally stressed females 
compared to the control females in the BLA. A recent study showed that young women 
exposed to stress as fetus had a reduced proportion of grey matter in the BLA (Favaro et al. 
2015). This indicates that the decrease in PV positive cells could be a contributing factor in 
the alterations of BLA. Another study, using a treatment-resistant depression model in rats, 
investigated the effect of maternal ethanol exposure during gestation with a 3,3'-
Diaminobenzidine (DAB) staining of PV positive cells. The study indicated that a 
disruption in positive PV cells occurred in the BLA when exposed to ethanol twice a day 
between GD10 and GD13 (Kigawa et al. 2014). This suggests that other prenatally stress-
inducing effects may have an effect on the PV cells and thus the brain structure. Different 
stress models may affect the outcome of the stress-inducing effects on structural brain 
changes, and may thus be hard to compare to the present study.  
A contextual fear conditioning study using adult male rats indicates that fear memory is 
affected by modulation of stress influence caused by GABAergic signaling in the BLA. 
Additionally, it is suggested that the dorsal CA1 region of the hippocampus is a 
downstream target of BLA neurons after stress-inducing effects. This may indicate that the 
GABAergic system in BLA modulates structural changes in the dorsal hippocampus  
(Giachero et al. 2015). However, in the present study, the alteration in PV positive cells 
was only significantly affected in the BLA and not the dorsal hippocampus.  
Furthermore, the ventral hippocampus and BLA have fiber connections and the changes 
found in these two regions may affect how the two regions communicate in prenatally 
stressed offspring. The two distinct results for PV positive cells and S100B cells may be 
due to different roles in the developing brain. 
The same offspring brains as used in this study have been investigated before the sectioning 
with a MRI technique. The MRI results for the BLA suggest that the fractional anisotropi 
(FA) value is higher in prenatally stressed animals, which is an indication of a more 
organized structure in prenatally stressed offspring. However, no sex differences were

found. One would have expected a less organized structure as a consequence of prenatal 
stress. More results can be extracted from the MRI study and this is needed to finally 
conclude the link between MRI and the structural findings in this study. In our study, only 
two cellular markers were investigated. It may be suggested to investigate other cell types 
such as cellular markers for neurogenesis and mature neurons. 
 
6.4. Structural brain differences between males and females 
In humans, the prevalence of e.g. mood disorders is higher in women compared to males. In 
several animal studies, behavioral sex-differences have also been shown (S. Maccari et al. 
2003; V Glover 2014). However, it is less clear what the underlying mechanisms for these 
sex-differences are and if they are associated with structural changes in the brain.  
Several factors are suggested to be involved in the behavioral and structural sex differences. 
The sex-difference may arise already at the fetal stage. For instance, the placental functions 
have been shown to be sex dependent due to the sex specific placental gene expression and 
the response to cortisol (Wainstock et al. 2015). Furthermore, sex hormones that are 
developed during gestation are thought to have an effect on the structural differences 
between females and males (Nazeri et al. 2015). The present study, have confirmed that 
sex-difference exists in the brain structure in both the BLA and the hippocampal regions.    
Markham et al. (2010) have demonstrated, with a repeated variable stress model in the third 
week of gestation, more impaired cognitive functions in 70-90 days old male offspring 
compared to female offspring. This could suggest that male offspring are more prone to 
develop schizophrenia (Markham et al. 2010). In different models of depression, including 
the present study, it is shown that after acute stress at an adult age (in addition to prenatal 
stress) females have a more depression-like behavior compared to the counterpart males 
(Glover & Hill 2012; Sickmann et al. 2013). These sex dependent alterations could be due 
to changed stress response after prenatal stress and thus induction of structural differences.  
The present study showed a significant difference in the S100B positive cells between 
females and males in the control group and a trend towards a sex difference in the 
==PAGE:61==
Michelle F. Tengberg 
 
Master’s Thesis 
Page | 51  
 
prenatally stressed group. In both the dorsal and ventral DG region of the hippocampus, the 
number of S100B positive cells was higher in the male offspring, suggesting a general 
higher cell number in males compared to females. A study by Van den Hove (2006), found 
that hippocampal S100B in neonates were related to sex specific parameters. This indicated 
that the corticosterone level was negatively related to S100B positive cells in males, while 
the BDNF level in females were suggested to be correlated with the S100B cells (D. Van 
den Hove et al. 2006). Although, the present study investigated the S100B cell number in 
young adult offspring, there may be a relation to the sex differences seen in this study and 
the suggested results from Van den Hove et al. (2006). Furthermore, the BLA is also 
sexually dimorphic in relation to S100B positive cells suggesting that the number of S100B 
cells is higher in male offspring compared to female offspring.  
A recent study performed in guinea pigs, revealed that PV density is sexually dimorphic in 
the BLA with a higher density of PV cells in females compared to males (Równiak et al. 
2015). In the current study, trends were also found for a sexually dimorphic number of PV 
cells in the control rats with a higher PV cell number in females. This may indicate that 
GABAergic interneurons in the BLA are contributing to the sexually dimorphic functions 
and thus the differences in brain structure. This could explain the trend towards a difference 
in PV positive cells between prenatally stressed males and females that indicates a higher 
number of PV cells in males compared to females. Furthermore, it may indicate that PV 
positive cells, and GABAergic interneurons in general, are affected by different factors 
during gestation.

7. Conclusion and future perspectives 
The prenatal stress model was conducted to investigate if prenatal stress induces 
structural changes in the brain of the offspring. It was found that prenatal stress induced 
some structural changes in offspring at a young adult age. The number of astroglial 
S100B positive cells was increased in only the DG region of the ventral hippocampus 
after prenatal stress exposure. However, in the dorsal hippocampus no changes in the 
number of S100B cells were observed. This emphasizes the importance of subdividing 
the hippocampal region in both dorsal and ventral, as well as CA1 and DG. For the 
GABAergic interneurons, the number of PV positive cells was found to be altered in the 
BLA region in prenatally stressed offspring. Furthermore, both the number of PV 
positive cells and the S100B positive cells were dependent on sex in the control 
offspring and in the prenatally stressed offspring. This indicates that sex differences do 
exist and that structural differences between males and female may be considered of 
significance (i.e. suggesting that the male offspring have a higher cell number). 
Altogether the findings in the present study may contribute to explain the sex difference 
both in cognition and behavior in both non-treated and prenatally stressed offspring. 
However, the findings in the study do not elucidate how the structural differences alter 
the brain functions or the behavior. This suggests that other studies are needed in order 
to understand the consequences of prenatal stress in children. 
It is still to be elucidated which additional mechanisms that may contribute to possible 
long-term alterations in synaptic transmission after exposure to prenatal stress. Other 
neuronal markers such as the neurogenesis marker doublecortin and the neuron-specific 
nuclear protein NeuN could be investigated in order to elucidate potential changes in 
the number of these in prenatally stressed offspring. Investigation of potential structural 
changes in other stress-mediating brain circuitries e.g. the hypothalamus and the 
prefrontal cortex could provide further insights into the consequences of prenatal stress 
on both behavior and structural changes. Furthermore, the MRI studies should be 
expanded to other stress-mediating brain regions to give more insights regarding 
structural changes.
