Abstract
Since the opening of the borders of Myanmar to foreign countries in recent
years, Myanmar’s economic growth has rose to signiﬁcant levels. Uncon-
trolled growth, together with scarce governamental measures and protec-
tionism, threatens, among others, the water quality of the major rivers of
Myanmar, especially Ayeyarwaddy river basin. Due to a weak water quality
monitoring system along Chindwin, which belongs to Ayeyarwaddy basin,
this research has the aim to evaluate the eﬃciency of current locations of
the water quality monitoring stations that were designed by Directorate of
Water Resources and Improvement of River System (DWIR). In order to
pursuit this objective, a one dimensional hydrodynamic and water quality
model built in Sobek 3.4.1.33581 was set up to predict the fate of pollu-
tants discharged into the river between Homalin and Monywa, upstream
and downstream cities respectively. To cope with the absence of reliable
sources of information, a stay of 4 months was needed. During the stay,
ﬁeld work and exaustive information, water quantity and water quality data
gathering was carried out. Outputs from the analysis of the ﬁeld work sam-
ples showed that arsenic is present at high concentrations in shore and bed
sediments. Aluminium has higher concentrations at Monywa station than
Homalin station, suggesting the presence of Al source in between. Mercury
was found below detection level.
The hydraulic model was tested using
the model performance indicators PBIAS, NSE and RSR. All three outputs
indicate that the model ﬁts very good the observed discharges at Kalewa
station. BOD, NH4, DO and Hg fate into the river was modelled. Minimum,
average and maximum BOD and Nh4 loads and Hg concentration found in
literature were used as input. Results showed that optimization process on
the existing locations can be conducted only for the ﬁrst two stations which
should be removed in case of average and maximum loads of BOD and NH4.
In case of minimum loads, the ﬁfth station should be removed as well. After
the ﬁfth station, any stations can succesfully detect low values of DO. With
regards to Hg, every station from U-Yu down to 70 Km will detect levels
above the standard limits.

1.1
Problem Deﬁnition
Myanmar is a developing country in South East Asia that recently increased
its cooperation with foreign countries. By doing so, many projects are set
up, including water related projects. The Ministry of Infrastructure and
Environment of The Netherlands planned to assist the Burmese government
in realizing a IWRM for Myanmar. The aim of the collaboration is to de-
velop a water management system to cope with the recent economic growth
in a sustainable way. According to Asian Development Bank (2014), the
GDP growth (% per year) reached 7,2 % in 2015 and it is estimated to
reach 8,4 % in 2016 (see Figure 1). The registered growth is the result of
economic agreements between Myanmar and mainly China, India and Thai-
land consisting in selling oﬀnatural resources, including billions of dollars
from gas, hydropower development and mining (BEWG, 2011). Unforeseen
increase of foreign investment, enhanced by a scarce initiative from pol-
icy makers on economic, social and environmental measures and regulatory
framework, could lead to a negative impact on the rivers and its habitat
(BEWG, 2011). Ayeyarwady and Chindwin rivers have a valuable role in
development of Myanmar.
Aung San Suu Kyi stated in the Irrawaddy Appeal of August 2011 that
”Today, the Irrawaddy is under threat. (...) A relatively new problem is
pollution. The proliferation of factories and the unregulated panning for
gold over the last decade have introduced waste matter that constitutes a
threat to rare species of ﬁsh, including the famous Irrawaddy dolphin, and
to the health of people who rely on the river for water and food”. It is
in this context, underlined clearly by the appeal of the political leader ac-
tivist of Myanmar, that, among others,eﬀective measures on management
of the water quality are becoming increasingly necessary. Reliable and ac-
curate assessment of trends in water quality has emerged as urgent need
for Myanmar to monitor, control or minimize the river pollution phenom-
ena.Therefore, continuous measurements and monitoring data are essential.
Data on water quality of rivers in Myanmar are not easily accessible due
to the fragmented water quality monitoring over several water related orga-
nizations. Khon Ra, director of the Hydrology department of the Ministry
of Agriculture and Irrigation department, identiﬁes 5 main organizations
which monitor water quality for their own purposes.
Ministry of Health
(MOH) is responsible for monitoring water quality in relation with private

1.2
Research objective and questions
The main objective of this thesis is to set up a water quality model of Chind-
win river between Homalin and Monywa, upstream and downstream cities,
in order to optimize the existing monitoring locations designed by DWIR.
The process to achieve the proposed objectives started with an in-depth
investigation of all factors and activities which aﬀect directly or indirectly,
the water quality. The collection and evaluation of data is thereby an impor-
tant aspect to pursuit the above explained objectives. Due to lack of data
available,to explore the site and to better understand which actual pollu-
tants the river system carries and potential threats, a ﬁeld work in May
2016(dry season) was organised.
The objective of this thesis leads to the following main question:
Which are the possible alternative water quality monitoring lo-
cations in the Chindwin river based on present estimated sources?
In order to help achieving the objectives and build a structural framework
for this reasearch, the next sub research questions have to be answered:
• According to the literature and to attended meetings and workshops,
which are the current sources of contamination in the Ayeyarwaddy
Basin and Chindwin Basin?
• Which agencies carry out water quality monitoring in Chindwin Basin?
Which are the most relevant existing data collected until now and how
11
==PAGE:12==
these are comparable with the ﬁeld work data?
• What are the estimated or calculated loads and resulting concentra-
tions of the most relevant substances in Chindwin River?
• Which is the fate of the modelled substances into the river system?
Which stations would detect levels of these substances below standard
limits?
A mathematical water quality model provides an approximated quantitative
description of one or more water quality state variables in a given water
system. A model delivers a simpliﬁed image of reality and is accurate as
needed or as possible.For this study, given the few data available, a model
can give a ﬁrst approximate insight on how the substances are diluited by
the river system and how far from the sources it takes for the substances to
reduce their eﬀect on the water system.

2
Case study site
2.1
Ayeyarwady and Chindwin River
The Nmai and Mali rivers originate in the glaciers of the high mountains of
the Himalayan region. After the conﬂuence of the two rivers, the Ayeyarwady
is formed. The total area of its basin is 412600 km2. More than 90% of the
basin is located in Myanmar and only 10% is located in China and India
(Varis et al., 2012). It is the main commercial water way in Myanmar, with
a length of 2170 km and an average annual discharge of 13000 m3/s with
minimum and maximum discharge of 2300 m3/s in dry season and 32600
m3/s in monsoon season respectively (Bowles, J.,2013)(see Figure 2). The
Chindwin river is one of the ﬁve major rivers in Myanmar, and is a major
tributary of the Ayeyarwady river situated in northwest Myanmar, with a
length of approximately 850 km. Its basin has an estimated area of 115300
km2 and lies mainly in Sagaing State, a smaller part also covers Chin State,
Magway State and Kachin State(Stockholm Environment Institute, forth-
coming)(see Figure 3) . Following the geological structure of the country,
Ayeyarwady and Chindwin predominantly ﬂow from north to south.
Myanmar belongs mostly to the tropical climate zone, except the north-
ern mountainous regions located instead in the sub-tropical and temperate
climate zones. Three distinct seasons alternate along the year: the rainy sea-
son, from mid-May to October, the cold season, from November to January
and the hot season, from February to mid-May.

2.2
Responsible authorities and access to existing data
Monitoring water quality has the main goal of examining whether the ob-
served water quality is appropriate for speciﬁc uses. Historical water quality
data could give an overview on the baseline conditions of the system. Time
series data, instead, could help investigating trends in the quallity of the
aquatic system and how it is impacted by the loads of contaminants from
14
==PAGE:15==
human activities.
This section presents the past and current water quality monitoring in
Chindwin River Basin.
Irrigation Department (ID) is in charge of assuring eﬃcient irrigation water
services and providing adequate drinking water. ID is responsible for water
quality sampling with regards to irrigation projects. ID performed water
sampling at 3 sites along Chindwin: Homalin, Kalewa and Monywa. Water
sampling has been carried out twice per year, one during dry season and one
during wet season. Unfortunately, it was not possible to get the results of
ID’s sampling campaigns due to the long procedure required to hand over
the data.
The Directorate of Water Resources and Improvement of River Systems
(DWIR) covers the role to prevent river water pollution and to protect
river bank erosion and river system. 12 stations on the Chindwin river were
designed to collect data. Results are shown in Appendix XX.
Stockholm Environment Institute (SEI) carried out water quality monitor-
ing activities along Chindwin under the ”Chindwin Futures Programme” in
join collaboration with Myanmar Environment Institute (MEI). The ﬁeld-
work executed 2 times in 2015, one in dry and one in wet season with the
objective of deﬁning the current status of water quality. The publication of
the report resulting from the ﬁeldwork perfomed by SEI is upcoming.
2.3
Sources of contamination
Many cities and millions of people in Myanmar rely on the Ayeyarwaddy
river for their water supply. Towns pump surface water is handled without
any treatment and distributed to the villages. In Yangon urban area water
supply comes instead from reservoirs (80%) and groundwater (20%).
In
the second largest city Mandalay, water in supplied only 10 hours per day;
90% of it is coming from the tube wells, while 10% from surface water.
Considering that Yangon registers almost 6 million people, while Mandalay
1 million and the overall population reaches 53 million, it can be aﬃrmed
that the majority of the population rely on surface water as supply. Fig.??
shows people’s daily use of river water.
2.3.1
Domestic waste
As mentioned, the current economy is heavily dependent on the agricultural
sector. Diversiﬁcation of the economy is needed and thus Myanmar planned
to expand its investments into industrial complexes. Economic growth in
developing countries often follows the urbanization process (Embassy of the
15
==PAGE:16==
Kingdom of the Netherlands, 2016). According to the Census(2014) 30% of
Myanmar’s population lives in urban areas. Six of Myanmar’s ten largest
urban centers are situated in the Ayeyarwady River basin, including Yan-
gon, Naypyitaw, Mandalay and Monywa. Yangon’s population has grown
from 4.57 to 5.21 million since 2006 and the population of smaller regional
centers Meiktila and Monywa have grown by 200% and 170% respectively
in less than 10 years.
Population growth is placing serious stress on urban infrastructures, espe-
cially water, drainage and waste disposal. Few urban areas in Myanmar
have adequate water supply and sewerage infrastructures, collection and
treatment of domestic wastewater are absent and storm water drains car-
rying untreated sewage in open channels. Only some areas of Yangon and
Nay Pyi Taw are connected to a conventional and old WWTP.
2.3.2
Mining
Myanmar has a number of rich mineral reservoirs such as tungsten, tin, zinc,
silver, copper, lead, coal and gold (Foreign Policy, 2007). Lack of protection-
ism and regulations for the prevention of damage to the environment has
been steadily increasing. Mining poses a big threat for the northern regions
which are rich of mineral resources. Gold mining is present particularly in
Kachin State, more speciﬁcally along the Ayeyarwady and Chindwin river
(see Figure4).
According to the Kachin Development Networking Group
(KDNG) in 2007 ” hydraulic mining blasts away soil, causing erosion on
river banks. Waste from the mining process, including mercury contam-
inated rocks and soil are discarded throughout the landscape”.
Further-
more, in the report of Images Asia and Pan Kachin Development Society of
2004, it is stated that the Ministry of Mining is willing to open even more
concessions to exploitation of mineral resources, above all gold, to foreign
investors. The Kyaukpahto is possibly the largest producing Au mine in
Myanmar, sited in North of Myanmar (see Figure5. The rock type in the
area where Kyaukpahto is located,is composed by veins of pyrite, chalcopy-
rite and arsenopyrite (Gardiner, N.,J. et al.,2014).
Mercury is the reagent of choice for gold mining in Kachin State. Mercury
is widely used since it is cheap and has the function to amalgamate the gold
creating a compact mixture which is then squeezed inside a domestic cloth
manually. Subsequently mercury is burnt oﬀand the solid gold that remains
is collected (KDNG, 2007). Inhabitants along mining areas and miners seem
not to be aware of the danger caused by mercury and they often handle it
by bare hand. Although most of the mercury is collected and reused, while
some amounts of it are released into the ground or into the rivers(Images
Asia and Pan Kachin Development Society, 2004).Mercury, once exposed to
atmospheric, aquatic and terrestrial inﬂuence, may undergo through trans-
16
==PAGE:17==
formations, eventually becoming methyl-mercury which is the most toxic
form of the metal ( Donkor, A., K. et al., 2006). Besides mercury, cyanide,
which is more toxic, is also used in gold mining activities. Cyanide reacts
with gold, oxidize it and form a gold- cyanide complex. The complex react-
ing with zinc, reducing again to metal gold. According to KDNG, although
cyanide is rarely used in Kachin State, it might potentially be widely em-
ployed in the future. According to both KDNG (2007) and Images Asia and
Pan Kachin Development Society, the most aﬀected and contaminated rivers
due to gold mining activities are Danai river and U-Yu river, both tributaries
of Chindwin. As mentioned, an eﬀect of protectionism and vast mining ac-
tivities is the erosion of the banks, increasing turbidity in the rivers. An
evident example of it is the U-Yu river, which is one of the biggest trib-
utaries of Chindwin. During the ﬁeld work executed in the beginning of

2.3.3
Agriculture
Agriculture, including crop, livestock, ﬁsheries and forestry, is the most im-
portant sector in the country’s economy. It is the main source of livelihood
for about 70% of the population, who lives in the rural areas. Agricultural
activities constitute 64 per cent of the labour force and it contributes with
around 42% of the gross domestic product (GDP) in 2011 (IWRM Strategic
Study, 2014). From the poverty reduction point of view, the agriculture
sector is of central importance. Improvements in agricultural productivity
are critical and essential to raise the livelihoods of around 36 per cent of the
total population which is living below poverty line. Myanmar’s government
highly prioritizes the development and improvement of this sector in order
to assure food security, employment and export promotion (ADB,2013).
According to Mckinsey (2013) in 2010 the GDP of Myanmar was estimated
to be just over 50 billion US$ and agriculture contributed with 21.2 billion
US$ (Figure 7). Rice is Myanmar’s main crop, followed by beans and pulses
which contributes largely to exports (Figure 8).Cultivation of pulses has in-
creased from 0.73 million hectares in 1988-1989 to 4.5 million hectares in
2010-2011 due to high export demand(Assesst, 2013). Cotton, rubber and
sugarcane are the main industrial crops in Myanmar. Although the pro-
ductivity of cotton remained steady at 0.7 tons/ha for many years before
2008, it sharply increased to 1.23 tons/ha in 2009 (Assesst, 2013). Eﬃciency
of water use is very low, irrigation schemes need restoration in order to be
able to produce enough for the increasing population and tourism and cope

==PAGE:21==
with the economic growth. According to Myanmar IWRM Strategic Study
of 2014, the use of fertilizers and pesticides is increasing and becoming an
issue. Fertilizers and pesticides were introduced in the late 1960s. The do-
mestic production of urea fertilizers was supplied by imported fertilizers.
After 1987-1988 the total procurement of chemical fertilizers from abroad
rose to an amount of 0.40 million mg per year. However, pesticides and fer-
tilizers are not largely used due to their high prices. Driven by new policies
enhancing agricultural production, it is expected that the price of fertiliz-

surface water or groundwater aﬀecting water quality.

2.3.4
Industrial waste
The Government of Myanmar has been undergoing a process of political
and economic reform since 2011 and is recently focusing on developing the
manufacture and industrial sector as part of its strategy of exports-driven
economic development. The industrial production growth rate is estimated
to be 12% annually. Many industrial zones are spread all over the coun-
try, mainly near large cities and villages. There are three types of industrial
zones: government industry zones, special economic zones (SEZ) and private
industrial zones. In Yangon, it was reported by Embassy of the Kingdom
of The Netherlands in Myanmar that most of the 18 government indus-
trial zones have no wastewater treatment plant. Beside the 18 industrial
zones already existing, 10 new ones are proposed, including 3 within the
Ayeyarwady river basin. The main objective of new industrial zones is to
foster private manufacturers and to attain equitable development among
states and regions (Jetro,2013). Since 2000, domestic private investors have
been developing industrial zones. Current and planned industrial zones are
shown in Figure 9. It is estimated that only around 10% of manufacturer
companies treat wastewater before releasing to the environment.
Inside the range of industrial chemicals, there is a signiﬁcant emphasis
on battery production, cyanide and arsenic from electronics production,
chromium and dyes from leather production, organochlorine compounds
from paper mills can deplete oxygen levels and cause eutrophication of
waterways.
Bowles (2013) in a short survey on water quality along the
Ayeyarwady observed that it is a common practice for cities such as Man-
dalay, Monywa, Yangon and Pathein to dispose solid waste, untreated stormwa-
ter, industrial wastewater and raw sewage into the rivers. As a result, lo-
calized pollution from urban areas represent signiﬁcant threat downstream
of urban areas. Increased pollution due to industrial waste causes water
related diseases.
For instance, the case of diarrhea and skin diseases re-
ported in Mandalay in February 2015, were caused by contamination in
Dokehtawaddy river, which was originated by wastewater from Mandalay
Industrial Zone waste dumping areas. Mandalay Industrial Zone was estab-
lished in 1990. The number of factories has grown signiﬁcantly starting from
2002, after which more and more wastewater was dumped. Villagers aﬃrm
“they were used to be able to drink from the river in the past, while now it
is even hard to bath in it”.

3.2
Hydraulic data acquired
3.2.1
Water level and discharges
Water levels and rating curves of Chindwin are measured by DMH at the
gauging stations. Water levels can be converted into a corresponding dis-
charge by the use of rating curves. For this study, discharges at homalin and
Kalewa were used for upstream initial condition and calbration respectively
and water levels at Monywa.
Reliable and comprehensive dataset of water levels and discharges is cru-
cial for any model, which needs calibration and validation to be accurate.
Depending on a trustworthy hydrualic dataset, a reliable water quality model
can be built.
Water levels trend give the ﬁrst insight in the behaviour of the ﬂow of the
river. As regard to Sobek model, water levels are relevant since they allow
the calculations of discharges through the use of rating curves and they are
necessary to calibrate the model. The water level at each station is read at
6h30, 12h30 and 18h30 during a day from staﬀgauges and then averaged
to one daily value. It is suggested to review the reference levels of each
27
==PAGE:28==
gauging station. As water level measurements by DMH have been refer-
enced to local gauging levels, the elevation of the zero point with respect to
MSL of each gauging level is often uncertain, aﬀecting the calibration of the
model.Moreover, the distance point between gauging stations is quite long.
As stated in the study conducted by Alwin Commandeur, for calibration of
models and reliable results, the distance between gauging stations should
not exceed certain values, i.e. 50 Km to start with.
Discharges resulting from rating curves previously calculated, are obtained
by Alwin Commandeur(2015), who built a 1-D morphological model of
Ayeyarwaddy in 2015.

A boundary condition can be applied at the locations where the model

3.6
Overview of main assumptions
Many assumptions were made during this study due to lack of data and
information.
Firstly, the loads of pollutants are assumed from literature found on internet.
The actual load values were not measured before this study was conducted.
Moreover, loads are modeled as point source coming from the towns while in
reality these are diﬀuse sources even if concentrated just in the urban area.
No WWTP are present therefore loads are not coming from a single pipe
but diﬀerent channels or directly dumped in the river. However. consider-
ing the small size of most of the towns, a point source load may give good
estimations.
Lateral discharges added in the model are not the measured discharges,
but the results of RR model previously built by Deltares. An NSE of 0.66
for the RR model, as reported by Deltares employees, is considered an ac-
ceptable value to build a water quality model.
Cross sections were measured during diﬀerent years. However, in the model,
they are inserted without taking into account diﬀerent years of measure-
ments. Considering that the river is highly dynamic, this may aﬀect the
hydraulic model in regards to the bed elevation and therefore water levels.
Moreover, reference level of cross section maps was missing. It is assumed
that the water surface elevation on Google Earth is the reference from which
water depth and therefore cross section shape were derivated.
42
==PAGE:43==
Most of the process coeﬃcients of DelWaq are kept as default settings,
therefore assuming that all the coeﬃcients stand for the current case study.
Reaeration processes, ﬂow velocity processes,temperature are instead set as
appropriate for the case. Initial BOD, DO and NH4 are assumed to be 5
gO2/m3, 7 mg/L and 0.2 gO2/m3 respectively.
Mercury is assumed to be discharged at the constant concentration of 0.025
mg/L from U-Yu river in the model.

4.1
Field work results
The tables below show the results of the ﬁeld work occurred in May 2016.
Overall, the results of the tested parameters reveal that concentrations at
both stations are much lower than international standard levels, in some
cases even below detection levels. However some parameters need close at-
tention. Nickel is found lower WHO standard limits in water solution, while
in sediments the ratio mg/Kg is high both in Homalin and Monywa station
for bed and shore sediments. As regards to As, the concentration in water
phase are much higher than the ones found by SEI, reaching 0.52 µg/L both
in Homalin and Monywa. Concentrations are all below the WHO thresh-
old. As is present in much higher concentrations in sediments than in water
phase, recording higher concentrations in Homalin than in Monywa. This
may be due to the self-cleansing mechanism of water, which generally occur
not only for As but also for a large number of metals. As usually forms
insoluble precipitates with cations (mainly Fe) that are dissolved and sus-
pended in natural waters. Therefore, the particles settle on the sediments
causing an increasing As content of the sediments.Generally, this mechanism
is common at pH ranging from 5.5 to 6.92 (Serfor-Armah,Y. et al., 2006).
The high levels of Fe found in SEI samples may support this hypothesis.
Due to lack of the right ﬁlter during samples collection in May 2016, Fe was
not analysed by DTU laboratory. The presence of arsenic in water phase
and sediments can be linked to intense mining activities at the area near the
stations. Indeed, arsenic release may be due to geogenic reasons.
Although Al does not have a WHO(2011) standard limit, its concentra-
tion at both stations is high compared to the estimated tolerable weekly
intake which is 0.9 mg/L (WHO, 2011). It can be seen that Al levels in
water phase increase from Homalin to Monywa suggesting that a possible
source discharging Al is located between the two locations. High levels are
also found attached to sediments.
Mercury is a parameter considered of high concern in this study due to
gold mining activities. While in SEI data of September 2015 high values
were found, in DTU/TU Delft dataset mercury was below detection level
in water phase as for DWIR dataset of 2016. Instead, traces were found
in bed and shore sediments. It is not clear the reason of the discrepancy
between the values obtained by SEI and DTU/TU Delft. Nevertheless, it

can be hypothesized that during dry season, in which the discharge of trib-
utaries is supposed to be low, load of mercury from tributaries to Chindwin
or ﬂushing from mining is low as well. This might explain why in September
2015, wet season, the concentrations were higher than May 2016 or March
2015 (DWIR campaign period). In addition, Hg is a heavy metal that bio-
accumulates in organisms. Elevated levels may be present in ﬁsh.
Additionally, a high level of Zinc is present in water phase at Monywa. Con-
centrations are found also in DWIR samples.

ibration and validation of water quality model is not performed. This is
a relevant limitation of the analysis, since it does not allow to verify the
reliability of the model.
Fate of pollutants in groundwater was not investigated.
High concern contaminants,i.e.cyanide or arsenic, were not modelled.
Economic analysis in order to suggest best monitoring techniques is not
included.
Industrial waste present or expected was not modelled.
Processes involved in mercury model transport into the river do not include
bioaccumulation or sedimentation.

Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to set up a water quality model of the
Chindwin river. In order achieve this objective,water quality data collection
was performed by executing a ﬁeld work.In addition,a desk study was car-
ried out to gather and evaluate existing data and an hydraulic model to be
coupled with the water quality model was set up. The domain of the model
was set from Homalin in the north to Monywa 480 Km down south.
Field work at the two stations of Monywa and Homalin was executed to ex-
plore the case study site and to measure concentrations of the main expected
contaminants. Mercury, which was found at high levels in SEI samples in
September 2015, is below detection level in DTU/TU Delft samples. Dis-
crepancy in concentrations may be due to low ﬂow from U-Yu river when
ﬁeld work was conducted. High levels of Al were found at both stations.
However, at Monywa levels of aluminium (Al) were 4 times higher than at
Homalin suggesting the presence of a source discharging Al between the two
locations.
Arsenic (As) is detected at low levels in water phase both in
Monywa and Homalin. Instead, higher concentrations were found in shore
and bed sediments.
Flow1D module in Sobek 3 software was set up by inserting observed dis-
charges at Homalin and measured water levels at Monywa from 1979 to
1999. Lateral discharges were obtained from a RR model built by Deltares.
Calbration of discharges at Kalewa for the period from 1979 to 1994 was
performed. Flood plain were increased by 2 meters, lateral discharges were
decreased and Chezy roughness was set to 47[m1/2 s−1]. NSE, RSR and
PBIAS determined the hydraulic model performance.
Very good ﬁt was
found by calculating NSE and RSR, while good ﬁt was found by calculating
PBIAS. Validation was deﬁned in the period from 1994 to 1999. Very good
and good ﬁt was found for NSE, RSR and PBIAS respectively. A good cal-
ibration of the hydraulic model is relevant since it aﬀects the ﬂow velocity
which is the driving force of advection-diﬀusion equations of DelWaq module.
BOD, NH4, DO and Hg are the parameters modelled in DelWaq. Over-
all, it can be aﬃrmed that the model can give information on the trend
of DO along the river during the year of reference, 1998.
As expected,
higher loads of BOD and NH4 cause much higher oxygen consumption than
lower loads input. High oxygen levels are reached during wet season, most
likely due to high reaeration processes related to high ﬂow conditions. This
suggests that measurements of DO should be executed during dry season
period. Optimization process on locations can be conducted only for the
ﬁrst two stations in case of average and maximum loads of BOD and NH4.
In case of minimum loads, C005 station does not record levels of oxygen
61
==PAGE:62==
below standard levels, therefore it is not suggested to monitor oxygen de-
pletion in that location. It is noticed that the ﬁrst two stations C001, C002
are too near pollution source (Homalin) to report an immediate oxygen con-
sumption. Therefore, they should be removed in case of average load and
one station should be placed at 75 Km downstream C002 to see ﬁrst oxygen
depletions eﬀects in case of maximum load. As regards to mercury model
transport, C002 station placed at the conﬂuence of chindwin with U-Yu will
detect high concentrations, therefore the location is accepted to measure Hg
levels. Stations more downstream than 70 Km from C002, will measure Hg
concentration lower than detection level.
Care should be posed on water quality condition changes due to future
land use modiﬁcations. Monitoring network system on water quality should
be ﬂexible for spatial and temporal changes in order to cope with a new
scenario of pollution threats.

Future Outlook
• The governance structure of water quality and quantity management
should be reviewd and adjusted to avoid the overlapping among dif-
ferent agencies. Increased responsabilities should be given at regional
level or to one new department only focused in managing water quality
studies.
• Greater collaboration, including data sharing and clarifying respons-
abilities of each agency involved are required to avoid data gaps or
costly overlapping.
• Integrated water quality management strategy and water quality mon-
itoring guidelines should be developed. Ideally, this could be developed
based on participatory decision making and include representatives of
all concerned stakeholders.
• Use of test kits as an early warning signal in case unusual concentra-
tions are found, in order to carry out further laboratory testing.
• More eﬀorts should be posed in water quality studies involving uni-
versity members and students from local universities. Water quality
issues are still considered ”low importance” issues.
YTU is slowly
moving into this direction, trying to involve more PhD students in
water quality studies.
• Plans to strengthen relevant capacities of local communities in water
quality monitoring activities is highly recommended. This allows to
monitor areas not easy to reach and raise public awareness.
• Monitoring for cyanide should be included in water quality testing.Care
should be given to the preservation of the samples due to high volatility
of the parameter.
• Acquire bed level data frequently (e.g. on annual basis) to create a
more reliable hydraulic model as regards to its calibration and valida-
tion.
• Calibrate the gauging stations annually to update rating curves.
• Investigate the bioaccumulation of Hg in the organisms and its depo-
sition into sediments.
• Investigations should be done in relation to the high levels of Alu-
minium in water phase.

Campaign Strategy 
 
Water quality sampling to analyze possible presence of Chlorinated solvents (CAH), 
BTEX+N and heavy metals, with particular interest in mercury, can be conducted in 
two location. C001 (Homalin) and C012 (Monywa) stations were chosen. General 
criteria to choose the two locations are the following: 
 
• Upstream and downstream of disturbances or anthropogenic inputs 
• Upstream and downstream of confluence between tributary and mainstream 
• Accessibility to sampling stations 
• Existing sampling points of other departments 
 
More specifically: 
 
• The sampling campaign is executed with a PhD student from TU Delft, who 
works for DWIR. Therefore, for logistic reasons, the locations chosen are 
among the sampling stations of DWIR.  
• Moreover, the stations are preferable coinciding with stations of SEI. Since 
coordinates of ID stations and results are not provided, ID campaign is not 
considered in the decision making of the locations.   
• Stations which revealed water quality contamination in previous campaigns 
were taken into consideration 
• In the upstream reach of U-Yu river gold mining activities are placed. This 
involves the use of cyanide and mercury, which inevitably are discharged in 
the main tributary of Chindwin. Fig.8 shows the point of confluence between 
the Chindwin and U-Yu rivers, in which is evident the difference of water 
color due to mining activities in upstream part of U-Yu. According to 
Gardiner N.J., 2014 , the biggest concentration of gold reserves is present in 
Sagaing and Kachin divisions (see Fig.9). Among the reported gold mines are 
Kyaukpahto situated in Kawlin township (Sagaing division) in the nord of 
Monywa and Daungyu mine near Monywa (see Fig.9). In the report by Images 
Asia & Pau Kachin Development Society, “Gold mining in Kachin State, at 
what price?”, it is stated that the Ministry of Mines is desirous of expanding 
the gold extractions giving more and more permissions to foreign investors. 
Moreover, it is reported that environmental protection related to mining is 
covered by Myanmar Mining law, considered weak, “having poor definitions 
and failing to cover obvious and critical aspects. This is most apparent in the 
lack of up-to-date laws regulating pollution releases, such the use of mercury”. 
This justifies the concern and the requirement of having more data on the 
concentration of heavy metals in the Chindwin. 
• Some oil tanks are present along the coast of the river, in order to provide fuel 
to the several boats transporting goods or people from south to north and vice 
versa (see Fig.10). Leakages may occur, therefore BTEX+N may be found in 
the samples.
