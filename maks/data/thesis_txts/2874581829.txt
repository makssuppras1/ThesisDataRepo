Abstract 
Icing is detrimental for wind energy production, and it poses structural and safety risks in 
wind farms. Since wind resources are often abundant at high latitudes and altitudes, icing 
conditions often coincide with desirable areas. This project presents a spatiotemporal analysis 
of wind turbine icing in parts of the Alps and Scandinavia based on long-term time series (30 
years) of the ‘iceBlade’ model results from the NEWA (New European Wind Atlas). The icing 
conditions are defined as ice accretion rates and ice load thresholds, initially set at 10 g/h and 
0.1 kg/m respectively, in accordance with previous studies. For the thresholds above, the 
results indicate a lower year-to-year relative variability in the locations experiencing longer 
icing per year. This study identified the terrain height as the primary factor influencing  icing 
in the Alps, while in the analyzed portion of the Scandinavian Peninsula maritime effects (via 
temperature and humidity) also play a role in addition to the terrain height. IceBlade returned 
a higher average number of hours per year under icing conditions according to the initially set 
ice rate and ice load thresholds, compared to the low temperature climate criteria specified 
in the IEC 61400-1 standard (Tair < −10 °C and RH > 95%). Setting higher ice load and ice 
accretion rate thresholds in the calculations reduced the difference between yearly values 
from the iceBlade and the IEC 61400-1 in the case of the Alps. The correlation between IEC 
61400-1 and iceBlade was found to be higher for the Alps than for the Scandinavian region. 
This is attributed to the different maritime and continental regions within the area covered 
by the Scandinavian dataset, making it more complex than the Alpine region analyzed. The 
year-to-year temporal correlation between IEC 61400-1 and iceBlade was analyzed for 
relevant locations, but the correlation was found to be generally weak and statistically 
insignificant, both for the Alps and Scandinavia. Lastly, the study shows that the icing 
conditions calculated with iceBlade and expressed in the form of IEA classes match reasonably 
well to the existing wind ice atlas from VTT.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Wind turbine icing is a risk for wind farms in cold climate areas, such as in high mountain 
ranges and high latitude regions. Since wind resources are often abundant at high latitudes 
and altitudes, the icing conditions often coincide with some of the most desirable areas in 
terms of wind production. Icing has been reported to cause a yearly production loss above 20 
% in some cases [1]. In addition to production losses, the icing conditions can shorten the 
lifetime of the wind turbines, increase the risk of structural failures and are a safety hazard 
(e.g., ice throwing). 
Therefore, the spatiotemporal investigation of the icing occurrence in the most exposed 
regions is key to anticipating the issues stated above, from the wind farm planning stages to 
short term forecasting. 
1.2 Objectives 
This project aims to analyse icing occurrence in wind turbines, by creating and investigating 
maps of icing via iceBlade [2] using long-term input data from the New European Wind Atlas 
(NEWA). The project focuses on the spatiotemporal investigation of the icing in the 
Scandinavian Peninsula and in a region of the Alps, where icing conditions are relatively 
frequent. 
Additionally, the project aims to provide a comparison of the iceBlade model to simpler 
methods, such as the low temperature climate conditions defined in the IEC 61400-1 
standard. Another objective is to evaluate the iceBlade model in relation to other existing 
icing atlas.

2.1 Icing phenomena 
2.1.1 
Introduction 
Atmospheric icing affects surfaces exposed to freezing conditions. Atmospheric icing can 
severely damage all kinds of structures, such as power line cables, ski lifts, towers, and wind 
turbines.  
Icing often forms from supercooled water droplets that impact and freeze on the exposed 
surfaces. The droplet impact and adhesion to the turbine blade surfaces are complex 
processes that depend on a variety of atmospheric factors such as size of the droplet, speed, 
and temperature, but also on the surface geometry, surface finishing, temperature of the 
exposed surface and rotational speed of the wind turbine [3]. 
The atmospheric icing can be divided in in-cloud icing, freezing rain, and wet snow [1] 
according to its origin, although freezing rain and wet snow are often grouped together within 
the category of precipitation icing, according to ISO-12494:2017 [4]. 
2.1.2 
In-cloud icing, freezing rain, and wet snow 
In-cloud icing 
This weather phenomenon occurs due to supercooled cloud droplets, typically of 50 µm 
[3], contained in the clouds. Often the clouds are below the structure’s height, in the form of 
low-level clouds and fog. This phenomenon is relatively common in mountain terrains in 
Europe during winter.  
The supercooled droplets impact and accrete on the exposed surfaces and structures [5], 
[6]. The first phase of solidification for the supercooled droplets involves a nucleation process 
upon impact [3] as shown schematically in Figure 1. Depending on the speed of the dissipation 
of the latent heat of fusion, different ice types will be formed on the surface, such as soft 
rime, hard rime, glaze icing or combinations [3], [7]. Rime ice occurs with immediate freezing, 
usually at below -10 °C [3] and it tends to grow on the exposed surfaces to the wind side of 
the structure [1]. Rime ice has a white colored aspect, because of air bubbles remaining 
trapped due to the fast nature of the process. It can be further classified in hard or soft rime 
depending on the wind conditions [7]. When the ambient temperature is high, close to the 
freezing point, only part of the droplet is solidified instantaneously, and therefore glaze icing 
is formed [1]. 
The ice tends to accumulate more on the leading edge, due to higher droplet collision 
efficiency in that area of the blade [1], although phenomena such as water run backs, often 
seen in the glaze icing conditions, contribute to ice accumulations in other areas [1] [3]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
4 
The density of the resulting ice from in-cloud icing varies between 200 and 900 kg/m3 [7].  
 
 
Figure 1 Schema of the freezing process of supercooled droplets [3] 
 
Freezing rain 
Freezing rain occurs due to the solidification of rain droplets onto exposed surfaces that 
are below 0 °C, forming glaze ice. 
The solidification process is slower, and thus the layer is more homogeneous, more 
transparent, and strongly adhered to the surfaces, with a high density [7]. 
Wet snow 
Wet snow accretion happens between 0 and 3 °C, by the adhesion of partially melted snow 
to the exposed surfaces [1].

2.2 Impact of icing per region 
2.2.1 
Introduction to Cold Climate sites 
According to the IEA report [1], a Cold Climate site (CC) can include a Low Temperature 
Climate (LTC), an Icing Climate (IC), or both (Figure 2). In those sites, the wind turbines will 
undergo temperatures below their operational limits, and/or icing. 
 
Figure 2 Cold Climate, Low Temperature Climate, and Icing Climate, from IEA report [1] 
Low Temperature Climate regions (LTC) are those that follow the criteria of minimum 
temperatures below -20 °C more than 9 days per year, and with a long-term average annual 
air temperature below 0 °C.  An Icing Climate (IC) includes areas with instrumental icing more 
than 1% of the year or meteorological icing during more than 0.5% of the year.  
Specifically, the IEA ice classification defines 5 types of IEA ice classes, based on the 
percentage of the year with meteorological icing, the percentage of the year with 
instrumental icing, and the icing loss as a percentage of gross annual production (Table 1). It 
is noted that this is a long-term classification, meaning that individual years may fall outside 
of the class. It is nevertheless noted that a validation study of the IEA ice Classification was 
conducted by Meteotest (Figure 3), wherein 30% of sites fell outside one of the IEA Ice 
Classes[8]

2.2.2 
Icing impact in Europe 
Wind farms in cold climate sites account for 25% of the total of installed wind farms. This 
covers the polar regions, but also some mid to high altitude wind farm at mid latitudes [9], 
[10] (Figure 4). 
 
Figure 4 World icing map from WIceAtlas at 150 m a.g.l. The low temperature regions are 
shown in blue [10] 
In Figure 5, an icing map for Europe is shown. In Europe, the icing impact varies greatly at 
local, small-scale level. Even if a general distinction can be made between Northern Europe, 
central Europe and Southern Europe, the local variabilities within these regions are of great 
importance [11].  
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
 
Figure 5 Icing map of Europe according to the VTT WIce Atlas. The colors show the IEC 
classes [12] 
 
One of the large-scale influencing factors of the climate in Europe is the Gulf stream 
current. The Gulf stream current is a current of warm water that reaches the European 
Atlantic coasts, causing a warmer climate at the Atlantic coast in Europe than in other areas 
in the world situated at the same latitude.  
In Finland, Sweden and Norway, icing may happen at every wind farm site, mainly in 
winter. However, there are strong local variations, namely between the coastal sites, where 
the temperature may be around 0 °C in the winter months, and inland, where the average 
temperature will drop much further. For example, the east coast of Norway will get a 
relatively milder climate in winter when compared to other regions in the world at similar 
latitude. In contrast, in northern Finland, above 350 m, icing is present over 100 days a year 
[11] 
In central Europe, such as in Germany, atmospheric icing may also occur at all sites, 
although the frequency will increase with the terrain heigh (Figure 6). In-cloud icing and 
freezing rain occurs mostly between 500 m and 1500 m [13]. Wind turbine downtimes are 
reported to be generally less than a week at low elevations, and up to two months above 800 
m [11].  
In the Alps, icing is common in many areas due to the terrain elevation. Wind farms in 
Switzerland, which is occupied in great part by the Alps, report icing and low temperature at 
altitudes from 1300 m. The probability of icing in the Alps increases with the altitude (Figure 
7), but it is also influenced by the orientation North-South [11]. Therefore, local exposure is 
also an important factor in addition to the height.

2.3 Impact of icing on the wind energy production 
In the cold regions, icing is one of the contributors to a wind energy production decrease 
during winter. It is estimated that the power losses can number in average above 20%, 
according to several sources [14], [15], see e.g., Table 2. 
Table 2 Power losses due to different icing conditions for a 1.5 MW wind turbine [14] 
 
 
Figure 8 Lift and drag coefficients of the turbine blades in function of rime and glaze ice 
accretion. 
One of the main factors causing lower production is the sub-optimal aerodynamic shape 
of the blades due to ice accretion. The ice tends to accumulate causing cord prolongation. 
This shape modification is therefore reducing the aerodynamic of the blade, and due to that 
shape change, the lift and drag coefficient change. In general, lift decreases and drag 
decreases, being the change dependent on the position and shape of the ice [15], [1]. The 
icing impact on the drag and lift is very dependent on the icing conditions, which cause 
different roughness’s of the blade profile. Glaze ice results in general in more complex ice 
accretion shapes, causing higher aerodynamic disturbances, as shown in Figure 8. 
Furthermore, the icing may induce malfunction of the measuring devices mounted on the 
nacelle, such as a stuck anemometer or wind vane, which will contribute to further power 
losses in consequence due to inaccurate information transmitted to the controller.

2.4 Structural integrity and lifetime reduction due 
to icing conditions 
In addition to the production losses caused by icing, icing has a detrimental effect in the 
lifetime of the wind turbine components. Icing and low temperature operating conditions 
shorten the lifetime of a wind turbine due to several reasons: the low temperature operation 
associated to icing conditions affects the wind turbine materials and the lubrication of turbine 
components such as the bearings, while icing itself influences the peak and fatigue loads of 
the rotor and tower. 
The weight of the ice and the ice mass per unit length have been simulated/estimated by 
different sources. Battisti et al [16] simulated the mass of ice, and he calculated an extra 10-
15% of the weight of each blade due to ice (Figure 9). In the study of Lehtomäki et al. [17], 
the ice on the blades of a 2 MW turbine with a 92 m rotor diameter in a wind farm in Canada 
was estimated to be almost 200 kg per blade under certain conditions. Hochart et al. [15] 
reported a 11% of ice over with respect to the initial mass blade (709 kg on an initial blade of 
6000 kg). Therefore, this significant extra weight caused by icing influences the loads of the 
turbine. 
 
Figure 9 Simulated mass of ice on the rotors [16] 
In terms of peak and fatigue loads, an (expected) asymmetric ice distribution will cause 
mass imbalances in the rotor. Most of the accumulated ice may likely fall from the blades at 
uneven times, leading to more load imbalance. Furthermore, and as explained in the previous 
section, the ice on the blades also causes a reduction of their expected aerodynamic behavior, 
which in turn changes the operating conditions of the turbine and may generate undesired 
turbine loads. Moderate fatigue loading leads to a reduction in the number of fatigue cycles 
in the wind turbine tower [17], [18]. 
==PAGE:23==
 
11 
The low temperatures during icing can be detrimental for the materials of the wind turbine 
such as the bearings or steel junctions. For example, the fatigue life of blade studs shortens 
significatively under low temperature operating conditions [11]. Additionally, the increase of 
viscosity of lubricants due to the low temperatures may harm the bearings and gearboxes, 
shortening their lifetime [11], [19]. The instrumentation is likely to be impacted by icing 
and/or low temperatures. For example, the cup anemometers or the wind vane may be stuck 
or malfunction.  
Additionally, several malfunctions may arise related to the electronic components such as 
those inside the nacelle, because of potential condensation and snow infiltration [20]. 
  
2.5 Ice prevention and de-icing mechanisms 
The accumulation of ice on the turbine blades can be considered from a preventive 
perspective, aiming at avoiding the ice accretion accumulation, and from a de-icing 
perspective, aiming at removing the ice from the blade. Both strategies can also be considered 
together. Another possible way to classify the icing mechanism is from an energy 
consumption point of view: active systems will consume energy, in opposition to the passive 
mechanisms [20]. The main systems are listed below: 
- 
Thermal systems: These are the ones that are most often investigated [21], and they 
can be split in two main groups: those based on warm air, and those based on 
electrical heating of resistive elements. Thermal systems can be adapted for 
preventive strategies (higher energy consumption) or de-icing strategies [20]. The 
energy consumption depends strongly on their configuration and use (either as a 
continuous anti-icing system or as a de-icing system), but also on the external 
conditions. Electro heating systems are based on resistive wires, fibers, or foils, 
generally along the leading edge of the turbine blade, below or on the blade surface. 
These elements are resistively heated by an electrical current. In the case of de-icing 
strategies involving electro heating systems, the heating action is assisted by the 
centrifugal forces of the turbine [22]. These systems can present some risks such as 
higher probability of lightning events or load imbalances because of partial 
malfunctions [21], [23]. Hot air systems are based on introducing hot air inside the 
turbine blades typically at the root and distribute it inside the shell, to heat it [24] [20]. 
It is mostly used for de-icing strategies [21]. 
- 
Microwaves and ultrasound systems: Microwaves systems are based on the heating 
of the blade via microwaves, both as de-icing or anti-icing, although are relatively new 
and experimental[21]. Ultrasonic systems are also rather experimental, based on 
==PAGE:24==
 
12 
transducers generating acoustic waves for de-icing. One of the main advantages is a 
lower energy consumption up to 10 times lower than thermal systems [25]. 
Combinations including microwave, ultrasonic and electro thermal systems are also 
potentially possible, as shown in [26].  
- 
Coatings and chemicals: Ice phobic or hydrophobic coatings, black paints to increase 
heat absorption, or chemicals on the surface of the blades can be used, although they 
are not often successful alone [20], [21], [23] 
- 
Flexible pneumatic boots: this system is based on tubes that can be inflated when 
there is ice on the blade, to expel the ice [20]. 
- 
De-rating techniques: In addition to simple shutdown approaches, tip-speed ratio 
approaches based on reducing the rotational speed to decrease ice accretion during 
icing have been proposed, showing further benefits in combination with thermal 
systems [23]

2.6 Icing detection and prognosis: IceBlade model  
2.6.1 
Icing detection and measurement 
Detection and measurement of the ice accumulated on the turbine blade is useful to 
trigger corrective actions and to collect data that can be used for future prediction and 
assessments. 
One approach is to estimate the icing based on the output of the wind energy production 
of the turbines, which is reduced during an icing event, as shown in Figure 11 [6]. The data is 
often retrieved from the SCADA data of the turbine. However, this approach has some 
disadvantages such as interference of other factors (such as wake losses) or calculations made 
differently. To improve these drawbacks, the IEA Wind TCP has developed a standardized 
statistical method for the assessment of said losses from the SCADA input [27], [28]. 
 
Figure 11 Effect of the icing conditions in the power curves of the wind turbines [6] 
Another approach is from the analysis of the instrumentation output, such as nacelle-
based sensors. Double anemometry methods are well-known methods based on the 
comparison between a heated anemometer and a non-heated anemometer. The principle is 
that the ice will affect the recording of the wind speed at the non-heated anemometer or 
==PAGE:26==
 
14 
even stop it completely. This method is advantageous since it is easy to install, although it has 
some limitation related to the number of icing, and inaccuracies in the determination of the 
start and end of the icing period [29]. Furthermore, instrumental icing is not always the same 
as rotor icing since rotor icing may start earlier [1].  
Optical methods are formed by a wide array of methods based on video, light scattering, 
optical reflectometry, infrared spectroscopy, fiber optic and absorption amongst others [30]. 
Image detection methods can be combined often with automated image processing and 
analysis [31].  
Other approaches are based on detecting the vibration changes of sensors, such as tower 
sensors that can detect for example the oscillation of the nacelle due to the 
aerodynamic/mass imbalances [32], or damping of ultrasounds due to the ice [29], [30]. 
2.6.2 
IceBlade model 
The purpose of the icing and forecast models ranges from assisting in the planning stages of 
wind farm projects, e.g., for assessing the expected icing conditions at certain locations and 
helping the decision makers on the installation of anti-icing mechanisms, to provide short-
term forecast of the icing conditions of a particular wind farm.  
The iceBlade model is an open-source model developed at DTU aiming to estimate the 
icing of a wind turbine using meteorological models and wind turbine characteristics as input. 
The present work uses the iceBlade model for the subsequent spatiotemporal icing 
investigations. 
A summary of the iceBlade model from the publication of Davis et al. [6] is presented 
below. The reader is directed to said publication for a more detailed explanation of the model 
equations. 
Overall, the iceBlade model is based on modeling a rotating cylinder to represent a 
simplified section of the wind turbine blade. The model computes icing occurring during in-
cloud icing conditions [6]. 
For the ice accretion calculations, iceBlade is based partially on the Makkonen model. A 
detailed explanation of the Makkonen model can be found in [33]. The Makkonen model 
calculates the increase of mass from the mass flux of cloud particles, according to the 
following formula (1):

wherein α1 is the collision efficiency, α2 represents the sticking efficiency, α3 represents 
the accretion efficiency, ω the mass concentration of particles, ν the speed of the flow with 
respect to the object, and A is the cross-sectional area.  
In practice, in the iceBlade model α2 corresponds to a value of 1, since iceBlade considers 
supercooled droplets, and α3 will be between 0 and 1, depending on if the conditions are glaze 
icing (and only some particles are frozen) or rime icing (all particles are frozen).  
Further assumptions/simplifications over the Makkonen model are that the ice grows 
causing chord prolongation and not increasing the leading edge, hence the surface against 
the mass of particles is kept constant [6]. In other words, the cylinder diameter is therefore 
kept static, with a length of 1 m and a radius of 0.144 m, see [6] [34]. The specific turbine 
properties and the rotational speed (rpm) from the wind speed are all inputs to the iceBlade 
model. 
The ice ablation is computed with respect to sublimation and shedding phenomena. 
Shedding is based on a loss of adhesion of the ice, that causes it to fall, and the model 
considers it in a simplified way, since otherwise would require specific data on the blade 
surface and ice interaction. The shedding phenomena is thus accounted by removing the ice 
when the temperature is above a certain threshold for a given period [6], [2]. Specifically, in 
the last iceBlade version, total shedding is set to eliminate all the ice on the blade when the 
temperature is above freezing for a duration of 30 minutes or more [2]. Melting phenomena 
is not taken into consideration, in view of the near zero temperature threshold assumed for 
the shedding phenomena [6]. With respect to sublimation, the iceBlade uses a modified 
equation from Srivastava et al [35], to account for a cylindric shape instead of a sphere. 
Further modifications have been made over the initial model, such inclusion of an additional 
erosion component in the ablation, coming from the wind stress [34].  
The ice load is calculated from the combination of the iceBlade ice accretion and the ice 
ablation outputs. To calculate the ice load, ice is added when there is new accretion, and it is 
removed when there is ablation. In some cases, the ice load can be set to a maximum (e.g., 5 
kg), under the assumption that extreme ice accumulations just fall off from the blade. 
The iceBlade model is designed to work as an extension for the WRF (Weather, Research 
and Forecasting model) and MPAS-A (atmosphere component of the Model for Prediction 
Across Scales), and it has been validated with respect to several wind farm locations [2].  
In this work, the iceBlade model is used in the simulation for NEWA (New European Wind 
Atlas, which is based on WRF (Weather Resource Forecast) model. For visualization, a 
schematic chart with the inputs and outputs for a standard iceBlade model, using WRF based 
data, is shown in Figure 12.

2.6.3 
Other icing models 
It must be noted that most of the existing icing models consider only in-cloud icing in the icing 
calculations [7]. 
The development of models based on machine learning has been increasing in the last years. 
Molinder et al. [36] [37] have developed machine learning methods, based on random forest 
regression algorithms, which are decision tree-based models. The models of Molinder et al. 
[36] [37] are intended to forecast the wind production on the next day(s), based on past NWF 
(Numerical Weather Forecast) and observational data. As it is the case with the iceBlade 
model described before [6] [34], these models make use of the Makonnen equation for the 
icing modelling.  
Some models take a probabilistic approach which, according to the authors, has the 
advantage of providing uncertainty information that can be useful for the energy wind 
producer [37] [38].

3.1 General data workflow  
The data was initially retrieved from NEWA (New European Wind Atlas). NEWA is a wind 
atlas, i.e., a database of wind speed at different heights. NEWA is based on mesoscale model 
simulations with WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting). The spatial resolution is 3 x 3 km, 
and it further includes a microscale calculation [39]. To obtain the best configuration for 
NEWA from WRF model, several sensitivity studies were performed. The reader is referred to 
[39] for details of the sensitivity experiments leading to NEWA. The NEWA results were also 
evaluated with wind measurements from different European regions, as shown in [40].   
The data corresponds to long-term time series of the ‘iceBlade’ model results in NEWA 
based on WRF. Ice accretion and ice ablation are thus obtained for each time step. Post 
processing is needed for the calculation of the ice load for each time step. As explained in 
section 2.6.2, it is noted that as with most icing models, iceBlade accounts for the accretion 
and ablation from in-cloud icing only. The present project focusses on further post processing 
and interpretation of the output data, starting from the iceBlade model outputs from NEWA. 
The dataset analyzed was stored in the form of a .zarr file. XARRAY was used to read the 
.zarr files. XARRAY is a Python package with labelled multi-dimensional arrays, which makes 
it convenient to analyze meteorological data. A visual example of a general structure of 
XARRAY is shown in Figure 15.

4.1.1 
Initial observations 
The ice accretion rate is based on the Makkonen model, as explained in Section 2.6.2, while 
the ice load involves a further calculation based on the ice accretion input and the ice ablation 
input from the sublimation, wind erosion and ice shedding [6], [34].   
Therefore, analyzing and comparing both outputs is advantageous, since the ice accretion 
rate requires one step less of computing, and mainly because if external heating systems are 
present in the turbine blade (Section 2.5), it may be advantageous to know the ice accretion 
rate as well, instead of just the final ice load output,  as it provides useful information on how 
quickly the ice might accumulate in the blade, and thus help in selecting the suited de-icing 
or anti-icing systems.  
To obtain a measure of the impact of the icing in both the Alps and the Scandinavian area, 
the average number of hours per year based on a certain threshold were calculated for every 
year of the 30-year dataset (counting each year from July to the end of June, as explained 
before). From this, further statistical information was extracted, such as the standard 
deviation, and the coefficient of variation. 
 Initially, the ice rate threshold was set at 10 g/h, since an icing episode is considered to 
happen starting from that threshold, in accordance with previous literature studies [43] [44].  
With respect to the ice load, an icing event has been considered when the ice load is > 0.1 
kg/m, in accordance with the threshold established in [6]. It must be noted also that the ice 
load calculation from iceBlade has been limited to maximum values of 5 kg/m, under the 
assumption that extreme ice accumulations just fall off from the blade.

Since the ice load is calculated from the ice accretion rate and the ice ablation, the 
differences may be attributed to changes in the ice ablation relative to the ice accretion rate. 
With warmer conditions, such as at lower terrain elevations, the ice load is expected to be on 
the blades proportionally less hours/year. As mentioned in 2.6.2, the iceBlade model removes 
all the ice on the blade when the temperature is above freezing longer than 30 minutes [2]. 
This would be more often the case in warmer areas. This explanation matches well with the 
expected areas having higher temperatures, i.e., lower terrain heights (as in the Alps) and the 
west Scandinavian region (as explained in section 3.2.1). 
 Therefore, for the studied thresholds (ice rate >10 g/h and ice load >0.1 kg/m), the 
correlation of the icing results from ice blade and ice load is not completely linear. Even if the 
icing conditions derived from ice accretion and ice load seem to correlate well, the climate 
conditions appear to cause the described differences between ice load and ice accretion rate. 
4.2 Comparison between iceBlade and IEC61400-1 
The ice accretion rate output from iceBlade is compared to the criteria of a low 
temperature climate, as defined in the IEC61400-1 (ed.4) standard, i.e., those periods with 
the following condition: Tair <−10 °C and RH > 95 %. As discussed in section 2.1.2, this 
temperature range is typical of rime ice conditions [3]. 
Although the initial threshold for the iceBlade ice accretion rate was initially kept at 10 g/h, 
higher thresholds (40 g/h and 80 g/h) were further included in the calculations for comparison 
with the IEC61400-1 standard. The goal is to further analyze the influence of the icing 
threshold in the correlation between iceBlade and IEC 61400-1 standard. The same was done 
for the ice load thresholds selected (0.1 kg/m, 0.4 kg/m, and 0.8 kg/m).  
4.2.1 
iceBlade vs IEC 61400 - Scandinavian dataset 
As suggested in section 3.2.1, there are different climates in the area covered by the 
Scandinavian dataset. The west portion of the Peninsula has a warmer climate than the east 
for similar elevations, due to the Atlantic influence. A colder climate is thus observed in the 
Scandinavian mountain range and in the continental east. In addition to the temperature, the 
relative humidity is a factor in the IEC output. As explained in section 3.2.1, higher relative 
humidity appears to correlate with higher altitudes.

The phenomena above may be explained in view of the climate and the variables affecting 
each model. As shown in Section 3.2.1, the precipitation is higher in the west, and the 
temperatures are warmer. As explained in Section 2.6.2, iceBlade is based on the Makkonen 
equation. This means that the model can still output ice accretion even if the temperature is 
warmer than -10 °C [6], [34], albeit at a lower accretion coefficient α3. Additionally, iceBlade 
also considers the wind speed, density, pressure, mixing ratio, cloud water mixing ratio, 
rainwater mixing ratio and cloud ice mixing ratio. Therefore, a link may also be made with 
respect to the precipitation and relative humidity in the area. An increased mass 
concentration of water droplets enhances the accretion rate according to the Makkonen 
formula. In contrast, IEC merely establishes fixed thresholds at -10 °C and 95 % of relative 
humidity. As shown in Section 3.2.1, since the climate in the east of the Scandes is colder than 
at similar heights in the west, it is likely that the IEC conditions could be present longer.

As explained for the Scandinavian dataset, some of the differences between iceBlade and 
IEC may be explained by the fact that IEC provided a specific defined limit conditions at -10° 
C and 95 % RH, while the ice accretion rate output in the iceBlade model is not strictly limited 
to such meteorological boundaries, and it is more complex. Factors such as the accretion 
coefficient α3, the mass concentration of particles and the wind speed will contribute to the 
ice accretion rates outputs of the Makkonen equation. Therefore, the icing output will change 
in function of the temperature (e.g.  the accretion coefficient α3 will be closer to 1 at rime ice 
conditions, typical of low temperatures, and closer to 0 for higher temperatures), the wind 
speed or mass concentration of particles, in contrast to the simpler conditions based on  RH 
and T from IEC.  
For example, temperatures between -10 °C and 0 °C are expected to be relatively frequent 
between 500 m and 2200 m, hence possibly triggering icing events not accounted for when 
using the IEC 61400 low temperature climate threshold. Above around 2000 m, the iceBlade 
model output and the IEC seem to converge better since icing conditions at the defined 
meteorological thresholds of -10 °C and 95% RH will be more frequent. 
It might be possible that some effect with respect to the north and south face of the 
mountains is present. This effect has also been reported experimentally by T. Laakso et al. 
[11], specifically in the Swiss Alps. In the south face, the average temperature is likely higher, 
and hence there are less hours a year fulfilling the condition of low temperature climate 
defined by IEC 61400-1. However, the map resolution makes it challenging to determine the 
extent of this effect.

4.2.3 
Temporal correlation between iceBlade and IEC

Starting from the previous spatial analysis, several points of interest were selected for a 
further temporal analysis. The goal was to study the temporal correlation between iceBlade 
output and IEC low temperature climate criteria. 
The correlation presented here is a yearly temporal correlation, i.e., the correlation 
between iceBlade and IEC is based on one point per year (yearly average), during the 30-year 
period. As discussed before, in addition to the influence of the heights of the Scandes in the 
icing outcome, the icing conditions in the Scandinavian region appear to be influenced by the 
ocean along the west coast with respect to the icing conditions inland. The Alps dataset seems 
to cover a region where the terrain height is the main driving factor. These factors were also 
considered in the temporal analysis. 
Therefore, based on the previous spatial investigations 4 representative single locations in 
each of the maps were selected (see Figure 43 and Figure 44). Afterwards, the average 
number of hours fulfilling iceBlade conditions for every year of the selected period was 
calculated. The calculations were done for the ice accretion rate, ice load and IEC 61400 low 
temperature climate criteria (RH > 95% and T < -10 °C ) output. Lastly, the Pearson correlation 
coefficients R between the iceBlade temporal series (ice rate and ice load) and the IEC 
temporal series is calculated. This way it can be assessed how they correlate over the years.

4.3 Comparison between iceBlade and VTT icing 
model. 
An initial comparison overview between the iceBlade model and the icing model from the 
VTT WIceAtlas [12] is shown in Figure 49 and Figure 50. To facilitate the comparison, the same 
legend and colors are used in the ice load results for iceBlade. 
The WIceAtlas was chosen for the comparison since it is one of the few icing maps easily 
accessible online. It displays colored regions according to the IEA class. Each IEA class is 
assigned in function of the % of time where icing conditions are calculated, following the class 
definitions from IEA Task Report, as shown previously in Table 1. 
The WIceAtlas map is based on cloud base height measurements (CBH) with vertically 
interpolated MERRA reanalysis data for the temperature [12].  
The icing conditions threshold in the iceBlade ice load calculation used for the comparison 
has been kept as 0.1 kg/m, according to the publication of Davis et al [34]. 
It is noted that for a more accurate and direct comparison, the comparison would be based 
on the same height above ground level, since it is well-known that the icing increases with

iceBlade model output, the icing results are calculated for 100 m a.g.l, while the WIceAtlas

publication from VTT on the matter [45]. 
The WIceAtlas map shows higher icing for the portion of the Scandinavian peninsula 
analyzed here, although the WIceAtlas and iceBlade seem to match relatively well on the icing

5 Conclusions  
In this work, the output from the iceBlade model implemented within WRF has been 
analyzed for larger portions of the Scandinavian Peninsula and the Alps. The dataset extends 
from 1989 to 2019. The 30 years of data was retrieved from the NEWA (New European Wind 
Atlas). To quantify the icing periods, the ice accretion rate and ice load derived from iceBlade 
were used to estimate the average number of hours/year for each location based on initially 
set thresholds (ice rate >10 g/h; ice load >0.1 kg/m). In some cases, the icing thresholds were 
further modified to better understand their influence. 
The spatial investigation of the iceBlade model shows that at higher altitudes the average 
time with icing conditions increases, as expected. Furthermore, the findings of this study 
highlight the significant influence of the Atlantic Ocean on icing conditions in the Scandinavian 
Peninsula, as demonstrated by the warmer climate in the west coast compared to the east. 
The standard deviation of the average number of hours/year based on the initial ice 
accretion and ice load thresholds (10 g/h and 0.1 kg/m, respectively) increases with the 
average. This indicates higher absolute year-to-year variability in the locations experiencing 
longer icing per year. However, the year-to-year relative variability is lower in the locations 
with a higher average number of hours per year subjected to icing, and vice versa. Thus, in 
regions characterized by milder climate conditions, such as lower elevations in the Alps or 
Scandinavia, and part of the Atlantic coast in Scandinavia, the year-to-year relative variability 
tends to be higher. Higher fluctuations in the icing conditions may pose an additional 
challenge when planning and selecting optimal de-icing systems.  
The results based on ice accretion rate and ice load have been compared. Although ice 
load is the ultimate iceBlade model output, using the accretion rate may be advantageous in 
some cases. For instance, it can help in planning the installation of suitable external heating 
systems on the turbine blade. Information on how quickly the ice might accumulate can be 
used to assess the requirements of those de-icing systems. The average number of hours/year 
with icing conditions based on the initial ice accretion rate and ice load thresholds (10 g/h and 
0.1 kg/m, respectively)  differ, but they appear to match well. However, colder locations 
exhibit a higher ice load to ice accretion rate ratio as defined in the equation ( 2 ). This is 
observed in the Alps, where higher ratios match with higher elevations, i.e., with colder 
conditions, and from the ratio in the Scandinavian Peninsula, where not only the altitude but 
also the ocean influences the climate. The reason may be that for milder conditions, the ice 
ablation plays an increasing role when calculating the ice load. Therefore, even if ice accretion 
rate and ice load seem to generally correlate well, the correlation is not fully linear, and the 
climate conditions must be considered. 
==PAGE:64==
 
52 
The iceBlade results were also compared to IEC 61400-1 low temperature climate criteria 
(Tair < −10 °C and RH > 95%). The spatial trend matches well with the iceBlade output. A higher 
correlation (correlation coefficient > 0.8) between yearly means of iceBlade ice load and IEC 
criteria is found in the Alps dataset, while the bulk correlation of the Scandinavian iceBlade 
output and the IEC output is weaker. This could be due to the distinct climates within the 
Scandinavian dataset at similar elevations, as shown by warmer temperatures and higher 
precipitations in the western coast until the Scandinavian mountain range, in contrast to the 
eastern part. Setting higher ice load and ice accretion rate thresholds in the calculations (ice 
accretion rate > 80 g/h and ice load > 0.8 kg/m) made the predictions between iceBlade and 
IEC 61400 more similar in the case of the Alps. This may suggest that at more extreme 
meteorological conditions, the match between iceBlade model and IEC is higher. 
The year-to-year temporal correlation between IEC and iceBlade output over the three 
decades period was also studied for relevant points identified from the previous spatial 
analysis. In addition, the correlation coefficients between the ice load from iceBlade and IEC 
61400 conditions along a constant longitude (Alps, to assess the terrain height) or latitude 
(Scandinavia, to assess terrain height and coast/inland areas) were calculated. This study 
found that the year-to-year correlation between IEC and iceBlade conditions in both the Alps 
and Scandinavia is generally weak, although the correlation appears to slightly improve at 
colder locations (e.g., at higher altitudes). 
Lastly, the iceBlade results were compared to the existing wind ice atlas from VTT. For the 
comparison, the iceBlade ice load output was converted into relevant IEA classes, defined by 
the % of time undergoing icing conditions. Despite the limitations in the comparison (the 
iceBlade results are given for 100 m a.g.l., and the VTT map shows results at 150 m a.g.l), the 
comparison highlights that the icing conditions calculated with iceBlade match reasonably 
well to the existing wind ice atlas from VTT.

6 Further work 
The work presented here is based on two regions of Europe where the wind resources are 
particularly relevant and that are subjected to important icing conditions during parts of the 
year. However, a similar analysis may be relevant to other areas in Europe such as Finland 
and central Europe, and it could be even extended to colder regions with high wind resources 
outside of Europe such as Canada. 
The current analysis covers a single height above ground level (100 m), but investigation 
of the iceBlade output at different heights above ground level may be relevant, in view of the 
trend towards bigger wind turbines. 
Lastly, the current study has focused on ice accretion and ice load. Further analysis of the 
ice ablation output may be done in view of its relation to safety hazards such as ice throwing.
