1.1
Background
This project aims to develop a tooling system that incorporates continuous fibre re-
inforcement in thermosetting polymer composites for extrusion-based additive manu-
facturing. The project is part of a larger research project called ThermoForm at the
Technical University of Denmark’s AM Lab, which seeks to explore the potential for
early-stage maturation of thermosetting polymer-based material extrusion additive
manufacturing. The inclusion of fibre reinforcement is a key aspect of the investi-
gation, as fibres can significantly improve mechanical properties. At the beginning
of this Master’s thesis, the earlier set-up consisted of a two-component thermoset-
ting polyurethane deposition tooling with a static mixing nozzle and a pump-driven
dispensing system. The tool motion is controlled by a six-axis articulated robotic arm.
The development of a new tooling system that can incorporate the deposition of con-
tinuous fibres is of high interest due to its numerous advantages. The most notable
advantage of fibre-reinforced polymer (FRP) composites is their high stiffness and
strength-to-density ratio, which are superior to those of many materials. Compared
to conventional methods, FRP AM-based composite manufacturing offers increased
automation and repeatability, eliminating the need for moulds to produce composite
parts. In contrast, conventional FRP composite manufacturing requires numerous
skilled workers, including obtaining CNC-milled metal moulds, a resin-infusion ap-
proach, and a manual wet-layup process, as well as the inclusion of single-use equip-
ment such as spiral and resin tubes, bagging, sealing, and adhesive tapes, peel-ply,
resin flow mesh, among many other types of equipment. The high scrap rate due
to human errors also results in significant variability of lead times and adds to the
high manufacturing costs. Therefore, most of the FRP composites market is in areas
==PAGE:12==

where strength to weight is critical, such as the wind, maritime, aviation, aerospace,
and automotive industries.
Another prospect of AM-based FRP composites is the geometric freedom offered by
gantry-based or articulated robots, as applied in this investigation.
For example,
the synergy of additive manufacturing, topology optimisation techniques, and ded-
icated fibre path planning can produce highly anisotropic fibre-orientated printed
components with complex geometric shapes and extremely high stiffness or strength-
to-weight ratios.
Although significant development has been made in fibre-reinforcing for material
extrusion based additive manufacturing (MEX) in industry as well as in academic
research, the current state of the art still has many limitations, as further demon-
strated in the following section. The current state-of-the-art MEX-AM-based FRP
composite technology focuses primarily on the use of thermoplastic-based matrix ma-
terials, and the literature on thermosetting polymer-based materials is still scarce.
Consequently, continuous fibre-reinforced thermosetting polymer-based MEX-AM is
still in its early stages. Many vital aspects have yet to be identified to be comparable
to conventional composite manufacturing processes.
Therefore, this thesis aims to pave the way for future research and exploration of
fibre-reinforced thermosetting polymer material extrusion additive manufacturing by
developing a tooling system that incorporates continuous carbon fibre tows.
The
project involves designing a versatile and re-configurable tooling system and experi-
mental testing of the system performance metrics.

1.3
Problem statement
Integrating continuous fibres into material extrusion is a research field in its in-
fancy.[11] According to the scientific a review of the current literature on the topic, a
reliable and effective fibre incorporation technique has not yet identified. In light of
this, a concept is proposed in which a a modular tooling system is developed. This
tooling system will consist of various essential subsystems that can be reconfigured
in relation to one another to facilitate the testing and evaluation of a broad range of
tooling concepts. The concept draws inspiration from modular STEM science kits,
such as the one depicted in Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: SGO STEM Blocks, an electronics building block system [6]
With the decision to provide the overarching research activity that this project is part
of with a solution that enables the eﬀicient exploration of how fibre reinforcement
can be integrated into material extrusion tooling, the project is primarily focused on
identifying the necessary modules, designing them appropriately, ensuring proper fit
between modules, and demonstrating how they can be configured and mounted onto
a tool plate. The solution will facilitate the building of experiments to investigate
the introduction of reinforcement fibres into tooling systems. As a result, the project

1.3.1
Research questions
1. What are the current challenges or limitations in the state-of-the-art continuous
FRP for MEX-AM, and how can these be addressed in the design of the tooling
system?
2. What essential components must be included in a modular tooling system that
incorporates continuous fibre reinforcement of the thermosetting polymer-based
MEX-AM system?
3. How can each subcomponent in a tooling system for continuous fibre-reinforced
thermosetting polymer-based MEX-AM be optimised to provide a functional
printing process?
Topics that have not been investigated include investigating different materials for the
tooling system and their respective properties, such as durability and wear resistance.
Furthermore, the effects of different nozzle geometries on the extrusion process and
the tool-path optimisation for maximising the final product’s mechanical properties
have also been excluded.
Although the following areas could have potentially provided valuable information,
it was necessary to prioritise other aspects of the investigation to achieve the overar-
ching goals of the project. By focusing on the development of the modular tooling
system, this project instead aims to establish the foundations for further research
that subsequently can push the boundaries of the FRP MEX technology.

CHAPTER 2
State of the Art
A comprehensive literature review has been conducted to analyse academic and in-
dustrial solutions of state-of-the-art FRP composite MEX-AM systems. The primary
objective of this review was to identify and highlight the limitations of the current
stage of development of the FRP composite MEX-AM. Additionally, this review was
an excellent source of inspiration to generate ideas for new tooling system setups to
overcome these limitations.
As illustrated in Figure 2.1, several crucial factors must be considered during ma-
terial extrusion, including the manufacturing technique, the materials used, and their
respective processing. Therefore, the interaction between these factors significantly af-
fects the final result. Thus, carefully selecting these parameters is essential to achieve
the desired results.
Figure 2.1: Materials utilised for MEX-AM. [2]
==PAGE:18==
8
2 State of the Art
Most of the existing literature on FRP MEX-AM solutions uses thermoplastic-based
matrix materials. However, the current project focuses on thermosetting polymers
which most commonly is used for conventional fibre composite components. This
presents significantly different MEX-AM processing parameters. Despite the differ-
ences in the matrix material, similarities can still be identified in the printing pro-
cesses.
Whereas is is speculative as to why thermoplastic matrix material is commonly used
for most MEX based FRP systems, it is assumed that this has to do with an active
avoidance of having to handle the curing kinematics of a thermosetting resin. This
adds to the complexity of the MEX process and further complicates the development
of a reliable system. This is assumed to outweigh the advantages of basing a solution
on the knowledge and experience that can be drawn from conventional fibre composite
manufacturing. Most work on the FRP MEX raise other arguments for why it is
almost exclusively thermoplastics that are used, and are often:
High strength and stiffness: Thermoplastic composites reinforced with fibres such
as carbon, glass, or aramid provide high strength and stiffness compared to unfilled
thermoplastics. This makes them ideal for producing parts that need to withstand
high loads or stresses. [12]
Improved dimensional stability: Fibre-reinforced thermoplastics are less prone
to warping and distortion during printing due to their improved dimensional stability.
This allows for more accurate and consistent printing of complex parts. [28]
Better processability: Thermoplastic composites are easier to process than ther-
moset composites since they can be melted and reshaped multiple times without
undergoing any significant chemical changes. This makes them ideal for 3D printing,
which relies on melting and extruding the material to create parts.[18]
Good environmental resistance: Thermoplastic composites exhibit good resis-
tance to chemicals, moisture, and UV radiation, making them suitable for outdoor
applications and harsh environments.[38]
Cost-effectiveness: Thermoplastic composites are often less expensive than ther-
moset composites, which can make them a more cost-effective option for producing
parts in large quantities. [3]
The processing mechanism of thermosetting polymers is as follows. When thermoset-
ting resin monomers and prepolymers are combined and heated, the mixture under-
goes a transient curing process and transforms from a liquid to an irreversible solid
shape.
The curing mechanism effectively crosslinks the covalent bonds, resulting
in the desired properties of high tensile strength and temperature-resistant degrada-
==PAGE:19==
2 State of the Art
9
tion.[31]
With respect to thermoset materials, the curing mechanism of thermosetting poly-
mers can be engineered to be compatible with extrusion-based additive manufacturing.
Thermosetting and prepolymer resins can be applied and processed in several ways
to create distinct curing properties, which has been demonstrated in previous studies.
Albeit MEX compatibility with thermosetting polymers has been limited due to the
aforementioned added complexity from their curing mechanism of these materials.
To address this issue, researchers have explored different approaches to modify the
curing mechanism of thermosetting polymers to make them compatible with MEX.
One approach is to use hybrid curing mechanisms, where the resin is cured using both
heat and light or heat and chemical crosslinking. For example, a recent study used
a combination of heat and light to cure a blend of epoxy and acrylate resins during
FDM, resulting in highly crosslinked parts with improved mechanical properties[33]
Another approach is to use reactive resins, such as prepolymers, which can be par-
tially cured before printing and then fully crosslinked during the MEX process. For
example, a study investigated the use of partially cured polyurethane prepolymer
filaments, which were then extruded using FDM and fully crosslinked during post-
processing, resulting in highly flexible and tough parts[19]
In addition, some researchers have explored the use of alternative curing mechanisms
such as UV curing, which can be used to crosslink resins during FDM at lower tem-
peratures. For example, a study investigated the use of a UV-curable resin system
with FDM, resulting in highly crosslinked parts with excellent mechanical properties
[10]
Overall, there is significant interest in developing thermosetting polymer materials
that are compatible with extrusion-based additive manufacturing processes, and re-
searchers are actively exploring different approaches to modify the curing mechanism
of these materials to make them compatible.
sectionComposite Additive Manufacturing
In extrusion-based composite 3D printing, various types of fibers can be used to rein-
force the printed parts. Here are some common types of fibers used in this process:
Carbon fibers: Carbon fibers are a popular choice due to their high strength-to-
weight ratio and excellent mechanical properties. They are often used to reinforce
thermoplastic matrices such as ABS, nylon, and polycarbonate. For example, a re-
cent study demonstrated the use of carbon fiber-reinforced polycarbonate filaments
for FDM printing, resulting in parts with improved mechanical properties
Glass fibers: Glass fibers are another popular choice for extrusion-based composite
==PAGE:20==
10
2 State of the Art
3D printing, due to their low cost and good mechanical properties. They are often
used to reinforce thermoplastic matrices such as PLA and nylon. For example, a
study investigated the use of glass fiber-reinforced PLA filaments for FDM printing,
resulting in parts with improved strength and stiffness[14]
Aramid fibers: Aramid fibers, such as Kevlar, are often used for their high strength
and excellent impact resistance. They are typically used to reinforce thermosetting
matrices such as epoxy resins. For example, a study demonstrated the use of Kevlar
fiber-reinforced epoxy resins for extrusion-based 3D printing, resulting in parts with
high strength and toughness [27]
Natural fibers: Natural fibers, such as bamboo and hemp, are also used in extrusion-
based composite 3D printing due to their low cost and biodegradability. They are
often used to reinforce thermoplastic matrices such as PLA and PETG. For exam-
ple, a study investigated the use of bamboo fiber-reinforced PLA filaments for FDM
printing, resulting in parts with improved strength and stiffness [22]
Overall, the choice of fiber depends on the specific application requirements and the
matrix material used for printing. For this project, focus has been narrowed down to
carbon- and glass fibres, as these are most relevant for the current researcha at the
AM group at DTU Construct.
Carbon fiber composites and glass fiber composites have different properties due to
their distinct material composition and manufacturing processes. Mechanical proper-
ties differ. Carbon fiber composites have higher tensile strength, stiffness, and fatigue
resistance than glass fiber composites. This is because carbon fibers have a higher
modulus of elasticity and a lower density than glass fibers.
However, glass fiber
composites have higher impact resistance than carbon fiber composites due to their
greater toughness [40]
Cost is often the governing parameter. With this regard, carbon fiber composites are
generally more expensive than glass fiber composites due to the high cost of carbon
fibers and the complex manufacturing processes involved. Glass fibers, on the other
hand, are more readily available and less expensive to produce. [34]
Also, the manufacturing process of the fibres needs to be taken into account. Carbon
fiber composites are typically manufactured using a high-temperature process known
as pyrolysis, where a precursor material is heated in an oxygen-free environment to
form carbon fibers. Glass fiber composites, on the other hand, are manufactured
using a lower-temperature process known as pultrusion, where continuous glass fibers
are pulled through a resin bath and then cured in a heated die.[20]

2.0.1
Analysis of continious FRP AM systems and methods
Various methods have been proposed in additive manufacturing to embed continuous
fibre-reinforced polymer (FRP) composites into printed components. [17] has iden-
tified three distinct approaches that are considered feasible, as seen in Figure 2.3.
Various approaches that use these methods involve pre-impregnated FRP tapes, di-
rect fibre feeding, or combining printing and braiding techniques to integrate the
fibres into the printed structure.
A review article by Zhang et al. (2021) provides an overview of various continuous
fiber reinforcement techniques, including pre-impregnated FRP tapes, direct fiber
feeding, and combining printing and braiding techniques. The article discusses the
advantages and disadvantages of each technique in terms of mechanical properties,
production time, and cost. The authors conclude that further research is needed to
determine the most suitable method for a given application.[41]
A study by Chen et al. (2021) focuses on direct fiber feeding technique. The study
investigates the influence of fiber pre-tension and print orientation on the mechanical

properties of 3D printed continuous fiber reinforced thermoplastic composite parts.
The authors discuss the advantages of the direct fiber feeding technique, including
the ability to produce parts with high fiber volume fractions and excellent mechanical
properties.[ZhangZ]
Another study by Yuan et al. (2020) provides a comprehensive review of various con-
tinuous fiber reinforcement techniques, including pre-impregnated FRP tapes, direct
fiber feeding, and combining printing and braiding techniques. The study discusses
the advantages and limitations of each technique and highlights the need for further
research to optimize the processing parameters and improve the mechanical proper-

CHAPTER 4
The Modular Tooling
Concept
The state-of-art analysis proved that there are no single good method for composite
additive manufacturing with continuous fibres, however, many different approaches
was identified. By breaking down the composite 3D printing process into modular
subsystems, it is possible to create a flexible and versatile tooling system that can
be customized for specific applications. The subsystems could be influenced by the
type of fibre reinforcement used, the method of fibre deposition, the type of matrix
material used, the curing process, and the post-processing steps.
4.1
Fundamental idea
A modular tooling system will allow for the mixing and matching of subsystems to cre-
ate different composite 3D printing methods. For example, the same fiber deposition
system could be used with different matrix materials, or different curing processes
could be used with the same fiber and matrix combination. This approach can offer
flexibility in terms of materials, processes, and cost to prototype. It would in other
words allow for rapid prototyping and testing of different configurations.
While this approach has not been found to be explored in the literature, the con-
cept of modularity in 3D printing is a topic of interest in the research community.
There have been several studies on modular 3D printing systems for different ap-
plications, including modular deposition systems mixed with milling, laser and 3D
scanning capacity so a modular tooling solution for composite 3D printing could offer
a promising avenue for advancing the field.
The fundamental idea behind this proposed modular tooling solution for compos-
ite 3D printing is to break down the process into subsystems that can be mixed and
matched to create a customized tooling system for a given application. By creating a
modular approach to tooling, users can adapt to changing requirements for compos-
ite 3D printing processes without having to spend research effort and money for new
equipment or overhaul existing processes.

4.2
Identification of Primary Subsystems
The modular tooling solution is proposed to be comprised of subsystems that can be
found as a repeating element in the technologies that was identified in the state-of-the-
art review by means of the population of a modified morphology matrix diagram as

Figure 4.1: Morphological diagram of the repeating CRRP MEX-AM features
By developing each of these key subsystem as a standalone module, user can quickly
and easily swap out components as needed. For example, if a new composite 3D
printing process requires a different type of fiber or matrix material, the fiber drive
and cutting modules can be easily reconfigured to support the new material. This
flexibility allows user to rapidly iterate on their composite 3D printing processes.
In summary, the fundamental idea behind this modular tooling solution is to base
it on generic modular versions of the identified subsystems to yield the most flexible
and adaptable research tooling platform possible.
4.3
Fiber Drive Module
The fiber drive module is a critical component of the modular tooling system. This
module is responsible for feeding the fiber into the print head, where it can be com-
bined with the matrix. The fiber drive module should consist of a motorized mecha-
nism that can control the rate and tension of the fiber as it is fed into the print head.
The fiber will be supplied from a bobbin or other storage mechanism and the primary
role of the drive module is to drive it into the print head.
One important consideration for the fiber drive module is the type of fiber being used.
Carbon fiber, which will be the primary material used by the AM group at DTU, is
==PAGE:33==
4.4 Fiber cutting module

prone to breaking if not fed with proper tension and alignment.
The fiber drive
module must be designed with these requirements in mind. To ensure versatility and
adaptability, the fiber drive module is to be designed to accommodate various fiber
types and sizes by using feeding rollers with adjustable offset to adjust the tension
settings. This allow the modular tooling system to handle a wide range of fibers.
In addition, the fiber drive module will need to be integrated with the controller to
enable real-time adjustment of the fiber feed during printing. This sets a requirement
to base the module on stepper motors for the drive mechanism as this is what the
printing platform supports.
4.4
Fiber cutting module
The fiber cutting module is the next critical component of the modular. This mod-
ule is responsible for cutting the fiber to the desired length and its configuration is
envisioned to exlusively be in a configuration where it is feeding from the fiber drive
module and cuts the fiber inbetween the fiber drive and the extrusion unit. The fiber
cutting module can be designed in various ways. Regardless of the specific design, the
fiber cutting module must be capable of cutting the fibers to the desired length with
high precision and consistency. This is critical to ensuring that the timing of when
the start and end of a tow of fiber is embedded to the workpiece matches up with the
workpiece geometry and thus ensures that the fiber is distributed evenly throughout
the part.
4.5
UV Curing Module
Traditionally, UV curing resins are used as a means to solidify the bulk component
or composite matrix from a photoinitiated thermoset resin. The UV Curing Module
proposed here has a completely different function. Considering method 3 in section
2.0.1, a two-nozzle approach is taken to embed tows of carbon fibre in-between layers
of matrix material. Whilst starting and stopping as well as for sharp cornering, it
may be necessary to tack down the otherwise loose fibre to the under-laying layer
of matrix material. A module that can dispense a droplet of UV resin to the bare
carbon fibre tow and tack it to the underlaying layer at start, stop and at corners,
can aid in ensuring that the fibre can evenly be integrated into the workpiece. The
UV curing module is thus responsible for dispension and emitting the appropriate
intensity and wavelength of UV light.
4.6
Resin mixing nozzle
The matrix material is a two-component system. Static mixing nozzles are a conve-
nient mixing solution for resin deposition because they provide eﬀicient and consistent

Feasibility study of in-situ nozzle impregnation
A simulated test experiment was carried out to obtain a qualitative idea of how the
fibre yarn would impregnate if a fully functional tooling system was implemented.
The test setup included a cut-off fibre yarn of approximately 50 cm and a prototype
nozzle developed by the thesis supervisor, Marco Brander. The fibre yarn was in-
serted at the entrance of the nozzle from the side. Thus, no motorised feeding system
or pretension of the fibre yarn was applied.
The visual inspection showed that the fibre was thoroughly impregnated in the ma-
trix and that the solution seems viable for further development. There was no further
inspection of porosity in the test piece, but that could be investigated in the future.
Moreover, the test experiment showed that the fibre yarn remained static at its po-
sition during the experimental testing, meaning that there was no suﬀicient pull
exhibited from the matrix flow to coextrude FRP composite beads.
Such examples have been seen in the production of flow-based pultrusion additives,
where glass fibre reinforced glass fibre was impregnated in cement [8]; however, ac-
cording to [32], a flow-based pultrusion extrusion, such solutions does not cater to
thermosetting polymers, due to difference of the rheological models.
Therefore, a motorised feeding system is deemed necessary to move the fibres. A
significant consideration of synchronised matrix and fibre feed rates must be taken
at the impregnation area and the relation of the movement given by the printer, i.e.
through a gantry- or robotic-arm-based setup.
