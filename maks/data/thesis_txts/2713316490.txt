Abstract
With its Clean energy for all package, the European Union has reinforced its willingness to promote renew-
able energy communities for clean energy transition. In Denmark, housing communities called Andelsbolig
are thriving these recent years. This thesis looks into the different modelling steps of an Andelsbolig as
a renewable energy community. Investment in PV and a battery are considered. First, an optimization
model based on stochastic programming determines that the optimal sizing for the PV rooftop panels and
the battery under uncertainty of electricity prices and solar irradiance are respectively 30kW and 25kWh.
The energy flows and operations of the systems are determined across 50 scenarios. Then, an economic
feasibility study assesses that the project which has a lifetime of 25 years is profitable and presents an NPV
of 54.4kAC. Lastly, an energy sharing mechanism, the virtual-net billing method, is used to compute the
virtual energy flows allowing the allocation of the shared energy and the distribution of the economic surplus
to the community members. An analysis based on game theory indicators evaluates the fairness of three
variations for this method.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Decarbonizing the electricity sector has been at the core of many goverments‚Äô policies now. Integrating
renewable sources in the energy mix will pave the way for the transition towards clean and non-polluting
technologies. However, the fact that, except for hydro-power and biomass, they are non dispatchable (i.e.
their power cannot be controlled by operators) and stochastic (having a random probability distribution)
make their exploitation challenging compared to non-renewable sources [1]. Indeed, the uncertainty on their
production makes electricity investment planning and production more difficult. The potential power that
can be generated from a renewable source at a specific period of time is based on highly variable and unpre-
dictable weather conditions. Forecasts are not accurate enough to avoid overproduction or underproduction.
Uncertainty also comes from other crucial elements like demand levels or prices. Therefore, when making
investment decisions for renewable technologies, it is essential to take into account different scenarios for the
variables.
On the other hand, renewable energy communities (REC) thrive as a niche for developing clean energy
use. In 2019, Europe counted more than 3500 REC (cf figure 1.1). Moreover studies show that the number
of energy communities initiatives (i.e.
cooperatives, eco-villages, small-scale heating organisations...)
in
European countries is booming: just in Germany, this number has quadrupled in 7 years [2].

empower citizens by allowing them to use, produce, and sell renewable energy. In absolute, such projects

been classified in five main categories: environmental, economic, social, political and technical as can be

Danish context
In Denmark, according to the Executive Order on net settlement for own producers of electricity (BEK

Chapter 2
Literature review
The modelling of EC comprises several aspects: the evaluation of the regulatory framework , the determina-
tion of the optimal characteristics of the community, the scheduling of the energy flows, the rules regulating
the energy sharing etc.
The research community provides a lot of resources and background in terms of decision-making op-
timization models for investment decisions and strategies in renewable energy sources. In [7], Ding and
Somani develop linear and mixed-integer linear programming models to expand the use of renewable energy
while minimizing investment costs on a long-term horizon. Paper [8] formulates a renewable energy planning
method for an energy company. In it, Ervural et. al develop a multi-objective decision-making method for
investments and capacity expansion rules to model the company‚Äôs different goals. Mohseni et.al use meta-
heuristics algorithms, as they are time efficient for large-scale problems compared to traditional methods
[9], to design and optimally size capacities of renewable technologies in microgrids. Moreover, paper [10]
presents an optimization model for investment planning of residential heat and power supply systems using
a dual dynamic programming algorithm in order to reduce complexity without making simplifications to
the model. These papers only carry techno-economic studies dealing with optimal design, and operations
without addressing collective self consumption.
The global electricity generation by solar has been multiplied more than seven times between 2012 and
2019 [11]. Therefore, developing models to optimize investments and operational planning in PV has received
a lot of attention from the scientific community. [12] conducts an economic study on the installation of PV
residential solar panels in Chile. The study focuses on the profitability of grid-tied PV systems and zero
feed-in PV systems by comparing LCOEs. In article [13], the authors focus on solar energy expansion in
Turkey. They offer a photovoltaic plant investment model for countries in deficit of energy supply. Paper
[14] implements a multi-objective optimization model which aims are to achieve high profitability and en-
hance the voltage conditions of distribution feeders of a grid-connected PV system. Their research results in
finding the optimal PV penetration level and the optimal Distributed Generation (DG) allocation (i.e. type,
location and size of DG units). PVG generation modelling and cost minimization are tackled but their is no
application case for energy community.
With respect to communities operation and energy sharing, paper [15] analyzes different approaches
to model energy communities with PV investments in agreement with European recent regulations [16]
[17].
Based on a case study composed of a Portuguese city and a local winery, paper several scenarios
depicting different types of collaboration between the two locations are developed. The study results in
assessing the change of PV penetration levels, electricity costs and the trade-offs of 3 scenarios: indepen-
dent self-consumption, alternating the prosumer/consumer role between the city and the winery, both actors
as prosumers.
In [18] Huang et. al offer a review of methods for planning energy community.
Energy
demand forecasting, energy resources assessment and optimization stages are topics addressed. The Com-
munity Energy Planning (CEP) method prioritizes the demand-side point of view while defining goals for
the community, setting up indicators of management requirement and building the architectural design. [19]

Net metering is a mechanism allowing a consumer to be partially compensated for the power production

deal with the collective aspect of modelling a community and address energy sharing assuming that the size
of the renewable generators and battery systems are already determined. Therefore, there is no pre-analysis
regarding the optimal characteristics of the EC.
Some works, like [25] try to be more complete about the topic and provides an overall modelling renewable
energy community (REC). If all the aforementioned step of REC modelling are adressed in this paper, the
optimization model of this paper does not account for uncertainties.
The literature review revealed that planning a renewable energy community involves different steps,
requiring different types of modelling, from investment planning to energy-sharing within the community.
Existing work has been done for all of the steps but each paper excludes one or several parts as pointed out.
The aim of this thesis is to address simultaneously the different aspects of the whole process of investment
planning and operational strategies of a renewable community. The Danish regulatory framework of An-
delsbolig is analyzed. Moreover, the optimal sizing of investment in PV technology and storage system, and
the optimal power dispatch considering the minimization of costs under uncertainty are determined with a
stochastic program. Once the optimal characteristics of the EC are found an economic analysis is carried out
and the feasibility of the project and the energy sharing strategies is assessed through a discounted cashflow
analysis. Finally, the energy sharing, the collective self-consumption and savings allocations are approached
by virtual net-billing method adjusted to battery operation.

3.2
Solution evaluation
The content of this section are based on both Introduction to Stochastic Programming, J.R Birge and F.
Louveaux [26] and Decision Making Under Uncertainty in Electricity Markets, chap. 2, Conejo et. al [27].
To determinate if the solution given by the stochastic programming improved over a deterministic ap-
proach and if this solution is reliable and robust in other scenarios, it is important to have some evaluation
methods. One way to do it is to compare the stochastic solution to the solutions obtained by the WS and
EEV approaches. This comparison enables to quantify how much stochastic programming perform better
than these simpler and computationally less complex approaches. Another way is too evaluate the solutions
for scenarios that are possible but not considered in the optimization.
Expected value of perfect information (EVPI)
This metrics uses the solution obtained by solving the problem of the wait-and-see approach. It consists
in solving each scenario individually assuming perfect knowledge of the uncertainty. This is not possible in
practice because some decisions (those corresponding to the first-stage ones) need to be decided before the
realization of the uncertainty.
The expected value of perfect information can be interpreted as the maximal amount of money the planner
would be willing to pay to have information on the uncertainty. If WS defines the expected-value of the
wait-and-see solution and RP the stochastic solution, the EVPI is given by:

A small value of EVPI means that the recourse solution is close to the wait-and-see solution which uses

The expectation of the expected-value solution derives from a deterministic model that uses the expected
value of the uncertain data instead of scenario.
The value of the stochastic solution is obtained by comparing the stochastic solution (RP) to the expected-

3.3
Scenario generation: Block-boostrapping method
If the uncertain data for the model, X(t, ¬∑), are from a continuous distribution, (e.g solar irradiance), there
is an infinite number of possible combinations.
The processes should then be approximated by discrete
stochastic processes, which means considering finite sets of possible realizations.
Bootstrapping is a method commonly used for inferring statistical properties of a distribution [28]. It can
also be used to create realistic time-series for a random variable from historical observations as described
in [29]. After attaching probabilities to these scenarios, the generated samples can be used in stochastic
programming. Block-bootstrapping is a known variant of this technique.[30]

The method consists in sampling the sample itself by randomly drawing blocks and constituting the new
scenario by iteration. This technique enables not to loose auto-correlation in the data and time-dependency
[29].

3.7
Virtual net-billing
When prosumers are involved in collective self-consumption, they share their energy surplus and benefit from
the generation of others. A way of calculating their energy bill at the end of each netting interval (typically
a month) is to offset their consumption by the energy that their local generator or share of generator pro-
duced; this is net-metering [36]. This process only uses the accumulated energy quantities and disregard the
real-time energy exchanges. This means that prosumers with large generation capacities are advantaged with
high financial compensations whereas prosumers with low generation capacities bear almost all the costs.
The actors are not charged on the volume of their imports.[36] This method raises fairness issues among
the members of the energy community. One way to overcome this issue is to introduce sharing keys that
distribute the energy inside the community more equitably. The sharing keys are defined in a more finer
temporal resolution so they can report on actual energy flows between the grid and the prosumption site
(typically 1h or 15min). This method is called net-billing.[37]

Methodology
To answer the first research questions ‚ÄĚWhat are the optimal investments and size for PV installations on a
building under uncertainties?‚ÄĚ, this thesis will define a stochastic program.
4.1
Formulation of the model
This section defines the optimization model used to determine the optimal sizing of residential PV panels
installed on roofs and a collective battery with the objective of minimizing costs. All programs are mixed-
integer problems.
4.1.1
Uncertainty
To model electricity generation from solar energy, one must know solar irradiance in advance. Dependant
on non predictable factors like weather conditions, pollution, etc), solar irradiance is an uncertain data
[39]. Electricity prices constitute the second set of uncertain data. Because of the way prices are formed
on liberalized markets (see paragraph 5.3.2.1) it is difficult to forecast them. In this thesis, scenarios for
solar irradiance and electricity prices are used in a stochastic program. These scenarios are generated from
historical data, as described in section 3.3. 1000 scenarios are generated and then reduced to the number of
50. The blocksize chosen is 24 in order to sample data corresponding to one day at a time.
Moreover, in this context, the sources of uncertainty show seasonal patterns, the periodicity of which can
be greater than the block length used in the block-bootstrapping method (cf data analysis in section 5.3).
Simply sampling as described in section 3.3 could then lead to non-realistic scenarios. For example, while
sampling blocks of 24hours in the solar irradiance historical data, since the process is random, the algorithm
can choose to sample consecutively 24h corresponding to June 21st (longest day of the year) followed by
24h corresponding to December 21st (shortest day of the year). In the same way, one could imagine that
while sampling the prices, a block of 24h supposed to represent a Thursday is sampled by picking original
data corresponding to a Saturday (see section 5.3.2). In this thesis, to overcome these issues and conserve
the seasonality and properties of the data, an additional criteria is added to block bootstrapping. When
sampling data for the day DD/MM, the data can only be sampled from the same month, MM. Additionally
if DD/MM corresponds to a working day then only working days of this month can be sampled and if vice
versa. Furthermore, to preserve correlation between solar irradiance and electricity prices, the data sets of
solar irradiance and electricity prices are sampled simultaneously [40]: the 24h block sampled to represent
prices of day DD/MM, is the same 24h sampled chosen to represent the solar irradiance of day DD/MM. 5

4.3
Energy sharing and fairness
The optimization model cited in the previous section determines the optimal sizing for the system and the
optimal quantities of energy flowing between the grid, consumption site and the energy community over the
time horizon across scenarios. This modelling allows to plan for investment and schedule optimal opera-
tional strategies. However, the model assumes perfect knowledge of the input data to compute the optimal
decisions. In practice, the prices and PV generation profile are not known for the entire time horizon. They
would have to be forecast to apply the optimization process. The energy balances in real-life are therefore
sub-optimal.
Once the physical energy trading has happen, the actual operational costs can be determined by collecting
the actual values for prices, electricity generation, battery operation and consumption values. The consumer
can either be charged consequently for its prosumption in the community [42] or pay a bill according to a
distribution mechanism that has been agreed beforehand [43]. In the latter case, the billing method accounts
for virtual flows that do not necessarily coincide with physical flows. One the one hand, this bill must account
for the electricity sent to the consumption site, whether it is produced by the local PV panels, discharged
by the battery or imported from the central grid. One the other hand, the economic surplus coming from
the sales on the central grid has to be allocated. The individuals part of the community will most likely
have different consumption profiles, shares in the generation system, different flats features and this can
influence the fairness of the dispatch. The goal of this section is to adapt an existing sharing mechanism to
the structure of the Energy community modeled by 4.1 and assess its fairness.
The concept of fairness has different interpretations in the scientific literature as described in section 2.
The main interpretation of fairness when it comes to energy sharing are:
21
==PAGE:23==
‚ÄĘ Individual rationality: each member benefits from the energy sharing and has a positive payoff [44].
‚ÄĘ Equality: the differences between the payoffs are minimized [45]
‚ÄĘ Coalition stability: no member is better-off when not taking part in the energy sharing [46]
Subsection 4.3.1 describes the difference between the problem solved by [37] and the actual case study of
this thesis and why the VNB method cannot be directly applied. Then in subsection 4.3.2, the adjustments
made to fit the structure of the Andelsbolig (that is described in 5.1.1) will be presented.
4.3.1

5.1
Background
5.1.1
Andelsbolig
The following paragraph is referenced by [47]. A cooperative housing association (Andelsboligforeningen)
is an association that is founded for the purpose of acquiring, owning and operating the property in which
the association‚Äôs members - (Andelshaver) - live. This means, as a cooperative member, one buys a share
of the association‚Äôs assets, and thus have the right to live in one of the homes (Andelsbolig). It is thus
cooperative housing associations that own the property itself, while the cooperative member buys a share in
the association. This also means that it is the cooperative housing association that must pay property value
tax and not the individual member. Decisions on the operation and maintenance of a cooperative housing
association are decided at the annual general meeting, where all members can have an influence on the house
rules, articles of association and finances of the cooperative housing association. It is up to the individual
association and its members to set the framework for the community.
Even though the prices of cooperative housing have risen in recent years, buying an Andelsbolig remains
a more attractive opportunity than a similar owner-occupied home. In an Andelsbolig, co-operative mem-
bers do not pay property value tax which make the monthly rent cheaper than an owner-occupied house.
Andelsbolig are subject to a maximum fixed price and therefore their prices are less receptive to demand
changes. As an other benefit, it is possible to make improvements in one home and the residual value of
improvements after depreciation can be added to the unit value [48]. In brief there are many benefits in
investing in an Andelsbolig and the demand is increasing from them.
What is the link with PV investments?
As depicted in the previous paragraph, with approximately
200,000 cooperative homes [49], Denmark has a booming cooperative housing market. In the mean time,
the European parliament is providing incentives to help local actors and citizens carrying out clean energy
project in Energy Communities [50]. A local energy community that, through the management of production,
conversion and to some extent also consumption of energy, is able to provide flexibility to the overall energy
system.
It will constitute an important complement to the regional and international supply networks
as well as the major energy producers. Flexibility is about reducing the demand for electricity and heat
during periods of peak load and as a result also reduce the need to expand the capacity of supply networks
both locally and regionally.Andelsbolig can constitute a great opportunity to develop the usage of renewable
energy.
Indeed, in a cooperative housing associations there is typically a close connection between and
decisions about investments and operations in daily life.
Andelsbolig are decision-making structures on
political and administrative levels.
In addition, the housing organizations can carry out activities that
25
==PAGE:27==
have a natural connection to the homes that are based on the knowledge that the housing organization has
acquired through its business.
This thesis investigates the model of an Andelsbolig as a community that can own and operate electricity
generation based on solar energy and have a storage system on the side.
5.1.2
Danish regulatory framework about net-metering
Currently in Denmark there is no subsidies specifically for investing in residential solar panels and battery
systems. Since 2019 there is no feed-in-tariffs neither for prosumers selling surplus energy. The only incentives
for them are the offset of their electricity costs obtained by the electricity sales on the grid and partial tax
exemption [4].
The net metering is allowed only on a 60-minute basis in Eastern Denmark (DK1) [51].

7.1
Discussion and analysis of results
The objective of this thesis was to bring the different steps in energy community modelling together. A
method assessing optimal investments and scheduling of energy transactions under uncertainty, feasibility
studies and fair energy sharing for collective self-consumption was to be developed.
The study shows that stochastic programming provides optimal decisions for investments in PV rooftop
panels and a BESS. By optimizing the expected values of total costs across scenarios, the sizing and energy
balance accounts for under uncertainty of electricity prices and solar energy. It was found that under the
assumptions of the case study, the investment in PV + BESS system for collective operation in an energy
community is feasible. With the objective of minimizing costs and under danish regulatory framework, an
Andelsbolig will have annual economic savings by self-consuming energy coming from PV generation and
exporting and storing its energy surplus. Considering the lifetime of the system, the project is worth invest-
ing in since it shows a positive NPV and an IRR greater than the WACC. It is also shown that a virtual
net-billing scheme adjusted to the structure of the energy community it applies to is a practical and fair way
to share energy since it redistributes the savings equally between net-exporters and net-importers. Indeed,
the VNB scheme ensures individual rationality, equality and coalition stability.
This thesis assesses that there are social and economic incentives for people to gather in energy communi-
ties as the European commission and other countries are promoting. Economic savings from self-consumption
should attract people to become prosumers. Energy communities enable the development and integration
of renewable energy and therefore contributes to the lowering of carbon emissions and pollution. Higher
electricity prices which is the most likely scenario for the future pushes the investments in solar and batter-
ies. The geopolitical context in Europe caused the gas prices to rocket dragging along electricity prices. On
another hand, optimal investing and planning coupled to a fair energy sharing mechanism guarantees justice
in the energy trading. Not just maximizing the savings of the community is important. Achieving fairness
in the energy sharing and net-billing is crucial too because it incites prosumers to stay cooperate despite
without having to trample their personal interests. The maximum excess indicators shows that operating an
energy community like modeled in this thesis pushes the members to operate jointly and not leaving since
this is the best economical option for them.
The particularity of the functioning of an Andelsbolig can also constitutes an incentive in investing in renew-
able technologies and storage systems. The possibility of having an added-value on the share when selling it
over if improvements have been made can push members to invest. Indeed their share in the PV generation
and storage system can be pass on the next inhabitants, leading to greater economic gains.

7.2
Limitations and future research
The first limitations of this thesis concern the data. If the selling prices used are historical and accurate data,
the buying prices are estimations based on the breaking down of these costs. However, electricity suppliers
usually have a membership fee that is added on top of the buying prices computed[73]. This tariff might
vary from one supplier to another and nothing guarantees that all the members of the energy community
have the same. In practice, the prosumers will not be subject to the same electricity prices but this is an
assumption that has been made for simplicity.
Another critical point of these results is that the parameters and input data other than electricity prices and
solar irradiance have uncertainty. More precisely, technology costs and features are uncertain and likely to
drop in the next years due to a more or less performing learning rate. For instance LIB prices ranges from and
400 AC/kWh and 1 250 AC/kWh at residential scale [66]. The results are sensitive to the technology costs since
the costs reductions of a technology means reduction of the objective function. Under low-costs assumptions
the savings from being part of an EC could be greater which opens the perspective for more storage system
investments at a residential scale in the future. Moreover, new solutions for building integrated PV (BIPV)
have been developed in the recent years and are now commercialized [74]. Since the EU energy performance
directive (2018/844/EU) incites the creation of zero energy buildings, it could be interesting to evaluate the
feasibility of this emerging technology instead of classical rooftop PV panels.
Regarding the scenarios generation, this thesis has used a modified block-bootstrapping algorithm to account
for the specificity and seasonality of the data. If the scenarios generated are more realistic there is also less
variability. This can be overviewed with the closeness of the recourse program solution, expected value of
using the EV solution and the expected value of the Wait-and-see solution. The selection of the scenario
generation technique presents a trade-off here.
Lastly, the energy sharing mechanism has been applied with optimization intervals of 1 hours. This interval
can be reduced even more which would allow the virtual bulling to account better for the real-time physical
transactions [36]. A possible future research could investigate how much additional savings can be gained
with a finer netting interval.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This Master‚Äôs thesis aims at developing a method accounting for the different steps of modelling a renewable
energy community. The steps considered go from its investments to its energy-sharing mechanism. This
thesis offers to appliy the method to the case study of an Andelsbolig in Denmark. The literature review
highlighted the lack of studies tackling simultaneously all the following aspects: the evaluation of the reg-
ulatory framework for the renewable community, the determination of the optimal characteristics of the
community under uncertainty, the scheduling of the energy flows, the assessment of the economic feasibil-
ity and the rules regulating the energy sharing. This thesis presents an approach to go through all these steps.
It is determined that the investment in rooftop PV panels and a battery system for an Andelsbolig in
Denmark is feasible. In order to minimize costs under uncertainty of prices and solar generation, the optimal
capacity of the solar panels are 30kWp and the optimal size of the battery is 25kWh. This configuration leads
to annual economic savings of 2235AC thanks to the self-consumption and the revenues brought by selling
the surplus energy. The energy community therefore achieves a high self-sufficiency rate. The economic
feasibility study assesses that investing in the project is profitable and that the NPV is expected to be
54.4kAC. The project is expected to yield returns up to 11.8% with a payback time of approximately 11
years. A sensitivity analysis reveals that under high prices assumptions the project is still feasible even
though the economic savings are reduced. It also revealed that higher electricity prices incites the energy
community to invest in more PV panels.
It is shown the the energy sharing mechanism virtual net-billing can be adapted to an energy community
with investments in PV and battery.
This scheme dispatches the economic surplus to the members of
its community in a fair way by dividing equally the shared electricity between the net-importers and net-
exporters. Net-importers are not as penalized by the costs of their consumption as they can be with other
schemes like net-metering. With this method, the virtual energy balance of the community is determined and
their individual bills are computed. Three variations of this method are analyzed based on the determination
of the PV generation shared allocation. Equal shares, shares relative to consumption and shares relative to
dwelling areas are considered. The three methods are deemed fair according to the following criteria of game
theory: individual rationality, equality and coalition stability. However, it is shown that if the equal shares
scheme evens out the payoffs more, the consumption-related schemes attributes higher payoffs to flats with
consumption flats and the area-related flat to small area flats.
