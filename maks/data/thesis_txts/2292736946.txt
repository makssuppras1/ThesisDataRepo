Summary
This study address the problem of how to manage the currency risk that appears when
an American investor buys an Exchange Trade Funds (ETF) that tracks the stock index
of a foreign country. The ETFs considered in this study are denominated in US dollars
but with a diﬀerent underlying currency. It is interesting to look into currency risk
because international diversiﬁcation is becoming more used and at the same time it is
getting easier to diversify a portfolio internationally through ETFs.
Currency risk management is studied through the use of modiﬁed versions of the
Conditional Value at Risk asset allocation model. Two models are developed: one that
combines the Universal Hedging Formula with a normal CVaR asset allocation model
and a holistic model based on CVaR that hedge each asset individually (Black 1989). For
the scenario generation a plain and weighted version of the bootstrapping algorithm are
used. The ETFs that used in this study are chosen to represent the biggest economies
of the world. The currency hedging is done with constructed currency forwards.
Backtesting of the models ﬁnds that it is possible to currency hedge intelligently to
minimize the currency risk and increase the return compared to diﬀerent benchmarks
such as normal unhedged CVaR models or hedged and unhedged One over N strategies.
Resumé
Dette studie undersøger problemstillingom om, hvordan man skal håndtere valuta risikoen
forbundet med, når en amerikansk investor handler med Exchange Trade Funds (ETF’er),
som tracker andre landes aktieindekser. ETF’erne, som bliver betragtet i dette studie,
er denomineret i US dollars men med en anden underliggende valuta. Det er interessant
at studere valuta risiko, da international diversiﬁcering bliver mere udbredt, samtidig
med at det bliver lettere at diversiﬁcere internationalt gennem ETF’er.
Valutarisikostyring undersøges ved brug af modiﬁcerede udgaver af Conditional Value
at Risk aktieallokeringsmodellen. De to modeller, som benyttes, er en der kombinerer
Blacks Universal Hedging Formula med en normal CVaR aktieallokeringsmodel og en
holistisk model baseret på CVaR som valuta kurssikrer hver aktie individuelt. Til sce-
nariegenerering bliver der brugt en simpel og vægtet udgave af bootstrappingalgoritmen.
ETF’erne, der bliver brugt i dette studie, er udvalgt, så de største økonomier i verden
er repræsenteret. Valutakurssikringen bliver udført med konstruerede valuta forwards.
Backtesting af modellerne viser, at det er muligt at valutakurssikre på en intelligent
måde, så kursrisikoen bliver minimeret, samtidig med at afkastet forøges. Modellerne
er bedre, når der bliver sammenlignet med strategier såsom normale CVaR modeller og
normale eller valutakurssikrede En over N strategier.

1.1
Background
In search for higher return and less risk investors try to diversify portfolios through
international investing. The performance gain and risk reduction from international di-
versiﬁcation of portfolio has been known for a long time and as investors want to diversify
their portfolio through investing internationally new problem arises (Levy et al. 1970).
One of the problems is what to do with the currency risk and how to hedged against it.
For example take Table 1.1 as a hypothetical example: although the underlying asset
of the Exchange Trade Funds (ETF) doubles its value the ETF’s value stays the same
because the EUR weakens compared to the USD. As the example shows, although it is
a quite extreme example, currency risk hedging is interesting to look into both for risk
minimization and return maximization.
1.1.1
Exchange Trade Funds
An easy way to diversify a portfolio is to invest trough ETFs. ETF’s are investment
products issued by investment companies such as iShares and can be bought on the stock
markets. Investing through ETF’s gives investors easy access to stock markets from all
over the world (Nasdaq 2015 and Commission et al. 2004, 2013).
ETF’s are funds that is managed by Investment companies and is mostly structured
as an openend fund. There are primarily two types of ETF’s passively and actively
managed funds. The passive ETF’s usually tracks indexes or sample of indexes while
the actively-managed ETF’s does not follow an index but instead tries to reach an
investment goal through investing in diﬀerent stocks, bonds and other assets.
Nearly all ETF’s are under the Investment Company Act of 1940 which is a set of
laws that regulates how investment companies operates and also give investors increased
protection. As mentioned earlier almost all investment companies operated under this

1.2.1
Motivation
Today an investor is able to diversify a portfolio over several countries by buying just one
ETF e.g. iShares MSCI ACWI ETF1. As easy it is to diversify a portfolio with ETFs just
as easy is it to overlook what new risk arises through these investment products. Over the
years ETFs has gotten more advanced by oﬀering for example USD denominated ETFs
where the underlying asset might come from one or several diﬀerent countries which
oﬀers an easy way to diversiﬁcation. But an investor might not be aware of the currency
risk they get exposed to through the underlying asset of the ETF. An example from the
stock markets where an investor will lose if not hedging is shown in Figure 1.2 where
the performance of the iShares MSCI Germany ETF (EWG) is compared to the hedged
version (iShares Currency Hedged MSCI Germany ETF – HEWG) and the EUR/USD
exchange rate is shown.
From the ﬁgure it is seen that over the period 7 July, 2014 to 5 December, 2014 EWG
has dropped 8.09%, HEWG has increased 2.11% and the exchange rate from EUR to
USD has fallen 9.56%. The diﬀerence between EWG and HEWG is 10.2% which almost
corresponds to the fall in exchange rate. The diﬀerence can be explained by the tracking
error of the ETF. From this real world example it is clear that currency hedging indeed
is an important topic to address as an investor but it might be hard as an investor to
be mindful of all the risk involved with an investment.
So when investing in foreign ETFs there will be currency risk which need to be addressed.
The ﬁrst one is the currency risk associated with the currency that the ETF is denom-
inated in. For example if an European investor buys the ETF IVV i.e. iShares Core
S&P 500 which is denominated in USD then the European investor has a currency risk
towards the USD with regards to the EUR. This risk is quite obvious since an investor

1.2.2
What has been done
In Beltratti et al. 1999 they discus whether or not it pays of to hedge currency risk and
they come up with the answer “it depends”. One of the reasons is that international
market return correlation increases if currency risks are hedged and thereby some of
the beneﬁts of diversifying a portfolio with international assets are minimized. In some
situations it pays of, but other times it does not which is probably not that surprising.
Also if an investor decides to hedge, the next important question is how much to hedge?
Should it be fully currency hedged in order to remove the currency risk completely at
the cost of potential proﬁts or rather a partial hedge in order to keep some volatility in
the portfolio? The next question is should each asset be hedged individually or should
all assets be hedged the same way?
In Perold et al. 1988 they ﬁnd that currency hedging is good for risk minimizing and
that “investor should look at currency hedging as having zero expected return” but that
is found by using a hedging ratio of 1 i.e. 100% currency hedged portfolio. This leads
to the question “is it possible to currency hedge in a smart way that also contributes
positively to the return?”. That question is partially answered in Topaloglou et al. 2002,
2004, 2008 where they investigate smart currency hedging by using models such as the
Conditional Value at Risk i.e. CVaR asset allocation model3. They ﬁnd that currency
hedging can be used for risk minimization through studying the returns from stock
markets and bonds from four countries. It could be interesting though to see if this is
applicable if stock markets from all over the world are used. Another thing about the
studies in Topaloglou et al. 2002, 2004, 2008 is that they in some of the studies use quite
advanced theoretical ﬁnancial products for currency hedging such as currency options
and quantos4 which might not be applicable to the real world. Therefore it is interesting
to see if it is possible to use currency hedging in a smart way where risk is minimized
and the return is increased at the same time for a more broad range of countries while
using simple hedging tools such as currency forwards.
1.2.3
What will be done
After an initial analysis of what could be interesting to look into several areas has
appeared.
The ﬁrst question is “does currency hedging pays oﬀ?”. Which will be tested through
the use of diﬀerent models and backtesting. For benchmarking several portfolio com-
binations will be used such as the One over N strategy, fully hedged CVaR portfolios,
unhedged portfolios etc.
The next question that will be looked into is “should currency hedging should be
passively or actively done?”. That is does it adds more value to reviese the hedging
ratio as the portfolio changes? This will be done by comparing fully hedged portfolios
with partial hedged portfolios.

strategies
In this chapter the base model, which will be the core of the diﬀerent models used for
constructing the portfolios, will be presented and discussed. The base model consist of
the scenario generation methods which will be two kinds of bootstrapping and the CVaR
model which will be the core of the selecting and the weighting of the diﬀerent ETFs.
Later, the diﬀerent hedging strategies will be presented and discussed. There are used
two diﬀerent strategies the dynamic hedging and the uniform hedging strategy.
2.1
VaR and CVaR
In this study the base model for asset allocation, will be the Conditional Value-at-
Risk model CVaR. This model allocates assets according to a given risk proﬁle. More
speciﬁcally the model minimize risk by minimizing the CVaR and at the same time
maximizing the return. The CVaR model is a reﬁned version of the Value-at-Risk VaR
model so ﬁrst the VaR model is explained.
VaR in short measures the maximum loss (ζ) at a given conﬁdence level (α) usually
(100α%) over a speciﬁc timespan. So if the conﬁdence level is 95% it means that in 95%
of the cases the loss will not exceed ζ (Duﬃe et al. 1997; Linsmeier et al. 2000; Zenios
2007). VaR is deﬁned as:
VaR(x, α) = min{ζ ∈R | Ψ(x, ζ) ≥α}
where Ψ(x, ζ) is the probability function for the return of asset x.
Through optimization models it is then possible to construct portfolios that mini-
mizes ζ for a given conﬁdence level and at the same time maximizes the expected return.
But one very important thing to notice about the VaR is that in the last 5% cases VaR
does not say anything about the size of the loss which can be quite substantial. Another
reason for choosing CVaR over VaR is that VaR is not a coherent risk measure (Artzner
et al. 1999). For being a coherent risk measure it has to be: monotonic, sub-additivity,
positive homogeneity and translation invariance. VaR is not sub-additivity that is that
the sum of the risk of two separate assets i.e. A and B is larger or equal of to a portfolio
containing asset A and B.
Risk (A + B) ≤Risk (A) + Risk (B)

which the concept of diversiﬁcation is based i.e. that holding several assets is less risky

It is here that CVaR is interesting since the CVaR measures the average size of

Where L(x, P l) is the loss of asset x for a given price P in scenario l.

The objective function is a modiﬁcation of the mean-variance eﬃcient portfolios
where λ is the investors risk proﬁle and has a value between zero and one. When λ is

zero and one would mean that the model would try to maximize the return while at the
same time minimizing the CVaR.

nonlinear programming model and because it is a linear model it is also a lot faster to
solve to optimum. Of course using LP models it limits the types of equations in the

2.1.2
Scenario Generation Methodologies
To generate scenarios for the CVaR model the bootstrapping approach will be used.
Bootstrapping has several qualities: it is a very simple way to generate scenarios from
historical data, does not require the data to have a speciﬁc distribution and it keeps the
correlation of the data (Bjerring 2012).
Bootstrap works by generating a set of scenarios by picking a random date of his-
torical asset returns from a range of historical dates – see Algorithm 1 for the simple
bootstrap algorithm. In this study the dates are picked from a range of data that is
three years old. There exist various versions of the bootstrapping model and in this
study there will be used two types the plain (simple) bootstrap (Algorithm 1) and the
weighted bootstrap (Algorithm 2). Another alternative is to use blockbootstrapping.
The good thing about blockboostraping is that it to some degree keeps the autocorrela-
tion of the original data. Blockbootstrapping does this by picking blocks of dates instead
of only single dates. Although blockbootstrapping is an good algorithm it will not be
implemented due to time limitations.
Plain bootstrapping
Plain or also known as simple bootstrapping is the most basic form of the diﬀerent
bootstrapping methods. See Algorithm 1 for the algorithm. The characteristic for the
plain version is that the possibility for picking a speciﬁc date from a historical range of
dates is the same for all dates. The plain bootstrap incorporates the risk and possible
proﬁts for the whole historical time period considered - so it is not inﬂuenced so much
for example by a sudden crisis in the recent past. But since the sample size from where
the dates are picked is ﬁnite (in this study three years) the model is considering only
that range of dates and in that way it still has some time dependency.
The way plain bootstrapping works means that if an investor has no stance to if recent
assets returns are more relevant than old asset returns, this is the scenario generation
model to use. Also if an investor believes that old assets returns are just as import as
recent data this is also the model to use.
In this study the input data for the bootstrap is the last three years weekly returns
(the sample size) of the selected ETFs (see Chapter 3 for which ETFs) and then four
weeks are randomly picked and put together to simulate the returns of a whole month.
Then the sample size is move four weeks forward so the oldest four weeks is deleted from
the input data and the four weeks that has just gone is added to the sample size. Then
four new weeks is picked and so forth – see Algorithm 1 for more details.

2.2.2
A simple view on currency hedging
A simple way to look at currency hedging is to use a hedging strategy for lowering
the total volatility of the portfolio. this can be done by minimizing the currency risk
exposure by looking at the correlation between a foreign market’s return and exchange
rate to determine when and how to hedge against currency risk. If the market return
and exchange rate are negatively correlated i.e. the foreign currency lose value when
market rises in value, then it is possible to lower portfolio volatility by under-hedging
the portfolio i.e. buying more of the foreign currency. If there is no correlation between
the markets return and exchange rate then the volatility of the portfolio can be lowered
by fully hedging the portfolio against the currency risk. If there is a positive correlation
between the market’s return and exchange rate then the volatility can be lowered by
over-hedging the portfolio (going short in the foreign currency in excess of a fully hedge
portfolio) (Campbell et al. 2007).
For simplicity over-hedging i.e. a hedging ratio larger than one h ≤1 and under-
hedging i.e. a hedging ratio lower than zero h ≥0, is not allowed since the scope of this
study is to look at how to protect a portfolio against currency risk optimal and not how
to speculate with currency.
Now the goal is not only to lower the volatility but also to increase the return of
the portfolio at the same time through currency hedging. For this there are chosen two

In Black 1989 the Universal Hedging Formula is proposed where it is argued that all

As it can be observed the formula requires three inputs:
1. µm, the average across investors of the expected excess return on the world mar-

countries.
2. σm, the average across investors of the volatility of the world market portfolio.
3. σe, the average exchange rate volatility across all pairs of countries.
The values for the formula can then be estimated from historical data and modiﬁed
by investors expectation for the market. The Universal Hedging Formula diﬀers form
the Selective Hedging Model in a least two important areas. The Universal Hedging
Formula is not dynamic – the hedging ratio is determined before the asset allocation
and furthermore it is the same hedging ratio for all markets where in the dynamic model
every market has it own hedging ratio.
µm is estimated by taking the mean monthly return over a speciﬁc time period of
MSCI ACWI world index which contains equities from 46 markets2 and then subtracting
the mean yield (over the same time period) of a 3 month US government treasury
security3. The time period that is used for measuring the mean over is respectively one,
two and three years. σm is estimated by taking the variance of monthly return of the
chosen time period. σe is estimated by taking the variance over the same time period of
the exchange rate across all the currencies used in this study.
A special thing about this formula is that it is the same hedging ratio no matter
what the base currency is. Black argues that the reason it is the same ratio is that if
Investor A (in country A) wants to hedge against currency risk in country B there must
be an investor (Investor B) in country B willing to take the risk and hedge (to increase
expected return) in country A. So one of them will try to minimize risk and the other will
try to increase expected return and they will hedge the same proportion of their stock
holdings. Also it is the same hedging ratio for all the currencies in the portfolio which
is a major diﬀerence to the dynamic model where each currency is hedged diﬀerently.

should not be to positively correlated – for example investigating currency risk

positively correlated. When choosing the ETFs from the diﬀerent continents the
biggest economies should be represented since they have the largest impact on the
regions.

possibly aﬀect the data of the ETFs. It was decided that around 100,000 in volume
pr. trading was the limit and that no more than 10% of the trading days pr. ETF
was allowed under this limit.
• The last criteria was that the ETFs should only have one underlying currency
which was purely for simplicity.

CHAPTER 4
Analysis
In this chapter the results from the simulations will be examined and analyzed. First
there will be an introduction to the diﬀerent kinds of simulations that have been run and
all the combinations. Then the ratio used for currency hedging in the uniform model is
reviewed and then the results from the simulations will be analyzed.
All simulations made with the uniform model when written in short form will have
an U in front. Example Unihedged Plain 1 Years 250 Scenarios – UP 1Y 250.
All simulations made with the dynamic model will be written in short as in the
following example: Dynamically Plain Hedged 250 Scenarios – PH 250.
All the density plots in this chapter has been focused on the part where their is a
diﬀerence between the curves so part of some of the right tail has been cut oﬀbut all
the density plots can be seen in Appendix E in full length. The density plots has been
made in R with Gaussian Smoothing Kernel function. Because of the smoothing the

4.1
Overview of the simulations
As mentioned in Chapter 2, two models were chosen to see how hedging impacted the
performance of a portfolio.
There was the uniform model in Section 2.2.3 and the
dynamic model in Section 2.2.2. For the uniform model there is 18 diﬀerent combinations
– the diﬀerent kinds of combinations can be seen in Figure 4.1(a). For the dynamic model
there is also 18 diﬀerent combinations – the diﬀerent kinds of combinations can be seen
in Figure 4.1(b).
The models in Figure 4.1 are to be read as ﬁrst either uniform or dynamic hedging
is chosen. If the uniform model has been chosen it needs to be decided if it should
be based on plain or weighted bootstrapping and how many scenarios i.e. 250, 1,000
or 10,000 to generate in the bootstrapping. Also the numbers of years i.e. one, two or
three years to base the hedging ratio on should be decided. If the dynamic hedging has
been chosen then the next step again is to decide if it should be based on the plain or
weighted bootstrapping and how many scenarios i.e. 250, 1,000 or 10,000 to generate in
the bootstrapping and then last if it should use fully hedged ETFs, unhedged ETFs or
a mix of the former two.

There were also made portfolios based on the One over N strategy. That is where
all assets have the same weight and then after each month, which is the revision period
of the portfolio, the portfolio is rebalanced after the last month’s returns have been
calculated. This makes sure that at the start of each month i.e. period all the assets in
the One over N portfolio has the same weight.
It should be noted that all the models included the ETF from USA. Since it is not
possible to hedge this ETF since it is from an american investor’s point of view this ETF
has the same performance for all models.
The simulations called something with Hedged are only fully hedged ETFs. The ones
called something with Mixed are where the model decides on the hedging ratio. If in
doubt see Appendix A for a description.
4.1.1
Uniform hedging ratios
The hedging values that was used in the uniform hedging model Section 2.2.3 can be
seen in Figure 4.2. The two diﬀerent graphs are when the hedging ratio is calculated
with the use of one and three years of data. The graph for two years is not shown
since it is not used in the chosen simulations. Due to the very low interest rate and
the negative return on the world index the hedging ratio from (2.15) was sometimes
negative. Also sometimes the equation returned values larger than one. But as stated
earlier in Section 2.2.2 the objective of this study was to investigate currency hedging
and not currency speculation so therefore values smaller than zero are not allowed so
therefore the lower limit was set to zero. Likewise values larger than one was also not
allowed so the upper limit was set to one.
During the ﬁnancial crisis it can be seen that the hedging ratio is one until 2010-2012.
That is until one year after the end of the ﬁnancial crisis and the model had time to
adjust for it. Then after the crisis it takes a value between zero and one. It is also
especially in this period that the unihedged portfolios gains a lot of value compared
to the other types of simulations Figure 4.4 – but that will be discussed later in this
chapter.
From Figure 4.2 the eﬀect of basing the ratio on one or three years of data can be
seen. It can be seen in the way that the one year based i.e. blue one is the ﬁrst to go
under a hedging ratio of one and then the hedging ratio based on three years i.e. black.
Another interesting thing is that the standard deviations for a ratio based on one year
and three years are around the same size 0.184 and 0.189 respectively while the standard
deviation for the ratio based on two years is 0.339. Normally it would be expected that
the ratio based on the largest sample should be more stable. Therefore the standard
deviation for the ratio based on three years should be the lowest, then the one based on
two years and last the one based on one year. The argument for this is: when moving the
sample size one month forward, it will have the smallest impact on the largest sample
size e.g. if one month is changed in a sample size of 24 i.e. two years, it should have less
impact than when one month is changed in a sample size of 12 i.e. one year.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In the problem statement there was three questions that was going to be the foundations
of this study in currency risk management and will now be answered.
5.1
Does currency hedging pays oﬀ?
From the analysis it was found that through currency hedging it was possible to add extra
value to a portfolio in most of the cases. For example if comparing the two simulations
Unihedged Plain 1 Year 250 Scenarios and Dynamically Plain Hedged 250 Scenarios to
the Dynamically Plain Unhedged 1,000 Scenarios simulation.
For the simulations based on the weighted scenarios it was also seen that the currency
hedged simulations outperformed the unhedged simulations and One over N strategies.
So in terms of performance of the hedged simulations against the unhedged simula-
tions currency hedging improved the performance in nearly all the simulations.
Also if comparing with the S&P 500 to see if it is even worth considering the in-
ternational diversiﬁcation and therefore interesting to look at currency risk hedging it
was seen that through international diversiﬁcation it is possible to outperform S&P 500
while at the same time lowering the standard deviation and max draw down.
It was also seen that through the use of Blacks Universal hedging formula combined
with the CVaR asset allocation it was possible to make a model that optimized the
hedging ratio and asset allocation correctly. That is that the model increased the hedging
ratio when the underlying currency of the ETFs was weakened compared to the US dollar
and decreased the hedging ratio when the underlying currency of the ETFs was getting
stronger compared to the US dollar.
5.2
Should hedging be passive or active?
There was no clear indication of whether a constant hedging i.e. passive or a variable
hedging ratio i.e. active was best. Although it looks like that a varying hedging ratio
might be better as the Unihedged simulations was performing better than the constant
fully hedged simulations.
5.3
Should assets be hedged individually?
From the results it was clear that hedging assets individually with the CVaR model
is not better than using a fully hedged or uniformly hedged portfolio where the asset

allocation is still done with the CVaR model. So at least in this study it was not possible
to come up with a model that can hedged assets individually better than hedging all the
assets with the same ratio.
5.4
Plain versus weighted scenarios
In the study it was also seen that using the more advanced scenario generation method
Weighted Bootstrapping did not add any extra value to the simulations. Although it
was design to react quicker to recent market movements.
So to sum up the most important conclusion it was found that it is possible to use
currency risk hedging to improve the return of a portfolio and for recommendation for
what could be looked into in the future see Chapter 6.

CHAPTER 6
Future work
Through out this study several new areas for further research was discovered.
It was seen that a more extensive study into individually hedging of assets could be
interesting since it was not possible in this study to ﬁnd a model that could hedg indi-
vidually and outperform a model with a uniform hedging ratio. This could be combined
with a study in currency hedging based on the correlation of market performance vs. the
exchange rate. This was brieﬂy mentioned in Section 2.2.2.
Another thing that could be interesting was to look into if it is possible to currency
hedge by using only a few major currencies since some currencies tends to be correlated.
It could also be interesting to look into if it is possible to currency hedge on the basis
of regime shifts in market trends. That is if it is possible to identify a regime shift in
the market and then react on it.
Lastly it could be interesting to see if it is possible to make a high performing portfolio
by using sector indexes combined with currency hedging. One problem though, is that
it gets more complicated since sector indexes could easily contain several underlying
currencies which makes the currency hedging a lot more complex.

APPENDIX B
UCITS
In 1985 the European Union (EU) took the ﬁrst step towards making a uniform market
for investment funds with the goal to protect investors and make in easier for funds to
operate through out the EU (Council 1985) - these funds where called Undertakings
for the collective investment in transferable securities (UCITS). UCITS is investment
funds working under a legal framework composed of several EU directives. By being
a registered as a UCIST found there is several beneﬁts such as being able to market
and sell in all the member countries of the European Union. Although an UCITS fund
comply with the EU directives it also has to comply with the speciﬁc countries laws that
it operates in. That is because every country in the EU is allowed to implement stricter
laws with regards to advertising and marketing of a UCITS.
Some key facts about the UCITS (Council 2009):
• A UCITS is only allowed to invest in transferable securities and money market
instruments.
• A UCITS is not allowed to acquire either precious metals or certiﬁcates represent-
ing them.
• A UCITS may in generally not invest more than 10 % of its assets in transferable
securities or money market instruments issued by the same body (index-based
ﬁnancial derivative instruments are not exempt from this rule).
• A UCITS is no allowed to short sell.
• It was only in 2001 that UCITS fund’s were allowed to track indexes.
