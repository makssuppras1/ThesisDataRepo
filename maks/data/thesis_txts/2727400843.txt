Abstract 
The main goal of this MSc Thesis was to create a complete and robust model for a photobioreactor 
(PBR) that cultivates microalgae under different conditions. A first model of a continuously stirred 
tank reactor (CSTR) has been built, and it has been used to create a second model for a tubular 
PBR. The models predict the evolution in time of biomass, lipid, stored nitrogen and substrates 
concentrations given the inlet flow rate, the species concentrations at the inlet, the geometry of 
the reactor, the incident light irradiance and the species concentration at the start of the operation. 
The CSTR is composed by six mass balance differential equations that account for species 
concentrations and volume variations, three constitutive equations that compute the kinetic rate 
expressions for cell growth, lipid production and nitrogen uptake, and four algebraic equations 
that relate model variables. Finally, the PBR model also accounts for a recycling flow rate that 
recirculates the product at the end of the reactor until the start for obtaining higher conversion. 
Simulations have been carried out in Python by solving numerically the system of equations and 
plotting the results under different conditions. The model describes well the observed phenomena 
in experiments and provides a good description of how each parameter influences the outlet of a 
bioreactor. Sensibility analysis have been done for each of the input parameters of the model to 
check its validity and robustness. The CSTR model is very complete and can provide an accurate 
representation of the variables’ dependence on the input parameters. The PBR model presents 
a clear representation of the behaviour in a tubular reactor and role of recycling flows in chemical 
reactors. A deeper analysis in fluid dynamics should be assessed to improve the model in future 
work to better describe the phenomena inside and predict with higher accuracy the product 
concentrations at the outlet. 
One of the main applications of the model is to predict the working conditions to maximize lipid 
productivity, which has shown to be important in many applications in industry. At the same time, 
the provided model gathers many phenomena presented in literature until now and improvements 
in rate equations can be implemented without changing the main mass balances. Therefore, it is 
a good starting point for future research to build an even more complete and robust PBR 
mathematical model.

1. Introduction 
In the recent years, microalgae have gained a lot of popularity due to their astonishing metabolic 
behaviour and the wide variety of potential applications in industry. They have been widely studied 
because of their possibility to hold a high lipid content when cultivated under certain conditions 
and their use for biofuels production. One of their key aspects is that they can grow in many 
different environmental conditions and tune their biomass composition depending on this. Many 
models and mathematical relations have been proposed relating microalgae cell growth, lipid 
production and capacity for nitrogen storage with the environmental conditions, such as light 
intensity, nutrients availability, autotrophic or heterotrophic metabolism, temperature or pH. These 
models have normally been studied separately and have never been presented together in a solid 
model for a microalgae bioreactor. 
Consequently, this MSc Thesis aims to create a complete and robust model for describing the 
behaviour of a microalgae bioreactor, which can predict the dynamic evolution of the outlet 
depending on the inlet and environmental conditions. The model should gather all phenomena 
described in literature for microalgae cultivation and their corresponding lipid production and 
nitrogen storage. It should predict the biomass, lipid and stored nitrogen concentration over time 
by consider parameters such as the substrates concentrations, the reactor inlet and outlet flow, 
light intensity, initial concentrations of the cells after inoculation and the geometry of the reactor. 
An additional goal of this MSc Thesis is to test the model with different case studies to check its 
validity in different scenarios and conditions. The final objective is to create a model that can 
describe the Jumbo® photobioreactor from Synoxis Algae. 
The importance of this MSc Thesis project relies on the fact that no robust models on microalgae 
photobioreactor can be found in literature. Modelling is a mathematical representation of reality 
that is highly useful for making predictions without doing real experiments. The main advantage 
of the bioreactor model is that it could predict the behaviour of microalgae growth in many 
conditions and find those at which the lipid or protein production can be optimised. The model 
would enable the user to tune the reactor conditions by predicting the most optimal output for the 
desired application. Another feature of the model that is of paramount importance is that it makes 
it easy to scale up the results from the laboratory into industry-scale calculations, which can save 
up research time when implementing a process in industry. 
1.1 Objectives 
The main objective of this MSc Thesis is to create a complete mathematical model that describes 
the behaviour of a microalgae bioreactor in different conditions by deriving it from mass balances 
and current models in literature. 
In addition to the main objective, there are several specific objectives to be accomplished in this 
study: 
• 
Create a model that predicts the behaviour of the outlet of the bioreactor based on the 
following parameters: inlet flow, outlet flow, CO2 concentration, glycine concentration,

•

1.2 Thesis outline 
The present thesis is divided in the following sections: 
1. Introduction. The current problem with modelling in microalgae bioreactors is presented, 
together with the project scope, motivation, main objectives and the importance of it. 
2. Theoretical framework. This section presents the background information for the use of 
microalgae in industry and their implementation in photobioreactors. The topics assessed 
in this section are the biomolecular composition of microalgae, their metabolism, growth 
phases and common bioreactors used in industry. 
3. Relevant phenomena in photobioreactors in models. It describes the most reported 
rate models for microalgae growth, lipid production and nitrogen uptake. Mathematical 
relations are assessed between these three phenomena with substrates concentrations, 
light intensity, reactor geometry. 
4. Model of a continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) for microalgae. A model is 
created from mass balances together with the rate expressions described in section 3. 
The model will contain balance, constitutive and algebraic equations to describe the 
behaviour of the microalgae system in a CSTR. 
5. Model of a photobioreactor (PBR). The model from CSTR is used to construct the 
photobioreactor model to describe the PBR from Synoxis Algae. 
6. Case studies: Results and discussion. Three case studies are proposed to evaluate 
the validity of the model and predict the outlet of CSTR and PBR under different 
conditions. The evaluation will include sensitivity analysis of different input parameters, 
as well as description of the results to evaluate the optimal conditions to maximize lipid 
production. Finally, further research steps will be proposed to continue with the obtained 
model and results. 
7. Conclusions and future work. This section summarizes the discussion of the project 
results and proposes future research lines to continue with the current advances of the 
project.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Microalgae and applications in industry 
 
Microalgae are unicellular photosynthetic organisms that have a simple cellular structure 
compared to other plant cells. They can be both eukaryotic protists and prokaryotic cyanobacteria 
cells [1]. Due to their simple structure and easiness to duplicate it, they can grow rapidly compared 
to other microorganisms [2]. They can be found in a wide range of aquatic environments, such as 
freshwater, saltwater or even in lands with moist environments. They are often also called 
phytoplankton, especially when referring to their importance in the food chain. 
They can produce a wide source of valuable macromolecules with high relevance in industry [3]. 
The reason why they have gained importance in industry recently is that they can either produce 
lipids, proteins, or carbohydrates. Interesting examples of these could be carotenoids, long-chain 
polyunsaturated fatty acids and phycocolloids. If these microorganisms are used in bioreactors, 
a wide range of valuable heterologous proteins can be obtained. Currently, there are many 
available options of bacterial and yeast fermentation used for production of most of the 
commercially available heterologous proteins [4], [5]. One of the reasons why they are gaining so 
much importance nowadays is that many of them are resistant to salty environments as they are 
found in the saltwater. This halotolerance can be crucial to study protein production in this type 
of media and in environments where other species wouldn’t survive [6] 
2.1.1 Biomolecules and their synthesis 
 
The three main biomolecule groups (proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates) are synthetized by 
common metabolic pathways and can be found practically in all microalgae strains. However, 
their respective synthesis can be enhanced or inhibited by external environmental factors such 
as temperature, light intensity, nutrients type of biomolecule and nutrients’ concentration in feed. 
These can normally be tuned according to the intended application to maximize the output 
products of interest. 
There can be many operational and culture parameters that can influence on the enhanced 
biomolecule group that is produced by microalgae’s metabolism. In addition, it is important to 
choose the adequate microalgae stream for each application, in case protein, lipid or 
carbohydrate production is preferred [7], [8]. Microalgae in general are considered quite primitive 
species, which means that their DNA is quite simple and sometimes easier to manipulate. 
Consequently, genetic modifications can also be part of the industrial processes with microalgae. 
Another important factor is that they have very simple growing requirements, such as light, sugars 
and CO2 as carbon source, nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P) and finally potassium (K) on some 
occasions [9]. This makes microalgae the focus of many scientists’ attention since a wide variety 
of macromolecules can be obtained with very limited resources. 
Proteins 
Eukaryotic microalgae can provide good alternatives to the current microorganisms used for 
protein production in industrial bioreactors and there are many studies that support it [3]. One of 
the most outstanding protein production applications is antibody production. The efficient 
production of complex antibodies still remains a problem when it comes to industrial scale.

However, microalgae have shown efficient expression of large single-chain antibodies in their 
chloroplast, in which high concentrations of these proteins can be achieved [10]. Other types of 
proteins that could be produced would be insecticidal proteins or virus proteins [10], [11]. In this 
last example, future vaccine applications could arise from this, and large-scale production of 
vaccines could be implemented by using microalgae bioreactors. Further research needs to be 
done on how to overcome technical aspects and viability of this industrial scale production [6]. 
The concentration of proteins in microalgae depends a lot on the species, although in many cases 
it can be the majoritarian component of their dry matter [12], [13]. There are many studies that 
show that proteins with an origin from algae can have a higher quality for applications that those 
compared to others coming from conventional plant cells. Indeed, the protein content in 
microalgae tends to be higher, and sometimes even double, compared to conventional protein 
sources, such as egg, soybean, wheat, bovine milk and meat [14]. However, the major problem 
is that robust and economically viable processes are missing to extract these proteins from 
microalgae to separate and purify them [14] Several disruption methods have been proposed to 
extract proteins from microalgae. The most effective ones have been found to be high-pressure 
cell disruption, chemical treatment, ultrasonication and manual grinding [15]. However, more 
efforts need to be made in order to scale-up all these procedures to obtain large quantities of 
proteins from microalgae in industrial scale. Some of the techniques that would be easier to scale-
up could be microwave disruption, high-pressure homogenizer, and bead mill. Other procedures 
might be more difficult to scale up, such as high-speed homogenizer or enzymatic disruption, or 
very costly, such as enzymatic disruption itself or chemical reagent disruption [14]. 
Lipids 
Lipids is the most studied biomolecule group produced by microalgae. In certain conditions, it can 
take up to 60-70% of the dry cell weight [9]. Therefore, very high yields can be obtained when 
extracting lipid molecules from microalgae. Also, it needs to be considered as well that these 
microorganisms grow at very high rates. Given these two factors, some studies show that 
microalgae can be an efficient alternative for biodiesel production [16]. 
Lipid productivity can depend on many factors. Apart from genetic characteristics of specific 
microalgae strains that could lead to them producing higher quantities of lipids, there are many 
external factors that can influence. Fatty acids and triacylglycerol accumulation can greatly 
depend on salinity, medium pH, temperature, and light intensity as well as enhanced when there 
is nutrient starvation [17]. 
Nutrient starvation in eukaryotic microalgae is the most well-known phenomena that enhance 
high lipid accumulation. It has been shown that nitrogen starvation presents strong indication to 
be the one that has a highest impact on the lipid productivity [18], [19]. It has also been shown in 
medium with decreasing phosphate availability, in which different kinds of lipids also increased 
concentration [20]. While in nitrogen deprived conditions, it is widely shown that there are some 
strains that decrease their lipid productivity when phosphorous is the removed nutrient, such as 
N. atomus and Tetraselmis sp. [21]. It depends on the microalgae strain, but it has been 
demonstrated experimentally for many of them [17]. This phenomenon could be explained by the 
fact that cells tend to store energy in lipid molecules when they undergo starvation conditions. On 
normal nutrient concentrations, microalgae would use them to produce biomass on normal rates 
through photosynthesis. However, the lipid storage can be seen as a survival mechanism when 
these conditions can’t be achieved. In addition to all this, nitrogen starvation can also lead to 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
18 
 
change the ratios between concentrations of different kinds of lipids, such as transitions between 
free fatty acids to formation of triacylglycerol [22]. 
In contrast, cyanobacteria react oppositely to eukaryotic microalgae in starvation conditions. In 
Anacystis nidulans, Microcystis aeruginosa, Oscillatoria rubescens, and Spirulina platensis, some 
fatty acids such as C18:2 decrease in concentration, while others like C16:0 and C18:1 increase 
concentration when nitrogen is missing in normal concentrations in medium [23]. Even in nitrogen 
deprivation conditions, it has also been shown that in some marine cyanobacteria, the lipid and 
fatty acids synthesis is reduced [24]. Therefore, cyanobacteria can have a very different medium-
dependence than other eukaryotic microalgae. 
Temperature is also a highly determinant factor for fatty acid composition in microalgae. To start 
with, temperature can have a high impact on the kind of lipid that is produced. At low temperature, 
fatty acid unsaturation is enhanced, while at high temperatures, the production of saturated fatty 
acids will prevail [25]. In terms of the relation between biomolecules ratio (protein, lipids and 
carbohydrates) and temperature, there is no consistent model that can predict it for all species 
[26]. Some species enhance lipid and carbohydrate production at high temperature and significant 
decrease in protein production [27]. Nevertheless, this is not a general rule that can be applied to 
all microalgae species since there are other species where the trend is completely reversed [28]. 
Light intensity has also a remarkable impact on the lipid synthesis, composition, and 
photosynthetic performance [29], [30]. Light is one of the most important sources to conduct 
photosynthesis in microalgae. However, the effect that it has over the metabolism is not only an 
on/off switch mechanism, but it also regulates lipid synthesis. Some studies with Nannochropsis 
sp. have shown that light can enhance the production of certain types of lipids according to its 
intensity [31]. For example, a higher production of fatty acids is achieved when the light intensity 
is higher. This has been observed with both saturated and unsaturated fatty acids as a general 
trend, and differences were mostly found out according to the length of the fatty acid chains. Other 
studies show that the proportion between saturated and unsaturated fatty acids can vary 
depending on light [32]. For light-limited conditions, the concentration of saturated ones would 
remain quite stable but at saturating light conditions, the saturated or even mono-unsaturated 
fatty acids could prevail on concentration over highly more unsaturated ones. In addition, a low 
light intensity enhances the synthesis of polar lipids, such as phospholipids of the membranes, 
normally associated to chloroplast’s activity [33]. In contrast, at higher light intensity, normal 
storage nonpolar lipids are synthesised. This can result in a survival mechanism to maximize light 
exploitation in occasions where light is not supplied constantly. Finally, it is also important to 
mention that microalgae growth can also be scarcely observed in dark conditions, meaning with 
no light at all. A baseline yield conversion from substrate to biomass is measured in dark 
conditions although it is associated to the lowest possible productivity values for these species 
[34]. In general, however, the total growth is normally zero under these conditions [32].  This gives 
an overview of how complex is lipid synthesis in microalgae since many different kinds of lipids 
are synthesised and the ratios between their concentrations can easily vary just with changing 
light intensity. 
Another important factor regarding lipid synthesis is the CO2 availability, as it is another source of 
the photosynthetic metabolism and the carbon source to synthetize all biomolecules. Many 
studies have shown that, in contrast to what common impression would lead to, higher CO2 
concentration doesn’t necessarily imply a higher lipid synthesis [35]. Indeed, low concentration of 
CO2 in the aeration of the bioreactor leads to higher lipid and biomass productivity. It is obvious 
that this will depend on the different microalgae strains, since there might be others that would 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19 
need a higher C concentration to synthetize their biomolecules [36]. However, this can show that 
the carbon source is not normally a limiting factor at low concentrations but precisely the opposite. 
This is of course once a threshold value has been reached, since at very low concentrations, 
getting more C source would of course induce a higher lipid productivity. After reaching this 
threshold conditions that each strain could have, the behaviour might be practically the same [22]. 
Although there is a general idea that lipid accumulation in cells must be enhanced when extracting 
lipids from cultures, a high lipid accumulation has been found to induce a decrease in total 
biomass productivity [37]. In general, microalgae start lipid accumulation mechanisms when there 
is nitrogen starvation or when N concentration becomes the limiting factor [9]. Also, it has been 
also found that in N-starvation conditions, lipid content increases at the expense of other 
biomolecules, mainly proteins. 
Carbohydrates 
Carbohydrates are produced in microalgae during photosynthesis as in many other plant cells. 
The photosynthesis uses CO2 as a C source and synthetize small sugar molecules such as 
glucose. In order to store these sugars, starch is also typically synthetized and stored in plastids. 
Photosynthesis consists of two primary reactions: the light reactions and the dark reactions. 
During the light reactions, pigments such as chlorophyll absorb solar energy, which then splits 
water into protons, electrons, and oxygen. These electrons and protons are subsequently utilized 
to produce energy carriers such as NADPH and ATP that satisfy the organism's metabolic 
requirements. Conversely, during the dark reactions, the Calvin cycle facilitates the conversion of 
carbon dioxide into carbohydrates, such as glucose, through triose phosphate molecules utilizing 
energy derived from NADPH and ATP [38]. 
In general, however, microalgae have low carbohydrates concentration compared to other 
microorganisms, which can lead to certain constraints when trying to use them for applications 
such as biodiesel [38]. The carbohydrates in microalgae are mainly found as starch in plastids as 
an energy reserve source, or as main components of the cell wall (such as pectin, cellulose, and 
sulphated polysaccharides), which can have either an inner or an outer layer [39]. The 
composition of the outer cell is normally polysaccharides, such as pectin, agar or alginic acid [39], 
[40]. In addition, the inner cell is normally made of cellulose and other additional components 
such as hemicellulose and glycoprotein [40], [41]. For many of the common microalgae strains, 
glucose polymers produced via cellulose or starch are the predominant components in cell walls 
and can be extracted with several techniques [42]. 
As similar to lipids, carbohydrates synthesis is also stimulated in nutrient limiting conditions, 
especially with nitrogen, since it is thought to shift the synthetic metabolism from proteins to these 
other two biomolecule groups [41]. In general, when carbohydrate production is stimulated is 
intended for industrial applications of biomass production and bioenergy obtention from 
bioethanol or biomethanol [43], [44]. 
When increasing carbohydrate concentration in microalgae, these microorganisms become a 
focus of attention for bioethanol production [45]. Since they have a very fast carbon fixing 
mechanism and their cellulose-based walls, microalgae can be very promising candidates for 
feedstock in bioethanol. Starch and similar kinds of cell wall polysaccharides can be easily 
converted into fermentable sugars for bioethanol production.

and robust models that can describe microalgae behaviour in different conditions. The parameters 
that could lead to enhance or inhibit the production of any of these biomolecules will be described 
along this project and included in the mathematical model. 
2.1.2 Autotrophy, heterotrophy and mixotrophy 
 
Microalgae can use different metabolic pathways to obtain the energy and nutrients needed for 
growth. These metabolic routes are usually classified according to their inlet substrates into 
autotrophic metabolism or heterotrophic metabolism. When both are combined at the same time, 
it is said that they conduct a mixotrophic metabolism. In general, the choice of metabolic pathway 
in microalgae depends on various factors, such as the species, culture conditions, and the 
availability of nutrients and light. Nowadays, photoautotrophic production in industrial scale is 
preferred by its economic feasibility, low cost, and wide range of conditions in which this type of 
metabolism is enhanced [9]. They are specially grown in tubular or flat-plate reactors when 
photoautotrophic metabolism wants to be enhanced, especially to maximize the light availability. 
In contrast, heterotrophic metabolism can be normally carried in open pounds since the 
operational cost is much lower and no light is needed in this case. Several research projects have 
come up with some of the conditions in which each type of metabolism is favored. 
Autotrophic (or photoautotrophic) microalgae produce their own organic molecules by carbon 
fixation from inorganic carbon sources, which is usually CO2, through photosynthesis. The CO2 
can be taken directly from the atmosphere or from gas industries, and even from dissolved 
carbonates in aqueous media [46]. Autotrophic microalgae use light as an energy source, and do 
not require any external source of sugar or lipid as carbon source, since they synthetize them by 
themselves. However, their internal production is essential for their own growth. This implies that 
usually the limiting factor for cell growth in these conditions is light availability [47]. All in all, 
microalgae can be cultivated in a medium containing only inorganic nutrients, such as nitrate and 
phosphate [48]. They need these other nutrients to form biomolecules, such as proteins, DNA, 
RNA, or lipids. 
Heterotrophic metabolism microalgae require an external source of organic carbon to grow. They 
are capable of using a wide range of organic compounds as a carbon source, such as sugars, 
amino acids, or organic acids, and they decompose these substrates to obtain energy for their 
growth. Heterotrophic microalgae do not require light for growth and can be cultured in dark 
conditions. The substrate sources are normally small organic molecules, such as acetate, 
glucose, or similar small sugars [9], [49]. The main advantage of this metabolic alternative is that 
it doesn’t require the light source, which is the complicated part of the reactor set-up. Indeed, 
heterotrophic microalgae are much simpler for scale-up possibilities since the reactors can have 
smaller surface-to-volume ratios [50]. Therefore, options such as stirred tank fermentors or open-
pond reactors can be chosen with this purpose. Given that light is not needed, much higher cell 
densities are achieved in these reactors in comparison to cultures of autotrophic microalgae [51]. 
Although it may seem that heterotrophic cultivation of microalgae is a cheaper solution, they still 
need an organic carbon source as feed for their metabolic activity, which are usually produced by 
photosynthesis in crop plants, and makes the overall process less efficient [52]. 
There are some microalgal strains such as Chlorella protothecoides, that can be grown either 
autotrophically or heterotrophically, according to the environmental conditions [53]. Indeed, these 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21 
species have been suggested as good candidates for oil production since they can have high 
productivity without light. In general, this type of metabolism is used for several applications with 
industrial interest. One of the advantages of bioreactors made for microorganisms with 
heterotrophic growth is that cleaning and sterilization is easier and prevents contamination of 
other species [54]. This will be analyzed more carefully in the section about photobioreactors. 
Finally, when mixotrophic metabolism is shown in microalgae, they use both light and organic 
carbon sources for energy and growth. Mixotrophic microalgae can have a competitive advantage 
in environments with fluctuating nutrient or light conditions, since they have the flexibility to use 
different metabolic pathways depending on the availability of resources [55]. Normally, the limiting 
growth factor is not strictly one in particular, since either light or organic molecules can support 
metabolism and growth [49], [55]. Another advantage is that these microorganisms reduce the 
impact of photoinhibition, which means that they can keep similar productivity levels in wide 
ranges of conditions [9]. It is important to highlight that each species can have a maximum specific 
growth rate in each of these three metabolic conditions. However, the maximum specific growth 
rate in mixotrophic conditions doesn’t coincide with the summation of the maximum individual 
values for autotrophic and heterotrophic [56]. On some occasions, it is shown that for some 
species it is not possible to use 100% both metabolic routes at the same time, since the conditions 
at which each one is enhanced are very different. 
For Chlorella vulgaris, the mixotrophic specific growth rate is higher than each of the other two. 
However, for some species like Spirulina platensis or Scenedesmus acutus, the autotrophic 
growth rate is even higher than the mixotrophic one [56], [57]. Results show in general that in 
autotrophic conditions, microalgae are much more effective and also require low substrate supply, 
which makes them very interesting for industrial applications. Bioreactors designed for efficient 
mixotrophic cultivation require usually sterile condition to prevent contamination, a high light 
supply to enhance photoautotrophic metabolism and good mass transfer characteristics such as 
mixing for efficient heterotrophic metabolism [54]. All in all, it is rather expensive to design a 
bioreactor with all these conditions at the same time.

2.1.4 Growth phases of microalgae 
The cell growth phases of any kind of microorganism have been widely studied for further 
applications in the biotechnology industry. In batch cultures, many species present a practically 
the same shape for the growth diagram and react similarly to nutrient accessibility or starvation 
conditions. In Figure 2, the typical cell growth profile over time is plotted and the different growth 
phases can be appreciated [56]. 
 
 
Figure 2. Growth curve for microorganism in a batch system. a) From Pauline M Doran [58] b) From E. 
Lee et al. [64], it shows nutrient concentration in dotted line and cell concentration in full line 
 
The plot in Figure 2 assumes a batch system in which the nutrients have been initially placed into 
the broth for cells to grow. Initially, cells go through a lag phase right after cell inoculation. Cells 
take some time to adapt to new conditions, synthetize the essential enzymes or proteins that will 
be used for metabolism in these conditions. Once the growth phase starts, cells grow 
exponentially consuming all nutrients available in the environment. As these nutrients become 
exhausted, at some points there are too many cells for the available nutrients, so nutrient limitation 
induces a decline phase in which growth rate starts decreasing until reaching a zero value. Also, 
inhibitory products start to increase in concentration. In the stationary phase, no further growth 
occurs, and the nutrients are exactly enough to maintain cell internal metabolism but not to 
enhance cell duplication. Finally, a death phase arrives and that might occur for several reasons. 
Firstly, cells are found in nutrient starvation conditions so some metabolic routes might be 
stopped. Secondly, a high concentration of cells has been reached so any toxic auto-produced 
metabolites reach concentrations that may have harmful effects on the cells. And lastly, since few 
nutrients are dissolved in the culture medium, many cells start to uptake water by osmosis and 
may end up dying by lysis [58], [65], [66].

toxic chemical species produced during the growth. In microalgae applications in industry, CO2 is

reached, the replacement can be performed. A part of the current broth is taken away and cells 
are recovered, and the same volume is added in the reactor with new substrate for the remaining 
cells. Therefore, a new growth cycle starts and the cells that have stayed in the reactor can start 
duplicating again. The most optimal way to do it is to perform the replacement once they have 
reached the decline or stationary phase. In this way, the outlet cell concentration is maximized 
and most of the added substrate is utilized [35].

2.2 Photobioreactors in industry 
There are many kinds of photobioreactors in industry designed for autotrophic species that can 
be suitable for growing microalgae at phototrophic and/or mixotrophic conditions. In general, they 
have very high substrate conversion efficiency, although they require a large surface area of the 
reactor exposed to light [67], [68]. That is the reason why many of them are built with large tubes 
with tiny diameter, so that a greater volume of the culture gets close to the walls and gets exposed 
to light. There is a usually a constant flow of cell culture, as well as an airflow of CO2 entering to 
the reactor. During the illuminated phase, cells capture CO2 and keep on growing and produce 
O2. The O2 needs to be eliminated from the reactor through a degassing exit, since it ends up 
being toxic for the cells and it can stop the cell growth [67]. Sunlight might be limited or time-
unpredictable when it comes to growth of a very large or sensible culture of microalgae. Artificial 
light has been implemented in pilot scale units to ensure consistent light supply at the expense of 
increased energy consumption [69]. 
Generally, a photoreactor could normally store the main matrix in a stirred tank. From there, a 
small fraction of the matrix is continuously taken out of the tank, irradiated with light, and returned 
to the tank. One of the main reasons for that is that all the reactants end up being fully converted 
faster and the final products are obtained before [70]. According to Levenspiel, the maximum yield 
of an intermediate in irreversible series reactions is obtained when fluids at different conversion 
stages of the reaction are not allowed to mix. The closest configuration to this is a Plug Flow 
reactor [70]. However, this obviously depends on the kinetic model of the reaction. When there is 
inhibition, as it happens with some photochemical reaction under light saturation conditions, PFR 
might not be the best configuration since cells are greatly exposed to the light irradiance for a 
long period of time. The dark storage tank without light irradiance can be a good solution for this 
problem. 
Photobioreactors can be classified as open systems and closed systems. Firstly, open outdoor 
systems are natural or artificial ponds with no overall flow of liquid matrix and open on the upper 
part [56], [71]. It typically has light coming in from the top surface and the walls can be completely 
opaque. According to the depth of the reactor tank, higher light intensity will reach the matrix and 
a higher yield will be obtained. They are typically between 0.2 and 0.5 m deep with mixing and 
circulation to stabilize the algae growth and productivity [9]. Although these kinds of reactors have 
a relatively low cost, due to low energy requirements [37], productions last longer compared to 
flow reactors and present massive loss of water by evaporation and higher exposure to microbial 
contamination [71]. In contrast, the closed photobioreactors present many advantages, such as 
low CO2 losses, higher reproducibility of conditions and smaller volume needed since surface 
area in illuminated zone is maximized. However, the system needs to be continuously degassed 
since the O2 acts as an inhibitor of many growth processes. The most typical shape for this 
bioreactor would be the tubular photoreactor, in which the aqueous matrix passes long tubes with 
small diameters for highest possible light exposure [71]. In addition to this, also flat-plates and 
helicoidal tubular reactors can be found. 
Biomass productivity in both types of photobioreactors can vary according to the species used 
and the geometrical configuration. Both types can have very similar values of productivity and 
should be selected against each other according to other parameters, such as the limiting factors 
for cell growth in each species [56]. However, in average, open reactors have lower values of 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
27 
productivity since there is lower exposure to light [52]. Given, the photoautotrophic production of 
microalgae is the most economically feasible way to produce them in large-scale, reactors with 
great exposure to light should be chosen for industrial applications [9], [72]. This is, indeed, tubular 
or flat plate reactors.

2.2.1 Open-pond reactors 
The open reactors are typically ponds or man-made wells in which bacteria are grown inside. 
They are not a good option for photosynthetic microorganisms since light can only be provided 
from the top surface, which is the one exposed to the outdoors. The walls of the reactor are 
opaque and provide no light at all. Circular open ponds were widely used in the 20th century, but 
nowadays more sophisticated options have replaced them in industry [56]. A picture is shown in 
Figure 3a. Although it might seem straightforward to think that not enough light is provided to the 
cells in such configuration, there are authors that have shown that even in the open raceways 
there is light photoinhibition for some species, such as Spirulina platensis [74]. Other limiting 
factors in these reactors can be the difficult temperature control or the easiness of microorganism 
contamination in these bioreactors. Open pond reactors are shown to be one of the easiest 
reactors to clean and sterilize, since the open configuration permits a good mechanical abrasion 
and manual cleaning if needed [54]. Industrial bioreactors should be designed for clean-in-place 
(CIP) practices for the cleaning and sterilization before and after use. The normal procedure is to 
spray the cleaning solution with spraying balls and spread it over the reactor [75]. In the bioreactor

design stage, the reactor should be thought to have enough volume and large dimensions so that 
the spray can reach all sections [76]. That is why the open pond reactors are ideal for this type of 
practices and can be easily sterilized compared to other closed reactor configurations. 
The capital cost of open bioreactors tends to be very low, which is the reason why it is still an 
option in some countries for specific industries [9]. However, the production cost can be a bit 
higher in these cases in comparison to other similar production in the health food industry, such 
as soy or fishmeal [56] 
In this type of open-systems, contamination can be a frequent issue. The best way to obtain a 
monospecies culture is by enhancing extreme conditions, such as very high salinity, extreme pH 
or high concentration of specific substrates [74]. Chlorella is a good candidate for open-system 
reactors given its high growth rate, also Dunaliella for tolerating high salinity or Spirulina for 
growing in very alkaline media. However, many authors claim that these reactor configuration 
present very low productivity and should be replaced by closed systems in industry [74]. 
2.2.2 Stirred tank bioreactors 
Stirred tank fermentors can be very useful for their simple geometry and reduced complexity. In 
the chemical industry, they are widely used since they present homogeneous mixing of the 
reactants and reduce the diffusion limitations in the kinetic rate. However, in photoreactions, they 
are not that common since light can only be supplied through the walls of the tank [77]. This 
means that light needs to penetrate through the mixture and absorption is observed across the 
depth of the reactor, especially if it is not a homogeneous solution [78]. In cell cultures, light is 
attenuated drastically by cells even at typically low working concentrations and at optical paths of 
around 15 cm. Therefore, if a stirred tank fermentor is chosen as photobioreactor, a good stirring 
must be assured in order to make all cells get exposure to light. 
Using a stirred tank fermentor can ten-fold the outlet concentration and productivity of the reactor 
compared to hatchery outdoor tanks, where the light is only provided on the top surface [69]. In 
these bioreactors, the stirring configuration is crucial for maximizing the productivity. A possible 
solution to this issue can be to insert optical fibres with LEDs emitting light inside the reactor so 
that cells in the centre of the reactor are also exposed to photon emission [79]. Matsunaga et al. 
used 661 light-diffusing optical fibres inside a cylindrical reactor and showed that light attenuation 
was barely noticed in all parts of the reactor with cell concentrations around 108 – 109 cells/mL  
[79]. In general, however, this option has a very high energy and capital cost, so it is not very 
feasible to implement it in all types of industries [56]. 
As a conclusion, stirred-tank reactors are normally a better option when it comes to heterotrophic 
growth of cells. In general, they provide conditions that make it easy for bioreactors to achieve 
the maximum heterotrophic productivity values [80]. However, it is important to pinpoint that in 
most microalgae species, the heterotrophic growth rate can be much slower than the autotrophic 
one [56]. This implies that although a very high productivity is achieved, in photosynthetic 
autotrophic conditions a higher product formation could be achieved. For example, in Chlorella 
vulgaris, autotrophic has been shown to be higher than heterotrophic rate at any glucose 
concentration [9], [57]. However, in mixotrophic conditions, which means in constant supply of 
light and glucose, the growth rate was shown to be the highest possible. Therefore, stirred tank 
reactors could be a good option for mixotrophic growth conditions.

2.2.3 Tubular photobioreactors 
Closed tubular reactors are gaining a lot of popularity in microalgae production since they have a 
very much larger productivity than any other open reactor or even a stirred tank reactor [9]. The 
most typical configuration is to have a tank where the mixture is stores and a pump keeps flowing 
the culture through a bended tube. The tube is bended in many parts as a U-shape to optimize 
the space with the maximum length. In the most common configurations, there is a storage tank 
where the mixture is stored before entering the light zone is normally opaque. Since no light is 
shined, no reaction takes place there. It is in the tube where cells are exposed to light and 
photobioreactions can occur [81]. Since the tubular reactors consist of a closed system with a 
large surface-to-volume ratio, it is difficult to sterilize it efficiently by using heat and clean them 
with common CIP practices used in some industries like Pharma [75]. If a chemical oxidizer needs 
to be used for sterilizing the reactor, very large quantities of clean water need to be flushed 
through in order to eliminate them [56]. Consequently, many of these reactors may not satisfy the 
Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) requirements for pharmaceutical industry. One of the main 
drawbacks of these closed systems is that their operational costs are substantially higher than for 
open-pond reactors, as well as as very large amounts of cleaning solvents flushed to achieved 
sterilization standards [82], [83]. 
The most typical configurations for a tubular reactor are shown schematically in Figure 4. Pictures 
of real tubular reactors in industry are presented in Figure 3c and Figure 3d, both inclined and 
horizontal continuous set up, respectively. The inclined version of the reactor can be used to 
maximize solar light capture and increase productivity. It can be seen that culture medium is 
stored in a tank where a pump forces the flow through a tubular space. In the tubular space, the 
culture is exposed to solar or artificial light and finally returns to the tank. The culture medium, 
prior to the tubular section, is exposed to the introduction of CO2 flow, which will be used as 
substrate for the photosynthetic reactions. After all the tubular circuit, the culture reaches the airlift 
system, which enables the removal of the produced O2 and inserts more CO2 that will be used as 
substrate. The air inlet is used to enhance the turbulence of the mixture when flowing. The 
pressure is normally set to provide a low shear stress and good rate of homogenization [82]. 
Typical linear velocities of the flow may lie between 15 and 50 cm·s-1 [82], [84]. Apart from the 
shown geometry, tubular photobioreactors can be oriented horizontally, vertically, in helical 
configuration or in α-shape. The advantages of more sophisticated geometries can be various but 
at the same time the operational cost can also increase together with the obtained productivity 
[82]. However, for most of the microalgae species in these reactors, the major limiting factor is 
not the light capturing phenomenon. The bottleneck is usually the conversion of photosynthetically 
fixed carbon into structural biomass [85]. Consequently, during the reactor design stage, more 
effort should be put on great substrate availability throughout the path than in the light exposure 
area, since the first one has more impact on the overall production rate.

There are many advantages for closed reactors with tubular configuration. First of all, given that 
it is closed, it allows effective sterilization of the reactor. The tubular geometry enhances a better 
control of gas transfer inside the mixture and ensures a better illumination on all cells due to a 
large surface-to-volume ratio [84]. In comparison to stirred cylindrical tanks, cells have a higher 
exposure to light and therefore there is a higher attainable cell density on these reactors. In 
addition to this, the turbulence in the flow of tubular reaction normally enhances light availability 
for all cells as well as the transfer of gases, such as CO2 [86]. This reduces the diffusion limitations 
and the rate reductions due to light availability. The key operational configuration to maximize 
productivity is to ensure a very narrow tube for the culture to travel through and give the maximum 
light exposure during this section. It is of paramount importance to get a high circulation flow 
velocity and enhance turbulent flow [87]. Increasing the internal turbulent velocity ensures that all 
cells get exposed to light. At the same time, the fact that the storage tank is not exposed to light 
can be an advantage because this will prevent cells to reach the photoinhibition stage for high 
light exposure. 
In general, tubular photobioreactors are much more efficient than raceway open ponds, as shown 
by many studies. In tubular reactors, the volumetric productivity can be 10 times higher than in 
open ponds, they achieve higher substrate conversion in lower time, present lower reactor 
volumes to achieve the same conversions and consume practically the same amount of CO2 [52]. 
Therefore, it is a highly interesting alternative for large-scale production. 
2.2.4 Flat plate photobioreactors 
Flat plate photobioreactors consist of fully transparent glass or PVC plate reactors where the 
culture mixture flows through a very narrow space while exposed to a light source [88], [89]. The 
plate can usually be inclined in order to maximize the solar light exposure. Since these reactors 
may use solar light instead of artificial light sources, the best way to assure efficient light exposure 
from the sun depends on the time of the year and place on Earth. In any case, the use of solar 
light as light source should influence very positively in the total operational cost of the reactor 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31 
since artificial light can be an expensive utility. In general, inclined flat plate reactors show higher 
productivities than horizontal ones [88], [90]. A picture of a flat plate reactor is shown in . Even in 
this configuration, mixing and turbulence is attempts to maximize productivity as much as 
possible. The large surface-to-volume ratio and high exposure to light also ensures a very high 
productivity and has made these reactors very attractive for microalgae production in the past 
recent years [89]. The fact that the culture is spread among the plate let the system reach very 
high densities of cells in the cultures. Miyachi et al. have shown to obtain cell concentrations of 
80 g/L by using a flat plate photobioreactor [91]. However, such high cell densities have limited 
significance because the productivity at these conditions might be limited by the extreme light 
attenuation. The optimal cell density has been reported to be around 15-20 g/L [91]. In general, 
the achieved outlet concentrations in flat reactors might be on the order of the ones in tubular 
reactors or even higher [56] 
One of the main advantages of flat plate reactors compared to tubular ones is that the 
concentration of the accumulated dissolved oxygen in flat plate photobioreactors tends to be 
much lower [89], [92]. This of course enhances very high photosynthetic efficiency and overall 
reactor productivity. It also improves the light exposure compared to tubular reactors by 
enhancing the absence of dark zones throughout the culture path [89]. Due to the flat geometry, 
it makes it easy to have a flexible orientation in order to optimize solar radiation use. Also, it 
improves the homogeneity of this exposure enabling a constant growth throughout the whole 
reactor [82] 
In contrast to these advantages, high production rate also implies high formation of O2 and heat 
emission during the process. This will mean that a stricter temperature control will be needed in 
comparison to tubular reactors, as well as a higher loss of the reactor volume by gas will be 
observed [90]. Finally, by having such a great exposure to sun, photoinhibition effects can also 
be observed in comparison to other configurations.

3.6 Light intensity 
Another crucial parameter to consider in a photobioreactor is light intensity. The highest cell 
growth rates for most of the species of microalgae are related to autotrophic growth rate, which 
is obtained through photosynthetic metabolic routes [56]. Therefore, the light intensity that 
reaches these cells is of paramount importance when considering the kinetics of their lipid and 
biomass production. 
Many studies show that for each photosynthetic species, there is a minimum light irradiance that 
should be given to the cells in order to conduct photosynthesis. Irradiance is usually measured in 
W·m-2 or, equivalently, in (μmol photons)·m-2·s-1, and when it is lower than the threshold value, 
no photosynthetic activity is measured [39]. At low light irradiance, the relation between net 
photosynthetic activity and irradiance is practically linear. However, at large incident irradiance 
values, it reaches a plateau where the productivity is the same even if more light is given to the 
system [39], [115], [116]. Many studies have shown that even at very high supplied irradiance, a 
photoinhibition effect can be detected [39], [54]. A plot showing the relation between net 
photosynthetic activity and light irradiance is presented in Figure 5.

photosynthetic activity is measured with respect to oxygen evolution reaction. The compensation

Photoinhibition is a widely studied phenomenon that induces depression of the photosynthetic 
activity when irradiance is way higher than a certain level. The response of cells to photoinhibition 
can greatly depend on temperature or nitrogen deficiency, in which they would be more 
susceptible to this phenomenon [39] Vonshak et al. discovered with Spirulina platensis that it is

photosynthetic activity [117]. They found out that a shading over the 15-20% of their circulation 
time was the most optimal condition to maximize the net productivity. Lower shading would induce 
too much photoinhibition while higher shading would also decrease the cell growth due to a light 
depletion situation. All in all, it is beneficial for a photobioreactor to have an opaque tank in which 
the culture is not irradiated with any kind of light, in order to prevent photoinhibition consequences 
in the long term.

4. Model of a continuously stirred tank reactor 
(CSTR) for microalgae 
The goal of the project is to create a general and broad model for a photobioreactor. The best 
starting point should be to create a model for a simple reactor configuration, such as a 
continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR). Once this model is ready, it will be easier to implement 
it into broader models for complete description of a photobioreactor. 
4.1 Description of the CSTR 
A continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) is a tank with constant stirring where all reactants and 
solvents are introduced as inlet with a continuous flow and the outlet is also taken out in a 
continuous mode. Since the inlet is constantly being supplied and it has a different composition 
than the content of the tank, the stirring makes it possible to homogenise the reactor content 
instantaneously. The outlet is removed with a certain flow as products are generated inside the 
tank. 
The CSTR is designed to cultivate microalgae, so the inlets must be the substrates that are 
needed for microalgae growth, as described in section 3. These are mainly a carbon source and 
a nitrogen source. The carbon source is usually CO2, since microalgae normally operate at 
autotrophic conditions and require CO2 as a substrate for photosynthesis. However, some authors 
suggest that same models can be used if the carbon source is glucose instead, as for accounting 
for heterotrophic metabolic routes [64], [107]. The nitrogen source can easily be a small organic 
molecule, such as glycine, which is easy to metabolise by microalgae and can be used as a 
nitrogen source to build amino acids and nucleotides. Cells will also be provided in the inlet since 
they are needed to start the reaction, given its autocatalytic. 
In the cells, the nitrogen and lipid content will be monitored and quantified with quota levels, and 
these will be the parameters to include in the model to predict the cell growth. The produced 
biomolecules, such as genetic material and proteins, are stored in the cells and can be considered 
functional biomass (x). The sum of functional biomass, nitrogen quota and lipid quota will 
constitute the concentration of total biomass accumulated in the reactor (X). The model will 
monitor the concentration of 5 important species that are either reactants or products of the 
bioreactions of interests. Three of them are contained in the mixture of the reactor, and their 
concentrations are calculated using the total amount of species divided by the total volume of the 
reactor. The last 2 are measured as quotas so their concentration is based on the amount that 
occupy inside the total biomass. All 5 are summarized in Table 2.

4.2 Assumptions of the model 
The CSTR model is aimed to be as general as possible but keeping a certain simplicity to describe 
the behaviour of a microalgae cultivation system in a bioreactor. It should be used to describe a 
wide range of different realistic scenarios and close to real photobioreactors used in industry. The 
list of assumptions and considerations for this model is presented below: 
• 
Continuous, instantaneous, and ideally homogeneous mixing in the reactor. No spatial 
dependence on species concentrations. 
• 
Constant density and viscosity. The properties of the mixture remain unchanged during 
the whole reaction, such as density and viscosity. No wall effects are considered and the 
mixing is supposed to make the whole system as homogeneous as possible. 
• 
The CSTR has a cylindrical shape. It is a very common shape for a bioreactor since it 
enhances good mixing conditions. Furthermore, the walls of the reactor are considered 
to be transparent and present zero light absorption. If absorption was accounted, simple 
Lambert-Beer law could be used for calculating the real irradiance reaching the culture. 
• 
The irradiated light in the bioreactor will be equally supplied through the walls of the 
reactor at a constant irradiance value I0. Although the light intensity might not be the same 
in all points inside the bioreactor, an average value can be estimated and used for 
calculations with the rate equations of the model. This can be assumed given the fast 
mixing of the content inside the reactor. 
• 
The reaction will only take place inside the reactor, and not in the inlet and outlet pipes. 
This is a valid assumption given that pipes or any other storage tank are deprived of light, 
so the growth rate is zero if there is no light supplied to the system. 
• 
Constant temperature. The reactor will be provided with a jacketed wall that provided heat 
transfer when needed in order to maintain the temperature inside the reactor. As a 
consequence, no temperature dependence on the kinetic constants is considered. 
• 
Free Monod kinetics, which means no cell death during their residence time and all are 
equally capable of duplicating and growing. 
• 
CO2 is supplied in gas form by bubbling into the mixture and it is assumed to be in 
chemical equilibrium with its dissolved form. Therefore, the dissolved CO2 concentration 
as carbonate can be computed by using the equilibrium constant and the partial pressure 
of CO2 in the inlet. 
All these assumptions are considered to be reasonable given the usual set-up of an industrial 
bioreactor and feasible to implement. 
4.3 Model equations 
The equations of the model are presented in this section. Firstly, the balance equations are the 
main equations of the model, and the ones that describe the flows and the behaviour of the 
bioreactor. In addition, constitutive equations will also be proposed, which define the kinetic 
models for the growth rate, nitrogen uptake and lipid production. With this model, it is easy to 
adjust the constitutive equations for different species of microalgae by changing the kinetic 
parameters or equations and maintaining the balance equations at the same time.

as reaction rates or enthalpies [123]. In the microalgae CSTR model, reaction rates are defined 
for cell growth, nitrogen uptake and lipid production. These rates are used in the main balance 
equations and can be defined using input parameters considering the configuration of the reactor 
and its setup. 
As shown in Section 3, there are many different factors that influence the value of the rates for 
each of these processes. In general, a mathematical expression that included them all should be 
found to describe the system’s behaviour. This expression should contain the most important 
limiting factors such as nitrogen concentration, temperature, CO2 concentration and light intensity. 
This model is called threshold model [64].

The variable interdependence matrix shown in Table 8, expresses all the explicit relations in the 
rate equations with each of the variables with green squares, while non-explicit dependence with 
red squares. The diagonal of the matrix, which is also marked in green, represents the self-
dependence of variables, in which the rate equation for that variable can be solved to obtain the 
value of that variable over time. If all the green squares appear to be below the diagonal, then the 
model can be solved sequentially, meaning one equation after each. In this case, the equations 
should be solved from the upper one on the matrix until the last one obtaining one by one all the 
variables over time. However, this is not possible with this model since there are clear 
interdependencies that make it impossible to solve it sequentially. 
For example, it can be seen that the equation that describes the rate of functional biomass 
concentration depends on the growth rate, and to calculate the growth rate lipid and nitrogen 
quotas need to be solved first, which at the same time need the calculation of the total biomass, 
which is calculated by summing terms, one of them the functional biomass. This loop makes it 
impossible for the system of equations to be solved sequentially and should be all solved at the 
same time. Also, the concentration of carbon and nitrogen source (S1, S2) depend on the lipid 
production and nitrogen uptake rates, since they both contribute to a decrease in their 
concentration. However, at the same time these rates clearly depend on these concentrations by 
using the Michaelis-Menten expressions. Finally, the lipid production and nitrogen uptake rates 
contribute to increase lipid and nitrogen quota, respectively. However, these quotas need to be 
solved before calculating the rates since they act as driving forces of the processes. Given the 
autocatalytic nature of the processes, both equations need to be solved at the same time as well.  
To sum up, the whole model can’t be solved sequentially and all equations will need to be solved 
by using an iterative method until convergence.

4.4 Final considerations 
The model is complete and presents a solution to all the relevant phenomena that take place 
inside the bioreactor and the cells. The balance equations are general and valid for any kind of 
stirred reactor and can be presented as a solution to a batch, continuous and fed-batch reactor.  
In the batch or fed-batch configuration, the outlet flow should be set to zero, but the balance 
equations and constitutive equations remain unchanged. The model can be solved dynamically 
to obtain the solution of the concentrations over time. In this situation, it is possible to predict how 
the concentrations will evolve during the set-up of the reactor until they reach the steady state. 
To get the steady state solution, the differential terms must be set equal to zero, to get the 
solutions that lead to no time variation of the species concentration. The steady state solution is 
interesting in the reactor is being operated in continuous mode. 
If different species of microalgae follow other kinetic models, it is easy to implement the changes 
in the constitutive equations or the adjustable parameters. The same applies for a different 
geometry or light incidence distribution. The solution for all configurations should be obtained 
exactly the same since the balance equations are unchanged. Finally, if more parameters want 
to be added in the model, such as temperature or pH, these should be added in the constitutive 
equations. 
In addition to this, the balance equations would need to be revised in case a mixing model wants 
to be implemented as well. If mixing conditions need to be considered, a compartment model with 
spatial variations will need to be implemented as well. However, this was out of scope for this 
model for a CSTR.

5. Model of a photobioreactor (PBR) 
To design a model of a large-scale photobioreactor (PBR), a real photobioreactor has been taken 
as a reference. The model will be based on Jumbo XL reactor, by Synoxis Algae. This reactor 
can be used to cultivate microalgae at an industrial level for different sectors, such as aquaculture, 
agri-food and cosmetics. 
5.1 Reactor configuration 
The whole process configuration is presented in the P&ID of the reactor in Figure 10. It consists 
of a tubular reactor, where liquid flows in one direction while it is irradiated with white light. The 
inlet flow can be adjusted by the reactor, since the substrate mixture is stored in a tank/bottle 
outside the tubes. There are no algae in the tanks since they are only inoculated inside the reactor 
tubes. The culture goes through 50 vertical tubes, which are placed in series. The tubes where 
reaction takes place have been illustrated in the P&ID. 
 
Figure 10. P&ID of the Jumbo® photobioreactor from Synoxis Algae. 
At the entrance of each tube there is an inlet of pressurised air for mixing. The CO2 is used as a 
substrate for cell growth but at the same time it provides a good pH control in the broth. The 
provided air flow mixture is also used to enhance a good and fast mixing, as well as a turbulent 
flow in all tubes. The outlet after the last tube is split into a flow that is recirculated back into the 
storage tank and another flow that is sampled out. After the storage tank, it will enter again to the 
reactor for further cell growth. The flows can be quite fast and the maximum cell growth may not 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
 
55 
be achieved after one cycle in the reactor loop. However, recirculation enhances a higher 
conversion and cell growth and maximize the use of the substrates. 
Each of the tubes has a cylindrical shape, with a height around 2 m and a diameter of 3-4 cm. 
The flow goes uphill and downhill interchangeably one after the other, while the air inlet is always 
at the bottom of the tube so that it can go up. A scheme is shown in Figure 11. 
 
Figure 11. Scheme of the flow through the reactor tubes. 
Light is irradiated in the whole chamber and equally in the space due to a wide distribution of 
LEDs. Therefore, it is valid to assume that all tubes are equally irradiated by light. 
5.2 Assumptions of the model 
The assumptions considered for this model are listed below: 
• 
The CO2 bubbling present in all tubes creates a turbulent flow that enhances good mixing 
throughout the tube. This will assure homogenization of the culture inside the tubes and 
no spatial variations of the species concentrations will be considered in the tube. The flow 
is always on one direction but plug flow can’t be assumed since the flow is highly 
turbulent. Consequently, the whole tube is seen as a homogeneous system with the same 
concentration throughout the height. It is feasible to assume it since flows can be 
reasonably large and the residence time in these tubes is rather small. Therefore, each 
tube is considered as a CSTR unit. 
• 
The irradiated light in the bioreactor chamber is equally supplied through the walls of the 
tubes at a constant irradiance value I0. 
• 
Constant temperature. The air flow in the tubes is provided with a relatively large rate 
compared to the liquid flow rate. Therefore, the air flow is also suitable for maintaining 
the desired temperature. Consequently, no temperature dependence on the kinetic 
constants is considered. 
• 
The inlet and outlet flow are the same (Fin = Fout). In this case, volume of the system in 
each tube will be constant and based on overflow condition.

5.3 Model equations 
The model is presented as several CSTRs in series, in which the outlet of one reactor is the inlet 
of the following one. To keep the model as general as possible, the number of reactors is Nreact. 
The reactors are placed in series, as shown in Figure 12. 
 
Figure 12. Scheme of the PBR model. 
The configuration allows recirculation, which is done from the outlet of the last reactor up to the 
inlet of the first reactor. In this case, both the recirculation flow and the inlet flow merge before 
entering the first reactor. The recirculation flow can be tuned to obtain the desired product 
concentration. 
In this model, the first reactor has an inlet flow Fin+FR, with the corresponding concentrations. 
Using the CSTR model described in section 4, the concentration inside the reactor and at the 
outlet can be predicted over time. This will be used as the inlet for the following reactor. The PBR 
model should solve the system of differential equations from the CSTR model, and there will be 
as many systems as tubes are composing the PBR. It must be noted that the equations from 
different systems will be coupled, since the inlet parameters of one system of equations will be 
the variables that can be calculated in the previous system. In addition, the equations of the first 
reactor will highly depend on the output of the last reactor, since the recirculation flow enables 
the outlet of this last reactor to enter again into the cycle. Therefore, the Nreact systems of 
equations are all coupled circularly and can’t be solved sequentially. They must be solved all at 
the same time by using numerical methods. 
All reactors will be described by solving the CSTR model. In contrast to the rest of them, the first 
reactor will depend on the last one since a recirculation flow is added on the inlet, as shown in 
Figure 12. Considering that, the concentrations in inlet of the first reactor need to be recalculated.

5.4 Final considerations 
This model shows a complete description of a PBR with the configuration as the one from Synoxis 
Algae. The model is described so that it can be solved dynamically over time and predict the 
concentration profiles and how long does it take the system to reach the steady state. The model 
can also be solved at the steady state by equating the formation rates to zero. In this case, the 
model is converted into a system of 5·Nreact algebraic equations with 5·Nreact unknowns. 
The equations can’t be solved sequentially one by one if there is recycling from the last to the first 
reactor. If the recycling is set to zero, then all concentrations on the reactors depend only on their 
inlets, which is the outlet of the previous reactor. The only sequential calculation that can be done 
is the reactors state at the steady state. For the first reactor, the model equations would only 
depend on the system’s inlet concentrations, which are constant and can be chosen. Therefore, 
if the set of differential equations is solved for this reactor, one can obtain the concentration profile 
over time for the species in reactor 1. The second reactor’s equations would depend on the output 
of the first reactor, and as these are already solved, the concentrations can be used to calculate 
the concentrations on the second reactor. For the rest of the reactors is done sequentially and 
any of the results will interfere with an already calculated solution. 
However, when recycling is configured, the inlet of the first reactor would also depend on the last 
reactor’s outlet. This means that if the system of equations is solved sequentially, after the last 
reactor calculation, the inlets of the first reactor would change. Therefore, another round of 
calculations would need to be done, which at the same time would affect again to the inlet of the 
first reactor. All in all, several iterations need to be done in order to perform a calculation for the 
PBR state. The iterations need to be set both for calculating the steady state or the dynamical 
change of the system.

6.1.1 Dynamical model 
The system has been solved dynamically to simulate the reactor and obtain the evolution of the 
concentrations over time. The system of differential equations of the CSTR model, shown in Table 
7, have been solved as an initial value problem by using the Runge-Kutta method of 4th order. It 
is a numerical method that solves differential equations by giving the value of the function at a 
given time. It is called 4th-5th method order since the local truncation error is on the order of O(h5) 
and the total accumulated error is of the order of O(h4). The concentrations of the 5 species have 
been assumed to be zero at the beginning of the simulation, and all of them are supplied by the 
inlet flow. 
One of the most important factors that will determine the behaviour of the system is the inlet flow 
rate and inlet flow composition. Depending on the concentrations at t=0, the inlet flow will 
determine how fast the reactor is filled or flushed out with substrate or biomass. If the flow is too 
low, the substrate will not be supplied sufficiently fast as the cells need. In contrast, if the flow is 
too high, almost all content of the reactor will be replaced fast and no cell growth will be observed.

6.1.2 Steady state of a CSTR 
As it can be seen from the plots in the previous section, the system tends to reach a steady state, 
where concentrations remain constant over time. This is because the system reaches a level of 
biomass and quotas that makes the consumption, production, inlet and outlet rates of each 
species be compensated against each other. This corresponds in some cases to the maximum 
production of biomass for given conditions and therefore will constitute a goal for maximum 
productivity of the bioreactor. However, the time needed to achieve the steady state can vary a 
lot depending on the operating conditions. To calculate the concentrations of species at the steady 
state, the rates in the model equations are equated to zero and the remaining system of algebraic 
equations is solved. With a reactor with a cell inlet source, calculations of the steady state in 
different inlet flows have been done. The initial conditions are presented in Table 14, and plots in

As shown in Figure 19 and Figure 20, the inlet flow has a great influence on the output of the 
reactor at the steady state, especially at low flows. At low flows, conversions are higher and more 
biomass is produced, since the average residence time for the substrate and products is much 
higher. When the flow is increased, biomass spends less time inside the reactor and can’t acquire 
all the substrate available in the medium. Therefore, biomass is taken out when it reaches a lower 
concentration. In contrast, the substrate concentrations S1 and S2 increase at higher flows. The 
rationale behind is exactly the same. Since the flow is higher, biomass doesn’t have enough 
residence time inside the reactor to consume the available substrate so the substrate 
concentration in the medium is higher. As mentioned before, when a reactor of this kind is 
implemented to industry, an assessment should be done whether high outlet concentration wants 
to be achieved or fast achievement of the steady state and maximum production. Although 
conversions are higher at low inlet flows, the high residence times can make the applications not 
worthy for the large waiting times to get the desired output. 
A sanity check for the steady state calculation is to check that the steady state concentrations 
coincide with the concentrations at large operating times in the dynamic model. If compared to 
steady the results in the dynamical simulation in Figure 16b, with Fin = 5 L/h, the reached steady 
state corresponds to the value shown in this plot. At the steady state, total biomass concentration 
is around 1.6 g/L and x, Q, L are 0.3, 0.6 and 0.7 g/L respectively. Another sanity check is to see 
that the model can describe well the behaviour of the system since at very large inlet flows, the 
steady state concentrations are close to the inlet values. This is reasonable since the species 
don’t have enough time either to be consumed or grow. The total volume of the medium of the 
reactor is replaced very fast and the residence time of the species is shortened considerably.

6.2 Case Study 2: Steady state of CSTRs in series without recirculation 
A second case study is proposed to get closer to the PBR model. In this case, the system consists 
of Nreact CSTRs in series, where the outlet of each reactor is used as the inlet of the following one. 
In contrast to the PBR model, the system doesn’t present any recycling of the product outlet in 
the last reactor. A scheme is shown in Figure 21. 
 
Figure 21. Scheme of the system in case study 2. 
The system is solved to obtain the steady state for this configuration. Since there is no 
recirculation on the system, every reactor is only affected by its own inlet, which depends on the 
previous reactors’ performance. This means that the behaviour of the following reactors won’t 
affect the reactor itself. Mathematically, the system can be solved by solving the CSTR model in 
each reactor one by one and in the same order as the flow goes. Once the outlet of one reactor 
is calculated, the solution is used as the inlet of the following reactor and the model is solved 
again for the next reactor. As explained in Case Study 1, the steady state is found by equating 
the accumulation rates to zero, in order to find the state in which concentrations are kept constant 
over time. 
To simulate the system, the concentration of the species at the steady state is plotted against the 
reactor number. The model has been used with the same parameters and inlet conditions as 
shown in case study 1 in Table 11 and Table 12. For the simulation, an arbitrary value of Nreact=50 
has been chosen. The only parameter that is changed is the volume, which is V = 5.40 L, so that 
the sum of the volumes of the 50 reactors sums up to 270 L. In this way, the results can be 
compared with the ones from case study 1. Results are plotted in Figure 22, for Fin = 1.5 L/h and 
Fin = 2 L/h.

7. Conclusions 
This section presents the conclusions and findings from the model and the case studies 
simulations. In addition, future research lines from the current point will be presented. 
A complete mathematical model for cultivation of microalgae in a continuously stirred tank reactor 
(CSTR) has been presented. The model describes the behaviour of a microalgae culture in a 
CSTR under specific conditions and it’s capable of predicting the time evolution of the 
concentrations of biomass, lipid, stored nitrogen and substrates. The model uses as input several 
parameters, such as the volumetric inlet and outlet flow rates, the incident light irradiance, the 
geometry of the reactor, the species concentrations in the inlet flow and the initial concentrations 
of each species in the reactor. It assumes constant temperature and homogeneous mixing. The 
model also contains some adjustable parameters that will vary across species, such as substrate 
to product yield coefficients and constants from the kinetic models, and that can be adjusted with 
experimental data. This mathematical model is composed by 6 differential equations that are 
derived from mass balances for the five studied species (functional biomass, lipid, nitrogen, CO2 
and glycine) and volume variation; 3 constitutive equations that describe the kinetic rates for cell 
growth, lipid production and nitrogen uptake; and 4 algebraic equations that describe relations 
between the variables of the model. The output of the model is the time evolution of the species’ 
concentrations, as well as their concentration at steady state. In addition to the CSTR model, a 
simplified model for a tubular photobioreactor (PBR) has been derived. In the PBR model, each 
tube is assumed to behave as a CSTR given the high air flow rate compared to a lower liquid flow 
rate, which enhances a fast mixing in the reactor. The model is built considering the connection 
of several CSTRs in series, in which the output of one reactor is the inlet of the following reactor. 
The main goal of the model has been to represent the Jumbo® PBR from Synoxis Algae. 
Therefore, a recycling flow from the last reactor to the first one has also been considered in the 
model. The PBR provides a realistic approach to a real reactor, although it assumes a perfect 
mixing across tube sections that might not represent closely the experimental evidence. 
Three case studies have been proposed to test the validity of the model and simulate the reactors’ 
behaviour under different conditions. To start with, a CSTR outlet has been simulated with 
different inlet flow rates and it has been shown that for low flow rates, the system reaches a steady 
state with a non-zero concentration of biomass and lipids, while very high flow rates will end up 
in a washout situation of the reactor. Low flow rates imply high residence times for cells in the 
bioreactor and ensure that growth kinetics is faster than the outlet rate. In contrast, when the 
residence time is too low, cells don’t duplicate fast enough to compensate the outlet flow rate of 
the reactor. However, within the range in which washout is not observed, higher flow rates present 
higher steady state concentration, since a higher flow of substrate is supplied to the cells and 
increase their kinetic rates. At the same time, however, the steady state is reached out later given 
that cell growth needs to compete with a higher dilution factor. The steady state of the reactors 
clearly depends on the inlet flow. Nevertheless, at low inlet flows, the reactor outlet is more 
sensible to flow variations, while at residence times lower than 25 h, the difference is very small. 
The radius of the cylindrical reactor plays a significant role in the reactor outlet. In general, a 
smaller radius implies a higher surface-to-volume ratio, which induces higher light availability on 
the cells in the culture due to less absorption. Therefore, higher biomass productivities are 
observed for smaller radius, which is not the case for lipid productivity. The most optimal geometry 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
78 
 
for lipid production maximization has been found to be around V/R = 135 m2. Incident light 
irradiance has also been evaluated and, above a certain threshold around 400 μmol·m-2·s-1, 
higher light irradiances have shown to enhance photoinhibition, providing lower biomass 
productivities and lower lipid outlet concentrations. 
A possible alternative to a CSTR photobioreactor is to create a system of smaller CSTRs that 
equal its total volume. It has been shown that in equal total volume, a series connection of CSTRs 
proofs to provide lower concentrations of biomass and lipid quota at the system’s outlet. The 
general behaviour is practically the same as with a CSTR, showing larger conversions at lower 
flows and higher residence times. In addition, very low flows can induce a misuse of the reactors 
at the end of the chain, since all substrate would be consumed and cell growth ceases. When 
flows are too low, the residence time for substrate molecules is too high that they are consumed 
in the first reactors. Consequently, an optimal residence time should be chosen by tuning the inlet 
flow or the reactor volume to achieve a maximum productivity at the outlet of the system without 
consuming all substrate or flowing cells too fast. 
Finally, the photobioreactor model has been shown to provide an accurate representation of the 
species concentrations evolution over time in each of the tubes of the reactor. Larger flows also 
imply lower outlet concentration, although sooner in time. The effect of the recycling flow rate has 
also been analysed. It has been shown that lower recycling flows induce to higher outlet 
concentrations, leading to further renewable cell cultures. However, these maximum 
productivities are achieved at later times, which also means that the decision of a recycling flow 
is a balance between time optimisation or maximization of product concentration. 
All in all, the CSTR model describes very accurately the behaviour of a microalgae culture and is 
capable of predicting the species concentrations evolution at different conditions. The PBR model 
is derived from a simplified representation of a real photobioreactor but gives a realistic overview 
of a PBR’s behaviour. Nevertheless, the assumptions and limitations of this model might suggest 
several future research lines that could improve the current model and its applicability. First of all, 
a more complete CSTR model should be created by considering kinetic rate dependence on 
temperature and pH. In this project, temperature variations have been neglected but would of 
course imply changes in the kinetic rates. At the same time, CO2 has been assumed to be 
dissolved in water before being supplied to microalgae. However, given that CO2 is an acid, its 
dissolution in water implies a lowering of the culture pH, which will obviously have an impact in 
the enzyme’s productivity and kinetic constants. Both phenomena could be included as 
constitutive equations, so the mass balance differential equations would remain the same. 
Secondly, another improvement of the model would be to fit the equations’ parameters into 
experimental data. The yield coefficients, maximum and minimum quota levels and kinetic rate 
constants have been found in literature but don’t correspond to this exact model, which means 
that values should be considered as approximate. Moreover, some phenomena have been widely 
studied for the most common microalgae species, such as Chlorella Vulgaris, Spirulina platensis 
and Chlorella protothecoides. For checking the validity of the model and its applicability range, 
experiment with different microalgae species should be done by cultivating them in bioreactors 
and measuring biomass, lipid, stored nitrogen and substrates concentrations at different times. 
After this, the model could be adjusted to find the values of these parameters and check if the 
model is valid for all species or has limitations for certain strains or under extreme conditions. 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
79 
Finally, a third potential project would be to improve the PBR model by considering fluid dynamics 
inside the tube. The current model lays under the assumption that there is homogeneous mixing 
at each of the tubes. Although it is obvious that a fast mixing is provided by the gas inlet at the 
entrance of each tube, the tube might be too large so as not to consider spatial variation of species 
concentrations. Therefore, a fluid dynamics study and mixing analysis should be done to describe 
the flow profile and describe it better in the model by considering spatial variations on the 
concentration.

38. def f_kinetic_biomass(mu_m, q, q_0, l, l_0, S_1, K_S1, I, K_I, XX): 
39.     mu = mu_m * (1-q_0/q) * (1-l_0/l) * (S_1/(K_S1+S_1)) * 
(I/(K_I+I+(I**2)/K_I2)) 
40.     mu = mu*(mu>=0)*(q>=0)*(l>=0)*(q>=q_0)*(l>=l_0) #Zero if exceeding max or min 
limits 
41.     if mu > mu_m: 
42.         mu = mu_m 
43.     return mu 
44.   
45. #Nitrogen uptake rate into the cell: 
46. def f_kinetic_nitrogen_uptake(rho_m, S_2, K_N, q, q_m, q_0): 
47.     rho = rho_m * ((q_m-q)/(q_m-q_0)) * (S_2/(K_N+S_2)) 
48.     rho = rho*(rho>=0)*(S_2>=0)*(q>=0)  #Zero if exceeding max or min limits 
49.     if rho > rho_m: 
50.         rho = rho_m 
51.     return rho 
52.   
53. #Lipid production rate: 
54. def f_kinetic_lipid_production(pi_m, S_1, K_L, Q, XX, l, l_m): 
55.     pi = pi_m * (S_1/(K_L+S_1)) * (1-Q/XX) * ((l_m-l)/l_m) 
56.     pi = pi*(pi>=0)*(S_1>=0)*(Q>=0)*(l>=0) #Zero if exceeding max or min limits 
57.     if pi > pi_m: 
58.         pi = pi_m 
59.     return pi

62. def f_culture_irradience(XX, Q, I0, z): 
63.     alpha = 25.4804  # Light absorption coefficient [m2/g] 
64.     BX = 0.04*XX  # Chlorophyll concentration [g/L] 
65.     I = 2*I0/(alpha*BX*z**2)*(z-1/(alpha*BX)+np.exp(-alpha*BX*z)/(alpha*BX)) 
66.     return I
