Abstract
Streamers are channels of cold ionized plasma, that occur when sufficiently many electron
avalanches develop such that they create an electric field in the same order of magnitude
as the ambient field. Electron avalanches require an ambient electric field stronger than
the breakthrough field, which is smaller than naturally occurring fields in thunderclouds.
Icy hydrometeors have been observed as a possible enhancer of the electric field to sus-
tain a streamer.
In this thesis the physics behind streamers will be explored as well as the role of hy-
drometeors as field enhancers for streamers. Then the numerical implementation for
streamer simulations will be by using a fluid model for the charged particles. Capsule
shaped hydrometeors of radius 1 mm and total length 4 mm are made to interact with an
initial distribution of plasma with maximum density of 1020 m−3 to obtain field amplification
and increase of the ionization.

Introduction
Streamers occur naturally in Earth’s atmosphere, as lightning leader precursors in the
troposphere or sprites in the mesosphere [1, 2]. Therefore, a deep understanding of
the streamer phenomenon can be an instrumental step towards fully understanding the
lightning phenomenon in nature.
Streamers are ionization fronts that move fast in an electric field strongly enhanced at
their tips that form filaments of plasma. The electric field accelerates a cloud of electrons
that interacts with air molecules mostly through: elastic collision, excitation, ionization or
attachment. When the electric field is sufficiently strong, electrons accelerate to energies
where the likelihood of ionization surpasses that of attachment, increasing the total num-
ber of electrons and creating a chain reaction called electron avalanche; the threshold
value for the electric field is known as the breakdown field. Despite the fact that the elec-
tron avalanches locally enhance the electric field an external electric field is necessary,
even if heavily localized, for the first electron avalanches to be generated [1, 3]. If enough
electron avalanches occur in a region the Raether-Meek criterion is fulfilled and a transi-
tion happens from electron avalanche to streamer [4, 5, 6]; which means that the electric
field from the electric avalanches is in the order of the ambient field.
The electric field field inside of thunderclouds is approximately 1 kV cm−1 [7, 8]. However,
experimental and numerical data suggests a breakdown field of at least 1 kV cm−1 [9]. This
means that there needs to be a enhancement mechanism for the electric field to be locally
larger than the ambient field. One such proposed mechanism is through hydrometeors
[10, 11, 12, 13]. Therefore the aim of this project is to simulate a streamer discharge
initiation from a hydrometeor.
Firstly, the Poisson equation solution will be studied for the hydrometeor itself. Consider-
ing the hydrometeor as a perfect conductor would allow to fix the electric potential at its
surface to a constant value and thus only calculations outside of it have to be performed.
A more accurate representation would be to consider the hydrometeor as a dielectric, but
that will require an additional magnitude to compute to account for charge displacement
on the surface of the hydrometeor [13, 3, 10, 11].
Secondly, streamers have to be modeled either as a fluid or as independent particles.
In fluid simulations electrons are treated as densities following deterministic continuity
equations that average all possible interactions. In particle simulations, each particle
will have its own collision. This means that particle simulations are better for taking into
consideration stochastic elements of the problem, but also face harsher computational
constraints. Therefore, the main implementation considered will be a fluid simulation with
the possibility to try a combination of both [14, 15].
Starting from previous work [16, 17], the first objective of this project is to combine and
build upon exisiting 3D implementations of a hydrometeor and a streamer discharge in
the AMReX framework for C++.

2.1
Streamer Physics
Streamers are regions of ionized air that propagate through the effect of an electric field.
Due to the field, the charges in the plasma organize themselves enhancing the electric
field in the tips of the streamer, which drives further ionization.
2.2
Electron motion fundamentals
The motion of a free charged particle in an electric field E is described by :
ma = qE,
(2.1)
where m is the mass of the particle, a is the acceleration and q is the charge of the particle.
Here, it is appropriate to consider the specific characteristics of air. First, in air, the charge
particles that will exist will be electrons or ions, either positively or negatively charged. Due
to the fact that the mass of the electrons is much smaller than the mass of the positive
or negative ions (me ≪mp, mn) while the absolute value of the charges is the same, the
motion of the electrons will be more significant than that of the ions, which will remain
virtually static. Therefore, for streamer physics, it is usually correct to consider only the
motion of electrons.

2.4
Streamer discharge
When an electron avalanche grows enough there starts to appear an electric field due to
the displaced charges (electrons against and positive charges towards the electric field).
Streamers are weakly ionized channels that grow towards one or both of the electrodes.
The conductivity of said channels can locally change the electric field so that it is no longer
uniform. This means that magnitudes that depend on the electric field will no longer be
constant but become scalar functions depending on time and position.
Depending on the propagation direction, streamers can be:
• Negative Streamers. These are the streamers that move against the electric field.
They can be explained just by the motion of electrons, which accelerate and increase
the number of free electrons through impact ionization in the same direction the
streamer propagates .

Hydrometeor
Hydrometeors are water particles either in liquid or ice form. Therefore, it will have a
relative permittivity ϵr. This value will depend on the state of the hydrometeor as well
as how fast changes the electric field due to the relaxation time. In practice, the relative

permittivity is either 3−4 with rapidly changing electric field and liquid water or 80 otherwise
[10, 13, 23].
To account for the hydrometeor in simulations numerous approaches exist, each with their
own trade-offs.
The simplest approach is done for high relative permittivity and is to assume that the
hydrometeor is a perfect conductor. This makes it so the hydrometeor does not need to
be considered itself, only that its boundary will have a constant potential [10].
A more thorough approach is to try to calculate the evolution of the bound charges in the
dielectric. Unlike in the streamer, where each charge corresponds to a particle, charges in
the hydrometeor are bound to a water molecule, that reorientates according to the electric
field, creating a non-zero charge density ρhm inside the hydrometeor [13].
To model the bound charges many approaches exist, one of them, discussed in Sadighi et
al. [11], is to model the hydrometeor column as a ionization column, with a limited number
of positive and negative charges that move freely in the hydrometeor. This is reasonable
because both water and ice have high dielectric constants and, when polarized, the effect
of the dielectric is equivalent to a plasma; as well as the fact that the seed electrons for
the positive electrons are created only by photoionization [11].
The final approach discussed and the one used in the current work is the one from [13].

Numerical framework
AMReX is a software framework containing all the functionality to write massively par-
allel, block-structured adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) applications [33, 34]. In block-
structured AMR, there is a hierarchy of logically rectangular grids. The computational
domain on each AMR level is decomposed into a union of rectangular domains while a
parallel data structure is used to allocate the values of each field to said grid.
Despite the AMR not being fully developed at the time of this thesis, AMReX is still use-
ful due to its integrated ghost cell system as well as some prebuilt linear solvers in the

2.9
Boundary conditions
When implementing the hydrometeor, their are two types of boundary conditions that can
be called problem boundaries, which refer to the boundaries of the whole problem domain
and hydrometeor boundaries that separate the inner and outer region of the hydrometeor.
The only fields that will need to have explicit boundary conditions will be ϕ (which will
implicitly impose boundary conditions on E), ne and ρhm. Other magnitudes like np and
nn do not have a flux term meaning that the value at a future step can be calculated
entirely from the values in the same position at the current step.

4
Conclusion
Baseline analysis of the electric field shows that a single hydrometeor can have sufficiently
large charge to create a local electric field above breakdown field.
Trying to run streamer simulations close to a charged hydrometeor that is not a perfect
conductor is complicated due to a number of factors. Most evidently, there are significant
boundary effects that cause disturbances in the electric field; which, in turn, affects all
densities and coefficients. This disturbances require a larger domain to be resolved but
other numerical restriction, such as the number of cells the initial streamer patch should
occupy, make it difficult to find a compromise. An ideal way to tackle this is with an adaptive
mesh refinement, meaning the grids can be larger or smaller dynamically depending on
the complexity of its contents.
Another limitation of this approach is the omission of collision between positive ions and
electrons, or collisions of negative ions.
Simulations run for sufficient time, show an evident increase on ionized particles but not
necessarily propagation of the charges. In the specific case of the problem at hand, this
propagation is as a positive streamer, which requires of photoionization to be explained.
For a hydrometeor, where the enhanced electric field is heavily localized, the effect of
photoionization seems much harder to implement and not necessarily strong enough to
ensure propagation.
When the streamer is immediately in contact with the hydrometeor. The hydrometeor
serves as a sink of electrons, and therefore of negative charges. This will therefore mean
two dipole moments: between the tip of the hydrometeor and the air that is left with an
excess of positive charge, as well as between the tip of the hydrometeor and the rest
of the hydrometeor. This later moment can only happen when the hydrometeor is not
considered as a perfect conductor.
4.1
Outlook
The 3D approach with conductivity, permittivity and charge of the hydrometeor as parame-
ters seems as a worthwhile simulation due to its adaptability to different particle enhanced
breakdown electric field problems. For example, by changing these parameters and the
shape, the hydrometeor could just be made of liquid water instead of ice. Besides chang-
ing parameters it is also compelling to try different asymmetric initial conditions to take
advantage of the fully 3D implementation.
However, as it stands now, the code needs improvements to be of use in such endeavors.
A pivotal improvement is the adaptive mesh refinement to have finer grids and results
with the minimal burden on the memory of the computer. Another area for improvement
is regarding the solve for the electric potential; for AMReX there is a noticeable difference
between solving ∇· (ε(r)∇ϕ(r)) = f(r) and ε(r)∇· (∇ϕ(r)) = f(r). Since ε is different in
and out the hydrometeor the second equation is not to be used instead of the first, but it
would be reasonable to compare both to see if there is a significant different value that
justifies a much larger computation time.
Instead of making the code more efficient it could also be improved by making it more
exhaustive; which would be done by considering more terms in the evolution of densities
such as collisions between charged particles or considering the motion of ions.These
