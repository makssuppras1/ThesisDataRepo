Abstract 
The climate crisis presents one of the greatest challenges of our time, with prognoses estimating a 2 degree 
increase in temperatures towards the middle of the century. According to international agreements in the 
energy field within the EU and the UN, Denmark is to be climate neutral in 2050. One of the great 
contributors to CO2 emissions in Denmark is the Danish building industry, totaling to 30% of CO2 emissions 
in Denmark. Emissions from buildings must therefore be decreased, if we are to reach the Danish national 
climate goals. Renovation of the inefficient building stock is a cost-efficient way to reduce the energy use in 
buildings, and thus reduce CO2 emissions from the building industry. 
The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate how the simulation and calculation behavior of building 
consultants influence the design of natural ventilation in renovated apartments. This was investigated 
through interviews with consultants working in the industry, followed by calculations and simulations to 
replicate the process of the consultants, and thereby investigate how the choice of calculation and 
simulation tools and detailing may influence the final design decisions. 
The investigations found that simulations of natural ventilation were very rarely prioritized in the building 
industry, due to mistrust of simulation tools, in addition to limitations to time and budget. Instead, most 
consultants interviewed worked in simpler tools like Excel, with equations from guidelines like SBi 213 & SBi 
202, and occasionally 1 zone simulations. 
In this project, it was found that the simple calculations based on guidelines used by consultants in low 
budget projects are not suited to investigate if stack-effect driven natural ventilation could cover the 
minimum air change rate required by the Danish building regulations. 1 zone simulation results being an 
average of the apartment, does not accurately depict individual room conditions, and therefore might give 
incomplete data as basis for the design choices. 
When replicating the methods of the consultants, the calculations made in this project all found larger 
opening areas necessary, than what was found sufficient by simulations to reach the requirements. This 
underlines the importance of working with dynamic simulations, as calculations based on average monthly 
values tend to give inaccurate results.  
This study shows, that the results found by the consultants’ calculation and simulation behaviors might not 
demonstrate that natural ventilation can be a sufficient and useful alternative to mechanical ventilation. 
This project has shown through use of multiple zone simulation tools, that stack-effect driven natural 
ventilation could reach the building requirements for overheating hours and air flow rates in renovated 
apartments. Therefore stack-effect driven natural ventilation may be a feasible and cheap solution to 
maintaining indoor climate when renovating apartments. 
This project also shows that using some of the most common calculation tools and guidelines would 
provide compromised results, which could lead to over or under dimensioned ventilation systems, if not 
entirely ruling out the possibility of having a stack-effect driven natural ventilation system. The tools used 
in the industry might therefore greatly impact the design choices made by consultants in the industry.

Project presentation 
The scope of this master’s thesis is to investigate how the simulation and calculation behavior of building 
consultants influence the design of natural ventilation in renovated apartments. This will be investigated by 
conducting interviews with consultants from the Danish building industry, which will be considered 
representative for the design process in the building industry. On basis of the responses from the 
interviews, there will be conducted calculation and simulations, to replicate the process of the consultants, 
and thus investigate how the choice of calculation and simulation tools and detailing may influence the 
final design decisions. 
This study will examine one apartment which will be modelled with two different systems for ventilation. 
One will have only natural ventilation, with trickle vents installed for supply air and stack-effect by a 
chimney for exhaust air, while the other system is a mixed-mode system with trickle vents for supply air 
and a mechanical fan for exhaust air. 
By this analysis, the following questions should be answered:  
- 
How does the calculation and simulation tool results differ between simple and advanced tools?  
- 
To what degree do the consultants’ calculation and simulation behavior and simulation detailing 
influence the design of natural ventilation?  
- 
How can there be compensated for inaccurate simulations?  
The impact of choice of tool and simulation behaviors on the results will be evaluated by comparing key 
parameters found in the simulations and calculations. As this project is done from the consultants’ 
perspective, performance parameters are chosen from the building regulations. Among those, focus will be 
on overheating hours, air change rates and heating demand.

Introduction 
The climate crisis presents one of the greatest challenges of our time. Current estimates predict more than 
2 degrees increase in global temperatures towards the middle of the century [1], forcing us to use all 
existing measures to minimize the global climate crisis.  
The Danish building industry totals to 30% of CO2 emissions in Denmark [2]. The international agreements 
in the energy field within the EU and the UN, targets 30 pct. reduction of emissions from buildings, 
agriculture, and transportation in 2030, and Denmark is to be climate neutral in 2050 [3]. To achieve these 
goals, it is therefore necessary to find and use the most efficient ways to reduce CO2 emissions from 
buildings. 
Renovation of the inefficient building stock is a cost-efficient way to reduce the energy use in buildings. In 
2017, less than 3% of the total building stock in Europe was considered highly energy efficient, with an 
energy performance label (EPC) of at least label A [4], and renovation of the 97% inefficient buildings would 
therefore greatly attribute to achieve the climate goals for 2050. According to prognosis for the building 
industry, about 3% of the building stock is expected to be renovated each year [5]. 
One of the best ways to reduce energy use in buildings is the control and reduction of the building 
envelope air leakage (increasing air tightness). The heat loss due to envelope air leakage has been 
quantified to represent up to 30% in a new study of the total heat loss from buildings [6]. As the 
airtightness improves, there is a need to compensate for the lost infiltration to maintain good Indoor air 
quality. 
As we spend about 90% of our lives indoors [5], indoor air quality in buildings should not be neglected. This 
means, that the quality of the building and the indoor environment are significant factors for our health 
and wellbeing. Fresh air can be provided by designing a mechanical or natural ventilation system as a part 
of the renovation of the apartments [7], with each type having their own possibilities and challenges.  
This project will investigate how different design approaches in the industry influences the design choices, 
when designing natural ventilation in renovated apartments. This is investigated through interviews with 
consultants working in the industry, as well as simulations and calculations to explore the different 
approaches described by the interviewed consultants. It’s thereby the hope to discover how renovation 
decisions are made in the industry, when renovating apartments, and if these can be improved to advance 
the building industry towards a more environmentally friendly future.

Background 
Climate change and the building sector 
Climate change constitutes one of the great challenges of our time. Current estimates predict more than 2 
degrees increase in global temperatures towards the middle of the century [1], with vast consequences for 
the environment. 
According to international agreements in the energy field within the EU and the UN, Denmark is to be 
climate neutral in 2050 [3], while The Danish primary minister Mette Frederiksen in 2022 proclaimed that 
Denmark is to reduce emissions by 70% before 2030 [8], be climate neutral by 2045 and have a total 
reduction of 110% compared to 1990 [9]. 
One of the great contributors to CO2 emissions in Denmark is the building sector, responsible for about 30% 
of the total CO2 emissions in Denmark [2]. In order to reach the climate goals, emissions must be decreased 
significantly. The international agreements for the energy field within the EU and the UN targets a 30 pct. 
reduction of emissions from buildings, agriculture and transportation in 2030 [3], which forces the building 
sector to minimize their emissions fast.  
In order to achieve these goals, it is important to find the most efficient ways to reduce CO2 emissions from 
buildings and the building sector. Renovating buildings is a cost-efficient way to reduce CO2 emissions. A 
report from The International Renewable Energy Agency shows, that the cost of reducing CO2 emissions 
through optimizing energy efficiency is less than half of the same reduction through new renewable energy 
sources [5].  
Within the lifespan of buildings in Denmark, for new energy effective buildings, most of the CO2 emissions 
tends to be in the construction phase. However, for old, energy inefficient buildings, most of the CO2 
emissions are in their use phase [2]. Despite having the highest share of energy efficient buildings in 
Europe, topping at 8% of buildings having an EPC label A, Denmark still has 92% energy inefficient buildings 
[4].  
According to Ramboll, renovation is generally the most sustainable solution when it comes to both CO2 
emissions and cost, when compared to demolishing and rebuilding [2], and extensive renovations are 
anticipated in the years to come. Prognoses estimate that of the total building stock existing today, 70-90% 
of the existing buildings are expected to be in use in 2050 and about 3% of the building stock is expected to 
be renovated each year towards 2030 and 2050 [5].  
Renovation of the Danish building stock is therefore a significant contributor to achieving the European 
climate goals of 30% reduction of emissions from buildings, agriculture and transportation in 2030 and the 
Danish national goals of being CO2 neutral in 2050. 
 
Renovation  
In Denmark, one third of the population live in apartment buildings, while in Copenhagen the number is 
86% [10]. 65% of the current building stock in Denmark is built before 1980, and thus before any energy 
requirements were established [2]. Therefore, most of the existing apartment buildings to be renovated 
were originally built with passive natural ventilation [11]. 
Old apartments with natural ventilation often does not hold enough space for centralized ventilation 
systems [12]. This was also the conclusion of a questionnaire to consultants in the Danish building industry

conducted by DTU master students in 2022 [13]. In this questionnaire, consultants were asked about the 
biggest challenges when renovating apartment buildings. Here the most commonly replied was “Lack of 
space for installation”, followed by “Limitations of project budget” This shows that for some renovation 
projects, natural ventilation is the only possible solution within the budget and physical limitations. 
In 2020, Ramboll published a report on the benefits of renovating old buildings, compared to demolishing 
and reconstructing new buildings [2]. This report is based on LCA (lifecycle assessment) and LCC (lifecycle 
cost) of 16 real or planned renovation projects in Denmark. In the report, it was concluded that the most 
optimal solution, both emission-wise and cost-wise, in most cases were to renovate, instead of demolishing 
the old buildings and having new energy-efficient buildings constructed. For the 16 buildings in the report, 
4 scenarios were investigated for each of them (see Figure 1):

-

-

- 
Scenario 4: the existing building is demolished, and a new building is constructed.

The results of the analysis show that especially scenario 2 and 3 had both cost and emission savings, 
compared to having a new building constructed. However, the small difference between the two scenarios, 
2 and 3 indicates that having new technical systems in general was not cost-efficient. 
Around 75-90% of the existing buildings in OECD countries are expected to still be in use by 2050, 
underlining the importance of renovating the existing building stock. According to a 2018 report by 
Rockwool, the entire existing building stock must be renovated to be low-energy buildings by 2050 to keep 
the global temperature increase at “well-below 2 degrees”.  
An analysis of 365,000 houses on sale done by Copenhagen Economics, found that for each increase on the 
energy label scale, the prices would increase by 5600-8100 EUR for a house of 100m2 [5], see Figure 2.

One of the most effective ways to reduce energy use in buildings is the control and reduction of the 
building envelope air leakage (increasing air tightness). Heat loss due to envelope air leakage has been 
quantified to represent up to 30% of the total heat loss from buildings [6], and is therefore a great 
improvement point for building renovations. One of the most cost cost-effective improvements when 
renovating old buildings is replacement of windows. This may both reduce heat loss through the window, 
but also decrease the infiltration rates, as old windows are a source of infiltration.  
The Danish building regulation (BR18, Byggereglementet) defines the building regulations, demands, and 
detailed requirements all buildings have to adhere to. These requirements secure that buildings are 
constructed satisfactorily, in terms of both safety and health of the user. In the Danish building regulation 
two voluntary renovation classes are presented. To reach the renovation class 1 in the Danish building 
regulation, the buildings energy use must be reduced by at least 30 kWh/m2, compared to before the 
renovation, and the total energy use pr. m2 must not exceed 52.5 kWh/m2 annually. To reach the 
renovation class 2 in the Danish building regulation, the building’s energy use must be reduced by at least 
30 kWh/m2, compared to before the renovation, with the total energy use pr. m2 not exceeding 70 kWh/m2 
annually.  
For renovation class 1, there are additional requirements regarding indoor climate that must be met [14]: 
1. Documentation of the thermal indoor climate must be done for the critical zones. Calculations must 
be based on Design Reference Year (DRY 2013), however, documentation for residence buildings 
can be more simple and done according to SBi 213. 
2. There must be a fresh air supply of at least 0.3 l/s pr. m2 heated floor area. 
3. Renovation measures must not result in risk of humidity damage. 
4. The air change must be provided by a ventilation system. If the ventilation system is mechanical, 
this needs to have supply air in the dry rooms, while exhaust must be in the wet rooms. The 
ventilation system must have heat recovery. However, if another ventilation system is used, this 
must meet the building regulations, and the primary energy use must not be increased.  
According to “SBi 2020:12 Hvidbog - Ventilation af eksisterende etageboliger” [11], the requirements in the 
Danish building regulation apply to new buildings and for “in-depth” renovated buildings. For renovations 
at level 1 and 2 in Figure 1, the requirements are, that the existing ventilation performance must remain at 
the same state as when established. Another paragraph leaves space for dispensation from installation of

mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, when there are physical limitations to the ventilation system in 
the renovated residence building. 
 
Airtightness and indoor climate 
One of the effects of renovation is a decrease in air infiltration rates in the renovated buildings. Infiltration 
describes the flow of fresh air through leaks in the building envelope. New buildings tend to minimize the 
infiltration by the use of membranes or other seals to avoid unnecessary heat losses [15].  
In a study of renovated buildings in Latvia, a blower door test showed an air infiltration rate of 0.71-0.8 l/s 
pr. m2 at 50 Pa in the study cases [16]. While no tests were performed on the studied buildings before 
renovation, tests on a similar building showed an air leakage rate of 1.81 l/s pr. m2. If the study cases and 
the non-renovated building are comparable, the renovation thus saw a reduction in air infiltration rate of 
about 56-61% in the renovated buildings.  
The building sector has likewise improved the airtightness of newly built houses, focusing on reducing draft 
and minimizing heat loss. An investigation from 2000-2002 on 31 newly built row-houses in Germany, 
showed an air infiltration rate of average 0.27 l/s pr. m2 @50 Pa in 2000. After 2 years, only a small increase 
was seen, with air infiltration rate now averaging at 0.31 l/s pr. m2 @50 Pa [17]. In the Danish Building 
Regulations of 2018 (BR18) set requirements for the airtightness of new buildings are found in §263 [14]. 
Current regulations are 1.0 l/s pr. m² of the heated floor area at a pressure difference of 50 Pa. The 
airtightness of the building envelope can be documented with a blower door test. If a pressure test is not 
conducted, 1.5 l/s pr. m² should be used when calculating the building’s heating demand. This requirement 
is set in order to generate motivation for always having a pressure test conducted. 
As evident from published research, renovation of buildings can lead to significant reduction of the 
infiltration rate. While increased airtightness of the building envelope may reduce heat loss, and thus 
energy use in the apartment, this also have some negative aspects. As the infiltration through leaks in the 
envelope is reduced, the age of air is increasing, and might be leading to poor indoor air quality, if this is 
not compensated for in other ways.  
Replacing old air with fresh air in renovated apartments can be done by refurbishing the existing natural 
ventilation system, installing a new mechanical ventilation system with heat recovery or simply having 
mechanical exhaust fans in the wet rooms. This project will solely look at ventilation solutions by natural 
ventilation and natural ventilation with a mechanical exhaust in the wet rooms, as these are the ventilation 
systems appliable for projects with space limitations, small budgets or both. 
In the Danish building regulation, there are no requirements for the indoor air quality for residence 
buildings. Legal requirements are in place for overheating hours and air flow rate. As this project is 
conducted from the consultants’ perspective, the key parameters investigated are overheating hours, air 
flow rate and total heating demand, with overheating hours and air flow rate used to design and evaluate 
the performance of the ventilation system, while total heating demand will be included in the final 
evaluation.

Natural Ventilation 
Natural ventilation is often categorized as the three options: single-sided ventilation, cross-sided ventilation 
and stack-effect ventilation (Figure 3). The driving forces for natural ventilation can be either wind or 
buoyancy or a combination of the two. Here wind-driven natural ventilation can be the dominant 
ventilation form during summer months, where windows are open to avoid overheating, and buoyancy-
driven ventilation is dominant during the cold months, where windows are rarely open and the 
temperature difference between indoor and outdoor increases. 
Single sided ventilation is present in rooms with window openings on just one side of the room, while cross-
sided ventilation happens if there are openings on two opposite facades of the room or apartment with 
airflow crossing through. Buoyancy-driven stack ventilation (stack-effect) utilizes thermal buoyancy forces 
to provide airflow. Buoyancy-driven stack ventilation runs on from air density differences, causing warm air 
to rise through the chimney and create an upward airflow. The resulting negative pressure forces outdoor 
air in through the adjustable air inlets (manual or automatic) or leaks in the building envelope. To ensure 
sufficient buoyancy forces a chimney, stair shaft or atriums can be utilized [18].  
This project will be looking at all three types of ventilation, but with focus on stack ventilation, as this is 
often the existing ventilation strategy in the type of buildings that will need renovation in the near future. 
Many old apartments are built with a chimney, for this purpose [15, 19]. 
 
Figure 3. Natural ventilation principles. A) Single-sided ventilation, B) Cross ventilation, C) Stack ventilation. From [15]. 
In renovated residence buildings, where the tightness of the building envelope has been reduced, 
adjustable air inlets, also known as trickle vents, can be installed when changing windows as a part of the 
renovation of the apartments [7]. Having trickle vents installed in the window frames would help ensure a 
supply of fresh air.  
The success of natural ventilation is based on its stability to ensure indoor air quality, along with sufficient 
air change rate and cooling of the buildings. However, the performance of natural ventilation is largely 
depending on outdoor conditions, with factors such as building orientation, temperatures, windspeed, 
humidity and topography being key parameters affecting the use of natural ventilation.  
Propper and highly detailed simulations of natural ventilation could therefore be important to document 
the indoor climate and ensure thermal comfort all year around and avoid unnecessary heat losses, resulting 
in higher energy bills [15]. 
As there are several possible opinions, decision strategies, guidelines, and approaches in the building 
industry, when working on renovation of apartment buildings, it’s highly relevant to investigate some of the

simulation and calculation behaviors of consultants and companies in the Danish building industry. This 
investigation will hopefully reveal how the different simulation and calculation behaviors influences the 
design decisions for ventilation of renovated apartments.

Methodology 
Interviews with consultants 
The goal of this project is to investigate the impact of simulation and calculating behavior in the building 
industry. To gain this insight, this project has sought to replicate the work process and behavior of 
consultants in the field to explore and demonstrate how different approaches can affect the final design 
choices of the consultants.  
In order to investigate how decisions are made in the industry, it was important to interview people 
working in the industry. Consultants in the building industry might work according to different priorities 
than what is expected or anticipated, e.g. time spend on the project, cost for the costumer, performance of 
the design, authority requirements, expectations from the building constructer, etc. Values and priorities 
might depend on the individual consultant or engineering firm. Therefore, it’s important the get input from 
consultants that are hands-on with renovation projects. In this project, investigation will be limited to the 
design of natural ventilation and mixed-mode ventilation. 
Interviews were selected as investigative method, as qualitative answers to the questions could lead to 
unexpected insights, not disclosed in multiple-choice surveys. Potential findings might be further 
investigated and elaborated in these interviews. The main limitation to conducting individual interviews, is 
fewer respondents to interviews than would have been preferred and possibly obtained, had multiple-
choice surveys been conducted instead.  
For this project, 5 consultants from 4 different companies were interviewed individually. Each interview 
was approx. 45 minutes and conducted via Microsoft Teams. The questions for the interview were selected 
with feedback from Kevin and Jakub, with all participants being anonymous. The questions were directed to 
people who work with renovation and ventilation systems, or people who have knowledge about the 
simulation and calculation behavior in the company they represent. Interviews were conducted in Danish 
and questions and quotes have been translated to English for this report. The combined feedback from the 
interviews will in the following thesis be assumed to represent existing calculation and simulation behaviors 
in the building industry.

Presentation of case building 
The location of the apartment used as model in this project is in Copenhagen on the address Ryesgade 25, 
2200 Copenhagen (Figure 4) The apartment building was constructed in 1906 and was in-depth renovated 
in 2018 [20, 21].  
 
Figure 4. Ryesgade 25 apartment building. From [21]. 
As a part on the renovation, new insulation were added to the façade walls, new roof was installed, as the 
old attic has been transformed to new rooftop apartments and all windows have been replaced with 
“dannebrosvinduer” with linked frames of 1 + 2 layers. It is estimated that the total energy spend on 
heating has been reduced by 92.5 kWh/m2, corresponding to about 70% reduction. It is expected that the 
energy used will be slightly higher in reality, as window openings by the occupants have not been included 
in the estimate.  
The total floor area of the apartment building was increased from 3500m2 to 4500m2 and consist of a total 
of 42 individual apartments between 62-142m2. The estimated value pr. m2 was doubled as a result of the 
improved quality of the apartments. After the renovation, the apartment building reached the 
requirements for “renoverings klasse 1”, “energimærke a2010 “and achieving DGNB silver.  
The apartment used in the project, is a 3-room apartment of 61.2m2, with one adult bedroom, one child 
bedroom, a living room, a kitchen, and a corridor with the entry door. The two external facades are 
oriented towards southwest and northeast (Figure 5).

Calculations and simulations 
Detail level of simulations and calculations based on interviews 
Based on the responses from the 5 consultants interviewed, several approaches are identified. 
According to those approaches calculations and simulations will be performed at different detail levels. Not 
all guidelines and tools used by the consultants are suitable or made for the purpose to investigate the 
performance of trickle vents and stack-effect with exhaust via the chimney, however, attempts will be 
made to follow all the approaches identified based on the interviews.  
The ventilation systems investigated in the project will be stack-effect driven natural ventilation, with fresh 
air supplied through trickle vents installed in dry rooms and exhaust through the chimney. For the purpose 
of comparison, simulations will also be done on a ventilation system with mechanical exhaust installed in 
the wet rooms.

If the pressure difference across the façade is unchanged or with limited changes, it would indicate that 
stack-effect driven natural ventilation or ventilation with mechanical exhaust would be sufficient with just 
fresh air through leaks in the façade. However, if there is a significant change in the pressure difference 
across the façade, as the infiltration rate changes, it indicates that trickle vents will have an influence on a 
stack-effect driven natural ventilation, and that the pressure rise that the exhaust fan needs to provide can 
be limited by adding fresh air inlets in renovated apartments with low infiltration rates.

Interview findings 
The following interviews highlight some of the approaches and methods used in the industry to simulate 
and calculate natural ventilation in buildings. Interviews were conducted with 5 consultants from the 
industry of different backgrounds and years of experience; some consultants worked as project managers, 
some was newly educated and had limited experience, some used to work with natural ventilation, but 
worked in other areas now, one was the owner of a consultant company, and one worked with ventilation 
of mostly offices. Most were educated at DTU. The findings in the interviews are conjectured to be 
representative of the methods used in the building industry to simulate and calculate ventilation. The 
simulation tools and calculation methods used in the industry varied between the companies, with no 
common tool nor standard settings preferred.  
One consultant stated that their preferred simulation tool used is BSim, due to the short process time from 
input is set to results can be obtained. The consultant most often worked in just one critical zone, as it was 
their expectation, that 1 zone simulations give 70-80% of the data needed and therefore provides sufficient 
data to design the ventilation system in the building.  As general input they worked with DRY annual 
weather files, airtightness based on SBi 213, using single sided or cross-ventilation, wind profile according 
to surroundings and with wind pressure coefficient from the software. The simulations are usually run with 
one sample every 2-4 hours. The consultant expressed worries about using IDA-ICE for simulations, finding 
it too complex, with a risk of wrong input or getting lost in the software. Small projects in the company of 
this consultant were done by simple calculations, based on guidelines. To the consultant’s best knowledge, 
the company approach does not consider stack-effect driven natural ventilation. If stack-effect is taken into 
consideration, it is modelled as tall zones with roof top windows, and mostly at room level. One reason for 
this, is the assumption that the pressure loss in the chimney will be too great to be efficient on lower 
floors. This consultant mostly worked with mechanical ventilation and automatic control of window 
opening, shading etc., with PI-controlled setpoints.  
Another consultant stated that they most often use an Excel sheet made in the company, which includes 
input like internal gains, external gains, window opening area, building envelope, etc. The consultant finds 
that calculations in Excel are enough to sufficiently estimate needed window opening area to ensure air 
change rate to maintain thermal comfort. The primary function of the Excel sheet was to calculate heat 
transfer in a single critical zone with single sided ventilation. When doing simulations there would only be 
occasional multiple zone investigations, as they found that 1 zone calculations and simulations provided 
most of the needed information. This consultant stated that ventilation based on natural ventilation is “a 
thing of the past” for new buildings, and only looked at window opening area for the summer month, as 
overheating is the only factor regulated with natural ventilation.  
The third consultant stated that they rarely performed any in depth investigation or simulations of natural 
ventilation, mostly using simple guidelines to avoid overheating.  For the winter months, mechanical 
ventilation with heat recovery was their usual solution, with the control typically based on CO2 
setpoints. When working on apartments, the consultant stated that the calculations are often limited to 
rules of thumb rather than simulations.  For renovation projects the company rarely performed 
simulations. In bigger projects, however, the consultant reported that they would use the software IES-VE, 
to simulate indoor climate and energy demand. When working in IES-VE, the modelling would be of high 
detailing, with most inputs considered e.g. airtightness, with assumptions based on guidelines and 
regulations. This company usually did not consider stack-effect, the exception for this being some bigger 
projects involving simulations in IES-VE, however, here stack-effect is also only included if roof windows are 
present. The consultant was not aware of chimney components in IES-VE to include stack-effect, and upon 
presentation, expressed concerns about the pressure loss in the chimney and on how to legally document 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
that natural ventilation would be sufficient. The consultant reported that they mostly worked with 
automatic systems, and that the company, after installation, has remote access to indoor climate data from 
installed sensors and can adjust setpoints, upon demand from the costumer. 
In the company of a fourth consultant, they used both the dynamic simulation tool IES-VE and calculations 
based on SBi 213, SBi 202 and “Branchevejledning for indeklimaberegninger”. Calculations and simulations 
were performed on critical zones or critical apartments, based on window size and orientation, usually on 
the top floor. In projects with a high budget, simulations are done at a fairly high level of detailing in IES-VE 
and weather-files, wind profile and constant infiltration rate, based on SBi 213, would be included. This 
consultant voiced mistrusting the simulation software, as there is not enough transparency on how the 
calculations are done. Stack-effect was considered when skylight windows are installed, however, stack-
effect via chimney was to the consultant’s knowledge never considered a possible solution. Upon 
presentation of the concept, the consultant responded that they expected that an individual chimney had 
to be used for each apartment to avoid air pollution between the apartments. In general, mechanical 
ventilation was preferred, due to expectations from the costumer, as mechanical ventilation ensured 
greater guarantee that indoor air quality classes would be met at all times. In cases where IES-VE 
simulations were performed, the airflow through windows would be compared to the airflow calculated 
from SBi 202 formulas, and the results with the biggest needed opening area would be considered the best 
solution. To their statement, they never asked the architects for smaller openings, as they had only 
experienced cases, where opening area had to be increased. Again solutions based on natural ventilation 
were investigated only on summer scenarios, and natural ventilation would happen through manual or 
automatic openings of windows. This consultant worked with an infiltration rate equivalent of 0.25 air 
change rate, based on recommendations from more experienced colleagues.  
A fifth consultant used primarily BSim with the standard package. Input, factors, and parameters used in 
calculations and simulations were based on experience and SBi 213 and SBi 202, while schedules were 
based on “Branchevejledning for indeklimaberegninger”.  
One consultant had recently worked on a project involving a protected heritage building. As this building 
was protected under Danish law, it was not possible to make any changes to the building, limiting the 
options for mechanical ventilation. The solution was to ad fan-assisted exhaust ventilation in the chimney, 
but with no supply air, as the building was considered untight and it was therefore assumed that infiltration 
through leaks would be sufficient to maintain good indoor air quality. Typical investigations performed is 
how often windows should be open to maintain thermal comfort and good indoor air quality.  
Based on the feedback from the interviews, it is clear that the consultants in the industry have varying 
approaches and concerns when working with natural ventilation. The different approaches will be 
investigated further in the following, where the responses from the interviews have been used to layout 
five different approaches, which will be investigated in this project. These are not necessary covering all 
practices in the industry but will be considered representative of the common approaches in the industry 
for this project.

Discussion 
Consultant interviews 
The interviews conducted were used as the foundation on which the five approaches investigated in this 
project was based. These five approaches were assumed to be representative for the approaches used by 
the consultants in the industry, when working on projects regarding renovation of apartment buildings, 
where natural ventilation is used, or where mechanical ventilation with heat recovery cannot be installed 
due to physical or budget limitations. 
However, none of the consultants interviewed had previous experience working on projects similar to the 
project case used in this master’s thesis. Basing general approaches of the entire industry on five 
consultants might be inaccurate, meaning that the approaches investigated in this project might differ from 
the approach from consultants working specifically on renovation of residence buildings. The simulation 
and calculation tools used in this project are unlikely to be equivalent to the tools used by all consultants. 
None of the consultants interviewed used the simulation tool IDA-ICE, and the exact calculations done in 
the consultants’ own Excel sheets are unknown. Lastly, there was not done any following up interviews of 
the consultants as this project progressed, due to timewise limitations. Despite these limitations, interviews 
with consultants will still be viewed as the best way to get insight into the methods and decisions by 
consultants, and for the sake of the project, these will be viewed as representative. 
 
Natural ventilation 
In the interviews conducted, all consultants said that simulations of natural ventilation were very rarely 
prioritized, with one consultant referring to natural ventilation as “a thing of the past”. Often priorities are 
made based on limitations to time and budget. This project, however, shows that it’s possible to document 
the performance of an inexpensive natural ventilation system, through slightly more advanced simulation 
tools, if the consultant companies prioritized the time and budget.  
This documentation of the performance of a stack-effect driven natural ventilation system is not possible 
by the use of the simple calculation tools used by the consultants. Therefore, the results found by the 
consultants might not be similar to the findings in this study, that natural ventilation can be a sufficient and 
useful alternative to mechanical ventilation. 
Several of the consultant stated, that if an exhaust fan was installed, fresh air would always find a way into 
the apartment, in cases where no trickle vents were installed, or when the trickle vents are closed by the 
occupants. The results from the investigation of pressure rise in the exhaust fan and infiltration rate show, 
that there can be a significant increase in the pressure rise that needs to be provided by the exhaust fan 
when the building envelope is tighter than 1l/s pr. m2 @50Pa. This was verified by comparing the 
performance of the same rooftop fan working at two different conditions, both of which had the same low 
infiltration rate through the envelope. One scenario, however, had trickle vents installed in the façade. 
Here it was shown, that there was an increase in the fan power for the rooftop fan without trickle vents in 
the apartment façade, compared to the rooftop fan where trickle vents where utilized. This means that the 
use and impact of having trickle vents largely depend on how tight the building envelope is after the 
renovation. 
All the simulations of the ground floor apartment with stack-effect driven natural ventilation had very few 
hours with air change rate of less than 0.5h-1, with the case with lowest time outside the requirements 
having only 20 hours (equivalent of 0.2% of the year). This also means that the exhaust through the 
==PAGE:63==
63 
 
chimney could be reduced, and thereby reducing heat loss, while still providing the required air change 
rate. Reducing the chimney exhaust could be done by decreasing the size of the chimney shaft for the 
apartment at ground floor, or by having a damper installed at the chimney inlet. This will ensure that all 
apartments always have the minimum needed air change rate, without unnecessary heat losses. A key 
benefit of having a mechanical exhaust fan, is that it makes it possible to have a more stable air change rate 
in the apartment, without being as dependent on external conditions.  
Having cheap automatic systems developed and accessible to the marked could in the future make it 
possible to optimize the control of natural ventilation systems. Automatic solutions with CO2-controlled 
trickle vents and dampers would ensure good indoor air quality, as CO2 works very well as a proxy for other 
air pollutants, while also limiting the heat losses. This would greatly benefit the use of stack-effect driven 
natural ventilation systems.  
 
Calculations and simulations 
The simple calculations based on SBi 213 used by consultants in low budget projects is not made for the 
purpose of investigating, if stack-effect driven natural ventilation could cover the minimum air change rate 
required by the Danish building regulations. These calculations are based on a simplified relation between 
air flow rate and percentile opening area for buildings with single sided and cross-sided ventilation. The 
“Sommerkomfort” tool integrated in Be18 is calculating the maximum air flow rate needed to avoid 
overheating hours. Here it has not been possible to find any indications on how those calculations are 
done, except that cooling by window openings starts at indoor temperatures of 23°C. Therefore, these 
tools are not optimal when designing natural ventilation systems and are met with skepticism by some of 
the consultants.  
The Excel tool used in this project likewise has several relevant limitations. Firstly, it’s based on SBi 202, 
which are guidelines set for natural ventilation in office spaces, and therefore might be less accurate for 
apartment buildings. Another limitation is the lack of options for multiple zones, with only 1 zone 
calculations possible, which may therefore not accurately depict conditions in all rooms. Most calculations 
are furthermore based on monthly average values, such as temperature, solar radiation, windspeed and 
wind-direction, with a few peak load calculations as supplement. Lastly, the chimney has been simplified as 
a normal rooftop window, meaning that pressure losses in the chimney are neglected in calculations. 
Therefore, it is likely that the results are simplified and not showing the whole picture. 
The calculations based on SBi 213 and SBi 202 all had opening areas calculated much higher, than what was 
found sufficient in the simulations to reach the requirements for the minimum air change rate. Also, the 
amount of overheating hours and maximum temperatures in the apartment were significantly higher for 
calculations, than in the results based on simulations. This shows the importance of working with dynamic 
simulations, as calculations based on average monthly values tend to give inaccurate results, consequently 
ruling out the option of having stack-effect driven natural ventilation and underestimating the actual 
heating demand of the apartments. 
Most consultants never considered stack-effect driven natural ventilation and for those who did, 
considerations often concerned cooling of office atriums, where automatic temperature-controlled rooftop 
windows are utilized. Having stack-effect utilized by the chimney was meet with some skepticism from the 
interviewees, as some of the consultants expressed previous experience with significant pressure losses in 
chimney shafts. None of the consultants had knowledge of the possibility to use components like or with 
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
similar use as the chimney component used in IDA-ICE for this project. Therefore, it’s understandable that 
the consultants do not consider stack-effect driven natural ventilation utilizing the chimney shafts in the 
apartment buildings. However, this project has shown that this might be a feasible solution worth 
considering when renovating apartment buildings. 
The results from the 1 zone simulations and multiple zone apartment simulations in IDA-ICE, shows that 1 
zone simulation results are an average of the apartment. Working on an apartment as an average of all 
rooms does not show if some dry rooms do not reach the minimum requirements. Relying on just 1 zone 
simulations therefore does not ensure that all rooms in the apartment are within the conditions needed for 
thermal comfort, or that the air change rate is within what’s minimum demanded. The choice of 
consultants to only investigate in 1 zone may therefore result in some rooms not reaching minimum 
requirements for air change rate, temperature etc. This was confirmed in the multiple zone simulations, 
where none of the apartments on 4th floor with stack-effect driven natural ventilation reached the air 
change rate required when only 1 trickle vent was installed in each dry room. Therefore multiple zone 
simulations provides additional data, which can significantly affect the design decisions. 
 
For all calculations and simulations, the air change rate is higher on the ground floor. This has shown to be 
due to more stack-effect. A consequence of the intensity of the stack-effect on ground floor is, however, 
that the heat-losses are approximately doubled for the ground floor apartment, compared to the 
apartment on 4th floor. This is an important aspect to be aware of, when having stack-effect driven natural 
ventilation in renovated apartments. The difference between the floors also shows the importance of 
simulations and calculations on not just the apartment expected to be the critical zone from a thermal 
condition perspective, but to also include apartments on different floors. In the simulations where the 
chimney was replaced by a mechanical exhaust, all the apartments reached the requirements for minimum 
air change rates and overheating hours, while also having lower heating demand. Mechanical exhaust thus 
tends to be more stable solution, if the budget allows. 
 
According to the description of the apartment building on Ryesgade 25, the actual building reached 
renovation class 1, however, the energy demand found for the apartments in this investigation largely 
depended on the approach used. All calculations done according to SBi 213 and by use of Be18 show an 
energy demand within renovation class 1 for the apartments on both ground floor and 4th floor. However, 
this is contradicted by the calculations done in the Excel sheet based on SBi 202. Here only the 4th floor 
reached renovation class 1, while ground floor only reached renovation class 2. When energy demand is 
found through simulations, none of the ground floor apartments with stack-effect driven natural ventilation 
reached the requirements for neither class 1 nor 2. For the 4th floor, only the 1 zone simulation with stack-
effect driven natural ventilation reached renovation class 1. When mechanical exhaust was used, all the 
apartments modelled at standard or advanced level reached renovation class 2. This investigation thus 
shows that the renovation class calculated for buildings greatly depends on calculation or simulation 
approach used for the estimation. 
 
The work on this project has shown the importance of being able access the calculations behind the 
simulation tools. This allows the consultants to verify that the model simulated is constructed in a way, that 
can be used to investigate natural ventilation. The complexity of IDA-ICE might result in unexpected 
==PAGE:65==
65 
 
performances of the components used. As seen in the results from the advanced simulations, the air 
change rate decreased when the trickle vents were modelled based on a damper component. This was not 
as initially expected, as the first tests of the volume-flow through the two trickle vent models performed 
approximately similar. By having access to the code behind the calculation, a probable explanation was 
found; that the power-law coefficient calculated was based on a performance test at 20˚C and therefore 
only accurate at this temperature. The mistrust of the consultants may therefore in some cases be valid, as 
transparency can be limited in some of the most used simulation tools in the industry. This is a clear 
example on how simulation tools can be so complicated that sources of errors can go unnoticed. 
The best approach to avoid inaccurate simulations and calculations is therefore to use simulation tools and 
components made for the purpose. Here the preferred simulation tool is one that gives access to the code 
behind the software and with possibility to extract data, which makes it possible to confirm that the 
program works as intended. Alternatively, the results from simulation tools can be compared to results 
found by calculations based on SBi 202, “Branchevejledning for indeklimaberegninger” or “Naturlig 
ventilation til køling af boliger”. However, for these calculations to give relevant results, it’s important to 
use equations made for the exact purpose and not simplifications, to get a relevant refence point. If the 
results from the calculations and simulations are approximately similar, it is probable that the simulation 
results are accurate, otherwise it may be best to be conservative, when making the final design and 
dimensioning decisions.  
In cases where no simulations are performed, it’s unlikely that the calculations of the performance of stack-
effect driven natural ventilation is accurately estimated. Having stack-effect driven natural ventilation 
based on inaccurate assumptions might lead to high heating demands. Therefore, to ensure a more stable 
ventilations system, one solution could be to have trickle vents installed in all dry rooms, while having 
mechanical exhaust with a fixed air flow rate, providing required air change rate in all rooms. For this 
project it was found that an air flow rate of 0.5 l/s pr. m2 was needed by the exhaust fan to ensure the 
minimum air change rate in the critical zone furthest from the wet rooms, however, other building 
geometries might give other results. 
At the time of writing, limited data is available on the infiltration rate of renovated apartment buildings. 
Investigations of infiltration rates in renovated apartments at different levels of renovation could therefore 
be of great use. One point of interest could be the impact of installation of modern energy-efficient 
windows. This is one of the most common and most cost-effective improvements, that can be done in the 
old apartment buildings, and it would be interesting to investigate the effect on the tightness of the 
building with only this change, as opposed to an in-depth renovation. Having more knowledge of what 
infiltration rates could be expected after renovation, could also impact the decision to install trickle vents 
or not.

Conclusion 
In this project the calculation and simulation behaviors of consultants in the industry were investigated. 
The consultants said that natural ventilation was rarely used and that time to calculate and simulate on 
natural ventilation was rarely prioritized. 
This project has documented that by using multiple zone simulation tools, the performance of natural 
ventilation can be documented, showing that stack-effect driven natural ventilation could reach the 
building requirements for overheating hours and air flow rates in renovated apartments. This project also 
shows that using some of the most common calculation tools and guidelines would provide compromised 
results, which could lead to over or under dimensioned ventilation systems, if not entirely ruling out the 
possibility of having a stack-effect driven natural ventilation system. 
It’s been shown that having an uncontrolled natural ventilation system often results in higher heat losses, 
as the air change rate for periods of the year will be unreasonably high. Having detailed simulations for 
apartments on both the lowest and highest floor, can provide the foundation needed to ensure that all 
rooms and floors meet the requirements, while measures to prevent unnecessary heat losses can be taken.  
Having the air change rate controlled by a mechanical exhaust fan has shown a more stable overall 
performance, with overheating hours and air flow rate within the requirements, while the total heating 
demand is significantly reduced.  
This report shows, that for renovated apartment projects, required air change rate can be maintained after 
renovation through cheap ventilation systems, and therefore not being limited by physical or budget 
limitations.
