Abstract 
 
The rapid growth of the human population demands increased food production, 
which places significant strain on the environment—particularly through the agricultural 
sector, a major contributor to the transgression of planetary boundaries. This study ex-
plores Pseudomonas spp. as Plant Growth-Promoting Rhizobacteria (PGPR), focusing 
on their potential to reduce reliance on agrochemicals. Specifically, it examines the role 
and mechanisms of the cyclic lipopeptide (CLP) viscosin, which exhibits several plant 
growth promoting properties. However, viscosin can be degraded by other microorgan-
isms, potentially limiting its beneficial effects. This research investigates how degrada-
tion of viscosin by Rhodococcus globerulus D757 affects the ability of Pseudomonas 
fluorescens SBW25 to colonize tomato roots. A growth setup based on Transparent Soil 
was constructed, enabling microbial colonization of plant roots to be assessed through 
dilution plating, microscopy, and mass spectrometry imaging. No correlation was found 
between viscosin presence and root colonization, as viscosin could not be detected in 
the plant growth system—likely due to growth conditions that did not support its pro-
duction. Future research should focus on developing experimental conditions that better 
support viscosin production and clarify its role in root colonization.

Preface 
 
In the context of creating sustainable agriculture, it is mandatory to move away 
from the excessive use of agrochemicals such as fertilizers and pesticides. At the same 
time, they have increased the yield of our farms and are indispensable for secure food 
production. Especially with climate change causing increasingly extreme weather, fur-
ther increasing the demand for a resilient food production system. Plant growth-promot-
ing rhizobacteria have shown potential to increase the resilience of crops, along with in-
creasing the yields without having the same negative impacts on the environment and 
human health as chemical fertilizers and pesticides.  
Pseudomonas spp. have shown a wide variety of plant-beneficial mechanisms, 
which can be utilized in a biofertilizer context. An issue that impedes the implementa-
tion of biofertilizers on a large scale is their inconsistent results in field trials. This is re-
lated to many different factors, one of these being the establishment of bacteria on the 
plant roots. It has been found that cyclic lipopeptide viscosin, produced by Pseudomo-
nas fluorescens SBW25 plays a role in movement and colonization of plant roots. This 
compound can be broken down by other species such as Rhodococcus globerulus D757, 
possibly impeding colonization. This led to the hypothesis: If viscosin is broken down 
during plant root colonization, the ability of SBW25 to colonize will be reduced. The 
main research question for this thesis was then: does the breaking down or presence of 
viscosin affect the ability of SBW25 to colonize tomato plant roots?  
To investigate this, a comprehensive review of the relevant scientific literature 
was conducted. This expanded on the issues related to the agricultural industry, Pseudo-
monads as a plant growth promoting rhizobacteria, the role of CLPs in this manner and 
how to investigate this. To test the hypothesis, a characterization of the hydrolysis of 
viscosin was done in vitro. It was then attempted to create a SBW25 mutant deficient 
for production of viscosin, to investigate the importance of viscosin in the colonization 
of the plant roots. Then the Pseudomonas’ ability to colonize tomato roots was investi-
gated with and without the viscosin degrader D757. The results are then discussed, and 
what the following steps would be is suggested, to further investigate the role of CLPs, 
their metabolic fate and how they affect plant root colonization.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The agricultural revolution and environmental impacts 
 
The human population has seen an exponential growth in its numbers. For most of 
human history, the entire population was less than one million, to more than 8 billion to-
day. This stark increase in growth rate became especially clear in the 19th century, grow-
ing from 1 billion in 1800 to our current population in only 200 years. And even though 
the growth rate is decreasing, and the human population is expected to peak by 2086, it 
is still estimated to reach more than 10 billion people(1). To support this, it is para-
mount to have a stable food production. This has so far been made possible by the effec-
tivization and expansion of our croplands that came with the green revolution of the 20th 
century. Factors that made this possible were related to the mechanization of the previ-
ously hard manual labour tasks, but also the introduction of chemical fertilizers and pes-
ticides has played a huge role in increasing the yields of croplands(2). Even in the 21st 
century, we continue to improve the yields, seeing an increase in Net Primary Produc-
tion (NPP) of 25% from 2003-2019(3,4). And even though yields are improving, there 
are still large issues related to yield loss. Depending on the crop and region, it is esti-
mated that we lose between 26-40% of all crops produced yearly to pests, pathogens, 
and weeds(5). Furthermore, climate change and degradation of croplands pose signifi-
cant threats to food production systems(6).  
Increased frequency of extreme weather and rise in global temperatures increase 
the stress on crops. At the current global rate of greenhouse gas reductions, there is also 
an indication that keeping under the 1.5°C increase in temperature from the Paris agree-
ment is becoming increasingly unlikely, which makes for an uncertain future(7). To 
make matters worse, the regions that are most vulnerable to climate change also coin-
cide with being the places with the largest increase in population, stressing the need to 
increase the resilience of our food production(1,8). It is not possible to keep using agro-
chemicals as previously, since it has been shown that excessive use does not come with-
out a cost. Leaching of chemicals into ground water, reduced biodiversity, and eutrophi-
cation are some of the issues related to the use of fertilizers and extensive agriculture, 
pushing us past the planetary boundaries(9–11). The consequences for biological life 
when exposed to chemicals via food and water are difficult to measure, but environmen-
tal toxicology studies suggest the effects might be quite relevant, having effects on en-
docrine and neurological systems in humans(12).  
The effects of eutrophication have especially been apparent in the near-coastal re-
gions of the Baltic Sea, where oxygen depletion creates uninhabitable environments for 
animal and plant life(13). In Denmark, 70% of the nitrogen flux into the near coastal 
water bodies comes from agriculture, which emphasizes that measures to reduce the 
runoff of nitrogen is necessary (14). So, there is a dilemma in how to address the need 
for stable food production systems while protecting our environment and health. One 
way to address these issues is to substitute chemical fertilizers, utilizing the inherent in-
teractions between plants and microorganisms to create a sustainable agriculture.

1.2 Pseudomonas spp. as a plant growth-promoting rhizobac-
teria 
 
In nature, plants and microorganisms exist together in codependent life cycles, 
each fulfilling essential roles for each other. Microorganisms inhabit every part of the 
plant, from inside the stem as endophytes to around the roots(15). In this section, the in-
teractions between plants and microorganisms in the rhizosphere, the area immediately 
surrounding the roots, will be expanded upon. The rhizosphere is a microbially dense 
area of the plant immediately surrounding the roots(16). Within this community of mi-
croorganisms Plant Growth Promoting Rhizobacteria (PGPR) show many beneficial 
traits and are essential for plants to thrive, sketched in Figure 1(17). These effects in-
clude important functions such as nutrient fixation, protection against pathogens, pests, 
and abiotic stress factors, and others. Harnessing these inherent capacities of PGPRs and 
optimizing the microbiome could be a way to reduce our dependence on agrochemicals 
while reducing the stress on the environment globally and locally(18).  
Pseudomonas spp. are common and widely distributed soil bacteria and have shown a 
wide range of plant growth-promoting properties (PGPP). They have even been applied 
in a commercial context as biofertilizers in several countries(16). In the following sec-
tions, some of these effects and their mechanisms will be described and in what manner 
they could have possible applications in agriculture. The limitations that are to be ad-
dressed before consistent effects comparable to chemical fertilizers are achieved will 
also be expanded upon(5,19). 
 
1.2.1 Bacterial mechanisms for plant growth promotion  
 
Solubilization of organic and inorganic minerals 
The inorganic minerals nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium are essential elements 
for plant growth, and therefore they are also the most applied fertilizers in salt form as 
NPK-fertilizers(20). In the environment, they can be found in the ground and in the at-
mosphere, but they are primarily not in a bioavailable state. Phosphate (PO4) can be 
found in amounts of 400-1200 mg/kg in the ground, which is higher than what is neces-
sary for plant growth, but in insoluble form. Solubilizing bacteria can make it available 
for the plant via acidification, chelation and exchange reactions common in plants (16). 
The most common mechanism for solubilization of inorganic phosphate has been found 
to be the excretion of organic acids(21). Zhu et al did a characterisation of a novel phos-
phate solubilizing bacteria and found the strain YCWA18, a proposed member of the 
Kushneria genus, could make clear phosphate solubilization zones on agar containing 
Ca3(PO4). They further saw a clear correlation between pH and phosphate availability, 
with the highest amounts available at pH4 and lowest at pH7(21,22). Organic phos-
phates, typically found in phytate, nucleic acids or other types of xenobiotic phospho-
nates, also contribute significantly to the amount of phosphate available in soil(21). 
Richardson et al. describe the phosphatase activity of several soil-native Pseudomonas 
spp. proving the importance of phosphatase in making organic phosphate available. 
From a enrichment culture experiment, they found 4 strains from 200 able to use inosi-
tol hexaphosphate as sole carbon and phosphate source, indicating that they were posi-
tive for production of phosphatase able to cleave the bond between the 6-carbonmember 
aromatic ring and the phosphate salts attached to it, making it not only available to it-
self, but to plants from its natural environment as well(23).

Potassium also plays an important role in root development. If there is a defi-
ciency of potassium, the plant roots will remain underdeveloped, growth will be slower, 
fewer seeds will be produced, and overall productivity will decrease. Additionally, the 
plants will become more vulnerable to diseases and pests. Yue et al did a selection ex-
periment for the most efficient potassium solubilizing Pseudomonas spp. from a series 
of cotton soil samples. They then inoculated cotton plants with potassium and the most 
efficient strain, and saw that treatment increased the total root length, root weight, plant 
height, and aboveground dry weight by 41, 23, 35, and 46%, respectively. They attest 
this to most likely be a result of acidification of elemental potassium as well as chela-
tion mechanisms(24). 
Nitrogen is the most applied fertilizer by mass and is important for protein synthe-
sis in plants. It is widely abundant in the atmosphere attributing to approx. 78% of the 
volume of atmospheric air. However, plants cannot benefit from it and need it solubil-
ized, which happens naturally in a denitrification process, converting from N2(g) to bio-
available ammonium salt NH4+(25). Most importantly are the nitrogenases a complex 
enzyme encoded by the genes nifD, nifK and nifH, found in a wide variety of soil bacte-
ria.  Pseudomonas spp., among others, have been found to have nitrogen-fixing capaci-
ties in sugar cane. Kang et al. found several genes related to nitrogen fixation in Pseudo-
monas spp. Nitrogen fixing capacities is thus an indispensable part of a biofertilizer 
product(16). 
Some of these mechanisms can be further expanded when it comes to solubilizing 
trace metals such as zinc and iron. Pseudomonas and other PGPRs produce sidero-
phores that can bind iron not readily available to plants and significantly shift expres-
sion of genes in plants. For example, the siderophore apo-pyoverdine of a P. ﬂuorescens 
strain had an impact on more than 2000 genes of A. thaliana(16). 
Microorganisms are thus essential for making key nutrients available to plant 
roots and applying them effectively has the potential to increase the yield of important 
agricultural crops.

Bacterial phytohormone production 
Pseudomonas spp. has been shown to increase the biomass of plants compared to 
control groups by production of phytohormones. Phytohormones are produced both in 
plant and bacteria, but in bacteria primarily function as a chemical communication me-
diator between the plant and bactaria, rather than a specific growth factor. An example 
of induction of increased plant growth comes from regulating the expression of indole-
3-acetic acid (IAA), an important auxin, in plants. Auxins are plant hormones important 
in regulating several key growth aspects in plants, such as cell division, expansion and 
alleviation of abiotic stress(17). A study found that coculture of Pseudomonas putida in-
duced increased root growth of the plant compared to inoculations with an IAA-defi-
cient mutant. The increased root development then in turn led to increased exudate ex-
cretion, which the bacteria can use as a carbon source(16). 
Gibberellins are another important class of molecules that are involved in the reg-
ulation of seed dormancy, quiescence, germination, flowering, ripening of fruits, promo-
tion of root growth, and root hair abundance. These types of compounds are like auxins 
produced in both plants and bacteria. A study showed that inoculation of a gibberellin-
producing P. putida increased the length, fresh and dry weight of shoot, and chlorophyll 
content in gibberellin-deficient mutant Waito-c rice seedlings over the control. The in-
creased plant growth was partly attributed to the increase of other plant hormones such 
as Jasmonic-Acid, which reduces oxidative stress, as a direct consequence of the bacte-
rial inoculation(26).  
Molecules produced by Pseudomonas have then been proven to have a direct im-
pact on plant hormone regulation, which in turn has a wide array of effects in the plant, 
in these examples inducing increased growth and excretion of molecules that benefit mi-
croorganisms.  
 
1.2.2 Antimicrobial activities 
 
One of the most interesting aspects of using Pseudomonas spp. in biofertilizers 
comes from its wide arrange of antagonistic effects against plant pathogens. There are 
several mechanisms related to the effects on plant pathogens. It can be related to direct 
antibiosis against the pathogen, competition for nutrients or induction of plant re-
sistance(5). 
 
Direct antibiosis and Induced Systemic Resistance  
Examples of antibiosis come in many forms. An important molecule is the phenol 
2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (DAPG) produced by P. fluorescens by the gene phlD. Its 
antimicrobial mechanism of action has only recently been elucidated in bacteria, but Jul-
ian W. et al prove by microscopy and growth experiments that it can interact with bacte-
rial cell walls, inducing increased permeability in Escherichia coli and can cause inter-
nal leakage in Staphylococcus aureus, which in turn reduces their fitness in rich me-
dia(27). Importantly, in the context of agriculture, DAPG has proven to be antagonistic 
towards important plant pathogens such as the “take-all” wheat pathogen Gaeumanno-
mycis graminis and the black rot causing Thielaviopsis basicola of tobacco(5). Another 
effect of DAPG is related to induced systemic resistance (ISR) in plants.  
Induced systemic resistance, is when the plant increases its own defensive capa-
bilities against potential pathogens as a response to environmental stimuli. In an experi-
ment, A. thaliana plants with increased expression of DAPG-hydrolase gene phlG and

Another molecule which is well studied for its toxic effects is Hydrogen Cyanide

and other metalloenzymes, it is toxic to all aerobic organisms. Several Pseudomonas

of growth of the pathogen, both by disc diffusion assay and in a split disc. This indicates 
that HCN plays a major role in the inhibition of the pathogen, both by direct interaction 
and as a volatile(29).  
Single molecules can thus have more than one effect in the rhizosphere system, 
and can interact in a multitude of ways, in these cases both by direct antibiosis seen by 
production of DAPG and HCN and by induction of resistance by production of DAPG.  
 
1.2.3 Competition 
 
Rather than having a direct antibiosis Pseudomonas spp. can also inhibit the abil-
ity of potential pathogens to infect the plant by limiting their ability to effectively colo-
nize and grow on the plant. One of the most common methods of competition is by 
competing for scarce nutrients. Pathways sequestering different redox states of iron are 
well developed in bacteria(17). Leinweber et al showed that Pseudomonas aeruginosa 
could adjust its production of siderophore pyoverdine, the pigment which gives fluores-
cent Pseudomonas their characteristic fluorescence, in competition with Burkholderia 
cenocepacia. Pyoverdin is an iron chelating agent, binding iron in the environment re-
ducing the amount available for other microorganisms. In this experiment, they showed 
that during early growth phase Pseudomonas can outcompete B. cenocepacia by upreg-
ulating production of pyoverdine, which is a stronger iron-chelating agent than ornibac-
tin produced by B. cenocepacia. By being able to adjust production of siderophores in 
response to the presence of competitors and possibly plant pathogens, Pseudomonas 
spp. can protect the plant from possible pathogens(5).  
 
1.2.4 Stress tolerance 
 
As mentioned earlier, there is increasing stress on our croplands. Examples of abi-
otic stress could be toxification of soil by heavy metals and increased risks of drought 
and salt stress. Microorganisms including Pseudomonas have been shown to increase 
the resistance of plants to these conditions, such as by biofilm formation and metabolic 
chages(6,16,26).  
In an experiment by Kang et al, they investigated the effects of salt and drought 
stress on soybean plants inoculated with and without P. putida. They found that plants 
inoculated with P. putida had increased shoot and root length and increased amounts of 
chlorophyll. Also, they found that plants under salt stress had an elevated production of

antioxidative compounds. This production was reduced in plants treated with Pseudo-
monas, suggesting that they were under less abiotic stress when inoculated with the 
strain, which was also confirmed by measuring a lower sodium content in treated plants. 
They suggest several mechanisms by which tolerance is inferred. They saw a reduced 
amount of hormonal stress caused by high amounts of abscisic acid and jasmonic acid. 
Also, reduced oxidative stress was suspected to play an important role for the better 
plant growth(26).   
Heavy metals are increasingly common in the environment from industrial pollu-
tion. Here, microorganisms can play a role when it comes to increasing plants’ tolerance 
to these compounds. A study showed that by inoculating plants with Pseudomonas spp. 
plants had an increased biomass and shoot length in contaminated soils. This is related 
to the chelation of Cr and Pb, which then interferes less with plant iron-dependent me-
tabolism(15). 
 
From this section it is apparent that Pseudomonas spp. has many applications in 
the context of supporting the growth of plants, with a variety of mechanisms. This in-
cludes solubilization of nutrients, protection against pathogenic bacteria and increasing 
the tolerance of plants to pathogens and stressful environments(5,15,26,29). But one of 
the reasons Pseudomonas spp. are also very attractive as a biofertilizer, is its strong ca-
pacity for colonization. Lipopeptides are a very important class of molecules related to 
colonization amongst other things, and the target of this study(30). 
 
1.3 Cyclic lipopeptides in Pseudomonas 
 
Lipopeptides (LP) are a diverse class of molecules with a multitude of different 
purposes in bacteria playing a role in motility, colonization and antimicrobial effects. 
They can be divided into 14 distinct groups with structurally heterologous members. In 
bacteria they are mostly produced by Non-Ribosomal Peptide Synthetases (NRPS), 
where in some rare cases, such as for the iturin group, they are constructed through hy-
brid NRPS-and polyketide synthase systems(31). And while being very diverse in struc-
ture, they share a certain structural pattern. They all have a peptide and a lipid moiety 
that is N-terminal acylated to be linked together.

1.3.1 Motility and colonization 
 
Since CLPs have a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic region they are known as am-
phophilic compounds. This renders them able to reduce the surface tension of liquids, 
and function as biosurfactants. This is important in several biological functions related 
to bacterial movement, biofilm formation and root colonization. An easy way to visual-
ize this is by drop collapse assays, where the reduction in surface tension from biosur-
factants will result in water droplets collapsing on a hydrophilic surface, compared to a 
non-treated droplet(32). 
CLPs are important for motility in Pseudomonas. There are three types of move-
ment: twitching, swimming, and swarming. To test the motility of bacterial colonies, 
semi-solid agar plates (0.6% w/v) are often used, since swarming gives distinct colonies 
on these types of plates. The very recognizable fingering pattern of swarming colonies 
is thought to be caused by liquid flows mediated by concentration gradients of CLPs. 
Here, it has been proven that CLP deficient mutants are unable to do swarming(30,31).  
Alsohim et al. showed that P. fluorescens SBW25 strains unable to produce CLP 
viscosin lost their ability to swarm comparable to producers. They removed the ability 
of the strain to swim by knocking out the gene fleQ which is involved in the production 
of flagella in the bacteria. This changed the mode of mobility from swimming to 
swarming. By random mutagenesis of the fleQ deficient strain they then found mutants 
unable to move at all on semi-solid agar. This suggests that viscosin is important for the 
motility of the strain. The role of viscosin in plant root colonization was then tested. 
They found that bacteria unable to produce flagella or viscosin was unable to move on 
the plant root at all and thus had poorer colonization ability. They then tested their mu-
tants and their effect on plant growth in the presence of a pathogen Pythia. Here they 
found that plants inoculated with a flagellum and viscosin mutant did not show any sig-
nificantly different growth compared to the un-inoculated plants, while plants inoculated 
with wildtype had a significantly increased growth. This might be more complex than 
just related to movement, since CLPs are also known for the antibiotic activities(35).  
However, it has been proven that that CLP viscosin is directly related to the root 
colonization of P. fluorescens SBW25. Guan et al. inoculated two cultivars of wheat in 
soil with red fluorescent tagged SBW25 WT and a viscosin deficient mutant. They 
found by CFU and qPCR quantification, that there could be up to an at least 10-fold 
higher concentration of the WT compared to the viscosin deficient mutant. The differ-
ence in the colonization ability of the Pseudomonas appeared to be dependent on the 
genotype of the wheat, as there was a difference between the two cultivars. They further 
supported the quantification in a qualitative manner by using confocal microscopy, tak-
ing pictures of the plant roots and the red fluorescent strains. Interestingly, they also 
showed that viscosin played a role in community assembly across both cultivars of 
wheat (35). By looking at the community using metagenomics, they found by Principal 
Component Analysis (PCOA) that distinct communities were formed based on the

whether the cultivar was inoculated with a viscosin producing mutant or not. This high-

Biosurfactants plays a role in the formation of biofilm. There are conflicting re-
ports on whether the production of biosurfactants reduces or decreases the amount of 
biofilm in colonies. One study found that production of viscosin in P. fluorescens 
SBW25 did not change the amount of biofilm produced initially, but a viscA deficient 
mutant had increased biofilm formation after 13.5 hours of cultivation. They found by 
linking the production of viscosin to a fluorescent reporter that little viscosin was pro-
duced in the early stages of colonization and thus speculate that it might be involved in 
the dispersal of biofilms(32). 
The role of CLPS as biosurfactants is thus many, and the mechanisms are compli-
cated. This further underlines the importance of studying the role of CLPs and the exact 
mechanisms, so the exact effects can be elucidated and applied. 
 
1.3.2 Antibiotic properties 
 
Another aspect that makes CLPs from Pseudomonas spp. an interesting class of 
molecules in the context of biocontrol in agriculture, is their antagonistic effects towards 
several types of organisms, such as gram-positive bacteria, fungi and protozoa(31). Due 
to their diverse structures there are potential for discovering new antagonistic effects. 
And by the introduction of more sophisticated tools for genome mining and in silico 
structure elucidation, the potential for finding new CLPs have only increased(34). 
A review by Geudens et al describes this specificity towards different organisms, 
depending on the CLP. They found that tolaasin was able to inhibit the growth of Cryp-
tococcus neoformans and Candida albicans, whereas viscosinamide along with several 
other CLPs did not. One of the effects against fungi is a dysmorphia of hyphae during 
growth of Risoctonia solani, a plant pathogen with several hosts, by viscosinamide. 
Fungal samples treated with the CLP had stunted growth, swelling and increased hyphal 
branching. They suggest its due to the amphophilic properties of the CLP, which can in-
teract with the cell wall of the fungus, but also changes in enzymes activity and mito-
chondria organization. Similar effects can be seen in bacteria, where much of the anti-
bacterial activity has been attributed to pore formation in the membrane. This might also 
be able to explain why many LPs lack activity against gram-negative bacteria, due to the 
presence of an outer membrane(31,34). 
The effect has not only been isolated to prokaryotes and fungi, but also to proto-
zoa and insects, which predates on bacteria and plants respectively. In the same study by 
Guan et al. that related colonization to the production of viscosin, they found that the 
ability to produce viscosin, also significantly changed the protozoan community compo-
sition(36). The effect of Pseudomonas CLPs to combat insects appears to be multifac-
eted, but it was determined that production of orfamide A and B were important with re-
gards to the oral toxic dose response in insects(34). 
The antibiotic properties of CLPs from Pseudomonas has effects on multiple dif-
ferent plant threats, which make them important molecules for the effects of Pseudomo-
nas spp. in a biocontrol context. But what is also clear is that the mechanism is not al-
ways unilateral, but can have effects on complex systems, such as metabolism in fungi 
and on community composition in rhizosphere environments. Studying these effects in a 
meaningful setting is the first step to elucidating the effects and mechanisms. A place to

1.4 Studying root colonization 
 
There are many factors to consider when it comes to applying Pseudomonas spp. 
and the production of CLPs in a practical setting. As described above, the effects of 
Pseudomonas spp. as PGPRs are diverse, but essential to see an effect is a stable pres-
ence on the plant root. A consistent approach for successful colonization on the plant 
root continues to be one of the hurdles for applying many PGPRs in a commercial set-
ting(37–39). Studying this process and the mechanisms are therefore essential. For stud-
ying effect and mechanism the level of complexity stretches from one specific mole-
cule’s interaction with another organism in vitro, to in situ experiments with a multitude 
of factors impacting the system. In the same manner, different methods give different 
types of information, and in many cases cannot stand alone to give the full image. Com-
mon methods and how they are applied to study the chemical, physical, and biotic pa-
rameters in a plant root system will be further expanded upon in the following sec-
tions(34,40). 
 
1.4.1 In vitro studies 
 
In vitro studies of PGPRs are common to investigate the effects and mechanisms 
of single variables in simple systems. By being able to control media and the microor-
ganisms present, in vitro studies are the first step to investigate the effects and mecha-
nisms during root colonization. Even though a plant is not incorporated in in vitro ex-
periments, it can still give valuable information when studying mechanisms known to 
be important for plant root colonization. 
A simple setup for doing a metabolomic study could be a spot assay on agar 
plates. Hansen et al. used this method to investigate degradation of Pseudomonas prote-
gens DTU9.1 CLP orfamide A by Rhodococcus globorolus D757. The power of using 
agar plates comes from the ease of use, naked eye visual analysis of phenotype and 
compatibility with mass spectrophotometric methods. In the setup by using High Pres-
sure Liquid Chromatography coupled to Mass Spectrometry (HPLC-MS) they could 
show that orfamide A was broken down and find the hydrolysed product in the media. 
By using MALDI-Mass Spectrometry Imaging (MALDI-MSI), they could show the 
spatial distribution of Orfamide A and the hydrolysed product(41).  
Another power of in vitro studies lies in the ability to do higher throughput in 
smaller volumes. 96-well plates are a commonly used setup for investigating bacterial 
responses to environmental stimulants in small volumes. López-Farfán et al. applied 96-
well plates when investigating the transcriptional response of chemoreceptors in re-
sponse to corn root exudates in a P. putida strain. Chemosensory pathways are known to 
regulate different types of motility in bacteria. What the research found was that the up-
regulation of chemoreceptors was inverse proportional to the concentration of root exu-
dates. In turn they found that a higher amount of root exudates would promote for-
mation of biofilm. They propose this is due to the bacteria having increased chemotaxis 
towards the plant, as root exudate concentration would be lover further away from the 
root. This experiment proves that bacteria have important responses with regards to mo-
tility and lifestyle depending on the presence of plant root exudates(42).

1.4.2 In planta 
 
After having confirmed an effect in vitro, the next step is to investigate in planta. 
A common method is growing plants on agar plates with media. While there is good 
control of the biotic and chemical properties of this system, it poorly mimics the physi-
cal aspects of soil. This is important for root development, where the physical resistance 
of soil impacts the root. The difference in root development is nicely shown by Ma et al, 
who compare the root development of A. thaliana in a hydroponic system compared to a 
Transparent Soil system. TS is proposed as a middle ground between soil and hydropon-
ics or agar, by keeping the control of the chemical and biotic aspects, while providing a 
more solid matrix for plant growth. They show that there is a significant difference be-
tween several aspects of root growth between plants grown in hydroponics or TS. Both 
related to different physical properties of the root, but also gene expression. The plants 
grown in TS had more similar characteristics to soil compared to hydroponics, showing 
that it could be a good compromise to better mimic in situ growth conditions(15). To 
compliment the use of TS for studying microbial dynamics on the roots, Andrade-
Lozano et al used it as a model to study population dynamics in a bacterial community 
of 5 strains, as well as investigate if it could support Bacillus subtilis colonization of 
plant roots. They then quantified growth dynamics by use of several techniques, such as 
flow-cytometry, CFU count and 16s-sequencing. They then did a chemical analysis of 
the system by HPLC and MALDI-MSI, which proved that Bacillus LPs could be de-
tected in the system. Finally, they used confocal microscopy to detect the bacteria over 
several days, proving that it supports bacterial colonization(43).  
How to apply these different matrices for studying root colonization, there are 
several and creative examples of. Carroll et al grew plants in tubes using a hydroponics 
setup for quantifying root colonization, where they inoculate the plant roots by forming 
a small ledge of agar inside an Eppendorf tube, where they then inoculate the plant 
growth media with bacteria. This method in combination with the TS is applied in this 
research as a method for inoculation bacteria on to the roots(44). Another paper applied 
3D-printed setups to grow plants in growth chambers built for confocal microscopy. 
This has the advantage of not having to destroy the plant before pictures can be taken, 
which enables a temporal perspective, where the other experiments require destruction 
of the plant for sampling(45). 
But as mentioned earlier, the dynamics in a soil system with the natural microbi-
ome are complex. Studying these dynamics in simplified in vitro setups might not give 
the full image, and important mechanisms might be missed(30,37). Using soil as in 
Guan et al. with the natural microbiome of the wheat plant, is closer to how microbes 
and plants interact in the environment. The development of Next Generation Sequenc-
ing (NGS) methods has made it more available to do metagenomic analysis, but this can 
also come short. Dependent on the similarity cutoff important species within the same 
genus might be missed(36,37). Many soil experiments are still performed in vitro which 
most importantly means the system is controlled with regards to growth conditions and 
biotic factors. Moving to in situ studies, the results have in general been less convinc-
ing(16).

1.5 Background and hypothesis for this experiment 
 
The largest hurdle before large scale implementation of biofertilizers can be 
achieved, is related to inconsistent results in situ. Convincing results in laboratory can 
be difficult to replicate in greenhouse or field trials. Not to say there do not exist exam-
ples of commercial biofertilizers, but they still compose a very small part of the fertiliz-
ing market. There are many aspects of commercial biofertilizers which can be improved 
upon, that are more or less practical(46). The basis of this thesis is related to investigate 
how to ensure effective colonization of the plant root by Pseudomonas PGPR and espe-
cially related to the role of CLPs in the process. What was previously discovered by 
Hansen et al, was that while investigating community invasion of a P. putida in a syn-
thetic community (SynCom), they found that CLP orfamide A was not present in the 
community compared to in axenic growth. This was further investigated using molecu-
lar networking methods, and they found that the product was linearised by hydrolysa-
tion in the SynCom(41). Stallforth et al. calls this process a Loss-of-function Mediator 
Modification, where an alteration of a molecule leads to a loss of its function. Other ex-
amples of this could be the ability of plant pathogenic fungi Phelinnius noxus to modify 
otherwise toxic Pseudomonas siderophore Pyuochelin by esterification, reducing its af-
finity for iron(47).  
CLPs are important biosurfactants and play a role in colonization. This paper aims 
to investigate the chemical fate of CLP viscosin produced by Pseudomonas SBW25 dur-
ing plant root colonization, and if it influences the ability of the bacteria to colonize to-
mato roots in a system based on Andrade Lozano et al and Carroll et al(43,44).

2.4 Tomato plant germination and growth setup 
 
2.4.1 Germination on agar plates 
Tomatoes of species Sylanum lycopersicum (Moneymaker, Weibulls) were steri-
lized by adding 7 seeds to 1 mL 2% sodium hypochlorite (VWR) and vortexed (Science 
Industries, Vortex Genie 2) for 20 minutes. The seeds were then washed 4 times in 1 
mL sterile milliQ-H2O and transferred to 12 g/L solid agar plates then incubated at 25°C 
in darkness for 4 days.  
 
2.4.2 Solid and liquid germination matrix setup 
After germinating on solid agar, the seeds were transferred to a hydroponic setup 
for final germination. The hydroponic setup was constructed by adding 10 mL of liquid 
12 g/L agar to a falcon tube and keeping it on a slope until solid. A small indent was 
then made in the top of the agar to immobilize the seed. 1 seed was transferred to each 
Falcon tube along with 10 mL of 1x Murashige and Skoog (MS) (Sigma-Aldritch) me-
dium. The tubes were then left at 25°C in darkness for 4 days. 
 
2.4.3 Transparent soil construction 
TS beads was constructed according to Lozano-Andrade, N. (49). 9.6 g/L of Phyt-
agel (Sigma-Aldritch) and 2.4 g/L sodium alginate (Sigma-Aldritch) was dissolved in 
demineralized water, autoclaved and left to cool to room temperature. The solution was 
then added as droplets from a 30 mL single use syringe to in a ratio of 5/8 of gel solu-
tion and 4% CaCl2·6H2O under stirring for 30 minutes. The beads were strained from 
the salt solution using an autoclaved strainer and spoons, then soaked for 2 hours in MS 
media under stirring. The beads were then stored at 4 °C until inoculation. 
 
2.4.4 Inoculating TS and Roots 
The TS was inoculated with O/N adjusted to an adequate OD in MS at a volume 
ratio of 1/11 (v/v) TS/ON culture and mixed on a shaker. From the hydroponic setup, 
the liquid media was removed, without disturbing the plant and root, and replaced with 
10 mL of inoculated TS ensuring the root is covered by TS. These samples were used 
for HPLC-MS analysis for detection of viscosin according to 2.5.3.

Samples for MALDI were conducted according to Lozano et al. (43). To survey 
the spatial distribution of metabolites on the TS and on the root a MALDI-MSI experi-

was also embedded in CMC and both types of samples were immediately frozen at −80 
°C and storage until cryosectioning. Embedded samples were cross-sectioned at −24 °C 
using a SLEE MEV cryostat (SLEE medical GmbH, Nieder-Olm, Germany). The sam-
ples were freed from the plastic mold and Falcon tubes and mounted on a cryostat disk

trimmed until the root was clearly visible and later cryo-ﬁlm (SECTION-LAB Co. Ltd., 
Yokohama, Japan) was adhered to the ﬂat surface, and 12 μm sections of root in CMC 
and the whole growth setup were sliced. The pieces of cryo-ﬁlm with sample were 
mounted on MALDI IntelliSlides (Bruker, Massachusetts, USA) using 2 Way Glue pen 
(Kuretake Co., Ltd., Nara-Shi, Japan) on the edges of the tape with the TS or root side 
facing up. Then the slides were covered by spraying 0.5 ml of 2,5-dihydrobenzoic acid 
(DHB) (40 mg/ml in ACN/MeOH/H2O (70:25:5, v/v/v)) in a nitrogen atmosphere and 
dried overnight in a desiccator prior to IMS measurement. The samples were then sub-
jected to timsTOF ﬂex (Bruker Daltonik GmbH) mass spectrometer for MALDI MSI 
acquisition in positive MS scan mode with 50 µm raster width and a mass range of 100–
2000 Da. Calibration was done using red phosphorus. The settings in the timsControl 
were as follow: Laser: imaging 50 µm, Power Boost 3.0%, scan range 10 and 50 µm for 
roots and whole growth setup respectively in the XY interval, and laser power 70%; 
Tune: Funnel 1 RF 300 Vpp, Funnel 2 RF 300 Vpp, Multipole RF 300 Vpp, isCID 0 
eV, Deﬂection Delta 70 V, MALDI plate offset 100 V, quadrupole ion energy 5 eV, 
quadrupole loss mass 100 m/ z, collision energy 10 eV, focus pre TOF transfer time 75 
µs, pre-pulse storage 8 µs. After data acquisition, the data were analyzed using SCiLS 
v2023a software.

4. Discussion 
One of the major challenges that humanity will face in this century is the increase 
of the human population to more than 10 billion(1). Feeding a growing population will 
require increased yields of our crops or expansion of cropland. This cannot be done with 
the same regimes as we have today, as agriculture is the major contributor pushing us 
past the safe zone of operation into the risk zone for 2 of the planetary boundaries, bio-
geochemical flows and biosphere integrity(11,55). Agrochemicals are a major driver for 
these impacts. Plant growth promoting rhizobacteria offer an interesting alternative to 
limit their use, while increasing the yield of croplands(16). Amongst these microorgan-
isms, Pseudomonas spp. have shown themselves to be interesting candidates due to their 
broad range of functionalities and natural presence in soils(37,56). This includes im-
proved plant growth and protection against pathogens(28,40).  
An important class of molecules related to the effectiveness and function of Pseu-
domonas as PGPR are the Cyclic Lipopeptides. Their amphophilic properties give them 
their properties of being biosurfactants with importance for to motility, biofilm for-
mation, colonization of plant roots as well as their antibiotic properties(30,31). 
This project focused on the importance of the CLP viscosin produced by Pseudo-
monas fluorescens SBW25 for the colonization of tomato plant roots. It has been proven 
that CLPs and viscosin play a role in plant root colonization(36,44). At the same time, it 
has been shown that CLPs can be degraded in coculture with other organisms, resulting 
in a Loss-of-function by mediator modification(41,47). It is suspected that one of the 
reasons PGPRs have varying consistency in field trials is because of failed colonization 
of the plant root(40). This led to the hypothesis that if viscosin is broken down during 
plant root colonization, the ability of SBW25 to colonize will be reduced. The research 
question for the experiments was then; Does the breaking down or presence of viscosin 
affect the ability of SBW25 to colonize tomato plant roots? 
The study investigated this using a set of plant root colonization experiments, us-
ing in vitro and in planta setups. The in planta setup was constructed with inspiration 
from Carroll et al. and Lozano-Andrade et al using an agar slope and transparent soil 
beads(43,44). In this setup, the SBW25 could colonize the tomato plant roots, which 
was proven by CFU counting by serial dilution, metabolites in the root zone and on the 
TS by MALDI-MSI unique to samples treated with Pseudomonas SBW25 and by con-
focal microscopy. This study did not prove a correlation between the breaking down of 
viscosin and root colonization as there was no significant difference in cell count for the 
samples grown with SBW25 alone and in coculture with SBW25 and a degrader of vis-
cosin. It was not possible either to find viscosin in the TS bead setup by HPLC-MS or 
on the plant roots by MALDI-MSI. Lastly attempts to create a SBW25 mutant deficient 
in viscosin production were unsuccessful.  
The importance of these results is discussed below, why they do not hold up to the 
hypothesis and what future investigation could look like.

4.1 In vitro experiments and detection of viscosin 
 
The initial investigation was regarding whether Pseudomonas fluorescens SBW25 
was a suitable model organism for studying plant root colonization and the metabolic 
fate of viscosin.  
SBW25 has previously been shown to be a PGPR and the NRPS responsible for 
the production of viscosin has been reported(57). The effect of viscosin on colonization 
has even been studied using this exact organism(32,44,57). Also, SBW25 has been ge-
nome sequenced most recently in 2023 by Grote et al.(58). It is thus a well-studied or-
ganism, with the exact properties relevant to investigating the hypothesis. Viscosin is 
furthermore one of the most studied CLPs with regards to biological experiments(34). 
This project also confirmed that viscosin is being produced on KB media by SBW25. It 
was then also confirmed that D757 was able to hydrolyze viscosin into a linearized form 
which was shown in Figure 4. This system was then deemed viable for investigating if 
degradation of viscosin would have an impact on the colonization ability of SBW25. 
A key finding in the research is that it was not possible to detect viscosin by 
HPLC-MS or MALDI-MSI in the plant growth setup using TS soaked in MS, despite 
growth of the bacteria. Explanations could be, but not exclusive to media composition, 
the use of TS or biotic factors. It was not possible to find reports specifically dealing 
with production of CLPs in MS-media. However, it has been reported that surfactin, an-
other CLP, was produced and detected by MALDI-MSI by a Bacillus subtilis grown on 
TS soaked in 0.1X tryptone soy broth (TSB)(41). Even though it is a different species 
and media, these results could suggest that the beads can support production and detec-
tion of CLPs. Regarding the media choice, a report by Nielsen et al did a study compar-
ing the production of CLPs in different types of soil and found that CLP production was 
increased in nutrient poor soils, possibly giving the cells a competitive advantage when 
producing CLPs in nutrient poor environment. Carroll et al. also used 0.5X MS media in 
their agar slope setup. Changing the media composition to a less rich nutrient-rich sys-
tem might thus affect the production of viscosin (44,59). 
These results cannot elaborate on the hypothesis, since it was not possible to find 
viscosin in the TS-setup, and changing nutrient availability in the in vitro setup could be 
important for the detectability of viscosin. 
 
4.2 In planta root colonization and viscosin  
 
4.2.1 Experimental assumptions in root colonization setup 
It was necessary to take the experiments onto plant roots to investigate if the pres-
ence of D757 would impact the colonization rate of SBW25. 
The setup for plant growth and colonization was based on three articles, Lozano-
Andrade et al, Ma et al and Carroll et al.(43,44,60). These three articles together support 
that: CLPs can be detected by MALDI-MSI on the TS and that bacteria can colonize 
roots in the setup, root development in TS is closer to soil compared to a hydroponic 
setup and that SBW25 can colonize plant roots and be counted.  
It was not possible to find descriptions of the exact method for root inoculation 
and growth. Some presumptions were thus made.  
It was presumed that the inoculation method would yield an equal concentration 
of bacteria across the beads, meaning that every plant would be inoculated with the

It was also presumed that Pseudomonas could colonize on the roots in the system. 
This was shown to be possible with several methods, which will be elaborated upon in

4.2.2 Bacterial ecology and detection limitations in the growth setup 
 
Three methods were used to detect bacteria in this project. CFU on plates (Figure 
7), microscopy (Figure 8) and metabolite tracking (Figure 12). The counting method 
used was described in Carroll et al.(44). It was not possible to find a significant differ-
ence in the colonization of SBW25 grown axenically or together with the degrader of 
viscosin D757. 
There are strengths and drawbacks to the counting method applied. The strength 
of the method lies in the accuracy and visual cue from the plates and the ease of execu-
tion (Figure 3)(40). It was thus possible to show that the two strains were easily distin-
guishable from each other on an agar plate Figure 9, proving that this counting method 
does allow for determining the colonization of the two strains within the detection 
threshold. Considering the colonization rate, where little increase in colonization was 
seen after day 3 (Figure 7), it is well in line with Carroll et al., who find that the primary 
colonization of SBW25 on lettuce roots happens in the first 48 hours, and very little af-
ter 75 hours(44). Studying colonization in this setup in a 3-day period should thus give 
an adequate overview of the initial colonization period. The specific dynamics and rates 
of early colonization, particularly within the first 48 hours, require further investigation 
with more timepoints to be more precisely understood. Also, since Carroll et al. uses let-
tuce as plant host and a hydroponics setup compared to tomato and TS in this project, 
some variation in plant root colonization is to be expected. There are however also im-
portant limitations, which will be expanded upon. 
Sensitivity of the setup limits the establishment of the precise growth dynam-
ics of D757 and SBW25: The CFU counting method does however have some limita-
tions. As can be seen in Figure 10 and Figure 11, there was a problem with D757 not 
being detectable on day 3. The lack of detectability might be due to either the sensitivity 
of the assay or D757 not surviving in the environment. It is thus impossible to tell 
whether an equilibrium settles between the two strains after 3 days. An approach to in-
crease detectability and maybe increase the likelihood of hydrolyzation in the same turn 
would be to favor growing conditions for D757. The inoculation ratios could still be ad-
justed to favor D757 even more. In Hansen et al. the OD ratio between SBW25 and 
D757 is 1:2000, which is 20 times higher than what was ever applied in this experiment 
at 1:100(41). An uninvestigated interaction is if the strains are not inoculated at the 
same time. Considering the use of Pseudomonas spp. as PGPR applied after planting on 
the field, it would be relevant to study if a degrader of viscosin such as D757 hinders 
colonization if already established in the community. To say something more qualified 
about these dynamics, it would be necessary to understand the exact regulation of the 
degradation. It might also be an issue that for the inoculation methods used in this study, 
the detection method was not sensitive enough.  
Since it was not possible to screen selectively for D757 using agar plates it could 
only be detected on the same plates as SBW25 as could be seen in Figure 9. Using less 
==PAGE:39==
 
39 
diluted samples would however have been overcrowded on LB plates by SBW25, and 
thus it was not possible to increase the sensitivity specifically for D757 in this setup. 
Methods for overcoming this issue could be using molecular methods. Vallance et al re-
ported detection of various microorganisms colonizing plant roots in quantifiable and 
sensitive manner using q-PCR(62). This would overcome the detection limit. Other mo-
lecular methods such as described by Guan et al, quantifying by metagenomic analysis 
would also resolve this. Metagenomic analysis is very suitable for complex systems, 
whereas the strength of the PCR is related to its specificity, which would make it suita-
ble for a 2-organism setup(36). These methods however are not inherently sensitive to 
the spatial aspect, as it would be necessary to sample different parts of the plant root, 
and this does not give the exact colonization patterns in any case. 
A final question could be whether D757 is the best suited organism for this exper-
iment. It grows slower in the setup as can be seen in Figure 10, where after one day 
D757 reaches ~3∙103 compared to ~1.3∙105 for SBW25. Another point Hansen et al 
showed is that D757 is sensitive to DAPG which is produced by SBW25(35,41). Re-
search has found other degraders of CLPs, such as Streptomyces spp. that could be used 
as model organisms for degradation(63). But there are also other approaches which 
could be beneficial to consider when it comes to gaining more knowledge on the coloni-
zation dynamics of both D757 and SBW25. The quantification methods used were suit-
able for giving a snapshot of the amount on the root, but there were no spatial or tem-
poral perspectives. 
The CFU counting in this setup does not allow for spatial detection of the 
bacteria: Since the entire root is sampled in the CFU counting of this experiment, it 
does not give an overview of the spatial distribution of the cells. There is also a point, 
whether weight is the optimal measure for normalization. Using root surface, which can 
be calculated by software from microscopy images such as in Guan et al., would be an-
other option, yet more time consuming(36). Also, the counting method in this experi-
ment does not give any insight into the distribution of the cells on the plant root. Cao et 
al. were able to do spatial profiling of complex microbial communities using Fluores-
cent in situ hybridization (FISH). The strength of this method lies in giving both a spa-
tial aspect as well as being able to differentiate different cell lines on the root. This 
would also overcome the issue related to sensitivity, as single cells can be distinguished 
on the root(64).  
CFU counting does not allow for detecting temporal development of coloniza-
tion on a single plant: A limit with the method employed in this research as well as the 
Cao and Vallance paper is that it is necessary to destroy the root for sampling. This 
means that the temporal aspect of root colonization on individual roots is lacking. An 
interesting experiment dealing with this is described in Knights et al. They describe a 
setup that by a hydroponics system, can monitor real time colonization of fluorescent 
bacteria. They do this by inserting an Arabidopsis root into a flow system, where a 
small section is being monitored by a microscope. Like this, they can monitor the colo-
nization of the bacteria in real time on the observed site, overcoming the temporal issue

device adds a broader spatial aspect compared to Knights et al(66).  
This setup is simplified and does not reveal in situ dynamics, that could be 
important: A final consideration to these experiments is that co-inoculation studies like 
this very poorly mimic the in situ conditions. Even though some compensation is done

4.2.3 Considerations as to why viscosin could not be detected in the setup 
 
Part of the hypothesis was related to whether viscosin played an important role for 
the colonization of SBW25. As described earlier, it was not possible to detect viscosin 
in the setup in vitro. It was however possible to detect a metabolite with m/z=637.0518 
on both a plant root grown with D757 and SBW25 and on the surface of the beads in a 
cross section of the growth setup with SBW25 grown axenically as seen on Figure 12. 
Although it was deemed out of scope to investigate the exact origin of the compound, its 
presence suggests that the metabolite is unique to SBW25 grown in MS and thus sug-
gests that bacterial metabolites can be tracked on plant roots in this setup. It also shows 
that the sampling method for MALDI-MSI is viable for detection of Pseudomonas me-
tabolites. But that viscosin cannot be found neither in vitro nor in planta means that 
something in the experiments made them unfit to investigate the role of viscosin. There 
are several factors that could be important here regarding the actual experiment for de-
tection or growth dynamics of the strain. 
Viscosin might not be produced because of lack of stress in the environment: 
As discussed earlier regarding the in vitro experiments, maybe the same holds true for 
the in planta setup and viscosin is not produced at detectable amounts. Since viscosin 
was not detectable on the root or on the cross section of the growth setup, it can be in-
ferred that in this setup viscosin production is not induced by colonization compared to 
in vitro. However, Nielsen et al. and Guan et al. described that the expression of NRPS 
related to production of CLPs were increased during drought stress(36,59). They pro-
pose that the production of CLPs might give a competitive advantage in the soil. This is 
well in line with the functions of CLPs related to increased colonization abilities, antibi-
otic capacities and increased plant growth(30,31). The plants in this research were not 
methodically exposed to drought stress, which could be important for detection of vis-
cosin. Possibly, another plant growth setup would have increased detection. The experi-
ments showing increased production of CLPs were all conducted in the soil. Maybe 
since there is little stress on the bacteria or the plant in this setup, they do not produce 
the compound. Furthermore, Both in Guan and Nielsen, the experiments run over longer 
time, 4-5 and 2 weeks respectively, in comparison to three days in this experiment. It 
was, however, possible to see viscosin produced on KB-media after only 2 days of 
growth as seen in Figure 4, so it is not exclusively reliant on time. Zhou et al. do pro-
pose that CLP production is increased in the presence of glycerol, which could increase 
the production of CLPs specifically on KB containing glycerol(36,68).   
Cross sectioning of plants might be too specific for detection: Another issue 
might be related to the sectioning of the samples in the MALDI-MSI experiment. In this 
case, the sample was made by a horizontal cross sectioning. As can be seen on Figure 8, 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
the cells do not distribute themselves evenly on the root. It is thus possible that the part 
of the plant being investigated has very little cell growth, which would make it harder to 
find Pseudomonas metabolites produced in low amounts. A way to deal with this could 
be to try and make the slice longitudinally, which would cover a larger surface area of 
the plant. Another MALDI matrix creation protocol could also be applied. Velickovic et 
al. applied what they call the RhizoMAP, a rhizobox setup where they could see the 
plant roots through glass slides in. To the roots, they applied thin polycarbonate sheets, 
which could then be exposed to MALDI-MSI. Here they were able to show the spatial 
distribution of 500 metabolites. They focused on root exudates and not bacterial natural 
products, so it can only be speculated that it could work for viscosin. This method offers 
a non-destructive way of analyzing the spatial distribution of metabolites on the longitu-
dinal plane of the plant roots, enabling the ability to link detection of metabolites with 
different colonization studies as in this research along with plant growth investiga-
tions(69).  
Increased scale of the setup could increase the statistical power: A limit to the 
experiments conducted in this research is related to scalability. In this experiment the 
most extensive sampling for one treatment was conducted according to Figure 11. Here 
6 samples for each treatment and timepoint were analyzed. In comparison, Caroll et al. 
did 30 samples, to increase the statistical certainty. It would be favorable to increase the 
number of samples done for both counting and detection of viscosin.  
A strong strengthening for many of these hypotheses would have been if it was 
possible to compare the colonization rates and the metabolic profiles with a viscosin de-
ficient mutant. This would have given a true insight to whether viscosin was important 
for colonization in this setup. This was however not possible within the timeframe and 
will be discussed below. 
 
4.3 Mutant creation and the intermediate steps 
 
The method for scarless genome engineering in Pseudomonas using the two-plas-
mid system pSNW2 and pQURE6-High is described Volke et al.(54). The strength of 
this method lies in its visual markers, making it easy to select candidates in each step 
simply by picking colonies based on fluorescence cues and verifying with standard 
methods, such as PCR and plasmid sequencing. In this research, these methods allowed 
for detection of correct insertions of the homology regions in the pSNW2 plasmid (Fig-
ure 13, Figure 14) where the PCR-bands and the sequencing were well aligned. The flu-
orescent cues also resulted in easily distinguishable colonies (Figure 15), showing that 
there is receptance to the pSNW2 in SBW25. Furthermore, it offers a very precise ge-
netic editing method, leaving no genetic scarring. There are several reasons which might 
explain why the mutation failed along with some interesting observations. 
As mentioned earlier, the construction of the plasmid pSNW2ΔviscA appeared to 
be successful based on the sequencing result. There was a slight discrepancy between 
the calculated and the sequenced lengths of the two plasmids of 35 bp. All functional 
parts of the plasmid, however, were intact as can be seen in Figure 13, and the homol-
ogy regions were correctly inserted around the viscA gene.  
It was possible to find three phenotypes of SBW25 after the mating inserting 
pSNW2ΔviscA. A non-fluorescent, and two with varying degrees of fluorescence which 
can be seen in Figure 15. What was common for both the fluorescent strains was a 
change in morphology compared to the non-fluorescent strains. The colonies had a

42

larger circumference, which could be a motility dynamic or better growth on the kana-
mycin containing plates. The increased fluorescence could be due to insertion in a dif-
ferent locus on the genome, which is described in Volke et al. as a possibility for in-
creased expression of the GFP(70). The strong green was maybe not the correct choice 
for continuing with the next mating. This would have to be resolved by sequencing.  
In the mating procedure for inserting the pQURE6-High and resolving the mer-
odiploid structure seen in Figure 16, it was not possible to obtain a mutant. It was possi-
ble to obtain red colonies but not resolve the green fluorescence. Somehow the removal 
of the pSNW2 backbone and the homologous recombination that should be induced by 
the double stranded break at the I-SCEI sites did not occur. Considering the size of the 
gene at 6222 bp, this should not exclude it as a target, as Volke et al. managed to delete 
a gene of >10000 bp(54). An approach could still be to decrease the size of the cutout 
and target specific domains in the NRPS gene for deletions, which would hinder synthe-
sis, such as the lipoination, also called Cstart domain, linking the fatty acid to the peptide 
domain. This could be attempted to be located by software such as An-
tismash(30,68,71). It could also be of interest to target other genes related to the vis-
cosin synthesis. There are three genes related to the synthesis viscA, viscB and viscC. 
The two latter are localized together at 2793980 to 2818158 and viscA at 4428720 to 
4434941. They are thus not located in a single BGC, and the regulatory mechanisms are 
not well described(68). Another possibly important aspect is that this two-plasmid sys-
tem is optimized in P. putida and has not yet been shown to function in P. fluorescens 
SBW25 that were used in these experiments. It is thus possible that some stage in the 
protocol is toxic or not effective in SBW25. 
Due to time constraints the construction of the mutant was not further investi-
gated. A viscosin mutant will however be necessary to establish whether viscosin has an 
important effect on the root colonization, and to further investigate the exact mechanics 
of colonization in the setup and the production regulation.  
 
So, in conclusion to the experimental work, it is not possible to confirm whether 
viscosin plays an important role for colonization in this setup. This is because it could 
not be detected, and there was no significant inhibition of colonization of SBW25 when 
grown together with D757. It is possible that viscosin does not play an important role in 
the colonization under these conditions, but this cannot be confirmed. Previous research 
established the importance of viscosin in colonization in soil, using a viscosin deficient 
mutant and under different biotic and abiotic conditions(36,59). Some or all these as-
pects would have to be addressed in later studies to confirm the role of viscosin in this 
setup. 
 
4.4 Future adaptations to the protocol in short and long term 
perspectives 
 
To further investigate the hypothesis, the research question still stands, does the 
hydrolysis of viscosin have an effect on the ability of SBW25 to colonize plant roots. To 
investigate further in the same setup, some parameters could be changed to try and de-
tect viscosin in the setup. This would be to avoid changing too many parameters at 
once, systematically ruling out options that would limit the production of viscosin. Af-
terwards, it would be of interest to adapt the methodology, to give even more insights 
into the precise dynamics of root colonization and metabolome.  
==PAGE:43==
 
43 
4.4.1 Mutant creation and changing the parameters of the growth setup could in-
duce production of viscosin and elucidate bacterial colonization dynamics 
 
The very first step would be to construct a viscosin deficient mutant. This would 
give some insights into the setup used in this research, whether viscosin was important 
or not for root colonization. 
Then, it would be to make a setup where viscosin can be detected in vitro in the 
TS setup. Changing the media composition to mimic other colonization experiments to 
0.5xMS could be interesting(44,60). Also considering Nielsen et al. who found viscosin 
after a week of growth, reducing the growth rate of the bacteria by using a less rich me-
dia might be interesting to shift more of the colony towards secondary metabolism. This 
could also be achieved by sampling after a longer period, such as after 1-2 weeks. This 
would however not give insights into the colonization rate considering the rate of colo-
nization dropped after 2 days(59). Zhou et al. also proposed that the presence of glyc-
erol could increase the production of CLPs(68). These steps are easy to implement and 
investigate. 
Regarding the degrader, D757 could still be an interesting candidate to investigate 
further. It is known that it can degrade viscosin, so once a setup where viscosin is con-
sistently detectable, it could be interesting to investigate the effects of co-inoculation. 
Two factors appear especially relevant: concentration and timing. Increasing the inocu-
lation ratio from 1:50 (SBW25/D757) as shown in Figure 11, it could be interesting to 
increase to numbers as high as 1:2000 similar to Hansen et al.(41). This might empha-
size the competitive capacities of D757 if any against SBW25. Then, it could be inter-
esting to change the time of inoculation for SBW25. According to Figure 11 D757 colo-
nizes the plant roots after 3 days. Delaying the inoculation of SBW25 to 3 days after in-
oculating with D757 could show whether the capacity for SBW25 to invade the rhizo-
sphere is reduced when a degrader of viscosin was present. This is also relevant consid-
ering the possible application of SBW25 as a commercial biofertilizer.   
Lastly it could be interesting to use a more sensitive detection method, especially 
considering that the growth dynamics of these two strains are quite different, where 
D757 went below detection limit after 3 days using agar plate CFU based methods. Q-
PCR based methods would have the sensitivity and ease of execution for quantification 
of both strains, even after D757 is undetectable by plating after 3 days.  
These steps are all relatively easy to implement and can be done without changing 
the growth setup. To get a richer understanding of dynamics, it might be interesting to 
apply other methods of detection of both viscosin and the cells. 
 
4.4.2 Expanding in to complex setups could further explore the dynamics and 
role of viscosin and its hydrolysis in situ 
One of the major issues related to the methods used in this research and the pro-
posed steps to investigate the role of viscosin further is that they are all temporal and 
spatially limited. Since the sampling for growth quantification and viscosin detection re-
lies on destruction of the sample, it is not possible to track the development on a single 
plant root. This could be solved by having a system, where data generation does not re-
quire intervention in the rhizosphere system. This could be done by using rhizoboxes, of 
which there are many examples of. They offer a setup which allows live monitoring of 
the roots in general through glass slides, and Kaduchová et al showed how using their 
EasyClick setup directly mountable on to a confocal microscopy, they were able to do in 
planta monitoring of root colonization by fluorescence(45,72). This would allow for 
==PAGE:44==
44 
Degradation of Pseudomonas produced cyclic lipopeptides in vitro and in planta 
both a spatial and temporal monitoring of the fluorescent bacteria, which would give a 
better understanding of the colonization dynamics of SBW25. 
It would also be necessary to address the detection of viscosin in this setup. The 
method applied in this research has been shown to show the spatial distribution of Pseu-
domonas metabolites in a horizontal crosscut. But to give a better picture of the spatial 
distribution, knowing that bacteria do not colonize evenly on the plant roots, investigat-
ing the distribution of viscosin on the longitudinal plane might give a more precise im-
age(37,39,73). It could also be possible to apply methods such as described by Velicko-
vic et al, where using a rhizobox and their polycarbonate sheets were able to in a non-
destructive way measure the spatial distribution of metabolites(69). In an ideal world, 
combining two setups as proposed here, giving the possibility of directly linking the 
spatial distribution of metabolites and cells could give great insight into the production 
of metabolites of colonizing bacteria. 
In the furthest extent of this, it would be interesting to expand to more complex 
systems using both soil and native microbiomes to the host plants. This is relevant to in-
vestigate the potential importance of viscosin in root colonization dynamics in an ap-
plied setup on to fields.  
 
4.5 Conclusion 
 
This research paper aimed to investigate the hypothesis: If viscosin is broken 
down by another organism during plant root colonization, the ability of SBW25 to colo-
nize will be reduced. The research question which was the basis of the experiments was 
then; Does the breaking down or presence of viscosin affect the ability of SBW25 to 
colonize tomato plant roots?  
Due to the inability to detect viscosin in the experimental setup and unsuccessful 
mutant construction, this study could not conclusively determine whether viscosin deg-
radation impacts colonization. It was possible to establish that SBW25 can colonize the 
roots in the proposed setup. D757 was undetectable after 3 days in co-culture with 
SBW25 but in isolation was detectable after 3 days. It was also possible to detect metab-
olites most likely produced by SBW25 by MALDI-MS. Viscosin was not able to be de-
tected, and it was not possible to construct a viscosin deficient mutant within the 
timeframe. Therefore, it was not possible to assess the importance of the molecule in the 
colonization dynamics of SBW25, but there are many aspects which could be adjusted, 
such as: co-inoculation parameters, media composition, analytical methods and growth 
setups. Future work would have to focus on creating a system where the growth dynam-
ics and CLPs can be investigated spatially and temporally. Ultimately, the experiments 
would have to expand, investigating dynamics in soil and in situ to test the importance 
of viscosin in a setup closer to the field. 
PGPR continues to be interesting to replace agrochemicals for a more sustainable 
agriculture. Pseudomonas spp. has many of the biological tools relevant for applications 
in the field such as: Plant growth promotion, antibiotic tools and resistance to 
stress(30,34,59). But it is still paramount to overcome some of the major hindrances re-
lated to the implementation of PGPRs before they can start to replace agrochemicals on 
a large scale. One of these hindrances is related to consistent colonization of the plant 
roots(39,41). Better understanding the dynamics of root colonization is then one of the 
key aspects to investigate further. Understanding the exact mechanisms could offer val-
uable insights on how to increase the consistency of successful colonization. CLPs are 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
important molecules for all of the beneficial effects PGPRs exhibit as well as for root 
colonization(30,34). That it was not possible to show a correlation between colonization 
rate and the metabolic fate of viscosin in this research, does not mean that viscosin is 
without impact on the colonization of Pseudomonas spp. in soil. Future work will have 
to focus on creating setups which allow for precise and efficient study of the interac-
tions between microorganisms and plant roots.  
Safeguarding stable food production is one of the key challenges of humanity in 
this century. Harnessing the power inherent in nature could be one part of the puzzle, 
making sure that we can continue to benefit from the earth without destroying what na-
ture has made.
