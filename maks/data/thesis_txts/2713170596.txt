Abstract
Subsea pipelines transporting natural gas are vulnerable to formation of gas hy­
drates, causing blockage. A method of inhibiting gas hydrate formation is by dehy­
dration of natural gas with a glycol, such as tri­ethylene glycol (TEG). Cubic­Plus­
Association (CPA) is an equation of state capable of modelling phase equilibria at
these conditions, but experimental data is needed to evaluate the model. Equinor
has measured new multicomponent data for VLE’s relevant to subsea natural gas
processing, which we have been allowed to publish. This experimental data have
been thoroughly studied in this thesis and have served as the foundation for eval­
uating and improving the thermodynamic model at these subsea conditions. The
data have been modelled with CPA in this work, using pure component parameters
and binary interaction parameters from literature. Several association schemes
for TEG were considered and compared. Furthermore, several binary interac­
tion parameters have been optimized in this work, yielding new parameter sets
for TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane pending publication. Optimized parameters
have been subjected to uncertainty analysis by statistical bootstrapping, to obtain
confidence intervals. An extensive sensitivity analysis has been carried out for the
multicomponent VLE mixture, where the sensitivity of all involved pure component
parameters and binary interaction parameters are measured. A major academic
conclusion from this thesis, is that model evaluation supports the hypothesis that
the TEG 4C association scheme is the superior association scheme. The other
major achievement, is the newly reported binary interaction parameters, now re­
ported with confidence intervals. An implication from the results and knowledge
obtained from this thesis, is an improvement in thermodynamic modelling and un­
derstanding of fluid phase equilibria involving TEG, which hopefully can influence
decision­making in process design of natural gas dehydration plants in the future.

1
Introduction
This work is a continuation of the article ”New association schemes for tri­ethylene
glycol” [1] and the bachelor thesis ”Thermodynamic modelling of associating com­
pounds for gas processing” [2]. In these previous works, the molecule Tri­ethylene
Glycol (TEG) have been thoroughly studied, with modelling and parameterization
for new association schemes.
When natural gas is extracted, it is typically saturated with water. The presence
of water in the natural gas can lead to the formation of gas hydrates in subsea
conditions. Furthermore, the water content contributes to corrosion of pipelines
[3]. For these reasons, effort must be put into inhibiting the gas hydrate formation,
and one method of doing so is by dehydration with glycols. Injection of glycols has
proven successful due to their high absorption efficiency, low cost of regeneration
and its non­corrosive properties [4]. Tri­ethylene glycol (TEG) is the most used
glycol for this application [5], [6], and is also the glycol of focus in this thesis.
Thermodynamic models exist for calculating the complex fluid phase equilibria,
and these models have demonstrated the ability to accurately calculate multicom­
ponent phase equilibria. However, the model performance is deeply dependent
on the availability of accurate experimental data. For advanced equations of state
(EoS), which are utilized in this thesis, each associating compound requires five
pure component parameters, which are fitted to pure component data, and in­
teraction parameters between compounds are fitted to binary data. Then model
performance is measured by comparing model results with multicomponent phase
equilibria data. However, very few multicomponent data exist in literature for sys­
tems related to natural gas dehydration.
This thesis seeks to alleviate the lack of multicomponent data by publishing ex­
perimental data measured by Equinor in scientific literature and then use this data
to evaluate model performance.
This report is structured in four general parts:
• Theory and Methodology
• Parameter Optimization and Uncertainty Analysis
• Thermodynamic Modelling

• Extensive Sensitivity Analysis
Theory and Methodology covers theory behind thermodynamic equations of
state, with Soave Redlich Kwong (SRK) as an example, and detailed description
of the Cubic Plus Association (CPA) equation of state (EoS) and corresponding
model parameters. A brief description of the modelling software is presented. An
introduction to the mathematics of parameter optimization is given, along with the­
ory and methodology of statistical bootstrapping, which is the method of choice
for uncertainty analysis. Sensitivity analysis is introduced, and its implementation
explained.
Parameter Optimization and Uncertainty Analysis covers the optimization of
binary interaction parameters for the TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane binaries, fol­
lowed by uncertainty analysis by statistical bootstrapping, mirroring the procedure
used in Qvistgaard et al. [1] and Kruger et al. [7], and inspired by Bjørner et al.
[8]. Optimization is performed for several associations schemes of TEG. Obtained
parameters and corresponding confidence intervals are pending publication in the
journal Fluid Phase Equilibria.
Thermodynamic Modelling covers detailed modelling of multicomponent VLE
data measured by Equinor. The measured system contains water, TEG and a
natural gas mixture. Flash calculations are performed at physical conditions mea­
sured by Equinor, and modelling results are compared with experimental data.
This section serves as an evaluation of CPA’s ability to model multicomponent
mixtures, and also to measure improvements to model performance from previ­
ously described parameter optimization. Furthermore, results from this section
are used to evaluate which association scheme for TEG is the best since new
schemes have recently been proposed [1].
The previously described multicomponent VLE mixture is subjected to Extensive
Sensitivity Analysis, where all pure component parameters and binary interac­
tion parameters are independently increased and decreased incrementally, while
measuring model performance, to investigate which parameters are the most in­
fluential in this multicomponent VLE system.

Literature Review
2.1
Equations of State
In thermodynamics, an equation of state (EoS) is a mathematical relationship used
to obtain thermodynamic and physical properties of a system from a set of physical
conditions, such as volume, temperature and pressure. The ideal gas law is the
simplest of such EoS, relating temperature, pressure and volume via the following
famous relationship:
PV = nRT
(2.1)
The development of the Van der Waals EoS in the 19th century was a significant
improvement to the ideal gas law. Improvement came from adding an attraction
parameter and a molecular volume parameter, which served as a ”repulsive” force.
These parameters appear in modern EoS with slight modifications. Later, building
on top of Van der Waals, a modern EoS called Soave­Redlich­Kwong (SRK) is
introduced, which will be the first EoS to be introduced and explained in this thesis.
2.2

2.3
Cubic Plus Association
2.3.1
Purpose and applications
The SRK EoS is often the model of choice in the petroleum industry, however
some complex mixtures are beyond the capabilities of SRK. Examples of such
complex mixtures are hydrogen bonding systems, whose effects cannot be ac­
counted for by SRK. In this thesis, the use of tri­ethylene glycol in natural gas de­
hydration is studied, which is a molecule capable of associating (hydrogen bond­
ing).
For these reasons it was found necessary to develop a model capable of describ­
ing associating systems, e.g. systems with hydrogen bonds, and also base the
model on the widely used SRK EoS. This lead to the development of the CPA EoS
[10] in 1996, and the theory behind this model will be described in this section.
2.3.2

terms from SRK and the association term accounting for hydrogen bonding de­

2.5
Statistical Bootstrapping
Parameters of thermodynamic models, such as CPA, are typically found through
a process of fitting parameters to experimental data, which is prone to measure­
ment errors. By extension, some uncertainty must exist for the parameters of
the thermodynamic models. Therefore a method of assessing the parameter un­
certainty is important. Unfortunately, often parameters published in literature are
not reported with any uncertainty. In this thesis, a method called ”statistical boot­
strapping” will be used to assess the parameter uncertainty. While the method
has been outlined decades ago (1970’s), it was first used for assessing the un­
certainty of parameters in thermodynamic models in a paper by Bjørner et al. [8].
The method has since been used successfully in other publications [1][7]. Statis­
tical bootstrapping involves repeating the optimization for a number of iterations,
where the fitting data used in each iteration is a random sample (with replace­
ment) from the original data set. This approach yields parameter distributions,
from which a confidence interval may be derived. Furthermore, the distributions
of pairs of parameters may be plotted against each other on a scatter plot, to
check for correlation between parameter pairs. This method is useful for quanti­
fying parameter uncertainty, since experimental uncertainty propagates through
this method into parameter uncertainty.

Optimization Procedures
3.1.1
Objective Functions and Decision Variables
In scenarios of optimization, a key property is sought to be maximized, minimized
or approach a certain value. For this particular project, optimization will be used to
optimize binary interaction parameters, by reducing model error. In mathematical
terms, this means reducing the error between the model predictions and experi­
mental data. The property sought to be minimized, in this case the model error, is
called an objective function. In order for any optimization to occur, there must be
adjustable parameters. These parameters are known as decision variables. Ob­
jective functions and their respective decision variables will be represented using
the following notation:
OFsubscript(x1, x2, ..., xn)
(3.1)
where x1, x2, ...xn is a set of decision variables, where n is the amount of decision
variables. The subscript of the objective function determines whether the function
is to be minimized or maximized. For minimization, which is the only case in
this project, the objective function is denoted as OFmin. The objective function
is necessarily a function of the decision variables, and the method of performing
optimization of these decision variables is known as the optimization algorithm.

When given such a problem it is often necessary to apply numerical methods for
minimizing the objective function. The numerical method of choice in this thesis
is the Levenberg­Marquardt Algorithm (LMA). LMA was presented in a paper by
Levenberg, K. in 1944[26], and has since become one of the most widely used
optimization algorithms. This algorithm has been successfully used in previous
work involving parameter estimation for equations of state[1][27][7], and is there­
fore also utilized in this work for kij fitting. LMA is an elaborate algorithm capable
of finding a local minimum of the objective function. Since only local minimas are
found, it is important to supply the algorithm with an initial guess for decision vari­
ables, if such initial guesses are available. The importance of a good initial guess
is also underlined by the fact, that LMA is an unbounded optimization algorithm,
meaning bounds for decision variables cannot be set. LMA is an iterative method,
and as such making it a lucrative option for software implementation. A Python
implementation of LMA is utilized in this thesis. This implementation is included
in a package called SciPy, in a function called least_squares()1.

4.5
Conclusions
A high degree of measurement uncertainty is present in gas phase measure­
ments, for both TEG and H2O. In the case of TEG, low levels of concentrations
made accurate measurements difficult (<1 ppm). A source of uncertainty in gas
phase H2O content is contamination of moisture from the atmosphere. Measure­
ments and modelling show that presence of water in TEG leads to lower TEG
content in the gas and gas content in the liquid, and an increase in temperature
leads to lower TEG and H2O content in the gas phase. These observations are
true for both Synthetic Gas and Real Gas. Pressure increases lead to increase
of the gas content in the liquid, increase of the TEG content in the gas and a de­
crease of the water content in the gas phase. For the Real Gas, an increase in
temperature leads to lower gas content in liquid phase at 60 bar, and higher gas
content in the liquid phase at 125 bar.
Modelling work shows that the well established 4C association scheme for TEG
provides better results than the remaining association schemes (4F, 5F, 6F and
5C). However, since the remaining association schemes used binary interaction
parameters fitted to TEG­Alkane binaries with TEG modelled as 4C, improve­
ments may likely be found in future work by fitting binary interaction parameters
with the remaining association schemes. The quantity of measurement for the
model’s performance is the AARD, which shows high model to data errors for
gas phase measurements (AARD > 100%), while model performance in liquid
phase composition is higher (AARD < 30% for 4C association scheme). Poor
performance in H2O content in the gas phase modelling is probably due to con­
tamination from atmosphere, as discussed. Despite high AARD for TEG content
in the gas phase (as reported in table 4.6 and 4.5), visual inspection reveals that
the model can predict the trend of the experimental data, and the modelling may
be adequate for process design purposes.
A manuscript has been written in this thesis, documenting the experimental data
by Equinor and the modelling results in this work, which is pending publication in
the scientific journal Fluid Phase Equilibria. This manuscript is denoted ”Manuscript
1” in this thesis, and more information regarding this manuscript may be found in
section 8.2.

5.4
Conclusions
Binary interaction parameters for the TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane binaries
have been fitted to experimental VLE data by Jou et al., and fitted parameters
were subjected to uncertainty analysis by statistical bootstrapping, where confi­
dence intervals for the parameters have been derived. The confidence interval
for the TEG­Ethane kij is narrow (±0.70%), indicating a low degree of uncertainty,
whereas the fitted TEG­Propane kij exhibited a high degree of uncertainty. The
TEG­Propane kij was fitted with a temperature inverse dependent term, which
is the source of high uncertainty. Statistical bootstrapping showed that the tem­
perature dependent term was negatively linearly correlated with the temperature
independent term, with r2≥0.989 in all cases, indicating a clear linear correlation.
This finding suggests that the TEG­Propane interaction is over­parameterized, as
the two fitted parameters in effect are dependent on each other, through a linear
relationship. This is also the reason the parameters have a high degree of un­
certainty, since there are essentially many solutions to the optimization problem.
Observing this correlation, we could ask the question whether the TEG­Propane
kij should be temperature independent. We observed that the none of the 95%
confidence intervals for the temperature dependent term included zero, meaning
that the temperature dependent is statistically significantly different from zero, and
thus we recommend using the temperature dependent kij for TEG­Propane.
Findings from this thesis (kij optimization) have been reported in Manuscript 2
(see section 8.3, which has has been submitted to Fluid Phase Equilibria for pub­
lication. The manuscript is currently pending peer review.

6.4
Conclusions
The newly fitted parameters have been used to model multicomponent data pre­
sented in section 4, and improvements in modelling have been observed for the
4C association scheme, whereas the remaining association schemes exhibited
worse performance. Parameter optimization of TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane
kij ’s had no measurable impact on gas phase water content modelling for any of
the association schemes considered.
Even though kij optimization improved TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane binary
VLE modelling for all association schemes considered, it is evident that the 4F, 5F,
6F and 5C exhibit poor performance when extending to multicomponent VLE data.
On the other hand, the 4C scheme seems to outperform the remaining schemes
in every multicomponent VLE category considered in this modelling. Previous
modelling by Qvistgaard et al. [1] showed that 4C was superior in a ternary sys­
tem containing TEG­H2O­CO2. Due to 4C’s flexibility in modelling multicomponent
systems, I recommend the use of the 4C scheme for TEG over the newly proposed
alternative schemes (4F, 5F, 6F and 5C).

Extensive Sensitivity Analysis
7.1
Introduction
The Equinor multicomponent VLE data represents a complicated fluid phase equi­
libria, with many compounds of varying concentrations.
Some of these com­
pounds exhibit complicated behavior, such as water and TEG being highly asso­
ciating, and CO2 being a solvating compound. In the previous section, optimized
kij’s were evaluated by measuring the improvement in model performance for the
multicomponent VLE. This evaluation showed only slight improvement or deteri­
oration in model performance, depending on the association scheme. However,
relying on this metric as a way of measuring model improvement relies on a funda­
mental assumption that the TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane kij’s have any signif­
icant impact on model performance for this complex system. In other words, it is
assumed that the system is sensitive to the value of these kij’s. Since ethane and
propane are of relatively low concentration in the studied mixtures, this assump­
tion may not hold up. In light of this, the assumption of the system’s sensitivity
to TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane kij is investigated for its validity, by performing
sensitivity analysis. For academic interest, this sensitivity analysis is extended to
include all utilized kij ’s, and also all pure component parameters of compounds
involved in this mixture.

Conclusions
Equinor has performed measurements on a multicomponent VLE system contain­
ing TEG, water and natural gas. As of yet, this data has not yet been published. In
this thesis, the aforementioned multicomponent VLE data is modelled for the first
time, using the CPA EoS. Several association schemes for TEG were considered.
Most association schemes demonstrated the ability for CPA to adequately model
this multicomponent VLE system. Quantitatively, the 4C association scheme for
TEG has proved to be superior in modelling this data, and with results from other
works demonstrating the superiority of 4C in modelling multicomponent VLE’s, it
is concluded in this thesis that 4C is the superior association scheme for TEG,
due to its flexibility in modelling both pure component properties, binary VLE and
multicomponent VLE.
Binary interaction parameters for TEG­Ethane and TEG­Propane (for all consid­
ered association schemes) have been successfully optimized using the Levenberg­
Marquardt algorithm, and later subjected to uncertainty analysis by statistical boot­
strapping to obtain confidence intervals. Binary VLE data by Jou et al. were used
for the optimization. The TEG­Ethane kij has a narrow confidence interval, indi­
cating low uncertainty. The TEG­Propane kij has a wide confidence interval in
both the temperature dependent and independent terms, indicating high parame­
ter uncertainty. This point is further discussed in section 5.
The multicomponent VLE studied in this thesis has been subjected to an extensive
uncertainty analysis, where the sensitivity to all pure component parameters and
binary interaction parameters was investigated. The parameters of highest influ­
ence were the pure component parameters, while the binary interaction parame­
ters were much less influential to model performance. TEG, water and methane
had the most influential pure component parameters, which is unsurprising since
these are the compounds of highest concentration in the multicomponent VLE.

A.1
Multicomponent VLE Experimental Procedure
To preface, credit for the entirety of section A.1 goes to Julia Trancoso, whom
wrote this section for our joint Manuscript 1 (see section 8.2).
The experiments were conducted using a high­pressure equilibrium cell manufac­
tured by Sanchez Technology (now Core Laboratories), which has been previously
described [29]–[31]. The loading and equilibration procedures are those used by
Kruger et al. [30], with the exception that natural gas mixtures have replaced pure
methane. The analytical apparatus is also the same used by Kruger et al., with the
micro gas chromatograph (μGC) for the quantification of natural gas compounds
(figure A.1).
Therefore, typically 10 gas samples (≈0.5 l each) were routed, via valve V­1 A,
through ATD tubes and analysed by GC−MS­2 to calculate TEG vapor composi­
tion (y1), while the water content of the gas phase (y3) was measured by routing
5­8 vapor samples (≈0.5 l each), via valve V­1 B, to the KF analyser. For the liq­
uid phase, the gas meter (GM­1) volume change in addition to the liquid sample
mass (Ohaus Explorer Pro, m ± 0.001 g), water content (Metrohm 915 Ti­Touch, u
= ± 2%), and density (Anton Paar DMA 4500M, ρ ± 0.00007 g.mol−1) were used
to determine the dissolved methane content in the liquid phase (x2). The Agilent
3000 μGC with four channels (details in table A.1), in its turn, was used for the
quantification of each component dissolved in the “flash gas” (xNG) and in the
vapour composition (yNG). For that, it was taken, respectively, 5­8 samples from
the GM­1 via valve V­4 A, and 4−6 samples directly from the vapour phase of the
equilibrium cell through V­2 B and V­4 B (green pathway in figure A.1).
The μGC was calibrated using four calibration gases, allowing for the quantifi­
cation of up to 15 components. Periodic verification and recalibration were per­
formed in order to ensure accurate measurements. Also, the first few samples
were used to flush the sampling system until a predefined threshold (i.e., GC peak
area) for O2 content was met, as air ingress into the sampling pathways could not
be completely negated. Thereafter it was still necessary to reprocess the N2 com­
position, by subtracting the amount of air contamination. It was assumed that the
N2 contamination from air was related to the O2 content according to a 79:21 ratio.
