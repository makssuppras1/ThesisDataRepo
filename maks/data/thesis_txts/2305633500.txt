Title: Vibration analysis and comparison of two- and
three-bladed wind turbines
Abstract
In the current thesis, the vibrations of wind turbines
with a balanced three-bladed rotor, an imbalanced three-
bladed rotor and a two-bladed rotor are studied.
A
laboratory scale turbine is described by a mathemat-
ical model, based on spring theory, and the stability is
regarded by a combination of Floquet analysis and the
Multi-Blade Coordinate-transformation. Campbell dia-
grams are used to study the frequency behaviour for dif-
ferent rotor speeds. The results are compared with aer-
oelastic simulations with HAWC2 and measurements.
The used method for the stability analysis will be seen to
be a good measure for three-bladed rotor conﬁgurations,
but inappropriate for two-bladed turbines. In contrast
to the balanced three-bladed turbine, the turbine with
the imbalanced and the two-bladed rotor conﬁguration
are found to show additional frequencies for the tower,
the ﬂapwise rotor modes, as well as the edgewise rotor
modes, depending on the rotational speed of the rotor.
These are considered to be whirling frequencies. They
are seen to be arising from the interaction between the
rotor and the tower. Due to the diﬀerent periodicity of
the eigenfrequencies of the imbalanced three-bladed and
the two-bladed turbine, the whirling behaviour is shown
to be diﬀerent.
While the mathematical model shows the advantage of
very clear results, it’s simpliﬁcations are seen to over-
predict whirling frequencies. From the comparison with
the HAWC2 simulations the general frequency behaviour
is conﬁrmed, mainly for ﬂapwise related vibrations. The
measurements on the other hand show clear veriﬁcation
of the results achieved previously for asymmetric edge-
wise vibrations.

1. Introduction
With a commercial use since the 1980th, wind turbines are one of the oldest generators
of renewable energy (Verstergaard et al. (2004)). Especially in northern Europe, wind
energy has a signiﬁcant share within the national energy uses. Denmark for example
covers up to 39% (2014) of the energy consumption with wind energy (Vindmølleindus-
trien 2015). A reliable energy production over 20 years lifetime makes wind turbines the
most competitive technology among the renewable energy technologies available on the
market today (International Energy Agency (2014)).
The lifetime of a turbine is dependent on the fatigue load of the turbine. Varying loads
with high amplitudes cause fatigue damages over time, that need to be minimized. For
this purpose it is essential to reduce vibrations of the structure and to avoid any type of
resonances during the operation (Muhammad and Ristow (2010)).
In order to guarantee the stable operation of the turbine, it is essential to know the
natural frequencies of the structure and their dependency on the rotational speed of the
rotor. Only if this is the case, a control strategy can be set up, that guarantees a stable
operation outside those regions (Staino and Basu (2013)).
Since most of the commercially available turbines have a three-bladed rotor, their dy-
namic behaviour is well known. However, two-bladed machines were recently shown to
reduce the extreme loads at stand still drastically. This makes them economically and
technically interesting for sites with high maximum wind speeds in storm conditions, for
example Taifun areas (Kim et al. (2014)).
A three-bladed rotor is dynamically balanced, thus symmetrically loaded around the
rotational axis. In contrast to that, the two-bladed rotor shows a periodically varying
dynamic behaviour with the rotation. Due to this, the established analysis methods
known from the three-bladed machines can not be used. Instead, the dynamic beha-
viour needs to be analysed with methods known from helicopter industry, like Floquet
analysis and the Multi-Blade Coordinate (MBC) transformation (Stol et al. (2002)).

1. Introduction
Aim of the Thesis
The present thesis is supposed to give an understanding of the purely structural dynamic
behaviour of a wind turbine with diﬀerent rotor conﬁgurations and to observe the eﬀect of
diﬀerent rotor conﬁgurations. These will cover a balanced three-bladed rotor, as well as
two anisotropic conﬁgurations: a two-bladed rotor and a mass imbalanced three-bladed
rotor. The approach is to set up a very simple model with a two- and three-bladed rotor
to observe the general structural behaviour. The results of this model will be compared
with the results from available literature, simulations with an aeroelastic code, as well
as measurements, in order to verify observed phenomena.

2. State of Art
As wind turbines are designed for 20 years lifetime, it is essential to know the turbine
dynamics, for both, the fatigue analysis in the design stage of the structure, and the
appropriate design of a control strategy (Staino and Basu (2013) and Muhammad and
Ristow (2010)). Several previous works regarded the dynamics of wind turbines, for
example Petersen et al. (1998) or Larsen and Kim (2014).
This chapter is ﬁrstly regarding the state of the art of the structural wind turbine
dynamics. Finally, the required background theory for the conducted analysis will be
given.
2.1. Previous vibration analysis of two- and three-bladed wind
turbines
This section regards the previously conducted research and summarizes the state of the
art of the vibration analysis of two- and three-bladed wind turbines.
In 1998 the research of Petersen et al. (1998) regarded local blade whirl and global rotor
whirl interactions of a three-bladed wind turbine. On the example of stall-induced vi-
brations, the coupling between the edgewise blade vibration and the yaw-tilt whirl mode
was examined. It was found out, that the relative value of the edgewise frequency and
the yaw and tilt frequency has an important inﬂuence. In this work, the dominant inertia
force was assumed to be originating from the edgewise blade vibration. The inﬂuence
of this force on the supporting structure, the nacelle and tower was considered. The
following text will consider the inertia force resulting from the edgewise blade vibration:
the inertia force was seen to be resulting from the two asymmetric edgewise modes, one
of them related to the sine of the blade azimuthal position, and the second one related
to the cosine of the blade azimuthal position (Further consideration of this can be found
in the work of Bir (2008) or the later section 2.2). Anticipatory to section 2.2 these two
modeshapes will be called the sine mode βs and the cosine mode βc. Petersen et al.
(1998) calculated the inertia force on the rotor from the blade vibration as a total force
on the rotating hub, acting in two main directions, denoted F R

The research of Stol et al. (2002) regarded the inﬂuence of periodicity of the describ-

certain structural parameter, like the yaw stiﬀness and precone angle, on the stability

of the model was studied. Stol et al. (2002) set up a simple model with 7 degrees of
freedom for a two-bladed turbine, that was expected to be hardly inﬂuenced by the peri-

damping was observed to be the only parameter to strictly stabilize the turbine dynamics.
For helicopter industry a stability analysis method was developed to regard the rotor
as a collective of several blades by Coleman and Feingold in 1958. The great success in
describing the physics of a full rotor increased the eﬀort among researchers to make the
Coleman- or multi-blade coordinate (MBC)-transformation applicable to wind turbines
since 1997. In 2008 Bir presented a transformation scheme for variable speed and pitch
controlled turbines, that is applicable to dissimilar blades and covers disturbances used
for the control. His application to the three-bladed 5MW NREL reference wind turbine
showed, that the MBC- transformation is required before averaging over eigenvalues to
analyse the stability of wind turbines via eigenanalysis correctly. Further, he presented
the change of the modal frequencies with increasing rotor speed. Results from his studies
are presented in Figure 2.1.

His work shows, that the rotor modes consist of a collective, a progressive and a regress-
ive mode. The progressive modes are whirling forward (in the same direction as the
shaft rotation) with increasing frequency, as the rotational speed increases. The regress-
ive modes are the backward whirl modes, with decreasing frequencies, as the rotational
speed increases. Due to centrifugal stiﬀening, the frequencies of the collective modes are
increasing. It is pointed out, that the collective mode frequencies do not originate at the
same stand still frequency as the forward and backward frequency, since the blades are
interacting diﬀerently with the tower, when it comes to diﬀerent modes. Especially the
frequency of the collective lag mode is shown to be much higher than the frequencies
of the according progressive and regressive modes. The interaction of the shaft torsion
and the collective blade movement in the rotor plane is giving a higher modal frequency
in this case. Finally Bir states, that strictly a Floquet analysis should follow the MBC-
transformation for modal analysis, especially if the rotor is anisotropic with dissimilar
blades.
The work of Skjoldan and Hansen (2012) regarded the modal analysis of a 2.3MW tur-
bine from Siemens with the MBC-transformation, as well as implicit Floquet analysis.
The turbine was analysed with the isotropic three-bladed rotor conﬁguration, as well as
with an ice load on one of the blades. The periodic matrices of the turbine were extrac-

In 2014 the potential of extreme load reduction by two-bladed partial pitch turbines
came into focus. The research of Kim et al. (2014) regarded therefore among others the
dynamics of the two-bladed turbine compared to the three-bladed turbine. Campbell
diagrams were generated from time series simulated with HAWC2. Firstly, it was shown,
that the rotor position has an inﬂuence on the stand still frequencies, showing diﬀerences
between the I and the T-conﬁguration. It was observed, that the tower frequencies dur-
ing operation showed the inﬂuence of a 2P-frequency1 and the higher harmonics, while
the three-bladed turbine showed the 3P-frequencies and it’s harmonics in the tower vi-
bration. The blade frequencies where observed to whirl with 1P as well as higher whirl
frequencies forward and backward. For the three-bladed turbine, only a whirl with ±1P
was observed.
The ongoing numerical and experimental analysis of Larsen and Kim (2014) considered
the eigenfrequency, of a two-bladed turbine. It was shown, that the frequency of the
ﬁrst asymmetric ﬂapwise and the ﬁrst collective edgewise mode are dependent on the
rotor position and are periodically varying with a period of half a revolution, as it can
be seen in the Figure 2.2.

2.2. Multi-blade coordinate-transformation
The work of Bir (2008) and Henriksen et al. (2011) was regarding the Multi-Blade
Coordinate-transformation. With this transformation the motion of the single blades
is summed to coordinates, that represent the displacement of the rotor corresponding
as a collective system of all blades. This means, that the motion is expressed by the
contributions of the diﬀerent modeshapes.

of the non-rotating parts of the system (e.g tower, nacelle). In order to describe the
rotor motion, instead of the singular blade motions, the multi-blade coordinate MBC-

2.3. Floquet analysis
Floquet theory is used, when it comes to the stability analysis of systems with period-
ically varying components. In this case x is a n-sized vector of state variables of the
position from the linearised equation of motion in the form of
˙x + A (t)x = G (t)
(2.17)
where both, the n × n-matrices A (t) and the n-sized vector G (t) vary periodically with
the period of the system T. In this case a transition matrix Q is deﬁned, that transfers
the state of a variable at t to the new state at t+T, exactly one period later than the
original t.
x (T) = Qx (0)
(2.18)
each column of the n × n-transition matrix Q can be calculated from the integration of
the equation of motion, for the initial conditions of xij = 1 if i = j and xij = 0 if i ̸= j,
thus the identity matrix I (Peters and Hohenemser (1970)).
The stability of the solution of the equation of motion can than be predicted from
the eigenvalues of the transition matrix Q, called the characteristic multiplier ρ. The
system is asymptotically stable, if the characteristic multiplier | ρ |≤1. A critically
stable system will have a characteristic multiplier of | ρ |= 1 and the unstable system
will always be characterised by at least one characteristic multiplier of | ρ |≥1. For each
characteristic multiplier exists a Floquet exponent µ
µ = 1

All Campbell diagrams are presented in contoure plots, which means, that the data
points are evaluated via a bin analysis between the highest and the lowest value. The
bin with the lowest values is indicated by dark blue, medium values by green, the next
higher values are yellow, and the highest values are red. Thus, every Campbell diagram
gives a relative picture of the energy contained in the system.
The modes are, according to Bir (2008), the symmetric or collective modes, the ones
that have constant frequencies over the rotational speed.
Here, the blades all move
into the same direction. Further, there are two asymmetric modes, where one blade
moves in the opposite direction than the other blade or blades. Here, the ﬁrst mode
has a frequency decreasing with the rotational speed with −Ω, called the regressive
or backward whirling mode. The second asymmetric mode, the progressive or forward
whirling mode, has an increasing frequency with the rotational speed of +Ω. In the
ongoing work, the collective, and the two asymmetric modes are called the primary
modes.
The two asymmetric modes can here also be denoted as the primary whirl
modes. Whirl modes that show higher dependencies on the rotational speed, than the
primary whirl modes will be denoted generally as higher or additional whirl modes. It
should be noticed that the rate of change for the frequencies is given in the following
as a rate, expressed as multiples of Ωin
1

have diﬀerent units on the axis. The term "increasing with Ω" means, that the denoted
frequencies are proportional to Ωwith a proportional constant of 1.

Discussion
Regarding the characteristic multiplier, a permanent instability for the two-
bladed rotor conﬁguration is observed, while the three-bladed rotor conﬁguration always
operates in stable conditions. However, the instabilities for the two-bladed rotor con-
ﬁguration are caused by the MBC-transformation, rather than an increase of the dis-
placement amplitude in the rotating coordinate frame. Since the initial rotor position
is at θ=0◦, the sine mode βs will never be excited, since the transformation includes a
multiplication with 0. The transformation of the cosine mode βc will give the opposite:
as soon as the cosine mode βc is exited, the blades are displaced by 1 and -1 in the
rotating frame. If a multiple of the reciprocal of their natural frequency 1/fn coincides
with the length of a full revolution, the back transformation into the ﬁxed frame will give
2, implied by the summing function in Equation 2.6. This will lead to the observation
of instabilities, even though the amplitude of the displacement did not grow. The later
was observed from the Floquet multiplier in the local frame of reference, which can be
found in Figure B.1 in the appendix.
For the three-bladed rotor conﬁguration the diﬀerences in the the local blade angle lead
to an average rotor displacement that is independent of the initial rotor position.
Tests showed, that the observed pattern is comparable with a squared sine shape, with a
growing period. Since the maximum values occur as soon as a multiple of the reciprocal
of the natural frequency coincides with the length of a full revolution of the rotor rota-
tion, this pattern would be observable for the whole simulation range if the discretisation
of the rotational speed range was inﬁnitesimally small.
The Campbell diagrams show, that all rotor frequencies for both turbine conﬁgurations

dynamically stable, while the two-bladed turbine shows instabilities, introduced by the

Including the tower has the eﬀect, that it’s natural frequency is introduced into the
spectrum. Further, clear interactions with the rotor modes can be observed. Since the
tower moves the blades into the same global direction, the eﬀect on the collective ﬂap
mode, and the asymmetric edge modes can be observed.
In case of the ﬂapwise motion, coupled to the tower fore-aft motion, the frequency of
the rotor modes is increased. While the additional mass at the hub would introduce a
frequency decrease, the additional stiﬀness of the tower increases the rotor frequency,
since the two springs are in series. Thus, the total stiﬀness, and therefore the frequency
are increased. Over all, the stiﬀening eﬀect of the tower is dominating. The ﬂapwise sine
mode βs would interact with the tower torsion, which is not implemented in the model.
From the tilt motion a coupling can not be seen either. This one would transfer a mo-
ment to the tower top, not a force displacing the tower mass. A transfer of moments to

In the case of the edgewise motion, the tower side-side motion is directly coupled to the
cosine mode βc. The eﬀect of the additional mass of the hub and the stiﬀness of the
tower is dominated by the stiﬀening eﬀect, so the frequency is increased. The sine mode
βs on the other hand, is not interacting with the tower, since the model does not allow
any tower motion in ZI-direction.
For the two-bladed turbine, the range of frequency origin for the asymmetric edge modes
should also be discussed: from the MBC-transformation it became clear, that the ex-
citation of an asymmetric mode leads to the following situation: With the initial rotor
position at 0◦, the cosine mode βc is excited, while the sine mode βs contains no energy,
since it is not excited. With the rotation of the rotor, energy of the blade vibration will
be shifted into the sine mode βs, until the rotor is at a position of 90◦, where the cosine
mode βc contains no energy, while all the energy is contained the sine mode βs. Since
the diﬀerent modes are associated with slightly diﬀerent frequencies, the intermediate
states have intermediate frequencies. Thus, a range of frequencies is observed for the
low rotational speeds.
However, as the rotational speed increases, the inertia of the
rotor leads to the eﬀect, that the frequency shift is too slow compared to the rotational
speed. Thus, the frequencies within the range average and clear lines, originating from
the middle of the range can be observed.
At this point, a clear diﬀerence between the two-bladed and the three-bladed turbine
should be stated, anticipatory to Section 4.2.2: While the two-bladed turbine shows a
frequency variation for the asymmetric frequency between the cosine mode linked fre-
quency and the sine mode linked frequency over the rotor revolution, the three-bladed
turbine always shows two distinct frequencies for every rotor position: one linked to the
sine mode βs and one linked to the cosine mode βc.
Other than that, only for the two-bladed turbine, implementing the tower changed the
overall whirling behaviour of the system.
While the three-bladed turbine shows the
whirls that were previously observed in the study for the rotor without the tower, the

5. Conclusion
In this chapter, the ﬁnal results and conclusions from the present thesis are summarized
and further improvements of the studies suggested.
The studies, regarding the classical balanced three-bladed turbine, showed, that the Flo-
quet analysis combined with the Multi-Blade Coordinate-transformation gave reasonable
results regarding the stability analysis. Further, it was seen from the mathematical model
without structural or aerodynamic damping, that the gravity stiﬀening term introduces
a tendency for whirling frequencies, additional to the primary ones. Including structural
damping in the HAWC2 simulations showed, that the additional whirling frequencies
become negligible. These results agreed well, with the respective literature. Additional
to the previously observed primary frequencies, constant frequencies were introduced
into the Campbell diagram, which coincided with higher tower harmonics. From the
measurements it was seen, that the model of the three-bladed turbine was object to
a slight mass imbalance. This had the eﬀect, that the stand still frequencies for the
edgewise asymmetric frequencies showed a periodicity over the rotor revolution. Fur-
ther, this slight imbalance introduced a doubled frequency in the Campbell diagram, for
the edgewise asymmetric frequency, as well as it introduced additional whirl frequencies
to the diagram. These were not observed in previous measurements, but predicted in
literature from simulations of turbines with imbalanced three-bladed rotors.
Further, the work regarded two turbine types with anisotropic rotor conﬁgurations:
the imbalanced three-bladed rotor and the tow-bladed rotor. Even though they were
both anisotropic, signiﬁcant diﬀerences were observed. The introduction of the mass-
imbalance to the three-bladed rotor introduced a periodicity to the stand still frequencies
with a period of a full rotor revolution. This occurred due to the periodically changing
describing inertia terms, which represent the same situation once per revolution. The
values of the stand still frequencies were further seen to show variation, that was close
to a periodicity of half a revolution, since the cosine and the since mode are inﬂuenced
in the same way twice per revolution, as the center of gravity of the rotor rotates around
the center of rotation. In the Campbell diagrams only few additional whirl frequencies
were seen from the HAWC2 simulations for the asymmetric ﬂap mode frequencies, as
68
==PAGE:74==
5. Conclusion
well as for the tower mode. The reason was assumed to be, that the additional mass was
too low compared to the blade weight. However, the measurements showed very clearly
additional whirl frequencies for the asymmetric edgewise frequencies, and the symmetric
ﬂapwise frequency. Those additional frequencies were predicted by simulations in cited
literature. Further, a central frequency for the asymmetric edge mode was observed,
which was also seen in previous measurements, but not in simulations.
For the two-bladed turbine the combination of the Multi-Blade Coordinate-transformation
and the Floquet analysis was found to be inappropriate in order to predict the stability
of the turbine dynamics. The two-bladed turbine showed a clear periodicity of half a
revolution, thus the cosine and the sine mode are excited twice per revolution. While
there were two asymmetric mode frequencies co-existing for the three-bladed turbine
conﬁgurations, only one frequency existed for the two-bladed turbine, varying sinusoid-
ally between the cosine and the sine mode frequency. This varying frequency and it’s
varying direction of the resulting forces were seen to interact with the tower. Thus, the
tower motion in each direction was excited varying twice per revolution, causing whirl
mode frequencies in- and decreasing with twice the rotational speeds and their multiples.
As a result, the motion of the tower was seen to excite the rotor motion itself, causing
the additional whirling frequencies. This coupling eﬀect with the tower was seen to be
so dominant, that even without damping the inﬂuence of the gravity stiﬀening term
became negligible. These eﬀects observed from the mathematical model were conﬁrmed
by the HAWC2 simulations from the Campbell diagram for the ﬂapwise motions. Thus,
a distinct whirling behaviour was observed here, showing whirling frequencies additional
to the primary ones, that additionally in- and decrease with twice the rotational speed
and their multiples. With the measurements this distinct whirling behaviour was mainly
conﬁrmed by the asymmetric edgewise vibrations and the tower vibrations. From com-
parable literature the general whirling behaviour could be conﬁrmed, but diﬀerences
were observed as well. Further, a tendency to additional frequencies, not related to the
tower or rotor, as well as whirl frequencies, in- and decreasing with multiples of the
rotational speed were observed. The later was interpreted as a sign for a not perfectly
balanced rotor.
The presented mathematical model was seen to over-predict the eﬀects of the grav-
ity stiﬀening term, since structural damping was not included. However, it gave a good
insight into the diﬀerent whirling behaviour of the three-bladed and two-bladed turbine.
With such model, the eﬀect of imbalances should be regarded as well, especially focussing
on the central frequencies for the asymmetric rotor modes, as well as the change in the
69
==PAGE:75==

whirling behaviour for a two-bladed turbine. In an additional study like this, structural
damping should be included, to avoid over-prediction of whirling frequencies.
Regarding the stability analysis, it was seen that the current way of combining the Flo-
quet analysis with the Multi-Blade Coordinate-transformation needs to be improved.
Integrating the Floquet analysis into a eigenanalysis might be a possibility. However,
the observation and evaluation of additional whirling frequencies would be limited.
The simulations with HAWC2 were generally seen, to show a low contrast in the conture
plots in the spectra, leading to Campbell diagrams with only a few visible frequencies.
Further, it was noticed here, that the models tend to show harmonics of the tower, as
it was previously known for impulse excitations. Since some of the frequencies of the
rotor modes were coinciding with multiples of the tower frequency, no conclusion could
be drawn for higher frequencies, that were constant over the rotor speed. Here it would
be required to redo the studies with a model, with eigenfrequencies of the tower and the
rotor, that do not coincide. This could be done for example by using blades from other
material, leading to diﬀerent blade and therefore also rotor mode frequencies.
The two anisotropic rotor conﬁgurations were seen to result into signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
vibrational behaviour. Here it was seen, that the used mass imbalance was too small
to get clear results, especially from the simulations, while the measurement showed sig-
niﬁcant diﬀerences. Since Campbell diagrams hardly depend on the impulse given to
excite the system, it would be beneﬁcial to measure the input forces, in order to adjust
the impulses used in the simulation. Additionally to this, it should be evaluated, if the
assumed damping, used for the HAWC2 simulations was reasonable.
It should be regarded, that turbines will always be object to anisotropies, for example
due to production tolerances or ice loads. The measurements in this study showed, that
these do have an impact on the vibrations of the turbines, leading to additional whirling
frequencies. Here, the impact of diﬀerent percentage of imbalances on the frequency
spectrum should regarded in ongoing research. Further, it would be beneﬁcial to study
the impact of turbine aerodynamics, that are known to introduce aerodynamic damping.
In this case, it would be especially interesting, to which extend those can be assumed to
compensate for additional whirling frequencies, introduced by imbalances. This would
require, that the rotor has a balanced conﬁguration as a reference and that the input
impulse is measurable.
Finally, especially the two-bladed turbine showed the tendency for additional frequen-
cies.
In order to design a two-bladed turbine, it would be required to calculate the
fatigue load. With a tendency to further frequencies, the fatigue load would increase.
At this point, the inﬂuences of imbalances should be regarded as well, in order to deﬁne
70
==PAGE:76==

a tolerance level for the production of two-bladed turbines. From the presented results,
the two-bladed turbine would need stricter tolerances to reduce the level of vibrations.
In this case, the economical advantage of a two-bladed turbine, reducing extreme loads
might be questioned, if the production tolerances need to be stricter. In order to con-
clude on this subject, a more detailed study on the structural behaviour of a two-bladed
turbine, including the eﬀect of diﬀerent levels of imbalances, as well as full aerodynamics
is required in the ﬁrst place.
