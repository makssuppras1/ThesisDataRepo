Abstract
As a part of the green transition in the construction industry, the Danish government has introduced the
voluntary sustainability class in 2020 with the purpose of becoming mandatory requirements integrated
in the Danish building regulations by 2023. This change in regulations is characterised by a voluntary
implementation period, where it is possible to get familiar with and develop skills and expertise to handle
the upcoming requirements.
This thesis aims to investigate the expectations of a voluntary program expected to become regulations
in the construction industry with basis in the Multi-Level Perspective framework, hereby considering the
consequences on the macro-, meso-, and micro-level. The purpose is to assess the influence emerging with a
change in regulations by investigating the impact of existing knowledge, the impact on companies in the
industry, and the expectations for the regulations. The investigation is done by assessing the voluntary
sustainability class through qualitative research interviews with relevant key stakeholders in the industry
with basis in Grounded Theory.
The results showed that the existing conditions such as knowledge and experience within the topics of
the regulatory changes could lessen the impact of the changes if they are not suﬀiciently innovative or
demanding. The results also revealed that for medium to large companies with a medium to high level of
ambition regarding sustainability, the change in company structure was expected to be small. However, the
voluntary sustainability class might target the companies in the construction industry with lower levels of
ambition regarding sustainability, and the expectations are that the impact for companies in that faction
will be higher. From a Multi-Level Perspective, the results point to how gaps between these factors are
crucial for how the regimes at the meso-level will embrace the new regulations - leading to either adaption
or deconstruction of the regime - and how the types of niche innovation at the micro-level will escape to the
regime.

Resume
Den danske regering har som led i den grønne omstilling i byggebranchen introduceret den frivillige
bæredygtighedsklasse i 2020. Formålet med klassen er, at den skal blive en integreret del af Bygningsre-
glementet i 2023. En sådan ændring i reglementet er kendetegnet ved at der indgår en frivillig imple-
menteringsperiode hvor det er muligt at blive kendt med de nye krav og udvikle kompetencer til at takle
dem.
Dete speciale har til formål at undersøge forventningerne til en frivillig klasse i et reglement, med hen-
blik på at blive krav i reglementet ud fra et Multi-Level Perspective framework hvor der tages stilling til
konsekvenserne på et makro-, meso- og mikroniveau. Formålet er at vurdere den opståede indflydelse fra
en ændring i reglementet ved at undersøge indflydelsen fra eksisterende viden, indflydelsen på branchens
virksomheder og de forventninger der er til reglementet. Undersøgelsen er gennemført ved at undersøge
indflydelsen fra den frivillige bæredygtighedsklasse gennem kvalitative forskningsinterviews med centrale
interessenter fra byggebranchen med udgangspunkt i Grounded Theory.
Resultaterne viste at eksisterende omstændigheder så som viden og erfaring med emnet for ændringerne
i reglementet kan medføre en lavere indflydelse af ændringerne hvis de ikke er tilstrækkelige innovative
eller krævende. Resultaterne viste også at der for mellem til store virksomheder med et mellem til højt
ambitionsniveau vedrørende bæredygtighed, er en forventning om at strukturelle ændringer i virksomhed-
erne vil være mindre. Det kan dog være at den frivillige bæredygtighedsklasse er tilsigtet virksomheder i
byggebranchen med et lavere ambitionsniveau vedrørende bæredygtighed og derfor er forventningen, at
indflydelsen vil være højere for disse virksomheder. Set fra et Multi-Level Perspective, peger resultaterne på
forskelle mellem disse faktorer er essentielle for hvordan regimer på mesoniveau vil takle det nye reglement -
med henblik på enten at adaptere ændringerne eller være nødsaget til at dekonstruere regimet - og hvordan
de forskellige typer af nicheinnovationer på mikroniveauet vil undslippe og lande i regimet.

Chapter 1
Introduction
"Change is coming, whether you like it or not." - Greta Thunberg, 2019
The world is changing with a speed that is sometimes unbelievable. Before you have adjusted and become
familiar with new standards, they has already changed again. This fast development can be intense, but if
utilised in the right way, it could benefit the sustainable development tremendously.
Sustainability is a broad term. It is used in many ways and is today something you, as a consumer, encounter
almost everywhere you go: In the grocery store, the milk carton is produced with a lower CO2 consumption,
and the canned tuna is labeled with MSC; the wood you buy for your terrace and the wooden shelf from
IKEA is FSC-certified, and you have six diﬀerent garbage cans for diﬀerent waste fractions in your kitchen.
You might not think: "I choose this particular product because it is sustainable", but you have an idea of how
the FSC label helps preserve world forests and that less CO2 emission is beter for the environment. It is
slowly becoming integrated, and maybe soon it will not be possible to choose "non-sustainable" solutions
for some products or services.
In the perspective of the construction industry, the change, the sustainable transition as Greta Thunberg
is referring to, is underway. A new initiative in the transition in Denmark is the voluntary sustainability
class. The class is a part of the goal of a 70% reduction in greenhouse gasses in 2030 compared to 1990
[Klimarådet, 2020].
To understand sustainability, the three pillars of sustainability is an important tool and definition of the term.
The definition from the 1992 Rio declaration is still framing the understanding and use of sustainability
today. In 2016, the 17 sustainable development goals (SDGs) set up by UN added to the global initiatives
and fight for a sustainable future. With basis in the three pillars of sustainability from 1992, the voluntary
sustainability class is brought into the world to further the sustainable development and transition in the
construction industry [Danish Transport Construction and Housing Authority, 2020b].
"The voluntary sustainability class is a central element in the transition of the construction industry
and will be a specific practical tool which can help the individual project owner on their way to
more sustainable constructions" - Kaare Dybvad Bek, Minister for Housing, 2020
[Danish Transport Construction and Housing Authority, 2020b]

INTRODUCTION
The sustainable transition proposed with the voluntary sustainability class is a macro-level change based
on extensive knowledge and litle experience. The result of such a change is therefor unknown but can be
compared to similar sustainable transitions. The success of implementing the low energy classes is a solid
frame of reference with many similarities but also with diﬀerences. This kind of change in regulations is
characterised by the implementation period, a period where it is possible to try out the new demands, but
with the possibility of not being able to succeed, and the possibility to give feedback along the way. It is
thus possible to prepare for the change with basis in the experience gained through trying out the demands.
It is a unique possibility but although the voluntary sustainability class is new, the sustainable transition and
the green transition in the construction industry is already going strong. Building Sustainability Assessment
Tools (BSATs) such as the DGNB-certification can be seen as a result of an increasing demand for sustainable
solutions in the industry. It is not only in the construction industry that the green transition is moving
forward. Climate strikes, electric cars, sorting of waste in personal homes, and shared cars are just some of
the examples to mentioned which is geting more atention in the general public. In the construction industry,
the green transition is built upon a voluntary interest and demands for a more sustainable future. So far, the
green transition in the construction industry is carried much by voluntariness as the voluntary sustainability
class is the first regulation to target sustainability in a more specific and direct way. Thus, the interest,
demand, and power to do something has existed for years, and the individual sense and responsibility has
been starting the movement. How the new voluntary sustainability class will influence the green transition
is still unknown, and it interesting to see how it will fit in the already well-established movement towards
sustainability. Thus, the following main question and associated sub-questions are outlined.
1.1
Problem statement
Main question
How can macro-level changes, introduced by voluntary programs to become regulations, lead to structural
reorganization in the construction industry, and how will they influence the transition towards sustainability
for the individual organisations in the industry?
Sub questions
1. How are existing knowledge utilised in the process of becoming a sustainable industry?
2. How will a change in landscape (according to the multi-level perspective) influence the construction
industry towards sustainability?
3. How will the voluntary sustainability class influence the sustainable movement in the future?

2.1
Sustainability in society
When mentioning sustainability, many people associate it with diﬀerent industries and parts of society.
However, sustainability can be defined with terms that are more related to a specific type of sustainability.
An example of a definition of a sustainable future set by the Danish government is "A society where our
children, grandchildren and later generations have the possibility to benefit from at least the same quality
of living conditions as we do" [Regeringen, 2014]. With a rising focus on sustainability in Denmark, the
consumers are asking for more sustainable services and products like energy, clothing, food and packaging
[Frijs-Madsen, 2018]. Studies show that close to 44% of the Danish population have a high interest in
sustainability in general [Tid og tendenser, 2020].
In 1992 a definition of sustainability was determined at the Rio declaration on environment and development
and included the three pillars of sustainability, environmental sustainability, economical sustainability and
social sustainability [DK-GBC, 2016] (illustrated in figure 3).

2.2
The green transition
The green transitioning is together with sustainability a rising topic in Denmark. One of the main fo-
cuses of the green transitioning is to convert from fossil fuel to more renewable sources to create a green
economy [Bjerregaard, 2013]. The Danish government has set up specific goals for reducing the CO2 to-
wards 2050 with a goal of a 70% reduction in 2030 compared to 1990 and the goal of climate neutrality in
2050 [Klimarådet, 2020]. Looking into the green transition in the construction industry, sustainability and
construction waste are two large factors.
Buildings are a large part of the green transition with great potential for optimisation sustainability-wise.
In Denmark, 40% of the energy use is from buildings and 30% of the CO2 is related to buildings. With a
building mass where 65% were built before 1980, where no regulations on energy consumption or insulation
power existed, there is great potential for optimising existing buildings [Nielsen, 2020]. Another big player
in the green transition related to the construction industry is construction waste. Approximately 35% of
produced waste is related to the construction industry. In this case, a more circular perspective with focus
on recycling of waste has great potential in the green transition [Nielsen, 2020]. Although the goal of a
green transition is reachable and initiatives are being realised, according to Michael H. Nielsen (2020), there
is still a long way, and it is therefore important to consider the construction industry if the goals of a CO2
reduction of 70% in 2030 should be realised [Nielsen, 2020].
2.3
Sustainability in the construction industry
Focusing on sustainability specifically in the construction industry, a big part of the work with sustainability
happens through Building Sustainability Assessment Tools (BSATs). Some of the used BSATs are DGNB,
the Nordic Swan Ecolabel, BREEAM, and LEED among others. In Denmark, the DGNB-certification is the
most popular certification for buildings [CASA A/S, 2021], and the Nordic Swan Ecolabel has experienced
increasing popularity with a doubling of certifications from 2018 to 2020 [Ecolabel.dk, 2020]. Sustainability
is not only worked with through BSATs. The SDG goals and other benchmarks are also used when discussing
and working with sustainability in the construction industry [Dansk Industri, 2021].

The Nordic Swan Ecolabel is a certification based on a building’s life cycle with focus on energy consumption,

process [Nordisk Miljømærkning, 2016]:
• Low energy consumption.
• High environmental and health demands for building materials and chemical products.
• Healthy indoor environment and low emission rates.
• Qality assured construction process.
To obtain the Nordic Swan Ecolabel, the buildings are required a number of points depended on the type of
building. For apartment buildings, 17 out of 44 possible points are required; for small houses, 16 out of 42

to obtain the certification [Nordisk Miljømærkning, 2016]. The popularity of the Nordic Swan Ecolabel is
rising. In the beginning of 2020, more than 12,000 buildings have obtained the Nordic Swan Ecolabel in the
Nordic countries and more than 22,000 new certified buildings are on their way. The label is well known by
consumers and have a high credibility in general [Ecolabel.dk, 2020].

Green washing
Along the green transition green washing is a factor going against the transition. Green washing is happening
in many industries, and is also a problem in the construction industry. It can make it harder to see through
what is actually sustainable or environmentally friendly and what is not [Frederiksen, 2019]. Green washing
can be an issue when using the UN SDGs as targets or framework for sustainable work in companies. The
lack of required documentation of the sustainable actions can lead to a level of green washing. However, a
more systematic approach and more requirements for documentation seems to be helping preventing green
washing [Johnsson et al., 2020].

2.3.3
The low energy classes
A voluntary program with at test-cycle before being implemented in the Danish building regulations has
been seen before. In 2010, a set of voluntary low energy classes was introduced. It consisted of two classes:
low energy class 2015 and low energy class 2020. The demands within the two classes were representing the
expected mandatory demands for respectively 2015 and 2020 [Energistyrelsen, 2014]. The early launching
resulted in a five year and ten year implementation period with possibilities for testing and developing
new processes, methods, and products for the new demands. Illustrated in figure 12 are the expected
advantages of an early announcement with a longer implementation period compared to a shorter period
[Energistyrelsen, 2014].
Figure 12: Advantages of an early announcement [Energistyrelsen, 2014].
A long implementation period has some advantages compared to a short implementation period. It makes
it possible to not only develop the first prototype to follow the new demands, but to work with a longer
process of innovation, where it is possible to reduce the cost and optimise the eﬀectiveness. As illustrated in
figure 12, the faster the expected demands are released, resulting in a longer implementation period, the
higher probability of reducing the cost will be by innovative solution development [Energistyrelsen, 2014].

Chapter 3
Theory
This chapter introduces the theory and framework behind this study. In the first part, the multi-level
perspective theory used as framework for the study is described. It is an important part in understanding the
consequences of macro-level changes. The next part of the chapter considers an assessment of stakeholders.
3.1
The multi-level perspective
This study will be based on the framework of the multi-level perspective (MLP). The framework is used as a
guide for understanding and analysing the changes happening along the green transitioning. In this study,
the framework is used to support and understand the possible transitioning happening due to the green
transitioning and implementation of the new sustainability class in the Danish building regulations.
Technological transitions
Technological transitioning is a broad term for a major technological transformation in society. This
transformation includes functions such as communication, transportation, and housing. Technological
transitioning considers wider changes in society such as change in regulations. New technologies can have
a hard time breaking through because of the standardised processes and factors, such as regulations and
user practices aligned with existing technologies [Geels, 2002].
Socio-technical system
A way of working with and understanding technological transitions is through the MLP framework. The MLP
is a framework describing the transitioning towards sustainability of a socio-technical system [Geels, 2002].
The MLP framework explains changes on diﬀerent levels in society and seek to understand technological
transitioning and exploit possibilities. The socio-technical system is considering both the social and technical
aspects. It considers the interactions between humans and technologies and considers complex societal
infrastructure and human behaviour. The socio-technical constellation is important to consider when talking
about the MLP framework and its levels [Holm et al., 2014]. The top-level in the socio-technical system is
the landscape (macro-level), the middle level is the regimes (meso-level), and the botom level is the niches
(micro-level). The nested hierarchy, illustrated in figure 13, indicates that the regimes are embedded in the
landscapes and the niches within the regimes [Geels, 2002].

At the macro-level of landscape, changes in society and global changes can occur. This could e.g. be a change
in legislation or regulations. The meso-level of regimes includes the mainstream activities and structures. At
the micro-level of niches is where new ideas, which lay outside the mainstream, are evolving [Geels, 2002].
1. Socio-technical landscape - macro-level
The socio-technical (ST) landscape is the external conditions which frame the regimes. The external
conditions or heterogeneous factors are e.g. political agendas, oil prices, economic trends, environmental
problems, cultural, and normative values [Geels, 2002, Holm et al., 2014]. Changes in the landscape are
possible, but it is harder and usually a slower process to change the landscape than the regimes. Changes
at the landscape-level are able to put pressure on the regime-level, which can create opportunities for
innovations at the niche-level to escape to the regime-level, which could lead to a technological transition
[Geels, 2002].
2. Socio-technical regimes - meso-level
The socio-technical regimes are the stable part of the socio-technical system. The ST-regimes are still
dynamic and innovation is happening within the regimes, but it is of an incremental nature [Geels, 2002]. It
is possible to change the ST-regimes radically, as they are never fully closed because they are aﬀected by
the landscape. If a change of landscape is happening, the ST-regimes must adapt and will ofen be able to
absorb the changes, but sometimes a reconfiguration or a deconstruction of the regimes and construction of
new ones are necessary [Holm et al., 2014].

3. Socio-technical niches - micro-level
In the ST-niches, the more radical innovations are evolving. Where more incremental innovations are
generated in the regime, there are space in the niches for more ’non-standardized’ solutions and technologies
[Geels, 2002, Holm et al., 2014]. The niches are protected markets for innovation or markets where special
conductive conditions are present. Niche innovations can be divided into two categories: Fit and conform
and Stretch and transform. The innovations in Fit and conform are developed to adapt to the existing regime
to improve the regime, where the innovations in Stretch and transform are not related to the existing regime
and are developed towards a regime-changing perspective [Holm et al., 2014]. Niche innovations ofen have
a hard time to go further from the niche-level and ofen require a change in landscape or regime to create a
window of opportunities to escape through [Geels, 2002].
Change and escaping
For a technological transitioning to happen, a change in the system needs to happen. A possible change
in the regimes will occur when a change in landscape puts pressure on the established regimes, which
then allows niche innovations to utilise the transitioning and become a part of the regimes. Tension in the
regimes can also lead to a window of opportunity for the niche innovation to escape through. With many
factors aﬀecting the regimes, uncertainty is present and can create tension weakening the linkage in the
regimes [Geels, 2002, Schot and Geels, 2008]. In addition to the changes in the landscape and regime-level,
a niche-cumulation can result in an escape of innovations as shown in figure 14 [Geels, 2002]. For the escape
to happen, more factors must influence the process. In the protected niches, the degree of protection, the
compatibility with the existing regime, and how the innovations are connected to ongoing processes at the
regime-level and landscape-level, influence the success of the niche innovation escaping to the regime-level
[Koch-Ørvad, 2019].

The dynamics of technical transitioning in the socio-technical system starts in the niches but are depending

3.1.1
MLP and innovation in the construction industry
From the MLP theory, the regimes are known for their stability but also for the diﬀiculty to change or
transition and by only being able to change by incremental processes [Holm et al., 2014]. The same can
be seen in the construction industry. The escape of innovations from the protected space in the niche to
regime-level is known to be somewhat diﬀicult. Innovations in the construction industry ofen happen in
connection with building projects. It can therefore be hard to extend innovation initiatives from one project
to another due to the variation from project to project [Koch-Ørvad, 2019]. In addition, it is found hard
22
==PAGE:36==

to change the dynamics of the construction sector due to the very established behavioural paterns and
standardisation in the industry [Koch-Ørvad, 2019]. Koch-Ørvad (2019) identify four factors aﬀecting the
innovations’ journeys in the construction industry:
• Innovations take place in projects
• Standardised roles of actors involved
• Strong institutionalisation with strict regulations
• Conservatism
The reason for where the innovations takes place is a consequence of loosely coupled companies. This is
because of the ofen high degree of uncertainty in the construction industry. Together with the very varying
projects in the industry, the best conditions for innovations is seen in the projects. The standardised roles of
actors can be a hindrance due to the diﬀiculty of new procedures and ways of doing things when certain
roles are setled and standardised. With construction projects spanning over a long period of time and being
complex projects, the roles and responsibility has become very standardised in the industry. The strong
institutionalisation with strict regulations can be seen as a lock of the regimes with strict rules aﬀecting the
innovation process and making it less free and open [Koch-Ørvad, 2019]. Lastly, the general conservatism
experienced in the construction industry can be seen as a hindrance for innovation. This is closely related to
the issues surrounding the roles and actors, where everyone is set in their way and not willing to change for
the uncertainties it might bring [Koch-Ørvad, 2019, Koch-Ørvad et al., 2019].

4.2.1
Qalitative interviews
The purpose of this study is to understand how changes in a ST-landscape aﬀects niches and regimes. It is

Kvale & Brinkmann (2014) introduce the seven stages of an interview investigation from thematization,
design, interview, transcription, analysis, verification to reporting. They state that the beter the preparation
for an interview, the higher the quality of knowledge from the interview will be, which in the end will lead
to an easier processing of the interviews [Kvale and Brinkmann, 2014]. By using the qualitative research

1. Thematization The purpose of the investigation is set up and the general why and what are being

2. Design A plan of the design of the investigation in relation to the seven stages is developed. The purpose

what analysis to use.
6. Verification Verify the interviews. Do also look at the reliability, validation, and generalisation of the
results.
7. Reporting The results should be communicated in an understandable way in context with the purpose
of the investigation. It is important to choose a display, which shows the results in the best possible way.
4.2.2
Grounded Theory
With basis in qualitative interviews, grounded theory (GT) is used as the qualitative thematic analysis of
data. GT is a theory with an inductive approach based on systematic and flexible guidelines that provide a
frame for qualitative research. Combined with general knowledge on the qualitative interview as a research
method provided by Kvale & Brinkmann (2014), the basis for a path to a successful data gathering is formed.
The guidelines are set up to help collecting and analysing qualitative data and to thereby be able to
construct theories based on the data itself. The aim is to generate a theory based on the data that ex-
plains structures and events in the field of study rather than testing an existing theory [Charmaz, 2014,
Kvale and Brinkmann, 2014]. In GT, it is essential to make early stops and analyse what is found along
the way which expedites the progress towards the goal [Charmaz, 2014]. Compared to concept-driven

==PAGE:41==
4

approaches, data-driven approaches such as GT have a more open approach towards the data and its
possibilities [Kvale and Brinkmann, 2014].
The processes of GT are not necessarily a linear path but can include many stops and circular paths, where
new ideas lead back to previous steps [Charmaz, 2014]. Figure 16 illustrates a simple path with seven stages
starting from developing the research questions to writing up, with the possibility of a more circular path in
the middle of the process. The four steps, Requirement and sampling of participants, Data collection, Initial
coding and Focused coding and categorising are ofen not a linear process, but can be circular including stops
and developments of new ideas, which can lead to other ways to pursue [Charmaz, 2014].
Figure 16: The seven stages of Grounded Theory (modified from [Charmaz, 2014]).
The first part of GT is to set up one or more research questions that form the basis of the data collection. The
next step is to start the data collection by determining the data collection method followed by the collection
of data. It is also at this point, it is important to consider how many interviews are ideal for the research
[Charmaz, 2014]. The next step is to start coding the data. The initial coding is helpful in constructing an
analytic skeleton for GT and to see the first connections between data. It is now possible to do a more
focused coding, which enables a more organised coding process, where it is possible to sort larger amount of
data. In the light of that, it is possible to build a theory based on the collected and coded data. Throughout
the entire process of GT, memo-writing is an essential part. They can be useful throughout the entire study,
but are especially useful in the stages 3 to 6. The memos help to consider what is happening in the moment
and to assess and use emerging ideas from the data in the further process [Charmaz, 2014].
The seven stages in GT from Charmaz (2014) somewhat reflect the seven stages of an interview Kvale &
Brinkmann (2014). For this study, both models for collecting qualitative data are considered and used as
guidelines for conducting the qualitative research interviews.
The seven steps presented by Kvale & Brinkmann (2014) and the seven steps of GT presented by Charmaz
(2014) somewhat follow the same procedure for conducting qualitative research interviews. However, the

Chapter 6
Method for analysis
In this chapter, the method used for analysis of the collected data is presented and elaborated. The process
of conducting qualitative research interviews is now in the steps 4 and V Initial coding and Focused coding
and categorising in the seven stages of GT by Charmaz (2014) and Transcription and Analysis by Kvale &

6.1
Transcribing and initial coding
Transcription of the interview is, as mentioned in section 4.2.1, an important part of conducting a qualitative
study with basis in interviews. Transcription leads to the next step of initial coding of the interviews. When
conducting initial coding, it is important to remain open towards the data [Charmaz, 2014]. The initial
coding should be conducted closely to the data itself, but it is important to not apply pre-existing categories
to the data to be able to code the data as actions rather than topics or themes [Charmaz, 2014]. The codes
in GT are ofen short and define the action or experience described by the respondent. Coding can be either
concept-driven or data-driven. Concept-driven coding uses pre-developed codes based on some of the data
or based on existing literature. In GT, the coding is data-driven, which indicates that the codes are not
made beforehand but are rising from the data itself. In this stage of coding, the codes are more descriptive,
whereas the focused coding becomes more theoretical [Kvale and Brinkmann, 2014]. Four questions can be
asked in the process of coding [Charmaz, 2014]:
• What is this data a study of?
• What do the data suggest? Pronounce? Leave unsaid?
• From whose point of view?
• What theoretical category does this specific datum indicate?
39
==PAGE:53==
6
METHOD FOR ANALYSIS
These four questions were asked throughout the initial coding of all the interviews. Especially question
number three from whose point of view? was important to closely consider because some of the interview
persons represented more than one role, e.g. manager in a consulting company and member of a commitee
in an industry association. According to Charmaz (2014), the initial coding is important in the construction
of GT:
"Grounded theorists aim to code for possibilities suggested by the data rather than ensuring complete
accuracy of the data. This approach helps you to define the range of variation of your studied
process or phenomenon and provide leads for conceptualising it further and checking your ideas
with other data." [Charmaz, 2014], p. 120
Approach
Diﬀerent approaches of initial coding are available to use when conducting GT. Most common is word-by-
word coding and line-by-line coding. In this study, line-by-line coding has been chosen as the initial coding
step because of the possibility of comparing incident with incident more easily. In line-by-line coding, as
the name states, every line is coded. This type of coding can be a very useful tool to identify ideas and
knowledge that may have escaped during conducting or reading through the interview [Charmaz, 2014].
Line-by-line coding is also a useful tool to help refocus later interviews. When coding the data initially
insights on what data to pursue next is gained. If something important is identified in the line-by-line coding,
it is possible to return to earlier interviews to see if it explains events or experiences. It is also possible to
seek new respondents to compare their responses, which in the end makes the process of data collection
and coding more focused [Charmaz, 2014].
In practice, the transcribed interviews were read through to detect possible mistakes and, if necessary,
relevant passages of the recordings of the interviews were listened to again. Aferwards, the transcriptions of
the interviews were divided into a table with one column for each type of codes and one for the transcription.
6.2
Focused coding
Focused coding is the next step afer the initial coding. The process of focused coding is to find and use the
most significant or frequent codes made in the initial coding process. The chosen codes should represent
the codes that make sense to analyse by being able to compare and categorise them with other codes.
Sometimes, the initial codes need to be coded again to get the most value of them, if the codes are too
long, unspecified or unclear [Charmaz, 2014]. A part of focused coding is to compare initial codes with data
and to look for possible paterns. Thus, some of the initial codes will rise to become focused codes, while
40

6

other initial codes will be excluded. This process of GT makes it possible to interact with the data instead
of reading passively through data, and unexpected ideas may emerge during the process. The constant
comparison of data and codes is eﬀicient in discovering new ideas and paterns [Charmaz, 2014]. Charmaz
(2014) targets the possibility of missing information in the data. Thus, she proposes to ask the question:
"What kind of theoretical categories does these codes indicate?" [Charmaz, 2014] p. 144
This question has been a returning question in this study throughout the coding process. A part of focused
coding is theoretical coding. Theoretical coding is a way of conceptualising the codes’ relations to each other
as hypotheses for the purpose of being integrated into a theory [Charmaz, 2014]. Theoretical coding leads
to the next step of theoretical sampling. An example of all three coding process can be found in appendix
A.5.
Constant comparative methods
Constant comparative methods are an integrated part of coding in GT. It is used to analyse data to construct
theories. Comparison of data with data to find similarities and diﬀerences is a part of the process. This in-
cludes comparing data within the same interview as well as between two or more interviews [Charmaz, 2014].
According to Charmaz (2014), the constant comparative method is present in the steps from data collection
throughout theory building. It is an important part of GT as it helps the constant awareness and openness
towards new possibilities in the process. It is used in both the initial and focused coding processes, where
the codes are constantly compared to each other both from the same respondent as well as across interviews.
This is present in the memos made during the coding. In the later theoretical sampling and theory building,
the comparison is present when sorting and categorising codes and memos.
6.3
Memo-writing
Memo-writing is a big part of the GT process from data collection until building the theory. When writing
memos in GT, stops are made where ideas are analysed in the specific moment. By doing so, the analysis
is begun early in the research process. Memos should be spontaneous and informal and for personal use
[Charmaz, 2014].
There are diﬀerent methods for writing memos, but they are few. Examples of memo-writing methods are:
a methodological journal where methodological dilemmas, directions, and decisions are writen down; the
free-writing technique, which is simply writing down for 8 or more minutes to encourage composing fresh
and new material in a natural way; and clustering where clusters of codes are set up in relation to each
other. Charmaz (2014) emphasises the importance of choosing the method or methods that works best for

the person writing the memos [Charmaz, 2014]. Thus, it is chosen to use the free-writing and the cluster
technique for writing memos in this study, as they were found to create the most value for this study.
Free-writing opens the mind and can help the further processes by already having something writen down
on paper [Charmaz, 2014]. By using the guidelines, including 5 points for free-writing provided by Charmaz

1. Get ideas down on paper as quickly and fully as you can
2. Write to and for yourself

As the guidelines indicate, the free-writing technique lead the writer to be very open and accept what ever

made in the process. Ofen the clustering was made with basis in the free-writings and initial codes to help

Chapter 7
Analysis
In this chapter, the collected and coded data is analysed according to grounded theory (GT). This include
the important part of theoretical sampling of the data until the point of saturation. The theoretical sampling
is divided into two parts: one very open part with no objectives related, which invites to look at the data
objectively in section 7.1; and one where the data is related to the three research objectives in section 7.2.
With basis in the theoretical sampling, the theorising is initiated, and the theory is constructed. Thus, the
process is moving forward in step 5 and into step 6, Focused coding and categorising and Theory building in
the seven stages of GT by Charmaz (2014) and Analysis and Verification by Kvale & Brinkmann (2014).
7.1
Theoretical sampling - first iteration
Theoretical sampling is a necessity in GT and helps developing and refine the categories emerged through
the data. Theoretical sampling is a method for narrowing down the focus area for constructing the the-
ory. It is possible to elaborate the meaning of the categories and to see variations and gaps in the data
[Charmaz, 2014].
As a part of theoretical sampling, the memos and all three types of codes (initial, focused, and theoretical
coding) are important to include, as they lay the ground for the sampling of the data. The data is sorted
through with basis in the memos, codes and the three research objectives. To be able to remain as open and
objective towards the data and to be able to include as much information as possible, to ensure nothing is
lef behind, the first iteration of theoretical sampling is focused on the data more objectively. Thus, the data,
codes, and memos are sorted through without any pre-existing categories or objectives to consider. However,
to be able to use and present the results of the theoretical sampling, the results have been divided into three
views: Relation to sustainability, The power of the voluntary sustainability class and Future green transition.
The three views emerged from the first iteration of theoretical sampling and covers all the results. In the
analysis, paterns, concepts, and categories are looked for to be able to build the theory in the end. This
is done before directly connecting the results to the research objectives and to the multi-level perspective
(MLP) theory to ensure a thorough and objective analysis of the data. The process of theoretical sampling is
an iterative process where all the data is sorted through until a point of saturation. This happens when it is
no longer possible to make new findings from the data [Charmaz, 2014].

Discussion
This chapter will include a three-part discussion: a discussion of the interviews and stakeholders, a discussion
of the three research objectives in relation to the multi-level perspective (MLP) based on relevant issue that
emerged from the analysis, and recommendations for future work.
9.1
Interviews and stakeholders
The qualitative research interviews are based on two sets of guidelines provided by Kvale & Brinkmann
(2014) and by Charmaz (2014). The combination of the two will be discussed below in relation to the
overall boundaries for this study. This includes an assessment of the stakeholders chosen, the impact of the
limitations, and the possibilities for other directions to pursue.
Grounded theory (GT) has been a valuable tool for analysing the data. With constant comparing data
with data, GT strives to include everything relevant. With the voluntary sustainability class (VSC) being
relatively new and untested and with very litle frame of reference, GT has been valuable not to close oﬀand
intercept possible relevant issues connected to the VSC. The seven stages of GT in combination with Kvale &
Brinkmanns (2014) seven steps for qualitative research provided a good flow and structure to the study from
the first stages of planning the interviews to the last of presenting the results. The two set of guidelines take
diﬀerent approaches to conduct qualitative research interviews, but have somewhat a similar structure with
a seven step procedure, which makes them ideal to combine. Kvale & Brinkmann (2014) focuses more on the
general qualitative research interview, where Charmaz (2014) focuses on the GT approach of qualitative
research interviews. Kvale & Brinkmanns (2014) initial steps 1 and 2 provided a good guideline of the initial
design and planning of the interviews, where Charmaz’ (2014) step 3-5 have a more detailed set of guidelines
for data collection and coding. Thus, the two set of guidelines complement each other and together provided
a more detailed set of guidelines for qualitative research interviews. Other research methods could also
have been beneficial in this field of study such as focus group discussions. Where the qualitative research
interview only includes one interviewee at a time and with only the interviewer to ask questions and lead the
discussion, focus group discussions can lead to other ideas and themes or tendencies to emerge. By either
including stakeholders from the same group (e.g. architectural companies and project-based organisations)
or including stakeholders from more groups (e.g. project-based organisations, contractors, and project
owners), other aspects can be revealed through the discussions. From the interviews, it appears that the

their roles, contributions, and responsibilities in the green transition. Thus, it could be an interesting

9.2
Multi-level perspective
The multi-level perspective theory (MLP) was used as the framework for this study. The MLP theory has
been a very useful tool to describe, understand, and analyse the consequences related to the changes that
the VSC brings to the socio-technical system.
9.2.1
Sustainable transition
The voluntary sustainability class (VSC) is a change in the socio-technical (ST) landscape. According to the
MLP theory, a change in the ST-landscape can lead to a variation of diﬀerent consequences and lead to a
transition towards sustainability.
The size and significance of such a change seems to depend on various factors, influencing each other as
well as individually impacting the result of the transition. With the VSC still being voluntary and not a
mandatory part of the Danish building regulations, the actual influence from it is still unknown. However,
the construction industry has experienced similar changes in the ST-landscape before with the low energy
classes. Some of the relevant issues emerging through the analysis of the interview, circles the actual impact
from the VSC in the ST-regimes.
The expectation from the respondents towards the VSC regarding company structure seems to be that
the impact will be less significant for the ones already working with sustainability in a medium to large
size company in the industry and more significant for the ones not working with sustainability in smaller
companies in the industry. However, since the respondents are all working with sustainability already
and their companies are of a certain size, they only represent one faction of the industry. It is therefore
their expectations, but not experience, that the smaller and less ambitious stakeholders will have a harder
time adjusting to the change in the ST-landscape. However, the respondents all agree that for their own
company, they do not expect a significant change in company structure. They build their argumentation
on the experience with the implementation of the low energy classes as well as their current work with
sustainability and building sustainability assessment tools (BSATs).
The experience with the low energy classes seems to rise the expectations for the VSC for the respondents.
Some of them expect and hope for the same successful results for the VSC. Common for them are, that
they hope the VSC will start the process of innovation and product development when the demands are

concerning everyone in the industry. With innovations escaping from the ST-niches to the ST-regimes, it
could be possible to establishing new sustainable standards, whether the innovations will fit and conform or
stretch and transform the ST-regimes.
9.2.2
Existing conditions
The green transition in the construction industry is already ongoing which the development and usage of
BSATs, such as the DGNB-certification, is a proof of. Especially with the development and the rising interest
and demand for sustainable initiatives, as seen with the rising number of DGNB-certifications and the rising
interest described by the respondents, it is clear that something is happening in the movement for a more
sustainable future in the industry. This movement or transition is happening unaﬀiliated by the regulations
and the VSC. This also influence the development of new products, processes, and innovative solutions.
The existing foundation for working with sustainability is good and solid and has been developed for some
time. An example of that is the awareness from and responsibility taken by the respondents and their
companies. They have set up goals and requirements to follow regarding sustainability in their companies,
both regarding their internal footprint and for some of them as well for their services.
The respondents somewhat represent the more ambitious (regarding sustainability) and the medium to
large sized companies in the construction industry. This does not aﬀect the fact that the knowledge and
experience are present, but an issue might be the distribution of the knowledge and experience throughout
the industry. As mentioned by some of the respondents, the tools and knowledge to act more sustainable is
present, but some of the processes needs to be changed or adapted to fit the new demands in the VSC. Thus,
the development of innovations in the ST-niche-level might not be as similar to what happened with the
low energy classes. The development and the niche innovations escaping to the ST-regimes will perhaps be
focused on optimising the processes rather than the actual development of new innovations. One thing does
not rule out the other, but the focus might be diﬀerent. This is connected to the respondents’ expectations
of the impact from the VSC. They expect to have to upgrade their workforce and processes to fit the new
requirements, but they have the tools to do it - they know how to do LCA, but they have to figure out an
eﬀective and maybe more standardised way of doing it on all projects with the VSC. It could therefore
be expected that the niche innovation will rather fit and conform to the existing regime for the larger
ambitious companies than stretch and transform it. However, it is more diﬀicult to predict for the smaller
less ambitious companies with only the expectations from the respondents.

9.2.3
Future regulations
With the VSC being a change in the ST-landscape, the forthcoming implementation in the Danish building
regulations is important for the transition towards sustainability. When talking about the influence from
the VSC both as voluntary and when it become integrated as demands in the Danish building regulations, it
is important to consider the intention and target of it.
It is also important to consider the expectations. Several of the respondents mention regulations regarding
sustainability in the construction industry as something they have been wanting and waiting for. However,
the VSC seems to be disappointing to some of the respondents. This might have something to do with
their expectation compared to their own capability and to their view on the industry’s capability. It is being
called unambitious for the industry by some of the respondents, but some of them are also aware of the
very divided construction industry regarding size, knowledge, resources, and experience with sustainability.
However, with the implementation period of two years, there is time to prepare for the changes and new
standards, which also make some of the respondents question the level of ambition in the VSC. Thus, there
should be time for a longer process of innovation development at the niche-level with time to improve
and optimise the innovations. This is also consistent with the already existing development of tools and
procedures.
Looking overall at the VSC, it becomes clear that the respondents see the VSC as being a tool to move up the
botom of the construction industry. Move them from nothing to a level with at least some sustainability. It
is seen as a starting point - a good basis for more. With this in mind, it appears to be the general expectation
among the respondents that the VSC will develop with time. Some hope it will be a litle faster than it seems
to be the intention at the moment, but the general hope is that it will keep moving the botom level up. This
is also consistent with the expectations for the diﬀerentiation of impact on the companies regarding their
size and level of ambition. The impact of a change in the landscape will thus vary from regime to regime, and
the type of innovations at the niche-level will depend on the regime’s underlying basis before the change.
9.3
Future work
In this study, the delimitation of factions to include made the study focus on one perspective due to the
time limitations. However, to create a beter frame of reference it could be beneficial to include the other
factions (smaller and less ambitious) in a future study. This could lead to a more varied perspective of the
implications of the consequences of the VSC and a beter prediction of the general consequences for the
construction industry in relation to the multi-level perspective.

This study of the impact of a change in the macro-level on the construction industry is made before the
actual change has happened. It is therefore only a prediction build upon expectations and experience with
frames of reference as comparison. One of the valuable things regarding the VSC is the two-year test cycle.
With this study ending less than a year afer the launch of the VSC, not much experience has been made
working with the VSC yet. In continuation of this study, a study of the experience gained from testing the
VSC could be very valuable to the investigation of the macro-level change.
There are many diﬀerent stakeholders involved in a construction project. In this study, the stakeholders
were grouped based on overall similarities such as roles in the projects. This was done to be able to focus
the study to fit the qualitative research interviews. Thus, it is not possible through this study to investigate
the connection between the individual stakeholder groups and factions. A study of a possible connection
could be made in continuation of this study by using quantitative research methods such as questionnaires
with standardised questions based on the results from the interviews in this study. The questions could be
regarding: responsibility in the industry, expectations for change in company structure, and issues experienced
(e.g. green washing, documentation, divided knowledge) among other subjects.
Another interesting aspect to pursue in continuation of this study is to look deeper into the actual demands
in the VSC. This includes the level of ambition, intentions, and predictions. This study only addresses the
approach and overall view of the VLC but does not target specific initiatives or solutions for the future green
transition. It could therefore be useful to investigate the prospects as well as consequences of the VSC and
to consider the possibilities to improve the outcome.

CONCLUSION
Chapter 10
Conslusion
Through the theory, analysis and discussion, it is shown how a change on macro-level can lead to structural
changes in the construction industry and how the regime- and niche-level are impacted by such a transition.
The macro-level changes by the voluntary sustainability class (VSC) do not appear to have a direct impact
on the assessed companies as described by the respondents, but the respondents suggest that it may lead
to structural changes on small and/or less ambitious companies. The transition caused by the structural
changes appears to be aﬀected by (1) the existing conditions for the companies undergoing (2) the sustainable
transition towards the future conditions defined by (3) the future scope of the regulations.
It was found that the existing conditions regarding sustainability in the construction industry appears to
have an influence on the impact of the VSC. The respondents emphasise that their work with sustainability
is more advanced than the requirements in the VSC, but at the same time, there is an awareness that not
everyone in the industry have reached the same level of development within sustainability. The respondents’
companies have experience and knowledge about how to handle the VSC because they are already working
with the tools and processes through e.g. BSATs. Thus, there is a small gap between the respondents’
level of ambition and the VSC. The rising awareness regarding sustainability appears to push the green
transition - which also influences the respondents’ level of ambition and possibly pushing the wishes for
more streamlined and definitive regulations regarding sustainability. Thus, the existing conditions can be
influencing the impact of a regulatory change at macro-level and possibly lessen the influence from it for
the ambitious parts of the industry, due to their work already being further than the voluntary requirements
from the VSC.
The respondents belong to the faction of medium to large companies with a medium to high level of ambition
regarding sustainability. There appears to be minimal need for change in company structure for these
regimes as their existing conditions are already further ahead than the (to-be regulatory) requirements
proposed by the VSC, due to their own voluntary ambitions within sustainability. Therefore, the possible
innovations that might emerge from the niche-level are expected to fit and conform to this existing regime.
The respondents expect that companies outside their own regime (smaller and/or less ambitious companies)
could be aﬀected by the VSC raising the lowest standards for sustainability, which could lead to innovations
emerging from the niche-level to stretch and transform the regime.
86
==PAGE:100==
10
CONCLUSION
In addition, it was found that the VSC might target less ambitious companies regarding sustainability in
the construction industry. The expectation is that the impact for companies with a lower level of ambition
will be diﬀerent and possibly higher, as the gap between the voluntary requirements and their existing
conditions is described as being larger than for the respondents’ ambitious companies. Additionally, the
respondents express a general opinion of inertia in the construction industry compared to their own voluntary
development. Therefore, while the VSC can contribute to bring the industry closer together in regards to the
ambitions and development work being done within sustainability, there is a certain expectation that the
VSC should at least follow the general development and be updated continuously. Otherwise, the ambitious
regimes will seek higher levels to follow the rising awareness leading to outrunning the regulatory progress
that the VSC is seeking to create.

A.6
Example of free-writing memo afer qualitative interview in this study
The text below is an example of a free-writing memo constructed just afer a finished interview with one of
the respondents. The free-writing memos are writen in danish to be able to get as much information down
without having to stop and consider proper translation in the memo-writing process
Arbejder med bæredygtighed tidligt i processen med projekter og prøver at løfe på de parametre
der kan være svære at løfe niveauet på. Arbejder på initiativ-niveau omkring integration af
bæredygtighed i projekter og prøver at være med til at udvikle bæredygtighedstiltag, inspirere
andre og blive inspireret. Arbejder med dokumenterbar bæredygtighed – prøver at sæte det på
formel. Kigger meget på materialer og optimering indenfor materialerne i byggeprocessen. Vil
være med til at flyte branchen i den rigtige retning mod bæredygtighed, men det er afgørende
hvilke bygherrer der arbejdes med. De har meget at skulle have sagt. Har manglet initiativer så
som FBK, men forventer ikke anden betydning end ændring i formalia. FBK indeholder meget
om dokumentation – mangler reelle krav for mener ikke at dokumentation i sig selv rykker noget
stort. Tror udfordringerne med FBK kan ligge i noget med chain supply omkring materialernes
oprindelse, men ser at det kan løses. Synes ikke FBK er ambitiøs nok i sig selv – savner ambitiøse
krav om f.eks. afryk. FBK kan skubbe på nogle punkter så som digitalisering. Værdiskabelsen
er vigtig i snakken om bæredygtighed. Vigtigt at tænke de andre dele af bæredygtighed ind i
snakken.
