Abstract
CO2 refrigeration systems are a promising alternative to the criticized systems using HFC as re-
frigerant. However they still need to be improved. This master thesis investigates experimentally
whether an increase in cycle performance can be achieved by implementing a two-phase ejector in
a transcritical operated CO2 refrigeration system located in building 412 on Technical University
of Denmark. The considered system is a small scale refrigeration system and is able to deliver
2-3 kW of cooling in transcritical operation. The experiments are carried out by modifying the
existing system to switch between standard booster- and ejector operation. The purpose of the
ejector expansion is to utilize the loses from the throttling process. This utilization by the ejector
lifts the compressor suction pressure above the evaporation pressure thus saving compressor power
because of decreased pressure ratio. Another advantage according to ejector operation is the fact
that the evaporator can be ﬂooded.
The ejector operation was tested with super heat equal to 9-11K and with ﬂooded evaporator.
The best ejector performance was obtained with super heat. Regarding this operation the CO2
gas cooler outlet temperature was 35.3◦C (gas cooler pressure optimized for maximum COP) and
the evaporation temperature -7◦C/-8◦C. The best increased cycle performance was achieved for
ejector operation in combination with ﬂooded evaporator for similar conditions in the gas cooler.
When the evaporator is ﬂooded the evaporation temperature can be elevated 5K for supermarket
application = -2◦C/-3◦C.
With super heat the ejector elevates the suction pressure 4.3 bar. Relating to this pressure lift
the ejector efﬁciency equals 22%. The ejector is recovering 22% of the maximum recovering rate
of the throttling loses. This increases the COP from 1.11 to 1.25 compared to the standard booster
cycle. This corresponds to a relative COP of 1.12. For ﬂooded operation the ejector lifts the pres-
sure between the receiver and the evaporator 3.8 bar. The COP is increased from 1.11 to 1.47. This
corresponds to a relative COP of 1.32 which is a signiﬁcant increase caused by the ejector pressure
lift and the elevated evaporation pressure from -7◦C/-8◦C to -2◦C/-3◦C for ﬂooded operation. The
ejector efﬁciency is still 22%. The decreased pressure lift is caused by the increased mass ﬂow in
the evaporator for ﬂooded operation.

Introduction
CO2 was one of the ﬁrst working ﬂuids used in mechanical compression refrigeration machines
in the late nineteeth century. The refrigerant especially gained ground in the shipping industry.
However the synthesized halogenated safety refrigerants was introduced in 1931 and from this
year the number of CO2 refrigeration systems declined [1].
The European F-gas regulation No 517/2014 introduces a phase-down approach where the main
goal is to reduce the consumption of HFC by 79% before 2030. This goal is achieved by dis-
tributing quotas between HFC producers and importers of in order to control the amount of HFC
supplied to the union market. The higher the global warming potential (GWP) on the refrigerant
the more quotas required to sell or import [2].
Because of the increased awareness of global warming and this regulation tool from EU, the
refrigeration industry will be pushed towards using refrigerants with low GWP like the natural
refrigerants (ammonia and the hydrocarbons, e.g. propane, isobutane and CO2). These refriger-
ants have zero or almost zero GWP.
Especially CO2 has gained ground in the refrigeration industry in the last decades. CO2 is promis-
ing because it is cheap, non-ﬂammable and non-toxic. However CO2 as refrigerant obtains low
cycle performance in warmer climates where the system has to be operated transcritical many
hours a year to reject heat. The reason why, is that the critical temperature is equal to Tcrit= 31◦C.
The refrigerant is not condensed in the same way as common systems in a transcritical process.
The super heated gas from the compressor is cooled isobaric in a gas cooler and then expanded
by a long path towards the two-phase region of the refrigerant. By this long expansion path ir-
reversibilities occur in the high pressure control valve [3]. This expansion loss can be recovered
by using an ejector. The ejector consists of two nozzles, two ﬂow chambers and a diffuser. It has
no rotating parts, it is simple to construct and cheap to manufacture [4]. The high pressure gas
from the gas cooler is lead through a (motive) nozzle and the ﬂow is accelerated. The pressure
energy is turned into kinetic energy and the pressure drops below the evaporation pressure. This
lower pressure entrains the mass ﬂow from the evaporator to the mixing chamber where the two
ﬂows mix and momentum is exchanged. The ﬂows are decelerated in the diffuser and the pressure
increases, a pre-compression effect called the pressure lift is achieved [5].
1.1
State of the art on ejector based CO2 refrigeration cycles
It is important to investigate ideas or issues that have not previously been investigated in the lit-
erature. This is why the last experimental studies regarding experimental investigation of ejector
based expansion are reviewed. This is done to discover how far the science within this ﬁeld has
come and to contribute with more knowledge.
In 2007 Elbel et al. [6] performed experimental investigation of ejector based expansion. The
scope of their study was to ﬁnd the most suitable ejector geometry according to the motive nozzle
and diffuser angle. They created an experimental setup (5.3 kW cooling capacity) which enabled
bypass of the ejector with high pressure valve to compare the two cycles. One of the major con-
1
==PAGE:10==
siderations in this study was to create a system comparable to real air condition systems. They
observed that the use of small diffuser angles (5◦) increased the cooling and COP by 8% and 7%
respectively compared to the baseline (standard booster cycle). This study did also introduce a
more applicable way to calculate ejector efﬁciency where the approach is to calculate the ratio
between ejector recovery rate and maximum possible recovery rate.
Nakagawa et al. [7] investigated in 2009 how a suction line heat exchanger could inﬂuence the
two-phase CO2 ejector cycle. They obtained results where a 60 cm suction line heat exchanger
combined with a two-phase ejector improved the COP with 27%. Additionally they reported that
regarding to their system, a signiﬁcant amount of liquid was ﬂowing to the compressor through
the gas outlet in the receiver in ejector mode.
Banasiak et al. [8] designed, manufactured and experimentally investigated a multi ejector pack
for throttling loss recovery in a CO2 refrigeration system (70 kW) on a test facility at SINTEF in
2015. Their experimental approach was to enable a suitable number of ejectors according to the
operating conditions. They also tried to control the high pressure with the ejectors combined with
a high pressure expansion valve. The results showed ejector efﬁciencies above or equal to 30%.
They did not calculate COP improvement because the test facility differed too much compared to
real applications.
Also at SINTEF in 2015 Banasiak et al. [9] performed experiments in order to investigate ejector
geometries. They did not report the cooling capacity of the system but they used the same com-
pressors as used in [8]. Due to this their capacity is assumed to have been 70 kW. Their approach
was also to test different ejectors, but using one single ejector at a time. They also reported ejector
efﬁciencies above 30% with the highest obtained efﬁciency being 36.8% (pressure lifts between 2
and 10 bar). Their investigation showed that the pressure lift forced by the ejector was signiﬁcantly
inﬂuenced by the ejector efﬁciency.
Basnaiak et al. [8, 9] mentioned the fact that a refrigeration system ﬁtted with a two-phase ejector
can be operated with ﬂooded evaporator, but none of the studies performed the experiment due to
lack of the right test equipment. In addition to this, all of the presented studies investigated air
condition and heat pump systems. Non of the mentioned studies tried implementing an ejector on
a small scale CO2 refrigeration, with a cooling capacity of 2-3 kW, also called a condensing unit.
Neither did any of the mentioned studies perform experiments with ﬂooded evaporator.

1.2
Thesis statement
The scope of this study is to experimentally investigate whether it is possible to obtain increased
cycle performance in an existing condensing unit size (2-3 kW) CO2 refrigeration system. The
increased performance should be achieved by implementing a two-phase ejector that employs the
throttling loses from the high pressure expansion process. Additionally, it will be investigated if
the cycle performance will increase even more if the evaporator is ﬂooded.
1.2.1
Method
The investigation is carried out by modifying an existing small scale CO2 booster cycle located
in building 412 on Technical University of Denmark. The modiﬁcations of the system will allow
operation in standard booster mode or in ejector mode. To ensure continuous operation in both
modes, two water loops are designed and constructed to measure the heat balances in the system.
Figure 1.1 shows the modiﬁcations done to establish an experimental ejector based expansion
refrigeration setup. How the experimental setup has been developed and modiﬁed through the
time by several students are shown and explained in appendix A.

The basic function of the ejector is to create a higher outlet pressure "4" compared to the evap-
oration pressure "9". This is called the pressure lift. The lift is achieved by utilizing the kinetic
energy that occur from the expansion of the high pressure ﬂow coming from the gas cooler. This
high pressure ﬂow is often referred to as the motive ﬂow in the literature.
In detail: The high pressure refrigerant is coming from the gas cooler and is expanded through
the motive nozzle. This process is accelerating the motive ﬂow and the pressure decreases be-
cause pressure energy is converted to kinetic energy. This lower pressure is used to suck the ﬂow
from the evaporator into the receiving chamber of the ejector. After the receiving chamber the two
ﬂows mix and momentum is transferred between the ﬂows. Finally some of the pressure energy is
restored in the diffuser where the ﬂow is decelerated even more and the lift is achieved [3]. This
lift is decreasing the pressure ratio and increasing the mass ﬂow from the compressor thus higher

To control the direction of the ﬂow allowing the RS to be operated in the two different modes, 2
ball valves are used in combination with a shut off valve inside the ejector. The ball valve before
the high pressure control valve was ﬁtted because this valve was leaking. The other ball valve was

Ejector mode
The hot gas bypass valve (ETS) is completely open and the ball valve before the high pressure
control valve is closed. The shut off valve in the ejector is open and the ball valve on the ejector
suction line is open. The check valve shown on ﬁgure 2.8 ensures the increased pressure in the
compressor suction line is not ﬂowing towards the evaporator.
Standard mode
The ejector shut off valve is closed together with the ball valve in the ejector suction line. Flow
from the gas cooler is lead through the high pressure control valve by an open ball valve. The high
pressure is controlled by this valve (SAG) and the receiver pressure is controlled by the bypass
valve (ETS).
Suction line heat exchanger
An experimental study [7] has reported that by running a transcritical ejector cycle signiﬁcant
amount of liquid was present in the bypass line. In order to ensure no droplets reach the compressor
a suction line heat exchanger (SLHX) is implemented. The SLHX is implemented before the
compressor and after the gas cooler. The counter current SLHX state points "3" to "4" and "11" to
"1" can be seen in ﬁgure 2.7

Heat balance
The heat absorbed in the evaporator plus the electrical work added in the compressor should be
rejected in the gas cooler and from the compressor surface. The following equation should be
equal to zero.
˙Qe + ˙w−˙Qgc −˙Qhl = 0
(3.21)
If this is not the case, heat is either rejected or absorbed other places in the RS. By rewriting
equation 3.21 and assuming that the remaining heat in the system is transferred to the ambient by
the compressor surface one can calculate this heat loss.

Discussion
Optimum pressure
While logging data to form the basis of the obtained results, the RS was controlled to reach the
optimum pressure. This was done by a combination of controlling the rotational speed of the
compressor and add more or less heat in the evaporator. That was necessary to reach the right
evaporation and gas cooler pressure. The pressure has to be optimal right where the ﬂow is enter-
ing the high pressure control valve or the ejector. This means that the pressure loss in the CO2 ﬂow
meter was estimated assuming isenthalpic expansion in the ﬂow meter. If one observe the log(p)-
h-diagrams in appendix P it shows that the Danfoss optimum pressure was achieved for all data
sets with the highest deviation on the std30. In this data set the difference between the optimum
pressure and the actual pressure was equal to 0.4 bar. Due to this the results according to how
much compressor power is needed to deliver 1 kW cooling is more comparable to reality. CO2
refrigeration systems will control to reach this pressure if maximum cooling is not required. How-
ever, data according to how much 1 kW of delivered cooling for supermarket conditions would
cost in consumed compressor power was not found.

SINTEF because the increased suction pressure on SINTEF = 45 bar.
In the litterature [8, 9] ejector efﬁciencies above 30% are reported. This is not the case in this
project where the efﬁciency is 22%. Numerical investigations in this project have shown that to
obtain a better efﬁciency the lift by the ejector has to be increased. The reason why the lift is
missing can be due to a combination of the following:
• Oil return solution: The experimental setup is not equipped with an oil return solution. Oil
is ﬂowing around in the system with the refrigerant. This can cause problems in the ejector
because it is optimized by Danfoss to the properties of CO2 and not oil. Oil ﬁlm can stick
to the ejector inside and change the geometry of the ejector.
• Pipe between ejector outlet and receiver: The pipe between the ejector diffuser outlet and
the receiver have an inner diameter of 5 mm. This is 2 mm smaller than the diffuser outlet.
This can probably decrease the diffuser effects where the internal energy in the refrigerant
should be recovered. The volume ﬂow can be estimated from the data reduction tools by
knowing that the volume ﬂow is equal to the velocity times the cross sectional area of the
pipe. The velocity in the pipes is estimated to be 10 m/s for the data set ejec40.
• Ejector geometry:
Different operating conditions need different ejector geometries to
obtain good efﬁciencies. The 3kW high entrainment ejector delivered for test in this project
could turn out to be, suitable for other operating conditions than carried out in this thesis.
If one compare all the standard deviation bar graphs in appendix O it shows that especially the
pressure in the receiver and the evaporation pressure has a higher standard deviation than the other

sensors. This is because of how the AKV expansion valve functions. It expands the refrigerant
into the evaporator in pulses. This will affect the pressure inside the receiver and the evaporator
in pulses according to the same frequency. This will also affect the conditions inside the ejector
because the entrainment ratio will ﬂuctuate. This could potentially disturb the physics inside
the ejector especially in the diffuser where the lift is achieved. The ﬂows inside the ejector will
probably never get fully developed because of the AKV expansion valve.

Flow meter
The CO2 ﬂow meter measurement can be questioned because of the following:
• Maximum ﬂow: For all the ejector data sets the ﬂow measurement was equal to or above
the limit of 0.0152 kg/s.
• Steady state: It is stated in the Micro Motion ﬂow meter documentation that the sensor
reaches steady state after 30 min. of operation. This was not known when acquiring the data
sets.
• Pressure ﬂuctuations: The ﬂow meter measures ﬂow from the vibration pattern in two
tubes inside the sensor. It was originally placed after the gas cooler to ensure that pressure
ﬂuctuations from the compressor was equalized. However a brazed plate heat exchanger was
installed as gas cooler in this project. This is a more effective heat exchanger and maybe the
pressure ﬂuctuations are not completely equalized.

vestigated with the error propagation tool in EES. The absolute maximum error in the propagation
calculation is assumed to be 0.003 kg/s. This is the maximum difference observed between the
mass ﬂow measurements and the mass ﬂows calculated using the compressor polynomials and

Increased cycle performance
The relative cooling COP obtained in the ejector data sets is between 1.08 and 1.12 compared to
the standard cycle. The COP improvement increases as the gas cooler pressure increases. This is
due to the fact that the ejector can lift the evaporation pressure to a higher level when the motive
nozzle inlet pressure is increased. This improvement, implementing an ejector, is not expected to
increase the demand for ejector CO2 refrigeration cycles in Denmark. This is due to the fact that
these systems only are operated transcritical in 1 % of the year [14]. However, in other and warmer
climates of the world this COP improvement can potentially save the consumers for money and
therefor might increase the demand.
The relative cooling COP for the ﬂooded ejector data sets is between 1.27 and 1.32 which is a
signiﬁcant improvement. This is due to a combination of the ejector lift and elevated evaporation
temperature from -7◦C/-8◦C to -2◦C/-3◦C. If an ejector is implemented on commercial systems to-
gether with an efﬁcient evaporator this can save compressor power and thus increase the demand
for these systems further in warmer climates. The beneﬁts from this demand will inﬂuence the
environment positively.
4.1
Observations
When the experimental setup is not in use the temperature will reach the ambient temperature in
the laboratory. Most of the days where data was acquired the temperature was 23◦C. The saturation
pressure according to this temperature is 61.4 bar. However this pressure was not observed in the
RS (Analog pressure gauges located in the CO2 pipe work). This could indicate that the amount
of refrigerant charge in the system was too low and all the CO2 was gas phase inside the RS.
4.2
Future work
The following issues could be considered by other students.
• Implementing oil return solution in the RS to prevent oil from disturbing the conditions in
the ejector and ensure sufﬁcient lubrication of the compressor.
• Fitting 3 pressure sensors at the motive nozzle inlet, suction nozzle inlet and diffuser outlet
on the ejector. This could potentially uncover why the ejector is not as efﬁcient as the ones
reported in the literature [8, 9].
• Increasing the inner diameter of the pipe between the ejector outlet and the receiver. This
pipe contains the "whole" mass ﬂow in ejector mode causing high velocities and thus pres-
sure loss.
39
==PAGE:48==
• Finding a method to decrease the pressure loss in the hot gas bypass valve. In the existing
setup 0.7 bar of the pressure lift is not reaching the compressor.
• Replacing the ﬂow meter with a bigger one and maybe reconsider the location of the meter
in the cycle. The present ﬂow meter is not operated within its range for the Dorin CD180H
compressor. Additionally the pressure loss through the ﬂow meter is signiﬁcant.
• It could be interesting to ﬁt another type of expansion valve. e.g an ETS-valve-type that
ensures a continuous ﬂow of refrigerant through the evaporator. In other studies [8, 9] the
so called metering valve is a ETS-type. This would ensure that the ﬂows inside the ejector
will get fully developed and perhaps better lifts and efﬁciencies will be obtained.
• It is difﬁcult to control the gas cooler pressure to be optimal in ejector mode. This is due
to the unchangeable ejector geometry. It could be interesting to develop an ejector control
method to the experimental setup. One could combine the ejector and high pressure control
valve like in [8] and or the compressor. A PI control is available inside the AK-LM-340
controller to control the gas cooler pressure.
• The evaporator is under-dimensioned. High temperature differences are needed to transfer
heat. Due to this it was not possible to investigate the statement that one could elevate the
evaporation pressure in supermarket application from -7◦C/-8◦C to -2◦C/-3◦C when running
ﬂooded. It could be interesting to buy a plate heat exchanger as evaporator in order to get a
deeper investigation of the ﬂooded evaporator operation.
• One of the issues when dimensioning compressors for CO2 refrigeration systems especially
in Denmark, is the fact that the compressor should be able to handle the cooling load at the
highest ambient temperatures a year. Due to this the compressors are operated at low partial
load the rest of the year. Low partial load decreases the performance of the compressor.
An advantage by using two-phase ejectors in CO2 refrigeration cycles is the compressor
size can be decreased regarding the swept volume. This is due to the increased pressure
in the suction line caused by the pressure lift. This is increasing the volume ﬂow of the
compressor. It could be interesting to investigate this advantage experimentally with the
experimental setup. It was not possible to do with the results obtained in this thesis due to
the problems with the CO2 mass ﬂow meter.
• The receiver in the RS is equipped with a differential pressure sensor. However the range of
the sensor can not measure the liquid level inside the receiver. If a bigger one is ﬁtted it is
possible to estimate the liquid level inside the receiver. This allows estimation of refrigerant
charge in the system.

Conclusion
An existing CO2 condensing unit size refrigeration system was modiﬁed to investigate experimen-
tally whether the loses from the throttling process could be recovered by an two-phase ejector
(3kW high entrainment ejector developed by Danfoss A/S). The pipe work in the system allows
bypass of the ejector or high pressure control valve, meaning that the system can be operated in
standard booster mode or ejector mode.
The ejector was tested according to retail supermarket application with a evaporation tempera-
ture equal to -7◦C/-8◦C. The gas cooler pressure was attempted optimized for maximum COP
according to 3 CO2 outlet temperatures of the gas cooler. These temperatures was within the
range of 27◦C, 31◦C and 35◦C. At these conditions the ejector efﬁciency was up to 22% which
implies that the ejector recovers 22% of the maximum recovery rate. This corresponds to pressure
lifts equal to 2.6 bar, 3.4 bar and maximum 4.3 bar.
The pressure lift is the difference between the evaporation pressure and the ejector outlet pressure.
The ejector pre-compresses the CO2 before it reaches the compressor and power consumption is
decreased. By ejector operation the cooling COP is improved from 1.69 to 1.85 for a CO2 outlet
temperature of 27◦C, 1.40 to 1.51 for 31◦C and 1.11 to 1.25 for 35◦C. The maximum relative COP
is equal to 1.12.
Another advantage of the two-phase ejector cycle is the fact that the evaporator can be ﬂooded
to utilize the whole area for evaporation. According to this operation it was assumed that the evap-
oration temperature could be elevated to -2◦C/-3◦C, the gas cooler CO2 outlet temperature was
unchanged. This inﬂuenced the pressure lifts and thus efﬁciencies negatively because the mass
ﬂow in the evaporator was increased. The pressure lifts for these settings were 2.3 bar, 2.8 bar
and maximum 3.8 bar. Together with the elevated evaporation pressure this lead to cooling COP
improvements from 1.69 to 2.21 for a CO2 outlet temperature of 27◦C, 1.40 to 1.78 for 31◦C and
1.11 to 1.47 for 35◦C. This is a maximum relative COP of 1.32.
The experimental studies conducted in this master thesis has shown signiﬁcant improvement by
implementing a two-phase ejector in a condensing unit size CO2 refrigeration system running tran-
scritical. This is due to the fact that the cycle can be operated ﬂooded and in combination with the
pressure lift performed by the ejector the pressure ratio of the compressor decreases thus power
is saved. If implemented on commercial systems this can potentially expand the area where the
demand for these refrigeration cycles is high, especially in warmer climates. This will contribute
to a green and more clean environment in the future.

Appendix A
History of the experimental setup
The history of the experimental setup contain a couple of projects. These are outlined below.
2007 Fredslund et al. commence their diploma bachelor thesis [11] concerning the use of a CO2
system as a heat pump in a milk farm. They proved by simple mathematical models that
potential energy savings existed. This was due to the warm cow milk needed instant cooling
to 4◦C. Their idea was to use this excess heat to warm up the appurtenant buildings. They
needed to validate their mathematical models so they planned and executed the construction
of a small scale CO2 booster refrigeration system. The ﬁrst steps in development of the
present system was taken. The base of the system is a table ﬁtted with wheels, which
enables relocation of the experimental setup. The ﬁrst design can be seen in ﬁgure A.1.
Figure A.1: Design on ﬁrst project [11]
2010 3 years went by and Fredslund et al. [12] commenced a new project. This time their master
thesis. They wanted to investigate if it was possible to save fan power when the refrigeration
system (RS) was running transcritical. Their idea was to decrease fan speed and increase the
gas cooler pressure. This would inﬂuence the heat transfer in the gas cooler positively and
improve the performance of the system. They did not run any test because the compressor
broke down late in the project phase. Their modiﬁcation of the system can be seen in ﬁgure
A.2.

B.1.1
ETS / hot gas bypass valve
When the RS is operated in ejector mode this valve should be completely open and cause minor
pressure losses between the receiver and the compressor. In addition to this it should be able to
control the pressure in the receiver while running in standard mode. In earlier projects the ETS
valve have shown its capabilities to regulate the receiver pressure and due to this it is not evaluated
furhter.
When the ETS valve is completely open it has a Kv-value of 0.65 [15] for CO2 ﬂow. The Kv-value
deﬁnes the required ﬂow to obtain a pressure loss through the valve equal to 1 bar. The maximum
displacement rate by the compressor is 1.12 m3/h and if the maximum volumetric efﬁciency is
assumed to be 80% the maximum pressure drop is equal to 1.9 bar, see equation B.1

B.2.1
Electrical heater
To add heat to the evaporator loop in order to keep the inlet temperature of the evaporator constant
an electrical heater was needed. In the basement an electrical heater from an earlier project was
found. The heater contained 3 electrical circuits, one to each phase which corresponded to a total
of 6 kW if the circuits where star connected.
Unfortunately the coating on one of the heating elements was destroyed and the element lead
current to the earth connection forcing the residual current device to cut the power. Due to this
fact it was only possible to add 4 kW in total electrical heat to the loop. The compressor docu-
mentation was studied and it seems to be enough electrical power. The evaporator cooling do not
exceed 4000 watts with a evaporation temperature between 0◦C and -10◦C as seen in table B.1.
B.2.2

The old gas cooler solution was ﬁtted on the back of the RS table with several different temper-

decided to buy a plate heat exchanger to cool the gas from the compressor.

contacted and with the fact in mind that the gas cooler plate heat exchanger should not be under

To balance all the PI-controllers inside the AK-LM-340 a PC program called minilog is used.
Minilog is able to read and write values to the danfoss controllers. This is very usefull when
the RS is balanced and tests are performed. In addition to the read/write feature, monitoring the
pressure, temperatures and how the PI controllers are acting is also possible. This is very useful
when the different values (Kp and Tn) according to the PI control are found. While monitoring,
minilog is saving all possible information according to pressures, temperatures, references, plots
and different changes made.
To use minilog one need to program a LogSpec- and a PlotSpec-ﬁle, these ﬁles are attached on the
CD.

• Control mode - By changing this value one can turn the control on, off or set to manual.

changed by 12.
• Tn - The integral time. For how long the controller should integrate the error.
• Min ctrl output - This input can be used to deﬁne the lower limit in which range the output
should be. e.g. min. 20%.
• Max ctrl output - The upper limit in the range. e.g. max. 80%.

controllers are turned on.
• Startup output - Which output the controller should start controlling from.

Appendix I
Averaging in data reduction tool
The data from LabView is logged in comma separated values (CSV) each time the Agilent do one
multiplex cycle (about 2.1 sec.). The CSV data ﬁles are imported to a lookup table in EES. EES
can read the headers of the CSV ﬁles and name the columns in the lookup table after this. This
allow one to specify in which column EES should look up. The pressure after the compressor is
deﬁned by equation I.1.
Pcomp;out = Sumlookup(table$; ’216 Press. Comp. out [bar]’ ; RowStart; Rowstop)
Rowstop−rowstart +1
(I.1)
The numerator in equation I.1 is a EES function called SumLookup. It sums the values in lookup
table "table$" in the column with the header named "216 Press. Comp. out [bar]" from the rowstart
to the rowstop. The rowstart and rowstop are deﬁned as follows where timestart is the start of the
desired time interval and timestop is the end:
Rowstart = Round(Lookuprow(table$; ’Time [s]’ ; Timestart))
(I.2)
Rowstop = Round(Lookuprow(table$; ’Time [s]’ ; Timestop))
(I.3)
The rows are deﬁned by the EES function lookuprow. This function returns the row in the column
"Time [s]" where the value in the variable timestart or timestop are equal. The output are rounded
because EES somehow can deﬁne rows as a decimal number.
As seen in the denominator of equation I.1 the average is computed using the (rowstart - row-
stop +1) value. This is the number of pressure readings used to obtain the average.

Appendix J
Structure in data reduction tool
The data reduction tool consists of one (main) procedure called lookupdata and two sub-programs
called ejectortrue and ejectorfalse. If-statements are used in the procedure to choose between the
right sub-program in order to evaluate the data corresponding to the mode (standard or ejector).
To use the main procedure two different calls are used, one for ejector mode and one for stan-
dard mode. The call consists of different inputs and outputs, these are described below
Input
• table$ - Deﬁnes which table the tool should get the values from.
• ejec_true - a value, 1 or 0. Used to call the right sub-program in the main procedure to pick
the right calculation method according to the mode of the data.
• Timestart - Start of desired time interval.
• Timestop - End of desired time interval.
Output
• names$[1..36] - A vector which contain the header names of the imported CSV ﬁles. This
is usefull when plotting the standard deviation for the desired time interval for all columns
in the data ﬁle.
• stdev_ejec[1..36] - Also a vector. Containing the standard deviation calculation.
• COP - The coefﬁcient of performance of the RS
• ent_ratio - The entrainment ratio. Ratio between mass ﬂow in evaporator and gas cooler in
ejector mode.
• SH - The super heat.
• T_e - The evaporation temperature.
• Q_e_outer - The heat removed from the evaporator loop.
• Q_c_outer - The heat rejected to the gas cooler loop.
• P_lift - The lift created by the ejector.
• h[1..11] - A vector containing the enthalpies from point 1 to 11.
• W_dot_Voltech - Measured power consumed by the compressor
• eta_is - The isentropic efﬁciency of the compressor
74
==PAGE:83==
• eta_vol - The volumetric efﬁciency of the compressor
• m_dot_comp - The measured compressor mass ﬂow
• t_gc_out - The inlet temperature to the high pressure valve or ejector.
• t_comp_in - Temperature in compressor suction line
• V_dot - Compressor volume ﬂow
• q_hl - This variable were intended to contain the heat loss from the compressor surface but
are not used in the results because the temperature out of the compressor was not completely
stable.

Background
Because of increased awareness of global warming, the natural refrigerants have gained
ground in refrigeration systems world-wide. The natural refrigerants consists of am-
monia and the hydrocarbons, e.g. propane, isobutane and CO2.
CO2 has the advantage compared to the other because 1) it is cheap and can be
extracted from ongoing industrial processes and is therefore not harmful if leaked to
the environment. 2) It is non-ﬂammable and can only harm humans at high concen-
trations. However, the use of CO2 requires the refrigeration system can be operated
in transcritical conditions in order to reject heat at the ambient temperature during
summer time in Denmark. This is due to the fact that the critical temperature and
corresponding pressure of CO2 is equal to Tcrit= 31◦C and Pcrit= 73.8 bar. This
implies a CO2 refrigeration system at Danish weather conditions will reach pressures
higher than 100 bar
When the system is running in transcritical mode the COP of the system decreases
signiﬁcantly.
However, simulations performed by Danfoss have estimated that for
Denmark a given CO2 refrigeration system will only run transcritical for less than 1%
of the year [1]. Additionally CO2 systems are able to condense at lower temperatures
during winter compared to HFC based systems, thus operating with higher COP dur-
ing winter.
It is obvious that CO2 systems are more advantageous in colder climates because the
transcricitcal operation is limited. The environmental advantages of CO2 could be
utilized in hotter climates too if the system was more eﬃcient with respect to seasonal
coeﬃcient of performance (SCOP).
This master thesis will investigate whether an increase in COP can be achieved by
implementing an ejector in a transcritical operated CO2 refrigeration system located
on DTU. The purpose of the ejector is to utilize the throttling loses in the high pressure
control valve. This utilization by the ejector reduces the evaporation pressure below
the suction pressure in the compressor inlet, thus decreasing the pressure ratio in the
compressor. This will lead to increased system eﬃciency. Additionally, the evaporator
may run ﬂooded in ejector operation, thus increasing the evaporation temperature
compared to basic CO2 systems.
Problem statement
Is it possible to validate the increase in COP on a existing small scale (<6 kW) CO2
refrigeration system by implementing an ejector that employ the throttling loses from
the high pressure control valve?
Sub question
Will the performance of the ejector be reduced in oﬀ-design operation and how could
the ejector be controlled?

Challenges
Several challenges showed up during the construction process. The most time consuming and
difﬁcult ones is described below.
U.1
Silver solder / TIG Welding
The sweb gas cooler connections was 3/4 inch G pipe thread. This would not ﬁt with the stainless
steel hydraulic pipes the RS was build from on the gas side of the gas cooler. It was necessary to
design a round ﬁtting which should ﬁt in the gas cooler connection on the outer diameter and ﬁt
the 6 mm steel pipes on the inner diameter. The ﬁtting was manufactured by brass so one were
able to silver solder the ﬁtting between the gas cooler and the steel pipe. Before the ﬁtting could be
soldered to the connection it needed thorough cleaning and heating. The gas cooler was placed in a
water bath, see ﬁgure U.2. This was necessary to prevent destructive over heating of the gas cooler.
This ﬁtting was also manufactured to the 3 ejector ﬂanges. These should ﬁt the ﬂanges and the 8
mm steel pipes going to and from the ejector. The ﬂanges soldered with the brass ﬁtting, 8 mm
steel pipes and swagelok union can be seen in ﬁgure U.1.

U.4
Safety valves and high pressure switch
There is 3 pressure levels in the RS. Each level is equipped with a safety valve. The new safety
valve and the old ones needed adjustment in order to ensure they would release the pressure in case
it was too high. The magnitude of the pressure forcing the valve to open can be adjusted with the
spring inside each valve. If the spring is compressed by turning the top of the valve, the pressure
to open the valve have to be off greater magnitude. The opening pressure on the safety valve was
adjusted to 75 bar for receiving and evaporation pressure and 130 bar for the high pressure end of
the RS. This should ensure no CO2 leaving the system even at high summer temperatures within
the laboratory.

U.5
The ﬁrst run
The 7. of April 2016 were the ﬁrst run with the ejector ﬁtted CO2 refrigeration system on Techni-
cal University of Denmark.
The ﬁrst part of the day was used to ﬁnd minor problems with the setup. Some mistakes was
found regarding the wiring of all the controllers, valves, pressure readings and temperature read-
ings. This caused bad control of the RS. The day ended very well because a pressure lift in ejector
mode was observed. During the day the following lessons was learned:
• Purity of CO2 - The in house CO2 was not pure enough for CO2 refrigeration. A new bottle
was ordered with a classiﬁcation of 4 which imply the purity of the CO2 is equal to 99.995%.
Luckily there was one bottle of class 4 CO2 left in Denmark otherwise the delivery time was
6 weeks. This could potentially have delayed the project extensively.
• High pressure valve upside down - Some issues occurred while attempting to close the high
pressure valve completely in ejector mode. The valve seemed to leak. After some research
it was observed that the high pressure valve was ﬁtted upside down. This could probably be
the reason why the valve leaked.
• Check valve in ejector - While running in standard mode ice formation was observed on
the ejector suction pipe. This indicated that the check valve inside the ejector was mal-
functioned. It was decided to implement a ball valve on this pipe to ensure the ﬂow was
restricted.

550 or manually. The evaporator was ﬂooded and the super heat did collapse all the time

During the next days the AKV valve was disassembled to investigate the problems. Some
problems in the AKV where detected caused by some metal shavings from pipe work and a

U.6
Oil return
The receiver was designed and constructed in an earlier project [11]. The only oil return solution
was performed by letting the pipe going from the receiver to the bypass valve through the liquid
in the receiver by a u-shape. At the bottom of the U a small hole (Ø = 4 mm) was drilled. The
purpose of this construction is to suck accumulated oil back to the compressor. Nevertheless the
ﬁrst compressor broke down probably because oil was lacking. Due to these facts the oil level in
the compressor was visual inspected through the oil sight glass on the compressor at the beginning
of every data set. During the day the oil level was shifting but every morning all the oil seemed to
be returned to the compressor.
U.7
AK-CC-550 controller
During the test runs the CC-550 had some problems controlling the super heat. The issue is shown
in ﬁgure U.11.

Figure U.11: Control parameters from CC-550
The yellow line is the opening degree of the AKV-valve. The green line is super heat. The control
algorithms inside the CC-550 should try to get the super heat to reach the reference of 9◦C (red
line). If one observe the beginning of ﬁgure U.11 the CC-550 is controlling the super heat to a
satisfying level but then something happens. The AKV is closed and this is forcing the evaporation
pressure to decrease and the super heat is rising because mass ﬂow of refrigerant is restricted by
the AKV. In the case shown in ﬁgure U.11 the CC-550 succeeds to re-establish the super heat but
in many other cases the control is lost and one have to control the super heat by enabling manual
deﬁnition of opening degree in the CC-550.
Even though the control parameters in the CC-550 was adjusted the control can break down and
the evaporation pressure and super heat will start ﬂuctuating alot.
U.8
Valve position
When the controllers are powered on the valves turn to open and fully closed to learn their position.
Sometimes they forget this position and one can not count of the reported opening degrees by
minilog. This is causing strange behaviour of the RS and one need to restart the controllers to
move on with the experiments. This is an annoying issue because the controllers need a start up
time of a couple of minutes.
