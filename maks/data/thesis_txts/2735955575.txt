Abstract
The workload for General Practitioners (GPs) in Norway have been increasing over several
years. This have led to poor work­life balance and increased risk of burnout. The integra­
tion of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into medical practice signifies a potential paradigm shift in
healthcare delivery, particularly concerning the administrative workload, time management,
and work­life balance. This study explores the promise of AI technologies through a qual­
itative research lens, systematically analyzing the implications of their adoption within the
Norwegian healthcare system. Central to this is Noteless, a groundbreaking Software as a
Service (SaaS) AI tool developed to alleviate the burdensome administrative tasks that en­
croach upon GPs’. By automating patient documentation, Noteless promises to streamline
administrative efficiency, allowing GPs to redirect their focus towards a more patient­centric
care. In­depth interviews with GPs provides a foundation for understanding the nuanced
realities of AI’s impact on day­to­day general practice.
The findings indicate a complex interplay between the perceived benefits of AI adoption and a
set of persistent challenges. While enhanced efficiency and the potential for increased work­
life balance are lauded, concerns about technology acceptance, heightened ’technostress’,
and ethical dilemmas present significant hurdles. Moreover, the data uncovers a distinct
need for robust support mechanisms tailored to GPs, advocating for a structured approach
encompassing training and phased integration strategies.
The research culminates in a synthesis of insights that delineate both facilitators and ob­
stacles to AI integration, yielding strategic recommendations for the deployment of AI within
general practices. Notably, the findings underscore a demand for AI tools that harmonize
with the inherent requirements and aspirations of GPs, engendering user­friendly, secure,
and clinically supportive technologies.

Introduction
It is becoming more and more important to address the difficulties General Practitioners
(GPs) encounter in the current healthcare environment. GPs are responsible for managing
a broad range of medical conditions and are frequently the initial point of contact for patients.
This position necessitates not only a high level of medical knowledge but also an important
amount of emotional intelligence and empathy in order to manage the various and intricate
health issues that patients face. Burnout is a condition of physical, emotional, and mental ex­
haustion that impedes general practitioners’ ability to provide the best possible care for their
patients. It can be brought on by the emotional and mental strain of this responsibility as well
as the high­stress environment of clinical practice. This may lead to poor decisions; display
hostile attitude toward patients; make more medical errors; and have difficult relationships
with co­workers [1].
Furthermore, the psychological and emotional costs associated with patient care are signif­
icant and frequently overlooked. GPs often see patients with a variety of health concerns,
from acute and chronic illnesses to mental health issues and preventive care. The work
of general practitioners becomes increasingly complex as a result of the need for each pa­
tient interaction to be sensitive and unique. GPs are placed in a challenging position where
they must constantly strike a balance between the complexities of patient care and their own
well­being because of this multifaceted role that encompasses both medical and emotional
dimensions. This dynamic is further complicated by the stigma that surrounds mental health
issues in the medical community, which can prevent GPs from getting the help or treatment
they require. This unwillingness to take care of their own mental health needs can feed a
vicious cycle of stress and burnout, which further undermines their ability to function in their
personal and professional lives and, ultimately, lowers the standard of care provided. The
requirement to constantly refresh their knowledge in accordance with the quickly changing
medical field presents another difficulty for GPs. It’s important but time­consuming to stay
up to date on the most recent medical guidelines and advancements, and it frequently takes
more time than they have available given their already hectic schedules [2].
In September 2023, a general practitioner (GP) named Maiken tragically took her own life as
a result of an overwhelming burden of stress associated with her occupation. Consequently,
a campaign titled ”#legermåleve” arose as a consequence of her husband’s Facebook post
regarding the unfortunate incident. In a short period, a multitude of individuals have swiftly
joined and endorsed the effort. The broad dedication signifies more than just a demonstration
of involvement in Maiken’s unfortunate demise [3]. The provision of support should also be
interpreted as an acknowledgment of the reality that numerous doctors perceive their daily
work in the healthcare system as a great deal of stress on both their own lives and the lives
of others. This indicates a significant necessity to simplify their workday.
GPs face a challenging balancing act, navigating the complexities of patient care amidst a
backdrop of increasing administrative duties. This growing workload, fueled by legislative
changes and the transfer of tasks from secondary to primary care, encroaches significantly
on their capacity to deliver direct patient care. The study highlights the multifaceted nature
of this issue, from the direct implications for patient services to the personal toll on GPs,
including physical exhaustion and diminished job satisfaction. The consequences extend
beyond the individual, impacting the healthcare system’s ability to attract and retain the next
generation of GPs. This pervasive cycle of administrative overload and the ensuing strain

on GPs’ well­being and professional fulfillment underline the pressing need for systemic re­
forms to alleviate these pressures and safeguard the quality of healthcare services[1]. It
may be essential to streamline these administrative procedures using AI. Using this tech­
nology to automate and streamline administrative work could greatly potentially lessen the
workload. AI with its correct use can be seen as a highly efficient secretary. By automating
routine paperwork, scheduling appointments, managing patient records, and handling billing
processes, AI technologies can significantly reduce the administrative burden on GPs. This
allows them to devote more time and attention to patient care, improving both the quality of
service and patient outcomes. The incorporation of AI in handling these tasks also aims to
enhance accuracy, reduce the potential for human error, and streamline office operations,
leading to a more efficient healthcare delivery system [4]. Then GPs can concentrate on
what they are trained and passionate about: providing high­quality medical care. This can
ultimately lead to improved patient outcomes and increased professional fulfillment. It also
can free up more time for patient interaction and care [5]. But before these solutions are put
into action, care must be taken to make sure they are easy to use and actually solve the
problems general practitioners face on a daily basis.
It is important to carefully consider the challenges that come with integrating new technol­
ogy into general practitioners’ workflows to avoid adding to the already high stress levels
and burnout risks. Even though integrating technological solutions, like AI­based tools, may
prove to be highly advantageous in the long run, it can initially cause disruptions to estab­
lished routines. With these new systems, GPs might have a steep learning curve that takes
a lot of time and effort to get used to. Their work may temporarily become more complex
during this adaptation phase, which would increase rather than lessen their cognitive load
[6].
The smooth integration of new technology into routine tasks is critical to its efficacy. General
practitioners may become frustrated and resistant to new technology if it is not well suited
to current practices or if it necessitates substantial adjustments to the clinical procedure
[7]. This opposition to change is a major obstacle that is frequently caused by people’s
natural inclination to stick to known routines and their fears of the unknown [8]. GPs may
see the adoption of new technologies, such as AI­based tools, as an additional challenge
rather than a solution because they are already overworked and overburdened [1]. This
resistance is often fueled by worries about potential complications, increased workloads,
and the perceived threat of depersonalizing patient care. Concerns can also be heightened
by the unknowns surrounding data privacy, new system dependability, and interoperability
with legacy technologies. Managing change well is essential to solving these kinds of issues.
It entails addressing concerns and misunderstandings, providing GPs with clear information
about the short­ and long­term advantages of new technology, and involving them in the
decision­making and implementation process. To facilitate the transition and give general
practitioners the confidence and skills to effectively use new systems, training and support
are equally important. Effective technology management is crucial in guaranteeing that new
technology is easy to use, integrates seamlessly with current processes, and actually lessens
workloads instead of making them more complex. This strategy needs to be carefully thought
out, patiently implemented, and have ongoing feedback mechanisms in place so that the
technology can be adjusted to the needs and experiences of the users. The integration
of strategic technology management and change management can facilitate the adoption
of new technological solutions and encourage general practitioners to view these changes
more positively [8].
The issue of continuing support and maintenance for these technologies also exists. The
already stressful environment can be made worse by frequent technical problems and in­

adequate support, which can cause disruptions in patient care. Therefore, even though the
introduction of new technology has the potential to improve patient outcomes and streamline
operations, its implementation must be sensitive to the workload and stress levels that gen­
eral practitioners currently face. To ensure that these technological advancements truly help
GPs simplify their workday and reduce overall stress, it may be imperative to offer sufficient

As of the fourth quarter of 2023, Norway faced a significant challenge in its healthcare system,
with 214,095 people lacking access to a general practitioner (GP) [9]. While the straightfor­
ward solution to this issue might appear to be the recruitment of more GPs, the reality is far
more complex. The role of a GP involves a considerable mental and emotional burden, mak­
ing it less appealing for many medical professionals. Often, doctors opt for specialties that
are perceived as less stressful and more balanced in terms of work­life integration. This pref­
erence can be attributed to the demanding nature of general practice, which encompasses
not only a wide range of medical knowledge but also the need to manage complex patient
relationships and extensive administrative tasks [2].
The policies and infrastructure that currently govern healthcare also have a significant impact
on the career decisions made by physicians. The heavy administrative workload and the
high standards for patient care can increase workplace stress [2]. Consequently, a career
in general practice may be discouraged for both recent medical graduates and experienced
physicians. This possible pattern makes the lack of GPs even worse, creating a vicious
cycle where the burden and patient load per GP rise and the profession becomes even less
appealing.
It may take a multifaceted approach to solve this problem. In addition to providing better
support systems and manageable workloads for general practitioners, it involves rethinking
the way primary healthcare is provided. Cutting­edge approaches, like integrating AI, could
streamline administrative work while improving patient care, relieving some of the strain on
general practitioners [10]. GP availability could perhaps be bridged by investing in these
technologies and offering GPs comprehensive training and support to help them adjust to
these new tools. This can potentially make a career in general practice more fulfilling and
long­lasting. The challenges in the healthcare industry can motivate the integration of these
technologies. These technological developments, which amount to more than small adjust­
ments, mark a fundamental shift in the way healthcare is delivered.

1.1
Problem Formulation
The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into healthcare represents innovation aimed at
enhancing service delivery, patient outcomes, and operational efficiencies. This qualitative
study is positioned at the heart of this transformation, with a focus on GPs, which are the
frontline of healthcare services. It seeks to unravel the complex interplay between the an­
ticipated benefits of AI adoption in general practice against the backdrop of its multifaceted
challenges. The deployment of AI technology, as Noteless in a general practice in Bergen,
Norway, serves as the case study, providing a concrete example to illuminate these dynam­
ics.
Despite widespread enthusiasm for AI’s potential to transform healthcare, there remains a
stark gap in the literature regarding its direct impact on the daily routines and workflows of
GPs. This thesis endeavors to bridge this gap by delving into the specific benefits and chal­
lenges associated with AI integration in general practice settings. Moreover, the exploration
extends to understanding these effects within the unique context of Norway’s healthcare
system. The nuanced relationship between the promising advantages of AI technologies
and the barriers to their adoption in clinical environments demands a thorough investigation,
particularly through the lens of GPs’ lived experiences.
The necessity of this study is underscored by the current under representation of empirical re­
search focusing on the real­world implications of AI technologies on GPs. By examining how
AI adoption influences GPs’ workload, time management, and overall work­life balance, this
research aims to offer a holistic view of AI’s practical implications in general practice. Such
insights are pivotal for identifying the facilitators and barriers to AI integration, thus provid­
ing a grounded perspective to guide future technological deployments, policy developments,
and practice guidelines within the healthcare sector.
1.1.1
Research Objectives
The primary objective of this study is to identify the key factors that influence GPs’ willingness
to incorporate AI technologies into their workday routines. Through a qualitative research
approach, this study will:
• Examine the anticipated benefits of AI adoption in general practice and contrast these
with the challenges faced by GPs.
• Utilize the implementation of Noteless in a general practice as a case study to illustrate
these dynamics.
• Explore the impact of AI technologies on GPs’ workload, time management, and work­
life balance, aiming to provide a comprehensive understanding of the implications of
AI in the field.
1.1.2
Research Questions
The following research questions will guide this investigation:
• What are the key factors impacting GPs’ willingness to implement AI technologies into
their workday?
• How do the anticipated benefits of AI adoption in general practice compare with the
challenges it presents?
• In what ways do AI technologies influence GPs’ workload, time management, and over­
all work­life balance?

2
General Practice in Norway
In Norway, a General Practitioner (GP) is refereed to as a ”Fastlege” or ”Allmennlege”. Res­
idents of a Norwegian municipality are entitled to be registered with a GP, a provision over­
seen by the municipalities as part of their responsibility to offer necessary health and care
services. The GP scheme, introduced in Norway in 2001, is not regulated by law or central
authorities in terms of the number of GP agreements; it’s up to each municipality to enter
into the necessary operational agreements. GPs in Norway have reported increasing work­
loads due to the transfer of tasks from specialist healthcare services, highlighting a need for
more GPs. Recruitment challenges have arisen, exacerbated by demographic, technolog­
ical, and medical advancements, as well as increased expectations of health services and
greater patient responsibility assigned to municipalities through reforms. A study titled ”Leg­
ers arbeidstid og tid til pasientarbeid i perioden 1994­2014” based on a 20­year survey of
a representative group of doctors, including GPs, revealed an increase on reported weekly
working hours. The time spent on direct patient care slightly decreased, with differences
noted between male and female GPs in working hours and time allocated to patient care
[11].
2.1
Workload
Historically, GPs in Europe have expressed high levels of job satisfaction, thanks to signifi­
cant autonomy and compatibility with family life. Nevertheless, there has been a noticeable
trend towards a heavier workload in general practice across various European nations, such
as Norway. This increase in workload causes recruitment challenges and raises concerns
about the well­being of both GPs and their patients [1]. On average, a GPs patient list has
approximately 1100 patients. Although this number has dropped in recent years, and may
be a consequence of an increased workload per patient [12].
On average, GPs in Norway work 55.6 hours per week (with a median of 52.2 hours), though
there is significant variation. Ten percent of doctors work more than 75 hours a week, while
25 percent work more than 62 hours. Only ten percent of doctors have a total work week
of up to 37.5 hours. Differences between sexes are relatively small. Over 80 percent of
general practitioners work more than the maximum 40 hours per 7 days stipulated by the
Working Environment Act. On average, a GP in Norway works 18,1 hours more than what
is considered a standard work week [11].
A study on GPs in Norway have shed light on the nuances of workload fluctuations, revealing
through participant feedback the factors leading to periods of increased work demand. These
studies found that the workload varied not only by day of the week, but also seasonally.
In particular, both respondent groups pinpointed epidemics, such as influenza, which bring
an influx of patients, as a significant source of increased workload. Moreover, the studies
highlighted that for GPs, especially busy times were often linked to either their own or their
colleagues’ leaves of absence, including the preparation for and the return from such breaks
[1].
Reflecting on the challenges of managing workload upon returning from brief absences, one
female general practitioner (GP) shared her experience: ”The reality is, taking more than a
couple of days off seems unmanageable because the backlog of tasks awaiting upon return
feels overwhelmingly insurmountable” [1]. Following a similar sentiment, another practitioner
emphasized the unsustainable nature of their current working conditions, expressing a critical

2.2
Time Management
A study dated 2019, explains that GPs in Norway expressed that they now perceived busy
days as the “new normal”. As response to this, the GPs said they were forced to adjust
their way of working by prioritizing harder. They prioritized patient consultations, postponed
documentation and administrative work to evenings and weekends, and were left with little
time for personal rest and recuperation. System­level work, such as participating in meet­
ings, forums, and other arrangements at the municipal level, and preventive care were given
less priority due to lack of time. Additionally, physicians expressed concerns that their pro­
fessional development may be negatively affected by, for example, postponing or skipping
educational courses [1].
Adding to the insights on workload challenges, a GP highlighted the overwhelming impact of
administrative duties: “The workload comes mostly from the paperwork. I sit with paperwork
until seven or eight o’clock every evening. I’m done with patients about four o’clock, so it’s the
paperwork that makes it impossible to pick up the kids, or cook dinner”. GPs note a marked
increase in the complexity and demand of administrative and economic responsibilities within
their practices in recent years. They identified employer­related tasks, such as managing
pensions and processing sick leave for staff, as particularly burdensome [1].
2.3
Work­Life Balance
Poor work­life balance can lead to burnout, which is a serious and widespread problem that
affects a sizable percentage of medical professionals worldwide. Research shows that in
many different nations, professions, and work environments, there is a high prevalence of
depersonalization, emotional fatigue, and decreased personal accomplishment. The fre­
quency varies; according to some reports, up to 80% of physicians in particular fields or spe­
cializations may be suffering from burnout [2]. This problem underscores the urgent need
for efficient intervention and prevention strategies because it affects not only the health of
medical personnel but also the standard of patient care.
According to research on preventing burnout, changing work procedures is an essential pre­
ventive measure. This strategy, which is classified as one of the three suggested change
measures, focuses on modifying the work processes to successfully lower the risk of burnout.
The justification for this strategy comes from its ability to improve workplace health by ad­
dressing risk factors that are static or difficult to change, but are directly associated with
burnout. Occupational health psychology has also come to see the improvement of work
engagement, which is defined by vigor, dedication, and absorption, as a preventive strategy
against burnout. Organizations can foster an environment where physicians feel more ener­
gized, dedicated, and engaged in their work by restructuring work processes to support these
positive attributes of work. Positive engagement is presented as the opposite of burnout, in­
dicating that by fostering a more rewarding and interesting work environment, an emphasis
on process improvement could considerably help to reduce burnout among physicians [2].
In a comprehensive study involving nearly 10 000 doctors, a notable degree of dissatisfac­
tion among GPs with their work routines has been uncovered, with almost half of the re­
spondents expressing uncertainty about remaining in their positions over the next two years.

This discontent stems from work­related stress and a reduction in autonomy, with doctors
facing increasingly hectic schedules that limit their ability to impact their work environment
and subsequently, patient care quality. Compounding the stress of an already demanding
workday is the challenge posed by outdated and inefficient IT systems. The study highlights
a critical need for smarter investments in healthcare technology, specifically in developing IT
solutions that are both user­friendly and conducive to the fast­paced environment of medical
practice. The current state of IT infrastructure in healthcare not only impedes the efficiency
of medical practitioners but also detracts from their ability to focus on patient care. With the
majority of a doctor’s time being consumed by administrative tasks facilitated by these sys­
tems, there is a clear and present need to overhaul this aspect of healthcare to restore the
focus on patient­centered care. The findings from this study serve as a crucial call to action
for healthcare policymakers and administrators [13].
2.4
Barriers to New Technologies
Amidst the backdrop of escalating workloads and the quest for improved work­life balance,
the adoption of digital technologies in general practice emerges as a potential alleviator. Yet,
the integration of these innovations faces significant barriers, underscoring the complexity of
modernizing healthcare delivery in Norway.
2.4.1
Digital Competence Among Patients
A notable challenge in the digital transition within GP offices is the varying level of digital
competence among patients. Approximately 55% of GP offices have identified a deficiency
in digital skills among their clientele, particularly pronounced among the elderly population.
This demographic tends to visit GP offices more frequently and exhibits lower digital liter­
acy, complicating the shift from traditional to digital modes of communication. Despite these
hurdles, the necessity to cater to a digitally adept patient base remains, advocating for a
balanced approach that accommodates all segments of the population. This scenario under­

4.1
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) is a theoretical framework developed to explain
and predict the acceptance and use of information systems and technology by individuals.
Originating from the work of Davis in 1989, TAM is rooted in the Theory of Reasoned Action
and aims to clarify how users come to accept and utilize technology.
The model identifies two primary factors that influence a user’s decision regarding the adop­
tion and use of technology.
• The first of these factors is Perceived Usefulness, defined as the degree to which
a person believes that using a particular system or technology will enhance their job
performance or productivity. The underlying assumption is that if users perceive a
technology as beneficial, they are more likely to adopt and utilize it.
• The second factor, Perceived Ease of Use, pertains to the degree to which a person
believes that using a particular technology will be effortless. This aspect focuses on
the user’s belief that the technology will not be cumbersome and can be seamlessly
integrated into their daily tasks. The model suggests that technologies perceived as
easy to use are more likely to be accepted and integrated by users [17].
Within the framework of the Technology Acceptance Model, these two factors are not only
directly associated with the intention to use technology but are also interrelated. For example,
if a technology is perceived as easy to use, it may also be regarded as more useful because
the ease of use can contribute to enhanced performance by simplifying task execution [17].

TAM has been widely utilized in research to explore user acceptance across various tech­
nology types, including information systems and online services. The model offers insights
into the motivations behind technology acceptance or rejection and assists in the design of
technologies that are more likely to meet user approval [17].

4.2
Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology
(UTAUT)
While the TAM primarily focuses on perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness as
the main determinants of technology adoption, the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use
of Technology (UTAUT) presents a more comprehensive and nuanced framework. Devel­
oped by Venkatesh et al. (2003), UTAUT integrates key constructs from multiple technology
acceptance theories, including TAM and the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), to examine
the factors influencing technology adoption and usage across various organizational settings
[18].
This framework emphasizes the roles of Performance Expectancy, Effort Expectancy, Social
Influence, and Facilitating Conditions as the primary determinants of technology adoption
behaviors, while also accounting for the moderating effects of user characteristics such as
gender, age, experience, and voluntariness of use. This multifaceted approach provides a
detailed understanding of the dynamics at play in technology adoption, revealing how these
factors interact within specific contexts and offering insights into the nuanced dynamics of
technology acceptance across diverse user groups [18].
Performance Expectancy is conceptualized as the degree to which an individual believes
that using a system will enhance their job performance. Drawing from models like TAM, this
construct is identified as the most potent predictor of intention to use a technology, empha­
sizing the critical role of perceived utility in technology adoption decisions [18].
Effort Expectancy represents the perceived ease associated with the use of a technology.
It emphasizes the critical role of user­friendly design and intuitive interfaces in fostering tech­
nology acceptance. This construct implies that technologies which are perceived as easy to
use are more readily adopted, accentuating the importance of optimizing user experience in
the development and deployment of new technologies [18].
Social Influence measures the extent to which individuals believe that important others
(e.g., peers, superiors, societal norms) expect them to use the new technology. This con­
struct highlights the influence of social context and norms on technology adoption decisions,
demonstrating how the perceptions of peers and institutional pressures can significantly im­
pact individual behaviors and attitudes toward technology adoption [18].
Facilitating Conditions are defined as the degree to which an individual believes that an
organizational and technical infrastructure exists to support use of the system. This construct
underscores the importance of having the necessary support mechanisms and infrastructure
in place to enable effective technology use, suggesting that the availability of such conditions
can greatly enhance the likelihood of technology adoption [18].

Methodology
It was found that the best way to research the willingness of GPs to implement AI to their
workday is by using an exploration method. The reason for this is that the research focus
is unexplored yet. Hence, the overall goal is to become familiar with unknown concepts.
Given the exploratory and nuanced nature of the research questions, a qualitative method­
ology has been selected for its strengths in uncovering in­depth insights and understanding
complex phenomena. This approach is particularly suited to dynamic and evolving fields
like AI in healthcare, where pre­existing models and theories may not fully capture the lived
experiences and perspectives of healthcare professionals [20]. Through in­depth interviews
and a case study analysis of Noteless AS, this research seeks to provide a comprehensive
understanding of the factors influencing GPs’ willingness to implement AI into their practices.
5.1
Inductive Research
This study adopts an inductive research approach to delve into the role of AI in healthcare,
with a specific focus on GPs willingness to implement AI technologies into their practice.
The inductive method, moving from specific observations to broader generalizations, is par­
ticularly well­suited for exploratory research in dynamic fields like AI within healthcare. This
approach is chosen for its ability to allow theories to emerge naturally from the empirical
data gathered, closely aligning with our aim to uncover the factors influencing GPs’ readi­
ness to embrace AI technology. Utilizing an inductive strategy enables the study to ven­
ture into the relatively unexplored impacts of AI in healthcare without being constrained by
pre­established theories or frameworks. This method is useful in identifying new patterns,
themes, and insights regarding AI’s practical application in the healthcare setting. It is espe­
cially beneficial for ensuring the research remains responsive to the continual advancements
in AI technology, capturing the evolving nature of AI in healthcare. The research emphasizes
qualitative methods, such as in­depth interviews with healthcare professionals, to thoroughly
explore GPs’ experiences and perspectives on AI. This focus on qualitative data collection
facilitates a comprehensive understanding of AI’s impact, free from the limitations of existing
theoretical models. The inductive approach’s inherent flexibility allows the study to adapt
based on the findings from these interviews, an essential feature given the rapid pace of
technological change in the healthcare sector. By prioritizing an inductive methodology, the
research stands to offer valuable insights into the complex interplay between AI technology
and healthcare practices. It positions the study to not only capture the current state of AI inte­
gration but also to shed light on potential future developments and obstacles. Opting for this
approach underscores the commitment to generating theory from qualitative insights and to
providing a rich, grounded understanding of the transformative potential of AI in healthcare
[21].
While a deductive research approach on the other hand, starts from a hypothesis based on

context was evaluated against several critical considerations. The first and foremost consid­
eration was the exploratory essence of the study. The integration of AI technologies into
healthcare, especially within the practices of GPs, is an area ripe with innovation and con­
tinuous evolution. The application of a deductive approach, which relies on the testing of

5.2
Case Study
Established in October 2023, Norway­based Noteless has emerged as an innovative player
in healthcare technology. Under the leadership of the founders, William Vossgård (CEO)
(who also is the author of this thesis), and Mikkel Dahl Slettebø (CTO), the company’s mission
is to alleviate the administrative burden on GPs through their SaaS AI platform. The easiest
way to explain how the platform works in by comparing it to a sectretary sitting in the doctors
office, noting down and writing complete journal notes for the doctor. The way this works
technically in the platform, is that the system transcribes the doctor­patient interaction in real­
time and then automatically generates medical journal notes with advanced fine­tuned Large
Language Model (LLMs) afterwards. The transcribing functions by using a speech­to­text API
that is specialized in transcribing Norwegian medical conversations. The transcription model
is fine­tuned to catch Norwegian medical terms so that it fits for this specific application. The

5.2.2
Theoretical Saturation
The authenticity and precision of data derived from interviews can be swayed by various
elements, such as the bias of the interviewer, biases from the participants, and the caliber
of the questions posed during the interview. It’s crucial to take these factors into account to
safeguard the data’s integrity and the analysis that follows.
Bias from the interviewer encompasses the potential impact of the interviewer’s own beliefs,
views, and presuppositions on the collection and interpretation of data. This can appear as
leading questions, inadvertent signals, or a tendency for selective hearing. Furthermore,
there’s the issue of social desirability bias, where interviewees may tailor their responses
to what they believe is socially acceptable, rather than being forthright about their actual
thoughts or experiences [22]. Conversely, bias from the participant denotes the inclination
of interviewees to tailor their responses based on their perceptions of the interviewer’s expec­
tations. Influenced by social norms, fear of being judged, or the pursuit of personal benefit,
this bias is noteworthy. For instance, the Hawthorne effect illustrates how individuals might
change their behavior or answers when they are aware of being observed [23]
To counter these biases and fortify the data’s reliability and validity, several strategies can be
employed. Encouraging genuine responses and reducing leading questions can be achieved
through the utilization of open­ended questions [24]. Building a rapport and trust with intervie­
wees via a neutral and open demeanor can also diminish participant bias [25]. Furthermore,
enhancing the data quality can be achieved through a meticulous and thorough approach
in designing and analyzing interviews. This involves formulating precise research queries,
adhering to standardized interview protocols, conducting preliminary interviews for adjust­
ments, and applying multiple coding or cross­validation methods for consistent and accurate
interpretation of data [26]. Also, since the interviewer is the CEO of the company providing
the platform a layer of complexity to managing biases is added. The CEO’s dual role could
lead to unconscious bias, potentially steering the conversation to highlight positive outcomes
or downplay negatives. This situation risks collecting data that may overly favor the product’s
perceived effectiveness and customer satisfaction. Participants, aware of the interviewer’s
significant role, might also adjust their responses, leaning perhaps towards positivity to main­
tain good business relations or out of respect. This could result in data that does not fully
capture user experiences or issues with the platform.
To mitigate these risks, some strategies needs to be employed:
Rigorous Reflexivity Recognizing and reflecting on the potential biases of the researcher
and the research process to maintain objectivity.
Open­Ended Questions: Utilizing open­ended questions during interviews to minimize bias
and allow participants to express their views freely.
Diverse Participant Selection: Ensuring a broad representation of GPs to capture a wide

The study sought to employ a thematic analysis of interviews with GPs, intending to identify

The limited number of participants presents a methodological constraint, particularly for con­

With only four interviews, each lasting approximately 15 minutes, the potential for generat­

matic saturation is a key concept in qualitative research, referring to the point at which no
new themes or insights are identified from the data, thereby ensuring the comprehensive
coverage of material pertinent to the research questions [20].

8.6
Limitations
One of the most prominent limitations of this study is the relatively small sample size of
four GPs who have adopted Noteless in their practice. While the insights garnered from
these interviews are valuable, they represent a narrow slice of the broader GP population.
The experiences and perspectives of these GPs may not fully encapsulate the diversity of
challenges and opportunities presented by AI implementation in healthcare settings across
Norway. Additionally, the selection process for these participants might introduce biases,
particularly if those who have had either exceptionally positive or negative experiences with
Noteless are more inclined to participate in such a study.
The qualitative nature of this study, while providing depth and nuance to the understanding
of AI integration into general practice, limits the ability to quantify the prevalence of reported
experiences and attitudes among the wider GP community. Furthermore, the study’s focus
on a single AI platform may not capture the full spectrum of technological solutions avail­
able or in development for healthcare settings, potentially skewing the findings towards the
specificities of Noteless rather than generalizable insights about AI in healthcare.
In conducting this research, it is crucial to acknowledge the researcher’s positionality and
potential biases. As the thesis progresses from conceptualization to completion, the re­
searcher’s preconceptions about AI in healthcare and interactions with the participating GPs
could influence the interpretation of the findings. Reflecting on these influences is necessary
to maintain the integrity of the research process and ensure that conclusions are drawn from
the data rather than pre­existing biases. Given these limitations, the generalizability of the
study’s findings to all GPs in Norway or to other healthcare contexts is constrained. While
the insights provided offer valuable contributions to understanding the challenges and oppor­
tunities of AI integration in general practice, they should be considered preliminary, serving
as a foundation for more extensive investigations in the future.
To address these gaps and build on the current study’s contributions, future research should
aim to include a larger and more diverse sample of GPs, possibly incorporating a quantitative

provide a more comprehensive understanding of the technological landscape and its implica­
tions for clinical practice. Additionally, investigating the patient perspective on AI integration
and its impact on the patient­GP relationship would offer a more holistic view of the technol­
ogy’s implications for healthcare delivery. While this study provides important insights into
the integration of AI technologies like Noteless into general practice, its limitations under­
score the need for ongoing research in this field. By expanding the scope and scale of future
investigations, the healthcare community can better understand how to harness the poten­
tial of AI to improve patient care, enhance GP satisfaction, and navigate the complexities of
technological innovation in healthcare settings.

9.1
Recapitulation of Research Aims
The central aim of this qualitative study was to uncover the factors that impact the willingness
of GPs to implement AI technologies into their workday. With the rapid integration of AI
across healthcare sectors, this investigation focused on understanding the dynamic balance
between the anticipated benefits and the multifaceted challenges posed by AI adoption in
general practice. Utilizing the implementation of Noteless in a Bergen general practice as a
pivotal case study, the research sought to elucidate these interactions.
The rationale behind this exploration was driven by the recognition that, while AI promises
substantial enhancements in administrative efficiency within general practices, its real­world
implications for the clinicians who are at the forefront of healthcare delivery remain less un­
derstood. Specifically, the study aimed to examine how the introduction of AI technologies
impacts GPs in terms of workload, time management, and work­life balance, addressing a
notable gap in the current literature that has yet to fully grasp AI’s direct effects on GPs’ daily
routines.
Thus, the research was guided by the following questions:
• What are the key factors impacting GPs’ willingness to implement AI technologies into
their workday?
• How do the anticipated benefits of AI adoption in general practice compare with the
challenges it presents?
• In what ways do AI technologies influence GPs’ workload, time management, and over­
all work­life balance?
9.2
Assessment of Research Objectives
Reflecting on the research objectives and their fulfillment is key to assessing the study’s suc­
cess and laying the groundwork for future exploration. In this thesis, the primary objectives
were to understand the key factors influencing GPs willingness to adopt AI technologies in
their practice, the comparative benefits and challenges of such adoption, and the influence
of AI on workload, time management, and work­life balance.

standards, paving the way for more widespread and effective use of AI in healthcare.

9.4.1
What are the key factors influencing GPs’ willingness to integrate AI
technologies into their workday?
GPs’ willingness to adopt AI technologies like Noteless is multifaceted, influenced by prac­
tical, professional, and personal considerations. At the core, they seek technologies that
simplify and streamline tasks without compromising the quality of care, AI systems must be
user­friendly, reliable, and enhance efficiency to be appealing. Younger GPs tend to be more
receptive, likely due to greater digital fluency, while their senior counterparts may be cau­
tious, valuing proven methods over new, untested ones. Critical to adoption is the assurance
that AI will not add to the workload due to errors requiring correction, which could erode the
benefits of time saved. The influence of peers and the organizational culture towards tech­
nology significantly sways GPs’ willingness, with a trend toward more favorable views when
colleagues endorse the system. Training and ongoing support are vital, GPs are more in­
clined to embrace AI when they feel equipped to use it effectively, and the system aligns
with the existing workflow. Moreover, GPs consider the long­term sustainability and ethical
implications of AI, including data privacy, regulatory compliance, and the cost of implemen­
tation. They prefer AI tools that support, rather than disrupt, their professional autonomy and
work­life balance. These factors collectively shape GPs’ decisions, pointing to a need for AI
solutions that are as conscientious about the human element of healthcare as they are about
technological innovation.
9.4.2
How do the anticipated benefits of AI adoption in general practice
compare with the challenges it presents?
The integration of AI into general practice offers significant benefits, notably streamlining
clinical documentation, which can lead to enhanced patient care by freeing up time for GPs
to focus on their patients rather than paperwork. These improvements promise not only
to elevate the efficiency of healthcare services but also to potentially contribute to better
health outcomes through more personalized and attentive patient care. Alongside these
benefits, GPs face several challenges with AI adoption. Technical issues, such as errors
in AI­generated notes, pose risks to patient safety and can negate efficiency gains if GPs
must frequently correct mistakes. Seamless integration of AI tools into existing healthcare
workflows is also essential to avoid disruption and ensure that these systems truly augment
rather than complicate GPs’ work. Trust in the reliability of AI systems is paramount, as is
ensuring that they are intuitive enough to require minimal additional effort from already busy
healthcare providers.
Balancing the benefits and challenges of AI in general practice hinges on designing and
implementing AI solutions that are both technologically advanced and in tune with the real­
world needs of healthcare providers. Achieving this balance is crucial for AI to be a viable
and welcome innovation in the field of healthcare.
9.4.3
In what ways do AI technologies influence GPs’ workload, time
management, and overall work­life balance?
AI technologies hold the potential to revolutionize general practice by lessening workload and
enhancing time management through the automation of monotonous administrative work,
thereby freeing up GPs to focus on more critical aspects of patient care. For instance, one
GP reported reducing the time spent on administrative work from 1.5 hours to just 10 min­
utes daily, showcasing the significant efficiency gains possible with AI. However, the broader
application of AI in general practice underscores the need for these technologies to be finely
tuned to individual GP requirements, ensuring accuracy and seamless integration into exist­
ing systems. It is important to note that the platform researched was the first beta version,
and improvements in accuracy still have a lot of improvement potential.

depth over breadth, aiming to generate grounded theories and understandings from the data
collected through interviews with GPs regarding the implementation of Noteless.

from these interviews are valuable, they may not capture the full spectrum of experiences
across the broader GP population in Norway . Additionally, the selection process for these
