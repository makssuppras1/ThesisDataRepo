Abstract
This thesis explores the application of optimisation techniques to enhance the structural
design of transition pieces in jacket structures for a 20 MW next-generation offshore wind
turbine. Acting as a critical component in jacket structures, the transition piece needs to en-
sure a seamless load transfer between the tower and the substructure. The increasing size
of wind turbines, and the immense forces that this entails, are posing major challenges in
their structural design. This research leverages the power of parametric and topology op-
timisation techniques to optimise their design through ﬁnite element modelling. Key ﬁnd-
ings reveal that plated-strutted transition pieces offer superior performance with reduced
mass levels. Additionally, gathering inspiration from the topology-optimised designs, a
novel concept that enhances stress distribution and ensures material efﬁciency, the curved-
strutted transition piece is proposed. The outcomes demonstrate the remarkable potential
of optimisation techniques in structural design and their role in contributing towards a
sustainable future.
Keywords: Fatigue, Finite Element Analysis, Jacket Structures, Offshore Wind Turbines,
Parametric Optimisation, Topology Optimisation, Transition Piece.

Introduction
The offshore industry has been a ﬁeld of innovation over the last few decades. As the world
shifts towards sustainable energy solutions, there is a need for harnessing wind power
with more efﬁciency. To this end, research has been continuously conducted in pursuit of
lowering the so-called Levelized Cost of Energy (LCoE) in the foreseeable future. Yet, this
objective is not without challenges.
Before diving into the speciﬁcs of this research, it is essential to clarify some relevant terms
related to Offshore Wind Turbines (OWTs), as minor discrepancies in terminology exist
within the industry.
This work adopts the deﬁnitions established in [1].
The support
structure of an OWT comprises three main elements: the tower, the substructure, and the
foundations. The tower, generally a steel tubular element, supports the turbine and rests
upon the substructure, which extends from the tower’s base to the seabed. The substructure
then transfers the loads to the soil through the foundation piles. The term foundations,
however, is also frequently adopted in the industry to encompass both the substructure
and the foundation piles.
Diverse structural concepts have been developed for OWT substructures, each suited to
speciﬁc site conditions such as water depth, soil characteristics, and wind and wave load
magnitudes, among others.
The choice of the structure type is also inﬂuenced by the
turbine scale, which is related to its power rating. A number of the existing concepts, and
the main components of an OWT, are depicted in Figure 1.1, where their suitability with
increasing water depth is illustrated.

A common association exists between the site’s water depth and the suitability of the struc-
tural types. In the past, monopiles, seeing their simplicity and advantages in manufactur-
ing, have been the most popular option for water depths up to 30 meters. Yet, for larger
machines, and deeper sites, going from 30 up to 90 meters, the monopile solution became
less viable. Among the options, the Jacket Structure (JS) seemed to be a natural choice,
given not only the vast experience gained within the oil and gas industry but also due to
their structural versatility, lower weight, and higher transparency under wave loads.
The increasing size of turbines is leading to massive structures, even for shallow water
depths. Thus, the increase in foundation weight, which made monopiles infeasible for
deeper sites, is currently less critical and has pushed their feasibility for sites above 60

meters. An overlap between jackets and monopiles thereby exists in the applicability of
these types for a certain water depth range. The design decision mainly depends on the
soil conditions and current fabrication capabilities.
For depths exceeding 100 meters, where ﬁxed-bottom structures are impractical, ﬂoating
solutions are emerging. Far from being exhaustive, other existing solutions are also in-
cluded in Figure 1.1. Nevertheless, this work speciﬁcally focuses on Jacket Structures.
Acting as the connecting component at the interface between the tower and the substruc-
ture, the Transition Piece (TP) plays a major role in ensuring a seamless load transfer. The
immense forces that arise from external loads, mainly due to wind and waves and their in-
teraction, pose a challenge in obtaining designs that are within the current manufacturing,
transportation and assembling capabilities. In particular, ensuring the required fatigue life
due to the inherent cyclic nature of the loading is challenging, yet paramount.
Several TP design concepts can be found in existing Wind Farms (WFs). However, there
is no clear consensus in the industry on a leading design. This is presumably, amongst
other reasons, due to varying site conditions, where different concepts might prove to be
more optimal than others, the scale of the wind turbines, economic considerations, and
project-speciﬁc functionality requirements.
The leverage of structural optimisation techniques is becoming increasingly relied upon
to seek improvements in concepts and designs across the Architecture, Engineering and
Construction (AEC) industry. Although this has often appeared before at an academic
level, optimisation is also being embraced at a quicker pace in the industry. Yet, historically,
due to resource limitations, designs have been developed based on past experience, and
optimisation has been kept at the trial-and-error surface. It is becoming crucial to harness
the use of computational power towards the current goals of a green future.
The upsurge in size of next-generation Offshore Wind Turbines, herein distinguished mainly
in terms of their power rating surpassing 20 MW, leads to a substantial increase in the loads
acting upon the substructures. In particular, the torsional loads see a relative increase com-
pared to the total thrust and resulting bending moment at the interface. This poses major
challenges in design, especially concerning fatigue failure.
In pursuit of contributing to a sustainable future, this project aims to address these chal-
lenges by harnessing the power of structural optimisation techniques, in the ﬁeld of transi-
tion piece design for jacket structures, supporting next-generation offshore wind turbines.
In particular, although three and four-legged jackets coexist in the offshore wind indus-
try, considering the time constraints, the investigation focuses solely on the three-legged
conﬁguration.
This thesis starts with a review of the existing literature in Chapter 2, covering the evolution
of Offshore Wind Turbines, studies in the ﬁeld of Jacket Structures with focus on applied
structural optimisation, and a detailed and critical review of research on Transition Pieces.
In Chapter 3, the foundational assumptions and design requirements, as dictated by rel-
evant standards, are established. Following this, Chapter 4 conducts a preliminary eval-
uation of selected Transition Piece concepts. This involves assessing their structural be-
haviour, and identifying key advantages and disadvantages, using parametric optimisation
techniques for a comprehensive comparison.
Despite the clear advantages of parametric optimisation, its reliance on predeﬁned struc-
tures imposes inherent constraints that limit design ﬂexibility. Thus, Chapter 5 shifts the
focus to Topology Optimisation (TO) aiming at exploring beyond the constraints of its
parametric counterpart. Based on the ﬁndings obtained therein, new conceptual design

2
State-of-the-art review
During the last decade, numerous studies have been performed in the ﬁeld of the offshore
wind industry in a continuous pursuit of improving existing knowledge with cutting-edge
technologies and novel concepts. This state-of-the-art review intends to cover the current
research in this domain, shedding light on next-generation Offshore Wind Turbines, the
use of jacket structures as foundations in deeper sites, the complex design of transition
pieces, and the evolving application of structural optimisation in the ﬁeld.
2.1
Next-generation Offshore Wind Turbines
The recent trend towards increasing the power rating of Offshore Wind Turbines is mo-
tivated by the need for an improvement in the efﬁciency of energy production. Larger
turbines with higher power ratings can harness more wind energy, leading to increased en-
ergy output and, consequently, a reduction in the cost of energy produced. This, however,
comes at the cost of enlarged substructures, as the magnitude of external forces rapidly
rises. Consequently, various attempts have been carried out aiming at optimising both the
supporting structures and the turbines from a design and technological perspective.
Despite the aim of this project not being directly the investigation of the turbines, some of
the primary research projects are brieﬂy touched upon.
The evolution of the developed reference turbines has been conveniently summarised in [3].
Until recently, the well-known 5 MW reference wind turbine from the National Renewable
Energies Laboratories (NREL) has been widely used for research purposes [4]. This was
followed, some years later, by an increase in power rating with the development of the
DTU 10-MW RWT [5]. However, during the past years, the demand for turbines of even
higher powers resulted in further investigations.
In [6], seeing the lack of public information on large turbines, a ﬁrst 20 MW reference OWT
has been published. The novelty in this study was the application of a multi-disciplinary
optimisation approach attempting to minimise the Levelized Cost of Energy. A few years
later, in [7], a preliminary deﬁnition of another conceptual 20 MW OWT has been car-
ried out. Again, the objective targeted the LCoE using optimisation techniques, focusing
particularly on the optimisation of the rotor.
Further, not only in terms of sizing but also in terms of technological advancements, under
the framework of the IEA Wind Task 37, three new OWTs, at power rates of 3.4, 10 and 15
MW, respectively, were developed.

2.1.1
The UpWind and Innwind Projects
The UpWind project, a European initiative intending to develop advanced technologies for
large-scale wind turbines which took part from 2006 to 2011, made signiﬁcant contributions
to the sector. In [1], among other investigations, existing concepts of supporting structures
were assessed, and new ones were developed. In particular, a new design for a 3-legged JS
was carried out for a 5 MW turbine, and, foreseeing an upcoming growth in power rating,
a preliminary design for a 20 MW turbine was undertaken.
The absence of a readily available reference 20 MW OWT, resulted in an unrealistically
large and heavy structure, due to the application of classic up-scaling rules using the 5 MW
reference turbine. This reﬂected the signiﬁcance of both reﬁning the up-scaling theories,
and developing higher-power reference turbines for research purposes. Due to resource
limitations, the design of the Transition Piece was not conducted in this work.
More recently, from 2012 to 2017, identiﬁed as the ambitious successor of the UpWind
project, the Innwind project [9] took part. Its main objective was to take the current state-
of-the-art designs and technology to the next level, focusing on the potential that large
10-20 MW machines seemed to present, placing special focus on the LCoE.
Among the newly derived concepts, a 4-legged jacket structure for 10 and 20 MW turbines
and a water depth of 50 meters was developed in Work 4.36 [10]. As a 20 MW OWT was
not readily available, again, an upscaling methodology, this time from a 10 MW unit, was
adopted for load derivation. Thus, more reﬁnement in the new design was still expected
given the level of conservatism.
No details, however, are provided concerning the TP
design. It is only brieﬂy mentioned that a box-girder type with estimated dimensions was
selected. An indicative mass value of as low as 450 t is indicated.
2.2
Jacket Structures
Developers in the offshore wind industry are increasingly opting for turbines with higher
power ratings and selecting deeper WF sites with water depths far exceeding 30 meters,
which is leading to a shift in preferences to jacket structures. A background of the past
research on these structures, far from being comprehensive, is hereby presented, where
special attention is placed on studies that focused on the use of optimisation methods. The
section begins by covering the fundamentals of structural optimisation and its terminology.
2.2.1
Introduction to structural optimisation
In this section, the concepts of parametric and non-parametric optimisation are introduced.
Subsequently, the three most common types of the non-parametric category are described.
The different algorithms or techniques available for the resolution of optimisation prob-
lems, however, are not covered here. The reader is referred to the extensive literature on
the matter [11], [12].
The optimisation methods can be classiﬁed primarily in two categories: parametric, and
non-parametric. In the former, a model lies within the bounds of predetermined selected
parameters or variables. The aim is to fulﬁl an objective (or various objectives) by modify-
ing, in the framework of an algorithm, the parametric variables. For instance, the geometry
of a model can be deﬁned parametrically using software capabilities or scripting to then
undergo an optimisation technique for structural weight reduction.

Conversely, in non-parametric optimisation, the bounds mentioned above are released.
While still requiring an initial design domain, the ﬂexibility of these methods is signif-
icantly greater, as novel concepts can be obtained by permitting the algorithm to adapt
more freely within the domain, not restricted to particular geometric parameters, while
fulﬁlling the intended objectives. Some clarifying examples are provided later.
In [13], Bendsøe and Sigmund, two of the most renowned precursors of Topology Optimisa-
tion, describe the main three types of non-parametric optimisation. In Sizing Optimisation,
the optimal distribution of thicknesses across a plated structure, or the cross-sectional areas
of bars in trusses or frames, are obtained while pursuing a goal measured in terms of a
physical magnitude, such as stiffness, stresses, or deformations. Yet, the design domain is
ﬁxed and predeﬁned.
It can be argued that this could be also identiﬁed as a parametric optimisation type. In
principle, this might hold true, yet in the parametric case, the number of parameters that
can be handled is highly reduced.
In contrast, in Shape Optimisation, the nodes of the domain boundaries are modiﬁed. As
the name suggests, the optimum shape is found to achieve a particular objective. Generally,
shape optimisation is related to stress minimisation in areas of high-stress concentrations
achieved by conveniently modifying the free edges of the component.
In general, this
optimisation type is adopted at late stages of a design, after the concept has been deﬁned
and only minor modiﬁcations are allowed.
Lastly, the most general form receives the name of Topology Optimisation. In general
terms, this mathematical method aims to optimise the material layout, by simultaneously
ﬁnding the connectivity of the design domain and the shape of local features. The primary
objective of TO is to meet a speciﬁc goal, deﬁned by a performance metric while complying
with equilibrium, boundary conditions, and various design constraints.
A frequent aim in such problems is to maximise structural stiffness or, more commonly, to
minimise compliance. However, the objectives can vary widely depending on the speciﬁc
problem being addressed. Other examples of objectives include reducing mass or max-
imising structural frequencies. Each objective is selected based on the unique requirements
and goals of the project at hand. Applied examples of this could be ﬁnding the optimal
location of holes, or optimising the connectivity of a domain.

2.2.2
Optimisation methods applied on jacket structures
Optimisation methodologies have been applied in various academic studies with focus on
jacket substructures. Although this work does not centre primarily on jackets, this section
provides an overview of some of the recent examples on the matter.
An early contribution in the ﬁeld of jacket optimisation was made in [14].
The study
addressed the optimisation of a JS supporting the 5 MW reference NREL turbine using
a Sequential Optimisation and Reliability Assessment (SORA) technique. This consists of
not only pursuing an optimal design, in this case in terms of the structural weight but also
including a reliability assessment to increase the robustness of the design from a probability
perspective. A Micro-Genetic Algorithm (µGA) was used to reduce the computational time
of a conventional Genetic Algorithm (GA), where the objective function was the weight,
subject to stress constraints that included buckling capacity reduction effects.
In this work, an early important contribution was made. However, some key considerations
in jacket design were not considered within the scope of the study, such as frequency
requirements and fatigue checks. In Figure 2.3a, the targeted structure is shown, where
a curious strutted TP is depicted, with tubular-shaped struts and variable cross-sections
framing onto the tower extension, hereafter referred to as the main barrel.
A novel holistic optimisation model was introduced later in [15]. A more comprehensive
approach including the tower-substructure interaction was carried out. Seeing the overall
cost that substructures imply in OWTs, an integrated optimisation model was performed
for the NREL 5 MW turbine, in this case for monopiles. Importantly, fatigue and frequency
constraints were included, apart from stress, buckling, and deformation constraints. A fully
parametric FE model was combined with the application of a Genetic Algorithm technique
aiming at weight minimisation. A ﬁnal reduction of 20% was achieved relative to their
baseline case, where fatigue and frequency criteria proved to be driving the design. This
constituted another example of the use of a Genetic Algorithm in achieving cost reduction
indirectly by weight minimisation.
The NREL 5 MW reference OWT served as an application example again in [16]. In this
work, a meta-heuristic algorithm called Colliding Bodies Algorithm (CBO) was used to
minimise the weight of a JS, subjected to Ultimate Limit State (ULS) and frequency con-
straints. The study focused on a 4-legged JS without the inclusion of tower and TP inﬂu-
ence. The application of this technique yielded a reduction of 50% compared to the baseline
model and, although the study, admittedly by the authors, not being a full design of an
OWT, showed to be efﬁcient for the initial steps of concept design.
The application of optimisation procedures saw an upgrade when a 10 MW OWT was
addressed later in [17]. The DTU 10 MW OWT mentioned in Section 2.1 served as the
reference unit used for the optimisation of a 4-legged jacket at a water depth of 50 meters.

technique was adopted for dimensioning the jacket, while on the inner level, a gradient-
based Sequential-Quadratic Programming (SQP) technique was used for the sizing of the
elements. The minimisation of frequency was chosen as the objective, with further fre-

Comparison
In offshore wind farms, both 3 and 4-legged jackets can be seen coexisting due to their
distinct advantages and disadvantages, that make them more suitable for particular sce-
narios. Yet, as shown before, research has been mainly focusing on the latter, presumably
due to having been prioritised in the ﬁrst research studies, and continuing with these was
the natural way forward.
The two types can be compared on various fronts. From a structural perspective, it is
not difﬁcult to observe that, for the similar footprint dimensions, the 4-legged jackets are
stiffer, as they present a higher lever arm compared to the 3-legged case. In addition, the
structural redundancy of the latter is compromised due to the lack of a fourth stabilising
leg.
Reasonably, the resulting internal forces on the 4-legged jacket members tend to be smaller,
and so do the tube sizes upon dimensioning. Furthermore, smaller forces require reduced
foundation pile diameters. This holds major importance in some sites where hard soils, for
instance, rocks, are encountered, where driveability might be a challenge. Even if fewer
piles are needed for 3-legged jackets, their required length may be unfeasible.
The shorter members for 4-legged jackets, apart from leading to smaller sizes due to fewer
stability problems, are also less prone to dynamic excitations, which might be an issue for
3-legged ones. However, importantly, more members imply more manufacturing opera-
tions, and speciﬁcally, more number of welded joints. This is a major aspect in terms of
manufacturing costs. In contrast, 3-legged members and cans tend to be larger and heavier,
thereby increasing the difﬁculty in handling operations.
Lastly, the absence of right angles and longer distances to legs, lead to more ﬂexible, larger
and complex designs for the transition pieces.
Far from being exhaustive, since transportation, operation and maintenance, and secondary
steel features have not been touched upon, these are high-level comparison aspects to bear
in mind in jacket concept designs.

foundations, which are in the designer’s jurisdiction. These are highly complex elements

It can be argued that the problem shall not be deﬁned as a Multi-Objective Optimisation

Pareto Optimality is a key principle in MOOP, named after the economist Vilfredo Pareto.
It describes a state where improving one objective in a system results in the deterioration
of at least one other. This concept is vital in cases that require a balance between multiple
objectives, such as in structural design. Pareto Optimality helps identify solutions where
objectives are effectively balanced, ensuring the most efﬁcient allocation of resources given
the constraints.
A Pareto plot is a visual tool illustrating the trade-offs between various objectives. It dis-
plays potential solutions with each point representing a different compromise between
objectives. Those points lying on the so-called Pareto front are Pareto optimal. These plots
are crucial as they provide a clear visualisation of the comparative effectiveness of different
solutions, aiding the decision-making process. The Pareto fronts will be employed later for
each TP in their optimality assessment.

4.3.1
Box-girder TP
The BGTP, as mentioned in 2.3.1, is essentially transferring the loads through beam action.
A pair of forces develop in the ﬂanges to carry the bending moment, and diagonal principal
stresses are the result of shear transfer in the webs. Given the height-to-span ratio, the
beam action referred to does not correspond to the conventional beam action. In contrast,
the structure represents a so-called D-region, where Bernoulli-Euler theory is not valid.
In the model, extended top and bottom base plates can be observed. This is done aiming to
provide membrane action and torsional stiffness to the structure. A representation of the
FE model can be observed in Figure 4.7.

maximisation of stiffness), considering an upper bound in the total volume of material.

5.1.2
Remarks on Tosca Structure
The optimisation is carried out using Tosca Structure, a structural optimisation package
from SIMULIA. In addition, the FE model is built using Abaqus software. Some relevant
features are brieﬂy described below.
Solvers
Two solvers are available for performing Topology Optimisation in Tosca Structure: a
controller-based algorithm based on one variant of the Optimality Criteria approach, and
a general sensitivity-based algorithm.
The controller-based algorithm begins with a full design domain (maximum stiffness) and
removes material until the pre-deﬁned volume constraint is achieved. It is highly stable,
requires a low number of iterations (usually 15 is sufﬁcient), and yields designs with either
solids (relative density close to one) or voids (relative density close to 0). Nevertheless,
the algorithm is limited to solving compliance minimisation problems with only volume
equality constraints.
The sensitivity-based algorithm is general, as it allows several design responses to be used
as objective functions and constraints. In contrast to the previous one, it initialises on a
domain with a scaled relative density (0.50 by default) and re-allocates material along the
process until the convergence criteria are fulﬁlled, both in terms of relative densities and
the objective function. However, it requires more iterations and the resulting design can
yield more grey elements with intermediate densities due to ﬁltering.
Filtering
To avoid checkerboard patterns in the resulting structures, mesh-independent ﬁltering tech-
niques are implemented in the programme. The user can select among density or sensitiv-
ity ﬁltering, as well as deﬁne the ﬁltering radius. The default ﬁltering radius, deﬁned as
1.3 times the average size of the ﬁnite elements across the model, proved to be successful
in many applications but can be modiﬁed by the user.
The selection of the ﬁltering radius has an impact on the outcomes. For instance, the larger
the ﬁlter size, the larger the minimum feature that can be obtained. Thus, thin features are

Interpolation schemes
Different interpolation schemes are available, being SIMP one of the most popular, specif-

Case studies
In this section, the designs obtained from Topology Optimisation are presented and dis-
cussed. First, given that it is always desirable to aim for the stiffest possible structure,
or, in other words, achieving a structure that ﬁnds the most efﬁcient load paths to resist

5.2.1
General remarks
Manufacturing constraints of symmetry are applied in all the optimisation tasks. The de-
sign variables are constrained to be symmetrical with respect to three vertical planes rotated
at 120° (see Figure 5.1). Note that due to the non-symmetric load cases, the full numer-
ical model is analysed within the FE solver and these correspond only to manufacturing
restrictions within the optimisation procedure.
In addition, in all the analyses that include stresses as design responses, the ﬁnite elements
adjacent to nodes that either receive point loads from the kinematic coupling or are sup-
ported, are excluded from the stress design responses, given that they might be part of
singularity regions.
Lastly, a freeze restriction is applied to the edges where both boundary conditions and
kinematic coupling are applied, ensuring these critical regions are not modiﬁed along the
optimisation process.

A fairly simpliﬁed approach has been adopted for the assessment of structural ﬂexibility.
However, different levels of reﬁnement can be further pursued. A ﬁrst simpliﬁed possibility
is to deﬁne linear translational and rotational springs at the centre of circular edges at the
bottom of the design domain using suitable stiffness values.
A second, more complex alternative, is to condense the effect of the jacket into a so-called
super-element, also referred to as a substructure. In short, a super-element consists of an
equivalent representation of a system that accurately condenses its properties into a single,
simpliﬁed element. Thereby, based on more comprehensive FE models of a structure, a
super-element can be conveniently linked to the model at hand through the corresponding
DoFs, to represent the global behaviour of the structure without loss of accuracy. A detailed
description of the use of super-elements can be found in [48].
Lastly, the overall jacket per se could be modelled using appropriate ﬁnite elements in the
topology optimisation problem, which would, however, increase the computational time.
The aforementioned analyses are not without complexity. The stiffness of jackets depends
on several factors and can vary signiﬁcantly across sites, due to loading conditions, sea-
bed depth, among others. Due to time limitations, these more reﬁned analyses are left for
further work on the matter.
5.3
Discussion
Topology optimisation allows to obtain helpful insight to conceive proper starting points
for conceptual designs. The minimum volume fraction used in this work was 10%, which
is a seemingly low value. However, the corresponding mass climbs to a value of around
2260 t, which is nearly four times an expected reasonable mass for a jacket transition piece
for a 20 MW OWT. Attempting to lower volume fractions would demand even ﬁner dis-
cretisations. In solid models, due to their volumetric nature, the number of degrees of
freedom increases proportionally with the third power of the number of nodes. Hence, the
computation time would drastically increase.
For the aforementioned reasons, and observing the complexity of achieving plated struc-
tures with Topology Optimisation, the analysis performed above serves only as a basis of
inspiration for improving existing concepts, or, in turn, conﬁrming their advantages.

The analyses performed above have shown the relevance of exploring the individual ef-
fects of force components on the resulting topologies and related quantities. As in many
engineering tasks, this is a recommended design practice. Not only is it important to in-
vestigate the separate effects of external loads, but also to construct the models gradually,
from a lower to a higher complexity.
As showcased in the different study cases, the most efﬁcient structural topologies seem to
resemble that of braced-type Transition Pieces. In general, the load is directly transferred
through braces or struts to the supporting legs. In none of the cases do the topologies prove
that box-girders transition pieces are optimal in terms of load paths. This is reasonable and
intuitive, as the most efﬁcient structures attempt to transfer loads through pure tension or
compression, rather than bending, aiming at minimising their strain energy. This conclu-
sion therefore favours the strutted transition pieces against their box-girder counterparts.
Interestingly, it has been observed that the component referred to as main barrel might
not be strictly necessary, as long as the supports are sufﬁciently stiff. Although this might
be beneﬁcial, given that, as shown in section 4, the main barrel constitutes one of the
components with the highest mass, achieving stiff connections without the barrel to transfer
the high localised bending moments at the top of the transition piece might be complex
and expensive, particularly in terms of fatigue. Besides, in these scenarios without bracing
components, a signiﬁcant horizontal thrust is transferred to the jacket legs, which might
prove detrimental to their structural performance.
This "barrel-free" effect has not been observed in the reference literature on the topic [35]
[36], as the barrels have been deﬁned therein as frozen regions, thereby not being part of
the optimisation tasks.
Furthermore, in all the study cases it is evident that the three bracing elements, transfer-
ring the loads from TP-top to the supporting legs, intend to cover the entire upper surface,
which leads to an enhanced stress distribution. The observed curvature additionally in-
creases their out-of-plane stiffness.
The inﬂuence of boundary conditions on the optimisation problem has been preliminary
addressed.
It has been observed that the ﬂexibility of the jacket structure impacts the
optimisation outcomes. While most of the primary conclusions previously drawn remain
valid, it would be beneﬁcial to extend the investigation. This extended analysis should
focus on examining the effects of realistic jacket structures using the more detailed methods
discussed earlier.

The last design proposal described above is adopted for further analysis. Similarly to the
approach in Chapter 4, a fully parametric FE model using shell elements is built in Abaqus

6.3.1
Description and methodology
The challenges in efﬁciently converting the results of topology optimisation in solids into
a plated-like structure have already been emphasised. This transformation has been per-
formed in this work through engineering judgment, guided by both the outcomes of topol-
ogy optimisation, and the existing concepts and common practices within the steel and
offshore wind industry. Yet, the obtention of the most optimal structure is far from being
ensured. At the current stage, the author identiﬁes potential paths that could be followed
in an attempt to further enhance the proposed design concept.
On the one hand, again, the application of topology optimisation, in this case for shell
elements, could be pursued. For instance, further vertical stiffeners can be added to the
design domain, which may allow the solver to provide efﬁcient designs by selecting the best
load paths through stiffness maximisation, including volume and stress constraints. The
drawback of this method is that the thicknesses of the plates need to be deﬁned upfront,
which would already be constraining the outcome signiﬁcantly, as will become clear later.
An alternative might be the application of sizing optimisation to the plated structure. This
consists basically in allowing the thicknesses of the plates to vary within a predeﬁned
range, and run a non-parametric optimisation procedure. Similarly to the previous case,
the downside lies in that the results are overly constrained, as the location, number, and
even existence of stiffeners, and all the relevant geometric features, are deﬁned in advance.
For the aforementioned reasons, although the methods described above might prove useful
if sufﬁcient iterations on different geometries were performed, an alternative methodology
is selected here.
Firstly, preliminary heuristic simulations are performed for different geometries, by mod-
ifying thicknesses and geometric features of the structure. This allows a proper under-
standing of the structural behaviour and the effects that certain parameters have on the
outcomes. In these simulations, the utilisation ratios in ULS and the design fatigue damage
in FLS are adopted as measurements of performance, following the requirements described
in Section 3.3.1 and Section 3.3.2, respectively.
The potential improvements derived from the insight gained in the preliminary iterations
are applied in modiﬁed revisions of the original design proposal and the parametric mod-
els are adapted accordingly. Lastly, parametric optimisation is conducted to obtain the
most optimal set of geometric parameters for speciﬁc objectives, as will be detailed later in
Section 6.4. A scheme of the workﬂow is shown in Figure 6.8.

Ultimate Limit State (ULS)
As explained in the basis of design, yielding is deﬁned as the failure criterion. However,
given the simplicity of the FE model using shell elements, singularities in sharp corners
and intersections are inevitable. Therefore, the analysis is generally conservative and local
plasticity shall in principle be allowed in design. In addition, geometric non-linearities
are not considered. The failure check of buckling and the effects of imperfections are not
considered here and need further assessment within a detailed design stage.
Fatigue Limit State (FLS)
The fatigue checks performed here constitute a simpliﬁed approach for preliminary assess-
ment, which is presumed to be conservative. For instance, the damage equivalent loads
provided by Wood Thilsted correspond to maximum envelopes which, in principle, do not
necessarily occur simultaneously.
Furthermore, the ﬁnite element models are also simpliﬁed, as, for instance, the plates are
represented by shells rather than solids, which tend to increase the stresses at the hot
spots, and welds are not explicitly included in the model. Besides, as will be described
later, stresses used for damage calculation are extracted from the numerical model directly.
They are not extrapolated properly using the hot-spot extrapolation method recommended
by DNV standards due to the complexity associated with including this in the parametric
model.
Nevertheless, the method adopted here, though simpliﬁed and conservative, is deemed
adequate for optimisation purposes. The enhancements achieved in subsequent designs
are case-wise relative, as they all fall within the same framework.

Loads and boundary conditions
The loads for ULS and FLS assessment have been indicated in Section 3.1.2. The same four
load cases deﬁned in previous sections are adopted here. Moreover, the loads are again
applied through a kinematic coupling to the edges of a segment of the tower, which is
connected to the transition piece using tie constraints.
The boundary conditions are deﬁned slightly differently than in Section 4.2.2. Rather than
ﬁxing the displacements of the boundary edges per se, these are connected through a
kinematic coupling to a reference point deﬁned at the centre of the legs. The displacements
(Ux, Uy, Uz) and rotations (Rx, Ry, Rz) of the reference point are ﬁxed. The reason for this
modiﬁcation is to obtain reactions at only one node, avoiding the necessary post-processing
of the nodal reactions. The boundary conditions are illustrated in Figure 6.11a.
Meshing
Fatigue is highly sensitive to stresses. It is therefore recommended to use higher-order
elements to capture steep stress gradients. A global mesh size of 100 mm and an element
type S8R (8-node doubly curved thick shell with reduced integration) is selected.
The
resulting number of ﬁnite elements climbs to around 2 × 106. The high computational
capabilities of the Sophia HPC Cluster [49] that the author has at the time of writing
decreases the computing time considerably. The mesh is illustrated in Figure 6.11b.

analysis, no veriﬁcations in terms of energy are carried out.

Additionally, one of the issues highlighted before as a drawback of the use of parametric
optimisation in linear models is the presence of singularities in certain local regions of
the models. A potential strategy to address this issue is to exclude the elements or areas
prone to singularities, by creating predeﬁned sets or groups within the parametric model.
However, this approach adds considerable complexity to the geometry deﬁnition and has
not been implemented in this case.
An alternative, although not entirely accurate, solution to mitigate this issue is to extract
the stresses at the centroids of the ﬁnite elements. Although the effect of singularities does
not disappear, the stress values at the centroids are lower thereby smoothing their effect.
This approach is considered sufﬁcient for the analysis, as it successfully demonstrates con-
siderable enhancements throughout the iterations within the deﬁned design space, as will
be presented next. However, for future works in the matter, it is recommended to develop
quantitative regularisation methods. These shall be mesh and singularity-independent, and
enable a more appropriate quantiﬁcation of the design damage. This is particularly rele-
vant in scenarios where higher damages in localised regions might be preferable to lower
damages over extended areas, an issue not addressed by the current methodology, given
that only the magnitude of the damage at an element level is considered for the evaluation.

Effects of torsional loads
The progressive increase in offshore wind turbine sizes has already been highlighted to
be leading to higher torsional loads acting upon transition pieces.
Therefore, it seems
reasonable to assess this effect in terms of Fatigue Limit State stresses, and conduct a
comparison with the effect of bending and shear loads.
The analysis begins by illustrating the load path for the torsional load, represented through
the principal stresses in a vector form. Following this, a comparative assessment of the
principal stress magnitudes is conducted, focusing on the differences between the torsional
moment T, and the joint action between bending moment M and shear load V.
The principal stresses arising from the individual action of the torsional moment TFLS
are presented in Figure 6.24, illustrating the two torsion-resisting mechanisms previously
qualitatively shown in Figure 6.7. It can be conﬁrmed that the load is transferred from the
tower to the legs, primarily via the membrane action in the bottom plate, and the forces
in the braced vertical stiffeners. The efﬁciency of the vertical stiffeners in transmitting this
load has been enhanced due to their positioning, close to being tangent to the main barrel.
To evaluate the relative effect of torsion and bending-shear loads, the principal stresses for
FLS are visualised in Figure 6.25. Contrary to the initial expectation of torsion having a
higher impact on the principal stresses, the opposite is observed. The stress values are
shown to be considerably higher in the case of bending and shear force. In addition, the
peak stress in the case of torsion is conﬁned to a localised region at the tip of the base plate
stiffener, which leads to an even more pronounced difference.
In summary, the current design proposal appears to be efﬁcient in the transfer of torsional
loads, in view of the fairly low stresses resulting from the two load-bearing mechanisms.

Final considerations
The preliminary screening of existing concepts of transition pieces presented in Chapter 4
only touched upon the ULS veriﬁcations using relatively simpliﬁed numerical models, for
the sake of simplicity. Drawing a conclusive comparison with the newly derived design
might therefore not be entirely appropriate. It is nevertheless worth considering whether
the Plated-strutted TP (PSTP), as evaluated therein, and with further modiﬁcations, could
potentially match the performance of the proposed CSTP.
The load path shown for the loads acting at 0 degrees for the PSTP has been depicted
through the principal stresses vector plot in Figure 4.32d. It is readily observable that a
considerable portion of the load is transmitted from the tower bottom through the ﬂat
plate towards its edges. In contrast, a lower density of principal stresses is seen at the
central part of the plate. This pattern explains the extension of the yield surface described
previously, and illustrated in Figure 4.32c.
The ﬁndings observed for the Curved-strutted TP suggest that similar modiﬁcations could
enhance the structural performance of the ﬂat plate design. These modiﬁcations include re-
ducing the thickness of the ﬂat plate, positioning the vertical stiffeners close to the tangents
of the main barrel, and adding stiffener extensions on top of the plate. Essentially, this
would represent a speciﬁc variant of the CSTP with zero curvature. The reduced curvature
softens the effect of the plate, particularly notable at the highly concave intersection with
the main barrel.

7
Conclusions and final remarks
The growing development of the offshore wind industry, driven by the goal of a sustain-
able future, is leading to the development of larger turbines. These larger units introduce
signiﬁcant forces that are posing additional design challenges for engineers. The purpose
of this thesis was to harness the still-underused optimisation techniques to enhance the
design of Transition Pieces in three-legged Jacket Structures for Next-generation Offshore
Wind Turbines.
The investigation commenced in Chapter 4 with a preliminary high-level assessment of
selected existing TPs from the industry. A comparison across the concepts has been con-
ducted based on their mass and the Utilisation Ratio for yielding failure at Ultimate Limit
States as design responses. Due to the high stiffness of the TPs relative to the substructure
and soil, stiffness was not considered a primary criterion for optimality.
An initial design space exploration using parametric FE models focused on understanding
the impact of geometry parameters on design. The identiﬁcation of the most inﬂuential
parameters was aided by Pareto and main effect charts. Given that linear regression with
full quadratic polynomials quantiﬁed these effects, caution is advised in their interpretation
due to potential accuracy limitations when dealing with many parameters.
For a fair comparison of the concepts, parametric optimisation has been performed to
minimise the weighted sum of the mass and the Utilisation Ratio. The outcomes, presented
in the form of Pareto fronts, revealed the plated-strutted TP type as the most efﬁcient
design, showing a superior structural performance at lower mass levels compared to other
concepts. This already suggested that the conﬁguration of structural components in these
structures is more optimal as it ensures a more efﬁcient stress distribution.
The predeﬁned deﬁnition of geometry conﬁgurations imposed the inherent constraints that
are characteristic of parametric optimisation. To overcome these limitations, in Chapter 5,
the focus of the study shifted to Topology Optimisation, the most general form of non-
parametric optimisation, allowing a wider exploration of design possibilities.
Compliance minimisation with volume constraints was applied gradually as a ﬁrst ap-
proach, investigating the effects of the load components, including their independent and
joint action, along with their loading direction. The signiﬁcance of bending in the ﬁnal
quantities compared to the other load components was showcased.
The optimisation results predominantly favoured brace-type topologies, where extended
braces, resembling plated structures, efﬁciently covered large areas. This ﬁnding aligns
with the preliminary assessment and indicates an efﬁcient stress distribution. Additionally,
the formation of Λ−like frames along the tower tangents in the optimised designs emerged
as a signiﬁcant insight, suggesting an efﬁcient material distribution. This is considered a
key ﬁnding of this work.
Secondly, other optimisation problem formulations were explored. Speciﬁcally, mass min-
imisation with compliance and stress constraints, and compliance minimisation with vol-
ume and stress constraints were performed, and comparisons were made. The volume
fractions of 10% and 20% used in the optimisation problems led to unacceptably high
masses for TPs. Therefore, for the resulting thicknesses in the solid designs, low levels of
stresses, and thereby inactive stress constraints, were always encountered.

The study also assessed the impact of boundary conditions, using simpliﬁed models of
the tower and jacket substructure. This analysis underlined the signiﬁcant inﬂuence of
their ﬂexibility on the outcomes, particularly highlighting a need for a tower extension and
additional bottom bracing for more ﬂexible substructures.
The optimised topologies served as an inspiration for the development of new TP design
proposals in Chapter 6, as the plated-like nature of these structures limits the direct use
of the resulting solid designs. Two design proposals are presented as novel concepts. The
ﬁrst proposal, "a barrel-free" concept, dispenses with the main barrel aiming at minimising
the total mass. However, deviating considerably from the current practice in the industry,
it has been solely presented as a conceptual idea.
The second design, a variant of the Plated-strutted TP existing concept, received the name
of Curved-strutted TP. Based on the ﬁndings from Topology Optimisation, a curved strut
or brace replaced the ﬂat strut of the existing design, aiming at improving the load path
and the stress distribution. This concept has been carried further to advance the design
from mere conceptualisation to a more detailed analysis.
A preliminary evaluation of the CSTP’s performance is conducted heuristically with FE
simulations. At this stage, the assessment of Fatigue Limit State has also been included
adopting the simpliﬁed Damage Equivalent Loads approach. The initial proposal evolved
into a reﬁned version using engineering judgement, yet based on the ﬁndings from Topol-
ogy Optimisation, with the goal of improving the fatigue damage that was conﬁrmed to
be design-governing. Upon deﬁnition of a reﬁned proposal, the design is assisted again
by parametric optimisation proving to be paramount in further achieving mass and fatigue
damage reduction.
One major issue with next-generation turbines is claimed to be the comparatively higher
increase in torsion fatigue. The FE analysis of one of the optimal TP designs from paramet-
ric optimisation has been performed, and a comparison between torsion and bending cases
showed considerably higher stresses in the latter. This validated the design’s adequate
torsional resistance and strength.
The analysis concludes by including recommendations for a future more adequate compar-
ison between the PSTP, the optimal concept arising from the preliminary assessment, and
the newly reﬁned design of the Curved-strutted TP.
Although the focus of the study was placed on three-legged jacket structures, the quali-
tative ﬁndings from Topology Optimisation, which served as a basis for the subsequent
analyses, appear to be sensibly extrapolated to the four-legged case. In particular, the in-
corporation of plates as tangentially to the tower as the welding requirements allow, and
their bracing using reasonably slender curved struts for improved stress distribution, are
recommended for the four-legged conﬁguration.
The ﬁndings acquired in this work demonstrated the potential that optimisation techniques
have on structural design. The inspiration gathered from Topology Optimisation, the ability
of parametric optimisation to handle more parameters than an engineer by nature, and their
combined beneﬁts, are undeniably crucial towards achieving optimal designs. On many
occasions, Clients are known for selecting the designers with the lowest fees. Yet, rarely
does this conclude with the lowest costs and footprint in the environment. It is the author’s
hope that this work contributes to proving otherwise, showing that design optimisation can
lead to both lower costs and a reduced environmental footprint over the long term.

9
Future work
In concluding this thesis, while the primary objectives have been successfully achieved,
the limited time frame available demanded certain assumptions and limitations that have
also been critically explained. The ﬁndings also raise new questions that require deeper
examination.
It is with the intent to provide a more solid foundation for the ongoing
research in both the academic and industry ﬁelds that these suggestions for future work
are put forward.
In Chapter 4, the yielding criterion was evaluated using the maximum Von Mises stress
across the structural components, yet the extent of the yielded area was not captured.
Likewise, in Chapter 6, the maximum value of the fatigue damage was evaluated without
capturing the extent of the high-damaged regions. A modiﬁed measurement of perfor-
mance for both failure criteria might be implemented to achieve improved comparisons.
For instance, the capabilities offered by Abaqus scripting may be harnessed to introduce
a measurement that accounts for both the magnitude of the failure quantity and its area
extent through a weighted average.
Moreover, in some of the studies covered in the literature review in Chapter 2, the impor-
tance of introducing costs in the objective functions was highlighted. Commonly, manu-
facturers do not disclose cost details publicly, hindering designers from including them in
the design process. Nevertheless, accounting for the costs of the design in the objective
function is deemed valuable. The integration of various cost-related parameters such as
mass, number and type of welds, and their lengths, amongst others, is suggested to en-
hance design optimisation. This is however complex, and likely not within the budget and
timelines available for designers.
The results obtained through Topology Optimisation proved beneﬁcial to obtain optimal
designs. However, two improvements could be included in further studies. First, lower
volume fractions, and thereby ﬁner discretisations, are required to obtain ﬁner features
resembling plated structures. This was not conducted due to the computational time de-
mand. Secondly, the substructure ﬂexibility proved to have a signiﬁcant impact on the
topology-optimised designs. Therefore, it is suggested that the jacket be integrated into
the FE model in the form of a super-element, leveraging the substructuring capabilities of
Abaqus. Alternatively, the jacket may be directly modelled using a global frame model, yet
including a detailed transition at the connection through solid models.
The main barrel has been identiﬁed in Chapter 4 as a candidate for further optimisation,
given the high amount of mass concentrated in it. In particular, Sizing Optimisation seems
to be an adequate option. The optimisation may be approached at two levels. First, free
Sizing Optimisation can give an overview of the thickness requirements across the bar-
rel. Then, the problem can be reﬁned while including manufacturing constraints, such as
clustering (areas of equal thickness), thereby limiting the weight of manageable cans, and
minimum thickness limitations, ensuring reasonable thickness values.
The use of parametric optimisation techniques on FE models is computationally demand-
ing. To obtain speed-up in the computation of optimisation processes, surrogate models,
also known as approximations, can be employed to approximate the FE models. Tech-
niques such as Response Surface Models (RSM) and Radial Basis Functions (RBF), or even
more complex machine learning methods, might be employed. However, the development
of reliable surrogate models is not without its complexities, and the trade-offs between
accuracy and computational efﬁciency require careful evaluation.
Optimisation of transition pieces in jacket structures for next-generation offshore wind turbines.

==PAGE:114==
In addition, the Pointer-2 technique in Isight has been used in this work given its versatility
for various optimisation problems. However, a case has been exempliﬁed where, for a set
of more than 10 parameters within a discrete domain, the technique presented a poor
performance. Although this cannot be stated as a general rule, speciﬁc methods are likely
to have an increased performance in the problem at hand. Therefore, a more in-depth
study of the available optimisation techniques is suggested.
The reﬁned design proposal in Chapter 6 seems to have a minimum possible amount of
structural components to handle fatigue damage. Notably, it eliminates any need for inter-
nal stiffeners within the main barrel. Nonetheless, as reiterated throughout the work, this
assessment was kept at a high level, as performing the full detailed design of a TP is com-
plex. Therefore, full detailed engineering of the TP needs to be conducted. This includes
the addition of geometric and material non-linearities at ULS to capture potential buckling
failure, the assessment of imperfections and manufacturing tolerances, and the inclusion
of the effects of secondary steel elements.
Furthermore, a more in-depth analysis of fatigue details shall be performed. On the one
hand, proper stress extrapolation methods deﬁned in standards (hot-spot method) are to
be used, particularly for the details with higher damages. On the other hand, the fatigue
veriﬁcation shall be carried out in the time domain, rather than using the conservative
yet simpliﬁed DEL method. This would allow to capture the correlation of load-induced
stresses for the various load components (bending, shear, and torsion).
The simpliﬁcations and assumptions adopted in this work, however, are deemed reasonable
for its high-level scope.
As indicated in several studies from the literature and hereby
conﬁrmed, optimisation techniques are found paramount at the conceptual stage, where
simpliﬁcations are adopted, key high-level design decisions are made, and thereby where
their impact can be signiﬁcant.

In Section 5.2.3, a mass minimisation problem has been performed with stress constraint,

ent goals. If mass reduction was the main optimisation objective, this might be a proper
approach.
In Figure D.3, the same topology as in case B1 is encountered, as expected. Again, the
material concentration in certain parts of the bracing component are evident. It appears,
however, that near the supporting legs, the material is not properly connected. On one
hand, this might be an indication that a ﬁner mesh is needed, given that the volume frac-
tions are low. However, it also shows that more material is located to the inner part of the
legs, which reﬂects that either the design domain might beneﬁt from a higher height, or
that the legs separation might be reduced.
Lastly, it is again observed that for the ULS loads applied the stress constraints are not
active along the optimisation process, even for a low volume fraction of around 6%.
