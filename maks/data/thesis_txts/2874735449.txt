Abstract
Humanoid robots are increasingly considered for use in industrial, healthcare, and domesôÙ
tic environments, where safe and fluent humanôÙrobot collaboration is essential. Existing
safety standards, such as ISO 15066, primarily target fixedôÙbase manipulators and do
not capture the wholeôÙbody motion and dynamic interactions of humanoids. To address
this gap, this thesis proposes a dynamic danger zone framework that defines physically
verifiable safety boundaries at the level of individual joints and aggregates them into bodyôÙ
part and wholeôÙbody danger zones. The method integrates stop trajectory envelopes and
forceôÙcone constraints with simplified humanoid dynamics, enabling adaptive and taskôÙ
sensitive spatial partitioning of protective zones.
The framework is implemented in MuJoCo simulations using a simplified Kondo KHRôÙ
3HV humanoid model.
Three representative geometric strategies are developed and
evaluated, namely spherical, ellipsoidal, and envelopeôÙbased boundaries, across multiple
scenarios including baseline walking, humanôÙrobot crossing, and speed variation. VisuôÙ
alization and quantitative analysis demonstrate that all three models ensure safe separaôÙ
tion, with spherical and ellipsoidal zones offering computational efficiency, while envelope
zones provide the most accurate and stable representation under highôÙspeed locomotion.
And these models are further organized into a layered structure, where spherical zones
are applied in the far field, ellipsoidal zones in the mid field, and envelope zones in the
near field. This hierarchical formulation enables adaptive switching of geometric comôÙ
plexity with proximity, balancing computational efficiency and accuracy in real time.
The results confirm the feasibility of jointôÙlevel DDZs as a foundation for humanoid robot
safety. This thesis makes three main contributions: introduction of a dynamic danger zone
framework tailored to humanoid robots, systematic comparison of geometric modeling
strategies for safety boundaries, and validation of the framework in simulation, offering
insights for integration with realôÙtime humanoid control loops. Future work will extend this
approach toward taskôÙbased decision making and experimental validation on physical
humanoid platforms.

1.1
Research Background
HumanôÙrobot collaboration (HRC) has emerged as a crucial paradigm in the transition
towards more intelligent, adaptive, and humanôÙcentered automation. As industries, serôÙ
vice sectors, and even domestic environments increasingly integrate robotic systems, the
ability for humans and robots to coexist and cooperate safely and effectively has become
a defining challenge for future technologies [1]. Traditional robots, often confined to strucôÙ
tured industrial settings, have demonstrated significant productivity gains, but their limited
adaptability restricts their role in environments designed primarily for human presence.
In this context, humanoid robots are gaining growing attention as a potential solution.
With their anthropomorphic morphology and humanôÙlike kinematic structures, humanoids
are inherently more suited to operate in spaces originally designed for people, from inôÙ
dustrial shop floors to healthcare and household scenarios [2]. Their ability to replicate
human motion patterns and adapt to diverse, unstructured environments enables more
natural interaction with human partners, making them particularly promising for tasks that
require close physical collaboration. According to Grand View Research [3], the global
humanoid robot market was valued at USD 1.55 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach
USD 4.04 billion by 2030, with a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 17.5% beôÙ
tween 2025 and 2030. This strong growth reflects not only technological advances but
also the expanding demand for humanoid systems across manufacturing, logistics, eduôÙ
cation, healthcare, and domestic assistance. However, this rapid growth also amplifies
the urgency of addressing unresolved challenges, particularly in ensuring reliable safety
standards and effective humanôÙrobot coexistence in shared environments.
Despite this accelerating progress, significant challenges remain that limit the widespread
adoption of humanoid robots in practice. In recent years, both academia and industry
have made notable advances in humanoid platforms, ranging from improved actuation
and perception to the integration of advanced artificial intelligence for decisionôÙmaking
and interaction. Companies such as Boston Dynamics, Tesla, and UBTECH have inôÙ
troduced increasingly complex humanoid prototypes, while research laboratories have
demonstrated robots capable of locomotion, manipulation, and even complex social inôÙ
teractions[4]. Representative examples of these industrial platforms, together with the
iCub humanoid model widely used in academic research, are shown in Fig. 1.1.But, this
rapid technological development has outpaced the establishment of standardized safety
frameworks, creating a critical barrier for realôÙworld deployment. Existing international
standards, including ISO 10218ôÙ2 for industrial robots[5] and ISO 15066 for collaborative
robots[6], primarily address robotic manipulators in controlled industrial settings.

manoid HRC[12]. By integrating kinematicôÙdynamic models with compliant control strateôÙ

the adaptability necessary for humanoid robot deployment.

Overview of Prior Work
A detailed discussion of prior work is provided in the Literature Review section, the followôÙ

As outlined in the previous section, the concept of dynamic danger zones has emerged to
overcome the limitations of static boundaries in humanôÙrobot collaboration. Static zones
provide protection but often impose overly conservative constraints that hinder efficiency,
whereas dynamic zones adapt boundaries in real time to robot motion and human interôÙ
action.
Current research on dynamic protective zones can broadly be divided into three cateôÙ
gories, based on a hierarchical progression from information acquisition to information
inference and finally to information constraint: perceptionôÙbased, predictionôÙbased, and
controlôÙbased approaches. PerceptionôÙbased methods rely on sensors such as RGBôÙ
D cameras, LiDAR, or wearable devices to track human position and motion [9]. While
these methods are accurate in structured environments, they are susceptible to occlusion,
lighting changes, and sensor noise, which undermines reliability in complex workplaces.
PredictionôÙbased methods attempt to anticipate human trajectories and intentions, adôÙ
justing safety zones proactively [13]. These approaches improve responsiveness but are
computationally demanding and tend to lose accuracy in multiôÙhuman or highly uncertain
scenarios. ControlôÙbased methods embed safety constraints directly into motion planning
and compliant control strategies, enabling robots to adjust trajectories dynamically [14].
Recent extensions include dynamicsôÙbased scaling methods, which calculate safety marôÙ
gins from robot velocity, acceleration, and physical constraints, dynamically shrinking or
expanding zones during execution [15, 16]. While these works are often framed as dyôÙ
namic safety zones, their underlying principles are also relevant to the stricter notion of
dynamic danger zones, where physically verifiable guarantees are required. These have
shown promising results for collaborative manipulators but have primarily been validated
on fixedôÙbase robot arms.
Beyond methodological differences, another key evolution concerns the spatial dimenôÙ
sionality of danger zones. TwoôÙdimensional (2D) approaches represent the starting point.
Early work introduced the notion of a danger field, formalizing robot kinematic and dynamic
properties into a twoôÙdimensional representation for safety assessment [17]. Subsequent
studies brought these ideas closer to practice by adapting robot motion boundaries dyôÙ
namically in response to predicted human trajectories, as already discussed in the context
of predictionôÙbased methods [13]. More recent developments advanced this direction with
realôÙtime formulations of dynamic danger fields, enabling direct computation and continôÙ
uous boundary adaptation during interaction with collaborative manipulators [18]. Taken
together, these studies illustrate the progression of 2D approaches from conceptual modôÙ
els, to predictionôÙdriven adaptation, to realôÙtime implementations.
ThreeôÙdimensional (3D) approaches emerged to overcome the inherent limitations of plaôÙ
nar models. Initial efforts demonstrated the feasibility of constructing volumetric protective
zones around industrial manipulators using multimodal sensing [19]. Later work extended
this by introducing dynamicsôÙbased scaling of volumetric zones, where safety margins
shrink or expand according to robot velocity and acceleration, validated on collaborative
arms [15, 16]. Parallel research proposed sensorôÙdriven volumetric partitioning for flexôÙ

When moving to humanoid robots, the challenge becomes greater. Several directions

1.3
Research Motivation
Humanoid robots are increasingly envisioned for industrial and societal applications, rangôÙ
ing from manufacturing and logistics to healthcare, domestic assistance, and public serôÙ
vice. In all these contexts, safety is a prerequisite for deployment, as emphasized by
international standards such as ISO 10218 [5] for industrial robots and ISO 15066 [6] for
collaborative operation. These safety standards specify requirements such as speed and
separation monitoring, power and force limitation, and formally verifiable safety guaranôÙ
tees. However, they are primarily designed for fixedôÙbase manipulators and collaborative
robot arms. At present, there are no dedicated safety standards that address the unique
safety challenges of humanoid robots, which combine high degrees of freedom, wholeôÙ
body mobility, and operation in unstructured human environments. This absence further
underscores the importance of defining reliable and adaptive danger zones specific to
humanoid robots, ensuring both functional effectiveness and public acceptance.

From an academic perspective, however, research on humanoid danger zone remains
fragmented and incomplete. Existing studies have advanced two main directions. On the
one hand, PPS approaches have demonstrated the feasibility of assigning local, bodyôÙ
partôÙspecific danger zones on humanoids such as iCub and HRPôÙ2Kai, enabling fineôÙ

collaborative environments, danger zone frameworks must be both interaction friendly
and formally verifiable. This requires combining the intuitive and humanôÙcentered spatial
partitioning of PPS with the formal rigor of dynamicsôÙbased safety guarantees, ensuring
that humanoid robots can operate naturally around humans while maintaining certifiable
safety.
1.4
Objective and Scope
The entire project is centered around one clear goal:
This work aims to develop a dynamic danger zone framework for humanoid robots
that integrates spatial partitioning according to body parts with safety guarantees
grounded in robot dynamics, thereby ensuring both safety and natural interaction
between humans and robots.
Building on the limitations outlined in the previous section, this thesis sets out to develop
a danger zone framework that is both interaction friendly and formally verifiable, with a
particular focus on humanoid robots that must combine balance, mobility, and safe interôÙ
action in real time. The ultimate aim is to provide humanoid robots with adaptive danger
zones that are grounded in their dynamics, while also reflecting humanôÙcentered spatial
segmentation.

Literature Review
The increasing deployment of humanoid robots in collaborative settings has improved the
urgency of establishing reliable safety frameworks. While international standards provide
baseline prescriptions for industrial and service robots, they remain largely static and inôÙ
sufficient to capture the dynamic and wholeôÙbody interactions characteristic of humanoid
systems. To address this gap, a growing body of research has investigated adaptive proôÙ
tective strategies, with dynamic danger zones emerging as a particularly promising apôÙ
proach. This chapter reviews the state of the art in dynamic protective zoning for humanôÙ
robot collaboration, highlighting methodological classifications, the transition from twoôÙ
dimensional to threeôÙdimensional representations, and extensions to humanoid robots.
By systematically analyzing these developments, the review identifies both the progress
achieved and the persistent challenges that motivate the framework proposed in this theôÙ
sis.
2.1
Introduction to Dynamic Danger Zones
In both industry and academia, different approaches to ensuring humanôÙrobot safety have
been proposed. These are generally grouped into three main categories[2, 12]: intrinsic
safety, protective separation, and controlôÙbased methods, with some recent contributions
also exploring supervisory or hybrid frameworks that combine external monitoring with
robot control. Intrinsic safety emphasizes lightweight structures, compliant actuation, and
hardwareôÙlevel limits on force and velocity to inherently reduce risks. ControlôÙbased methôÙ
ods instead embed safety constraints into robot planning and compliant control, enabling
realôÙtime adjustments such as automatic slowdown, collision detection, or emergency
stop. By contrast, the protective separation approach establishes spatial boundaries beôÙ
tween robots and humans, either defined statically in accordance with international stanôÙ
dards or adaptively through realôÙtime sensing and prediction, and has become particularly
relevant for closeôÙproximity collaboration. ISO 15066 further formalizes this perspective
by structuring collaborative operation around four modes: safetyôÙrated monitored stop,
hand guiding, speed and separation monitoring, and power and force limiting, each of
which relies on the specification of spatial or forceôÙrelated safety boundaries[6].
Within the protective separation paradigm, the distinction between safety zones and danôÙ

A safety zone represents a buffer region in which humans may enter but the robot must

2.2
Methodological Approaches to Dynamic Danger Zones
Methodological approaches to dynamic danger zones (DDZs) can be grouped into three
broad categories: perceptionôÙbased, predictionôÙbased, and controlôÙbased. This classifiôÙ
cation is based on the informationôÙprocessing hierarchy underlying any realôÙtime safety
system. Before a robot can enforce a dynamic boundary, it must first acquire information
about humans and its own state, then infer risks such as possible collisions or unsafe
trajectories, and finally impose constraints on its motion to ensure compliance with safety
requirements. In this sense, the categorization mirrors the operational pipeline of realôÙ
time decisionôÙmaking: perception provides the raw data, inference transforms this data
into actionable risk assessments, and constraint ensures that the robotãs behavior remains
verifiably safe despite dynamic uncertainty.
On this basis, the following review is structured into three subsections. PerceptionôÙbased
approaches examine sensing technologies and methods for detecting and localizing huôÙ
mans in relation to the robot. PredictionôÙbased approaches discuss models that anticipate
human motion and evaluate collision risks over time. ControlôÙbased approaches address
how these risk assessments are integrated into motion planning and control architectures
to enforce safety. Finally, a summary synthesizes insights across categories, highlighting
complementarities and research gaps in the methodological landscape.

3.1
Theoretical Formulation of Dynamic Danger Zones
The concept of dynamic danger zones (DDZs) can be formalized by translating physical
and kinematic constraints into mathematical models that define safety boundaries in real
time. While traditional twoôÙdimensional approaches often relied on fixed protective disôÙ
tances derived from standards such as ISO 13855 [10], humanoid robots require volumetôÙ

In this thesis, DDZs are defined as parametric regions whose shape and extent depend

physical constraints including kinetic energy and stopping distance.
Geometrically, these regions can be represented as ellipses, circles, or more general conôÙ

embedded into realôÙtime decisionôÙmaking, the formulation is expressed in matrix or optiôÙ
mization form, enabling integration with motion planning and safetyôÙaware control. The
following subsections develop this formulation step by step, beginning with the physical
principles that govern stopping distances and energy constraints, and proceeding to comôÙ

The development and validation of dynamic danger zones (DDZs) in humanoid robots reôÙ

Within this framework, the first step is the selection of a suitable simulation platform. The

3.2.1
Simulation Platform Selection
Modeling and validating DDZs in humanoid robots requires a simulation environment with
specific capabilities. Unlike industrial manipulators, humanoids involve many degrees of
freedom, wholeôÙbody contact dynamics, and humanôÙcentered interaction scenarios. To
support DDZ research, a platform should provide the following properties. It should allow
highôÙdegreeôÙofôÙfreedom humanoid modeling, so that the robotãs body can be represented
in a segmented way with danger zones defined per body part. It must include accurate
contact and force modeling, such as force cones, frictional contacts, and impact dynamics,
which are critical for computing stopping distances and dynamic envelopes. RealôÙtime
adaptability is also necessary to ensure that DDZ boundaries can be updated continuously
as the robot moves, while at the same time avoiding excessive computational load on
the platform. In addition, the platform should support clear twoôÙdimensional and threeôÙ
dimensional visualization of protective regions to aid both debugging and interpretation.
Compatibility with external planning and control frameworks is required for extensibility,
while ease of use and ecosystem support are desirable to reduce the learning curve.
Finally, the simulator should allow flexible experimentation, including sensor simulation,
environment configuration, and the integration of human motion data.

3.3
Danger Zone Design
In the previous chapter, the simulation framework was established, including the huôÙ
manoid robot model and the environment setup for evaluating dynamic danger zones.
With this foundation, the next step is to design the danger zones themselves in a way
that balances physical realism, computational feasibility, and adaptability to humanoid
motion. While the theoretical formulation provided general mathematical definitions of
DDZs, their practical use in simulation requires concrete design choices. This section
therefore focuses on the design of geometric danger zones for humanoid robots, considôÙ

their dimensions should be adjusted dynamically according to motion states, and how
local zones can be integrated into fullôÙbody representations.
3.3.1

The design of DDZs aims to achieve a balance between safety, interaction fluency, and

3.3.3
Combination Strategy
To ensure that danger zones are both computationally efficient and sufficiently precise, this
thesis adopts a layered combination strategy that balances lightweight approximations
with highôÙfidelity modeling. The strategy divides the protective space into farôÙfield, midôÙ
field, and nearôÙfield regions, each associated with a different level of geometric complexity
and update frequency.
In the farôÙfield, defined as distances greater than the static protective boundary plus a
safety margin derived from ISO 13855[10], robots and humans have sufficient time to
react, so rough, lowôÙfrequency spherical approximations are sufficient. The midôÙfield corôÙ
responds to the intermediate region between the protective boundary and the stopping
distance. Here, directional sensitivity becomes important, and ellipsoidal models are emôÙ
ployed to capture anisotropy due to velocity and posture. Finally, the nearôÙfield is deôÙ
fined as the region smaller than the robotãs braking distance, where the risk of collision
is highest. In this zone, precise volumetric envelopes are used and updated in real time,
ensuring highôÙfidelity protection in critical interactions. In this region, where the primary
objective is fast detection with minimal computational load, spherical approximations are
employed. Each body joint or segment is enclosed by a sphere whose radius is defined
by the static geometry and ISOôÙbased protective distance. These boundaries are updated
at a relatively low frequency, sufficient to indicate whether a human is entering the robotãs
workspace without requiring fineôÙgrained detail.
In the midôÙfield region, directionality and segmentôÙspecific dynamics become more critical.
Here, ellipsoids are used for slender body parts such as arms and legs, capturing velocityôÙ
dependent extensions along the primary motion axis, while spheres are retained for joint
coverage. This combination provides both efficiency and greater fidelity in reflecting the
robotãs dynamic structure. MidôÙfield zones are updated at higher frequency to ensure
responsiveness within the control cycle.
In the nearôÙfield region, where human proximity poses the highest risk, highôÙfidelity modôÙ
eling is necessary to avoid false negatives or overly conservative responses. For this
purpose, sweptôÙvolume envelopes are employed to capture the full trajectory of limbs
or tools during braking, as defined in paper[18]. Since envelope computation is more
demanding, it is triggered only when humans enter very close proximity to critical body
parts. This ensures maximum safety precision while avoiding unnecessary computational
overhead.
In summary, this layered approach from spheres in the far field to ellipsoids in the mid field
and envelopes in the near field achieves a balance between computational tractability and
physical realism. The hierarchical transition across far, mid, and near fields is illustrated
in Fig. 3.10 It allows humanoid robots to maintain realôÙtime performance while preserving
safety in critical interactions.

In the previous chapter, the theoretical formulation and simulation framework for dynamic

4.1
Spherical Boundaries
The spherical representation provides the simplest baseline model for dynamic danger
zones, as it corresponds directly to the constant protective distance formulations defined
in international safety standards. In this model, each joint or body segment is enclosed

the dynamic safety margin derived from velocity, stopping distance, and human approach
speed. Although computationally lightweight, this model already captures the fundamenôÙ
tal scaling of danger zones with walking speed and provides a clear reference against
which more complex geometric models can be evaluated.

4.4
Comparative Analysis Across Geometric Models
The three modeling approaches provide complementary perspectives on dynamic safety
zones. The spherical model (Fig. 4.3) offers the simplest abstraction, with isotropic expanôÙ
sion around each joint, suitable for fast computation but limited in capturing anisotropic
limb motions. The ellipsoidal model (Fig. 4.6) refines this representation by introducing
directional radii and axis lengths, which reflect the oscillatory dynamics of legs and the lonôÙ
gitudinal elongation of arms at higher speeds. Finally, the envelope model (Figs. 4.10 and
4.12) integrates multiple capsule segments into continuous profiles along limbs, yielding
the most detailed boundary evolution and capturing both cyclic expansions and transient
overshoot. Especially, envelope dynamics exhibit the most regular patterns: even when
the arms suddenly initiate swinging, their envelope fluctuations remain relatively small,
maintaining stability compared to radiusôÙ or axisôÙbased representations. Together, these
results show a clear tradeôÙoff: spherical zones are computationally efficient, ellipsoids
better reflect directional dynamics, and envelopeôÙbased methods provide the richest and
most stable representation for safetyôÙcritical applications.

4.6
Summary of Results
This chapter has presented the modeling and simulation results of three geometric repreôÙ
sentations of dynamic danger zones (DDZs): spherical, ellipsoidal, and envelopeôÙbased
boundaries. The analyses verified that all three models can be consistently applied within
the simulation framework, achieving the primary objective of demonstrating feasibility and
consistency of the proposed formulations.
The spherical model provided the simplest and most lightweight representation. It diôÙ
rectly reflects the effect of velocity on protective boundaries, scaling isotropically around
each joint in accordance with ISO 15066 formulations[6]. The ellipsoidal model introduced
anisotropy by extending along limb directions, thereby capturing the distinct motion dyôÙ
namics of legs and arms during walking. The envelopeôÙbased model delivered the most
detailed representation, reconstructing swept volumes that reflect the spatial extent of limb
motion. Its results were smoother and more regular than those of spherical or ellipsoidal
models, though at the cost of higher computational complexity.
Comparative analysis showed a clear tradeôÙoff among the three approaches. SpheriôÙ
cal zones are computationally efficient but coarse, ellipsoidal zones provide a balanced
compromise between accuracy and efficiency, and envelopeôÙbased zones offer the most
precise description but with higher computational demands. Notably, in simulation, all
three models ran in real time without significant bottlenecks, indicating that the tradeôÙoffs
are more conceptual than practically limiting in the tested MuJoCo environment.
Validation was confirmed by the agreement of simulation outcomes with theoretical conôÙ
straints. The temporal evolution of boundaries followed gait cycles and scaled consistently
with walking speed, supporting the physical interpretability of the models. Nonetheless,
limitations remain. The evaluation was restricted to simplified MuJoCo simulations with
reducedôÙorder SIP dynamics, and no comparisons with static baselines, alternative methôÙ
ods, or physical robot trials were conducted.
The full source code, including simulation scripts, plotting routines, and model configuraôÙ
tions, will be released in an openôÙaccess GitHub repository to facilitate reproducibility and
support future research in this area[73].

5.1
Discussion of Results
The findings of this thesis demonstrate that spherical, ellipsoidal, and envelopeôÙbased
danger zones are all valid representations for capturing the dynamics of humanoid walkôÙ

distance equations, braking distance constraints, and velocityôÙdependent scaling[6, 15,
16, 18, 13]. The simulation results further confirm that these zones adapt in a consistent
and interpretable manner to step cycles and speed variations. This provides a proofôÙofôÙ
concept validation that the proposed modeling strategies are both theoretically sound and
practically implementable within the simplified humanoid framework.
At the same time, the present evaluation does not constitute a full assessment of efôÙ
fectiveness. The results show that the danger zones update dynamically as intended,
but no comparison with static baselines or alternative approaches has been carried out.
Likewise, no quantitative measures such as task completion time, pause duration, or subôÙ
jective fluency ratings were collected to assess the impact on humanôÙrobot collaboration.
In addition, no physical robot trials were conducted, and the evaluation was restricted to a
limited set of simulation scenarios. As such, the conclusions here should be interpreted as
preliminary and indicative, rather than definitive evidence of improved safety and fluency.
These limitations are largely due to the complexity of humanoid robot evaluation. BuildôÙ
ing simulation environments for locomotion and wholeôÙbody control is substantially more
demanding than for fixedôÙbase manipulators. To reduce this workload, the study relied
on a simplified SIP model and tested only on planar ground conditions, leaving aside
more diverse environments such as uneven terrain, cluttered spaces, or human crowd
interactions. No longôÙduration walking sequences were tested, meaning issues such as
fatigue, balance loss, or cumulative instability could not be examined. Moreover, the
physical models available in the MuJoCo simulation platform, while efficient for contactôÙ
rich scenarios, do not provide the same level of accuracy in dynamics computation as
libraries such as Pinocchio. To complement MuJoCo, Pinocchio may be employed in
future work as a backend for fast and accurate rigidôÙbody dynamics computations, espeôÙ

Despite these constraints, the results remain valuable in showing that dynamic danger

5.2
DecisionôÙMaking and Control Integration
In the present study, the decisionôÙmaking strategy is deliberately kept simple: whenever a
human enters the defined dynamic danger zone (DDZ), the humanoid robot immediately
halts its motion. This binary logic ensures safety by design, since no further movement
is allowed once the protective boundary is violated. While this approach is straightforôÙ

of humanôÙrobot interaction, such as maintaining task continuity, prioritizing competing
objectives, or adapting control behavior to the level of human involvement. As such,
it represents a conservative baseline rather than a fully integrated decisionôÙmaking and
control framework. However, recent research in humanôÙrobot interaction demonstrates
that such binary stop strategies are insufficient for complex, realôÙworld tasks, particularly
for humanoid robots that must balance multiple objectives simultaneously.
Recent advances in decisionôÙmaking for humanôÙrobot interaction have moved beyond
binary stopôÙorôÙgo strategies toward more adaptive, contextôÙaware mechanisms. For inôÙ
stance, Zhao et al.[46] proposed a realôÙtime humanôÙrobot interaction framework that inteôÙ
grates 3D human activity recognition with a humanôÙmimicking decision mechanism, enôÙ

reconcile gait stabilization, obstacle avoidance, and emergency stopping within a uniôÙ

decisionôÙcontrol architectures, which aim to guarantee safety while maintaining efficiency

Future research should focus on embedding danger zone decision making within the conôÙ

adopted in this thesis. A key challenge lies in task coupling: humanoid robots must siôÙ
multaneously preserve gait stability, perform obstacle avoidance, and enforce safety conôÙ
straints. This implies that danger zones should not only trigger emergency stops, but also
serve as inputs to a decision hierarchy where tasks are dynamically prioritized depending
on context, for example, selecting between maintaining balance, reôÙplanning foot placeôÙ
ment, or executing a controlled deceleration. Such taskôÙpriority frameworks have been exôÙ
plored in recent work on realôÙtime and trustôÙaware decision making, and extending these
concepts to humanoid locomotion would allow for more adaptive and humanôÙcentered
safety responses.

dynamic balance, generate stable gaits, and handle diverse environments, limited the
scope of implementation. Moreover, simply scaling danger zones through heuristic asôÙ
sumptions would lack theoretical grounding without integration into wholeôÙbody control.

5.3
Implications and Challenges
The jointôÙlevel dynamic danger zone (DDZ) framework proposed in this thesis represents
a concrete solution to the safety challenges faced by humanoid robots operating in close
proximity to humans.
By assigning a dynamic protective boundary to each joint, the

overly conservative global zones and instead yields a fineôÙgrained, adaptive safety enôÙ

suitable for integration into existing humanoid control architectures, enabling more natural
and efficient interaction while preserving compliance with safety requirements. In practiôÙ
cal terms, this approach has the potential to enhance collaboration in industrial workcells,
service robotics, and healthcare scenarios, where humanoid robots must operate in close
proximity to humans without compromising fluency or safety.
Despite these promising implications, several challenges and limitations remain. First,
the evaluation has so far been restricted to simulation, where simplified assumptions

scaling to highôÙDoF humanoid platforms in realôÙworld environments will demand further

sensing setup, whereas practical deployment would require robust sensor fusion across

danger zones on real humanoid platforms, moving beyond simulation to capture fullôÙbody
dynamics, ground variability, and longôÙterm stability constraints. Another direction is to adôÙ

in real time without compromising control responsiveness. Equally important is sensor
fusion: integrating vision, depth, IMU, and tactile sensing will not only improve the robustôÙ

environments. Finally, userôÙcentered evaluation should become a priority. Human trust,
comfort, and perceived fluency are crucial for safe collaboration, and systematic studies

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the problem of ensuring safe and fluent humanôÙrobot interaction
in the context of humanoid robots, where the challenges of wholeôÙbody mobility, dynamic
balance, and multimodal interaction make existing safety standards insufficient. To adôÙ
dress these challenges, a dynamic danger zone (DDZ) framework was proposed, offering
physically verifiable, geometryôÙbased representations of safety margins that adapt in real
time to humanoid locomotion and interaction.
The main contribution of this work is the development and validation of jointôÙlevel DDZ
models using three complementary geometric strategies: spherical zones for simple conôÙ
servative protection, ellipsoidal and spherical combinations for directionôÙsensitive adapôÙ
tation, and envelope models for highôÙfidelity representations of swept volumes. By emôÙ
bedding kinematic and dynamic constraints, including braking trajectories and velocityôÙ
dependent scaling, the proposed framework ensures that the modeled safety zones are
not only geometrically defined but also physically realizable. A simulation platform was
implemented to visualize these zones during humanoid walking, allowing systematic evalôÙ
uation of their evolution across baseline locomotion, human crossing interactions, and
speed variations. Results demonstrated that all three models capture the essential influôÙ
ence of gait dynamics and velocity on the adaptive danger zones, validating the feasibility
of the approach.
Several limitations of the present work must be acknowledged. The evaluation was conôÙ
ducted exclusively in simulation, without realôÙworld experiments on a physical humanoid
platform. The humanoid walking model relied on the simplified Spherical Inverted PenduôÙ
lum (SIP), which does not capture the fullôÙbody dynamics of humanoid locomotion. The
experimental scenarios were limited to flat ground and singleôÙagent interactions, leavôÙ
ing out more complex environments with uneven terrain, multiple humans, or dynamic
obstacles. Furthermore, although the envelope models provide the most accurate repreôÙ
sentation, their computational cost poses challenges for realôÙtime control integration.
These limitations also point to several promising directions for future work. First, realôÙ
world validation with existing humanoid platforms is essential to assess the practical perôÙ
formance and robustness of DDZs under physical constraints. Second, integrating DDZ
decisionôÙmaking with the humanoid control loop remains an open challenge. Future sysôÙ
tems should couple locomotion control, obstacle avoidance, and emergency stopping into
a taskôÙpriority framework, balancing fluency and safety in real time. Third, the extension
of DDZ modeling to complex scenarios,will be critical for practical deployment. Fourth,
improving perception through sensor fusion, combining vision, IMU, and tactile modaliôÙ
ties, will enhance the accuracy and robustness of DDZ estimation. Finally, the human
factor dimension should not be overlooked: subjective studies on human acceptance of
dynamic safety margins are necessary to ensure that DDZs not only guarantee physical
safety but also foster harmonious and effective humanôÙrobot collaboration.
In conclusion, this thesis has introduced and validated a physically grounded DDZ frameôÙ
work for humanoid robots, demonstrating its feasibility through simulation and providing a
pathway toward safer, more adaptive, and more natural humanôÙrobot collaboration. While
significant challenges remain, particularly in decisionôÙmaking integration, realôÙtime impleôÙ
mentation, and humanôÙcentered validation, the contributions of this work lay a foundation
for future research at the intersection of safety, control, and humanôÙrobot interaction.
