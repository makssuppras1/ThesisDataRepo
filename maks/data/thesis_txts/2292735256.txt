Abstract
This thesis presents a novel system featuring a tangible user interface for digital 3D modeling.
With our system users can create 3D models by combining a set of real-life cube shaped building
blocks. We utilize a depth camera and our software to register placed cubes. The depth camera
can be moved and cubes can be registered on not flat surfaces. After cubes are registered the
system creates extra geometry to: close gabs between registered cubes, create smooth declines
and apply a selection of themes. The system is intended for interactive usage and features a
web-based user interface and visualization. We find that our solution can scale to hundreds of
cubes and provide users with new and interesting methods for human computer interaction.

Preface
This master thesis was written in the first semester of 2015 the project was started 01.02.2015
and ended 27.07.2015. The author of the thesis is Jeppe U. Walther who have used 35 ECTS
points for the partial fulfillment of the requirements for the degree of Master of Science in
Digital Media Engineering. The project has been supervised by Associate Professor Henrik
Aanæs and Associate Professor Jakob Andreas Bærentzen at the department DTU Compute at
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU).
The thesis subject is on tangible user interfaces (TUIs), more specific the sub domain con-
structive assembly and digital 3D modeling. A novel solution is presented where users can
performed 3D modeling by placing a set of physical building blocks in front of a depth camera.
Focus of the thesis is on the technical challenges of creating such a system. There are two parts
to the thesis work a software implementation and the documentation of the project work, this
document.
Jeppe U. Walther
Lyngby, Juli 2015

Introduction
For three decades the way in which we as humans interact with computers has stayed virtually
unchanged at least in mainstream usage. Before the introduction of touch interfaces together
with smartphones and tablets the only widespread tools for human computer interaction were
monitor, keyboard and mouse. Touch devices brought users closer to the computer as they
could now more directly interact with the presented graphical user interfaces (GUI). However
both methods of interaction have a very clear separation between the virtual world and the
physical world. Since evolution created humans with five senses and a well developed spa-
tial understanding, it could be argued that this separation and restriction of interaction to flat
surfaces hold us from achieving our true potential in both realms. Traditionally digital 3D
modeling have been performed using CAD software. These systems are often hard to master
and have a steep learning curve. The way these systems are controlled most often with mouse,
keyboard and a graphical user interface, is fundamentally different from the methods of tra-
ditional artists. Our claim is that these artists have a closer more physical relation to their
medium, whether that medium being a sculptor’s clay, a painters brush and canvas or mallet
and chisels of the stone mason.
We present a novel system Cube Builder 2. With this system users can perform digital 3D mod-
eling by interfacing with a computer through a set of real-life physical building blocks, these
cube shaped blocks can then be combined to from larger structures. Our system works by
registering orientation and position of placed cubes in a computer system via a depth camera.
Having registered cube poses allows us to digitally reconstruct the cubes geometry and to per-
form further processing on that geometry. By post precessing cube geometry we allow users
to build shapes that would otherwise have been impossible to achieve with simple cubes. An
example on how the system is used is shown in Figure 1.1 on page 4 and an example of our
geometry enrichment is shown in Figure 1.2 on page 4.

1.1.1 Tangible User Interfaces
Traditionally most people think of vision as the most important sense. However as stated by
[RDLT06] touch might be equally or even more important. Touch overlaps with some non-
traditional senses such as our kinetic sense, that is our constant awareness of our body and
body parts relative position. Balance and acceleration sense, temperature and pain. TUIs aims
to incorporate all of these including tactile touch into easy and intuitive interfaces that allows
users to draw on experiences from real life. TUI’s and the problems they aim to solve are well
described by [Ish08].
“”Where the sea meets the land, life has blossomed into a myriad of unique forms
in the turbulence of water, sand, and wind. At another seashore between the land
of atoms and the sea of bits, we are now facing the challenge of reconciling our dual
citizenships in the physical and digital worlds. Our visual and auditory sense organs
are steeped in the sea of digital information, but our bodies remain imprisoned in
the physical world. Windows to the digital world are confined to flat, square screens
and pixels, or “painted bits”. Unfortunately, one cannot feel and confirm the virtual
existence of this digital information through one’s hands and body””.
-Hiroshi Ishii [Ish08]
Another derived sense is our spatial understanding it draws input from both vision and audio
and to a high degree touch. Especially when working with virtual objects or tools with a high
degree of freedom (6D). An example could be a user that where to hammer a virtual nail into a
wall using a virtual hammer in some augmented reality system. Or imagine tightening a screw
under a table. If the user is presented with no form of touch feedback this would obviously be
a difficult tasks.

1.1.2 Augmented Reality
Augmented reality allows a user to see a mix of the real world and the digital [Ao97] it can be
seen as a special case of virtual reality, where an all digital world is presented to the user most
often with a set of virtual reality glasses [Ste92]. In the last few years virtual reality have shown

glasses Oculus Rift. The device is currently only available in a developers edition However many
interesting applications of the device have been shown. An example of this is [SJS14] where an
Oculus Rift is modified into an augmented reality device using two web cameras. This allows
virtual objects to be rendered on top of the real-life stereo video input.
Very recently (January 2015) Microsoft presented a new device for augmented reality named
Hololens [Hem15]. This device could hold great promise for the field. It has the ability to
project 3D renderings on top of the real world in the field of view of the user. The device is
also equipped with several Kinect like depth sensors that allows applications to adjust rendered
graphics to real world geometry. The device is self contained meaning that it does not need an
external computer

1.2 Research Proposal
In this thesis we design, implement, describe and test a system for tangible digital 3d modeling.
That delivers a user experience as the one described in the beginning of this chapter. We do
this because previous work have shown that there can be gained advances in human computer
interaction, by bridging the physical and digital work with TUIs. Especially since 3D modeling
is about manipulating virtual objects in 6DF. Which can be a daunting task when working on
a flat computer screen. Similar solutions have been proposed within the field of constructive
assembly. However there seems to be a gap in the research from around 2001 (generation 1) to
around 2010 (generation 2). This might be due to the introduction of low cost depth cameras
in 2010. It seems that there are methods from generation 1, that are yet to be implemented in
connection with depth cameras. For example no solutions from generation 2 does post process-
ing on reconstructed geometry as shown by [AFM+00]. Secondly the solutions using a depth
camera for tracking require a static setup. It is also worth noticing that none of the newer solu-
tions demonstrates models with as many blocks as those presented in [AFM+00]. Even though

It is the right time to conduct this research now because of the announcement of new aug-

• Can demonstrate promise for a future system based on a augmented reality system.

Section 3.1 points to a number of shortcomings of CB1 that we seek to address in a new and
improved version CB2.
1.2.1 Limitations
CB2 is a prototype system. More weight will be put on demonstrating a concept and investigate
technologies than on making a polished product. Some weight is put into enhancing usability
but it is not a focus of the research.

2.1 State of Cube Builder
At the end of the Cube Builder 1 project a prototype system was presented. With this prototype
users are able to create a digital 3D model by placing physical cubes within a building area

to be moved within the building area. The tracking method used in CB1 are interesting for
models consisting of many cubes as it scales with the number of cubes in constant time.

2.2.2 Foreground Background Segmentation and Point Cloud Processing
To track a placed cube, CB1 needs to find its corresponding points within the point cloud.
Unless the cube of interest is the first cube placed, several other cubes will be in the point
cloud, which is likely to confuse a tracking algorithm. To address this problem a foreground
background segmentation is performed. Background is in CB1’s context defined as all static
surfaces in the scene before adding a new cube. When a new cube is added, users will reach
into the sensors field of view. When this happens both the cube and the users visible body
parts are regarded as FG objects (2.17b). Once the cube is placed and its pose registered it
ceases to be in foreground and moves to background. The point here is that cube tracking in
later pipeline steps can disregard all points being part of the background. Method description
in this subsection corresponds to step 7 - 13 in figure 2.4.

2.2.3 Cube Detection and Pose Estimation
Lets summarize, we now have the organized point cloud 𝒫𝑝that is transformed by 𝐏and has
background points labeled as NAN. There is also the normal map 𝒩𝑝and the curvature map
𝒮𝑝. As mentioned the foreground-background segmentation is not perfect. There is both the
question of noise and that of unwanted foreground elements, typically body parts of the user.
What we now want is to separate points belonging to the current cube from these other ele-
ments. So that a cube pose estimation can be calculated. Fortunately a cube holds geometric
features that are unique when compared with body parts and noise points. Each side of a cube
can be represented by a plane. The pairwise intersection of these planes will be the cubes edges,
that can be represented by lines. Then again the pairwise intersection of these lines will be the
cubes corners. This makes planes the strongest feature for our purpose. Since it will be rep-
resented by the most points, which is important when dealing with noisy data. Unless a cube
is facing one of its sides directly towards the sensor, there will be at least two sides of a cube
visibly to the sensor. Planes representing these two sides will be perpendicular to each other.
Finally the size of the cubes are know 90 × 90 × 90 𝑚𝑚. This means that if the correct planes
can be found a cube pose can be calculated.
2.2.3.1 Multi Plane Segmentation
To detect planes CB1 uses “Organized Multi Plane Segmentation (OMPS)” that is part of PCL
and described by [RL01]. The algorithm is suitable for realtime purposes and gains its perfor-
mance from utilizing the inherent connectivity saved in an organized point cloud. The algo-
rithm uses 𝒫𝑝, 𝒩𝑝and 𝒮𝑝to detect all planes that lives up to a number of criteria. The criteria
are
• Minimum number of inlier points
• Tolerance for difference in normal direction between neighboring points, to be consid-
ered within the same plane
• Tolerance for distance from point to plane between neighboring points, to be considered
within the same plane
• Maximum curvature for a plane region

2.2.4 New Cube Detection
With CB1 the cube tracker is active for all frames. This means that in the ideal case a cube is
tracked from the moment it enters the sensors FOV. When it is placed in the scene and when
the placing hand leaves the FOV (figure 2.17b). The challenge now is to determine when a cube
have been placed. CB1 uses a simple approach to determine cube placement. A cube is consid-
ered placed when it on average does not translate more than a certain threshold 𝑑max = 0.01m
over 𝑛min count = 7 frames. When a cube is placed it’s pose is temporary saved as 𝐂ICP-registered.
To avoid placing the same cube multiple times the cube to be registered 𝐂ICP must have moved
more than a distance 𝑑min = 0.07m relative to 𝐂ICP-registered. The first cube to be registered is
saved as an initial basic 𝐂initial basic.

2.2.5 Pipeline Overview
Detection of new cubes marks the end of CB1´s cube tracker pipeline (figure 2.4). Next step
from here is the geometry processor which is implemented as an separate software. Data is
send over a network connection from the tracker to the geometry processor running on an-
other computer. Data is sent in packages containing the information needed by the geometry
processor. These packages are sent for each frame at roughly 30Hz. Packages contain current
cube pose 𝐂aligned, camera pose 𝐏aligned and variable new_cube that equals 1 if a cube have been
placed in that frame otherwise it equals 0.
2.2.6 Geometry Processor
The geometry processor part of CB1 has two main purposes. First to place cubes and apply build
modes. Secondly to provide users with a realtime visualization of the building process and a
simple user interface. The solution is integrated into Autodesk Maya. The reason for using
Maya is that it exposes a great set of tools for geometry processing and visualization through a
well documented API. Further Maya is a popular 3D modeling tool within several application
fields. As described in the previous subsection the tracker at each frame sends a packet to the
geometry processor, with sensor pose and pose of the currently tracked cube. The current cube
translation and sensor pose is offset by half an edge length in X, Y and Z directions yielding
𝐂aligned offsetand 𝐏aligned offset. Inside a custom Maya scene a virtual camera is posed in realtime
with the pose in 𝐂aligned ofset. Simultaneously a colored indicator cube (2.10) is positioned with
the current cube pose in 𝐏aligned ofset. When a packet with new_cube = 1 is received from
the tracker, cube placement is triggered. When this happens several new cube geometries are
constructed and posed with 𝐂aligned. There is one new cube geometry for each build mode.
Finally a larger combined geometry are created as the result of Grid Lock Smooth.

3.1.1 Discussion of Test Results
Detection Errors and Robustness
CB1 have serious problems with the robustness of cube
detection. This is obvious when comparing the images in Figure 2.17 on page 30 and Figure

3.1.2 Usability and Design Issues
When building a system for tangible 3D modeling, the user-experience is important. However
the work on CB1 did almost exclusively touch upon technical challenges. No formal user ex-
perience testing were performed on CB1, instead the issues listed here are based on our own
observations and opinions.
User can not stay in sensors FOV
One of the greatest annoyances when using CB1 is that the
user need to place the cube as quickly as possible. If placing a cube takes too long foreground
elements will begin to integrate into the KF reconstruction. When this happens the camera pose
is distorted (Figure 2.14 on page 28). One way to counter this is to increase the time it takes
for objects to be integrated. However this will affect the time for moving placed cubes from
foreground to background. CB1 standard integration time of 32 frames can be cumbersome as
it has an unnatural delay, where the users need to wait before placing another cube.
No possibility to remove cubes
Not having the possibility to remove cubes are problematic.
Both because users need to be quick about placing cubes and because of the lack of robustness
in pose estimation. But also because users can make mistakes and rarely have an entire model
planned out.
Cubes are too large
The cube size 90𝑥90𝑥90mm is simply too large to be practical for many
purposes. For larger models it is impractical to use a table since models will simply grow too
large. Instead we typically used the floor, this resulted in uncomfortable working positions.
Geometry Processor in Maya
Having the geometry processor as a maya plugin worked great
for the prototype system that CB1 is. However Maya is not build for realtime applications such
as ours. Another issue is that Maya is a large and complicated software that is hard do operate
for someone not trained in the software. Maya is also a computational demanding application,
this means that a power full computer will have to be accessible in the building area. This was
to some degree solved by using a laptop.

3.2 Sensor Input
In 2014 Microsoft released its second generation of RGB-D sensors KinectToF together with the
gaming console Xbox One [SO14, PDM+14]. An early choice in our project was to use this
new and improved sensors. The original KinectSL was released in 2010 and because of its low
cost and high quality it spawned a small revolution within computer vision [JLDS13] . KinectToF
uses time of flight technology, this means that instead of projecting a known light pattern as with
the older KinectSL, the new sensor performs a per pixel measure on the time i takes for a NIR
light source to be reflected and returned to the sensor and can from this information calculate
a per pixel distance. Both [BBA14] and [Zen14] performs good overall quality measurements
on KinectToF and compare it to KinectSL. However none are as thorough as [SLK15]. All three
papers comes to the conclusion that KinectToF outperforms KinectSL in almost all cases.

3.2.3 Sensor Limitations
There exist a number of error sources for ToF cameras. In our work some of these error sources
have caused challenges for cube detection. These error types are listed below together with a
short description.
Random Noise
The distance measurements made with KinectToF are better than its predeces-
sor but surely not perfect. [SLK15] finds that from 0.5m to 1.5m the standard deviation for
non corner points lies within a few millimeters. We should remember that this low error only
applies to pixels near the principle point and for well light surfaces. In general darker surfaces
yields stronger noise.
Range
The sensor KinectToF can deliver distance measurements until around 10m, what is
more than enough for our purposes. However it also has a near limit of approximate 0.5m
this is more problematic. When estimating cube poses we want as many points as possibly to
represent as cube. A simple way to maximize the number of visible cube points is to move the
sensor as close to the cube of interest as possible. The importance of this is enforced by KinectToF
wide FOV 70∘horizontal and 60∘vertical. In Figure 3.2a on page 37 it can be seen that the 3-4
cubes closes to the sensor have point cloud errors, this is because they are closer than 0.5m to
the sensor, the reason is that the intensity of reflected lights from the IR light source becomes
too bright. In other words the sensor will blind itself when objects are too close. In 4.1 it is
explained how we can lower the minimum distance by lowering IR intensity.
Stray Points
In Figure 3.2b on page 37 there are scattered points on cube sides facing towards
the virtual camera marked with red, they can also be seen between the cube towers forming
a fake surface. These points are not actual measurements from this location, but are actually
error points from the cubes edges. These errors occur when a pixel covers surfaces with a large
depth difference, since the pixel receives light from multiple depths a strongly offset depth value
is computed.

When the sensor fails to calculate a depth value it will

Multi Path Effects
Since ToF cameras emit a uniform light with no pattern it can only rely on
what it sees in a single pixel. This means that ToF cameras are sensitive to multi path effects.
The phenomenon is also know as global illumination within the field of computer graphics. The
basic concept is that light can bounce multiple times on surfaces. An example is how a room can
be fully lit by a small window since photons will be reflected into every corner. When KinectToF
receives light in a pixel that have been bouncing more than one time that light will appear to
have traveled a longer distance than the distance to the point that pixel directly points at. In
our context we meet the problem when a cube is placed on a reflective not necessarily specular
surface. An example is shown in Figure 3.3 on page 38 where it can be seen that light are
reflected from the table surface onto the cubes and by so resulting in a deformation of the cube
points. When the reflective surface is replaced with a black one the deformation disappears.

In short our results from CB1 pointed to a number of issues, that we seek to improve upon with
CB2
• Physical building cubes are too large and unhandy
• Users can not stay in the sensors FOV for too long
• Sensor pose estimation seems to drift
• Precision of detected cubes is simply not good enough to ensure a satisfying experience

Cubes and Hardware Setup
In order to be practical cube size should be smaller than that of
CB1. For example a 4×4 DUPLO LEGO brick is 40×40×28mm and have since its introduction
in 1969 shown building potential for children of all ages. Having cubic building blocks showed
some potential with CB1 and can make pose estimation simpler. Smaller cube size also means
fewer pixels to represent a cube in sensor input, unless the sensor is moved closer to the cube.
However a problem here is the sensors minimum distance of 0.5m. A compromise would be
building blocks of shape 40×40×40mm which would ensure cubic shape and a hand able size
while still being big enough to provide quality sensor data. With CB1 some benefit was found
from not being restricted by an interlocking system like LEGO. However one limitation here
is that it is impossible to make building cubes levitate. A possible solution to this problem is
thin plates that can be placed on top of cubes in order to levitate other cubes, without the plates
them self being registered. Another solutions could be small sticks of various length and fitting
holes in all cube faces.
With CB1 there is a strong separation between real life building blocks and the digital recon-
struction. Enforced by users having to move between the building area and a computer screen.
An interesting solution could be achieved using augmented reality glasses like HoloLens. How-
ever these have not been released for public use at the time of writing. Instead a solution could
be achieved using a tablet that could function like a augmented window into a virtual world.
Further having a tablet at the building area would allow users to control the system through a
touch interface.

Maintaining a dynamic sensor is important both for

the sensor is not handheld but placed on a tripod. Users can then when needed move the tripod
and sensor. CB1 allowed cubes to be placed on non flat surfaces. CB2 should aim to keep that

CB2’s design have four main parts: hardware, cube tracker, geometry processor and user in-

4.1 Hardware
Our hardware setup consist of a KinectToF sensor mounted on a tripod (Figure 4.2 on page 44).

low specular paint. Finally there is a set of thin plates with a width of 43 ⋅3mm or 43 ⋅4mm

minimizing problems with drift and users in sensor FOV. The downside is that some manual

4.2.1 Pipeline States
As mentioned the tracker has five pipeline sates. Below follows a brief description on the pur-
pose of each state
Moveable Sensor
In this state users are allowed to move the sensor device. Background in-
tegration is set to active, meaning that all objects in the sensors FOV will be added to KinFu’s
reconstruction. This also means that when this mode is active, dynamic objects should not
be present in the sensors FOV. If the system is put in “Moveable Sensor” state, the selectable
options will be limited to ”Placed Sensor”
Placed Sensor
When place sensor is activated background integration is deactivated and the
sensor pose is locked, this means users may no longer move the sensor. By default KinFu uses a
maximum distance threshold for its ICP camera alignment step of 0.1m that works well when
the goal is to compute a sensor pose. However with a cube edge length of 0.043m, foreground
cubes are not registered as outliers, to counter this the threshold is set to 0.04m when in “Placed
Sensor” state. Further details on changing the threshold can ben read in 4.2.3.1. As soon as the
system is set to “Placed Sensor” users are able to choose Initialize System.
Initialize System
This state is used to calculate an initial calibration. The simple idea is that
there is a need to determine in what direction up is. In practice users will go to “Placed Sensor”
state then place a box formed object in the building area, then finally trigger “Initialize System”.
The initialization object could simply be one or more building cubes but is recommended to use
a larger object. When an initialization have been performed the system is set back to “Placed
Sensor” and users are allowed to choose “Add Cube” or “Remove Cube”
Add Cube
When users place a building cube in the sensors FOV they will need to activate
“Add Cube” in order for CB2 to calculate and register a cube pose. Once the cube has been
registered the system will return to “Placed Sensor” state. Since background integration is dis-
abled in “Placed Sensor” state users can use as long as they want to place a cube.
Remove Cube
This works in the same way as “Add Cube” where users will remove a cube
from the sensors POV then activate “Remove Cube”. When the system has unregistered the
corresponding digital cube it will return to “Placed Sensor”.

4.2.4.1 Surface Normal Computation
High quality surface normals are important for cube face segmentation. We use a method for
surface normal computation that is part of PCL (NormalEstimationOMP, [RC11]) to calculate
surface normals for 𝒫𝑝. The approach is different from that of CB1 and is more computational
demanding. However since 𝒫𝑝normally only has 200 to 1000 points, because of the masking,
this does not present a grave problem. Also the fact that normal computation is only needed
when adding cubes, removing cubes or initializing makes this method applicable. The method
works by fitting a plane to all points within a certain radius 𝑡𝑟= 0.01m of an interest point.
This is done for all points and results in 𝑛2 distance calculations. To accelerate performance,
points are first added to a K-d tree, which greatly accelerates computational performance. Lets
label the resulting normal map 𝒩𝑝
4.2.4.2 Cube Face Segmentation Using K-Means Clustering in Normal Space
Having masked current cube points in 𝒫𝑝and a corresponding set of surface normals in 𝒩𝑝we
want to take the first steps against fitting a cube, this is in the end done by using ICP, but since
ICP tend to get stuck in local minima we first calculate an initial cube pose 𝐂initial (equation 4.1).
The cube pose is calculated by finding two plane equations, one for each of the two largest cube
faces facing towards the sensor. Before fitting planes 𝒫𝑝is segmented into three segments, one
for each face, by performing a clustering on 𝒩𝑝. The idea is that surface normals on a cube will
lie in three clusters when mapped onto a unit sphere (Figure 4.8a on page 54). In the ideal case
there would simply be three clusters where all normals in each cluster would be exactly equal
to other normals in its group and perpendicular to normals in other groups. Such optimality
is surely not present since 𝒫𝑝will contain noise, instead normals are distributed around three
centers on a unit sphere.
K-Means clustering [KMN+02] is an algorithm that have gained popularity in machine learning
for unsupervised clustering. The algorithm works by creating 𝐾clusters each with a single
initial point, the point nearest to the centroid of each cluster is then added to the cluster and
the centroid is recalculated. This process continues until cluster centroids does not move more
than some threshold. For our purpose the clustering is computed with K-Means on 𝒩𝑝in ℝ3
using an euclidian distance measure. The number of clusters sought is always 𝐾= 3, since that
is the maximum number of visible cube faces. Results are three normal clusters where only the
two clusters with the most points are used 𝒩𝐴and 𝒩𝐵. There is a known relation between
𝒫𝑝and 𝒩𝑝so it is simple to create two point clouds 𝒫𝐴and 𝒫𝐵with points corresponding to
clustered normals. Cubes will often only have two visible faces. However in practice this is not
a problem since only a few noisy points will end up in the third cluster and there is only need for
two planes and two centroids in order to calculate a cube pose. In Figure 4.8b on page 54 results
of a K-Means clustering on surface normals is listed. CB2 uses a version of K-Means clustering
included in OpenCV. Using K-Means for normals clustering seems to be quite robust even for
cubes with strong multi-path effects (3.2.3) and other types of noise. However the method is
sensitive to outliers that may offset centroids. Therefore some further filtering is needed.

4.2.4.5 Iterative Closest Point
The pose we get from K-Means normal segmentation and RANSAC plane fitting is of higher
quality than the one given by Multi Plane Segmentation and Plane Filtering in CB1. However
it still can’t beat ICP for a quality alignment, so in order to get a refined cube pose 𝐂ICP, ICP
is applied in much the same manner as with CB1 where an artificial point cloud 𝒫ICP-Source is
aligned with the one in 𝒫ICP-Target (2.2.3.5). Although there are some important differences. The
shape of the artificial point cloud 𝒫ICP-Source have changed so that it is now in the shape of a top-
cap instead of having four fully sized sides (Figure 4.10 on page 56). This is done to make the
system more accurate in the presence of multi-path noise (3.2.3) where we often see a curved
surface (Figure 3.3b on page 38). The idea is that by making cube faces on 𝒫ICP-Source one third
shorter, noisy points in the bottom of 𝒫ICP-Target are disregarded from the alignment. The point
count in side faces are heavily reduced since they are only there to stabilize ICP alignment and
avoid sliding. Remember that 𝒫ICP-Target only contains points from the two sides with the most
points. The reason for not including points from a third face is that these points are often stray
points 3.2.3 and will be offset at an angle, causing errors in pose estimation. Another difference
to CB1 is that we run the “Add Cube” pipeline 𝑛= 4 times, each time for a new frame. This
allows us to select the pose with the best correspondence score 𝐂ICP-Best.

4.2.6 Removing Cubes
For users to remove cubes from the model, the system must be in “Placed Sensor“ state, then
users can remove a cube from the model and activate “Remove Cube . To remove cubes CB2
utilizes the reconstruction created by Kinect Fusion (Figure 4.11 on page 58). Remember that
when in “Placed Sensor” state KF’s background integration is disabled, this means that when a
cube is removed from the model it will show up in the outliers map 𝒪𝑓just the same as when
cubes are added. A removed cube will then still be part of the reconstruction saved in KF’s
TSDF volume, so in order to to get a organized point cloud 𝒫Raycasted that includes the removed
cube, a ray-casting from the sensor’s POV into the TSDF volume is performed. Ray-casting
might appear to be computational demanding but is actually computed in a few milliseconds
since everything is executed in parallel on the GPU. In fact KF performs such a ray-casting for
all frames in its internal pipeline.
𝒫Raycasted is then treated the same way as 𝒫TPPMF in the “Add Cube” pipeline. It is masked using
𝒪𝑓, transformed by [𝐑, 𝐭] and surface normals are computed. When removing cubes there is
no need for as accurate a pose estimation as when cubes are added, since there is only a need
to figure out what cube in 𝐿Cube-Poses that have been removed. Hence the ICP step is skipped
and a cube pose 𝐂Removed is computed from two planes after applying K-Means Normals Seg-
mentation With RANSAC Plane Fitting (4.2.4.2) . The cube pose to be removed 𝐿Cube-Poses is
simply found by finding the pose that has a small translation distance to 𝐂Removed.

4.3 Geometry Processor
The geometry processors job is to take registered cube poses in 𝐿Cube-Poses an turn them into
geometry, in our case triangulated polygonal meshes. CB2 can perform several levels of ge-
ometry refinement from a simple representation of placed cubes to creating geometry between
cubes and extending with extra geometry. We present a solution that can combine placed cubes
into a single mesh even when cubes are placed with gabs between them and can preserve sharp
edges and corners. Our design explores geometry enhancement through theming of cubes and
refinements such as smooth decline.
As mentioned the geometry processor have access to some data from the tracker. More specifi-
cally a list of registered cube poses 𝐿Cube-Poses and the sensor pose [𝐑, 𝐭]. These data are received
at roughly 30Hz. However the full geometry processor pipeline is not executed at all frames.
Instead geometry is only updated when the number of cube poses in 𝐿Cube-Poses changes.
4.3.1 Basis Alignment
When cube poses are computed it is done in the coordinate system of the CB2 tracker, the
system has no idea about how cubes are placed inside that coordinate system, since the sensor
may be posed at any angle to the cubes. To fix this we use the inverse of the first registered
cube pose to define a basis 𝐁Align = 𝐿Cube-Poses[0]−1 . The basis is then matrix multiplied on
all cube poses in 𝐿Cube-Poses this will surely mean that 𝐿Cube-Poses[0] = 𝐈which is exactly the
wanted result, the first cube is placed in 0, 0, 0 with a standard XYZ basis as rotation and all
other cubes will be placed relative to this pose. Figure 4.12 shows an example of cubes with and
without basis alignment. The new list of basis aligned cube poses is labeled 𝐿Aligned-Cube-Poses

4.3.4.2 Connecting Many Cubes
In order for cube connection to work well with many cubes there is a need to expand upon
what was described for two cubes. Since cubes have six sides it can connect to up to six other
cubes. The simple approach would be to connect each faces on a cube to the nearest face from
other cubes. However there is a need to limit the distance that cubes can connect over, lets add
such a maximum distance threshold 𝑡max-distance. There is also situation where two cubes would
connect two or more faces on each other, this is also unwanted, therefore cubes are restricted
to a single connection to another cube. Another restriction is that only faces facing towards
each other should be connected. If two faces are at a too narrow an angle to each other (Figure
4.17 on page 64 left) they should not connect. In 4.17 it is shown than the angle 𝛼between a
face normal from a cube 𝒜(green arrow) and a vector from between faces (blue arrow) can be
used to determine faces are at narrow angels.. The angle between face normals 𝛽can be used
to find if the two faces are facing towards each other. This adds two new thresholds for these
angles 𝑡cos 𝛼= 0.34 and 𝑡cos 𝛽= −0.34. Cube poses are registered with some errors and often
stacked cubes will intersect a little. When this happens cubes will not be connected unless a
cheat distance threshold is added 𝑡cheat-distance = 0.01m. With these restrictions the algorithm
for connecting a cube 𝒜to 𝑛other cubes becomes the one listed in algorithm 4.6. To connect
all cubes the algorithm is applied to cubes in 𝐿𝒞. See Figure 4.18 on page 65 for examples on

4.3.7 Theming
Placed cubes can form a model in several ways, with a short connection distance models like the
house in Figure 4.20b on page 66 can be build and with a longer connection distances complex
models can be achieved with only a few cubes (Figure 4.18b on page 65). However small details
are hard to model unless many cubes are used. A solution to automatically add details to models
is what we call themes, in our prototype there is only a single “Castle theme” available, but
other types of themes could be imagined. The “Castle theme” makes the assumption that users
are building a medieval castle or fortress, the system then tries to add detail like tower roofs
and parapet. Adding details with themes require the system to have some understanding of
structure between placed cubes, for example a tower roof should only be added to the cube
at the top of a line of stacked cubes. Fortunately cube connections and the graphs 𝐺and 𝑌
provide much of the needed information.
In order for theme geometry to be added to cubes, there first need to be found the correct cubes
to add the geometry too. CB2 defines a number of cube types that can later be used to add the
correct geometry. Types are defined by simple rules based on the degree of a vertex and how
its faces are connected to neighbor cubes Table 4.1 on page 68. For some cube types like “Top
Arc” the logic can be more complex and include information about connection on neighboring
cubes (algorithm 4.8). In Figure 4.22 on page 69 a visual overview of cube types are shown.
Having a method of identified cube types make the task of adding “Castle theme” geometry
simple, roofs are added to “Peak” cubes and parapet is added to “Peak Corner” cubes. Many
other possible additions could be imagined for example “Top Arc” cubes could be replaced by
a cube with an arc shaped hole, as it is known from stone arcs.

4.3.8 Smooth Declines
Besides theming a method for creating smooth declines was suggested in 3.3. CB2 creates
smooth declines between selected cube faces byfirstcreating a listof candidatecubes 𝐿Decline candidates,
with the simple restriction that there may be no connection on 𝒞𝑓[𝑦+]. The process is then much
like the one for cube connection (algorithm 4.6), however it is only applied to 𝐿Decline candidates
and further restriction are required before faces can be accepted as face candidates. Face nor-
mals must be close toperpendicular and cos 𝛼must be positive −0.1 < cos 𝛽< 0.1 and cos 𝛼>
0.0 and 𝑑< 0.04. Instead of using cubic bezier curves to connect cube faces there is instead
simply used straight lines, except from these facts the geometry creation process is similar to
(4.3.4.1). In Figure 4.23 on page 70 we show an example with smooth decline applied.
4.3.9 Merge Vertices
As in can be seen in Figure 4.23 on page 70 and Figure 4.18b on page 65 there can be left
unwanted holes an gabs between cubes, connections, and decline geometry. To remove these
holes and create a single mesh, CB2 implements a merge vertices action. When the merge
vertices action is triggered all polygonal meshes for cubes, connection, declines and theme
geometry are combined into a single mesh 𝑀all. The mesh vertices are stored in a k-d tree in
order to accelerate distance lookups. To remove gaps all vertices in 𝑀all are set to an average
positions of all other vertices with in a distance 𝑡merge-distance (algorithm 4.9). Having merged
vertices gabs are removed, however the merge distance must be set in such a way that it does not
close wanted openings. For example in Figure 4.3 on page 45 gabs between decline geometry
is merged while doors and windows are kept open.

Implementation
As mentioned in previous chapters the CB2 system consists of two main parts the cube tracker
and the geometry processor, however from an implementation perspective there are more
pieces to the puzzle. Our software stack consist of several processes running in parallel and
communication through ROS (Robot Operating System) [QCG+09]. CB2 uses ROS as a fun-
damental part of its implementation described later in this chapter. ROS allows multi language
software development, which we have utilized in order to take advantage of the strengths from
several programming languages. The final CB2 source code consist of approximately 4000 lines
of C++, 2000 lines of Java Script, 500 lines of Python, 700 lines of CUDA-C and finally around
1000 lines of XML, YAML and JSON configuration files, these numbers are not counting com-
ments and blank lines. We have created a custom version of KinFu Remake [Bak], the Open
Source version of Kinect Fusion. Our version has support for KinectToF as opposed to the orig-
inal version that only worked with KinectSL . Further we have added a number of functions,
especially for CB2 among them the TPMF filter and the depth aware connected component
analyses.
As mentioned there are more than two parts to the CB2 implementation, however the two
most important ones are still the cube tracker and the geometry processor. The cube tracker
and geometry processor are implemented in fundamentally different ways. The cube tracker is
because of its demanding computations programmed in C++ and and is compiled as a GNU
Linux executable while the geometry processor is implemented in Java Script and run through
a modern web browser. The geometry processor utilizes WebGL through the Three.js library.
Having the geometry processor and its visualizer as a web applications allows us to run the soft-
ware on a wide range of platforms. For example we use the same codebase to run the geometry
processor on both PC and tablets.
CB2 uses a number of open source software libraries many of them have been previously men-
tion, however we will list them her for the sake of completeness ROS, OpenCV [Bra00], KinFU
Remake [Bak], PCL [RC11], IAI Kinect 2 [Wie14], Libfreenect 2 [BEK], Three.js [Cab15], An-
gular.js [Goo15], and JSNetworkX [Kli15].

5. Implementation
5.1 CB2 in ROS
A valid question from readers would be “Why use a operating system designed for robots to
build a system for human computer interaction?”. The reasons are many, first of all ROS is
not an operating system such as Microsoft Windows or GNU Linux. ROS is a general purpose
robot development platform running on top of GNU Linux. Often advanced robots have mul-
tiple input sensors such as cameras, depth cameras, force feedback devices and alike. These
many data inputs needs to be processed in parallel so ROS exposes sub systems both for in-
ter process communication and input device handling. Further ROS originates from the same
company (Willow Garage) as PCL and OpenCV meaning that there is build in support for PCL
point cloud messages and OpenCV image messages. All in all there is in our opinion a large
overlap between the needs of robotic developers and what we want for developing a system such
as CB2. With ROS programs are written as smaller independent processes called “Nodes” that
communicate witch each other in parallel over network connections. Nodes can both pub-
lish and subscribe to what is called “topics”. A topic publishes a certain “message type” for
example one topic could publish depth image messages with the type “sensor_msg/image”. In
this way complex programs can be achieved by combining smaller programs each doing their
smaller part. A bonus from letting processes communicate over network socket is that mul-
tiple computers can be used, in fact each node would be able to run on a separate computer.
ROS prepackages a large number of such programs in “packages”. The system includes soft-
ware libraries for amongst others languages C++, Python and Java Script to interface with the
underlying system.
5.1.1 CB2 ROS Node Graph
Our setup consist of five ROS nodes and an extra “node” (Geometry Processor and Visualizer)
that we will for now also consider a ROS node (4.3). The first node to consider is the “Kinect
2 Bridge” node, this node is not of our design but is a part of the IAI Kinect 2 ROS package.
The node serves as a bridge between the Libfreenect2 driver and the ROS infrastructure, it
grabs depth, infrared and color frames from the driver then performs undistortion based on a
stored calibration and then exposes the image streams as topics at 30Hz. Each image topic is
accompanied by a camera info message that contains camera parameters and other meta data.
The next node to consider is the “Controller” node this node uses another ROS concept called
actions, we can for the purpose of this description understand them as simple functions that
can be triggered by other nodes. The controller node has 5 of these actions “Place Sensor”,
“Move Sensor” “Add Cube”, “Remove Cube” and “Initiate System” these actions corresponds
one to one with the pipeline states described in the previous chapter, when one of the actions
is activated the controller will publish a controller.msg message.
Next node is the cube tracker node, it subscribes to image and camera info topics published by
the “Kinect 2 Bridge” node and receives data for each frame. The tracker node also subscribes
to the controller nodes messages containing information about how its should configure its
pipeline. Based on data from the controller the image data are then processed as explained in
the chapter 4. The final result is a list of cube poses and a sensor pose that is exposed as a topic
with the custom message type ct_state.msg.

6.1.1 Sensor Input With Temporal Per Pixel Median Filter

100 depth frames yielding 10 TPPMF frames these 10 frames are then averages pr pixel in order
to give a base line point cloud. Points belonging to the flat surface is then manually segmented
in a 3D software and a plane is fitted to the points using PCA (Figure 6.3 on page 79). A point
to plane distance is then calculated for all points and in turn a mean and standard deviation
6.1. Same process is repeated for a single TPPMF frame and for a raw depth frame.

poses. To give a baseline for orientation, normals is calculated for all manually segmented
plane points. The normals are clulated by fitting planes for each point and its neighbors within
a radius 𝑟= 0.0225mm, which gives the approximate area of a cube face. Angle differences
between point normals and the plane normal is then calculated. As with point to plane dis-

6.7 Usability Testing
A usability test were performed on two test users, having only two people testing our system is
surely not enough to identify all problems. However our tests did expose a number of problems
and gave us new ideas for improvements. Both test users are adults age 27 and 40, the oldest of
them has experience with 3D modeling and is employed as a technical artist at a game company,
the younger one has no experience with 3D modeling and is employed as a waitress. The design
of our usability tests is inspired by the micro-usability test guide [Kun03]. At the day of the user
tests there were problems using the CB2 user interface on tablets instead a 15” laptop was used.
The goal of our tests is both to identify problems and find what works well with our system.
We are aware that CB2 is a prototype system and therefore not fit for end users, therefore we
decided to first give users a short introduction on how to use the system. Further we allowed
users to use both the web interface and the development interface (4.24b). Our test guide has
the following five steps.
1. The test subject is introduced to the system an given a short demonstration on how cubes
can be added and removed, how build modes work and how the maximum connection
distance can be set. They are told that they will be given three test scenarios where they
are supposed to use CB2 to build 3D models. They are told that they can ask questions
but should try their best to solve problems on their own, if they ask questions it will be
noted as a retry.
2. The user is then exposed to “test scenario 1” where they are presented with a simple model
consisting of 8 cubes that they are asked to rebuild using CB2. The model is presented to
them in a 3D viewer so they can rotate the model and see it from all sides.
3. “Test scenario 2” is a more complex model where test users will be forced to use building
plates in order to achieve the model. They will also be forced to either move the sensor

Discussion
This chapter features a discussion of the results presented in the previous chapter. First we
discuss results from the cube tracker and geometry processor. Then we evaluate results from
our usability tests and compare our solution to CB1 and other constructive assembly solutions.
Finally we suggest possible subjects for future work.
7.1 Cube Tracking
Chapter 6 features a number of test that measures precession and accuracy of the cube trackers
pose estimation. To get an idea about the fairness of these results seen against the amount of
noise in sensor input, a baseline was established (6.1.1). In this baseline we saw that the mean
point to plane distance was around 𝜇= 1mm for a TPPMF frame and would have a standard
deviation of 𝜎= 0.7mm. Surface normals on average was offset by one degree and would
vary with 𝜎= 0.6∘. We should of course be aware that these baseline results themselves are
affected by noise. Another thing that should be kept in mind when evaluating our results is that
we are dealing with physical cubes that themselves have varying sizes. Our guiding objects are
also simply made from wooden planks and will have some variations. We have performed no
measurements on these physical sources of noise. With our three test sets “Horizontal Line”,
“Vertical Line” and “Circle” translation standard deviation is found to be between 𝜎= 1mm
to 𝜎= 2mm. For rotation we find a standard deviation from 𝜎= 1.1∘to 𝜎= 2.6∘except for
the Z rotation in “Vertical line” that is affected by a strong outlier.
When evaluating these numbers the baseline presents better results than our cube estimation.
However the baseline also have a better basic for its results. Cube surfaces are more rough than
the flat plate used for the baseline, also cubes have edges that can cause stray pixels. Multi path
effects are also an error source that is not present for the baseline test set since light cant bounce
much around on a flat plate. All in all we believe that our results are acceptable given the quality
of the physical objects and sensor input.
CB1 had problems with sensor pose drift, with CB2 we have takes steps to avoid these issues
by adding a minimum threshold. It would seem that we have not been able to eliminate the

displaced below ground level and is therefor not rendered. A possible solution could be not
to use the first cube to orient the rest of the cubes and instead use the basis computed in the
initialization step. In general we believe the system to be robust but this is an issue that should
be fixed in future work.
7.2 Geometry Processor
With our novel geometry processor many new concept have been introduced. Creating con-
nection between cubes have given users a way to create closed smooth surfaces. If longer con-
nection distances is used larger more complex models can be achieved with fewer cubes Figure
6.23 on page 90. Using long connection distances lets us work with cubes almost as was it a
spline modeler where cubes are the control points (Figure 6.24b on page 91 and Figure 6.24d
on page 91). Our impression is that cube connection in general just works and it seems to be the
same case with the test users. In some cases users might get unwanted connections, this typi-
cally happens when using a long connection distance. For example when one of our test users
constructed the model in Figure 6.29 on page 95 he uses a long connection distance, which
resulted in unwanted connection. The solution was to lower the connection distance and place
more cubes to create connections.
Connections cubes based on our simple rules (4.3.4) formed the foundation for representing
the cubes as a graph, which in turn created the possibilities for the “Y-lock Y-average” mode
that have shown to work well. Both test users activated this build-mode since they found it gave
the best mix of precision and creative freedom. Both test users expressed interest in the themes
and one of them used it to create his free model (Figure 6.29d on page 95).

7.4 Computational Performance
In Figure 6.26 on page 92 it is shown that the average calculation time per frame is approximate
65ms or 15 FPS. This is around half the rate that sensor input is received at meaning that the
integration time after a cube is placed is longer that it has to be. This could have an effect
on sensor pose estimation were lower FPS means longer distance between matching points.
However 30ms is used for visualizations in the development and debugging interfaces if this
interfaces could be removed we would be on pair with the rate of sensor input. An interesting
observation is the runtime of the cube face segmentation using K-Means clustering and the
following RANSAC plane fitting step that for all cubes executes in less than 10ms (Figure 6.27
on page 92). This is interesting for future work if our solution should return to a totally dynamic
sensor and therefore would need cube detection on all frames. As it can be seen from the graph
in Figure 6.26 on page 92 the tracker scales in constant time to the number of cubes, this means
that there in relation to the tracker is really no upper limit to the number of cubes that could
be pose estimated. Even though we have used OpenMP and GPU acceleration to optimize
computational performance there is still rome for improvement. Most of our functions are still
single threaded and bound to the CPU.
The geometry processor does not scale as well as the tracker, especially the creation of con-
nections is expensive. For large cube counts (30+) this makes our solution almost unusable on
tablets. However there are numerous ways this could be improved. Steps could be taken to only
update cube geometry that needs to be updated, the current algorithm recreates all connection
each time a new cube is added. Further our decision to let the geometry processor run on the
client side (in the browser) may have been unwise. A better solution would have been to let the
geometry processor do the processing server side and then send the resulting geometry to the
web client.

7.6 Comparing to CB1 and Other Systems
When comparing to the old version CB1 we see that several improvements have been made.
We can simple compare the renderings in Figure 6.17 on page 87 and Figure 2.18 on page 30
where pose estimation for the CB2 example is clearly much more accurate and keep in mind
that the CB2 building block only have 25% surface area comapared to the old building blocks.
The models in Figure 2.18 on page 30 and Figure 2.12 on page 26 were the most complex models
ever achieved with CB1, we need only compare them to our building scenarios in Figure 6.22
on page 89 and Figure 6.23 on page 90 to see that the system have greatly improved. However
CB1 did have one feature that CB2 have sacrificed in order to get gain robustness, the dynamic
camera and automatic cube detection. Based on our results it sees like an asceptable tradeoff.
One of the main inspirations for the CB2 system was as mentioned [AFM+00], Anderson’s
system inspired us to post process the registered cube poses and add themes. More recent re-
search within the field of constructive assembly was DuploTrack [GFCC12] and Lattice-First
[MWC+12]. However all of these systems was based on interlocking systems and static setup.
CB2 has no restrictions on how cubes can be placed and has a semi dynamic camera: The
semi dynamic camera allows users to create models larger than what can fit in the sensors
FOV (Section 6.4). Furthermore our tracking solution scales in constant time as opposed to
DuploTrack. The CB2 approach to creating curved connections and declines between non-
interlocking building blocks are to our knowledge unique.
7.7 Future Work
CB2 present new ideas to the field of constructive assembly, however the system is surely not
perfect. One of the greatest frustrations with CB2, both for its developers and test users is the
inability to place multiple cubes at once. Also the process of having to remove ones hands from
the working area every time cubes are added is sub optimal. We suggest that future research
will investigate methods for creating a more dynamic system. One possibility could be to work
directly with Kinect Fusions volumetric data, there are however certain problems with this
solution. As far as we can see from our work the data in the TSDF volume is actually of poorer
quality than sensor input from the new KinectToF sensor, even when using larger volume sizes
than 5123.
We believe that the ideas introduced with CB2 holds potential for augmented reality applica-
tions. The new augmented reality glasses from Microsoft (HoloLens) , features a build in depth
camera. If CB2 could be coupled with such a device we believe a truly novel and useful way
of interfacing with computers could be achieved. This will however demand that the dynamic
sensors are reintroduced to CB2 and that drift issues are fixed.

Conclusion
With this thesis we have sought an answer to the question “How can a system for, tangible digital
3D modeling with physical building blocks, be achieved?”. We have answered this question by
designing and implementing such a system Cube Builder 2. Our system uses non-interlocking
building cubes that can be combined to form larger models consisting of more than 100 cubes.
Cubes and their poses are registered in a computer by placing them in the field of view of a depth
camera. The system is dynamic and does not require a static setup in the sense that the depth
camera can be moved even in the middle of a construction session. In fact moving the depth
camera allows users to build models larger than what can fit in the sensors field of view. After
cubes are registered their geometry is reconstructed in our geometry processor and further
post processing is applied. The geometry processor can fill gabs between cubes, create smooth
declines and apply themes. The system is interactive, reacts in real-time and features a web-
based visualizer and user interface that can be used on both tablets and personal computers.
Our system mounts a tablet on top of the depth camera in order to create an augmented reality
window where registered cubes and their refined geometry can be inspected.
Cube Builder 2 is to some extend based on previous work Cube Builder 1. The new cube tracker
have showed to be sufficiently robust and accurate to ensure the creation of large models, even
with the new much smaller building cubes. There are several reason for this improvement, one
is that we now know when the sensor is stationary and can therefor use our temporal TPPMF

Our new geometry processor has almost nothing in common with the one in Cube Builder

them. Using bezier curves proved to work well for connecting cubes with smooth curves, even

A.1.1.2 Depth-Aware Connected Components Analysis
To remove remaining noise from the outliers map a connected components analysis (CCA) is
applied. This method identifies a number of connected blobs of image points from a binary
2-dimensional map. This means that all objects that are not directly connected to another are
registered as a blob (figure A.2). From this method it is straight-forward to calculate the size
of each blob, and thereby making it possible to filter away all blobs with a size smaller than
some threshold 𝑡𝑏∈[0, 512 × 424]. For an outliers map like the one presented in A.2 A, B
this approach would work perfectly. However, it is possible that not all edges are removed by
the erosion, as shown in figure A.5. Here edges from the background are connected to the
outline of the human silhouette by grouping them into one blob. To avoid such effects, values
from the depth map can be taken into consideration and in that way extending the connected
components analysis into a depth-aware version (figure ?? A,C).
The standard version of connected components algorithm [? ] is not well suited for parallel
acceleration. An alternative solution were sought out and a version of the Label Equivalence
algorithm prepared for GPU-acceleration were found in [? ]. There are three main steps to the
algorithm: initialization, linking and relabeling. The first step is run only once, while linking
and labeling are run in an iterative manner until a successive state is reached (algorithm A.2).
Initialization and Labeling
This is the first step of the algorithm where a new outliers map
𝒪𝑐∈ℕis initialized. Here, all pixels with a corresponding pixel in the original outliers map
with value 1 is set to its index number (see A.4). In the same process a filtering is performed to
exclude invalid depth measurement coming from the depth map. Besides 𝒪𝑐a corresponding
blob size map 𝐵∈ℕis initialized with the value 1 in each pixel that does not contain 0 in the
outliers map (A.4 A, algorithm presented in algorithm A.3).

neighbor it will exit successfully (figure A.3 C).

A.1.1.3 Filtering Connected Components
