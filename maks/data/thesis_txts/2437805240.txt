Abstract
The aim of the study is to elucidate host-virus interaction mechanisms in swine nasal mucosa
explants infected with Inuenza A virus (IAV). Because of its similarity with human respi-
ratory and immune system, the pig was chosen as animal model. The usage of ex vivo organ
culture mimics more accurately the in vivo situation than using in vitro systems, without
the complexity in vivo studies.
The overall hypothesis of this study is that swine mucosal explants could represent a
powerful system to examine the viral life cycle and gene expression of immune related genes.
The use of nasal explants has been well implemented at Gent University and the aim of this
study is to characterize and optimize the ex vivo system for optimal viral replication, virus
shedding and host transcriptional studies of antiviral immune responses.
A literature study was required in order to investigate variables known to inuence the
viral infection and tissue culture in explants and optimize them for our experiment. Nasal
mucosa explants were isolated from three pigs, cultured for 20 hours and then inoculated
with mock-medium or the virus A/swine/Denmark/12687/2003 (H1N2) propagated in eggs
or MDCK cells, undiluted and diluted dierently so that ve dierent treatments were
performed. Explants as well as media were harvested at 0h, 1h, 24h and 48h post infection;
further three explants were taken right after pig's slaughter.
Gene expression analysis was performed by high throughput real-time quantitative PCR
(RT-qPCR) using Fluidigm dynamic arrays focusing on genes involved in innate immune
response. Several genes were found up-regulated suggesting that the ex vivo system is able to
mimic IAV infection. The same RT-qPCR technique was used in a collateral study to assess
handling and culturing eects on media-treated nasal explants. Viral loads were measured
and showed by RT-qPCR to increase only in some of the samples, but no infectious virions
were detected by TCID50. Further optimization is then required.

Introduction
1.1
Inuenza A virus (IAV)
Inuenza A virus (IAV) is an enveloped negative-sense single stranded RNA ((-)ssRNA)
virus that cause a respiratory disease in humans and many other mammalian hosts, as for
example pigs [1, 2, 3]. Symptoms caused by IAV infection are mild and consist of: fever,
coughing, fatigue, mucus over-accumulation and respiratory epithelium inammation.
IAV belongs to the family of the Orthomyxoviridae family and can be classied in sub-
types based on two glycoproteins exposed on the surface of the virion structure: hemag-
glutinin (H or HA) and neuraminidase (N or NA) [4]. Until now, 16 hemagglutinin and 9
neuraminidase subtypes have been discovered and they can be mixed in several combinations
giving a subtype name of the kind HxNy.
The HxNy subtype and in particular the hemagglutinin type is very important for host
range specicity of the virus. Hemagglutinin binds to the sialic acid receptor on the epithelial
host cell membrane, initializing the viral life cycle [5]. Sialic acid receptors can be of two
dierent types: human and swine virus strains show a clear preference for binding α-2,6-
linked sialic acids receptors, while avian virus strains prefer to bind to α-2,3-linked sialic
acid receptors [5, 6, 7, 8]. Also the distribution of sialic acid receptors changes along the
respiratory tract in dierent animals and all this determines the host specicity for a certain
virus strain [7, 8].
1.1.1
Genome and particle structure
Inuenza A virus lipid bilayer originates from the host cell membrane and contain three viral
transmembrane proteins: two glycoprotein spikes, hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase
(NA), and the matrix 2 (M2) ion channels. Right beneath the envelope is present the matrix
1 (M1) protein, which forms a structure enclosing the virion core[9].
The virus core is
composed by nuclear export protein (NEP), also called non-structural protein 2 (NS2) and
the ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complex, consisting in the eight viral genomic RNA segments
coated with nucleoprotein (NP) and associated with the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase
complex (polymerase protein 1 (PB1), polymerase protein 2 (PB2), and polymerase protein
(PA)) [9]. Having a segmented genome allows IAV to be subjected to antigenic shift, that
causes the generation of new virus variants. It consists in the exchange and reassortments of

1.1.2
Replication cycle
The replication cycle of Inuenza A virus starts with its attachment to the host cell though
the binding between the viral HA and the sialic acid receptor on the host cell membrane.
Dierent cell lines have dierent composition in sialic acid receptors and the anity of
binding with dierent viral strain is aected, as previously described. After binding to host-
cell sialic acid receptors, IAV is internalized via receptor-mediated endocytosis. The acidic
environment inside the endosome is triggering conformational changes of HA, which enables
the fusion between the viral and the endosomal membranes, and the protons entrance in
the virion through the ion channel M2, causing internal acidication and virion dissociation,
releasing the RNP complex into the cytosol of the infected host cell [4, 9].
The RNP complex is transported into the host-cell nucleus thanks to the nuclear local-
ization sequence (NLS) present on the viral proteins. Due to the nature of the virus RNA,
the synthesis of the complementary positive stranded RNA has two functions: to transcribe
mRNAs and to give a full length positive-stranded RNA intermediate used as template for
genome replication by subsequent re-transcription into negative sense RNA. This transcrip-
tion is driven by the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase complex already present in the
virion structure. The mRNA export and translation requires: presence of the cap at the
5' end, given by cap snatching, a process in which the cap is stolen from cellular mRNAs;
polyadenylation of the 3' end, given by the presence of a polyuracile sequence on the negative

stranded RNA [4, 9]. The viral mRNA in the cytoplasm is translated into proteins exploit-
ing the translational system of the host. NP, M1, PA, PB1, PB2 and NS2/NEP proteins
are then transported again inside the nucleus to form the RNP complex. The RNP com-
plex associates with the NS2/NEP protein and is actively transported outside the nucleus.
Meanwhile, HA, NA and M2 are synthetized on the rough endoplasmatic reticulum (ER),
passed through and modied by the Golgi apparatus and directed to the host cell membrane
[4, 9].
At the end, budding occurs thanks to the M1 protein that interacts with both the cell
membrane-intermembrane proteins complex and the RNP complex. The virus is released
and, if all eight segments are present, able to infect other cells nearby or infect a new host
if excreted by aerosol.

1.2
Innate immunity
The immune system is the host important mechanism of defence against infections. It is
divided into two interconnected parts called innate and adaptive immune system. Innate
immunity is the rst activated system and it is characterized as an immediate and relatively
non-specic response. It is activated in the early phase of infection (0 to 96 hours) by a
wide range of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) targeting a broad spectrum of antigens,
without discriminating which antigen has already been encountered. Only when the innate
immunity fails in eliminating the pathogen, the adaptive immune response begins (after
96 hours).
It has the advantages of being pathogen and antigen specic and of giving
3

immunological memory, so that is possible to recognise and eliminate faster specic pathogen
when encountered again [13]. We will focus only on the innate immunity.
Innate immunity has the role of sensing pathogen as soon as they enter with the host
and block the infection in the early phase. The very rst defence of the organism is barriers
constituted by the epithelium covering the body internally and externally surfaces. Those
barriers act physically and chemically by blocking the pathogen, making dicult the sur-
vival of the pathogen, such as low pH and presence of proteases or defensins [13]. Some
pathogen may infect the host by wounds or, by physical or chemical damaging of the barrier
or by attachment and consequent receptor mediated entrance in the epithelial cells. In the
submucosa the pathogen can be recognised by the macrophages present in the tissue. These
cells have the function of phagocyting the pathogen and to produce and release cytokines
and chemokines able to attract other eector cells at the site of infection.
The mechanism by which the pathogen is sensed is by recognition of pathogen-associated
molecular patterns (PAMPs) by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs). PAMPs are dened
as recurrent molecular structures present in microbes but absent in the host. A common
example of PAMPs is lipopolysaccharide (LPS). The PRRs are present on a wide range of
cells, such as macrophages and dendritic cells, but also on e.g. epithelial cells [14, 13]. There
are four dierent classes of PRRs: Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and C-type lectin receptors
(CLRs) are transmembrane proteins, while retinoic acid-inducible gene(RIG)-I-like receptors
(RLRs) and nucleotide-binding oligomerization domain (NOD) like receptors (NLRs) are
cytoplasmatic. CLRs are present only on the plasma membrane, while TLRs are located
both on the plasma membrane and on the endolysosome membrane [14]. Upon activation,
PRRs stimulate the transcription of genes involved in inammatory response, such as pro-
inammatory cytokines and chemokines, including interferons (IFNs), interleukins (ILs) and
tumor necrosis factor (TNF) [14, 13]. Upon binding to their receptors, they further activates
several genes involved in enhancing phagocytosis by recruitment of eector cells, aecting
neighbour cell dierentiation and, in certain circumstances, giving apoptosis [13, 15]. Some
NLRs are found in the formation of the inammasome that cause activation of caspases
causing apoptosis [16].
All these events are aimed to clear the infection or at least to
temporarily block it while the adaptive immune system gradually builds up.
The innate immunity activation will be described more in detail for inuenza A virus
infection.
1.2.1
Antiviral innate immune response
Inuenza A virus infection starts with viral transmission to a new host and there are few
commonly accepted routes of infection: exposure to droplets or aerosol emitted when cough-
ing and sneezing that get into contact with the oral and nasal mucosal tissue; direct or
indirect contact with an infected host or contaminated objects [17].
Respiratory epithelia cells, however, are covered by a mucus layer that the IAV must go

through [18]. The mucus layer function are to maintain hydration, but also trapping external
stranger particles and pathogens, such as IAV. One of the most abundant components of the
mucus are mucins, highly glycosylated protein that have, among their properties, an high
sialic acid content [19]. The IAV binds to the sialic acid residues on mucins through HA.
The NA protein on the virus particle is capable of cleaving the sialic acid residues of mucins,
letting the virus go through the mucus layer [19, 20]. The infection starts when the HA on
the virion surface attach to the sialic acid receptor on the host cell membrane. Once entered
inside the host cell, the viral replication cycle starts as described in paragraph 1.1.2.
As soon as the IAV infection starts, immune response is triggered by pattern recogni-
tion receptors (PRRs), leading to chemokine and cytokine production that recruit innate
immunity eector cells, in particular macrophages and NK cells, and cause a general state
of inammation. The PRRs classes able to recognise IAV molecular patterns are Toll like
receptors (TLRs), retinoic acid-inducible gene(RIG)-I-like receptors (RLRs), and nucleotide-
binding oligomerization domain (NOD) like receptors (NLRs) [18]. A schematic overview of
the innate immunity signaling pathways is shown in gure 1.3 [21].

1.3
Explants isolated from pigs as a research tool
Inuenza A virus has been found to infect several dierent animals, from birds to many
mammalian species [1]. To better understand the IAV pathogenesis some of them have been
used as animal models, including pigs [2, 3]. In particular, pigs share a wide range of features
with humans that makes them ideal animal models for many studies. It is remarkable that
pigs have high similarities in anatomy, genetics, physiology and, very important in our study,
the immune system [3]. Pigs and humans are both infected by the same IAV subtypes (H1N1,
H1N2 and H3N2) and they show similar clinical symptoms upon infection. As described
before, the α-2,3 and α-2,6-linked sialic acid receptor distribution in the upper respiratory
tract is important for viral host tropism. Humans and pigs share a high similarity of sialic
acid receptor types distribution [8, 7, 3]. All those aspects thus suggest that pigs are a good
animal model for IAV infection studies.
The ex vivo organ culture system is an additional aspect that characterize the present
study. This system allows to accurately mimic the in vivo situation in respect to in vitro

(1959): replacement (nding alternatives to living animals), reduction (minimization of the

particular, using explants as a tool: we replace living animals with ex vivo organ culture;
we reduce the number of animal since more explants are produced from each pig; we rened

Explants isolated from the respiratory tract of the pig have been used in several other

Moreover, replication of IAV in ex vivo organ cultures has been reported [8, 27]. However,
host transcriptional responses upon culturing and viral infection has not been described in

Materials and Methods
2.1
Nasal explants experimental set-up
Prior to the actual experiment, a literature study has been carried out in order to assess which
parameters and respective values are important for culturing and infection of pig's nasal
explants. Parameters taken into consideration were: animal and type of tissue from which
the explant has been isolated; explant culture time; inuenza A virus strain used to infect
the explant; virus culture method; number of passages a cultured virus was subjected to;
virus suspension concentration used for inoculating the explant; presence in the media and,
in case, concentration of trypsin, a protease important during inuenza A infection; usage
of antibiotics and fungicides; time post harvest and incubation time of virus inoculation.
Based on this literature study, the optimal value for each parameter was assessed.
17 nasal mucosa explants (NE) were isolated from each of 3 6-weeks old piglets with
umbilical hernia provided from a herd located in Holbæk, Denmark, for a total amount of 51
explants isolated. Among these 48 were cultured while 3 were collected right after slaughter.
The 48 explants were cultured for 20 hours and then treated with mock-media or with the
virus A/swine/Denmark/12687/2003 (H1N2) with dierent propagation method and used
at dierent concentrations. Virus quantication prior to infection of explants was performed
by TCID50 assay (values in appendix A). The explants were then harvested at dierent time
points: 0hpi, 1hpi, 24hpi and 48hpi (hpi, hours post infection). For further details on how
the explants were extracted and cultivated and on the composition of the media, please refer
to the next sections.
2.2
Explants isolation, culture and inoculation

prolongatum vet (ScanVet, Fredensborg) for three days before slaughter. They have been
anesthetized by administration of Zoletil 50 VET (tiletamin 25 mg/ml, zolazepam 25 mg/ml)
solution (1ml/10kg). A blood sample was recovered in order to test them for possible pre-
existing serum anti-IAV antibodies. No sows were vaccinated in the farm from which the
piglets came from. Later on, the animals were euthanized using natrium pentobarbital 20%
(KELA, 300 mg/ml). The nasal mucosal explants have been isolated from the pig's snout

Virus quantication
Virus quantication of two original virus isolates and a subset of explants and media sam-
ples were quantied in this project is assessed using the tissue culture infectious dose 50
10
==PAGE:17==
Materials and Methods
(TCID50), also called end-point dilution assay. The subset of samples tested was: all sam-
ples with C and E treatment at 1 hpi, 24 hpi and 48 hpi for tissue samples and 24 hpi and 48
hpi for the supernatant; wash media harvested at 1 hpi for mock-media supernatant sample
pf pig 10 and pig 12); all samples at 48 hpi for the other two remaining treatments, C−x
and E−y; supernatant of pig 10 and pig 11 treated as E−y. The TCID50 value is a measure
of the concentration of the virus that is able to infect 50% of the cell culture. The TCID50
procedure can be divided into three phases: cell culture seeding, infection, and readouts.
Seeding cells consists in preparing a suspension of about 45'000 cells/ml of MDCK cells
in a 96-well plate 200 µl per well. The culturing media used is MEM containing 5% FBS,
L-Glutamine 2 mM, 1x non-essential aminoacids and 1x penicillin-streptomycin. The plate
is incubated overnight at 37°C, 5% CO2. We aim for about 70% conuence of the well for
the day after. A dilution row of the virus is made as described in appendix B. Cells are
inoculated with 200 µl per well of the viral dilution suspension and incubated at 37°C, 5%
CO2 for 3 days. Together with our samples, we used a positive control and just VGM as
negative control for each series of plates.
After 3 days of incubation, the virus dilution
in the plate is removed.
Cells are washed in cold PBS, xed adding 200 µl per well of
absolute ethanol (99% ethanol) and the plate is stored at -20°C. Two dierent readouts were
performed: turkey red blood cells hemagglutination and immunocytochemical staining.
Detection of infectious viral particles by turkey red blood cell hemagglutination in the
supernatant is performed. 50 µl of the virus dilution are transferred in a 96-well plate with
v-shaped bottom of the well. 50 µl of the 1% solution of turkey red blood cells in PBS is then
added to the 96-well plate and left at room temperature in the fume hood. After about 20
minutes, the erythrocyte sedimentation pattern is observed. If a sample is negative a single
red spot at the bottom of the well is formed, while if a sample shows turbid and disordered
sedimentation, it means that hemagglutination reaction happened between red blood cells
and virus particles. The former sample is negative and the latter positive. The number of
positive samples are registered and used for calculations as described below.
Immunocytochemical staining is instead performed on the cells previously xed in abso-
lute ethanol as described more in detail in appendix B. Cells are incubated with an in-house
primary polyclonal rabbit anti swine Inuenza A virus antibody, a secondary biotinylated
anti-rabbit antibody, a solution of HRP-streptavidin (Invitrogen) and ethyl-carbazole (di-
luted in ethanol, additioned with 0,05% of H2O2), that acts as substrate for the HRP enzyme
and gives the colouring. The reaction between HRP and ethyl carbazole must proceed in
absence of light. After washing the plates in milliQ water, it's possible to read the plates.
The staining is observed at the microscope, registering how many positive samples we have
through the dilution. A sample is positive if it has at least one stained cell.
For both immunocytochemical staining and turkey red blood cells hemagglutination read-
outs, we then proceeded with the calculation of the TCID50 in accordance with the Reed-
Muench method [29].

2.7
Data processing and analysis
The RT-qPCR experiment and data handling has been performed in accordance to the
Minimum information for publication of quantitative real-time PCR experiments (MIQE)
[30, 31]. Before the raw data from the qPCR can be analysed a pre-processing step is required
in order to discard the technical variation and leave only the relevant biological variation to
be analysed. The data set generated either by 96.96 or 192.24 dynamic arrays (Fluidigm)
were rst visually inspected, reactions were removed from any subsequent analysis if they
did not live up to the following criteria: single peak of the melting curve in order to insure
that the amplied product is specic, avoiding cases in which primer dimer and unspecic
products amplication occurred. Regarding the amplication curve, it is important to see if
the curve has a clear sigmoidal shape. Afterwards, it's important to examine the negative
controls the -RT (without reverse transcriptase) and the NTC (non template control). Data
from each gene (primer pair used) was then corrected for eciency, calculated based on the
slope of the standard curves. An eciency between 80% and 120% and a R2 value greater
than 0,95 were accepted.
If the samples from the same set-up have been put into two dynamic arrays, a rst step
of interplate calibration is needed. Subsequently data were normalized using highly stable
reference genes. The type and number of reference genes included was based on normnder
[32] and genorm [33]. In the 192.24 dynamic array we used 4 reference genes: GAPDH,
RPL13A (not used for normalization), HPRT1 and TBP. Instead on the 96.96 dynamic array
the chosen reference genes were: B2M (not used for normalization), RPL13A, PPIA, TBP,
YWHAZ and ACTB (not used for normalization). cDNA replicates with greater variation
than 1,5 or lower than -1,5 were highlighted before and after data normalization. We can
compare how many highlighted samples I have before and after normalization, expecting the

Materials and Methods
amount to be lower in the latter. If more than 15% of the samples show a Cq value dierence
higher than 1,5 or lower than -1,5 for a dened primer, the primer was removed from data.
Cq values was converted into quantities on a linear scale, relative to the sample with
maximum value of Cq (lower expression) to which a value of 1 is given. Data was then
reconverted into log2 scale and used for data analysis. Further analyses were done based
on quantities too, but additional calculation was done to have them relative to a specic
treatment or time as specied time by time.
Since the low numbers of animal used in this study, no statistical calculations were
performed but rather descriptive data analysis.
The standard deviation (SD) and standard error mean (SEM) were calculated for each
sample mean. Upregulated or downregulated genes are considered to be of biological impor-

A Principle Component Analysis (PCA) was performed using data in log2 scale. Through

Discussion
The RNA extracted from the explants and used for the Fluidigm high throughput RT-
qPCR shows a very good quality, integrity and quantity, giving a solid and reliable starting
material for the subsequent experiments. As only a small number of animal were used in
the present study gene expression analysis has been conducted in a descriptive way and
not statistically. The results obtained can still give some important take home-messages on
host-virus interaction and antiviral innate immunity.
PCA
The results obtained by PCA suggest that culturing time is an important parameter having
high impact over the NE expression proles, and clustering according to time were seen in
both infected and mock-media treated animals. Adaptation to the new in vitro environment
compared to natural in vivo settings plays an important role. Physical and chemical damage
that might occur during transport, isolation and harvest of the explant itself must also be
considered. It can be excluded that a damage has occurred at the RNA level since the
results for RNA integrity are good at all time points. Viability of the tissue over time was
not assessed in this particular study. However, several previous studies have assessed that
simlar ex vivo organ culture can remain into culture for up to 60-72 hours post harvest
without signicant morphologic changes [8, 27].
Gene regulation and key role of MX1, MX2 and RIG-I
Expression analysis showed regulation of genes known to be involved in anti-IAV innate
immune response, with particular focus on RIG-I, MX1 and MX2. The highest number of
genes were regulated at 24hpi compared to fewer at 1hpi and fewest at 48hpi.
What really is interesting is that the two most down-regulated genes at 1 hpi are also the
two most up-regulated genes at 24 hpi: RIG-I and MX2. This correlated regulation pattern
is however not seen at 48 hpi (see gure 3.6).
RIG-I is coding for a pattern recognition receptor (PRR) of the RLRs class. This protein
is present in the cytoplasm of immune and other cell types, such as epithelial cells. RIG-I has
been reported to have a key role in detecting viral genome of negative stranded RNA viruses,
such as Inuenza A virus [35]. RIG-I up-regulation upon IAV infection of the explant was
therefore expected.
27
==PAGE:34==
Discussion
MX1 and MX2 are interferon stimulated genes (ISG) coding for a dynamin-like large
GTPase protein that has been previously shown to confer protection against some viral
infections[36, 37]. Surprisingly, MX2 does not show any antiviral function against inuenza
A virus [36]. It is rather the paralogue gene MX1 that has been found to have an anti-
inuenza infection role by blocking the RNP viral core transport to the nucleus [37]. In this
study, both MX genes were found to be regulated with MX2 at a higher FC.
The up-regulation of PRR and ISG at 24 hpi suggest that explant infection with inuenza
A virus took place. Indeed, since RIG-I is an intracellular PRR, the virus must have been
able to enter in the cell and start replicating.
Subsequently stimulated MX genes than
exerted their antiviral function. The up-regulation of RIG-I was not extended to 48 hpi,
whereas MX1 and MX2 continued to be up-regulated (except in the case of E−y treatment
for MX1). This might be due to the fact that while RIG-I is stimulated in an early phase
of infection, MX genes are instead regulated at a later stage, after IFN production, sensing
and transcriptional stimulation.
Lack of full viral replication cycle
The virus is shown to be able to infect and replicate its viral RNA in some of the treatments
resulting in innate immune response stimuli, as described above, but it is not able to com-
plete a full replication cycle. Viral RNA quantity was measured by RT-qPCR using both by
Rotorgene and Biomark (Fluidigm) instruments. The Fluidigm dynamic arrays are consid-
ered more sensitive than Rotorgene for RT-qPCR because it is a two-step RT-qPCR instead
of one-step as the latter. Data from Fluidigm assay are partly in disagreement with the
ones obtained with Rotorgene. These dierences might be accountable for dierent preser-
vation and storage conditions, RNA extraction protocols and RT-qPCR technique used to
measure viral RNA amount. Further, during viral gene analysis, after the Rotorgene based
technique, data was not normalized. Normalization to remove technical variation due to e.g.
pipetting errors or cDNA synthesis eciency was only performed on the data from Fluidigm
dynamic array. Viral RNA is not detected in many of the supernatant samples and in both
supernatant and tissue samples, no infectious viral particles were found by TCID50. Not
detectable viral RNA for most of the supernatant samples suggest that there is no sustained
viral shedding into media upon infection. Budding of completely formed virions does not
occur in any of the samples, not even the ones in which RNA was seen to increase, suggesting
that even if IAV infection take place, the viral complete replication cycle does not appear to
happen.
Eects of propagation method
Data from both instruments suggest that a more ecient infection and RNA replication occur
in explants infected with virus propagated in embryonated chicken eggs compared to virus
propagated in MDCK cells, despite the initial concentration of the virus suspension. Indeed,

the initial virus suspension concentration seems to inuence only the absolute amount of viral

Eects of handling and culturing
Doubts arisen on the role of culturing and handling of explants and their eect on the
transcriptional point of view had led to the setting up of a second gene expression analysis
experiment that will be further discussed, in which a wider range of samples was analysed.
The PCA clustering pattern over time for experiments L and V is exactly the same as it
was seen previously for the samples of the experiment A alone. The same behaviour observed
in the experiment A is seen also for the experiment L and V. Thus, only slight dierences
in culturing eects over time occurred in the three dierent experiments. The fact that
culturing eect over time are similar in experiments A and L suggests that culturing might
not be the main reason for the absence of viral shedding and complete replication cycle
observed in experiment A (and also V), that however occurred in experiment L (data not
included, [28]). Likewise, the unproductive replication can not be attributed to handling
procedures aecting the overall integrity of the explant. Similarity in expression proles
between mock-inoculated NE might be interpreted as similarity of experimental parameters.
This means that even if virus growth media (VGM) composition and location were dierent
in experiments A and L, the overall explant integrity and translationally response during
culture is not aected by these parameters. The only dierence between the experiments A
and L consisted in the complete virus replication cycle that occurred in L and not A. It is
possible that the VGM composition has aected the viral replication eectivity on explants
even though it did not impact the replication eectivity on cell culture nor inuenced the
overall related host immune gene expression. On the other hand, the concentration of an-
tibiotics and fungicides in the media might be the parameter responsible for the expression
prole dierence between the samples of the experiments L and A compared to V.

Conclusion and future perspectives
At the end of this study, it can be concluded that ex vivo organ culture of nasal mucosa
explants, is a promising tool for studying inuenza A virus (IAV) infection, but further
optimization is needed before it can be used as standard experimental assay. Gene expres-
sion analysis showed regulation of genes known to be involved in antiviral innate immune
response. The virus was able to infect the tissue and propagate its viral RNA within the
host cell. However, and most importantly, no infectious viral particles could be isolated from
the media during cultivation. Not nding infectious virions in the supernatant might be a
result of lack of viral budding from the host cell. Handling and culturing eects have been
analysed in mock media treated samples and showed that the gene expression prole is not
much dierent from a previous experiment L in which viral replication and infectious viral
particles occurred.
In conclusion, further optimization is needed but the lack of infectious particle seen in
this study does not close any hope that pig nasal mucosa explants could be used in future
as an ex vivo model for IAV infection.
In future explant studies I would suggest to use the VGM media composition used in
experiment L with all the other parameters and settings used in experiment A. Since the
undiluted egg propagated viral strain showed a higher viral RNA replication and was much
easier to detect, I would also suggest using this virus propagation methods for future exper-
iments. Future perspectives might also concern further analyses of several other dierent
viral genes, and not only the matrix and HA genes, by Fluidigm high throughput RT-qPCR.
Moreover, I suggest including the supernatant samples that were not included in the Flu-
idigm dynamic arrays of the present study. This would not explain why infectious viral
particles were not found by TCID50 but would give a more sensitive measurement of viral
RNA quantity. It needs also to be considered whether no virus particles were found because
they were not present in the supernatant samples or because they were present but in such
low amount that the TCID50 assay was not sensitive enough.

mucosa from the septum, and release it by gently “cutting” the adhesions between the mucosa and the septum 
with a scalpel (light touches with a scalpel is sufficient, do not attempt to use cutting movements). When the 
wished amount of mucosa is released from the septum, it is transferred to a Petri dish with preheated TM, be 
careful to place the tissue with the epithelial side facing upwards. 
 
Mucosa from conchae: the procedure is very much like septum. Define the area of interest with an incision along 
the edge using a scalpel. Grab the edge with a scalpel and lift the mucosa from the underlying cartilage. Most 
often it will not be necessary to break the adhesions using a scalpel; the mucosa on the conchae is very easily 
released. Transfer the dissected mucosa to a Petri dish with preheated TM - remember to make sure that the 
epithelial side is facing upwards. 
 
If mucosa from the opposite half of the snout is needed, is this part opened in the same fashion as before: cut 
the lower and upper parts along the septum, take care not to touch and damage the mucosa. Break the septum 
and conchae parts from each other (keep an eye on not disrupting/tearing the mucosa), and release the desired 
amount of mucosa. 
2-3 spare pieces of mucosal tissue are collected and stabilized in RNA later. 
Mucosa from the septum and conchae will be isolated in Bülowsvej (Fredriksberg) and transferred in TM in a 
petri dish with the epithelium side upwards at 37 degrees to Lyngby. 
 
Dissection and culturing nasal explants (NE): when cutting in the isolated mucosa: use a sharp scalpel and a 
downwards pressure, no cutting movements. The edges may have some mechanical damage from the extraction 
(e.g. where the tweezer was used to hold onto the tissue), these are trimmed. The trimmed mucosa is dissected 
into approximately 0.5x0.5 cm squares. We need 15 explants per animal and 1 extra as 0 hpi if possible. The 
modified cell strainer and 2ml preheated DM is placed in the wells of a 6-well plate. The mucosal squares are 
placed on the cell strainers, in the interphase of the media and with air on the epithelial side. Explants are 
cultured around 15 hr at 37 °C, 5 % CO2, before inoculation. 
 
==PAGE:44==
Wash and inoculation of NE: After 20h (from 14.00 to 10.00) hours of cultivation each NE is placed in a well of a 
12-well dish. Wash is performed twice in 0.5ml preheated VM. 0.6 ml virus suspension/mock-medium is added 
to each NE, and is incubated for 1hr at 37°C, 5 % CO2. The virus suspension/mock-medium is removed by a 
pipette, and additional 3 washes are performed in 0.5ml preheated VM. Leave the NE in the VM at the last wash, 
while preparing the 6 well dishes for re-introduction of the NE: remove as much of the old DM as possible, leave 
the cell strainers in the wells, add 2ml of fresh, preheated DM. Remove the last VM from the NE in the 12 well 
dish, and place the NE back into the 6 well dish. From this point count “hours post inoculation” (hpi) – this time 
is 1 hpi.
