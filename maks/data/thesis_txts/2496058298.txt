We have established a global economy that 
erodes our natural resources and confl icts 
with the natural capaciti es of the earth. Shift -
ing towards a more Circular Economy would 
not only stabilize and regenerate resources 
but would provide a prosperous opportunity 
for enterprises and the business industry. A 
circular transiti on requires that the current 
systems are challenged and restructured. 
Circular transiti on processes are oft en 
prolonged and highly resource-demanding, 
while focus remains on opti misati on instead 
of more radical change. Since design provides 
methods of concept development and crea-
ti ve co-creati on, the thesis explores the role 
of design in the context of circular transiti on. 
The thesis focuses on the method Design 
Sprint which is applied to a new context 
on a real-life case. The method is tested 
and adapted as part of a circular transiti on 
project driven by an NGO (non-governmen-
tal organisati on) called Gate21. The project 
‘Bæredygti g Bundlinje Bornholm’ (Sustaina-
ble Bott om-line Bornholm) facilitates a long-
term circular transiti on process with small 
and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). This to 
develop greener business models and opti -
mise their energy- and resource use. 
A literature background of the domains 
Sustainable Development, Systems Think-
ing and Design Sprint is conducted to build 
a theoreti cal understanding of the fi eld. 
Empricical data is collected from fi eld stud-
ies, through interviews and interventi on with 
diff erent of stakeholders associated with the 
case. Design Sprint is conceptualised through 
conti nuous prototyping, testi ng and adap-
tati on of the concept. The concept reaches 
a fi delity of a 1:1 Design Sprint workshop 
which is tested with two case SMEs. The 
concept is refi ned and opportunies of inte-
grati ng Design Sprint as part of the case 
organisati on is explored  through a series 
of interventi ons. Tools to distribute knowl-
edge and experiences, and thereby enhance 
integrati on, are developed as a fi nal design 
contributi on.
The results indicate that Design Sprint 
processes are able to support SMEs in 
the circular transiti on. This by translat-
ing complex challenges into concreti sed 
concepts in a short amount of ti me, with the 
potenti al to accelerate and enhance imple-
mentati on. The thesis has identi fi ed multi ple

nary creati ve collaborati on enhances novelty

01 Introduction
Designers have been key drivers in building 
the society we have today. A society, that 
since the industrial revoluti on has acceler-
ated the economy by eroding the earth’s 
resources and natural systems. Therefore, 
we need to rethink and redesign society to 
circulate and regenerate resources to a much 
greater extent. 
This thesis examines what role design can 
play in the sustainable transiti on, and how 
systemic change towards sustainability can 
be enabled by design. The thesis explores the 
connecti ons between the domains Sustain-
able Development, Systems Thinking and 
Design Sprint, both in theory and in practi ce. 
The thesis applies and tests Design Sprint in 
a real-life case of circular transiti on of small 
and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). The 
design method is applied within a context of 
circular transiti on, to develop new tangible 
links between these areas and examine what 
impact can be created. 
Literature points towards design having an 
enabling role within sustainable transiti on. 
Design has an ability to translate abstract 
understandings into something concrete, 
which might be a core quality in solving 
complex issues, such as the sustainable tran-
siti on of society. However, tangible examples 
of how it can be applied and practi sed within 
this context, are insuffi  ciently represented. 
Design Sprint is a method based on Design 
Thinking and other agile methodologies, 
which has gained a lot of att enti on in respect 
to accelerate development and test new 
business ideas among start-ups, as well as 
big corporati ons. The method is comprised of 
multi disciplinary teamwork, creati ve ideati on, 
human-centred approaches and prototype 
testi ng in a ti me-constrained process. Design 
Sprint, traditi onally developed for digital 
product development, will be applied and 
adapted to the context of circular transiti on 
A core part of transiti oning society towards 
sustainability is to push how enterpris-
es produce, use and dispose of materials. 
Parti cularly, SMEs fi nd it challenging to 
realise the potenti als of a Circular Economy, 
due to insuffi  cient knowledge and capacity to 
transiti on. Nati onal funding strategies have 
enabled organisati ons to initi ate regional 
projects to facilitate the circular transiti on of 
SMEs. Gate21 is an NGO (non-governmental 
organisati on), who work to accelerate green 
transiti on and growth together with regions, 
municipaliti es, enterprises and knowledge 
insti tuti ons of Greater Copenhagen. Gate21 
runs a series of sustainability projects, among 
these a project called ‘Bæredygti g Bundlinje 
Bornholm’ (English ti tle: Sustainable Bott om-
line Bornholm), that focuses on helping SMEs 
to develop new green business models and 
enhance their energy- and resource eff ec-
ti veness. This thesis is conducted in collab-
orati on with Gate21, and is associated with 
‘Bæredygti g Bundlinje Bornholm’. Testi ng and 
adapti ng Design Sprint into the context of 
this regional project and examining what role 
design can play in an NGO like Gate21. 
This thesis raises awareness for the role of 
design in sustainable development, challeng-
ing the current percepti on of design among 
practi ti oners and current work practi ses 
within this fi eld. The thesis brings a pragmat-
ic approach to investi gati ng and developing 
design as an enabler of sustainable transiti on.

1.3.2 Systems Design approach
The thesis establishes the hypothesis: 
To achieve radical and sustained change, 
impact on all system levels must be considered 
and accomplished. 
Therefore, the thesis combines two core 
approaches inherent in the domains of 
Systems Thinking and Design Thinking, 
respectively the N-Model (42090, Lecture 
02: Elements of Engineering Systems 
Design, 2018)  and the Double Diamond 
(Design Council, 2015). The Systems Design 
approach is visualised in figure 2. The 
N-Model represent the systemic approach, 
where the Engineering, Complex and Arte­
fact system level are continuously consid­
ered. The N-Model outlines three core phas­
es, System analysis, System Requirements 
and System Integration, which represents the 
core milestones of this thesis. However, the 
N-Model omits a vital aspect of the approach 
performed in this thesis, which is represent­
ed in the Double Diamond.  The Double 
Diamond represents the design process that 
combines divergent and convergent think­
ing in continuous iterations, through the 
phases Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver. 
The Double Diamond supports thinking that 
entails human-centred approaches. Howev­
er, since the thesis suggests that radical and 
sustained change require a system-centred 
approach, it is combined with the systems 
thinking present in the N-model.  Following 
this mindset, the design approach of the 
thesis has been a combination of the two 
models.
The thesis has had an agile approach, where 
fieldwork has generated the core founda­
tion of data and where objects continuously 
have been placed in “real” environments, to 
intervene and receive feedback, contributing 
to the development of the final design. This 
aspect manifests itself as a cornerstone of 
the design approach.

1.3.3 Field work
The thesis takes on a constructi vist stance 
while scatt ering across other philosophical 
stances, all building the foundati on to select 
research methods and judging what consti -
tutes as valid knowledge (Shull, Singer, & 
Sjøberg, 2008). A constructi vist believes that 
scienti fi c knowledge should always be ti ed 
to the context in which it is based, focusing 
on the social interacti ons that occur. Explor-
atory case studies and qualitati ve data are 
therefore commonly used in this philosoph-
ical stance (Shull et al., 2008). The research 
methods chosen in this thesis are case study 
and qualitati ve data gathering, conducted in 
parallel with the system analysis. The means 
of fi eldwork are elaborated in the following 
secti ons.  
Case study
A case is used to bring the theoreti cal knowl-
edge to a real-life context, that gives a specif-
ic example of how and why certain things 
occur. The case is both exploratory and 
confi rmatory (Shull et al., 2008) as it is used 
to gather empirical data through interviews 
and observati ons, as well as performing as 
context of applying Design Sprint in practi se. 
The case in an NGO running circular transi-
ti on projects, in which they perform hands-
on transiti onal processes with SMEs. 
Qualitati ve data gathering
Qualitati ve data is gathered through inter-
views and observati ons throughout the 
project. Interviews are conducted with key 
actors in the case, mapping their needs and 
challenges, as well as interrelati ons. Obser-
vati ons are conducted during workshops 
part of the case. This has enabled and iden-
ti fi cati on of challenges and needs, having 
a “fl y on the wall” approach. Additi onally, 
semi-structured interviews  are performed, 
moving towards a more acti ve collecti on of 
qualitati ve data. 
1.3.4 Interventi on process
As part of both gathering qualitati ve data 
and develop fi nal design contributi ons, 
both Prototype testi ng, Design Sprint tests 
and Design Sprint imitated interventi ons is 
conducted (fi gure 3). This work has acted as a 
pilot project able to generate knowledge and 
make a “proof of concept” in future circular 
transiti on projects of the case organisati on.  
The interventi ons have formed an iterati ve 
development process of conceptualising and 
applying Design Sprint, as well as refi ning 
and integrati ng the method into the real-life 
context of circular transiti on. 
Prototype testi ng
Multi ple prototype tests are conducted to 
iterati vely conceptualise Design Sprint in 
the specifi c context of the case. The proto-
types manifest themselves through diff er-
ent objects and in diff erent environments, 
through multi ple iterati ons.  They represent 
Boundary Objects (Leigh Star, 2010) to 
provoke discussion and concreti se feedback. 
In the preliminary testi ng phase, the Design 
Sprint concept manifests itself in a work-
shop script, that is discussed with the case 
organisati on. When reaching a higher level 
of fi delity, it is manifested as a 1:1 Design 
Sprint workshop. The Design Sprint concept 
is tested with two case SMEs, creati ng a 
core foundati on of further development (see 
‘Design Sprint tests’ below). 
Design Sprint tests
As part of applying and refi ning the concept

02 Literature Background
2.1 Sustainable development 
We have built a global civilisation that 
pays little or no regard to the fundamental 
processes that enable a healthy functioning 
of our natural systems. Wahl (2016) puts it 
as “our current economic system disrespects 
our planetary boundaries” since economic 
arguments are justifying and driving erosion 
of natural resilience. It has created an imbal­
ance of a high amount of natural resources 
utilised and depleted, compared to natural 
resources brought back into the system. 
Over time this has led to the degradation of 
ecosystems and scarcity of raw materials. 
As a response to a capitulating earth system, 
a movement towards a more sustainable 
future has had its uprising during the last 
century. The World Commission on envi­
ronment and development (1987) defines 
sustainable development as meeting “[..] the 
needs of the present without compromising 
the ability of future generations to meet 
their own needs”. Hildebrandt (p. 15, 2016) 
presents the 21st century as the period 
of sustainable development, entailing the 
implementation of the sustainable devel­
opment goals (SDG’s), the understanding of 
the planetary boundaries and efforts within 
robustness and resilience. The SDG’s is a 
framework that presents tangible goals on 
how humans can take responsible action 
to tackle the imbalance seen in the cycles 
of the planet (Hildebrandt, 2016). The 12th 
development goal focuses on responsible 
consumption and production, an inherent 
and core goal for this thesis as well. It aims to 
ensure sustainable and effective use of natu­
ral resources, decrease food waste, reduce 
the amount of waste being generated and 
ensure responsible handling of chemicals and 
waste (Hildebrandt, 2016). Means for accom­
plishing this SDG are to motivate enterprises 
to act and document their sustainable initi­
atives, and generally ensure awareness and 
knowledge among the world’s citizens. 
2.1.1 Circular Economy
Circular Economy has explicit connections 
to sustainable development, in the aims 
of pursuing economic prosperity, environ­
mental quality and social equity (Kirchherr, 
Reike, & Hekkert, 2017). Since the industrial 
revolution linear economy – the ‘take-make-
use-dispose’ model – has been dominant as 
society’s pattern of consumption. Circular 
Economy involves re-directing the linear 
material flows into restorative and regen­
erative cycles. A coherent description that 
encapsulates the essence of Circular Econo­
my is written by Kirchherr et al (2017):
“A Circular Economy describes an economic 
system that is based on business models 
which replace the ‘end-of-life’ concept with 
reducing, alternatively reusing, recycling 
and recovering materials in production/
distribution and consumption processes, 
thus operating at the micro, meso and 
macro level, with the aim to accomplish 
sustainable development, which implies 
creating environmental quality, economic 
prosperity and social equity, to the benefit 
of current and future generations.” 
Circular Economy covers the activities of 
‘reduce, reuse, recycle and recover’, called 
the 4R framework, all part of strategic 
actions transforming the economy from 
linear towards circular (Kirchherr et al, 2017). 
‘Recover’ entails incineration of waste-pro­
ducing energy, that can be retransferred back 
into our district heating systems. ‘Recycle’ 
imply reprocessing of materials manufactured 
into new products, and ‘Reuse’ enables an 
increase of material-effectiveness in connec­
tion with repair or refurbishment. Lastly, 
‘Reduce’ entails decreasing or eliminating 
material use. With Circular Economy being 
promoted widely, the most policy focus has 
been on recycling, although in literature the 
==PAGE:27==
27
02 Literature Background
circularity of recycling, are questioned. Kirch­
herr et al (2017) argue practitioners’ frequent 
neglect of reducing and instead empha­
sis towards recycle, are connected with, 
assumingly, that reduce can imply curbing 
consumption and economic growth. Reduce 
is a key element in the waste hierarchy, 
however has received less attention in the 
last decade. If reduce is not explicated, enter­
prises might choose the transformation path 
found “easiest” to adopt to, only improving 
their recycling and not reducing over all other 
Rs (Kirchherr et al, 2017). Consequently, 
Circular Economy introduces a new model 
for enterprises to transform their business, 
minimising and/or eliminating resource use 
of production and consumption. 
2.1.2 Circular transformation of enterprises
Circular adoption can mitigate price vola­
tility and supply risks, trigger new means 
of employment and create resilient growth, 
shifting the economy away from energy-in­
tensive materials and primary extraction 
(The Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2012). 
Enterprises are starting to see the benefits 
of rethinking their business, however even 
though academically promoted and politi­
cally encouraged, Circular Economy has not 
widely been put into practise (Andrews, 
2015). Kirchherr et al (2018) present core 
barriers of transitioning towards a Circular 
Economy, being culturally related. Lack of 
consumer interest and awareness and hesi­
tant company culture are main obstacles. 
These are self-reinforcing as both parts 
expect to respond to one another. Circular 
Economy is often restricted to less influential 
departments, e.g. environmental depart­
ments, instead of operations and finance. 
Furthermore, enterprises find it difficult to 
collaborate across value chains, since Circu­
lar Economy is not as distributed among all 
players. These findings suggest that Circu­
lar Economy appears to be a niche among 
sustainable development professionals. The 
study concludes that not a single techno­
logical barrier is ranked high as a significant 
barrier to transition towards circularity.
To overcome the acknowledged barriers and 
initiate successful transitions towards Circu­
lar Economy, literature presents approaches 
and strategies. Kirchherr et al (2018) state 
that current market barriers can be influ­
enced by governmental interventions accel­
erating the transition. Ghisellini (2019) put 
forward, that creating favourable contexts 
for innovation supporting enterprises and 
contributing to reduced risks and uncertainty 
related to investment, enhance creativity and 
development of competencies. Education is 
a key driver to create new skills-sets, mana­
gerial and technical competencies that can 
promote changes in life cycle processes. 
In the last decade, circular business models 
have been emphasised as a key enabler to 
circular transition however, tangible connec­
tions between business models within the 
Circular Economy discourse are only minimal­
ly covered. The Ellen MacArthur Foundation 
(2012) highlights novel business models 
being a significant enabler, advocating the 
need of a ‘functional service’ model where 
manufacturers or retailers keep ownership of 
products, yet selling use of the products to 
consumers. 
Transitioning towards a more circular model 
shows core benefits for enterprises, but 
competencies are not sufficiently distributed 
and processes not yet systematised.
 
2.1.3 System perspective in circular transi­
tion
In the last decade, more emphasis has been 
brought towards systems perspective within 
Circular Economy. Circular transition requires 
us to fundamentally rethink and restructure 
production, distribution and consumption 
processes, not only pursuing recycling but

not only focuses on individual behaviour but 
the holistic orchestration of elements across 
the system structure (Burns et al., 2006). A 
system is a set of elements with inherent 
qualities that when combined, create new 
qualities, so-called emergent properties of 
the system (42090, Lecture 02: Elements of 
Engineering Systems Design, 2018). Systems 
Thinking aims to understand the behaviour 
of the system, defining the elements and 
interrelations inherent in the system from 
which the emergent properties arise.  De 
Weck et al (2011) underlines that thinking 
holistically is no “silver bullet”, since a system 
without boundaries would incline the observ­

structure and conclusive understandings, 
limits is crucial to define.
Engineering Systems are a core part of 
fulfilling important functions of society; 
transporting people, information and matter, 
providing energy, producing products etc. 
What characterises an engineering system, 
is that they exist in the real world, are partly 
or entirely artificial, have dynamic properties, 
a hybrid state and more or less controlled 
by humans (Olivier L. de Weck, Daniel Roos, 
2011). When a system consists of interacting 
and/or interrelated people, e.g. employees in 
enterprises, this system is a socio-technical 
system, comprising both sociological and 
technical elements (Züst & Troxler, 2006).
In both Engineering systems and Circu­
lar Economy, the system perspective is a 
multi-level structure, moving from a bigger 
to a smaller scale. The Engineering Systems 
perspective comprises the framework of 
the Engineering System Level, the Complex 
System Level and lastly the Artefact System 
Level, while the Circular Economy are struc­
tured as mentioned in 2.1.3. For the sake 
of simplicity, the thesis will focus on the 
framework and terminology presented in 
Engineering Systems Theory however, impli­
cate elements from Circular Economy when 
defining and elaborating the case specific for 
this project.
2.2.1 Sustainable transition of systems
Having touched upon the definition of a 
system, the thinking of systems and how 
to observe them, this section will draw the 
link between Sustainable Development and 
Systems Thinking, by elaborating on how to 
transition these systems towards sustain­
ability and what means can be used in this 
process. Gaziulusoy & Ryan (2017) state that 
“system innovations and transitions involves 
striving towards the ideals of the waste 
hierarchy (Kirchherr et al, 2017). A transition 
towards Circular Economy needs to occur at 
three system levels presented as the Micro 
level (products, companies, consumers), 
Meso level (eco-industrial parks) and Macro 
level (city, region, nation and beyond) (Kirch­
herr et al, 2017). Kirchherr (2017) state that 
Circular Economy requires complete reform 
of human activity on all levels however, most 
connections in literature are drawn towards 
the macro level. Having a systems perspec­
tive, simultaneously taking all levels into 
account, may enable a holistic view on flows 
and connections over time, and potentially 
realise radical adoption of Circular Economy 
principles and pursuing regenerative condi­
tions, not only rethinking parts of the system. 
The need for intervening on all system layers 
demand having a Systems Thinking approach. 
           
2.2 Systems Thinking
Society is increasingly faced with complex 
challenges, highly messy and ambiguous in 
nature, connected to multiple elements on 
a bigger and smaller scale (Burns, Cottam, 
Vanstone, & Winhall, 2006). The transition 
of these systems towards a more sustaina­
ble future requires structural and systematic 
changes, a Systems thinking approach, that

reconceptualisation of whole systems and a 
creative imagination of alternative and desir­
able futures”. According to Loorbach (2010), 
transition and system innovations require 
three fundamental activities: 1. Strategic 
activities, 2. Tactical activities and 3. Oper­
ational activities. Strategic activities involve 
the formation of long-term goals with a great 
focus towards, not just product and servic­
es, but the transition of norms, values and 
social identity. Tactical activities can entail 
actions that can cause a change in structure, 
such as investments, regulation, incentives 
and underlying infrastructures, all part of 
an agenda that can lead to desired goals 
and relate to interactions between different 
actors. Here, an important aspect is that the 
actors involved are capable of “translating” 
the transition vision and consequences to 
their own transition agenda in their own 
organisation. Operational activities relate 
to transitional experiments and disruptive 
actions or innovations, that might enable 
radical changes, and, if successfully complet­
ed, scale-up into higher levels of the system. 
Transition experiments are “iconic projects 
with a high level of risk that can make a 
potentially large innovative contribution to a 
transition process” (Loorbach, 2010). Exper­
iments can take years and be costly, which 
is why there should be a focus on creating a 
portfolio of experiments all contributing to 
the overall objective, that can be scaled and 
measured (Loorbach, 2010). Design provides 
an approach to collaborate and develop new 
desirable futures, and therefore might be 
able to have a role within the transitional 
activities towards sustainability. 
2.2.2 The role of design in sustainable tran­
sitions 
The challenges of sustainable transition 
entail both technical, socio-cultural and 
behavioural issues – these challenges are 
called wicked problems. Design can arguably 
help to frame and to solve these challenges 
(Dorst, 2003). In design, humans and systems 
are observed in their contexts, from which 
insights are derived, framing a problem with 
emphasis on the human experience. Chris­
tian Bason (Head of Danish Design Center) 
explains in a podcast (Center for offentlig 
innovation, 2019) that designers have the 
ability to look at the problem, understand the 
“owner” of the problem and the context in 
which they are present. He says that design­
ers have creative power in ideation which 
can connect actors across organisations, 
citizens etc. and “transform abstract ideas or 
solutions into something tangible” (Center 
for offentlig innovation, 2019). Christian 
Bason finds this competence crucial to create 
change in any society. 
In the discourse with design and system 
innovation, the role of design is seen as 
inspiring, informing and triggering large, 
systemic, societal transformations towards 
sustainability. A tangible example of a 
recently launched toolkit connecting Design 
Thinking and circular transition is the Circular 
Design Guide (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 
2017), developed by the leading roles with­
in their respective fields, IDEO and Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation. However, design and 
transitional innovation are yet an emerging 
practise, why fields are pointing towards new 
research and practise areas to be developed 
(Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 2017).
Design is generally in society, understood 
by the output it creates, which means that 
“without a product or a process it remains 
somewhat invisible” (Gaziulusoy & Ryan, 
2017). Design can play a very tangible, 
technical and skill-based role in sustainable 
transitions (e.g. sustainable product design), 
but an opportunity for design to contribute 
through “invisible” intangible roles of receiv­
ing, processing and synthesising information 
are seen as well. Wahl (2016) define this role

as “the immaterial dimension of the metade­
sign of concepts and inclusive multiperspec­
tives from which a holistic/integral world­
view can emerge”. The Design Council (Burns 
et al., 2006) see the design process, and the 
skills inherent in designing, being a part of 
the approach to tackle complex issues. The 
human-centred approach can enable collab­
oration across disciplines, “a rapid, iterative 
process that can adapt to changing circum­

2.3 Design Sprint
Design Sprint is based on Design Thinking 
and other agile methodologies, but whilst 
Design thinking is flexible, Design Sprint 
prevails a more formatted method, with a  
predetermined process and roles of the team 
(Mendonça de Sá Araújo, Miranda Santos, 
Dias Canedo, & Favacho de Araújo, 2019). 
Taking a step back, looking into what Design 
is, this section will elaborate on Design 
Thinking to understand what mindsets and 
methods lies inherent within this domain and 
then what and how Design Sprint has origi­
nated from these mindsets. 
2.3.1 Design Thinking
Today, several companies and organisa­
tions have incorporated Design Thinking 
to continuously redefine themselves and 
create change (Brenner & Uebernickel, 
2016). Design Thinking is often explained 
in a certain dualism between ‘Thinking’ and 
‘Doing’, since it characterises how design­
ers approach and make sense of the world, 
but also how they actually do it (Kimbell, 
2012). Brown (2008) CEO of the world-lead­
ing practitioner of Design Thinking IDEO, 
defines Design Thinking as a human-cen­
tred activity that exists in the intersections 
of three concerns: Desirability, Feasibility 
and Viability. Design thinking is a combi­
nation of divergent and convergent with 
a strong orientation towards the needs of 
the users, structured in a micro as well as a 
macro process. The micro process consists of 
following steps; ‘Defining the problem’ (often 
referred to as Empathise), ‘Need finding 
==PAGE:31==
31
02 Literature Background
and Synthesis’ (often referred to as Define), 
‘Ideate’, ‘Prototype’ and ‘Test’, that contin­
uously iterate within the macro process, 
consisting of micro iterations that each 
prototype development represents (Brenner 
& Uebernickel, 2016).  At a recent confer­
ence about Design Thinking in sustainability, 
an entrepreneur articulated his definition as 
“Design thinking is more of a mindset, where 
you find the fitting method, or even adapt­
ing methods, for the given challenge you are 
about to solve” (Conference, 2019). Design 
Thinking is adaptable to who and where it 
is used, however mainly mastered by the 
designer. Even though highly practised across 
fields, Design Thinking has met resistance 
in some respects. Kimbell (2012) questions 
designers being the main agents in design, 
suggesting that design should become a set 
of routines collectively and across fields, 
emerging in the context of intervention. 
Wetter-Edman, Vink, & Blomkvist (2018) 
however, warns against applying general­
ised Design Thinking approaches, as this can 
cause less attention to situational aspects, 
namely continuously finding the methods 
relevant for the certain context. Pedersen 
& Clausen (2019) claim the importance of 
actors across disciplines and value chains, 
co-creating values in order to align interests.
2.3.2 Design Sprint process
Design Sprint is a design process put on a 
“recipe”. The process is traditionally devel­
oped for digital product development, 
however over time widely expanded over 
many different challenges. One of the most 
prominent is Google Ventures’ 5-day Design 
Sprint (Knapp, Zeratsky, & Kowitz, 2016). 
This thesis will mainly focus on exactly this 
framework. 
A Design Sprint is structured into five 
defined phases, which in Google terminol­
ogy are referred to as “Five days to solve 
big problems and test new ideas”. Design 
Sprint enables not just the designer, but 
several disciplines to execute and facili­
tate a design process. Sprints are designed 
to reduce politics, increase collaboration 
across functions and in addition to under­
stand the problem, also get answers and 
feedback, that will complete the iteration 
(Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 2019). Its 
structure allows organisations more easily 
to mobilize human resources, bring agility 
and unity of the team into the organisation, 
as well as create an incentive to accelerate 
and innovate organisational culture. Design 
Sprint serves as a way to focus on a specific 
problem, test a tangible solution, and without 
huge investments, learn the quality of the 
development path. Design Sprint is a fixed 
framework however, adapted and further 
developed since the Design Sprint book by 
Google Ventures was launched. AJ&Smart 
(Design Sprint Agency | Google Design 
Sprints | AJ&Smart) have in collaboration 
with Jake Knapp develop Design Sprint 2.0 
(Jonathan Courtney, 2018) and other Design 
Agencies has as well condensed the Sprint 
to fewer, rearranged exercises and put their 
touch on the creative method. This following 
description of Design Sprint will focus on 
the initial framework launched by Google 
Ventures, briefly elaborate on the content of 
the five days (figure 5). See specific exercises 
that correspond to each day of the Sprint in 
Appendix C.

Monday: Understanding the problem and 
mapping needs
The first day, the team will dive into the 
problem, to understand the overall challenge. 
The team formulates a ‘Long term goal’, that 
describes the desired impact within two 
years and map ‘Sprint questions’ that docu­
ment all fears and potential obstacles, the 
team can meet in reaching the long-term 
goal. The team then maps a simplified User 
Journey, that focuses on the involved users 
and their needs and goals. Hereafter, by 
sharing the knowledge present across disci­
plines, the team goes through the exercise 
called ‘Ask the experts’, from which insights 
are clustered and converted to ‘HMW’s’ 
(How-might-we do this?) that through its 
phrasing engage to ideation. The team ends 
the day by selecting a customer/user and 
part of the map, that they want to focus on 
forward in the Design Sprint.  
Tuesday: Sketching ideas
On the second day, the team converges 
looking into inspiration and conducting 
a sequential ideation exercise. The team 
looks into solutions from other companies 
or organisation, from which they produce 
‘Lightning Demos’, that briefly communicate 
external ideas to other team members. Then, 
the Four-step sketch is initiated, where team 
members individually undergo different idea­
tion exercises. Finally, all team members have 
a Solution Sketch, ready to be presented the 
next day. 
Wednesday: Decide and develop concepts 
In the morning on Wednesday, the Solution 
Sketches are pitched and through differ­
ent rounds of voting, and a final concept is 
chosen to be prototyped. The concept is 
further developed and made into a story­
board, that works as an overall framework for 
continuous development.
Thursday: Prototype 
Thursday is dedicated to build a prototype. 
Tools for prototyping are chosen and roles 
are delegated. During the day, illustrations, 
text, icons, wireframes are made, and later, all 
stitched together in one or more prototypes. 
One team member prepares the interview 
guide for the test and recruits test-persons. 
A test-run of the prototype are conducted to 
refine details before Friday. 
Friday: Test and validate
On the last day, the prototype is tested 
with users of several rounds (five being the 
recommended number of rounds), while the 
team interviews and documents the feed­
back. The Sprint ends by evaluating the test 
and the overall Design Sprint process.
2.3.3 Sprint Team
The Sprint team consists of employees of 
the enterprise, representing expertise within 
finance, marketing, customer, design experts 
etc., and together forms a strong team. One 
from the team acts as a ‘Decider’, who is 
granted with more influence during deci­
sion-making. This person should be someone 
who understands the problem in-depth and 
have the authority to make decisions.
A facilitator manages time, conversations 
and leads the overall process. This person 
should be unbiased, profitably one outside 
the enterprise.

element of the Design Sprint will be elaborat­

02 Literature Background
Sustainable transition is articulated as a 
wicked problem, highly complex and chal­
lenging to change. Designers have a skill-set 
that enables them to centre around human 
behaviour and need and transfer something 
complex into something tangible.  Collabo­
ration across disciplines and reformation at 
multiple system levels will be essential to 
perform sustainable transitions. The focus 
must expand, moving away from an isolated 
view on the user or enterprise need, convey­
ing perspectives from other parts of the 
system. Experimental creative processes can 
have big potential to act as an operational 
activity of a system transition, and thereby to 
create a space for innovation among SMEs, 
where low-risk development and experi­
ments are favoured. Design Sprint offers a 
pre-defined framework of collected design 
methods, that enables creative ideation, 
teamwork and testing of ideas.
Paradoxes
Design has invisible value
Design offers an “invisible” element in the 
form of the mindset and set methods the 
designer practises. Design processes have 
become more promoted as an enabler to 
innovation and collaboration across disci­
2.4 Summary of key points
plines, but it can be challenging to explicate 
its value and thereby also measure it.  
Circular Economy is crucial yet niche
Circular Economy is well explicated in liter­
ature, but not yet fully put into practice by 
professionals. Circular transition is reformed 
often on the Macro level, while tangible 
examples of the transition of lower levels are 
absent, as competencies and knowledge are 
not well distributed. It is crucial to integrate 
Circular Economy principles into society’s 
production and use patterns, to transition 
towards sustainability. However, Circular 
Economy is only a niche among practitioners 
and professionals. 
Circular is optimisation
Most circular initiatives are done within recy­
cling, however isolated focus on this strategy 
will be insufficient moving towards society, 
that fully respect the planetary boundaries 
and regenerate with natural systems. Liter­
ature point towards reducing across all R’s 
of the waste hierarchy, radicalising not only 
optimising current systems.

3.3.1 Engineering System Level
On the broadest level, Engineering System 
Level, the European Commission and the 
Danish Government allocates funding 
and spending priorities within innovation 
and sustainability, that drives develop­
ment further down the system. The EU 
commission provides a framework for the 
coordination of economic policies across 
the European Union. The EU2020 strategy 
(European Commission, 2010) impacts both 
the Danish regional funds, as well as the EU’s 
regional fund. This analysis will focus on the 
EU regional development fund (ERDF), as it 
is the main contributor to the case project. 
Looking into the investment guidelines of 
ERDF, the primary priority axis investments 
focus on the enhancement of innovation 
of SMEs, to contribute to growth and job 
creation (Ervhervsstyrelsen). Encouraged 
activities implying funding, are innovative 
collaboration between enterprises and 
knowledge institutions for the development 
of new products and dialogue with potential 
users of the products, covering the usabil­
ity, safety and market potential of the new 
products (Ervhervsstyrelsen). To gain funding 
the project must provide measurable success 
criteria and indicators, demonstrating inno­
vation and increased productivity. 
Sustainability is represented in priority axis 3, 
that focuses on energy and resource effec­
tiveness of enterprises and connected value 
chains (Ervhervsstyrelsen). In respect to the 
EU2020 climate goal, this will contribute 
to a decrease of greenhouse gas emissions 
and environmental impact. This through 
interventions such as screening of SME’s 
potentials of becoming more resource-effec­
tive, development of green business models 
and creation of collaborative networks and 
consultancy. The ERDF underlines that key 
gains of incorporating sustainability in busi­
ness models of the SMEs is a boost in devel­
opment and creation of jobs (Ervhervssty­
relsen).
On the boundary between Engineering and 
Complex System Level, Gate21 investigates 
needs and green opportunities between 
municipalities, enterprises and knowledge 
institutions. They find common interests 
between stakeholders across disciplines and 
geographical locations. In this way, Gate21 
can establish projects, where the involved 
can utilize and evolve their competencies 
collaboratively (appendix A.3). In the estab­
lishment of new projects, Gate21 must 
apply for funding within the defined guide­
lines given by the funding priority axes. The 
guidelines therefore impact the opportunities 
for the given projects Gate21 establishes 
(appendix A.3). Within the project periods of 
2-4 years, Gate21 might observe new oppor­
tunities to adapt and redirect the project 
process. However, due to preliminary fixed 
boundaries of the project, they will not be 
able to make radical changes (appendix A.3). 
Gate21 channelises resources given at Engi­
neering level down at Complex level, where 
the circular transition projects are conducted. 
3.3.2 Complex System Level
Complex level depicts the main arena of the 
circular transition of the case project, where 
Gate21, SMEs and partners of the project

transition project. Since ‘Bæredygtig Bundlin­
je Bornholm’ is initiated for SMEs to rethink 
their business models and optimise their 
total energy- and resource use, it is a key 
opportunity to gain new knowledge, compe­
tencies and professional consultancy. Project 
participation have no economical cost for 
the SMEs, however they will have to dedi­
cate 30 hours (Workshopkataloj: Bæredyg­
tig Bundlinje Bornholm, 2018). The reason 
why SMEs choose to participate differs

3.3.3 Artefact System Level
On Artefact System Level, single stakeholder 
needs and roles, are investigated. Gate21 
trains the partners in project leadership and 
tools (Roadmaps, Business Model Canvas 
etc.) to make them well equipped to drive the 
transition process together with the SMEs. 
The consultant enters the process later to 
provide expertise and build a new business 
case. 
Sustainable transition is an emerging area 
within business development, and many 
SMEs experience barriers, that can restrain 
them to invest and initiate the green transi­
tion. SMEs lack sufficient knowledge within 
Circular Economy, to initiate relevant tran­
sition for their business (Ervhervsstyrelsen, 
n.d.). Furthermore, the limited capacity of the 
SME makes it challenging to allocate resourc­
es for additional development projects 
(Ervhervsstyrelsen, n.d.). Sustainability is not 
an integrated part of the SME work process­
es. The SME ‘Møbelfabrikken’ is an innova­
tion house, housing start-ups and hosting 
events. The enterprise articulates their work 
within sustainable as chaotic and sporadic: 
“Our environmental work is characterised by 
emotions and agility” (appendix A.2) - here 
agile is perceived negatively as a process that 
now follows a controlled path. This empha­
sises a need for integrating knowledge and 
competencies, that enable SMEs to initiate 
and sustain a transition path.
A barrier can also occur if the knowledge and 
competencies gained within sustainability are 
not distributed back into the enterprise. “You 
need to have people with you, it is important 
that I do not just keep it all in here” (Appen­
dix A.2). Since the entire SME is not able to 
participate in the case project and drive the 
circular transition, it requires continuous 
dissemination of information to the remain­
ing SME employees. This will be crucial to 
complete a successful transition.  
Brand and customer perception are key 
factors, making SMEs hesitant to integrate 
green initiatives. “We do not want to demand 
too much from our customers, it is a delicate 
balance” (appendix A.2). The SMEs find it 
important to keep the high quality of their 
product/service, not influencing the custom­
er experience negatively. Pension Sandbo­
gaard, a family-owned bed & breakfast, have 
made small food waste initiatives and more 
conscious use of food and materials, howev­
er been aware of the potential negative 
impact of the service experience, if not fully 
explicated to the guests.
A paradox emerges looking at the simultane­
ous transition processes at both the Complex 
and the Artefact level.  A SME called ‘Kalas’ 
produces and sells ice cream locally. The 
enterprise has shifted towards more sustain­
able materials for their packaging however, 
even though this material could be separated 
and recycled, the overall infrastructure of the 
island is not yet able to treat it. The majority 
of the waste generated on Bornholm, are 
currently incinerated. This paradox resonates 
up through the Complex level, since the 
waste infrastructure that BOFA manages, 
only reuse and recycle a few types of waste. 
It emphasises the importance of sustainable 
transition happening on multiple system 
levels, continuously developed in parallel. 
The levels are interdependent and able to 
influence each other both ways. The associa­
tion Folkemødet have enabled an expansion 
of waste fractions at their festival by means 
of local agreements. However, the partici­
pants of the festival have until now put all 
waste in one fraction. The new infrastructure 
will demand new behavioural practises to 
evolve (appendix A.2).

High level funding guidelines have strong 
influence on the opportunities of lower level 
circular transition projects. Focus remain on 
optimisation of energy and resource use, 
which prevents radical transformation. Both 
partners and SMEs lack sufficient knowledge 
and competencies within Circular Econo­
my and sustainable business development, 
which refrain them from intiating the transi­
tion. However, overarching projects such as 
‘Bæredygtig Bundlinje Bornholm’ allow for 
regional collaboration and professional facil­
itation to accelerate the circular transition of 
Bornholm. 
The analysis gives examples of Complex level 
being able to influence Artefact level and the 
other way around. This gives rise to inves­
tigate low level processes further, with the 
opportunity to influence high level impact.
Paradoxes
Disharmony across system levels 
A barrier for the sustainable transition of the 
island of Bornholm is a lack of connection 
between local initiatives and the overarching 
infrastructures. SMEs conducting sustaina­
ble initiatives have their impact diminished 
by a waste infrastructure not able to treat 
demanded fractions. This draws emphasis 
on the importance of system levels following 
each other’s development path, and sustain­
able transition requires holistic intervention 
on all system layers.
One-way influence of the development path
The means to evoke and drive circular tran­
sition are dictated by overall funding guide­
lines, in the end influencing the developing 
path for organisation and SMEs. Currently, 
design is perceived as resource exten­
sive, time-consuming and technologically 
advanced. Due to this, design is often not 
prioritised and formerly incorporated in the 
high-level guidelines. Gate21 has a big inter­
est in integrating creative design methods in 
their repertoire however, they are challenged 
by the high dependency of pre-defined 
high-level guidelines. 
Growth needs to be a co-enabler
The core incentives and overall objectives 
coming from the higher-level system remain 
to focus on growth and job creation. Inno­
vation is articulated with respect to produc­
tivity and efficiency, not as an enabler to 
increase sustainable development indicators. 
This accentuates the importance of economic 
prosperity in circular transition. 
User experience transitions in parallel
Sustainable transition is able to restructure 
current material flows and work processes 
of SMEs, which might impact the end-user 
experience. To create an incentive for SMEs 
and complete their transition, it will be 
crucial to incorporate the user experience, 
ensuring both the experience and brand 
perception not being negatively impact­
ed. This refers back to the former paradox, 
emphasising sustainable transition needing 
to be good business.

A Process Journey Map (figure 10 on next 
page) is utilised in order to accumulate a 
deeper understanding of the process of 
transitioning SMEs towards a more circu­
lar business. The Process Journey merg­
es perspectives from both User Journey, 
Service Journey and Service Blueprint 
(Servicedesigntools, 2018), to generate 
a format that fits for the purpose of the 
thesis analysis. The Process Journey Map 
was mapped during an interactive session 
with Anne-Sofie Hattesen, project leader at 
Gate21 (appendix A.3) (figure 9). The map 
depicts a generic journey since no enterprise 
journey is identical. The process was mapped 
in the perspective of pre, during and post 
transition, and later titled Enrol, Map, Define, 
Develop and Implementation.
4.1.1 Enrol phase
The process journey is initiated with the 
Enrol phase, prior to the project kick-off. 
Gate21 mobilises the necessary funds and 
partners, to initiate the project. The partners 
are trained through a series of workshops 
and recruit SMEs for participation through 
their already well-established network. The 
partner guides the SME during workshops 
and refines enterprise deliveries (ex. Business 
Model Canvas) throughout the project. 
4.1.2 Mapping phase
The mapping phase is initiated by the kick-
off workshop, where SMEs and partners 
map products, material flows and processes 
collaboratively. Gate21 is the main facilitator 
of the overall process, facilitating kick-off and 
thematised workshops (e.g. food waste), and 
work as guidance for the partners. Gate21 
facilitators find it challenging to continuous­
ly explain the purpose and process of the 
project, since the field and terminology of 
Circular Economy, are new to most of the 
participants. 
The journey differs significantly, according 
to what maturity level the SMEs initiates at. 
Some enterprises already know what transi­
tioning actions they will conduct, and others 
start from scratch. Over time however, some 
SMEs start to shift their mindset, acknowl­
edging Circular Economy as part of their 
business. The level of engagement will differ 
as well, which is why partners play a crucial 
role in maintaining momentum and provide 
expertise. This enables and sustains the 
enthusiasm of the SME. Partners can experi­
ence the process time-consuming and admin­
istratively heavy, which can cause infrequent 
communication between enterprise and part­
ners. This increases chances of the transition 
phase to end and enterprises falling back into 
old patterns. The stakeholders have “clashing 
cultures” (appendix A.3), since enterprises 
have faster and more agile processes, while 
partners (often from municipal institutions) 
are used to longer reaction times. 
4.1.3 Define phase
The core purpose of the mapping and 
define phase is for the enterprises to gain 
knowledge, creating a network and become 
inspired for what green initiatives they can 
enable. From the previous mapping ‘Green 
potentials’ are defined. ‘Green potentials’ are 
entities of material consumption which either 
decreased or increased will have environ­
mental and/or economic positive impact. 
Obstacles present in the mapping phase, 
might also appear at this stage, emphasising 
the need to withhold momentum and contin­
uous communication. 
4.1.4 Develop phase
In the Develop phase, the last efforts to 
complete the project are conducted, that 
impacts the future environmental footprint 
of the enterprise. All data developed during 
previous phases are passed on to a selected 
consultant, to progress during development. 
In some cases, the enterprise will already

Design is not currently an explicit part of 
‘Bæredygtig Bundlinje Bornholm’, partly 
due to design not being incorporated in the 
formal guidelines of project funding. “I think 
many have the perception of prototypes as 
these digitally “put-together things” that are 
almost ready to enter the market” (appendix 
A.3). Aspects of Design, e.g. prototypes, are 
perceived as resource extensive and there­
fore omitted in the priority axes. Design 
Sprint offers an alternative framework that 
entails short-term development and rapid 
prototyping with smaller iterations, which 
give rise to think the method potentially 
can become an integrated part of circular 
transition processes.  As part of the Process 
Journey Mapping, opportunities of apply­
ing Design Sprint to support the circular 
transition of SMEs in ‘Bæredygtig Bundlinje 
Bornholm’, are explored. As a result of the 
analysis, three areas are indicated (figure 12). 
Design Sprint has an opportunity to play a 
role during both ‘Mapping’, ‘Define’, ‘Devel­
opment’, which is elaborated in the following. 
Mapping phase
In the mapping phase, Design Sprint can 
work as a creative process, where a multi­
disciplinary team maps challenges and 
opportunities, enabling space and dedicated 
time for collaboration. The ‘Mapping phase’ 
is currently time-consuming and resource 
demanding as it takes time to get all stake­
holders on the right track. Design Sprint 
might be able to enhance the collaboration 
between the SME and the partner, which 
might accelerate finding “green potentials”. 
Define phase
Successful enterprise transition processes 
realise initiatives aside from optimisation, 
namely more radical and innovative imple­
mentations. Design Sprint can become an 
enabler of finding new and more novel ideas, 
that in the end can influence the overall 
impact of the new implementations. In this 
way, Design Sprint will be a tool to mature 
both partners and enterprises, “expand­
ing their horizon by not only looking at the 
low-hanging fruits but go deeper into a 
system change” (appendix A.3). The Design 
Sprint may trigger the so-called “AHA!-expe­
riences” that can accelerate the maturation 
of enterprises and thereby the transition 
process.
Development phase
In the development phase, the new business 
case and the effect-measurement, will influ­
ence what initiatives will be implemented. 
The prototype developed during a Design 
Sprint can supplement these outputs with 
concrete and visual elements of new initia­
tives, which might enhance the chances of 
receiving a grant to support implementation. 
In the end, this can become crucial for radical 
circular initiatives to be implemented.

The analysis clarifies that the circular transi­
tion is conducted by collaborative processes 
between Gate21, the SME, the partner and 
the consultant. The circular transition entails 
a restructuring of resource use in the busi­
ness processes of the SME. This transition 
can entail both optimisation of resourc­
es or more radical restructure of material 
flows/work processes that can minimise 
or eliminate resource use. This transition is 
reinforced by a parallel mindset shift of the 
SME and the partner which, by receiving 
new knowledge and competencies within 
circular economy and sustainable business 
development, are more equipped to develop 
and implement sustainable initiatives. The 
circular transition process is challenged by 
complexity, an extensive need of commu­
nication, lack of competencies and a dimin­
ishing momentum over time. Design Sprint 
offers a time-constrained process that has 
the opportunity to enhance collaboration, 
accelerate development and trigger matura­
tion. The method can be applied in several 
parts of the transition process that comprise 
both the SME and the partner.  Gate21 and 
the consultant are mediators of information 
during this process, which is why they must 
also be encountered in the future design. 
Having outlined the context in which Design 
Sprint is be applied and with whom it should 
be put into practise, the thesis enters the 
development phase, conceptualising Design 
Sprint in the depicted context.

05 Conceptualising Design Sprint 
The previous process led to a final Design 
Sprint concept, a Sprint process with 
connected Sprint templates and supplies.  
5.3.1 Sprint process overview
The concept entails a condensed and priori­
tised set of exercises, divided into four phas­
es with a supplementing Pre-Sprint Scoping 
and Post Sprint Evaluation (figure 18). Into 
every phase is developed a set of materials to 
facilitate the process and engage creativity. 
Every material is indicated with a letter that 
correspond to the figure. 
Scoping
In the scoping phase the main contact person 
(also the appointed Decider) from the SME 
and the facilitator, scopes the purpose of 
the Design Sprint. It will be essential to have 
a narrow scope the team can “solve” in one 
day, and that the challenge is testable. The 
Sprint is scoped from a set of exploring ques­
tions and general expectation alignment. In 
this process step, relevant test persons will 
be selected.
The Scoping phase results are outlined in 
a one-pager (a) with practical details, the 
challenge at hand and the goal of the Sprint. 
The participants receive the one-pager prior 
to the Sprint, which enables the team to have 
a common understanding of the challenge 
when the Sprint begins.
Phase 1 – Map challenge
The purpose of phase 1 nuances the chal­
lenge and aligns the understanding among 
the participants, by sharing and document­
ing their different perspectives. Initially, the 
exercise ‘Expert Interview’ encourages team 
conversation. Every participant is “inter­
viewed” with questions such as “How do you 
perceive the challenge? Who is it a chal­
lenge for? Why is it important to solve?”. The 
remaining participants document the articu­
lated challenges on post-its. The post-its are 
then organised and clustered into overarch­
ing themes. The team vote individually for 
the three most important challenges, which 
are collectively rephrased to ‘How-Might-
We’s’ (HMWs). The Decider has higher voting 
power during this process. Following this 
exercise, the team puts themselves in the 
position of the user(s) by mapping a simple 
User Journey (b) within selected phases (e.g. 
Learn, Consider, Buy, Use). The team places 
the HMW(s) on the User Journey to indi­
cate focus before the entering development 
phase.
Phase 2 – Develop concepts
The purpose of phase 2 is to develop a 
concept that can act as a basis for prototyp­
ing and testing. The team initiates ideation 
by the exercise ‘Crazy 8’s’ (c). Initially, they 
look back into generated insight and takes 
notes. Then they start generating eight ideas 
individually. During this process, pre-devel­
oped ‘Lightning Demos’ are presented by the 
facilitator to spark inspiration and new ideas. 
The team might briefly share some ideas 
during ideation. Following this step, every 
participant picks their best idea and visual­
ises it on a Concept Poster (d). Lastly, all 
concepts are pitched, and the best concept is 
picked by voting.
Phase 3 - Prototyping
The Prototyping phase allows for the team 
to specify and materialise core aspects of 
the idea. This makes it testable. The team 
initiates by framing the test, defining means 
for prototyping, test practicalities, roles etc. 
The prototype building can differ among 
prototype methods, such as Mock-up, model 
visualisation, roleplaying etc. However, 
generating a Storyboard is beneficial in most 
cases. The team outlines the Storyboard on a 
whiteboard or the provided template (e) (This 
template was added after learnings from the

test prepared collaboratively. 
Phase 4 – Test
The questions and process of the test 
are prepared during prototyping and/or 
afterwards. The team is provided with a 
Test Guide (f) with generic questions (This 
template was added after learnings from the 
first test). The test is conducted with test 
persons, which can be direct users or people 
representing the users. The test is conducted 
with 1-3 test persons of approximately one 
hour. Insights gained from the test is docu­
mented and evaluated after the test. 
Evaluate
The Design Sprint entails a post phase of 
evaluation, to enhance possibilities of imple­
mentation. A PowerPoint document (g) 
including generated insights from the Sprint 
are distributed among participants. In this 
way, participants can easily bring insights 
into further development and distribute 
it among other employees. An evaluation 
meeting is conducted with the main contact 
person to discuss learnings and define 
actions for implementation.

5.3.2 Sprint Supplies
Accompanying the Sprint process, tangible 
materials and tools are essential to encour­
age and creative, dynamic and efficient 
working process (figure 19 next page). For 
the Design Sprint concept a set of supplies­
supplements the process.  
Post-its! 
An essential part of the supplies, since they 
are where participants document their 
thoughts and collectively map insights to find 
patterns. 
Agenda
An Agenda poster outlining the plan for the 
Design Sprint Day.
Manifest
A Manifest poster presenting some guide­
lines for the Sprint. The poster outlines for 
the participants to “Write one thing per 
post-it”, “Build upon each other’s ideas”, “Be 
positive” and “work visually”.
Lightning Demos
Inspirational examples of current solu­
tions, that solves similar problems or entail 
elements, that can be transferred to the 
investigated problem. Traditionally, these are 
made by the participants themselves, but for 
the condensed 1:1 Design Sprint workshop, 
the facilitator prepares them prior to the 
Sprint. 
Templates
Traditionally, the Sprint does not entail 
specific templates, but for the condensed 
prototype it was found essential to more effi­
ciently control the process by a guiding set 
of temlates, that can diminish confusion and 
supplement facilitation. 
Dots
Oval-shaped stickers, that are used for the 
participant to visually declare areas of inter­
est and best ideas during the Sprint. The 
‘Decider’ receives higher voting power during 
the Design Sprint.  
Time-timer
A visual watch for the participants to 
set clear time frames for every exercise, 
controlling the intensity level, and thereby 
enhancing the chances of providing a good 
environment for productivity.
Whiteboard
Optimally a Sprint environment should 
provide the opportunity to use a whiteboard. 
This allows the facilitator and participant, 
fast and flexible, to visualise and outline 
findings. 
Markers
Essential to document on whiteboard and 
post-its and should optimally allow partici­
pants to read text from a distance. 
Documentation gear
To be able to share the process and evaluate 
it is important to continuously document the 
process with a camera. 
Creative gear
When prototyping or just “putting out 
fires”, tape, scissors, glue e.g. is beneficial to 
possess. 
Music player
To accompany ideation and bring energy into 
the team, music can be used.

The thesis has contributed to the knowledge

This thesis has explored the three research 
questions outlined below:
RQ 1: What stakeholder needs and barriers 
are prevalent in circular transition 
processes? (chapter 3 & 4)
Circular transition is an emerging field with­
in enterprises and they are challenged by 
lack of knowledge and capacity to transition 
their business. Transdisciplinary projects 
are enabled by initiatives such as ‘Bæredyg­
tig Bundlinje Bornholm’ driven by Gate21, 
whose means are highly dependent on 
predefined guidelines. Participating partners 
allow for collaboration across value chains, 
but are challenged by lack of time which 
can harm a continuous momentum, and at 
worst prevent the transition. These mecha­
nisms prolong the timespans before reaching 
concrete outputs and mindset shifts, which in 
the end, is what drives a business’s sustaina­
ble transition. 
RQ 2: How can Design Sprint assist SMEs 
to transition their business towards a 
more circular use of resources?  (chap­
ter 5 & 6)
Design Sprint encourages dialogue, accu­
mulating divergent perspectives and creat­
ing tangible outputs which can contribute 
constructively to the overall transition. This 
process generates novel perspectives and 
ideas, which can affect more radical transi­
tion. Also, it provides both SMEs and their 
partners with new competencies within 
ideation, concept development, prototyping 
and user tests, which can shift their current 
work practises. Design Sprint provides a 
“language” for collaboration that makes the 
enterprises and domain experts accept the 
design premise. Complex challenges are 
translated into accessible, playful and crea­
tive learning experiences that can empower 
people to start tackling sustainable issues. 
RQ 3: How can the Design Sprint method 
have a systemic impact on multiple 
system levels? (chapter 6, 7, 8)
Design Sprint enables a “space” for address­
ing issues at a systems level by equipping 
a transdisciplinary team with knowledge 
and experience on challenges involving 
both technology and people. Design Sprint 
employs an “invisible design” and facilitates 
ineffable dynamics which foster change. It 
exchanges long-term path dependent plan­
ning with co-creative, iterative prototyping. It

This chapter reflects on the systemic impact of 
applying Design Sprint in the context of circular 
transition (9.1). Secondly, the chapter reflects on

09 Reflection
9.1 Reflection on the systemic impact
Circular transition entails a complex restruc­
turing of resource flows and an overall matu­
ration of the SMEs. This demands a strategic 
long-term process in which Design Sprint can 
contribute constructively, accelerate mind­
set shifts and concretise stepwise develop­
ments. However, if there is no overall goal of 
a long-term transition, SMEs risk Sprinting 
in “different directions” throughout their 
transition. This creates a chaotic process that 
does not necessarily accelerate and radi­
calise development as aimed for. An overall 
objective continuously maintains a focus 
throughout the transition. The implemen­
tation is also essential for making progress. 
The condensed process of Design Sprint risks 
the Sprint outputs to not be implemented. 
These outputs relate to aligned perspectives 
not being communicated further into the 
enterprise or the prototype not being incor­
porated into further designs. To enhance 
the systemic impact of incorporating Design 
Sprint, the process must be combined with 
post implementation phases. The case 
project of this thesis comprises five phases 
of a long-term process, namely ‘Enrol’, ‘Map’, 
‘Define’, ‘Develop’ and ‘Implement’. If this 
process structure corresponds to other tran­
sitional processes, it may be useful to utilise 
the already constituted Implement phase. 
This underlines that Design Sprint could 
have a beneficial role in a circular transition 
project equal to the one explored during this 
thesis. Overall though, that Design Sprint 
must constitute within a long-term transition 
process.
 
Integrating Design Sprint has the ability 
to change the current work cultures and 
processes in enterprises and of stakehold­
ers involved in the circular transition. It 
provides a framework enabling non-design­
ers to perform ‘Design Thinking’ and ‘Doing’. 
This can essentially exclude the designer’s 
role. The tests identified Design Sprint as 
an unpredictable process with significantly 
differentiating challenge scopes. It is not 
possible to fully standardise the process, 
which indicates the need for a method that is 
both flexible and adaptive. This emphasises 
the importance of improvisation of meth­
ods (e.g. during prototyping) and adaptation 
to situational aspects, which demand an 
expanded understanding of Design Thinking 
principles. Therefore it may be harmful to 
the process if the designer is entirely exclud­
ed. This demonstrates the importance of 
maintaining designer skills within the Design 
Sprint, which could be represented either 
through a participant, prototype developer or 
facilitator.  
 
We find ourselves at a crucial point in histo­
ry, faced with the responsibility of restructur­
ing the current economy by inherently rede­
fining how enterprises produce and utilise 
resources. Enterprises must rethink their 
current business models, which is a highly 
complex challenge. The future relevance of 
a certain product is essentially determined 
by its ability to demonstrate impact. This is 
the same case for Design Sprint as future 
method in circular economy. The current 
evaluation approach that defines what 
that impact is, is solidly incorporated in the 
funding guidelines which controls the overall 
boundaries of circular transition nationally. 
The approach revolves around productivity 
and job creation, while sustainability indica­
tors are represented through energy- and 
resource efficiency.  Consequently, indicators 
are comprised of measurable outputs. This 
obviates focus on the culture and process in 
which the products are developed, and omits 
indicators of user experience throughout this 
process. This diminishes the opportunity of 
integrating Design Sprint, since its value is 
not measurable in terms of current indica­
tors. One could argue that radical change of 
==PAGE:117==
117
09 Reflection
the products/services enterprises produce 
and consume, will demand a change of how 
they are developed. Incremental change out 
of the same development path will essen­
tially not create radical transitions. There­
fore, integrating and scaling Design Sprint 
throughout organisations and enterprises 
must approach evaluation in a different way. 
It must substantiate the degree to which 
the design process fosters transdiscipli­
nary collaboration and agility, as well as 
the quality and relevance of the innovation 
that is generated. By tracking the quality 
of sustainable products and services devel­
oped from creative collaborative processes 
(Design Sprints), it would aid the enter­
prises in understanding the conditions for 
success.  This would identify the potentials of 
performing Design Sprint which could there­
by be continually strengthened and scaled. 
The ability to be measured and in that way 
become an integrated part across system 
layers, is a key barrier of integrating Design 
Sprint. To gain the full potential of applying 
Design Sprint in circular transition of SMEs 
and thereby unfold its impact, it must as be 
scaled-up into higher levels.
 
9.2 Reflection on linkage of domains 
 Literature claims a systemic perspective 
as essential to achieve circular transition. 
Linking the domains of Circular Economy 
and Systems Thinking, the approach entails 
systems mapping, emerging properties and 
feedback loops. This approach would corre­
spond more to current work processes within 
Gate21, working with complex systems 
maps over prolonged timespans with great 
uncertainty of future impactful aspects. 
Accumulating both Sustainable Develop­
ment, Systems Thinking and Design Sprint 
it adds the perspectives of human-centric­
ity, co-creation, as well as prototyping and 
agility. It revolutionises current perspectives 
and inspires innovation based on the needs 
of the people that will function within tran­
sitioned systems. Radical changes in material 
flow and work processes of enterprises will 
inevitably impact the user inherent in the 
system. If the user (end-customer as well as 
production worker) is not thought into this 
change, it might have a negative impact. If 
new more sustainable systems do not figure 
into current use patterns, they will not be 
used. Additional focus on the user enables 
SMEs to immerse themselves into the chal­
lenge and maintain a holistic view on the 
system. Getting inspiration and feedback 
from users will enhance this focus and there­
by the final design.
 Principles of circular economy are not 
directly incorporated into the artefacts and 
elements of Design Sprint. One could argue 
that it would be essential to incorporate, 
expanding the current isolated view on the 
user experience, including interconnections 
and loops of the remaining entities of the 
system. The view remains to a great extent 
on the user experience and the context 
in which a certain product is experienced. 
Design Thinking measures the human expe­
rience in terms of Desirability, Viability 
and Feasibility. In the case of generating 
sustainable products/services this will not 
be sufficient. Incorporating sustainable 
aspects within this framework, will allow for 
designers and other developers to have this 
focus before it is implemented. Incorporat­
ing Circular Economy principles to a greater 
extent might reinforce and create awareness 
around the environmental impact. However, 
the integration of the principles needs to 
correlate with current context.
 
9.3 Reflection of design contributions
The Design Sprint concept encompasses 
facilitation, participatory design, itera­
tion and prototyping, which are tactical 
and operational activities meant to push 
sustainable change. The approach legalises

collaborative development in enterprises 
influenced by isolated operational work 
processes. Essentially it encourages stepwise 
progress by providing a structure of meet­
ings or workshops, enforcing documentation 
and visualisation. The concept is adapted to 
current work routines challenged by time 
scarcity, which has compressed its timespan 
and excluded essential parts of a traditional 
Sprint (e.g. Sprint Questions). The short time 
span may also seem superficial to SMEs, 
which might affect its final outcomes and 
overall impact during transition. It is essential 
that the SMEs experience a useful output 
from the Sprint. By expanding the concept, 
and incorporating timing and flexibility as 
core aspects, the otherwise negative impact 
of a condensed process may be diminished. 
It can enhance chances of gathering the 
right team for the Sprint and allocate time 
resources post Sprint to realise the imple­
mentation. The pre-defined research incor­
porated in the concept enables immersion 
into field specific topics, which can provide 
more substance to the Design Sprint and 
ensure SMEs experience a fruitful process.
 The concept represents a disruptive action 
which can push sustainable transition. 
Circular economy principles are manifested 
in this concept through Circular Economy 
expert participation, Sustainability long-term 
goal setting and pre-defined substantial 
research. The domain expert represents a 
systemic contribution of having stakehold­
ers across value chains co-developing. It 
allows the team to gain perspectives on the 
infrastructure, the use context or material 
opportunities. The sustainability goal setting 
and pre-collected research will be tangible 
indicators of sustainability, that allows for 
designers to expand the focus of only look­
ing at the user experience to a wider view 
across environmental, social and economic 
sustainability. It would require further study 
to conclude to what extent this will provide 
a sufficient perspective on sustainability to 
enhance and accelerate impact.
 
While the concept represents the process 
for product/service development at low-lev­
el, the tools are a means of integrating and 
scaling the method at higher levels.  The 
tools entail means such as storytelling and 
education. These can help to communicate 
the value of implementing Sprint to stake­
holders responsible to define the overall 
conditions for sustainable transition. The 
booklet and video give future practitioners 
hands-on examples and guiding elements, to 
more easily put it into practice with enter­
prises. Because of the flexible and generic 
Design Sprint concept it will be essential to 
have qualified planners/facilitators, able to 
understand the needs of the given SME. The 
booklet can act as guidance throughout the 
phases of the Sprint.  
 
The process of applying and integrating 
Design Sprint within Gate21 is fundamen­
tally a design output from the thesis as well. 
Demonstrating design practices such as 
simple post-its mappings, visualisation and 
concept evaluation was able to give Gate21 
employees hands-on experience with the 
tools. Having the project leader of ‘Bære­
dygtig Bundlinje Bornholm’ following the 
thesis process expanded her understanding 
of design, resulting in her becoming more 
familiar and taking on methods in her own 
project. This triggered mindset shifts among 
employees able to reshape inherent cultures 
of Gate21.
 9.4 Reflection on application approach
 Design Sprint has applied within a prede­
fined project facilitated by an NGO, which 
has influenced the findings throughout the 
thesis. It has provided an in-depth qualita­
tive example of Design Sprint in a particular 
context however, omitted a generic over­

view across circular transition cases. There is 
reason to believe that transitional processes 
differ significantly, which is why the study 
could be nuanced by combining a quantita­
tive overview or an expanded view on quali­
tative cases. In this way the thesis would be 
able to give a more generalised perspective 
on Design Sprint in circular economy cases. 
The approach of this thesis has allowed for 
an in-depth understanding of the contexts of 
the case SMEs, which has provided substan­

The Design Sprint concept was applied in 
the context of circular transition with two 
enterprises. Both Sprint challenges revolves 
around behaviour change and communica­

focus of solving these challenges. The field 
work would have been nuanced applying 
Design Sprint to other types of challenges. 
It would be interesting to investigate how 
the Design Sprint concept would respond to 
challenges revolving around material optimi­

material use. This would allow the thesis to 
draw broader conclusions of how Design 
Sprint can perform across circular economy 
challenges. And also test whether the meth­
od would create value in contexts not direct­

essential to substitute exercises such as user 
journey with material flow, and how Design 
Sprint can provide the necessary depth that 
these redesigns demand.  
 
Both case SMEs represented small enterpris­
es, which influenced time able to be dedicat­
ed before during and after the Sprint, as well 
as employees able to participate.  This may 
have diminished the quality of the Design 
Sprint output as well as limited the oppor­
tunity of taking insights and design further 
after the Sprint. The evaluation of the tests 
point towards enterprises of higher capacity 
would benefit from applying Design Sprint 
to a higher degree.  Testing the Design Sprint 
concept with more differentiated Sprint chal­
lenges and enterprise types would nuance 
insights and therefore enhance validity of 
conclusions.
Being the facilitator of the Sprint tests 
have expanded the role of just being a “fly 
on the wall” to be a participating observer, 
controlling and characterising the outcomes 
of the tests. This has allowed for direct reac­
tion to elements not performing as expected 
or unpredictable situational aspects. Not 
only observing “mistakes” but also reacting 
to them, improvising and thereby redesigning 
the concept during test. In this way, quali­
tative data have been collected quicker and 
more efficiently. However, performing both 
facilitation and observation of the process 
might have diminished the ability to gath­
er all the details. Prior knowledge of the 
field and the method have enabled a better 
understanding of how and why occurrences 
happen. However, being both designer, plan­
ner and executor of the application process 
might also have made me somewhat biased. 
To qualify findings, it would be essential to 
conduct tests with both spectating observers 
and participating observers and differentiat­
ing facilitators executing the Design Sprint 
concept.

10 Conclusion and further work
This thesis explores the role of design in 
circular transition of SMEs. The method 
Design Sprint has been applied into the 
context of SMEs aiming to rethink their busi­
ness models and restructure their current 
resource flows. The thesis has acted as pilot 
project within a regional initiative called 
‘Bæredygtig Bundlinje Bornholm’ facilitated 
by the NGO Gate21. A continuous interven­
tion process has acted as transitional process 
of the internal culture and work processes 
within the organisation of Gate21, while 
generating insights to develop a Design 
Sprint concept and tools to put Design 
Sprint into practice with SMEs. By analysing 
a circular transition project from an Engi­
neering Systems perspective and in parallel 
intervened with status quo by applying the 
component of Design Sprint to the system, 
new properties have emerged. 
Circular transition is an emerging field, in 
which enterprises lack knowledge and capac­
ity to transition their business. The tran­
sition encompasses enterprises to rethink 
their core business but is also a mindset 
shift of considering sustainable aspects 
and challenge their current cultures. Transi­
tional projects are challenged by prolonged 
time spans and demanding communication 
between stakeholders, which can harm a 
continuous momentum. These mechanisms 
diminish opportunities of reaching restruc­
ture of the business and mindset shifts, 
which in the end can decelerate or prevent 
implementation of change. 
The result implies Design Sprints as disrup­
tive actions able to shift mindsets, empower 
fundamental rethinking of businesses and 
form new partnerships among key stake­
holders. Design Sprint enables a “space” 
for experimenting which can create radi­
cal system change. The method favours 
co-creation, human-centricity, continuous 
documentation and iterative time-intensive 
processes, which generate concrete outputs, 
representing steps towards circular transi­
tion. Complex challenges are translated into 
accessible, collaborative and creative learn­
ing experiences that can empower people to 
start tackling sustainable issues. 
This thesis exemplifies Design Sprint in the 
case project and translates the gathered 
insights into a Design Sprint concept with 
associated tools for future practitioners to 
use in this context. The concept transforms 
the method into strategic actions within a 
long-term circular transition process, timed 
with work-related flow and adapted to the 
maturity of the SME. The tools assist prac­
titioners to communicate, stage and plan 
Design Sprints with SMEs in their context. 
The tools represent first step of integration 
of Design Sprint, namely to equip practition­
ers with competencies to consult and enable 
use of the method with SMEs and for SMEs 
to understand the core gains of applying the 
method into their context.  
The future circular transition can move 
towards more innovative, collaborative and 
sustainable business development through 
the integration of Design Sprint. Howev­
er, integrating the new approach brings 
challenges that can prevent realisation of 
the potential impact. The main challenges 
are fixed high-level guidelines that reso­
nates across system levels, influencing 
how low-level transitional processes can 
be conducted. Impact and scale only occur 
if sustainability challenges are approached 
in a holistic manner across value chains to 
capture system perspectives and deploy 
inherent synergies. Integrating Design Sprint 
within predefined guidelines enables SMEs 
to strengthen their core business creative­
ly, while expanding their perspectives to 
surrounding infrastructures.

FIND NYE VEJE TIL EN 
BEDRE BUNDLINJE
Få gratis hjælp til en bedre bundlinje og giv din virksomhed 
en grønnere profil med Bæredygtig Bundlinje Bornholm 
Ved at spare på materialer eller energi, kan din virksomhed både blive grønnere og mere rentabel. 
Det kan Bæredygtig Bundlinje Bornholm hjælpe dig med. Du kan også få sat din forretningsmodel 
under lup efter ny grøn merværdi, særlige kundebehov eller grønnere partnerskaber. Det sker 
gennem et forløb med workshops og sparring.

DET FÅR DU UD AF AT VÆRE MED
• Vi gennemgår din virksomheds produkter og 
processer for at afdække grønne potentialer
• Du får lavet en indledende energi-audit på din 
virksomhed  
• Du får konkrete anbefalinger, der kan forbedre 
din bundlinje   
• Deltagelse på en indledende workshop med 
10-20 andre virksomheder, hvor I får de første 
værktøjer til at forberede og udarbejde en 
enkel og overskuelig grøn forretningsplan 
gennem det efterfølgende temaforløb 
• Tema- og netværksmøder efter eget valg (se 
næste side)
• En afsluttende workshop til at færdiggøre den 
endelige grønne forretningsplan, der skal sikre 
dig en bedre og grønnere bundlinje  
• Adgang til at kunne søge om 
investeringstilskud

Små og mellemstore virksomheder med 
potentiale for materiale- og energibesparelser 
på Bornholm kan deltage i projektet.
Det er gratis at deltage, men det kræver 
engagement, motivation og cirka 30 timer. 
Virksomheden skal udpege en kontaktperson  
på ledelsesniveau til samarbejdet.
