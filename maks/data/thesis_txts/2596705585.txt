Summary
The project undertaken in this masters thesis is designing and simulating a robot system
which is intended to replace a system within an Aluminum smelter in Iceland called ISAL.
The system is meant to clean carts that carry spent anodes as the same carts are also
used to carry new anodes back into the factory. The current system has a number of
shortcomings which the system designed in this thesis is supposed to solve.
A practical approach is taken to design a system that would solve all the problems
related to the old system in the factory and the system is then simulated to validate
the design. The simulations are meant to give an answer to the question if the system
design could function as intended in the factory. The resulting outcome of the simula-
tions undertaken in this project fail to do so and the system can not be called a good
replacement as it only shows the ability to solve 1 out of the 4 problems of the old
system.
The thesis shows the analytical approach taken in designing the system and choosing
the hardware for it as well as how the observations of the simulations were set up and
used to come to the conclusion given in the report.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Computing power has been growing at a high rate ever since the first computer was
created as well as the cost of computing power going down [1]. With faster and more
robust technology an age of automation and robotics has taken over manual labour in
many areas in heavy industries, both to cut costs as well as to protect personel from
injuries caused by what are often high risk jobs. Although some people see this as a
threat to low level jobs open to anyone without much experience or specialization, the
reality is that in some cases the jobs that are being removed are jobs which are ill suited
for people and often times personnel are instead moved to better suited jobs which are
created from the fact that robots have entered the environment.
The project being discussed in this thesis is the design and simulation of a robot
system aimed to replace an outdated system currently in operation in Rio Tinto ISAL
aluminum smelter in Iceland. Isal is located in an area called Straumsvík near the town
Hafnarfjörður on the south-western coast of Iceland. They produce close to 212.000 tons
of aluminum per year for over 100 customers in 20 countries including France, Germany
and Holland [2].
The thesis will be split into two parts where the first will take on the task of designing
the system as well as analytically choosing the hardware for the system. The second
part will focus on simulating the system which has been designed to test and confirm
it’s validity to be commissioned into the factory.
The goals are thus
• Design a system to solve the shortcomings of the operational solution
• Simulate the design to test if the system can run as designed
1.1

The operational system in the factory has the following shortcomings which must be
solved by the new design so that the system can be considered a good replacement.

CHAPTER 2
Process description
2.1
Aluminum smelting 101
Aluminum smelting is a process where alumina, which is extracted from bauxite ore, is
combined with a fluoridly rich electrolysis melt and a high DC current. This process is
done in the potrooms of aluminum smelters in specifically designed pots at a temperature
close to a 1000 degrees Celsius. This results in aluminum separating from the alumina
and sinking to the bottom of the pots from where it is extracted and poured into furnaces
and then cast into ingots or extruded into poles etc [3].
Figure 2.1: The Aluminum Smelter Process[2]
==PAGE:18==
4
2 Process description
2.2
Potrooms, pots and anodes
The pots of the are the vessels which house the electrolysis melt and is the place where
the aluminum forms before it is cast into the final product. The potrooms in ISAL
aluminum smelter house a collection of 480 pots, each of which contains 24 anodes. The
pots are run on DC current that can be over a 100 kA and a very low voltage, often
around 5 VDC [3]. This current is fed to the electrolysis melt via carbon anodes that
are lowered into the pot and a graphite cathode which is situated at the bottom of the
pot [2].
Figure 2.2: The potroom pots [4]
The anodes are created in the anode factory of the smelter and are composed of a
metal fork and a baked carbon block attached to the metal fork. This carbon block
reacts with the electrolysis melt in the alumina - aluminum separation and evaporates
away creating CO2 in the process. When the anodes of the pot have been spent which
happens in around 30 days they need to be replaced with new anodes to continuously
run the pots. It is here, at the end of the anodes lifespan where the project in this thesis
is located. When the anodes are removed from the pots, they can have electrolysis melt
and carbon blend stuck to them which hardens as the anode cools.

The anodes are cooled down and moved to the anode room on carts where they
are removed and sent to the cleaning robots which use spinning chains to break the
electrolysis melt of the forks. When the fork cleaning is complete the forks are sent to
the anode factory where they are used again or recycled.

2.3
Cart cleaning

The carts which carry the used anode forks are also used to cart new anodes into the pot
rooms to replace the ones heading back to the anode factory. The hardened electrolysis
melt on the used forks can flake up and break off the fork tines leaving debris and dirt

the carts when heading into potrooms so the carts need to be cleaned.

The current solution at ISAL aluminum smelter is a scraper which sits on a short
track and drives toward the carts until the scraper blades are at the back of the cart.
The scraper then lowers the blades and then reverses on the track, pulling the dirt and
debris off the carts.

result in a lot of unnecessary extra work on cleaning.

CHAPTER 3
Simulation software
The simulations of the final product from this project will be done using ROS, or Robot
Operating System, Gazebo which is a Robot simulation software and visualized using
Rviz, a robot visualization program.
3.1
ROS
ROS or Robot Operating system is an open source robot application development plat-
form.
It’s origins can be traced back to a project by Morgan Quigley at Stanford
University as a part of it’s STAIRS robot project but most of the later developement
happened at a nearby robot company called Willow Garage. The field of robotics can
be extremely complex and diﬀicult to manouver in and one of the primary goals in the
creation of ROS was code sharing and reuse to facilitate and further robotics research
and development. Having a common platform like ROS can thus help people share their
code and ideas and prevent development delay’s related to having to write complete
software infrastructure before seeing the robot finally moving [5].
Figure 3.1: The ROS equation
==PAGE:26==
12
3 Simulation software
Figure 3.2: ROS technical overview, the pipeline between programs is handled by the
ROS master which must run at all times. [7]
ROS will be used in this project to run the programs created for the robot along
with visualisations of the robot and working environment in Rviz. One of the biggest
reasons for choosing ROS was the aforementioned tools and capabilities in the ROS
equation. The programs associated with ROS (Gazebo and Rviz) as well as the planning
algorithms (Moveit) and product libraries make it a fantastic way to really approach
realism. Realism is important as the project is a real life project which is in the plans
for the factory and will be implemented in the near future. The closer the simulation is
to reality, the better the results are for the aluminum smelter. The project is at the top
of the investment plan for this area of the factory so depending on the outcome of this
thesis it could be used for the final product.
3.2
Gazebo
Gazebo is a robot simulation software which originally started development in 2002. It
started as an answer to the need for a good program for outdoor environment simulations
for robotics in the University of Souther California but in today’s robot simulation world
it is mostly used to simulate indoor environments. ROS and PR2 were integrated into
Gazebo in 2011 by Willow Garage and since then it has become one of the primary tools
used in the ROS community [8].
Gazebo will be used to simulate the robot in the project since it is the primary
simulation tool for ROS. It will also provide the simulation of the sensors used for the
robot as Gazebo can simulate the sensor data from various laser scanners and camera
sensors. There are countless libraries for vendor specific sensors for Gazebo which will
come in handy during the development of the project solution.

CHAPTER 4
System Design
As stated earlier, the system is to be designed to replace the current solution in the
factory which is a motorized scraper. In the current configuration the debris removal
is performed in steps for each cart with the scraper cleaning one part of the cart, the
cart moving forward and cleaning the next part until it has reached the other side since
the scraper is confined to moving forward and reversing. Using this information it was
decided to look at the design going in 1 of 2 directions.
Table 4.1: Design directions
Nr.
Type
Description
1
Robot
keeping the cleaning in stages, having the robot clean one side,
moving the cart forward and cleaning that side.
2
Robot
robot can reach the whole cart from one position so the movement
of the cart is not necessary for cleaning suﬀiciently.
Going with option number 1 would keep most of the functionality of the systems
around the new robot as well as being able to install the robot with minimal changes
around the factory. Option number 2 could result in a faster cleaning process but would
require significant changes in the system moving the carts as well as needing changes
around the system area.
4.1
Hardware
4.1.1
The Robot
To solve the rigidity issue of the operational scraper the new cleaning system will be
designed around a 6 axis industrial robot arm. As the working environment is in an
aluminum smelter and constitutes dragging and cleaning debris off the carts, the envi-
ronment is harsh, specifically in regards to dust formations. This requires the robots to
be considered for the system to have a corresponding IP rating to protect its internal
circuitry and motors, thus having a minimum IP rating where the first number is a 6
making it fully protected agains dust, eg. IP6x [9]. The debris chunks on the carts
can get to a respectable size so to be able to perform the tasks needed of the robot in
removing the debris, the robot will have to have a suﬀicient load capacity. To prevent

CHAPTER 5
System Simulations
To validate the design of the system and to test it’s capacity to function, the system was
simulated using ROS, Gazebo and Rviz. Gazebo would be the simulation environment
and the physiscs engine were the robot and cart model would be spawned with Rviz
serving as a visualization tool for planning and ROS facilitating communications between
Robot and controller nodes and the scripts used to move the robot. The ROS version
used was Melodic on Ubuntu 18.04 were rospy, the python scripting version of ROS was
used to program the scripts. For path and position/orientation planning, an open source
robotic manipulation platform called Moveit is used as it is integrated into ROS. This
removed the need to calculate the forward and inverse kinematics of the robot model
and provides a more robust way of using the robot and planning paths to specific poses
and coordinates.

model was loaded into the simulatoin environment along with some boxes to represent
the cart. The setup consisted of the robot and the cart with the back of the cart being
2.6 meters from the base of the robot. With the cart being 1.3 meters deep that meant
that the system had the robot mounted with the torso of the robot around 1 meter from
the cart.
The whole robot sequence was split into sections during the simulation to evaluate
specific parameters of the system with the parameters being based on the requirements
found in table 1.1. The parameters were if the system could calculate the displacement
of the cart, if the system would be able to move and correct for the displacement before
continuing, if the first sequence would be successful and would move through the new
corrected points, if the second cleaning sequence would start at the right place according
to the displacement and move to its second point correctly and if the second cleaning
sequence would run through and finish. The cleaning feedback system would also be
tested where large jumps in distance values between scans while running over the cart
floor would indicate debris still laying on the cart floor
The system was also designed to have a force sensor to determine if the system had
good contact with the floor of the cart. The force sensor was tested separately
The robot script was run and the simulation parameters were evaluated after each run
and the results recorded as well. After each run the cart boxes were moved to a random
skew along the y-axis in a positive direction to simulate the positioning discrepancy with
the distance of the skew being between 0.0 and 45.0 cm for the next simulation run.

6.2.1
Laser scanner simulations
The system was run a total of 20 times, although only in the configuration of cleaning
the first part of the cart. It was assumed that the robot was holding a scraper tool with
the blade having an overall length of 1.5 meters in the first cleaning sequence and an
industrial vacuum cleaner nozzle with a diamater of between 15 and 20 cm similar to
street vacuum cleaners. While setting up the laser sensor in the simulation environment
it was observed that the laser sensor value was jumping up to around 4 cm between
scans with the biggest distance jump being close to 5 cm so it was decided to use a
calculated mean over 1 second of scanning when trying to adjust for dispositioning of
the cart. To ensure the scan was correct a wait of 2 seconds was set at the first position
for the robot inside the confines of the cart. The full simulation results can be seen in
Appendix A.

6.2.1.1
Cleaning sequence 1
The first 3 paramaters were related to Cleaning Sequence 1 which drags and scrapes
rubble of the carts.
For simulation parameter 1 the system calculated the cart displacement from the
laser sensor value successfully in 95% of the runs with a mean accuracy of 1.12 cm with
the one exception being run number 4 where the whole simulation simply aborted and
the value ended up being infinity. As the accuracy of the laser sensor is plus minus 2
cm the system was well within range. The same goes for simulation parameter 2 as the
system was able to adjust the starting position according to the calculated displacement
in every run except for run 4 as that one was a complete failure with a full abort.
When testing for simulation parameter 3 the sequence intended to drag and scrape
the larger debris of the cart was observed.
The sequence has 3 positions, the start
position which is corrected according to the dipslacement of the cart, a middle position
which the system runs through and a final position at the front edge of the cart.
The system had a success rate of 90% on cleaning sequence 1 with only 2 failures.
One of them was a partial failure where the system was still able to finish the task but
run 4 was a full failure. Runs 6 and 8 were successful and finished correctly, however
there was a minor error in the orientation of the robot as the ”elbow” joint rotated
180° CW but because of the goal position also having a goal orientation the ”hand”
joint roatated CCW to correct for the roatation. This was most likely due to the ROS
environment and Moveit using quaternions while the robot poses were created using
a function converting euler angles to quaternions. Having the same orientation goal
through all of the movement seemed to cause small error acumulating in the actual
orientation of the robot making it sometimes use unnecessary or far away joint values
to solve the same tasks that were solved in the previous run without problems.
6.2.1.2
Cleaning sequence 2
Simulation parameters 4 through 6 are related to cleaning sequence 2 which is a vacuum
process intended to vacuum all the remaining dust in a zig zag pattern over the area of
the cart being cleaned at that time, see figure 4.29.
The results for simulation parameters 4 and 5 showed that the system was able to
correct the home positions for cleaning sequence 2 in 95% of the runs. This however was
expected as the correction of the home positions is done in the same manner that the
home positions in cleaning sequence 1 are corrected. It is in the results for simulation
parameter 6 that the second cleaning sequence is fully tested.
for parameter 6 the system was able to complete the sequence with a success rate
of 70% which is an un-acceptable result.
The errors encountered in the simulation
surfaced as path plans to points within the sequence using joint values outside of the
joint limitations of the R2000iC-210L.

Simulation parameter 7 was the cleaning system designed for feedback after running

==PAGE:67==
6.2 Simulation Results
53
Allthough many of the simulation parameters show promising results and a high
success rate the fact of the matter is that between the two cleaning sequences only 13
full runs were executed without any errors or failures.
A success rate of 65% for a
system designed to operate in a volatile environment of a factory that runs 24 hours of
the day for all 7 days of the week is un-acceptable. Based on the results, an argument
can be made that the use of a laser sensor like the one used in this project could be
a valid option for solving problem 1 from table 1.1 however the system as a whole can
not be considered a good replacement for the currently operating system in the factory
according to the results.
Some errors in the simulation can possibly be attributed to the simplicity of the
robot model, especially in runs were the robot solved the tasks using weird orientations
and invalid joint values. Other errors can be related to the simulation environment and
the path planning library in use for the system. It is however impossible to declare these
errors neglible or to devalue them as it is possible they happened because at that specific
point of the simulation run it would be the most viable solution. The real robot could
have come to a halt or have moved into a deadlock position which it couldn’t plan out
of. Because of that the results are not good enough to call the system as a whole a valid
substitute.
6.2.3
Force sensor simulations
The force sensor simulations would be carried out only 4 times vs the 20 simulation runs
of the laser scanner simulations. In each simulation the system was made to lower the
tool representation down on to the cart to find the cart floor. The cart floor would be
registered when the force read on the force sensor was over 2 N indicating contact with
the floor. The force limit was deliberately kept low as the simplicity of the robot model
and simulation environment was not thought to handle higher outside forces. The force
sensor simulations were thus relegated to test only if the system could find the cart
rather than applying the correct amount of force while performing its task.
Even though measures were taken in terms of outside forces the robot model could
not handle pushing down onto the box meant to represent the cart floor. In 4 runs
the system managed to find the floor once but still crashed while the other runs simply
crashed the whole model and controllers. The simplicity of the controllers and model
were clearly at fault in this scenario.
The force sensor simulations are thus determined a failure as the system could not
be used to solve the problems it was intended to from table 1.1.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis designs with a practical approach an industrial robot system around a robotic
arm which would be installed into an Aluminum smelter in Iceland. To find the correct
hardware an analytical approach was taken and 5 major manufacturerers of industrial
robot arms were contacted regarding their models which fit within the criteria set forth
in the design of the system and in the end one was chosen. A functional description of
the intended behavior of the system was then created.
The system design was then set up in a simulation environment which was used to
try and confirm the design and that it could solve the shortcomings of the operational
system found in table 1.1.
Simple robot models serving as a representations of the
robot chosen in the design part of the thesis was created and loaded into the simulation
environment as well as a representation of the carts being used by the aluminum smelter.
From there the robot was moved manualy into positions to find home positions for the
system to move through while completing it’s tasks to clean the cart. When all positions
had been found a laser sensor was created in the simulation environment and configured
to have the same specifications as the sensor type chosen in the design part of the thesis
and a force sensor was attached to the other model. The system was then programmed
according to the functional description and then run while data was collected on the
performance of the system.
Overall this thesis gives a full design of a system but is unable to fully confirm the
design to be a working one. It does give valid grounds for the use of a laser sensor to
correct for the positional offset of the carts used in the factory, arguably solving problem
1 from table 1.1 and partially problem 3. The simulations results for cleaning sequence
2 only ended up having a 70% success rate and the system as a whole could only finish
it’s tasks in 65% of the simulation runs. The force sensor simulations give off even worse
results than the laser sensor simulations and can therefore not confirm the system design.
According to the results, the simulations are not able to show that the cleaning feedback
system can work as designed in this thesis.
7.1
Future Work
As only 1 problem from the requirements in table 1.1 can be arguably marked as fully
solved there is a lot of work that is needed to be done to improve on the project and
hopefully get the system to a commissioning stage.
==PAGE:70==
56
7 Conclusion
As a start the robot model could be improved on to move it closer to the real model.
To do that the joints should be updated to be revolute joints and adding in the joint
limits of the Fanuc robot as joint limits do not work on continuous joints. This would
cause the controller to also need updating as they would need to be changed to effort
or velocity controllers and tuned better. It is believed that this would also benefit the
force sensor testing as the robot model would become more robust with added realism.
The cleaning system would need to be re-designed as the current design falls short
of its target. Either a more accurate sensor would need to be used, the system fully
re-designed as a whole or simply scrapped from the system.
The force sensor system has a possible design flaw as the system would not be able to
distinguish between applying force on the floor to insure contact and applying the same
force on a piece of debris situated between the tool and the cart floor. An alternative
should be considered if the current design would fail to produce the desired results.
To sum up the future work needed to improve the simulation results:
• Update robot model closer to realism.
– Update joints
– Update controllers
– Add mesh files from real life models when available
• Sensor update, re-design or scrapping the cleaning feedback system.
• Alternative design to force sensor system intended to find the cart floor to solve
distinguishing between debris and cart floor.
