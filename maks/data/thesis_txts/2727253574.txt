Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate if the Statistically Optimized Near-field Acous-
tic Holography (SONAH) can be used to obtain the scattering properties of a sample in
the wave-number domain. Therefore, 3 samples with different shapes were measured in
free-field using an omnidirectional source and a robotic arm to scan the microphone posi-
tions. Then, the total sound pressure data was processed and with the SONAH method,
the solution of the amplitude of the incident and reflected plane waves was analyzed in
the wave-number domain. The method proposed has shown great promise in displaying
the reflection patterns, and with improvements in signal processing, the results could be
even further refined. The importance of finding new methods to measure the scattering
characteristics is to improve the quality of input data for simulations and open new possi-
bilities for more practical techniques for obtaining this data. The SONAH technique is a
powerful tool that holds significant potential for further investigation.

Introduction
The importance of good acoustics in rooms has a great impact not only on people’s enjoy-
ment when experiencing live music, but also on people’s hearing and mental health [1].
Therefore, several tools have been developed with the objective of predicting sound qual-
ity and comfort in rooms. The acoustic properties of materials are necessary input data
for any room acoustic project and simulation. In order to design a simulation, all surfaces
have to be described with coefficients that translate their acoustic properties, such as the
absorption and the scattering coefficients. Figure 1.1 shows how a plane wave behaves
when hitting a surface, i.e. a wall.

Figure 1.1: The incident energy generated by the source is reflected, absorbed and trans-
mitted at the surface. Adapted from [2].
After being generated by a source, the wave propagates through the air and meets ob-
stacles and boundaries. When hitting a surface, part of the sound energy is reflected,
absorbed or transmitted. For instance, the absorption coefficient indicated how much of
this energy is absorbed by the surface: 0 for no absorption and 1 for maximum absorption.
The scattering coefficient is also a value from 0 to 1, but it determines the ratio of the en-
ergy reflected non-specularly and the total reflected energy. A low scattering coefficient
means that the reflection tends to be specular. This study focuses on the reflected and
scattered parts of the sound.
In comparison with the absorption coefficient, it is a lot harder to have access to the scat-
tering information of surfaces, which raises questions from the user side when setting the
input data for room acoustic simulations. The existing measurement methods for scatter-
ing are very challenging because they require expensive equipment, an anechoic cham-
ber and it can also be very time-consuming to perform the measurement and process the
data.
Furthermore, the coefficient does not carry all information for a complete analysis of the
reflection pattern of a surface. Often, polar plots are used to show how a surface scatters
sound per frequency band and angle of incidence. These data are frequently simulated
instead of being measured. However, it is hard to utilize several polar plots as input for
room acoustics simulation. In fact, it would not even make sense, based on how the
simulation algorithm works.
In order to improve the quality of the simulations, it is important to improve the input data.
The round robin on acoustical computer simulations [3] proved that the software that had

the best results compared to the measured reference had something in common: they
included diffusion data in their calculation. With easier methods to measure scattering,
these data would be more available and consequently, there would be more awareness of
how to use them. In addition, it would encourage manufacturers to measure their diffusers
and provide more accurate data.
The Near-field Acoustic Holography (NAH) is a method that could be used to measure
sound reflection. The characterization of the sound field in the vicinity of a surface is
obtained from the measurement of sound pressure with a microphone array. The variation
of pressure and phase between the microphones can be used to reconstruct information
such as sound pressure, particle velocity and intensity [4]. With this method, it is possible
to characterize sound sources or any other object interfering in the sound field, such as
diffusers and absorbers [5]. It is a great tool to improve the study of the acoustic properties
of a surface. This study uses the Statistically Optimized Near-field Acoustic Holography
(SONAH), which is a variation from NAH.
1.1
Related work and applications
This thesis is related to Brandão and Fernandez-Grande’s research [5], which analyzed
the influence of propagating and evanescent waves in the wave-number domain using the
SONAH method. Its original contribution was the investigation of the diffraction caused
by finite absorbers in the wave-number domain. Simulations were performed and the
method was verified when compared to sound absorption measurements.
This thesis aims to perform sound pressure measurements near diffusers with an array
of microphones in the free-field with the objective of investigating the accuracy of the
SONAH when analyzing diffuse reflections in the wave-number domain.
For future research, the SONAH technique can be utilized to further investigate the in-
fluence of diffusers in the sound field. Moreover, this technique can provide valuable
data for the reconstruction of additional parameters, including particle velocity and sound
intensity.

2
Research focus
The research question of this work is:
Can the near-field acoustic holography method be used to evaluate the scattering
properties of a sample in the wave-number domain?
To answer this question, the project is divided into two main parts. First, a simulation is
implemented in Matlab in order to study the SONAH method and define the measurement
configuration. Secondly, the sound pressure measurement of different diffusers scattering
the sound generated by a source is performed in the anechoic chamber with an array of
microphones and the data is processed with the SONAH code implemented. The focus
of this study is to verify if the SONAH is a good tool to evaluate how a diffuser scatters
the sound, so the aim is to obtain the reflected wave-number spectra generated from
the measurements and analyze if they are a potential representation of the scattering
properties of diffusers. The samples measured are: a flat circular plate, a large half-
sphere mounted on a circular plate, and 4 small half-spheres also mounted on a circular
plate, because they consist of simple geometries so it is a good start for studying the
method’s effectiveness.

Theory
The theory section is divided into two parts. First, the concept of diffusers and the basic
theory of sound reflection are presented. Then, the method used in this study is detailed.
3.1
Sound reflection and diffusers
The two main types of acoustic panels used for indoor acoustical treatment are sound
absorbers and diffusers. This study focuses on measuring sound diffusers, which are rigid
panels with irregularities designed to scatter the sound. There are endless possibilities
for diffuser designs, and each one of them has a different diffusivity pattern that varies in
the frequency domain and angle of incidence. Figure 3.1 has an example of two different
diffusers, one in the ceiling and another placed at the walls.
Figure 3.1: Studio acoustic project from the Brazilian company Giner. Taken from [6].
Some of the applications presented in Cox and D’Antonio’s book [7] of how diffusers can
be used are: to control echo, give musicians some reverberance while minimizing col-
oration in small music rooms, promote diffuse fields in reverberation chambers and im-
prove speech intelligibility in underground or subway stations. The listener’s subjective
impact of placing the right diffuser in the room can lead to great advantages such as
improving sound clarity and intelligibility, which has a direct positive impact on people’s
communication, music performance and enjoyment.
The most popular ways of evaluating the diffuser influence on the sound field, are from
their polar response, scattering coefficient, or diffusion coefficient. Each one has its flaws
and there is no ideal coefficient because it will always depend on the application.
The polar response graphs are a visual representation of the spatial sound energy dis-
tribution. Figure 3.2 shows an example of the polar response of a plane surface from
normal incidence. The different curves display how the pattern changes per frequency,
depending on the ratio between the wavelength and the surface width. Each frequency
band and angle of incidence results in a new polar response, so in the end there is a large
amount of information.

With the objective of simplifying the information given by several polar responses, the dif-
fusion coefficient was created. It consists of an auto-correlation of the reflected pressure,
which describes the degree of sound spatial uniformity reflected by a sample [6].
However, the polar responses and the diffusion coefficient are not useful input data for
room acoustic software, because they are both based on the energetic relation between
incident pressure and reflected pressure. The statistical theory used in the software re-
quires the input of the relation between the non-specularly reflected sound energy and
the total reflected sound energy, which is known as the scattering coefficient [6]. Nev-
ertheless, the polar response, diffusion coefficient and scattering coefficient are equally
relevant because they have different applications.
Both diffusion and scattering coefficients are a single value between 0 and 1 (0 for spec-
ularly reflecting surface and 1 for maximum diffusion or scattering effect).
The room acoustic software users usually empirically chose the scattering coefficient
based on some guidelines, for instance in the Odeon software [8], the user gives only
the scattering coefficient at 707 Hz (logarithmic mean from 500 to 1 kHz) and the soft-
ware assigns the values for the rest of the frequencies according to Figure 3.3. Other
frequencies in between are interpolated. Of course, this is only a way of guiding the
users, but it is not always accurate.
Figure 3.3: Scattering coefficient curves used in Odeon. Taken from [8].

In general, the scattering coefficient remains very low or even 0 at lower frequencies. The
tendency is for the scattering to increase more for higher frequencies. This is a common
behaviour for general surfaces, but it is important to note that the edge effect is being
disregarded.
When the incident wave is reflected by an infinite flat rigid plane, it results in a specular
reflection. However, there is no perfect infinite, flat or rigid plane. In reality, the reflection
angle is affected by the relation between the incident wavelength and the dimension of
the surface’s irregularities. The reflection is scattered if the wavelength is smaller than
the dimension of the irregularities. This means that very low frequencies are scattered
only by very large irregularities, sometimes even bigger than the dimension of the room
itself. Relatively small irregularities are seen as ”flat” by large wavelengths. Figure 3.4
illustrates this behaviour with an example of a wall with a sawtooth-like design (from left
to right): first, the irregularities d are a lot smaller than the wavelength λ, so the wall is
seen as flat; then, for d ≈λ the reflections are diffuse; and for d ≫λ the reflections are
specular in relation to the angled design.
Figure 3.4: Behaviour of sound reflection depending on the incident wavelength. Taken
from [9].
Furthermore, measuring diffusion can be very challenging, which is discussed in the fol-
lowing section.
3.1.1
Measurement of diffuse reflections or scattering
There are several methods to measure diffuse reflections or scattering. This section gives
a summary of the main methods.
The ISO 17497 has two parts: the first part describes the measurement of the random-
incidence scattering coefficient in a reverberation room [10], and the second part is the
measurement of the directional diffusion coefficient in a free field [11].
In the reverberation chamber, it is possible to measure the random incidence scattering
coefficient, which is the input data used for room acoustic simulations. The advantage of
this method is that it is quicker and simpler than the free-field method, but on the other
hand, it is limited as it is not capable of giving the diffusion coefficient or polar responses.
The standard is based on Vorländer and Mommertz’s publication [12], which proposes
the measurement of a sample that is rotated in a turntable. The impulse responses are
measured for different sample orientations and averaged to obtain the specular energy
from the reflected pulses. Figure 3.5 shows a typical measurement with fixed source
and receiver positions and a turntable rotating the sample, in a scale model reverberation
room.

In contrast to the diffuse field, in the free-field it is possible to obtain angle-dependent
spatial diffusion. The standard describes two methods of placing the microphones to
obtain the polar response: using a 2D goniometer on a semicircle (fig. 3.6a) or a 3D
goniometer over a hemisphere (fig. 3.6b). These methods were introduced by Hargreaves
et al. [13] and D’Antonio [14], respectively.
In the case of a diffuser designed to scatter the sound in 2 dimensions, the semicircle
method is enough, otherwise the hemispherical is required to get a complete response.
However, the 3D measurement is expensive because it requires a complex acoustically
transparent structure in an anechoic chamber and it is very time-consuming due to the
large number of receiver positions.
There is also the possibility to measure in a non-anechoic room, if the room is large enough
for the reflections to be removed by windowing the impulse responses in the time domain.

To avoid near-field interference effects, the source and receivers should be placed in the
far field [13]. However, this is not always possible because the far-filed can be hundreds
of meters from the sample, so the information document from AES [14] recommends
that 80% of receivers are outside the specular zone. Furthermore, if the diffuser will be
used in the near-field for both source and receiver positions, the measurement has to be
performed in both near-field and far-field [11].
The NAH, which this thesis is about, is a solution for the difficulty of measuring in the
far-field. According to Kleiner [15], this technique makes it possible to measure in the
near-field and project the sound field to the far-field.
Moreover, Richard [16] measured both pressure and particle velocity with a sound in-
tensity probe in the near-field, then the far-field was extrapolated. This method is much
simpler and easier than building a half-circle in the far-field, but it is also expensive to
obtain a sound intensity probe or a microphone array.
3.2
Statistically Optimized Near-Field Acoustic Holography
(SONAH)
The SONAH method was introduced by Steiner and Hald [17] several years ago as a vari-
ant of NAH. An interesting aspect of SONAH is that it does not rely on spatial transforms,
which allows it to avoid the truncation effects that come with such transforms. As a result,
SONAH can work with smaller measurement arrays compared to NAH, which makes it
quite advantageous.
Inspired by studies that measured the sound absorption of ill-posed inverse problems (see
Section 3.2.1) utilizing different types of arrays [18, 19, 20], and by the analysis of the
sound field above finite absorbers in the wave-number domain [5], this study’s purpose is
to analyze the sound reflections of diffusers in the wave-number domain with the SONAH
method. With both NAH and SONAH, it is possible to determine, for instance, sound
pressure, particle velocity, acoustic vector intensity field, surface velocity, the intensity of
a vibrating source, etc [21].
The traditional approach for NAH involves using a microphone array to measure sound
pressure data, which is then subjected to discrete spatial Fourier transforms. However,
to prevent significant truncation errors resulting from the finite two-dimensional spatial
transforms and the consequent ”leakage” in the wave number domain, the array needs
to extend well beyond the sound source. This extension is necessary to ensure that the
sound pressure has decayed to a negligible level near the edges of the array [22]. One
advantage of the NAH technique is that the contribution of evanescent waves is present,
which is lost in classical methods of measuring diffusers because typically the source and
receiver are placed too far from the sample. This means that the SONAH is capable of
measuring more precisely, including information that is lost in other methods.
The process of reconstructing a sound field involves several key steps. First, sound pres-
sure is measured using a microphone array. Next, the measured data is expanded into
elementary plane waves. The reconstructed sound field is then generated using regular-
ization techniques (see Section 3.2.2) to account for any errors and inaccuracies [21, 22].
Finally, the reconstructed sound field can be analyzed in the wave-number domain, also
known as k-space.

According to Hadamard [23], a well-posed problem should have three properties: (1) a
solution exists, (2) the solution is unique, and (3) the solution depends continuously on

Experiment
The measurement was performed on 25th May 2023 at the large anechoic chamber from
DTU. The air condition was of 21oC and 44% relative humidity. There were 3 different
samples, which were measured utilizing an omnidirectional source (B&K OmniSource
Type 4295, fig. 5.2a) and an omnidirectional microphone (G.R.A.S. 46AC 1/2” free-field)
mounted in a robotic arm (Universal Robots). As shown in Figure 5.1, the samples were
laying on the net of the chamber, the source was hanging above the sample and the
robotic arm was mounted to a pole next to the sample. The excitation signal used was an
exponential sweep going from 400 Hz to 7 kHz in 2 s, followed by 2 s of silence.
Figure 5.1: Measurement of the flat sample. The source is hanging on the top, the sample
is laying on the net of the chamber and the microphone is connected to the end of the
robotic arm.
All samples have a circular base of 780 mm in diameter. The large half-sphere (fig. 5.2c)
has a diameter of 350 mm but it was cut above the center, so the distance between the top
of the half-sphere and the circular base is 165 mm. The 4 small half-spheres (fig. 5.2d)
have a diameter of 100 mm, the distance between the top of each half-sphere and the
circular base is 60 mm and the distance from the center of one to the other is 255 mm.

5.2
Large half-sphere diffuser results and discussion
According to [34], the scattering by a rigid sphere starts when the Helmholtz number (ka)
is equal to or greater than 1. Related to the dimensions of the large half-sphere, ka = 1
at 312 Hz. However, in this case, the sphere is cut above the equator and placed on a
circular baffle, so the ka estimation is not precise. The real ka is probably shifted to a
higher frequency as the height of the half-sphere is shorter than the radius. Also, this
estimation considers a whole sphere and no baffle around it.
The grid reconstruction of the full circumference was done following the same procedure
as the flat sample. The reconstructed total sound pressure for 600 Hz and 2.5 kHz are
presented in Figures 5.12a and 5.13a (other frequencies are shown in Appendix B). In
comparison with the flat sample at 600 Hz, there is a clear difference in the total mea-
sured pressure: a centred area of higher SPL, probably because of the pressure build-up
resulting from the presence of the half-sphere and the fact that the reflections start hap-
pening closer to the source. At 2.5 kHz, the ka relation is higher meaning that the results
agree with the theory because the total sound pressure contains oscillations in the SPL,
and it tends to vary more rapidly in the spatial domain as the frequency gets higher.

6
Conclusion
This thesis presented the use of the SONAH method in free-field with the objective of
obtaining the reflection sound field in the wave-number domain. First, a simulation of
simple cases was implemented and tested to be used for the signal processing of the ex-
perimental data. The total sound pressure of each position of the measurement grid was
estimated with Green’s function, simulating a monopole in the free field. Two main cases
were studied: with and without reflections resulting from an infinite rigid baffle. It was con-
cluded that the simulation had representative results of the incident and reflected sound
field in the wave-number domain. In addition, it was observed that even though the sim-
ulation creates an ideal case, some aspects were negatively affected by not having real
data input, for instance, the regularization parameter could not be automatically obtained
by the L-curve method and the aperture of the measurement array limits the coverage
of the rigid plane, as it is infinite. Those problems were not evident when analyzing real
measured data.
Then, 3 samples were measured in the anechoic chamber: a flat circular plate, a large
half-sphere mounted on a circular plate, and 4 small half-spheres also mounted on a
circular plate. As the plates had the same dimension, the influence of adding a large half-
sphere or several small half-spheres was analyzed in the wave-number domain. It was
concluded that the flat sample presented expected reflection patterns when compared to
the literature, and the samples with one or more half-spheres presented significant non-
specular reflections at high frequencies, according to the relation between the spheres’
dimensions and the wavelength. Therefore, this answers the research question: ”Can the
near-field acoustic holography method be used to evaluate the scattering properties of a
sample in the wave-number domain?” Yes, the scattering properties of the samples mea-
sured were present in the reflected wave-number spectra plots. Furthermore, this method
can be improved and further studied to achieve better results and different analyses.
For future studies, the SONAH could be implemented on the basis of spherical waves
instead of plane waves, for a better estimation. The digital signal processing could be
improved by using better resolution. The measurement array could be designed with a
larger aperture to cover the whole sample, instead of assuming symmetry. Then, different
angles of incidence could be evaluated, which was not possible in this study because
the source needed to be centred for the symmetry to be assumed in the measurement
procedure. More diffusers of different shapes could be measured. Finally, a numerical
simulation could be performed in order to compare with the experimental results.
