Abstract
Unmanned Surface vehicle (USV) have gained significant attention in recent years due
to their potential for autonomous operation in hazardous or remote environments, which
probably also have been defining this year’s Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics
Challenge (MBZIRC).
As a part of Technical University of Denmark (DTU)’s contribution to the MBZIRC, the ob-
jectives for this thesis is to design and implement a controller for the USV, which support
fully dynamic maneuvering. For the controller design, a dynamic model has been created
in MATLAB based models for surface vessels. Although only three degrees of freedom
(3DOF) are needed for this thesis, for further work, the model incorporates six degrees
of freedom (6DOF), including surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch and yaw. It considers vari-
ous forces and moments acting on the USV, such as hydrodynamic forces and external
disturbances.
The controller is designed as Proportional Integral Derivative (PID)-controllers for the
surge and sway velocity, a Proportional Integral (PI)-controller for rotational velocity and a
Proportional (P)-controller for the yaw positions. In addition, the system contains a Motor
Mixer Algorithm and a numbers of limiter functions.
The performance of the implemented control system is evaluated through simulation and
test on the physical USV. The USV’s ability to maneuver free in all direction, rotate, main-
tain a desired position, and respond to external disturbances is assessed. The results
demonstrate that the implemented controller effectively stabilizes the USV and enables
accurate maneuvering. In conclusion, the implemented controller successfully employed
dynamic control of the USV. The utilization of PID, PI and P controllers facilitated effective
maneuvering of the USV.

Chapter 1
Introduction
As autonomous robots have become a normality, the potential for unmanned processes
has grown – no matter wether it concerns a company’s optimizations, people’s pleasure
in everyday life or just gadgets.
This development is clearly seen within self-driving cars and drones, but it is also becom-
ing a big topic within boats and especially vessels for commercial use, which require high
precision in order to operate.
The current world situation with unsafe areas due to wars as well as the fast develop-
ment of unmanned autonomous systems have had an impact on this year’s Mohamed
Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge (MBZIRC). The competition includes several
sub-tasks, such as a drone part that aims to detect a specific vessel and a Unmanned
Surface vehicle (USV) that aims to autonomously sail from a harbor and to a designated
vessel, to load some packages. The packages are delivered by using a robotic arm. After
the packages have been delivered the USV must return to the harbor. In addition to the
more extensive sub-tasks, it is also challenge that the GPS - Global Positioning System
must not be used, as the system must be able to sail without any external communication.
This thesis covers the design of the USV’s controllers to control the angle and thrust for
the two motors based on its velocities in surge and sway direction and its orientation.
The final goal is therefore to be able to control the USV 100% dynamically based on some
local sensors. The USV must be able to; rotate, sails in all directions with an arbitrary
heading and keep a desired route and position without drifting.
Originally, the task was only to design the controllers, but due to a faulty simulator, a large
part of this thesis covers the design of a new model for the system.

Problem Statement and Objectives
The overall problem that this thesis addresses is the implementation of the necessary
functions and systems that are needed to operate/control the specific USV autonomously
in an efficient and optimal way. This includes the design and the software implementation
of an overall control systems which is necessary for controlling the USV dynamically. The
task is illustrated by the green box in Figure 1.1.The original schedule for project execution

However, due to deficiencies in the available material for the design of the controller, it
has been necessary to extend the task with the mathematical modeling of the USV. This
task has not been insignificant in the overall work, which is also reflected in this report.
The main objectives that need to be solved in the above task are described in general in
the following.

The objectives and success criteria are to:
• Create a dynamical model for the USV, from which it will be possible to design the
controllers and test the different implementations
• Come up with a solution for a Motor Mixer Algorithm (MMA) for determining the

• Design the controllers for the surge velocity, sway velocity and the heading of the
USV respectively
• Implement the functionalities in software which can be run on the real physical USV
• Implement and use sensors for returning velocities and heading of the USV
• Perform specific tests both in simulation and in implementation

1.2
Risk Assessment and Limitations
A major physical limitation is that the motor pods are mounted in the middle of each hull
and limited to only rotate ±88°. This will cause some difficulties if it is desired that the USV
should be able to be controlled 100% dynamically and thus be controlled with full control in
all scenarios. This makes it difficult to sail completely sideways, as the controller always
will be affected by a force in the surge (forward/backward) direction. It is a risk that this
result in some undesirable scenarios where the controllers either become unstable or the
motor pods rotates back and forth, and thereby affecting the movements of the USV.
In addition to the physical limitations of the USV, there are some limitations in terms of
lack of documentation and a flawed simulator which complicates the design process of
the controllers. In the early studies of the USV, it turned out that the given simulator is
a very simplified version of what the real world looks like. e.g. it does not contains any
implementation for going in the sway direction, which means that the USV is going about
5 times faster when going in the sway direction. Moreover, it has not been possible to
identify the implementation of the dynamic model.
Furthermore, the specification from the competition and SpinItalia is very variable in qual-
ity and hold divergent informations. This makes it difficult to understand the implemented
model as well as describe the implemented hardware for the simulator and the USV re-
spectively.
Another and significant risk is the missing insurance of the USV, which could reduce the
access to test the final implementation on the physical USV.

2.1
Framework for the Design
This project is part of a competition where five teams have advanced to compete to design
the best solution to the given problem. The entire project contains a numbers of subtasks,
based on both known theories and solutions, and some completely new issues/tasks.
The USV is produced by the Italian engineering company SpinItalia. They have been
responsible for designing and getting the physical boats produced. Additionly, Spinitalia
has delivered a simulator which should describe the boat and its dynamics. However, this
simulator is very flawed.
The boat (USV), which is designed as a catamaran has an unique setup in relation to the
motors placement and functionality. Where the motors are normally placed at the end of
the hulls, the two motors are placed in the center of each hull. Since this is not a normal
solution, no mathematical models and controller exist for a similar boat.
There are models for catamarans with two motors, but since these have the motors placed
a the stern, the boats can only be steered in the surge direction. When modeling the (USV),
it will therefore be possible to draw some parallels to existing catamaran designs, but not
in relation to the motor control, where a dynamically controlled catamaran is desired.
If it is desired to be able to control a catamaran dynamically in all directions, a known
implementation is to use 4 motors, placed at each end of the two hulls. This configuration
is a simple solution that always ensures that the boat has the same maneuverability, no

2.2.1
Controller Design
Proportional Integral Derivative (PID), Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), and Model Pre-
dictive Control (MPC) are widely used control techniques in various engineering applica-
tions. The 3 types of controllers are all based on a dynamic model of a given system.
The PID-controller is a classic Single Input Single Output (SISO) control technique that,
due to its simple structure and low complexity, is used in many different industrial pro-
cesses.
PID algorithm consists of three basic coefficients; proportional, integral and
derivative. The purpose with the proportional component is to reduce the steady-state
error. It responds to the current error value and produces an output proportional to the er-
ror. The integral component sums the error term over time. Even a small error will cause
the integral component to increase slowly, and helps to eliminate the steady state error
and thereby controls the system closer to the set point. The derivative component takes
into account the rate of change of the error and predicts the future trend of the error. A
rapidly change of the process variables will result in a change of the output, which helps
to reduce the system’s response, overshoot, and oscillations. [1]
The controllers lead terms can be placed in the measuring part (return path). This gives a
smaller bandwidth for the reference than if they are placed in the forward path. The smaller
bandwidth means that the settling time is slightly longer, but in general the overshoot is
lower. This ensures that the system does not provide a huge change in thrust for a small
adjustment of the system.
The transfer function for the closed loop system ends up being the same for the two
solutions, so the choice has no influence on the location of poles and zeros during the
design.
Another type of controller is the LQR-controller, which is based on optimal control theory.
The LQR-controller can handle Multiple Inputs Multiple Outputs (MIMO) systems and the
theory works by optimizing the performance by minimizing the cost function. To obtain
the optimal gain matrix, the algebraic Riccati equation is solved, which depends on the
system dynamics and the cost function weights. This part ensures that the LQR-controller
estimates its control input based on the current state. This way, stability guarantees and
optimal control actions are achieved based on a weighted combination of state variables.
The LQR-controller is an efficient controller type for linear systems, but its performance
may be limited when dealing with a non-linear or time-varying system. To work optimally,
the LQR-controller also requires an accurate linear model that describes the dynamics of
the system. [2]

A third control type is Model Predictive Control (MPC), which is an advanced control strat-
egy based on a dynamic model. MPC works by optimizing a performance criterion that
simultaneously takes into account constraints on inputs and states. This way, MPC can
predict future system behavior. This type of controller is particularly useful for systems
that contain constraints, but it has the disadvantage of requiring a very accurate reliable
model. [3]
Instead of the traditional control techniques, the task could be solved by using Fuzzy Logic
Control (FLC). This controller type is not based on a dynamic model of the system, but
uses fuzzy logic. Fuzzy logic is a rule based AI passed algorithm based on ”if then state-
ments” Compared to PID and LQR, the FLC has several advantages for dynamic systems.
First the FLC is incredibly robust to disturbances due to it’s non-linear capabilities.
Secondly, the controller does not need a model of the system to work. This makes it’s
very easy to design and implement a robust and intelligent controller without the need of
deriving a set of equations that describe the system dynamics.
Finally, the FLC also tends to have a faster settling time with little or no overshoot. A
disadvance is that it’s requires a large computing power to function. Furthermore, it is
very difficult to tune the controller if it does not fit the final physical implementation. [4]
Decision
Based on the advantages, it could be interesting to use Fuzzy Logic for the USV controller
implementation, but because the FLC requires a lot of computational power, as well as
the risk of having problems with fine-tuning the controllers, this solution is not chosen.
Instead a more traditional solution based on a dynamic model for the system is chosen.
A PID-controller is chosen exclusively to limit the complexity of implementation, and to
ensure the possibility for easy fine tuning of the controller if it does not fully fit the physical
implementation.
The solution with the lead parts in the return path is chosen because the USV is a slow
system, where a small oscillations has no influence on the USV, whereas it is ensured
that a small change in input does not causes large overshoot of the control signal.
2.2.2
Dynamic Model
In order to be able to design the controllers for the USV, a dynamic model must be set up
which describes the effects and movements of the USV. Already in the early phase of this
thesis, it was clear that the delivered simulator model is not representative of the delivered
USV. This is evident from the fact that the USV sails about five times faster in the sway di-
rection (sideways) than it does when sailing in the surge direction (forward and backward).
To solve this problem, it is necessary to have access to the exact documentation for the
implemented simulator which has not been possible. Secondly, it is necessary to know
the correct hydro parameter coefficients of the real USV. It turns out that the given hydro
parameters coefficients for the surge direction do not matched with those expected for a
similarly modeled vessel. Furthermore, the delivered conversion formulas from velocity
to thrust do not match SpinItalia’s own tests. Due to these problems, it is not possible to
base the design on the accompanying simulator as originally planned.
A widely recognized method for describing surface vessels is the model described by (Fos-
sen 2011) [5]. The model is based on the theories of Rigid body kinetics, Restoring forces
for surface, Seakeeping and Maneuvering. Rigid body Kinetics describes the motion of
rigid body vessels, hydrodynamics and hydrostatics. The restoring forces describe the
forces caused by the pressure of a liquid acting on a submerged surface. The Seakeep-
ing theory is a mathematical model which describes the movements of a certain vessel
Implementation of Controllers for Operating an USV
7
==PAGE:20==
when it is affected by waves and flow. Maneuvering theory, on the other hand, aims to
describe the vessel’s movements through the water and response based on the given
inputs and influences. [6]
Decisions
Due to the problem of identifying the parameters and modeling the dynamic model, it was
decided early in the process to design a model from scratch based on a known theory.
This model is used for creating up and testing the transfer function for the overall system.
Once the design of the dynamic model is completed, the hydro parameter coefficients will
be estimated by examining the USV and similar vessels.
The dynamic model will be created based on a 6 Degrees Of Freedom (DOF) rigid body
model for a surface vessel described by (Fossen 2011). In theory, it is only necessary to
take 3 DOF into account, as the USV only operates in 3 DOF.The reason for choosing a
6 DOF model is that it is one of the most frequently used models for computer simulation,
as it describes the totale dynamics of an USV [6]. In addition, the model can be used for
further work. There will thus be an opportunity to work with the roll and pitch when, for
example, the robot arm is to be implemented.
2.2.3
MMA
The Motor Mixer Algorithm (MMA) is an important part of the control unit for the two mo-
tors. When maneuvering the USV, the MMA is responsible for controlling the input to the
actuators for angle and thrust of the two motors.
There are several setup options to achieve optimal control of the USV. To reduce the
complexity of the final solution, the controller can implement different modes for e.g.: to
sail long distances or to sail sideways during e.g. harbour manoeuvres. This way, it will
be possible to switch between modes and thus be able to control the USV in all directions
and orientations. At the same time, some fixed standard setups for the motor pods will
provide some simpler control scenarios.
A more advanced solution would be to implement a fully dynamic model, which means
that the MMA constantly calculates an angle and thrust for the two motors. This way, the
motors are constantly adjusted freely to move the USV in the desired directions. The
advantages of the dynamic solution is that it takes into account that the boat does not drift
during various maneuvers or is set by current and wind. This advantage makes especially
good sense as the USV must sail very precisely, as during the final competition it will be
limited in getting location data from e.g. Global Positioning System (GPS) signal. The
USV must be able to sail long distances based solely on a direction from a Gyroscope
(Gyro) and local measurements of the surge and sway velocities. Here, drift from external
influences, as well as any changes in orientation or circular movements to avoid obstacles,
will impact the USV’s final destination.
During the implementation of the MMA, the previously described limitations caused by the
location of the motors as well as the limit to rotate the motors by more than ±88° must be
taken into account. It is necessary to draw up some solutions where the motors do not
give thrust in a direct line. At the same time, it must be taken into account that the USV
always has a direction or orientation change doing sideways sailing.
One way to avoid the problems that arise when sailing 100% sideways would be to turn
the USV by e.g. 45◦, after which the USV can counteract with thrusts at an opposing
angle. However, this solution just causes some problems with not being able to get close
and hold position close to objects.

Decisions
To achieve the most optimal solution for the entire project, the 100% dynamic solution
is chosen, but to reduce the errors derived from the physical limitations of the USV, the
solution will be divided into some overall states.
Besides the MMA, it is necessary to create a limitation block, which ensure that the USV
does not receive commands that are outside the limit values and cause that the USV does
not sail as desired. The limitation block must take into account different scenarios. In
situations where the USV is either going to rotate or sailing sideways, it can be necessary
to reduce the velocity to have enough thrust to ensure correct maneuverability of the USV.
Since maneuvers as rotating and sideways sailing are often happen when the USV is
docking or doing precision work, it is important that the limitation block takes into account
these scenarios and prioritized orientation over the velocity.
This means that the velocities of the two directions will be deprioritized if the USV’s thrust
exceeds its limitation. If it is not possible to prioritize the orientation, the original thrusts
are scaled.

2.2.4
Code Implementation and Interface
The USV is supplied with two physical motor controllers which each handles a motor.
The two motor controllers are connected via a Controller Area Network (CAN)-BUS, from
which they receive motor commands. Furthermore, a CAN-BUS converter which contains
a small micro-controller has been delivered with the boat. This converter converts a given
Robot Operating System (ROS)-topic into the relevant CAN-BUS messages. It is there-
fore necessary to add a Personal Computer (PC) or a dedicated controller. The ROS
publisher must ensures that the ROS-topics are sent with the desired update frequency,
which must be between 5 and 10 Hz. The publisher can either be implemented in C++ or
Python.
An example of a widely used controller is the Pixhawk-controller. The Pixhawk-controller
is a flight controller designed to control autonomous vehicles based on open source. How-
ever, the Pixhawk is primarily designed to handle Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV)s, but it
is possible to use it as control for autonomous boats, cars, etc. The Pixhawk controller can
either phrase ROS topics, or send CAN-BUS messages directly to the motor controllers.
But due to Pixhawk’s very specific setups and functionality combined with selected sen-
sors, it is very limited in terms of controlling the given USV.
Another solution would be to design a new controller unit that runs on a dedicated com-
puter. The sensors are then connected directly to the computer and can run in a com-
pletely separate thread. The reasonable choice for programming language is either Python,
C or C++. Python is a very simple programming language, but on the other hand, threads
are not completely separate as known from C and C++ programs. The C language offers
great efficiency and high performance. With both the C and C++ compiler it is possible to
generate a library that can be called from a Python script. C++ has the advantage that it
has many standard libraries and it is easy to define classes in. If it is desired to use the
ROS conversion box, both C++ and Python can be used.
All these solutions are 100% open source and can freely be changed and corrected.

Chapter 4
Motor Mixer Algorithm
This chapter describes the Motor Mixer Algorithm (MMA), which is responsible for the
dynamic and individual control of the two motors. The MMA receives its input directly from
the designed controllers and sends the motor thrust and angle commands to the physical
motor controller integrated in the USV. The following section describes the implementation
of the MMA and the associated considerations and calculations.
The MMA must at the same time handle the USV’s limitations caused by the motors loca-
tion, the motor pods rotation limit and the maximum motor thrusts.
4.1
The Mixer Algorithm
When it comes to the motor settings, there are in principle infinite numbers of possible
solutions to make the USV sail in a given direction with the same resultant vector force.
It is therefore important to find the most optimal solution, which is the one with the lowest
power consumption.
4.1.1
Motor Settings
The wish for the final design is a solution that is 100% dynamically controlled. This means
that the motor pods of the USV can be freely adjusted to achieve full control. However,
it is already known that the limitations of the USV and the motors must be taken into
account. It must be clarified which possible solutions exist and how they will affect the
USV in different scenarios.
The biggest challenges are related to sideways sailing. If e.g. both motors are set with
a constant and equal trust at an angle of 88◦, the USV will quickly achieve a velocity
in the surge direction. Due to the dynamics of the USV, it does not require as much
power to move back and forth as it requires to move laterally. Another problem that is
related to sideways sailing is that the two motors almost push in a straight line which
makes it difficult to control the orientation of the USV. As these scenarios mainly occur in
relation with harbor maneuvers or when the USV has to moor to a vessel, it is necessary
to prioritize orientation over velocity. For this reason, it is decided to have both motors

4.2
Limiter
This section describes the designed limiter function. The purpose of the limiter is to ensure
that the USV does not receive commands that are out of range in terms of too high thrust

4.2.1
Thrust Limiter
As the USV must be able to sail in all directions, specific controllers are designed to handle
the velocity for surge, the velocity for sway, and rotation respectively. Due to the motors
thrust limit at ±1000N, scenarios arise where the limit may be exceeded due to input
from multiple controllers. For example, if the USV sails in 45◦with a velocity in the sway
direction of 2m
s , which is the maximum velocity, and at the same time must rotate with a
given angular velocity, it is a risk that one or both motors have to deliver a larger thrust
than possible.
If the thrust values get out of range, the motor inputs will be set to its maximum, which
causes that the USV does not sail as desired. To avoid this, some limiters are set up.
The scenarios where the problem often occurs are when the USV must rotate during
sideways or fast forward sailing. This is often the result of either harbor maneuvers or
precision sailing on long distances, why the orientation of the USV is being prioritized.
This is done by calculating the value by which the thrust exceeds the maximum thrust.
If both motors have a positive or negative coefficient, the excess is subtracted or added
from both motor thrust, otherwise the excess is added to the one with negative coefficient
and subtracted from the one with positive coefficient.
Some scenarios may arise, where e.g. the thrust of one motor is much higher than the
thrust of the other motor, or if the USV sails sideways with an angle of α1 locked to 70◦.
If this happens, it will not be possible to use the limitation option mentioned above. Here,
a scaling of the thrust is chosen instead. Compared to the other limitation solution, the
rotational force is reduced. This can be shown by a simple example where the thrusts

Since the output of the MMA is not necessarily a real solution where the angle of the two
pods are within the maximum rotation, it is necessary to check whether the solution is
within the maximum angle of ±88◦. If the angle is not within ±90◦, the motor pods are
rotated 180◦and the thrust is reversed.
The code is structured so it first checks if the angles of the pods are within ±180◦. If this is
not the case, 2π is subtracted or added depending on the coefficients. It is then checked
whether the angles are within ±90◦. If this is not the case, the motors are rotated and get
the opposite coefficient.
This solution may result in an angle that is just outside the constraint of ±88◦if the USV
sails 100% sideways. For this scenario, α2 is limited to ±88◦.

Chapter 5
Controllers
This chapter describes the implemented controllers. Throughout the chapter, it will be
explained how the non-linear dynamic model described in chapter 4 is linearized to the
considerations of each of the individual controllers. A controller is designed for surge,
sway, rotational velocity and yaw position respectively. All the controllers are based on
the PID theory and are originally designed as pure P controller from which the I and D
terms are added after inspection of the systems.
5.1
Linearization
Due to the complexity of the implemented model, it is decided to linearize the model by us-
ing Matlab instead of manually transforming the system equations into a state space form
based on Jacobi. However, it should be mentioned that the model cannot be linearized
directly from Matlab’s built-in functions due to the process used to implement the dynamic
model. Among other things, the implementation contains different sample times.
For this reason, it has been chosen to describe the system based on a black-box modelling.
Black-box linearization is a method to describe a non-linear system with a linear model
without knowing the system’s internal dynamics. The black-box linearization is solely
based on the relationship between input and output for the operating points of the given
system. A linear model is designed for each controller by entering the desired operating
point plus some white noise. Based on input and output, it is then possible to describe
the system’s behavior in the event of changes around the operating point. [15]
First, input and output are matched since the dynamic model results in a faster input fre-
quency than output frequency. This is done very simply by creating a function in matlab
which only saves every 5th value. This way, two vectors with associated input and output
values are obtained. These values are stored in the ”iddata object”, which is a way of han-
dling input-output data. The Matlab function ”tfest” is then used. This function estimates
a model based on the ”iddata object”, as well as a description of the number of poles and
zeros for the system using a non-linear model.

the surge dynamics is set up based on a first-order system with one pole and one zero.

The transfer function use a cruising speed of 4knots = 4 · 0.5144 = 2.0576 m

5.2.4
Anti Windup and Physical Controller design for the PI and
PID-Controllers
In a control system that contains integrators, an error can occur due to windup. Windup
refers to the error that the integrator accumulates even when the system is saturated or
constrained. Various solutions exist to avoid the controller’s integrator term continuing
to count up and thus causing instability. A very easy way in terms of implementation
is to saturate the integral with a maximum and minimum value based on the system’s
limitations. This solution ensures that the value of the integral is kept within the saturation.
Another and slightly more complicated method is to add a saturation on the output of
the controller which indicates the USV’s maximum and minimum velocity. This solution
provides a dynamic limitation of the integral term, by taking the difference from the output
before and after the saturation block and adding this to the integral value. Furthermore,
an anti windup constant must be added, where a low constant leads to a large contribution
from the integral term and a high one leads to a small contribution. [17] [18]
For the surge direction, the dynamic solution for the anti windup is chosen based on sev-
eral considerations. At first, it must be easy to adjust the parameters afterwards, as it must
help to smooth out the output when the maximum values are reached. Secondly, it must
be easy to make the solution fully dynamic, so that the contribution from the integrated
part is reduced by e.g. low velocities. This way, instabilities based on error values can be
minimized. This will especially help solving the major stability problems seen when the

6.1
Sensor Used for Tests
In order to test the implemented controller, it is necessary to have reference signals for
the USV’s three degrees of freedom; rotation, surge and sway. It is obviously to use GPS
for reference, but the GPS will only be able to deliver reference data for surge and sway
in the form of velocity and heading. As reference input to the rotation (orientation of the
USV) a Gyroscope is chosen. The following sections hold an overall description of the
GPS system and the Gyroscope that is used for the test.
6.1.1
Gyro
To generate the reference signal for the rotation a KVH DSP-1760 Gyroscopes is chosen.
This Gyroscope is of the type Fiber Optic Gyroscope (FOG), and it measures angular
velocity and orientation by exploiting the properties of light in a closed circuit. The FOG
type works by sending two laser beams in opposite directions into a fiber optic coil. When
the fiber optic coil is rotated, the beam traveling against the rotation experiences a shorter
path than the other beam, a phenomenon called the Sagnac effect. The difference in
phase shift between the two beams is then used to estimate the rotation rate. Compared
with the Ring Laser Gyroscope (RLG) which use several mirrors to transport the laser
beam around mirrors instead of a coil. The construction of the RLG type is very critical to
vibrations, which is why mechanically it is more expensive than the FOG. For this reason,
the FOG type has also become the default choice in applications that require long-distance
navigation in non-GPS environments. [21]
6.1.2
GPS
Global Positioning Systeym GPS also known as a basic Global Navigation Satellite Sys-
tem (GNSS) receiver provides a coordinate based on longitude and latitude. These coor-
dinates set can be transformed into a polar coordinate system, from which it is possible
to calculate the velocities. The problem with a standard GNSS is that they often have
an update frequency of 1HZ, which means that an observer filter for estimation of the
position data is needed. Furthermore, a standard GNSS only has a precision in meters,
which could result in large jumps at especial low speed. If GNSS is going to be used for
Implementation of Controllers for Operating an USV

==PAGE:56==
the initial test, it will be necessary to use a Real Time Kinematic (RTK) based GNSS. This
type has simultaneous known reference stations, which gives a precision in centimeters.

6.2
Sensors for the Competition
In the final version, the USV must be able to sail without external communication. It will
therefore be necessary to install some local sensors on the USV which do not depend on
external inputs. As for the test of the controller, the gyroscope will be used to measure
the orientation of the USV. If the gyroscope is combined with two accelerometers it will by
integration of the acceleration be possible to calculate input for heading and velocity in the
surge and sway direction. Often accelerometers are integrated in an Inertial Measurement
Unit (IMU) together with a gyroscope. Unfortunately, an IMU with a precision that is high
enough for this application is quite expensive, why it early in the project was decided not
to use accelerometers. Instead of accelerometers the reference signals for surge and
sway can be generated by speed logs.
The following describes different types of speed logs and how they work. The various
log types will be briefly reviewed with advantages and disadvantages. Finally, the section
holds some recommendation for selection of log sensors. Speed logs, or just logs, mea-
sure the speed of a vessel. The speed can either be determined with reference to the
water flowing by the hull (Speed through water) or to the seabed (speed over ground).
The typically types of speed logs
• Impeller log
• Pitot meter log
• Electromagnetic log
• Doppler log
6.2.1
Impeller log
An impeller log is based on an impeller or small propeller that protrudes from the bottom
of the vessel. The movement of the vessel’s hull through the water causes the impeller to
rotate. The rotation of the impeller produces an electric impulse that is proportional with
the speed. The counts or revolutions of the impeller can then be translated into speed
through the water.
6.2.2
Pitot meter log
The basic technology of the pitot meter log is similar to that known from aircraft pitot tubes.
The biggest difference is simply that the pitot meter log uses water to compress the air
inside the tube.
Typically, the pitot meter log has a long tube that usually has two openings: one facing the
direction of motion used to measure the dynamic seawater pressure, and one facing 90°
to the direction of motion to measure the static seawater pressure. The speed can then
be calculated as function of the difference in static and dynamic pressure. Because speed
is calculated as a function of the difference between pressures, the pitot meter does not
give an accurate result at low speed, where the static and dynamic pressure are nearly
equal. [23]
6.2.3
Electromagnetic log
The electromagnetic velocity log is based on the law of induction, which states that if
a conductor moves across a magnetic field, an Electromotive Force (e.m.f) is set up in
44
Implementation of Controllers for Operating an USV
==PAGE:57==
the conductor. An e.m.f will also be induced if the conductor remains stationary and the
magnetic field is moved relative to it.
Using a coil, the log transducer generates a vertical magnetic field in the water around the
transducer. As the conductor (water) moves through this magnetic field, an electromotive
force is induced. This e.m.f is picked up by 2 electrodes. Due to the length of the conductor
and the magnetic field are maintained constant, the generated e.m.f.is proportional to the
speed of the vessel.
When placing the transducer, it is important not to place it too close to the hull, as the
non-linearity of the hull can result in a water flow that is incorrect in relation to the actual
speed of the vessel. Another thing that affects the measurement is pitching and rolling,
but the effect of these can be reduced by having an electrical time constant longer than a
period of the vessel’s motion. Two types of sensors exist, a single-axis measuring surge
velocity and a dual-axis that measures both the surge and the sway velocity. [24]
6.2.4
Doppler log
Doppler log is based on the principle of doppler shift in frequency measurement, e.g. will
a reflected signal relative to the source change frequency when the distance between
source and observer changes. By measuring the received frequency and knowing the
value of the transmitted frequency and velocity of sound in seawater, the speed of the
vessel can be determined.
The doppler log responds to reflections of the transmitted signal from the seabed, and
as the only type of log, the doppler log is able to measure the Speed Over Ground. If
the beam is propagated from one layer of water to another of different composition or
temperature, a small part of the signal will be reflected. There will also be a doppler effect
if the second layer moves relative to the first layer. If the speed is found on basis of water
layers the speed is called ”Speed Over Water”. The doppler log is only able to receive
echoes from the seabed down to a depth of approx. 200 meters. But as mentioned it will
still be possible to measure the speed based on water layers as long as they are in a dept
between 10 to 30 metres below the hull. It is possible to get doppler logs as a single-axis
that measuring only the surge velocity, and as dual-axis that measures both the surge and
the sway velocity. As with the electromagnetic log, the effect of rolling and pitching will
cause errors in velocities, The effect of pitching will cause an error in surge velocity but
has no effect on the sway velocity. Similarly, roll will only impact the sway velocity. [25]
6.3
Sensor Catch Up
To test the controller, it was initially chosen to use a GPS, but it quickly turned out that a
normal GNSS receiver is not accurate enough to describe the velocity of the USV’s in the
sway direction. A test of the entire functionality of the controller would therefore require
the use of a RTK-Base GNSS.
The Gyro is a necessary sensor for the tests. A Gyro or other form of electronic compass
is the only option to generate a reference signal for the orientation of the USV.
To ensure precise control of the USV, so that it can autonomously navigate to a certain
position or hold its position, it must have access to precise data for both orientation and
velocities .
In order to obtain the necessary data for the orientation of the USV, the Gyro from the
test will be used. Additionally, one/more sensors will be needed to measure the USV’s
velocities in the surge and sway direction. All the described logs can be used, but the
Implementation of Controllers for Operating an USV
45
==PAGE:58==
most obvious choice would be a dual-axis doppler logs. Even that the doppler log is the
most expensive type, it is chosen because it is able to measure the Speed Over Ground
(SOG), which includes the contribution from drift caused by wind and current. If one of
the other types of log is used, there is a risk that errors from drift are accumulated, and
causing the USV to miss the desired destination when it is sailing longer distances.

This chapter has reviewed the possible sensors that can be used to test and verify the
controller. In addition, the chapter contains recommendations for which sensors can be

For testing the implemented controller, a Gyro and a RTK base GNSS will be sufficient,

doppler log, which provides speed over ground for both surge and sway.

Chapter 10
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results obtained from the test in chaptor 9.
10.1
The Dynamic Model
Already early in the project it was clear that the delivered simulator did not describe the
actual behavior of the USV, and it was decided to describe the USV with a dynamic model.
The dynamic model is based on the kinematic models for Seakeeping and Maneuvering
of a Rigid Bodie. However, the available data has been very limited. Important data as
the hydro coefficients have not been disclosed, and it has not been possible to make new
CFD calculations. The dynamic model is therefore created from a limited data set and
analysis of video recording from demo sailing with USV.
Due to the lack of information and the lack of opportunity to launch and measure the USV,
it has been difficult to document that the model describes the physical USV.
It may therefore be necessary to make some adjustments to the final controllers in order
to achieve a complete match with the physical USV. Specifically, it may be necessary to
adjust the formula for converting velocity to thrust for the sway direction. Apart from these
uncertainties, the dynamic model is considered a realistic model for the specific USV, and
it has thus been possible to use it for designing the controllers.
10.2
Controllers
Based on the design theory of PID-controllers and some hand tuning, the final controllers
ended up giving some very good results. None of the controllers had a rise time of more
than 4 s and with minimal overshoot. Considering that USV is a relatively slow system, a
rise time of approx. 4 s without much overshoot is acceptable for the desired operating
points. The only controller that has a slight overshoot is the rotational velocity controller,
and it might be beneficial to change it to a PID instead of a PI-controller. On the other
hand, this is not considered necessary due to the small overshoot, which is only 5.08◦.
The final controllers therefore describe some realistic systems where the rise time is not
infinitely fast, by delivering infinitely high thrusts.

10.3
MMA
The main objective of the project was to create a system that make it possible to sail
the given USV 100% dynamically. The USV should be able to sail in all directions, hold
its position, and correct for drift caused by current and wind. Due to the dynamics of the
USV, it requires a larger thrust to sail in the sway direction than in the surge direction. This
means that the motors already reach the outer positions when sailing around 45◦, from
which it does not make sense to control the two motor pods synchronously anymore. The
three designed state for the MMA are therefore necessary to maintain the controllability.
One of the major problems in being able to dynamically control the USV concerns the
limit of the motor’s pod angles. To avoid instability from rotating motor pods when sailing
sideways, the sideways limiter state is activated.
This state shuts off motor thrust and rotate the motor all the way around, then holds for 2
seconds. This fix helps to maintain maneuverability, but the fix causes the USV to be out
of control for 2s. Another solution would have been to be able to freely control the USV
only with ±45◦. Here it will be possible to turn the USV 45◦and then sail to the desired
destination with ±45◦. However, this solution would introduce a problem in relation to
mooring and approaching the side of a vessel. Additionally, this solution would introduce
major restrictions on input to the system. The implemented model is therefore considered
to be the best and most flexible solution, as it supports fully sideways navigation, but at
the same time includes the sailing pattern from the alternative solution, as long as the
angle is less than ±45◦.
As the USV is able to sail freely in all directions, it is also possible to keep the USV
stationary in one position even if it is impacted by external forces. At the same time, the
implemented algorithms ensure that the USV takes drift into account.
If the controller should support that the USV can stay on the same position when it is
affected by sideways drift, it will be necessary to look at the criteria for activating the
various limiter functions. An additional scenarios can with advantage be implemented,
that change the limiter functions for sideways mode, when the references for surge and
sway are 0m
s and the USV measure a drift that is within the range for sideways mode.
Since the USV constantly get input from the path planner, the drift is not seen as a problem.
The path planner will be able to take a little drift into account and thus correct the reference
inputs.
Alternatively, a special command can be implemented to rotate the USV upstream. The
USV will then be able to lie completely still only by looking at the direction of the drift and
thereby keeping the reference inputs as 0m
s . This solution will also work if the path planner
is unable to detect the drift.
10.4
Code Implementation and Physical Tests
Since there has been no access to test the software on the physical USV during sailing,
the test is limited to a test of the individual controllers. The testing of the physical imple-
mentation is limited to checking that the c-code works in the same way as in the simulation
of the dynamic model. Due to the lack of testing during sailing, it must be expected that
the implementation requires some fine-tuning before it works correctly on the physical
USV. The root cause for these uncertainties must be traced back to the limited amount of
data that has been available to design the dynamic model.
Due to the above, a slight adjustment is expected to be necessary. In particular, it may be

necessary to adjust the time when the motors are locked during sideways sailing. When
the controllers are implemented so they easily can be adjusted, it is expected to be easy
to fine-tune the implementation during test sailing.
10.5
Sensors
In this thesis, possible sensors for use in tests and in the final product are reviewed. To
ensure accurate and precise control of the USV, the controller needs reference inputs for
rotation, surge and sway velocity. It is obvious to use a Gyroscope to describe orientation
and rotational velocity. As a reference input for the surge and sway velocity, it will be
obvious for the controller test to use input from an RTK based GPS. The RTK based GPS
has an accuracy that is measured in cm. As the USV in the competition must be able to
sail without external communication, it will be necessary to equip it with a speed log. To
ensure that the controller corrects the course for drift, it is a requirement that the speed
is measured as the absolute speed (Speed Over Ground). Based on the requirement,
it makes sense to choose a dual-axis doppler log. The dual-axis doppler log measures
surge and sway velocities simultaneously and, as the only type, it able to measure speed
over the ground. However, compared to the other log types this solution is also the most
expensive.
10.6
Overall Discussion of the System
Although the limitations of the system have caused major challenges throughout the de-
sign phase, it has been possible to design the implemented functions and controllers so
that the USV can be controlled 100% dynamically. The delivered solution fulfill all the ob-
jectives described i Section 1.1. But due to the physical limitations of the USV, it has been
necessary to create some limiter functions that compensate for these. If the angle of the
motor pods had not been limited to ±88◦, or if the USV had been equipped with 4 motors,
the limiter functions could have been omitted. Secondly, the system would have been
much more robust and the USV would have been able to handle the various maneuvers
with higher velocities, just as it would have been possible to have constant thrust during
sideways sailing.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This thesis has addressed the design and implementation of the necessary controllers for
a 100% dynamic control of the USV to be used in MBZIRC.
As a design basis for the necessary controllers, a dynamic model in Simulink has been
created that describes the movements of the USV. The dynamic model is based on the
Kinematic Model for Seakeeping and Maneuvering of Vessels described by (Fossen 2011).
Although this task only requires 3 degrees of freedom, the model is made with 6 degrees of
freedom for later extensions. Based on the provided data for the USV as well as analysis
of videos, it is concluded that the developed dynamic model describes the USV sufficiently
to form the basis for the final design of the controllers. After a linearization of the dynamic
model, the following controllers have been designed; PID-controllers for the surge and
sway velocity, a PI-controller for rotational velocity and a P- controller for the yaw positions.
In addition to the individual controllers, the system contains a Motor Mixer Algorithm, as
well as a number of limiter functions that compensate for the physical limitations of the
USV.
Before the design was implemented in C-code, a thorough simulation was carried out on
the entire system to verify that all requirements were fulfilled. In order to implement the
controllers in C-code, they were discretized using the Tustin approximation. Due to the
lack of opportunity to sail with the USV, the test of the final test was limited to a functional
test on shore.
The simulations and testing of the implemented controllers shows that implemented con-
trollers are able to rotate and control the USV freely in all directions. If the USV is equipped
with a doppler log that measures the speed over ground, it will be able to autonomously
sail longer distances under influence of drift. The implemented controller thus supports
fully dynamic control of the USV, whereby it fulfills all the objectives for this thesis.
