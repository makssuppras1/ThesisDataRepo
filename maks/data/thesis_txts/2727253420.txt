Assessment of AC based topology for offshore energy islands
Abstract
In the coming decades, large scale offshore wind power will play a key role in supplying the
onshore grid. In order to meet the increasing demand for renewable energy, the windfarms
will be built farther and farther at sea and combined with a new kind of transmission system:
offshore energy hubs connected to each other and to the onshore grid by HVDC cables. The
energy islands will exhibit unprecedented characteristics, especially a very low inertia, and
will need carefully chosen control strategies to ensure their operational stability and resilience
to faults. This project focuses on assessing the feasibility of a 4 GW AC offshore energy hub.
A model of the hub is built using the simulation software PowerFactory. Different fault and
disturbance scenarios are then simulated to assess the capacity of the implemented control
solutions to ensure that key operational parameters (such as frequency, voltage and active
power dispatch) remain within acceptable ranges.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In December 2015, the 196 countries participating in the United Nations Climate Change
Conference (COP 21) agreed to reduce their greenhouse gases emissions as soon as possible
and aim at containing global warming "well below 2°C". In order to reach this target, the
European countries intensified their efforts to phase out fossil fuels from their energy mix.
For the electrical power system, this means replacing coal, gas or oil-fired power plants by
Renewable Energy Sources (RES) such as wind turbine farms or solar panels, all the while
increasing the total power production to meet the demand created by the electrification of
many sectors (for example transportation with the Electric Vehicles). In 2018, the European
Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-E) presented several
scenarios for the future development of the european power system. They are shown in
Figure 1.
Figure 1: Scenario-building framework indicating bottom-up and top-down scenarios [1]
All these scenarios include a very high increase in the power production of wind, with
the mass-scale implementation of offshore windfarms appearing as absolutely necessary. In
2020, while presenting the European Green Deal, the European Commission reaffirmed its
commitment to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared to 1990
and become climate neutral by 2050, and announced its "Strategy to harness the potential
of offshore renewable energy for a climate neutral future" [11], in which ambitious objectives
are set: the installed capacity of offshore wind should reach at least 60 GW in 2030 and
300 GW by 2050. Starting from the 22.1 GW of capacity installed at the end of 2019, the
investments required are estimated at up to 800 billion e.

1.2
Offshore Energy Hub (OEH)
It is thus certain that in the near future, many new windfarms will be built in the waters of
the European countries. The already existing ones are located close to the shores, and are for
most of them linked to the onshore transmission grids thanks to point-to-point High Voltage
Alternating Current (HVAC) cables. However, the coming capacity will be built farther and
farther off the coasts. As a consequence, the high amount of reactive power compensation
equipment required by the HVAC cables will prevent their use, and the transmission tech-
nology implemented will be High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) cables. Moreover using
point-to-point connections will prove too costly, so the windfarms located close to each other
will instead be connected to an Offshore Energy Hub (OEH) / Offshore Energy Island that
itself will be a node of a meshed, multi-terminal offshore grid. Figure 2 shows a schematic
representation of such an OEH. This topology presents a number of advantages[12]:
• The cost-efficiency of the offshore transmission infrastructure would be greatly im-
proved.
• The security of supply would be improved as alternative paths could be used to transmit
power through the meshed grid in the event of the failure of a HVDC cable.
• The integration of new projects would be eased as the transmission infrastructure could
deliberately be oversized at the time of its construction to take into account the future
expansions of the system.
• The expansions of the system would also be eased by the modular nature of the energy
islands.
• Less reinforcement of the existing onshore grid would be required.
• Since HVDC technologies offer good controllability of the power flows, the offshore grid
would be very flexible.
• The energy islands could meet the grid-code requirements and remove their constraints
from the the windfarms.
• The energy islands could be good points where to integrate PtX technologies.
However, operating an OEH could prove to be very challenging.
Indeed the OEHs will
be "extreme power systems" for which Transmission System Operators have no previous
experience.

1.4
Motivation and formulation of the problem
In consequence, although the timeline of the construction and commission of the first Offshore
Energy Islands is already known, a great research effort is simultaneously undertaken to
identify the topologies, equipment type and control strategies that would enable the reliable
and efficient operation of the Offshore Energy Islands and allow the performance expectations
for such promising systems to be met.
Feasibility assessments of the already identified
potential solutions are also required.
It is with this goal in sight that this project will
focus on modelling a realistic AC based Offshore Energy Hub thanks to the simulation
software PowerFactory, simulate its response to different kinds of fault scenarios and assess
the stability and resilience of the system design.

2
Building of the OEH model
This chapter presents the different elements that constitute an Offshore Energy Island as
well as some of the different design possibilities.
2.1
Comparison of AC-coupled and DC-coupled OEHs
The most important choice when designing an OEH is the decision to couple the different
HVDC links on the AC bus (in which case the system is described as an AC hub) or on the
DC bus (the system is then described as a Multi-Terminal HVDC (MTDC)) of the offshore
substation. Several key criteria of the OEH that designers try to optimise are substantially
impacted by the type of coupling chosen, such as the CAPEX of the project, the transmission
losses while in operation or the resilience of the system to disturbances. The designers must
also take into account the readiness of the technologies used and the possibility to easily
integrate the OEH in an expanded network.
The advantages and disadvantages of the AC side coupling are listed below [13]:
• Advantages
• The DC cables can be of different voltage levels, which gives more freedom to the
designers.
• All components used in this topology are technologically mature and widely used world-
wide.
• Disadvantages
• High risk of AC side control and stability issues since the offshore AC grid will form a
low-inertia (potentially zero-inertia) system.
• Higher losses when used as an interconnector since the power flow undergoes several
AC/DC conversions.
• Operation of the system will require cooperation from HVDC vendors and windfarms
owners.
• The reconfiguration of the OEH into an MTDC can prove difficult if it is not anticipated
correctly during the design phase (especially in the case where several DC voltage levels
are used).
The advantages and disadvantages of the DC side coupling are listed below [13]:
• Advantages
• Lower transmission losses when used as an interconnector.
Page 5 of 39
==PAGE:16==
Assessment of AC based topology for offshore energy islands
• Lower control and stability risk and complexity on the AC side, since the different
parts of the offshore AC grid are electrically separated in smaller grids, which results
in lower short-circuit currents and lower power losses in case of AC grid faults.
• If the already installed offshore converters have the sufficient power rating, the DC
grid can be expanded without building new offshore substations or converters.
• Disadvantages
• The technology is less mature and european system operators lack of experience with
MTDC systems.
• Risk of system operators becoming dependant of one vendor since multi-vendor inter-
operability is still uncertain.
• The expansion of the AC-side grid can prove difficult in the absence of a HVAC bus.
• A single DC voltage level must be used, which constrains the choices of the system
designers.
• Most importantly, the safe operation of a MTDC relies on HVDC circuit breaker
technologies that are still being developed.
The reduced power losses and ease of expansion of the MTDC topology make it a better
choice than the AC-coupled topology for the future meshed offshore grid, as identified by the
European H2020 projects "BestPaths" [14] and “PROMOTioN” [12] . However, uncertainties
over the degree of maturity of this technology has made system operators reluctant to imple-
ment it in the coming OEH projects : the two main issues being the lack of reliable HVDC
circuit breakers and of multi-vendor interoperability [15]. Multi-vendor interoperability is
defined by ENTSO-E as the ability of the different components of a transmission system to
function together, seamlessly allowing the transmission of electricity at the required power
quality and level of security of supply, even if they are provided by different manufacturers
or use different technologies. As a consequence, a hybrid solution where both type of cou-
pling can be used at different points in time appears as attractive: first the AC-side coupling
would be implemented and tested, and the MTDC topology would only be used after all its
required elements
2.2
Choice of HVDC link topology
Several topologies are possible for the HVDC links used to connect the OEH to the shore.
The simplest scheme is the symmetrical monopole topology, which is represented in Figure 3.
In this configuration, only one converter is used in the offshore and onshore substations: each
is connected to two HVDC cables of opposite polarity (the positive cable operates at +525
kV while the negative one operates at -525 kV). This solution requires the lowest CAPEX
and has already been used in the past for offshore HVDC links [16]. But its main drawback
Page 6 of 39
==PAGE:17==
Assessment of AC based topology for offshore energy islands
Figure 3: HVDC symmetrical monopole topology
is that this topology is more prone to complete outage than the other ones. Indeed, a fault
at one of the poles or one of the cables would result in the loss of the entire system. More-
over, no power can be transmitted during maintenance operations on the equipment. Such a
tendency to complete failure would seriously undermine the security of the network in which
the HVDC system is integrated, especially since the cable is intended to operate at a very
high voltage and transmit a lot of active power (2000 MW).
Figure 4: HVDC rigid bipole topology
Another possible topology is the rigid bipole scheme, shown in Figure 4.
It comprises two series connected converter per station, that also supply opposite polarity
HVDC cables. As a consequence, each converter has a rated power of only 1000 MW. The
main advantage of this scheme is its improved resilience: in the event of a pole failure,
the remaining operational converters can be used in an asymmetrical monopole topology so
that the transmission capacity is only reduced of 50 % as the HVDC cable connected to 2
functioning converters can still operate. However, it should be noted that long term ground
current operation is prohibited in the North Sea because of the negative environmental effects
it has on fish [17] and boats [18]. To avoid this, the cable connected to the faulty pole is
used as a metallic return, and so in the case of a fault happening on one HVDC cable, the
entire system must be disconnected.
Finally, a third possible topology is the bipole with Dedicated Metallic Return (DMR)
Page 7 of 39
==PAGE:18==

scheme. It is very similar to the rigid bipole scheme, but a metallic return cable is added
to connect the neutral terminals of the converters. The bipole with DMR topology is repre-
sented in Figure 5
Figure 5: HVDC bipole with dedicated metallic return topology
The two HVDC cables still operate at opposed polarities and thanks to the DMR cable, the
system can turn into a monopole topology in case of a fault in either one of the HVDC cable
or one of the converters. As a consequence, this topology offers the guarantee of having at
least a 1 GW transmission capacity available at all time. It should be noted that although
the DMR cable increases the CAPEX of the system, it is only subject to a very low voltage
given by Ohm’s law:
UDMR = RDMR · IDMR
(3)
As a consequence the DMR cable is rated to operate at the full DC current but only requires
little insulation which reduces its cost. Overall, the better reliability of the bipole with DMR
topology makes it the preferred choice for the OEH model.

Connecting the turbine to the grid through the back-to-back power converter enables the
speed of the machine to vary, so that the quantity of energy extracted from the wind is
optimised for the widest possible range of wind conditions. The turbine-side converter is
able to supply the generator with the reactive power it requires, while the grid-side converter
ensures the satisfaction of the grid operator requirements and can even provide voltage or
frequency support to the grid depending on the control strategies implemented.
Each windfarm will simply be modelled by connecting wind turbines in parallel to a 66 kV
bus. This voltage level corresponds to the current maximum voltage of wind turbines. It
is expected to increase in the future to reduce transmission losses in the offshore AC grid,
especially if windfarms are built far from the OEH [13]. The number of wind turbines in a
farm is chosen to achieve the desired power production. In this study, the dynamics of the
wind turbines are not considered, all the turbines in a given farm are assumed to exhibit an
identical behavior, and the speed of the wind is assumed to be constant during the short
duration of the simulations.

2.6

2.7
DC chopper
The DC cables must be protected from the severe overcurrents and overvoltages that can
happen during a fault in the system. The cables can also be damaged if the onshore grid
suddenly stops absorbing the power transmitted by the cables (this can be the consequence
of a fault in the onshore AC grid). If this happens and the controllers of the windfarms do
not readjust their power generation, then the cables will act as condensators being charged
and their voltage will increase. To avoid these overvoltages, the DC cables are fitted with
DC choppers: resistors that enable them to dissipate the power production of the windfarms.
The diagram of a DC chopper is shown in Figure 9.
Figure 9: Diagram of a DC chopper [4]
The resistor Rc is connected in parallel to the cable thanks to a power electronic switch T
and an anti-parallel diode D. The switch opens in reaction to a signal Tc if the DC voltage
reaches a threshold. The excess power accumulating in the cable is then discharged through
the resistor until the voltage reaches a lower value. Being important protection equipment,
the choppers must be carefully designed to be able to dissipate the rated power of the cable.
It also is to note that the DC choppers do not only protect the cables but also help the
offshore wind turbines to ride through onshore AC grid faults with minimal disturbance.

2.10
The VSC converters
The converters are the key components of the HVDC transmission system. Their role is to
link the AC and DC parts of the energy hub network, by acting as either a rectifier (when
the signals are converted from AC to DC) or an inverter (when the DC signal is converted
into an AC one).
In the last decades, a new type of converter has been developed thanks to the improvement
of power electronics: the Voltage Source Converter- HVDC technology (VSC-HVDC). The
previous generation of converters, the Line Commutated Converters (LCC-HVDC), uses
thyristor valves, that can be switched on by the converter’s control system but cannot be
actively turned off as they require the current to be zero to be turned off. On the contrary, the
VSC-HDVC is built with Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBT), that can be switched
on and off by the control system.
The VSC technology represents a great improvement as it corrects several of the issues that
prevented the use of the LCC in offshore HVDC networks. Commutation failure can often
happen in an LCC: during sudden voltage variations due to faults in the AC network [21],
a thyristor may not turn off when it should, which results in short-circuits on the DC link
that can potentially damage the equipment. This is because the thyristors’ commutation
operation is dependant of the AC voltage frequency. On the contrary, since the IGBTs are
very reliable solid-state components that do not need an AC voltage to commute (they can
even operate when the converter is linked to a passive network with no power source[22]),
the risk of commutation failure is much lower in a VSC. The full controllability of the IGBTs
also gives the VSC a faster control over the power flows, which enables its use in combination
with variable energy sources such as offshore windfarms.
LCC’s active and reactive power are not independent, with the reactive power consumption
of the device typically around 0.5 pu at rated active power. As a consequence, LCCs need
costly and space-consuming reactive power compensation devices. Moreover, the LCC can
only be used if the AC grid is strong enough (SCR is superior to 2) as its reactive consumption
will cause voltage instabilities if the AC grid is too weak [23]. This requirement is not met
when there are no synchronous generators in the grid like in the offshore energy islands.
By contrast, a VSC can regulate both its active and reactive power flows independently,
and so not only does not require reactive power compensation but can also be inserted in
weak grids and offer blackstart support to the AC grid [24].
Another issue of the LCC
is the creation of harmonics by the thyristors, that must be filtered. The VSC produces
less harmonics and so smaller filters can be used. All these improvements make the VSC
particularly adapted to a use on offshore energy hubs.

3
Implementation of control schemes
The model of the OEH containing all the elements described in the previous chapter is built
by using some templates on the simulation software PowerFactory. The present chapter will
describe the control schemes associated with the templates.
3.1
Grid-forming and Grid-Following converters
Over the years, many different control structures for VSCs have been proposed, so as to
correct performance issues observed in simulation studies or installed equipment in the power
system. A particularly challenging problem was the operation of VSCs connected to very
weak AC grids, where the control strategies that focus on controlling the injected currents
thanks to Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) devices can be a source of failures. Depending on their
control structure, the converters can be classified in two broad categories: the Grid-Forming
(GFM) converters and the Grid-following (GFL) converters. The concept of GFM converter
was introduced to improve grid stability issues observed with the GFL converters when
the converters are connected to weak systems with high penetration of power electronics-
based generation. Overall, the GFL converters can be considered as behaving like controlled
current sources with a high parallel impedance and the GFM converters can be represented as
a voltage source with a low series impedance. As a consequence, it is thanks to the injection
of controlled currents that GFL converters provide the wanted active and reactive power
flows, whereas the GFM converters directly control the voltage at their output terminal.
The two types of converters are represented in Figure 14, where the grid to which they are
connected to is modelled by a voltage source Vg with a series impedance Zg.
Figure 14: (a) Simplified representations of a GFL converter, (b) simplified representation
of a GFM converter [8]
The result is that GFL and GFM exhibit very different responses to grid events. For example
in the case of a perturbation of the grid voltage, the current phasor of a GFL remains
constant so its voltage phasor instantaneously varies. To the contrary, in response to the
same perturbation, the voltage phasor of the GFM converter remains constant so its current
phasor instantaneously varies. This can lead to overcurrents that can damage the equipment,
if no current control mechanism is implemented.
Page 18 of 39
==PAGE:29==

Figure 15: (a) Phasor diagram of a GFL converter according to a perturbation of the grid
voltage; (b) phasor diagram of a GFM converter according to a perturbation of the grid

The second main difference between the GFM and GFL converters is their synchronization
process. Indeed, to be able to supply the grid with the targeted amounts of active and reactive
power, the GFL converter injects its currents with a precisely computed phase shift compared
to the grid voltage angle. To do this, the grid voltage angle at the point of connection of the
converter is continuously measured thanks to a PLL and fed as an input to the converter’s
controller. The consequence is that the good performance of the GFL converter relies heavily
on the AC grid voltage parameters, which must be estimated as precisely as possible. The
poor quality of the AC grid voltage becomes particularly problematic for GFL converters’
operation in weak grids with a low SCR [26].
On the contrary, the GFM converters are self-synchronizing with the grid by emulating the
working principle of a synchronous machine. This enables them to control the grid voltage
and frequency, and to operate in weak grids.
3.2
Choice of control scheme for converters: GFL or GFM
The onshore converters are connected to a strong system, where the synchronous machines
supplying the onshore transmission grid are establishing the voltage. As a consequence,
the onshore converters can all operate as grid-following converters. The offshore AC grid,
however, is entirely supplied by converter-interfaced wind turbines. This means that one of
the converters must be a GFM converter to control the voltage of the grid so that the other
offshore converters can use this voltage signal as input for their PLL and operate as GFL
converters.

3.3
Coordination of the two ends of a cable
In conventional AC systems, the grid frequency is a global indicator of the energy balance in
the network: in case of an excess of generation, the frequency increases as electrical energy
is stored in the rotating parts of the machines in the system in the form of kinetic energy.
Conversely, if the consumption is greater than generation, this kinetic energy is fed back to
the grid as the frequency decreases.
In DC cables, the DC voltage has a similar role: in case of an excess of generation, the
DC voltage increases as electrical energy is stored in the capacitors and cable capacitance
in the form of electrostatic energy. And if the cable is not supplied with enough power,
this electrostatic energy will be converted back into electrical energy so the DC voltage will
decrease [27].
For the safe operation of HVDC cables, the DC voltage must be kept close to its rated value
in order not to damage the equipment. It is thus important that the two converters located
at the extremities of each DC cable are coordinated with each other to maintain the balance
of the energy flows going in and out of the cable. To achieve this goal, a Master/Slave
method is applied: one converter (the slave) will set the active power setpoint while the
other (the master, also called the slack because its role is similar to the one of a slack bus in
an AC system) is tasked with controlling the DC voltage of the cable by adjusting its power
flow to compensate for the losses occurring in the cable.
In the project, for each cable, the master converter is the one located in the onshore sub-
station, which controls the DC voltage and the reactive power flow supplied to the onshore
AC grid. The slave converter is the one located in the offshore substation, which controls
the active power flow supplied to the DC cable and the reactive power flow supplied to the
offshore AC grid.

4.4
Adding synthetic inertia
In order to improve the system’s response in the very first moments after the perturbation,
some inertia must be added to it. As the OEH is only connected with converter-interfaced
elements, it forms a zero-inertia system, which is why the grid events can create so sudden
perturbations. It is possible to control the FLC converters so that they provide synthetic
inertia by emulating the behavior of a synchronous generator (which provides inertia due
to its very nature thanks to its rotating parts). When the dynamics of the frequency of a
power system containing synchronous machines are dominated by the system inertia (that is
in the very first moments, after the balanced initial situation), the following swing equation
applies:

the GFL will emulate the working of a synchronous machine and provide synthetic inertia
to the system.
Such a control scheme is implemented on the offshore GFL converters for H = 4s. The
simulation results for the onshore grids frequencies and their voltage magnitudes are shown
in Figure 30: the implementation of the virtual inertia has absolutely no effect on the onshore
synchronous machines. This can be explained by looking at Figure 31 and Figure 32, which
show the DC voltage levels and active power flows of the cables 1 and 3. The synthetic
inertia works as expected, as the converter B3 provides active power to the GFM converter
B1 because it reacts at the decrease of frequency induced by the decrease of active power
flow in B1. But all the energy provided by B3 to B1 and then sent by the converter B1 to
the onshore grid through the cable 1 is energy that is not sent to a synchronous machines
directly through the cable 3. So the synthetic inertia can have a local effect in the offshore
grid but will have little if no influence on the onshore grids.

5
Conclusion
In this project, the model of a 4 GW AC-coupled offshore Energy Island has been studied
and implemented using the simulation software PowerFactory. The different elements needed
in an offshore energy hub were detailed, and some of the design choices that impact the most
the topology of the hub were presented. The different types of controllers implemented on
the converters used to operate the HVDC cables of the OEH were studied, as well as the
different types of coordination (Slave/Master or Grid-forming/Grid-following) required to
operate them in a stable way. Then a particular focus was put on the active power control
in case of generation imbalance.
