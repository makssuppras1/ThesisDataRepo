Abstract
Stochastic programming models are used to deal with unknown data and uncertainty in
decision­making problems. Among the different applications, the facility location prob­
lem is a well known and largely discussed topic in literature. It has several uncertain
parameters that need to be modelled as random variables, and they are represented by
scenarios. A scenario can be described as a single realization of stochastic process and
they need to cover the most plausible number of processes. While a large number of
scenarios lead to a precise description of the uncertainty, their computational times are
significant. For this reason the so­called scenario reduction techniques aim to reduce
the number of scenarios while preserving a sub­optimal probability distribution. Despite
different existing scenario reduction methodologies, in this work the so­called K­means
and the Fast Farward selection will be analyzed. The application of the two techniques
will be adapted to a stochastic facility location problem already formulated and modelled
in literature. The specific facility location problem will be solved by using the software
Julia and it will be optimized by the optimizer Gurobi highlighting relevant analogies and
differences from the solutions found in the literature. Moreover, this work wants to be a
contribution and demonstration that it is possible to handle the uncertainties by clustering
and selecting the most representative scenarios while preserving the most essential in­
formation. An exhaustive discussion of the results through principal component analysis
(PCA) and within cluster sum of squares (WCSS) is performed highlighting limitations and
possible future developments.

Introduction
“Facility location is a branch of operations research related to locating or positioning at
least a new facility among several existing facilities in order to optimize (minimize or max­
imize) at least one objective function (like cost, profit, revenue, travel distance, service,
waiting time, coverage and market shares).” Based on the decision to locate a facility,
according to a set of variables and an objective function, the facility location problem is
part of the science of management science, as a decision making analysis. [1]
Due to the increase of natural extreme disaster, such as tornadoes, earthquakes or hurri­
canes, the facility location problem plays a great role in this field. Notably hurricanes such
as Katrina and Rita, several tsunami in India but also the terrorist attack in New York in
2011 are few examples. [2] Moreover, according to the United Nation Office for Disaster
Risk Reduction, there has been a rise in climate disaster between the last 20 years. Com­
paring the period 1980­1999 and 2000­2019 the number has been increased from 3656
to 6681. In particular the biggest impact has involved the floods, increased from around
1457 to around 2034. [3] The main goal of emergency response efforts is to provide as­
sistance and commission to the victims in a short time period. To achieve this goal a
preparedness planning is essential and facilities and supplies need to be pre­position. [4]
In the phase of pre­positioning of the facilities there are many unknown parameters, such
as the availability of supplies facilities or the edges between facilities. To incorporate the
uncertain characteristics that are associate to the disasters, the stochastic programming
provides an accurate representation. Generally speaking in certainty situations all the
parameters are deterministic and known, whereas risk and uncertain situations involve
randomness. [5]
The main contribution of this Thesis work has been from the paper ”A metaheuristic for
the stochastic facility location problem”. The model formulated by Turkes, Sorensen and
Cuervo (2020) [6] is used as based model, to handle with uncertainty and determine a
pre­positioning phase. It is a two­stages stochastic linear programming model, with the
purpose of maximize the met demand, setting budget for transportation and capacity.
In the first stage, the decisions before the occurrence of the events are predicted and
organized, such as the allocation of facilities. In the second stage, after that full information
is received from some random vector, corrective actions are taken. [2] The instances that
are taken into consideration in this work, whose take their values from [6], are real case
instances. However, to validate the model and ensure a further analysis of the problem,
some random scenarios have been considered.
With the term scenario is considered the single realization of a stochastic process. More
precisely, a random variable whose value changes over time is known as stochastic pro­
cess. The pool price over one week can be considered as an example. Therefore, while
the price at noon is a random variable, the collection of all the hourly prices of the day
creates the stochastic process. Scenarios can be used to represent stochastic processes
over a particular time horizon. [7] Keeping as an example the one mentioned before, to
represent all the possible combinations of pooling over a week, thousands of scenarios
need to be generated. For this reason, the stochastic programming problem associated
to the scenarios, can be computationally intractable due the large number of combina­
tions that the program will run to cover all the scenarios. The procedure to reduce the
generated scenario is named ”scenario reduction” and it is largely discussed in this work.

The techniques presented and developed in this study to perform the scenario reduction
are two: the Fast Forward selection and the K­means. The purpose of both techniques
is to reduce the number of scenarios, preserving the largest amount of information while
decreasing significantly the computational time. The main difference between the two
is that the former selects existing scenarios as the most representatives one, whereas
the latter generates new scenarios based on the centroids of the clusters. Moreover, the
algorithm of the Fast Forward is based on the Kantorovich distance that measures the pair­
wise distance, in terms of probability, between scenarios. The K­means, having as input
the number of representative scenarios needed, creates the desired number of clusters
based on similar features of the full set of scenarios which generates the centroids. This
centroids are the mean of all the data objects in the cluster, for each parameter affected
by uncertainty. Since the objective of the scenario reduction technique is to minimize the
computational time, in the following analysis only the instances with high computational
time have been considered.
The paper is divided as follows. An extensive literature review is presented in chapter 2,
where the facility location problem is described, as well as its applications, formulation and
methodology. chapter 3 is divided into three main sections. In the first part, a comparison
between stochastic programming and robust optimization is provided. It is then described
the mathematical model under consideration. The chapter ends with the mathematical
formulation and the theory of the two scenario reduction techniques introduced above.
The methodology used to approach the problem is largely discussed in chapter 4. In
particular, the validation of the model from [6] is discussed through example and data
analysis. Moreover, the two scenario reduction techniques and the methodology used in
this study are illustrated. In chapter 5 the analysis of the results is presented, discussing
the performance of the scenario reduction techniques. The limitations of the study and
future developments are summarized in chapter 6.

1. Allocation problem to decide the transportation between demand and service facili­

2. Facility problem to choose the location of facilities.
Several formulation of the problem exist in literature to locate the facilities, such as the
set covering, maximal covering, p­center, p­dispersion, p­median, fixed charge, hub, and
maximum. [8] The p­median problem is one the most discussed and that gained more
attention in the literature. Among an infinite set of location, the discrete facility location
problem chose a p finite set of location, with the aim of reducing costs and distances. [11]
In any case, placing a certain facility carries fixed costs and a variable cost for transporta­
tion.
In their paper Melo et al. [11] perform an accurate review of all the facility location prob­
lems to be applied in the resolution of supply chain networks. Due to the large num­
ber of variables that can be changed within the supply chain network, during time, the
most appropriate model to simulate these scenarios is the stochastic. The authors report
four variables that need to take into considerations in these types of problems and they
are: multi­layer facilities, multiple commodities, single/multiple period(s), deterministic/s­
tochastic parameters. Moving to the decision variables of the problem, the authors do not
consider just the classical allocation decisions , but they found relevant to mention also
the planning decisions.
Stochastic programming models are used to evaluate the problems under uncertainty and
characterized by the lack of perfect information. Despite the lack of certainty in solving
these kind of problems, decisions need to be made. If the input data of a decision­making
problem are “well defined and deterministic”, the solution is calculated by solving the prob­
lem and it is then implemented to obtain the optimal outcome. In case of uncertainty, the
decision­making problem can be formulated in two different ways. First, the input data,
describable by probability functions, could be replaced by the expected values and the
problem can be then solved as a deterministic optimization problem. However, the risk is
that the results are not the optimal ones. On the other hand, to each set of input data is
associated a probability of existences. A single solution, feasible for all the sets of input
data, is calculated solving the stochastic optimization problem. Therefore, also the objec­
tive function is not a real­valued function but it behaves as a random variable. [7]
The formulation of a facility location problem needs to take into consideration the uncer­
tainty of these problems. Uncertainty is a constant in several parameters of these prob­
lems, such as demand levels, travel time, cost for supplying the customers and price for
commodities [5]. To create real solution, the formulation needs to anticipate and predict
the uncertainty. These types of problems are classified as risk and under uncertainty,
whereas the problems within all the parameters are deterministic and known are defined
as certain. In risk situation, according to Rosenhead that provided this distinguish in 1972,
each parameter’s value is linked to a certain probability, and they are known as stochas­
tic optimization problem. On the other hand, problems under uncertainty are known as
robust optimization problems and, like their parameters, also their probabilities can be de­
termined. [15] In both their modelling the purpose is to find a solution that will perform well
and an appropriate performance measure. Then, the random parameters can be either
continuous or described by discrete scenarios. The scenario approach allows to have
parameters statistically dependent. The dependence is often used to model real cases,
such as the demand that is often correlated to the geography and cost to suppliers.
Snyders in his paper performed a review of stochastic models for facility location. Accord­
ing to his work, the most common formulation is named “minisum location problems” that
aims to optimize the mean outcome of the system, for example minimize the expected

as “minmax” and “minsum”. In the former, the objective is to optimize worst­case perfor­

The scenario approach used in this method has been formulated in different ways. In the

Problem description
The chapter introduces the two main techniques to handle the uncertainty with particular
attention to the stochastic facility location problem. The formulation of a specific stochas­
tic model to solve a facility location problem in case of natural disasters will be presented.
The model is formulated by Turques, Sorensen and Tuerco in [6] and in this chapter the
assumptions taken by the authors, the formulations and further considerations are pre­
sented. As previously mentioned the uncertainty is modelled through the set of scenarios
s. It often happens that the big amount of scenarios lead to a huge computation time and
sometimes, to sub optimal results. Therefore, in the last part of the section, two tech­
niques to handle with uncertainty and reduce the number of scenarios will be described.
3.1
How to handle uncertainty: robust optimization and
stochastic programming
The facility location problems is characterized by many parameters, such as demand
levels, travel time or cost for supplying the customers, location of the customers, presence
or absence of the customers, and price for the commodities. In facility location problems
it often happens to deal with uncertainty as one or several of these parameters can be
uncertain. Based on how these parameters are built, if they are discrete or continuous,
the models and the methodology can vary. [5]
The two main problem formulations to model the uncertainty are the following: [5]:
• Stochastic programming model: if probabilistic information is available, the uncer­
tain parameters can be represented by random variables and the decision is defined
as under risk.
• Robust optimization: if probabilistic information is not available, the decision is de­
fined as under uncertainty.
In both the cases, the realization of all the uncertain parameters is called scenario. The
type of model to be considered can vary based on the following aspects [5]:
• Risk neutral decision: The decision maker does not consider risk when making a
decision, and a linear function is a valid representation of the decision maker’s utility.
A probability is associated to each scenario and the objective is to minimize the
expected cost.
• Risk averse decision: The decision maker is represented by a concave utility func­
tion. The decision maker wants to avoid unnecessary risks and the objective is to
maximize costs toward all the scenarios.
• Ex ante decision: the decisions must be implemented before the uncertainty is clar­
ified.
• Ex post decision: the decision must be implemented after the uncertainty is clarified.
In the rest of the section, the two type of problems mentioned above will be presented
and explained. In particular, the Stochastic Facility Location Problem will be analyzed in
detailed since it is the methodology used in this work. The extensive analysis is taken
from [5] and it refers to other work in literature.

3.3
Scenario reduction
The uncertainty model utilized in the scenario­based stochastic consists of a set of scenar­
ios. Each scenario is a probability­based representation of a possible realization of uncer­
tainty. For each scenario set, the scenario­based stochastic generates a single schedule.
The output of generators, on the other hand, would fluctuate depending on the scenario.
Because severe possibilities may have low probabilities, the scenario­based stochastic
schedule sacrifices optimally for these scenarios in order to reduce the operating cost
for the more likely (and less extreme) scenarios. However, the computational burden
increases rapidly with the number of scenarios considered [21]. The scenario­reduction
technique consists in shrewdly reducing the number of scenarios. While a high number
of scenarios is usually required to make accurate the approximation of the stochastic pro­
cess, reducing these scenarios need to reduce and improve the computation burden and
tractability [7]. In other words, It allows to manage the amount of time required and the
uncertainty in term of scenarios considered, variables and occurrence [21].
In the rest of the sections two heuristic scenario reduction techniques, the Fast Forward
selection and the K­means, are described.

3.3.2
K­means
When dealing with uncertain decision problems, one commonly uses a simulation ap­
proach to describe the unpredictable outcomes. This would result in a vast number of
separate data pathways, forming the so­called fan­favorite scenario tree. Simulated path­
ways and the related tree are expected to appropriately reflect uncertainties. However,
these simulated pathways are accompanied by a large­scale optimization challenge that
is difficult to address. Clustering analysis, such as the k­means approach, can be used in
this situation to aggregate the simulated paths into a smaller scenario tree. [24] By mini­
mizing the sum of squared distances between each point and its nearest cluster mean, the
k­means (KM) algorithm groups N data points into k clusters (centroid). Sum­of­squared
errors is the name of this objective function (SSE). Although k­means was created to
reduce SSE in numerical data, it has also been used for various objective functions (in­
cluding ones that aren’t numerical).[25]
Even though many other algorithms can give better results in term of clustering, the k­
means is popular due to its facility in the implementation. Moreover, it is more scalable
than other in term of limitations that are know and not unknown or hidden as in many other
techniques. Third, the local fine­tuning capability of k­means is very effective and it can
be used as part of better algorithms such the genetic one, the random swap, the particle
swarm optimization and density clustering. [25]
K­means begins by selecting k random data points to serve as the initial set of centroids,
which is subsequently enhanced in two steps. Every point is assigned to the cluster of the
nearest centroid in the assignment stage. The centroid of each cluster is recalculated in
the update stage as the mean of all data points assigned to the cluster. These two steps
make up a single iteration of k­means. Both the cluster bounds and the centroid locations
are fine­tuned in these steps. The algorithm is iterated a predetermined number of times
or until convergence is achieved (no further improvement is obtained). [25] Below the
k­means logical algorithm is described. [22]

4.3
Scenario reduction
The focus of this study is mainly based on how to handle the uncertainties of the facility
location problem by using scenario reduction techniques. The objective of using these
algorithms is mostly related to performance purposes such as reaching good quality solu­
tion in less computational time. Not only that but also finding a sub­set of representative
scenarios which can still give an acceptable probability representation of the full instance
is one of the main goals of this study. What this means is that even if the stochastic
problem is considering many possible events, this set of scenario can be shrink down
keeping a good representation quality of all the possible scenarios. On the other hand, a
sub­optimal solution is more likely to be given by the model since the representative sce­
narios do not include all the uncertainties. The following parameters are the ones affected
by the uncertainty, in other words are dependent from the scenario variable:
• Ls
ij: determines the distance from each vertex i ∈V to every vertex j ∈V in each
scenario s ∈S;
• Rks
i : determines the percentage of commodity k ∈K stored in i ∈V’ that remains
usable in scenario s ∈S;
• Dks
i : determines the demands in every vertex i ∈V for the commodity k ∈K in each
scenario s ∈S;
The size of these three parameters increases exponentially as the number of vertex
grows. However, we can not reduced the number of vertex as it is a deterministic value
given by the instance. On the other hand, we can benefit from the scenario reduction
as can be seen in Figure 4.4. It is noteworthy to mention that as the number of vertex
increases the instance complexity increases as well, whereas if the number of scenarios
decreases the performance can be improved in regards to the computational time. In
addition, many instances in Table 4.1 have high computational run times as the solver
takes long time to find the optimal solution given either the high number of scenarios or
how tight the constraints are. However, in case the constraints are quite rigid, such as
low budgets or low capacity of the facilities, the scenario reductions techniques can really
make the difference as the solution would not be trivial to find.

beneficial for reducing the complexity and uncertainty of the instances.
The scenario reduction techniques rely on some assumptions which have been made by
considering the nature of the facility location problem. The following are considered:
• The demand is taken as the clustering or selecting parameter for the scenario re­
duction in the k­means and Fast Forward selection;
• The minimum reduction factor is 2 which means that the largest sub­set of scenario
is S
2 , where S is the total number of scenarios;
• The probability distribution for the new subset is the sum of the scenarios’ prob­
abilities represented by the selected scenario (Fast Forward Selection) or by the
centroid of the cluster (k­means);
The reason why the demand is taken as clustering or selecting parameter is because it
impacts directly the objective function and, in turn, it can be a relevant factor to select
the most representative scenarios. Moreover, this study has the ambition to halve the
necessary scenarios from the original set in order to have concrete results in regards to
computational run time while maintaining a fairly good objective value.

2. Assigning each existing scenario to the closest centroid.
3. Determining the new centroid for each cluster by calculating the mean of all the
scenarios assigned to the considered cluster.
4. The steps number 2 and 3 are reiterate until the centroids are stabilized and they
do not change anymore.

6
Conclusion, Limitations and future
research
The main purpose of this work has been to experiment existing techniques to handle the
uncertainties when solving facility location problem. The importance of this topic is well­
known in operation research and it becomes even more important if the problem is related
to natural disasters. In case of natural disaster, many parameters need to be taken into
considerations and, due to the nature of these events, they are often uncertain. It has
been showed that using probabilistic information, these uncertain parameters can be rep­
resented by random variables and the situations can be simulated by the formulation of
stochastic programming model. Based on real natural events and using the formulation
presented in [6], a stochastic model has been described and the solver Julia has been
used to test the model and validate the one already created.
While running these kind of problems, the uncertainty can be predicted by using large
amount of scenarios. For each scenario, a new set of values is assigned to the param­
eter and it covers the largest realization of stochastic processes as possible. However,
once left running the problems for long times, sometimes for more than hours, the optimal
solution cannot been reached yet. In other cases, the optimal solution was obtained but
the computational time of these runs was extremely high. For this reason, the so­called
scenario reduction technique has been applied with the intention of reducing the time
while keeping great performances in terms of sub­optimal solution.
Overall, the Fast Forward selection provides better results in term of objective function,
while the k­means technique has been defined the best technique in regards of run time.
However, the volatility of the obtained results, has not allowed to achieve a unique and
clear analysis about the trend of these techniques. In particular, the limitation of this study
are listed below.
• There are only few real instances where the scenario reduction algorithms can have
an impact. Despite the large number of real instances provided by [27], just the once
with huge computational time have been taken into consideration in this study. On
the other hand, random instances have been generated. However, the nature of
these instances, does not allow to perform a robust analysis.
• Clustering or selecting by considering only the demand it is considered a limitation
because the scenario could have similar demands but other different parameters.
For example, as can be seen in section 5.13, also the remaining usable parameter
can play a key role in clustering.
• The scenario reduction techniques have only been applied to one facility location
problem along and, in turn, to its assumptions.
• Most of the instances have loose constraints, which is why the run times are mainly
low. Having high budgets for opening facilities, acquiring commodities and trans­
porting the commodities may not reflect the reality.
These limitations have a great possibility for future development as it opens new roads
for improvement both for the considered facility location problem and for new ways to

handle the uncertainty. For example, by creating new real instances the reducing scenario
techniques can be performed in order to rate their efficiency when solving the facility
location problem. Moreover, it could be interesting to investigate new ways of clustering.
For example, the k­medodid has not been taken into consideration in the analyse since
the idea was to diversify the techniques as much as possible. However, the k­medodid
selects the scenarios which are closer to the centroids and it uses a similar logic than the
fast forward selection. Therefore, this method could be applied in future to perform the
scenario reduction technique in solving a facility location problem.
