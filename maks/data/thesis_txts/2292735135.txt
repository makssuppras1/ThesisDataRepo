ABSTRACT 
Permanent magnets are used in a wide range of machines throughout the industry. By 
using permanent magnets in generators the need for excitation of the rotor is completely 
removed. Which makes these types of the generators interesting when the weight of the 
generator is a concern as it is wind turbines. 
 
In this project the possibility of reusing the entire magnet block is investigated. The idea 
is to create a larger pole of smaller magnets and glue it together. By doing this most 
shapes can be approximated by a combination of different number of elements. 
 
This project investigates the effects on the magnetic performance of having a segmented 
pole instead of a solid pole. This will be through experimental test and finite element 
modelling. 
 
A method of removing the small magnets from each other after they have been glued 
together is analyzed and the results will be presented in this report.

INTRODUCTION 
Permanent magnets are used in a wide range of machines throughout the industry and 
hereunder also in the wind turbine industry. Permanent magnets are used in the wind 
turbine generators mainly because of its high ratio of high torque per volume ratio. By 
using permanent magnets the need for excitation of the rotor is removed and by increas-
ing the number of poles in a permanent magnet machine will increase the frequency of 
the machine and reduce the total size of the machine. From a high power production 
view all magnets are made from a compound made of rare-earth materials because of it 
has the highest torque per volume ratio. The focus in this project are direct drives gener-
ators because it is more common to see permanent magnets generators. 
 
For this project the work carried out is only done with NdFeB magnets since these have 
the highest torque ratio and this leads to a lower total mass if compared to the SmCo 
magnets and for designing a wind turbine the total mass at hub height is critical and is 
kept low. SmCo magnets is also mainly used in application where the temperature is 
relatively high in the surroundings of the magnet. 
 
The increasing demand on rare earth magnets leads to a rise in the price of NdFeB mag-
nets which have not only an economical but also an environmental impact when produc-
ing wind turbines. This sparks a focus to be put into finding ways of an environmental 
correct process that will reuse the magnets coming from wind turbines and other larger 
permanent magnets machines from the industry. The technology right now allows new 
NdFeB magnets to be created from old magnets grounded into powder and mixed with 
the powder for creation of new magnets. 
 
This project will investigate the possibility of reusing the permanent magnet as a whole 
without having to ground it up into powder and to be reused into the cycle of creating a 
new permanent magnet. The method used in this project is making a large magnet pole 
from smaller magnet elements and glue these together. By this way it would be possible 
to create many different shapes and sizes for the varying designs of wind turbine gen-
erators.

Introduction 
16 
In order to analyze a method of disassemble these segmented magnets it was tested to 
see if would be possible to heat the magnets up to its Curie temperature to dissolve the 
glue and make the permanent magnets magnetic neutral. There is focused on how much 
the protective coating will be damaged by being brought to Curie temperature. And by 
making the magnets temporarily magnetic neutral would give the possibility of stacking 
and gluing the magnets more easily. 
 
1.1 
Problem statement 
This project will investigate the possibility of reusing the permanent magnet as a whole 
intact block. In order to analyses the possibility of reusing the whole magnet three ele-
ments are looked into these are listed below:  
 Design of a practical setup 
- Experimental testing of the magnetic performance 
 Construct a finite element model for simulation 
- The model would be verified by the experimental setup 
 Investigating the effects on magnets coating from a heating process of 340° C 
 
1.2 
Method and limitations 
The work of this report is limited to the design, finite element modelling, testing on five 
coatings, and the production of a testing rig to test permanent magnets performance in a 
pure magnetic circuit. 
 
In addition it is assumed that it is possible to create a permanent magnet pole in a wind 
turbine as a number of stacked magnet elements. 
 
1.3 
Reading guide 
This report is divided into three main parts. The first part will cover the design of the 
magnets and testing rig used to analyze the method of reusing the magnets. Together 
with the production of the found testing rig. 
 
The second part will investigate the effects of reaching the Curie temperature on five 
types of coating. The effects will be evaluated by three test; coating tensile strength, 
microscopy of the coatings and BH-measurements. 
 
The third part is creating an accurate finite element models to emulate the experimental 
setup. These finite element models are verified by comparing the results from the exper-
imental part.

DESIGN PHASE 
During the design phase there were two designs that were investigated and one became 
more preferable because it had a lot of advantages over the other one. It was a priority 
that the practical setup was simple and easy to use without a lot of preparation before 
testing any type of magnets. The aim was to design a test setup that could be used to test 
more than one set of magnets and allow for the magnets to be of a different height and 
length. This would also allow for the glue thickness to be of any size for the segmented 
pole. 
 
2.1 
Rotational design 
The first design was having it as a rotational design which is close to a real machine and 
still only make a single pole pair in practical and in the simulations because of the 
symmetry in a generator. And in order to avoid having to wind teeth on the stator side 
and creating another uncertainty factor the field is generated by another set of perma-
nent magnets which will be kept as solid poles. This makes it significantly easier to con-
struct the practical setup since it wouldn’t have any electrical part by the design itself 
and thus the whole test becomes a pure mechanical setup also called a magnetic cou-
pling. In Figure 2-1 the design is seen as how it would be drawn the FE tool MagNet. 
 
 
Figure 2-1: Sketch showing the intial idear of a rotational design

As seen in Figure 2-1 it would necessary to cut into the core backs to fit a rectangle 
magnet onto it. This relatively simple feature in the model is challenging to create in a 
workshop because of how precise the tool bench has to be set when working on it so 
that the core back was as close as possible to the magnets. 
 
Another natural limitation by having this design is that it wouldn’t be possible to vary 
the length of the magnets. Also the rig that would have to be used for this setup has to 
suspend both core backs in the air and hold it extremely stiff so the forces only acts in 
one direction where the moment is measured. 
 
Taking it all into consideration will a design that rotates make it more expensive to con-
struct and less flexible with what magnets there can be tested on the testing rig. There-
fore another design analyzed which is described in the following subchapter. 
 
2.2 
Flattened design 
The limitation and cost of the rotational design gave way to a design that was much 
more simple which was a flat design that would remove the need for having any rotating 
parts for the practical setup. The argument was that proving the effects of having a stack 
of smaller magnets instead of one solid block doesn’t need to be in a practical design 
that necessarily rotates since the moment is easily giving by the force multiplied by the 
length of the air gap radius as seen in 2.1. 
 
 
( 2.1) 
 
This would reduce the time needed to create the produced parts for the practical setup 
and it will also simplify the control system and measuring system of the practical setup. 
This also gives the opportunity of having the whole system placed on a flat surface with 
simply using aluminum to separate the two core backs instead of suspending core backs 
in the air. In Figure 2-2 a sketch of the new design can be seen from the side without 
the aluminum blocks to separate the core backs.

THEORY 
In this chapter the general machine equation will be shown. For this project it is noted 
that there will not be a core back which is made by the means of winding the teeth of 
the core back. 
 
3.1 
Magnetic bodies 
There are several types of magnetic bodies which is listed below: 
 Diamagnetic bodies 
 Paramagnetic bodies 
 Antiferromagnetic bodies 
 Strong magnetic bodies 
- Ferromagnetic bodies 
- Ferrimagnetic bodies 
 
For this project only the strong magnetic bodies is used. The metal and alloy used in this 
thesis are ferromagnetic which iron and Neodymium magnets are and therefore it is 
only ferromagnetic bodies that are further investigated.  
 
Even though both of them are in the category of ferromagnetic bodies they react quite 
differently to external fields. Iron will hold the magnetic direction as long as an external 
field is in the same direction as soon as the field either disappears or shift direction the 
field inside the iron will also either disappear or shift direction to match the external 
field. The Neodymium magnets when magnetic neutral also get the same magnetic di-
rection as the field when exposed to an external field but when the field disappears it 
will hold on the magnetic properties. Together with the fact that Neodymium magnets 
are the strongest magnet that can be made makes them quite valuable for designing 
permanent magnet electrical machines.

EXPERIMENTAL TEST 
In this chapter three experimental tests are done, these are listed below: 
 
1. Testing of the measuring equipment U9B 
2. Testing of a reference poles (solid block of Neodymium material) 
3. Testing of a glued stack of small magnets with same total amount of magnet ma-
terial as the reference block 
 
During these tests the instrument used to measure force is a force transducer created 
with a full bridge. The schematic of the full bridge force transducer system can be seen 
in Figure 5-1. The first test is done mainly to ensure that the instrument works as it is 
supposed to. This is done simply by applying a known force and measure the output of 
the instrument.  
 
 
 
Figure 5-1: Schematic of the force transducer used to the force measurements doing the 
experimental tests 
 
The way the force transducer works is by supplying a voltage of 5 V DC at E and meas-
ure the output at ∆e. The configuration created such that a zero force acting on the 
transducer would yield an output of zero volt. It shows that this type of transducer can 
measure up to 500 N with an output that ranges from 0 mV to ±5 mV. The voltage re-
late to force by a factor of 100 N/mV with the sign of the voltage indicating the direc-
tion of the force. 
 
The second test is to create a reference where the poles used in the mechanical setup is 
of a solid block of Neodymium material with dimension of 14 mm x 28 mm x 20 mm. 
The test will be performed by measuring the output voltage at discreet location while 
the magnets on the lower core back is moved under the upper core back. This will simu-

COATING ANALYSES 
In this chapter the effects on the coating and Neodymium material with heating the 
magnets up to its curie temperature will be investigated. The following sections is di-
vided into four parts where the first will go through the heating process of the magnets 
and the last sections will be on three test. These three test that the magnets will undergo 
is similar to what would be seen when doing a quality check. 
 
The reason to do these test is to identify any possible problems with using an oven to 
demagnetize the magnets and remove the glue from the segmented magnet poles. Heat-
ing the magnet to its Curie temperature is one way of demagnetizing the magnets. There 
are other ways of demagnetizing magnets but they will not be investigated in this report. 
 
A total of 90 circular magnets with the dimension of Ø25 mm and 7 mm high were used 
to do the test explained in this chapter. For each test five coatings were tested these 
were; Nickel-Copper-Nickel, Copper, Zink, Tin and Epoxy. The reason to test several 
coatings is to see if they would react differently to being brought up to the magnet Curie 
temperature. Because the coatings are what is protecting the Neodymium magnets 
which is a brittle material and the environment can damage and reduce the flux density 
in the magnets. In Table 6-7 the number of magnets used for each test can be seen.

6.1 
Heating process 
The grade of magnet used is N40H where as previously stated the grade informs about 
the magnet strength, the maximum operating temperature and the Curie temperature. 
The maximum operating temperature of a N40H grade Neodymium magnet is 120° C 
and the Curie temperature is 340° C. These two temperatures sets the boundaries of the 
magnets performance under certain temperatures, an illustration of this can be seen in 
Figure 6-1. 
 
 
Figure 6-1: This figure illustrates what happens to the Neodymium at certain tempera-
tures 
 
==PAGE:49==
 
Coating analyses 
47 
In order to bring the magnets up to its Curie temperature an oven that could heat up to 
340° C 1 is used. Before heating the magnets used for testing, a pretest were done in 
order to find the needed time for the magnets to stay inside the oven to get a tempera-
ture of 340° C. The test consisted of taking two magnetized magnets which were put 
inside the oven and checked every 5 minutes to see if the magnetization remained at 
each time step. From the test it were found that a time of 35 minutes inside the oven was 
sufficient for the magnets to reach their Curie temperature. Therefore all magnets that 
were heat treated got 35 minutes inside the oven in order to bring them to the Curie 
temperature.

system down to a simple one. This is then further reduced with the program into small 
numerical elements. Before the drawing of the specific problem can begin an under-
standing of what this program actually does is needed in order to ensure a model that is 
fast, accurate and reliable.

CONCLUSION 
8.1 
Results 
In this report a design of a flattened pole pair setup was chosen to compare solid magnet 
poles with stacked magnet poles. The design was used to create a testing rig that could 
test the magnets magnetic performance both for solid magnets and stacked magnets. 
The magnet dimension of the solid block was 14 mm x 28 mm x 20 mm and for the 
stacked magnet elements they each had a size of 2 mm x 4 mm x 20 mm.  
 
It was desired to create the testing rig such that it were easy adjustable to a change in the 
magnet size and for different air gaps which it was proven to be possible. The testing rig 
could measure force at a displacement of the core back down to 1 mm which was shown 
to be sufficient to find the peak forces. 
 
A total of five coatings were tested on how reaching the Curie temperature would affect 
the coating. As explained in the report the purpose of heating the magnets up to 340 ° C 
were to demagnetize the magnets and remove the glue. In order to be able to separate 
the magnets after gluing. While it was seen by the results that some coatings wasn’t of a 
good quality and some did better than others all five coatings took damage that were 
unrecoverable for it to be reused. Therefore it is concluded that this method of demag-
netize the magnets and removing the glue was not possible and another method would 
have to be devolved. 
 
A finite element model was created in the software MagNet. The simulation results 
were compared to what was found from the experimental tests. And as it was shown for 
the BH-measurement they prove not to be conclusive and the minimum values for 
N40H grade from a generalized table was used as the magnetic properties in the simula-
tion model. The finite element models that were created was accurate with a percentage 
range of 3.4 % to 5.7 % which with the data available of the magnets was quit reliable. 
The decrease in maximum force between using solid poles compared to segmented 
poles with a total glue thickness of 0.4 mm was shown to be maximum 6.4 %.

8.2 
Future work 
This project was limited to cover the design of the testing rig and magnetic circuit, finite 
element modelling of the experimental setup and testing of five coatings.  
 
The proposed method of demagnetization and removal of the glue layers was shown not 
to be a possible solution. Therefore another method of separating the magnets and re-
ducing the magnetic potential would have to be developed. If it is possible to find a so-
lution that doesn’t damage the coating material. The magnets themselves could be used 
more than one lifetime which is now is 25 years 
 
Another approach to the coating issues shown in this report is to find another type of 
coating that would protect the magnets better and might resist going to temperatures that 
lies near the Curie temperature of Neodymium magnets. 
 
It would also be interesting to see a study on which temperature is necessary in order for 
the magnets to be easy to handle while they are being glued together to form pole.
