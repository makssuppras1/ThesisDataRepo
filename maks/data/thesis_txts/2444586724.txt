1 Abstract 
Nitrite-preserved meat products such as bacon, salami and sausages can cause formation 
carcinogenic nitrosamines (NAs): both volatile (VNAs) and non-volatile (NVNAs). That is why 
authorities have limited the addition of nitrite in foodstuffs. In Denmark, it is allowed to add 
maximum 60 mg/kg nitrite and in the rest of EU, the limit is between 100-150 mg/kg nitrite. 
The NAs are typically found at low concentrations, which makes it difficult to measure their 
actual concentrations. Three different experiments were performed in order to measure the 
levels of the NAs with focus on NDMA and NPYR: SPE with digestive fluids, QuEChERS 
extraction of sausage extracts and precursors experiment with DMA and PYR in a sausage meat. 
The outcome of SPE showed that the method was not suitable for analysis of NAs in water and 
therefore neither in the digestive fluids. The QuEChERS method was validated and was found 
to be an improvement of a previous study for analysis of VNAs that obtained a LOD of 0.5 μg/kg. 
The procedure from the validated method was carried out in the precursor experiment to 
illustrate if the formation of NDMA and NPYR are enhanced whether nitrite is present or not 
and at different concentrations of the precursors DMA and PYR. The results from the precursor 
experiments showed a positive relation between formation of NDMA and NPYR with increasing 
precursor concentrations of DMA and PYR with or without nitrite present.

5 Introduction 
Sodium nitrite (E249-250) also referred to as nitrite has been used for preservation of meat in 
the food industry for decades. Examples of meat products that can be preserved with nitrite are 
salami, bacon and sausages. The purpose of preservation with nitrite is to significantly increase 
the safety of the consumer due to the fact that nitrite has inhibitory qualities towards microbial 
bacteria such as Clostridium botulinum, which can cause botulism and in the worst case lead to 
death. Besides the positive outcomes of using nitrite, there is also a hazard of using it because 
nitrite can result in formation of carcinogenic compounds like nitrosamines (referred as NAs) 
(Fødevarestyrelsen 2018). The reason why it is relevant to study these NAs further is because 
studies have shown that there is an association between dietary intake of processed meat and 
increased risk of cancer e.g. colon and gastric cancer (Milner & Romagnolo 2010). 
NAs are chemical compounds that can be formed during drying and smoking of e.g. nitrite-
preserved meat, giblets and fish. NAs are formed when certain precursors are available. They 
can be formed when adding nitrite but also indirectly from nitrate due to its conversion to 
nitrite. In addition, nitrite can also react with secondary amines that can be present in the meat 
and form NAs. There are many different kinds of NAs and they are present and formed at 
different concentrations where some of them are carcinogenic. NAs have long-termed effects 
because they can be either genotoxic or carcinogenic (Tricker & Preussmann 1991).  
The NAs are divided in two groups: volatile NAs (VNAs) and non-volatile NAs (NVNAs).  
The VNAs are N-nitrosodimethylamine (NDMA), N-nitrosodiethylamine (NDEA), N-
nitrosopyrrolidine (NPYR), N-nitrosodipropylamine (NDPA), N-nitrosopiperidine (NPIP), N-
nitrosomethylethylamine (NMEA), N-nitrosodibutylamine (NDBA), N-nitrosomorpholine 
(NMOR), N-nitrosodibenzylamine (NDBzA) and N-nitroso pipecolicacid (NPIC).

In a study by Herrmann et al. 2014, all of the VNAs and NVNAs were analyzed to investigate if 
there was a relation between addition of nitrite and the amount of formed NAs. For some NAs, 
this relation was shown but it was not the case for two VNAs: NDMA and NPYR. NDMA and 
NPYR are often formed at very low concentrations (Tricker & Preussmann 1991). This makes 
it difficult to see a difference at various nitrite concentrations. Because of this difficulty, it would 
be useful to optimize the “Herrmann et al. 2014”-method so the optimized method can show a 
relation between added nitrite and formation of NDMA and NPYR and obtain a lower limit of 
detection (LOD).  
The aim of this project and the belonging experiments is to develop an improved method of the 
one used in Herrmann et al. 2014 that can quantify all of the VNAs and NVNAs and obtain lower 
LODs, especially for NDMA and NPYR. Solid-Phase Extraction (SPE) is tested to see if the LODs 
of the various NAs can be lowered. After testing, the method’s performance will be evaluated 
based on different validation parameters such as LOD, recovery, linearity etc. The aim is also to 
perform a model study with specific precursors to test if their initial amounts contributes to the 
formation of their corresponding NAs. After validation, a model study is performed by adding 
nitrite and the precursor’s dimethylamine (DMA) and pyrrolidine (PYR). This is done at various 
concentrations to test if they are promoting the formation of their corresponding NAs, NDMA 
and NPYR. If the LODs can be lowered, then it will help to illustrate at what concentrations the 
different NAs are present and if preservation of nitrite should be further limited.

6 Background 
6.1 Nitrite 
Sodium nitrite (E249-250), also known as nitrite, is used for preserving meat products. The 
European Commission authorizes it as a food additive where its purpose is to preserve the meat 
products by effective inhibition of some pathogenic microorganisms e.g. Clostridium botulinum. 
Preservation with nitrite in meat leads to development of a characteristic pink color, heat 
stability, specific flavor and a change in texture, which is different from fresh meat. Nitrite is a 
reactive compound, which is added to many different meat products that may react with 
secondary amines. This results in formation of NAs, which can be carcinogenic. Animal studies 
with rats have shown that NAs are the only carcinogens that are capable of inducing tumors in 
the 
esophagus 
leading 
to

cancer 
(Fernández-Alba

2005;

In order to reduce the risk of having formation of NAs and thereby cancer, governments have 
to determine limits of nitrite addition and optimize the processes of drying and smoking. In 
Denmark, it is only allowed to add maximum 60 mg/kg nitrite whereas the maximum limit in 
the rest of EU is either 100 or 150 mg/kg nitrite (Honikel 2008; Cassens et al. 1978; 
Retsinformation 2018).

The total exposure of NOCs to humans can be divided in two: exogenous and endogenous 
exposure (figure 1).  
Exogenous exposure is defined as the intake of preformed NOCs from the surrounding 
environment, which is further divided into life-style and occupational exposure. Life-style 
exposure is the biggest and most evidenced exposure group where foodstuffs are the majority 
(Hill 1988). Meat products that are preserved with nitrite and/or nitrate can lead to formation 
of NAs whereas meat products, which are smoked, will have a further increasing concentration 
of NAs (Tricker et al. 1984). Microorganisms can reduce nitrate to nitrite if nitrate is added in 
raw meat products (Honikel et al. 2007). 
The concentration of VNAs measured in cured meats are between 1-56 μg/kg whereas NVNAs 
are between 10-1620 μg/kg (Tricker & Preussmann 1991). Traces of 0.1-1.0 μg/kg NAs, 
primarily NDMA, are also found in fish and seafood (Webb & Gough 1980). NDMA was 
measured in Japenese salt-dried fish to be 3.0-34 μg/kg and was increased to 300 μg/kg after 
coal gas broiling (Kawabata et al. 1980). NDMA in cheese and dairy products are measured 
between 1-6 μg/kg (Tricker & Preussmann 1991). In pickled vegetables there has been 
measured <5-96 μg/kg VNAs including NDMA, NPYR and NPIP (Tricker & Preussmann 1991). 
The second exogenous exposure to humans is the occupational. The associated data available 
is very limited but it is known that the highest concentrations of NAs preformed exogenous 
exposure are found in working environments such as dye manufactory, leather tannery and fish 
processing (Hill 1988). 
Endogenous exposure is defined as both the uptake and formation of precursors in vivo 
although the exposures cannot be quantified. The uptake of the NOCs’ precursors can be 
nitrosating agents such as nitrite and nitrous gases (NOx) by inhalation or digestion of 
nitrite/nitrate from foodstuffs. The presence of the precursors are not sufficient in order to 
form NOCs. However, the formation of the NOCs often occurs in the human body. The stomach 
has favorable acidic conditions where digested foodstuffs with nitrite/nitrate can lead to acid 
catalyzed nitrosation and form NAs. Nitrosation is described further in section “6.3.1.1” (Hill 
1988). Nitrate that may be present in heat-treated meat products can lead to natural reduction 
to nitrite by microorganisms when acidic conditions are available (Honikel et al. 2007).

Endogenous formation of NOCs were shown in two studies. In the first study of Ohshima & 
Bartsch from 1981, endogenous formation of NPRO was demonstrated. This was done by 
measuring a male volunteer’s excretion in the urine who had ingested vegetable juice, which is 
a source of nitrate and proline (PRO). The amount of NPRO excreted in the urine was 
proportional to the proline dose and increased exponentially with the nitrate dose ingested 
(Ohshima & Bartsch 1981). The second study of Postlethwait & Mustafa from 1983 
demonstrated endogenous formation of NMOR. A rat lung was isolated and ventilated with NO2 
whilst being purged with a medium that contained morpholine (MOR). After 60 minutes of 
exposure to NO2 and MOR, NMOR was detected in both lung tissues (Postlethwait & Mustafa 
1983).  
6.3 Nitrosamines 
The NOCs are divided in two groups: N-nitrosamines (NAs) and N-nitrosamides. NAs are the 
relevant group for this project, which are N-nitroso derivates of secondary amines where a NOC 
is bounded to a secondary amine (see figure 2) (Fernández-Alba & Agüera 2005).  
 
Figure 2. Structure of a NA group where R1 and R2 = alkyl and aryl groups (Wikipedia 2018). An alkyl group is a functional group that contains 
carbon and hydrogen atoms, where the general formula is CnH2n+1 (Reid 2018). An aryl is a functional group that is derived from an aromatic ring 
compound, where one hydrogen atom is removed from the ring (Helmenstine 2017).

N-nitrosation 
The NAs are formed due to N-nitrosation where a nitroso group replaces a hydrogen atom, 
which is attached to a nitrogen atom. This is illustrated in figure 3 (Hill 1988).

The focus are on NDMA and NPYR because they are usually often found in the literature at low 
concentrations and can be carcinogenic (Restek 2018; NCBI 2018b; Tricker & Preussmann 
1991). Therefore, it is useful to optimize a method in order to measure the low concentrations 
of NDMA and NPYR and show a relation between added nitrite and formation of them. The 
relation between added nitrite and formation of carcinogenic NAs such as NDMA and NPYR 
could be useful for the authorities to discuss whether the maximum limit of nitrite addition in 
foodstuffs should be lowered. 
6.3.1.3 
NDMA  
NDMA is classified as a probable human carcinogen (EPA 2017). NDMA is shown to be toxic to 
the reproductive system in regards to fertility and fetus and is used in laboratory research to 
induce tumors in experimental animals (Restek 2018). NDMA is measured at different 
concentrations in foodstuffs such as cured meats (1-5 μg/kg), fried bacon (<23 μg/kg), dried 
milk products (<7 μg/kg), oils and fats (<1 μg/kg), beer (<0.5 μg/kg), dairy products (1-6 
μg/kg) and broiled squid (<300 μg/kg) (Tricker & Preussmann 1991).  
6.3.1.4 
NPYR  
NPYR is also used in laboratory research to induce tumors in experimental animals as it is 
anticipated to be a human carcinogen (NCBI 2018b). NPYR is measured at different 
concentrations in foodstuffs such as cured meats (1-5 μg/kg), fried bacon (<130 μg/kg), pickled 
vegetables (<96 μg/kg), mixed spices (<10 μg/kg), dried chillies (<6 μg/kg) and broiled squid 
(2.4-13 μg/kg) (Tricker & Preussmann 1991).

6.4 Toxicology 
The reason for a growing interest in studying NAs is that there are evidence presenting their 
mutagenic and carcinogenic properties. Even though NAs are classified as suspect human 
carcinogens there is lacking direct evidence (Fernández-Alba & Agüera 2005). The NAs’ 
exposure to humans can be from formation of NAs in food during their storage and preparation 
or endogenously in the human body e.g. in the stomach. It has also become more acknowledged 
that nitrosation also occur in vivo in the human body and is a crucial source of the human 
exposure (see section “6.2”) (Hill 1988).

NAs are shown to induce cancer and tumors in experimental animals that resemble their human 
counterparts (Restek 2018; NCBI 2018b). Data suggest that humans are susceptible to 
carcinogenesis by NOCs and that their presence in foodstuffs leads to a huge risk factor for 
specific human cancers e.g. esophageal cancer based on animal studies (Fernández-Alba & 
Agüera 2005). The first outbreak of NAs was toxic hepatic lesions in sheep where traces of 
NDMA were found in the sheep feed. The feed was fishmeal consisting of herring that was 
nitrite-preserved (Tricker & Preussmann 1991; Hill 1988). 
NAs are able to react with DNA and this is due to activation by liver enzymes of the cytochrome 
family P450 such as CYP2EI. After reacting with the DNA, it is not repaired correctly and results 
in modified transition of base pairs guanine-cytosine (G-C) and adenine-thymine (A-T). This 
failed repairing could lead to carcinogenicity (Fernández-Alba & Agüera 2005).  
6.5 Different methods of analysis 
Many studies have used different methods and techniques to analyze both NAs and their 
corresponding precursors (secondary amines). This section creates an overview of the different 
techniques that may be used as an inspiration for this project’ methods when analyzing the 
different NAs and secondary amines. 
 Studies on analysis of NAs 
In a study by Herrmann et al. 2014, processed meat products were analyzed for NAs in μg/kg 
with extraction by acetonitrile (ACN). Separation and quantification were performed in a 
developed LC-MS/MS method and were tested with two different ionization methods: 
atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI) and electrospray ionization (ESI). The 
method was also validated by using spiked samples. For the extraction procedure, the meat 
samples were homogenized in a tube, spiked with standards where ACN with 1 % formic acid 
was added and shaken. Then ceramic homogenizers were added and centrifuged. The 
supernatants were transferred to a new tube and they were set to freeze in order to obtain 
precipitation. The extracts were then thawed and centrifuged again. An aliquot of the 
supernatants were set to be evaporated to ~0.25 mL with nitrogen gas (N2) at 30 ºC. They were 
then diluted with water to 1 mL. The extracts were analyzed in the LC-MS/MS with both ESI 
and APCI with different gradient programs where water and methanol were used as eluents 
and the injection volume was 3.5 μL. Recoveries were acceptable at 50-130 % and relative 
standard deviations (RSDs) at 2-23 %. APCI improved the sensitivity of the method because the

In another study by Lee et al. 2013, wastewater samples were analyzed for NAs in ng/mL by

 Studies on analysis of secondary amines as precursors 
Secondary amines could contribute to the formation of certain NAs but before testing, it would 
be beneficial to find out what results and conclusions other studies on precursors had.  
In a study by Mitch et al. from 2003, wastewater samples were collected to measure the amount

to measure the formation of NAs including NDMA and NPYR. The treated water samples were

molar yield of 1.27 % NDMA converted from DMA and 2.42 % NPYR from PYR. The method 
indicated that the formation of NAs converted from their precursors were inefficient (Bond & 
Templeton 2011).

 Calibration and internal standard (IS) 
The concentration of the analytes in the samples are calculated by comparing peak areas of the 
samples with peak areas from external and/or internal standards. Standard solutions, also 
called calibration standards, are made with an external standard that contains a known amount 
of analyte within the desired concentration range. These are analyzed and the peak areas of 
them are plotted against their concentration to produce a calibration curve. The sample at same 
volume as the calibration standards is then analyzed where an area of the sample’s peak is used 
to determine its concentration by the calibration curve (Nielsen 2017).  
An internal standard (IS) is a known amount of a compound that is different from the analyte 
of interest and is added to the sample. Prior to analysis, losses of the analyte can occur during 
sample preparation. When adding IS, the losses can be corrected when calculating the 
concentrations and the extraction efficiency can be monitored. If a known concentration of IS 
is added before extraction, the ratio of IS to analyte will remain constant because the same 
fraction of each is lost from sample preparation to analysis of them. The criteria for using an IS 
is, that it must be chemically and structurally related to the analyte, elute at a different time and 
not be naturally present in the sample (Harris 2010; Nielsen 2017).

7.2.1.1 
Pre-experiments with SPE 
The SPE column is conditioned by 7 mL methanol, which was an organic solvent that solvated 
the nonpolar functional chains present and activated the material of activated coconut carbon 
(stationary phase). Methanol cause nonpolar functional groups to interact with very-polar 
matrix, which allowed isolate retention. After that, the column was equilibrated two times with 
7 mL water to displace the organic environment so the particles could interact with the sample. 
The spiked sample of 20 mL water was then loaded onto the column by allowing the sample to 
interact with the sorbent as long as possible (slow sample flow of 3 mL/min). This would 
promote a successful retention of the NAs to the stationary phase. The last step was elution, 
where the retained NAs were forced to elute from the column. 5 mL ACN was added and was 
eluted with the desired NAs. The eluted liquid was then evaporated (SuperTherm from 
Mikrolab Aarhus, Højbjerg, Denmark) until there was no liquid left with N2 gas at 30 ºC. After 
drying, the remaining was diluted with water to 1 mL, mixed on a whirl mixer (MS2 Minishaker 
IKA from Bie & Berntsen, Herlev, Denmark) and an aliquot was transferred to a vial for analysis 
in LC-MS/MS (see LC-MS/MS method in section “7.2.5”) (Lee et al. 2013). A detailed procedure 
of the SPE method was shown in appendix 1.

LC-MS/MS theory 
7.2.5.1.1 Separation on LC 
High-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) is the classic abbreviation of the method liquid 
chromatography (LC). Both LC-MS and LC-MS/MS have the same purpose and principles but 
the only thing different is the detector. LC-MS has a mass spectrometer with one mass analyzer, 
whereas LC-MS/MS has a tandem mass spectrometer with two mass analyzers (Niessen 2010). 
They are both methods used to separate analytes in a solution. The method used in this project 
is LC-MS/MS. The separation is based on how an analyte is distributed between two non-
miscible phases: a stationary and mobile phase. If the relation of the distribution is different, 
they will then separate in the LC and be registered in a MS/MS. The method is defined as “liquid 
chromatography” because a liquid is used as the mobile phase that transports the sample to a 
stationary phase. The stationary phase is a solid material such as Pheny-hexyl in this project, 
placed in a column (Hansen 2018; Nielsen 2017).  
The LC-MS/MS consist of several units where some of them are shown as a schematic 
illustration in figure 13. 
 
Figure 13. Schematic illustration of HPLC (Nielsen 2017). 
The eluent reservoir is the storage of the mobile phase (see figure 13). The mobile phase is 
transferred through the system under pressure by a pump. The pump transfers the mobile 
phase through the system with a stabile flow and a gradient elution. The elution can be either 
isocratic or gradient. In isocratic elution, the mobile phase’s eluents composition are held

constant throughout the run, whereas the eluents’ composition is varying during gradient 
elution. Gradient elution is best suited for samples that contains several analytes of interest 
which inherent polarities that vary a lot or less between each other, while the isocratic elution 
will elute all components because of the small differences (Nielsen 2017).   
After the pump, the sample is injected into the system by an injector. This step is often 
automated by an autosampler. The sample is in a vial sealed with a septum, and stored in a vial 
tray in the autosampler. A thin needle punctures the septum and transfers the sample into the 
system (Nielsen 2017). 
The column is a pipe that consist of rust-free steel and is filled with column material that is held 
together by a nut in each end. The column is packed with silica particles where the particles are 
bound to nonpolar C18 chains with some side groups of phenyl-hexyl. The column is used for 
reverse-phased chromatography where the analytes of interest are retained because of 
hydrophobic interactions with the stationary phase, which is nonpolar. The hydrophobic 
analytes in the mobile phase usually absorb to the nonpolar stationary phase that leads to 
elution of the analytes in order of most to less polar. Additionally, the column is coupled with a 
guard column to protect the column from strongly adsorbed sample analytes and particles from 
the injector before it reaches the main column. (Nielsen 2017). 
7.2.5.1.2 Detection on MS/MS 
In a mass spectrometer (MS), molecules must be ionized or electrically charged in order to 
produce ions but the analysis requires that the molecules or atoms of interest can be ionized. 
The MS-instrument analyzes the molecules by relating the mass of each molecule to the charge. 
This identification is characteristic to each molecule and is defined as the “mass-to-charge 
ratio” (m/z). This means that if a molecule has a single charge of z = 1 then the m/z ratio will 
be equal to the molecular mass (Nair & Clarke 2017).

7.2.5.1.3 Ionization 
In order to enter the MS, the sample must be ionized and must go through an ion source before 
reaching it. The two ionization methods tested in this project are ESI and APCI. 
7.2.5.1.3.1 Electrospray Ionization (ESI) 
ESI is an ionization method that normally is best suited for polar compounds and analytes that 
still exists as ions in the LC eluent (Nielsen 2017). The method is illustrated in figure 14. 
 
 
Figure 14. An illustration of the ionization method ESI (Shimadzu 2018). 
ESI consists of a nozzle that serves to transfer the LC eluent to a stainless-steel capillary tube. 
The capillary tube functions at atmospheric pressure where a solvent spray is formed by high 
voltage held between the capillary and the instrument orifice (see figure 14). This is coupled 
with an axial flow of nebulizing gas (compressed N2 gas) outside the capillary in order to spray 
the solvent spray. The solvent droplets from the solvent spray evaporate in the ion source and 
release ions to the gas phase for analysis. Two main processes reduce the droplet size. The first 
process is the velocity difference between the rapid moving nebulizer gas (N2 gas) and the slow 
moving LC eluent. This results in a spray of highly charged droplets. The second process is using 
heated gases in order to increase the efficiency of droplet size reduction and desolvation 
(Nielsen 2017; Shimadzu 2018; Nair & Clarke 2017). 
The ions produced are dependent on the initial charge in which positively charged compounds 
will produce positive ions whereas negatively charged compounds will produce negative ions. 
The negatively charged compounds could be NAs that contains functional groups of carboxylic 
acid such as NHPRO, NMTCA, NPIC, NPRO and NTCA (Herrmann et al. 2014).

7.2.5.1.3.2 Atmospheric Pressure Chemical Ionization (APCI) 
APCI is a gas-phase ionization method that normally is best suited for volatile and low or 
medium polar compounds (Nielsen 2017). The method is illustrated in figure 15. 
 
 
Figure 15. An illustration of the ionization method APCI (Shimadzu 2018). 
APCI consists of a capillary tube that serves to transfer the LC eluent to a heated ceramic 
vaporizer tube, which functions at atmospheric pressure. APCI uses heat and a nebulization gas 
to form a solvent vapor of the LC eluent (see figure 15). The solvent vapor is then ionized by 
corona discharge near the exit of the vaporizer tube where ions are formed in the gas phase. 
The corona discharge is a needle that is applied high voltage in order to generate stable reaction 
ions. The ions that are released in the gas phase transfer charge to the analyte molecules to 
create analyte ions, which are analyzed by the MS. During ionization by APCI, there can occur 
some thermal degradation and therefore it is important that the analytes of interest are heat 
stable and volatile for best results (Nielsen 2017; Shimadzu 2018; Nair & Clarke 2017).  
7.2.5.1.3.3 ESI compared to APCI 
ESI is a sensitive ionization method for highly polar, least volatile or thermally unstable 
analytes or ions that are generated in solutions. However, APCI provides better sensitivity for 
non/medium polar, volatile and thermally stable analytes, which is illustrated in figure 16 
(Nielsen 2017; Shimadzu 2018; Nair & Clarke 2017). 
==PAGE:34==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis

Figure 16. Applicable compounds to the different ionization methods (modified from Shimadzu 2018). 
Both ionization methods have in common that the ionization takes place under atmospheric 
pressure. However, in ESI the analytes are ionized when leaving the capillary tube whereas the 
analytes are ionized in a heated ceramic vaporizer tube in APCI. Additionally, ESI is more 
sensitive to matrix effects because of ion suppression whereas APCI is often less sensitive to 
matrix effects (Nair & Clarke 2017). Ion suppression reduces the ionization efficiency of 
analytes of interest due to presence of interference compounds that compete for ionization 
(Harris 2010). The reason why APCI is less sensitive to ion suppression could be that the 
analytes of interest that are ionized must be able to withstand the thermal degradation that can 
occur (heat stable). 
APCI is anticipated to give a better response for the VNAs according to figure 16 because they 
have small molecular masses. Additionally, the volatility of the VNAs makes them overlap the 
area of being applicable to both GC and LC. On the other hand, ESI should be better suited for 
the NVNAs because the majority have bigger molecular masses than the VNAs and are not 
volatile. The classical ionization method is ESI but APCI is also tested because several studies 
have observed better response for the VNAs. 
Prior to this project, it had been tested which ionization method was best suited for the 
individual NA (Herrmann 2018). A study by Herrmann with unpublished data from 2018 has 
tested which NAs are best suited for either ESI or APCI. A specific standard with same NA 
concentration as the LOD of them had been analyzed by observing the area of peak and 
signal/noise relation. The optimum was a high area/response and a large difference between 
the signal and noise. The conclusion of this study was that the NAs, which were best suited for

ESI were NHPRO, NMTCA, NPIC, NPRO and NTCA and best suited for APCI were NDBA, NDEA, 
NDMA, NDPA, NMEA, NPIP and NPYR. Some NAs can be used for both ionizations but for the 
NAs examined in this project were the VNAs, where APCI was preferable (Herrmann 2018). 
7.2.5.1.4 Triple quadrupole 
The type of mass analyzer used in this project is a MS/MS called triple quadrupole. This consists 
of two quadrupole mass analyzers that are connected by a collision cell (Niessen 2010). An 
illustration of the triple quadrupole is shown in figure 17: 
 
Figure 17. Illustration of a cutaway view of a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (Harris 2010). 
A quadrupole mass analyzer consists of four rods that are arranged in parallel with space in the 
middle where the opposing pair of rods are electrically connected to each other (see figure 18). 
These mass analyzers separates ions based on the stability of their flight through the oscillating 
electric field in the analyzers. The electric field is generated by applying a radio frequency (RFQ) 
voltage between a pair of the opposing rods, and then a direct current (DC) offset voltage is 
applied to the other pair of rods. During this, only ions with a certain m/z will stably fly through 
the quadrupoles whereas other ions will not. Therefore, the remaining ions will not reach the 
detector. The RFQ and DC voltages in the quadrupole can be adjusted in order to filter the 
different masses or detect analyte-specific ions of a certain m/z (Nair & Clarke 2017). 
==PAGE:36==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis 
Page 36 of 89 
 
 
10-02-2019 
 
Figure 18. Illustration of a quadrupole mass spectrometry (Honour 2003). 
In the first quadrupole (Q1) (see figure 17), a mixture of ions enters where the precursor ions 
are selected. In the collision cell (Q2), the selected precursor ions are fragmented by collision-
induced dissociation (CID). CID is the result of a collision with an analyte of interest and an inert 
gas (argon). Products of the collision are specific product ions, which are an expression of the 
bond energies that is inherent in the molecular structure of the precursor ion, collision gas and 
energy used (Nair & Clarke 2017; Honour 2003; Harris 2010; Nielsen 2017).  
In the final quadrupole (Q3) (see figure 17), the product ions are selected and then passed on 
to the detector. It only allows specific product ions to reach the detector. This results in high 
specificity and is referred as selected-reaction monitoring (SRM). In case of multiple 
transitions, which is the case in this project, monitoring during a run, the data acquisition is 
called multiple-reaction monitoring (MRM) (Nair & Clarke 2017; Honour 2003; Harris 2010; 
Nielsen 2017). 
An example of NPYR with a nominal mass of 100.06 g/mL shows that when it is ionized by APCI 
or ESI it obtains a mass of 101.1 m/z in Q1. It is then applied a collision energy of 11-19 eV in 
Q2. In Q3, two different product ions are observed with two different masses of 41 and 59 m/z. 
The values of NPYR are shown in table 2, section “7.2.5.1.5”. 
7.2.5.1.4.1 Scan modes 
A MS separates ions according to their m/z ratio referred to as a scan. A single MS scan can be 
utilized to determine the molecular weight of an analyte. However, MS/MS scans are utilized 
for identification or confirmation of a molecular specie. A precursor ion is fragmented in a 
collision cell where the produced fragments are characteristic of the precursor ion and the scan 
is used to confirm the identity of it. There are different kinds of scan modes used in a MS/MS: 
==PAGE:37==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis 
Page 37 of 89 
 
 
10-02-2019 
Full scan in Q1/Q3, Selected Ion Monitoring (SIM), Multiple Reaction Monitoring (MRM) and 
Product Ion Scan or Precursor Ion Scan. Product ion scan, precursor ion scan and MRM are 
illustrated in figure 19. 
 
Figure 19. Illustration of some scan modes in MS/MS: Product ion scan, precursor ion scan and multiple ion monitoring also called MRM 
(modified from Lin et al. 2014).  
Full scan in Q1 is where only one mass analysis occurs. Ions from the ion source are scanned in 
the mass analyzer Q1, which produces a complete mass spectrum. The other quadrupoles Q2 
and Q3 acts as ion transmissions. The complete mass spectrum produced are equivalent to the 
mass spectrum obtained from a MS with only one mass analyzer. Full scan in Q3 has the same 
principles, but differs from the scanning of the ions that takes place in the mass analyzer Q3. 
The ion transmissions are Q1 and Q2 (Novatia 2019).  
Selected Ion Monitoring (SIM) is monitoring a particular precursor ion where Q1 is scanning a 
very small mass range e.g. one mass unit. This scan mode is convenient for detection of small 
quantities of an analyte of interest in a complex mixture when the mass spectrum of the target 
analyte is known. However, it does not contain any information on the characteristic fragments 
(IonSourceSM 2016). 
Multiple Reaction Monitoring (MRM) is a scan mode used for quantification where a preselected 
precursor ion passes through Q1, fragments in Q2 and a preselected product ion is monitored 
in Q3 (see figure 19) (Lin et al. 2014; IonSourceSM 2016; Nielsen 2017). 
==PAGE:38==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis 
Page 38 of 89 
 
 
10-02-2019 
Product Ion Scan is a scan mode used for identification of all fragments from a precursor ion, 
where Q1 is adjusted to transmit a specific precursor ion and Q3 scans a specific mass range 
(see figure 19) (Lin et al. 2014). 
Precursor Ion Scan is a scan mode used for identification of analytes of interest sharing a 
common product ion where Q1 scans a specific mass range. Q3 is adjusted to transmit a specific 
product ion (see figure 19) (Lin et al. 2014). 
The scan mode used in this project was MRM because Herrmann et al. 2014 had identified the 
individual precursor and their product ions for the individual NA.

7.2.5.1.6 Identification and quantification 
Analysis of a LC-MS/MS response can be carried out when the analytes of interest in a sample 
are separated into individual peaks, as they migrate through the stationary phase in the column. 
Proper chromatographic techniques with high resolution are selected. When it leads to 
sufficient separation of the analytes in a mixture and peaks of the different analytes in 
chromatograms, both qualitative and quantitative analysis can be performed (Nielsen 2017).  
After proper identification of analytes in the sample, quantification of them are performed by 
measuring and comparing peak areas of the sample with standards of known concentrations. 
Software with data analysis integrates each peak by recognizing the start, maximum and end of 
==PAGE:40==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis 
Page 40 of 89 
 
 
10-02-2019 
them. The software then determines values such as retention time (RT), peak height and peak 
area. The time it takes each analyte from injection to detection of a maximum signal of it, is 
defined as RT. The peaks of the individual chromatogram are observed as the height of the peak, 
and it should only be increased in the spiked sample without changes in the RT, peak width or 
shape (Nielsen 2017). A qualify and quantify ion are based on the product ions that are 
measured in chromatograms which are used for identification and quantification of the 
precursor ion. The qualify ion is selected from the mass spectrum of the target analyte where 
the presence of the qualify ion with the correct amount relative to the quantify ion leads to 
correct identification of the target analyte. The amount of the qualify ion related to the quantify 
ion is referred to as ion ratio (Agilent Technologies 2019). In MS/MS, the mass transitions are 
used for identification and quantification where ion ratio is determined and compared with the 
calibration standards (Nielsen 2017). An example of a chromatogram from the instrument’s 
integration is illustrated in figure 20.

8.1 Pre-experiments with SPE 
The performance of SPE was evaluated by the recoveries of the NAs after SPE were studied by 
adjusting different parameters such as pH, spiked concentration, dilution, drying and elution 
solvent. The pH was modified by having some water samples with a pH 2 to observe whether 
the low pH could affect the RT compared to water samples with no pH modification. The spiked 
concentration in the water samples were increased to observe if the analytes were detectable. 
The sample volume was reduced to check if the dilution was causing the problem of detecting 
peaks in the chromatograms e.g. if the analytes ran through the SPE column during sample 
loading without retention. Before drying, the eluted sample with analytes was added an aliquot 
of water and was evaporated until there was a little liquid left to see if complete drying was 
causing the NAs to evaporate. To test if the NAs were lost during the SPE step, it was excluded 
as a control. Lastly, the elution solvent ACN was compared with DCM to see which one was more 
effective because in the article from Lee et al. 2013 they used DCM as elution solvent and 
obtained acceptable recoveries.  
The results of the SPE optimization experiments showed poor results of recoveries and no 
opportunity for quantification. In addition, the results indicated a very small difference 
between the two different elution solvents ACN and DCM but no greater recovery than before. 
It was decided not to use SPE columns because they could not retain the NAs in the water 
samples or they were evaporated during the drying. Besides, the concentration in the vials left 
to be analyzed were too low to be detected in the LC-MS/MS. Since, many materials and 
chemicals had been used to obtain peaks in the chromatograms, another method had been 
chosen to analysis of NAs.

9 Discussion 
9.1 Validation experiments 
 Matrix effect 
No significant matrix effect were indicated by studying four different calibration curves. No 
difference when comparing the slopes of the calibration curves trend line (RSDs) from table 5. 
The RSDs for NDMA and NPYR are 11 %, whereas the only VNAs that differs are NDBA (23 %) 
and NDEA (31 %) (see table 5). These values shows that there is no obvious matrix effect and 
the non-matrix matched calibration curve is used for further experiments and quantification. 
In addition, the recoveries of the matrix-matched calibration curves are similar to the non-
matrix matched calibration curve which is another factor leading to selection of the non-matrix 
matched calibration curve for further quantification of VNAs.  
9.1.1.1 
Spiked samples of 5 μg/kg without PSA treatment 
The spiked sausage samples with a concentration of 5 μg/kg had three replicates with PSA 
treatment (A, B and C) and three others without PSA treatment (D, E and F). It was tested to see 
if the PSA treatment provided a better response and signal/noise relation than without PSA 
treatment. The signal/noise relation shows that there is not any significant difference whether 
it is treated with PSA or not, but the relation is relatively lower for the NDMA and NPYR 
compared to the other VNAs seen in appendix 8 (sheet 3).  
The spiked samples with PSA treatment showed recoveries between 48-102 % and RSDs of 3-
14 % whereas the spiked samples without PSA treatment showed recoveries between 40-92 % 
and RSDs of 4-12 % (see table 4). It seems there is not an obvious difference if the spiked 
samples are treated with PSA since the RSDs are <20 %. However, the robustness might be 
strengthened if the samples are treated with PSA because the instrument may analyze more 
samples before cleaning of it is needed. This was indicated by sausage extracts without PSA 
treatment that were darker than the ones treated with PSA. 
 The overall recoveries, RSDs, LODs and LOQs 
The non-matrix matched calibration curve are based on calibration standards that are not 
matrix-matched and analyze all samples. The recovery for every replicate is stated to see if 
some of them are different from the rest and potential outliers (see table 4). Recoveries are not 
corrected using the IS but was only used for identification of NDMA and NPYR.  
==PAGE:55==
Anela Sajnoska, s133957  
Master’s Thesis 
Page 55 of 89 
 
 
10-02-2019 
The low recoveries for the lowest spiking concentration of 0.5 μg/kg of NDMA was between 11-
36 % with a RSD of 43 % whereas NPYR had recoveries between 53-73 % and a RSD of 14 %. 
NPYR’s recoveries seemed to be acceptable based on the low RSD (see table 4). However, 
NDMA’s recoveries were very low compared to NPYR and the RSD was high. NDMA’s replicate 
A has a recovery of 11 %, which has a big influence on the RSD calculation and may be an outlier. 
The remaining VNAs showed recoveries of 20-76 % and RSDs between 5-28 %. The spike 
concentration 1 μg/kg in table 4 also showed a relatively lower recovery of the VNAs in the 
replicate C, which contributes to slightly higher RSDs. The reason why the low spike 
concentrations had poor results in terms of recoveries were due to the amount of VNAs in the 
blank samples that are subtracted, which also caused bigger uncertainty. The higher spike 
concentrations of 5 and 10 μg/kg in table 4 showed acceptable recoveries of the VNAs between 
40-102 % and RSDs between 2-16 %.  
If the blank samples did not contain amounts of VNAs, it would lead to much better recoveries 
because the low spike concentrations are more affected by the blank samples’ amount than the 
higher spike concentrations. The source of the NAs could be originated from the water used in 
the experiments or the pig’s (from the organic wiener sausage) drinking water or feed, which 
includes greens or vegetables that contain a natural amount of nitrate. The nitrate from the feed 
can be reduced by microorganisms to nitrite, which contributes to the NA content in the blank 
samples. Analysis of more replicates such as six instead of three over different periods would 
lead to better assessment of whether one or more of the replicates are outliers.  
Furthermore, the peaks of the VNAs in the chromatograms are automatically integrated by the 
instrument’s program. In case of manual integration, a minor change of the areas would lead to 
large changes in the concentrations. The concentration in the blank samples are the reason why 
the calibration curves becomes more flat in the region of low spike concentrations. This causes 
the large difference in concentration when a slight change in integration of an area occurs. 
Based on the validation experiments’ results, recoveries and RSDs, the LODs for the VNAs are 
determined to be 0.5 μg/kg from the non-matrix matched calibration curve. The LOQs for the 
VNAs are 0.5 μg/kg but for NDMA it could indicate that a LOQ of 1 μg/kg is more appropriate 
because of its high RSD of 43 % in the spike concentration of 0.5 μg/kg (see table 4).

 Comparison to other analysis methods 
The overall purpose with developing an improved method was to be able to measure all NAs by 
LC-MS/MS: VNAs in the organic phase and NVNAs in the water phase. However, the obstacles 
was that the NVNAs were not detectable in the water phase. This could be because of high 
concentration of ammonium formate. That is why the focus of the validation experiment are the 
VNAs. 
The developed method used in the validation experiments included a step of phase separation: 
an organic and a water phase. The organic phase contained the VNAs and the water phase 
contained the NVNAs. The VNAs in the organic phase that generally occurs at low 
concentrations were able to be concentrated. After evaporation of the organic phase, it was 
diluted with water in order to be analyzed in LC-MS/MS. However, if the VNAs were kept in 
ACN, a GC-MS/MS could be used for analysis of the VNAs. The VNAs may be better analyzed in 
GC-MS/MS because the analysis is generally better suited for volatile analytes, where the 
separation is based on the boiling points of the analytes instead of interaction with the 
stationary phase in LC-MS/MS.  
The phase separation was inspired by the method used in Lehotay et al. 2015, which obtained 
acceptable recoveries, RSDs and a LOD of 0.1 μg/kg by analyzing bacon with GC-MS/MS. The 
sample preparation and LC-MS/MS method was inspired by Herrmann et al. 2014, which 
obtained acceptable recoveries, RSDs and LODs between 3-18 μg/kg for the VNAs. Based on the 
obtained LOD from Lehotay et al. 2015, an analysis of the VNAs from the validation experiments 
by GC-MS/MS instead of LC-MS/MS may lead to lower LODs. The developed method used in the 
validation experiments shows that it is an improvement compared to the method used in 
Herrmann et al. 2014 where the LODs obtained for VNAs in the validation experiment are 0.5 
μg/kg.  
9.2 Precursor experiments with DMA and PYR 
The aim of the precursor experiments was to observe the effect of nitrite, precursors (DMA and 
PYR) and meat compounds on formation of NDMA and NPYR. The used method from the 
validation experiments have been tested for NDBA, NDEA, NDMA, NDPA, NMEA, NPIP and 
NPYR. This is done based on matrix from the organic wiener sausage whereas the precursor 
experiment are based on matrix from minced pork meat made into a sausage meat.

The spiked sausage samples had acceptable recoveries of VNAs between 60-118 % whereas the 
water samples had recoveries between 2-38 % (see table 6). NPRO was also measured in the 
spiked samples and had acceptable recovery of 56 % in sausage samples and 48 % in water 
samples (table 6). Thus, extraction on the sausage meat in the precursor experiments should 
be appropriate using the extraction method from the validation experiments.   
The unacceptable recoveries of the VNAs in the water samples are not very reliable because the 
actual amounts are probably even higher than the measured. This is due to the method, which 
is not optimized for extracting VNAs from water. The validated method is appropriate for the 
sausage samples based on the recoveries whereas the low recoveries of the water samples 
indicates that it needs further optimization in order to be used for extraction from water. 
However, the sausage and water samples can only be compared to a certain point because they 
each were added different amounts of formic acid in the extraction step. 0.1 mL of formic acid 
should have been added to the sausage samples and water samples, but by mistake, 1 mL was 
added to the sausage samples. It is also shown by the extraction efficiency in the spiked samples 
where the water samples did not extract as much VNAs as the sausage samples extracted. This 
could be because the water samples is a much cleaner extract where there is not anything 
forcing the analytes to be extracted from the water.

and 0.98 μg/kg NPYR (see figure 22).  However, the spiked water samples showed lower 
amounts of NDMA and NPYR than the non-spiked samples with same precursor concentration 
(see table 6).   
The sausage samples with nitrite also shows a positive relation with increasing precursor 
concentrations and nitrite addition where the concentration of NDMA and NPYR is further 
increased when nitrite is added (see figure 23). The sausage samples with nitrite are

0 and 2 are relatively lower than NPYR. The water samples have 10 times less formic acid added 
than the sausage samples but there are still observed an increase in formation of NDMA and 
NPYR. This also shows that the formation of NAs are not only acid-catalyzed.  
Generally, the same tendency of positive relations are seen in both sausage and water samples 
with present of nitrite, increasing precursor concentrations and formation of NDMA and NPYR. 
Thus, the water samples without nitrite has relatively higher amounts of NDMA and NPYR than 
the sausage samples without nitrite. This can be because the water initially contains higher 
amounts of NDMA and NPYR.  
 Formation of other NAs 
The formation of the remaining NAs measured in the precursor experiments does not have a 
common tendency whether nitrite is present or when the precursor concentrations are 
increasing. The precursor experiment measured NPRO, NTCA, NHPRO and NPIC and even 
though they are not evaluated in the validated method, they are used for indications of what 
tendency they might have. They are measured because their values are incorporated in the used 
MS/MS method and the results are only indications. The only NAs that are validated in this 
project are the VNAs: NDBA, NDEA, NDMA, NDPA, NMEA, NPIP and NPYR. 
The sausage samples without nitrite had only measurable levels of NDBA, NDPA and NPRO, 
which showed that the increasing precursor concentration have no obvious tendency on the

group of NDBA to benefit their own formation. The decrease and no obvious tendency for some 
of the NAs can indicate that their precursors are not available anymore.

The equation 1 illustrate, that there should be a correlation between precursor (secondary 
amine) and NA concentration. By looking at the equation, if the nitrite or precursor 
concentration increases then it should multiply the formation of NA. It also shows that if either 
the nitrite or precursors are used up there cannot be formed anymore NA because both factors 
must be present. In the precursor experiment, a high amount of nitrite was added (250 mg/kg)

10 Conclusion 
The conclusion of the validation experiment with VNAs is that there are no significant matrix 
effect observed when analyzing by LC-MS/MS with APCI. The LODs for VNAs are determined to 
be 0.5 μg/kg. The validation experiments also show that the developed method is an 
improvement compared to the method used in Herrmann et al. 2014 since generally lower 
LODs are obtained for the VNAs. 
The validation method include a step of phase separation where the outcome of an organic 
phase, containing the VNAs that usually occur at low concentrations, makes it possible to 
concentrate the sausage extracts. Furthermore, GC-MS/MS can be used for analysis of the VNAs 
if a verifying method is needed because the organic phase does not contain water. 
The conclusion of the precursor experiment for NDMA and NPYR is that there is a positive 
relation in the sausage and water samples between presence of nitrite with increasing 
precursor concentrations and formation of NDMA and NPYR. The remaining NAs measured in 
the precursor experiments does not have a common tendency when nitrite is present or when 
the precursor concentrations are increasing. Although NDBA shows a negative relation in both 
sausage and water samples between presence of nitrite and increasing precursor 
concentration. 
The increasing concentration of the precursors DMA and PYR results in formation of their 
corresponding NAs of NDMA and NPYR that is relatively higher compared to the remaining 
VNAs. The concentration of NDMA and NPYR is further increased when nitrite is present where 
the amount in the sausage samples with nitrite is approximately 1000-10,000 times higher than 
the sausage samples without nitrite present. However, when nitrite is not added, the formation 
of NDMA and NPYR is limited because of low concentrations of nitrite. The water samples show 
same tendency as the sausage samples for NPYR. The extra amount of formic acid added in the 
sausage samples contribute to the high amounts measured but may also illustrate what can 
happen in the stomach when digesting meat preserved with nitrite.

11 Recommendations for future studies 
The recommendations for future studies would be to optimize the SPE method for water and 
the digestive fluids that were supposed to be analyzed. There were many tests performed for 
SPE optimization in water, but none of them gave results that could be used for reporting how 
much NAs were retained in the SPE columns. Although the used column in this project was 
specifically shown to be effective with NAs in water, another SPE column could be tested. 
Additionally, to obtain consistency it would be recommended to utilize an automated SPE.  
In future studies, the QuEChERS method is recommended to be tested several times to obtain a 
robust method for analysis of both VNAs and NVNAs from meat extracts in LC-MS/MS where 
more validation parameters can be evaluated. Furthermore, GC-MS/MS could be used as a 
confirmation method for the VNAs because it is more fit for volatile analytes. It would be 
optimal to use IS for correction of losses during sample preparation plus for identification of 
the different NAs. Although there was not found any matrix effects, it should be noted that the 
outcome of NAs might vary from one meat product to another. Therefore, the validated method 
should also be tested for other meat products. 
The precursor experiments showed high formation of especially NDMA and NPYR which other 
studies had not found. Since, there was found an increase with nitrite present it could be 
interesting to vary the added amounts of nitrite to observe at what nitrite concentration there 
was an effect. Furthermore, the amount of acid added when extracting could also be varied to 
obtain an illustration of how much the acid contributes to the enhancement of NA formation.
