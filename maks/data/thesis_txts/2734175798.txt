Preface
This study is a master of science in engineering (30 ECTS) project written at DTU (Technical University of
Denmark) Wind and Energy Systems. The project collaborates with Siemens EA (Electrification and Auto-
mation) in Ballerup, Denmark. Major developments in the Danish power systems will affect the grid stability
and high-voltage protection systems. Power-electronics-based production requires analysis and evaluation
over broad frequency ranges using EMT (Electromagnetic Transient) simulations. DTU PowerLAB owns
modern RTDS (Real-time digital simulator) racks for real-time EMT simulations with physical relay inter-
facing. To propose optimized protection systems for renewable power systems projects, this study examines
wind farm responses to variable transmission line faults using the RTDS. The objective is to challenge WPP
(Wind Power Plant) control systems and observe which critical aspects could challenge two Siemens Siprotec
5 7SL87 line protection relays. Finally, a variant of the POTT scheme distance protection will be proposed.
The RTDS supports relay test setups using digital fiber communication based on IEC 61850-9-2. This study
will subsequently apply Process Bus to form a digital HIL (Hardware-in-the-loop) reference project. The
outcome is to gain a better insight into the challenges related to digital HIL and the general protection of
renewable power systems.

Abstract
Denmark will face significant power system changes, and large-scale renewable energy could challenge the
grid stability. VSCs (Voltage Source Converters) used in HVDC and wind turbines are often designed to
deliver a maximum of 1.1 pu current level. Conventional AC line protection relies on significant fault contri-
butions and could be challenged by weak grid conditions.
This project investigated the POTT (Permissive Overreaching Transfer Trip) scheme distance protection in
large-scale renewable power systems. An RTDS formed the foundation of modeling a simplified section of
the DK1 400 kV network as a reference grid. Furthermore, a 1 GW type-IV wind farm was introduced to
examine FRT (Fault Ride Through) behaviors. Two Siemens Siprotec 5 7SL87 line protection relays were
used to interface the RTDS in a digital HIL (Hardware-in-the-loop) arrangement. The WPP (Wind Power
Plant) model was suitable for fault studies and could inject positive or mixed positive and negative sequence
currents in FRT mode. Major findings were that POTT schemes are suitable for renewable power systems
and offer selectivity and backup protection. If the reference grid voltage is interrupted, grid-following VSCs
can not provide deterministic fault responses. Negative sequence injection was the preferred FRT mode, and
the WI (Weak Infeed Echo) addition to the POTT scheme was an essential implementation.
The work showed that robust renewable power systems require digital communication between substations
to localize and interrupt faults. While future substations will implement modern technologies such as IEC
61850, old stations may need to retrofit new designs in existing protection systems. Digital HIL worked
without any issues and formed an effective way of developing research studies with scalable protection use
cases.
These results contribute to a better understanding of considerations related to the protection of
renewable systems and RTDS testing in general.

Introduction
Denmark plans to serve as a large-scale renewable center for global energy trading [1]. The Danish power sys-
tem consists of two synchronous areas linked by HVDC (High-Voltage Direct Current). DK1 covers Jutland
and Funen, while DK2 covers Zealand. The DK1 400 kV network faces extensive developments, eventually
changing the grid conditions. Power-electronics-based production lacks the natural fault response and could
challenge existing protection systems. Generally, renewable protection studies call for advanced methods to
verify designs and reduce associated risks.
Digital HIL (Hardware-in-the-loop) using RTDS (Real-time digital simulator) forms a scalable way of evalu-
ating power systems while interfacing physical protection relays. This project will analyze POTT (Permissive
Overreaching Transfer Trip) schemes in DK1 under large-scale renewable conditions. The case studies will
subsequently evaluate the IEC 61850 capabilities of the RTDS to serve as a test-bed for even larger research
projects.
EMT (electromagnetic transient) simulations generate real-time responses compared to simple
phasor representations. While EMT is more computationally heavy, it could be required when analyzing
systems over broad frequency ranges [2].
The project will enlighten renewable power systems protection aspects and answer the following questions.
Are POTT schemes suitable for weak grid conditions? Is selectivity preserved in variable wind production
and under different fault types? Will a grid-following wind farm be challenged in FRT (Fault-ride-through)
if the reference waveforms are interrupted during faults? What is required to continue local production in
islanding conditions post faults? Subsequently, all these case studies imply that the IEC 61850-based digital
HIL is fully operational. The performance and user experience evaluation will conclude if Process Bus is
recommended for future projects. The project will propose a variant of the POTT scheme for renewable
conditions based on extensive analysis. The results discuss what could challenge the relays in general. The
concept throughout the project development is to consider automated and scalable solutions.
1.1
Renewable Power Systems
The transition towards renewable power systems will eventually solve multiple objectives. The primary tar-
get is to reduce the global CO2 impact and thus limit global warming. Furthermore, it is of general interest
to be independent of fossil fuels. The latest COP-28 meeting confirmed that countries may target individual
emission goals. However, most participating members are still obligated to satisfy the Paris Agreement [3].
Keeping the global temperature increase below 2 ◦C should be in everyone’s interest. The consequences
would have a broad impact if current changes continue. Some Middle Eastern regions could occasionally
become uninhabitable, and Kuwait City is expected to reach 60 ◦C by 2071 during peak events. Furthermore,
melting arctics will result in critical sea levels and threaten countries like the Netherlands [4]. Even countries

1.2
Danish Grid Development
Denmark is recognized as a maritime nation with a long history of utilizing our waters. Our coastline spans
over 8500 km [6], and our seas are known for being harsh. With average wind speeds around 9 m s–1 [7],
shallow water depths, and clay foundations, the North Sea has been selected as an optimal location for
large-scale wind energy harvest. New types of wind turbines [8] are designed to produce up to 15 MW. Such
ratings make it possible to utilize wind farms such as Thor and Baltic Eagle, rated around 1 GW [9]. In
comparison, a large conventional power plant such as Avedøre Power Plant is rated at 757 MW electrical
power [10].
Wind farms are generally considered great investments [11] and require significantly less maintenance than
combustion units. The Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen) has initiated screenings of potential wind
harvest areas up to 10 GW [12] based conditions such as minimal natural impact, limited visual disturbances,
and great power export opportunities. The preliminary research promotes sustainable energy development
and reduces potential investment risks. Furthermore, Energinet is currently looking into dedicated energy

The active power production is cubical dependent on the wind speed (V3) and could vary in turbulent areas.

1.3
Line Protection
DK1 is divided into multiple voltage levels based on the applications. Low-voltage distribution is 0.4 kV-
10 kV, medium-voltage transmission is 60 kV, and the high-voltage transmission lines utilize 150 kV to
400 kV.
Overhead lines and cables are exposed to potential faults due to natural events, human error,
or incorrect operation of the systems. To clear faults and hopefully avoid permanent damage, line protection
is a critical design component that automatically measures, computes, and isolates faults.
The extent of protection depends on the resulting power system impact. An uninterrupted 400 kV line fault
could cause significant blackouts and potentially split large power systems into multiple synchronous areas.
During a 2006 event in CESA (Continental European Synchronous Area), an interrupted 400 kV line almost
caused a total blackout in a large area network [16]. The fault severance is proportional to the clearance
time (I2t). Thus, rapid interruption could eventually be reconnected in a non-critical time frame. To achieve
high availability, only the faulted section should be isolated while the healthy parts of the power system can
remain connected. Selectivity and telecommunication between substations minimize the fault impact and
provide optimal post-fault conditions.
Conventional line protection relies on significant short circuit levels to localize the fault location. However,
converter outputs could challenge the existing protection systems by lacking a rotating machine response.
A solution could be to utilize 87L (Line Differential Protection) and effectively interrupt the faulted line.

1.3.2
POTT Schemes
POTT (Permissive Overreaching Transfer Trip) schemes enhance distance protection by establishing com-
munication between multiple 21 relays. The objective is increased selectivity and reduced operating time for
zone 2 faults. The functionality requires dedicated high-speed communication between two substations and
is often considered when fast clearance is required in large short-circuit levels. Furthermore, POTT schemes
make the protection robust if one relay is located at the end of a radial with limited fault injection. This
concept is further described in Section 1.3.3.
Fig. 6 illustrates how POTT sends information about the zone 2 fault pickup to the coordinated relay. If
both relays see the fault in zone 2, they may trip instantly. Regular zone 2 interruption of faults could take
up to ten AC cycles and can be limited by around 85 %. The inter-substation communication travels a few
milliseconds, being the only addition to the zone 1 operating time. The relay communication may utilize
PDI (Protection Data Interface) as dedicated fibers or utilize modern ethernet-based communication such
as IEC 61850-90-5 (R-GOOSE).

must be acknowledged in the design phase when offshore wind farms or small generators are located in radial
configurations.
1. The weak-end source may be temporarily out of service. Thus, the local relay will not operate during
line faults. When the generating unit continues production, current will be injected into an uncleared

2. The weak-end source may generate power, but the limited short circuit current will not represent a
fault. The relay may measure a healthy impedance outside pickup zones during faults.
3. If the fault is not picked up in zone 2 at the weak source relay, permissive tripping is not transmitted
to the strong side relay. Regular backup protection is applied, and POTT is eliminated.
An enhancement to the POTT scheme is called Weak Infeed Echo (WI) and effectively operates both relays
in all considerable conditions [17]. WI acknowledges the missing fault current contribution but relies on a
local voltage measurement. If the weak side voltage dips below a certain threshold, it may represent a fault.
Furthermore, no local zone pickups and receiving a POTT message will confirm the fault’s presence. If all
three requirements are satisfied, a POTT echo will be returned. As a result, the strong source relay will
operate in zone 2 without any operational delays.
A backward zone is defined to maintain selectivity because echo signals are transmitted in the lack of zone
pickups. Say a fault appears behind the weak source relay; the voltage level may dip, but the backward zone
will pick up and block the echo signal from clearing the healthy line. Fig. 7 illustrates a grid configuration
which will be tested in Section 5. The protected line will be subjected to various faults while the WPP

Park Transformation
The WPP model utilizes SPWM (Sine Pulse Width Modulation) by comparing ABC reference voltages to
a 2 kHz triangle carrier signal. The reference signals are computed in real-time based on the demanded
setpoints.
Fig. 10 illustrates the concept of applying the Clarke- and Park transformation at the ABC
system into single-phase and DC equivalents. PI controllers expect a converging input. Thus, sinusoidal
reference signals would not stabilize the outputs. Designing a closed-loop control system in the dq-domain al-
lows the computation of reference values for the Vd and Vq voltages, converting towards the power demands.

Hardware-in-the-Loop Protection using RTDS
Protection schemes are usually developed and evaluated upon station commissioning.
Omicron devices
are well-established in testing environments by safely representing instrument transformer waveforms [21].
Unfortunately, renewable conditions with large-scale power electronics, increasing THD (total harmonic dis-
tortion), and limited short-circuit power could challenge conventional testing applications. The consequence
could be that some faults are not picked up and cleared as intended.
Using EMT simulations, an RTDS can provide realistic waveforms and support a similar relay interfacing.
The grid models can include transients, harmonics, and a larger system representation in closed-loop feed-
back. This type of configuration is referred to as a HIL (Hardware-in-the-Loop) setup and is illustrated in
Fig. 12. The following steps describe the HIL arrangement:
1. The RTDS runs a real-time power system model and produces analog outputs of CT and VT waveforms.
Saturation curves and other details can be added if needed.
2. The low-level (±10 V) analog outputs interface an Omicron CMS amplifier which provides amplification
at suitable levels for the relay modules. Steady-state values could be calibrated to 1 A and 100 V in
Europe.
3. The relay compares the instantaneous analog values with protection settings and if fault conditions are
picked up, a dry contact will close.
4. A DC power supply will introduce a positive voltage of 5 V to the RTDS GTDI input card through
the relay dry contact. The positive voltage will be translated to an active bit in the simulation model,
ready for further processing.
5. Mapping the active bit to a CB (Circuit Breaker) model can interrupt the fault. Realistic CB models
can be added if needed.
The drawback of the conventional HIL setup is the limited scalability of the Omicron CMS amplifiers. Each
CMS 156 amplifier outputs four voltages and four currents at a price tag of around $10.000 USD per device
[22]. The significant investment and wiring complexity could limit large-scale hardware-in-the-loop setups.
Furthermore, the physical relays are usually prepared for a single set of instrument transformer wiring. Large
setups would thus require additional protection relays. Section 3 introduces a digital HIL setup that operates
without Omicron amplifiers and provides improved scalability and overall capabilities.

IEC 61850-based Digital Protection in RTDS
IEC 61850 defines communication protocols across substation vendors and provides a high level of device
interoperability. Relay manufacturers, testing device suppliers, and station monitoring applications are ex-
pected to comply with the IEC 61850 data model. The objective is to define all station devices in a common
SCL (Substation Configuration Language) file and effectively mix any vendors to satisfy protection require-
ments.
Communication protocols such as IEC 61850-8-1 MMS (Manufacturer Message Specification) and GOOSE
(Global Object-Oriented System Event) define methods to define monitoring and control applications. IEC
61850-9-2 PB (Process Bus) is a dedicated subset for publishing voltage and current instantaneous values.
MUs (Merging Units) are physical devices that directly interface with the instrument transformers and pub-
lish SMV (Sampled Measured Values) for remote protection subscriptions. Say, a centralized busbar relay
should protect a large set of feeders; all the instantaneous currents could be mapped via SMV to avoid
excessive copper wiring [23]. Overall, digital substations can enhance commissioning time, reduce the in-
strument transformer wiring, and provide galvanic isolation between devices.

3.1
Digital HIL Scalability
Each GTNETx2 interface card has two SFP (Small Form Factor Pluggable) modules and can be configured
independently using either the GOOSE or SMV protocols. Regarding HIL scalability, one SFP can output
24 channels of current or voltage when configured as SMV. A GTNETx2 card can thus represent 12 power
system feeders if both currents and voltages are published. When dedicating an SFP module to the GOOSE
protocol, a total of 2048 independent signals can be created and should not be the limiting factor. Fig. 15
illustrates a realistic model to implement in the RTDS. A single physical relay can have multiple logical
nodes (LN), each protecting independent lines, optimizing the cost and wiring. Large-scale applications can
be relevant in advanced research cases both in academic and industrial applications. The fact that a single
RTDS can represent 12 feeders confirms the scalability and overall opportunities related to digital HIL setups.
The SFP module supports 100/1000 copper, 100BASE-FX, or 1000BASE-SX cards to match various network
specifications. The equipment is preferably isolated using fiber communication. One single SMV stream
(IEC 61850-9-2LE 4800 Hz 1 ASDU) consumes 12.98 Mbit/s [27] and thus full size models requires gigabit
interfacing [28]. As illustrated in Fig. 15, VLAN 10 tags are used for busbar protection, and VLAN 20 tag
is used for multiple feeder protection functions. Section 3.2 will describe how VLANs effectively can group
the traffic in large substation networks.

3.2
Layer-2 Multicast Traffic
SMV and GOOSE operate at the link layer of the OSI model [29]. Utilizing Layer-2 enables low-latency,
deterministic communication in time-critical applications such as protection schemes and interlocking. The
layer-2 multicast traffic is identified by a MAC address syntax starting with an LSB (Least Significant Bit)
of 01 [30]. The default layer-2 operation is to route the traffic to all switch ports. Clients can listen to
these values in a publish/subscribe relation compared to the slower client/server relations. The drawback
of utilizing multicast applications is client overloading in large networks. An IED could subscribe to SMVs
from a single MU but would have to discard a bunch of unwanted traffic, taking up unnecessary bandwidth.
IEC 61850 suggests that the Process Bus and Station Bus networks are load-balanced using VLANs or
multicast filtering [31]. Separating a large physical network into smaller logical networks isolates the traffic
impact into individual device groups. IEC configuration tools such as Siemens System Configurator support
logical network separation by predicting the traffic based on the SCL file. Fig. 16 shows an example SCL file
including a dedicated virtual multicast address of 01-0C-CD-01-00-04. The unique MAC address is specific to
the GOOSE application and is used as a filtering argument. Alternatively, adding VLAN tags to the layer-2
frames allows an RTDS-based merging unit to be connected at a VLAN trunk port of the switch. The
trunk port looks at the incoming frame tags and forwards the messages to the correct VLANs as illustrated
in Fig. 17. Only relevant IEDs receive this traffic and can send confirmation messages based on the same
topology. In small-scale applications, multicast filtering is sufficient to optimize packet delays. In scalable
setups, VLANs provide a more effective way of designing the network flow directly in the SCL editor instead
of accessing the switch management interface for every new multicast application.

5.5
Automated Scripting
Identifying worst-case conditions in protection studies could require extensive parameter sweeps and hun-
dreds of measurements. Previous figures, including Fig. 35 and Fig. 44 were generated using manual adjust-
ments of around 100 combinations per plot. Python offers some level of bi-linear interpolation to generate
smoothing effects in the visuals. Although the previous results provided valuable insights, manual testing
is time-consuming and not very scalable. RTDS automation allows users to generate large data sets over
several hours, making the output data more consistent. Repeating manual operations introduces a variable
WPP start-up time. The PI-controller conditions could be different at the time of the fault and may affect
the FRT response. Performing a range of automated sequences would thus reduce the deviation uncertainties
by ensuring equal pre-fault steady-state conditions.
The scripting syntax is based on C and provides great flexibility. The built-in IDE offers some dedicated
functions, including a recording feature to track GUI interactions and translate to equivalent script macros.
Advanced use cases, such as loop iterations, array calculations, and writing the processed data to CSV
output, require some overall experience with the C syntax. Simulation data is treated as external arrays,
which can be used for further calculations, such as finding maximum values. Listing 1 in Appendix C.5
introduces a script developed to compute high-resolution data sets. The functionality can be described by
the following steps:
page 45 of 63
==PAGE:62==
5
Case Studies
1. The wind farm is initialized by respecting a 15 s startup period before reaching steady-state.
2. Two nested for-loops sweeps parameters in a desired range and resolution.
3. Fault conditions are triggered, and the operating time is measured as a floating point value.
4. The operating time is saved in a collection array, which is finally saved to a text file for post-processing
in other tools, such as Python or MATLAB.
5. The script releases the RTDS hardware to other users after finishing the simulations.

This case study constitutes a final evaluation of how well Revsing can enter islanding conditions after clearing
a line fault while keeping the WPP online. Although this does not directly focus on protection schemes, it
addresses how renewable power systems are met with multiple challenges and which grid aspects are essential
to address after clearing faults.
The WPP model is grid-following and offers no frequency control by default. Maintaining a constant fre-
quency in islanded conditions requires a controller to adjust the active power output. Fig. 51 illustrates the
concept of a general frequency controller. The integrated frequency error is added as a contribution to the
existing Id control loop. As a result, the WPP can regulate active power to stabilize the frequency [19].
A synchronous condenser is required to support the operation. Eq. (31) states the power systems swing
equation [39] and is used to dimension the synchronous condenser size. The objective is to isolate Revsing
from the remaining grid while preserving a local load. The system will face a sudden load change and a
resulting change in frequency. Using the values in Tab. 5, Eq. (35) finds a suitable inertia constant H of 10 s.
The circuit breaker maximum clearance time is 200 ms, and a maximum load change of 400 MW is selected.
Tab. 10 in Appendix C.6 highlights the suggested IEEE exciter parameters for synchronous condensers in
weak grids [40]. The exciter and the inertia constant are modeled in an aggregated 400 MVA synchronous
generator with zero mechanical torque.

6
Discussion
This section will discuss the project findings and how they contribute to a larger understanding of renewable
power systems and the general scalability of digital HIL setups using an RTDS. The discussion will account
for real-life developments of the DK1 power system and general aspects of upcoming grid developments.
Finally, future works will be introduced, indicating a suggested continuation of the project.
6.1
Case Study Findings
POTT-WI is a good match for 400 kV renewable power systems. The scheme includes backup protection and
the selectivity is preserved. However, if POTT should fail, zone 2 faults are not protected. These situations
could be solved by utilizing differential protection or temporarily unblocking voltage/current protection.
Alternatively, the relays could subscribe to external WPP trip signals when the PLL synchronization is lost.
However, protection systems will probably not rely on external vendor systems.
While Revsing was connected to Kassø, the distance protection worked as intended. The maximum deviation
was 0.4 km, and the average operating time was 19.40 ms. Increasing the fault resistance for single-phase
faults indicated a maximum deviation of 5.5 % and confirmed the reasoning for applying an overreaching
zone. When Revsing entered weak conditions, either positive- or mixed positive/negative sequence response
was suitable for FRT. The limited current contribution affected the relay performance, but many faults were
cleared as intended. However, some far-away faults revealed that grid-following WPPs are only partly suit-
able for FRT in distance protection. When the PLL lost synchronization, it was uncertain whether the relay
would trip or not. Analyzing the waveforms indicated that the POTT scheme is essential. Furthermore,
impedance oscillations could confuse the relays close to zone borders and increase operating times. This
turned out to be a challenging aspect of distance protection in renewable power systems but justifies that
POTT is a critical enhancement.
The wind speed and active power output affected the operating time. The WPP produced 1.1 pu reactive
current during any FRT event, but the radial location reduced the offshore bus voltage during line faults.
Thus, the absence of wind production made it challenging to provide significant reactive support. Say the
offshore voltage dips to 0.3 pu, and the DC link is either fed by the machine or the grid-side converter; it
makes it challenging to generate capacitive reactive power. WI is powerful by ensuring deterministic trip-
ping in all conditions and requires fewer contributions from wind farms. During low production, both relays
tripped, but only the strong side relay provided the fault location. If the strategy was applied to an offshore
cable between two wind farms, an equal lack of fault location could be critical considering repair operations.
However, at least WI was tested to preserve the selectivity during backward faults, which makes it more
secure than regular transfer trips or under-voltage protection.

The case studies justified that some schemes must be tested through a large set of combinations. At some
point, manual testing became challenging and unwanted. The automation script was effective in generating
high-resolution plots with minor user interactions. Fig. 49 on page 47 indicated that point-on-wave has little
effect on either two-phase or single-phase faults. Local variations confirmed that the instantaneous current
level affected the relay response, but a maximum operating time of 40 ms was sufficient to approve the pro-
tection. The individual combinations could be further analyzed by knowing the exact relay computations.
However, this information is not available publicly and is probably considered classified by Siemens. Overall,
the final transmission line protection is approved and could be fundamental to realistic implementations.
The protection was finally evaluated while clearing a line fault and continuing the WPP protection. The
maximum frequency was tracked to be 50.52 Hz at a load change of 400 MW. The Id controller worked as
intended and regulated the grid-side converter output to the changed conditions. The aggregated 400 MVA
synchronous condenser with H = 10 s provided the frequency and voltage stability and turned out to be a
suitable match for the wind farm. The controller gains were manually adjusted, and the suggested IEEE
exciter parameters [40] worked as intended. Fig. 54 visualizes the active power requirements for danish wind
farms [15] and the normal production area spans from 49 Hz to 51 Hz. The wide tolerance requirements
confirm that the inertia was sufficient. The blade pitch angle controller was designed by minimizing the
DC link chopper current and indirectly matching the local demand over time. However, the implementation
will not decrease the pitch angle in case of increased loads.
A more sophisticated controller could rely
on minimizing the DC chopper current as a primary action and then changing to a frequency-dependent
reference as a secondary action. A negative change of frequency would thus be translated to a negative
change of pitch reference. Overall, the islanded conditions were not completely satisfying and could only be
suitable for controlled shutdowns of local loads.

6.2
Scalability
The project aimed to provide a digital, automated, scalable solution for advanced relay testing using RTDS.
The outcome can be considered as a template for further research studies, including larger models and more
extensive digital HIL. IEC 61850 GOOSE and SMV were highly suitable for relay testing and faced no
issues during the case studies. Once the digital substation was successfully configured, further testing was
similar to conventional hardwired interfacing. Furthermore, the start-up sequence did not require dedicated
initialization per testing session. In this way, other users could modify the power system model and continue
testing without significant IEC 61850 experiences.
Digital HIL could reduce the chances of wiring failures and merge multiple protection applications in fewer
devices.
Siemens has released universal protection relays, referred to as Siprotec 5 7UX85, and states
”Leverage benefits in terms of investment and operation by always having precisely tailored functionality in
the device. Pay only for what you apply!” [42]. This statement indicates the fact that the hardware platform
is uniform, but the actual protection functionality is based on software configurations. Thus, using Process
Bus, a few universal relays could cover multiple protection zones, including line protection, transformer pro-
tection, and generator protection zones. Siemens states that the universal relays are intended to save OPEX
and CAPEX by optimizing the initial investment and operational costs. Although this refers to real power

systems projects, it could also be relevant for research institutes. Investing in the entire range of conventional
protection relays could be challenging for universities, and without proper guidance, some relays could be
less useful. Interfacing a single RTDS Novacor to a few Siemens Siprotec 5 7UX85 relays via Process Bus

The overall combination of digital HIL and automation is considered the optimal way of utilizing an RTDS

engineering post-graduation.
6.3
Wind Energy Stability
Energinet has succeeded over 50 % VRE (Variable Renewable Energy) generation, but a change of 1 m s–1
could change 500 MW in a 5 GW total production. Fortunately, large area networks will limit the frequency
response by having sufficient power reserves. An article from Energistyrelsen states ”The stability of the
power system and the generation adequacy should not be seen entirely as a product of the development of
the Danish power system alone, but also as the result of the Danish power grid being strongly connected to
neighboring countries. In brief, the many grid connections provide stability through inertia and frequency
stability via the AC interconnectors and opportunities for balancing across large land areas with different
generation mixes and sources” [44]. The consequence could be that the DK1 and DK2 power systems are
too reliant on neighboring networks to maintain operation. AC interconnectors serve as critical inertia and
frequency stability components, while HVDC links are the key to balancing supply and demand. Power
systems connections could be interrupted in case of political conflicts throughout the European continent.
In such emergencies, it is relevant to examine what could support the continuous operation of our local grids.
The project examined Revsing in weak grid conditions, including fault responses and islanding conditions.
The stability was regained with a system inertia of 4 GW s per 1 GW wind production. This ratio could
indicate the general stability guidelines.

6.5
Further Work
Upon finishing the case studies, it makes sense to consider further developments for future projects. Recall-
ing the grid model in Fig. 22 on page 24, the entire system is 400 kV. The initial design was based on no
previous experiences related to digital HIL and POTT. The overall concept was to simplify the model while
making it representative.
Introducing high-voltage transformers and generally utilizing multiple voltage
levels would be a reasonable enhancement. Furthermore, extending the scope to cover multiple wind farms
would demonstrate steady-state coordination and shared FRT contributions. Fig. 57 illustrates an expanded
digital HIL concept. Three wind farms are located offshore, sharing a common step-up transformer. Two
POTT schemes are included to analyze selectivity in additional conditions. Finally, HVDC, individual feeder
protection, and transformer differential protection are added. The entire system is within the Process Bus
limits of a single RTDS and should ideally not face any issues. Utilizing Siemens 7UX85 universal relays
would allow the combination of multiple logical relays per physical relay, as illustrated. This design is only
relevant for research cases with an RTDS, as redundancy and real physical locations are neglected. However,
it will limit the implementation costs and allow a large range of fault analyses. Three physical relays cover
ten feeders in this case. The HVDC connection is included to acknowledge the current developments of the
DK1 power system. Considering the upcoming wind farms located close to Thorsminde [9], DK1 could rep-
resent Idomlund, while the HVDC link represents Viking Link in Revsing using the new 400 kV connection.

7
Conclusion
7
Conclusion
The case studies concluded that Denmark is prepared for the extensive 400 kV grid developments using the
available technology. Power-electronics-based production makes it relevant to introduce advanced protec-
tion, including POTT schemes, and telecommunication in general. The WPP fault response affected the line
protection relay, and either positive or mixed positive and negative sequence FRT was suitable. Negative
sequence FRT was preferred, as it enabled more backup protection options. POTT was highly suitable for
weak power systems, and the WI enhancement made a key difference. Wind production appears to affect
the distance protection if WI is disabled, making it a critical component. Interrupting the grid side of the
line challenged the wind farm. Once the PLL synchronization was lost, the FRT response was uncertain.
However, by implementing a simple frequency controller with local spinning reserves, the wind farm could
continue some simple islanding operations after faults.
EMT simulations and digital HIL were effective ways of analyzing and optimizing protection systems in phys-
ical protection relays. The project increased knowledge and general concepts regarding POTT, especially
in renewable grids. The RTDS was confirmed to comply with IEC 61850, and the Process Bus interfacing
was very effective and seamless. The time synchronization remained stable throughout all testing sessions,
making the HIL work like a conventional hardwired alternative. EMT simulations revealed signal details
that phasor representations would lack and indicated the influence of individual power system components,
including resonance and transient responses. In general, renewable protection systems are highly frequency-
dependent and justify a strong link to RTDS studies. These case study results were not obtainable using
regular Omicron or digital twin alternatives.
Grid-following end nodes, including offshore wind farms and HVDC, are essential to analyze. The DK1
short circuit levels are expected to increase, which could make the suggested POTT-WI less relevant onshore.
However, the case studies introduced general ideas about protection designs between energy island nodes and
the onshore POC. Overall, the project is considered a success by investigating and analyzing the objectives
within the project period. The match between the automated testing and the IEC 61850 confirmed the idea
of providing a scalable test-bed reference RTDS project for research-level investigations while evaluating a
relevant use case.
