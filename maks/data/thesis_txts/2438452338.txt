Abstract
We have implemented two diﬀerent variants of the autoencoder, a class of neural network,
to reconstruct time-domain I/Q waveforms that have been modulated according to the LTE
downlink standard and propagated through the wireless communication channel. An autoen-
coder with a basic design was implemented ﬁrst, after which convolution layers were added
to make a convolutional autoencoder. This was done with the aim of using the autoencoders’
latent encodings of the waveforms to extract useful features relating to the channel impair-
ments imposed upon the individual waveforms, and thereby perform a form of unsupervised
channel characterization. To construct our dataset, we used the Matlab LTE Toolbox to
generate the waveforms, and a Matlab implementation of the state-of-the-art WINNER-II
channel model to simulate channel impairments according to 3 diﬀerent discrete channel sce-
narios. To evaluate whether the latent encodings contained information distinguishing the
diﬀerent channel scenarios from each other, we used the t-SNE algorithm. While our two im-
plemented autoencoder types were successful in encoding and reconstructing the waveforms
in our dataset to varying degrees, our results indicate no interesting channel characterization
within the latent encodings beyond trivial information about the waveforms’ amplitudes. Fi-
nally, we discuss possible reasons for this, and suggest an alternate approach to unsupervised
channel characterization using an LSTM network and simpler sequences of power measure-
ments as data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
It is diﬃcult to dispute the great importance of mobile telecommunication systems for the
whole of modern society. The ubiquity of today’s smartphones makes it easy to take for
granted the on-demand communication, information, and entertainment that they make
possible. The on-demand nature of mobile communication is due to the signal being able
to travel through a wireless channel, eliminating the need for a wired connection and thus
allowing for the portability of mobile phones without the user having to carry around a very
large spool of cable. However, this wireless communication channel is both a blessing and a
curse, the curse being its unconstrained nature, i.e. the arbitrarily complex conﬁgurations of
obstacles, stationary or mobile, that can occupy the space through which the signals prop-
agate. This complexity is only exacerbated by the interference caused by multiple users’
signals having to share the air interface with not only each other, but with every other elec-
tromagnetic wave traveling past. Channels such as copper and ﬁber optic cables are designed
by humans with relatively well-deﬁned physical characteristics. This is hardly the case for
the wireless channel, in which the signal travels from transmitter to receiver unbound by
any kind of wire or similar. In this case, the channel through which the signal is transmitted
is all of space, as electromagnetic waves can scatter, reﬂect, and refract in any number of
ways through the air while traveling from transmitter to receiver. In fact, there does not
even need to be a direct line-of-sight path for the signal to reach its destination because of
this. This ﬂexibility comes at the cost of a host of diﬀerent eﬀects that independently distort
and attenuate the signal. Some eﬀects like the free space path loss are more easily mod-
eled than eﬀects like signal interference patterns caused by multipath propagation through
constantly changing conﬁgurations of obstacles. The complicated and highly varying nature
of the channel characteristics makes it diﬃcult to model mathematically without making
signiﬁcant simpliﬁcations and assumptions about the state of the channel, such as assuming
1
==PAGE:12==
certain densities and kinds of obstacles, receiver velocities, etc. Yet despite the diﬃculty
of modeling the wireless channel, there is a deﬁnite necessity for increasingly sophisticated
descriptions of it. This necessity stems from the ever-increasing demand for network data
traﬃc capacity, as more and more users want on-demand access to data-heavy applications
like video streaming [1]. Further, the density of users is often highest in the most densely
populated areas, which unfortunately tend to be the environments with the highest densities
of obstacles like buildings and vehicles. To meet the increasing capacity demands, the data-
rate speciﬁcations for future 5G systems have been set high [2]. To meet these speciﬁcations,
a variety of novel technologies and techniques, such as multiple-input and multiple-output
(MIMO) transmission, smaller and more local base stations, and higher frequency bands,
are being introduced [3]. The success of such technologies is heavily dependent on a detailed
understanding, or model, of how the wireless channel and the obstacles within aﬀect signals
traveling through it.
1.1
Current State of Channel Modeling
Through the years, a wide range of so-called ”channel models” have been developed to
attempt to describe the wireless channel for an equally wide range of channel scenarios.
A scenario in the context of wireless channel modeling is a certain set of circumstances,
such as a certain type of environment, a certain range of receiver/transmitter velocities, a
certain density and type of obstacles, or a combination of these and other factors aﬀecting
the signal’s propagation. The diﬀerent scenarios deﬁned in a given channel model can be
arbitrarily speciﬁc, but are in practice usually restricted to a few broad and often binary
categories, such as indoor/outdoor, rural/urban, pedestrian/vehicle, and with/without line
of sight between transmitter and receiver. A channel model is typically made by deﬁning a
number of such scenarios, and then attempting to deﬁne how transmitted signals are aﬀected
in each of the deﬁned scenarios. A small number of diﬀerent scenarios considered and deﬁned
leads to a relatively simple channel model, yet such a channel model will have to group more
cases together than a model with a higher number of more speciﬁc scenarios deﬁned. Models
can also diﬀer in the level of randomness they incorporate. A model that always predicts the
same modiﬁcation of a signal within each deﬁned scenario is said to be deterministic, while
a model that uses random variables to introduce randomness into its predicted modiﬁcation
is said to be stochastic. Aside from randomness being a factor in channel modeling, some
channel models also have time-dependence, meaning that the way the channel modiﬁes a
signal changes with time beyond purely stochastic (yet independent) eﬀects. There have
been many attempts at modeling the wireless channel with diﬀerent levels of complexity; at
2
==PAGE:13==
the lower end are the simple path loss models like the seminal Okumura-Hata model [4], and
at the higher end are large-scale projects giving rise to models such as ”WINNER-II”, which
is stochastic, geometry-based, and time-dependent in nature [5]. The WINNER-II channel
model, which is itself based on the earlier WINNER model, has since been expanded upon by
the QuaDRiGa model [6]. The WINNER-II and QuaDRiGa models are relatively complex
in the number of scenarios and parameters they take into account, and can be considered to
be state of the art within channel modeling.
1.2
Project Motivation
Due to the recent, enormous increase in available computing power, deep learning, a subset
of machine learning, has now become mainstream. Deep learning algorithms, loosely inspired
by biological brains, excel at ”learning” alternate representations of data. The learning, or
training, process can either be supervised or unsupervised, with supervised learning requir-
ing manual labeling of the data and unsupervised learning requiring only the data without
any labeling. Deep learning algorithms are often used for so-called ”feature extraction”,
which involves extracting useful information about data from the alternate representations
learned by the deep learning algorithm. Combining the feature extraction and unsupervised
learning aspects results in a model that can take unlabeled data and potentially ﬁnd struc-
ture and information within it that would be extremely diﬃcult for a human to ﬁnd for
one reason or another. Considering the complex nature of how the wireless channel aﬀects
signals propagating through it, we wondered whether a suitable unsupervised deep learning
algorithm could extract features pertaining to speciﬁc channel conditions by training on sig-
nal waveforms received after having propagated through the channel. An investigation of
the literature revealed that machine learning had been applied in many areas of telecommu-
nication, but much of it focused on tasks related to self-organizing networks (SON) [7], with
signiﬁcantly less work so far having been done on channel modeling and signal waveforms
[8][9]. We decided in our own project to attempt to perform feature extraction with the
autoencoder neural network architecture, and to train it on time-domain I/Q waveforms
propagated through diﬀerent channel conditions. The general autoencoder model takes an
input vector, transforms it into a hidden representation, or ”latent encoding”, and uses this
latent encoding to attempt to reconstruct the input. Depending on the data and design
of the autoencoder, the autoencoder can be encouraged to encode input vectors into latent
encodings containing useful information about the input. An autoencoder trained on I/Q
waveforms could therefore potentially produce latent encodings containing interesting chan-
nel characteristics hidden within the shapes of the input waveforms. If an autoencoder can
3
==PAGE:14==
be trained to produce such latent encodings, then the latent encodings could serve to provide
a new parameter space for deﬁning channel conditions. Such a parameter space could prove
useful in characterizing channels in a more granular fashion than existing, scenario-based
channel models. A study of the literature showed that using autoencoders on I/Q wave-
forms was a relatively novel approach, with the foremost pioneer being Tim O’Shea, who
had successfully trained autoencoders on I/Q waveforms for a variety of telecommunication
applications [10][11]. We therefore decided that his I/Q waveform dataset constituted a good
starting point for our own work.
1.3
Project Objective
The main objective of this project is to train an autoencoder to perform unsupervised feature
extraction on I/Q waveforms propagated through a wireless channel. This project details
our work in attempting to achieve this objective by implementing 2 diﬀerent kinds of au-
toencoders, and constructing an I/Q waveform dataset with the WINNER-II channel model
to train them with the goal of producing latent encodings able to characterize the channel
conditions in some way.

Chapter 2
The LTE Downlink System
In this project we wish to characterize the wireless communication channel through unsuper-
vised learning. This chapter will introduce the concepts necessary to understand the nature
of this task, as well as the form of our data, which is complex-valued, time-domain I/Q
waveforms modulated according to the LTE downlink standard and propagated through a
wireless channel simulated by the WINNER-II channel model.
An overview of the general mobile communication system is given in section 2.1. In mobile
communication systems, the signals that are used to carry information are electromagnetic
waves, and the channel through which they propagate from transmitter to receiver is all the
space and obstacles in between. This channel is also referred to as the wireless channel,
as it diﬀerentiates from channels like coax and ﬁber optic cables, which are designed by
humans to best guide the signal from point to point. This marks a fundamental diﬀerence
between the wired and wireless channels, namely that the wireless channel is not and cannot
generally be designed with the purpose of carrying the signal, but the signal is most deﬁ-
nitely designed to best reach its intended destination somewhere within the wireless channel.
By the ”design” of a mobile signal, we primarily mean how information is modulated into
the signal in a variety of ways, and how multiple signals can successfully carry information
through the channel simultaneously through various multiple access schemes. The deﬁnition
of modulation and multiple access schemes is the subject of mobile communication stan-
dards. Such standards, often lumped into ”generations”, deﬁne a set of rules and deﬁnitions
that the various components compliant with a given standard must follow. The most recent
standard in widespread use is called LTE (Long-Term Evolution). In our project, we focus
on the speciﬁc standard of LTE downlink. This standard uses the quadrature amplitude
modulation (QAM) scheme and orthogonal frequency division multiple access (OFDMA)

as a multiple access scheme [12]. The LTE downlink standard is brieﬂy introduced in 2.2,
after which QAM and OFDMA are described in sections 2.2.1 and 2.2.2. The ”I/Q” rep-

waveforms comprising our dataset will have been explained from the transmitter point of

The wireless channel is a harsh and diﬃcult-to-predict environment for electromagnetic
signals to traverse. When detected by the receiver antenna, the signal will for a realistic
mobile communication scenario almost always have fallen in power by orders of magnitude.
The main types of ”channel impairments”, meaning the attenuating and distorting eﬀects
applied on the signals traversing a wireless channel, are described in section 2.3. The types
and severity of diﬀerent channel impairments on a mobile communication signal depend on
many factors, such as the paths the signal takes from transmitter to receiver, the receiver’s
velocity, transmitter/receiver antenna characteristics, etc. One state-of-the-art mathemat-
ical model for simulating channel impairments for a wide range of situations, or ”channel
scenarios”, is called the WINNER-II model. A detailed description of the WINNER-II chan-
nel model is beyond the scope of this report, but the general modeling approach is explained
in section 2.4. As stated, we use the WINNER-II model to simulate channel impairments
on I/Q waveforms, with the resulting, attenuated waveforms constituting our data. Finally,
the stage is set for our unsupervised channel characterization approach, as we attempt to
make an autoencoder (described in chapter 3) learn its own characterization of the channel

The general mobile communication system has a number of base stations, which are sta-
tionary, and mobile stations, which are mobile. The base stations and mobile stations send
information to each other, with the term ”downlink” being used to describe data sent from
base stations to mobile stations, and the term ”uplink” used for the reverse situation. The
base stations are the mobile stations’ access points to the rest of the mobile network and
potentially the ”core” network. Base stations are spaced out into ”cells”, typically so as to
be reachable by as many mobile stations in as many locations as possible, subject to the
budget of the network provider. The set of locations where mobile stations can communi-
cate with a base station is called the network’s coverage, the amount of information/data
being transferred at any given time is called data traﬃc, and the maximum amount of data
traﬃc that a mobile network can support is called its capacity. The higher the coverage and
capacity of a mobile network, the better. A diagram showing a mobile system with 2 base
stations and 3 mobile stations is shown in ﬁgure 2.1.
Mobile communication uses the wireless channel, and as previously mentioned, the signal
is the electromagnetic wave. Information can be put into electromagnetic waves through
the process of modulation, and can be retrieved through demodulation. Therefore, if an
electromagnetic wave can be retrieved unchanged by a receiver after traversing the wireless
channel, then whatever information was modulated into the signal can be retrieved error-free
8
==PAGE:19==
upon demodulation of the signal. However, channel impairments always change the signals
propagating through them in one way or another, which can cause errors in the information
retrieved through demodulation. The ratio of information lost depends on a combination
of how much the channel distorts the signal and how robust the used modulation scheme is
toward the distortion. Besides the channel itself aﬀecting the signal through the geometry
and composition of the obstacles within, there is also the issue of interference from other
electromagnetic waves. The electric and magnetic ﬁelds obey superposition, so any electro-
magnetic waves propagating through the same space as the signal of interest will add their
own contributions to the electric and magnetic ﬁeld amplitudes [13]. Such interfering waves
include everything from FM radio waves to visible light to x-rays, and the receiver must
be able to separate this noise from the signal. Luckily, the receivers in mobile systems are
antennas, and antennas’ geometries are generally sensitive to a speciﬁc frequency range, so
most of the electromagnetic spectrum is eﬀectively nulliﬁed by this frequency selectiveness
of the receiver. However, a bigger issue arises when multiple users, or mobile stations, want
to communicate at the same time while close enough to each other to signiﬁcantly interfere
with each other’s signals. This issue necessitates a so-called multiple access scheme, meaning
some scheme that allows multiple users to share the channel capacity, a simple example of
which is time-scheduling the transmission of diﬀerent transmitters’ signals so that they do
not overlap in time and therefore do not interfere. The concepts of modulation, multiple
access, and wireless channel fading eﬀects are ﬂeshed out a bit in the following sections.

2.2.1
Modulation in LTE Downlink
Modulation is the process of encoding information into an electromagnetic wave. In modern
mobile networks, data is represented digitally when arriving at the transmitter, meaning the
data is a sequence of binary values called bits. These bits could for example arrive at the
transmitter via some transmission line in the form of line code, which is a baseband, or low
frequency, signal. Line codes come in many formats, but one example could be a transmission
line with a voltage varying between two diﬀerent values. The data carried by the baseband
signal would then be given by the voltage of the transmission line, sampled with a speciﬁc
sampling frequency, and the voltage value of a given sample would indicate whether the
binary sequence is a 0 or 1 at that time. There are diﬀerent ways of encoding binary data
via line code, but the important thing is that the transmitter receives the bits, and must then
modulate these bits into a waveform that can be transmitted as an electromagnetic wave
through the wireless channel to the receiver. This modulated signal will consist of a carrier
wave, which is a waveform with a much higher frequency than the baseband signal, and this
carrier wave will have one or more of its physical attributes varied with time in order to
convey the information to be carried by the wave. The attributes that can be varied are the
amplitude, frequency, and phase. In order to best understand the modulation scheme used
in LTE downlink, not to mention the multiple access scheme and the format of the data in
our project, an understanding of the I/Q representation of waves is required.

In addition to a mobile communication system requiring a scheme for encoding informa-
tion onto a waveform for transmission, a scheme is also required to allow multiple users to
transmit and receive signals at the same time. If multiple users send their individual sig-
nals into the wireless channel at the same time and frequency, then they will most likely
interfere in such a way that it is not possible to demodulate and retrieve the information
within any of the signals. The wireless channel can essentially be thought of as containing
a limited amount of resources which can be used to send signals though it. These resources
are time, frequency, and space. If two signals overlap in all three, meaning they propagate
through the same point in space at the same time and at the same frequencies, then they
will interfere. A scheme for planning out the usage of the three resources in a way that
allows multiple users to use the wireless channel simultaneously is called a multiple access
scheme. There are many such schemes, but the one used in LTE downlink is orthogonal
frequency-division multiple access (OFDMA). OFDMA is based on OFDM, a multiplexing
13
==PAGE:24==
scheme used in many current and developing wireless communication standards, including
the next generation WLAN 802.11ax wireless standard, due to its eﬃcient splitting of the
available frequency bandwidth into a large number of narrowband ”subcarriers” [14]. An
illustration of the OFDMA scheme is shown in ﬁgure 2.3.
The OFDMA scheme starts by taking the input sequence of bits, parallelizes them, and
maps them into a series of symbols (constellation points) according to one of the 3 QAM
modulation schemes available in LTE downlink. A complex discrete inverse Fourier transform
is performed on the vector of symbols to produce an I/Q-representation composite baseband
signal. This operation is equivalent to individually modulating the QAM symbols onto a
series of so-called ”subcarriers” of narrow frequency bandwidths, which are separated in the
frequency domain. A transmitter that needs to send information in the OFDMA scheme
is allotted a number of subcarriers and time slots to transmit on, meaning that OFDMA
schedules resources both in terms of time and frequency.
2.3
The Wireless Channel and Fading Eﬀects
We have now covered how the signal in a mobile communication system looks like: an
electromagnetic carrier wave, modulated with both a modulation scheme for encoding data
and a multiple access scheme to allow multiple users to use the same wireless channel. We
shall now see how the channel itself modiﬁes, or fades, the signal in a number of diﬀerent
ways. A natural starting point for this is to introduce the concept of a link budget. A link
budget is a calculation of the power received by a receiver antenna as a function of the power
transmitted by a transmitter antenna and every modiﬁcation to the signal along the way.

a completely relative one, so in order to actually express the magnitude of the power of a

The path loss of a signal can also be increased by the interaction of the signal with diﬀer-
ent obstacles in the wireless channel. As previously mentioned, a signal does not generally
propagate uniformly in all directions, as the wave can be reﬂected, refracted, absorbed, and
diﬀracted at diﬀerent points in space. Often, the propagation of an electromagnetic wave
through a realistic wireless channel from one point in space to another is modeled as a series
of rays. These rays indicate the paths that diﬀerent portions of the waves travel, and they
are perpendicular to the wavefronts, since electromagnetic waves are transverse waves. If we
use rays to indicate the diﬀerent paths that the signal takes, we can more easily describe
the electromagnetic ﬁeld strength, and therefore the power of the signal, as a superposition
of diﬀerent parts of the wave taking diﬀerent paths through the wireless channel. These
diﬀerent parts are called multipath components, and the propagation of a signal from one
point to another through diﬀerent paths is called multipath propagation. The existence of
multipath propagation means that the signal will attenuate in diﬀerent ways and at diﬀerent
rates dependent on the speciﬁc path taken. This inserts some ambiguity into the meaning
of the term ”path loss”, and it is therefore used in many diﬀerent ways. Often, it is used to
mean some average, distance-dependent attenuation of the signal between the transmitter
and receiver in a certain scenario. The distance-dependence of the loss, which we recall from
equation 2.9 is equal to 2 for the free-space path loss, ranges from below 2 to well above
that for diﬀerent models of diﬀerent propagation scenarios. We shall deﬁne the path loss in
perhaps the most literal way, namely as the attenuation of a signal along one speciﬁc path
(i.e. the loss of the path), and if the signal’s power at a particular location is the result of the
superposition of multiple multipath components, then the resultant power has contributions
from each component, each with its individual path loss. As these individual path losses are
heavily dependent on how the waves reﬂect, refract, and diﬀract along the way, a uniform,
simply distance-dependent path loss model cannot be made with this deﬁnition. The usage
of this deﬁnition also makes path losses with distance exponents less than 2 nonsensical, as
such cases are only possible for scenarios where the channel’s geometry acts as a kind of
waveguide, such as in the case of a corridor, and the lower distance-dependent loss is in real-
ity a consequence of the superposition of multiple multipath components being guided. As
path loss is often taken to mean some average distance-dependent attenuation in a channel
scenario, large deviations from such averages are termed ”shadow fading”. The name makes
intuitive sense: some obstacle(s) block the main path(s) of the signal, leading to greater
attenuation of the signal. Shadow fading is often called ”slow fading”, as opposed to the
soon to be discussed ”multipath fading”, which is also called ”fast fading”. Shadow fading is
called slow, because it describes relatively slowly occuring phenomena such as the increased
attenuation of the signal reaching a UE when the person carrying the UE walks behind a

signal paths from the eNodeB to the UE. Multipath fading, discussed in the next section,
happens over much shorter time scales than shadow fading, which is why it is called fast

2.3.3
The Doppler Eﬀect
Aside from signal power gains/losses due to circuitry, antenna geometry and directionality,
path loss, shadowing, and multipath phenomena, it bears mentioning that the Doppler eﬀect
can cause a shift in the frequency of the received signal. Furthermore, this frequency shift
is path-speciﬁc, meaning that each individual path can have a unique frequency shift due
to the Doppler eﬀect.
This can lead to frequency-selective fading beyond that which is
due to frequency-dependent absorption by propagation media and frequency-dependencies
in for example the receiver antenna’s eﬀective area. The Doppler eﬀect may play a more
or less signiﬁcant role dependent on a range of factors such as carrier frequency, frequency
bandwidth, channel scenario (i.e. vehicular scenarios being worse due to higher mobile station
velocities), and how robust the modulation and multiple access schemes are toward Doppler
shifts. Given that we in this project deal mainly with low-velocity channel scenarios and
OFDMA, which is well-known for being resistant to the aforementioned frequency-selective
eﬀects, perhaps these do not warrant much further attention.
2.4
The WINNER-II Channel Model
Now that the wireless channel and the signal have been introduced, as well as the main chan-
nel impairments, the actual modeling of the channel can ﬁnally be discussed. In essence,
channel modeling is the mathematical modeling of how the wireless channel modiﬁes the
electromagnetic signal as it propagates from a transmitter to a receiver. This is a very broad
task, as there are virtually inﬁnite ways to arrange the transmitter, receiver, and obstacles
in the channel, not to mention the fact that all of these (except perhaps the base station
transceivers, for the most part) can be in motion. The frequency-dependence of several fad-
ing phenomena also adds to the complexity. Because of the broadness of the task, it is safe to
say that it is necessary to specify some aspects of the channel and thereby deﬁne a so-called
channel scenario, in order to be able to make any kind of model of the types and severities of
diﬀerent channel impairments. Such a scenario might be called something like ”rural hilly”
if the channel is relatively devoid of obstacles but prone to induce shadow fading due to
20
==PAGE:31==
rolling hills, or something like ”dense urban” if the obstacles within the channel are akin
to the densely spaced, tall building blocks of highly populated urban environments. Many
diﬀerent channel models of varying complexity have been developed through the years, but
the one that we use for generating the main dataset of our project is called WINNER-II.
First and foremost, the WINNER-II channel model is scenario-based.
This means that
it identiﬁes a list of discretely deﬁned typical propagation scenarios.
Some examples of
WINNER-II’s deﬁned scenarios include ”A1 - Indoor oﬃce”, ”B1 - Urban micro-cell”, ”B2 -
Bad Urban micro-cell”, and ”D1 - Rural macro-cell”. In addition to the scenario, the model
also distinguishes between line-of-sight (LOS) and non-line-of-sight (NLOS) conditions, with
some of the scenarios not allowing for either NLOS or LOS due to the nature of the sce-
nario. Our dataset uses 3 diﬀerent conﬁgurations: B1 LOS, B1 NLOS, and B2 NLOS. In
addition to the scenario, the model takes a long list of other parameters into account, like
UE location and velocity, antenna height, simulated building grids, signal angle of departure
and arrival, and more. The speciﬁcs are largely scenario-dependent. The ﬁnal output of the
WINNER-II model is a channel impulse response to be convolved with the transmitted wave-
form, producing the simulated received waveform. The simulation is done stochastically, i.e.
by sampling from diﬀerent probability distributions, which are based on empirical measure-
ment campaigns done in the WINNER and WINNER-II projects. The many parameters of
the model serve to deﬁne the distributions on which the simulated channel impairments are
based. Finally, the model allows for the simulation of sequences of time-dependent impulse
responses, like for a UE moving around the channel. Chapter 4 details speciﬁcally how we
have used the WINNER-II channel model to generate our dataset.

Chapter 3
Deep Learning
A central part of our project is the implementation and usage of the autoencoder variant
of neural networks, which in turn are a subclass of machine learning models. Our choice of
the autoencoder architecture is motivated by our goal of performing feature extraction on
complex waveforms that have been modiﬁed in some way by the wireless channel. Feature
extraction is the derivation of alternate representations of data to capture useful informa-
tion about it. The features that we wish to extract from our simulated waveforms would
ideally be able to separate the waveforms based upon not only the information modulated
into the waveforms, but also some useful information about how the wireless channel has
modiﬁed them. A successful feature extraction would in this case be tantamount to a form
of unsupervised channel characterization, which is the ultimate goal. The autoencoder has
a general architecture that attempts to achieve feature extraction by its very design. This
chapter will brieﬂy go through the main components of a neural network, and then specify
the general architecture of the autoencoder.

In order to train a NN, it is necessary to somehow deﬁne good and bad performance. This

suggests, can be thought of as the value or vector that the output should ideally be. The

trainable parameters in a NN can be changed so that the average output produces a lower
loss, its performance can thereby be improved according to the metric deﬁned by the loss
function. One example of a loss function which is used for many applications is the sum of

3.1.3
Prevention of Overﬁtting
When training a NN with a dataset of input vectors and corresponding target vectors, one
might encounter a phenomenon called ”overﬁtting”. This occurs when the NN’s parameters
are trained too speciﬁcally for the input data, performing well (i.e. producing low losses) for
those particular data samples but performing poorly for new data samples taken from the
same distribution but not trained on by the NN. This can be thought of as the model ﬁtting
to speciﬁc noise values in the dataset rather than capturing the underlying distribution that
generated the data. The degree to which a model overﬁts to the data trained upon can be
evaluated by splitting one’s data into two separate parts: a training dataset, and a validation
dataset. The training set will then be used for training the model in the way discussed in
section 3.1.2, while the validation set is only used to evaluate the loss at regular intervals
during the training process. The average loss of the samples in the validation set is called
the validation loss, while that over the training set is called the training loss. If the itera-
tive training process causes the training loss to fall as a function of the iteration number,
then the model is training. However, if the validation loss at the same time becomes higher
than the training loss, then the model is said to overﬁt to the training data. The degree of
overﬁtting is then dependent on the diﬀerence between the training and validation loss. In
practice, it may be diﬃcult to make the losses exactly equal, not least because the validation
set could contain higher noise values by pure chance, and therefore have samples that on
average deviate slightly more from their respective target vectors than the samples in the
training set. A NN with a validation loss a little higher in value than the training loss may
well have a suﬃcient predictive capability (the ability to approximate target values of unseen
data samples well) for whatever application the NN is designed for.
While the validation error is an eﬀective method of evaluating the degree of overﬁtting of a
NN, it does not prevent it. There are several methods of preventing it, one of which is the
introduction of a regularization term in the loss function, with the goal of mathematically
”discouraging” overﬁtting by making overﬁtting parameter conﬁgurations produce higher
losses than non-overﬁtting conﬁgurations. This regularization term can diﬀer depending on
how overﬁtting manifests itself in a given machine learning model, but one that is often used
is the ”l2” regularization error over the weight parameters of a model:

(neurons) and their connections in a NN at each training iteration. The probability p that a

of 0.25 in our implementations.
3.2
The Autoencoder Architecture
Now that we have described the main components of the more general layered NN, we can
specify the architecture of the autoencoder. The autoencoder is a NN that takes an input
vector x, and feeds it through two or more NN layers to output a vector of equal length to
the input vector. The goal of the autoencoder is to output a vector as close to the input as
possible, so the target value for the output vector o is the input vector x, and a natural loss
function is the l2 loss, given in equation 3.5, between o and x. The reconstruction of the
input based on the input may seem nonsensical at ﬁrst, but the true goal of the architecture
is feature extraction, meaning a set of derived values that contain useful information about
the input. In an autoencoder, these derived values are the activations of some layer within
the NN. We shall call this representation of the input as the activations of a hidden layer
the ”latent encoding” of the input vector. The aim is for this latent encoding to contain
information about the input that describe the input, but are of a fundamentally diﬀerent
nature than the input itself, as is implicit through the term ”derived” values. The function of
the autoencoder is thus to ”encode” the input into the latent encoding by passing the input
through one or more layers, and then decode this latent encoding back into an approximation
of the input vector by passage through one or more additional layers. The feeding forward of
the input into the latent encoding can be termed the encoding step, while passage through
28
==PAGE:39==
the remainder of the layers comprises the decoding step. If the length of the latent encoding
is lower than the length of the input, the autoencoder is called ”undercomplete”, and it is
called ”overcomplete” if the opposite is true.
3.2.1
Convolutional Layers
For some applications, the characteristics of the input data make it useful to incorporate so-
called ”convolutional layers” into the NN. A convolutional layer contains one or more ﬁlters,
which are vectors with some chosen shape. The convolutional layer then takes the input, and
outputs the result of the convolution between the input and each of the ﬁlters. This produces
approximately n × m output values for n inputs and m ﬁlters, the exact value depending on
the type of zero-padding used in the convolution. The trainable parameters in a convolutional
layer are the values in the ﬁlter vectors, and one would expect these to be trained to produce
ﬁlters that capture characteristic shapes in the data. We introduced convolutional layers
in our autoencoder implementation, as the input data consists of waveforms, which contain
a lot of sinusoidal shapes. Thus, convolutional layers could potentially extract some useful
information out of the waveforms by exploiting such patterns.
3.2.2
Feature Extraction
As previously mentioned, the goal of the project is to extract features, ideally containing
information about the channel characteristics, from simulated received waveforms modu-
lated according to the LTE downlink standard. Our chosen method for this is the usage
of the autoencoder NN architecture to extract a so-called latent encoding of the waveform
in an unsupervised manner. The usage of the term ”unsupervised” simply means that the
autoencoder is not given any kind of data about the channel that the waveform has tra-
verse (called the channel scenario), but instead tries to learn its own representations of the
waveforms that it can best use to reconstruct them. However, while the trained autoencoder
may or may not produce latent encodings that fulﬁll this purpose, there is no immediately
obvious way to visualize whether the latent encodings do indeed deﬁne some new ”scale”
on which to measure channel characteristics. This is because they are just vectors of val-
ues, and the autoencoder does not by design construct human-interpretable representations.
Thus, a method for evaluating whether there is useful structure in the latent encodings is
required. Two popular techniques for visualizing structure in data are principal component
analysis (PCA) and t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) [20]. Due to the
non-linearity inherent to neural networks, we choose t-SNE as a visualization tool, as t-SNE
separates data through a nonlinear dimensionality reduction, while PCA performs an or-
29
==PAGE:40==
thogonal linear transformation to a coordinate system that captures the most variance in
the data in the fewest possible dimensions. If the t-SNE visualization produces clustering
in the latent encodings of the waveforms, then that could suggest that the autoencoder has
learned some representation of the channel characteristics that it can extract from received
waveforms. If there is clustering in the latent encodings, and that this clustering correlates
with the diﬀerent channel scenarios used in the generation of the corresponding waveforms,
then that would suggest that the latent encodings characterize the channel in a similar way
to the model used to generate the data, at least partially. However, the interpretation of
features in data, especially those revealed by the t-SNE algorithm, is always a dangerous
game, and any clustering or lack thereof in a t-SNE plot should only ever be taken as an
indication of the presence or nonpresence of structure in the data.

Synthetic Data
In order to characterize the wireless channel, we need a dataset of samples that in some
way represent what the channel does to a wireless signal. From the start of the project,
we decided to look at time-domain waveforms that have traversed the wireless channel. We
decided to narrow our focus to a modern, widespread telecommunication standard, namely
the LTE downlink standard. Speciﬁcally, we chose to look at complex-valued I/Q waveforms,
modulated according to this standard, that had been received by a receiver after travers-

introduced too many diﬃculties and was unfeasible for our project. We therefore decided
to simulate such waveforms using a channel model generally considered to be state of the

WINNER-II model acts as a kind of yardstick for seeing whether our autoencoder’s latent
encodings contain features with a clear separation between quantiﬁably diﬀerent channel
conditions (with some caveats, as discussed in section 3.2.2). Furthermore, a practical moti-
vation for choosing the WINNER-II model instead of another state of the art channel model,
like the QuaDRiGa channel model for instance, was the availability of a reasonably well-
documented Matlab implementation, which we used together with Matlab’s LTE Toolbox to
generate the data.

• Symbol Modulation
First, the size of the LTE resource grid is determined based on the chosen amount

of information bits of the appropriate length is generated. We chose to use randomly
generated bit sequences rather than some predetermined sequence. We felt that both
approaches had merit, but had to choose one, and went with random bits as we thought
this ﬁt the ”unsupervised learning” approach better. All the complex-valued modula-

the eNodeB settings.
• OFDM Modulation
The resource grid is now modulated into the OFDM symbols, the namesake of the
OFDMA multiple access scheme. This is done by the application of the inverse discrete
fast fourier transform on the sequences of modulation symbols comprising each compos-
ite OFDM symbol. This operation brings the symbols into the time domain from the
frequency domain. The type of cyclic preﬁx speciﬁed by the eNodeB settings is inserted
between the OFDM symbols to produce the ﬁnal, time-domain, I/Q-representation Tx
waveform.
• WINNER-II Channel Model
The WINNER-II model produces an impulse response (called h in ﬁgure 4.1) which
simulates the channel by modifying the generated Tx waveform through a convolution.
A new impulse response h is generated stochastically each time the model is invoked,
based on a number of parameters speciﬁed and contained within two main settings
structures.
The ﬁrst settings structure is called ”cfgModel”, and contains settings about the model
features, for instance whether pathloss or shadow fading are activated or not. It also
contains the sampling density (meaning the time resolution of the modeling) and the
number of time samples used to produce the ﬁnal impulse response h. These settings
are globally set once, and used for all the created h’s in our implementation.
The second setting structure is called ”cfgLayout”, and contains settings of the speciﬁc
execution of the model.
Here, the number and positions of the eNodeB’s and the
UE’s are speciﬁed, as well as UE velocity vectors. In our implementation, the single
eNodeB is always placed in the same grid position, but the single UE’s position and
velocity are randomly chosen by the WINNER model, with a speciﬁed constraint on the
maximum distance from the center of the grid. The cfgLayout structure also contains
the propagation condition (line-of-sight (LOS) or non-line-of-sight (NLOS)) and the
WINNER-II channel scenario (B1 and B2 for our dataset). Finally, the antenna layout
of the eNodeB and the UE are also speciﬁed.
• Convolution
After the Tx waveform and the channel impulse response have been generated, they
are convolved together to produce an Rx waveform. Before this convolution can be
performed, the channel impulse response is resampled to match the sample rate of the
34
==PAGE:45==
Tx waveform (as the impulse response generated by the WINNER-II model need not
have the same time resolution as the waveform sample rate). After resampling, the
convolution is carried out.
• OFDM Symbol Cut
As discussed in section 4.2, we decided to cut out individual OFDM symbols from
the Rx waveforms to shorten the waveforms in our dataset.
The cut positions are
calculated based on the resource grid dimensions and the cyclic preﬁx used. After
cutting, we are left with the ﬁnal Rx OFDM I/Q waveforms to be put in our dataset.

5.1
Initial Trials
At the outset of the project, we had no experience with autoencoder architectures, and the
application of machine learning techniques on complex-valued waveforms. This meant that
we had a series of concrete decisions to make in order to realize our goal of implementing an
autoencoder for usage on the time-domain, complex I/Q samples of our chosen domain.
The ﬁrst decision was which machine learning framework to use.
A natural choice was
Matlab’s machine learning toolbox, as this has a ready-made autoencoder implementation,
is easy to use relative to many other ML frameworks, and ﬁts nicely with the data-generation
part of the project, as we had set our sights on the Matlab implementation of the WINNER-
II model as a means of generating our I/Q waveforms. However, after some initial trials with
Matlab’s autoencoder implementation, we quickly concluded that we wanted more ﬂexibility
in the deﬁnition of the architecture. We settled on open-source Tensorﬂow library as our
chosen framework, due to the availability of a python API for it, the ﬂexibility in architecture
deﬁnition provided, and the relatively large amount of documentation available due to its
widespread usage.
After deciding on the framework, we needed some initial I/Q waveform data to test the
whole concept of training an autoencoder to encode and decode complex time-series wave-
forms. When making our initial investigations into the existing work in this area, we found
the work of Tim O’Shea, who had used autoencoder architectures to do such things as synthe-
sizing new physical layer modulation and coding schemes for communications systems [22].
The dataset that he used in some of his work, the so-called ”radioML” dataset discussed
39
==PAGE:50==
in section 4.1, was the one that we decided to use in our initial testing of the autoencoder
architecture.
Finally, we needed to decide how to feed complex-valued data into our autoencoder, since
there is no support for this data type in Tensorﬂow. We decided to split the imaginary and
real parts of each time sample in our input vectors into two separate, real values. We then
interleaved the real and imaginary values, resulting in an input vector twice as long as the
original complex one.

5.3
Implementation
This section details the implementation of our ﬁrst autoencoder by going through the func-
tionality of the relevant parts of the code, step by step.
5.3.1
Data Import and Preparation
The I/Q waveform data is imported as a numpy array.
This data is three dimensional.
The ﬁrst dimension in the array is used for separating diﬀerent waveforms, or ”samples”
(meaning whole I/Q waveforms, not individual time samples within these).
The second
dimension separates the imaginary values from the real ones. The third separates the time
samples. Thus, the data is of shape
(# of waveforms) × 2 × (# of time samples per waveform)
which is (2000, 2, 128) for our radioML dataset with BPSK modulation, which contains
and 1000 waveforms with an SNR value of 0, and 1000 with an SNR of 18 (in dB). For the
WINNER-II dataset that we generated, the dimensions are (40000, 2, 179). Here, we have
10000 waveforms with two diﬀerent scenarios (3 and 4) and LOS (line of sight) and NLOS
for each scenario. The waveforms are a bit longer too, at 179 time samples.
After the data array is imported, it is normalized to be in the range between -1 and 1.
This is done by ﬁnding the maximum absolute value amplitude in the entire data set, and
then dividing every value in the data set by this number. Next, a certain ratio of the data
is set aside as the validation set (for ensuring that the model does not just overﬁt to the
training data), while the remainder is used as the training set. The ratio of data that we
used for all of our training runs was 0.2, meaning 20% of the data was never trained on, but
only used for calculating a validation set loss as a function of the training iteration in the
training process. Additionally, the training data is given a random shuﬄe after importation,
but the validation set is kept in its original order. The reason for the validation set not
being shuﬄed is so that diﬀerent models and training runs can more easily compared, due
to speciﬁcs with result exports. In essence, the location of a waveform in the validation set
acts as an identiﬁer for that speciﬁc waveform across diﬀerent runs.
Finally, some data manipulation speciﬁc to Tensorﬂow’s input pipeline is done. This in-
cludes constructing Tensorﬂow ”Dataset” objects, and making iterators for these objects.
The iterators are simply used at every training iteration to extract the next data batch to
be trained on. A data batch is a grouping of a number of data samples into one ”batch”,
41
==PAGE:52==
which is then trained on. Thus, the calculations in the training algorithm are done on all
of the samples in a batch in the same training iteration, and the optimization of the algo-
rithms parameters (i.e. learning) is done as an average of the contribution from all of the
data samples in the batch. In the construction of the Dataset objects, some parameters are
deﬁned: batch size, shuﬄe count, repetition count, and prefetch. Shuﬄe count is how many
data samples are lumped into each data batch. Repetition count is how many times the
data is repeated before ending. For example, if the repetition count is 2, then the Dataset’s
iterator will pull batches from it until the end of the data is reached, after which it will
start from the beginning of the data again, and ﬁnally stop once it reaches the end of the
data for the second time. The shuﬄe count indicates how many of the next data samples
the iterator can pull new data from for the next batch. Prefetch is a value indicating how
many data samples should be prepared in memory at any time, and can be used to try to
make the input pipeline a bit faster. Shuﬄing, repetition, batching, and prefetching are in
practice implemented as method on the Dataset objects. For the repetition of the data, we
use the method repeat() without a repetition count argument, which makes the data repeat
indeﬁnitely. Batch size is generally chosen as a multiple of 2, as this is often optimal for
computer architectures. As for shuﬄing, this is used mainly to prevent periodic patterns in
each epoch (one loop through the data) of the training process. In practice, it is diﬃcult to
determine optimal values for shuﬄe count, batch size, and prefetch. We use a shuﬄe count
equal to the number of training data samples, a batch size of 32, and a prefetch value of 8
times the batch size.
5.3.2
Dense Layers
After the data has been imported and manipulated in preparation for being used as input
to the machine learning model, the actual architecture of the model can be deﬁned. For the
simple autoencoder, the architecture is quite straight forward. Each data sample in a data
batch is ﬁrst ﬂattened. This means that the sample is transformed from its 2-dimensional
shape (I/Q-separating dimension and time dimension) to a 1-dimensional vector (with I/Q
values joined into one ﬂat vector). This is necessary for the data to be input into a fully-
connected, or dense, layer. Such a layer ﬁrst takes a vector of length n, performs a matrix
multiplication between the vector and an m-by-n weight matrix W, yielding an new vector
of length m. A vector of m bias values B is added to the vector, and the resulting vector
is input into an activation function. This activation function is applied element-wise to the
vector. The layer described here is considered to have an input size of n, and m ”neurons”, or
outputs. The values of the neurons for a given input are also called the layer’s ”activations”
42
==PAGE:53==
for that input. The parameters that are optimized are the weights in the W matrix and the
biases in the B vector.
For the simple autoencoder architecture, two dense layers are used. One layer takes the
n-length input vectors and turns it into a vector of m, i.e. the activations of an n-to-m dense
layer. We call this m-length vector of activations the ”latent encoding” of the given n-length
input vector. This latent encoding is used as input to the second and ﬁnal dense layer, which
has n neurons, the same number as the length of the ﬂattened input vector. A ”reshape”
operation then changes the dimensions back into 2 × # of time samples per waveform. The
autoencoder thus needs to learn weight matrices and bias vectors that make this output as
close to the input as possible, according to the loss function.
5.3.3
Loss Function
The loss function, the one that makes training and learning possible, is now deﬁned. Given
the fact that we seek to make an autoencoder, which needs to learn how to reconstruct the
original input, we deﬁne the loss function as the mean squared error between the output,
which was just described in the previous section, and the input. The mean-squared error
takes the input and the output, calculated the squared diﬀerence element-wise, and takes
the mean of this value. Diﬀerent regularization terms can be added to this loss, for various
reasons, such as ”l1” or ”l2” loss terms to encourage smaller weights, more sparse activations,
etc. Such things are often done to prevent overﬁtting, which is often accompanied by weights
with very large magnitudes. We experimented with this in our implementation, but ended up
just using a technique called ”drop-out”, which we found to be suﬃcient to prevent serious
overﬁtting to the training data. The usage of the drop-out technique on a tensor involves
setting a portion of the values in the tensor to 0 and scaling the rest of the values up so that
the expected value of the sum of the tensor’s values is not changed. We use a drop-out value
of 0.25 on the outputs of our dense layers when training. This means that the outputs from
the dense layers have 25% of their values set to 0 during training iterations.
5.3.4
Training
The training of the neural network involves iteratively changing the trainable parameters
according to the gradient of the loss function with respect to the parameters. Tensorﬂow
implements a variety of so-called optimizers, which each have their methods and algorithms
for performing these calculating how much to change each parameter at each training step.
We decided to use the Adam optimizer, brieﬂy discussed in section 3.1.2, which we found to
43
==PAGE:54==
train much faster than any of the other optimizers that we tried, such as the ”RMSProp”
optimizer. The Adam optimizer, like the others, takes a ”learning rate” parameter, scales
how much the optimizer changes the trainable parameters per training step. We used the
default value for the learning rate (0.001), as well as the other parameters speciﬁc to the
Adam optimizer.
Now, in order to actually train the model with the imported data, a so-called ”session”
needs to be started. This is a Tensorﬂow-speciﬁc thing. Everything described described
about our implementation so far is deﬁned before this session is started, and they make up a
so-called computational graph, which is made up of a series of interconnected operations and
data pipelines making up the architecture of the model. The session can then be used to run
whatever part of the graph that one desires. We therefore start the session after the graph
deﬁnition, and then set up a training loop, which runs for a certain number of iterations.
In the graph, we have deﬁned a ”training step” operation, which is given by the optimizer’s
”minimize” method on the loss. The loss is deﬁned earlier in the graph, and this in turn is
dependent on the output of the second dense layer. This is deﬁned even earlier in the graph,
and has it’s own dependencies. This backtracing of dependencies, right back to the data
manipulation operations, is taken care of by the Tensorﬂow framework if the training step
operation is run in a session. We run this training step operation at the beginning of every
iteration of our training loop, and the model thereby learns (if properly designed, that is).
Due to the fact that the operations in the graph need to be deﬁned before the session is run,
we need to use Tensorﬂow ”placeholders” for some of the variables in the graph, which are
then fed with actual values in the session whenever an operation dependent on the place-
holders is run. While usage of these placeholders is a signiﬁcant implementation challenge,
the speciﬁcs are hardly necessary to understand the essence of our implementation.
5.3.5
Result Visualization and Data Export
A signiﬁcant part of successfully training the autoencoder was the ability to see visualize
the outputs from the model. Due to the length of time used to train a model for a large
number of training iterations, it was useful to visualize results while the training was taking
place, and not just at the end. For this, we used Tensorboard, Tensorﬂow’s ”visualization
suite”. Tensorboard allows for the visualization of results such as training and validation
loss, input and output waveforms, etc., as a function of training iteration number. However,
using it requires the deﬁnition of ”summary” operations throughout the graph, and these
summary operations need to be run within a session to produce their output for visualization
44
==PAGE:55==
in Tensorboard. We generated summaries every 100th training iteration. While such visu-
alization techniques do not have any eﬀect on the actual performance of the model (besides
making the training slower due to summary generation), much of our code, especially in the
training loop, has to do with summaries. However, these summaries output data in a special,
aggregated format speciﬁcally for the Tensorboard environment, and there was no easy way
of extracting the data from this format. Therefore, exporting results for other uses, such as
graphing validation and training error outside of just Tensorboard, required yet more code
in the loop.

7.2
Discussion
We started this project with a goal in mind: unsupervised channel characterization. Quite
early in the project, we decided to use autoencoder neural network architectures for feature
extraction, and focus solely on time-domain I/Q waveforms as our data. These choices may
not have been the best ones to achieve our goal, but they nonetheless guided the rest of the
project, right up to the results discussed in the previous section. The biggest weakness in our
methodology was our choice of data format, which in retrospect seems less than ideal for our
purposes for various reasons. Section 7.2.1 discusses limitations and challenges with our data
format, and suggests what other kinds of data might lead to better results. Section 7.2.2
discusses potential improvements in the autoencoder architecture/design. Finally, section
7.2.3 discusses alternative approaches to unsupervised channel characterization that do not
make use of autoencoders at all.
7.2.1
Data Format Issues
Upon reﬂection, there are several issues with our WINNER-II dataset that might make it
unsuitable for channel characterization with autoencoders, the main ones being:
• Length and contents of the vectors
The length of the complex time-domain waveforms we have dealt with in this project
make them diﬃcult to apply neural network techniques on. Our initial data generated
with the WINNER-II Matlab implementation and LTE Toolbox were around 8,000
complex time samples long, and were very diﬃcult to work with due not only to
the diﬃculty of training an autoencoder to reconstruct them, but also because of
prohibitively large ﬁle sizes for relatively small datasets. Additionally, complex values
are not a data type generally supported by machine learning frameworks, and needed
to be split into two separate values, further doubling the vector sizes. Realistic I/Q
vectors in the LTE downlink standard are also not very ”neat” in shape, and are thus
diﬃcult to visualize eﬀectively when vector lengths reach the hundreds, not to mention
thousands. Such lack of neatness in the vectors is of course because they are designed
to carry information, not look pretty. This information contents was yet another issue,
as an autoencoder’s latent encoding would have to somehow take all the possible bit
sequence permutations into account in order to produce a good reconstruction for every
possible waveform. This wastes ”capacity” in the latent encoding, as the information
contents of the waves are really not the most interesting thing for our purposes; the
channel propagation conditions are. This particular issue may have been mitigated if
64
==PAGE:75==
we had used a constant bit sequence to modulate our waveforms instead of randomly
generated ones. However, as mentioned in section 4.2.1, we made the contrary decision
quite early due to concerns that predetermined bit sequences would make our data less
realistic, and perhaps make our methodology less ”unsupervised”.
• Narrow time and frequency range
Due to the data issues just mentioned, we had to reduce the number of LTE subcarriers
down to the minimum allowed in order to achieve any kind of reasonable reconstruction
of the waveforms with our autoencoders. However, this reduction was not enough, so
we cut out individual OFDM symbols from the LTE resource grids, as explained in
section 4.2, which ﬁnally gave us workable waveforms. These were practical decisions
made in order to get any kind of autoencoder results at all.
However, the single
resource grid already occupies a very short time frame (0.5 ms) compared to the time
it would take for the receiver to move the distance required for both multipath and
shadow fading to change the signal amplitude signiﬁcantly. This is especially true for
our chosen urban WINNER-II scenarios, which use relatively low simulated receiver
velocities (0-70 km/h). Because of this relatively short time frame, it is perhaps no
wonder that our latent encodings are only separable in overall amplitude.
• Synthetic nature
We used the WINNER-II channel model because we wanted good reproducibility and
control of it, not to mention avoiding the diﬃculties of making some kind of signal
measurement campaign resulting in clean enough data to use for machine learning. At
the beginning of the project, we considered WINNER-II a way of generating suﬃciently
realistic data, but one can imagine many beneﬁts of using measured data. Not only is
it more realistic, but there are no dangerous quirks and simpliﬁcations to worry about
which might make WINNER-II data unsuitable for unsupervised channel characteri-
zation, at least in the way we attempted to do it. For one, the WINNER-II channel
model applies the same channel impulse response over an entire simulated resource
grid, which may very well eliminate many fading phenomena entirely, as this makes
the entire resource grid shorter in time than the channel coherence time per deﬁnition.
Thus, in hindsight, the result that the t-SNE could seemingly only separate the latent
encodings in amplitude may have been entirely predictable from the start to an expert.
However, it is still diﬃcult to say whether something more useful could possibly have
been learned about the channel conditions by an autoencoder of superior design.
65
==PAGE:76==
7.2.2
Autoencoder Architecture
The autoencoder’s very design makes it suitable for many feature extraction tasks, as the
latent encoding within it must contain useful information about the input in order to re-
construct it. However, a danger of using autoencoders is their propensity for trivial latent
encodings, like for example a one-to-one mapping of the input to the latent encoding if the
latent encoding length is equal to the input and free of any additional constraints. Due to
the implementation challenges of data generation, usage of the Tensorﬂow framework, and
using a relatively novel and diﬃcult type of data (i.e. I/Q waveforms), we inevitably focused
less on trying to ”overcomplicate” our model, and more on getting it to train on our data
whatsoever. However, better results may have been achieved with additional constraints
placed on the latent encodings, such as increased ”sparsity” by introduction of an l1 regu-
larization term on the latent encoding activations. Such a regularization term can penalize
latent encodings with average neuron activations deviating from a chosen sparsity parameter,
thereby encouraging non-trivial latent encodings [23]. Our only proper method of encourag-
ing the autoencoder to ﬁnd correlations and structure in the data was simply dimensionality
reduction through shortening of the latent encoding length. However, a sparsity constraint
would have enabled an overcomplete latent encoding (i.e. longer than the input length) while
still avoiding the trivial 1-to-1 type mapping from being trained. In addition to introducing
sparsity in this way, there are a number of other autoencoder architectures that one could
have tried which may have ﬁt the application better, but if we were to start from square one
with a new approach for unsupervised channel modeling, the autoencoder would not be our
model of choice.
7.2.3
Suggested Approach for Future Work
If starting the project from scratch with commitments yet made with regards to data format
and unsupervised machine learning model, we would most likely have made fundamental
changes to both. For the data format, a much simpler power measurement would be prefer-
able. This power measurement could either be for the entire bandwidth or per subcarrier,
and span one or multiple OFDM symbols in time. Regardless of the exact choice, the data
would undoubtedly be neater than I/Q waveforms in time domain, and contain more direct
information about the channel impairments. Given the right measurement equipment, one
could then relatively easily gather long sequences of power data by walking around with the
equipment, occasionally changing setting by for example going inside a building or out into
an open area. Such sequences could then be fed into an LSTM network, which is a class
of recurrent neural network of increasing popularity. Such a network can be fed with data
66
==PAGE:77==
sequences of arbitrary length, and contains a hidden state to describe the current state of the
system producing the given input sequence. In order to train the LSTM network to make
useful hidden states based on power measurement sequences fed to it, it could for example
at each time step predict something about how the power of the signal will evolve in the
near future based on input values going back a certain amount of steps. The reason for the
somewhat ambiguous description of the LSTM variant of NN is because they have many
components and great ﬂexibility. We believe that this kind of NN could learn to encode
useful information about channel characteristics in its hidden state, especially if fed with
realistic power data from real UE’s. For instance, a sequence of power values that suddenly
falls sharply and stays low could indicate that the UE has entered a building, while a slowly
varying power sequence could put the LSTM into a state more indicative of someone walk-
ing around between buildings or trees. These are just general ideas, with the details of an
actual implementation having to be ﬂeshed out much more, but we truly believe that this
approach is worth looking at if a better, unsupervised form of channel characterization is to
be achieved in the future. Interestingly, we subsequently found a brand-new article detailing
the implementation of an LSTM-based approach for predicting channel state information,
which is conceptually very similar to our own suggestion [24].

Chapter 8
Conclusion
As evidenced by our results, we did not have great success in our goal of unsupervised
channel characterization through feature extraction from autoencoder latent encodings of
time-domain I/Q waveforms. While the concept seemed promising at the beginning of the
project, more and more issues with our chosen data format and general methodology started
to emerge. We did our best to follow through with the idea, making some practical decisions
like generating synthetic data, shortening our waveforms as much as possible, and more in
order to at least successfully train the autoencoders to be able to encode and reconstruct
waveforms of the chosen format. We produced a few diﬀerent conﬁgurations of the autoen-
coder and compared their performance both in terms of waveform reconstruction and channel
characterization. While reconstruction was possible, and some interesting convolutional ﬁl-
ter shapes were observed in the process, no interesting or nontrivial channel characterization
result came out of the latent encodings. Towards the end of the project, we had gathered a
signiﬁcantly greater amount of domain knowledge and machine learning experience than at
the outset, but had made too many commitments to our chosen approach to simply start
anew. However, in the ﬁnal model evaluation discussion we have attempted to identify some
of the main problems and suggested a diﬀerent path for future work in the area of unsuper-
vised channel characterization that we think would both be far more eﬀective and avoid the
major issues present in our own work.
