There are two sinks for N2O that is produced in the atmosphere, either by
chemical reaction in the upper atmosphere, or by microorganisms. Due to this
lack of reactions, N2O is a long-lived greenhouse gas with a half-life of over 110
years. In order for accurate and effective budgets and mitigation strategies
to be made, a greater understanding of the communities that produce and
consume N2O is required. Therefore a comparison between different available
conditions is needed, as enriching these communities in laboratory conditions is
a significant obstacle to understanding them and their role in the environment.
It was theorised that using a inhibitor to denitrification might reduce the
competition for these microbes, however two different media procedures was
also considered worth investigating. This thesis found that using Clorite and
Azide inhibitors at 10µM, in any combination, reduced the concentration of
nearly all tested genes (with one exception, the nirS gene from an enrichment
that used a stagnant ponds sediment). However, the use of these inhibitors
also on average increased the ratios of the norZI and norZII genes to the Total
Bacteria. No uniform differences were identified due to using one or the other
media, and no significant differences were found between which combination
of the two inhibitors was used.

Introduction, Background, and State of the
art
1.1
Background
The preindustrial levels of N2O for the majority of a million years has been
between 200 and 300 ppb. However, since the industrial revolution the level
has been increasing more year on year, from the preindustrial 270ppb to
today’s 335ppb.
Microbes represent the sole natural source of fixing stable atmospheric
nitrogen (N2) into organic N needed for biological life [4]. Similarly, microbes
are one of the only two sinks for taking N2O back to N2, the other being
reactions in the high atmosphere. Due to the stability of N2O, it has a half-life
in the environment of around 110 years. This places microbes at the heart
of both natural N2O production and consumption, as well as anthropogenic
climate change and the greenhouse effect.
The largest contributors to anthropogenic N2O emissions are agriculture
and the modern use of fertilizer, by artifically fixing as much N2 as the
environment, and then releasing it into the soil and water. It is currently
estimated that total anthropogenic emissions are approximately on the same
order as natural emissions, however due to a lack of in-depth understanding
of the natural production and consumption cycles, all current estimates have
large errors which depend on method. Without mitigation, N2O levels will

1.2
Project aims
This thesis aims to help N2O reduction investigations by evaluating the
relative performance of different batch enrichments concerning N2O removal,
norZI/II presence and ratios between the tested genes with the different
inhibitors and media used.
The inhibitors and media chosen have been
correlated with the number of norZI/II genes, as well as overall concentration.
The project aims are:
• Perform batch enrichments of environmental samples with variations of
three operational parameters (sample source, inhibitor, and media).

1.3
Overall Summary
To see how the use of inhibitors affects the enrichment, several samples were
enriched separately in order to potentially promote the use of other metabolic
pathways such as N2O reduction
The experiment used sediment/soil samples from the environment for
enrichment. Two types of media are compared. Then 4 combinations of
inhibitors are used: none, A, B, and A+B. This with 3 environmental samples
results in a total of 24 combinations of parameters for the experiment. A
repeat of each enrichment was made, resulting in a choice of 48 samples for
further analysis.
After the third enrichment, 16 samples were chosen to be observed using
gas chromatography, optical density, DOC and SKALAR, with DNA samples
for qPCR of the functional genes norZI, norZII, nirK, nirS, and Total Bacteria.

Methodology
2.1
Inoculum, Inhibitors and Media
The starting inoculum samples were two sediment and one soil sample. They
were harvested in the week of the first inoculation. Once gathered, they were
sealed in serum bottles with the same volume of distilled water added to form
a suspension that could pass through a needle, and to prevent the samples
drying out. They were stored in the dark and at room temperature until they
were needed for use.
Sodium Chlorite and Sodium Azide were the inhibitors to nitrification
and denitrification used, at concentrations of 10µM. This concentration has
been shown to inhibit these metabolisms.
Two types of media sets were chosen based on documented success to
enrich N2O reducing species, the "Minimal Saltwater Medium"[1], and the
two "Enrichment media" and "Denitrification media"[3] (Where Enrichment
media is used for the first two enrichments, and then Denitrification media
for the last two)

The carbon sources used were butiric acid, propanoic acid, and lactic acid,
at 10mM concentrations.
As the trace element solution made for the Enrichment media had less
micronutrients than the Modified Wolfes Mineral Solution A.4, but contained
some of the ingredients, we decided to use the Modified Wolfes Mineral
Solution for both reducing the number of trace element solutions made, and
to give the microorganisms more potential nutrients during enrichment.
Some changes had to be made to the recipes used for the media, since the
trace element solution specified for the Denitrification media [3] proved to
have insoluble concentrations of several chemicals, this was substituted with
the similar in chemicals but not concentrations SL-4 Mineral SolutionA.4.
Detailed recipes of the formula can be found in appendix A.
Once the inorganic chemicals had been added, the media was pH balanced
to between 7 and 7.1, using 2M HCl.The media was then autoclaved again,
before the organics were then added to the "Saltwater" media through a
sterile filter.
• The "Saltwater" media took approx. 4mL of 2M HCl per L, with a
further 1mL after being autoclaved to redissolve the suspension that
forms when it was white.
• The Enrichment media required 1mL per L
• The Denitrification media required 2.5mL.
Achieving identical pH levels between two media immediately prior to inoc-
ulation has proven challenging, both analytically and experimentally. This
difficulty arises from several factors, including the presence of multiple buffers,
the pH fluctuations brought on by autoclaving and degassing with nitrogen
(N2), potential risks associated with the addition of inhibitors, and the use
of butyric acid as a carbon source, posing the additional concern of contam-
ination in shared laboratory spaces and equipment due to its unpleasant
odor. Balancing these considerations requires a comprehensive approach
that takes into account the buffering capacity of each medium, adjustments
post-autoclaving, cautious addition of inhibitors, and measures to mitigate
the impact of butyric acid on shared lab environments.

2.2
Experimental Setup
Batch experiments unfolded as follows :
• The first rounds of enrichment used 60ml serum bottles with 40ml of
media, sealed in serum bottles with rubber stoppers and aluminium
seals. These were then flushed for 15 minutes. This was executed by
running pressurised N2 gas through a sterile 0.22 µm gas filter and a
sterile needle into the bottles’ head space, with another sterile needle
as a gas exit. Each bottle had 1mL of a 0.4M mixture of the carbon
sources injected through a sterile filter.
• The bottles were labelled, and then injected with 1mL of the appropriate
inhibitor at 0.4mM or sterile water, through a 0.22µm sterile liquid
filter.
• The bottles were then dosed with pure N2O gas through a sterile filter,
9mL of standard pressure gas for each 40mL of media.
• The bottles then had 2ml of the inoculant (5% v/v) injected directly.
(Due to the presence of rocks in the samples, the needles would often
block, which could result in an unintentional filtration effect)
• The serum bottles were stored in a 26C dark room in the lab, on a
shaking table at around 120 rpm. 26C was chosen as the optimal
temperature as Chlorite is recorded to form various gases above 30C.
• Along with having N2O injected into the head space after the inoculation,
the head space was flushed with sterile filtered N2 again and N2O injected
again according to the schedule found in appendix ??
• The headspace N2O concentration was measured at various intervals for
initially 10, then 14 likely suspects. All 48 individuals had their concen-
trations measured within 3-4 and 13-14 days after their headspaces were
flushed and redosed. This limitation was imposed due to the extended
time required for N2O to run through the current protocol (9 minutes
instead of the usual 2) and the instrument being concurrently needed
for multiple studies. This data can be found in the appendix.
• The sampels were flushed and redosen, and transfered to a new enrich-
ment acording to the schedule below 14
A second set of 16 200ml of media in 250ml serum bottles were prepared in
an identical way as the previous set. All quantities were scaled by 5, leading
to a active volume of 220ml.

2.3
Analytical methods
2.3.1
Heaspace N2O concentration
Throughout the enrichments, gas chromatography (Thermo Scientific TRACE™
1300 Gas Chromatograph, equipped with a HP-Plot/Q column (Length 15 m,
diameter 0,32 mm, film 20 m, Agilent Technologies, USA)) was used for CH4
and CO2 measurements.
O2 measurements also used gas chromatography (Thermo Scientific TRACE™
1300 Gas Chromatograph, using an HP-Molesieve column (Length 30 m, di-
ameter 0.53, film 50 m, Agilent Technologies, USA)).
CH4, O2 and CO2 concentrations were measured daily Calibrations for
0.2ml of headspace at standard pressure.
This was repeated for N2O, CO2, CH4, and O2.
Runs took 9 minutes per sample for N2O measurements, and 2 minutes
for the other gasses. The amount of equipment and time needed prevented
repeat measurements, as well as testing every single sample.
2.3.2
Biomass
During the in-depth final enrichment, the biomass concentration was moni-
tored daily using optical density (OD) at 600 nm (Cary® 50 Bio UV-Visible
Spectrophotometer, Agilent Technologies, USA). Measurements were taken
every time a SKALAR or DOC sample was taken to provide a quantitative
track of the media’s opacity. Before measurements, the serum bottles were
heavily shaken to ensure that no biomass had been sedimented. OD of sterile
media was also tracked every other day.
2.3.3
Nutrients
PO4, N, NO3, and NH3 concentrations were monitored using a continuous-
flow auto-analyser (SKALAR San++, Netherlands). Samples were taken
each day. The samples observed to be the fastest metabolisers were tested
twice daily. A 7 mL sample was extracted and filtered using a 0,22-µm filter
(Econo filter) into glass tubes used for the TOC and SKALAR tests with
plastic SKALAR stoppers. [Samples] were then stored horizontally at -20℃,
and subsequently thawed to liquid at room temperature in a lined tub .. (to
catch any leaks occurred prior to freezing), before being stored for analysis in
a refrigerator at 4C.

Results and Discussion
3.1
Initial 48 enrichments
Almost all serum bottles exhibited visual changes in comparison to sterile
media. These changes manifested as a white shade, gray/black clusters, a
white film on the glass surface, or yellow/white transparent groups. The
GC data indicated that over two-thirds of samples didn’t show a significant
deviation from most samples, which as a group trended upward from the
initial 10% to 25% as the samples were flushed and redosed.
Given this observed behaviour, when selecting samples for the fourth
enrichment with in-depth testing, it was decided that a sample that had
a recorded end N2O concentration of below 20% would be enriched. This
resulted in 13 samples being chosen for N2O consumption, and 3 being
enriched to provide a comparison to the dismissed samples where there wasn’t
obvious N2O consumption.
10 samples had their headspaces drop below 20% N2O concentration
after either 3 days or 13 days Given the calculated headspace equilibrium
concentration is 17%, and that the 3 samples tested right after being degassed
showed a range of positive values (2 →3%, 21 →12%, 22 →15%), it’s clear
that 15m being degassed with sterile N2 was not enough to flush all the N2O,
possibly due to the much higher solubility of N2O in water, or an unreported
contamination of N2O in the N2 supply or GC testing.
The fact that the flushing of N2 does not eliminate N2O suggests an
accumulation of dosage, leading to an enrichment of N2O in samples where
the metabolism rate is slower than the accumulation rate. This may explain
the upward drift in most concentrations beyond the predicted equilibrium.

concentrations primarily results from the media being withdrawn for OD,
SKALAR, and TOC analyses without replacement. This occurs in a system
with a relatively small headspace (220 ml media in a 250 ml bottle, as
larger quantities were unavailable), which reduces the headspace pressure

Conclusion
This study utilized Chlorite and Azide as inhibitors, to enrich denitrifying
communities.
The main conclusions of this study are:
• Every combination of inhibitor led to a reduction in the concentration
of every tested gene (except for the nirS genes with the pond sample,
which went up).
• Most enrichment’s with an inhibitor had higher average ratios of the
norZI/II gene to the Total Bacteria.
• No uniform difference was found in the norZI/II gene concentrations
between the two media choices.
• No significant difference was found between the usage of either inhibitor,
or in using both.
