Foreword 
This master thesis constitutes the conclusion of our study programme at DTU Engineering Management, 
with a specialisation in “Design and Management of Complex System Structures and Projects”. The work 
going into this thesis has stretched over a period of five months, to an estimated 30 ECTS points.  
The thesis has been written in collaboration between the two authors with primary supervision from Ph.D. 
student Baris Bekdik, DTU-MAN supervision by Christian Langhoff Thuesen and External supervision from 
“Head of Department” (Case Company). The thesis has been written in cooperation with the Case 
Company, which will be confidential. All personnel interviewees and organisations both internal and 
external, remain confidential.          
The inspiration for this project came from the authors´ BSc. thesis, which investigated Lean Design within 
the same Case Company and its benefits within the design phase in construction project. The BSc. was a 
stepping-stone for the Master Thesis in Engineering Management, which resulted in an interest within the 
design phase and the troubles relating to it. The holy grail of modularity was first introduced to us by 
Christian Thuesen and the concept of constructing buildings with “Lego” bricks was exciting.  
Through a range of iterative cycles, our work ended up focusing on the modularity concept within facades, 
which is an interesting building component. As we grew with the project the focus ended up investigating 
the underlying design process of facades that have a high degree of complexity, which are to be revealed.

Abstract - English 
Modularity has shown great potential in the manufactory industry, reducing order lead-time and creating 
multiple products assortment with limited resources. This thesis investigates to what degree the concept of 
modularity can be applied to façade projects, and the possibilities of generating the potential and benefits 
discovered in the operation-based industry.  
The processes of design, production and execution of facade projects incorporate a high amount of 
disciplines and stakeholders. The building component has a high influence on the holistic design and the 
performance of the building. These aspects and the unique project-based characteristics require and 
iterative and integral design approach and transforms the façade to a highly complex system, which is 
diminishing the application potential and opportunities of the modularity concept. 
The thesis reveals the complexity and the integral nature of facades, by applying the theoretical perspective 
of Complexity and Modularity. The analysis and discussion are separated in two major subjects; the physical 
façade and the design process of facades. The research is founded on these two dependent tracks and by 
applying an analytic framework, clarifies the three interconnected dimensions of product, process and 
organisation, in relation to architecture and interfaces. The thesis verifies how the concept of modularity 
can be applied to a façade project in the quest of revealing the complexity and challenges within. 
Moreover, the research explains where the complexity of the building component originates and how it 
thrives. 
Multiple problems have been identified both in the in the design process of facades and in the design 
organisation of the case company. The research tries to discover how the problems can be addressed with 
an approach of the modularity concept. A multidisciplinary Workshop has been arranged and executed in 
order to reveal and understand the associated organisations, disciplines and activities entangled in the 
integral process of facades. Dependency Structure Matrix (DSM) was the primary tool used to map 
interfaces and dependencies between activities. By analysing the output with the Cambridge Advanced 
Modeller software, enabled the research to identify highly iterative process modules, with a high amount of 
interfaces and dependencies. The Workshop evaluation and analysis of the output has verified great 
potential of the approach; in relation to plan and schedule the complex design process, solve major 
problems in the design phase and create an applicable management tool.  
A Design Guide of façade presents major findings and reflects the recommendations collected in the 
qualitative analysis, which in the perspective of the authors establishes a foundation for successful Design 
Management. The major findings contain: 
 
Implement inter-organisational DSM-workshop, to identify interfaces of design 
activities, create detailed design schedule and establish commitment. 
 
 
Establish efficient and effective Information Flow among the design organisations by 
developing detailed and realistic Collaboration Terms and ICT-agreements.

Abstract - Danish 
Modularitet har vist stort potentiale inden for fabrikation, ved at reducere leveringstiden og producere en 
række produkter med begrænset ressourcer. Denne rapport har til hensigt at undersøge i hvor høj grad 
modularitet konceptet kan benyttes på et facade projekt og hermed overføre de fordele og muligheder der 
ses i den operations-baseret industri. 
Inden for et facade projekt er der en lang række discipliner og interessenter, der har stor indflydelse på 
hvordan design, produktion og udførelse bliver håndteret. Herudover er facaden med til at udtrykke 
bygningen og bygningens ydelse i form af bl.a. energiforbrug. Byggeindustrien er i høj grad projekt-baseret 
hvor der kræves iterative processer og sammenhængende design for at kunne opnå en funktionel facade, 
hvilket resultere i et komplekst system. Denne komplikation mindsker potentialet og muligheden for 
brugen af modularitets konceptet. 
Projektet har til formål at klarlægge kompleksiteten af den integrale natur i et facade projekt, ved at 
benytte de to grundlæggende teoretiske koncepter Kompleksitet og Modularitet. Analysen og diskussionen 
er opdelt i to hovedafsnit; den fysiske facade og facadens designproces. Hvor målet er at identificere de tre 
sammenkoblet dimensioner, produkt, proces og organisation, med relation til opbygning og 
afhængigheder. Analysen og diskussion er vejledt af et analytiske værktøj der bekræfter at modularitet 
konceptet kan blive benyttet på et facade projekt, samtidig med at det tager højde for problemer og 
udfordringer der bringer researchen nærmere på en afsløring af kompleksiteten. Rapporten har også til mål 
at klarligge hvor kompleksiteten opstår og hvordan den trives inden for facade projekter. 
Undersøgelsen har identificeret en række problemer inden for design processen af facader samt inden for 
samarbejds-virksomheden (Case Company), som projektet vil prøve at udrette ved hjælp af modularitets 
teori og principper. Der er blevet faciliteret en Workshop for at afdække og forstå hvilken organisationer, 
discipliner og aktiviteter der er repræsenteret i et facade projekt. Dependency Structure Matrix (DSM) 
(”Afhængigheds Sammensætnings Matrix”) var det primære værktøj der blev brugt for at identificere 
afhængigheder og grænseflader mellem aktiviteter. Ved at analysere resultatet fra Workshoppen med 
Cambridge Advanced Modeller software (CAM), blev der identificeret en lang række iterative proces 
moduler med store afhængigheder og grænseflader. Resultatet fra Workshoppen har bekræftet et stort 
potentiale for at kunne planlægge den komplekse og integrale design proces. Derudover har DSM vist sig at 
kunne løse problemer relateret til design fasen og hermed være et nyt brugbart ledelsesværktøj. 
En Design Guide for facader er blevet udarbejdet. Den repræsentere de vigtigste resultater der er blevet 
identificeret og indeholder en række anbefalinger der vil være med til at skabe fundamentet for succesfuld 
projekteringsledelse. De to vigtigste tiltag er: 
 
Implementer DSM-Workshop hvor alle organisationer der er involveret i facade 
projektet er repræsenteret. Identificer afhængigheder og grænseflader mellem design 
aktiviteter, udarbejd detaljeret projekteringstidsplan og skab engagement. 
 
 
Klarlæg effektiv informations strøm mellem design organisationer, ved at udvikle en 
detaljeret of realistisk Samarbejdsaftale og IKT-Aftale (Informations og 
Kommunikations Teknologi).

1 Introduction  
The implementation of the modularity concept has revealed great potential in many industries and 
contributed with high productivity and to the industrialisation of the world we are living in.  
The production strategy towards product modularity has resulted in a range of benefits; handling product 
variety and manage the complexity of products. Among other benefits in the life cycle of a product, the 
concept has entailed component economies of scale, a reduction of the order lead time and created 
innovative solutions through high competitiveness in the supply chain. 
The construction industry is no exception, which has translated the concept of modularity into 
prefabrication of building components. The approach has accelerated the execution process of construction 
projects and increased the productivity of its supply chain. The great benefits of prefabrication has paved 
the way to growth in the application of; concrete elements, roof elements, bathrooms modules, building 
blocks and facades elements. The façade of a construction project is a complex building components and 
has been addressed with a modular approach in form of prefabrication of standard façade components and 
façade elements. 
However, the approach of the modularity concept and the prefabrication within the construction industry 
has increase the workload in the design phase dramatically. When initiating on prefabricated elements in a 
construction project, early decisions of the building design has to be made. Furthermore, the information 
flow within the design organisation is growing significantly, due to the need of involving subcontractors and 
suppliers early in the project life cycle. 
An internal investigation of the design management in the Case Company has identified crucial problems in 
the design phase; 
 
Insufficient design schedule resulting in a low degree of detail  
 
Invisible dependencies and interfaces of the design activities across the design organisation 
 
Low collaboration among the internal and external design organisations 
The combination of the increased workload in the design process and current problems of the design 
management collides and rise major challenges and threats towards the Case Company. The challenges are 
tackled insufficient which result in project changes, delay of project material and frustration among the 
organisations. 
Interestingly, Ericsson & Erixson (1999) describe a symmetry between product and process and 
Campagnolo & Camuffo (2009) argue that a modular product must have an underlying predefined modular 
process. According to Wilson & Perumal (2015) this is where complexity arise.      
The challenges in the design process and the statements of the modularity gurus are rising following 
questions, which creates the foundation and motivation for the thesis and the following research.  
 Research Question 
 In what degree can the concept of modularity be applied on the underlying processes of façade 
projects? 
 How can the concept of modularity resolve the problems in the design process of facades?

 Hypotheses 
By acknowledging the problems in the Case Company and by the identification of the potential of 
modularity in production industry; the research are proposing following two hypotheses; 
 The application of the modularity concept will transfer opportunities of modularity to the 
underlying processes in the facade project. 
 The implementation of modularity will affect the product, process and organisation of the façade 
project, enable parallel work and erase the dependencies between.

2 Methodology 
 Research Structure 
The deductive structure is reached through; literature research, empirical data, analysis, discussion and 
conclusion. The majority of literature discusses the concept of complexity and modularity. Most literature 
references focus on the manufactory industry. Other relevant theories and concepts will be presented 
throughout the analysis in relation to relevance. The research is a combination of qualitative and 
quantitative analysis. The qualitative analysis is based on interviews and a workshop arranged in the Case 
Company. The result from the workshop can be considered as the quantitative research, which shall 
support and strengthen the key findings of the qualitative data.    
As complexity is visible on a detailed level, an entire construction project is too large for the lifecycle of a 
master thesis. Initially the design process was in focus, nevertheless the research had to shift gear as the 
findings within the design process implied that the research had to focus one specific structural 
component. Through interviews and supervision, the research was scoped to investigate the design process 
of facades. Facades was chosen, due to a high degree of complexity and iterative processes, even though 
the physical interface between the façade and the construction project is limited and the product itself act 
modular. The approach from construction projects, to design process, to the design process of facades are

 Complex systems 
Complexity is not about large numbers, it is more about the amount of connections and dependencies 
between objects that adds to the magnitude of complexity. Appelo (2011) describes that “even a water 
molecule can be complex; because of its actions can be unpredictable” (Apello, 2011). This statement 
implies that the molecule acts differently depending on which substance it is interacting with or 
surrounding forces. H. Simon (1962) explains complex systems as a system made up of a large number of 
parts that interacts in a non-simple way (Simon H. A., 1962). Züst and Troxler (2006) argues further that the 
cause and effect relation between partial systems and their elements are partial non-linear and not 
constant (Züst & Troxler, 2006). Complex systems are thus very difficult to predict and nonlinear as the 
cause and effect is dominantly un-known within the system.   
Züst and Troxler state, in their book about complex systems in engineering and management, that thinking 
in systems is essentially interdisciplinary and can be a helpful perspective to solve urging problems and are 
thus superior to traditional disciplines and way of thinking (Züst & Troxler, 2006). 
There are several benefits written by authors that emphasize the usage system thinking; Züst and Troxler 
state that; system thinking  helps to understand a complex situation, emphasize the functional and process-
oriented view, integrating different perspectives and in general makes communication easier (Züst & 
Troxler, 2006).  
Steven Alter, the author of The Work System Method which is a method for businesses to understand 
systems in their organisation, state that thinking in system and how they interact will help improve 
performance (Alter, 2006, s. 11). 
Wilson and Perumal Inc. evaluate the characteristics of Complex systems: 
1. 
Non-linear reactions 
2. 
Emerging properties 
3. 
Feedback loops 
4. 
Unknown interactions 
These characteristics make complex systems almost impossible to predict and control (Perumal, 2015).

 Dimensions of Complexity 
Wilson & Perumal argues that there are three dimensions of complexity; Product (and service), process and 
organisational oriented complexity (Wilson & Perumal, 2009). They argue further that analysing complexity 
within process and product without analysing organisational complexity will reduce the efficiency of the 
organisations involved. Analysing parts of complexity is not sufficient, as product, process and organisation 
is integrated and all three aspects have to be managed simultaneously.      
 Product Complexity 
 
“Product complexity describes the variety of and  
within the products or services you offer the customers” 
(Wilson & Perumal, 2009). 
All variety adds costs to a business, but not all products add value to the customer. Wilson and Perumal 
further describe that variety in the products that offers customers something they are willing to pay for is 
good complexity; variety that they won’t pay for—or won’t pay enough for—is bad complexity (Wilson & 
Perumal, 2009). They distinguish between good and bad complexities are strong in theory but difficult to 
distinguish in practice. The advantages of getting rid of “bad” product complexity are only visible when a 
company choose to eliminate it (Wilson & Perumal, 2009). Wilson & Perumal sums up; bad product 
complexity is anything a customer do not value enough to provide an adequate return, thus bad product 
complexity should be the obvious target for companies to pursue. New products and product innovation 
adds significantly to the product complexity as well as the process complexity; as the variety of 
“items”/products (Figure 1) increase through the whole value chain (Ashkenas, 2007).  
M. L. George, writes in his book about Lean Six Sigma that the 4th law of complexity and costs; “The 
complexity of product and service offering generally adds more non-value-add cost and WIP (Work in 
Process) than either poor quality (low sigma) or slow speed (un-lean) process problems” (George, 2003). 
This adds to the understanding that the product variety have to add value to the customer otherwise a 
company waste resources.  
 Process Complexity 
 
“Process complexity is the number of processes, process steps, handoffs, etc.  
involved in executing and delivering its products”  
(Wilson & Perumal, 2009). 
Within process, Wilson & Perumal define bad complexity as; duplications, rework and complex related 
work-arounds.  Furthermore, Wilson & Perumal argues that process is “easy” to change, and can be 
changed overnight, whereas changes in other dimensions (products and organisation) takes more time 
(Wilson & Perumal, 2009).

Human and uncertainty increase Complexity 
One aspect not included by the complexity cube, is human behaviour that originates in organisation and 
acts in processes. Pich et al. (2002) argue that inadequacy of information is caused either by events or 
causality being unknown (Pich, Loch, & Meyer, 2002), which they relate to through the term ambiguity. 
They further argue that inadequacy of information is based on an inability to evaluate the effect of actions 
within a system with many variables interactions (Pich, Loch, & Meyer, 2002), which is the essence of 
complexity. The trio argues that to combat inadequacy of information requires a combination of learning 
(be innovative while a project is still running) and selectionism (pursue the best solution through multiple 
loops) (Pich, Loch, & Meyer, 2002), both solutions got relation to evolutionary theory. 
Oehmen et al. (2015) interpret ambiguity to uncertainty, inspired by the research of Pich et al. (2002). They 
research complexity within projects, programme and portfolio, and argue that the “drivers of complexity” 
consist of uncertainty of project execution and built in behaviour of humans, which they state to increase 
the complexity in projects (Oehmen, Thuesen, Ruiz, & Geraldi, 2015). They further argue that the human 
behaviour is complex as teams and individual act situational and differently dictated by the external and 
internal forces and the environment. This result in three boundaries navigating human behaviour within 
projects (Oehmen, Thuesen, Ruiz, & Geraldi, 2015):  
1. Limited information access  
2. Limited cognitive capacity and thinking biases  
3. Limited time to make decisions  
The forces that drive uncertainty within projects are following (Oehmen, Thuesen, Ruiz, & Geraldi, 2015):  
1. Unreliable intuitive perception of uncertainty  
2. Many of our current uncertainty and risk management tools do not fit the purpose  
3. Long life cycles and emergent behaviour increase uncertainty  
These aspects result in an understanding that complexity lies within product, process and organisations 
with an additional complexity concerning human behaviour and uncertainty factors within a project.

As proven, all systems are complex – dependent on the level of detail. This is generic for projects and the

mistakes. This implies that the traditional way of construction projects – learning through experience – 
does not necessarily increase efficiency and are arguably not the way to manage unique construction 
projects.

 Modularity 
The concept of modularity dates back to the 1960s (Simon H. A., 1962). Compagnolo & Camuffo (2009) 
performed an extended literature review on modularity they found that the beginning of modularity as a 
design principle dating back to the 1980´s by the work of Henderson and Clark (1990) and Ulrich & Tung 
(1991). Modularity as a design principle for organisations dates back to the mid 1990´s (Compagnolo & 
Camuffo, 2009). During the last decade, there have been an increased focus on modularity from 
management scholars, whom have seen the benefits and possibilities within the concept. One of the major 
reasons why interest suddenly increased could be its capabilities of handling complex systems in the 
industry, thus supporting managers in their line of work.   
The term modularity is used informally in many ways. Within design of complex engineered system, 
modularity often refers to independent units. In architecture, the term refers to standardised components 
within a construction project it refers to prefabrication and in manufacturing modularity refers to 
interchangeable units to create product variants (Ulrich & Tung, 1991). Although different industries got 
different perspective on modularity, all perspectives are highly interrelated. Ulrich and Tung (1991) 
identified that “independence of components is the property of a design that allows standardisation and 
interchangeability”.    
The Swedish duo, Ericsson & Erixon (1999) define the term module as "a standardised unit; a combinable, 
changeable part or component; a class of numbers; any in a series of standardised units for use together; a 
usually packaged functional assembly of electronic components for use with other assemblies” (Ericsson & 
Erixon, 1999). Corresponding to Figure 4 (Market Complexity, above), Ericsson & Erixon (1999) states that 
the concept of modularity aims to increase efficiency by reduce the complexity. The modular approach 
suggests that companies have to build an optimal product assortment that account for; development, 
design, variety, manufacture, quality, purchase and after-sales service, which is basically the entire life-
cycle of a product. It is further argued that the cornerstone of modularity is the adoption of a common view 
of the product within the entire organisation (Ericsson & Erixon, 1999). 
To some extent, every system is modular, or can be modulated. Compagnolo & Camuffo (2009) states “very 
few systems are composed of parts that interact and effect each other so tightly that there is no opportunity 
to mix-and-match the subsystems they consist of” (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009). Other authors describe 
modularity in a similar perspective; as an attribute of a complex system that advocates design structures 
based on minimising interdependence between modules and maximising interdependence within them. 
Furthermore can be mixed and matched in order to obtain new configurations without loss of the systems 
functionality or performance (Baldwin & Clark, 1997); (Langlois, 1992). Baldwin & Clark argues that in a 
modular system, each module communicates and interacts with the others via standardised interfaces that 
allow modules’ decoupling (Baldwin & Clark, 1997) (Baldwin & Clark, 2000). By modularising systems, the 
degree of modularity will be higher if it is established through a modular structure compared to those 
achievable through an integral one (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009). Ulrich and Tung (1991) have a similar 
perspective and notes that; by letting the physical and functional design work together within components 
it enables a modular design, which is only possible when the number of interactions between components 
are limited otherwise they will not be interchangeable (Ulrich & Tung, 1991). This is equivalent to Simon´s 
(1962) definition; modularity creates a high intra-component dependency and low inter-component 
dependency.

 The Promises of Modularity 
Through Campagnolo & Camuffo´s literature review it is seen that; from a theoretical perspective, 
modularity represent a conceptual tool that discover the benefits and costs of interdependence, degrees of 
coupling, reconfigure-ability and imitation in the design of technological and organisational systems. 
Moreover, it connects organisational economics, research on network externalities and the role of 
standards and compatibility in industry dynamics (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009).   
From a practical perspective modularity has largely influenced new product development processes, the 
design and management of vertical and horizontal inter-organisational relationships, the adoption of 
formal and informal standards, the design of flexible and scalable production systems based on cells, sub-
assembly and pre-testing (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009). Moreover, Compagnolo & Camuffo (2009) argue 
that a modular system is the opposition to an integral one, the difference is described in the subsequent 
section to generate an understanding of the opposites.

related to the theory of complex systems and dependent on how these are viewed modularity have a range

 Integral vs. Modular 
To clarify the different between integral and modular - Figure 5 - an explanation is heading your way:  
Modular: Imply “a one-to-one mapping from functional elements in the function structure to the physical 
components of the product, and specifies de-coupled interfaces between components” (Ulrich, 1995). 
Modular products are highly flexible in its structure, with highly standardised interoperability and standard 
connections for sub-systems. A modular system can typically be upgraded be replacing lesser components 
with better ones. Any innovator can create a module for the product as long as it obey the standard 
interface (Fine C. H., 2005). Some example are computers, speakers and automobiles. 
Integral: Characterise a “one-to-many or many-to-one and/or by tight and complex coupling between 
physical parts” (Ulrich, 1995). 
Integral architectures typically link sub-systems with tightly coordinated relationships and distinctive or 
unique features that cannot be easily connected to other systems. Products with integral architecture tend 
to have complex and nonstandard interfaces and the sub-systems are built or customised explicitly for a 
particular product (Fine C. H., 2005). Some examples are Apple, wings of a jet airplane, (customisation).  
Figure 5 – Integral → Modular (Authors) 
Modular and integral are oddly connected as you can have 100% modular and 0% integral parts within a 
system and other parts of the system are 0% modular and 100% integral. Meaning that within a system 
there will both be integral and modular aspects to consider, and the total amount of modularity and 
integrality is a subsume that must be analysed in detail to differentiate and understand.  
In a sense every system or components of a system, starts as integral. A company’s strategy towards a 
modular design dictates the degree of modularity and are reliant on other modular products, interfaces and 
the uniqueness of the final product.   
As an example: a jet airplane wing is integral, hence the wing must support special function as lift and 
navigation, it must contain fuel and lighting, and must perform its best every time. The architecture and 
function of the wing are customised in close collaboration between designers and engineers. The wing is 
part of a system – the airplane – which have windows, seats and wheels which might be modular, and have 
standardised fixtures that seat-, wheel- and window-producers can compete on. This example show that 
within a system there can be both modular and integral areas to consider, and the engineering system as a

 Product Modularity 
The architecture of any product can be more or less modular or integral. An integral product includes 
components that perform many functions, are in close proximity or close spatial relationship to each other 
and are highly synchronised. Normal, a change made to one component requires a change to other 
components for a correct functioning of the total product. In contrast, in modular product architecture, 
components are interchangeable, autonomous, loosely coupled and individually upgradeable through 
standardised interfaces (Fine C. H., 1998). Fixon (2006) states that the functional structure can be applied in 
the beginning of the product design, where the design involves puzzling with modules to enable the 
required functionality of the product (Fixon, 2006).

Architecture: It specifies the structure of the system. This includes specifying modules which are 
included in the system and also what functionality these apply to the system. 


interact physically and functionally, which includes physical joining, connections and 
communication. 
 
Standards: These involve testing conformity of the modules to the system and associated design 
rules. Standards are also applied for testing modules according to equivalent modules (Baldwin & 
Clark, 1997). 
Design Rules can be established by visualising a system before and after modularisation (Baldwin & Clark, 
2000).

 Process Modularity 
In relation to the Complexity Cube Figure 3, process modularity relates to the underlying processes for a 
given product. Campagnolo & Camuffo (2009) argue that a modular product must have an underlying 
predefined modular process. The Swedish duo, describe the symmetry between product and process 
should be a predefined goal when designing the product architecture (Ericsson & Erixon, 1999).  
Another perspective towards process modularity is the outsourcing of products and their underlying 
processes. The initiative of outsourcing products or activities requires cautious management of the 
information, design rules and output from the given organisation. Campagnolo & Camuffo (2009) explain 
how outsourcing can lead to a modular product architecture, simultaneous with an outsourcing process. 
Sako (2003) outline three different paths towards module outsourcing see Figure 7.  
 
Figure 7 – Paths towards module outsourcing, Sako (2003) 
In the first path (acd), the firm defines a modular product architecture before outsourcing one or more 
modules. In other words, by following the first path (acd) a firm competes as an ‘architect’ creating visible 
information and attracting module designers to its design rules (Sako, 2003) – equivalent with Baldwin & 
Clarks literature (design rules). In the second path (abd), the firm starts to outsource some product 
components before moving towards a modular design. For a firm operating in a mature industry the second 
path (abd) appears more likely, because firms have probably already started to outsource some product 
components (or activities), in order to achieve efficiency in the manufacturing and assembly phases. In the 
third path (ad), the firm simultaneously implements product modularity and outsourcing (Compagnolo & 
Camuffo, 2009). However, the second and third path (abd and ad, respectively) appear to involve higher 
risks in terms of choosing in-house capabilities and control, because of the role played by suppliers who are 
both architect and module maker. Following this rationale and relying on external providers for design, 
engineering and production, the outsourcing firm may be forced to base its competitive advantage in other 
complementary value chain activities. The outsourcing is also irreversible, because the firm has lost its 
system integration capabilities (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009). 
Subsequent research demonstrate that the specific trajectory is likely to emerge depending on the specific 
attributes of the product, the industry (in terms of capabilities available in the supply chain, for example), 
the firms strategy and its capabilities as well as the task itself when considering the shift in the firm 
boundaries (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009).

 Organisational Modularity 
In relation to the Complexity Cube Figure 3, organisational modularity relates to the process modularity as

project must contain the knowledge and experience, in order to perform their processes and deliver a 
functional product. As mentioned in the section above, outsourcing is highly related to organisational

Integral products should be supported by integral organisations (tightly connected to reduce risk of 
opportunism and increase communication) and modular products should have an underlying organisation 
that are loosely coupled, easily reconfigurable and autonomous (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009). They

knowledge within the module does not need to be shared. The modular organisation operates more as a 
network rather than hierarchy (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 2009).

 Perspective on Modularity in the Manufactory Industry 
The manufactory industry have adapted the concept of modularity with great benefits. One of the major 
reasons is that the underlying process for a given product has a high degree of standardisation within 
machinery, functionality and supply chain. In manufactory facilities, it is often seen that when a new 
profitable innovation is at bay, new machinery and processes are co-created among organisation and 
suppliers, thus replacing components to enable a higher quality (Fine C. H., 2005). 
This allows a high degree of standardisation, where interfaces and function are predetermined and known, 
which allow the concept of modularity to flourish. The opportunities of modularity (Ulrich & Tung, 1991) 
increase by the level of mass production – which in turn creates more standardised and identical products.   
 
The machinery and the product range is best determined in close collaboration with everyone involved, 
including the supply chain (Russell & Taylor, 2009).  
Pure production adapt and benefits from modularity, because machinery is the main production tool. 
Machines are complex in its creation but not in its function that are linear. 
The collaboration in the design phase of a product is truly important (Russell & Taylor, 2009), it enables all 
machinery and supplies to cooperate and function as a unit. Nevertheless, when the process are created for 
a product it freeze the production and the range of products is determined by the architecture (Baldwin & 
Clark, 1997). Manufactory facilities only have to collaborate “once” to enable a range of product to be 
created, thus determine interfaces guided by standards.   
Suppliers and machinery is thus standardised and known until a new product or product combination are to 
be created.

 Measuring modularity in construction 
In the following section, a presentation of how product, process and organisational modularity are 
measured in construction projects, based upon the research of; (Voordijk, Meijboom, & Haan, 2006). 
In construction projects, “product modularity can be analysed by focusing on the degree of component 
independence and on the degree of interface standardisation between these components regarding the 
following two generic levels of a building” (Voordijk, Meijboom, & Haan, 2006). 
1. Exterior, the most rigid level of the building design; the floor plan, volume elements, which 
determine the shape or exterior of the building. 
2. Interior, the finishing design parts of a building; roofs, facades, bow windows, use of materials, 
which determine the infill or interior of the building. 
In construction projects, process modularity is the underlying processes for producing a product (out-put). 
Wolters (2002), states that it is about the manufacturing techniques used – which determine the final 
appearance of the building (Wolter, 2002). Therefore, process modularity in construction systems are 
dictated by how a product is made or created. Voordijk et al. (2006) extent the measurement by adding 
that the number of prefabricated elements is combined with process modularity (Voordijk, Meijboom, & 
Haan, 2006). This is arguably true, as product, process and organisation are intertwine, and a measurement 
of the total modularity and not only process. If an organisation decides to outsource to prefabrication, the 
organisation outsource the entire modular system that is both the product, the underlying process and the 
sub-organisation, and its expertise. 
When investigating organisational modularity it refers to who does what, the division of labour, allocation 
of task/activities/output among organisation involved and within organisations involved – and how 
different actors interact with each other (Voordijk, Meijboom, & Haan, 2006). In a construction project, 
different actors will appear in different phases in the construction system, why it is important to 
differentiate. 
The focus in the analysis will concern facades, which have a modular appearance and take advantages of 
product modularity. However, the majority of the analysis will focus on the underlying design process and 
organisation of facades. The three visible design rules (Baldwin & Clark, 1997) will be used when creating 
the framework. Which will provide the research with a platform to discuss and reveal the complexity within 
a façade project.

2. Interfaces; are the interactions between modules. By defining these in detail modules can 
interact physically and functionally, which includes physical joining, connections and 
communication.

 Curtain walling 
The term “curtain wall” origins from the medieval time and is referring to the wall between two towers 
(bastions) as a main defensive line enclosing the sites of a castle, fortress or town (Hughes, 1999) (Friar, 
2003). 
In modern times, the term “curtain wall” is defined in most literature to be any building wall of any material 
that is designed to resist lateral loads. Lateral loads are wind, earthquake and the facades own weight, 
which is why a curtain wall is a non-load-bearing wall (CMHC, 2004). 
The American Architectural Manufactures Association (AAMA) has published a “Design Guide Manual for 
Aluminium Curtain Walls” which provides following definitions (AAMA, 2005): 
Metal Curtain Wall – An exterior curtain wall which may consist entirely or principally of metal, or may be a 
combination of metal, glass and other surfacing materials supported by or within a metal framework. 
 
Window Wall – A type of metal curtain wall installed between floors or between floor and roof and typically 
composed of vertical and horizontal framing members, containing operable sash or ventilators, fixed lights 
or opaque panels or any combination thereof. 
The definitions implies that the metal curtain wall is a particular type of curtain wall and a window wall is a 
subset of metal curtain wall. The classification of the different curtain walls are based on the type of wall, 
the erection method and its glazing method, see Figure 21. (CMHC, 2004) 
 
Figure 21 – Curtain wall classification, modified by author (CMHC, 2004)

components. The primary materials are aluminium, steel and glass, along with secondary materials such as

 Glazing Method 
The classification of glazing method are based on specific details of how infill panels are attached within the 
openings of a framing grid. In this section three glazing methods are presented; exterior batten, stop and 
structural silicon. 
 Exterior Batten 
The most common used glazing method is exterior batten. The technique is based on a metal batten, which 
is fixed to the framing with a pressure plate from outside, see Figure 28. The batten apply pressure to the 
gaskets at the edge of the infill and creates the seal of the wall. The glass is installed from the outside. The 
exterior gasket is mainly a water intrusion barrier, but is not directly waterproof. The interior gasket is 
providing an air barrier between the frame and sealed unit. The other gaskets between anti-rotation 
spacers provide separation and movement capability (CMHC, 2004).

 Stop

 Functions and Loads 
Exterior enclosure systems have to perform multiple functions, which are interrelated and influencing each 
other. According to C.K. Boswell the building enclosure follows four primary functions that include (Boswell, 
2013): 
1. Structural function: The ability of the system to support itself and the applied loads  
2. Weather-tightness: Keeping natural elements outside 
3. Energy efficiency: Performing to high levels by reducing energy consumption. Energy efficiency 
goes hand in hand with weather-tightness 
4. Accommodating building movements. This goes hand in hand with structural function 
The additional function of the wall system is depending on the design requirements, which may include 
acoustics, blast/threat resistance, and other force resistance or performance features.  
A glass metal curtain wall is a selective filter, which must be a barrier for some effects and allow passaging 
others. The wall must provide a barrier for rain, wind and snow, while filtering and control light and heat 
through optimised glass products (Boswell, 2013). 
The concept of the wall as a filter is not unique to metal a curtain wall. However, unique features of metal 
curtain wall technology means that the design of these walls requires attention. Performance parameters 
specifically effecting the function and performance of the glass and metal curtain wall must include (CMHC, 
2004):

Structural integrity

structural function, the weather-tightness and energy efficiency, which are the major iterative issues

 Structural Function 
The facade system must be of sufficient strength in order to support its own weight (often referred to as 
dead load), accommodate the applied loads and forces, and transfer these through enclosure anchorage 
assemblies to the primary structure. The structural performance characteristics and capacity of each 
component dependent on material properties, size, thickness, orientation, method of attachment and 
geometry, which must be understood in order to develop the basic system structural design approach. 
Materials performing as supports in the system structure have inherent strengths and weaknesses. The 
goal is to accentuate the strengths and minimize—or eliminate—the weaknesses (Boswell, 2013).

 Weather-tightness 
One of the important functions of a façade system is to act as a membrane to separate the external 
elements and forces from the interior spaces and occupants, which requires weather-tightness. In this 
section the research are considering air and water. The forces of sunlight, temperature and wind are also 
influencing the weather-tightness of a façade system, but are considered in the next section – Energy 
efficiency.  
“Water penetration is one of the most persistent performance problems with all wall types” 
                                                                                                                                                       (CMHC, 2004) 
Water intrusion and air filtration must be controlled, in order to achieve a weather-tight façade. The water 
penetration must be managed and discharged to the exterior without intrusion of the interior occupied 
spaces and allow the system to dry after the source of the water is removed. The façade must also be 
protected against excessive air infiltration within the prescribed limits. 
The function of protect the interior of these forces is obvious, but the methods to actually achieve a  
weather-tight enclosure are often not fully understood or systematically tracked through façade design 
with associated graphic details; defining materials, connection and the layers of weather force control. The 
local climate and geography has a great influence on the actions of the air and water and its effect on the 
system. The material of a glass metal curtain wall is impervious to moisture which reduced the area of 
water penetration, but increases the importance of the joints, connections and materials between, which 
are crucial to achieved and maintained a weather-tight façade. Especially on costume wall which are 
designed from scratch. 
The ability to control water penetration is according to CMHC evaluated by a number of different standard 
test methods. While some tests have been developed for field use most are meant to be applied to a 
representative sample of wall installed in a chamber at a testing facility (CMHC, 2004). 
The wall sample, also referred to as a mock-up, is used as a visual test to insure the design performance, 
but even more important the mock-up is tested for how it perform. The testing activities and approval of 
materials from all integrated disciplines are crucial and have to be integrated in the design process of 
facades. (Façade Contractor 2)

 Energy efficiency 
The energy efficiency of buildings has gained enormous attention in the last decades, which has entailed 
the focus on exterior building envelopes and the related performance requirements, which tightened the 
restriction of the building energy consumption.  
“Energy requirements and technical requirements of facades are getting higher and higher with the main 
focus on energy efficiency – which is raising big challenges” 
                                                                                                                             (Architect 1) 
Current trends in energy supply and use are patently unsustainable in an economic, environmental and 
social perspective. According to the report from the International Energy Agency (IEA) buildings are the 
largest energy-consuming sector in the economy with over one-third of all energy and half of global 
electricity consumed. The building envelope, which is the boundary between the conditioned interior of the 
building and the outdoors, can be improved significantly by reducing the energy use for cooling and heating 
through Innovative technologies such as advanced facades. (International Energy Agency, 2013) 
The requirements towards an energy-efficient facade are the transmission of natural light, while minimising 
the heat gain or heat loss. Additionally, the façade must exploite insulation performance in the opaque 
areas, in order to control the temperature and the condensation. Energy efficiency of facades are also 
influenced by air leakage, which have to be minimised to heat or cool interior air in the conditioned space. 
There are two goal that the design team tries to achieve: maximise energy efficiency and occupant comfort. 
According to Boswell energy-efficient enclosure must consider:  
1. Local climate and geography 
2. Building shape and orientation 
3. Material selections, quantity, and placement 
5.9.4.1 
Local Climate and Geography 
The design team of the façade must understand and interpret the natural characteristics of the micro and 
macro climates of the region and local site of the construction project in order to design a functional 
façade. There are three main climate characteristics, cold, warm and mix climate, which is determent from 
the location of the project, but the seasonal weather patterns have to be analysed too in terms of; heat, 
cold, humidity, sunlight and wind (Boswell, 2013). 
5.9.4.2 
Shape and Orientation 
The shape and the orientation of the façade has a direct impact on the energy efficiency of the building and 
has furthermore an influence the choice of an appropriate ratio of transparent area to opaque insulated 
area. Thoughtful orientation can give the opportunities for innovative exterior building enclosures, by 
maximise passive solar heating when needed and avoid or minimise heat gain when cooling is needed. 
Furthermore, the orientation of the building can provide opportunities for natural ventilation and 
daylighting throughout most of the year by utilising and maximising the characteristics of the local climate. 
Shape and orientation of the building enables the coexistence with the natural climate and environment 
(Boswell, 2013).

5.9.4.3 
Material selections, Quantity and Placement 
The shape and orientation of the building are influencing the type and quantity of vision glass area, the 
positioning, isolation, overhang and sunshades. But the main criteria’s of the façade component 
specification are the thermal qualities of the materials and the transmission of sun energy, in order to 
achieve the required energy efficiency. Another important parameter is the light transmission, which has a 
strong relation to the indoor climate of the building. (Boswell, 2013) 
The thermal quality is measured by the U-value which states the thermal performance of the component. 
The coefficient is calculated for the glass and metal components, but even more important the U-value is 
for the entire façade element. The U-value is the overall heat transfer coefficient in a non-solar (i.e. exterior 
and interior temperatures) condition and defines how well the material functions as a conductor and 
indicates the rate of heat flow due to conduction, convection and radiation resulting from temperature 
differences between the exterior and interior. The U-value is measured in W/m2K, as indicated in Figure 34 
below. (Boswell, 2013) (Bøjsø Døre & Vinduer a/s, Januar 2012)

The transmission of sun energy trough the façade is defined by the Sun Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC), which 
is the fraction of solar radiation from sunlight that passes through the glass and becomes heat inside the 
building. The coefficient is expressed as a number between 0 and 1, which states the percentage of the 
solar heat the glass is transmitting, see Figure 34. Because of the heat contribution to the building has this 
parameter of the facade a relation to the energy efficiency; a lower solar heat gain of a building in a hot 
climate leads to smaller requirements towards the cooling system design. In cold climates the reversed 
application can be applied (Boswell, 2013) (Bøjsø Døre & Vinduer a/s, Januar 2012).

 Indoor Climate 
There is no climate on earth that provides humans with a comfortable environment for long term stays. 
Buildings enable the domain for humans to control the climate to create a comfortable, secure, healthy and 
productive climate for long term stay, which defines the indoor climate. According S. Petersen and C. A.

 Product Modularity on Facades 
The façade is a building component with high requirements towards its design and its functional 
performance. Nevertheless, the building component has been addressed with a modular approach by the 
construction industry. The approach is based on the assembly and composition of standard product, which 
can be applied on various projects and/or prefabricated elements with standardised interfaces to 
accelerate the execution phase.  
 Product Architecture on Facades 
The modular architecture of the façade product is based on the wall type and the erection method. 
There are two different types of walls; standard and custom. Standard facades are created by standardised 
components with standardised dimensions, which have multiple assembly options and give the façade a 
range of applications. The performance of the façade is corresponding to the chosen material and its 
composition. According to Ulrich & Tung (1991) and Ericsson & Erixon (1999) the degree of modularity 
within standard walls are high mainly due to its standard components, which can reach a range of 
applications. Alter (2006), argues that systems that are too simple or standardised are not able to address 
additional features which entails the risk inflexibility. In consideration to facades, this perspective can be 
applied to the design performance but also to functionality. Facades have a tightly connected underlying 
process where a change in the desired function creates changes elsewhere, which can imply the need of 
flexibility. 
Custom curtain walls are designed from scratch in order to reach specified performance and functional 
requirements from the client, construction restriction and regulations. In cases of large construction 
projects in search of an extraordinary exterior, a custom wall is chosen. Fine (2005) defines custom and 
unique systems that cannot be applied in other system as integral. In addition, Compagnolo & Camuffo 
(2009) states integral systems should be supported by an integral organisation, which imply that the 
process and organisation must be closely coupled, with fast information flow and decision-making.   
In addition to the degree of modularity, the erection method dictates the modular approach of the façade 
project. The two most common erection methods are stick and unitized systems. With a stick system are 
the components of the façade assembled on construction site. This system is a compound of tightly fit 
components, in relation to Fine (1998), Ulrich & Tung (1991), Ericsson & Erixon (1999) are considered as an 
integral system. Moreover, stick systems are produced on the construction site and obtain both time and 
space, which is not viewed as a modular approach by Voordijk et al. (2006). 
Unitized systems consist of a prefabricated and preglazed element, which are installed with prefixed anchor 
on the structure. As this aspect result in parallel work with the construction site and is produced as a 
module it is considered by Voordijk et al. (2006), MBS (2015) as a modular construction system.

 Process Modularity on facades 
The process of a façade (product) has three main underlining processes; design, production and execution 
as illustrated in Figure 36 above. In this section the analysis discuss the architecture and interfaces of the 
processes with the focus on the production and execution, due to the argumentation in the introduction of 
the section.   
Campagnolo & Camuffo (2009) argue that the process modularity is strongly related to the product 
modularity. Standardised interfaces and clearly defined design rules of the product module are effecting 
the degree of the process modularity; due to these processes can be executed in parallel. The 
independency of the modular processes enables the opportunity of outsourcing (Sako 2003) 
In this section the effect of modular façade systems (standard wall, unitized) will be discussed in relation to 
the underlining processes of design, production and execution.   
 Process Architecture on facades 
The architecture of the process is defining the structure of the processes and its modular or integral 
characteristic in consideration of dependencies between the different processes. 
The design process consist of three different sub-phases; tender, preliminary design and detailed design 
phase, which in them self are highly integral, due to their iterative nature of the product development. The 
design process can be divided in different sub-deliveries from the involved disciplines which are working 
parallel. This perspective can give the design process a modular approach, but the information required for 
the sub-processes and deliveries highly dependent on each other, thus become integral. The selection of a 
modular façade system with predefined performance parameters can erase some of these dependencies 
and increase the modularity of these processes, but entails further challenges which are elaborated in the 
next chapter. 
The process of production includes the manufacturing of the façade components; glass, profiles, 
sealing/gasket, sunshades system and other matters. Furthermore, the process of assembly (unitized) and 
testing (customised walls) are also major processes in the production. The processes can be placed in the 
supply chain of the product, which is managed by the general contractor and the façade contractor. 
According to Sako (2003), the outsourcing process is a modular approach and can pave the way towards 
modular product architecture. In consideration to facades does this theory perspective apply with standard 
walls and its stock components, though customised wall are strong dependent on the input on specialised 
suppliers in respect to the constructability and the other way around for the production, which makes the 
characteristic of the process more integral. 
The execution of the façade (product) is the third process and is containing the assembly (stick) or 
installation (unitized) of the façade components/elements on the construction site.  The process is 
sequentially and follows a predefined installation plan. According to Façade Contactor 2 the process of 
mounting are in some projects outsourced to other subcontractors than the facade contractor, it is usually 
seen within standard wall and not costume walls. The example underline the effect on the process in 
relation to modular products.

 Process Interfaces on facades 
The interfaces of the process are defined as the dependencies and interconnection between the different 
processes, which mainly are based on information. The modular degree of the process interfaces are 
discussed in relation to the interface characteristics; standardisation and predefinition of the information 
and output.  
The process of the façade can be seen as subsequently dependent, as the design of the façade is the basis 
for the production of the components and furthermore the input for the execution and installation of the 
system. The process interfaces can therefore be interpreted as the communication and information flow 
between the process modules and involved disciplines. Numerous interviewees state that façade-
contractors have to be involved early in the process, in order to contribute with their expertise. “As a 
facade contractor, you want to get in the project process as early as possible, thus you are able to steer the 
project and optimise for the sake of the project as a whole” (Façade contractor 2). “Sub-contractors should 
be managed and involved early in the process” (Design Manager 2). The early involvement of the façade 
contractor is making the process and the interfaces more integral, due to the associated organisational 
modules are integrated across all processes of the façade. Though, the interfaces are getting more 
standardised, as the façade contractor becomes the main link to the supply chain of the components. The 
information flow will become more standardised through the main link and achieve a higher degree of 
constructability, as the façade contractor know what they are capable of producing and know their supply-
chain and what they are capable of delivering. A façade contractor expresses “We like to work in close 
collaboration with the client, so that the super-optimisation link with the contractor is absent” (Façade 
contractor 2). He refers to the general contractor whom split the execution and production of the building 
enclosure system into many subcontracts to save costs, which can be seen as a modular strategy. This 
approach dramatically increases the degree of links/interfaces in the different processes, which entails an 
increased risk of miscommunication and fault in the execution.  
The utilisation of a modular façade system with standard components enables to erase a long row of 
interfaces within and across the subsequential processes. The processes of the design are affected due to 
the predefined performance parameter, which are decoupling the subprocesses of the different disciplines 
and are accelerating the iterative process of the design to adjust the performance towards the unique 
structure. Furthermore, the link and interface from the design process and the production is decoupled due 
to the components are from stock. With the utilisation of standard facades is it possible to decouple the 
execution towards an external montage contractor with the thoroughly prepared procedure of assembly. 
The effect on the process modularity with the utilisation of a modular façade system(product) is great and 
can delete a range of interfaces. According to the design managers of the Case Company is the quality and 
especially the performance of the building more uncertain with the use of these systems.

 Organization modularity on facades  
The capability and competences of the organisation behind the processes has great importance of the 
product. The general contractors must ensure that the architects, engineers, façade contractor and its 
supply chain has the capabilities to execute the processes and are able to deliver the product. The 
organisational modules are directly coupled to the processes and their degree of modularity in 
consideration to the architecture. The interfaces of the organisational modules are seen as the 
collaboration; how do they need to coordinate and collaborate in relation to the different processes and 
how these collaboration platforms are standardised. 
 Organisational Architecture 
The architecture of the organisational modules depends on the product/process needs and on the 
composition of the different involved organisation and their in-house competences, in order to fulfil the 
project requirements. The size of the façade contractor and the in-house competences are therefore 
depending how many external experts/specialists/consults have to be involved in the project. The great 
variety of the project (unique structures) and the differences of the involved organisations and their 
competences are making the architecture of the organisational modules diverse for each project with leads 
to low standardisation.  
Small facade entrepreneur is not dressed for a huge project, they mostly look at the contractual 
content. Bigger more experiences players are more easy to move - thus they have more knowledge 
and know that a smooth process is where we want to go. (Façade contractor 2) 
Other benefits in relation to façade contractors are previous collaboration experience, as the general 
processes are known: One clear benefit of having the same sub-contractors; is that they know the process 
(Design Manager 2). The Case Company has through earlier projects established a relation to several 
partners firms, which can be interpreted as an attempt of organisational architecture standardisation in 
order to increase efficiency between the organisations. 
Due to the close relation to the processes, does the organisational architecture depend on architecture of 
the product, but also the approach of the general contractor in consideration of the involvement of the 
subcontractors; when are the organisational modules coupled to the project. The qualitative research has 
shown that the approach of the general contractor is dependent on the project, the contract form and the 
project manager, which leads to the conclusion that there is no standardised process for involving the sub-
contractors. 
 Organisational Interfaces 
The organisational interfaces are by the authors interpreted seen as the communication and collaboration 
between the different organisational modules.  
The collaboration between the modules is contractual determined in relation to the collaboration terms of 
the general contractor (Design & Build). The collaboration terms are standardised contract from the general 
contractor, which states how the modules have to work together (meeting, workshops, document 
exchange etc.). These collaboration terms must be adjusted in accordance with the complexity of the 
product and the necessity of an integral process. The strong relation towards the product and the 
underlining processes are again supporting the effect of a modular product.

 Façade Design Process 
The design process of facades is not a single process, but a range of simultaneous and cross disciplinary 
activity. As many disciplines are involved in the design organisation, both internal and external, the process 
highly dependents on communication, information flow and decision-making (Linde, 2012). 
According to the multinational façade contractor Permasteelisa Group, the design process depends on a 
series of distinct concurrent multidisciplinary processes that require following (Linde, 2012): 
1. Thinking on building rather than component level 
2. Holistic system performance achieved by iterative design approach including design to 
specification, cost, program and appearance 
3. Effective and reliable decision making in design 
4. Design streamlining and efficiency 
According to L. Urup (Industrial Ph.D. Case Company, 2015), do an efficient design process result in a higher 
quality in construction projects. Nevertheless, most of the utilised methods to plan and coordinate the 
information flow in design process are based on personal and informal communication and the experience 
of the management. 
The qualitative investigation and desk research has shown similar results – there are no standardised 
design process for facades or for curtain walls for instance. In the Case Company the design process is 
based on the sub-deliveries of the different disciplines and spread amongst the internal and external 
organisations. The design manager for façades is acting as connection point between the different 
disciplines and the subcontractor. The process is therefore based on the experience on the design manager 
and has from the perspective of the authors an ad-hoc characteristic.

The design process of the façade development can be considered as a 
performance based design process described by Kalay (1999), illustrated 
in Figure 41. In the process of performance based design the first step is 
to define performance requirements, which for instance could be a 
maximum use of energy, desired amount of daylight, range of 
acceptable indoor temperatures, conceptual thoughts on architectural 
expression, structures etc. The second step is to make a design 
proposal. The performance of the proposal is then predicted, verified 
and scrutinised by the other involved disciplines. The design team is 
then determining whether the performance is desirable or not. If the 
design proposal is desirable, the design can continue to the next phase. 
If not, the designer enters an iterative process where new designs are 
proposed, performance predicted and evaluated until a desirable 
solution emerges. In this traditional workflow, the designer use intuition 
and experience come up with design proposals (Kalay, 1999).

 Façade engineering 
The development of an aesthetic and functional façade requires great expertise, knowledge and experience 
within the design organisation which includes actors above. According to M.R. Patterson (2008), there are 
only a few architects who have the expertise to exercise the capabilities of the façade technology. 
Furthermore, just few engineers have the skills and experience to provide the required analysis of the 
façade system as the design develops. Several façade contractor companies are offering standard pre-
engineered products of limited scope that can be successfully applied to a limited range of façade 
problems. Nevertheless, according to Patterson (2008) specialised companies who provide a complete 
Design & Build services perform most of the custom work. Alternately, there are a few engineering firms 
noted for dealing with these special structures, but even here the involvement of the engineer is as part of 
the design team, and final detailing and engineering, and the role engineer-of-record, typically falls to a 
Design & Build subcontractor. (Patterson, 2008) 
The tightened building regulation and restriction towards the building have increased the complexity and 
the multidisciplinary requirements of facades. This development in the last decades has led to the 
establishment of a new education – Façade Engineering. Façade Engineering is a discipline that builds 
bridges in-between the different disciplines. According to Kragh (2012); the definition of the role of the 
façade engineer has to address the differences in backgrounds and responsibilities of the professionals 
across a varied section of the industry. Different forms of façade engineering are being carried out by 
architects in architectural practices, by engineers in consulting firms and by technicians in different parts of 
the supply chain. While there are different perspectives and the services serve different purposes - the 
subject remains façade engineering. (Kragh, Facade Engineering and the design teams of the future, 2012) 
“Façade Engineering is the art of resolving aesthetic, environmental and structural 
issues to achieve the enclosure of habitable space.” 
                                                                                     (Society of Façade Engineering) 
The competent people dealing with the façade are referred to as façade engineers and the relatively new 
field is becoming increasingly important as part of an integrated approach to design and delivery of 
buildings. The impact of facades, there is according to Kragh (2012) a lack of industry-wide regulation of the 
professionals in this field (Kragh, Facade Engineering and the design teams of the future, 2012).

 DSM - Dependency Structure Matrix 
The Dependency Structure Matrix (DSM) is a method to visualise and analyse systems in a variety of 
application areas. The DSM is used to relate entities of one kind to each other. The entities can be 
components of a product or tasks to complete a project and can be used to identify appropriate teams, 
workgroups and an ideal sequence of the tasks (TUM - Technische Universitet München, Prof. Lindemann, 
2009). The DSM represents the relations, dependencies and the information-flow in a project and can 
handle sequential, parallel, and coupled activities (A. Oloufa, 2004). Furthermore, DSM is useful to optimise 
information flow in construction design project (C. Yi-ping, September 2009). According to Goark (1993), 
the management of information flow is the key to plan a design process successfully (Groak, 1993). There 
are a lot of research project within the DSM method in different areas. McCord and Eppinger (1993) apply 
DSM to a lot of engineering projects, such as semiconductor design and automobile engineering design 
(Eppinger, 1993). Chen (2003) investigates in the product development in terms of project programming 
and framework reconstruction (C.H. Chen, 2003). 
A DSM is a square matrix with an equal number of rows and columns that shows relationships between 
elements/tasks in a system. Since the behaviour and value of many systems are determined by interactions 
between its constituent elements, DSM have become increasingly useful and important in recent years.  In 
comparison to other system modelling methods DSM has two main advantages (TUM - Technische 
Universitet München, Prof. Lindemann, 2009):   
 
It provides a simple and concise way to represent a complex system  
 
It is amenable to powerful analyses, such as clustering (to facilitate modularity) and sequencing (to 
minimise cost and schedule risk in processes)

An elements (nodes) degree in an un-directed graph is defined as the number of elements that are 
connected to that specific element (S. Wassermann, 1994). In the DSM of the workshop the elements are 
directed, hence it is a process analysis. The approach results in an in-degree and out-degree. The In-degree 
indicates the number of activity elements, which are depending/sub-sequentially. A high in-degree of an

The out-degree is representing dependency of prior activity elements and indicates how many activities and 
output the element is depending on. An element with a high out-degree value can therefore be interpreted 
as an activity which shall proceed late in the process, though can the perspective not be taken for given if 
the in-degree value is high as well. The cost calculation has the highest out-degree, which is reasoned by 
that all activities in the design or changes in the development are affecting the cost of the façade. 
Furthermore, the selection of the façade system has a high out-degree, because most design decisions are 
effecting the component composition of the facade system. 
The betweenness centrality is an indicator of an elements centrality in a network. It is equal to the number 
of shortest paths from all vertices to all other elements that pass through that node. An element with high 
betweenness centrality has a large influence on the transfer of information through the network (S. 
Wassermann, 1994). In relation to the DSM output from the workshop the elements related to; the cost 
calculation, the selection of the façade system, the design and geometry, and the design of sunscreen have 
==PAGE:102==
Revealing the Complexity in the Design Process of Facades through Application of Modularity 
A Foundation for successful Design Management 
87 
 
a high influence on the network, due to the indication of a high betweenness centrality. But the choice of 
materials, the structural design and the choice of façade contractor are playing central role in the activity 
network. 
The closeness, as intuitively indicates by the term, focus on how close the element is to all the other 
elements in the activity network. Closeness centrality describes the extent of influence of an element on a 
network (S. Wassermann, 1994). In consideration to the workshop results, the activities with a high 
betweeness centrality also have a high closeness, which underline the tight connected activities of the 
façade design process.  
The clustering coefficient is a measure of the degree to which nodes in a graph tend to cluster together. A 
large number of networks show a tendency for link formation between neighboring vertices, i.e., the 
network topology deviates from uncorrelated random networks in which triangles are sparse. The tendency 
is called clustering and it reflects the clustering of edges into tightly connected “neighborhoods” (J. 
Saramäki, 2007). In consideration of the structural profiling data the clustering coefficient of all notes are 
quite high, which is grounded in the high degree of dependencies in-between. The analysis of acoustic and 
the calculation of indoor climate have the highest measurements of the clustering coefficients. It is 
assumed that the few but iterative dependencies are causing this coefficient.  
According to Watts & Strogatz (1998) most real-world networks and in particular social networks are 
tending to create tightly knit groups, characterised by a relatively high density of ties (D.J. Watts, 1998). 
This tendency is also represented in the network of the design process of facades.

7.5.2.3 
Discussion and critics of DSM analysis results 
The DSM method has managed to identify a long row of dependencies and iterative processes across the 
activity elements in the design phase of facades and give a perception of the complexity in the process. The 
identified activity elements are main activities and not detailed action of specific actors or department, 
which induce the cross functional characteristic of some elements, e.g. indoor climate and choice of 
materials. 
The clustering algorithm CAM-software has enabled the research to create several process modules of the 
façade development. The simplification and exclusion of the parallel but highly effected elements (design & 
geometry, cost calculation, selection of façade system) has made it possible to create a more clear picture 
of the process by clustering the remaining activity element with high dependencies. Nevertheless, there are 
still a high degree of dependencies outside the cluster which pinpoint the integrality of the process. 
The multifunctional process modules and the high degree of dependencies outside of the clusters can be 
read as the need of collaboration and communication between the actors due to the information flow, 
which is elaborated in the following sections.  
Data input; the activities and the dependencies of the DSM were identified by the cross-functional 
participants in the workshop. Not all actors and organisations involved in a real facades development 
process was represented. The lack of knowledge and competences result in uncertainty of the results, 
because not all activities and dependencies in the process are identified. The results of the DSM analysis 
can therefore not be seen as a trustworthy or reliable design process of the facades, but more as an 
example and test of the applicability of this method. 
The visual indication of dependencies and cross functional process clusters, indicates that the DSM method 
can be applied as a management tool in the design process as a foundation to monitor dependencies, 
create cross functional workgroups and establish meeting/workshop plans. In order to use the method as a 
planning or management tool is it essential that all disciplines are represented in a workshop, so all 
dependencies and activities are identified. Furthermore it is important that actors in the different clusters 
are elaborating on their cross functional process within, by creating a more detailed DSM sub-set or an 
Information Pull (IP) with a higher degree of detail.  
The authors believe that the planning method indirectly will influence the collaboration ability of the cross 
functional design team through the visualisation of dependencies across the internal and external 
organisations, which eventually also will influence the understanding of the other side of the table.  
The evaluation of the workshop [Appendix A – Workshop] has provided a good feedback of the facilitation 
and supported the usability in consideration of other design processes.

 Project changes 
According to Bertelsen (2002), the design phase and construction phase have a wicked nature, which 
implies that there is no optimal solution, as solutions often are discovered throughout the project within 
the iterative processes, which makes project changes unavoidable (Bertelsen, 2002). 
The iterative cross-functional and inter-organisational process of façade implies that effective designing 
often means being aware of the various effects of all design parameter at the right time – which again 
points to project change procedure and the communication platform between the actors (Linde, 2012). 
Project changes are a part of any given project, it support the project in the sense that; without project 
changes the project will not evolve. A large proportion of the current project changes and subsequent 
problems connect to how the design phase is planned and executed. Project changes are inevitable; 
nevertheless, a change in the project has to be communicated and evaluated by any actors dependent on 
the change, thus balancing the value it adds to the project.  
In relation to causality theory, the change can be seen as an effect, which is a result of a cause. A large-

 Collaboration 
According to the experiences of Society of Façade are successful projects generally depending on a high 
degree of collaboration across the design team and throughout the supply chain. If the collaboration and 
the contractual set-up are handled competently, value can be added through excellent technical and 
aesthetical detailing and risk can be managed in terms of cost and program (Kragh, 2012). S. Bertelsen 
(2002) also state that the wicked nature of the design phase actors have to collaborate in order to 
“complete the project successfully” (Bertelsen, 2002). 
The term collaboration meaning: “to work together, especially in a joint intellectual effort” (Dictionary T. F., 
2015). In relation to the Business Dictionary is collaboration defined by following: “working together 
towards a common goal” (Dictionary(II), 2015).  
C. Jones (2010) describes that the collaboration of a project team are influenced by following six key 
factors: Engagement, Being authentic, Learning and Discovery, Respect, Positive vibe and Focus on results 
(Jones, 2010).   
Architecture, Engineering & Construction (AEC) projects create formalised but temporary organisations that 
include many groups – design, construction and fabrication. Higher standards, tighter regulations, and 
owner demands to deliver attractive buildings that are on-time and at lower cost add to the pressure. 
Without strong collaboration, coordination and consistency a project can result in tremendous waste and 
cost overruns (Systems, 2014).

 Design Responsibility and Communication 
It is often seen that construction projects start with the production/execution, before the total designs of 
the building is finished because of time limit. The result of this approach is an overlap of the design and 
execution phase. In order to ensure the design cost are within budget and the smooth implementation of 
the project, the importance of design management is increasingly recognised in recent years (Austin, 
Baldwin, Li, & Waskett, 1999).  
One characteristic of construction design projects is that the relationships among the stakeholders contain 
information flow conflicts. Traditionally, the techniques used to plan building design “do not allow the 
effect of variations and delays to be fully understood within an iterative process and they monitor progress 
based upon the completion of drawing work and other ´design deliverables´ as opposed to the availability of 
key pieces of information” (Austin, Baldwin, & Newton, 1996). 
The responsibility of design can vary between projects, which is dependent on the contract form and 
organisational composition. The Case Company is a contractor with an integrated Design & Engineering 
(D&E) department, where the main disciplines in the engineering fields of construction and civil work are 
represented. The Case Company can through its brought competences provide consultancy of design, 
engineering and execution, and manage Design & Build projects, where the overall design responsibility 
lays within the hands of the contractor. However, the curtain wall system is in the most typical scenarios 
are delivered as one of a number of Design-Build systems on a Design-Bid-Build façade.  
According to K. Kazmierczak (2015) is the design responsibility a key issue in the façade design process. 
Kazmierczak (2015) has identified a gap in communication and misunderstood delegation of responsibilities 
which have led to major failures of building enclosures. The connection between each façade system lies in 
a grey area between the scopes of the architect and engineers, as illustrated in Figure 50. The coordination 
of the functional façade control layers for the transition of thermal waterproofing, air, vapour, and other 
control layers are not addressed efficiently. The communication gap is affected in the shift from the first 
design stage to the next. The architectural oversight of the structural engineering, mechanical engineers 
and other consulting disciplines like acoustic, blast, lighting and fire in the first stage can becomes a 
challenge; because the disciplines often do not speak the same language. In the observations of K. 
Kazmierczak (2015) are the transition details, the coordination of the secondary structural design and the 
specifications of design data only described sufficiently when a specialised façade engineering professional 
is involved – narrowing the gap between architectural documentation and submittals. (Kazmierczak, 2015)

 Product Modularity on Façade Design 
The product of the design phase is challenged by the high requirements towards the facade. The design 
needs to be compliant with the building restrictions and regulations, fulfil the design and functional 
performance of the stakeholders (Client, Architect, Engineers, Contractors etc.), deliver the precise input 
for the production facilities and furthermore be constructible in the execution and mounting phase. 
The product contains the information in form of detailed drawings, specifications, calculations, execution 
descriptions and reports. The information packages are interpreted as sub-product of the main product – a 
case specific building project. The subsection will discuss modularity in the information package with the 
perspective of product, process and organisation in relation to architecture and interfaces. 
 Product Architecture on Façade Design 
The architecture of the information (façade design) and the components (detailed drawings, specification 
etc.) are strongly influenced by the geometry, the structural components of the building, the type of 
façade, the climate and surrounding environment of the proposed project. The influencing factors are 
challenging a standardisation of the information composition. One of the main competencies of the Case 
Company lies within unique commercial construction project: ”What we build is unique structures” (Design 
Manager 2). The unique structures and project-based characteristics of the construction industry requires 
renewed and redefined project-material corresponding to the final product. The aspect strongly challenge 
the modularity concept of the product, also in relation to the component-swapping or component-sharing 
modularity perspective of Ulrich & Tung (1991).  
The anchor system is standardised by the façade contractor and can be interpreted as a modular design 
component for several independent projects. The anchors design still need to be tested and proved 
corresponding to the specific project.   
The perspective has shown that the components of design are case specific. The composition of the 
components are the same, due to all design aspects like the structural function, weather-tightness, energy 
efficiency, fire analysis, acoustic analysis etc. have to be addressed for all kinds of projects, which can 
create a framework for a standardise product architecture. ISO and EN/DA standards have in this 
perspective a clear influence of the architecture of the façade design. The application of the standards are 
contributing the standardisation of calculations methods and testing reports.  
The utilisation of a best practise design manual, e.g. Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation - Best 
Practice Guide can influence the architecture of the product (façade design information). As all aspects of 
the design and its functional performance are considered, as all chapters of the sub-products (energy 
efficiency calculation, structural analysis, acoustic report etc.) are addressed and composed in the same 
structure. Nevertheless, dictating that everyone must use a best practise as such seems unreliable – and 
can stop the creative process of finding innovative solution. It is assumed that general contractor have the 
power to dictates what is needed to enable a successful project, they could create standards for façade 
projects that façade contractor can follow. No such standards have been located within the Case Company, 
and are assumed to be knowledge and experience based. As reports and drawings often are specialised 
within construction projects, the interchangeability is vague (Ulrich & Tung, 1991) (Fine C. H., 1998) and 
result in an integral product. 
Nevertheless, even if the architecture of the product (structure and composition of the design) is 
standardised, the information of the sub products is highly synchronised to each other and towards the 
other main designs of the building, like the primary structure and the building service systems, which gives

 Product Interfaces on Façade Design 
The interfaces of the product has two different perspectives; the Interfaces of the components within the 
façade design and the interfaces towards the other main designs of the building. Interfaces of the product 
are viewed as information dependencies. 
The information links within the façade design is as mentioned in the subsection before highly 
synchronised. The information interfaces between the components of the design are hard to distinguish 
and verify, due to the integrality of the product. However, the aspect related to the interfaces of the 
physical façade components are crucial and challenging the design (K. Kazmierczak, 2015). The coordination 
of the functional façades, waterproofing, air, vapour, and other control layers are not addressed efficiently.  
The interfaces towards other designs can be revealed through a DSM analysis of the design process. The 
analysis has verified the main interfaces as; primary structure design, the building service systems design 
(HVAC, indoor climate, el etc.), building energy design, fire analysis and logistics/execution. The central 
information interface of the façade design towards the building service system and the building energy 
design are the U-, VLT- and SHGC-values. The primary structure is the structural function (stiffness) of the 
façade and load transferred through the anchor. In correlation to the fire design of the building, the 
information interface is the fire resistance of the façade system. The interface to the execution planning lies 
within erection method, the geometry, weight and size. The series of physical interfaces in relation to the 
façade are vast and include several different disciplines, and add to the amount of interfaces and iterative 
processes.    
Another important interface of the product (façade design) is towards the production and execution in 
correlation to constructability. ”It is truly important that a contractor have someone who can validate the 
projects constructability” (Façade Contractor 2). In order to improve the product towards constructability 
and reduce project changes and costs in the process, it is necessary to incorporate this initiative early in the 
process. ”The cost is excessively greater, when changes occur late in the process” (Architect 1).  
The information in the final design must be identical to the information used for the calculation and 
specifications in the other designs. Which result in a set of interfaces that are strongly interconnected and 
are fundamental to deliver a functional design. The information transfer must be managed closely in the 
iterative design process, with a detailed design plan that indicates all dependencies.

 Process Architecture on Façade Design 
The architecture of the façade design process is structured in a tender, preliminary design and detailed 
design phase. The phases are to some extend a standardised process frame within the construction 
industry. The qualitative analysis has shown that there are no standardised procedures towards the 
different phases and procedures are shaped for the project by the stakeholders (client, architect, design 
managers, general contractor, façade contractor). Furthermore, the tender, preliminary design and detailed 
design phase are sub-sequential processes and are highly dependent on the output (sub-product) from the 
previous phase, due to the iterative nature of the product development. These perspectives lead to the 
assumption that these processes are highly integral, because these processes and their sub-products are 
not coupled loosely in reference to Fine´s modular perspective.  
On the other hand, the design phases can be divided into different sub-deliveries or sub-processes from the 
involved disciplines. The disciplines are working parallel in their department or external companies. The 
division of disciplines can be interpreted as a modular perspective, but the information required for the 
developed of their deliveries is highly dependent and synchronised to each other, thus integral in reference 
to the product and process symmetry of Ericsson and Erixon (1999).  
Another perspective of process modularity lies within the outsourcing process of sub-products (Sako 2003), 
which according to Campagnolo & Camuffo (2009) can pave the way to modularity due to the indirect 
influence on the product architecture. Outsourcing and contracting out the division of labour is a common 
practise of the general contractor. The initiative of outsourcing products or process modules requires 
cautious management of the information and standardised design rules in order successfully achieve a 
modular product and process architecture (Baldwin & Clark, 1997). Standardised design rules are only 
applicable for standard façade systems. Custom facades have high integral product architecture due to the 
performance dependencies, which in reference to the paths towards module outsourcing (Sako, 2003), 
makes the façade industry stuck at point b (Integral Product Architecture/Outsourced Manufacturing). Yet 
again, this relates back to the missing in-house standards of the general contractor.  
The DSM analysis has identified the links and dependencies across the activity network and created the 
perception, that the design process of facades and its architecture is highly complex and integral. The 
clustering algorithm of the Cambridge Advanced Modeller (CAM) software has provided an overview of 
which different activities have the highest dependencies to each other. The DSM clusters are interpreted by 
authors as process modules and can be seen as an approach to standardise and modulate the architecture 
of the design processes. The activities in the workshop were not case specific and had a brought 
perspective, which enables the procedure to be applied on other processes as well. 
The authors believe that the DSM method can provide the framework to plan the design process, to initiate 
effective project changes communication, create collaboration among the design team and establish 
effective cross-functional design meetings. The implementation of the method would influence the 
approach of the current design process and shape a standard for the process architecture. This is plausible 
because the range of standards, requirements, activities and actors of façade projects are the same but act 
differently corresponding to the project.

to validate the functional performance and to evaluate and agree on the visual performance with all 
involved stakeholders. “The mock-up gives you references and an overview on how the module is connected 
and assembled. One clear benefit is approval on several components at once” (Façade Contractor 2). “A 
mock-up is a very helpful accessory, it is high in price and resources, nevertheless you end up with a solid

 Process Interfaces on Façade Design 
The interfaces of the design processes have two perspectives; the interfaces between the different design 
processes and the interfaces within the different design phases, see Figure 51. The interfaces are 
interpreted as information flow and dependencies between activities which are approach through 
communication, design meeting, electronic information exchange etc. 
The tender phase usually consists of the client, the architects and consulting engineers. The processes in 
the phase can be executed by an external organisation before it transferred to the actual design 
organisation. Due to this approach the phase is interpreted as loose coupled (Compagnolo & Camuffo, 
2009) (Fine C. H., 1998). The level of detail and the complexity in the tender design is low and can be seen 
as modular process. In the preliminary design are the functional performance issues addressed in an 
iterative design process, which increases the involved organisations, the complexity and the multiple 
interconnections and interfaces (Fixon, 2006). The degree of complexity and interfaces makes this phase 
highly integral. The detailed design is reliant on the output of the previous phase and should first be 
introduced after the components of the mock-up are approved and tested. The mock-up and the test 
reports are therefore seen as the main interface between these two phases. The detailed design process 
focus on detailed drawings and solve the last problems in relation to special component, which also is 
manage through a iterative process between discipline. It is assumed that the detailed process is more 
modular, because the major issues are solved, which minimise the interfaces between the design 
organisations. Project changes in detailed design process increase complexity exponentially, as one change 
starts a ripple effect and the effect of a change is difficult to trace and manage (Alter, 2006).

 Organisation Architecture on Façade Design

The organisational proximity is discussed in reference to Fine´s (1998) definitions of proximity, which

The geographic proximity is based on the physical distance of the organisations and can be met by

integral process. Several interviewees are stating that a project office entails great beneficial for the

The geographic proximity can also be related to suppliers. An international contractor can entail low 
geographical proximity and effect the degree of organisational modularity, but requires a standardised 
information flow in order to support the integral process. 
The cultural proximity captures commonality of language, business mores, ethical standards and laws, 
among other things. Through interviews and qualitative analysis has it been identified that the involved

significant gap between the cultures within a construction project”, “There is a very traditional conflict”

design and those who execute the design” (Design Manager 4). “The construction industry is based on

more of them self than others” (BIM Coordinator). The conflict could be rooted because of separated 
budgets and lack of understanding of the deliveries and challenges in other involved departments. 
The interest conflicts and differences in the cultures and mind-sets of the affiliated disciplines are resulting 
in a low cultural proximity, which can entail major challenges in the integral process of the façade 
development. In order to combat the interest conflict is a strong inter-organisational collaboration platform 
is needed, in order to establish a common goal and interest (Alter, 2006) (Russell & Taylor, 2009). This 
initiative lies within the responsibility of the design management.

managerial control and the inter-personal and inter-team inter-dependencies. The design process of 
facades involves many disciplines and actors spread amongst different companies. Due to the 
characteristics are the organisational proximity interpreted as low. The design management is

9 Standpoint 
The research have discovered that the design process of facades has a high degree of complexity. Mainly 
because of a range of independent actors whom all are affiliated in the process, which creates many 
interconnections and interfaces in-between with a high degree of dependency, resulting in integrality. 
Moreover, the actors are involved in iterative process throughout the design phases; additional 
dependencies make the process fluctuating and difficult to manage.  
At the construction site, in the execution phase, modularity has obvious benefits and create a more smooth 
execution phase, moving manufacturing away from the site and speeding up the mounting. The research 
imply that when prefabrication is used, the design have to be more thorough and precise and thus need 
more time to design. This aspect demand that sub-contractors are involved early in the process and the 
decision in relation to the façade are locked on an early stage, which result in limited project changes and 
increased constructability.  
The construction industry have not yet adapted to this concept, as internal interview and investigation 
states that the time in the design phase are limited, and decision and planning tools are not sufficient. As a 
result, decisions are often delayed and courses project changes and a new set of iterative process, which 
further extent the design process. The authors believe that this transition needs to be handled more 
efficiently with stronger design management and better planning tools. 
The findings and result of the DSM tool, could possibly be an excellent tool for managers to use in the 
design process of facades, as it enables a mapping of dependencies. Moreover, by applying DSM design 
managers could plan the design process, structure meetings and gather the team of experts in respect to 
human and social values.  
Like a module façades can accelerate the execution phase, it will accelerate the process as well if the 
proposed system is a standard system are adequate for the façade project. The concept of unique 
structures dictated by surrounded environment, architectural design, wishes of the client, functional and 
structural requirements, directly contradict with the standardised systems. As unique structures often, 
have a unique envelope or custom curtain wall the principle of finding it in a catalogue are dispelled.  
Nevertheless, the concept of custom module facade can and are used with great benefits, as the custom 
walls are alike thus benefitting from the concept of repetition, but the utterly important initiative is that 
more time and resources are put in the design phase and the time is used more sufficient, eventually with 
tools like DSM.     
As seen in the modular construction examples, the use ability of standardised façade modules are most 
relevant in projects like e.g. housing units, shopping mall and hotels chains, where the amount of repetition 
are great. Numerous organisation practice this with great benefits as they benefit from economics of scale.   
Disregarding new buildings, building restrictions and regulation are pushing the construction industry to 
synchronise building service system and the building envelope in order to reach the imposed energy 
consumption of the building. This development challenges the market of the modular façade systems and 
their predefined performance parameters. As energy expenses are rising and renovation project do not 
need to comply with the ruling regulations, the renovation-market eventually carrying new opportunities 
for modular system.

10 Design Guide 
The design of a façade encompasses highly complex, iterative and integral process and requires great 
attention of the design management, in order to develop a functional façade that accommodates the 
performance requirements from the client, architect and engineers.  
This section is a preliminary Design Guide that reflects the recommendations, which are gathered from the 
literature review and interviews with Design Manager, Architects and Façade Contractors in Denmark.  
Choosing a facades system 
The first step when initiating a façade project is to categorise the type of façade and determine what kind 
of façade system can be utilised to fulfil the desired design and functional performance, see Figure 52: 
Standard: Limitation of architectural design, shorter design process (predefined performance 
parameters/no testing), fast production (components from stock), lower costs 
Custom: Possibility of unique design performance, able to reach high functional performance, vast iterative 
design process, performance test required, higher costs 
Stick: Assembly on site, quick delivery, smaller investment and advantage at smaller construction projects, 
very long spans possible, suitable for complex facades with little repetition and varying modules. 
Unitised: Assembly of façade elements in facility, greater investment but enables acceleration of the 
execution process (building enclosure), possibility of integrated sunscreen, logistic constraints. 
Glazing method: Method is determined by the architectural design and the thermal performance of the 
connection. Exterior batten is the most common method.

Critical façade performance parameters – focused planning and common goal 
1. Indoor climate vs. energy efficiency; these functions are highly dependent and must be solved in an

Planning 
The Façade Contractor must be integrated early in the preliminary design phase in order to include the 
main expertise in the design, reduce the rework and evaluate the projects constructability. 
A cross-functional workshop with all involved disciplines are strongly recommended in order to plan the 
design process of facades. The participants have to utilise following planning tools for the design schedule; 
 
Dependency Structure Matrix (DSM); the primary tool to investigate dependencies between the main 
design activities of the involved disciplines and the functional requirements (http://www.dsmweb.org/)  
 
Cambridge Advanced Modeller; outperform cluster algorithm to create cross functional process 
modules (http://www-edc.eng.cam.ac.uk/cam)  
 
Information Pull planning (IP); Identify the production time for each activity and create a detailed 
design schedule and information flow for each process module in collaboration with the involved 
disciplines. If the process module is too complex another DSM subset can be applied. 
A holistic view followed by a detailed view of the operations in the project will be visualised and create a 
coherence around the complexity within the design phase, which all participants have to work towards in 
order to deliver successfully.  
 
Minimise the amount of sub-contractors affiliated in the façade contract. Splitting the contract into 
sub-contracts create increased risks and uncertainty.  
 
All challenges in relation to structural- and function-requirements of the façade must be concretised to 
the specific project.  
Competencies 
 
Ensure that the façade contractor and its supply chain got the competencies to lift the entire façade 
contract. 
 
Ensure that the design organisation got all required specialist and competencies to design the façade. 
Especially constructability, which often result in unforeseen project changes.

11 Reflection 
 Reflection on Methodology 
The thesis has investigated module façades using a combination of system thinking with a baseline study in 
complexity and modularity. The concept build on product, process and organisations, which is seen as 
systems that are highly interconnected. While the research has not been focusing on human or social 
values, it has nevertheless tried to underline that this aspect is utterly important when dealing with project-
based industries.  
The concept of applying manufactory theory on a project-based industry like construction is believed to 
have a positive output as it produces a more system-based holistic view for managers. Manufactory 
benefits from repetition and standardisation, nevertheless, the construction industry’s wicked nature and 
flexible design challenge the concept of repetition and standardisation across construction projects. That 
being said, repetition and standardisation are applied within each construction project independently. The 
knowledge created in each independent project is valuable knowledge that lies within the team and 
managers, but as new projects calls for a new team - resource management allocates the team to 
independent projects which splits the knowledge and destroy what was created.          
In relation to the analytical framework, the complexity within the physical product of facades and the 
design process of the façade was revealed. By revealing the complexity, it enables the research to create 
modules and a design guide towards a modular design process.  
It has been a challenge to categorise and identify within the design process of facades corresponding to the 
framework. This could be because of the façade project in itself is vast and complex. Counter wise, the 
framework was much more manageable on the physical façade. One challenge occurred, in design process 
of facades when adding interfaces to the three interconnected dimensions of product, process and 
organisation. Differentiating between the interfaces in relation to other systems is challenging as the 
research deals with a design process and both product, processes and organisations work together in a non 
structured way. One interface has effects in another systems product, process and organisation, which 
leads to the perception and proves that the true challenge in the design process is that every actor is 
dependent on each other and the lack of structure adds significantly to the amount of rework, changes, 
frustration and cost of the project.         
The empirical data comes from both internal and external forces, which has strengthened the output, but 
more interviews and supplemented interviews would add value to the investigation. The workshop adds 
value to the output, but the lack of expertise and disciplines resulted in a shallow result, of which output 
cannot be directly used in practice. It is more a holistic view on the complexity and a structured way to 
reveal it. Moreover, the DSM and the workshop poses as a reliable and interesting perspective on how to 
become a better design manager. Tool number two for the workshop was Information Pull, which proved 
to be too detailed as a tool to map large design process; it was simply too specific to get a holistic view.

 Future research 
Following aspects would be worth pursuing in future research: 
 
Future research could investigate and use the DSM on other design processes. It would be 
beneficial to manage it on a smaller and less complicated system than the façade system on unique 
structures. Applying DSM on a smaller design process could prove the true applications of the tool. 
 
It would be interesting to discover the dependencies and information flow within each cluster 
discovered in the analysis of the DSM, mapping them in detail and revealing the next level of 
complexity. Tools like Information Pull are able to map on a detailed level, and are able to map 
parallel activities and describe exactly who will deliver what to whom in relation to time. Another 
tool is MDM (Multiple Domain Matrix), which is similar to DSM, but in addition add people 
responsible for activities.            
 
Investigating how BIM (Building Information Model) and VDC (Virtual Design and Construction) can 
benefit the design phase and what challenges it bring might provide an interesting topic for future 
research. Further on, investigating the possibilities of standardised micro models within the 
program and by using information within the micro models calculations, material etc. would be at 
hand could be of interest.  
 
Research on factors within collaboration and human/social values in the design process and how it 
truly effect a projects output could provide a topic for future research. How can construction 
projects benefits of using the same composition of team members?  
 
The possibilities and benefits of standardising a constructions projects supply chain.  
 
How can a large organisation implement tools such as DSM on design processes?  
 Contribution  
The thesis is assumed to have contributed within following aspects. 
 Contribution to modularity and complexity studies 
The major contribution of the thesis in relation to modularity and complexity, is the analytical framework. A 
combination of system thinking and modularity theory reveals the underlying complexity. By using the 
concept from Wilson & Perumal (2009), product, process and organisation, along-side with the visible 
design rules from Baldwin & Clark (1997), architecture and interfaces (and relevant standards), the 
framework is a structured way of viewing and thus revealing and understanding the complexity within a 
processes in multiple levels.  
Another contribution is the way the research deals with products and sub-products alike. The perspective 
gives a view on what a system specifically delivers and how it contributes to other systems. Moreover, 
applying modularity and tools to manage modularity within process and organisations have had limited 
attention in reason history. The approach is believed to have a true impact on the way design processes are 
structured and executed. It is found that even though a process is integral it is possible to apply the 
perspective of modularity and benefit the management. 
 Contribution to design process in Construction 
The first contribution to design management is the “Design Guide”, seen in Chapter 9. With the knowledge 
we gained throughout the report, this is our recommendations on how to ease and structure design 
management within facades.

 Reflection on Case Company 
The Case Company is evolving gradually which is a healthy sign but it still has to adapt to the modern way 
of constructing. As proved, the Case Company is challenged by managing external actors and decisions, and 
thus project material is often delayed.  
As one of the core competencies of the Case Company is planning and management, and many design 
managers do not think they can plan corresponding to need. Which result in an assumption that the design 
process is lacking behind in relation to the production on the construction site. The need for better 
planning tools is urgent as complexity gradually rises due to the increase of modularisation and 
prefabrication, which causes the design process to be efficient on a level it is not adapted to.

standards should consist of communication, information and collaboration as presented in the design 
guide. Moreover, making a standard for how the design process of façade contracts should be managed 
and planned will be a necessity. The DSM tool can support the Case Company to plan the design process of 
facades more thoroughly. 
Ongoing projects, individualism and experience-based knowledge challenge standardisation of such 
documents, as managers believe that this knowledge is the value that they represent. This makes the use of 
standards difficult in practice, as not all will get on-board. If the Case Company follow our 
recommendations and standardising interfaces, they have to do it step-by-step in order to prove that this is 
a more efficient way of reaching the goal and a manager can still be innovative and knowledgeable within 
management.  
It will take years to redirect the design process of facades, as agreements etc. have to be standardised 
before initiating on a new project. As parts of the personnel are scattered around on construction sites and 
offices a turning point have to be decided upon. By introducing the concepts in new façade projects, it will 
gradually change the way it is in practise, but it will take years to implement it throughout.   
A possibility is to standardise the supply chain in order to standardise deliveries and known work practises. 
This aspect is difficult to pursue, as the nature of the industry´s supply chain is tender-based. The tender 
result in innovative solutions in chase of the best price, but the result is different sub-contractors in 
different projects. By creating standards that are more detailed, it will support the design managers in a 
more structured approach towards sub-contractors.

12 Conclusion 
The literature review has revealed the high potential of the modularity concept in the production industry, 
through the effect of component economy of scale, outsourcing, reduction of the order lead time and the 
ability to manage product variation and complexity. The construction industry has translated this concept in 
prefabrication of building components. 
The thesis investigated to what degree the concept of modularity can be applied on the underlying 
processes of façade project. The research has identified that the modularity concept can unfold and 
transform some opportunities in the production and execution of the façade project, but the analysis of the 
design process has shown that this process is difficult to untangle. The unique project-based characteristics 
and the required integral design processes are diminishing the application potential and opportunities of 
the modularity concept in the design process of facades. The façade project is a complex system, where 
activities are proceeding simultaneously and the involved organisations are working highly integral. The 
main reasons for the integral nature is that the design needs to be addressed towards the unique 
construction project and its environment, and that the façade performance is highly synchronised with 
other building system designs. Fragmented organisations, high requirements, project changes, insufficient 
planning and slow decision-making, adds significantly to the challenges within the design process of façade 
projects. The utilisation of standard wall systems can accelerate the design process and decouple some of 
the sub-processes, due to predefined performance parameter. Nevertheless, the thesis has discovered that 
standard façade systems only are applicable to a limited amount of façade projects and that the design 
need to be adjusted to the specific project, which are weakening the modular concept and its 
opportunities.  
The modular approach of unitized elements has a high potential to accelerate the execution process, as the 
workload and coordination are transferred to the design process, which in turn require enhanced design 
management. In relation to the research question, the modularity concept is applicable on the façade 
production and execution, where the elements thrive on repetition and standardisation. However, the 
underlying process and organisations, producing the facades are usually unknown and diverse, which do 
not comply with modularity. The physical facade and the components of the system are acting modular as 
they are produced with standardised processes within the production facility. As façade contractors and 
their supply chain vary in their expertise and product portfolio the concept of organisational modularity 
disappears and become unchangeable. The characteristic of the varying expertise and competency is seen 
throughout the construction industry, in relation to architects, sub-contractors and engineering 
consultants. 
The internal investigation of the Case Company has revealed crucial problems and challenges in the design 
organisation, which are grounded in insufficient design scheduling, invisible dependencies, low 
collaboration among the internal and external disciplines and time pressure. The problems are amplified by 
the increasing workload of the prefabrication within modern construction projects. The thesis has 
investigated if the perspective and concept of modularity can resolve the problems in the design phase. The 
DSM method has successfully enabled the identification of dependencies and interfaces between the 
crucial cross organisational design activities which are related to the façade design process. Furthermore, 
the method has successfully established cross-functional process modules. The results and the evaluation 
of the workshop conclude that the DSM, a production tool from the modularity concept, is a valid planning 
tool for the design management. The results of the workshop and the DSM analysis reflect great potential 
to address the problems of time, design scheduling and invisible dependencies in the design phase of the 
Case Company. Additionally, the planning method indirectly influences the collaboration ability of the

cross-functional design teams. Visualising dependencies between internal and external organisations will 
eventually influence the understanding and respect to one another. In relation to the research question, 
project-based processes challenge the concept of modularity. But the concept can be applied and resolve 
some of the major problems discovered in the integral and iterative design process. 
The analysis has identified that the product of the design process is detailed, project specific and highly 
synchronised information, which gives this product a complex and integral nature. The product is 
knowledge-based and produced by human resources which make complexity and uncertainty thrive and 
process management difficult. The design process of facades involves many activities and disciplines, which 
results in a high degree of multidimensional information interfaces. The current management tools of the 
design process are insufficient and based on the individual design managers knowledge, expertise and 
experience. A standardised approach to identify and coordinate the interfaces is crucial in order to 
establish an efficient design process and fulfil the performance and function requirements. The thesis has 
identified that an efficient information flow and collaboration between organisations are necessities to 
execute a successful project. The information flow and collaboration between the internal and external 
design organisations rely on detailed planning and on contractual documents like ICT-agreement and 
collaboration terms.  
The implementation of the DSM method and the improvement of the collaboration terms and ICT-
agreements will be the first step in the right direction to handle the complex nature of the façade design 
process and enable future opportunities of modularity. The research leads to following recommendations 
when initiating on a façade project:  
 
Implement inter-organisational DSM-workshop, to identify interfaces of design activities, 
create detailed design schedule and establish commitment. 
 
 
Establish efficient and effective Information Flow among the design organisations. By 
developing detailed and realistic Collaboration Terms and ICT-agreements.
