ABSTRACT 
The complexity of project management (PM) processes is growing, and the 
need to understand them is becoming more important. This study examines 
the implications of deploying Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning 
(ML) technologies in PM processes while focusing on ethical and security 
concerns that bridge the gap between AI and humans. Literature reviews 
were conducted to identify the potential of AI in PM processes, and key 
findings and discussions are presented. The study highlights the benefits of 
using AI, such as improving productivity, cutting project timelines, and 
providing insights through data analysis. However, there are also risks and 
threats associated with AI applications in PM. A conceptual model is 
proposed to integrate AI into project processes while addressing ethical and 
security concerns. The research findings can help project owners and 
managers better understand AI capabilities and limitations, improve trust and 
transparency between humans and AI, and prepare for future AI-focused 
projects. 
 
Keywords: Project Management, Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, 
Project Complexity, Change Management, AI Ethical Principles

1 INTRODUCTION  
According to a conference paper by PMI® (1), Projects are multifaceted and have many elements. 
Each project is different from the other based on the application yet they all fundamentally depend 
on strategic goals that are set using Project Management (PM) principles from the book, a guide 
to PM Body of Knowledge(PMBOK®® Guide) (2). With many elements in PM each of them 
interrelated and having complimentary effects on each other, the complexity of a project varies 
from project to project. The article’s (1) report on project complexity there are statistics on the 
distribution of projects by level of complexity. 19.1% out of 303 PM officials classified projects as 
“very High Complexity followed by 30.2% classifying projects have “High Complexity”. Nearly half 
the projects managed are classified as having High complexity or very high complexity. The 
“Medium Complexity” percentage added to the “High Complexity” percentages, shows that 80% 
of the projects managed are medium to high complexity.  
There is a growing need to understand the complexity of PM processes. Scope, People, and 
Communications are the keys to dealing with a  complex project, according to the (1) article. Thus 
the need for managing processes that directly influence these 3 key factors is essential for project 
development. In an article published on PM Network® Digital Exclusives (3), a list of implications 
for Artificial Intelligence (AI) for PM is discussed. These implications are addressed on how PM 
can keep up with the emerging trends caused by the digitalization of technology. The 5 
implications are: 
 
Organizations and Project Managers see an imminent impact of AI.  
 
AI-Powered tools will take over administrative tasks for Project Managers. 
 
AI systems can help keep projects on schedule and in Budget.  
 
AI tools can analyse data from current and previous projects to provide insights 
 
Project Managers will increasingly be called upon to implement AI-focused projects.  
AI systems help analyse and synthesize data, which helps Project Managers and project owners 
to interpret the data more efficiently. With a better understanding of data and the AI system, 
Project Managers can communicate the scope and expectations of the project output. Another 
advantage of having a better understanding of AI systems is that it allows the Project Managers 
and owners to develop human skills that make them better professionals like creativity and 
emotional quotient that AI systems cannot replicate (4).

1.3.1 PROJECT MANAGEMENT (PM) 
“PM is a temporary endeavour used to build a unique new product, service or result” is how PM 
is defined in the PM Body of Knowledge (PMBOK®®Guide) (2). The PM Body of Knowledge 
(PMBOK®®Guide) is the flagship publication of the PM Institute and is a fundamental resource 
for PM in any industry. Using this standard for this literature review to understand the basics of 
PM is beneficial for the extended study of AI integration in projects and how it affects PM. The 
fundamentals of PM revolve around 3 main principles: Time, Scope, and Cost; as they are 
depicted in the model of the Iron triangle (Figure 1). Success in project management has been 
traditionally associated with the ability of the project manager to deliver in scope, time, cost, and 
quality (9).  
 
Figure 1 Iron Triangle of Project Management (10) 
The Increasing complexity and uncertainty of projects make the traditional iron triangle method to 
measure success obsolete. Many changes along the project lifecycle put a burden on project 
managers to revise project success criteria. Changes during the project are inevitable and it does 
not necessarily mean the project will fail. There is no problem with changes as long as they are 
transparently managed. Trust and accountability are important conditions to determine the right 
success criteria for a project (11).   
The success criteria and the success factors are determined by the effective management of PM 
knowledge areas (2). The knowledge areas of project management (2) are as follows:

1.3.2 ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE (AI) AND MACHINE LEARNING (ML) 
Before studying the effects of AI on PM, it is crucial to understand what exactly is AI. Artificial 
Intelligence (AI) is a very broad term with many definitions even in the common English language. 
For example, the Oxford English Dictionary defines AI as: “The theory and development of 
computer systems able to perform tasks normally requiring human intelligence.”; Merriam-
Webster, on the other hand, defines AI as: “A branch of computer science dealing with the 
simulation of intelligent behaviour in computers.” But the crucial point of all definitions is that 
AI is a technology that can imitate tasks or interpretations that are similar to that of human 
cognition. AI as a whole is a web of interrelated subsets like Machine Learning (ML) and 
Deep Learning (DL), and their branches as seen in Figure 2. But the main understanding to 
be considered is the definitions and applications of ML and DL in general. A blog on the 
Advancing Analytics website (15), gives a general understanding of AI, ML and DL by using 
a comprehensible example as shown in Figure 2. This blog shows the easy form of

MACHINE LEARNING (ML) 
The basic understanding of Machine Learning is that it teaches computers to do what comes 
naturally to humans. This involves training itself by the the use of algorithms that can learn 
from available data and these algorithms adaptively improve the outputs as it learns more 
(17). 
According to Bangert (18), ML is often divided into two groups based on different attributes as 
seen in Figure 3. The grouping is Supervised and Unsupervised. The attributes are Classification, 
Regression, or Clustering.  
 
Supervised methods – They deal with datasets for which there is empirical data on both 
input and the desired output. They are more accurate and are used for getting specific 
results that are desired. 
 
Unsupervised – They deal with datasets for which there is empirical data only on inputs. 
They are mainly used for pattern recognition in the given data sets 
 
Classification – These methods aim to place data points into one or several groups. 
 
Regression – These methods aim to compute some numerical value as accurately as 
possible using several data points.  
 
Clustering – These methods aim to use exploratory data analysis to find hidden patterns 
or groupings in data.

1.3.3 RESPONSIBLE USE OF AI 
AI is changing the business world through the usage of different techniques and systems. 
Considering the disruptive power of AI, studies on the adoption of AI in project processes are 
becoming more relevant. Along with the theoretical research, it is equally important to keep 
in mind the real-world applications. A survey report of 295 PM professionals, IPMA members, 
and PwC contacts done by the International PM Association (IPMA) in association with PwC 
(19) covers many of the aspects of adopting AI in PM. One of the survey results was about 
“the top 5 most important barriers in using AI tools in PM today”, and they are: 
1. Limited Understanding of AI Technology 
2. Difficulties in deciding the best AI applications, due to limited experience 
3. Data Privacy, Digital Ethics, and Security risks 
4. Limited IT capabilities (IT technical skills, standard IT processes/procedures) 
5. Many AI solutions are not fully mature for deployment.  
In the report, “Data privacy, digital ethics, and security risks” is the second most frequent 
barrier indicated by agile leaders, functional managers, and executives (with 68.18%). It is the 
third most frequent barrier observed for PM-related professionals (with 60.13%) and on the 5-7 
places in the case of IT specialists (with 53.52%).” The 3 most important steps to overcome the 
AI adoption barriers in the report are given by the following:  
 
Defining AI Strategy, aligned to business goals 
 
Investing in AI talent and training professionals 
 
Establishing standards and methodologies for safe and sound data-driven processes in 
projects.

2 METHODOLOGY AND DATA 
The following Methodology and Data selection will be about how the data i.e. the literature papers 
are selected, filtered, and reviewed for the Systematic literature review with a thematic approach. 
The author chose to do a Systematic Literature Review where the objective of the review is to 
address methods for integrating or comparing the findings from qualitative studies (19). It is a type 
of review that looks for themes or constructs that lie in or across individual qualitative studies, in 
this case, the necessary knowledge to implement and deploy AI systems in PM processes from 
the current state of research. The requirements for this literature review are mapped out by the 
SALSA (Search, Appraisal, Synthesis, and Analysis) framework (19), where the following 
objectives are found:  
 
Search – In the Systematic literature review, the search of the literature papers for the 
review aims for a selective or purposive sampling of literature on AI in PM. A Boolean 
search strategy is used for this which will be explained further in this section.   
 
AppraisaL – Quality assessment is typically used to mediate a message in a systematic 
review type. Due to the vast number of literature papers in this area of focus, the author 
eliminated any unnecessary papers that are not relevant to the research question. This 
will keep the study within the time constraint and the overall scope of the thesis.  
 
Synthesis – Synthesis for the Systematic literature review is done with a qualitative 
narrative. In this case, the narrative is the theme of the thesis which is AI in PM processes 
> Impacts and Implications > Conceptual model to deploy AI systems > AI Ethics 
considerations in PM.  
 
Analysis – Systematic review analysis is done thematically with conceptual models i.e. 
what are the implications of AI and a potential model to apply for deploying AI systems

4.1.1 NEED FOR AI IN PM   
The basic understanding of PM processes comes from the PMBOK® (2), a standard in PM where 
a general knowledge base is set for managing PM processes is explained. PM process groups 
and Knowledge areas together give a framework to assess projects on a holistic level. Multiple 
papers in this review say that having an understanding of the PM processes is the gateway to 
realizing the potential to use AI in PM. The utility of AI always begins with the understanding that 
to realize project success, projects need to be realized within a time frame and a budget, with the 
best quality of work (12) (24). A lot of elements go into making a project successful and their 
complex interactions are what makes project success difficult. According to (12), 3 main factors 
are determinants of why projects fail, they are  
 
Projects are getting more and more COMPLEX – As complexity is increasing there is 
a need to be more accurate in estimations and applications which in turn make it difficult 
to have judgments and make decisions. 
 
Uncertainty of DYNAMIC Project Environments – New technical as well as 
technological advancements make way for newer and more innovative practices that 
change traditional practices. Most projects fail because they are not able to keep up with 
these advancements.  
 
STAKEHOLDER Management – As the complexity of projects is increasing there is a 
rise in different project roles and with that the diversity in skills and how they are managed 
has a big effect on project success.  
By the above points, there is a need for project managers to use advanced tools to understand 
project complexities better with clear definitions of roles and usage of these tools. The paper on 
AI application (13) lists the following human reasons and Project Manager incompetencies for

development mindset of Project Managers (16).

AI is developing faster than managers can comprehend and with this, the complexity of 
projects increases too. The interconnectedness of different facets of PM due to AI

4.2.2 AI TECHNOLOGY  
AI tools and techniques specifically in ML have great opportunities in terms of versatility of usage 
in a plethora of varied applications. This is only possible for ML models because of their 
capabilities to handle large amounts of data and their ability to train themselves after every

Data integrity should be maintained by spending enough time cleaning and processing

Neural Networks (NN), Fuzzy Cognitive Maps (FCM) with Fuzzy Logic (FL), Genetic

analytical capacity of project processes (23) 
 
Project Managers need to know what AI should be used and how to use it to provide 
value. They can do this by having knowledge of data science and how to utilize the right 
data to train the ML models. It is crucial to have reliable data-feeding processes and data 
structures to generate self-learning algorithms (8)  
 
There is a shift towards an on-demand, customizable, and problem-specific mindset of 
solving problems. Project Managers need to be innovative and need to know data science 
and the digital “know-how” of AI. These data science skills contribute to identifying 
patterns and trends to use AI in PM (16) 
In summary, there is a growing need for Project Managers to develop knowledge of AI 
technology, the basic theory behind it, and the required data inputs necessary for PM.

4.3.1.2 TECHNOLOGY 
When considering which AI tool to be used in the specific project process, Project Managers need 
to understand the basics of ML. This specific chapter on ML from a book written by Patrick Bangert 
explains the basics of ML and the necessary information for Project Managers (18). ML has three 
main parts.  
 
First, it consists of many prototypical models that could be applied to the data at hand. 
Models like Neural Networks, Fuzzy Logic, Decision Trees, etc are used.  
 
Second, each of these models come with several “prescriptions”, so-called algorithms that 
tell how to calculate the model coefficients from a data set. These calculations are called 
training. After training the initial prototype will be turned into a model for the specific data 
set provided by people. 
 
Third, the finished model must be deployed so that it can be used.  
From these three parts, Project Managers can know what is required to use ML. Having a 
complete understanding of ML needs expertise and experience. Project Managers might not have 
the required expertise to develop models on their own so they need to work together with 
developers who have the experience to build these ML models. Although Project Managers can 
provide the developer with key information on the project that only they have expertise in. Before 
the Project Managers provide the data and information that is needed for the ML model, they need 
to be able to adapt to the requirements of the ML models. ML has many methods and all methods 
are classified into two types of groupings, either supervised or unsupervised and either

4.3.1.3 DATA 
ML has many benefits and the key reason for those benefit is that it needs to be embedded in the 
right infrastructure to unfold its potential. The data that ML is built on must be suitably prepared 
for learning and it must be adequately tested (18). In literature, it is studied that ML has 
revolutionized our lives and has real-world consequences but it is also subjected to a lot of biases 
due to the amount of data it can process (26). In the literature paper called “Overcoming the 
Pitfalls and Perils of Algorithms: A Classification of ML Biases and Mitigation Methods”, a case 
study is researched to identify the ML biases as well as ways to avoid and mitigate them. A total 
of 8 distinct ML biases have been summarized using the Cross-Industry Standard Processing for 
Data Mining (CRISP-DM) to account for all phases of ML projects and outline 24 mitigation 
methods. These insights synthesize the literature on ML biases in a concise manner and point to 
the importance of human judgement for ML algorithms. The reasons why ML biases are 
necessary to be studied are because: 
 
ML models are extremely literal and will pursue an objective single-mindedly unlike 
humans who understand trade-offs.  
 
ML models have the black box effect i.e. they can provide results in great accuracy but do 
not provide causes or reasons for them.  
Biases can occur in many ways like in a series of subjective choices, training and building the ML 
model, or from unseen biases in the provided data itself. A CRISP-DM methodology is used to 
identify and classify them as it is crucial for any ML application (26). In the CRISP-DM there are 
a total of 6 phases and Data that is in circulation in the whole process. The phases in the CRISP-
DM are as follows:  
 
Business Understanding – It is the phase where ML project objectives are understood 
from a business or project perspective. 
 
Data Understanding – It is the phase where data is collected, described, explored, and 
verified according to the business understanding.  
 
Data Preparation – In this phase, the final data set is built for the ML algorithm. 
 
Modelling – It is the phase where the ML technique and algorithm are applied using the 
dataset.  
 
Evaluation – In this phase, the results are reassessed and the outcomes are reviewed 
according to the business understanding objectives

 
Deployment – This phase is subjective to requirements and it can be in the form of either

AI systems are very error-prone and they are difficult to adapt to even with all the ethical principles 
considered. The following reviewed paper highlights that AI systems are prone to latent biases 
and provides 10 considerations that do not usually follow the general patterns of AI ethics (30). 
These considerations or anti-patterns of AI are added considerations to the ethics of AI for people 
in the AI adoption process and they are:  
1. Learning from the past without remembering the past – AI is a really good promoter and 
enforcer of past data. All the good results are overemphasized and the bad results or 
biases are overlooked. AI Project Managers should understand that AI doesn’t just 
==PAGE:79==
 
AI in Project Management 
DTU Management 
71 
 
reproduce past biases but amplifies them. So Project Managers must be aware that past 
data might reflect injustice and may have structural inequalities. 
2. Making spurious correlations – When AI is used to find patterns in data, the AI might 
identify attributes that do not necessarily cause the outcome but may be correlated to it. 
3. Learning from humans without the possibility of malicious training – Directly or Indirectly 
AI systems learn from humans, absorbing both the good and bad traits of human learning. 
Project Managers need to be aware that there might be the possibility of ML models being 
poisoned by humans.  
4. Re-using long pipelines of systems in unanticipated contexts – Project Managers need to 
be aware that working results from efficient AI systems do not guarantee the same level 
of efficiency in all cases, every time. 
5. Using inaccurate data – Data is the core of AI or an ML system. If data is wrong every 
outcome will be wrong no matter how good the algorithm is.  
6. Using data at hand rather than data that is needed - Having inaccurate data is bad but 
having incomplete data on top of that might result in drastic outcomes that may have 
serious consequences. 
7. Aggressively resisting review – Due to the complexity of the AI systems, people might not 
be incentivised to reveal their source code for benchmarking and without the governance 
of 3rd parties, the improvement of the AI system will get difficult. 
8. Failing to measure the social impact of deployed systems – Damage caused by AI is 
inevitable if they are not assessed for impacts.  
9. Inappropriately defining the roles and responsibilities of people in the decision-making 
process – With AI increasingly relieving people of the burden of decision-making, the 
moral cost of those decisions and the responsibility becomes unclear.  
10. Providing transparency without specifying accountability – Explanation and transparency 
must be provided to reduce the black box effect of AI. This helps to understand the flaws 
and raises the willingness of people to improve those processes.  
These 10 anti-patterns with the 7 ethical principles are aimed to be a guide for people deploying 
and using mature AI systems.

5 CONCLUSION 
Projects are getting more complex with uncertainty due to many dynamic elements. Human skills 
are not enough anymore and the use of AI to relieve project complexity is inevitable. The elements 
of the iron triangle of PM to determine project success is benefited from using AI. AI does the 
same task a human does in reduced time and improved quality. Apart from this AI helps project 
managers to detect threats of a project, reduce human biases and errors, and consistently 
maintain the quality of the results better than humans. Concluding the immediate and long-term 
effects of AI use in PM, AI will improve process innovations and existing standards of PM will 
change drastically.   
Research in AI in recent times has been at unprecedented rates and naturally, project managers 
need to keep up with AI. Among many ML techniques, 5 of them have been researched as 
prospective tools of AI in PM namely, Neural Networks (NN), Genetic Algorithms (GA),  Fuzzy 
Cognitive Maps (FCM), Bayesian Models, and hybrid models like Evolutionary Fuzzy Hybrid 
Neural Network (EFHNN). They are used in project process groups such as Project Initiation, 
Project Planning, Project Execution, and Project Monitoring & Controlling. Although the Project 
Closing process group did not reveal any AI applications, it can be assumed that AI intervention 
in it will be part of future PM.  
From this study, it is observed that both PM processes and the Project Managers are supported 
by the use of AI in PM. AI optimizes value-driven processes better than people and traditional 
methods in substantially reduced time. AI provides support to make better quality decisions and 
allows managers to be creative and focus on human qualities that AI cannot replace like empathy. 
With these benefits, AI also comes with risks and limitations. The most common risk of using AI 
in PM is the state of employment of people but this can be avoided if the risks are addressed 
actively as AI is applied. 
Based on the study, the future of Project Manager’s roles are depended on focusing on project 
development with innovative use of AI and gaining knowledge of AI tools and technology in project 
processes. To maintain the trust that AI will perform as intended, managers need to have a 
standard or framework to assess AI adoption in PM processes with ethical considerations.   
This thesis proposes a conceptual application of the People, Process, Technology, and Data 
(PPTD) model. The PPTD model considers the AI Technology applied in specific PM Processes. 
These two aspects of the PPTD model are supported by identifying the potential Data biases that 
==PAGE:92==
 
AI in Project Management 
DTU Management 
84 
 
can arise in AI development. The foundations of AI are laid when the necessities of these three 
aspects are realized as proposed in the thesis. For the Adoption of AI People need to be aware 
of their functions and understand that the success factors of their tasks to contribute to the ethical 
principles of AI.  
This proposed conceptual PPTD model hypothesises that Project Managers can be on the 
“Journey”  of developing mature AI systems by making sure that ethics are considered by people 
in every task of an AI project. Project managers of the future need to focus on bridging the gap 
between internal environmental dimensions like the availability of resources, strengths & 
weaknesses, capabilities, etc. of AI projects and managerial decisions at the strategic, tactical, 
and operational levels with ethical considerations using the Ethical Management of AI framework 
(EMMA).  
5.1 LIMITATIONS AND CONSIDERATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK 
This thesis is subjected to many limitations that future researchers can refer to before they 
address the proposed model. Before the limitations are addressed, readers must be aware that 
this thesis was written from the literature available, and the theories behind the application of 
conceptual framework were developed subjective to the author’s interpretation of the literature.    
The first limitation was Issues with sample size and selection. The overwhelming results of the 
literature search and time constraints could cause selection bias where the author has chosen 
the literature based on inclusion due to particular attributes and filters. This can lead to excluding 
literature that could have improved the findings for the review. Due to access issues, the source 
of literature is only ScienceDirect. Other sources of literature may have literature that could have 
been a better fit for the thesis. The limited access to literature has contributed to other limitations 
in the thesis analysis. In the WHAT IS THE INFLUENCE OF AI IN PM? Section there were only 
7 papers that were reviewed. Other papers on the same topic might have other conclusions that 
may contradict the findings of this thesis. Another limitation of sample size was, in the 4.1.3 
POTENTIAL OF PROJECT PROCESSES FROM THE SUPPORT OF AI section under 
PROJECT CLOSING there was not enough literature on how this PM process is affected by AI. 
With more time and access to other sources of literature, the mentioned limitations could be 
overcome.  
The second set of limitations is with the proposed model. They are listed as follows with the 
considerations for future research:
