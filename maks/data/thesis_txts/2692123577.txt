Abstract
Environmental issues are an ongoing challenge in modern day society. Depletion of non-
renewable sources has been acknowledged and increased awareness has been brought to
ﬁnding sustainable and renewable ways to meet the rising energy demand as well as im-
proving energy eﬃciency. Wind is one of the renewable energy sources, it is a clean and
unlimited energy source and has been harnessed to generate electricity for decades. Den-
mark is one of the leading countries in harnessing wind energy and in the design and
construction of oﬀshore wind farms.
The Oﬀshore Wind Farm Layout Problem (WFLOP) is a NP-hard (nondeterministic poly-
nomial time) optimization problem with the task of placing turbines. More speciﬁcally, to
place n wind turbines at a subset of k available positions to maximize the expected power
production and minimize wake losses. In literature, either exact or heuristic strategies
have been used to solve the WFLOP. This thesis introduces the potential of using kites
to press air from upper layer down to the wind turbines to countervail power loss and
increase power production. It aims at ﬁnding the optimal arrangement of wind turbines
and kites with regards to maximum power production at a minimum cost to maximize
proﬁt.
In other words, co-locating wind turbines with kites for a given area with the
goal of maximizing the power production of the wind farm and along with keeping the
construction cost at a minimum.
This thesis introduces a new approach to the WFLOP by placing not only wind turbines
but also inserting kites to the layout. An initial solution is obtained based on the P-
Dispersion Sum Problem (PDSP) and a greedy heuristic and improvement strategy based
on Simulated Annealing (SA) algorithm. Furthermore, the thesis presents both exact and
heuristic strategies to solve the problem and uses Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) to
ﬁnd the exact solution. The study demonstrates how a practical optimization model can
be applied on realistic problems. The novelty of the model developed lies not only in the
integration of kites in the optimization model but also in the use of realistic instances of
wind farms.

Abstrakt
Miljøspørgsmål er en løbende udfordring i det moderne samfund.
Forringelse af ikke-
vedvarende kilder er blevet anerkendt, og der er forøget opmærksomhed på at ﬁnde
bæredygtige og vedvarende løsninger til at imødekomme det stigende energibehov samt
bidrage til forbedring af energieﬀektivitet. Vind er en af de vedvarende energikilder, den
er en ren og ubegrænset energikilde og er blevet udnyttet til at generere elektricitet i
årtier. Danmark er et af de førende lande indenfor udnyttelse af vindenergi og design og
konstruktion af hav-vindfarme.
Oﬀshore Wind Farm Layout Problem (WFLOP) er et NP-hårdt optimeringsproblem ved
at placere vindmøller. Mere speciﬁkt, at placere vindmøller i tilgængelige positioner for at
maksimere den forventede kraftproduktion og minimere såkaldt wake eﬀects. I litteraturen
har enten nøjagtig eller heuristisk strategi været brugt til at løse WFLOP. Dette forskn-
ingsprojekt fokuserer på at undersøge potentialet i at bruge drager til at presse luft fra
høje luftlag ned til vindmøllen for at udligne strømtab og øge kraftproduktionen. Formålet
er at ﬁnde det optimale arrangement af vindmøller og drager med hensyn til maksimal
kraftproduktion til en minimal pris. Med andre ord samlokalisering af vindmøller med
drager til et givet område med det mål at maksimere vindmølleparkens elproduktion og
samtidig holde byggeomkostningerne på et minimum.
Denne afhandling introducerer en ny tilgang til WFLOP ved at placere ikke kun vind-
møller, men også indsætte drager til layoutet. En indledende løsning præsenteres baseret
på P-Dispersion Sum Problem (PDSP) og en greedy heuristisk og forbedringsstrategi
baseret på Simuleret Annealing-algoritme (SA) som en udviklet optimeringsmetode. Desu-
den præsenterer specialet både exacte og heuristiske strategier for at løse problemet og
bruger Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) til at ﬁnde den exacte løsning. Forskningen
viser, hvordan en praktisk optimeringsmodel kan anvendes på realistiske problemer. In-
novationen bag den udviklede model ligger ikke kun i integrationen af drager i optimer-
ingsmodellen, men også i brugen af realistiske tilfælde af hav-vindfarme.

Introduction
This chapter introduces the background and motivation for the thesis. It begins with
highlighting the current status and the development of the wind energy sector worldwide
and particularly in Denmark and the Netherlands. Thereafter, the practical relevance of
the research is explained. Finally, the main contributions are stated and an overview of
the thesis given.
1.1
Background and Motivation
Sustainable energy is one of the fastest growing industries. The wind energy sector is
developing rapidly and is amongst the main assets for the European economy. Europe has
a wind capacity of 220 GW [1], with approximately 25 GW oﬀshore, and over the next 5
years an installation of 105 GW of new wind farms is expected, see Figure 1.1. In 2020,
the wind farms of Europe generated 458 TWh of electricity, covering 16% of the electricity
demand in Europe, thereof 83 TWh from oﬀshore wind energy production. Denmark holds
the far highest share of wind energy or 48% in their total electricity mix. The cumulative
capacity is in total 6,180 MW, thereof 1,703 MW oﬀshore. However, no new installations
oﬀshore were made in Denmark in 2020. In 2020, the Netherlands installed the most wind
capacity in Europe, most of it oﬀshore. The installed oﬀshore wind capacity more than
doubled, going from 1,118 MW to 2,611 MW. The number of turbines installed in 2020 in
the Netherlands was around 172 with average power rating being 8.7 MW for each turbine
[2].
Figure 1.1: New and total (cumulative) installations in Europe - WindEurope’s Realistic
Expectations Scenario, WindEurope (2021).
Market outlook for the next ﬁve years, 2021-2025, suggests an enormous increase in oﬀshore
installations and is expected to set a record year in 2021 [2]. It is estimated that from
the total of 3.7 GW oﬀshore installations, 2 GW will be implemented in the UK and the
rest in Denmark, France and the Netherlands. The cost of wind energy has decreased
tremendously in recent years and is now one of the cheapest energy sources. Thus, the
competitiveness of wind power has strengthened, making it one of the most promising

energy technology for the next decades. Figure 1.2 shows the development of the annual
oﬀshore wind capacity from the year 2000 and the predicted capacity until the year 2050.

During the last century, wind farms have been in constant development. The eﬃciency has
been increasing steadily with larger turbines, improved design and technology. Initially,
wind turbines were placed onshore but due to both visual and noise pollution, placing the
wind turbines oﬀshore was considered a better option. In 1991, the ﬁrst oﬀshore wind
farm was installed in Vindeby, Denmark. The oﬀshore wind farm consisted of 11 turbines
with the installed power of 5 MW in total, covering annual consumption of 2,200 Danish
households [3]. Since then, the number of wind turbines has increased rapidly and the
wind turbines have increased in size as well. In 2002, the ﬁrst large scale wind farm in the
world, Horns Rev 1, was installed with the capacity of 160 MW. The wind farm was built
in three phases, with the ﬁrst coming online in 2002, the second in 2009 (209 MW) and
the third in 2019 (407 MW) [4].
The advantages of locating wind turbines oﬀshore generally oﬀers higher average wind
speed and more consistent wind with less turbulence compared to an onshore case, driving
the development of the oﬀshore wind farm worldwide.
While the capacity factor for
onshore was 25% the average capacity factor for oﬀshore has increased signiﬁcantly, from
38% to 42%, representing the performance of the entire wind ﬂeet in 2020 including the
old installations. New onshore wind farms now operate at up to 40% and new oﬀshore
wind farms up to 60% [2].
Wind turbines extract energy from the wind to produce electricity, converting kinetic
energy in the wind into mechanical energy.
A generator is then used to convert the
mechanical power into electricity. In industrial applications, several wind turbines are
placed in a wind farm.
When two turbines are located close together, a phenomenon

==PAGE:15==
called wake eﬀects appears, also known as turbine wakes. The upstream turbines creates
shadowing eﬀects on the downstream turbine. The wind ﬂow then reduces and turbulence
intensity increases. These eﬀects degrade the absolute power and are considered to be
one of the main reasons for power losses in a wind farm. In fact, it is considered that the
downstream wind turbines lose 5-15% of their power relative to the front row [5]. The
wake eﬀect decreases with longer distance between the turbines. In general, the distance
between two turbine rows should be approximately 5 times the rotor diameter.
The Wind Farm Layout Optimization Problem (WFLOP) addresses this problem as the
objective of WFLOP is to determine the optimal placement of turbines inside a wind farm
to maximize e.g. the energy production and minimize wake eﬀects and foundation cost,
while meeting various constraints. Wake eﬀect is the result from the changes in wind speed
caused by the impact of the turbines on each other. If two wind turbines are located too
close to each other they interfere with one another. The two main eﬀects of a wake are
[6]:
(i) a reduction in the wind speed, which in turn reduces the energy production of the
wind farm
(ii) an increase in the turbulence of the wind, potentially increasing the dynamic me-
chanical loading on downwind turbines.
Innovative wind energy technologies are in constant development and in the next decades,
rapid growth is expected in the oﬀshore wind market. In recent years, scientists have
focused on a new approach to harness powerful winds at high altitudes, several hundred
meters above sea level. At these altitudes the wind speed is more stable than at lower
levels and provide more energy in a consistent way.
This relatively new approach is
using aerodynamically eﬃcient kites to generate electricity as wind energy harvested at
higher altitudes gives higher yield. The kite-technology is now considered one of the main
potential future solution to harness wind power [7], [8]. The kites can even be connected
to conventional wind farms, at altitudes where conventional wind turbines can not operate
[9]. If added to a wind farm, the kites would not only produce energy but also increase the
eﬃciency of the wind farm by surpressing air from upper layer, providing synergy eﬀect.
The thesis focuses on ﬁnding the optimal arrangement of wind turbines and kites in a
wind farm by taking wake eﬀects into account and investigates the potential of using
kites to improve the total energy production of an oﬀshore wind farm. The purpose of
the study is to model and estimate the eﬀects from adding kites to an oﬀshore wind
farm. An appropriate algorithm is created based on known metaheuristic-techniques and
the outcome investigated. Optimal locations of wind turbines and kites with regards to
optimal number and cost-eﬃciency are presented based on the suggested model.
1.2
Research Questions
In an oﬀshore wind farm, the main objective is to produce as much electricity with minimal
cost of capital and operating cost. Establishing an oﬀshore wind farm is an expensive and
a complicated process. However, recent development has led to improved technologies
and the cost of wind energy has decreased, especially compared to other renewable energy
production options.
It is of high importance, that the wind turbines do not occupy more space than needed

2.1
The Wind Farm Layout Optimization Problem
The design of wind farms is complex and relies on several optimization tasks. The three
most important optimization tasks are the turbine location, electrical routing and foun-
dation optimization [10]. It is of high importance to have a good knowledge and under-
standing on how diﬀerent factors aﬀect the proﬁt of a wind farm in the decision-making.
One of the steps of the wind farm design is solving the Wind Farm Layout Optimization
Problem.
The WFLOP is a NP-hard (nondeterministic polynomial time) optimization problem that
has the objective to optimally position wind turbines within a wind farm. The WFLOP
focuses on ﬁnding the location of turbines within a wind farm that maximizes the ex-
pected power production while minimizing wake eﬀects. Wake eﬀect is an interference
phenomenon caused by the impact of two turbines on each other. A wind turbine casts a
wind shade in the downwind direction. If two turbines are located too close to each other,
the upwind turbines, the turbine that faces the wind with the turbine blade in front, casts
a wind shadow to the downwind turbines, the ones that faces away from the wind. The
latter, then receives a lower wind velocity and thus generates less energy. For oﬀshore
wind farms, the power losses caused by wake eﬀects is particularly high and are estimated
to be between 5% - 15% [5]. Therefore, wake eﬀects are a signiﬁcant factor negatively
aﬀecting the proﬁtability of a wind farm.
The ﬁrst paper on WFLOP was published in 1994 by Mosetti et al. [11]. The paper
deﬁned the problem and presented a heuristic approach to solve the optimization problem
of large wind farms, using Genetic Algorithm (GA). The optimization problem was applied
to both the number and position of the turbines. A new approach to the optimization
of large wind farms was introduced by a wind farm simulation model with wakes and
genetic search code. Mosetti et al. [11] used a grid of 100 square cells, or 10 by 10 grid,
as a test case where the square cells indicated the available locations for turbines. Three
diﬀerent wind cases were deﬁned. The ﬁrst case was deﬁned by a single wind direction
and a constant wind velocity. The second case was deﬁned by constant wind intensity
with variable direction. Finally, the third case was deﬁned by variable wind intensity with
variable wind direction.
Ten years later, in 2004, Ozturk and Norman [12] proposed a non-linear programming
model with the objective of ﬁnding a location of wind turbines that maximizes the expected
proﬁt per kWh generated by the wind farm instead of the power output. To solve the
problem, a greedy improvement heuristic methodology was developed. The optimization
method consisted of the following three recursive operations: adding, removing and moving
The Oﬀshore Wind Farm Layout Optimization Problem with Co-location of Turbines and Kites
5
==PAGE:18==
a turbine. The wake model used was simple and did not consider wake interference in the
middle of the wind farm and the approach was limited to a low number of turbines.
The following decade showed an increasing interest in solving the WFLOP and yielded
several papers solving the problem with both continuos approach and discrete, see Table
2.1. In general, an oﬀshore windfarm is a continuous rather than discrete system, when
studying the expected level of energy produced. However, the discrete approach to the
optimization problem is more common in literature. The continuous approach considers
the available positions limited only by area boundaries, while the discrete approach con-
siders a set of available locations for the turbines. Kusiak and Song [14] and Kwong [15]
use the continuous approach and Ozturk and Norman used both approaches [12].
The discrete approach involves both heuristic and exact strategies. While exact algorithms
are almost guaranteed to ﬁnd the optimal solution for the problem, the larger the problem,
the more complex the solution space gets. This results in longer execution time or if the
solution space becomes too large, the exact algorithm can not handle it. On the other
hand, heuristic strategies do not guarantee optimality but obtain a fair solution in a shorter
execution time than exact algorithm can provide [13].
The most common heuristic strategy to solve the WFLOP is the GA. In short, the GA is
built on the analogy of natural selection and uses population groups and iterative processes
to evolve toward a better solution for each generation based on the survival of the ﬁttest
[16]. However, it has not been proven that it is the best technique. Grady et al. [17]
based their approach on the Mosetti instances with the three wind cases and improved
the GA by using smaller population groups and increasing the number of iterations of the
metaheuristic. A variation of the GA is used to solve the WFLOP and is presented in
several papers [14], [18], [19], [20], [21]. Mora et al. [22] used GA to solve the WFLOP
with the objective of maximizing the Net Present Value and were able to place 50 turbines
in a 20 by 20 grid. However, no wake model was used. Gonzales [23] et al. included wake
eﬀect in their model. In 2009, Huang [24] improved his own work [18] by using Hybrid
GA on the Mosetti instances with Katic wake model.
For the exact strategies, the ﬁrst Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) model on the WFLOP
was presented by Donovan [25] in 2005. Donovan presented three mixed integer models
based on variations of the vertex packing problem with the objective of maximizing power
generated by taking wakes into account. In 2010, Fagerfjäll [26] proposed two MIP models
for, the Wind Farm Production Optimization model and the Wind Farm Infrastructure
Optimization model with a maximum number of 30 turbines located. In 2011, Archer [27]
proposed a MILP model to solve the WFLOP to maximize harnessed wind energy. In
2014, Zhang et al. [28] presented a MIP model solving instances with a maximum of 400
positions. One of the disadvantages of MIP is that they are generally complex models to
solve due to the large number of variables and are limited by the number of variables that
commercial solvers can handle. To counteract to this problem, Fischetti and Monaci [29]
combined a proximity search heuristic with a MIP model in a matheuristic approach and
were able to handle the largest number of available positions known in literature.
Along with the Genetic Algorithm, Simulated Annealing has been used [19]. Similarly to
[12], the Simulated Annealing methodology presented used three temperatures associated
to each possible action (adding, moving, or removing a turbine). However, in the formu-
lation presented by [19], only power is maximized. Costs are not taken into consideration
and Jensen model is used for wake losses. Simulated Annealing is also used by Rivas [30]
with three possible operations (adding, moving or removing a turbine) and is tested on
an existing park, Horns Rev 1, and compared to the actual layout that was built. In this

2.2
Kites
Kites provide a novel approach to harness wind at several hundreds meters above sea level.
At high altitudes the wind speed is more stable than at lower levels resulting in increased
energy output. The kite technology is based on aerodynamic eﬃciency and more stable
winds in the upper layer and is believed to be one of the main potential future solutions for
harnessing wind power. The kite-technology is now considered one of the main potential
future solution to harness wind power and can even be connected to conventional wind
farms, at altitudes where conventional wind turbines can not operate.
One of the oldest methods of generating energy is harnessing the wind.
In fact, the
technology of using kites is based on an ancient method that has been used for almost
hundreds of years and has been progressing rapidly in recent years [41]. In 1980, M. Loyd
introduced crosswind kite power. As the kites ﬂy transverse to the wind at high speed,
lift is produced [42]. Kites use the undisturbed air or outer ﬂow above the wind turbines,
increasing the velocity in front of the wind turbines downstream and eventually yield a
higher eﬃciency. Adding kites to a wind farm layout is a relatively inexpensive option and
could increase eﬃciency, making wind farms more sustainable and cost-eﬀective.
Ploumakis and Bierbooms [9], conclude that the only source of kinetic energy to balance
the energy extracted by the turbines in large wind farms is the vertical transport of the
free-stream ﬂow kinetic energy from above the wind turbine canopy. The paper discusses
the generation of downwash, that is the change in direction of air producing lift force,
with the use of kites by a concept called: the Daeolus kite system. They introduced the
integration of a kite, suspended at a height of approximately 145 meters, and placed in
the middle of two wind turbines at the “boundary of the wake region and the undisturbed
ﬂow” [9]. The kite used, was connected by a tether to a ﬁxed ﬂoating device, buoy, ﬂoating
at sea level. The buoy, along with the attitude control system, controls the attitude and
position of the kite and keeps the kite at the designated location. This is illustrated in
Figure 2.1
Figure 2.1: Visualization of the proposed ﬂow entrainment based on interspersed kites [9].
Since wind turbines extract energy from the wind to produce electricity, the wind leaving
the turbine has lower energy content than the wind upstream of the turbine. As a result,
the downstream wind speed is decreased and the ﬂow has become turbulent. This explains
the wake eﬀect.

If the kite is located between the turbines, the kite generates lift and thus downwash. This
bends the outer undisturbed ﬂow into the wake region and results in higher wind velocity
at the turbine downstream. As mentioned in Section 2.1, if two turbines are located too
close to each other, the upwind turbines cast a wind shadow to the downwind turbines and
the latter receive a lower wind speed and thus generate less energy. Higher wind speed
at the turbine downstream yields a higher power output of the downstream turbine and
thereby increases the wind farm eﬃciency [9].
Wind turbines are expensive engineering systems and the initial cost of installing wind
turbines is high. Apart from the cost of the turbines, the main cost components for oﬀshore
wind farms are the foundation cost, the installation cost and the cost of grid connection
to shore [43]. Kites, on the other hand, have lower investment cost than wind turbines
due to elimination of e.g. rotor, steel, concrete and tower by lightweight structure. The
kite system consists of a kite, a tether, a generator and a winch. In general, kites use
a wing tethered to a ground station and ﬂy in loops as the wind moves through them.
Simultaneously, rotors on the wings spin and generate electricity that is sent down the
tether to the grid.
In conclusion, wind farms often experience turbulent ﬂow above and above and between
the turbines. Kites are being developed as a stand-alone solution [7], [8], but also as an
addition to conventional wind farms. This includes either kites that capture wind from
upper layer or sub-sea-level kites [44]. The integration of kites harnessing wind is believed
to yield an increase energy extraction by reducing the wind deﬁcit, leaving more energy
to the downstream turbine. The projects Nextwind (2017) coordinated by the Norwegian
company Kitemill AS (grant No 774974) [45], [46] and Powerkite (2020) coordinated by
Midroc Project Management AB (grant NO 654438) [47], are examples of projects funded
by the European Union on developing kite technology to capture wind energy and subsea
tidal wave, respectively.

The P-Dispersion-Sum Problem
The P-Dispersion-Sum Problem (PDSP) is the problem that has the objective to locate
p facilities out of n predeﬁned locations so that the sum of distances between the p es-
tablished facilities is maximized. The number of selected facilities is constant and thus,
maximizing the distance sum is equivalent to maximizing the average distance between
facilities. The PDSP problem is applicable to facilities that pose a threat to each other
and when facilities should be as far away from the closest other facility as possible. For
instance, in the telecommunication example, the dispersion of radio transceivers to ser-
vice cellular phones would minimize interference problems [48]. The PDSP has several
industrial application, e.g. in telecommunications.
In many ways, the WFLOP has a resemblance to the PDSP. Similar to the PDSP, the
WFLOP asks to locate p facilities (or turbines) at some of n predeﬁned locations. How-
ever, instead of maximizing the sum of distances between facilities, the aim is to maximize
the production by minimizing wake eﬀect. Both the aforementioned example of telecom-
munication and the WFLOP aim at minimizing interference problems. The PDSP is also
NP-hard which can be proven by reduction of a so-called clique problem [49] and [50].

of the objective function sums the expected power production from each turbine placed
along with the power losses due to wake eﬀect. The second term of the objective function
sums the expected power increase due to impact from the kites. The third term sums
the foundation cost of each turbine in the solution. The fourth and last term sums the
foundation cost of each kite in the solution. The ﬁrst constraint ensures that p turbines
are placed. The second constraint ensures that an available location can only be used
once. The third constraint ensures that turbines are not located too close to each other in
which rij is the minimum distance to satisfy between turbines in position i and j and dij
is the Euclidean distance between the two positions. The fourth constraint ensures that
turbines and kites are not located too close to each other in which r′
ij is the minimum
distance to satisfy between kites and turbines in position i and j. The ﬁfth constraint
ensures that two kites are not located too close to each other in which r′′
ij is the minimum
distance to satisfy between kites in position i and j. The sixth constraint deﬁnes xi as
binary variable and the last constraint deﬁnes x′
i as binary variable.
In this formulation, it is left to the optimizer to add kites to the layout as long as it is
proﬁtable. The optimizer will not place inﬁnite number of kites due to the cost of kites
and the minimum distance criteria.

3.3
The Greedy Heuristic
In short, the greedy heuristic starts by setting xi = 1 for all i ∈N and then iteratively
chooses the variable i with the worst contribution to the objective function and changes it
from xi = 1 to xi = 0. This is repeated until the iteration constraint is met, or ∑
i∈N xi = p
in Equation (3.4). The greedy heuristic makes the locally optimal choice at each step as it
attempts to ﬁnd the overall optimal solution. This approach is often used to build initial
solutions and has shown to be successful. However, sometimes the greedy heuristic fails in
ﬁnding the globally optimal solution since the decisions are only made on the information
it has at each step, regardless to the overall problem or data. Therefore, the solutions
found by greedy algorithm are not necessarily optimal. Instead, the initial solutions build
by greedy algorithm are often explored by local search or metaheuristics. The algorithm
makes the optimal choice at each step as it attempts to ﬁnd the overall optimal way to
solve the entire problem.
3.4
Initial Solution
An initial solution is partially based on the P-Dispersion Sum Problem (PDSP). The PDSP
asks to locate p facilities out of n predeﬁned locations, such that the sum of distances
between the p facilities is maximized [48], as described in Section 2.3.
Similarly, the
WFLOP asks to locate n turbines out of k available locations. Instead of maximizing the
distances among facilities as in the PDSP, this study aims at maximizing proﬁt from a given
wind farm by maximizing production and minimizing wake eﬀects. To obtain an initial
solution for the PDSP, Pisinger [48] proposed a greedy heuristic. For this study, the greedy
heuristic was implemented in the following way. A binary vector, x of length n, represents
the solution, with a value of 1 if a turbine is built and 0 otherwise. Correspondingly, a
binary vector, x′ of length n, represents the solution, with a value of 1 if a kite is built
and 0 otherwise. Initially, the heuristic places turbines in all available positions. Next,
the turbine that has the worst contribution to the objective function is removed from
the solution and this is repeated until the requested number of n turbines are left in the
solution.
When choosing the turbine that has the worst contribution to the objective
function, the following two factors are considered:
(i) The wake eﬀect that the turbine receives from other turbines.
(ii) The wake eﬀect that the turbine generates on other turbines.

3.4.1
The minimum distance constraints
To ensure that the solution is feasible, the arrangement of the requested number of turbines
in the layout needs to satisfy the minimum distance constraint, otherwise the solution is
not considered feasible. The distance constraints are as follows:
(i) The minimum distance between turbines is required to be at least ﬁve times the rotor
diameter of a turbine.
(ii) The minimum distance between a turbine and a kite is required to be at least once
the rotor diameter.
(iii) The minimum distance between kites is required to be at least three times the rotor
diameter.

3.5
Improvement Strategy
To improve the initial solution, a metaheuristic strategy is used. The metaheuristic strat-
egy chosen for this problem was Simulated Annealing. SA is a stochastic probabilistic
technique to approximate global optimum in combinatorial optimization problems [55].
It is based on an analogy process found in thermodynamics where metal is heated and
then cooled to strengthen its structure. A metal is heated to a high temperature to bring
it to a liquid phase and then gradually cooled back to a solid phase by slowly reducing
the temperature. This process allows the particles to arrange themselves in solid-state
which is only obtained if the temperature has been suﬃciently high and the cooling rate
is slow. To simulate the heating process, Simulated Annealing, uses two parameters, a
maximum temperature, T, and a cooling rate, α, to guide the solution. For each iteration,
the solution changes to an adjacent solution based on the neighborhood function. The
neighbourhood is evaluated and if the adjacent solution is better this becomes the new
arrangement of the solution. The algorithm is based on randomization techniques and a
Metropolis acceptance criterion.
A Simulated Annealing algorithm with three types of local search operations was built
and tailored to the problem. The operations are:
(i) Inserting a kite
(ii) Removing a kite
(iii) Moving a kite
As mentioned above, SA has two parameters; the cooling rate, α, and the initial tem-
perature, T. Since the objective function remains the same, the metaheuristic has only
one temperature, T, and one α instead of three of each. The SA starts with the initial
number of kites and for each operation the distance constraint is checked. Theoretically,
if the solution is better than the currently best found solution, the new solution is kept.
However, the SA algorithm also accepts worse solutions with a likelihood based on the
temperature and the magnitude of the change in energy and η is random ﬂoat number
between 0 and 1. By accepting worse solution, the SA allows for more extensive search for
the global optimal solution. This is repeated until the run-time reaches the predetermined
time limit.
There is no strict upper-bound of the number of kites allowed. However, the heuristic
strategy ensures that there will be ﬁnite number of kites since each kite comes with a cost.

3.5.1
Neighbourhood - Generation of candidate solutions
Neighbourhood solutions are generated to rearrange kites and create new candidate so-
lutions.
Candidate solutions are generated using the aforementioned three operations:
inserting a kite, removing a kite and moving a kite. The operation to use is selected at
random for each proposed solution. Further description of each operation is described
below.
Inserting a kite
The function that inserts kites generates an adjacent solution by adding a kite, or a
value of 1, to the existing solution vector. To generate a valid solution, the following
condition needs to be satisﬁed. Since kites can not be inserted at an occupied position,
the function starts with identifying all empty positions as potential candidates. Next,
a variable, to_add, that counts the number of available positions for kites is introduced
and deﬁned. The variable, to_add, then returns a random integer value in the range of 0
to the number of available positions or indexes for the kites to be placed at. The value
from to_add is then the index of the position to be chosen. In order to take the distance
constraint into consideration, a boolean variable, meets_distance_constraint, is introduced
and represents whether the distance criteria is met or not. The Euclidean distance between
the chosen index and every other indices is calculated and if the distance criteria is not met
for one case, then the boolean variable returns false and another iteration is generated. To
prevent the function from looping endlessly, e.g. if the distance constraint is never met,
a maximum number of iterations is deﬁned. However, if the distance criteria is met and
if this new neighbouring solution results in higher contribution to the objective function
compared to the current solution, a kite is added to the speciﬁc location and the function
returns the new arrangement of kites. The operation is illustrated in Figure 3.1a.
Removing a kite
The operation that removes kites generates an adjacent solution by removing a kite from
the existing solution. This is done by changing a value of 1 to 0 at a speciﬁc index. To
generate a valid solution, the following terms need to be satisﬁed. In order to remove a
kite, a kite needs to be located at the candidate position. It is required that a kite is
located at the particular position. Thus, the function starts with identifying valid indices
with occupied positions for kites. Next, a valid index is chosen at random and the kite
at the speciﬁc index is removed, indicated by changing the value from one to zero at the
speciﬁc index. If the length of valid indices is less than zero, no more kites are left in the
layout and the operation returns the previous solution without modiﬁcation. Since this
function removes kites, the distance constraint does not need to be taken into account.
The operation is illustrated in Figure 3.1b.
Moving a kite
The operation that moves kites generates an adjacent solution by moving a kite to another
available position. This is done by changing two values in the solution vector from 0 to 1
at one index and from 1 to 0 at another index. The operation starts with identifying all
empty positions as well as positions containing kites. The operation is based on a swap
with one ﬁxed kite at a position chosen at random and another position that is empty.
Similar to the function that inserts a kite, the function calls itself during its execution and
examines if the distance criteria is met. The distance between the chosen index and every
other indices is calculated and if the distance is less than any of the distance criterion, the
function looks for another solution. If the function ﬁnds a solution that meets the distance
constraints, the new arrangement of kites is updated. If the length of valid indices is less
than zero, no more kites are left in the layout and the operation returns the previous
solution without modiﬁcation. This operation is illustrated in Figure 3.1c.

Discussion
This chapter provides a discussion and interpretation based on the results presented in
the previous chapter. The relationship of the ﬁndings and previously conducted research
in literature are made clear.
5.1
General Discussion
The primary purpose of this study was to examine the eﬀect from integration of kites into
an oﬀshore wind farm layout. The study of kites has been evolving in recent years, as a
stand-alone solution [7], [8] and a sub-sea-level addition to wind farms [44]. The addition
of kites to counteract wake eﬀect by pressing wind from upper layer down to the wind
turbines as suggested in this study is new in the literature. The idea is based on prior
work by Pisinger and Cazzaro [32] on their approach using a heuristic technique to solve
the large oﬀshore wind farm layout optimization problem. Pisinger and Cazzaro proposed
a set of ten synthetic instances that simulate realistic setups. The model was successful
and was able to optimize the arrangement of a wind farm with hundreds of turbines.
The present work uses a similar methodology for optimizing the oﬀshore wind farm layout
optimization problem. The main diﬀerence lies in the addition of kites to the layout and
ﬁnding both the optimal number of kites and their locations. The aim of the thesis was
to answer the following research questions:
1. What is the most cost-eﬃcient arrangement of wind turbines in an oﬀshore wind
farm with regards to maximum power production and minimum cost?
2. Is it feasible to use kites with the purpose of pressing air down from upper layer to
the oﬀshore wind farm in order to enhance the eﬃciency of the oﬀshore wind farm?
3. If it is feasible to use kites, where should they be co-located within the wind farm
and its arrangement?
To answer these research questions, the problem was formulated, assumptions were given
and an optimization model was constructed. The assumptions were made in collaboration
with Pisinger and Cazzaro and are presented in Table 5.1. Some of these assumptions
were evaluated by a sensitivity analysis, see Section 4.3.
The parameters used in the model were based on the following prerequisites. The power
production of kites was considered to be zero. This assumption can be questioned since
kites could generate electricity [7] and [8]. Three diﬀerent minimum distance criteria were
used in the model. They were: 1) the distance between two turbines, 2) the distance
between a turbine and a kite and 3) the distance between two kites. It can be argued that
the minimum distance criterion between a turbine and a kite should also have a maximum
distance criterion to ensure the impact of the kite on the wind ﬂow to the turbine. In
addition, the literature presented a method of placing a kite between two turbines [9].
Finally, in the presented approach, the turbines are ﬁxed in a position after they have
been placed.
The model could be improved by allowing the wind turbines to change
locations taking the impact from the kites into account. This could result in a smaller
area for the wind farm with less space requirement.
The initial solution of locating turbines was inspired by the P-Dispersion-Sum-Problem
(PDSP) and the initial solution of kites based on a greedy heuristic. The models showed

According to the study the most cost-eﬃcient arrangement of wind turbines in an oﬀshore
wind farm with regards to maximum power production and minimum cost is to place them
at the end of the given area with appropriate distance between them to keep wake eﬀect to
a minimum. On the contrary, too large distance between the wind turbines would result
in over-exploitation of space needed to build the wind farm. The integration of kites with
the purpose of minimizing wake eﬀects in the oﬀshore wind farm was promising and would
enhance the overall eﬃciency. Then they should be co-located and arranged within the
wind according to the layout conﬁguration presented.
5.2
Limitations
This study is subject to several limitations. In order to estimate the impact of adding kites
to the layout, several parameters needed to be estimated for the model as presented in Sec-
tion 5.1. In some cases, the parameter estimates could have been based on more concrete
and calculable sources. Furthermore, the ﬂight operation of kites and several practical
aspects concerning their layout and type were neglected. Uncertainty in modelling and
simplicity in chosen parameters put further limits to the results.
The method can be executed in either one or two steps. In this thesis, a two-step execution
was chosen where wind turbines were placed ﬁrst and based on that solution, kites were
added to the layout as long as it increased the proﬁt. Alternatively, a one-step execution
could have been used to place both turbines and kites at the same time, e.g. by using The
Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure, (GRASP) [33]. The MIP model is limited
to a number of variables that it can handle and thus, could not ﬁnd an exact solution
for the instances with larger number of variables. A proximity search heuristic or other
heuristic could be combined with the MIP as done in [29].

Conclusion
The objective of the work was to solve The Oﬀshore Wind Farm Layout Optimization
Problem with co-location of turbines and kites. Speciﬁcally, placing both turbines and
kites to maximize the proﬁt from the 10 synthetic wind farms of diﬀerent sizes. The wind
farms consisted of diﬀerent numbers of positions, ranging from 3,238 up to 22,709. To
work towards this goal, both exact and heuristic strategies were used. An initial solution
for placing wind turbines was inspired by the P-Dispersion Sum Problem where the aim
was to maximize the power production while minimizing wake eﬀect instead of maximizing
the distance sum between p facilities and a greedy algorithm was used for placing kites. To
improve the initial solution, a Simulated Annealing algorithm (SA) was designed with three
types of local-search operations performed recursively until the model solution converges.
The numerical results from the model gave promising results and indicated that the in-
tegration of kites to the wind farms to lower wake eﬀects would increase proﬁt by 2% on
average. As the objective function highly relies on the wake eﬀect and the foundation cost
for turbines, several cases were tested based on diﬀerent prerequisites. The so-called base
case (see Section 3.6), assumed that the cost of a kite would be 1% of the foundation cost
for a turbine. Moreover, the expected increase in power production due to the harvested
wind from kites was estimated to be decrease by 5% of the wake eﬀects between turbines.
The sensitivity of some of the assumptions were tested by a sensitivity analysis and the
impact evaluated. The original assumptions are thought to be realistic and give a fair
assessment of the wind farm layout and the proﬁt made from the wind farm.
The integration of kites to conventional wind farms oﬀshore is considered to be an envi-
ronmentally friendly way to increase the energy-conversion eﬃciency of wind farms and
should be studied in more details. Moreover, the kites themselves are receiving increased
interest as the technology oﬀers an enormous future potential as a wind energy generation
solution. It is even predicted that the kites will replace wind turbines due to their low
cost, ﬂexibility and eﬃciency. The opportunities in developing innovative solutions that
contribute to a greener future are endless. Kites have received increased interest in recent
years for future wind energy generation. The integration of kites oﬀer an alternative that
should be investigated further, showing that for future wind power generation, the sky is
the limit.
6.2
Future Work
Due to the time constraints of the present study, some ideas that came up were not tested.
In the proposed model, the kites were added to the layout after the turbines had been
placed. Next, a local search was used to improve the initial solution. Alternatively, the
turbines and kites could be added to the layout at the same time. A possible heuristic could
be, e.g. a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP). Moreover, instead of
removing, adding and moving only one kite at a time, a greater number of kites could
be removed, added or moved simultaneously. Furthermore, a delta evaluation could be
used for a faster evaluation of the objective value of the solution instead of evaluating the
complete solution at every step since there is only a small change in the solution binary

In this study, the maintenance cost and transportation cost was omitted and the char-
acteristic of kites are based on a guesstimate. The objective function used in this study
relied on a parameter based on the energy price in the Netherlands to convert energy
production to proﬁt. An alternative could be using a power to cost ratio. However, since
the MIP model would not handle that, it was decided to use the energy price parameter.
Realistic assumptions were made when deciding on the parameters needed. In this model
only the foundation cost was considered and other cost omitted. Therefore, a suggestion
for a future work is to take maintenance and transportation cost into account as well as
other revenue streams. In addition, capital cost and eventual subsidies could be consid-
ered. Future research could also focus on the shape of the kites. Therefore, further studies
could be based on more detailed information.
