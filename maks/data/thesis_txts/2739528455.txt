Abstract
With the shift from monolithic architecture systems to microservice
architecture systems, observability has become a new pain point for prac-
titioners. To enhance observability in microservice systems, distributed
tracing tools and methods have been introduced. However, despite the
availability of these tools, many teams still struggle in adopting these
tools and methods.
This paper addresses this challenge by offering a structured approach
to distributed tracing. A conceptual framework has been developed to
guide organizations in designing a suitable distributed tracing system.
Additionally, a case study of implementing a distributed tracing system
for a microservice application has been conducted using the guidance pro-
vided by the conceptual framework.
The results indicate that the conceptual framework is generally effec-
tive in providing an overview of the architecture in a distributed system.
Moreover, the case study demonstrates that the framework can be used to
guide the design of a new distributed tracing system. However, the frame-
work offers limited guidance at the component level, and therefore future
work could focus on providing more detailed support for decision-making.

Introduction
In traditional software engineering, applications were designed to be self-contained,
adopting a monolithic architecture. One of the benefits of a monolithic applica-
tion is its tight coupling. This tight coupling generally facilitate easy exchange
of relevant messages and information between different aspects of the program
at any given point in the code, allowing for a straightforward tracing of mes-
sage sequences[1]. Due to the entire system being within a single code base,
observability is relatively easy to achieve. In this context, observability refers
to the ability to measure a system’s current state based on the available data
[2]. However, this high level of coupling also presents challenges in a monolithic
system, as it slows down the process of updating and scaling the system[3].
As technology has evolved, an increasing number of businesses have been
shifting towards a more well-separated microservice architecture[4].
From a
business perspective, the benefits of microservices are abundant. One major im-
provement, is that this architecture offers superior scalability and performance,
especially under heavy workloads. Additionally, the modular structure allows
developers to modify and deploy individual services, leading to easier mainte-
nance and updates[5]. However, with the shift from monolithic to microservice
architecture, developers are encountering new challenges. One such challenge is
the lack of observability that this separation brings[6]. Observability is essen-
tial for facilitating debugging and maintenance of data services[7]. The lack of
observability, makes it difficult to track the flow of requests, as microservices
typically consist of many services that interact within a complex network. This
uncertainty in the request lifecycle impedes the efficiency of troubleshooting and
debugging, making it more challenging for software engineers to detect and fix
bugs and errors in programs.
To assist programmers with troubleshooting and debugging in a microservice
system, the umbrella term ’distributed tracing’ has been gaining traction. In
software systems, tracing is generally useful for programmers to debug systems,
and they often uses logging to record information about the series of events that
occur during program execution. These logged events must include the necessary
information to help find errors and assist in the debugging process. Distributed
tracing extends the concept of tracing to microservices and addresses some of
the earlier mentioned pain points for debuggers and troubleshooters. As Yuri
Shkuro writes in his book, ”Distributed tracing, also known as end-to-end or
workflow-centric tracing, is a family of techniques that aim to capture the de-
tailed execution of causally- related activities, performed by the components of
a distributed system.”[8].
However, despite the theoretical advantages of distributed tracing tools,
many teams are still not using distributed tracing in their projects[9]. Instead,
they continue to spend hours tracking down bugs and issues through manual
logging and troubleshooting processes.

1.1
Problem Statement
With the increased use of microservice architecture, it has become highly com-
plex to monitor and diagnose performance issues, service latency, and errors.
Traditional monitoring and tracing approaches fall short in providing the nec-
essary information, especially in large scale distributed systems. Despite the
availability of tracing tools and techniques, a critical gap remains where project
teams do not adopt these methods in practice. Without clear guidance, organi-
zations struggle to design, implement, and utilize tracing systems in a way that
aligns with their business goals and objectives.
This project aims to address this challenge by developing a conceptual frame-
work that captures the essential components of a distributed tracing system,
providing structure and guidance. Furthermore, this project includes the de-
sign and implementation of a distributed tracing system developed based on the
conceptual framework. The problem statement addressed in this thesis is:
How can we guide organizations in designing a tracing system that aligns their
goals and objectives?
To maintain a clearer overview, the problem statement is broken down into
two separate parts. These questions will be investigated and answered through-
out the thesis to address the problem statement:
• RQ1: How can we provide a structured approach for designing distributed
tracing systems?
• RQ2: How can a distributed tracing system designed using this struc-
tured guidance assist developers in debugging and troubleshooting their
microservice systems?
The thesis roughly follows the order of the research questions, with the first
part focusing on developing a conceptual framework for designing distributed
tracing systems, and the second part focusing on the development of such tracing
system.
1.2
Methodology
The approach of this project is grounded in the principles of design science
research. As D. Johannesson proposes in his book, An introduction to design
science [10], this project will be divided into five overall phases that will be itera-
tively revisited throughout the project. The five phases are problem explication,
requirement definition, artefact design and development, artefact demonstration
and artefact evaluation. The five phases and their relationships are shown in
figure 1. However, it is important to note that the design cycle is an iterative
process, which means that phases will be repeated as more knowledge is being
gained.

State of the Art
As microservice architecture has become more widely adopted and the challenge
of tracing such systems has become more prominent, many technologies and
tools have been introduced to address the issue. This section will review some
of the currently available technologies for distributed tracing.
2.1
Conceptual Work
The concept of distributed tracing, which involves using a single trace to record
calls from all services involved in a single user request, has been around for a
long time. Google published a paper in this field[12], introducing key terms
such as ’trace’ and ’span’. A trace is conceptualized as a tree of spans, where
each span represents a unit of work performed by a single service[12]. A visual
representation of the relationship between traces and spans is shown in figure 2,
where each block is a span representing work done by a single service. In the
paper, context correlation is a major element, as it allows us to relate several
request back to a single user request by propagating a unique ID and relevant
context through the involved services.

(b) Trace and Span
Figure 2: Trace as a tree of spans
Yuri Shkuro, the creator of Jaeger, covers the fundamentals of distributed
tracing, including the anatomy of a distributed tracing system [8]. He explains
that microservices are instrumented through trace points that observe the exe-
cution of a request. However, his explanation is very specific to distributed trac-
ing systems built around metadata propagation and does not provide a broader
perspective of distributed tracing systems in general. Figure 3 illustrates the
anatomy of a typical distributed tracing system built around metadata propa-
gation, as explained by Shkuro.
6
==PAGE:15==
Figure 3: Anatomy of distributed tracing [8]
Austin Parker shares many viewpoints with Shkuro in his book[13]. Parker
divides distributed tracing into three main areas of focus. The first is instru-
mentation, which he describes as the process of generating trace data by trans-
forming existing telemetry data into trace data. Like Shkuro, he also mentions
spans as the building blocks of request based traces. The next area is deploy-
ment, which involves sending the generated data elsewhere. Here, he discusses
the trade-offs in overhead relating to how much data is pertained or discarded.
The final focus area is delivering value, where traces can be presented through
various observability tools and techniques to measure the key metrics for your
monitored system.
This literature is particularly useful for understanding the structure of a
distributed tracing system and will serve as the foundation for the conceptual
framework developed in this project. In addition to these works, the next section
will discuss the tools and techniques currently available in the market.
2.2
Distributed Tracing Tools and Techniques
In the literature review ”Open tracing tools: Overview and critical comparison”,
Janes, A., Li, X., and Lenarduzzi, V. investigate and discuss the features of 30
different tracing tools [14]. They compare the distinctive features of these tools,
their popularity among peers, and both the advantages and disadvantages of
each tool. Their results indicate that it is difficult to conclude that any one
tracing tool is superior, as different tracing tools offer different features. This
list of tools serves as a valuable starting point for developing my conceptual
framework and designing a solution for the case study. The list of tools can be
7
==PAGE:16==
found in Appendix A.
In the paper Service Mesh Based Distributed Tracing System[15], Cha, D.
and Kim, Y. address the challenges administrators face with traditional ob-
servability and tracing systems. They highlight the difficulties in recognizing
environments or languages for all microservices in a system, particularly when
new services are introduced to the system. They propose a tracing architec-
ture focused on ease of management for both managers and developers. The
proposed architecture creates a tracing system using a service mesh extension
that functions independently of changes in internal applications.
A service
mesh is an infrastructure layer which manages and optimizes communication
between microservices within a distributed application. The paper proposes a
solution utilizing this infrastructure combined with sidecars, a secondary con-
tainer running alongside the main application container in a pod, for handling
request/responses and exporting collected data to the desired collectors. Since
data collection occurs in a sidecar independent of the services, this architecture
is not significantly affected by changes or extensions to internal services or the
network and also has the advantage of being easily scalable. This architecture
can provide root cause analysis and detect bottlenecks in services, but also re-
quires the implementation of a service mesh.
Despite the wide range of available tools and studies showing that adopting
such tools can indeed provides benefits, such as improving software maintenance
and enhancing the quality[12, 16], there are also studies that discuss the chal-
lenges in maintaining traceability tools[17]. This highlights the fact that each
company’s selection of tools highly depends on their goals and resource limi-
tations. To obtain a broader view on distribute tracing, these existing tools
and techniques will be considered both in the development of the conceptual
framework and in designing a tracing system for the case study.

3
Conceptual Framework
This chapter presents the designed conceptual framework for distributed tracing
systems. The framework outlines the anatomy of such tracing systems, including
the components it consists of. Most of the components are derived from exist-
ing literature. For example, Yuri Shkuro presents the anatomy of distributed
tracing for a very specific type of tracing. As shown earlier in figure 3, Shkuro’s
anatomy model includes components like collection, trace storage, trace recon-
struction, data mining and presentation. My framework also draws inspiration
from Austin Parker’s work, where he divides distributed tracing into three main
focus areas: instrumentation, deployment and delivering value [13]. The goal of
this conceptual framework is to provide a structure that can describe the major-
ity of distributed tracing systems and to suggest a decision-making process for
designing a distributed tracing system. The framework incorporates elements
from both works, but splits certain areas into more detailed components.
Figure 4: Data flow between components in a distributed tracing system. The
colored boxes symbolize components, and the arrows symbolize flow between
them. For instance, x →y means that data flows from x to y.
In this framework, a distributed tracing system is considered to consist of six
different components: Request Correlation, Data Collection, Data Processing,
Data Transportation, Data Storage and Data Presentation. Request correla-
tion handles one of the key elements in a tracing system, which is to bundle
all requests related to a single case. The Data Collection component involves
the methods and tools used to collect traces and metrics. Data processing is
concerned with transforming data from one format to another for improved data
storage and visualization. The Data Storage component refers to the methods
and tools used to store the data. Data presentation defines which methods and
9
==PAGE:18==
tools used to visualize and present the stored data. Finally, the Data Trans-
portation component was added to emphasize the existence of the mechanic of
transporting the data between the other components. Figure 4 illustrates the
components and how data flows between them.
To determine which tools and mechanisms to choose for each component,
we need additional information about the system we are monitoring and the
goals we aim to achieve through tracing. This information includes not only
the objectives but also any limitations in terms of resources, technical aspects,
and attributes of the monitored system. Additionally, we need to consider the
data that must be collected to meet the defined goals and the format in which
it should be transformed. These additional considerations will be referred to
as information points throughout the rest of the paper.
Figure 5 illustrates
the various information points that must be addressed to design a satisfactory
tracing system.
Figure 5: Additional information needed to design a tracing system. The box
is the tracing system, the hexagonals symbolize the necessary additional infor-
mation needed, and the arrows symbolize dependencies. For instance, x →y
means that y depend on x.
The following subsections will provide details about the components of a
distributed tracing system as viewed within the scope of this conceptual frame-
work. Next, the information points will be presented and discussed. Finally, a
proposed workflow for designing a distributed tracing system will be outlined.
3.1
Components
The components of the system are the tools and mechanisms that make up a
distributed tracing system. The designed conceptual framework consists of six
distinct components, which will be presented in this section.

==PAGE:19==
3.1.1
Data Collection
The first component is Data Collection, which determines the method and tools
used for gathering data. Data collection refers to collecting the metrics and
information exchanged between or within the services in a distributed system
that are necessary to meet the defined goals and objectives.
Data collection can be divided into two main categories and is often closely
related to the chosen request correlation method. In general, the methods can
be categorized as manual instrumentation and auto-instrumentation. Manual
instrumentation methods require developers to explicitly add trace points to
their source code and specify what data should be collected. This method offers
several advantages, as it gives developers full control over what data is collected.
This means that developers can decide exactly what should be gathered, reduc-
ing noise and making the analysis more straightforward. Manual instrumen-
tation can also be easily adapted to different use cases, allowing developers to
modify the tracing as needed. However, one of the disadvantages is the addi-
tional effort required from developers to manually add and maintain the tracing
code. This process can be time-consuming and prone to errors, as everything
is handled manually. As a result, data collection may not be uniform, leading
to gaps in tracing data and incomplete traces. Additionally, manual instrumen-
tation requires ongoing maintenance, which further consumes developers’ time.
Some tools that support manual instrumentation include OpenTelemetry[18],
Zipkin[19], and OpenTracing[20].
Auto-instrumentation involves using libraries or agents that automatically
instrument the necessary code to collect data without requiring developers to
manually add trace points. One of the key advantages of auto-instrumentation
is the minimal effort required from developers, as the instrumentation is carried
out automatically. It also ensures that data is collected uniformly across all
services. Additionally, auto-instrumentation is usually quick to set up, allowing
for rapid deployment without significant code changes. However, the disadvan-
tages include increased performance overhead due to the automatic insertion
of tracing code, as well as limited flexibility in customizing traces, since the
instrumentation may be generic and not cover all desired trace points. More-
over, because the process is automatic, it collects all data, which can result in
large amounts of irrelevant information, creating noise that must be filtered out
later. Examples of auto-instrumentation tools are Datadog APM[21], Pixie[22]
and OpenTelemetry Auto-Instrumentation[23].
A summary of the advantages and disadvantages of both manual and auto-
instrumentation can be found in Table 1.

For manual instrumentation, the amount of instrumentation varies from so-
lution to solution. Some solutions demand a high level of instrumentation, while
others require less. If reducing the effort for developers is a key consideration
but there is still important data that needs to be gathered, it may be worth
considering a mixed approach, where the majority of data collection happens
automatically, with a few instances of manual instrumentation implemented as
needed.
Another way to find a middle ground between manual and auto-instrumentation
is to explore distributed tracing middleware. This approach involves integrating
tracing directly into the middleware layer of the code, allowing for some degree
of flexibility in tracing while keeping the required coding to a minimum. Ex-
amples of middleware-based tracing tools include service meshes like Istio[24],
Envoy[25], and Linkerd[26].
One important consideration is that the mechanism or tool chosen for this
component can be highly dependent on the architecture of the system you are
tracing. For example, if the monitored system already has a service mesh in
place, the implementation for data collection could easily build on the service
mesh to gather data. However, in an existing system without a service mesh,
this approach would require significant changes.
As a final note, it is important to mention that most data collection tools
usually support a variety of different data storages to store the collected data.
If the chosen data collection does not provide a natural storage selection, refer
to Section 3.1.3 for more information on storage types and uses.

3.1.2
Data Transportation
The data transportation component mainly refers to the transportation between
the data processing, data storage and data presentation/analysis components.
In many cases, the transportation tools often depends on the specific data
stores and visualization tools being used. For instance, data can be transported
through direct API calls, a method commonly available when the data storage
is integrated with the data presentation tool or for certain visualization tools
supported by the data stores. This approach is generally simple to implement,
as many storage systems already provide APIs for access. It is easy to use and
well-suited for systems with simple architectures where the volume of data is
not overwhelming. Additionally, since the access is immediate, latency is low,
making it ideal for real-time data processing and visualizations. However, the
downside is that the tight coupling between the data storage and the visualiza-

Since they typically

3.1.3
Data Storage
Selecting storage for the processed data is a crucial decision. The choice of data
storage can significantly impact other components in the system, as it must in-
tegrate well with both the data processing and data visualization components.
In the context of a tracing system, it is particularly important to decide
whether to use built-in storage or if an external storage solution is more suit-
able.
Built-in storage refers to storage solutions that are integrated into dis-
tributed tracing systems and are often tightly coupled with the tracing tools.
One major advantage of built-in storage is that it is typically optimized for
storing trace data, often offering useful features tailored to this purpose. Since
it comes as part of a tracing system, it usually operates seamlessly with the
selected tracing tools and is easy to set up and use. However, a downside is that
built-in storage may not scale well with large amounts of data. Furthermore,
developers have limited control over the storage, which can lead to constraints
in areas such as customization and optimization. Examples of tracing tools that
provide built-in storage include Jaeger and Zipkin.
14
==PAGE:23==
External storage is typically offered by third-party systems and can be used
alongside selected tracing tools. These solutions are usually more scalable and
capable of handling large volumes of data. They also generally allow developers
to optimize performance and integrate with other systems, providing a more
flexible solution. External storage is ideal for storing data that you want to
retain for a long time. However, using external storage increases the complexity
of your tracing system, as it adds another component to manage. It can also
introduce latency since the data must be retrieved before it can be displayed in
visualization tools. Additionally, it can be expensive to implement, especially in
cases of cloud-based services. Examples of external storages are Elasticsearch,
Amazon S3 and Cassandra.

3.1.4
Request Correlation
Another key component in a distributed tracing system is request correlation.
The purpose of request correlation is to identify and group together all requests
and messages that relate to the same case. This mechanism is crucial for appro-
priately bundling related data. In some cases, request correlation might even
be considered part of the pre-processing or a subsection of the data processing
component.
However, the primary reason for treating it as a separate com-
ponent is that every distributed tracing system must have a method of request
correlation, whereas the specific methods within the data processing component
are not always required in a tracing system.
Three options of request correlation methods have been identified: context
propagation, Black-box inference and schema-based inference [8].
Context propagation is the most commonly used method for request cor-
relation. It involves explicitly passing correlation information along with the
17
==PAGE:26==
requests as they move through the system of services. This correlation informa-
tion typically consists of trace IDs and span IDs, but it can also include other
relevant metadata. Context propagation can be divided into two sub-categories:
intra-process propagation and inter-process propagation. Intra-process propaga-
tion relates to communication within a single service. This is particularly useful
if you need to trace the flow of a request or task within a single complex service
that might involve multiple threads or asynchronous sub-tasks. Inter-process
propagation pertains to communication between services in a system and is es-
pecially useful when the application consists of multiple services communicating
over a network.
The advantages of context propagation include its high accuracy, as the
reliability of correlations increases when the context is explicitly propagated
with requests.
Additionally, it has low overhead, as the information passed
along is usually a relatively small amount of data. This method also enables
end-to-end tracing across all services, providing a complete picture of a user’s
requests as they move through the entire system.
However, context propagation also has some disadvantages. It often requires
additional code instrumentation, which can increase complexity for developers
who must ensure that the context is properly propagated. This can be par-
ticularly challenging in large systems or systems with a significant number of
third-party components. The method also heavily depends on the communica-
tion protocols supporting context propagation, which may not always be the
case. Furthermore, there is a potential for loss if a service fails to propagate the
context correctly, leading to gaps in the trace. Examples of tools using context
propagation are OpenTelemetry[32], Jaeger[33] and Zipkin[34].
Black-box inference is a no-coding solution that does not require modification
of the monitored system. This method works by inferring causality and request
correlation through the analysis of event records, examining metrics such as
timing, content, and patterns of messages exchanged
The advantages of black-box inference include its non-intrusive nature. Since
it requires no code changes, it is especially suitable for legacy systems or third-
party systems where the source code cannot or should not be modified. Addi-
tionally, this method can be applied to almost any system, regardless of whether
it was originally designed with tracing in mind.
However, black-box inference has its drawbacks. It often incurs increased
latency and higher costs compared to other methods due to its reliance on large
volumes of data.
It can also be challenging to accurately infer causality in
systems with a high degree of concurrency and asynchronous executions. Since
the correlation is based on heuristics, the resulting traces may also be incomplete
and less precise.
Examples of black-box inference tools include Splunk[35], which uses ma-
chine learning and pattern recognition techniques to infer relationships, and
Elastic APM[36], which can infer relationships based on analysis of timing and
patterns.
18
==PAGE:27==
The schema-based method is a technique that relies on manually written,
application-specific event schemata to extract causality from production system
logs. The advantages of this approach include improved consistency across all
services, as they are required to use the same identifiers, which simplifies the
process of finding correlations. Additionally, it provides a standardized way to
include correlation data, which can be enforced across all services. The schema
is also easily extensible, allowing for the inclusion of additional context or use-
ful metadata. However, there are some disadvantages to this method. Con-
sistently enforcing the schema across services can be challenging, especially in
heterogeneous environments or when dealing with external, third-party services.
Moreover, the schemata introduce additional implementation overhead, requir-
ing modifications to application code, which demands extra time and effort. If
the schema is poorly designed or inadequately enforced, it can also lead to in-
complete or incorrect correlations, undermining the effectiveness of the tracing
system.
Tools that assist in schema-based correlations include Prometheus, which
can correlate metrics using labels, and AWS X-Ray, which uses segment docu-
ments that include schemata to define structures and relationships

3.1.5
Data Processing
Another important aspect of tracing is data processing, which determines how
data will be transformed into the necessary formats required by the data presen-
tation and analysis component. It is crucial to filter and organize data to better
analyze and visualize the key aspects of the monitored system. Data processing
can be broadly categorized into two approaches: real-time data processing and
post-collection data processing.
Real-time data processing has the advantage of providing immediate feed-
19
==PAGE:28==
back, enabling developers to react swiftly. It also allows for detailed, real-time
insights during analysis. However, the disadvantages include the significant re-
sources and complexity required to implement such systems, leading to higher
costs. Real-time processing is particularly beneficial in situations where instant
reactions are critical, such as in financial trading systems or health monitoring,
where rapid responses are necessary if something goes wrong.
In post-collection data processing, data is processed only after it has been
collected. A key advantage of this approach is that it allows for more thorough
and in-depth analysis. It is generally easier to implement and often cheaper
due to the lack of constant monitoring. Additionally, it can be optimized for
resource efficiency, such as scheduling processing during times of lower resource
usage. However, a significant disadvantage is the delay in insights, which may
result in losing some real-time details and nuances. Post-collection processing is
not suitable for latency-sensitive tasks and is better suited for in-depth analysis
and scenarios where immediate insights are less critical.
Most implementations use a mix of real-time and post-collection processing,
depending on the goals and objectives defined for specific scenarios. The ad-

Another method is data aggregation, which combines data from multiple
spans, traces, or services to provide a condensed view of your system. This

mance of the monitored system. It also aids in managing large volumes of data

This additional information could range from user details and geographic loca-

broader understanding of the traces within a wider context and improving the
correlation process. It also allows for more detailed analysis by considering ad-
ditional aspects. However, enriching the collected data increases the complexity
of the processing pipeline and can negatively affect performance by potentially
increasing overhead, thus slowing down processing. Tools providing data en-
richment include Elastic Logstash, New Relic[37] and Fluentd[38].
Another processing method is normalization, which standardizes data for-
mats and structures across different services and technologies to ensure a more
consistent and fluid analysis. This method facilitates comparison and analysis
across multiple services and environments. The advantages of normalization
include the ability to easily compare and correlate data from different sources,
leading to more accurate analysis.
It also helps eliminate inconsistencies in
data formats and structures, thereby reducing errors. Additionally, since nor-
malization transforms data into a more consistent format, it becomes easier to
integrate traced data with other data sources, such as logs or additional met-
rics. However, normalization also has its drawbacks. It increases processing
overhead and can potentially lead to data loss. While standardization leads to
a more consistent format, some original data may need to be altered, which can
result in losses. Moreover, implementing a robust normalization process can be
challenging, adding to the overall complexity. Tools that can utilize data nor-

malization include Elasticsearch, Apache Nifi and the OpenTelemetry Collector.

data, identifying potential issues, flagging unusual patterns, and generally re-
ducing the amount of data that requires further analysis. One key advantage
of anomaly detection compared to the other methods mentioned is its ability to
detect issues in real-time rather than after the fact. It also reduces the need for
manual data inspection. However, this approach adds complexity, potentially
leading to more resource-intensive systems. Additionally, it can produce false
alerts or miss issues depending on the accuracy of the implemented algorithms.
All of the mentioned data processing methods are useful for both real-time
and post-collection processing. However, the use cases for these methods may
differ depending on whether they are applied in real-time or after data collection.

3.1.6
Data Presentation and Analysis
Data presentation and analysis are undoubtedly essential for distributed tracing.
The primary purpose of distributed tracing is to achieve specific objectives, such
as improving system performance and troubleshooting, by providing enhanced
observability across complex, distributed systems [39]. To efficiently reach the
defined goals and objectives, it is crucial to have a suitable method for display-
ing and analyzing the data.
23
==PAGE:32==
In the realm of distributed tracing, there are various methods for presenting
and analyzing data. One of the most common methods is trace visualization.
Trace visualization shows the entire flow of how a request moves through a
system of services. It is typically represented using a waterfall diagram or a
sequence diagram, where each horizontal bar represents the time spent in a
service or a process within a service. An example of such a trace visualization
is shown in figure 6. On the figure, the services the requests travel through are
shown on the y-axis, and the length of the bar symbolizes the time spent for
each request in the given service.
Figure 6: Example of trace visualization graph [40]
Trace visualization is especially useful in cases where requests experience
high latency, as it allows developers to quickly identify which services may be
causing the delay. The length of each bar indicates the duration of each opera-
tion, making it easier to pinpoint bottlenecks. Additionally, this type of diagram
is often easy to interpret, which makes it accessible for the entire team. How-
ever, it can be overwhelming to view all services in complex systems and may
not scale well for systems with many requests, as visualizing every trace can
lead to excessive data and clutter. Tools that are useful for trace visualization
include Jaeger, Zipkin, Datadog and many more.
Another well-known method is the service dependency graph. This graph is
typically visualized with nodes representing individual services and edges repre-
senting the interactions and communication between those services. As such, the
service dependency graph provides a visual representation of the relationships
and dependencies between services within a system. An example of a service
dependency graph is shown in figure 7. In the figure the nodes are services and
the edges represent requests. In this example, additional latency information is
shown for each service.

3.2
Information Points
The information points entail additional information needed to be made in order
to make a suitable decision for the components. The information is often very
situational and depends largely on the project, team, and individual situation.
I have identified five major information points, three of which can be identified
prior to anything else, and the last two are dependent on previous decisions.
These information points will be explained in the following.
3.2.1
Resource Limitations
One of the major questions that needs to be answered prior to anything else in
the conceptual model, is
What are your limitations on resources?
It is important to understand and acknowledge the limitations and con-
straints related to the team and resources, as these factors will often influence
which solutions are most suitable and whether they are feasible in the first place
Some of the specific limitations you need to consider are:
• Limitations on Budget
27
==PAGE:36==
• Limitations on Workload
• Limitations on Overhead
Budget limitations can impact which tools are available, as some can be
quite expensive.
If there are significant constraints on workload, it may be
worth considering auto-instrumentation or black-box inference to reduce the
amount of required work. Similarly, limitations on overhead may rule out some
of the more resource-intensive methods across all components.
3.2.2
Realistic Goals and Objectives
Another foundational question to answer is
What are we trying to achieve with distributed tracing?
To design or choose a satisfactory tracing system, it is essential to clearly
define the goals and objectives, as these will determine the purpose of the trac-
ing system. Careful consideration is needed when defining these goals, as it is
challenging to provide an effective solution for an ill-defined problem or objec-
tive. The less defined the goals and objectives are, the more generic the solution
will be.
However, while it is crucial to define the goals and objectives clearly, it is
also important to ensure they are realistic and feasible. You may aspire to de-
sign the perfect tracing system for your needs, but as mentioned in the previous
subsection, there may be constraints that limit what can be achieved. There-
fore, it is always important to ensure that the defined goals are aligned with the
existing limitations.
Some of the common goals for distributed tracing are as follows:
• Performance Monitoring
• Root Cause Analysis and Troubleshooting
• Optimization of Resource Usage
• Capacity Planning
• Compliance and Auditing
• Improving User Experience
• Service Dependency Visualization
Depending on the goals of your tracing system, the final solution can vary
significantly.
For example, if the goal is to analyze the internal flow of your services, code
instrumentation becomes essential to tailor the data collection and correlation
28
==PAGE:37==
methods to your specific system. In this case, you might also focus more on
intra-process propagation to closely examine the flow within a single service.
On the other hand, if the goal is to analyze the flow between services, inter-
process propagation is more suitable, as it is commonly used to reveal commu-
nication between services. In this scenario, auto-instrumentation could also be
an option, as many auto-instrumentation tools support this functionality.
3.2.3
Attributes of The Monitored System
The third information point focuses on the development state of the monitored
system. To tailor an effective tracing solution, it’s crucial to thoroughly under-
stand the current state and architecture of your system.
What does your system look like?
For instance, if you’re dealing with an existing, older legacy system, black-
box correlation might be beneficial since it allows you to avoid making changes
to the code, reducing the risk of inadvertently causing issues. On the other hand,
if the monitored system is new and you’re aiming to design it thoughtfully with
distributed tracing in mind, schema-based correlation might be a more suitable
approach. Additionally, if your system already has a service mesh implemented
for other purposes, such as security, it may be significantly easier to extend the
service mesh to include tracing capabilities, as the service mesh itself doesn’t
need to be implemented from scratch.
The architecture of the monitored system also directly impacts the tracing
system, particularly concerning the components within your system. Databases,
message queues, and programming languages can all influence which tools and
methods are more suitable. For example, tools like Appdash are tailored for
the Go programming language, while ElasticAPM requires an Elastic Stack
deployment to function effectively.
3.2.4
Needed Data
Another important consideration is the type of data that needs to be gathered,
which is highly dependent on the goals and objectives of the tracing system.
What data do we need to gather?
This is the stage where you need to consider what data is required to answer
the questions arising from your goals and objectives. It’s important to identify
both the immediate data relevant to your case and the raw data that needs to
be gathered for later use.
For example, if your goal is to visualize end-to-end tracing for debugging pur-
poses, it might be useful to collect requests and responses of all communications,
along with their bodies. This would allow developers to see the exact commu-
nication between services. Additionally, gathering timestamps, the sender, and
perhaps even the receiver of the messages could be beneficial.
29
==PAGE:38==
As will be explained in a later section, many components depend on this
module. To simplify the decision-making process for these components, it can
be helpful to compile a clear list of the types of data that need to be gathered.
In addition to determining the types of data that need to be collected, it is
also important to consider how much data should be gathered. In some cases,
collecting only a sample may be sufficient. However, in other situations, such
as debugging, it might be necessary to collect all exchanged data.
3.2.5
Data Formats
Apart from the raw data collected, it is also important to identify the formats
in which you want your data to be stored. This is crucial not only for efficient
storage but also for ensuring that the data can be easily retrieved and used for
visualizations and analyses.
What format does our data need to have?
The answer to this question depends on several factors, one of the most
important being how you intend to present and analyze the data. For instance,
if you plan to display full traces, it may be necessary to store the entirety of
all requests and responses. In other cases, you might only need an aggregated
view of the gathered information, in which case you could process the data into
averages and other aggregations.

3.3.1
Designing a Tracing System
As mentioned in their respective sections, each component depends on other
components and additional information that must be gathered. To assist in
the process of designing a distributed tracing system, a workflow model has
been proposed. This workflow model consists of information points and deci-
sion points. Information points involve gathering details about a specific area,
while decision points focus on selecting a particular tool or mechanism at the
component level. Information points with no incoming arrows indicate that they
are independent and can often be addressed without input from other areas of
the tracing system. In theory, you can begin anywhere in the workflow model,
but decisions should only be finalized after considering all dependencies. The
components and information points are shown in Figure 9.

hexagons symbolize information points, and directional arrows symbolize de-
pendency. For instance, x →y means that y depends on x.
The two independent information points are Resource Limitations and At-
tributes of the Monitored System. Resource limitations directly impact what
goals and objectives are realistic. While one could argue that goals and ob-

Resource limitations will influence decisions across all

developers. One of the direct impacts of the goals and objectives is on the Data

intention should guide the selection of the most effective method of presentation
or analysis to resolve the issue or achieve the goal. Additionally, the goals and
objectives determine what data should be collected, as there is a direct connec-
tion between the goals and the data needed to achieve them. Since the goals
are realistic, this will also indirectly affect the Needed Data information point.
Attributes of the Monitored System is the other independent information
point, and it plays a crucial role in determining the appropriate tools and meth-

characteristics of the monitored system significantly influence which tools and
methods are feasible. For example, if the monitored system is written in Go,
certain tools will be more suitable than others. However, to make informed de-

To decide on the appropriate data processing mechanisms, we have consider
both the needed data and the needed data formats. The reasoning behind this
is straightforward, as the needed data roughly translates to how the collected
data appears, and the needed data formats dictate which formats the collected
data needs to be transformed to.
Finally, we come to the Data Storage and Data Transportation components.
Selecting suitable data storage depends heavily on the format of the data being
saved. For instance, if you need to store full traces populated with timestamps,

data storage is selected. It also depends on the data presentation and analysis,

and the presentation components.
As a final note, it is important to view the design process as an iterative
one. The dependencies outlined are the most important and direct ones, but
when designing and developing a tracing system, unexpected dependencies be-
tween components may arise. Therefore, it is essential to continually review the
selected tools at the decision points to ensure that the designed system remains
feasible.

Case Study - Flex Platform
To work with a real-life example, the Flex Platform by IBM CIC Denmark

can sell their electricity consumption flexibility.
One of the major actors in this agreement are the Flex Providers.
Flex
Providers are companies with controllable assets that sign a contract with The

be adjusted or turned on/off to control the power consumption. The amount

aspect the flex platform team must work with, is to predict how much power

The Flex Platform team is relatively small where the technical team can be

about this type of regulation. Although the team speaks to each other on a daily
basis, miscommunication is bound to happen cross different teams, leading to
errors in the system that are quite difficult to debug. The small size of the
project’s team can lead to resource limitation, and the management might have
to prioritize on tasks.
To better understand the characteristics and possible requirements from such
a project, the structure of the Flex Platform is studied and presented.

Scenario 1: Race Condition
The first scenario relates to the async nature of message queues that reside
within Knative. To understand the scenario, we must take a closer look at the
communication to and from the services residing inside Knative. The service
inside Knative will be referred to as the AI models, and the forecast service
is prompting the AI models for data. The forecast service communicates to
Knative through REST to retrieve the calculated power flexibility of the assets
for the following day. Knative then keeps the communication channel open with
the forecast service, but places the incoming request as a message on the message
queue internally in Knative for the AI models to take. The ”correct” AI model
then takes the request, and places a new message on the message queue after
calculating the numbers. Finally, Knative takes this message and returns it to
the forecast service. The communication is roughly sketched in figure 12.

Proposed Solution
This chapter presents the proposed solution for creating a distributed tracing
system to address the challenges faced by the Flex Platform. The design of the
solution will be guided by the developed conceptual framework and workflow
model shown in figure 9 in Chapter 3.3.1.
5.1
Information Points
As mentioned in Chapter 3, a good starting point is to identify the resource lim-
itations as well as the goals and objectives related to tracing the Flex Platform.
Given that the Flex Platform team is relatively small, with each member already
assigned specific responsibilities within the project, one of the most significant
resource limitations is the need to minimize coding. The Flex Platform team
has expressed their preference for the solution to be as automated as possible,
requiring minimal coding. Additionally, it was also expected to find a solution
free of charge, as the costs of a tracing system had not been factored into the
budgeting. There are no specific limitations on performance overhead, as long
as it remains at a reasonable level and does not significantly slow down the
system startup and response times.
It was also noted that the Flex Platform team has spent many hours debug-
ging and troubleshooting system errors. Therefore, one of the main objectives
is to design and implement a tracing system that can point the team in the
right direction when errors occur, making it less time-consuming to locate and
resolve them.
To be better equipped in designing the system, the attributes of the Flex
Platform must be considered. As discussed in Chapter 4, the Flex Platform
is an existing system with approximately 40 services written in Python and
NodeJS. Additionally, the system utilizes message queues such as rabbitMQ
and redisMQ. Currently, the only method implemented for debugging and trou-
bleshooting across services is manually written logs by the developers.
The most fundamental data needed to achieve the goals and objectives in-
cludes the requests and responses along with their sender and receiver. Ad-
ditionally, we want to capture the bodies of these requests and responses, as
this would aid in debugging several of the example scenarios.
Furthermore,
timestamps would be valuable for determining when specific events occurred.
Therefore, the most crucial pieces of data to gather are:
• Requests and responses
• Body of requests and responses
• Sender and receiver of requests and responses
• Timestamps of requests and responses

==PAGE:51==
As mentioned in Chapter 3, the required data formats largely depend on
how we intend to present and analyse the data. Since we aim to display full-
traces in a waterfall format through trace visualization, formatting the data as
time-series data may be sufficient.
5.2
Components
This section presents the chosen tools for each component in the conceptual
framework. The conceptual framework outlines the necessary components for
a tracing system and provides guidance on selecting the most suitable tools
for the particular setting. Figure 17 shows an overview of the data flow and
the selected components for the designed system. As expected, there are a few
overlaps in tools. This is because many tools provide several functionalities, and
are often better optimized and integrated within their own ecosystem than with
other tools. We can see that OpenTelemetry handles both request correlation
and part of the data collection, Pixie contributes to data collection as well as
the data processing, and Jaeger is used for processing, storage and presentation.
The following subsections will detail the aspects for which the tools are chosen,
how they are expected to contribute, and why they were selected over other

Figure 17: Visual presentation of what tools and mechanisms have been chosen

Request Correlation
Since The Flex Platform is an existing distributed system and the team wants to
make implementation and maintenance as easy as possible, schema based infer-
ence is unsuitable, as it would require changes in all 40 existing services. While
black-box correlation is theoretically possible, it would be challenging to obtain
all the necessary information such as full-traces. Therefore, the most suitable
43
==PAGE:52==
solution is context propagation. Given that the requested data primarily con-
cerns communication between services, inter-process propagation was chosen.
Since one of the most important requirements for the Flex Platform team is
to obtain a no-code solution, we needed to find a tool providing auto-
instrumentation. Among the existing solutions for context propagation, most of
them either do not support auto-instrumentation, are pay-to-use, or have spe-
cific system requirements. For instance, Datadog, New Relic and AWS X-Ray all
support auto-instrumentation but are pay-to-use. Other tools like ElasticAPM
are free-to-use but require an Elastic Stack deployment to function. There are
also free tools providing auto-instrumentation, such as Hypertrace, Zipkin and
Lightstep, but they all require OpenTelemetry. Since OpenTelemetry is both
free and provides automatic context propagation as a standalone solution, it
was deemed the most suitable option. Additionally, it supports tracing for both
Python and NodeJS services. Therefore, it was decided to use OpenTelemetry’s
auto-instrumentation for request correlation in this tracing system. Its general
popularity and positive reception further solidify this decision.
Data Collection
As mentioned in section 5.1, we determined that full-traces and the bodies of the
requests are necessary. Since we aim to minimize code-instrumentation, while
still capturing the request bodies, our options are limited.
For the data collection, the original plan was to use OpenTelemetry. How-
ever, OpenTelemetry’s auto-instrumentation does not allow for gathering the
body of requests. It turns out that many tools require manual instrumentation
to obtain specific data such as the request bodies. The tools that do allow this
are typically larger, more complex, distributed system solution with an abun-
dance of tracing capabilities. Implementing such solutions seemed unnecessary,
as they would introduce additional performance overhead for the monitored sys-
tem. Additionally, these tools are often pay-to-use.
One of the free tools that allows for gathering body requests with auto-
instrumentation is Pixie. Pixie uses Linux extended Berkeley Packet Filter(eBPF),
which enables it to capture and filter network packets at the operating system
level. eBPF probes are set to trigger on a number of kernel events, and each
time they are triggered, Pixie collects the data through its collector, Vizier. The
data is then sent to a Pixie Edge Module (PEM), which parses and stores the
collected data. The PEMs are located within each node in your cluster stores
the data locally on the node. This allows Pixie to collect data without user in-
strumentation while ensuring that the data is not stored outside of the network.
Pixie provides an API solution that can be accessed through code, as well as a
command line interface used to deploy, run queries and manage resources. Pixie
uses its scripting language called PXL scripts, which determines which data is
exported. For data exportation, Pixie offers a plugin directly integrated with
44
==PAGE:53==
OpenTelemtry, as well as the option for manual collection through PXL scripts.
As for alternatives, there are a few tools that offer this option. However,
these tools are often a part of a larger solution, which can lead to vendor-
lock. This could be advantageous if you find a vendor that meets your specific
needs. However, even if they are ideal for the current goals of the monitored
system, your goals and objectives might change. If your objectives change, the
chosen tool might not provide the necessary functionality, forcing you to use a
workaround, rely on multiple tools, or select a new tool altogether. Therefore,
Pixie has chosen as the tool to collect the bodies of the requests.
Data Processing
The primary data processing is handled by Jaeger. Since we are mainly inter-
ested in full-traces, Jaeger is well-suited for this purpose. Additionally, Jaeger
integrates seamlessly with OpenTelemetry, which we have decided to use for
both context propagation and part of the data transportation.
Being open-
source, Jaeger offers a great deal of flexibility in the case you need additional
functionality. Furthermore, Jaeger has a strong reputation among peers and is
used by many large organizations, proving its maturity and stability are well-
proven in production environments.
Another aspect of data processing through the data collection with Pixie.
Pixie gives users significant control over what data to collect. This means we
must carefully decide which aspects to collect, including which endpoints to
exclude. Otherwise, we may end up with a lot of unnecessary data, such as
health checks and routine communication between architecture components,
that do not accurately reflect the internal states of system communication.
Data Storage
In the implementation, we decided to use Jaegers in-memory storage. While this
storage lacks a persistence layer, it is sufficient for a proof of concept. However,
due to the absence of a persistence layer and the relatively small storage capacity,
an external database would be preferred for a real implementation.In the Flex
Platform, this database could be ElasticSearch or another time-series database
supported by Jaeger.
Data Transportation
Since we decided to use Jaegers in-memory storage, the data transportation
component is straightforward. The in-memory storage allows the Jaeger UI to
directly access the storage for the relevant information.
However, it is important to note that there is an additional step of data
transportation for handling the data collection. We use OpenTelemetry to ex-
port the data from Pixie to Jaeger.
45
==PAGE:54==
Data Presentation and Analysis
Many tools are available for trace visualization and other data visualizations.
However, given the budget constraints and the general software engineering
principle of creating a minimum viable product, it was concluded that Jaeger
is sufficient for presenting the data for the Flex Platform. Jaeger is free to use
and offers a user-friendly interface with excellent graphics for trace visualization.
One alternative that offers similar features to Jaeger is Zipkin. Zipkin has
also been around for a long time and is known for being lightweight and easy
to deploy, which can be advantageous for smaller systems. However, Jaeger is
generally considered better suited for larger systems with high data through-
put. Jaeger is also regarded as having better integration and support for Open-
Telemetry, making it a more future-proof choice.
Another interesting option is New Relic, especially since we are already using
it to host our Pixie deployment. New Relic is an all-in-one solution, offers many
tracing capabilities, including trace visualization and more. It is considered easy
to use, but might feel rigid and limited in terms of configuration compared to
Jaeger, which offers more flexibility and customization options.

Evaluation of Design
The two test scenarios demonstrate that the tracing system functions as origi-
nally designed. The tracing system provides some additional information that
can assist debuggers and troubleshooters in identifying mistakes and discrep-
ancies in their code.
Although finding the errors still requires knowledge of
the services and their implementation, the process is still less demanding than
manually sifting through thousands of lines of logs. The tracing system narrows
down the scope by indicating where the error occurred, making the debugging
process more efficient.
Additionally, there are other solutions providing the same functionality as
the designed tracing system as a package deal. One such tool is Datadog, which
provides trace visualization and support both nodeJS, python and RabbitMQ.
However, one of the major drawbacks for Datadog is that it is a paid option.
The pricing model is usage-based, meaning you only pay for the resources you
use. While this could be advantageous in a small setting where resource us-
age is minimal, the cost could escalate rapidly as functionality scales. Another
downside common to complete solutions is the potential for vendor lock-in. In
the case that the goals and objectives have changed along with the monitored
system, you might require additional tracing functionality not provided by for
instance Datadog. In such cases you would either have to switch vendors, incur-

7.2
The Conceptual Framework
This section aims to provide answers for RQ1, as stated below:
How can we provide structure for designing distributed tracing systems?
To provide structure for designing a distributed tracing system, a concep-
tual framework has been developed. The structure includes an overview of the
components that make up a tracing system and the data flow between them.
Additionally, a workflow model has been proposed to guide the design of a dis-
tributed tracing system.
Through the evaluation of the designed tracing system for the Flex Platform,
we can confirm that the conceptual framework is effective in guiding the process.
The framework is particularly useful for organising thoughts and important in-
formation related to the monitored system, as well as for guiding decisions on
which tools and mechanisms to use for the components.
The conceptual framework is particularly effective for analysing the moni-
tored system and helping you choose tools based on specific requirements. For
instance, if budget constraints are a primary concern for a team, the framework
can be used to identify free tools for each component. Furthermore, since many
tools for distributed tracing cover multiple components, and some may even
cover all components, using this framework can also reveal overlaps in tool cov-
erage across different components. This can help in selecting the most suitable
tool for a specific goal or objective.
The framework is also helpful in highlighting the various aspects that must
be considered when creating a tracing system, especially from scratch. It is ben-
eficial for designers of new tracing systems to use the framework as a guideline
for what aspects their tracing system or tool should incorporate in the design
process.
However, this structure and fragmentation of distributed systems can also
become a limitation. Dividing the system into separate components can result
in a lack of overall perspective, making it challenging to view the solution as a
cohesive whole rather than just individual parts.
It is also worth mentioning that the framework and diagrams are simplified
representations of reality. This means that some aspects may be overly sim-
plified compared to what is actually needed, while others may be given more
focus than necessary. For instance, I decided to include Data Transportation as
a separate component because it is a major part of the system and can easily
be overlooked. However, in reality, the data transportation is almost always
66
==PAGE:75==
implemented as part of the data storage and presentation components and may
not necessarily need be considered a separate component.
Furthermore, since the framework is heavily based on the work of Yuri
Shkuro and Austin Parker, the framework might be biased. Efforts have been
made to consider additional literature and to investigate the available tools on
the market to reduce this bias; however, the framework is still influenced by
their perspectives and opinions on tracing in general.
As a final note, it is generally difficult to evaluate the framework based on
only one example. While the case study of the designed tracing system for the
Flex Platform demonstrates that the provided structure for designing tracing
systems was satisfactory in this instance, it is challenging to generalize this
conclusion based on a single case.

8
Conclusion
The goal of this project is to address organizations’ challenges in designing dis-
tributed tracing systems that align with their goals and objectives. To achieve
this, a conceptual framework has been developed to provide an overview and
serve as a guide for these organizations. This framework consists of a general
architecture that illustrates the data flow in a tracing system composed of six
different components, and a workflow model that can be used as a guide for
designing such a distributed tracing system. Additionally, a distributed tracing
system has been designed as a case study for the Flex Platform system by IBM
CIC Denmark.
The conceptual framework serves as a helpful guide for organizing essential
information needed to determine which tools and mechanisms to use in a tracing
system for specific objectives. The framework was tested and evaluated through
a case study of the Flex platform, where the goal was to assist developers in de-
bugging and troubleshooting their microservices. The designed system proved
effective in isolating errors and identifying the services where they occur. While
it still requires some expert knowledge and time to pinpoint the exact errors,
the system can assist by guiding the debugger or troubleshooter in the right di-
rection. Additionally, it can be concluded that the framework is a solid starting
point for understanding the architecture of a tracing system.
One of the limitations of the framework is that fragmenting the tracing
system into separate components can lead to a disintegrated tracing system if
the designer does not intentionally consider the tracing system as a whole. Ad-
ditionally, the framework also provides limited guidance at the component level.
Future work could explore how the different components can be converged
to guide the designer in maintaining a holistic view of the system. It could also
include details about how decisions at each decision point influence the options
available at other decision points.
While the devised conceptual framework
serves as a guide on a relatively high level, future work might focus on providing
more detailed descriptions of the dependencies between all information points
and decision points.
