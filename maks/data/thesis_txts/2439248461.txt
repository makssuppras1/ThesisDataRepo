Abstract
Computational multibody dynamics is a tool, which can be used to simulate the behaviour
of any given mechanism before even having it by hand. By making use of this method, it
is possible for constructors to examine whether a mechanism has the desired motion, ve-
locity, acceleration, and if the forces acting on each body within the system have suitable
magnitudes. Multibody simulation is a convenient method of analysis when the ﬂexibility
of a system lies within the joints. Its disadvantage is that it revolves around the study
of rigid bodies, which makes it less useful when studying the material behaviour of single
components. Even though the commonly used ﬁnite element method would be a great
asset to solve problems concerning material behaviour, this thesis has dealt with the de-
velopment of a dynamic analysis program, which can analyse the ﬂexibility of a system
using a theory called the ﬁnite segment method.
The objective of this thesis was to construct a multibody dynamic analysis program,
which could be able to simulate the motion of a 7-cylinder 2-stroke marine engine from
MAN B&W. The problem MAN B&W had, was that the engine showed irregularities in
the fourth cylinder, in the form of transverse oscillations in the piston.
The ﬁnite segment method examines the ﬂexibility of long slender beams. The theory
handles the ﬂexibility of a slender beam by dividing the body into segments and letting
the material properties act as springs and dampers between each segment to preserve
the connection. This theory was used to study the motion of the crankshaft while the
remaining bodies were kept as rigid bodies.
By combining conventional multibody dynamics with the ﬁnite segment method, it was
possible to simulate the motion of the marine engine. The results from the ﬁnal simu-
lations showed largest displacement at the fourth piston. Unfortunately, no conclusion
could be drawn to why the irregularities occur within the marine engine, this due to the
development progress of the dynamic analysis program being time-consuming. Unknown
parameters, such as the spring stiﬀnesses and damping coeﬃcients for the piston, were
calculated from estimated measurements from technical drawings. The validity of the
accuracy of the obtained results is therefore assumed to be minimal, but the obtained re-
sults show the same tendencies as described by MAN B&W; namely largest displacement
in the fourth cylinder.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Computational multibody dynamics is a tool to investigate the motion of any given sys-
tem without having it by hand. It makes it possible to see whether dynamic components
are interconnected correctly for, i.e. systems that need to ﬁt into certain circumstances.
This thesis focuses on developing a program, which uses the capabilities of multibody
dynamics to examine an engine of the model G80ME-C9 from MAN B&W, which is a
7-cylinder 2-stroke crosshead engine. The problem is that the engine shows unwanted
irregularities in the fourth piston, in the form of oscillations in the transverse directions.
Even though it is well known that a common approach of handling such problems is by
making use of the ﬁnite element method, it is within interest to examine the capabilities
of computational multibody dynamics within this research. Despite that multibody dy-
namics mainly addresses rigid multibody systems, there exist methods which can be used
to cover ﬂexible dynamic analyses as well. The method employed in this thesis is called
the ﬁnite segment method.
1.2
Objectives
• The main objective of this thesis is to develop a dynamic analysis program, based
on computational multibody dynamics. The program should be able to simulate the
motion of the load carrying part of a 7-cylinder 2-stroke marine engine from MAN
B&W.
• A stepwise progression of the complexity of the multibody system should show an
understanding of the multibody dynamic analysis of the marine engine. The validity
of the theoretical methods is to investigated from achieved results at each complexity
stage.
1.3
Thesis Structure
The rest of the work within this thesis is split up into seven parts, divided into twelve
sections.
Section 2 describes the fundamentals of multibody dynamics and how it can be used to
simulate the motion of the engine. The physical system is then presented together with
the acquired data which will be used to develop the dynamic analysis program. Further-
more, preliminary calculations are performed to obtain the last parameters needed for
deﬁning the multibody system.
Section 3 outlines basic vector and matrix notations together with key formulas used in
the multibody calculations.
1
==PAGE:12==
Section 4 presents the kinematics of a multibody system and how it can be used as a
preliminary tool to ﬁnd positions and velocities for each body in a multibody system.
Derivations of vectors and matrices used for kinematic- and dynamic simulations are also
presented.
Section 5 outlines the dynamic analysis and how it can be used to study the resulting
motion of multibody systems due to applied forces. A Lagrangian formulation based on
the Newton-Euler equations of motion is analysed. The section ends with a presentation
of how Matlab’s built-in time integrator can be used to simulate the behaviour of a multi-
body system over a user-deﬁned time span.
Section 6 presents the ﬁnite segment method, which is used to implement additional
ﬂexibility to a multibody system.
Section 7 describes in detail how the dynamic analysis program is organized with re-
spect to structuring, data implementing, calculations and postprocessing. The program
is described in three subsections for the sake of simplicity.
Section 8 to 10 present a stepwise approach of obtaining a fully ﬂexible multibody sys-
tem. Comparisons between numerical results form the basis for proceeding to the next
step of the analysis. Section 8 represents a rigid body analysis of 1- and 7-cylinders. Sec-
tion 9 presents an analysis of a semi-ﬂexible system of 1- and 7-cylinders. A fully ﬂexible
multibody system is achieved in chapter 10, where theoretical implementations serve as
a comparison to the unwanted irregularities in the fourth piston.
Section 11 discusses the results obtained from the dynamic analyses together with rec-
ommendations of how to bring the method of analysis closer to an actual movement of
the crosshead engine.
Section 12 summarises the outcome of this thesis. How things have worked out and
whether the method of analysis has fulﬁlled the expectations of the ﬁnal result.

2
Multibody Systems
Computer Aided Analysis provides the ability to simulate the movement and dynamic
behaviour of a multibody system before even having the physical system by hand. One
method of analysis is called the analysis of multibody systems. A multibody system is
deﬁned as a system of bodies that are interconnected by joints, which makes the bodies
able to move with respect to one another. One example of such a system is the well-known
slider crank mechanism that is used in combustion engines and is the primary focus of
this project.
Mechanics, in general, consists of statics and dynamics. The objective of this thesis is to
develop a simulation program concerning the motion of a 7-cylinder 2-stroke marine engine
which means that the dynamics of the machinery is the central focus area. Dynamics can
again be divided into two parts, namely kinematics and kinetics. Kinematics is the study
of how systems/mechanisms move with respect to time, without taking the external forces
into account. Conversely, kinetics studies the motions that emerge from the forces acting
on the system.
2.1
The Physical System
As mentioned, this thesis deals with establishing a simulation program of a 7-Cylinder
2-stroke cross-head engine which is designed by MAN B&W. In order to ease the simula-
tion progress, the model of the engine needs to be simpliﬁed. This is done by picking out
the components, which have the highest inﬂuence on the ﬁnal results, for further analy-
sis. The multibody analysis will, therefore, include the load carrying part of the engine.
That is the crankshaft, conrods, cross-heads, piston rods, pistons, damper and turning
wheel. The inﬂuences of the main frame in the form of stiﬀness and main bearings are
also included in the calculations.
An overview of the setup is shown in the ﬁgures below. Figure 1a illustrates the com-
ponents included in one of the cylinders within the engine, while ﬁgure 1b shows the
mechanism from a simpliﬁed CAD model.
The crankshaft is supported by eight main bearings that are mounted onto the bed-
plate. The bedplate is set to be the bottom frame of the mechanism. The lower ends
of the conrods are attached to each crankpin while the other ends are fastened to the
cross-heads, which take up most of the transverse forces acting from the rotary motion
of the crankshaft. The cross-heads are mounted to the cross-head guides, which also are
set to serve as a stationary frame. Piston rods are then mounted between the pistons
and cross-heads to transfer the motion from the combustion forces acting on the pistons,
downwards through the cross-heads and onto the crankshaft. The pistons are located
within the stationary cylinders, which serve as the top frame of the mechanism.

PV-Diagram
Figure 3 below shows the Pressure-Volume-diagram (PV) for the ﬁrst cylinder during one
revolution of the crankshaft. The initial position for the piston is located at the Top
Dead Center (TDC) which means that the volume at this point within the cylinder is
zero and the cylinder pressure has almost reached its maximum. Because of the high
pressure within the cylinder, the fuel ignites which causes the pressure to rise even more.
The energy obtained from combustion is converted to a rapid downwards motion for the
piston. While the piston travels towards the Bottom Dead Center (BDC), the volume
increases and the pressure decreases. After the BDC is reached, the combustion energy
makes it possible for the engine to maintain a reciprocal motion, and the compression part
of the PV-Diagram has started. During this part, the volume decreases and the pressure
increases as the piston approaches the TDC again, whereafter the process repeats itself.

4
Kinematic Analysis
Kinematic analysis is the study of position, velocity and acceleration for any given mech-
anism, without any forces impacting the system. Kinematic analysis is only dependent
on the motion of rigid bodies. For evaluating a mechanism using kinematic analysis, the
positions, velocities and accelerations have to be calculated. The positions can be found
by use of the kinematic constraints equations. The ﬁrst- and second time derivatives of
constraints equations yield to the velocities and accelerations for the mechanism. When
considering the velocities and acceleration of the mechanism, it is necessary to include a
driving constraint. A driving constraint is a constraint that drives the mechanism and ap-
plies velocity to the system - see Section 4.1 where the angular velocity of the crankshaft
is the driving constraint.
It is possible to make use of numerical solvers in order to estimate positions, velocities and
accelerations of a multibody system. The iterative Newton-Raphson method eﬃciently
solves such problems; it makes use of the zeroth method in terms of iterations if initial
guesses lie close to the exact solution. If initial guesses diﬀer too much from the correct
results, the Newton-Raphson method might diverge instead. Even though this method is
a usual manner of ﬁnding initial positions and velocities within multibody simulations, it
can be neglected within this thesis; this due to all lengths and angles being known, which
results in correct initial guesses from the ﬁrst iteration.
Furthermore, the Jacobian matrix [Φq] of constraints and the vector of RHS of the accel-
eration equations, {γ}, are described, as they are dependent on the kinematic constraints.
The Jacobian consists of partial derivatives of the constraint equations while {γ} is the
velocity part of the RHS of the second time derivative of the constraint equations.

5
Dynamic Analysis
Dynamic analysis of a multibody system is a study of motion due to the applied forces
on the system in contradiction to the kinematic analysis, which does not take forces into
account. When solving the equation of motion for kinematic analysis, the number of
DOFs for the system has to be equal to the number of driving constraints. Considering
the crankshaft mechanism in section 4.1, the kinematic constraint equations yield to a
total of (6 · 6 −1 = 35), where one DOF is left open to allow translational movement
for the piston in the y-direction. By adding a driving constraint, which is the angular
velocity of the crankshaft, there will be 36 equations and 36 unknowns. This applies for
kinematic analysis.
Dynamic analysis, in general, does not have to satisfy this statement, because there are
usually no driven constraints. Thus, there are not enough constraint equations compared
to the unknowns, and the equations of motion cannot be solved for in the same manner
as for the kinematic analysis. To acquire a solution in a dynamic analysis, the constraint
equations together with the diﬀerential equations of motion have to be solved for each
step in time.
To solve these equations, the initial conditions of the system must be
determined in advance and are solved by using a numerical method such as Matlab’s
built-in Ordinary Diﬀerential Equation solver (ODE45). ODE45 uses the fourth- and
ﬁfth-order Runge Kutta method (see Section 5.4).

Finite Segment
In order to ﬁnd out how the multibody system acts due to ﬂexibility, the ﬁnite segment
method can be used. The ﬁnite segment method is similar to the ﬁnite element method
for structural analysis, with its focus on multibody systems. The method splits the bod-
ies apart into segments so that the segments appear as smaller bodies with their own
reference frame. The ﬂexibility is then obtained in a framework of linear deformations by
adding springs and dampers between the segments.
Generally, it is possible to extract the same amount of reaction forces for a body as the
number of DOFs for the same body. In 3D-analysis this will result in six reaction forces
for each body. As the crankshaft assembly is one solid body, it is only possible to extract
six reaction forces from it, even though it is well known that eight main bearings support
the crankshaft. By making use of the ﬁnite segment method, the crankshaft can be split
up into multiple segments, which allows the reaction forces at the main bearing positions
to be calculated [4]. The disadvantage of the increase in DOFs is the extended amount of
governing equations that need to be solved for, which results in an increase in computa-
tional eﬀort.

Results - Finite segment
There are many methods available to describe deformations within a ﬂexible multibody
mechanism. The main focus, up until now, has been to apply deformation in the form of
springs and dampers to the bodies within the system. Translational springs and dampers
could have been implemented to the crankshaft from the previous section as well in order
to act as ﬂexibility from the bearings, but when looking at a multibody system, it will
only be possible to get one of the eight main bearings implemented. It is within interest to
achieve a fully ﬂexible system containing all components of the load carrying capacity from
ﬁgure 40 on page 57; in order to attain this, it is necessary to research other methods.
One approach of analysing ﬂexible multibody systems is discussed in [6], namely the
ﬁnite segment method. The method is explained in Section 6 and makes it possible to
apply ﬂexibility to the crankshaft by dividing it into rigid segments and adding ﬂexibility
between each segment. The geometry of the crankshaft was analysed where separations
were made at the centre of each crankpin. The separated bodies were then connected by
three types of springs and dampers, namely torsional, translational and bending springs
and dampers, which ensure the connection to maintain the same motion, with the ability
of deformation caused by material behaviour.
10.1
Spring Stiﬀnesses and Damping Coeﬃcients
Each spring stiﬀness used within the ﬁnite segment calculations is calculated according to
equations, 6.2-6.6. Areas, lengths and moments of inertia are calculated by using known
and estimated data from technical drawings of the crankshaft. The technical drawings
provided, only included one view of the crankshaft assembly. From here the cross-sectional
moment of inertia was estimated for seven angles of the crank segments about the axial
direction. The obtained values were then interpolated to give the cross-sectional moment
of inertia in x-and y-direction at any given crankshaft angle. Because of the interpolation
only being estimated from seven values, the spring stiﬀness during the simulation varied
a lot, which resulted in incorrect tendencies after simulation. The maximum calculated
bending spring stiﬀness for one revolution was therefore used as a constant spring stiﬀness,
which showed acceptable results. The translational spring elements between the segments
can be calculated by using the cross-sectional area of the crankpins.
The damping coeﬃcient for each translational damper is either calculated from equation
9.1 with a damping factor of ζ = 0.85 or obtained from the acquired model for torsional

Discussion
As mentioned throughout this thesis, computational multibody dynamics can serve as a
tool to analyse the behaviour of rigid systems. It is possible to implement springs and
dampers, but this only assures for the ﬂexibility being applied to each rigid body. In
order to study displacements of rigid bodies, it is necessary to look for other methods.
During the project, there has been taken advantage of the ﬁnite segment method. Despite
literature search revolving this method only showed usage of the ﬁnite segment method
being applied for long slender beams, the method was used to divide the non-slender
crankshaft into segments within this thesis. The separation of the crankshaft into seg-
ments was done in order for the main bearings to be implemented as support elements
between the crankshaft and bedplate. It is hard to say whether this method gives correct
results, since there are endless of possibilities of how to separate the crankshaft into seg-
ments, but the obtained results show valid tendencies in the form of highest displacements
at the pistons located around the middle. Even though some parameters were unknown,
the results showed the same irregularities within the fourth cylinder in the z-direction, as
MAN B&W presented it.
The unknown bending stiﬀness of the crankshaft was calculated as shown in Section 6.
During the simulations, it was assumed to be constant for the rotating crankshaft. A
better solution would be to investigate the cross-sectional moment of inertia from the
CAD-model for a full rotation around the z-axis in order to achieve better estimates of
the bending stiﬀnesses.
A model, where simpliﬁed constraints in the x- and z-directions were applied on the pis-
ton, resulted in the model collapsing; this because of the crosshead not being able to
comprehend for the sideways forces exerted by the crankshaft.
Due to lack of time, the increasing simulation time, and the development of the DAP being
time-consuming, it was not possible to ﬁnd a solution to why the irregularities occur at
the fourth piston. As the complexity of the DAP increased, in the form of implementation
of springs, dampers, and ﬁnite segment method, the computational-time (CPU) increased
extensively with it.
When dealing with a stepwise approach of program development, it is essential to outline
a desired structure for the program. The development of the DAP started with using the
same procedures learned from a course in Computational Multibody Dynamics, which re-
volved around dynamic analyses of simple systems. It was quickly realized, that it would
be diﬃcult to determine the matrices and vectors of larger mechanisms manually. A new
outline was necessary to ensure the program to remain structured during the progression.
By structuring the program as shown in the ﬂowchart from ﬁgure 14, it was possible to
58
==PAGE:69==
only assign variables within four structures in order to enter the simulation process.
As described in [7], it is necessary to determine initial guesses for the positions and ve-
locities for dynamic analysis by use of Newton-Raphson. Because of the local lengths
and angles being known initially, the method in section 4.1 can calculate the correct ini-
tial guesses for the ODE-solver immediately, which makes the iterative Newton-Raphson
method negligible for this system.
Future Work
In this thesis, it was mentioned that the CPU time became quite large when the ﬁnite
segment method was implemented. One way of decreasing the CPU time could be by
rewriting the Matlab code into FORTRAN; this due to FORTRAN’s capabilities of op-
erating eﬃciently with large matrices and vectors.
As multibody systems get large, implementation of joints may rapidly restrict the same
DOFs, and the implementations can be complicated. To simplify the implementation
of the constraints, a function eliminating the redundant constraints as discussed in [7],
should be implemented.
The iterative ODE-solver evaluates position and velocity by integrating velocity and ac-
celeration with respect to time. The results obtained are unstable due to numerical errors
from the iterative solutions. To overcome these errors, a constraint violation stabilization
method can be implemented as described in [7]. The method acts as a feedback controller,
which minimises the numerical error.
The DAP developed in this project relates to the speciﬁc problem revolving the marine
engine. Some modiﬁcations are necessary for the program to become a general DAP.
The program can be developed even further to eventually be able to include deﬂections
and vibrations within the analyses; to ensure this, it is necessary to implement the ﬁnite
element method and modal analysis.

Conclusion
The objectives of this thesis were to develop a dynamic analysis program, based on com-
putational multibody dynamics. The program should be able to simulate the motion of
the load carrying part of a 7-cylinder 2-stroke marine engine from MAN B&W. The prob-
lem was that irregularities occurred within the fourth cylinder, in the form of oscillations.
The engine consists of one crankshaft, which is supported by eight main bearings that are
connected to the bedplate. A TVD is mounted to one end of the crankshaft and a TW
on the other end. Each cylinder consists of a conrod, crosshead, piston rod, and a piston.
The motion of the engine can be imitated by making use of multibody analysis, which is
a tool that examines how interconnected bodies move with respect to one another. Even
though multibody analysis conforms to rigid body analysis only, it is possible to com-
bine this method with the ﬁnite segment method to imitate ﬂexibility within a multibody
system. The ﬁnite segment theory is based on dividing rigid bodies into segments and im-
plementing springs and dampers between the separated segments in order to preserve the
connection; the corresponding spring forces were calculated based on material properties.
By increasing the number of segments, the number of springs and dampers obtained also
increased, which resulted in higher CPU-time. Furthermore, the implementation of the
ﬁnite segment method allowed for the implementation of the eight main bearings.
When dealing with the development of a DAP in a stepwise manner, it is essential to
deﬁne a suitable structure for the program. The program had to be able to adapt to the
implementations occurring at each stage. The stepwise approach made it easier to outline
the simulation progress in order to obtain the motion that imitated the irregularities oc-
curring within the marine engine. Unfortunately, no conclusion could be drawn to why the
irregularities occur within the marine engine due to the development progress of the DAP
being time-consuming. Unknown parameters, such as the spring stiﬀnesses and damping
coeﬃcients for the piston, are calculated from estimated measurements from technical
drawings. The validity of the accuracy of the obtained results is therefore assumed to be
minimal, but the obtained results show the same tendencies as described by MAN B&W;
namely largest displacement in the fourth cylinder.
