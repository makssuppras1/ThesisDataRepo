Abstract 
The main subject of this Master thesis is to establish a 3D Finite Element Model of an anisotropic 
poroelastic shale borehole trajectory. Strength parameters are applied to the model and then 
subjected to a range of different drilling fluid pressures. The drilling fluid pressures correspond to 
Underbalanced Drilling (OBD), Managed Pressure Drilling (MPD) and Overbalanced Drilling (OBD). 
Borehole wall displacements and effective stresses with respect to their orientation of the far-
field stresses will be analyzed in conjugation the Mohr-Coulomb criteria and the plane of 
weakness model.  
The 3D model showed good correlation with real borehole wall displacements and stresses. The 
non-symmetric displacements and scattered principal stress concentrations around the borehole 
walls showed the necessity of creating a 3D model.  
Despite applying true Baxter shale parameters and stress configurations of true borehole 
instabilities only borehole wall displacements of maximum 5mm occurred in the horizontal part of 
the borehole trajectory.

1. Introduction 
It is estimated that more than     of worldwide well drilling is through shale formations. 
Borehole instability in shale is therefore more pronounced than in any other formations and may 
account for      of the borehole problems. Drilling expenses associated to this is estimated to 
one half billion U.S dollars per year. Borehole instability largely affects the wells integrity and can 
cause problems such as hole-narrowing and stuck pipe, hole-closure, hole-collapse, hole-
enlargement, washouts or fracturing. 
The nature of shale’s formation as well as necessary rock mechanical theory and borehole 
instability problems are outlined. Different drilling techniques, drilling fluids and types of borehole 
instabilities are introduced as being fundamental of understanding the risks associated with 
drilling boreholes in shale. Previous published papers of real shale borehole problems, laboratory 
testing of different shale and parameters obtained from testing are studied. The studied critical 
elastic and strength parameters are applied to a 3D Finite Element Model of a shale borehole 
trajectory and the instability risks are discussed.  
1.1 Scope of Study 
With the issues introduced the objectives with this study is to  
 
Understand the nature and behaviour of shale. 
 
Establish a 3D FE-model which can be used for this study of stresses and displacements, 
and one which may be extended with additional chemical and thermal properties for 
studying their effects on instabilities. 
 
Test and compare instability risks of 3 FE-models each with different degree of 
anisotropy.  
The study was divided into three parts.  
The first part covers how a shale formation is formed as well as necessary rock and borehole 
theory to understand the method of analyzing borehole instability. The section also introduces 
three drilling techniques and some of their associated drilling fluids which are closely connected 
to the borehole pressure.  
The second presents the creation of the anisotropic poroelastic 3D FE-model made in COMSOL 
Multiphysics version 4.3a. The modelling initiated in Excel plotting the Kirsch equations of 2D 
borehole stresses and displacements for an isotropic material with using an arbitrary far field 
stress regime. The plots made base and extent of a 2D isotropic model which were created as a 
“Plate with a Hole” in the Solid Mechanics module of COMSOL. The same far-field stress regime as 
for the Kirsch equations were applied to the 2D COMSOL model, and its design was refined until 
the simulated stresses converged towards the analytical of Kirsch. The 2D COMSOL modelling 
strategy was then utilized to create an anisotropic poroelastic 3D borehole trajectory with 4 
positions of “Plates with a hole”. The model was refined with respect to its boundary conditions 
until realistic stresses and displacements were obtained.  
==PAGE:16==
 
2 
 
The third part of the study evaluates the 3D simulation results of stresses and displacements. The 
results are analyzed using the Mohr-Coulomb failure criteria with cohesions and friction angles 
taken from published results of laboratory test of how bedding affects shale’s strength 
parameters.

2. Fundamentals of Shale Behaviour 
The origin of a formation rock is evident in understanding the response of the rock drilled through 
and hence the risks of mechanical and chemical borehole instability problems. This section 
presents the composition and mechanical properties associated with shale’s formation and 
geology. The formation and strength of shale varies with environment and is therefore important 
knowledge of understanding the rock’s stability and the parameters used for analyzing borehole 
instability.  
2.1 Geological Features of Shale 
The geology of shale comprises its origin from sediment to sedimentary rock, its structure and 
mineral composition. Shale’s tectonic history is also very important as it may contribute to 
identify possible areas of faults however it is beyond this scope of this study.  
Shale comes roughly in weak and strong types. The weak ones are the clay-rich “gumbo” (as 
baptised in the U.S. Gulf Coast) and smectite, and the strong ones are highly cemented such as 
siltstone. The common characteristic of shale is its very low permeability of up to a few nanoDarcy 
even unaffected by the highest porosity ranging 2-70%. Low permeability make shale an efficient 
hydrocarbon seal rock (cap rock) as illustrated in Figure 1 (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 
2008), (Fabricius, 2003), (wikipedia).  
 
Figure 1. Anticline cap rock and aquifer sections surrounding oil and gas layers in a reservoir (Schlumberger). 
2.1.1 Formation of Shale 
Shale is a fine grained clastic sedimentary rock formed by erosion of older rocks which are 
weathered, transported by waters or ice to place of deposition, aged and buried. After deposition 
a process called diagenesis takes place which is the transformation of sediments into a 
sedimentary rock. During diagenesis a solidification process called lithification accounts for 
hardening the rock. Here loose grains of <0.002mm clay sediments forms into cemented grains. 
During lithification the rock fluids and gasses are forced out of the pore space also causing a 
decrease of the rock’s porosity. Continuous deposition, increasing temperature and minerals 
precipitating from the solution of pore fluids accounts for the cementation of grains. When a thick 
mass of sediments are buried (1-3km) in an erosion free environment they together form a

of shale’s lifecycle where weathering grinds off sediments that are transported downhill by water 
or ice and ends on a seabed in lakes or coastal areas.

2.1.2 Composition and Texture of Shale 
Shale is composed of clay minerals that are composed of layers of sheet shaped crystals. Clay 
minerals occupy 50-70% of the rock and the remaining minerals are mostly quartz and also 
calcite. The clay mineral content is high enough that seen from a rock mechanical point of view, 
the matrix of the clay minerals constitutes the load bearing framework. In practical terms the rock 
is defined as shale when its clay content is more than 40%. Due to the abundant clay minerals the 
pore size in shale may range only from 5-25 nm. Clay is in mineralogical terms a hydrous 
aluminium silicate that is composed of natural radioactive clay-minerals such as kaolinite, 
smectite or illite. The concentrations of each type of radioactive mineral present in the clay, as 
well as the structurally bound water in the clay mineral and the water on the mineral surfaces, 
complicates accurate measurements of the bulk and shear modules of the solid grain in shale  
Shale may break along its planes of weakness in thin laminas or beddings planes with thicknesses 
of less than one centimetre. This is called the degree of fissility. The alignment of grains controls 
the patterns of breakage. The irregularly sorted grains are cemented in the sense of microcracks 
and grain orientation, shape and distribution. The grains tend to be elongated or with a plane 
structure formed by the velocity of the currents in streams and rivers where it is deposited. The 
grain packing which depends on grain size, grain shape and grain sorting affects the porosity and 
permeability of shale. Round grains tend to have a smaller friction angle than the more angular 
grains at the same porosity. (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008), (Rider & Kennedy, 2011), 
(wikipedia). An illustration of three shale cores which were extracted for a laboratory tests is seen

2.1.3 Deposition of Shale 
Pre-deposition and deposition 
As introduced, shale is a sediment rock type that originates from a pre-existing rock which, after 
broken into fragments by weathering and erosion, is transported by fluids and then deposited in 
its next location.  A variety in depositional environments causes the randomly ordering and size of 
grains and they greatly affect shale’s elastic and mechanical properties (bedding planes). The 
transportation, depositional and sediment environments where shale’s particles take form and 
are distributed may be through air (aeolian), water (alluvial/fluvial) or ice (glacial). Aeolian 
transported particles tend to be more fine-grained than those transported by water. This is due to 
the small buoyancy and air viscosity.  Particles transported in high energy and low energy water 
can be subjected to different loads from its surrounding environment and from its path along the 
sea bed. The transported sediments that are deposited in high energy environments are termed 
alluvial for poorly sorted particles, and those deposited in low energy environments are termed 
fluvial for well sorted particles. The amount of particles suspended in water determines the type 
of fluid flow where it is transported. A constant or low amount of particles yield a low fluid 
density and obeys the Newtonian fluid behaviour. An increase in particles may increase the fluid 
density and viscosity locally yielding a heavier fluid flowing in between its surrounding lower 
density fluid (turbidity current). This means that a part of shale’s design and thereby mechanical 
properties is highly dependent on the environments through which its particles propagate. Other 
influential factors to shale’s properties are its post deposition (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & 
Risnes, 2008) 
Post deposition 
The post deposition process is the long lithification process of the diagenesis which transforms 
sediments into a sedimentary rock. The younger deposited sediments act as an increasing 
overburden weight that compact the older deposited sediments. With increasing overburden the 
sediment will act as having increased cohesion (increased shear resistance). This is because the 
increasing overburden results in increasing horizontal stresses from the surrounding formation 
that confines the rock (far-field stresses).  (This phenomenon is simulated in laboratories where a 
core sample confined horizontally exhibits a larger resistance against shear failure). Uncompacted 
and soft clay, as a soil, will normally have a low friction angle due to its plate-like structure. As 
==PAGE:20==
 
6 
 
compaction and consolidation (time dependent deformation due to pore pressure dissipation) 
takes place pore fluid is squeezed out, porosity decreases and shale will develop increasing 
friction angle. Compaction also causes a closer grain packing which narrows in the pore channels 
and void space and hence decreasing the rock’s permeability. As mentioned in the section of 
formation of shale, mineral precipitation accounts for the cementation of grains. Reactions 
between the clay minerals and the pore water are the main basis of clay mineral’s diagenetic 
processes. When unstable minerals react with the pore water, new and stabile minerals are 
deposited. This process is only possible when pore water is free to move in a continuous flow in 
the sediment, or else it will stop and hence the stop the decrease of the rock’s porosity and 
permeability (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008).  
As indicated the depositional nature and minerals of shale may control how shale will interact 
with elements in borehole drilling fluids.  
2.1.4 Density-age of Shale 
During Shale’s compaction with depth of burial a series of textural and compositional changes 
occur. The larger bulk density changes are due to compaction whereas minor changes are due to 
compositional changes. An increase in calcium carbonate content occupies the porosity more and 
causes an increase the shale’s bulk density (overall density is grain+fluids). Opposite occurs if 
organic matter fills up the porosity and the shale’s bulk density decreases. The larger bulk density 
changes can occur over a significant thickness of sediment. Therefore, shale’s bulk density can be 
used for estimation of its age but it is no method of generalization.  The theory relies on the 
irreversible diagenesis, which is the irreversible process of the previous described chemical and 
physical change in deposited sediments during its conversion to a rock.  Shale formed in the 
earlier Cretaceous compared to shale formed in the later Cretaceous are buried or ‘fossilised’ and 
are therefore denser. Two figures are presented in Figure 4; the left showing a trend curve of 
Tertiary shale’s increasing bulk density to its increasing age. The unconformity marks an erosional 
removal of the top of the Cretaceous shale formation. The right shows scattering of shale’s 
increasing bulk density to its increasing age. Also presented in the right scattering plot is a result 
of a study from researchers of shale compaction on the Norwegian Continental Shelf (NCS). They 
found that physical squeezing due to overburden is dominant in the near-subsurface at low 
temperatures (Mechanical Compaction, MC). Below 2km, at temperatures around 60-80 degree 
Celsius, smectite transforms into illite and silica is released. Here formation of micro-quartz grains 
takes place and chemical precipitation stiffens the clay fabric (Chemical compaction, CC) (Rider & 
Kennedy, 2011)

2.1.6 Composition and Properties of Clay Minerals in Shaly Rocks  
The previous sections and studies of shale’s composition and compaction require an introduction 
of the composition and properties of clay minerals occupying shale.  
Ion and cation exchanges highly affect the mineral’s shrinkage- swelling capacity and hence 
shale’s communication with a drilling fluid.  As described in section 2.1.2 shale’s clay minerals are 
composed of layers of sheets. There are two types of sheets; one is a silica tetrahedal sheet and 
the other an octahedral sheet where silica is replaced by cations.   
==PAGE:25==
 
11 
 
The very soft clay mineral Montmorillonite (discovered in 1847 in the West of France) is a 
member of the expandable smectite group. It is layered 2:1 meaning two sheets of tetrahedral 
sandwiching one octahedral sheet. It swells greatly when absorbing water. The amount of 
expansion greatly depends on the type of exchangeable cation such as sodium. As per sheet, 
Oxygen and hydroxide ions make negative excess charged surfaces which attract cations. It may 
be used as a component in drilling mud making the slurry viscous helping cooling the drill bit and 
remove cuttings. Mineral density ranges from 2.0-2.7 g/cm3 (webmineral), (wikipedia). 
The Illite mineral (derived from its location in Illinois, USA, 1937) is 2:1 layered and formed by 
weathering of feldspars, degradation of muscovite, and from the transformation from smectite. 
Illite has a layered alumina-silicate structure where its interlayer space is occupied by poorly 
hydrated potassium cations. The negatively charged aluminum balances the radioactive 
potassium ions and yields a stronger bonding than in smectite which prevents swelling (Fjær, Holt, 
Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008). 
Smectite is radioactive with a 2:1 layered mineral with one layer of gibbsite in between two layers 
of two weakly bonded silica sheets. Together they measure approximately 1nm. The poor van der 
Waals’ forces bonding the layers permits access of water and exchangeable ions (calcium and 
sodium). This leads to a high shrink-swell capacity of the minerals of up to 10 times its weight at 
surface conditions (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008). 
Kaolinite is radioactive with a 1:1 layered silicate mineral with alternating layers of gibbsite and 
silicon tetrahedron sheets. It is also known as two-layered or as china-clay (Kao-Ling village in 
Jiangxi province, China, 1727). 1 unit measures 0.7nm in thickness. Due to strong hydrogen bonds 
water cannot access between layers providing Kaolinite a low shrink-swell capacity and a low 
cation-exchange capacity (due to shale’s narrow pore space the specific surface areas are very 
large and negatively charged which attract cations from the pore water. The hydrated ions are 
often attached to the mineral surface (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008). 
From this introduction it is evident that ion exchanges and bonding between sheets accounts clay 
mineral’s shrinkage-swelling capacity. However, if a sheet/bed is not permeable water is

2.1.7 Anisotropy of Shale  
Anisotropic rock modelling requires much more information about the rock to be drilled through. 
Data may be obtained from laboratory testing of retrieved samples or from previous testing of 
similar shales. But due difficulties in core retrieval and that shale has a very low permeability 
which makes them very time consuming to test and often sufficient parameters are not available. 
It is therefore necessary to make assumptions along the modelling phase.  
As mentioned in section 2.1.2 shale’s beddings planes may be formed with alternating directions 
dependent on the direction of the current of the water under which it was deposited. It is also 
possible for some grains to re-orientate under an applied load. These heterogeneities provide 
shale with an anisotropic nature which means that one or more layers may have different elastic 
modules, and their responses to loading and failure are dependent on the direction of a principal 
stress. (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008) classifies shale’s anisotropy according to how 
it was deposited. Shales with plane grains formed by streaming water are called lithological 
anisotropic shales. Shales where microcracks are abundant and formed by deviatoric external 
stresses are called stress induced anisotropic shales. The cracks are normally orientated 
perpendicular to the lowest principal stress. Orthorhombic anisotropy is a material that possesses 
orthotropic properties, i.e. it may be assumed that it has three orthogonal planes of symmetry. 
This means that three orthogonal planes of symmetry must exist at each point in a material and 
the planes must have the same directions.  
A special type of symmetry in a material is one where it may be assumed that the elastic 
properties are equal for all directions within a bedding plane and different properties along the 
other bedding planes. This means that each layer is locally isotropic, the layers are anisotropic 
when compared to each other and the layers altogether makes the material globally anisotropic. 
Such material is called transversely isotropic and is widely used for modeling shale (Khan, Ansari, 
Han, & Khosravi, 2011), (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008). Transversely anisotropic 
shale is illustrated in Figure 8 below with its rotational Z-axis of symmetry.

determined mud pressure is however also an indication of the pressure at which the borehole 
wall may fracture. To determine the fracture stress and extended leak off test (XLOT) is initiated. 
This test rely upon shut-in/decline phases which are used to determine the margins of fracture 
ignition and fracture closure which is where the minimum stress is found. Other methods of 
determining the minimum horizontal stress may be mentioned without further introduction rely 
on the overburden stress and tectonic stresses as well as correlations that include Poisson’s ratio 
and the normal pore pressure.  
The maximum horizontal stress is also difficult to assess. No direct measurements or empirical 
equations exist. In the North Sea it has been possible to estimate the maximum horizontal stress

3.9 Mud Cakes and Permeability of Borehole Walls 
Mud cakes are lumps of formation that sticks to the borehole walls and seals off communication 
between the drilling fluid and formation. They are formed by the mixture of drilled out formation 
cuttings in which minerals interacts with the chemicals in the drilling fluid and also the formation 
fluid. The term ‘perfect mud cake’ means that the mud cake has sealed of the surface of the 
formation borehole walls leaving the borehole walls impermeable. Consequently, the formation 
pore pressure is not influenced by the drilling fluid pressure/well pressure. According to (Fjær, 
Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008) due to shale’s very low permeability –however, still finite 
permeability, impermeable borehole wall conditions are valid only during drilling. After drilling 
the shale’s pore fluid pressure in the near borehole wall region will gradually approach the drilling 
fluid pressure/well pressure. In general mud cakes form only on shale borehole walls when drilling 
with a non-wetting fluid or fluids with e.g. polymers which blocks the shale surface.

4. Drilling Fluids and Drilling Techniques  
Drilling a borehole through a formation initiates stresses changes in the rock surrounding the 
borehole. The in-situ stress state of the rock surrounding the borehole originates also from the 
drilling fluid. This section will be described for general formation types and is not restricted to 
shale which due to its very low permeability has a reduced risk of losing its pore fluid into the 
borehole/well. The objective of describing this section to general formation types is to enhance 
the understanding of the possible severe consequences that incorrect use of drilling techniques 
and drilling fluid pressure may cause.  
A brief paper of this importance introduces this section 4.  
Tertiary shale, Southern Viking Graben 
An HPHT exploration well drilled in 2012 in the Southern Viking Graben in the Norwegian North 
Sea encountered severe stability problems. The initial drilling was performed with water based 
drilling fluid (WBF) through Tertiary shale sections interbedded with permeable sand layers. After 
2 unsuccessful weeks of drilling the section was plugged and a sidetrack was drilled with oil based 
drilling fluid (OBF) encountering no stability problems. The problems arose in the interaction 
between the formation and the salt in the drilling fluid. Drilling data, well logs and cuttings 
mineralogy were examined for improving the design of the drilling fluid composition and density 
(Nes, Bøe, Sønstebø, Kjertil, Saasen, & Fjogstad, 2012). 
The experiences from the Southern Viking Graben show that the drilling fluid makes up one of the 
most important tools for maintaining borehole communication and borehole integrity. The 
purpose with this section is to study advantages and disadvantages of three main drilling fluid 
categories and three main drilling techniques which in other words is the drilling fluid pressure 
applied to the FE-model’s boreholes.

4.1.1 Fresh- and Saltwater-based Drilling Fluids 
Drilling fluids that are based on water or saline water are named WBF and are the most 
commonly used (aapg). 
Advantages

property classifies WBF as shale inhibitor drilling fluid.

Disadvantages

4.1.2 Oil- and Synthetic-based Drilling Fluids  
Drilling fluids that are based on oil and synthetics are named OBF (aapg). 
Advantages  
1. Developed without water reducing saturating the formation rock.  
2. Acts as a shale inhibitor in highly smectite or “gumbo” shales where it reduces borehole 
instabilities and provides intact cuttings.  
3. Lubricates deviated wellbores which reduces the drag and torque drilling problems.   
4. More resistant to chemical contamination (Carbonate, evaporate) 
Disadvantages 
1. More expensive than WBF. 
2. Slower Rate of Penetration (ROP) than WBF. 
3. Increase pollution control on rig deck in case of environmental restrictions. 
4. Oil mud cuttings may have to be cleaned before disposal.  
5. Reduce kick detection due to that Hydrogen sulfide (H2S), carbon dioxide (CO2) and

Pneumatic drilling “fluids” are based on air, mist, foam and gas. They are not studied further since

(aapg).   
4.2 Drilling Techniques 
Three basic drilling techniques Overbalanced (OBD), Underbalanced (UBD) and Managed Pressure 
(MPD) are presented in the following subsections along with some of their advantages and 
disadvantages (aapg), (petrowiki/UBD), (Islam, Skålle, & Tantserev, 2009), (shell/UBD), (Ostroot, 
Shayegi, Lewis, & Lovorn, 2007). 
4.2.1 Overbalanced Drilling 
Overbalanced Drilling (OBD) is the conventional type of drilling technique where the mud weight 
is providing an initial hydrostatic pressure (Maximum Bottomhole Pressure in the well, BHP) at 
approximately             (200 – 1000 psi) above the formation pore pressure. The interval 
should provide a good starting point for later adjustment of the drilling fluid system. 
Advantages 
1. Significantly reduce the risks of well kick. Added polymers to water based drilling fluid 
(WBF) makes shale almost inhibitive and reduce the fluid of penetrating the formation. 
==PAGE:45==
 
31 
 
The OBD in combination with a shale-inhibitive WBF ought to ensure a reducing risk of 
well kick and thus a blow out.   
Disadvantages 
1. Water based drilling fluid (WBF) may invade the shale. Due to shale’s low permeability the 
mud filtrate in the formation may cause formation of mud cakes on the borehole wall and 
thus (thus =consequently) increase of formation pore fluid pressure that may lead to 
instabilities.  
2. Decrease Rate of Penetration (ROP). Larger bottom hole pressure in the well increases the 
resistance on the drill bit and thus impeding removals of cuttings. 
3. Increase risks of lost circulation (uncontrollable loss of mud invasion to the formation). 
Unwanted loss of drilling fluid to formation fractures may lead to temporary well pressure 
drop. A permeable layer may lead pore fluid (or gas) into the wellbore causing a rapid 
increase of well pressure (a kick) and hence a high risk of a blow out. 
4. Increase risk of differential sticking. Drilling fluid pressure is larger than the formation 
pressure which may push the drill string to stick to the borehole wall.   
5. Increase risk of damaging shale where shale’s are rich in swelling clay minerals and thus 
often associated with high pore pressures. 
4.2.2 Underbalanced Drilling 
Underbalanced Drilling (UBD) is a technique where the mud weight is set as low as possible to 
ensure that hydrostatic pressure (Bottomhole Pressure of the well, BHP) is always below the 
formation pore pressure. Value of this is found to be approximately         (200 psi) below the 
initial pore pressure. As for the description of OBD, this study is borehole in shale and not a 
reservoir. It is therefore assumed that the value of         also holds for the shale’s pore 
pressure. This should provide a good starting point for later adjustment of the drilling fluid 
system. UBD has long been employed to solve both drilling- and reservoir problems (aapg), 
(petrowiki/UBD), (Islam, Skålle, & Tantserev, 2009), (shell/UBD), (Ostroot, Shayegi, Lewis, & 
Lovorn, 2007). 
Advantages 
1. Significantly reduces formation damage by preventing drilling fluid invasion into the 
formation  
2. Increase Rate of Penetration (ROP). Lesser bottomhole pressure in the well lessens the 
resistance on the drill bit and thus eases cutting removal. 
3. Reduces risks of lost circulation (uncontrollable loss of mud into formation). 
4. Eliminates differential sticking. Formation pressure is larger than the drilling fluid pressure 
and thereby pushes the drill string away from sticking to the borehole wall.   
5. Lessens the formation fluids (e.g. clay bound water) to react with the drilling fluid. 
 
 
==PAGE:46==
 
32 
 
Disadvantages 
1. More expensive than OBD, especially when used in deviated wells. 
2. Increase of safety issues due to narrow mud weight windows. UBD may cause tensile 
failure of the rock forming blade-shaped cavings and consequently building up a tight 
borehole and hence a stuck drill string (section 5). 
3. The negative differential pressure                    encourages formation fluids 
entering the wellbore which may cause a kick and thus risk a Blow Out in case of 
insufficient closing vales (One of the breached barriers leading to the Deepwater Horizon 
incident was an acceptance of a performed negative differential pressure test although 
well integrity was not established (Janssen F. E., 2014)). 
4. Difficult to handle and hence more study and modeling required. 
The study of UBD in shale by (Islam, Skålle, & Tantserev, 2009) where the bottomhole pressure of 
the well (BHP) was kept less than the formation pore pressure, argues that UBD normally increase 
the risk of borehole instability due to yielding or failure of the rock adjacent to the borehole. The 
paper states that shear and radial tensile failure modes are the common mechanisms to cause 
mechanical instability in UBD in shales. Immediate shear failure is assumed to occur when 
borehole is lacking sufficient mud support and the rock has insufficient strength. Due to shale’s 
very low permeability, the pore fluid cannot flow freely, and causes redistribution of stresses. 
Therefore, collapse pressure and pore pressure behaviors in shale are the most crucial factors for 
wellbore design in UBD.   
4.2.3 Managed Pressure Drilling 
Managed Pressure Drilling (MPD) is a type of Underbalanced Drilling (UBD). Both techniques allow 
management of wellbore downhole pressures but differ in how this is accomplished. MPD is a 
technique where the mud weight is providing a hydrostatic pressure (Bottomhole Pressure of the 
well, BHP) slightly above or equal to the formation pore pressure (i.e. overbalanced or balanced 
drilling). MPD is primarily used for resolving drilling-related problems but some reservoir benefits 
may be achieved. Such reservoir-benefits are explained by being able to adjust MPD for any 
unwanted degree of overbalance, and thus decrease the impact of drilling fluid on virgin 
formation. Often MPD is considered easier to use than UBD. In some non-reservoir sections MPD-
design requirements may determine that a simpler equipment package will satisfy safety 
considerations for the well, and therefore, the day rate would be reduced compared to using full 
underbalance (Ostroot, Shayegi, Lewis, & Lovorn, 2007). 
Drilling-related problems that have driven the adoption of UBD and MPD include 
 
Need for eliminating formation damage. 
 
Narrow mud weight window in UBD. 
 
Desire to minimize well costs in terms of minimize fluid losses and eliminate differential 
sticking. 
 
Desire to increase the Rate of Penetration (ROP). 
 
Desire to extending bit life. 
 
Increase safety in drilling operations.

stress state and thus risks fracturing the rock from excessive high mud pressure. On the other 
hand the formation to be drilled may require OBD if the formation has abnormally high pressure 
(overpressured). An overpressure might originate from areas where burial of water-filled 
sediments by impermeable sediment such as clay was so rapid that fluids could not escape and 
the pore pressure increased with deeper burial. This is shown in the right side of figure 18 where 
the hydrostatic pressure gradient shows that the rate of change in the formation pore pressure 
tends to increase with depth.

5. Instabilities of Boreholes 
This section introduces various categories of borehole instability problems. Borehole instability 
often occurs in shales that are rich in swelling clay minerals often associated with high pore 
pressures. When stresses exceed the formation’s load bearing capacity various instability 
scenarios such as fractures at the borehole vicinity, oriented borehole enlargements (breakout) 
and collapse may form (petrowiki/Borehole instability), (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 
2008). 
Four different types of borehole instabilities may occur  
1. Hole closure or narrowing (creep/rock mechanical failure). 
2. Hole enlargement (washouts) 
3. Fracturing 
4. Collapse 
Their causes can be grouped into three categories 
 
Mechanical failure caused by in-situ stresses. 
 
Erosion caused by fluid circulation. 
 
Chemical caused by interaction of borehole fluid with the formation. 
5.1 Creep 
Creep is a process of borehole instability that is time dependent. It generally takes place in plastic-
flowing shale and occurs at a constant overburden pressure or stress.  Hole-narrowing affects the 
drill string such that the formation tightens around the pipe above the drill bit and the pipe can be 
stuck. With tightening formation around a drill string tried to be pulled out follows an increase of 
torque, drag or pressure on the drill. Hole-narrowing and hole-closure may also occur after the 
drill string has been pulled and yields problems landing the casings. Furthermore, if hole-
narrowing occurs in between landing the casings but just before cementing the annulus, a poor 
cement job concludes which can affect the wells structural resistance and its annulus that acts as 
a barrier against unwanted fluids. 
5.2 Washouts   
Washout is a borehole-enlargement problem and may be caused by  
a) Hydraulic erosion and hydraulic wellbore erosion 
b) Mechanical abrasion  
c) Inherently sloughing shale 
These causes may arise several problems such as 
 
Increase in cement difficulty 
 
increase in potential hole deviation 
 
increase in hydraulic requirements for effective hole-cleaning 
 
increase in potential problems during logging operations 
==PAGE:50==
 
36 
 
5.2.1 Hydraulic erosion 
Hydraulic erosion and hydraulic wellbore erosion depends on a variety factors. (Chemerinski & 
Robinson, 1985) argues that hydraulic erosion is the primarily reason for hole-enlargement due to 
the interaction of the clay materials in shales with water. However, hole-enlargement can occur 
either immediately or with a delay which indicates that hole-enlargement is initially independent 
of clay-water interaction. A delay in hole-enlargement is observed in wells drilled through hard 
shales baptized as “7-day” and “10-day” shales. The paper concludes that hydraulic wellbore 
erosion is due to high nozzle velocities (velocity of high pressure drill fluid when being lead out of 
a small nozzle on the drill bit) rather than annular velocity. The paper also concludes that the 
hole-enlargement is also due to the turbulent flow of the drilling fluid and the hardness of the 
formation drilled through. 
5.2.2 Abrasion 
Mechanical abrasion (wearing by friction) caused by the drillstring is when the drillstring itself 
grinds up against the borehole wall following a hole-enlargement. Abrasiveness  can also act on 
the drillstring where it is the formation’s mineral hardness, particles size and shape that grinds off 
steel on the pipes and drill bit (Schlumberger/bit nozzle), (Schlumberger/abrasion test). 
5.2.3 Sloughing shale 
Inherently sloughing shale (naturally sliding shale) is the ability layered shale has where two 
neighbouring layers loosens or slides away from each other. This may occur because shale is 
highly sensitive to water imbibitions. Shale is highly water sensitive due to its clay content and 
ionic composition of the clays in shale (Schlumberger/sloughing shale), (Talabani, Chukwu, & 
Hatzignatiou, 1993). 
5.2.4 Hole Cleaning and Stuck Pipe 
Cavings that are not transported away increase the risk of a potential stuck drillstring. Hole 
cleaning and the time associated with such must therefore be taken in to greater consideration if 
formations with brittle rocks such Gumbo and very soft and sticky shales (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, 
Raaen, & Risnes, 2008).  
5.3 Fracturing 
Fracturing may occur when the drilling fluid pressure is higher than the formation fracture 
pressure. Fracturing and shale stability has also been studied extensively and the associated 
problems are 
a) Lost circulation 
b) Well kick 
5.3.1 Lost circulation 
Lost circulation is the occurrence of uncontrolled flow of drilling fluid into fissures, fractures or 
caverns in the formation which is a risk when drilling OBD. Depending on the amount of lost 
drilling fluid into the formation the borehole is no longer full of fluid and does not return to the 
surface. If the fluid column has dropped there is no longer fluid pressure onto the formation 
above the fluid column. This may result in formation fluid flowing into the wellbore and can cause 
==PAGE:51==
 
37 
 
loss of well control. Continuous drilling with total loss of circulation of drilling fluid is known as 
blind drilling (Rider & Kennedy, 2011). Lost circulation may therefore be total or in zones, and can 
be caused by  
 
Inherently fractured formations 
 
Cavernous, high and low permeable formations 
 
Inadmissible drilling conditions 
 
Induced fractures caused by excessive downhole pressure 
 
To high mounting of the intermediate casing  
Inherent Fractured Formations 
Induced fractures are horizontal in shallow formation depths or vertical in depths greater than 
750m and can lead to excessive wellbore pressures. Excessive wellbore pressures can be caused 
by 
 
High flow rates of the drilling fluid.  
 
High surge pressure (tripping the wellbore too fast).  
These may lead to Equivalent Circulating Density (ECD) which is the apparent (effective) increase 
in density of the drilling fluid due to its motion (Schlumberger/ECD). 
 
Inadequate annular hole cleaning (transport of cuttings by drilling fluid). 
 
Excessive weight of drilling fluid. 
Cavernous Formations 
A cavern is a naturally formed cave in a soluble rock with an ability to grow Speleothem (mineral 
deposit) and large caverns often form in limestones. High pemerable formations with shallow 
sand are prone to quick loss of circulation, whereas formations with deep sands are low 
permeable and not prone to lost circulation (wikipedia/speleothem), (caverntours), (Rider & 
Kennedy, 2011).

6. Parameter Study 
The best foundation for modeling borehole instability is to use as accurate rock mechanical 
parameters as possible.  Parameters may be obtained and derived from good practices of 
formation logging or laboratory testing of retrieved core samples. But retrieving cores of 
formation rocks and shale is associated with high risks of damaging the rock by means of tensile 
failure and crack development. Also, the cost of acquisition may exceed available budgets. 
Furthermore, due to shale’s very low permeability laboratory testing of retrieved shale is very 
time consuming and may exceed project deadlines (Fjær, Holt, Horsrud, Raaen, & Risnes, 2008), 
(Dewhurst, Siggins, Kuila, Clennell, Raven, & Nordgård-Bolås, 2008). 
Input parameters for the FE-modeling are values found in published papers of laboratory testing 
of shale as well as of real borehole instability problems encountered worldwide. Three chosen 
papers are briefly introduced along with their obtained parameters. The section ends with a 
discussion of the parameters and concludes a table of chosen FE-modeling scenario.

7.2 FE-Model of 3D Shale Borehole Trajectory 
One 3D model is created in COMSOL where the 8 different drilling fluid pressures presented in 
table 11 in section 6.1 corresponds to 8 different simulations. The 3D model has 4 thin plates of 
3mx3mx0.1m placed around a circular borehole and scattered along a borehole trajectory 
positioned so that the boreholes in the 4 plates are inclined with 0o, 32o, 75o and 90o 
corresponding to one vertical, two deviated and one horizontal borehole respectively. The use of 
quadratic plates ensures that the horizontal far-field stresses act within the same distance to 
every borehole wall. This method significantly reduces the CPU-time due to much fewer nodes 
than a model with the 5 times more nodes within a rectangular block surrounding the entire 
borehole trajectory. The radius of the borehole trajectory is 10m and represents a ‘short radius’ 
which according to (wiki/aapg/wellbore) is the minimum allowed borehole trajectory radius.  
The radius in the circular boreholes through the four plates measure 0.21m which is 
approximately 17 ½” to 17 ¼” which is according to (Stjern & Horsrud, 2003) a borehole size that 
together with a low gel strength mud should provide easier hole cleaning of shale cuttings. 
Another reason of modelling the borehole of 0.21m instead the smaller 12 ½” is for the sake of 
visualizing the simulated results in the borehole walls. The final geometric step in COMSOL is to 
subtract the circular borehole trajectory from the 4 plates and the result is the 4 boreholes as

The drilling fluid pressure    is mounted as both as externally static mechanical pressures and as 
internal Darcy pressures to all the borehole surfaces as seen in the right side of Figure 26. 
The drilling fluid pressures are mounted internally as Darcy pressure because it is desired that 
shale’s far-field pore fluid pressure is free to equilibrate towards the value of the drilling fluid 
pressure near and at the borehole walls (see section 3.9 for permeable shale borehole walls). By 
mounting the drilling fluid pressures externally as static mechanical pressures on the borehole 
wall surfaces then the borehole walls are free to mechanically displace by action of various drilling 
fluid pressures. In this way the external drilling fluid pressure and the counteracting external far-
field pressures will cooperate towards displacing the borehole walls.

No Flow 
In the poroelasticity COMSOL initially provides all domain surfaces with ‘No Flow’ which simply 
means that there is no Darcy fluid flow in or out from these surfaces (expressed by the equation 
          where   is the Darcy fluid velocity and   is the normal vector to a surafce). The No 
Flow condition on each surface is then automatically overridden when mounting the different 
loads, and for all the borehole walls the Darcy pressures mounted enable Darcy flow in and out 
from all borehole walls. The permeable borehole walls allow a possible exchange/displacement of 
the shale’s pore fluid and the drilling fluid. However, since the shale’s pore fluid and the drilling 
fluid in this model are of equal compositions it is not possible to visually identify any fluid 
exchange. Only pressure analyses may indicate possible fluid translations. The permeable 
borehole wall should provide the shale’s pore fluid pressure near the borehole wall to equilibrate 
(increase) with the drilling fluid pressure when the drilling fluid pressure is higher than shale’s 
pore fluid pressure (OBD). Opposite, when drilling UBD the permeable borehole wall should allow 
shale’s pore fluid pressure near the borehole wall to equilibrate (decrease) with the drilling fluid 
pressure. In the OBD case it is not certain that there will be a measurable shale pore pressure 
increase near the borehole wall since the pore fluid is free to flow by Darcy’s law inside the plate. 
However, this also depends on the applied permeability of the shale. This will be analysed in

decided that buckling will not be analysed. Enabling constraints of the plate surfaces as just

The accuracy of the simulated results depends on the smoothness of the mesh. A drawback of a 
very fine and smooth mesh of a domain is followed by a significant increase in CPU-time for the 
simulations. The degrees of mesh smoothness with the four-node tetrahedral elements (3D 
triangles) were tested; COMSOL’s built-in mesh ‘Finer’ and ‘Extremely Fine’. COMSOL’s ‘Finer’ and 
‘Extremely Fine’ are seen in figure 29. 
 
Figure 28. Finer and Extremely Fine mesh of the plates. 
As seen in figure 29 letting COMSOL refine from ‘Finer’ to ‘Extremely Fine’ mesh keeps the use of 
four-node tetrahedral elements but downscale their sizes so that the domain contains the double 
amount of elements. The four-node tetrahedral elements and mesh smoothness chosen for the 
simulations is the ‘Finer’ mesh. This is evaluated sufficiently with respect to the fluctuations in the 
result-plots for this analysis, and when taking into account that the length of a side in one 
element is sufficiently small compared to the borehole circumference and plate thickness. 
Furthermore, the CPU-time for each simulation using the ‘Finer’ mesh is 9min whereas for the 
‘Extremely Fine’ mesh is 14min. Also commonly used are 10-node tetrahedral elements. These are

9. Conclusions 
The presented section of Fundamentals of Shale Behaviour underlines the necessity of gathering 
as much knowledge as possible of the geology and rock mechanical parameters of the formation 
rock to be modelled. Especially the assessment of shale’s pore fluid pressure as this largely affects 
the effective principal stresses and thus the strength of the borehole wall. Although not 
incorporated to this FE-model shale’s swelling/shrinkage capacity is also a very important feature 
to asses for the sake of analyses of shale and pore fluid interaction during drilling. 
The three different drilling techniques (Underbalanced Drilling, Managed Pressure Drilling and 
Overbalanced drilling) which were tested showed that the FE-model was highly sensitive to the 
different drilling fluids pressures and hence greatly affected the borehole’s shape and 
displacements. Despite not modelling various compositions of drilling fluids the shale pore fluid 
and drilling fluid showed good communication through the permeable borehole walls.  As 
expected the largest borehole wall displacements on the entire borehole trajectory occurred on 
the 750 deviated and horizontal boreholes in the directions of the overburden    and maximum 
far-field stress     Although the analytical kirsch equations of displacements were not altered into 
Cartesian coordinates and inserted to the COMSOL model for comparison, they showed almost 
the same magnitude as for those which FE-simulated.  
The results of the very small borehole wall displacements did not give a clear indication of 
borehole instability problems such as a tight hole. The reason for this was due to the chosen 
parameters that were applied to the FE-model. However, the combination of the displacements 
and effective stress concentrations around the borehole walls gave an early indication of potential 
problems.  The effective principal stress concentrations at the vertical and 320 deviated borehole 
were slightly symmetrical scattered around the walls for both UBD and OBD, and pointed in 
general in both directions of the horizontal far-field stresses    and     The effective principal 
stress concentrations at the 750 deviated and horizontal borehole walls showed more non-
symmetric scattering for both the UBD and OBD. Maximum values of 324MPa (compression) for 
the 750 deviated borehole were found in the borehole wall pointing in the direction of   the 
overburden     for 30MPa UBD, however, drilling with 30MPa UBD in a nearly horizontal borehole 
is not likely to be performed in real life. 
The analyses of the tensional and shear failures in the shale rock showed that the weak planes 
only had sufficient shear resistance for the 60MPa, 61.4MPa UBD and 64.4MPa OBD for all angles 
of boreholes. In general all planes of weakness had no sufficient tensile strength.  These results 
relied on assumptions of the used friction angles and cohesions which were found in published 
papers whereas laboratory tests could have revealed other values. For other choices of these 
parameters larger stress configurations could have been accepted in the shear failure analyses, 
and hence the shale could have provided more shear resistance to the boreholes to a larger range 
of the UBD and OBD drilling fluid pressures.
