Abstract  
Fatty acid synthases (FASs) are the enzymes responsible for the production of fatty acids, and are 
almost ubiquitous within all domains of life, due to the essential role of fatty acids as precursor 
molecules for the production of lipids and other essential molecules. The type 1 FASs of metazoans 
are structurally highly similar to the type 1 polyketide synthases (PKSs) and are believed to have a 
common evolutionary origin. 
FASs have received attention as a potential production platform for a variety of compounds including 
polyketides. In the current study, it has been attempted to evaluate the functionality of the genes 
encoding the S. scrofa phosphopantetheinyl transferase (PPT) and FAS in S. cerevisiae. Additionally, 
the effects of point mutations within the ketoreductase (KR) active site on overall FAS activity of S. 
scrofa FAS has been assessed. Finally, it has been attempted to convert the metazoan FAS into a 
production platform for non-reduced octaketides through combination of KR inactivation and 
targeted mutagenesis of the ketosynthase domain (KS). 
Mutagenesis of the KR active site of metazoan FAS has previously resulted in a removal of overall FAS 
activity. It has been proposed that this is due to KS specificity towards fully reduced substrates. 
Primers were therefore designed for the introduction of point mutations to change the specificity of 
the KS domain. To assess the functionality of the S. scrofa FAS and PPT in S. cerevisiae a knock-out of 
the endogenous FAS was performed. Determination of the effects of KR and KS were carried out by 
chemical analysis of the constructed yeast strains. 
It was within the timeframe of the project not possible to confirm the functionality of the S. scrofa 
FAS and PPT in S. cerevisiae and determination of the KR and KS mutations was therefore difficult.

Introduction 
Fatty acid biosynthesis is a metabolic process almost ubiquitous in all domains of life owing to the 
essential role of fatty acids as precursors of lipids (Finzel, Lee, & Burkart, 2015). The biosynthesis of 
fatty acids is carried out by fatty acid synthases (FASs). These can be divided into two markedly 
different types. The type I FAS consist of multifunctional proteins forming large stable protein 
complexes containing all the enzymatic domains necessary for fatty acid biosynthesis. The type I FASs 
are mainly found in fungi and metazoans but also in some types of bacteria belonging to the phylum 
Actinobacteria. In contrast the type II FAS systems of plants and most prokaryotes consists of many 
independent monofunctional enzymes each catalyzing a separate step of the biosynthetic 
pathway(Janßen et al., 2014)(Gago, Diacovich, Arabolaza, Tsai, & Gramajo, 2011). 
The FASs have been the focus of intense research due to their important role in primary metabolism 
and their potential applications as drug targets and production platforms. Fatty acids are essential 
components of a cell serving as the building blocks for the synthesis of phospholipids and glycolipids 
which are important components of the cell membrane. Furthermore, fatty acids are used as 
precursors for hormones, triacyclglycerols involved in energy storage, and are used in post 
translational modifications of membrane proteins (Resh, 1999; Tehlivets, Scheuringer, & Kohlwein, 
2007).  
 Aside from the important role of fatty acids in cells, FASs also represents an interesting drug target in 
the treatment of microbial infection. This is due to the large enzymatic differences between FAS 
system of humans and bacteria allowing for the development of drugs targeting the bacterial FAS 
without affecting the activity of the FAS system of the patient (Wright & Reynolds, 2007).   
FASs have also received a growing attention as a potential production platform for both fuels and 
other small molecules such as polyketides. Through the integration of an extra enzymatic domain the 
fungal type I FAS has been engineered for the production of short chain fatty acids which can be used 
for the production of biofuels (Zhu et al., 2017).  Rational engineering has in a bacterial type I FAS 
enabled the conversion of the FAS into a polyketide production platform (Gajewski et al., 2017). 
In the current project, I investigate the metazoan FAS which is structurally very similar to the type I 
Polyketide Synthases (PKSs). The project will assess if the FAS system of Sus scrofa (pig) can be 
functionally expressed in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Furthermore, the active site and 
specificity of the ketosynthase and ketoreductase domain will be investigated to assess if the product 
of the FAS can be changed.

Theory section 
Fatty acid biosynthesis   
The synthesis of fatty acids relies on the common building blocks acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA which 
are iteratively linked together, in a condensation reaction to form an acyl chain. Initially the acyl chain 
consists of acetyl from acetyl-CoA which is known as the starter unit. During each elongation cycle, 
the acyl-chain undergoes a condensation reaction with malonyl from malonyl-CoA known as the 
extender unit. This results in the elongation of the acyl chain by two carbon atoms. Following the 
condensation reaction, the acyl chain will possess a keto group on the β-carbon originating from the 
used malonyl. This β-keto group is subsequently reduced in three reducing reactions to produce a 
fully reduced β-carbon. This allows the acyl-chain to undergo another round of condensation and 
reductions to eventually yield the 16-carbon long compound palmitic acid as seen in Figure 1.(Smith & 
Tsai, 2007).  
The metazoan synthesis of fatty acids begins with the loading of acetyl derived from acetyl-CoA by the 
Malonyl-Acetyl-Transferase domain (MAT). MAT is responsible for the loading of both the starter unit 
and the extender units. Both starter and extender units are transferred from the MAT domain to the 
Acyl-Carrier-Protein (ACP). The ACP is responsible for the transfer of the growing acyl chain to the 
various enzymatic domains of the FAS and possesses a highly flexible protein arm allowing it to access 
and interact with the different active sites. In order for the ACP domain to accept any acyl substrate it 
must be post translationally modified by the addition of a phosphopantetheinyl group, which acts as a 
cofactor. The phosphopantetheinyl

Following the Claisen condensation the acyl chain carrying a keto group is transferred to the 
ketoreductase domain (KR). The KR domain catalyzes the reduction of the ketone to an alcohol in a 
NADPH dependent reaction (Step C). The alcohol is in turn further reduced by the Dehydratase (DH) 
domain yielding an alkene (step D) which is finally reduced to a saturated acyl chain by the NADPH 
dependent action of the Enoyl reductase (ER) domain (Step E). The process of elongation and 
reduction will generally proceed until the chain has reached a length of 16-18 carbon atoms. At this 
point the Thioesterase (TE) domain catalyzes the hydrolyzation of the ester bond between the ACP 
and the acyl chain, producing a fully reduced fatty acid (Step G) (Smith & Tsai, 2007).  
Structural differences in type 1 FAS group 
Type I FAS are large protein complexes that utilizes the same building blocks and give rise to the same 
product. However, within the type I FAS group there exist two distinct types of FAS protein 
complexes, the metazoan and the fungal FAS. The metazoan FAS consists of two identical polypeptide 
chains forming a homodimer creating two separate sets of catalytic domains. In contrast, the type 1 
FAS of fungi is a heterododecameric protein complex composed of six copies of two subunits termed 
α and β. The α and β chains are organized as depicted in Figure 2.

The 6 α subunits are organized into a central wheel upon which three of the β subunits form a dome 
structure on each side. This gives rise to a FAS particle with six sets of catalytic domains. In terms of 
structure the metazoan FAS is more flexible than its fungal counterpart owing to the fact that a larger 
part of the fungal FAS is devoted to structural scaffolding(Maier et al., 2008)(Pappenberger et al., 
2010). 
Aside from the obvious architectural differences between the fungal and metazoan FAS seen in Figure 
2 there are distinct differences in the domains present in the protein complex. In the fungal FAS the 
PPT responsible for charging of the ACP domain is an integrated part of the protein. In contrast, the 
PPT in metazoan FAS exists as an independent protein (Maier et al., 2010).  
In metazoan FASs the loading of both starter and extender units is carried out by the previously 
mentioned MAT domain. In contrast, fungal FAS loads the starter unit with a dedicated acetyl-
transferase domain (AT) while loading of the extender unit is achieved by a malonyl-transacylase 
domain (MPT). The MPT domain of fungal FAS is also responsible for releasing the fatty acid once it 
reaches a length of 16 to 18 carbons, this function is handled by the TE domain in metazoan FAS 
(Maier et al., 2010).  
Polyketide biosynthesis 
Polyketides are a highly diverse group of compounds with a wide range of bioactivities. They are 
economically highly interesting owing to their applications within the both the food and the 
pharmaceutical industry (Weissman, 2015; Woo et al., 2015).  
Like fatty acids polyketides can be synthesized from acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA but different types 
of starter and extender units can also be utilized. The types of enzymatic domains found in PKSs are 
essentially the same as those found in the FASs. However, while each cycle of condensation in fatty 
acid biosynthesis is followed by reductions performed by the KR, DH and ER domains this is not 
necessarily the case in PKS. In polyketide biosynthesis, some or all reductions might be excluded 
enabling the production of much more diverse compounds than the fully reduced fatty acid of FASs 
(Tsai & Ames, 2009). 
PKSs are classified into three categories based on the composition of the protein complex. Of these 
three categories, the type I PKS are of especial interest in this study due to the close structural 
similarity between these proteins and the type I metazoan FAS (Maier et al., 2008) 
The type I PKSs are characterized by consisting of single polypeptide chain containing several active 
sites and can further be divided into modular and iterative type I PKSs. Modular type I PKSs are 
organized into a sequence of individual modules that each catalyzes a single elongation cycle. The 
iterative type I PKS consist of a single module which iteratively elongates the ketide chain until a 
certain length is reached, as is observed in type I FAS (Smith & Tsai, 2007). 
==PAGE:10==

Aside from the structural similarity between the metazoan type I FASs and the type I PKSs the 
phylogenetic relationship between the enzymatic domains of these two types of protein complexes 
indicates a common evolutionary background. Phylogenetic analysis of the KS domains of various 
types of FAS and PKS systems have for example shown that the KS domain of metazoan FAS is more 
closely related to the type I PKS KS domain than KS domains of both the fungal type I FAS and the 
bacterial type II FAS systems. Furthermore the KS alignments also indicate that the type I PKSs and 
metazoan FASs have a common ancestor while the fungal type I might have evolved from the 
bacterial type II FAS system(Bukhari, Jakob, & Maier, 2014; Jenke-Kodama, Sandmann, Müller, & 
Dittmann, 2005). 
Individual domains 
The metazoan FASs are specific for the production of fully reduced fatty acids with a chain length of 
16-18 carbon atoms (Maier et al., 2008). In the following section the KS, KR and TE of metazoan FAS 
will be examined in terms of active site chemistry and substrate specificity to explain the overall 
specificity of the FAS. 
Ketoacyl synthase 
In the metazoan FAS the KS domain forms a homodimer. It is made up of residues from both 
polypeptide chains that are intertwined to form a single KS domain with two active sites as indicated 
in Figure 3 (Maier et al., 2008). 
The KS domain contains a long tunnel which allows binding of the growing acyl chain. Furthermore, 
the tunnel holds the two active sites which catalyzes the Claisen condensation necessary for the 
elongation of the acyl chain. The active site of the KS domain is a catalytic triad consisting of two 
histidine and one cysteine residue (Maier et al., 2008).  
==PAGE:11==

Elongation begins with a trans-thioesterification of the active site cysteine with the acyl chain of the 
ACP. This leaves the acyl chain bound to the active site cysteine and the length of the chain located 
inside the acyl binding tunnel. The ACP is recharged with malonyl-CoA which is transferred to the 
active site of KS where it is positioned in relation to the cysteine bound acyl chain and undergoes 
decarboxylation to form a carbanion intermediate. The carbanion can then perform a nucleophilic 
attack on a the α-carbon of the acyl chain to allow for the Claisen condensation to take place. Finally, 
the elongated acyl chain is transferred to the ACP for the subsequent reductions. 
Ketoacyl synthase Specificity  
The specificity of the ketosynthase within both FASs and PKSs have been the subject of several 
studies. It has been attempted to uncover the underlying mechanisms which determines why some KS 
domains are capable of accepting branched intermediates while others are highly specific for reduced 
intermediates (Murphy, Hong, Vance, Broadhurst, & Leadlay, 2016)(Watanabe, Wang, Boddy, Cane, & 
Khosla, 2003)(Gay et al., 2014). 
The specificity of the metazoan FAS KS domain was observed in an experiment where the FAS of 
Rattus norvegicus was mutated in the active site of the DH domain. This led to a halt of the reducing 
reactions following the KR catalyzed reduction of the β-keto group to a hydroxyl. The KS domain was 
unable to accept the hydroxylated substrate and catalyze elongation thus halting fatty acid synthesis 
after the first round of elongation. Instead of producing fatty acids the mutated FAS produced a low 
yield of β-hydroxybutyryl-CoA (Joshi & Smith, 1993). The strict specificity of the KS domains of

The ketoreductase domain catalyzes the reduction of the β-keto group to yield an β-hydroxyl as seen

Rossmann fold allowing for nucleotide binding

Thioesterase domain 
The TE domain is responsible for termination of the extension of the ACP bound acyl chain. This is 
achieved through hydrolysis of the thioester bond between the acyl chain and the 
phosphopantetheinyl group of the ACP (Chakravarty, Gu, Chirala, Wakil, & Quiocho, 2004). The active 
site of TE domain holds a catalytic triad consisting of a Ser, His and Asp residue and is located at the 
entry point of the TE acyl binding pocket. The acyl binding pocket is largely hydrophobic in nature and 
has a strong affinity for binding acyl chains of length C16. Shorter chains are bound in one of two 
smaller cavities which do not position the thioester bond in a catalytically productive conformation 
(Pemble, Johnson, Kridel, & Lowther, 2007). In contrast to the TE domain the KS domain has greater 
affinity for accepting acyl chain of short to medium length. The combination of the substrate affinities 
of these two domains thus ensure the fidelity of the metazoan FAS towards the production of C16 
fatty acids (Smith & Tsai, 2007). 
S. cerevisiae as a cell factory for polyketide products 
Few other organisms have been utilized as widely and for as long within the field of biotechnology as 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae. The yeast has been used as a cell factory to produce food stuffs, bulk 
chemicals and pharmaceuticals. S. cerevisiae represents a very comprehensively characterized 
eukaryote and tools for the genetic engineering are well established (Kavšček, Stražar, Curk, Natter, & 
Petrovič, 2015). Furthermore yeast can achieve relatively fast growth and are easily fermentable 
altogether making yeast a highly attractive cell factory platform (Mutka, Bondi, Carney, Da Silva, & 
Kealey, 2006) 
In terms of production of secondary metabolites S. cerevisiae has the advantage of naturally 
producing very few secondary metabolites compared to for example the filamentous fungi. This lack 
of natural secondary metabolite production means that there is little risk of cross talk between the

heterologous pathway and the endogenous metabolic network (Rugbjerg, Naesby, Mortensen, & 
Frandsen, 2013; Siddiqui et al., 2012).  
Various heterologous polyketide pathways have been successfully integrated into S. cerevisiae, such 
as the relatively simple polyketide 6-methylsalicylic acid from Aspergillus nidulans and rubrofusarin 
from F. graminearum, demonstrating that polyketide production in S. cerevisiae is possible (Mutka et 
al., 2006; Rugbjerg et al., 2013).  
Fatty acid metabolism in S. cerevisiae  
Fatty acid synthesis in S. cerevisiae occurs in both the cytosol and the mitochondria. In the cytosol, 
the synthesis is catalyzed by the fungal FAS, while the mitochondrial fatty acid synthesis is catalyzed 
by a type II FAS system encoded in the mitochondrial genome. The cytosolic fatty acid synthesis in S. 
cerevisiae differs from the metazoan pathway in that the product released from the fungal FAS is fatty 
acyl-CoA rather than a free fatty acid. Furthermore, the expression of acyl-CoA synthases that convert 
free fatty acids into their acyl-CoA counterparts ensures a relatively low intracellular level of free fatty 
acids. This help protect the cell from the potential toxicity of free fatty acids in regards to membrane 
integrity (Zhou et al., 2016)(Tehlivets et al., 2007).  The expression of the fas gene of S. cerevisiae is 
regulated via negative feed-back through the level of fatty acyl-CoA (Andre, Haslam, & Shanklin, 2012)  
Following the generation of the C16-C18 fatty acyl-CoA the acyl chain may undergo further 
modifications, including the introduction of double bonds, to yield monounsaturated fatty acids, and 
further elongation of the acyl chain to yield long long chain fatty acids. The introduction of double 
bonds is in S. cerevisiae achieved via an Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membrane bound desaturase 
OLE1p. The desaturation reaction catalyzed by OLE1p produces monounsaturated fatty acid. It is used 
extensively and approximately 80 % of all fatty acids in S. cerevisiae are monounsaturated (Martin, 
Oh, & Jiang, 2007; Tehlivets et al., 2007) 
The elongation reactions which generate fatty acyl of length up to C26 takes place in the ER and like 
FAS catalyzed reactions consist of a condensation reaction between an acyl chain and malonyl-CoA 
followed by reductions (Tehlivets et al., 2007).  
Phospholipids are a group of compounds that form the main components of cellular membranes and 
are thus essential for the viability of the cell. Phospholipid biosynthesis is initiated in a reaction 
between glycerol-3-phosphate and a long chain acyl-CoAs originating from fatty acid biosynthesis 
forming phosphatidic acid which acts a phospholipid precursor (van der Rest et al., 1995). 
Like the phospholipids the glycolipids serve an important role as constituents of the cellular 
membranes that stabilizes the membrane structure. The glycolipids are a diverse class of compounds 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
and are apart from their role in stabilizing the cellular membrane also act as signaling molecules and 
membrane anchors for membrane associated proteins (Malhotra & Singh, 2005). 
The triacyclglycerols mainly serves as a method for storing energy and acetyl-CoA, and once formed 
cluster together to form lipid bodies. The energy rich triacyclglycerols can then once needed be 
broken down to form fatty acids. These fatty acids can be further broken down in the β-oxidation 
pathway taking place in the peroxisomes where they are converted into acetyl-CoA that can be 
further utilized as building blocks or for the generation of energy (Li et al., 2014).

Project outline 
Aim of the study: 
The study aims to assess the functionality of the S. scrofa FAS and PPT in the microbial host S. cerevisiae. 
Furthermore, the study aims to reprogram the metazoan FAS into a non-reducing PKS. 
Hypothesis 
In terms of the functionality of the S. scrofa FAS it seems very likely that it will be functional in S. 
cerevisiae as previous experiments have demonstrated the functionality of the human FAS in S. 
cerevisiae (Leber & Da Silva, 2014a). The reason for using the S. scrofa FAS rather than the human FAS 
is that the S. scrofa FAS structure has been determined by X-ray crystallography enabling rational 
engineering of active sites. 
The conversion of the FAS into a non-reducing PKS will be 
initiated by targeted mutagenesis of the KR active with the 
aim of eliminating KR activity. The mutagenesis targets 
within the active site as seen in Table 1 have been 
selected based on experiments that have previously 
mutated active site in similar KR domains in FASs and 
PKSs. These studies have achieved significant decreases or 
complete removal of KR activity and based on the large 
degree of similarity between these previously mutated KR 
domains and the KR domain of S. scrofa FAS it is 
hypothesized that similar results will be obtained (Maier 
et al., 2008; Price, Zhang, Rock, & White, 2004; Reid et al., 
2003) 
As previously stated the KS domain of the metazoan FAS 
has been observed to have specificity towards fully reduced substrate and the expected effect of 
elimination of KR activity is therefore a halt to the condensation reaction and a non-functional 
FAS(Joshi & Smith, 1993). 
It will therefore be attempted to change the substrate specificity of the KS through mutations of the 
previously mentioned constricting residues of the acyl-binding tunnel of the KS domain. Two 
mutagenesis strategies will be employed for the constricting residues. One will be to mutate the 
residues to their smaller DEBS counterparts and the other is to replace the residues with alanine. The 
hypothesis is that these mutations will make the KS domain more promiscuous and allow for 
accommodation of non-reduced substrates.

Key techniques of the study 
USER cloning 
The assembly of all plasmids in this project have relied on the Uracil Specific Excision Reagent (USER) 
method which allows for the creation of specifically compatible overhangs between a number of 
fragments. This is achieved by amplifying all the fragments to be assembled with primers containing a 
single uracil near the 5’ end. Following amplification, the fragments to be assembled are mixed and 
treated with the USER enzyme mix which removes the uracil base. Removal of the uracil base causes 
all bases following it in the 5’ direction to dissociate. This creates overhangs that allow the fragments 
to fit together in a specific arrangement. The fragments are allowed time to anneal and are then used 
in a transformation of competent dH5α cells where the ligation of the assembled plasmids take 
place(Frandsen, Andersson, Kristensen, & Giese, 2008). 
Introduction of point mutations 
The introduction of point mutations has been achieved via PCR amplification of the FAS gene using 
two primer pairs with an overlapping region between a primer from each pair as seen in Figure 7. 
 
Within the overlapping region a mismatch of one to three bases between the template FAS and the 
overlapping primers ensures that a single codon is mutated and thus changing the amino acid 
encoded by the codon.

The project involves the expression of the mammalian FAS encoding gene and mutated versions 
thereof in yeast and for this purpose the plasmid system developed by Mikkelsen et al. have been 
utilized(Mikkelsen et al., 2012). Plasmids have been designed that allow for chromosomal integration 
of the gene of interest at specific sites. The benefit of chromosomal integration of a heterologous 
gene as opposed expression from a plasmid is a more stable expression level as there are no

The introduction of the linearized DNA and plasmid DNA in this project has relied on the LiAc/SS

achieve transformation. Incubation of yeast cells with PEG and DNA causes the DNA to adhere to the 
cell wall and is necessary for the transformation of intact yeast cells. The effect of the heat shock, LiAc 
and ss DNA is to make the cell wall more permeable so that the DNA may pass through. Transport of 
the DNA across the cell membrane is believed to occur by endocytosis, a process during which an 
invagination of the cell membrane results in the formation of an intercellular vesicle containing the 
transformation DNA. The process by which the transformation DNA is transferred from the

Metabolite analysis 
To assess the effect of the amino acid substitutions introduced in the S. scrofa FAS the CEN.PK-102-5B 
strains expressing the mutated FAS will be cultivated and an analysis of the fatty acid and potential 
polyketide production will be analyzed. For this purpose, mass spectrometry (MS) in combination with 
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) will be utilized. 
Prior to HPLC-MS the compounds need to be extracted to separate the metabolites of interest from 
the biomass and to produce a sample which is compatible with chromatography. The choice of 
extraction method has a large impact on the types and quantities of metabolites extracted as no 
extraction method will recover all metabolites (Mushtaq, Choi, Verpoorte, & Wilson, 2014).  
In the current project two chemically distinct compounds are investigated polyketides and fatty acids. 
If the mutagenesis of the KR and KS results in a polyketide producing FAS the product would likely be 
a non-reduced octaketide. The non-reduced octaketide would fold spontaneously to form SEK4 and 
SEK4B (Mizuuchi et al., 2009). SEK4 and SEK4B are more polar than the fully reduced fatty acids and to 
accommodate for these chemical differences two different extraction methods will be employed.  
For extraction of fatty acids and lipids an adapted version of the Blighe and Dyer method involving the 
use of chloroform to dissolve lipids will be used (Bligh & Dyer, 1959; Leber & Da Silva, 2014a). 
However, as the extracted fatty acids will be analyzed by LC-MS and not GC-MS there is no need for 
derivatization to make the fatty acids volatile which is required in GC-MS. Instead the fatty acids will 
be dissolved in methanol to prepare them for analysis by HPLC-MS (Zhang et al., 2016) 
The extraction of polyketides will instead of chloroform rely on ethyl-acetate in an extraction method 
which has previously used in the analysis of polyketides in yeast (Rugbjerg et al., 2013). 
For the analysis of complex mixtures of compounds MS is generally coupled with a form of 
chromatography to ensure the separation of the compounds of the mixture so that ideally a single 
compound can be analyzed one at a time (Pitt, 2009). The basic principle of chromatography is that a 
sample is injected into a mobile phase which may be a liquid or a gas that carries the compounds 
through a column containing the stationary phase which may be a solid or a liquid. Based on the 
affinity of the compounds in the sample towards both the mobile and stationary phase the 
compounds will be retained differently and leave the column at different times. In this manner 
chromatography achieves a separation of the compounds in the sample which can then be analyzed 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
by MS (Bird, 1989). In the current project, high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) has been 
utilized for the analysis of the production of fatty acids and polyketides. HPLC is a highly versatile and 
widely used type of chromatography and have been routinely used in the identification of secondary 
metabolites such as polyketides (Nielsen & Larsen, 2015)(Das & Khosla, 2009). Traditionally the 
analysis of fatty acids has been carried out using gas chromatography mass spectroscopy (GC-MS). 
However it has been demonstrated that identification and quantification can also be achieved by 
HPLC-MS (Bromke et al., 2015)(Valsecchi et al., 2007).  
Methods and Materials 
Experimental flow 
In the following section, a rough description of the intended experimental flow is presented and this is 
further visualized in Figure 9.  
The first step of the project was the assembly of plasmids carrying the S. scrofa fas gene or a S. 
cerevisiae codon optimized version thereof. Assembly required PCR amplification of the gene and 
promoter fragments followed by USER cloning of the fragments into the pX-3-ccdB plasmid. The     
assembly was verified by restriction enzyme digest analysis and sequencing.  
Following the verification of the plasmids carrying the fas-orig and fas-scopt the plasmids were used 
as templates for the introduction of point mutations in the KR domain via PCR amplification. The 
resulting fragments were used to assemble plasmids carrying versions of the fas-orig or fas-scopt 
containing point mutations in the KR domain. The assembly of the plasmids were checked by 
restriction enzyme digest analysis and the introduction of the point mutation was verified by 
sequencing. This process was repeated twice each time using the previously constructed plasmids as 
templates combine a point mutation in the KR domain with several point mutations in the KS domain.  
The plasmids were used in the transformation of S. cerevisiae strain carrying ppt-scopt. The resulting 
transformants were verified by colony PCR. Subsequently the strains were cultivated and an 
extraction of polyketides and fatty acids were performed and analyzed by HPLC-MS. The yeast strains 
carrying non-mutated fas-scopt and fas-orig were used in a transformation with the aim of knocking 
out the endogenous FAS of the yeast to assess if the fas-scopt or fas-orig could rescue the resulting 
transformants.

Following the PCR amplification of the gene fragments the plasmids pX-3-ccdB and pESC-LEU-USER 
were digested using AsiSI and Nb.BsmI from New England Biolabs to open the USER cassette as 
described below. 
An AsiSI digest mix of:  58 µL MiliQ H2O, 10 µL NEB 3.1 Buffer, 30 µL plasmid, 2 µL AsiSI was incubated 
overnight at 37 C°. 
The following day an additional 4 µL AsiSI was added to the overnight mix which was further 
incubated at 37 C° for an hour followed by inactivation by incubation at 80 C° for 20 min. 
Subsequently 4 µL Nb.BsmI was added and the mix was incubated at 65 C° for an hour. To isolate the 
linearized plasmid the entire volume was run on a 1 % agarose gel with SYBR safe. The band 
corresponding to the linearized vector was excised from the gel. A gel purification of the linearized 
plasmid was then performed according to the instructions in the GFXTM PCR And Gel Band Purification 
Kit.

Metabolite extraction 
Due to need for extraction of both fatty acids and potential polyketide products two extraction 
protocols were utilized.  
Fatty acid extraction 
Initially 8 mL of cultivation broth was spun down at 3000 g for 5 min. The supernatant was discarded 
and the cell were resuspended in 1 mL MiliQ H2O and transferred to an screw cap tube. The cells were 
spun down at 3000 g for 5 min and the water was removed. The wet cell mass was mixed with 1.2 mL 
2:1 methanol:chloroform and was vortexed with glass beads for 10 min. 400 µL chloroform was added 
and the mixture was further vortexed for 1-2 min. 400 µL of ddH2O was added and the mixture was 
centrifuged for 30 seconds. The mixture was then centrifuged at 1000 g for 4 min and the 0.75 mL of 
the lower chloroform layer was extracted and transferred to a new tube and evaporated with 
nitrogen gas.  
Following the drying the metabolites were resuspended in 0.5 mL of methanol. The methanol solution 
was centrifuged at 16000 g for 2 min and a 150 µL supernatant was extracted for HPLC-MS 
Polyketide extraction protocol 
The remaining cultivation broth was centrifuged at 3000 g for 5 min. The supernatant was transferred 
to new container while the cell mas was used for extraction of intracellular polyketides. The cell mass 
was resuspended in 25 mL ethylacetate with 1% formic acid and the mixture briefly vortexed and 
sonicated for an hour. Following the sonication 25 mL of the culture broth was added to the cells and 
the solution was mixed. The resulting two phase mixture was separated by removal of the water 
phase with a glass pipette. The ethyl acetate phase was then evaporated by nitrogen flow. 
Following the drying the metabolites were resuspended in 1 mL of methanol which was transferred to 
an Eppendorf tube. The methanol solution was centrifuged at 16000 g for 2 min and a 150 µL 
supernatant was extracted for HPLC-MS

HPLC-MS analysis 
Separation of metabolites of the extracted samples were performed by Ultra High-Performance Liquid 
Chromatography (UHPLC) on the Agilent 1290 Infinity system. The column used was the Poroshell 120 Phenyl 
Hexyl column (250 mm* 2.1 mm 2.7 µ). The liquid phase buffers used were A: water buffered with 20 mM

initially 10 % buffer B to 100% buffer B over 15 min.  Ionization was performed by positive electrospray 
ionization and MS detection was performed using Agilent 6545 QTOF system. 
Knock-out of gene encoding the β subunit of the endogenous FAS 
To test the functionality of the fas-scopt, fas-orig and ppt-scopt in S. cerevisiae a knock-out of the gene 
encoding the β subunit of the endogenous fungal FAS was attempted. Two primer pairs were designed for the 
amplification of genomic DNA upstream and downstream of the gene. The primers FAS-UP-fwd and FAS-UP-rv 
amplify a fragment from chromosome XI position 100071-100591 while FAS-DOWN-fwd and FAS-DOWN-rv 
amplify chromosome XI position 106868-107355 in the CEN.PK-102-5B strain.  
Furthermore, two primer pairs were used to amplify the ura3 gene in two fragments. Each of the ura3 
fragments had a tail that complement the tail of one of the genomic upstream or downstream fragments. 
These complementing fragments were combined in two PCR fusions. 
The two resulting fragments were used in a transformation of a CEN.PK-102-5B, YT2 and YT3. Prior to the 
transformation YT2 and YT3 single colonies were plated on SC-5FOA and incubated for 3 days. Single colonies 
from the SC-5FOA plate was further re-streaked on SC-5FOA and incubated for 2 days. Single colonies from the 
second SC-5FOA plate were streaked on both YPD and on SC-URA to check if the looping out of the ura3 gene 
had been successful. Single colonies from the YPD plate were used in a transformation with FAS-UP-ura2 and 
FAS-DOWN-ura1. 
The resulting transformants were plated on SC-URA+0.03% palmitic acid and SC-URA-LEU+ 0.03% palmitic acid 
respectively. Successful transformants were to be further streaked on SC-URA-LEU plates without the addition 
of palmitic acid to assess if the transformants would survive.

Discussion 
Uncertainty of results  
Due to time constraints, none of the measurements were made in replicates which means that the 
reliability of the results is not very high and that a true assessment of the standard deviation cannot 
be made. This is problematic since there are several ways in which variation between samples may 
arise. These include cell culture variations, variations in the final OD600 measurements used for the 
normalization of results and differences in the final volume of chloroform extracted for evaporation in 
the fatty acid extraction protocol.  
Lipid composition of S. cerevisiae has been shown to be influenced by the growth rate of the cells as 
well as nutrient availability(Hunter & Rose, 1972; Torija et al., 2003). While the initial OD600 and 
nutrient availability was the same for each yeast transformant cultivation the very varied OD600 
measurements of Day 2 shows that the growth rate was not the same. This makes comparisons of 
fatty acid concentrations difficult between transformants as the measured concentrations would to 
some degree be affected by the growth rate differences. 
In an attempt to assess the variation between samples the OD600 normalized concentrations of 
monounsaturated palmitic and oleic acid of YT10-YT16 were compared. All of these yeast strains carry 
a FAS with the aa substitution Y2034A in the KR domain combined with one aa substitution in the KS 
domain. While these mutants do not represent replicates, the S. scrofa FAS produced by these 
mutants are likely to be equally active in terms of fatty acid production. The OD600 normalized values 
are presented in Figure 39. The standard deviation was calculated for the normalized values and was 
inserted error bars in Figure 39.  As can be seen the values are highly variable indicating that variation 
is introduced either in the cultivation process or the subsequent extraction. As stated the HPLC-MS 
results of these strains do not represent true replicates and it is possible that some variation in fatty 
acid production could somehow be introduced by the various aa substitutions in the KS domain.  
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
The high uncertainty associated with the fatty acid concentration measurements makes it difficult to 
draw any conclusions from the results. I will however in the following sections discuss the results 
obtained in relation to the expected outcome. 
Functionality of the fas-orig and fas-scopt in S. cerevisiae 
The human FAS has previously been observed to be functional in S. cerevisiae and it was therefore 
expected that the S. scrofa FAS would similarly be functional(Leber & Da Silva, 2014b). The expected 
result of the introduction of a heterologous FAS in S. cerevisiae was that the intracellular fatty acid 
concentration would increase. The HPLC-MS results shown in Figure 35 does however not show any 
significant increase in the pool of monounsaturated palmitic and oleic acid in YT2 and YT3 expressing 
fas-orig and fas-scopt respectively compared to YT1. Instead the results show that in YT2 expressing 
fas-orig the concentration of both monounsaturated palmitic and oleic acid were approximately 20% 
lower compared to YT1 while the concentrations observed in YT3 were virtually unchanged. 
An explanation for the observed results could be that the ppt-scopt, fas-orig and fas-scopt are not 
expressed or produce non-functional proteins. There are however several other reasons why the 
introduction of a heterologous FAS would not lead to a significant increase in the fatty acid 
concentration of the cell. One of the challenges of engineering S. cerevisiae for the overproduction of 
fatty acids is the low cytosolic availability of acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA as well as the cofactors 
needed for the reducing steps. This means that a significant increase in fatty acid production may not 
be achieved without increasing the availability of acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA as well as cofactors. 
Furthermore, fatty acids are as previously mentioned precursors for a large variety of compounds and 
any increase in fatty acid production may lead to a correspondingly higher fatty acid consumption for 
the production of compounds such as TAG. Alternatively, the excess fatty acids may be consumed in 
beta oxidation pathway (Sheng & Feng, 2015)(Klein et al., 2016). Finally, the activity of the 
heterologous FAS may lead to a decrease in the activity of the endogenous FAS. This could occur

Effects of point mutations introduced in the KR domain  
The KR mutations introduced in the S. scrofa FAS has previously been performed in other FASs and 
PKSs resulting in removal or significant reduction of KR activity. Based on the close structural similarity 
between the KR domain of S. scrofa FAS and these other KR domains similar results were expected 
(Reid et al., 2003; Zha et al., 2004). 
Comparison of the YT2 expressing unmutated fas-orig and the YT4 and YT5 expressing fas-orig with 
the aa substitutions K1995A and S2021A respectively showed an increase in the concentration of 
intracellular fatty acids in the KR mutants. This is surprising as the presence of point mutations in the 
KR active site should not increase the overall fatty acid production in the heterologous FAS. 
The comparison of YT3 and YT6, YT7, YT8 and YT9 shown in Figure 37 indicated that the introduced 
point mutations lead to a decrease in the intracellular concentration of monounsaturated palmitic 
acid of 24-39 %. The concentration of monounsaturated oleic acid on the other hand showed a 
decrease of between 4-11 % for YT6-8 while an increase of 14 % was observed in YT9. The observation 
of a decrease in the intracellular concentration of palmitic and oleic acid matches the hypothesis that 
these point mutations should lead to a decrease or removal of KR activity and thus decrease or 
remove overall FAS activity. It is however highly surprising that the introduction of point mutation 
should lead to a change in the overall fatty acid composition by shifting it from palmitic acid towards 
oleic acid. A possible explanation could be that the metazoan FAS has a larger propensity for the 
generation of C16 compared to the fungal counterpart. However, if this was the case then the 
resulting fatty acid composition should match that shown for YT3/YT1 in Figure 35 which it does not. 
Fatty acid chain length in the metazoan FAS is governed by the relationship between the affinities of 
the KS and TE domains towards the growing acyl-chain. It is therefore not obvious how a change in 
the KR domain should affect the length of the fatty acids produced. 
Engineering S. scrofa FAS to produce polyketides  
As stated in the results no polyketide product was detected for any of the yeast transformants. A 
possible explanation for this could be that ppt-scopt or fas-scopt are not expressed or that the gene 
product of either gene is not functional. 
However, assuming that both fas-scopt and ppt-scopt are expressed and functional gene product is 
produced in the yeast transformants there are still a number of explanations as to why the introduced 
point mutations would not result in the production of a polyketide product. 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
The introduction of aa substitutions in the KS domain may have resulted in structural changes that 
have rendered the KS domain non-functional or with a significantly reduced activity. Such effects has 
previously been observed in mutagenesis of the KR domain where the introduction of the aa 
substitution G1886F resulted in a significant decrease in overall stability (Vangipuram S. Rangan et al., 
2001). Alternatively, the introduced aa substitutions may not have achieved removal of the 
constriction. The mutagenesis of all four constricting residues was not achieved in any single fas-scopt 
version created in this project.   
As mentioned previously the selectivity of the acyl-binding tunnel of the KS domain is possibly not 
governed by the constricting residues alone. Another factor which likely influence the selectivity of 
the acyl binding tunnel is the fact that it is relatively more hydrophobic in character compared to 
corresponding acyl-binding tunnel in PKS KS domains. This means that while the aa substitutions of 
the constricting residues may successfully remove the steric hindrance, the hydrophobic character of 
the tunnel itself may strongly select for fully reduced substrates(Xu et al., 2013).  
Aside from the KS domain the TE domain may also have a specificity towards reduced substrates. TE 
activity requires binding of the acyl-chain within an acyl-binding cavity (Pemble et al., 2007). If the 
presence of keto-groups disrupts the binding in the acyl-binding cavity it would result in an inability of 
the TE domain to catalyze the release of the fatty acid or a reduced catalytic rate.  
Knock-out of the endogenous FAS 
The transformation attempts using FAS-UP-ura2 and FAS-DOWN-ura1 yielded very few transformants. 
This is likely a reflection of the more complicated transformation procedure which requires the 
introduction of both FAS-UP-ura2 and FAS-DOWN-ura1 fragments as well as homologous 
recombination events for both fragments.  
Future work and perspectives 
Functionality of fas-orig, fas-scopt, and ppt-scopt gene product 
It has within the current project not been adequately demonstrated that the ppt-scopt and fas-
orig/fas-scopt are expressed and produce functional PPT and FAS. The test of viability of the YT3 
strain transformed with FAS-UP-ura2 and FAS-DOWN-ura1 should be concluded and if the 
transformants are viable a colony PCR should be performed to confirm the knock-out of the 
endogenous FAS. Additionally, sequencing of the TEF-1 and ppt-scopt region of the pESC-LEU-ppt-
scopt should be performed to check if any mutations are present.  
In vitro experiments 
The assessment of the effect of the KR mutations on the overall fatty acid production has in this study 
been challenging and a new protocol is likely needed for the cultivation and extraction procedure to 
obtain more accurate results. Alternatively the analysis could be performed in vitro as has previously 
been done(Vangipuram S. Rangan et al., 2001) as opposed to in vivo analysis performed in this 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
project. The benefit of an in vitro analysis would be that potential effect such as degradation of excess 
fatty acids or up-regulation pathways consuming fatty acids could be removed thus providing more 
accurate results. 
Metazoan FAS as a production platform for polyketides 
The structural similarity of the metazoan FAS and modular type I polyketide synthases indicates that a 
conversion of the metazoan FAS to a polyketide production platform should be possible. There may 
however be large hurdles to overcome in the form of the specificities of the KS and TE domains 
towards fully reduced substrate. If the hydrophobic character of the KS acyl-binding tunnel presents a 
strong determinant for specificity towards fully reduced substrate, changing the specificity would 
require extensive mutagenesis. This would furthermore increase the risk of introducing mutations 
that may compromise functionality or stability of the domain.  
If the TE domain of metazoan FAS is unable to accept non-reduced substrate I may be possible to 
replace the domain altogether. In milk producing tissue of mammals a second independent TE 
enzyme known as TE2 is produced which competes with the TE domain integrated in FAS. It is 
therefore possible for an external TE enzyme to access and catalyze the release of the acyl-chain from 
metazoan FAS(Ritchie et al., 2016).

Conclusion  
The aims of the study were: to establish if the S. scrofa FAS and PPT could function within S. 
cerevisiae, to assess the effects of KR domain active site mutations and to attempt to convert the S. 
scrofa FAS into a PKS. None of these aims have been adequately achieved during this study. The lack 
of confirmation of the functionality of the S. scrofa PPT and FAS in S. cerevisiae makes it difficult to 
determine the effects of the KR and KS mutations.  
Future work should therefore include a test of viability of the YT3 strain in which a knock-out of the 
endogenous FAS has been performed. This would allow for a determination of whether, the HPLC-MS 
results obtained are the result of a non-functional PPT and FAS system, or reflects the results of the 
introduced point mutations. 
For the assessment of the effects of the introduction of point mutations in the KR domain on the 
overall FAS activity, a new strategy should be devised. The reason for this is that the method used in 
this study is very prone to the introduction of variation through differences in growth rate. 
Furthermore, using the current method, changes in fatty acid production are also likely to be difficult 
to detect, due to the mechanisms by which S. cerevisiae regulate the production of fatty acids. In vitro 
experiments using purified FAS and PPT probably presents the best option for a proper assessment.  
While it has not been possible to achieve the aims of the study, the extensive molecular biology work 
that has been performed, has resulted in the construction of several plasmids carrying various 
versions of the S. scrofa fas gene containing mutations in the KR and KS domain. These plasmids may 
serve as a starting point for further probing of the effects of KR mutations and KS specificity as well as 
the generation of a metazoan FAS platform for polyketide production. The generation of such 
platform may however present a great challenge as it might require more engineering of KS domain 
than what has been proposed in this study. Engineering of the TE domain may also be necessary in 
future studies if the conversion of the FAS to PKS is to be achieved.
