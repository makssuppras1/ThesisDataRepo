Abstract
The present thesis investigates the corrosion behavior of low-grade and high-grade steels
used in Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) applications. The experimental environment
simulated CO2-saturated H2O contaminated with HNO3. Short-term experiments, in-
cluding OCP and CPP measurements, provided insights into the behavior of each material
under different temperature and HNO3 concentration settings.
Analysis of the short-term experiments revealed that low-grade steels exhibited higher cor-
rosion rates than high-grade steels, with the presence of HNO3 endorsing the corrosion
rates. Quantitative analysis indicated that corrosion in low-grade steels worsened with
increasing temperature, while high-grade steels displayed a stable passive layer.
Long-term experiments were conducted to assess material performance over extended expo-
sure times. EIS and LPR measurements were performed, and subsequent characterization
of the samples included LEXT, SEM, EDS, and XRD analyses. The results confirmed
the rapid corrosion rates observed in the short-term analysis for low-grade steels. The
morphology and protective nature of the corrosion products varied with temperature and
time. High-grade steels exhibited minimal corrosion and consistently low corrosion rates
below 15 µm/y.
Additionally, SECM was employed to investigate localized corrosion phenomena. The find-
ings indicated uniform corrosion for 1Cr samples, while 13Cr showed preferential corrosion
sites, potentially due to pitting. SAF3207 and Sanicro 29 showed negligible corrosion, even
at a localized scale.

Introduction
Fossil fuels represent a prominent source of the world’s energy production. Nonetheless,
combustion of carbon-based fuels produces greenhouse gases having adverse effects on the
global climate. As discussed by Tanaka et al., for the abatement of climate change, around
10 Gt/year of CO2 should be transported and sequestered by 2050 [16]. In this context,
nowadays, Carbon Capture and Storage technique (CCS) is considered of paramount im-
portance to effectively cut the emission of the greenhouse gas CO2, preventing its release
into the atmosphere, while easing the recovery of hydrocarbons through the application of
enhanced oil recovery (EOR) [13],[17].
The main course of action of CCS could be summarized as follows:
• CO2 capture from different sources (eg. refineries).
• Pressurization of CO2 to a supercritical (or liquid) phase.
• Transportation and storage into deep geological layers or depleted oil and gas fields
for sequestration or for EOR [18], [19].
At the present, pipeline transportation is the primary option to transport CO2 to seques-
tration sites, extending for over 6000 km, mostly located in the U.S.A and Canada [17].
By these means, it is mandatory to consider that such structures should be constructed
and designed at an optimal cost while ensuring reliability and safety during operation, and
constituting negligible threat to the neighbouring population and environment. In fact, as
carbon steel is the main material of construction, the principal threat to pipeline integrity,
during service, is represented by internal corrosion. A large number of failures has been
attributed to corrosion in dense-phase CO2 pipelines, thus indicating the importance of
understanding material susceptibility to such environments. From the work of Johnson et
al., it is possible to discern the failure modes for CO2 pipeline systems in the U.S.A, shown
in Figure 1.1 [1].
The results from the 29 incidents, assessed from 1986 to 2008, indicate that 45% of pipeline
failures was appointed to corrosion. Nowadays, the common types of carbon steel used for

1.1
Corrosion parameters and mechanisms
Presently, most of the studies on steel corrosion in dense CO2 phase in the presence of
impurities for CCS applications are focusing on understanding significant parameters, cor-
rosion product layers, corrosion mechanisms. The internal corrosion of pipeline steel, in
dense CO2 with impurities, is onset by a variety of parameters, such as environmental,
metallurgical, and physical variables. These parameters could, on occasion, endorse syner-
gistic or competitive effects, and thereby either step up or ease up the extent of corrosion.
In fact, understanding influential parameters is fundamentally important to reveal the cor-
relations between corrosion phenomena and mechanisms, and ultimately lead to corrosion
prediction models, corrosion control strategies and pipeline integrity management [17].

1.1.1
Impurities
Understanding the action and the effect of impurities influencing pipeline integrity in CCS
systems is of paramount importance. In fact as previously discussed, corrosion would not
be of any concern in pure dense CO2 environments. The work of Cole et al. highlighted
four degrees of corrosion risk based on interaction of impurities [3]:
1. Very low contaminant levels and extremely low water content.
2. Low contaminant levels and water content below the solubility limit.
3. Low contaminant levels and water content above the solubility limit.
4. Moderate contaminant levels and water content above the solubility limit
When considering the impure dense CO2 transportation systems, it is possible to recognize
that corrosion may arise from three different modes. As schematically illustrated in Figure
1.5, CO2-saturated water, with low concentration of impurities, in the well, represents the
primary systematic mode. Secondly, under the presence of H2O impurities, water con-
densation can occur on the walls of the pipelines as highly-impure droplets. Additionally,
due to the temperature gradient between well and pipeline system [17], evaporation and
condensation of H2O droplet with moderate impurity levels could occur in proximity of
the well.

1.1.2
Temperature
The operating temperature of the pipelines carrying dense phase CO2 varies through the
transportation systems between within -56.7 to above 44 °C as the temperature is set to
optimise the mass to volume ratio of CO2 carried in dense phase, and ensure the stability
of a single phase flow [17] [29] [30]. Furthermore, temperature represents a parameter that
cab affect the corrosion behaviour by several means. In fact the temperature has a direct
influence with respect to:
• Exchange current density.
• Chemical reaction equilibrium constants.
• Chemical reaction rate constants.
• Fluid viscosity.
• Solubility of corrosion product [31].
• Morphology of corrosion product layers.
• Properties (viz. Ion conductivity) of corrosion product layers.
Particularly, as the temperature has a strong effect on the surface morphology, the shielding
provided by the eventual product layer would determine the general and localised corrosion.
From the work of Z. Wang et al., performed on steel X70 samples, under supercritical CO2
conditions, a parabolic behaviour has been observed, as shown in Figure 1.9.

In the present work, four different steel materials were investigated, namely 1Cr, 13Cr,
Sanicro 29 and SAF3207. The first two materials are low-grade martensitic steels that

2.2.1
Electrolyte
For short-term experiments, the solution consisted of an aqueous 3.5% NaCl solution, with
3 different concentrations of HNO3 impurity, namely 10, 1000 and 10000 ppm. These
impurity levels can be considered to be present at different parts of impure dense CO2
transportation systems. As discussed in Section 1.1.1.1, under the presence of H2O im-
purities, condensation may happen. Depending on which part of the pipeline and storage
system condensation happens, water may be present as highly-impure droplets, moderately
impure bulk water or slightly impure droplets. In the case that condensation happens at
barely condensing conditions along the length of the pipeline, a droplet with a high amount
of impurities will form. During these conditions, large amounts of other impurities in the
stream, such as NOx will be present in the water droplets, forming acidic compounds and
lowering the pH locally to a great extent. This condition is represented in the current
work by the addition of 1000 or 10000 ppm of HNO3 in the solution. Finally, 10 ppm is
assumed to be an approximate concentration of NOx in the well during CCS.
From a corrosion viewpoint, low pH conditions are of particular importance. Thus, a low
pH reference condition that has no impurity present was prepared by addition of HCl to
the same volume of water as in the previous case, until the pH recorded was about 2. The
Cl−were balanced by measuring the total volume of HCl added and balancing them with
the appropriate amount of NaCl, in order to maintain Cl−levels constant between the
experiments.
The total volume of solution used depended on the geometry of the electrochemical cells
used. For elevated tempereature experiments (30 and 60 °C), larger cells were used and
the volume needed was equal to about 1.5 L, whereas for low temperature experiments the
cells used were significantly smaller to acommodate for cooling, and as a result the volume
of solution needed was equal to about 400 mL.

short term experiments, the solution was deaerated for at least 12 hours by purging with

4
Conclusion and Outlook
In the present work a multitude of methods have been utilized in order to understand the
corrosion behaviour of low grade and high grade steels employed for Carbon Capture and
Storage (CCS) applications.
The materials taken under consideration consisted of 1Cr, 13Cr steel, with martensitic
crystal structure, SAF3207, with duplex crystal structure, and Sanicro 29, as an austenitic
steel. The simulated environment consisted of CO2-saturated H2O with HNO3 as con-
taminant.
At an initial stage, short-term experiments have been performed. These consisted of OCP
and CPP measurements, and they intended to give insights, at a relatively fast pace, regard-
ing the behaviour of each material at different settings. The conditions under investigation
spanned within a temperature range between 5 to 60 °C and a HNO3 concentration range
between 10 to 10000 ppm.
This set of experiments, has been analyzed and proper characteristic outputs were com-
puted. Those revealed that in each case low grade steels were greatly influenced by the
simulated environment, displaying high corrosion rates, when compared to the high grade
specimens.
A reference condition has been employed to assess the effective influence of HNO3 in the
3.5% NaCl electrolyte. In this context, the presence of HNO3 in the solution has been
found to endorse the corrosion rates. A quantitative analysis has been performed to better
understand the significance and influence of the selected variables over the chosen outputs,
namely CR and Ecorr. The analysis disclosed that, as the temperature raises the corrosion
is aggravated for low grade steels. On the other hand, high-grade steels exhibited much
less significant CR, indicating the effective stability of the passive layer in the studied con-
ditions.
In order to better grasp the performances of the materials under investigation at longer
exposure times to the aforementioned environment, long-term experiments have been car-
ried out.
This set of experiments consisted of EIS and LPR measurements for 3 days of exposure and

an equivalent circuit has been proposed in each case. Subsequent characterization of the
received samples has thereby been carried out initially through LEXT technique in order to
assess the morphology of the surface after testing. Afterwards, SEM and EDS analysis was
performed and the formed corrosion products were investigated. Finally, to complete the
characterization an analysis of the phases present on the surface was conducted through
XRD technique.
The data received was examined and subsequently qualitative and quantitative findings
have been discussed. For low-grade steels, rapid corrosion rates were initially observed.
The morphology and protective nature of the corrosion product formed varied depending
on the experimental temperature.
Low-grade steels, confirming the short-term analysis presented high corrosion rates. In
addition to verifying the short-term analysis, longer exposure times revealed a time and
temperature dependent behaviour of the computed CR. This fact stemmed from the dif-
ference in corrosion mechanisms and byproducts.
While the 1Cr surface seemed to form a uniform corrosion product layer regardless of test-
ing temperature, 13Cr due to the initial presence of a passive layer, exhibited pitting at 5
and 30 °C. At 60 °C the morphology of the product layers on both 1Cr and 13Cr was very
similar, however the protectiveness of the layer formed on 13Cr seemed to be much higher,
potentially due to the increased Cr content.
After the long-term experiments were performed and characterization of the high-grade
steels was carried out, no noteworthy corrosion products were identified. Moreover, the
calculated corrosion rate of these specimens was consistently below 15 µm/y
In a similar fashion to the short-term experiments, a subsequent analysis of the results was
performed. The findings are in accordance to those received from the short-term analysis,
inasmuch low-grade steels presented a significant CR temperature dependence, while high-
grade steels were relatively unaffected. The analysis of the data through a least squares
method allowed for the extraction of a prediction expression for the CR of steels in these
conditions.
Finally, to investigate potential local corrosion phenomena, SECM was employed with a
less aggresssive electrolyte.
The findings suggest that uniform corrosion was occurring
for 1Cr samples, while for 13Cr preferential corrosion sites were noticed, possibly due to

pitting. According to the previous set of experiments, SAF3207 and Sanicro 29 did not
exhibit significant corrosion, even at a localized scale.
Moving onwards, LEXT characterization of the samples received after the short-term ex-
periments could possibly bring some interesting insights. This would allow for a prompt
understanding of the corrosion mechanism and a wider range of conditions could be stud-
ied. In this context, the synergistic effect of the addition of further contaminants in the
electrolyte could better grasp the performance of these materials at service conditions.
As far as long-term experiments are concerned, longer exposure times and different con-
taminant concentrations could be employed, especially in the case of high-grade steels.
Additional characterization methods, such as TEM together with XPS, could be utilized
in order to get an understanding of the dimensions and chemistry of the formed corrosion
layer, as well as its morphology.
To conclude, the described experiments performed in the present work should be addition-
ally carried out with supercritical CO2 as a working environment, which would allow for a
robust understanding of the behaviour of these materials under actual service conditions,
spanning from injection sites to storage wells.
