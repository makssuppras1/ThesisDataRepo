Abstract
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disease which aﬀect 33% of the global popu-
lation over the age of 85. The cause and mechanics of the disease remain relatively unknown,
though consensus models have been proposed. As we need to deepen our understanding of
AD as well as ﬁnd new methods of drug screening, new models have recently been proposed
which involved the diﬀerentiation of human induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) into neu-
rons. A new and fast approach to this is a cell line containing a cassette which induced with
doxycycline causes overexpression of Neurogenin2 (NGN2), a transcription factor involved in
neurogenesis.
Four diﬀerent experimental setups were performed for neural induction. These were induction
of the cell line’s NGN2-cassette via doxycycline for six and nine days, respectively. Further,
the same setup was performed with the addition of NIM medium for simultaneous Dual in-
hibition of SMAD. Samples from the ﬁnal day of diﬀerentiation were compared to expression
of four already established neural diﬀerentiation protocols (Dual SMAD without NGN2).
All eight diﬀerentiation setups also underwent immunocytochemistry for the detection of the
early cortical neural marker, TBR1.
It was found that there was much variation within the experimental replicates themselves,
and steps will have to be made to stabilise the expression proﬁle and make it consistent. Of
tendencies, it was shown there was an expression of neural progenitor markers PAX6 and
SOX1, which disputes the claim that the NPC step is bypassed. However, the expression
of NPC markers was more elevated when combining the NGN2-overexpression with Dual
SMAD inhibition, as expected. The level of neural progenitor marker expression was still
lower than expression of the tissues generated by Dual SMAD inhibition plus growth factors.
It was also indicated that a combined NGN2-overexpression and Dual SMAD inhibition for
six days proved the most forebrain-positive markers wheras NGN2-overexpression alone has a
higher expression of FOXG1 compared to NGN2-overexpression and Dual SMAD inhibition
combined. Immunocytochemical staining further conﬁrmed the presence of cortical neurons
in the NGN2-overexpressed ﬁxed tissues.

Introduction
When it comes to expanding our comprehension of human neural diseases and neural tissue
mechanisms, there is yet much to be found. The human brain has been less inaccessible for in
vivo studies. Therefore, neural tissue is mostly available post-mortem or through use of scans.
Other means of studying neural disease aetiology have also been employed. An example is
the use of non-human disease models like murine in vitro and in vivo models. However, these
pose certain issues, not only due to physiological diﬀerences, but also pathology. For instance,
rodents do not develop many of the disorders of the central nervous system (CNS) naturally
which we use them as models for [9]. McComish and Caldwell, 2018, stress the need for a
human neural model in order to create a more realistic representation of the human brain
for better development of suitable therapies and therapeutics [47].
Enter stem cells; the primordial cells from which our entire beings originate from. These
cells are self-renewable by deﬁnition, and capable of diﬀerentiating into any cell type. Via
induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), the recent means of obtaining viable cells, we are now
capable of assimilating organ and tissue mechanisms like never before. This has deepened
our understanding of many diseases and organ tissue development thus far. It continues to
do so, namely in this project.
5.1
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of the project is to develop a protocol for neural tissue which assimilates areas
relevant to areas aﬀected by Alzheimer’s Disease (AD). AD is responsible for the major-
ity of dementia-related diseases and aﬀects an estimated 33% of the global populace. The
model should assimilate the desired region within an accelerated amount of time compared
to pre-existing models, in order to accommodate cost-eﬀectiveness if adapted to industrial
applications.
A successful model would be able to contribute in two major aspects: the model will aid in
the study of disease aetiology, providing a further understanding of AD. Further, AD-positive
tissues can be used for pharmaceutical drug screening purposes to develop new medications
to combat the disease.

5.2
Overall Aims of the Project
The project will not include any putative AD-related pathomechanisms nor does it aim to
generate AD-tissue. Rather, the main objective is to direct diﬀerentiation and establish a
stable diﬀerentiation protocol prior to proceeding with cell lines which contain genes related
to AD. Thus, the following aims of the project are deﬁned as:
1. To optimise the ngn2-induction diﬀerentiation protocol in order to elicit heterogeneous
neuronal populations. This is performed using the isogenic stem cell line, BIONi10-C-15. It
has an incorporated doxycycline-induced cassette containing the ngn2-gene, which is essential
for the generation of mature cells. By inducing the cassette for nine days, we expect a ho-
mogenous population of glutamatergic dorsal neurons from the ngn2-overexpression. Thus,
by accelerating the induction period and/or adding certain inhibitors, we aim to obtain neu-
ral tissue containing a more heterogenous population and thus a more representative neural
tissue.
2. To characterise the various ngn2-cassette facilitated diﬀerentiation by comparisons with
tissues generated via Dual SMAD inhibition protocols. Neural tissue can already be obtained
by the dual inhibition of SMAD (Section 6.2.4), which commits pluripotent cells to a neural
fate. Adding certain growth factors or inhibitors directs the tissue from dorsal to ventral,
rostral or caudal neural tissue (Section 6.3). However, tissue generation by Dual SMAD inhi-
bition takes two to three months, and is therefore more time consuming and costly in reagents
in comparison to neural diﬀerentiation via ngn2-overexpression. By creating heterogenous
neural tissue via ngn2-overexpression, neural tissue can be obtained at an accelerated rate.
The nature of the tissue generated in this project can then be characterised by comparisons
with tissues generated via Dual SMAD inhibition.
The following Background section will elaborate on the relevant theory and discoveries in the
ﬁeld to deepen the understanding of the experimental work performed in the project. The
Experimental Strategy section will elaborate on the project aims and go into detail on the
experimental aspects, followed by the Materials and Methods section, which describes the
experimental procedures. Following the description of the experimental procedures, relevant
results will subsequently be presented and discussed. Lastly, there will be a discussion on
the ﬁndings with an overall conclusion, as well as suggestions to future prospects.

form has a late onset, meaning it typically manifests after the age of 65, whereas the familial
form has a much earlier onset. SAD makes up the majority of the cases of AD [27]. No cure

others [2]. It is important to stress that these mechanisms mentioned are consensus models.
However, some researchers are beginning to dispute these mechanisms, proposing they are
observable consequences of AD rather than causes [44, 36, 37, 18]. To elucidate the true
components and causes, new models need to take form to further our understanding of the
underlying mechanisms and subsequently new therapies.

6.2.1
Early Development of the Central Nervous System
During the third week of gestation, the embryo develops to consist of three germ layers; the
endoderm, mesoderm and ectoderm layers. The CNS is formed from ectodermal cells. A
groove forms in the ectoderm, termed the primitive streak or primitive groove, which ulti-
mately forms the CNS [46, 59].
First, mesodermal cells situated immediately under the primitive streak diﬀerentiate to be-
come notochords, see Figure 3. These cells induce change in the ectoderma, forming the
neural plate. The neural plate thickens, and the cells fold and fuse to form the neural tube,
which is embedded in the mesoderm [46, 16]. The tube can be divided into sections, which
later develop into diﬀerent sections of the CNS, such as the midbrain, the medulla etc. The
neural tube contains neural progenitor cells (NPCs), which diﬀerentiate into neuroblasts to
ultimately become neurons, see Figure 4 [46].

6.2.2
Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSC)
With the discovery of somatic reprogramming in 2006 by Yamanaka’s group, the dawn of
this entirely novel ﬁeld has lead to new discoveries [62], also within the neural ﬁeld. By in-
ducing somatic cells back to pluripotency, not only can neural tissues be obtained by relevant
induction pathways; neural genetic disease etiology by patient-derived somatic cells can also
be emulated [62, 20, 47].
Harvested from patients themselves, skin ﬁbroblasts or other cell types are induced to be-
coming pluripotent stem cells (hence, iPSC). This induction can be performed by lentiviral
transfection or via plasmids [63, 71]. iPSCs have opened up for novel discoveries. By diﬀer-
entiating these novel iPSC, genetic diseases can be modeled and examined in vitro. This is
one of the advantages of deriving cells from patiens as opposed to the previous method of
harvesting embryonic stem cells (ESC). Obtaining a suﬃcient number of ESC for research
purposes has long been a bottleneck [25]. Furthermore, the ethics concerning derriving stem
cells from embryos are circumvented by harvesting from consenting donors [47].
The pathomechanisms of genetic disease can be determined and novel diagnostic tools, (per-
sonal) therapies and treatments created by developing assays for the purpose. Tissue from
healthy donors can be utilised for the better understanding of tissues and organs, for the de-
velopment of assays, and for the introduction of putative disease genes to conﬁrm a pathogenic
phenotype. Diﬀerent possibilities are listed in Figure 5. [Reference]
Examples of current research are tissue grafts for transplants, for instance Nolbrant et

6.2.3
Organoids
Organoids are 3D-tissues with an organ-like structure [49, 31, 33]. Many of the protocols
developed involved the pluripotent cells’ intrinsic ability to self-assemble into structures with-
out the involvement of any external growth factors and similar molecules other than what
they produce themselves [66, 33, 35]. Organoids have been developed for the intestine, brain,
liver and kidneys, to name a few.[32, 35]. Liver organoids are recognised for their protential
for drug testing due to its metabolic capabilities [32, 35].
Lancaster and Knoblich, 2014, suggest the following three criterias for organoids[31]:
• They contain a heterogenous population pertaining to the organ in question.
• They express functions to that of the speciﬁc organ.
12
==PAGE:19==
Bioneer
• They should be organised similarly to the organ.
Thus, neural organoids assimilate several and speciﬁc areas of the brain at once. Several
organoids have been described, such as cortical spheroids [54], forebrain organoids [23] and
cerebral organoids [34]. Though even organoids are prone to variability, incomplete diﬀer-
entiation and randomization of the nature of the tissue [35, 69, 22]. For organoids such as
neural organoids, the structure of a particular area of the brain is not formed; it is still only
an assimilation of certain brain tissues.[31]
6.2.4
Neural Diﬀerentiation via Dual Inhibition of SMAD
Three main methods exist for neuronal diﬀerentiation; feeder-based diﬀerentiation, embryoid
body diﬀerentiation and Dual SMAD inhibition of cell monolayers, the latter of which will
be described in this section [25, 6, 70, 24].
This method of obtaining high-purity neurons was developed by Chambers et al., 2009 [6].
For the protocol, the SMAD signal was inhibited by two inhibitors, Noggin and SB431542,
in order to commit the pluripotent cells to a neural fate. The induction initially produces
neural progenitor cells (NPCs). NPCs are intermediates between pluripotent cells and mature
neural cells such as astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, neural crest cells and diﬀerent types of
mature neurons [68, 6]. Pax6, an early marker for neuroectodermal diﬀerentiation [5], was
observed when Dual SMAD inhibition was performed. This marker, along with an early
detection of SOX1 neuralectodermal marker expression, conﬁrmed the strong bias towards a
neuroprogenital fate [6]. In addition to the neural fate markers, Chambers et al. also found a
decrease in the endodermal marker SOX17 as well as the mesodermal marker Brachyury [6].
A loss of the pluripotency markers Oct4 and Nanog indicated the cell line had diﬀerentiated
and lost their pluripotency.
Following Dual inhibition, the rapidly produced NPCs can
subsequently be induced to mature into neurons. These can be obtained within approximately
two to three months [51, 45, 58, 12]. See methods Section 9.2.9 for the Dual SMAD inhibition
and subsequent neural maturation protocol.

Directing Neural Tissue
In this study, several growth factors were utilised in order to direct neuronal diﬀerentia-
tion to assimilate speciﬁc areas of the brain. To direct diﬀerentiation from dorsal cortical
glutamatergic neurons, previous studies have elucidated the eﬀect of adding speciﬁc growth

In order to obtain mesencephalic neurons, previous studies have shown using Sonic Hedge-

proliferation and the hinderance thereof when needed, as well as asymetric growth [64].

agonist was more eﬃcient [8, 13, 28]. Thus, in this study, the SHH protein Smoothened
Agonist (SAG) is used for ventralising the neural tissue. SAG activates Smoothened (SMO),
which is located on the SHH signalling pathway [14]. Further, the inhibition of glycogen

7.1
RT-qPCR
qPCR is a real-time quantitative PCR with the sample often being the transcriptome con-
verted to complementary DNA (cDNA) using reverse transcriptase (RT). It is a means of
looking into particular parts of a transcriptome, both in terms of seeking a particular strand
of RNA, but also in comparing the transcription quantitatively to that of housekeeping genes.
Housekeeping genes are genes which are typically expressed for maintenance proteins, and

==PAGE:23==
Bioneer
is exprected to be expressed constitutively. By substracting the expression of housekeeping
genes from the expression of the gene of interest, a baseline can be made. Gene expression is
of particular interest when it comes to neuronal diﬀerentiation using diﬀerent growth factors,
which was performed in the project. Should we see a diﬀerent expression patterning com-
pared to the regular protocol producing cortical glutaminergic neurons, then it could indicate
the diﬀerentiated cells may be of a diﬀerent nature.
Contrary to regular PCR, qPCR involved the detection of ﬂourescence, see Figure ?? for a
visual representation. The ﬂourescence levels rise with the almost doubling of amplicons per
cycles, which is reached after a few duplication cycles. This continues until the ﬂourescence
detected come to a plateau when the reaction components has been used up. More important
than the duplication when looking into trancriptome expression, is the quantiﬁcation of the
relative changes in the gene expression. One method presented by Livak et al., 2001, is the
2−∆∆Cq Method, which will be described below [41].
7.1.1
The Fold Analysis Method)
The Fold Analysis (2−∆∆Cq Method) is a relative quantiﬁcation method measuring the dif-
ference of expression compared to a calibrator sample [41]. This calibrator can be set as a
control sample, or in the case of samples taken over time, the fold-change of gene expression
compared to a speciﬁc time. Rather than determining the absolute quantiﬁcation by assess-
ing the exact copy number, the aim of this analysis is to compare the relative expression of
a gene in question compared to the calibrator [41].
Cq-values for target genes are normalised (∆CqSample) by substracting the diﬀerences in the
Cq-values of the generated reference gene(s). The ∆Cq-values of a sample is then substracted
the normalised value of the calibrator (∆CqCal):
∆CqSample −∆CqCal = ∆∆Cq
(1)
The fold change is ﬁnally calculated by putting 2 to the power of −∆∆Cq:
Fold change = 2−∆∆Cq
(2)

Immunocytochemistry (ICC) can be utilised as a means of qualitatively visualising the distri-

of already established works by Prehn, 2017 and Hoﬀmeyer, 2017 [56, 21], and uses the
isogenic iPS cell line generated from these prospects; BIONi-10-C-15. The cell line contains

9.2.2
iPSC cell line propagation using 0.2 % EDTA in DPBS
Reaching the desired conﬂuency, cells were propagated into new wells using a 0.2% EDTA so-
lution (Sigma) in DPBS (Lonza). The following describes the procedure for cell propagation,
with steps including cell counting for seeding speciﬁc number of cells.
1. Media in plates was aspirated and the plates washed with DPBS (Lonza).
2. A 0.2% solution of EDTA in DPBS was then dispensed in the plate for up to 5 minutes
at 20°C until cell detachment was observed under the microscope.
3. The EDTA solution was then aspirated carefully and the cells washed from the plates
using the relevant growth media.
4. For a speciﬁc number of cells needed, the cells were collected in a falcon tube.
A
100µL was taken and the cell nuclei counted to assess the amount of living cells using
NucleoCounter (Chemometec).
5. The falcon tube was spun down at 120 RPM for 5 minutes and the supernatant aspirated
carefully.
6. Cells were resuspended in growth media.
7. Cells were gently seeded onto new matrigel-coated plates.

iPSC cell line propagation using 0.2 Accutase and Revitacell
Propagating cells using Accutase is less damaging for the cells compared to detaching cells
using EDTA. Thus, cells sensitive to detachment were propagated using the following proce-

Software analysis:
1. Any deviations detected by the LightCycler® software which is deemed void (e.g. cur-
vature, diﬀerences in replicates) is removed from the dataset.
2. Means of Cq valiues and their error rates for technical replicates is calculated.

RNA extraction and puriﬁcation for quantiﬁcation by RT-

The quantiﬁcation of the cell expression proﬁles at diﬀering times and using diﬀering dif-
ferentiation methods constitute the foundation of this project. Thus, it was important to
consider the handling of the RNA samples extracted for this purpose. The checklist of the
Minimum Information for Publication of Quntitative Real-Time PCR Experiments (MIQE)
was therefore taken into consideration when performing steps prior to and during qPCR cy-
cling.
For the experiments performed, RNA was sampled over time. After RNA extraction, the
sample was kept at -80 °C until the conclusion of the experiments. The samples taken over
time were then thawed on ice and puriﬁed and diluted simultaneously to minimise variation.
The degradation of RNA over time during storage was taken into consideration. However,
the storage time was considered a lesser factor for causing variation than grouping the experi-
mental sample and running qPCR quantiﬁcation simultaneously. When preparing the qPCR
plate, the reagents were kept separate from any potential template source to prevent con-

==PAGE:47==
Bioneer
tamination, and the RNA samples were added last. Replicates were pipetted simultaneously
using an automatic multi-channel pipette to minimise error. Once sealed, the 384-well plate
was spun down to ensure all reagents were situated at the bottom of each well. The plate was
immediately transferred to the LightCycler® 480 II qPCR instrument from the centrifuge
without delay, and the qPCR cycling programme was commenced immediately thereafter.

Discussion
This section will elaborate on the results presented in this work to consider their validity.
Comparisons to other studies will be presented here.
11.1
Neural diﬀerentiation
In this study, the main objective was to optimise a neural diﬀerentiation protocol involving
the overexpression of NGN2. This was in order to create a model containing a heterogenic
neural population for studying the disease aetiology of AD, as well as a clinical in vitro assay
for drug screening. Four diﬀerent experimental setups were undertaken and characterised.
The doxycycline induced NGN2-GFP cassette [65] in the cell line BIONi10-c-15 lays the
foundation of the experimental work. Samples are taken over time for the duration of the
neural diﬀerentiation performed.
For the observations to hold validity, the experimental setups must be repeated to ensure
consistency within replicates. Once expression was quantiﬁed in this project, however, it
was clear the experimental replicates were diﬀerent in expression proﬁle. This is the main
challenge which needs to be addressed ﬁrst. A consistent expression proﬁle will need to be
created. Ho et al., 2016, uses NPCs for their NGN2-induction protocol, whereas iPSCs are
used in this study. The argument for using NPCs is their readiness for their adaptation to
high-throughput screens, both for drugs and phenotypes. Further, NPCs proliferate robustly
and the frequency of passaging and feeding is lesser compared with iPSCs [20].
In addition to this, what Lancaster et al., 2017, found, was that when seeding less cells in
Matrigel, the variation was diminished, as well as non-neuronal tissue limited. A means of
streamlining neural diﬀerentiation and its gene expression could be to reduce the number
of cells in the dox-induction protocol in order to improve the surface area to volume ratio.
Though if iPSCs are seeded into single cells, they may die instead of proliferating [35]
The diﬀerentiation protocol is only complete once the cell line used is replaced by one con-
taining genes which are considered to be involved in AD. Most easily will be genes involved in
FAD, as these are more well-characterised. Once neural tissue can be generated with the new
cell line, the task of establishing assays for AD detection and drug eﬃciency will commence.

11.2
qPCR and alternative quantiﬁcations
In this study, 12 target genes were chosen due to their relevancy when it comes to speciﬁc cell
markers. However, as thousands of genes are expressed simultaneously. Thus, the sequencing
of the entire transcriptome could be a more accurate means of comparing transcriptome pat-
terning of the NGN2-overexpression protocols with the diﬀerent Dual SMAD diﬀerentiation
protocols available.
More controls will have to be implemented. For qPCR, a series of template dilutions would
be ideally be performed to ensure that each cycle will provide a consistent doubling of
amplicons for each cycle. The reference genes used are GAPDH and HPRT1. These are
chosen for their stability and application as reference genes in various studies, as well as them
being the standardised reference genes in-house. In order to ensure a consistent expression
of the two, their Cq values were compared and found to be valid (data not shown). Had
one of the reference gene quantiﬁcations proven unstable, it would have been removed as
a reference gene and possibly replaced by a more stable one. In addition to this, the only
gene marker chosen as a control marker for qPCR quantiﬁcation was GABRA2.
As the
diﬀerentiated neurons were expected to be glutamatertic, it was also expected there would
be no expression of the opposing GABAergic neurons and therefore no GABRA2. However,
we have not ensured that the iPSCs were committed to an entirely neural fate. Therefore,
markers for mesodermal and endodermal diﬀerentiation should have been quantiﬁed as well.
The quantiﬁcation of the samples was performed in duplicates in order to detect technical
variations, while experimental variation was accounted for by performing the protocol in
triplicates.
The genetic markers quantiﬁed via qPCR represent only a fraction of the transcriptome
expressed by cells. While they are indeed some of the more relevant indicators for cell types,
comparisons between the dual SMAD inhibition protocol and the accelerated protocol pre-
sented in this study are more readily comparable when sequencing the entire transcriptome.
By using ventralised, rostralised, caudalised and dorsal tissue derived from dual SMAD inhi-
bition and the relevant growth factors, the four transcriptional proﬁles can work as reference
points to other neural tissue transcriptional proﬁles to determine an approximation of the
neural regions they assimilate. This can be represented on a coordinate system.

For this project, RT-qPCR was performed. This was done using the one-step method, mean-
ing the reverse transcriptase used to convert our transcriptome to cDNA was added during
qPCR ampliﬁcation. This is opposed to the two-step method, where mRNA is converted to
cDNA and then subsequently undergoes ampliﬁcation. The one-step method is ideal for a
fast handling and less chance of pipetting errors. However, though the same concentration
of sample RNA may be applied to each well, how can we ensure that the concentration of
cDNA remains the same? Further, the stability of cDNA when compared to RNA is higher.
In the end, it was concluded that the errors which could potentially occur when handling
exceeded that of template stability.
11.3
Immmunocytochemical Stanining
ICC was performed onto one genetic marker. Preferably, more should have been implemented,
but were not due to time limitations. Ideally, the targets used in ICC would also have be the
same as the markers used in qPCR. This provides the optimal comparisons. For example,
as NPC markers were present in the qPCR simultaneously with the observation of TBR1-
positive cells in the ICC, it would have been of interest to stain cells with either PAX6 or
SOX1 for visual conﬁrmation. As of the current ﬁndings, it is not conﬁrmed whether the
same cells show characteristics of expressing both NPC-related markers along with more
mature markers due to the overexpression of Ngn2, or if the cells are devoid of any NPC-
marker before expressing mature or cortical markers. When it comes to performing ICC,
more controls will have to be implemented. This involves a negative control for ICC, where
primary antibodies are not added to the well to assess the extent of nonspeciﬁc binding. The
ICC would then have been more valid. Furthermore, replicates would have been desirable
to qualitatively conﬁrm the expression of the markers stained for. With just one well, the
observations are only putative at best and will have to be repeated.
11.4
Modelling for disease models
In this study, the diﬀerentiation of neurons was performed. However, no glial expression
was detected. As astrocytes are involved in AD as the main contributor to ApolipoproteinE

these speciﬁc cells for an optimal disease model [60]. Furthermore, in order to understand
disease aetiology, conditions should ideally assimilate those of true in vivo conditions. If this
is indeed the case, then perhaps organoids is the means to a more profound understanding
of AD. Not only does it assimilate the brain in a 3D environment. It is also capable of
expressing several diﬀerent neural tissues at once.
11.5
Modelling for clinical assays
As mentioned, a means of optimising the model for a potentially more stable expression
proﬁle could be to induce the Ngn2-cassette in NPCs instead of iPSCs. While using NPCs
will elongate the diﬀerentiation process, it would still be faster than dual SMAD inhibition.
It is important to keep in mind the challenges of the blood-brain barrier, which the drug still
needs to traverse in order to have an eﬀect. Integrating this into the model developed for a
clinical assay may prove diﬃcult, and could perhaps be an entirely diﬀerent assay
Should a new human cell-derived AD assays be developed, it does not necessarily have to
replace already established assays entirely. Rather, they could complement one another, with
a crude screening being performed ﬁrst, and the more promising drug candidates then taken
into in vitro testing by human models. This is especially more attractive should a human
model still be costly to initially implement.

Conclusion
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disease which causes cognitive degeneration
such as memory loss and dementia. It is the most prevalent type of dementia and aﬀects
33% of the global population over the age of 85. The cause and mechanics of the disease
remain relatively unknown, though consensus models have been proposed. As we need to
deepen our understanding of AD as well as ﬁnd new methods of drug screening, new models
have recently been proposed which involved the diﬀerentiation of human induced pluripotent
stem cells (iPSCs) into neurons. A new and fast approach to this is a cell line containing
a cassette which induced with doxycycline causes overexpression of Neurogenin2 (NGN2), a
transcription factor involved in neurogenesis.
Four diﬀerent experimental setups were performed for neural induction. These were induction
of the cell line’s NGN2-cassette via doxycycline for six and nine days, respectively. Further,
the same setup was performed with the addition of NIM medium for simultaneous Dual in-
hibition of SMAD. The setups were performed three times in independent triplicates, and
samples were taken over the course of 21 days for quantiﬁcation of the expression of 12 genetic
markers, with GAPDH and HPRT1 as reference gene markers. Samples from the ﬁnal day
were also compared to expression of already established neural diﬀerentiation protocols in-
volving dual SMAD inhibition and three growth factors, which created dorsal, rostral, caudal
and ventral-like tissues of the brain. All eight diﬀerentiation setups were also and ﬁxated,
and cells were stained for the detection of the early cortical neural marker, TBR1.
It was found that for all variations of the experimental setups, the cells leave their pluripo-
tency states to commit to a neural fate, as expected. All setups showed expression of neural
progenitor markers PAX6 and SOX1, which disputes the claim that the NPC step is by-
passed. However, the expression of NPC markers was more elevated when combining the
ngn2-overexpression with dual SMAD inhibition, as expected. The mature neural marker,
MAP2, indicated the cells diﬀerentiated via ngn2-overexpression had not reached maturity
while a combined ngn2-overexpression and dual SMAD inhibition showed a rise in MAP2-
positive cells. No generation of glial markers and GABAergic neurons were detected, and
markers for neurotransmitters were inconclusive. Based on the observations, it was indicated
that a combined ngn2-overexpression and dual SMAD inhibition for six days proved the

most forebrain-positive markers, wheras ngn2-overexpression alone has a higher expression
of FOXG1 compared to ngn2-overexpression and dual SMAD inhibition combined.

Future Perspectives
Among a repetition to ensure the observations in this study can be valid, there are other
things to take into consideration. When it comes to future perspectives, one must consider
the diﬀerent levels in which to operate. Among them are:
• Consistency of neural diﬀerentiation
• Characterisation of diﬀerentiated tissue
• Eﬀectiveness as a model for AD and AD drug screening
13.1
Consistency of neural diﬀerentiation
As already mentioned in the discussion section, NPC cells as the starting point for diﬀeren-
tiation to replace iPSC would be the ﬁrst thing to implement for better stability.
13.2
Characterisation of diﬀerentiated tissue
Assuming dual SMAD inhibition generate tissues which have the same expression proﬁle
consistently, one can sequence tissue generated by more eﬀective means for transcriptomic
comparisons. By already having four reference points in the form of dorsal, ventral, rostral
and caudal-like tissue, a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) could scale the variables to
establish the nature of the newly generated tissue.
13.3
Eﬀectiveness as a model for AD and AD drug screening
For a screening model, components and consumables which do not fall under GMP and
similar standards, such as many xeno-derived products, will have to be replacesed by some
that are.
