Abstract
The built environment plays a vital role in the green transition and the EU’s objectives for a
net-zero future. Waste management is one of the phases in the construction process that has
the potential to make a positive contribution. Various opportunities and improvements can
be introduced to streamline the transition process and make it quicker and more efficient.
One of these is to use take-back systems at construction sites to promote a more circular
economy. This thesis focuses on minimising waste and aims to identify the different fac-
tors surrounding take-back systems and their potential influence on the Danish industry,
offering an optimised solution for their implementation. The project utilises quantitative
data collected in collaboration with the company NCC, as well as qualitative data from
interviews with various stakeholders and a literature review. It focuses on wood products
used temporarily at construction sites and the impacts that different end-of-life scenarios
for them can have. Supported by a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and Material Flow Anal-
ysis (MFA), a comprehensive analysis combined with the qualitative data is conducted to
identify the key factors concerning the life cycle of the selected products in the relevant
context. The results indicate that implementing take-back systems for construction waste,
particularly wood, can yield significant environmental benefits, including improved mate-
rial circularity and reduced ecological footprints. Interviews with industry professionals
highlighted the crucial role of stakeholder collaboration and the necessity for clearer def-
initions to overcome current barriers. By synthesising these quantitative and qualitative
findings, this thesis proposes an evidence-based framework. This framework acts as a
practical tool to encourage widespread adoption of take-back systems, ultimately optimis-
ing waste management practices and pushing the built environment in Denmark towards
a more sustainable and net-zero future.

Resumé
Det byggede miljø spiller en afgørende rolle i den grønne omstilling og EU’s mål om en
fremtid med netto-nul-udledning. Affaldshåndtering er en af de faser i byggeprocessen,
der har potentiale til at yde et positivt bidrag. Der kan indføres forskellige muligheder
og forbedringer for at strømline omstillingsprocessen og gøre den hurtigere og mere ef-
fektiv. En af disse forbedringer er at anvende take-back ordninger på byggepladser, for
at fremme en mere cirkulær økonomi. Denne afhandling fokuserer på at minimere af-
fald og har til formål at identificere de forskellige faktorer omkring take-back ordninger
og deres potentielle indflydelse på den danske industri og tilbyde en optimeret løsning
til deres implementering. Projektet anvender kvantitative data, indsamlet i samarbejde
med virksomheden NCC, samt(remove ’samt’) kvalitative data fra interviews med forskel-
lige interessenter og en litteraturgennemgang. Projektet fokuserer på træprodukter, der
bruges midlertidigt på byggepladser, og de virkninger, som(remove ’, som’) forskellige
levetidsscenarier kan have. Støttet af en livscyklusvurdering (LCA) og materialestrøm-
sanalyse (MFA) gennemføres en omfattende analyse kombineret med de kvalitative data

en bred anvendelse af take-back ordninger, hvilket i sidste ende optimerer affaldshåndter-
ingspraksis og skubber det byggede miljø i Danmark i retning af en mere bæredygtig og
netto-nul-fremtid.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The built environment is present in most human activities, significantly impacting economic,
environmental, and social aspects. For the environment, substantial negative contributions are
being made by the release of greenhouse gases (GHG) and the use of natural resources in the
construction sector [1]. According to the European Green Deal, to meet current demands us-
ing current practices, resources equivalent to three Earths would be necessary by 2050. Thus,
creating innovative systems that improve the current situation is a priority, as demonstrated in
the recent formulation of different European laws regarding better practices [2]. In response to
these critical challenges, the Circular Economy (CE) emerges as a promising tool. It is one of the
key strategies that aims to reduce or eliminate waste and minimise the use of virgin materials,
maximising processes such as reuse, maintenance, and recycling [3]. Implementing these CE
practices in the construction sector would significantly reduce environmental impact, resulting
in positive reductions in GHG emissions and yielding economic savings [4].
1.2
Rationale
To practically implement CE principles in the built environment, various systems are explored.
Among these, Reverse Logistics (RL) frameworks, which encompass strategies like take-back
systems, play a significant role in this context, as they enable the evaluation of strategies, motiva-
tions, and stakeholders involved, providing a comprehensive view of the system’s dynamics [5].
The RL frameworks can be applied to different types of products; it is necessary to know and
understand the characteristics of the products [5] and the requirements for their use to get finally
to the opportunities of how to put it back into a circular life cycle [6]. Moreover, in the context
of Denmark, it is possible to find company reports that also address the topic, highlighting its
current relevance and potential impact in the near future. One example is the Rambøll [7], which
connects take-back systems to circular practices, current legislation, and building certifications.
Another relevant report is the Ragn-Sells [8], which provides a summary of various events in
Denmark, Sweden, and Norway where different stakeholders shared ideas and concerns about
implementing more circular activities in construction. These reports are focused on construction
materials in general. However, as mentioned before, when implementing a system, it is impor-
tant to analyse individual elements, in this case, materials, and their characteristics. Drawing
on various studies that have evaluated different amounts and impacts of primary materials in the
building process, as well as their relationship with circularity [9], and data provided by NCC re-
garding the relationships between materials and waste, this thesis will specifically focus on wood
products to optimize waste management through the implementation of take-back systems
2
==PAGE:14==
1.3
Project statement
This thesis aims to determine the necessary factors and stakeholders to optimise waste manage-
ment within a circular economy framework by implementing take-back systems. The study will
be conducted from the perspective of a Danish construction company, NCC (Nordic Construc-
tion Company), addressing the following questions:
• What are the methods of handling waste in Denmark used by NCC?
• How can recyclability and reusability indicators contribute to waste management deci-
sions?
• Are take-back systems helpful in contributing to a more Circular Economy in Denmark?
• Which are the necessary stakeholders for the development of an efficient take-back sys-
tem?
• How feasible is it for a contractor to implement those take-back systems for wood in Den-
mark?

1.4
Definitions
This section presents definitions of terms relevant to the development of this thesis, specifically
in the context of construction and sustainability.
1.4.1
Circular economy
A system where materials are kept in a circular cycle via maintenance, reuse, refurbishment,
remanufacture, and recycling [3]. It aims to minimise waste, regenerate nature, and promote
resource efficiency by circulating products and materials [3], [10]. This system is primarily
a closed-loop system. Materials are continuously reused, avoiding the need for further virgin
material extraction [10]. However, there is also space for open-loop systems. These minimise
the use of virgin materials and postpone waste treatment processes by creating a new product
with a different purpose from the original [11]. Figure 1 illustrates this concept with the different
stages involved according to the Ellen Macarthur Foundation for renewavles and finite materials.
Figure 1: ‘Butterfly’ diagram representing circular economy [3]
1.4.2
Environmental Product Declaration (EPD)
A tool in the form of a standardized document that provides information regarding a product’s
environmental performance. It is a key element since it guarantees transparency and allows
comparability. It shows the effect of the life cycle stages on a range of environmental impact
categories [10].
4
==PAGE:16==
1.4.3
Global Warming Potential (GWP)
A way to describe a process’s carbon footprint. It shows how long a gas remains active in the
atmosphere. The reference gas is carbon dioxide with a 100-year GWP of 1. The higher the
value, the greater the negative impact [12]. Nonetheless, it is possible to have negative values for
GWP, representing materials that can absorb more gases than what is released during production.
1.4.4
Reuse
The practice where, instead of extracting virgin materials, existing ones are used to fulfil the
same purpose/function or a different one without significant alterations. It is a key process in
sustainability since it extends the life span of products, minimises waste, and reduces the use
of resources needed for material extraction, manufacturing, and waste management [10]. This
practice can happen at the product, material, and component levels and might provide additional
economic advantages [11].
Given the lack of specific definitions in the literature, the following types of reuse in construc-
tion and sustainability have been formulated by the author, drawing on existing knowledge and
contextual insight:
• Direct: the material is reused for the original purpose for which it was designed. They
could be entire products/systems or components.
• Semi-direct: before being reused for their original purpose, the materials are sent to the
suppliers, who transport and check them.
• Adaptive: extra processes are involved, such as repair and refurbishment, where the ma-
terial acquires an extra feature that allows a different use from the original.

1.4.6
Take-back systems
Systems in which reclaimed products are reused for the original or a different purpose, usually
by manufacturers or retailers. This is initially a voluntary process that is gaining force since
it presents an opportunity to reduce waste and the extraction of raw materials [14]. Figure 3
represents the path products follow when such systems are implemented and the stages involved.
Figure 3: Graphical representation of a take-back system in the life cycle of a product
The red path illustrates how the product moves in a circular path with take-back systems. The
product is used, and then it is determined whether reuse can be done 1:1, also called direct, or
if further repair or cleaning processes are needed. Moreover, depending on when it can be used,
the products can be used directly or stored for later use. The other phases, shown in blue, are
part of the product’s life cycle and thus have an impact on the take-back system. This is how it
is possible to see the connection between processes.

2
Methodology
Since this thesis investigates how to optimise waste management through take-back systems, a
two-phase approach was used to conduct proper research. First, it is important to describe the
characteristics of the current systems and factors involved. This description provides a baseline
for comparison and an understanding of the opportunities and limitations. The second phase
focuses on optimisation by establishing a causal relationship. Here, different scenarios are eval-
uated to determine which one performs best according to the established parameters.
The first phase consists of a literature review and interviews. The literature review presents
qualitative and quantitative secondary data on concepts, methods used in similar studies, re-
sults, trends, and identified gaps. The papers were found using tools such as Google Scholar,
scite_ai, and Connected Papers. The most common words used to search and select the papers
were ‘buildings’, ‘circularity’, ‘circularity indicators’, ‘Denmark’, ‘EU green transition policies’,
‘take-back systems’, ‘sustainability’, and ‘waste management’. Moreover, the articles selected
were based on their relevance to the topic, type of source, number of citations, year of publica-
tion, and geographical area.
After reviewing the selected papers, the most relevant definitions and conclusions were sum-
marised and presented in Chapter 3. The key points of the analysis are shown in Table 14
and Figures 6 and 7, illustrating how the articles relate, their similarities, and the methods used.
The relationships between the papers and the main topics related to the thesis goal established
a common ground that served as a foundation for the subsequent steps. The summary of the
methods also clarified that the methods selected in this thesis align with the objective. Finally,
this phase provided an overview of the main present and future polices that may affect the de-
velopment of take-back systems in Denmark.
Adjacent to the literature review are the interviews, which focus on the stakeholders and their
perspectives. Understanding the dynamics from their viewpoint is crucial before proceeding to
the optimisation process. They provide a real-life perspective, highlighting current opportunities
and barriers related to the topic. Stakeholders from various stages of the construction process
were selected to develop a comprehensive picture of the entire process from supply to end-of-
life. Although they are part of the same process, they differ significantly from one another.
Therefore, it was decided that the questions would be open-ended, resulting in unstructured
interviews, which allowed for flexibility in gathering detailed information. To avoid leading
questions and topic deviations, interviewees received general questions and information about
the topic and the project’s purpose in advance. Additionally, the interviewer, the author of this
thesis, had guidelines to support the conversation. The results are presented in Chapter 4, with
a summary in Table 15. The key findings from this part of the investigation are used mainly for
the subsequent framework development and feasibility analysis.
With the groundwork established, the second part was organised and conducted. Based on the
literature review and prior knowledge, it was decided that the use of the Material Circularity In-
dex (MCI), aided by a Material Flow Analysis (MFA), and a Life-Cycle Assessment (LCA) were
the best tools for this next phase. Here, quantitative data was needed—the first step involved col-
lecting the data. In collaboration with the construction company NCC, data on material quantity,
quality, purpose, and other factors were gathered from two real-life projects. Subsequently, the
MFA and LCA were performed. Four scenarios were created for the MFA, showing the effects
of using or not using a circular process for the input and output of materials in a construction

Literature Review
This section presents relevant literature that address definitions, previous research and their cor-

3.1
Waste Management
The Waste Management Directive of the European Union (EU) defines waste as ‘any substance
or object which the holder discards or intends or is required to discard. This broad definition
encompasses all kinds of materials, substances, and volumes present in most human activities,
making it a pertinent topic to address. Moreover, given its significance, the impacts arising from
both proper and improper management are vast. To illustrate these impacts, Raphela, Manqele,
and Erasmus [16] conducted a study in South Africa through semi-structured questionnaires and
descriptive statistics to analyse data to assess the impacts of improper waste management. They
concluded that when waste is not disposed of correctly, human health is compromised, leading to
various diseases, as well as water, soil, and air pollution, along with both market and non-market
costs.
While waste mismanagement can have severe consequences, some countries have implemented
systems to avoid it by using different methods. For instance, according to Statistics Denmark,
in 2022, the country generated 12.2 million tonnes of waste, with 75.1% recycled, 22.8% incin-
erated, and only 2.1% deposited in landfills. Compared to the average in the EU, where 40.8%
of waste was recycled and 30.2% went to landfills, Denmark demonstrates better recycling per-
formance and significantly lower landfill rates. Moreover, the incinerated waste in Denmark is
converted into energy used for district heating and/or generates power [17], thus minimising the
negative impacts of this process. However, as the European Green Deal aims to achieve the
goal of zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 [18], further waste reduction and more efficient
material use are needed.
A key point towards improving waste management comes from identifying which industries or
economic activities produce the most waste to develop a solution that would significantly impact
the situation in the shortest time possible. In Europe, the building sector consumes 50% of the
materials extracted and generates 35% of the waste [19], and in Denmark, according to statistics
from 2022, construction and demolition waste accounted for 56.1% of the total waste generated
(Figure A.1). This high percentage demonstrates why waste management in the construction
sector should be one of the focuses of different studies and discussions regarding the green
transition.
3.2
Circular Economy and Policies
One of the main theories aimed at reducing or eliminating waste and minimising the use of vir-
gin materials is the Circular Economy (CE), which maximises activities like reuse, maintenance,
12
==PAGE:24==
recycling, etc [3]. Iordachi [20] conducted a study in Moldova, analysing the growth of infras-
tructure projects between 2000 and 2021, accompanied by ongoing urban development. The
study highlights that the increasing waste generation demands the implementation of concepts
such as CE, practical plans, and consistent supporting legislation focused on construction.
As mentioned, the EU has various policies and frameworks, such as the European Green Deal,
that share the same goal of reducing negative environmental impacts while providing economic
growth [21]. An overview of the relevant legislation is provided in Table 1, and a more extended
version is provided in Figures A.2 and A.3.
These regulations have been introduced and implemented over the past 20 years. They set goals
for the near future and offer tools, definitions, and principles that guide companies to imple-
ment the right strategies to transition to improved practices, highlighting the implementation of
a CE. In addition, as shown by Niero and Rivera [22], implementing laws concerning manufac-
turing and disposal should be supported by existing evaluation tools and by applying integrated
frameworks to two case studies. One of those tools is the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), which
provides a comprehensive overview of resources and processes related to a product. According
to Jolliet, Saadé-Sbeih, Shaked, et al. [23], ‘LCA is a decision-making tool which specifically
addresses this need of selecting and optimizing available technological solutions’, emphasis-
ing the importance of prioritising specific processes given the urgency of the current climate
situation [24], as not all solutions can be implemented simultaneously by everyone.
3.3
Construction Process
Addressing waste management issues in construction also requires understanding how the con-
struction process is structured. It is essential to determine how new solutions can be better im-
plemented and prioritised, and where they will have a more significant impact. A construction
project is a complex system with various stages that allow for better organisation by grouping
multiple activities, facilitating more straightforward project management and control [25]. Ac-
cording to the standards on the sustainability of construction works EN 15978, there are four
phases in a construction project, later divided into more specific ones: product stage, construc-
tion stage, use stage, and end-of-life stage. An LCA is typically conducted for most stages and
can be expanded or narrowed based on the assessment’s goals. Furthermore, EU laws may apply
to the entire project or specific stages.
It is essential to highlight that even though waste is generated and primarily handled on-site
before moving to a further treatment stage, waste management is present through all stages.
As Lindhard, Wyke, Mahami, et al. [26] described, it is a requirement that from the early
stages, there is a clear strategy for waste management to evaluate related factors such as the
space needed, pollutant types and the stakeholders involved and that they align with the current
policies. Furthermore, as they demonstrated after evaluating various methods of predicting the
amount of waste at the end of the project, there are no specific methods that are highly accu-
rate or simple enough to estimate the amount of waste a project will generate. They analysed
two similar projects that were also geographically close and concluded they had very different
outcomes concerning the amounts and types of material waste produced. This highlights that so-
lutions for waste treatment should be addressed across various phases and need a certain degree
of flexibility between projects.

3.4
Tools
To enhance waste management effectiveness, complementary tools to LCA and EU regulations
can provide a more detailed overview of construction products from production to disposal or
new life, allowing for the monitoring and understanding of flows throughout the mentioned
phases. One of these complementary tools widely recognised is the Material Flow Analysis
(MFA), which offers an overview of the information on the different flows and treatments for a
specific product from extraction to its end of life [15], [27]. This information is crucial since it
is precisely the lack of it, especially in the later stages of the construction project, which poses
a barrier when implementing circular solutions [26], [28].
Berglund-Brown, Kedir, Riabova, et al. [29] conducted a study focused on how information cir-
culates and its quality in industrialised construction. Their background research showed that
there is no specific framework for evaluating this information. Thus, they propose four charac-
teristics for this purpose: completeness, availability, accessibility, and business strategy. Inter-
views and surveys were conducted to understand the flow of information within different com-
panies and across various departments. Regarding Circular Economy (CE) information, they
established that data on assembly instructions and other CE data is available. However, there is
limited information on reuse, remanufacturing, and recyclability throughout the life cycle. Fur-
thermore, companies involved in demolition, remanufacturing, and recycling often have little to
no access to information regarding building materials. These considerations can be viewed as
a preliminary step before assessing waste management systems. As Abadi, Moore, and Sam-
muneh [30] noted, the circularity model’s effectiveness relies on comprehensive information
with a proper flow between the various stages of a project and its stakeholders. The information
must be recent, relate to the geographical context and reflect the state of the technology used.
With a proper information base, one can assess the areas that can enhancecCE practices and,
more specifically, for this thesis, in terms of waste management. Abadi, Moore, and Sammuneh
[30] propose a conceptual framework called PLACIT, utilising CE requirements that focuses on
measuring circularity at a project level. Five circularity themes (CTs) were defined from relevant
literature, encompassing 12 circularity indicators (CIs). A systematic literature review subse-
quently supports the framework. Depending on the project and the company’s objectives, the
indicators are assigned scores and weights, resulting in a final percentage that reflects how cir-
cular the project is. Furthermore, examining each point allows for identifying areas that require
focus and improvement. The framework has yet to be validated with a real-life project. Nev-
ertheless, it builds on existing definitions and frameworks while incorporating contemporary
methodologies and corroborates previous research regarding barriers to circularity.
Similarly, González, Sendra, Herena, et al. [15] proposed another framework for evaluating the
circularity of a project. The study begins by researching suitable definitions of circularity prin-
ciples and existing tools. For instance, it points out how the definition of a circular building
complements the definition of a sustainable project and how existing certifications may provide
relevant tools for the correct implementation of CE measures. They establish five indicators
with defined boundaries and methodologies (four in percentages and one in monetary units).
These indicators correspond to the following aspects: energy, materials, water, social value, and
economic value. The framework is applied to a façade renovation with an External Thermal
Insulation Cladding System (ETICS) plus a polycrystalline photovoltaics (PV) system. The ma-
terial circularity is analysed using an MFA, relating to earlier considerations in this literature
review. Additionally, the Material Circularity Index (MCI) employs total material inputs and
15
==PAGE:27==
outputs, distinguishing materials from recycled sources and outputs that could follow a circular
path. The results indicate that retrofitting actions exhibit a low percentage of circularity, with
the energy aspect having the highest at 50%. The circularity percentages in the various stages
remain below 35% for materials. Moreover, for materials, it is concluded that the production and
construction stages have a higher potential for circularity. Finally, the authors underscore the
importance of such frameworks for policymakers, owners, and end-users, complemented with
sustainability certifications.
These two frameworks offer an overview of a project’s circularity context, allowing companies
to evaluate the focus areas and then choose an effective system/solution for waste management
and material reuse.
3.5
Reverse Logistics
One of the well-known systems for reincorporating production into the circular cycle is Reverse
Logistics (RL), defined as “the effective and eﬀicient management of the series of activities re-
quired to retrieve a product from a customer in order to either dispose of it or recover value”
([31] as cited in [5]). The study by Mallick, Salling, Pigosso, et al. [5] presents the barriers and
opportunities in implementing these systems and proposes a conceptual framework based on a
systematic literature review and data analysis. They identify various aspects to consider when
planning the implementation of such systems to determine the most suitable. Firstly, they sug-
gest defining the necessity of implementing an RL system, outlining the motivations and drivers,
which may include environmental or financial factors. Additionally, as mentioned previously, it
is essential to understand the main characteristics and processes involved in creating the prod-
uct. Next, another concept related to the discussion is understanding the legislative landscape,
including current policies and anticipating those that will soon apply. Lastly, they address the
value chain, which should illustrate the stakeholders, material flow, volumes, and financial as-
pects. Once these four aspects are fulfilled, companies can proceed to step two, where the system
is designed. Depending on the product and its context, the activities are planned, the involved
stakeholders are organised, and the process are be customised accordingly.

3.7
Take-back Systems
The sustainability directory defines take-back systems as “a circular model where producers
manage their products’ entire life cycle, especially end-of-life, promoting sustainability”. This
definition is connected to a circular responsibility model that extends beyond the point of sale
for producers [14]. As a model, the take-back system consists of various processes. Uhrenholt,
Kristensen, Rincón, et al. [34] outline these processes for end-of-life products as follows: collec-
tion, transportation, sorting and disassembly, re-qualification, and re-engagement in the forward
supply chain. Initially, take-back systems were simple retrieval steps mainly focused on the cus-
tomer. One example is the deposit refund system for cans. Over time, the described structure of
processes emerged from developing regulations and growing awareness [14]. A key component
of this development has been the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) policy, which em-
phasises the producer’s responsibility during the product’s entire life cycle and the importance
of collaboration among various stakeholders [34]. These stakeholders should include entities
from government, industry, education, and the market, and they need to be interconnected to
create a comprehensive system [14], [34].
Implementing a take-back system as a sustainability strategy for producers requires considera-
tion of various aspects when receiving back and creating products. The design must incorporate
techniques and characteristics that facilitate the reuse or recyclability of the product. This en-
sures that such systems do not become an additional process that generates costs. Design is a
factor that the company can control, along with other aspects such as processes and organisation.
These belong to the group defined as Company by Charef, Morel, and Rakhshan [33], who posi-
tion this category within the Supply Chain. This refers to collaboration among stakeholders and
simultaneously belongs to a broader category known as Context, which encompasses external
factors that influence the product but cannot be controlled by the company. Using this frame-
work, they evaluate costs and values in each category, ultimately presenting two proposals to
consider regarding financial aspects when implementing take-back systems. The first proposal
outlines the context and the supply chain as inputs for internal decision-making to enhance per-
formance. The second emphasises the importance of balancing the cost of processing with the
value recovered.
Even though take-back systems are becoming more popular in various industries, literature re-
garding them, especially in construction, is limited. They are mentioned in various papers re-
garding circularity and circular economy; however, they are not part of the focus, and thus, they
are not assessed in depth. This fact highlights the relevance of this thesis and the opportunities
for further research in the future.

3.8
Wood
Wood has been used throughout history for various purposes, ranging from small kitchen tools
to large buildings and boats. Its physical properties and durability are significant factors in its
preference over other materials. Nowadays, this material is gaining more attention due to the
18
==PAGE:30==
increasing need for sustainable solutions, particularly for environmentally demanding activities
such as construction. Within this field, wood exhibits a high degree of versatility; it can be
used to build structures, interiors, and façades, and support various construction processes. The
wood components evaluated in this project correspond mostly to elements used temporarily on-
site. This means wood is used to protect other materials, as well as in the creation of concrete
elements, and for handrails and bridges. However, it is worth noting that the available data
provides information on the type of wood, its characteristics, and quantities, but not its specific
function. For this project, it is assumed that none of the evaluated timber is used for a permanent
element in the building, but only for construction site operations.
Although the evaluated wood will not remain in the building, these elements are relevant due
to their contribution to the development of the construction stage. Regardless of the materials
chosen, most construction projects will likely need wood elements on-site to complete various
tasks. Making them relevant when developing innovative, more sustainable systems, such as
take-back systems.
Given the context of circularity and sustainability of this project, it is relevant to highlight that
all chosen components used by NCC come from responsibly managed forests. Using certified
products is recommended, given the environmental impact of the material extraction phase, even
though adding this characteristic for the product’s analysis is not straightforward in the current
frameworks [35]. Additionally, it is recommended to select products with no hazardous compo-
nents and with the least amount of material mix possible.
Moving from the early stages to the end-of-life, in Denmark, there is a focus on recycling over
incineration, and even the latter is used for energy recovery. As mentioned in the Chapter 3, this
is due to Denmark’s high commitment to EU policies, such as the Waste Framework Directive
(WFD). Nonetheless, reuse is the step that should follow prevention before moving to recycling
and recovery, according to the WFD. Reuse prolongs the product’s life by delaying the end-of-
life process. This would be particularly beneficial for wood, given its ability to capture carbon.
During the growing process, trees absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and store it. When
using the product, the carbon remains stored and is not released until the end of its life, when
the product decomposes in a landfill or through incineration.
The research conducted by Brunet-Navarro, Jochheim, and Muys [36] through a theoretical sim-
ulation demonstrated that both the increase in life span and the percentage of recyclability play
a crucial role in maintaining the carbon stock for as long as possible. They indicated that “(…)
carbon stock in wood products increases linearly when increasing the average lifespan of wood
products and exponentially when improving the recycling rate” [36]. Moreover, the study em-
phasises how increasing recyclability benefits products with a relatively high recyclability per-
centage; however, longer life spans could have greater advantages in the long term.
In line with Brunet-Navarro, Jochheim, and Muys [36], research by Bergman, Falk, Salazar, et
al. [37] concluded that reusing wood prevents the release of significant amounts of GHG. Reused
materials outperform new products in GWP when accounting for biogenic 퐶푂2 emissions. This
can be attributed to the use of biomass energy in the production of new components. Further-
more, different scenarios for the end-of-life demonstrated the importance of the carbon stock
present in wood. The scenarios in which burning wood replaces coal or gas are more effective
than landfill in terms of GHG emissions. As a final note, it is emphasised how essential it is to
understand what construction components are made of and to separate them correctly to avoid
releasing substances that might require further processes to be controlled. Their conclusions
align with the recommendations of the WFD and the purpose of this thesis.

Transport logistics is a barrier for 30% of people interviewed. The two main aspects of this point
are understanding who is in charge of transport and what is the most efficient way. The first one
asks who should move the material: the person who used it and is giving it away, or the new
user? On the other hand, understanding the most efficient way to transport the material means
optimising distances and quantities, saving money and emissions.
The last three barriers have similar percentages and are not organised in a specific order. In
relation to the key points, it was emphasised how challenging it can be to sort correctly since
achieving 100% clean fractions is not easy. This is closely linked to the time and space required
for sorting. The more time and skill that are invested in this process, the better the sorting quality,
and with proper storage facilities, the product’s condition will be preserved. However, as noted,
this entails additional costs. Finally, the space required for storage is a project-dependent barrier
and difficult to predict.
4.4
Opportunities
On the other side of the barriers are the opportunities. All the interviewees had an optimistic
perspective and mentioned several of them. Figure 10 shows the ones mentioned the most and
shared by more than two people.
The systems currently in use are primarily linear, so creating new systems is necessary to move
to a more circular approach. The interviewees view this as a window for possibilities and in-
novation. The new processes could bring not only more sustainable practices but also a change
in the way of thinking, impacting areas beyond the use and reuse of products. In adapting and
implementing these changes, collaboration with other stakeholders appears as the second most
common opportunity. When multiple companies responsible for different stages in a product’s
life are aligned, processes tend to be more efficient and balanced. Moreover, the collaboration
brings communication, making information flow easier.
Apart from the new policies pushing the green transition, the people interviewed think that there
is a general feeling of willingness to act and contribute to it. This is essential since new ideas
are carried out by people who believe in them and their purpose.
Moving on to a specific aspect of the construction process, it is considered that designing with
available materials instead of sourcing materials for the design (a practice now commonly em-
ployed) presents an opportunity to develop groundbreaking buildings and enhance creativity.
Furthermore, this practice in the early stages reduces the possibility of having unused or extra
27
==PAGE:39==
materials that might be wasted.
Likewise, policies present an opportunity to reduce waste. Although many directives and poli-
cies regarding the transition already exist, the interviewees believe they could be improved to
facilitate the quicker adoption of green practices. Furthermore, they can serve as guidelines for
companies to ensure a smooth and more effortless transition.
Ultimately, economic feasibility is seen as an opportunity when implementing more circular
systems. Despite the potential barrier of additional costs, more efficient processes and collabo-
ration can help to reduce expenses at various stages. Moreover, most end-of-life processes, such
as incineration or landfill, entail costs that are avoided by reusing.

This LCA aims to provide information that enables the comparison of scenarios, which then
serve as input for decision-making when implementing take-back systems. The intended au-

interested people in the topic.

Data:
As mentioned in the MFA section, NCC provided the information about the products. Since

extraction and production stages was obtained from the suppliers’ website. For these products,

Allocation:
The Cut-off approach is employed as the product’s data comes from the EPDs, which is the
preferred method among them. This technique allocates all the recycling benefits to stages A1-
A3, favouring the use of recycled materials over virgin materials. Furthermore, it assigns zero
benefits for the creation of recycled materials at the end of life. Therefore, it is also referred to
as the 100:0 approach [38].
Methods:
The LCA was conducted using the OneClick LCA platform, which offers a free license for stu-
dents. This license, however, comes with limitations that are outlined in the following sections.
According to their website, the Impact Assessment Categories used are CML and PEF for the
selected geographical area.
Depending on the purpose of the LCA, One Click LCA provides various frameworks that focus
the analysis on specific requirements, including DGNB, BREEAM, LEED, etc. For the student
license, there are two options: Level(s) and Building circularity. Even though options were
limited, these two are suitable for this project.
Level(s) is a tool that “provides a common language for assessing and reporting on the sus-
tainability performance of buildings”[39]. It is aligned with EN 15804 and EU Taxonomy and
is organised in three levels: 1. Conceptual design, 2. Detailed design and construction, and 3.
As-built and in-use. The available tool for this project is the Level(s) life-cycle assessment (EN
15804 + A2), which is suitable for Levels 2 and 3 [40].
Lastly, the Building Circularity is a tool by OneClick LCA that provides an overview of a build-
ing’s circularity, considering the source of materials, waste, and end-of-life (EOL) aspects. The
calculations are performed following ISO 59020 and are aligned with EN 15804 [40]. For the
settings of this tool, it is necessary to assign weighting factors for materials recovered and ma-

materials represent the end-of-life process of the product. The following factors for the sum and

Discussions
The outcomes of this research have provided the necessary data to identify the key factors and

7.1
Overview
This thesis used five questions to achieve its goal. In this section, the usefulness of them and
their impact on the development of this project will be discussed.
• Question 1: What are the methods of handling waste in Denmark used by NCC?
While obtaining the information for this point is a straightforward task, its relevance is
high, as it became the basis for choosing the scenarios to be evaluated. Moreover, it pro-

• Question 2: How can recyclability and reusability indicators contribute to waste manage-

Chapter 5 directly addresses this question using the MCI. Although it is not the only index

• Question 3: Are take-back systems helpful in contributing to a more Circular Economy
in Denmark?
This is a broad question that encompasses all the results and gives a focus to what is being

• Question 4: Which are the necessary stakeholders for the development of an efficient

Chapter 4 represents all aspects related to this question, and the results directly impact

The analysis of the literature was a key component in understanding the necessary factors

through take-back systems could generate positive impacts. However, the implementation

7.2
Key points from the findings
Definitions:
During the development of the thesis, two main misunderstandings appeared regarding the def-
initions of key terms. The first one is the definition of waste. When looking at the Chapter 3, it
is possible to see that when an element becomes waste, it is clear which processes follow in the
life cycle and which are the consequences of handling them. Likewise, statistics are made and
systems created to find solutions based on that definition. However, according to the policies, a
product becomes waste when the owner discards or intends to discard it. If it is not discarded, it
can be reused, retaining the ‘product’ term. However, to reuse materials, they need to undergo
basic processes such as sorting to identify the fractions of available materials and their condi-
tion. Nonetheless, the sorting process is considered done only for ‘waste’, referring to products
that have already been discarded. This creates a sort of paradox that does not benefit waste
minimisation and can lead to inconsistencies in data and statistics. Additionally, considering
the perception towards reused materials mentioned in the Literature Review and the Interviews,
the confusion might not contribute positively when good products are available, referred to as
‘waste’.
Secondly, in some cases, reuse and recycling are considered the same process. In literature, there
seems to be a clear distinction; however, when it comes to webpages and even when discussing
with people, there are situations in which they are thought of as the same. It is relevant to
clarify that reuse implies fewer further processes of the material and tends to preserve as many
characteristics of the original product as possible, compared to recycling, where the product is
transformed. It is therefore relevant to spread information about the correct use of these terms
and their distinctions.
Methodology:
The literature review supports the methods chosen for developing this thesis [4], [6], [9], [15],
[20], [26]–[28], [30]. It is worth noting that using multiple tools is necessary to cover as many
aspects as possible, especially when referring to complex systems that involve various factors
and stakeholders. The chosen methods provided the results needed to make an analysis cor-
responding to the aim of this thesis. Limitations regarding the MFA and MCI primarily stem
from a lack of information about the products and their functions. These, in turn, affect the LCA
analysis, which has two additional points to consider. The first one concerns the benefits of
Module D, which, depending on the chosen allocation, are considered in different percentages.
This might lead to discrepancies with other studies on similar topics. The second one is a tech-
nical limitation given the license type used for OneClick LCA. Results closer to reality can be
obtained by having full access to all the tools within the software.
In the case of interviews, although open questions do not allow for a direct comparison between
answers to the same questions, they have been found to be an essential tool for gathering infor-
mation [4]–[6], [16], [29], [32]. Especially when evaluating a topic such as take-back systems,
48
==PAGE:60==
where information is limited and various perspectives exist, this type of survey enables the dis-
covery of further steps, allowing for a more comprehensive analysis.
Finally, the framework is the tool that represents the outcome of theory and assessments. It
represents a compilation of key points and how to apply them to develop strategies in real-life
situations, which will hopefully lead to positive impacts on the environment.
Interviews
The interviews demonstrated that aligning the stakeholders’ objectives and focus throughout
a product’s life cycle is essential when implementing a circular system. The contributions of
each, in different ways, reduce the number of barriers and open up more opportunities for the
rest, given the circular nature of the cycle. Although all can create an impact, stakeholders such
as designers and contractors can have a greater one. This aligns perfectly with the purpose of
this thesis since NCC, a contractor’s company, has provided the data used. Additionally, they are
involved in stages such as use, preparation for reuse, and decision-making, which are the steps
that have more space for developing a circular cycle for wood. These stages and the role of the
contractors are also directly connected to take-back systems, creating a window for optimisation
and implementation that will benefit the whole cycle.
The stages with more items are the manufacturing stage, the use stage, and the decision-making
stage, with the pre-treatment stage. Some opportunities apply to all systems, and thus, when
utilised, they can have a more significant impact. Moreover, the number of opportunities and
barriers is almost balanced in the stages with the most items. Opportunities can help overcome
the obstacles since some are related, for instance:
• In manufacturing, the unfavorable perception regarding take-back systems can be coun-
tered through collaboration between stakeholders, which spreads knowledge and encour-
ages more people to participate.
• Decision-making issues might be addressed by creating new systems that adapt better to a
circular approach, making them more common and thus transforming the sorting and use
of space for storing materials on construction sites more frequently.
• Preparation for a ruse can occur in various locations and be handled by different stakehold-
ers. That is why collaboration plays a crucial role in improving the process. For instance,
a common space can be created among various companies, reducing the need for separate
spaces and storage costs.
• For surplus material, there are barriers in terms of space and money. However, when
looking at the bigger picture, time and money can be saved, even for multiple spaces,
since new raw materials are not being bought.
• Transport barriers are supported by willingness, new systems, and new polices which
contribute to optimisation and frequency in the use of alternative systems for reuse.
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) & Material Circularity Index (MCI)
Material flow analysis is a valuable tool for understanding the origin of materials, their usage,
and the resulting output. The formula used allowed the distinction between cycled processes
and non-cycled processes. By applying it, it was noticeable that the source of the input appears
to have a more significant impact compared to what happens to the product after use. This was
demonstrated by having high percentages of circularity in the scenarios where the input was
cycled, and by showing a significant difference compared to the other two scenarios, where the
49
==PAGE:61==
product was purchased as usual. When a company seeks to adopt more circular solutions, this
information is crucial in making informed decisions regarding material sourcing and disposal.
It should be noted that the formula distinguishes only between two big groups: cycled and non-
cycled processes. Thus, it is helpful for assessing circularity in a general sense, but it does
not provide information on which specific process in each of the two groups is better than the
other. A scenario with reuse as end-of-life will yield the same results as recycling, since both
are considered cycle processes. Same for incineration and landfill. Therefore, combining it with
LCA is necessary to have a complete analysis.
Moreover, the MFA tool can be maximised when information about the materials used in each
step of the life cycle (from A to D) is available. Additionally, it can be expanded for complex
elements, such as buildings, for a better understanding of the origin of materials, their fractions,
and the chosen disposal method.
Finally, the results of the MCI and the building circularity presented by OneClick LCA are
aligned in scenarios like Reuse to Reuse and Reuse to Incineration. However, for the others,
there is a greater disparity. This is understandable given the completeness of the data used in the
software compared to that employed for the MCI. In future research projects, comparing these
two with a comparable data set might yield a clearer understanding of this.

Proposal
The reuse of construction materials represents a significant impact on the industry and the en-
vironment. However, implementing reuse processes can be challenging due to the numerous
factors mentioned in previous sections. Based on the methodology employed in this thesis and
the information and results gathered, the following framework is presented.
Name: End-of-Life Evaluation Framework
Purpose:
Provide guidelines to NCC and future companies interested in implementing take-back systems
in various types of construction projects, along with their motivations to engage in the green
transition and make a positive contribution to biodiversity and circularity, as well as their com-
mitment to adhere to policies correctly and enhance the value chain.
Scope:
The focus materials for this project are temporary wooden construction elements used mainly
on construction sites for projects in Denmark. However, it has the potential to be used for other
types of materials.
Goal:
Facilitate the current transition to more sustainable practices in the construction sector that are
being required by policies and that are so needed for the environment. This will be achieved
with a focus on environmental responsibility and waste reduction.
Products:
Wooden components used temporarily to complete the construction of a building. These come
from pine and spruce trees, have standard measures, and are adaptable and versatile in terms
of functionality. Various products share the same characteristics, as they are made of the same
materials. Considering these factors, it is concluded that the chosen products are suitable for
implementing a take-back system.
Collection strategy:
There should be space available at the construction site for placing and sorting materials. Then
collection of the products takes place depending on who is the next user. Depending on the
project and the volume of materials, schedules for collecting and different ranges of spaces can be
established. Moreover, storage space should be defined when elements can not be immediately
used.

9
Conclusions
9.1
Outcomes
This thesis aimed to identify the necessary factors to optimise waste management on construction
sites by using take-back systems.
The integrated results show that indices like the MCI are useful tools when evaluating the pro-
cesses in the life cycle of a construction element. Their application, as demonstrated in this
study, can significantly contribute to waste management by providing data that can be used in
decision-making stages, offering flexible use across different projects, materials, and certifica-
tion requirements.
The results consistently indicated that reusing materials is a practice that can have a positive
impact on the green transition. Additionally, there is a clear contribution to the circular economy
when materials are reused through take-back systems, allowing their continuous circulation and
value retention throughout their life cycle.
The interviews provided qualitative data that revealed relevant insights, aligning with and en-
riching the qualitative findings. They not only offer a practical understanding of the necessary
stakeholders for the efficient implementation of a take-back system, but also serve as a bridge,
connecting theory with real-life operational challenges and opportunities on construction sites.
Furthermore, frameworks were demonstrated to be essential when creating and implementing
new systems. They can be combined with existing tools and policies, as shown in this project, to
deliver comprehensive solutions. A key contribution of this thesis is the End-of-Life Evaluation
Framework proposal, which was developed to address the complexity of balancing various fac-
tors at the decision-making stage for waste management, especially in the context of take-back
systems. The framework, which integrates MCI, logistics, and stakeholder analysis, offers a
comprehensive and adaptable approach. As shown, it can be combined with existing tools and
policies to provide holistic solutions for optimising waste flows. It is designed to act as a prac-
tical starting point, offering a flexible structure that can be personalised and modified according
to specific areas of focus and objectives.
9.2
Implications
The aim of this thesis was achieved by combining existing tools and methodologies in a unique
way that fit the provided data. This integrated approach, which links quantitative data from LCA,
MFA, and MCI, with qualitative stakeholder insights, offers valuable, data-driven information
for decision-making in construction.
The innovative proposal, embodied in the End-of-Life Framework, presents a clear opportunity
for a specialised consultancy service. Such a service could serve the Danish construction indus-
try by integrating this framework into companies’ internal platforms, providing tailored guidance
to optimise waste management processes, enhance material circularity, and help to clarify in-
consistent terminology in the industry. It can be used in early or later stages of a project, making
59
==PAGE:71==
it useful for various types of stakeholders and giving it a large market to cover. The framework
serves as a practical solution, adaptable to various company formats and sustainability goals,
offering a new approach to streamline the path toward more circular practices.
9.3
Recommendations
Recognising the limited empirical data on the real-world implementation of construction ma-
terial reuse systems and the urgent need for sustainable practices, this thesis identifies several
avenues for future research. It is recommended that future studies focus on:
• Empirical validation of the End-of-Life Framework: Conducting pilot projects to test and
refine the framework in various construction environments.
• Quantifying the broader impacts: Further research could explore the socio-economic im-
plications and scalability challenges of integrating take-back systems, extending beyond
the environmental scope of this study’s Life Cycle Assessment (LCA).
• Stakeholder engagement dynamics: In-depth qualitative studies could investigate specific
mechanisms of collaboration among the identified direct and indirect stakeholders, par-
ticularly regarding shared understanding of key definitions, barriers and opportunities.
• Technological enablers: Research into how digital tools and platforms can optimise infor-
mation flow and logistics within take-back systems.
