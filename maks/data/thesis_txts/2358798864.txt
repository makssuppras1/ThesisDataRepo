Abstract
Often major design choices is made based on intuition, rather than on the basis of
analysis and informed decision making. This is also the case for structural design, where
experience sometimes is mistaken with explicit knowledge. Although experience in many
ways can be irreplaceable it can be an eye-opener if the things we think we know is
challenged once in a while.
This thesis documents how structural designers can use parametric modeling and finite
element based structural analysis to get a better understanding of the cost implication
a specific change to the structural system have. Furthermore, an evolutionary solver is
added to the model allowing optimization of structures within boundaries outlined by
the user.
Using the model to rapidly generate numerous structural systems a set of nine hypothe-
sis were tested for concrete element structures with main beams made from either steel
or pre-stressed concrete. The result of the tests was then used to formulate the follow-
ing general conclusion; structural designers should strive to use long deck spans, long
concrete beams, and short steel beams in their structural design to minimize the cost of
the vertical load bearing structural system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
The main goal of this thesis is to automate the design process of the vertical load bearing
system of concrete element structures and to provide a tool that enables structural
designers to quickly assess different design solutions in the early-stage design.
The
secondary goal is to expand the capabilities of the program to use other beam types in
order to assess and compare more structural options.
The motive of addressing this problem is an increase in the interest in developing the
utilization of BIM tools in structural design, thus enhancing the quality and value of
the structural design.
A lot of the research within the field of parametric building
modelling is focused on the architectural and geometric capabilities, whereas this thesis
will be focusing on utilizing the structural aspects of parametric design and parametric
modelling.
Based on the previous paragraphs, the following research questions have been set up.
The goal of this study is approached by addressing these questions with engineering
modelling in combination with academic research.
1. Which advantages/disadvantages does parametric modelling have compared to the
traditional approach used to design concrete element structures?
2. Which aspects must be considered when automating the structural design process
of concrete element structures?
3. How does structural simplifications effect the results generated by the parametric
model?
4. Is it possible to get sufficiently accurate results using a parametric model approach
in the early-stage design?

1.2
Scope of the Study
Utilizing a combination of parametric modelling, finite element method (FEM) sotfware,
hand calculations, and load bearing capacity tables/calculator from element manufac-
turers1, this thesis investigates how the overall structural design can be chosen to give
a given project a better starting point, by giving architects and structural designers a
better understanding of what implication a certain structural layout will have on ceiling
height, the size of the structure, price, etc., compared to other options.
Providing the early-stage design team with a better starting point allows the end result
to obtain a higher quality. One way to illustrate this is shown in Figure 1.1, where
point A and B represent a high quality starting point and a lower quality starting point.
Throughout the project design choices are made which impact the quality of the design
- for better or worse. The grey triangles represent the spectrum of the design quality
over the course of the design project. It is the objective of this thesis to provide a tool
which allows designers to efficiently asses different structural layout, thus enabling the
design team to take an informed decision rather than choosing a structural layout based
on more or less qualified guessing.
Figure 1.1: How a good decisions in the beginning of a design process impacts the
quality of the end result.
It is important to acknowledge that the requirements and necessary accuracy of a script
developed to automate a design process varies greatly depending on the intended use
and especially in which design phase the script is intended to be used in. This thesis
focus on the very early-stage structural design process and how to enable/enhance the

1.3
Success Criteria
It is in the nature of the project that the building geometry will change, in order to
find the solution that best fits the three main criteria; structural performance, best
monetary value, and architectural aesthetics. The first criterion is somewhat easy to
quantify. However, the monetary value and especially the architectural aesthetics of a
building structure is difficult to quantify, and must be viewed as a whole. Therefore, it
is essential for the project to fulfill the criteria listed below. The developed model shall;
‚Ä¢ have a high degree of parametrization. Thus, allowing the overall structural geom-
etry to be easily modified.
‚Ä¢ produce models with a structural performance accuracy to such a degree that
allows the structural designer and other designers to assess and compare design
options.
‚Ä¢ implement optimization algorithms to allow quick and automatic testing of many
possible solutions within the user defined constraints.
1.4
Reference Building
Throughout the project a reference building have been used in order to compare and
verify the results from the parametric model. The reference building is an imaginary
building with a simple beam-column structure in order to replicate it as a parametric
model. The reference structure is up to four stories tall without basement consisting of a
beam-column system, as shown in Figure 1.2. Facades, foundations, stabilizing elements
and all interior fittings is not considered in the parametric model, except for in the loads
acting on the structure.
To avoid cutting in the hollow core slab elements the length of the building is a multiplier
of 1.2 m. In Chapter 6 the length is chosen to be 78 m, 79.2 m and 84 m long for beam
spans/column distances of 6.0 m, 7.2 m and 8.4 m respectively. The columns of the
reference building is placed along grid lines A, B, and C for two-span structures, see
Figure 1.3. The structural cost is calculated for building geometries with one or two
deck spans of 9 m, 12 m and 15 m (center-to-center of structural elements).
In Figure 1.4 the principal sections for structures with steel beams (HEB) and concrete
beams (KB/KBE) are shown.

CHAPTER 2
Methodology
Generic algorithms together with parametric modeling techniques can be used to gen-
erate optimized building structures based on performance driven geometry [1]. In this
particular thesis the performance-based parametric design method is used to develop a
program allowing the principals of value engineering to be applied in the very beginning
of the design process, rather than as an afterthought where a baseline cost is determined
and then the value engineering is carried out [2].
2.1
Optimizing a Manual Task with Automation
By automating the design process, even as an estimate in the early-stage design, it is
not only possible to speed up the design process, but more importantly it is possible
to quickly make numerous iterations. Thus, enabling the designer to choose the best
solution on an informed basis.
In this thesis automation is defined in a relatively broad sense as any combination of
man and machine similar to [3]. Using this definition most traditional engineering tasks
is automatic. However, automation in relation to engineering design comes in varying
degrees;

Parametric Modeling
Rather than rely on an intuitive search for a solution, parametric design often involves
precise, step-by-step techniques that yield a result according to rules and inputs [18].
This way of designing and thinking is close to the way engineers design and is referred
to as algorithmic thinking.
The engineering design process is based on scientific models and explicit knowledge, the
so called know-that principle, whereas the architectural design process relies more intu-
ition [19]. In spite of this, parametric modeling is more commonly used in architectural
design and to less extent in engineering design. This seem paradoxical given the nature
of parametric modeling, which to great extend is build on rationality, linear algorithms,
and scientific models - all traits describing the engineering design, see Table 3.1.

Since the introduction of parametric modeling it has been possible to design buildings
without defining the shape of the building, but rather the rules and parameters to which
the shape must conform [20]. Thus, when designing parametrically it is the parameters
of the design that are declared and not the design it self.
The majority of design decisions is made in the very early design-phases [21]. Therefore,
parametric modeling tools can be advantageous, as the script defining any given para-
metric model can be modified and enhanced throughout a project when new knowledge is
acquired. Thereby, the model becomes more complex over the course of the project and
can be used to simulate and optimize any of the build-in parameters and combinations
thereof.

3.2
Galapagos Evolutionary Solver
The evolution theory described by Charles Darwin in Origin of Species [27] lays the
ground work for evolutionary algorithms. The main matter hereof is the law of natural
selection, stating that the best evolved individuals will also be the ones to mate and pass
on their genes to the next generation.
Evolutionary optimization solvers is based on the principles of evolution theory. Search
algorithms based on evolution theory maintains a population where each individual
represents a possible solution [28]. A selection of individuals is selected by the algorithm
based on the fitness of each individual. These individuals, or solutions, mate and produce
offspring by mixing their genetic material. The new generation is therefore a mix of the
parents gene pool, but also slightly difference due to mutation.
Galapagos is, like Optimo for Dynamo, a multi-objective optimization tool that enables
users to optimize problems with single and multiple objectives using evolutionary algo-
rithms. [29].
When a series of genes (variables) is connected a fitness is generated in the evolutionary
solver. The generated fitness is illustrated as a fitness landscape in Figure 3.1 (a). In
the example shown in Figure 3.1 (a) the fitness is a function of the two genes Gene A
and Gene B. Figure 3.1 (b) show the first generation of the optimization process. In
the first generation the genomes are placed arbitrarily in order to, hopefully, cover the

of when using evolutionary algorithm based solvers;
Limitations:
1. Evolutionary Algorithms are very slow. When optimizing complex problems the
process may take days or even weeks.
2. The procedure do not guarantee a solution.
Unless a predefined ‚Äôgood-enough‚Äô
value is specified, the process will tend to run on indefinitely, never reaching The
Answer, or, having reached it, not recognizing it for what it is, [31].

Finite Element Method
In Figure 3.6 a simple finite element method (FEM) problem is shown. If one want to
calculate the stresses in the plate, one could write an appropriate differential equation
for each point of the plate. Finding a solution to the resulting differential equation,
however, is very complex when considering the plate as a single entity [32]. Splitting
the geometry up into smaller segments (mesh generation) with a simple geometry, for
which there is a known solution to the differential equation, allows the FEM software to
approximate a solution.
Figure 3.6: Geometry, loads, and finite element meshes for simple FEM problem, [32].
The basic philosophy behind the FE method, is to divide complex geometries into finite
elements connected with nodes, and thereby obtain an approximate solution [32].

4.3.1
Defining the Fitness Function
To achieve good results from the evolutionary solver it is of the out most importance
that the fitness function is properly defined, as described in Section 3.2.2. The fitness
function can only return a single value. For problems with more than one parameter
to optimize for it is particularly important to contemplate how the fitness function is
defined.
In this particular case there is only one parameter for which the evolutionary solver have
to seek the optimum solution. This makes defining the fitness function easier, but it is
not sufficient to just minimize the cost parameter in this case. The boundaries of the
problem have to be considered, otherwise the optimum solution is unlikely to be found.
The boundaries for this problem is the desired building area and the buildability of the
structure. If the solution is not within the boundaries of the problem the cost estimate
is multiplied with a scalar.
Ffit = 1
6 (Farea + Fbeam,in + Fbeam,out + Fcolumn,in + Fcolumn,out + Fslab) ¬∑ Fcost
(4.1)
where the sub-fitness values are;
Farea
1 for areas within the specified permissible deviation, otherwise 100.
Fbeam,in
1 if a sufficiently strong inner beam member exist, otherwise 100.
Fbeam,out
1 if a sufficiently strong outer beam member exist, otherwise 100.
Fcolumn,in
1 if a sufficiently strong inner column member exist, otherwise 100.
Fcolumn,out
1 if a sufficiently strong outer column member exist, otherwise 100.
Fslab
1 if a sufficiently strong slab member exist, otherwise 100.
Fcost
estimated total cost per unit area.
The fitness value, Ffit, is equal to the unit area cost for permissible solutions. Thus,
when using Galapagos the most cost efficient solution is found by minimizing the fitness.

The preliminary testing of Karamba and the developed program relies on two different
testing methods. Because the source code for Karamba is not available, Karamba is a
so called black box. Wikipedia defines a black box as;
‚ÄùIn science, computing, and engineering, a black box is a device, system or object which
can be viewed in terms of its inputs and outputs (or transfer characteristics), without
any knowledge of its internal workings. Its implementation is ‚Äùopaque‚Äù (black).‚Äù
Because, the internal workings of Karamba is unknown and it is desired to determine
the accuracy of the plug-in, a simplified version of the Black Box Testing Technique is
used as this allows testing without having any knowledge of the internal working of the
application [43]. The testing is made by modeling problems with know solutions and
comparing the result given by Karamba.
Unlike, the Karamba plug-in the internal workings of the developed script itself is known.
Therefore, a simplified version of the White Box Testing Technique is used to quality
control the program.

5.1.4
Combined Model
In Karamba (v1.2.2), unlike FEM-Design and other commercial finite element software,
it is not possible to create linear supports for shell elements, but only point supports.
This results in a model with floor slabs spanning in two directions and in the entire
width of the building regardless of the number of column lines.
Because of that the initial model, where the slabs were modeled as shells, had to be
abandoned in favour of a solution where each slab element is modeled as a beam. When
testing out the accuracy of a model consisting of a single slab elements, the results were
good. However, when implementing geometry for each slab element, it is necessary to
add an intersection where the reactions in the slab element ends can be transferred to
the main beams. By doing so the main beam had to be cut into a lot of small continuous
beam segments, which in theory should be fine. But when testing the model the resulting
section forces were way to big, and the model had to be discarded.
In the final solution a parallel model consisting only of a single slab element. The slab is
generated as a beam element in the parallel model within the program and later the self
weight, element type, and cost of the slab is reintroduced into the main model containing
only beams, columns and a surface to apply load.

6.10
Optimal Structure
Using the Galapagos evolutionary solver component in Grasshopper the most cost ef-
fective solution for steel beam structures and concrete beam structures, respectively, is
found. The fitness function described in Section 4.3.1 is used to asses the fitness of the
solutions found by the Galapagos solver.
Because Galapagos tests solutions within the entire spectrum of the predefined range
of the user inputs, it is important to define the range of the input sliders shown in
Figure 6.8, especially the four right sliders as these are the ones subject to manipulation
by the Galapagos solver.
Figure 6.8: User input for the developed Grasshopper program.
The user input sliders, which are being manipulated by Galapagos to find the most cost
effective solution, is chosen to the following ranges;
‚Ä¢ The beam span is length is chosen to be between 3.6 m and 12 m in increments of
1.2 m. This is done in order to allow both short beams for the steel beam structures
and long beams for the concrete beam structure. The beam span is controlled by
the slider denominated Slab Elements per Column.
‚Ä¢ The minimum permissible building with is set to 12 m, as it is estimated that office
spaces in a building narrower than 12 m is adverse.
‚Ä¢ In order to allow sufficient day light to enter the center of the building, the maxi-
mum building width is chosen to be 22 m. All though, it is assessed that an office
spaces with 11 m to the nearest window does not have sufficient day light, other
necessary functions such as meeting rooms, restrooms, copy areas etc. could be
located in these areas.
‚Ä¢ The Longitudinal column line slider is in the range 5 to 30. Thus, allowing struc-
tures down to 60 m for beams spanning 12 m and up to 108 m for beams spanning
3.6 m.

Interim Conclusion
It is apparent that the preceding conditions have an impact on the total cost of the
building, therefore they are well worth taking into account in the early design phase -
especially considering that the structural design remuneration only constitutes around
250 DKK/m2 according to Niels Kjeldgaard (Appendix B.3).
It lies outside the scope of this project to investigate it, but given the relative low uti-
lization of the concrete beams in many cases, one could imagine that the true optimum
solution is a concrete structure with shorter deck spans and concrete beams with extra
reinforcement in smaller cross sections. However, in order to check this the cost estima-
tion part of the program would have to be altered significantly. And before that is done,
the cost implications of added reinforcement in the cross section have to be analysed.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This project have investigated how the use of parametric modeling allows designers to
make design decisions regarding the vertical structural system based on quantitative
information rather than rough estimate and to some extent guessing. Using parametric
modeling and finite element based structural analysis the parametric model developed
during the course of the project can be used to calculate a cost estimation of specific
structural geometries, or the structural cost can be optimized by using the evolutionary
solver implemented in the model.
For two of the three beam types implemented in the parametric model getting realistic
results was achieved.
However, by limiting the parametric model to only take into
account the ultimate limit state, satisfactory results for long composite beams. This was
largely due to the assumptions made regarding simplifications of the structural system.
This is not the case for structures with wide flange steel beams and pre-stressed concrete
beams.
By using the generated data from the model from 42 different structures, nine hypothesis
where tested. What was learned here, was used to, correctly, predict what characteristics
the optimal design would have for a structure with wide flange steel beams and pre-
stressed concrete beams respectively. This kind of analysis is relative quick to make
with parametric modeling tools, contrary to performing the same task with traditional
tools.
Within the boundaries of this project, it can generally be said that structural designers
should strive to use long deck spans, long concrete beams, and short steel beams in their
structural design.
7.1
Future Studies
The geometry of the building is relatively simple. In future studies the script could be
modified to use a more complicated geometry. Furthermore, the script could be expanded
to include load bearing facades, structural stability elements, and long composite beams.

Approximate cost of steel beams:
HEB Beam:
‚Ä¢ HEB beams cost approximately 20 DKK/kg including assembly.
‚Ä¢ Fire protection on one side cost in the range 450-500 DKK/m2.
Delta/SWT Beam:
‚Ä¢ Delta beams cost approximately 14 DKK/kg.
‚Ä¢ A beam with a 7 meter span cost approximately 2000 DKK/m.
‚Ä¢ Assembly of a delta beam cost approximately 1000 DKK.
‚Ä¢ Fire protection cost approximately 250 DKK/m.
Neither of the manufacturers of Delta and SWT beams provide information on how
heavy the standard sections in the load bearing tables is. Therefore, it is not possible
to estimate the cost of these beams from the steel mass.
Limitations to composite beams:
For composite beams with spans from around 5 m and longer, the moment capacity of the
beam is not the limiting factor. Instead the requirements in DS/EN 1990 DK NA:2013,
[39], for acceptable natural frequencies and acceleration limits is the denominating factor.
In order to comply with DS/EN 1990 DK NA:2013 it is usually necessary to use con-
tinuous fixed beams. For shorter beams it can be sufficient to lower the bottom flange
of the beam and weld on steel profiles to support the hollow core slab, thus increasing
the stiffness of the beam. However, the advantage of the bottom of the composite beam
being flush with the slab structure is then lost.
