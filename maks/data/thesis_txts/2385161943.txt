Abstract
The building can be defined in one word, Unique: the quality of being the only one
of its kind 1.
The uniqueness of Opalen is visible not only with regards to the shape of the
building, but in the structural system of the building as well. Opalen is a proposed
design for an additional building at the domicile of The Danish Society of Engineers,
IDA, located between their two existing offices at Kalvebod Brygge in Copenhagen.
Opalen has a structural design which includes an oval shaped ring frame with an
egg-shaped roof structure. The ring frame is supported on four V-columns and is
pre-stressed at the opposite side of the cantilevered part of the building by a tensile
cable.
The complexity of the building causes challenges in the actual structural design
of the building.
The challenges include how to both prevent an overturn of the
structure and a large deformation on the structure. The purpose of this paper is thus
to design and document the bearing structural elements for such challenging design.
In designing and documenting the bearing structural elements this thesis utilizes
the sub-structure method. Hence diving the main structure into sub-structures and
calculate the stability and bearing capacity of each of the sub-structures.
The main structure was divided into 3 sub-structures; roof structure, floor struc-
ture and the ring frame.
For the roof structure, curved elements of timber were
considered and the calculations indicated that a glue laminated wood with a cross-
section of 90x270 mm would have sufficient bearing capacity with regards to bending
and buckling.
For the floor structure it was discovered that if using glue laminated wood the
floor beams would have to have significantly large dimensions in order to carry the
loads. Alternatively KERTO LVL beams were proposed to be used as the joists due
to the high bending strength of the KERTO material. The calculations showed that
even with the tallest KERTO LVL beam, the bearing capacity would not be sufficient.
In order to keep the dead loads to a minimum, castellated beams were used as an
alternative. The bearing capacity of the castellated beam with a height of 270 mm
was verified for moment and shear resistance.
The ring frame was the most challenging sub-structure. Initial calculations indi-
cated critically large deflections. After several design revisions of the beams, truss
members, column and bars the deflection requirements were met.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Danish Engineer Society (Ingeniørforening) founded in 1892, in association with
the Engineering Association (founded in 1937), merged to become IDA (Danish Soci-
ety of Engineers, Danish Ingeniørforeningen i Danmark) in 1995 1.
IDA is an union association for engineers and for professionals with relation to tech-
nology and today has more than 100.000 members across the country.
IDA has had their headquarters located at Kalvebod Brygge, Copenhagen since its
establishment, and due to its prominent location at the harbour, the demand for
renting the meeting rooms and meeting halls has been huge. To deal with the huge
demand of renting out the conference rooms and establishing and enforcing IDA’s
identity as an engineering union, IDA has decided to build another building on their
cadastral. Keeping their vision in mind and to widen the proposals, IDA sent out a
competition for the design of the building. The interest in drawing the design of the
building was enormous and various architect firms have participated in the competi-
tion with a draft of their design. There was designs such as ”Effekt” and ”Cope” but
considering the visions and the sustainability of the building IDA chose ”Opalen” as
the winning design. 2.
Opalen is designed by the architect firm Dorte Mandrup while Søren Jensen Råd-
givende Ingeniør acts as the consultants on the project. The uniqueness of the design
is visible in both the form and in the structure of the building. The architects have
given much importance to the facades and the interaction of the facades with the wa-
ter from the harbour. The facade alternates between the transparency and closedness
in the triangular layers. The outer sheating of Opalen consists of cloth placed on top
of LED bulbs, that will create a special staging of the building in the evenings. This
brings value not only to the building but to the city as a whole . 3
Even though the importance is given to the architectonic features of the building the
structure and the structural members of the building has their own uniqueness as
well. Considering the sustainability of the building materials having to be as C02
efficient as possible, the outer member of the structure is very light. Opalen is like
an airship, only resting on 4 column supports, and is stabilized against overturning
with a tension bar at one end.
Opalen, being such an unique structure, has a special bearing structural system. It

CHAPTER 2
Project scope
Since a static documentation of a structure or a building requires various elements and
details to be presented and verified and with the limited time period to complete the
project in mind making it difficult to address every aspect of a static documentation,
the report scope is thus limited to the following:
• Outline norms and standards concerning this project
• Outline load combinations acting on the structure
• Segment the main structure into adequate sub-structures
• Calculate stability analysis of each sub-structure
• Present the structural joints
This means that this report does not include any calculations on the foundation level.
It is therefore assumed that the geo-technical calculations of the structure has been
carried out and verified prior to this static report.
The fire safety requirements is studied in this report, but only on a general level.

5.1
Static system
The main static system is presented in Figure 5.1.
The static system consists of
curved roof elements (red), ring frame (yellow), truss members, columns (blue) and
tension bars (black).
The vertical loads from wind, snow, and dead loads from the roof are subjected on
the curved roof elements that transfer the loads to the ring frame. The ring frame
consists of beams that form an oval shaped frame. The upper ring frame is connected
with the the lower ring frame through truss members. The forces are then transferred
to the support via columns. To prevent the overturn of the structure the opposite of
the cantilevered side is prestressed with a tension bar.
Figure 5.1: Static system for the building, Opalen.

5.2.2
Imposed loads
Imposed loads or live loads are loads that are present only temporarily. These loads
are often referred to as probabilistic loads, since these loads cannot be determined
with full precision.
Live loads consists partly of forces that are enacting on the
building parts under construction process and partly while the building is in use.
The main contributors to the imposed load include movable objects, such as people.
Live loads depend entirely on the general function of the building section. Live loads
are generally determined for every section or for every area of the building.
As pointed out earlier Opalen consists of only two floors. The ground floor features a
restaurant/café, while the first floor is solely used as a conference hall. The live loads

5.4.1
ETFE - foil
ETFE-foil is a plastic polymer construction material. ETFE-foil (Ethylen-Tetraflourethylen)
was discovered and developed back in the 1970’s, but has only recently been recog-
nized as a potential construction material that can be used as an alternative to glass
panels. This membrane brings many advantages due to its uniqueness of having high
ultimate strength and a transparency that can be compared to that of the glass.
ETFE-foil as a single layer can be used as a membrane, but using it to withstand
loads it is essential to use multiple layers of ETFE-foil in order to give it stability.
The other advantage of ETFE-foil is its light weight.
The average weight of the
ETFE-foil is around 2 −4 kg/m2 which only is 1 % of the glass weight. ETFE-foil
can be as thin as 0.2 mm.
Figure 5.6(1) shows a picture of Ayr Central Shopping Centre located in Scotland.
As part of a transformation of the shopping center, the consultants on the project uti-
lized ETFE-foil to make an iconic and roof. Architen has used ETFE-foil in various
projects around the world.

5.6
The ring frame
The most critical substructure in this project is the ring frame, since the ring frame
carries all the loads from the roof structure and from the floors. The loads from the
ring frame is then transferred to the supports. The uniqueness of Opalen is that the
structure is ”floating” in the air like an airship and that the supports are placed in such
a way that it results in an extended cantilevered part of the structure. This makes
it challenging to design the ring frame and the supports. To stabilize the structure
against overturning, a cable has been chosen to be mounted on the opposite side of
the cantilevered structure part and is connected to a specially designed foundation to
withstand the tension in the cable.
The design of the ring frame is analyzed initially for the proposed design from the
architects and the consultants on the project. The only information available from
the proposed design is the elements that are used and the amount of truss members
that are constructed on the frame. If the proposed design from the consultants is not
stable, the design is adjusted and verified.
The design and the verification of the ring frame is carried out using Strusoft FEM-
design. The following steps are taken:
• The axis and the storeys are setup
• The beams and truss members are defined and constructed
• The supports are defined and constructed
• The loads cases and the loads that act on the structure or structure member
are defined
• The load combinations are defined
• The analysis are carried out and studied
It needs to be noted that the reactions calculated in Section 5.4 are defined as the loads
from the roof structure acting on each beam in the analysis. The reaction calculated
in Section 5.4 is only for the critical load on the critical curved element, thus the
reactions from the other curved elements are reduced according to the dimensions
and the load subjected area. The floor is designed as IPE240 beams with a distance
of 1m between each beam. The ring frame is to be designed for deformations and

Software modelling
In this report the use of software has been essential, since most of the designs, drawings
and calculations would have been very difficult to carry out in hand.
In the initial stages of the report Microstation - Bentley was used to prepare cross-

In order to calculate the cross-section forces of the roof and floor structure MatLab
was used. The model was coded in MatLab and the cross-section forces were obtained
as outputs from the software. Other alternative softwares that could carry out such

Mathcad was used to carry out the mathematical calculations. Alternative software
such as Maple could be utilized for such tasks.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The purpose of this report was to divide the main structure into 3 sub structures; roof
structure, floor structure and the ring frame and calculate the stability and verify the
load bearing capacity of the structural elements.
The calculations for the roof structure were conducted for the most critical load
combination and the curved roof element (timber) was verified for its bearing capacity.
The material used for the curved roof element was GL32h with cross-section of 90x270
mm.
For the floor structure it was preferred to use timber to keep the dead loads low.
While it was evident that using GL32h for this purpose would result in too high
cross-section dimensions.
A stronger timber product, KERTO LVL was proposed
and the results indicated that the bearing capacity of a 75x400 mm KERTO LVL
would be sufficient to carry the applied loads. It was thus decided to used castellated
profiles.
Castellated profile is an IPE- or HBE- profile that is transformed to a
castellated profile by cutting IPE- og HBE- profile in to two parts along a zig zag line.
Thereafter the two parts are butt-welded together. This method transforms the same
IPE-profile to a stronger profile, using the same amount of material. The moment
capacity calculations showed that an IPE-270 transformed to castellated beam would
have sufficient bearing capacity.
The initial design proposal for the ring frame revealed too large deformations on
the sub-structure. Increasing the amount of truss members did help to decrease the
deformations drastically but did not meet the deformation requirements. The size of
the beams, truss members, columns and bars were increased to lower the displacement.
It was discovered that the beams used for the ring frame had low stiffness along their
z-axis (weak axis) which caused large deformations. It was ultimately decided to use
box-profiles to reduce the deflections. Box-profiles with dimensions 1000x40x600x20
mm were used to achieve a deformation of only 15 mm ( 75 mm max deformation)
under dead load alone. It was discovered that the size of the box-profile should further
be increased to meet deformation requirements under critical load combination.
The stability analysis were conducted under simplified conditions. The curve roof
element was treated as a plane beam and since a wind tunnel test had not been
conducted it was difficult to determine the wind loads acting on the structure.
Even though most of the structural elements were verified for bearing capacities
Opalen still is a challenging structure. It was discovered that to limit the deformations
under critical load combination bigger profiles should be used.
==PAGE:60==

7 Conclusion
To get a more clear overview and a more exact verification of the structural elements
and optimization of the elements and to limit the deformations it is suggested that
a wind tunnel test is carried out and a stability analysis of the main structure as a
whole has to be conducted in Strusoft FEM-design or in a similar modelling software.
