Abstract
An experimental study of scattering from mirrored surfaces has been performed with
the purpose of ﬁnding optimal parameters for the coating procedure resulting in a
reduction of non-specular scattering from X-ray mirrors. This includes the eﬀects of
plasma cleaning of the mirror substrates, coating pressure, and the fraction of reac-
tive nitrogen present during sputtering deposition. The study was performed in the
X-ray laboratory at DTU Space, which employs a rotating cobber anode producing
8.04 keV X-rays. A long exposure rocking curve scan is performed to measure the
scattering intensity as function of incident grazing angle, and computer software
is used to ﬁt a distorted-wave Born approximation model to the data, yielding in-
formation on best ﬁt parameters for the coating roughness, correlation length, and
fractal exponent. A method for producing more complete 2D scans of the scattering
from a sample is presented, but the data extracted from this scan are saturated with
noise and thus do not produce as clear results.
The imaging performance of a high quality X-ray telescope is dependent on the scat-
ter properties of the coatings used. Iridium will be used in the mirror coatings for
ESA’s future Advanced Telescope for High Energy Astrophysics (ATHENA), due
to its high density and correspondingly high critical angle. After production of the
SPO mirror substrates, a number of contaminants may still be present on the sur-
face. Investigation using silicon wafers showed that plasma cleaning the substrate
surface prior to coating can eﬀectively reduce the intensity of non-specular scattering
by about an order of magnitude. This does not seem to be caused by a reduction in
surface roughness of the ﬁlm, but rather due to a decrease in the roughness correla-
tion length propagating through the coating layer. These results indicate that it is
highly beneﬁcial to plasma clean the substrate surface to remove any contaminants
prior to sputter deposition in order to mitigate non-specular scattering. Further-
more iridium coatings were found to be largely unaﬀected by coating pressure, but
it was shown that they are aﬀected adversely by the presence of reactive nitrogen
during sputtering.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Observation of photons is the most important way to gain knowledge about the ob-
jects in our Universe. From looking at structures and events in the Universe, we can
gain new knowledge about cosmology and the astrophysical processes taking place.
The visible part of the spectrum, however, can only tell part of the tale since, de-
pending on the source of, and physical process behind the emission, celestial objects
emit radiation of photons at diﬀerent energies. Hot and energetic processes give rise
to emission of very energetic photons, so doing observations in the X-ray part of the
electromagnetic spectrum can reveal information about objects such as accreting
supermassive black holes, galaxy clusters, and compact objects. Observing hot gas
in the intracluster medium can help further our understanding of the formation and
growth of galaxy clusters. Learning about early galaxy formation and the role of
their central black holes can teach us about how these structures grow, as well as
their eﬀect on our Universe.
The extreme environment of accretion disks serves as a testbed for nuclear and par-
ticle physics, and the behavior of matter and light in the gravitational potential of
compact objects is excellent for probing the limits of special and general relativity.
Observations of the motion of hot gas can be used to deduce information about the
gravitational interplay between baryonic and dark matter.
Due to the opaqueness of Earth’s atmosphere for X-rays, X-ray observatories are
spaceborne, and the European Space Agency (ESA) is currently developing the Ad-
vanced Telescope for High Energy Astrophysics (ATHENA) scheduled for launch
in 2030. It will be launched into an orbit around Lagrange point 2 in the Sun-
Earth system and has a planned mission duration of 5 years with a possible 5-year
extension. X-ray telescopes are inherently diﬀerent from optical light telescopes,
because the high energy of the radiation requires a shallow grazing angle of inci-
dence of the incoming radiation in order to facilitate reﬂection. ATHENA will use
a bilayer coating of Ir/B4C to achieve its eﬀective area goal, and it will employ a
single grazing-incidence X-ray telescope with 12 m focal length.
Its focal plane will contain two instruments. The Wide-Field Imager (WFI) will pro-
vide bright source observation capability and a large ﬁeld of view for imaging and
spectroscopy, and the X-ray Integral Field Unit (X-IFU) will deliver high resolution
X-ray spectroscopy over a narrower ﬁeld of view. Target of opportunity capability
in which ATHENA will respond within four hours will enable studies of transient
events [1].

CHAPTER 1.
INTRODUCTION
The scientiﬁc goals of the Athena mission are, among other things, to measure the
kinematics and velocity structure of intragalactic gas to map how jets from active
galactic nuclei are absorbed in the medium, and characterize ﬁlamentary structures
of the cosmic web, which requires good angular and spectral resolution [2]. The
large eﬀective area, energy range, and angular resolution will enable unprecedented
study of the emitting regions near compact objects, such as measurements of the
spin of black holes and turbulence in the accretion ﬂow [3].
Since X-ray mirrors reﬂect at grazing angles, the surface quality is very important
to how the light scatters oﬀthe mirror, and by studying the shape of the scattering,
knowledge about important features of the surface can be derived.
The purpose of this project was to study the eﬀect of a range of diﬀerent parameters,
pertaining to the coating procedure and the substrates themselves, on the scatter-
ing from a set of samples of X-ray mirrors similar to those used for ATHENA, and
analyze this scattering with well-developed scatter theories. It is important that the
scatter data can be modelled, providing a solid physics-based model of the roughness
spectrum of the interfaces of the coatings.
This project has not been one large in-depth investigation of a speciﬁc variable, but
rather a number of smaller individual albeit related studies of the eﬀect of diﬀerent
parameters pertaining to iridium coatings for the Athena mission.
This report will thus initially outline some of the most relevant theory regarding
X-rays and how they scatter, as well as a brief description of how X-ray mirrors
work and the processes they undergo doing manufacturing. Following this is a de-
scription of the instrumental setup used for experiments, as well as a walk-through
of how the experiments were performed and analyzed. Finally in the latter half of
the report, the results of my work are presented and from these, conclusions are
drawn both regarding the parameters resulting in optimal mirror performance, and
whether further study on the subject is needed.
The investigations performed were on the diﬀerence of scattering from cleaned and
non-cleaned mirror substrates, the inﬂuence of gas pressure during coating of irid-
ium, how reactive sputtering changes the characteristics of iridium coatings, and on
scatter performance for tungsten coatings as compared to iridium coatings.
All of the measured data presented in this report has been corrected for background
noise.

2.3
Bragg reﬂector
Imagine an electromagnetic wave with wavevector ⃗k as in (2.1), that scatters oﬀan
atom to obtain the new wavevector ⃗k′. The scattering vector is then ⃗Q = ⃗k−⃗k′, and
the phase diﬀerence between the incoming and outgoing wave at ⃗r is ∆φ(⃗r) = ⃗Q ·⃗r.
For a crystalline medium an incoming wave will reﬂect oﬀeach interface in the
crystal, causing interference between each of the reﬂected waves from the structure.
This interference can be constructive or destructive, depending on the phase shift in
the superposition of the reﬂected waves. For a periodic structure with lattice spacing
d, simple geometry (see Figure 2.1) yields a relationship between the wavelength of
an incoming wave and the angle of constructive interference as a function of the
d-spacing. This is known as Bragg’s law.
mλ = 2d sin α,
m ∈N
(2.12)
Figure 2.1: Illustration of reﬂection from a crystal lattice with spacing d. Here α denotes
the small grazing angle and k is the wavevector.
This principle is used in monochromators in which one wants to pick out a single
energy band from a continuous spectrum, and for high-energy telescopes where the
constructive interference is useful in increasing mirror reﬂectivity. For this purpose
what is known as an artiﬁcial Bragg reﬂector is created, in which the reﬂecting
interface structure is purposefully created in order to maximize the reﬂectivity of
the mirror.

Scattering
In practice the reﬂection oﬀa mirror is not perfect such that all photons are re-
ﬂected with an exit angle exactly equal to the angle of incidence. Rather, due to

3.1
Multilayers
On mirror substrates a heavy material is applied as a coating to increase reﬂectivity
as per (2.6). By applying several layers of coating and alternating heavy and light
materials however, an artiﬁcial lattice-like interface structure is created, in which
each of the interfaces contributes to the overall reﬂectivity of the mirror.
The simplest multilayer structure is one with constant d-spacing so the thicknesses
of the individual layers are the same throughout the coating. A more advanced
construction is a depth-grading, where the bilayer thickness is changed throughout
the coating in an eﬀort to improve the eﬀective energy range of the mirror. Using
thin bilayers at the bottom and thicker bilayers of the top of the stack will allow
the longer wavelengths to be reﬂected by the upper layers, while the more energetic
waves are reﬂected at the interfaces with smaller d-spacing at the bottom of the
stack.

coeﬃcient from a multilayer, the Fresnel equations (2.5) must be used recursively
because the wave transmitted through interface j has a reﬂection component at
interface j + 1 and so on.

3.2
Design of X-ray optics
Due to the high energy of X-rays, in X-ray optics the light is not focused through
a conventional lens at near normal incidence, but rather by reﬂection oﬀmirrored
surfaces. This requires a shallow grazing angle in order to reﬂect the X-rays, mean-
ing that such telescopes have a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent geometry than optical tele-
scopes.
3.2.1
Wolter type telescope
A number of diﬀerent X-ray telescope designs were proposed by Hans Wolter in
1952. The Wolter type I telescope design is typically used, as this design is more
compact and structurally sound than the other types. ATHENA will thus also fol-
low the design convention of a Wolter type I telescope.
In this design, two reﬂective surfaces of revolution are used to focus incoming par-
allel light rays into a detector. The two surfaces are co-axial and confocal segments
of a paraboloid and a hyperboloid that due to the consecutive reﬂections ideally
focuses the light into one focus point (Figure 3.1) [6].
Figure 3.1: Illustration of a Wolter type I X-ray telescope showing double reﬂection oﬀ
the two surfaces. The focal point of the telescope is the focal point of the hyperboloid
surface. The length of the hyperbolic section LH is constricted to be long enough that
the incident rays striking the ﬁrst part of the paraboloid will strike the last part of the
hyperboloid. Illustration from [6, p. 2].

The shallow grazing angle required means that the focal length has to be rather
large, and obtaining a good collection area is diﬃcult. Rather than increasing the
length of the mirror sections, which results in a loss of resolution, a large eﬀective
collection area can be in achieved by nesting the mirrors inside the original surface
in mirror shells that have varying grazing angles in order to achieve the same focal
point. Naturally, making the mirror shells thin allow for more shells and thus a
higher number of collected photons, but the allowed thickness of each mirror seg-
ment has a lower bound due to the substrate having to retain its geometric shape
under stress and external forces.
For the mirror substrate a number of diﬀerent materials can be used. For NuSTAR
glass substrates thermally formed over silicon dioxide mandrels were used [7], but
for ATHENA processed silicon wafers (SPO) will be used as mirror substrates. The
mirror substrates I have worked with in this project have been plain silicon wafers
of 70 × 10 × 0.75 mm, known as witness samples.
3.2.2
Silicon Pore Optics
Typically X-ray telescopes have used spokes to hold the mirrors in place, but a
new method using what is called Silicon Pore Optics (SPO) technology has been
developed, which will be used for ATHENA.
One goal in designing X-ray telescopes is to keep ﬁgure errors as small as possible
in order to reduce the Point Spread Function (PSF). A large PSF means that more
background counts will contribute to the noise on the observed source. SPO allow
for both a good angular resolution and a large collecting area. Instead of spokes to
hold the mirrors in place, ribbed silicon wafers are produced with a spacing between
the ribs allowing photons to pass through (see Figure 3.2), while the ribs themselves
act as contact strips bonding molecularly to and supporting the silicon wafer on the
next SPO.
The SPO are wedged so the pores in each stack have the same focal point, and are
curved on a mandrel to the radius of curvature that matches the surface of revolu-
tion forming the basis of the Wolter I geometry. This method of using radial ribs
rather than axial spokes provides good stiﬀness and stability of the substrates, as
well as low mass.

grazing angle and thus a larger beam footprint.

3.3
Mirror coating
X-ray mirrors are created by applying a layer of reﬂective coating on top of the mir-
ror substrate. As mentioned earlier, this reﬂective coating is typically made from
a rather heavy material, and may be part of a stack of alternating heavy and light
layers to improve overall reﬂectivity of the X-ray mirror. How this coating is applied
directly inﬂuences the roughness and therefore scattering from the mirror. Several
diﬀerent methods exist for depositing a thin ﬁlm on substrates, but for ATHENA,
DC magnetron sputtering has been chosen due to its uniformity, reliability, deposi-
tion rate, etc. [10]
3.3.1
Thin-ﬁlm sputter deposition
Sputter deposition is a common method used for depositing materials as a thin-ﬁlm
coating on substrates. The mirror substrates are placed in a vacuum chamber with
an atmosphere that contains only a small amount of argon gas, typically 3-5 mTorr.
Opposite the substrates are blocks of the coating material that are called the targets,
mounted on magnetron cathodes. Inside the cathodes, permanent magnets are sit-
uated to generate a magnetic ﬁeld around and in front of the cathodes. An electric
ﬁeld is applied that moves free electrons along the magnetic ﬁeld lines in front of
the cathodes. Interaction with the argon atoms knocks out electrons, resulting in
a plasma of positive argon ions. These positive ions are then accelerated by the
electric ﬁeld to hit the targets and, if the kinetic energy from the collision is larger
than the binding energy of the target material, atoms from the target will be ejected
and hit the substrates to form a thin ﬁlm.
During the sputter deposition the substrates are mounted on a rotating ring, moving
them in and out of the ﬁeld of sputtering from each target. In this way multilayers
can be created in one go by having the targets mounted such that each mirror al-
ternates in being coated from the targets of light and heavy materials.
Argon is an inert gas, but a reactive gas such as nitrogen may be introduced to
chemically react with the sputtered particles. This is known as reactive sputtering.
The reactive gas fraction as well as the gas pressure and a variety of other parameters
during the sputter deposition aﬀect the roughness of the coating. Varying the target-
to-substrate distance, for example, will change the deposition rate as more material
is deposited when the substrates are closer to the target. On the other hand, a
smaller distance will increase the angle through which sputtered atoms can hit the
substrate, reducing the uniformity of the coating. Another cause of possible non-
uniformity of the coating is that the ﬂux of sputtered atoms varies along the target

3.3.2
Substrate cleaning
Before applying the coating, the mirror substrates may be cleaned for impurities in
order to reduce the surface roughness of the substrate itself, as this roughness may
propagate up through the layers of coating. The substrates of the mirror samples
used in this project were delivered with a protective ﬁlm on top, that may have left
some organic material upon removal, which increases the substrate surface rough-
ness, and the SPO production process may leave contaminants and residuals that
ought to be removed.
A number of diﬀerent plasma cleaning methods exist [12]; the surface may simply
be submersed in the hot plasma, being bombarded with electrons and ions. Then
applying a slight voltage to the substrate allows one to adjust the energy ﬂux. The
energy input to the surface must surmount the bonding energy of the contaminant
to the surface, so heating can only be used to remove lightly bonded contaminants
unless the surface is heated to extreme temperatures.
Another method of cleaning is through sputtering which works by the same mecha-
nism as described in Section 3.3.1. This is naturally a non-discriminating cleaning
method, meaning that it may also remove some of the substrate material.
Finally a surface may be cleaned by plasma etching, where the plasma reacts chemi-
cally with the contaminated surface. Using oxygen plasma, one can initiate chemical
reactions between the plasma and organic compounds on the surface that may then
be removed from the surface. Naturally these cleaning methods can also be com-
bined in an eﬀort to enhance the cleaning process.

method
Analysis of the mirror samples comprises performing a series of measurements of
the reﬂectivity of the sample, and then using software to compare the measured
data with model predictions in order to quantify sample parameters such as surface
roughness, correlation length, etc.
4.1
Instrumental setup
The measurements for this project were performed in the X-ray laboratory in the
basement of DTU Space. Figure 4.1 shows an illustration of the instrumental setup
used.
X-ray reﬂectivity (XRR) is a non-destructive contact-less measurement technique
used to characterize and analyze composition and other parameters of the coating
applied to X-ray mirrors. The X-rays are produced by a rotating copper anode
emitting a bremsstrahlung spectrum. From this spectrum, a two-bounce monochro-
mator made from germanium crystals picks out the Kα 1.54 ˚A line which, according
to (2.2), corresponds to an energy of 8.04 keV.
The radiation passes through a couple of slits to reduce its divergence, travelling
for the most part through an evacuated tube to reduce air interference. Then after
bouncing oﬀthe monochromator, it passes through an aluminium ﬁlter that reduces
beam intensity through attenuation in order to ensure that the detector is not satu-
rated with too many photons, causing it to lose counts due to its dead time. Then,
after the ﬁnal ﬁlter shaping the beam, the X-rays hit the sample, pass through an-
other evacuated tube, and hit the detector placed a distance of 995 mm from the
sample. The beam divergence is 53′′1.
During specular scans, the sample is rotated by an angle θ while the detector is
rotated 2θ to keep it in the specular direction. For scattering scans the detector is
ﬁxed at an angle corresponding to the specular direction of an intensity peak, while
the sample is ”rocked” at a slight angle to produce a measurement of the scattering
in the region of the peak. This produces what is known as a rocking curve scan.

Before performing scatter measurements, the sample must be aligned properly in
the beam. This is done by ﬁrst placing the detector at 2θ = 0, measuring the free
(unobstructed) beam intensity, and then moving the sample into the beam until it
covers half the beam. Similarly the vertical position is moved until the sample is
aligned vertically with the beam. At this point the sample covers half the beam.
The sample is then rotated to the position where the detector records the most
counts, which indicates that the sample is parallel to the beam. This aligned proce-
dure is done with an integration window on the detector of 20 channels to exclude
non-specular counts during alignment.

The results of a scatter measurement can be seen in the following chapters as well

How the beam scatters is highly dependent on how the roughness correlates, so these
results can be used to determine ξ. A characteristic feature apparent here is the
Yoneda peak which is an increase in scattering intensity when the incidence or exit
angle matches the critical angle [16].
In reciprocal space the specular XRR scan is performed along the q-axis and scatter
scans are done in the transverse direction as indicated at the peaks on Figure 2.3.

measurements were generally done at θ ≈800-1000 mdeg, where the normalized
intensity was around 5 × 10−2.
For scatter measurements I had a mechanical slit installed in front of the detector,
allowing only exposure of roughly 35 channels on the detector, and used an integra-

4.4
The method of inverse problems
Once suﬃcient data has been recorded, the theoretical models must be compared
with the actual measurements to test the theory and determine accurate model pa-
rameters. This is done using what is known as the method of inverse problems,
in which the data simulated using the theoretical model is compared to the real
data set, and the diﬀerence between the two is represented by an error function.
Assuming the applied model is accurate for the analyzed type of data, the model
parameters can be varied until the error function takes on a minimum, indicating
that the best possible model parameters have been found.
This method is thus useful for determining unknown parameters of a physical sys-
tem for which one has developed an accurate model.
Several diﬀerent approaches to inverse problems exist. Some methods, such as the
Monte Carlo method randomly search the parameter space, meaning that they are
quite ineﬃcient, while other methods that take into account the shape of the error
function may become trapped in a local minimum without ﬁnding the best global
parameters.

4.5
Data analysis software
To analyze my data I have used a program called Reﬂectivity and Fluorescence Sim-
ulation (REFS). This software uses an algorithm called diﬀerential evolution that
employs somewhat random searches in the parameter space, but uses recombination
to direct the next search, focusing on regions of the parameter space that in the cur-
rent generation looks promising [5]. Since data from X-ray scattering spans several
orders of magnitude, the error function employed by the program is logarithmic in
nature, which reduces the relative importance of the very intense peak occurring in
the specular direction.
The model the program uses for calculating intensity in the inverse problem is one
in which the Fresnel equations are used recursively for each interface in the sample
multilayer as per (3.1). This reﬂectivity is modiﬁed to account for scattering due to
interface roughness as in the theory developed by Sinha et al.
Typically the IDL software package IMD is used for modelling and analyzing mul-
tilayer ﬁlms. I compared a number of ﬁts to specular scans with REFS and IMD,
and found that the agreement between the ﬁts was good enough to be practically
identical. Furthermore REFS allows for analysis of scattering data and allows the
user customizability in the roughness parameters.
4.5.1
Analysis procedure
One should already have a good idea of the layer thickness of the coating applied
to the substrate from the sputtering process, however small variations to the actual
layer thicknesses will always be present.
Using REFS, a model of the sample is created in which the layer parameters Ma-
terial, Thickness, Density7, and Roughness are set to initial values chosen based on
what the expected parameter values are.
The specular θ-2θ scan is then imported to the program as a reference data set to
which the model can be ﬁtted. Not ﬁxing the aforementioned parameters, but rather
choosing an initial model value and setting a range of values within the parameter
space, REFS can iterate through diﬀerent parameters within the speciﬁed range
to obtain a model that correlates with the measured data. Since REFS uses the
method of inverse problems to obtain optimal model parameters, it is highly depen-
dent on the applied model being accurate. For this reason, a variety of information
about the measurement setup such as beam divergence, slit sizes etc. must also be

speciﬁed. REFS will then tweak the model parameters within the speciﬁed ranges
in order to obtain a model for the specular scan that that best ﬁts the data.
Once the optimal model parameters have been determined, the scatter measurement
is imported as a reference for which one can vary the roughness correlation length
and fractal exponent to tweak the model to yield the same kind of scattering as the
one measured from the sample.
Since roughness, roughness correlation length, and the fractal exponent are all cor-
related, changing any one of the parameters will have an eﬀect on the others. For
this reason I ﬁtted in an iterative process, in which I ﬁrst used the specular scan to
obtain an estimate of the roughness. Then keeping this parameter ﬁxed, I switched
to the scatter measurement and varied the roughness correlation length and fractal
exponent to obtain a good ﬁt to the scatter shape. This also changes the modelled
specular reﬂectivity, which is why a new ﬁt must be made to get a more accurate
estimate of the roughness.
Iterating through this process with continuously smaller changes to roughness, cor-
relation length, and fractal exponent, I converge on the ﬁnal best-ﬁt parameters of
the roughness parameters for a given mirror sample.

CHAPTER10
Conclusion and future work
10.1
Conclusion
A number of investigations on the eﬀects of diﬀerent parameters of mirror substrates
and coating procedures were investigated. It was found that non-specular scattering
is mitigated from mirrored silicon wafers that were plasma cleaned prior to coating
with single layer iridium ﬁlms.
Cleaned witness samples coated with single layer tungsten ﬁlms showed scatter-
ing comparable to that from non-cleaned iridium ﬁlms. This is either caused by
improper handling of the substrates post cleaning and coating, or possibly due to
iridium sputtering simply creating a more even ﬁlm than tungsten.
An investigation of the surface characteristics of uncoated substrates using an 8
keV beam was inconclusive, with no precise determination of surface roughnesses of
either witness sample substrates or SPO substrates.
It was shown that the heavy iridium atoms are largely unaﬀected by variations in
pressure between 3 and 5 mTorr during sputter deposition, although it is speculated
that deposition of lighter materials suﬀer under increased pressure.
The use of reactive nitrogen when sputtering iridium was found to have an adverse
eﬀect on mirror quality. In case of positive inﬂuence on reactive sputtering of the
lighter material in a multilayer, it might be beneﬁcial to alternate between reactive
and non-reactive sputtering for each layer, although this would decrease the rate of
mirror manufacturing.
As a minor investigation in connection with commissioning of a new coating chamber
in the Netherlands, it was shown that the cleaning procedure used by Von Ardenne
GmbH is unsatisfactory, so this should be rectiﬁed before mirror manufacturing for
ATHENA commences.
Finally a more wholesome method for characterizing scatter from a sample was at-
tempted in creation of a 2D scatter map, but although the extracted specular cut
from the map was a slight improvement over the usual direct method of measure-
ment, the great enhancement of noise in the other channels of the detector means
that extracted transverse scatter cuts are much more diﬃcult to analyze than cor-
responding scatter measurements performed during a rocking curve scan.

 Type wa to check initial sample positions and change them if necessary.
 Note the angle θB for the Bragg peaks of interest, and move sample and
detector to measure the specular direction at the Bragg peak by
>
mv th -<θB>
>
mv tth -<2θB>
 Do another photon count lspc 2 and change the ﬁlter to obtain around 1000
cps at the peak.
 Manually change the mechanical slit in front of the detector. Close Shutter
1, press Door and enter the X-ray Laboratory. Use a caliper to ensure that
the slit width is changed to 1.5 mm, which corresponds to approximately 35
channels in the detector. Exit the X-ray Laboratory and open shutter 1 again.
 Do a scan with the detector to ﬁnd the position of the Bragg peak by
>
uscan tth -<2θB + 100> -<2θB −100> 20 1
which will scan a region of 0.2 degrees around the expected position of the
bragg peak in 20 steps of 1 second. Increase the interval if the peak is not
found.

 Move the detector to the center of mass of the peak and use
>
lspc 2

>
xrej <CCH-25> <CCH+25>
