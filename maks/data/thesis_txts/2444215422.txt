Summary
Abstract
The purpose of this project was to analyse PIV measurements from the streamwise plane
of a turbulent round jet in water by using the Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD)
method. This method decomposes vector fields in terms of eigenfunctions (or modes)
with corresponding eigenvalues, which quantifies the turbulence kinetic energy related
to the respective function. These modes are used to investigate the underlying dynamics
in the flow. There have previously been conducted similar experiments, among others,
Wanstrom 2009 and Hodžic et al. 2017. This project differs from the previous ones by
first of all being time-resolved, but also by being conducted in an aquarium with water
as fluid instead of a free jet in air.
In this project, two experiments were conducted. First, an octagonal aquarium was
used with a width of 20 jet diameters (D), a height of 37 D and a Reynolds number
of 2000 at the jet nozzle. The measurements from this experimental setup indicated
that both the velocity fields and the Reynolds stresses were affected by the enclosure
of the aquarium, and deviated significantly from the expected Reynolds stress profiles
of axisymmetric free jets. Because of this, an additional experiment was conducted in
a larger tank with dimensions 35 D x 35 D x 76 D and a Reynolds number of 13500.
In this experiment, the velocity fields and Reynolds stresses were more similar to the
measurements by Wanstrom 2009 and Hodžic et al. 2017, except near the centreline of
the jet where the stresses were higher than those in a free jet.
The data is then analysed using POD. The analysis is initially used to create the energy
spectra in the flow. The spectra are also similar to previous experiments and show how
the limited field of view (FOV) affects the energy distribution among the scales. Then,
the Reynolds stresses are recreated using POD modes. Here it is observed that the most
energetic mode can recreate this characteristic shape of the Reynolds stresses with a
peak in the centre of the jet. The energy distribution among frequencies, wavenumbers,
and modes was also investigated. It is concluded that it was possible to reconstruct most
of the flow using only a few modes, frequencies and wavenumbers. If the wavenumbers
and frequencies are integrated out of the system, it is shown that the first mode has 70
% of the energy in the flow, and the first ten modes have 96 % of the energy. The faster
convergence rate is somewhat expected due to lower Reynolds number in the current
flow, which reduces the range of scales in the eigenspectrum. Finally, the velocity field
is reconstructed using the first couple of modes.

Summary
Resumé
Formålet med dette projekt er at undersøge planen i strømningsretningen på en sym-
metrisk turbulent jet i vand ved hjælp af en metode kaldet Proper Orthogonal Decompo-
sition (POD). Denne metode kan bruges til at opdele strømningen i en række funktioner
med hver deres tilhørende mængde energi, der så kan bruges til at undersøge de under-
liggende dynamikker i strømningen. Der er tidligere foretaget lignende eksperimenter
blandt andet af, Wanstrom 2009 og Hodžic et al. 2017. Dette projekt adskiller sig fra
de foregående ved at være tidsafhængigt og desuden være målt i et akvarium i vand i
stedet for en fri jet i luft.
To forskellige eksperimenter er blevet foretaget. Først blev et 8-kantet akvarium brugt,
der er 20 jet diametre (D) bredt og 37 D højt med et Reynolds tal fra jetten på 2000.
Målingerne fra denne forsøgsopstilling indikerer imidlertid at både hastighedsfelterne
og Reynolds stressene bliver påvirket af siderne og bunden på akvariet. På grund af
dette blev der også foretaget målinger i et større akvarium med dimensionerne 35 D x
35 D x 76 D og et Reynolds tal på 13500. I dette eksperiment ligner hastighedsfelterne
og Reynolds stressene mere hvad der tidligere er målt på lignende opstillinger, på nær
stressene tæt på jet centerlinjen, der var højere end hvad der forventes i en fri jet.
Derefter blev dataen fra eksperimentet analyseret ved hjælp af POD metoden. Analysen
blev først brugt til at lave energispektrene i flowet. Disse minder også om tidligere resul-
tater, men det blev gjort klart at størrelsen på måleområdet har indflydelse på hvordan
energien er fordelt i spektret. Reynolds stressene blev derefter genopbygget ud fra POD
- funktionerne. Her kan man se at den første, mest energirige funktion står bag den
overordnede karakteristika for stressene. Dette indikerer at det er en enkelt struktur
i strømningen der skaber disse stress. Derefter blev energien i de enkelte funktioner,
frekvenser og bølgetal undersøgt. Det konkluderes at man kan rekonstruere det meste af
strømningen ud fra et par enkelte funktioner, bølgetal og frekvenser. Hvis man integrer
bølgetallene og frekvenserne ud, viser det sig at 70 % af energien i flowet er i den første
funktion, og 96 % i de første 10 funktioner. Til sidst blev hastighedsfeltet genskabt ud
fra de første par modes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The purpose of this master’s thesis is to analyse the basis functions in an axisymmetric
turbulent jet by using the Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) method.
This
analysis decomposes the flow into optimised modes that can be used to investigate the
underlying dynamics of the flow and therefore the behaviour of turbulence in general.
The reason behind analysing turbulence specifically in a round jet is because of its
symmetry and because the jet reaches a fully developed state at a relatively close distance
from the nozzle compared to other flows. Another more practical reason is that the flow
is easy to access and measure using optical equipment.
Many experiments have been conducted on turbulent jets in the past, among others,
Wanstrom 2009, and Hodžic et al. 2017. They have both captured the velocity field in
the streamwise direction and analysed the data using POD. However, both experiments
measured the time-independent snapshots of the instantaneous velocity field. The goal
of this project has been to include time in the analysis as well. Therefore, a series of
independent video samples were analysed instead of pairs of images. The idea is that a
time-dependent decomposition of the flow can give insight into how turbulent structures
behave and change over time.
The velocity field of the jet is measured using Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV). For
the POD analysis to work, it is essential to capture most of the turbulent kinetic energy.
Hence, it is necessary to capture most of the fluid motion also across the radial extent
of the jet, which in turn requires the PIV measurement area to be large. Increasing the
field of view for measurements reduces the light the camera sensor receives. This issue
has previously been solved by pulsing the laser, thus increasing its power. However, a
pulsed laser capable of illuminating the flow while capturing a video was not available,
so a continuous laser is used instead.
A consequence of this is that the seeding particles typically used in a jet do not reflect
enough light to be seen by the cameras. The original plan for this project was to solve
this issue using air or helium filled soap bubbles as seeding particles. Unfortunately, this
turned out to be too difficult, since constructing a seeding generator took longer than
expected, see appendix A. Therefore, the experiment was conducted in water instead of
air, since heavier, more reflective particles can be used in water. The jet in water behaved
differently than similar jets in air, likely due to the confined space of the aquarium and
the different nozzle geometries. Therefore, a large part of the project has been devoted
to analysing the characteristics of a confined jet in water and compare them to a free
jet in air.

1.1
What is turbulence?
Turbulence can be described as chaotic and unpredictable three-dimensional movement
patterns that occur in fluids at certain velocities. A perfectly structured and organised
flow can suddenly undergo irreversible changes due to an increase in velocity. Turbulence
dominates several characteristics of the flow and affects a variety of parameters such as
heat transfer, drag, and mixing. There are many unsolved problems with turbulence,
but at the core of it is a lack of understanding of how to characterise what one observes
in turbulent flows. Furthermore, the system of equations describing a fluid is notoriously
difficult to solve or model numerically.
When looking at a turbulent flow, such as the smoke from a chimney or candlelight,
one can see different structures appearing and disappearing periodically. However, pre-
dicting and reproducing such phenomena has proven difficult without resolving to use
Direct Numerical Simulations. So gaining insight into this natural phenomenon is of
great importance not only for scientists but also for engineers, who are forced to use
inaccurate turbulence models when simulating flows. Progress in this field will lead to
better weather forecasts, better designs of automobiles and air crafts, better prediction
of mixing in chemistry and much more. All this makes turbulence a fascinating research
topic.

2.1.3
Review of Kolmogorov’s 1941 theory
If one considers a flow with a high Reynolds number, a wide range different sized struc-
tures can be observed appearing and disappearing in the flow. The fundamental idea by
Kolmogorov is that kinetic energy enters the turbulence through the largest eddies in the
flow, Kolmogorov 1941. The largest eddies are anisotropic and defined in the spectral
region known as the energy-containing range. They transfer their energy through an
inviscid process to smaller eddies. This will continue until they become so small, their
motion is turned into heat due to the viscosity of the fluid, Richardson 1922. This is
known as the Richardson cascade. The overall idea is therefore that large eddies created
by the mean flow, will gradually break down, and create smaller eddies, that break down
as well, creating this cascade of eddies. At some point in this process, the eddies will
”forget” their initial condition and reach the so-called universal equilibrium range. This
means that even though the large eddies are anisotropic, the small scales will lose their
directional bias due to the chaotic breakdown, Pope 2000. According to Kolmogorov, not
only directional information but also statistics of these small scales are lost. He assumes
that this small scale motion evolves much faster than the large anisotropic eddies, which
causes the statistical equilibrium, Kolmogorov 1941. Therefore every flow is in some
sense universal at these scales, according to Kolmogorov. However, many experiments
do not agree with this claim, Vassilicos 2015.

dissipation range. In the inertial sub-range, the turbulence is unaffected by both the
large eddies and viscosity. A direct consequence of this is the aforementioned Zeroth law

time scale of τ = l

2.2

The turbulence research group at the Technical University of Denmark has for a long time
used the turbulent round jet as a platform for research into the behaviour of turbulence.
The purpose of this section is to explain the general characteristics of the jet, and how
it can be used to study the non-linear and non-local interactions of turbulence. Jets
belong to a group of flows called free shear flows, which are characterised by absent or
at least distant boundaries but still having a mean velocity gradient, Davidson 2015. An
example of a turbulent jet is shown in figure 2.1. The fluid moves out of the nozzle, with
uniform velocity, the so-called top hat profile. In the open area, the jet will exchange
momentum with the surroundings. This exchange results in a phenomenon known as
jet entrainment. When moving downstream, the momentum flux will stay constant, but
the total volume flow will increase, see Davidson 2015. This also increases the width
of the mixing layer, spreading the jet in the surrounding fluid. The jet is divided into
three regions: The potential core, where the flow is laminar and uniform, the developing
region, where velocity profile and other characteristics are gradually changing, and the
fully developed region, where the jet has reached a state where it is in self-preserved
equilibrium.
In the experiments performed at DTU, the jet is assumed to be fully
developed after 30 diameters downstream5, but this depends on the geometry of the
nozzle. In this region, many flow parameters such as turbulent kinetic energy production
and dissipation scale proportionally with the downstream coordinate, George 1992. The
jet half width, δ1/2, shown in figure 2.1, is an example of this.

2.3
Proper Orthogonal Decomposition
So far the focus has been defining the scales of motion in the flow using a Fourier basis.
This limits the analysis to completely homogeneous flows, which is a characteristic that
very few real flows have. Therefore, a tool known as proper orthogonal decomposition
is introduced.
This method decomposes a given vector field into orthogonal modes,
each with a corresponding eigenvalue. The sum of the eigenvalues is equal to the total
energy of the flow, so the goal is now to be able to decompose the flow into a few
modes that hopefully contains most of the energy, and reconstruct the flow with these
modes. If the analysis is successful, these modes will contain information about the
underlying structures in the flow. First, the general theory behind Proper Orthogonal
Decomposition is explained. The method is based on function spaces and inner products
of functions, so a brief explanation is given in appendix B.2.
Then, the composite
decomposition is implemented numerically.7

in water also has some limitations, which is the reason why air was the preferred medium
initially. This is investigated and discussed further in the following sections.
3.1
Particle Image Velocimetry
Particle image velocimetry (PIV) is a measuring technique, which unlike hot wires and
laser Doppler anemometry(LDA), can capture the instantaneous velocity field of the
entire plane of a flow.2
For most applications, the method consists of adding tracer
particles to a flow and then illuminating them, for instance with a laser sheet. The
scattered light is recorded by a camera in order to find a velocity field. The displacement
of the particles between each image is evaluated by calculating the cross-correlation
between sections of the images. For this to be accurate, filtering and post-processing of
the images are also required. If done correctly it is possible to obtain a velocity field,

either as a single snapshot or as a time dependent record by using several images.
3.1.1
Seeding particles
Rather than measuring the velocity of the fluid itself, PIV measures the tracer particles
inserted into the flow, which means the velocity is measured indirectly. Therefore, the
particle has to follow the flow as closely as possible. Furthermore, the more light the
particles scatter, the more information can be obtained, which in turn will reduce noise
in the images. The first step is to pick the right type of seeding. This can be difficult,
and for a detailed description, refer Raffel et al. 2018, but the main concerns are:
• The gravitational force on the particles due to the difference in density between
fluid and particles. This is easier to obtain in liquid flows compared to gas flows.
• Response time of the particles is also a concern if the density difference is too large.
If the flow is unsteady, and the density of the particles is high, they are no longer
able to follow the flow properly.
• The light scattering is also a concern.
More reflected light results in a higher
contrast between the particles and the surroundings in the image. Here parameters
such as the reflectiveness of the surface, the particle size, as well as the camera
and laser orientation are important.
Other issues also exist, such as generating and homogeneously distributing the seeding
particles in the measuring volume. These issues vary depending on the type of fluid, and
experimental setup, and will be described for the specific setups.

• Particle size should be 2-3 pixels in diameter on the resulting image. This helps

• There should be around ten particles per interrogation region, for the correlation
to work properly. If there are too few or many, the correlation will be noisy.
• The seeding should be uniform in the flow, to make sure all areas of the flow field
are calculated with a similar error margin.
• The seeding particles should both be able to follow the flow and scatter light.
• The maximum displacement should be around 1/3 of the interrogation area side
length. Otherwise, the correlation could overlook particles that have moved too
far away. The corresponding sample frequency should be adjusted to comply with
this requirement.
• The sampling frequency should not be too high, in order to avoid peak locking.
This is when the displacement of a particle is between 2 pixels, which can filter
out some of the lower velocities in the flow.

3.2.3
Filtering outliers
Before any data is analysed, it is important to consider how to deal with odd data points.
First, the raw data is loaded into MATLAB and checked for errors. Several data points
==PAGE:51==
3.2 PIV measurements on a Low Reynolds number steady round jet
35
are marked as NaN (not a number) instead of velocity. It has been discussed in the
literature on how to deal with this, Wanstrom 2009. She concludes that one can replace
the spurious data by a mean value of the velocity at that location. The problem is that
the mean is not necessarily known beforehand, and finding it is a part of the reason
behind performing measurements in the first place. Another strategy is to set the wrong
data equal to zero. The resulting mean is then divided by a factor that accounts for the
number of zeros in the calculation.
However, a different approach which the author finds more accurate is to replace wrong
data with neighbouring data points, in both time and space. This method seems better
at conserving coherent structures. For instance, imagine a vortex convected downstream.
The velocity field of this specific structure is different from the mean flow. So replacing
a point inside the vortex with the mean flow can result in a large velocity gradient be-
tween the data points. This method also has its drawbacks. If two or more neighbouring
points are corrupted, the interpolation can become very unreliable.
Another more significant issue is data points where the velocity seems unrealistic by ei-
ther being too large or small or pointing in the other direction compared to neighbouring
data points both in time and space. This is dealt with by filtering data that are several
times larger in magnitude than the mean velocity in the streamwise direction. This
significantly improves the statistics of the data. This does not solve this problem with
too small velocities or if the direction of the vector is different than the neighbouring
points. However, filtering too much could end up removing some actual features of the
flow.
In both the case of NaN data points or data exceeding the limit of the filter, they are
replaced by a linear average of the neighbouring data points. If the first or last data
point in the record has to be replaced, it is replaced with its one neighbour.

3.2.7
Errors and uncertainties related to the first
experiment
As mentioned several times in this chapter, the experiment did not perform as expected.
First of all, the Reynolds stresses were much larger when compared to other experiments
on jets, and also rather asymmetric. There are also several other possible errors and
uncertainties related to the experiment, which unfortunately could have had major im-
pacts on the obtained results. Since the experiment has been performed in water, the
obtained images could have been distorted by bubbles either in the flow itself or stuck
to the glass in the aquarium. The bubbles will create areas in the flow with little or
no velocity, which in turn will result in large gradients, which can skew the Reynolds
stresses.
Furthermore, the bubbles will often appear concentrated in an area of the
water tank, which means the noise cannot be regarded as white noise. A similar source
of error that was encountered is the reflection of light either on different metallic parts
in the experimental setup or from outside sources. This source of error was solved by
covering all such surfaces in either dark cloth or scotch tape. The experiment was also
performed inside a tent to prevent this kind of light pollution. These sources of errors
affected many of the preliminary experiments but were gradually reduced to the point
where it hopefully no longer affects the measurements.
Another source of errors the experiment suffer from is the formation of large particles in
the flow. After the experiments had run for some time, large particles (when compared
to seeding particles), were formed in the water tanks. It is uncertain how or what these
particles were. It was suggested by Madsen and Dalgaard 2018, that the particles were
algae, but it seemed unlikely since they started to appear after only 3-4 hours. Another
possibility is that the particles are solidified calcium and other similar minerals in the
water. The effect on the experiment was minimised by changing the water several times
every day, but these large particles could still have affected the velocity records since
they are assumed not to follow the flow as good as the seeding particles, which means
they could affect the autocorrelation of the PIV software. Another source of error dis-
cussed here is from the laser and the attached beam splitter. When post-processing the
images it became clear that the light intensity varied along the measuring area. For
instance in figure E.2, it is possible to spot several almost vertical lines with different
light intensities. Not much could be done about this since it was the only equipment
available at the time.
Finally, it was observed that over time, large structures appeared in the water tank,
such as a single or dual vortex, as sketched in figures 3.14a and 3.14b. These large
structures sometimes appeared in the tank after many hours of measurement, but since
the water tank was covered in black cloth, they were not always discovered until it was
too late. Whenever they were spotted, the experiment was recalibrated and restarted,
but they could still have appeared periodically in the data used in this report. This
could be caused by the geometry of the water tank, and especially its boundaries. It
was therefore decided to investigate this further. One way of calculating the effect of the
boundaries is to estimate the loss of momentum of a steady round jet. A model proposed

3.3.6
Composite decomposition
The composite decomposition is now performed. This means that the flow is decomposed
into Fourier modes in time and the streamwise direction and POD modes in the radial
direction. The purpose of this section is to analyse the results of the decomposition
and find out if it can be used to gain insight into the underlying functions in the flow.
As described in section 2.3, the POD decomposes the flow into eigenvectors or modes,
each with a corresponding eigenvalue. This eigenvalue corresponds to the energy of the
mode and therefore tells how much it contributes to the characteristics of the given flow.
Whether or not these modes correspond to the vaguely defined coherent structures in
the flow is debatable, see George 2013. However, they can be used to tell for instance if
single or multiple modes cause the different characteristics that are observed in the flow.

since it is difficult to plot four dimensions properly, the eigenvalues are integrated over

3.3.10
Issues with the current implementation of POD
After several conversations with supervisor Azur Hodzic throughout this project, it has
become clear to the author that the current method most people (including this author)
have used to implement POD might be too simplistic. A mentioned in section 2.3, when
changing coordinate system in a volume integral, one needs to multiply the integral with
the determinant of the Jacobian. In the case of similarity coordinates, the Jacobian
matrix becomes:
J =
[ dx
dξ
dx
dη
dr
dξ
dr
dη
]
(3.19)
From x = Deξ + x0 and r = ηADeξ, the determinant of Jacobian is det(J) = AD2e2ξ.
This is different from what has previous been used in literature but it was discovered too
late to be properly implemented and tested in the code, so a simpler but not completely
correct Jacobian, |J| = η was used instead. Another issue is that all dimensions are
non-dimensional except time. It has not yet been discussed what the proper approach
could be. Another issue is that currently the analysis has been performed in similarity
space. The Navier - Stokes equation is not the same as in Cartesian, so it is risky to draw
too many parallels between the spaces. Another issue with the POD implementation is
regarding windowing. When looking at figure 3.22a, one should immediately be worried
since the energy is infinite in the streamwise direction for a jet that has been running
indefinitely. This goes directly against the assumption in equation 2.55 being a part
of L2 space.
The final consequence of this is that Fourier modes might not be an
optimal basis as ”proven” in equation 2.46. Rather, the optimal basis might have to
take into consideration this violation of the L2 space.
Therefore, the optimal basis
would instead be something where its determinant of the Jacobian, results in a sinusoidal
with decreasing amplitude. However, it was decided to go with the more traditional
implementation because of the time restraints of a master thesis.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to analyse an axisymmetric turbulent jet using the
Proper Orthogonal Decomposition method. The original plan was to measure a free jet in
air, but due to the limitations of the available equipment, the experiment was performed
on a jet in an aquarium instead. The jet in water was measured by using Particle Image
Velocimetry (PIV), in two different experiments. The first experiment was in a small
octagonal water tank with a Reynolds number of around 2000. The Reynolds stresses
were calculated at several downstream positions. However, the stresses behaved very
differently when compared to similar experiments. One explanation for this could be the
confined space of the water tank, and the large momentum loss from the side walls. The
other experiment was performed in a water tank with larger dimensions. This gave some
results that more closely resembled a free jet. Here the Reynolds stresses were also overall
much more similar to what can be observed in a free jet in air. However, one exception
was near the centreline of the jet, where the stresses ended in a characteristic peak
around η = 0. Hence, the hypothesis that the difference between the two experiments
is caused by the different geometry of the aquariums seems likely.
The next part of the analysis was the composite decomposition of the obtained
data. First, a spectral analysis was performed where the energy and power spectra were
created.
Overall they resembled other similar experiments quite well, and they also
showed how the smaller field of view and larger interrogation area affected the measured
energy. This could also have led to unwanted effects such as spectral leakage. The
POD analysis showed that 70 % of the energy was contained in the first mode and that
the first ten modes contain 95 % of the energy in the flow. Next, Reynolds stresses
were analysed again, this time by reconstructing them with different combinations of
wavenumbers, frequencies and POD modes. Here it was discovered that the very first
mode, wavenumber, and frequency was responsible for the peak in the Reynolds stresses
near the centreline. This could indicate that is it a single large structure in the flow that
causes this peak to appear. Afterwards, first 28 eigenvectors for the first wavenumber
and frequency were plotted.
They indicated that the first energy containing modes
consisted of large movement patterns and the less energetic modes consisted of a more
oscillation motion. Then, the contours of the real part of the first five modes were plotted
for frequencies and wavenumbers. Both plots showed a large peak similar to previous
experiments between η = 1 and η = 2 for the first mode, but the rest were noisier and
challenging to interpret. Finally, the instantaneous velocity was recreated for a single
measurement and several of the most significant modes as well as combinations of modes,
frequencies and wavenumbers were presented. This revealed some underlying structures

Future work
Originally, the main experiment in this project should have been measurements on an
axisymmetric turbulent free jet in the air, seeded with air or helium filled soap bubbles.
Unfortunately, it turned out to be too difficult to conduct in the short time frame of a
master’s thesis. So this experiment has still not been performed. There are also several
other areas of this project that could be improved:
• Further investigation of the effect of the sampling rate: A rather high sampling
frequency was used in this experiment. This was among others evident in the
power spectrum where most of the higher frequencies seemed noisy.
This is a
trade-off, however, and when the measurements were performed, it seemed like
the right frequency when looking at particle displacement manually. Still, testing
the effect of the sampling frequency in more detail could be a small project in the
future. If the sampling time can be reduced the total amount of data will also
decrease, which reduces computational time.
• A more extensive time-resolved analysis: There is still valuable information hidden
in the obtained data. Especially with regards to comparing the spatial dimension
with the temporal.
• Full decomposition of the jet in all four dimensions: The azimuthal velocity was
not analysed in this report due to the limitations of the equipment available, and
it was too complex to do in 5 months.
• Optimizing the software: The numerical implementation of the composite decom-

results are shown here to aid future students with this kind of experiments. An issue
with standard a standard PIV setup is the limit to both the frame rate of the captured
images, and to the size of the measuring area. When the frame rates are increased, the
shutter time of the camera decreases, which means the pixel sensor receives less light.
This makes the particles harder to detect. Another issue is that when the measuring
area is increased, the particles will be too small to detect. These two issues can be
solved by introducing a larger particle. It reflects more light, and can be observed from
a larger distance. One solution to this problem is to use bubbles either filled with air or
Helium. They will have a diameter between 0.5 to 5 mm which results in up around 104
times more light reflected, Caridi 2018, when comparing with standard seeding particles
in air. The method is relatively new, and initially it was not recommended for quanti-
tative measurements, since the bubbles could not accurately follow the flow, Kerho and
Bragg 1994. However, recently, many studies have proven otherwise, Caridi 2018. These
papers all concluded that the measurements were in fact accurate, as long as the exper-
imenter took extra care into the fabrication of the bubbles, in terms of density, number
of bubbles per second, etc. Furthermore, the buoyancy of the particles is not the only
issue. The size of the particles will also determine which fluid structures are observable
Faleiros et al. 2018. Currently there exists few commercial setups for bubble generation,
so the papers previously cited have used partially in house build setups. This means a
lot of effort has been put into creating a similar setup, and it is at the time of writing
not ready for experimental measurements.

D.1
Energy density spectra
Even though the analysis of the Reynolds stresses were a bit discouraging, it was decided
to continue with POD analysis, by reusing the snap shots from the Reynolds stress
analysis.
First step of the decomposition described in section 2.3 was to create the
autocorrelation matrices of the Fourier transformed velocities, see equation 2.54. The
energy spectrum for the centreline of the velocity field is equal to Φ(1)
ij (κm, 1, 1), since it
is equal to the velocity squared for all wave numbers. The result is shown in figure D.1.
Here it fairly easy to see the aforementioned ranges in the energy spectrum. The first
third of the spectrum correspond to the energy containing range. Then at around κ =
100, the inertial sub-range begins, where the energy is transferred with the characteristic
-5/3 slope. Around κ = 300, the angle of the slope starts to change, which could mean
that the cut of frequency is reached. Since the resolution of the plot is unable to resolve
the dissipative scales, it was also not expected to be visible in this plot. When compared
to other energy spectra, for instance, Wanstrom 2009 or Hodžic et al. 2017, the overall
shape is similar, but the total energy is higher, which seems reasonable considering the
high Reynolds stresses. Furthermore, the energy spectrum is measured in parts of the
developing region of the jet, where the flow behaves differently. Another reason could
be the windowing effect caused by the limit of the field of view. If an eddie is larger
than the field of view, the Fourier transform will fail to capture it. This means that
the energy from the large eddy, which corresponds to a low wavenumber, will instead be
redistributed to higher wavenumbers. This effect is known as spectral leakage. So if the
field of view is too small, the energy will be distributed wrongly. Another reason behind
the differences between this energy spectrum and, Hodžic et al. 2017 and Wanstrom
2009, could be the different sources of noise. This includes the laser sheet, which could
have several black spots, air bubbles on the side of the water tank, low light sensitivity
in the cameras, or even algae in the water.

D.2
Composite decomposition
Now the full composite decomposition of the jet is performed based on the theory de-
scribed in section 2.3. The purpose of this section is to gain insight into the underlying
dynamics of the flow.
Since the flow is decomposed into modes per wavenumber, it is informative to plot
the eigenvalue, λ, for each mode and wavenumber, since this corresponds to the energy
in each. The eigenvalue is a function og both the POD number, n, and the streamwise
wavenumber κ. The eigenvector created by the POD can be seen as the building block
for the flow, where the value of the eigenvalue determines how much vector contributes
to the overall flow. Whether these modes actually correspond to the coherent structures
is debatable, see George 1988, but it is certain that they reveal the overall structure of
the flow.
First the distribution of energy is shown in figure D.2a and D.2b. Most of the energy
seems to be contained in the large scales (small wavenumbers) and in the first couple of
POD modes. On a log grid, the decline in energy per POD mode is easier to observe, and
the decline is steady throughout the entire spectrum. Figure D.3a shows the energy per
mode, integrated over wave number. The first mode contains 30 % of the total energy
in the flow, the second contains 12 %, the third contains 7.6 % and the fourth contains
6 %. Figure D.3b shows the cumulative energy of the modes. The plot shows that the
first 5 modes contains 61 % of the energy, and the first 10 modes contains 78 % of the
energy. This means it possible recreate most of the flow using only a few modes. Similar
