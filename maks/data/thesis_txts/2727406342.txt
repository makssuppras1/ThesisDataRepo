Abstract
Electroencephalography (EEG) data can provide important information regarding our
health and well-being. It is a recording of the brain’s electrical signals and EEG is used
for applications ranging from diagnosing and monitoring illnesses to providing paralyzed
people with the ability to control computers with their brains. These signals are relatively
simple to capture however, they are often noisy and polluted with artifacts.
Another
matter that affects the EEG reading is the subject variability as people’s recordings can
be starkly different from each other. Removing the subject variability by disentangling
subject and task responses could lead to better-generalized models across subjects and
improvements in the accuracy of the EEG readings. This project aimed to investigate the
possibility of disentangling EEG sleep data, a subject from their sleep stage, using novel
voice conversion methods that utilize deep auto-encoder embedded with vector quanti-
zation. It examines the effects of bottleneck design choices, different input lengths, 5,
10, and 30 seconds, and input-forms, spectograms images compared to raw waveforms.
These factors are evaluated using subject and sleep stage identification as well as a t-SNE
demonstration. The results did not show a strong disentanglement of the subjects from
the sleep stages. Different bottleneck constraints did show some subtle effects, namely a
positive one from increasing the number of codes in the bottleneck’s codebook. Shorter
samples were found to have a worse sleep stage accuracy than longer samples while raw
input models appear to be harder to train than the others.

1
Introduction
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a method used to examine brain activity by measuring
the electric signals it generates. EEG has been used for the diagnosis and monitoring
of numerous diseases and illnesses such as epilepsy, brain tumors, dementia, Parkinson’s
disease (PD), and stroke, as well as inspect sleep patterns and disorders [1]. EEG has also
been used as a part of brain-computer interfaces for instance in motor imagery (MI) [2]
and for the detection of visual stimulation for people with motor impairments [3].
When recording EEG signals, electrodes are placed on the subject’s scalp. These signals
are relatively simple to obtain however, they tend to be noisy, contain various artifacts,
and are complicated by subject variability, people’s own personal nosiness so to speak.
It has been shown that across-subject variation in EEG variability and signal strength is
stronger than the across-block variation and key task-driven changes in EEG activity are
best reflected in changes in the strength, rather than the variability [4]. Disentangling
subject and task responses can then lead to better-generalized models across subjects and
improvements the accuracy of EEG readings [5].
Another field that also requires explicit disentanglement of subject and task or content
is voice conversion (VC). VC is a method of transforming the voice of a source speaker
into the voice of the target speaker while preserving the linguistic information. In other
words, altering the style of a voice while maintaining the content. High voice similarity
and speech quality in many-to-one conversion systems have been achieved using models
that take in non-parallel data for training such as generative adversarial network (GAN)
and its variants [6, 7]. For a VC model to work well, it usually needs a strong bottleneck
to disentangle the latent space with constraints. Among the constraint methods used in
the models not needing parallel data for learning is vector quantization (VQ), which has
been yielding good results [8, 9, 10]. These models are also capable of one-shot learning,
needing only one utterance for the source and the target speaker during inference. Using
VQ is based on the idea that content information can be expressed as discrete codes, and
the speaker information can be viewed as the difference between the continuous represen-
tations and the discrete codes [9, 11].
The goal of this project is to be able to disentangle and separate the EEG signals’ sub-
ject response from the task response using deep autoencoders inspired by appropriate and
successful VC models, in particular VQ methods. An isolated content response would
prove useful in downstream tasks such as classification in MI and sleep staging, as the
subject-invariant representation aids classification accuracy in task determination. An in-
dividual’s EEG style could be interpreted as their biomarker and therefore used for subject
identification without such interference with the brain’s current tasks. It could become
plausible to use an individual’s EEG biomarker to monitor the progression of brain dis-
orders e.g. PD, which would provide aid in the diagnostic process and help determine
the next course of treatment. It would be beneficial to use a one-shot method, such as

the VQVC and its variants ([8, 9, 10]), in a clinical setting. Only a single EEG recording
from a patient would be necessary for separating the subject and task response for further

This project will in particular focus on EEG sleep stage data. Sleep staging is an im-
portant part of sleep science and analysis and one of the reasons for choosing sleep stage
data is the abundance of available open data. The field of sleep research has grown con-
siderably in recent years and the number of peer-reviewed sleep journals has more than
tripled since 2005 when the first issue of Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine came out [12].
Recent research has shown a strong relationship between inadequate sleep and disorders
ranging from hypertension [13] to neurodegeneration [14]. The data used in this project
originates from a polysomnogram (PSG). PSG is a process collecting physiological pa-
rameters, which include EEG, during sleep, and is used to evaluate underlying causes of
sleep disturbances. PSG is a gold standard tool for diagnosing sleep disorders related to
breathing, such as obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), central sleep apnea, and sleep-related
hypoventilation/hypoxia [15].
The adaption of VC models for EEG signals comes with a certain set of challenges. Voice
recordings and EEG signals are substantially different from each other, both in content and
how they are recorded. The EEG contains multiple channels and the number of channels
can often vary. VC models tend to take in voice recordings in the form of mel-spectogram,
a spectogram adapted for the scaling in human hearing, which typically is not applied to
EEG. Human voice recording and EEG signals is vastly different, both in regards to the
underlying frequency content and their sampling frequency. For the human vocal range,
the fundamental frequency of adult males typically ranges from 85 to 155 Hz, and from
165 to 255Hz for females [16], though human hearing can range from 20Hz to 20kHz [17].
For EEG signal analysis, sleep scoring in particular, the frequencies of importance lie
between 0.5 to 30Hz [18]. For the evaluation of classic VC models, a person can listen to a
conversion and qualitatively rank it based on how similar it is to the target speaker. That
method cannot be applied to EEG signals but the models can be evaluated using other
quantitative measures.
1.1
Research questions
The main research question of this project is whether it is possible to adapt the existing
VC models for the EEG domain. In particular, the ones that use the method of vector
quantization, for a meaningful latent space content and task separation. The research
question will be divided into the following three questions:
1. How can current VC methods be adapted for the conversion of multi-channel EEG
signals and what are the challenges that will need to be overcome?
The changes needed to be applied to a VC model for it to be able to tackle EEG
signals in the way it does voice recordings will be noted and evaluated.
2. To what extent does VQ model separate subject and task response in the context of
EEG sleep data?

The separation of subject and task response will be evaluated by qualitative and
quantitative measures. Qualitatively, t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding
(t-SNE) will visualize the latent embeddings to determine if they cluster differently
and in line with the intended response, subject or task. Quantitatively, two simple
classifiers using random forest and logistic regression will be used to determine clas-
sification accuracies. Better disentanglement should result in higher accuracies for
the downstream tasks of subject identification and task classification.
3. How reliable are model training and sensitivity to design choices including the ca-
pacity of induced bottlenecks?
Different bottleneck sizes, sample lengths, and sample input forms will be examined.
The baseline inputs will be spectograms of 30-second long samples, which align with
the 30-second epochs used for sleep scoring. Those results will be compared with
spectograms of 5 and 10-second samples and raw inputs of 30-second samples.
The following chapter will elaborate on the clinical and technical background as well as
describe the data being used. Chapter 3 will describe the model and training setup. In

chapter, chapter chapter 5 will go over the final conclusions.

Data and background
2.1
Sleep scoring
Common to all polysomnograms (PSG) monitoring are the measurements of signals gener-
ated from the cerebral cortex, electroencephalogram (EEG), eye movements, electroocu-
logram (EOG), and muscle movements, electromyogram (EMG), though they may also
include electrocardiograms (ECG) and respiratory measurements.
The PSG measurements are divided into 30-second epochs, where then each epoch is scored
based on its contents. The epoch length being 30 seconds stems from the history of 30s
of EEG being fitted to one arc of paper.
2.1.1
Sleep stages
Sleep is divided into 5 distinct stages, 4 pertaining to direct sleep and 1 to wakefulness,
according to the sleep scoring manual from The American Academy of Sleep Medicine
(AASM). There are three non-REM (NREM) sleep stages, N1, N2, and N3, and the REM
sleep stage R. The last stage is the wake (W) stage. An example of a hypnogram, a full
night’s PSG sleep scoring, can be seen in Figure 2.1. Descriptions of the different activities
used for sleep scoring are viewable in Table 2.1 and typical waveforms of EEG, EOG, and
EMG for each sleep stage are shown in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.1: Sleep cycles of subject 1
A W stage is recorded as W when over 50% of the epoch consists of alpha EEG activity.
The EOG channels would show slow eye blinking and rapid movements and as the subject
would get drowsy the EOG channels may show Slow rolling eye movements (SREMs).
From wake a subject typically goes into the N1 stage, sometimes only for a few epochs
before reawaking. Subjects rarely go directly from waking into REM sleep or stage N2

Stage N1 NREM sleep can also be referred to as transitional sleep or light sleep and is
characterized by low-voltage, fast EEG activity. In this stage the EEG patterns may vary
and change quickly though for it to be scored as stage N1 over 50% of the epoch must
consist of theta activity. Amplitudes of EEG activity are less than 50 to 75 µV. Sleep
spindles, K complexes, and rapid eye movements are never a part of the N1 stage though
there may be some sharp waves occurring near the end of the N1 stage.
Stage N2 NREM sleep stage may also be referred to as sigma, spindle, or intermediate
sleep. The amplitude of the EEG channels may increase from the N1 stage. The stage
consists in large part of theta activity with occasional bursts of faster activity. There can
only be delta activity for less than 20% of the epoch. In this stage K complexes and sleep
spindles appear, usually episodically, though K complexes occurring in an epoch without
sleep spindles are suﬀicient for N2 stage scoring. The EOG signal mirrors the EEG signal.
Stage N3 NREM sleep can also be referred to as deep sleep, slow wave sleep (SWS), or
delta sleep and is characterized by high amplitude slow waves. K complexes and sleep
spindles may appear in N3 sleep and there are no specific criteria from EOG or EMG.

2.3
Preprocessing
The original sampling rate of the C3 and C4 EEG recordings is 256 Hz and they are re-
sampled to 128Hz. All samples are filtered using a 0.3-35 Hz Butterworth bandpass filter
in the preprocessing stage. No overlap is between the samples. Signal preparation was
done using the mros-data Github repository, developed by Alexander Neergaard Zahid
[24]. In this project, the spectograms are produced using a window length of 2 seconds, a
hop length of 0.5 seconds, and a 256 STFT window size.

3
Methods
3.1
Voice conversion and vector quantization
Voice and EEG recordings are in some ways similar. For instance, voice recordings and each
channel of the EEG are one-dimensional signals where their frequency components play a
large role in their interpretation. Short-time Fourier transform (STFT) spectograms are
often used for EEG analysis. However, the original VQVC model takes voice recordings
as input signals that have been converted into mel-scale spectograms.
The mel-scale
is based on the human hearing interpretation of different pitches.
Human hearing is
subjective and frequencies are not perceived linearly, i.e. the same ”distance” isn’t observed
between steady increments of frequencies [25]. Therefore, the mel-scale spectogram has
been corrected for the spectograms linearity. Because mel-spectograms are not believed
to be as useful for EEG signals as they are for voice recording, regular STFT spectograms
are used as an intermediate step in the baseline model.
Vector quantization (VQ) is a powerful tool that has been used for data compression, where
it quantifies continuous data into discrete data, reducing the amount of data needed to
represent it. A template of vectors stored in a codebook is used to represent the data.
During the quantization, a vector that is the closest to the encoded signal is selected [26].
A simple example of VQ can be seen in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: An example of VQ. Gray dots, red circles, and blue lines are the input data,
codebook vectors, and borders of regions, respectively. [26]
Wu and Lee proposed the VQVC model, a one-shot method by vector quantization capable
of converting voices without any supervision [8]. This project is heavily based on this

3.2
Model architecture
Figure 3.2: Model architecture overview. C is generated by the codebook, Q, and S is

This project’s model architecture is based on Wu and Lee’s VQVC model [8, 27]. where
the fundamental idea is to have an autoencoder that can learn to represent the speaker
information by the difference between the continuous space and the discrete codes. Here,
the main model can be divided into an encoder, a quantization section, and a decoder.
Additionally, there are extra preparation and reconstruction sections, a pre-encoder and
a post-decoder. A model architecture overview can be seen in Figure 3.2
3.2.1
Pre-encoder and post-decoder
As mentioned above, the original VQVC model took in single channel mel-spectograms,
which are two-dimensional time/frequency images of the original single-dimensional signal.
This model on the other hand, deals with both multichannel spectogram and raw EEG
signals. To accomplish this, two separate pre-encoders and post-decoders are designed.
For the two-dimensional spectogram inputs, only a single Conv2d layer is used to collapse
the multi-channel EEG into a single input channel. Extra layers need to be added to the
pre-encoder for it to be able to take in the one-dimensional multi-channel raw EEG signal.

3.2.2
Autoencoder
The initial Conv1d layers of the encoder and ReLu activation process the frequency ac-
tivation at a time.
They are followed by two ResBlocks, which are to avoid gradient
vanishing. A norm-layer(x) normalizes the L2-norm of input to x on every time step. The
quantize layer then projects the input onto the closest point in the codebook. The task
representation cx is produced by the instance normalization (IN) and the quantization,
while the subject representation sx is the average of the difference between the continuous
and discrete vector at each time step. The decoder takes in the sum of the embeddings and
reconstructs them with a Conv1d layer, two consecutive ResBlocks, an upsampling, and
an additional Conv1d and a linear layer. Main model architecture is shown in Figure 3.5

4.6
Discussion Summary
There had been hope for a better disentanglement than the results convey. For all of the
models, the subject embedding S provides a better classification accuracy for both sleep
stage and subject identification than the task embedding C. If good disentanglement had
been achieved then C should have been performing better than S when classifying the
sleep stages but worse with respect to subject identification.
It would be beneficial to explore a bottleneck with a larger number of codes in the code-
book.
The original VQVC paper did not include a detailed qualitative assessment of
the code numbers, it only mentions leveraging the reconstruction loss, accuracy, and voice
quality. Here, of course, the reconstruction quality cannot be listened to however, the clas-
sification accuracy, t-SNE plots, and reconstruction plots and loss do not suggest large
drawbacks to increasing the number of codes from 256 to 512. As for the codebook size,
with looser constraints, the discrete codes will learn the subject information which is to
be prevented.
For samples that last shorter than the original 30 seconds, the classification accuracy
decreases quite drastically. That can be assumed because the subsample of the original
30-second sleep staged epoch does not necessarily contain the EEG events that are used
during the stage determination. Raw input models appear to be more diﬀicult to train as
they require a slower learning rate to provide adequate results comparable with spectogram
models’ results. Perhaps the other models might also benefit from a slower learning rate.
In all cases, the reconstructions for channels C3 and C4 are very similar to each other,
nearly identical, showing that the model, and especially the post-decoder, is not learning
the separate channels as well as had been hoped for. The reconstructions of the spec-
tograms are also much smoother than the original inputs. An assumption can be drawn
that the model prioritizes low-frequency signals as they convey more valuable information
and filters out the higher frequencies.
Proportionally the reconstruction of the higher
frequencies does not contribute as much to the loss as the lower ones. For the raw in-
put model, it can also be seen that along with missing the higher frequencies the signal
amplitude is also slightly lower as the autoencoder dampens the signal.
It was assumed that the sleep stages would be ”related” enough to the phoneme infor-
mation of voice recordings for them to become the most obvious task to be disentangled
from the subject response. Perhaps since subject embedding S provides a higher classi-
fication accuracy for the sleep stages than the task embedding C, the task embedding is
representative of other information than sleep stages.
The largest difference by far between the classifiers RF and LR is observed in subject
identification by S. Further investigation is necessary to answer why that is.

5
Conclusions
This project’s main research question was to answer if and how novel voice conversion
methods could be adapted for the EEG domain. In particular, a vector quantization voice
conversion (VQVC) method for EEG sleep data.
Extra convolutional layers needed to be added before and after the autoencoder to account
for the multiple EEG channels compared to the singular input in voice conversion, and
deeper layers were needed for the raw input model compared to the spectogram ones.
This study suggests that vector quantization does not provide a bottleneck that is robust
enough to successfully disentangle sleep stages as task response from the subject response.
The latent space variables, S and C represent the subject and task response, respectively.
For all of the tested models, S provided better accuracy than C for both subject identi-
fication and sleep stage classification. The t-SNE plots did not reveal a great separation
and clustering of the sleep stages in C. The S t-SNE plots did show distinct patterns of
grouping both for stages and subjects though they were not especially prominent. When
looking at C, the confusion matrices also showed a tendency for the sleep stages to nearly
only be predicted to be either the majority classes, wake or N2. Whilst, S had a better
distribution between all of the sleep stages.
As for the bottleneck design, when looking at the size of the codebook and the number of
codes in the codebook there does not appear to be a clear setting that outperforms the
other models. A trade-off can be observed between keeping the classification accuracies
high for C-sleep stage and S-subject with a large codebook size while keeping C-subject
and S-sleep stage low by restricting the codebook. This project found that the models
benefit from having the number of codes higher than in the original VQVC study. Samples
of smaller lengths are found to have significantly lower sleep stage classification accuracies
when using C. That supports a hypothesis that smaller sections of a sleep stage epoch
might not carry the necessary EEG activities to receive the same classification as their
30-second sample of origin. The raw EEG models needed to be trained at a lower rate
than the other models to give adequate results. They were on par with the spectrogram
models though more subject information remained in C.
For future work, it would be worth investigating the effects of applying contrastive learning
used to guide the latent representation and enhance the separation performance. As well,
it would be informative to train these types of models on other EEG datasets e.g. motor
movement/imagery or seizure detection datasets. With them, it could be examined what
other EEG information the C might be able to correlate more with than sleep stages.
