Abstract
The ability to accurately control the position and orientation of underwater vehicles is key to enable
the execution of many subsea operations. This problem is challenging due to the complex hydrodynamic
behaviour of rigid bodies moving in ﬂuid and due to the limits of the sensorial systems typically imposed
upon underwater vehicles. This thesis investigates the design of a dynamic positioning system for a small
fully actuated underwater vehicle of the type BlueROV using a model-based approach. A dynamic model
of the vehicle is presented and identiﬁed using an experimental approach based on free-diving tests. The
estimated parameters are validated using physical knowledge and compared to previous work on modelling
and identiﬁcation of the same type of vehicle with reasonable results. The estimated model is used for the
synthesis of an optimal DP-system in the form of a LQ-regulator with performance evaluated by simulation.
This is designed to reject disturbances in the form of unmodelled dynamics and constant ocean currents.
To test the DP-system on the physical hardware, the vehicle position and orientation must be known. This
cannot be obtained based on information from the internal sensors alone which speaks for an additional
measurement system. A working prototype of a camera tracking system based on a single IR-marker and
a standard webcam is developed and tested with promising results. The results of this thesis establish a
foundation for future work on more advanced control applications targeted at the BlueROV platform.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the MSc thesis project titled System Identiﬁcation and Dynamic Positioning of
Autonomous Underwater Vehicle. First, the project background and motivation will be given. The project
description and analysis is presented which leads to the problem formulation. Finally, the thesis outline is
presented and the main contributions of this thesis listed.
1.1
Background and Motivation
Operations performed in an underwater environment are often directly linked to high risks due to
very challenging, often unknown, and hostile working environments. This makes manned missions very
expensive because of the need for careful preparation and minimal tolerance for mistakes and as a result
very limited in numbers Antonelli (2014). Some of the factors that make underwater robotics challenging
are the combination of the complex hydrodynamical behaviour that occur when a rigid body moved through
water, and the limiting sensor possibilities for accurate estimation of orientation and position. Examples of
typical operations done in an underwater environment includes surveillance and maintenance of: off-shore
structures, oil rigs, pipeline, cables but also mapping of the seabed. Some tasks involve manipulation with
structures in the environment such as moving objects between locations or sub-sea welding in metal sheets
of e.g. an oil tank. Others are simple monitoring tasks. In recent years, controlling multiple robots at the
same time has become a hot topic. Technologically, this is very challenging, but it also opens up a wide
range of new applications. Common for most applications, is the need for a motion control system that can
accurately position the vehicle in the water.
1.2
Project Description
This project is carried out in the context of an ongoing project called REMORA (REconﬁgurable MOdular
Robotic system for Aquatic environment) where the goal is to develop an underwater robotic system that
enables fully autonomous and long-term operation (see Christensen et al. (2015)). The robotic system used
for development currently consists of a number of small underwater vehicles of the type BlueROV. Currently,
only little work has been done on the design on motion control systems for the BlueROV. Common for
most advanced control strategies is the need for a model of the vehicle that represents the main dynamical
behaviour and can be used for predicting future outputs in response to known control signals. Modelling and
identiﬁcation is often time-consuming as it requires a lot of experimental work and subsequent analysis.
Thi project deals with the identiﬁcation of a model of the BlueROV that can be used for synthesis of a
DP (dynamic positioning) control system, which is used for manoeuvring and positioning of the vehicle at
low speeds. Several others have worked with the BlueROV platform regarding the same type of problems.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
A recent MSc thesis Aili and Ekelund (2016) deals with model-based design and control of the BlueROV.
However, due to the lack of position measurements, all work is limited to identiﬁcation and control of
attitude and angular velocities which is measurable by the on-board sensors. Another recent MSc thesis
Sandøy (2016) deals with modelling and identiﬁcation of a completely decoupled 4-DOF BlueROV model
where experimental data based on towing tests was used in the identiﬁcation. It was not possible to ﬁnd
identiﬁcation results based on free-diving experiments.
A position measurement system is an important tool that can be used both for model identiﬁcation and
for subsequent test and validation of control systems. Commercial motion tracking systems for underwater
applications are expensive and such facilities are currently not available at DTU. Therefore it is of interest to
develop a simple alternative.
1.3
Problem Analysis
Synthesis of a model-based controller requires a model of the underwater vehicle. Hence identiﬁcation is
the ﬁrst natural task to consider. A model of the vehicle can be obtained using different approaches (referred
to as black, gray or white box methods, see Ljung (1999)) and the choice depends on the available knowledge
of the system. In order to exploit the available physical knowledge of the vehicle, careful modelling of the
physics must be done (see Fossen (2011)). In addition to the vehicle dynamics, also the combination of
thrusters and the thrust conﬁguration must be modelled and identiﬁed as this allows the computation of
forces and moments affecting the vehicle in response to a given known control signal. When the assumed
parameters of the system are clariﬁed, the design of experiments for identiﬁcation can be considered. The
individual thruster characteristics can be identiﬁed using force measurements, and the vehicle dynamics
can be identiﬁed using free-diving experiments carried out in a towing tank while motion is measured.
The candidate models for system identiﬁcation are chosen based on the formulated physical model and
the individual parameters are validated based on physical knowledge. In order to enable a closed-loop
demonstration of the DP controller, extra sensor information is needed in surge and sway. This problem
can be approached in several ways, such as using proximity sensors that can measure the range to nearby
obstacles. Another possibility is to use an external measurement system such as a motion tracking camera.
1.4
Problem Formulation
The project aims at developing a model based control architecture in the form of a dynamic positioning
system, that enables a single robot to either manoeuvre from a location P1 to a location P2, or simply hold a
ﬁxed position without stationary error despite model uncertainties and constant disturbances. The following
main objectives are investigated during this project:
• Identiﬁcation of dynamical models for manoeuvring based on experiments carried out in a towing
tank with motion capture facilities.
• Development of a dynamic positioning control architecture to be implemented on the available
hardware. The tuning and performance is to be evaluated in simulation.
• Design and implementation of a measurement system that enables the demonstration of a ﬁnal
closed-loop control test with motion in the horizontal plane.
The task is to be accomplished on an underwater vehicle platform of the type BlueROV equipped with
six thrusters and on-board electronics composed of PixHawk ﬂight-controller unit (with IMU and a depth

2.2
Thrusters
As for the mechanical platform, the six thrusters are also made by BlueRobotics and is of the type
T200. The ability of the thruster to apply propulsion in the forward and reverse direction is not the same.
BlueRobotics speciﬁes thrust characteristics (obtained from an open-thruster experiment) with a maximum
thrust of about 50 N in the forward direction but only 40 N in the reverse direction. The propeller of a T200
thruster is driven by a three-phase brushless motor which needs an electronic speed controller (ESC) to be
operated. The input of the ESC is a PWM signal where the pulse width is used to control the desired thrust.
A pulse width of 1100 µs corresponds to a maximum thrust in the reverse direction whereas 1900 µs results
in maximum thrust in the forward direction. A pulse width of 1500 µs corresponds to no thrust and the
T200-speciﬁcation list reports that a deadband in the range 1500±25 µs can be expected.

CHAPTER 3
Mathematical Modelling of AUV
A mathematical model of a system is a description of its behaviour in response to control inputs and
disturbances. Such a model can serve several purposes such as synthesis of model-based controllers and
observers. It is often a very time-consuming and expensive task to develop accurate models. The purpose
of this chapter is to formulate a model intended for controller design. As seen in Fig. 3.1, an underwater
vehicle is comprised of a different components that all must be modelled. The propulsion system deals
with the characteristics of the thrusters, i.e. the forces produced from the individual thrusters in response to
control signals. The thrust conﬁguration contain information about the geometry of thrusters and is used to
compute generalized forces and moments contributed by each thruster. The BlueROV Dynamics deals with
the kinematics and kinetics of the vehicle. Finally, the Sensors concern how quantities related to the vehicle
such as position, orientation and velocity are measured.
Figure 3.1: An overview of the components comprising an underwater vehicle.

BlueROV Dynamics
The dynamic modelling of an underwater vehicle can be divided into two parts; the study of kinematics,
and the study of kinetics. Where kinematics is about treating the geometrical aspects of motion, the kinetics is
about how forces and moments can explain the causes of motion. The purpose of this section is to formulate
a 4 DOF linearized maneuvering model that is suitable for linear control design. This is accomplished by
starting from the most general 6 DOF kinematic and kinetic equations, and then apply simpliﬁcations that
are relevant to the BlueROV.

3.8
Sensors
The sensors are used to measure the state of the vehicle and can in general be seen as some function:
y = h(η, ν) + noise
(3.44)
where the noise term is a measure of uncertainty and can be described by a stochastic process with some
statistical properties. Often, linear theory can provide a sufﬁciently accurate description of the noise. In the
linear framework, noise can be seen as being generated from white Gaussian noise propagated through a
linear ﬁlter. A special type of stochastic process that is often used in error modelling is the Gauss-Markov
Process. This is typically used to characterize random constants or slowly varying biases.
The available sensors can be divided into two categories:
• On-board sensors: IMU (accelerometer, rate gyro, magnetometer) and depth sensor from PixHawk.
• External sensors: Position and orientation estimators from camera tracking systems.
The Pixhawk ﬂight controller is running an inertial navigation ﬁlter in the form of an extended Kalman
ﬁlter (see ArduPilot (2016)), that is integrating all the on-board sensors for bias, scale factor and attitude
estimation. The external sensors are needed, ﬁrst of all because the on-board sensors do not measure linear
velocity or position, and also because the compass heading is affected by magnetic disturbances.
The accelerometer has several applications. One is to use the gravity vector for computing roll and
pitch. Another is to integrate the horizontal acceleration with a position measurement to obtain velocity.
The magnetometer can be used to compute a compass heading. This sensor has the disadvantage of being
sensitive to magnetic disturbances, which is often a problem for indoor applications.

CHAPTER 4
System Identiﬁcation
This chapter deals with estimation of the parameters included in the models formulated in Ch. 3. Where
the model indicates which parameters are expected to be relevant in describing its behaviour, it does not
explain how they are obtained. The overall approach used to identify the vehicle dynamics is to perform a
series of free-diving experiments while capturing its motion. During the tests, the thrusters apply forces and
moments, which is converted into generalized forces through the thruster conﬁguration. Hence, in order
to estimate the parameters with physical meaning, an accurate description of the thrust characteristics and
conﬁguration are essential. Since the dynamical model is based on ﬁrst-principles, the resulting parameters
can be validated by help from physical knowledge of the system. The estimated parameters are compared to
those obtained by Sandøy (2016), which is done using a so-called planar motion mechanism (PMM) where
the vehicle is towed through water while measuring forces directly. Due to the lack of information from the
on-board sensors, a camera-tracking based localization of the vehicle is applied to estimate the velocities in
surge and sway. All the experimental used in this chapter is conducted at the marine cybernetics laboratory
(MC-LAB) at NTNU Trondheim, where a high-speed underwater motion tracking system was available.
4.1

to output (monitored motion) during the free-diving experiments can be validated against physical properties.

of physics and hydrodynamics.

CHAPTER 5
Camera-Based Pose Estimation
A central issue in any type of navigation and motion control is the ability to measure the position and
orientation of the robot over time. For mobile robots, wheel odometry and GPS can be used to estimate the
current location. However none of this is as easily available for underwater robotic systems. The on-board
sensor package of the ROV is able to measure depth, but neither velocity nor position in the horizontal
plane. Due to magnetic disturbances, the compass heading provided by the on-board magnetometer turns
out to be biased. In order to demonstrate closed-loop velocity and position control, an accurate estimate
of position, velocity and heading is required. The objective of this chapter is to develop a motion tracking
system that can be used to obtain those estimates. A camera-based motion-tracking system will be presented.
The problem of ﬁnding the ROV’s position, denoted as the 3D-reconstruction problem, will be explained.
An observer is designed to compute a velocity estimate based on the position. The heading bias is estimated
by using an aiding heading measurement computed from the tangent to the trajectory. Finally, the chosen
implementation method is given, and the results from a free-diving test will be presented.
5.1
Motion Tracking System
Inspired by the Qualisys motion capture system that was introduced in Sec. 2.6, an alternative has been
developed based on a web-camera and an active IR-marker. The tracking system is intended to work in
closed-loop. Therefore, a design criteria was to use a capture device that could easily be interfaced and
integrated into a ROS system. Several ofﬁcial ROS packages are available for interfacing UVC1-compatible
USB-webcams. A web-camera with a high angle-of-view (AOV) was chosen to maximize the working area
of the system. A major challenge in motion capture is to reliably track the marker despite of changing scene
illuminations and colors. This is especially important, as the tracking system is intended work in closed-loop
control.
Different methods have been attempted such as using a simple passive colored marker. However, marker-
detection requires a lot of specialized ﬁltering to be usable in practice. Instead, an active marker with
IR-diodes (940 nm) is used. A robust detection strategy is obtained using a very simple light-intensity
threshold. The optical IR-ﬁlter placed in front of the lens was removed to make IR visible to the web-camera.
In order to increase the received light from the diode, another optical IR ﬁlter with a cutoff of 850 nm was put
in front of the camera. A small waterproof plastic sphere containing a PCB with 9 infrared (IR) light-emitting
diodes (LEDs) was designed. The sphere was glued on top of a light-weight and stiff carbon-rod which was
attached to the frame of the ROV as shown in Fig. 5.1a. A free-standing aluminium grid-structured tower
equipped with a transversal bar was used to hold the camera as shown in Fig. 5.1b. The carbon rod ensures

5.3
Target Motion Modelling and Estimation
For closed-loop velocity control in the surge and sway directions, it is necessary to have a measure of
velocity in those directions. A target motion model in the form of a discrete-time state-space model is used
to design a Kalman ﬁlter for estimating the velocity components. A short introduction to kinematic models
typically used for target tracking applications is given by Shimkin (2009). A kinematic model is chosen
due to its simplicity in ﬁlter design and implementation. A mathematical model of the ROV can be used to
improve the ﬁlter performance. However, this both adds complexity, requires a reasonable model, and makes
the target motion ﬁlter depend on the dynamics of the speciﬁc object being tracked (i.e. adding equipment or
completely changing ROV will require adjustments to the dynamic model and therefore the ﬁlter).
An important consideration is whether or not the model and ﬁlter should be considered time-varying. At
least two cases results in changing frame rate, which is best handled by a time-varying ﬁlter:
1. ROS messages passed between nodes are not performed in real-time leading to varying frame rate.
2. The ability to detect the marker can be worsened due to several reasons.
The considered kinematic model is based on the assumption that the velocity is a constant driven by
white noise corresponding to random acceleration. The noise will be assumed constant between sampling
instants. A separate ﬁlter will be used for motion along the x- and y-axis with the state equations written as:

5.4
Heading Estimation
A good heading estimate is important because the velocity that is being estimated in the WATER frame
has to be rotated back into the BODY frame for control purposes. This rotation matrix is a function of the
roll, pitch and yaw angles. Roll and pitch is accurately provided using the accelerometer, whereas accurate
information about the yaw angle is more difﬁcult to obtain in a single measurement. The onboard IMU
provides absolute heading information from a magnetometer. While a compass works well without magnetic
disturbances, it can be very unreliable in a practical work environment. The compass heading measurement
is assumed to be the sum of the true heading ψ and an unknown bias bψ that depends heavily on the position
of the ROV. A more detailed explanation about the assumption of the magnetic ﬁeld can be found in App.
B.1. Because of the unknown bias, the magnetometer itself is not sufﬁcient to provide accurate heading
information. In order to compensate for the unknown bias, a heading measurement unaffected by magnetic
disturbances is needed.
The motion capture system tracks a single marker which means that no information about the orientation
of the ROV is directly accessible. However, once the ROV starts moving the direction of the tracked path
provides a fair guess of the heading. The direction of the tracked path can be computed based on the
estimated velocity vector coming from the Kalman ﬁlter designed from the model given in Eqn. 5.10-5.9.
However, this measurement is also insufﬁcient on its own, since a reliable heading requires the ROV to be in
motion. The idea is to design a complementary ﬁlter, that combines the strengths of each measurement.

5.5
Implementation
The ﬁlter shown in Fig. 5.3 can be implemented in several ways. The current implementation is targeted
at implementation in ROS. A task-diagram of the chosen implementation method is given in Fig. 5.4. As
shown, there are three callback functions (CB’s). The three callbacks can be seen as threads that are called
sequentially from a callback queue. Therefore, accessing shared memory does not need protection from
race conditions using synchronization primitives such as mutexes. The IMU-CB is invoked whenever the
PixHawk navigation ﬁlter has newly updated Euler angle estimates as well as bias-compensated rate gyro
measurements. This happens at about 50 Hz. The values are saved in the shared space and used by the
Camera-CB which is invoked whenever a new raw image is received from the camera, which is at a maximum
rate of 30 Hz. This task handles blob-detection, computes the marker position (X, Y ) and maps that position
into the CG of the ROV using the newest available roll/pitch angles (φ, θ) and heading estimate ˆψ. Then it
runs the time-varying kinematic Kalman ﬁlter for estimating the velocity components in the WATER frame.
The Euclidean norm of the velocity vector is evaluated, and if the value exceeds the minimum threshold vth,
the vision aided heading ψv is computed. This value is saved as a shared variable and a reuse counter is reset,
i.e. set to 0. This counter indicates how many times the vision aided heading have been used without being
updated. The Timer-CB is a callback that is invoked at a ﬁxed user-speciﬁed rate of 30 Hz and this is where
the complementary ﬁlter for heading disturbance estimation runs. As opposed to the velocity estimator, this
ﬁlter is implemented using a ﬁxed sampling time where it simply uses the newest available values of ψm, r
and ˆvx, ˆvy. Whenever the vision aided heading is used, the reuse counter is incremented. When the reuse
counter value exceeds some threshold, chosen to be 5, the output equations in the complementary ﬁlter
is modiﬁed to reﬂect the fact that no vision aided heading ψv is available and that the prediction is done
based on ψm and r alone. In summary, this choice of implementation means that some of the measurements
provided by the IMU-CB will be thrown away, since the other two callbacks runs at a lower rate.

CHAPTER 6
Dynamic Positioning Control
A control system that is designed for the purpose of holding a ﬁxed position (stationkeeping) and/or
manoeuvring between two locations at a low speed is referred to as a dynamic positioning system, or
DP-system for short. The underwater vehicle will be exposed to disturbances in the form of unknown ocean
currents and unknown dynamics which must therefore be compensated for by the DP controller. This chapter
deals with design of such a DP control system for the BlueROV underwater vehicle. First an overall control
architecture will be presented, where the location of the controllers are highlighted in terms of the available
hardware and software. The control allocation, which handles the mapping from desired generalized forces
and moments to actual thruster commands, will be presented. An optimal DP-system will be designed as
a LQ-controller synthesized from the BlueROV model identiﬁed in Ch. 4. As the FCU (PixHawk) has an
existing local control layer that is a core part of its codebase, these will be kept without modiﬁcations and
the LQ-controller will work as a reference generator for these controllers. Typically DP systems include
the functionality of waypoint tracking where a user can specify a list of waypoint coordinates, and a path
generator constructs a path composed of straight lines and circular arcs, where the arc radius depends on the
turning radius of the vehicle. This functionality will not be considered.
6.1
Control Architecture
An overview of the proposed control architecture for the DP control system is given in Fig. 6.1. The
large block on the left is intended for implementation on either the laptop or the Odroid, which is done in the
ROS framework. The large block to the right shows the architecture of the existing PixHawk implementation
(in the ALT_HOLD ﬂight-mode) which is based on ArduSub. The control system for damping roll and pitch
motion is not shown. The starting point in the design of the DP control system has been to investigate the
existing low-level control layer implemented on the PixHawk. This is the part of the control system that has
the least ﬂexibility in terms of making changes and adding new features. On the other hand, its the only
place where hard real-time operations can be implemented, and therefore where the highest performance
can be obtained. By investigating the ArduSub codebase, it is seen that a core part of its implementation
includes: A heave velocity and yaw rate controller, a navigation ﬁlter and control allocation functionality.
Although there is a potential for better tuning of the controller and ﬁlter parameters the implementation is
thoroughly tested by many developers and will therefore not be changed. The control allocation block can
be seen as the inverse of the propulsion system and the thrust conﬁguration as it computes the ESC inputs
u = [u1, . . . , u6]⊤necessary to obtain the desired forces and moments τ = [Xd, Yd, Zd, Nd]⊤. Due to the
lack of sensor information about the horizontal velocity and position, the PixHawk does not have the basis
for controlling the surge and sway motion in closed-loop. The input signals for surge and sway are therefore

6.2
Control Allocation
Control allocation concerns the problem of ﬁnding the control input vector u = [u1, . . . , u6]⊤that
results in the desired generalized force vector τd = [Xd, Yd, Zd, Nd]⊤, where u is the ESC control inputs in

and moments to velocities and accelerations, and therefore the controllers are designed to produce forces and
moments. In Ch. 4 the propulsion system (mapping from u to F) and thrust conﬁguration (mapping from F
to τ) was identiﬁed. The control allocation can be seen as ﬁnding the inverse of these mapping functions.
First, the inverse propulsion system mapping will be considered, then the inverse thrust conﬁguration will be
found.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
Physical knowledge was applied to derive a 4-DOF nonlinear manoeuvring and diving model with surge,
sway, heave and yaw. The model was linearized around an operating point that depended on a selected cruise
speed and the structure of the system matrices revealed that surge and heave were decoupled from the rest of
the system whereas sway and yaw were coupled. Due to the non-symmetrical force characteristics of each
thruster in forward and reverse direction, two 2nd order polynomials with a deadband was used to describe
the relationship between control input and produced force. The thrust conﬁguration matrix was needed in
order to calculate the resulting forces and moments.
Free-diving experiments were used for identifying parameters of the vehicle dynamics where motion
were captured using an external commercial tracking camera and where forces and moments were computed
based on knowledge of the propulsion system. In order to obtain parameter estimates with physical meaning,
an accurate model of the thrusters is important. Therefore, each thruster were tested individually while the
forces were measured. The estimated parameters were validated using physical knowledge of the system and
reasonable results were in general obtained. The largest deviations were found in the parameters associated
with heave. In the end, the parameters of the model were compared to the ones identiﬁed by Sandøy (2016).
Again, this comparison showed the largest deviations in heave.
The sensors available on-board the BlueROV is not able to measure velocity or position in the horizontal
plane. Therefore, an external measurement system was needed for enabling any forms of closed-loop velocity
and position control. A basic camera tracking system was developed and implemented in ROS to work with
a standard webcam and a single active IR-marker ﬁxed to the vehicle. Computation of the center of gravity
was shown to require knowledge of depth and attitude of the vehicle. Good estimates of these were available,
except for the heading angle, which is computed based on measuring the magnetic ﬁeld which is affected
by magnetic disturbances present in the indoor environment. This problem was approached by designing a
complementary ﬁlter that used the tangent to the trajectory as a heading measurement used for estimating a
heading bias caused by the magnetic disturbances.
The control architecture of a DP system was designed under consideration of the available hardware
and software. The PixHawk ﬂight-controller was shown already to have an existing low-level control layer
for controlling heave velocity and yaw rate. This is a core part of the ﬂight-stack which makes it difﬁcult
to change and add new functionality to. The proposed way to deal with this was to design a DP reference
generator, that sends velocity and rate references to the low-level controllers. A LQ-regulator was used in
the design of an optimal DP system where controller synthesis and simulation was carried out based on the
identiﬁed model.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
7.2
Future Work
Due to lack of time, not all the intended work was carried out. A ﬁnal test where the designed and
simulated DP system is put in closed-loop with the motion tracking camera system is still to be accomplished.
In order to accomplish this task, the DP reference generator needs to be implemented in ROS. Also the
heading bias estimator of the camera tracking system is currently only simulated in MATLAB, and also
needs to be implemented in ROS. The ﬂume in B114 is capable of simulating a constant ocean current which
could be an interesting test to perform and see if constant disturbances could be rejected. Also, the the ability
to travel between a series of locations in the form of waypoint navigation, needs to be implemented. This is
a small list of experiments that could be interesting to carry out in practice:
• Setpoint regulation with decoupled motion, i.e. where a step is applied each DOF one at a time.
• Setpoint regulation with coupled motion, i.e. where motion in multiple DOFs are taking place at once.
• Setpoint regulation under the occurrence of constant disturbances in the form of ocean currents.
• Waypoint navigation travelling between a series of locations.
Some further investigation into why the parameter estimates related to heave is so far from the physical
values are needed. More experimental data in this DOF needs to be acquired with motion along both the
positive and negative z-axis.
The camera system developed in this project was based on a single IR marker, which made the marker
detection simple and robust. However, it also meant that the internal sensors of the vehicle were required
to obtain an accurate position and orientation estimate. This coupling between the camera tracking and
the internal sensors makes it impossible to replace the vehicle to be tracked without modiﬁcations to the
source-code. In other words, a camera system based on a single marker limits the capabilities of the system.
The following list of extensions to the camera tracking system could be interesting to investigate:
• Multi-marker functionality where orientation and position can be estimated without internal sensors.
• Adding the ability to track multiple vehicles at the same time.
• Usage of multiple cameras for increasing precision and accuracy.
The camera tracking measurements works like a GPS, which is possible to integrate into the navigation
ﬁlter of the PixHawk. Therefore, it could be beneﬁcial to send the measurements directly to the existing
navigation ﬁlter.
During the project, localization based on proximity sensing in the form of an acoustic transducer was
tested. Distance measuring based on a Pro-Wave 200LM450 was designed and tested in a small tank with
success. The test showed that it was possible to measure distance in the range ≈0.4 m to 5 m. Some work is
still needed to get this device integrated into the existing hardware on the vehicle platform.

APPENDIX B
Motion Tracking
B.1
Assumptions on the Magnetic Field
The magnetic ﬁelds affecting the magnetometer can be grouped into three categories. This is illustrated
in Fig. B.1 where:
• Be: Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld / geomagnetic ﬁeld.
• Bl: Local magnetic ﬁeld caused by magnetic materials in the nearby environment such a iron.
• Bb: Body ﬁxed magnetic ﬁeld caused by thrusters, cooling fan and current loops.
Figure B.1: Different types of magnetic ﬁelds in the indoor test tank.
The only desirable source of magnetic ﬁeld is the one coming from earth, as this can be assumed constant in
magnitude and direction within a limited working area on earth’s surface. The local magnetic ﬁeld is also
constant in magnitude and direction, but is heavily dependent on small changes in position. The body ﬁxed
magnetic ﬁeld depend on a lot of factors, such as thrusters, cooling fan, etc. The measured magnetic ﬁeld
can be seen as the sum of the three terms:
B = Be + Bl + Bb
where
Be = const
Bl = Bl(x, y, z)
Bb = Bb(u1, ..., u6, ...)
An accurate model of the magnetic ﬁelds would allow one to eliminate Bl and Bb. However, such a model
is not possible to obtain in practice.
