Abstract 2
Shiga toxin (Stx)-producing Escherichia coli (STEC) strains are zoonotic pathogens, which are
considered to be well known pathogens in developed countries. STEC are food and waterborne
pathogens capable of causing life-threatening disease in humans ranging from mild watery or
bloody diarrhea to hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS). Stx is thought to be the main virulence
factor, encoded by genes present on lambdoid phages, which inhibits protein synthesis of the
host cells resulting in death by apoptosis. Moreover the Stx encoding phages can be induced
by external factors such as antibiotics, potentially leading to an increased toxin release. Two
immunologically distinct groups of the Stx family exists i.e. Stx1 and Stx2, which can be further
divided into 16 diﬀerent subtypes in total. The diﬀerent subtypes of Stx exhibit diﬀerent biologic
activity and have been shown to aﬀect the severity of disease. Furthermore, the amount of Stx
produced is thought to be dependent on the anti terminator protein Q and can vary even within
the same Stx subtype.
This study aimed to evaluate suggested antibiotic treatment options of STEC infections, without
causing induction of stx phages, as this could lead to an increase in toxin production. In order to
assess the eﬀects of the antibiotics: DNA synthesis inhibitor, ciproﬂoxacin, the protein synthesis
inhibitors, azithromycin, tetracyline and tobramycin and the cell wall inhibitor, mecillinam,
several studies were performed, examining growth rate, cytotoxicity, phage induction, plaques
and whole-genome sequence analysis. Isolates tested in this study were chosen among the most
prevalent serotypes, virulence proﬁles and multilocus sequence typing (MLST) types isolated
from Danish patients from 2015-2017.
It was apparent in this study, that azithromycin and tobramycin caused a decrease in cytotoxicity
in the exponential growth phase, whereas tetracyline and mecillinam resulted in an increased
cytotoxicity in some isolates. Ciproﬂoxacin did not alter the cytotoxicity in any isolate tested
in this study.
However, plaques were not observed in any isolate, indicating that no phage
induction occurred, suggesting that the cytotoxicity merely was a result of spontaneous toxin
release. Additionally, phage gene investigations revealed Q genes belonging to the group QIV of
most isolates. However no Q genes were observed for the isolates harboring stx2b.
Consistent with previous studies, serotype, Stx subtype, MLST and phage genes did not predict
cytotoxicity levels on Vero cell assays.
Thus, additional investigations are required in order
to clarify the diﬀerences in the cytotoxicity displayed by the various isolates. Factors such as
bacterial characteristics, phage genome content and the number of stx phages harbored by the
isolates, have to be considered in future studies concerning prediction of STEC virulence.

Resume 3
Shiga toxin (Stx)-producerende Escherichia coli (STEC) er zoonotiske patogener, som anses for
at være udbredte patogener i udviklede lande. STEC er fødevarebårne patogener, som er i stand
til at forsage livstruende sygdomsforløb i mennesker, hvor symptomerne kan rangere fra mild
til blodig diarre og i som i sidste instans kan medføre hæmolytisk uremisk syndrom (HUS).
Stx som anses som den vigtigste virulens faktor i STEC, er kodet af lamdoide bakteriofager
(fager), som har evnen til at inhibere proteinsyntesen i værtscellen, og derved dør cellen ved
apoptosis. Stx kodende fager kan induceres af eksterne påvirkninger såsom antibiotika, hvilket
medføre en forhøjet toksin produktion. To immunologisk forskellige Stx subtyper: Stx1 og Stx2,
kan inddeles i 16 forskellige subtyper. De forskellige Stx subtyper udviser forskellig biologisk
aktivitet og påvirker derudover også sygdoms forløbet forskelligt.
Derudover er mængden af
produceret Stx mistænkt for at være afhængig af anti-terminatorproteinet Q, som kan variere,
selv indenfor samme Stx subtype.
I dette studie blev foreslåede behandlinger af STEC infektioner med antibiotika evalueret,
i håbet om at undgå fag induktion, da det kan medføre øget toksin produktion.
For at
evaluere den antibiotiske eﬀekt blev DNA syntese inhibitoren, ciproﬂoxacin, protein syntese
inhibitorerne; azithromycin, tetracyclin og tobramycin og cellevægs inhibitoreren mecillinam
undersøgt.
Studierne inkluderede vækst forsøg, cytotoksicitet, fag induktion, plaques og
helgenom sekvens analyser. Isolaterne testet i dette studie udviste de mest prævalente serotyper,
virulens proﬁler og Multilocus sekvens typning (MLST) typer isoleret fra danske patienter fra
2015 til 2017.
Det var tydeligt at behandling med azithromycin og tobramycin resulterede i nedsat
cytotoksicitet i den eksponentielle vækstfase, hvorimod behandling med tetracyclin og mecillinam
resulterede i forøget cytotoksicitet i nogle isolater.
Ciproﬂoxacin derimod, ændrede ikke
cytotoksiciteten i nogle af de testede isolater i dette studie. Der blev derimod ikke observeret
nogle plaques for isolaterne, hvilket indikere manglende fag induktion, men at den målte
cytotoksicitet derimod var et resultat af spontan toksin dannelse. Derudover, viste en analyse
af Q gener, at Q generne identiﬁceret i de testede isolater hovedsageligt tilhørte gruppen QIV .
Dog blev der ikke identiﬁceret nogle Q gener i isolater med subtypen stx2b.
I overensstemmelse med tidligere studier kunne analyser indeholdende serotype, Stx subtype,
MLST og fag gener ikke forudsige cytotoksiciteten på Vero celle assayet. Yderligere studier er
derfor nødvendige for at kunne redegøre for forskelle i cytotoksicitet for diverse isolater. Faktorer
såsom bakterielle egenskaber, fag genomer og antallet af stx phager fundet i isolaterne, bør også
tages i betragtning i fremtidige studier omhandlende forudsigelse af STEC virulens.

Introduction 5
Escherichia coli (E. coli) are gram-negative, rod shaped and facultative anaerobic bacteria that
are either motile or non-motile. E. coli is a highly diverse and normally reletive harmless organism
which is part of the microbiota of the human gastrointestinal (GI) tract. The ability to acquire
various combinations of mobile genetic elements can turn this organism into a pathogen capable
of causing a wide range of diseases, from mild diarrhea to infection of the central nervous system,
both in humans and animals [Croxen and Finlay, 2010]. Most virulence factors are encoded by
genes that are carried on mobile genetic elements (MGEs), hereunder bacteriophages, plasmids
and pathogenicity islands (PAIs). It has been estimated that the core set of genes in E. coli
comprises ∼20% of the pan genome [Lukjancenko et al., 2010].
A subset of diarrheagenic E. coli (DEC) pathotypes are commonly recognized and include
enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC), enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC), enteroinvasive E. coli (EIEC),
enteroaggregative E. coli (EAEC), diﬀusely adherent E. coli (DAEC) and Shiga toxin producing
E. coli (STEC). Other E.coli pathogens referred to as extraintestinal pathogenic E. coli (ExPEC)
includes uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC), meningitis-associated E. coli (MNEC), and avian
pathogenic E. coli (APEC) [Kaper et al., 2004; Nataro and Kaper, 1998; Soysal et al., 2016].
The DEC pathogens have acquired valuable traits such as virulence factors, which is a result of
horizontal gene transfer (HGT) of MGEs. This allows them to attach and colonize within the
intestine of both human and animals, thus competing with the microbiota at the site of infection,
potentially causing serious disease. Due to the transfer of MGEs E.coli are an ever expanding and
evolving organism, as new pathotypes emerge with cross-pathotypes array of virulence factors
[Croxen et al., 2013].
5.1
Shiga toxin producing Escherichia coli
Shiga toxin producing E. coli, also known as verocytotoxin producing E. coli (VTEC), is a highly
infectious food and water borne pathogen. The ﬁrst zoonotic incident of a STEC infection was
reported in 1982, displaying the serotype O157:H7 (the nomenclature will be explained in the
next section), which is still one of the most prevalent serotypes today. Since 1982 reports of
new outbreaks and sporadic cases have resulted in more than 400 STEC serotypes linked to
human disease [Kaper et al., 2004; Sperandio et al., 2015; Agger et al., 2015].
Cattle and
other ruminants are key reservoirs for STEC, however, a small fraction of STEC can infect
humans and are transmitted through contaminated water and food. It is estimated that each
year STEC infections causes 2.801.000 acute cases and more than 230 deaths on a global scale,
however, these numbers are thought to be higher.The lack of availability of surveillance and
asymptomatic patients, leads to many undiscovered cases [Majowicz et al., 2014]. In recent years
150-190 cases have been reported in Denmark annually, however, 318 patients were registered in

2017. This increase is thought to be a result of more advanced analytical methods and greater
awareness of STEC (SSI, 2018). STEC infections can cause enteric infections in humans with
symptoms ranging from mild to bloody diarrhea to potentially fatal hemolytic-uremic syndrome
(HUS). It is estimated that HUS occurs in 5-15% of infected individuals, and is characterized
by thrombocytopenia, hemolytic anemia and kidney damage, which can ultimately lead to
kidney failure [Croxen and Finlay, 2010; Agger et al., 2015]. Even though many patients remain
asymptomatic 15-20% require hospitalization due to critical symptoms such as HUS, with 3890
reported cases annually worldwide[Majowicz et al., 2014]. STEC infections are one of the leading
causes of renal failure in children, with a mortality rate of 1-4% in the acute phase, and up to
30% of patients developing long-term renal damage [Spinale et al., 2013]. Another potential
target seen with HUS is the central nervous system (CNS), it was estimated that up to 66%
of patients developed neurological manifestations during the O104:H4 outbreak in Germany in
2011 [Sperandio et al., 2015].
STEC strains have the ability to produce Shiga toxins (Stx) of which there are two major
toxins; Shiga toxin type 1 (Stx1) (the toxin is almost identical to Shiga toxin produced by
Shigella dysenteriae type 1) and the immunologically distinct Shiga toxin type 2 (Stx2). The
toxins share approximately 55% amino acid homology, and can be divided into 16 diﬀerent
subtypes based on their sequence identity: Stx1a, Stx1c, Stx1d, Stx1e, Stx2a, Stx2b, Stx2c,
Stx2d, Stx2e, Stx2f, Stx2g, Stx2h, Stx2i, Stx2j, Stx2k and Stx2l (Flemming Scheutz, personal
communication). The most prevalent STEC strains world wide produce Stx1a, Stx2a and Stx2c,
alone or in combination, and generally Stx2 is more frequently associated with bloody diarrhea
and HUS, than Stx1. Furthermore, Stx2 is described as being 1000-fold more toxic towards renal
endothelial cells than Stx1 [Scheutz et al., 2012; Allison, 2007; Krüger and Lucchesi, 2015; Kaper
et al., 2004; Louise and Obrig, 1995]. Not much is known about the biological activity of the
various subtypes as the nomenclature is fairly new (2012). However, applying the nomenclature
to prior studies, showed a correlation between subtypes and clinical manifestations [Karmali
et al., 2003] (Table 5.1).

An additionally important virulence factor is thought to be the Locus of Enterocye Eﬀacement
(LEE) pathogenicity island, which has been associated with STEC outbreaks. The LEE island
contains genes that are responsible for attaching and eﬀacing lesions, and consists of 41 open
reading frames and ﬁve major operons [Franzin and Sircili, 2015]. Even though the LEE-positive
strains have been associated with STEC outbreaks, LEE-negative isolates have also been found
to cause the same symptoms, thus demonstrating that additional virulence factors are still to be
characterized [Steyert et al., 2012].
5.2
STEC serotypes
Serotype classiﬁcation of E. coli is based on the immunologic characterization of the two surface
antigens; the O-polysaccheride (O antigen) which is a part of the lipopolysaccharide layer, and
the ﬂagella protein (H antigen) which is involved with mobility. Since the ﬁrst detected zoonotic
outbreak in 1982, the observed sporadic cases and outbreaks have resulted in more than 400
diﬀerent serotypes, which includes 182 O- and 53 H-antigens [Bettelheim, 2007; McQuiston et al.,
2011]. The most prevalent serotype registered globally is O157:H7, in fact 70% of infections in
North America and Western Europe are of this serotype, hence most research targets strains
of this type.
However, other serotypes have been found to cause zoonotic diseases, such as
O111:H8, O103:H2, O121, O145, O26, O113.
Furthermore, Stx1 infections have been more
commonly linked with infections in developing countries in Asia [Noris and Remuzzi, 2005].
As previously mentioned more than 400 serotypes have been isolated from humans, in order to
get a better understanding of the apparent diﬀerences in virulence between STEC serotypes,
STEC strains have been classiﬁed into ﬁve diﬀerent seropathotypes (A to E) by Karmali et al.
[Karmali et al., 2003], according to incidence and association with HUS and outbreaks (Table
5.2). The seropathotype scheme covers 25 STEC serotypes commonly isolated from humans
[Karmali et al., 2003].

5.3
Shiga toxin
A major virulence factor of STEC strains is Stx, which is encoded by Stx phages. The toxin is one
of the most potent biological toxins known. Stx is an AB5 toxin consisting of an enzymatically
active A subunit non-covalently bound to a pentamer of ﬁve B subunits [Croxen and Finlay,
2010]. The A subunit of Stx1 consist of 293 amino acids whereas the A subunit of Stx2 is 4
amino acids longer at the C terminus. The active site of the toxin, both for Stx1 and Stx2, is a
glutamic acid found at position 167 (Figure 5.1) [Hovde et al., 1988].
Figure 5.1: Structure of Shiga toxin A schematic presentation of the Shiga toxin structure.
The toxin consists of the A subunit, hereunder A1 subunit and A2 peptide, and a pentamer of
B subunits. In addition, the ribosome interaction region, the protease (furin) sensitive site and
the disulﬁde bridge connecting the A1 subunit and A2 peptide are shown. The region which is
important for translocation from the ER to the cytosol is shown next to protease siensitive site.

A region located at amino acid position 248-251 is trypsin sensitive (marked in green in Figure
5.1) and allows the A subunit to be activated upon cleavage near the C terminus, which results
in an A1 subunit and A2 peptide. The A1 and A2 peptide remains bound through a disulﬁde
bridge [Fraser et al., 2004]. As mentioned the A subunit has enzymatic activity, this resides in
the A1 subunit, which is linked to the B subunit by the A2 peptide. The enzymatic activity is
encoded by the amino acids found at position 1-239, whereas the amino acids located at position
240-245 are crucial for the translocation of A1 from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) into the
cytosol (discussed further below) [LaPointe et al., 2005]. In addition the A2 peptide is crucial
for activation of Stx2d, where two amino acids are a cleavage site when treated with elastase
[Melton-Celsa et al., 2002].
The B pentamer consist of ﬁve identical subunits that consist of 69 amino acids for Stx1 and
71 amino acids for Stx2. Toxin binding to globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) receptor on target cells
is enabled by the B pentamer [Croxen and Finlay, 2010; Krüger and Lucchesi, 2015]. However
weak unspeciﬁc binding to globotetraosylceramide (Gb4) occurs, though Stx2e preferably binds
to Gb4 instead of Gb3. The aﬃnity for Gb3 is thought to be a result two or three Gb3 binding
sites per B monomer [Melton-Celsa, 2014].

5.4
Mechanism of action of Shiga toxin
The toxin enters into systemic circulation through absorption by the epithelium layer, hence
allowing access to the kidneys [Sperandio and Pacheco, 2012]. When the renal endothelial cells
are damaged and the microvasculature is occluded. This occurs as a result of direct toxicity
through induction of local cytokine and chemokine production, ultimately resulting in renal
inﬂammation [Kaper et al., 2004]. Upon entering the bloodstream, the toxin binds to the Gb3
receptor found on erythrocytes and platelets in order to reach the target destination.
It is
presumed that platelets and erythrocytes are less sensitive to the toxin as they lack a nucleus,
thus binding of the toxin will not destroy all of these cells. The cells destroyed however, can
result in thrombosis or/and thrombocytopenia [Obrig and Karpman, 2011].
Upon reaching the target cell, the Stx B subunit interacts with the Gb3 receptor on the surface
of Paneth cells, in the interstinal mucosa, and kidney epithelial cells (Figure 5.2). However,
Stx can be found in Gb3-negative intestinal cells, and this may be a result of macropinocytosis
[Croxen and Finlay, 2010; Krüger and Lucchesi, 2015]. When bound to the target cell by the
Gb3 receptor (Figure 5.2), Stx is endocytosed by the cell where it will bypass the late endocytic
pathway and is carried by retrograde transport from the trans-Golgi network to the ER where
the A and B subunits dissociates.
Figure 5.2:

by endocytosis, from where Stx undergoes retrogradetransport to the trans-Golgi network to the

Once in the ER the A subunits is translocated to the cytosol and the nuclear envelope where its
N-glycosidase activity cleaves an adenosine residue from 28S RNA of the 60S ribosomal subunit,
resulting in inhibition of protein synthesis, thus causing cell death by apoptosis [Sperandio and

the Gb3-negative cells, in these cells Stx will not inhibit protein synthesis nor induce apoptosis,
however, the toxin is thought to decrease chemokine expression, thus suppressing inﬂammatory
responses [Croxen and Finlay, 2010].
Stx1 and Stx2 binds to diﬀerent epitopes on the Gb3 receptor, due to this, Stx1 binds and
detaches very easily from Gb3, where as Stx2 binds and detaches very slowly, thus Stx2 remains
attached to the cell long enough to be engulfed [Noris and Remuzzi, 2005]. This could possibly
explain why Stx2 is more toxic than Stx1 in human endothelial cells.

5.5
Shiga toxin producing phages
Acquiring new traits is vital for the evolution of pathogenic bacteria and can be facilitated by
point mutations, recombination events or horizontal gene transfer (HGT), the latter is meditated
by plasmids or bacteriophages [Ogura et al., 2015].
Bacteriophages (simply called phages)
are bacterial viruses that can infect bacterial cells and hereby introduce new genetic material
to the bacteria [Sulakvelidze et al., 2001].
The phages associated with STEC infections are
temperate lampdoid phages, phages that share similar genome organization and transcription
control schemes, that contains genes encoding Shiga toxins [Tyler et al., 2004]. Stx subtyping is
a useful tool for STEC characterization, however it has also proved to be valuable for diagnosis,
as some types and subtypes have been associated with diﬀerent clinical outcomes [Allison, 2007;
Krüger and Lucchesi, 2015]. However the amount of toxin produced is also thought to be crucial
for the potency of the strain, and may have an even greater impact than the toxin subtype and
genes [Ogura et al., 2015; Melton-Celsa, 2014].
Upon binding to the host the stx phage will insert its DNA into the host, where it will enter the
lysogenic or lytic phase. In the lysogenic phase the phages DNA is integrated into the bacterial
genome, hence forming prophages (Figure 5.3). Upon induction the prophage enters the lytic
phase in which Stx and phages are produced, and as a result of cell lysis these are released into
the surroundings where it can further infect new bacteria [Allison, 2007].
Figure 5.3: Lysogenic and lytic pathway of the temperate shiga toxin producing
bacteriophage. Upon bindning to E. coli the stx phage inserts its DNA into the host, from
here the phage can undergo two pathways; lysogenic and lytic. Lysogenic pathway: Ones the
phage DNA is inserted into the bacteria, the DNA integrates into the bacterial chromosome,
thus forming a prophage. The cell divides normally hereafter, resulting in the newly formed cells
containing prophages. Lytic pathway: Ones the phage DNA is inserted into the bacteria, the
host DNA is degraded and the nucleotides are used to form new phage DNA. Hereafter new phages
are formed and Shiga toxin produced, lastly, the cell lysis result in release of phages and toxin to
the surroundings.

5.6
Activation of prophage
While in the Lysogenic state, the prophage remains deactivated due to a binding of the cI-encoded
repressor protein on both the right (OR) and left (OL)operator sites, which inhibits transcription
of the early phage promoters PR and PL (Figure 5.4). The lytic state is activated as the phage
takes advantages the bacterial SOS response, which is a result of DNA damage where the cell
cycle is arrested and DNA repair and mutagenesis are induced [Nowicki et al., 2014; Tyler et al.,
2004; Sperandio et al., 2015]. When the SOS response is triggered, the protein RecA is produced
and activated, which promotes a conformational change of the cI repressor leading to self-cleavage
of the repressor, thus making PR accessible resulting in expression of the anti-terminators N and
Q. The antiterminator Q initiate transcription at the late PR promoter which results in synthesis
of a 26-kb mRNA which encodes all late functions. The stx genes is located downstream of the
PR promoter and upstream of the lysis cassette, thus Q transcription initiation of PR, which
transcends the tR terminator, will lead to an expression of stx genes and genes encoding cell
lysis. As bacteria undergo cell lysis the Stx will be released into the environment [Tyler et al.,
2004; Sperandio et al., 2015]. Induction of prophages occurs spontaneously at low levels, but in
the presence of some antibiotics or other inducing agents, induction occurs at a very high level.
Stx1 phages have an additional regulator pstx1, located directly upstream of the Stx genes, which
activity is regulated by the iron concentration found in the surrounding environment (Figure
5.5). The mechanism is an iron-depending Fur transcriptional repressor, which is believed to
bind at a site located near the pstx1 [Calderwood and Mekalanos, 1987; Calderwood et al., 1987;
De Grandis et al., 1987]. Initiation of transcription from the pstx1 promoter happens at low iron
concentration and terminates at the tstx1 terminater, located downstream of the Stx genes and
upstream of the lysis casette. However, the Q transcription proceeds through the toxin genes,
tstx1 terminater and the phage lysis genes [Wagner et al., 2002].

5.7
Treatment
As STEC cause a wide range of symptoms, the inclination is to treat the infection with antibiotics.
However, in general, treatment with antibiotics is not recommended in the acute phase due to the
possible risk of HUS. HUS will frequently occur 5-13 days upon onset of diarrhea, hence initiation
of treatment is recommended 14 days after onset of diarrhea [Agger et al., 2015]. Studies have
shown that sub-lethal doses induces the Stx-encoding prophage(s), thus increasing the expression
16
==PAGE:17==
Pharmaceutical Design and Engineering
of stx which is associated with development of HUS. Furthermore, treatment with lethal dosage
may lead to bacterial death, causing a release of large amount of toxins, which can also become
dangerous or even fatal for the patient [Bielaszewska et al., 2012]. In order to eradicate STEC
from the intestine without causing a release of toxins, a suggested treatment involves a protein
synthesis inhibitor given orally for three days followed by a bactericidal drug, preferably a cell
wall inhibitor, for seven days Agger et al. [2015]. In a suggested treatment plan proposed by
SSI (the suggestion have been listed in appendix A) tobramycin and azithromycin is proposed as
protein synthesis inhibitors and mecillinam as a cell wall inhibitor. Furthermore, tetracycline is
commonly used as a protein inhibitor, when treating STEC infection in other countries. Finally,
ciproﬂoxacin is occasionally prescribed for patients with STEC infections, as treatment of other
diseases, which display symptoms resembling those of STEC, require ciproﬂoxacin treatment
(personal conversation with Kurt Fuursted, Head of Bacteriology Reference Department).
5.7.1
Antibiotics
DNA gyrase inhibitors: DNA gyrase is an enzyme of the class of topoisomerase (Type II
topoisomerase).
It introduce negative supercoils into DNA in order to relieve the positive
superhelical tension which occurs while unwinding the DNA helix in a closed circular DNA
molecule. DNA gyrase consists of two subunits, which in turn have two subunits each, i.e. subunit
2A and 2B. DNA is bound to both the A and B subunits, which hydrolyze ATP, and introduce
negative supertwists. Nicking of DNA is carried out by the A subunit, whereas the B subunit
introduces negative supercoils, after which the A subunit reseals the strands. Fluoroquinolones
(here under ciproﬂoxacin) inhibits DNA gyrase thus preventing replication of bacterial DNA
during bacterial growth and reproduction.[Engle et al., 1982].
As DNA gyrase inhibitors such as ciproﬂoxacin target the DNA synthesis, this may result in
increased Stx production due to activation of the bacterial SOS response, thus causing phage
induction.
Protein synthesis inhibitors: Essential for all cells are their proteins.
The function of a
protein is deﬁned by the molecular function, localization within cell and the involvement in
particular biological processes. The components of protein function are deﬁned by the structure,
composition and conformation of the proteins, which is encrypted within the locus. Protein
synthesis generate new proteins through a complex and multi step process which involves enzymes
and conformational alignment, and is balanced by loss of cellular proteins via degradation or
export [Rothman, 1994; McGannon et al., 2010].
tetracyclines, including the tetracycline (bacteriostatic), inhibits the protein synthesis
by preventing binding of aminoacyl-tRNA at the aminoacyl site of the 30S ribosome by
blocking the site.
Aminoglycoside, such as tobramycin, also have an aﬃnity for the 30S ribosome.
tobramycin binds to the 30S ribosome, hence blocking the formation of the larger 70S
initiation complex, thus inhibiting the protein synthesis
Azalides, a subgroup of macrolides, contains the antibiotic azithromycin (bacteriostatic).
Azithromycin binds to the 50S ribosomal subunit resulting in inhibition of the translocation
transpeptidation, thus the capacity of the peptide chain is impaired, hence inhibiting the
protein synthesis.

Cell wall synthesis inhibitors:
In Gram-negative bacterial cells the cellular content is
surrounded by an inner peptidoglycan cell wall, an inner plasma membrane and an outer lipid
bilayer. The cell wall provides mechanical protection and supports the internal osmotic pressure.
Peptidoglycan construction begins in the cytoplasm with the synthesis of a muramyl peptapeptide
precursor containing a terminal Asp-Ala-Asp-Ala.
In the second phase of peptidoglycan
construction, muramyl pentapeptide N-acetylglucosamine is transferred to a C55 undecaprenyl
phosphate with the release of UMP to form a Lipid I intermediate.
Several transpeptidases
and transglycosylases connect the newly formed peptidoglycan structures to the cell wall
peptidoglycan matrix [Durán and Pérez, 2004; Thulin et al., 2015].
β-lactam, contains a subgroup, the penicillins, which include mecillinam a bactericidal, that
binds to the PBP2, a transpeptidase subgroup, which is responsible for the elongation of rod-
shaped cells. When treated with mecillinam the bacterial cells becomes nondividing enlarged
spheres that ultimately lyse [Thulin et al., 2015].

Aim of study 6
Shiga toxin (Stx)-producing Escherichia coli (STEC) strains are zoonotic pathogens capable
of causing life-threatening disease in humans ranging from mild watery or bloody diarrhea to
hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS). The main virulence factor is thought to be Stx, which
is encoded by genes on lambdoid phages. These can be induced by external factors such as
antibiotics. Diﬀerent subtypes of Stx have shown to be related to the severity of disease. The
amount of Stx is thought to be dependent on the antiterminator protein Q and can vary even
within the same subtype.
This study aimed to evaluate the suggested antibiotic treatment, provided by SSI, of STEC
infections, without causing induction of the stx phage.
This study focused on the eﬀects
of tobramycin, tetracyclin, azithromycin and mecillinam, which is often used to treat STEC
and ciproﬂoxacin, which is commonly used for symptoms resembling STEC infections. Thus
investigating the change in phage enumeration, growth rate, survival of bacteria in the lytic
phase and subsequently toxin production. The changes will be investigated using Stx isolates
representing the most prevalent Stx subtypes from Danish patients.

determined by the Sensititre Vizion System (Computer) from which the well with the lowest
antibiotic concentration without growth were selected.

7.7.3
Detection of toxin levels
Upon incubation the media was removed from the wells of the microtiter plates which were
then washed in 200 µl PBS. Hereafter 200 µl of 4% formaldehyde/10% formalin solution (VWR
Chemicals) were added to each well and incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature to ensure
ﬁxation of the cells.
Afterwards, the cells were washed in PBS twice and stained with 200
µl/well 0.1% Crystal violet solution. (1.35 ml Sigma-Aldrich Crystal violet solution + 33 mL
milliQ water) at RT for 30 minutes. The crystal violet was removed and the wells were gently
washed with H2O. The Vero cell assay was quantiﬁed by OD readings using BioTek EL800
(OD595) plate reader. The 50% cytotoxic dose (CD50) represented the amount of toxin required
to kill 50% of the cells in a well.

Whole-Genome Sequence analysis
Sequences of the 13 isolates were tested by using the analysis pipeline webtool, accessed through
the CGE website (https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/cge/), which provide detailed information
about the isolates, hereunder virulence genes, MLST, resistance genes, plasmids and more.
Furthermore, MyDbFinder, also found on the CGE website, was employed to determine
associated phage genes, i.e. the cI repressor gene, Q genes, late promoter PR′ and S and R
lysis genes. The content of the MyDbFinder database was constructed to perform a genotypic
detection of phage associated genes. All genes, and gene variants, represented in the database
was identiﬁed with a GenBank accession number. The sequences were originally obtained by
investigating the phage genomes listed in the article written by Ogura et al. [Ogura et al., 2015].
The genes used in this study have been listed in Table 7.2.
The Q gene database which was also included in MyDbFinder, was constructed by Flemming
Scheutz, and harbored seven diﬀerent groups of Q genes and their subgroups, some divided into
subgroups (Table 5.3). In total the database contained 47 alleles, one for each group or subgroup.
A database containing Stx genes, were downloaded from the VirulenceFinder webtool, accessed
through the CGE website, in order to assess the possible association with phage genes, this was
also a part of MyDbFinder. When using MyDbFinder, the threshold selected for the identity
was set to 90% and the selected minimum length was set to the default setting at 60 %.

8.3
Antibiotic phage induction
In order to investigate if STEC properties such as stx subtype and serotype could be linked to
cytotoxicity levels of the isolates, all isolates were tested for antibiotic induction. Subsequently,
general diﬀerences in cytotoxicity were determined in order to assess the best antibiotic treatment
of the isolates. All isolates were cytotoxic towards the Vero cells when assessing samples taken
after 60 and 110 minutes upon induction, except for isolate D5940 which did not display any
cytotoxicity upon any antibiotic induction (will not be discussed further). The cytotoxicity of
the isolates has been displayed as a CD50, which indicates the toxin needed to kill 50 % of the
cells in each well found in the Vero cell assay. The experiments were performed in duplicates,
twice, thus resulting in four readings per isolate.
8.3.1
Phage induction with ciproﬂoxacin
Investigation of phage induction with ciproﬂoxacin treatment revealed that only one isolate,
D5953 (Figure 8.4a), had a signiﬁcant increase in cytotoxicity after 60 minutes, when grown
in induced condition compared to un-induced. However, the same isolate did not display any
increased cytotoxicity levels after 110 minutes, where the level of toxin was virtually the same
as seen in the un-induced isolate. Isolate D5934 and D5936 showed a signiﬁcant decrease in

the various isolates. Decreased levels of cytotoxicity is observed in isolates D5932, D5934, D5936

8.4
Plaque
In order to conﬁrm that the stx phages were present in the isolates tested throughout this
study, the supernatants of the isolates were examined for plaques. However, no plaques were
observed in the ﬁrst two experiments. As a ﬁnal attempt to achieve plaques, all isolates used
in this study, except controls, were grown without antibiotics in Evans and LB medium. In the
previous attempts, samples were collected after 100 and 150 minutes, whereas samples collected
from the overnight cultures were used in this experiment. This experiment revealed plaques
in samples grown both in Evans and LB medium. The plaques are visualized in Figure 8.12,
however some plaques are not visible in the pictures. Isolates grown in LB medium all produced
plaques (Figure 8.12a and 8.12b), both with raw material and supernatants. Some isolates i.e.
D5932, D5934, D5938, D5943 displayed a small lysis zone, whereas D5933, D5951 and D5953
displayed larger zones, indicating a higher production of phages in these isolates. Furthermore,
bacteria appeared to grow in the spots from both raw material and the supernatant. However,
in the supernatant from D5933 the bacterial growth seems to be somewhat inhibited compared
to all other stains. Isolates grown in Evans medium also produced plaques (Figure 8.12c and
8.12d), both with raw material and supernatants. However, in contrary to the isolates grown in
LB, only some isolates i.e. D5932, D5933, D5934, D5938, D5943, D5951 and D5953 displayed
plaques when using both raw material and supernatants. All isolates grown in Evans medium
displayed lysis zones, however D5933, D5951 and D5953 showed larger zones, again indicating
higher phage production. Again, bacteria appears to grow in the spots of both raw material and
supernatants.
When examining the strokes of bacteria on the LB plates with imbedded DH5α and a disk
containing ciproﬂoxacin, it became clear that the lysis zone of all bacteria, grown in both LB
and Evans medium, were very similar. In the lysis zone, no bacterial growth were seen for any
isolate, regardless the growth medium, as a result of the ciproﬂoxacine. When investigating the
stokes even further, it became clear that a lysis zone was visible between the lysis zone from
the ciproﬂoxacin disk and the bacterial growth within the stokes. No bacteria is present in this
additional lysis zone, however, growth is present in this area between the isolates. This zone is
very clear in isolate D5933 (Figure 8.13a). For some isolates a lysis zone is visible throughout
the stroke i.e. D5933, D5938, D5951 and D5953, on both raw material and supernatant.

Discussion 9
This study aimed to evaluate the eﬀetcs of the suggested antibiotic treatment of STEC infections,
provided by SSI, without causing an induction of the stx phage. This study focused on the eﬀects
of tobramycin, tetracyclin, azithromycin and mecillinam, which is often used to treat STEC as
well asthe eﬀect of ciproﬂoxacin, which is commonly used for treating symptoms resembling those
with STEC infections. This was done investigating the change in phage enumeration, growth
rate, survival of bacteria in the lytic phase and subsequently toxin production. The changes
were investigated using Stx isolates representing the most prevalent Stx subtypes from Danish
patients.
In order to evaluate the eﬀects of the various antibiotics on the isolates used in this study, MIC
tests were performed to establish the dose of antibiotic that would induce the Stx phages without
killing the bacteria. It was determined that the antibiotics would be given at 1/4 MIC value in
order to ensure viable cells. This project included 22 isolates (Table 7.1) which were the starting
material for this study. Based on serotype, MLST and Stx subtype the number of isolates was
reduced to 13 isolates. Isolates displaying the same STEC proﬁle were prioritized, in order to
investigate if these would react diﬀerently towards the antibiotic treatment. Isolates harboring
two Stx subtypes were not prioritized as Vero cell assays were not able not distinguish between
the toxins produced by the subtypes.
The growth, and thereby the exponential phase, of the isolates were determined by investigation
using the control strain, D3743, by measuring OD600 every 20 minutes. This showed that the
exponential phase was initiated after approximately 40 minutes and ended after ∼160 minutes, it
was assumed that the additional isolates would display similar growth patterns. However, there
was a chance that this would not be the case and therefore has to be taken into consideration
when evaluating the results.
In order to investigate changes throughout the exponential phase of the 13 selected isolates,
antibiotic were administered after 40 minutes, which had showed to be the expected start of the
exponential phase. Samples were collected after 100 and 150 minutes, corresponding to mid and
end of the exponential phase. The OD600 were measured in order to assess the growth of the
isolates upon antibiotic induction. Additionally, samples collected at the given timepoints were
used to investigate phage inductions, hereunder, cytotoxicity and plaques in the isolates.
When investigating the cytotoxicity of the isolates, it was observed that the isolates displayed
higher cytotoxicity in samples taken 60 minutes after antibiotic induction, and that the cyto-

The Vero cell assay contain some uncertainties. During preparation of the assays, the Vero cells
were counted and based on this it was calculated how much of the Vero cell suspension was
needed to make the assays. Hence some assays may contain a higher cell count per well, leading
to variations in cytotoxicity.
Moreover, the staining process of the Vero cells also has some potential variable. One step step
include removal of the media from the wells by tapping the plate on a cloth, which could detach
some cells from the well. Furthermore, if the cells were not ﬁxated properly, this could create the
same deviation as well. The last step of the staining process is the actual staining. If excess color
solution was not removed completely, it could result in a lower cytotoxicity, as more color would
indicate more living cells. However, the biggest pit fall did not stem from the assay but from the
statistical analysis. The cytotoxicity of the isolates was based on four values representing the
four replicates. However, when the cytotoxicity was too low to measure, no value was obtained,
leading to fewer cytotoxicity values. However, this was not reﬂected in the mean shown as SEM
for the isolates, as only actual values were considered. Therefore, the SEM should have been
much higher if all values were taking into account. It was calculated that 11.72 % of the values
used to calculate the cytotoxicity in isolates taken 60 minutes after induction, were blank, com-
pared to 7.65 % for samples taken after 110 minutes. Consequently, the discussion will focus on
the cytotoxicity measured after 110 minutes of antibiotic induction.
Treatment with ciproﬂoxacin did not appear to aﬀect the growth of the bacteria, as the

isolates grown in un-induced conditions.
Vero cell assays have often been used to monitor

cytotoxicity levels between the two other subtypes.

Which is in accordance with the ﬁndings indicating that stx1a was more cytotoxic towards Vero

In order to assess the phage induction caused by the various antibiotics used in this study, the
supernatants of the isolates, collected after 60 and 110 minutes after antibiotic administration,
were tested for plaques, surprisingly no plaques were visible for any isolate. The isolates tested for
plaques were grown in Evans medium, however, various studies have used LB medium to optimize
plaque formation. An additional experiment was therefore performed, using both Evans and LB
medium in which all isolates were grown and incubated overnight. Plaques were observed in
samples grown in both Evans and LB medium, however, it was clear that all isolates produced
plaques when grown in LB, whereas only D5932, D5933, D5934, D5938, D5943, D5951 and D5953
produced plaques when grown in Evans medium. The optimal medium for plaque experiments
would therefore have been LB, however, it is not known if this medium could be used for Vero
cell assays. In order to compare Vero cell results and plaques, the same medium must be used.
In order to determine the optimal medium for both Vero cell assay and plaques, further testing
are needed. Since isolates produced plaques from the overnight culture and not from the samples
collected after 100 and 150 minutes, it must be assumed that phages were not formed within
the time frame used in this study. Furthermore, this indicated that it was not phage induction
measured by the Vero cell assays but rather spontaneous release of toxins from the bacterial cell.
As the various isolates tested throughout this study displayed diﬀerences in cytotoxicity and
plaque formations, a sequence analysis was performed in order to investigate variation in phage
genes. Due to the plaque results it became clear that phage induction could not be measured
with the parameters used in this study, nonetheless the cytotoxicity varies from one isolate to
another. Subsequently the sequence analysis evolved around the phage as they are responsible
for toxin production. The Q gene was of great interest as this is thought to be directly linked to
the toxin production. Other phage genes were also investigated i.e. genes encoding; cI repressor,
late phage promoter PR′, lysis genes S and R. The analysis identiﬁed Q genes for nine isolates
within all subtypes tested throughout this study, however, no Q gene was identiﬁed for isolates
harboring stx2b. This could be explained by a poor assembly, however as this regards three
isolates, this is not likely. A study performed by Steyert et al [Steyert et al., 2012] did identify
Q genes in isolates containing stx2b, thus indicatig that the Q gene database used in this study
may not contain all Q genes. To ensure whether the isolates did in fact harbor a Q gene, further
testing is needed. The Q genes identiﬁed in this study mainly belonged to the group QIV , where
two isolates harbored Q gene from the QII and QV group respectively.
The analysis also revealed that most isolates harbored more than one Q gene, thus indicating
presence of more than one phage in the isolate. Moreover this could possible explain why some
isolates with identical biological properties displayed diﬀerences in cytotoxicity, both in patients
and on the Vero cell assay. Whether or not the additional Q genes is associated to additional
stx genes is not known at this stage. Studies performed by Ashton et al. [Ashton et al., 2015],
indicates that if the coverage of one gene exceeded 1.7x, more copies of this gene could be present
in the genome. More studies would be needed in order to determine if more than one copy of
stx genes was present in the isolates tested in this study. Additional Q genes could also account
for diﬀerences in cytotoxicity, even if they were not associated to Stx phages, but to other types

However the Vero cell assays did reveal the highest toxicity in isolates harboring stx1a. Studies

Conclusion 10
The overall conclusion of this study is that the treatment of STEC infections suggested by
Statens Serum Institut, using a protein synthesis inhibitor, azithromycin and tobramycin does
not cause phage induction in the isolates tested in this study. Tetracycline, however, should
not be used as a protein synthesis inhibitor, as some isolates displayed resistance towards this
antibiotic in addition to increased cytotoxicity. Moreover, treatment with mecillinam did induce
one isolate. Moreover, treatment with mecillinam should occur after initial treatment with a
protein synthesis inhibitor, according to the treatment suggestion, thus further investigations is
needed. Ciproﬂoxacin did not cause phage induction in any isolates in this study, however, it
has previously been proven that ciproﬂoxancin should not be used for STEC infections due to
the antibiotics’ ability to cause phage inductions. Furthermore, it became clear that no phage
induction could be measured in this study, but rather spontaneous toxin release was observed,
as no plaques was observed for any isolates grown in Evans medium, when collecting samples
after 60 and 110 minutes. Finally, no correlation between biological properties, phage genes and
STEC virulence were observed in this study.

Perspective and
limitations 11
The experiments performed in this study, should be replicated in order to verify the results
obtained. This was not possible in this thesis due to time limitations. However, the experimental
setup should be altered in order to optimize growth conditions for the bacteria, thus controlling
air ﬂow and nutrition provided for the isolates. This can be done by using ﬂasks instead of test
tubes. When optimizing the growth conditions, the time frame of the exponential phase and
phage production should also be tested in order to ﬁnd optimal measuring points that take both
into account. In addition, the optimal medium for Vero cell assay and plaque formation should
also be investigated in order to be able to compare the results obtained from these studies.
Moreover, in order to properly investigate the eﬀect of treatment with a protein synthesis
inhibitor followed by treatment with a cell wall inhibitor, an experiment setup should be
investigated in order to mimic the treatment, thus one antibiotic is given to the isolates prior to
the other.
No correlation between biological properties, phage genes and STEC virulence were observed
in this study, thus indicating that further investigations of the bacterial and phage genome is
needed in order to determine how bacteria with the same proﬁle display diﬀerent growth rates in
addition to cytotoxicity. Very few phage genes and alleles were tested in this study, thus a more
extensive research has to be performed in order to create a much better database for genotypic
detection of phage associated genes.
In order to investigate the mechanism of azithromycin, the isolates should be tested to determine
if the treatment killed the bacteria or let them to hibernation. This could be tested by taking
some bacteria from the treated culture and re-cultivate them in new media. Cell growth in the
new culture would be a result of hibernation and no growth would be a result of cell death.
To avoid transfer of toxins from the overnight cultures, when investigating growth and collecting
samples for the Vero cell assays, the overnight culture should be spun down by a centrifuge and
washed in order to discard the toxins, leaving only the bacterial cells which could be re-cultivated
for further testing.

Suggested treatment, SSI A
The treatment suggested by Statens Serums Institut, SSI, have been listed in both danish and
english.
Behandling
1.
Symptomatisk behandling; der anbefales, ved indlæggelseskrævende tilfælde af VTEC
infektion, rigelig væske for at modvirke thrombedannelse og vedligeholde organ perfusion inklusiv
renalt ﬂow, dette anses som det mest eﬀektive middel til at forebygge udvikling af HUS.
Antibiotika anbefales ikke umiddelbart grundet manglende evidens for bedring af forløbet,
og grundet formodning om at risikoen for udvikling af HUS øges ved antibiotisk behandling.
Sandsynligvis er risikoen for udvikling af HUS dog størst ved antibiotika i suboptimale
doser, således skal allerede iværksat behandling i relevant og eﬀektiv dosering, bedømt ud fra
resistensproﬁl, fortsættes.
2. Ved længerevarende (mere end 2 uger) diare, hos patient inﬁcereret med VTEC, der ikke
er HUS associeret, kan der behandles med antibiotika. In vitro forsøg har vist øget toksin pro-
duktion efter behandling med DNA-hæmmere som Ciproxin og Trimethoprim/Sulfamethoxazol,
hvorimod makrolider (proteinsyntese hæmmer) har været associeret med mindsket forekomst.
Således kunne et regime bestå i makrolid i 3 dage, f. eks azithromycin 500 mg eller tobramycin
til IV brug indtaget peroralt, 200 mg (5 ml) i 3 dage fulgt af baktericidt antibiotika, f. eks
mecillinam 400 mg gange 3 i 7 dage (afhængigt af resistensmønster).
