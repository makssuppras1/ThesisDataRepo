Summary
Recent studies show how flat regions of the parameter spaces of deep neural networks
correlate with the modelsтАЩ generalization capabilities. Sharpness-aware minimization
(SAM) seeks such flat regions by updating the parameters according to the gradient
at an adversarial perturbation, depending on Euclidean geometry. This makes SAM
non-invariant under reparameterizations thus blurring the relation between sharpness
and generalization.
In this MasterтАЩs thesis, we establish a reparameterization-invariant and loss-aware
version of SAM - namely, Riemannian SAM - using concepts from differential geome-
try. We take inspiration from the reparameterization-invariant Fisher SAM that uses
the Fisher metric, however, we change the geometric understanding in the parameter
space to be defined by the Monge metric that is naturally induced by the training loss
surface. The inverse Monge metric is analytically expressible, resulting in a closed-
form expression for the adversarial perturbation which Fisher SAM does not have.
We reveal that the Riemannian SAM perturbation theoretically trades-off gradient de-
scent and SAM behavior by punishing steepness of the loss. This makes Riemannian
SAM more conservative than SAM, which e.g. makes it less prone to escape valleys
of the loss. SAM and Riemannian SAM can be attracted to suboptimal equilibria,
e.g. saddle points, but we reveal that Riemannian SAM finds these equilibria less at-
tractive than SAM. We also see indications of this when applying Riemannian SAM
to deep neural networks, yet the method does not necessarily generalize better than
SAM in practice due to its conservativeness. Nonetheless, Riemannian SAM seems
more robust to hyperparameter tuning than SAM when applied on a multi-modal
alignment problem.
We discuss whether SAM-based solutions gives more distinct conceptual regions in
the latent space of a multi-modal model and further propose analyzing correlation
between alignment and flatness using a more explicit metric like the Hessian trace.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
One fundamental aspect of any predictive modelтАЩs performance is its ability to gen-
eralize, meaning performing well not only on training data but also on unseen data.
It is well-known that an overparameterized model - with more parameters, p, than
available training observations, N, i.e. p > N - can learn to memorize and interpolate
the training data, even in settings with noisy or spurious relations between inputs
and outputs. This rarely means that the model generalizes well. In the traditional
machine learning (ML) regime, the bias/variance trade-off of fitting the training data
and generalizing well to unseen data is accounted for by selecting the model at the
тАЭsweet spotтАЭ of a U-shaped risk curve, thus neither under- nor overfitting the data.
Deep neural networks (DNN) have increasingly gained attention when it comes to
solving complex prediction tasks across domains, the reason being their flexibility
which allows for fitting arbitrarily complex functions. With the increased computa-
tional capabilities, overparameterized DNNs have rather become the rule than the
exception for complex modeling. According to the traditional ML regime overparam-
eterized DNNs would easily overfit, yet Belkin et al. (2019) [1] argue that the modern
interpolation regime of DNNs (when p тЙеN) introduces a double descent risk curve
where heavily overparameterized models can obtain even better generalization capa-
bilities than underparameterized models.
This raises an important question: what enable heavily overparameterized models
to generalize as well as they do? In an early work by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber
(1997) [2] they argue that low network complexity expressed through flatness of min-
ima corresponds to high generalization performance, where a flat minimum is a large
region in parameter space with similar low-valued loss. Thus sharp minima require
high precision and are highly sensitive to misalignment between the learned data-
dependent function and the true test function. Notably, recent empirical studies find
correlation between flatness and generalization capabilities [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8], including
downstream performance of pre-trained large language models (LLM) [9].
Though several measures of sharpness exist, most recent works consider either the
spectral norm of the Hessian or the trace of its eigenvalues. These are computationally

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
This chapter reviews relevant literature related to finding flat minima with standard
and sharpness-seeking optimizers. We also remark on geometry in optimization and
how the Monge metric has been used in deep learning contexts. SAM has been studied
extensively in the literature, thus we only review selected works.
2.1
Approaches to finding flat minima
2.1.1
SAM-based approaches
The vanilla approach to sharpness-aware minimization through explicitly including a
simple sharpness term in the objective function was proposed by Foret et al. (2020)
[10] where the sharpness term considered is the difference in loss between the param-
eter estimate and the worst-case perturbation constrained to a Euclidean ball with
fixed radius. They theoretically justified generalization properties of SAM through
a PAC-Bayes generalization bound and empirically showed that SAM improved on
stochastic gradient descent (SGD) - also in settings with noisy labels. The PAC-Bayes
generalization bound was however criticized by other researchers [16] as it relies on
random perturbations rather than the worst-case perturbation used in practice. In
another study of SAM, Behdin and Mazumder [21] argued that SAM can obtain bet-
ter generalization performance than gradient descent (GD) due to its implicit bias
favoring solutions with low bias but higher variance.
Injecting noise by decreasing the batch size showed increased generalization for SAM.
This was empirically found in the original paper and also explicitly analyzed by An-
driushchenko and Flammarion [16] who investigated the implicit bias of SAM and
GD. They found that SAM with lower batch sizes promotes sparsity and showed
that this property led to increased generalization performance when fine-tuning a
GD-trained model with SAM. A potential reason for this is that SAM is capable of
moving along minima as highlighted by Dai, Ahn, and Sra [22]. They criticized previ-
ous papers (e.g. [16, 21, 23]) for disregarding the normalization term when analyzing
the SAM perturbation and denoted the approach of previous papers as unnormalized
SAM (USAM). They argued that normalization stabilizes SAM and allows for moving
along minima manifolds, eventually finding flatter regions in parameter space. Their
empirical results suggested that USAM is practically like enhanced GD rather than

simplified SAM as USAM converged earlier on at sharper solutions with SAM giving
better generalization performance when fine-tuning from a bad global minimum.
A noteworthy, yet undesirable, property of SAM is the fact that it is attracted to
saddle points, which was observed by Kaddour et al. [24] and recently studied by
Compagnoni et al. [25]. Furthermore, a study by Kim et al. [23] considered stability
analysis of SAM and they proposed a theoretical criteria for saddle point attraction,
showing that saddle points becomes stable under SAM dynamics if the perturbation
radius is too large relative to the geometry of the surrounding loss.
As mentioned, Dinh et al. [11] discussed one major issue of the sharpness consid-
ered by SAM; it does not account for the geometry of the parameter space. They
based their arguments on the fact that rectifier units allow for non-identifiability -
i.e. yielding identical model output for several parameter settings - which allows for
arbitrary parameter rescaling without affecting the generalization gap, thereby blur-
ring the relation between flatness and generalization. In continuation Kwon et al.
[13] argued that SAM contains an inherent scale-dependency problem.
They pro-
posed a scale-invariant sharpness definition and a modified version of SAM - namely,
adaptive SAM (ASAM) - that computes the worst-case perturbation in a normalized
parameter space, most typically by element-wise parameter scaling with the abso-
lute values. Eventually, they showed superior performance to SAM on a variety of
tasks including image classification, robustness to label noise and machine translation.
Though ASAM is somewhat aware of the parameter space geometry, the choice of
normalization operator is rather ad-hoc. Furthermore, neural networks typically aim
at minimizing a probabilistic loss function - e.g. negative log-likelihood (NLL) or
cross-entropy (CE) - that exploits the model parameters, w, through a predictive
distribution, p (y|x, w). Thus, the parameter space geometry is a statistical manifold
that is not properly captured by Euclidean geometry. Concretely, introducing such
non-Euclidean geometry for SAM was addressed by Kim et al. [14]. They adjusted
the perturbation step by considering the KL-divergence between the predictive dis-
tributions associated to the current parameters and the parameters at the worst-case
perturbation and showed that this locally corresponds to the Fisher information ma-
trix (FIM). Thus they equipped the parameter space with the FIM when computing
the perturbation and eventually obtained slightly better generalization performance
on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 than standard SAM or ASAM using a variety of back-
bones, yet these performance gains were not necessarily significant. Recently, Yun
and Yang [15] proposed a general framework - that also contains Fisher SAM - for
applying SAM on Riemannian manifolds by exploiting the tangent space and expo-
nential maps; applying this method requires pre-defining the manifold on which to
optimize. With their Riemannian formulation of the method they presented conver-
gence analyses for the general case of SAM on Riemannian manifolds.

2.1.2
Other approaches to finding flat minima
A few years prior to the proposal of SAM, Izmailov et al. (2018) [5] proposed stochas-
tic weight averaging (SWA) that exploits averaging of weights over the iterations of
stochastic gradient descent (SGD). They found that learning with SWA led to wider
optima than SGD could find and provided empirical results for improved generaliza-
tion performance for a variety of tasks. A more recent study carried out by Kaddour
et al. (2022) [24] compared SAM to SWA and found that SAM finds flatter optima
than SWA in terms of eigenvalues of the Hessian. Through a comprehensive empirical
study they revealed that SAM more frequently generalizes better, however, that SWA
works better for graph representation learning (GRL) tasks.
Other approaches include the 1) randomly smoothed perturbation algorithm and the
2) sharpness-aware perturbation algorithm, both proposed by Ahn, Jadbabaie, and
Sra [26]. They proposed smoothing the gradient update by adding a perturbation di-
rection to the original gradient step when the gradient norm is suямАiciently low. If this
criteria is satisfied, the first version uses a randomly perturbed iterate while the second
version exploits a SAM-based perturbation. Both approaches use a тАЭprojecting-outтАЭ
operator that detaches the gradient information from the perturbation information.
The methods were not empirically compared with SAM and SWA.
2.1.2.1
Stochastic Gradient Descent
A study by Zhang et al. [27] revealed how stochastic gradient descent (SGD) - i.e.
gradient descent with noisy gradients - is capable of converging to well-generalizing
minima without explicit regularization, suggesting that the implicit bias of the opti-
mizer favors minima that generalize well. What was revealed in a study by Keskar
et al. [3] is that SGD with large batch sizes tends to converge to sharp minima of the
training function whereas SGD with small batch sizes finds flatter minima, thereby
increasing generalization. They argued that this is due to the fact that large-batch
methods are less explorative than small-batch methods which are more noisy, thereby
converging to qualitatively different regions with different generalization properties.
Different studies by Jastrz─Щbski et al. [28, 6] revealed that the learning rate to batch
size ratio works as a scaling of the stochastic noise term, thereby controlling the dy-
namics. Thus, larger step sizes mean convergence to flatter minima. Similar results
were found by Xing et al. [29]; they found that loss interpolation of subsequent pa-
rameter coordinates is a roughly convex function with a valley floor (minimum) and
thus showed that SGD moves in valley-like regions of the loss landscape during most
of the training. They argued that the learning rate controls the height above this
valley floor at which SGD takes steps while small batch sizes inject noise into the
dynamics, the combination of which allows for quickly exploring the parameter space
far from initialization, potentially finding flatter regions that generalize well.
The previous works all share the conclusion that noise in SGD impacts generalization

properties. Smith, Elsen, and De [30] provided a set of carefully designed experiments
to verify that the effect of smaller batch sizes on the generalization performance is
valid rather than a question about suboptimal hyperparameter optimization for larger

a study by Zhu et al. [31] also examined the benefits of noise in SGD and argued that
SGDтАЩs ability to eямАiciently escape sharp minima and approach flatter minima relates

2.2
Geometry in optimization and deep learning
A large body of work has focused on optimization on Riemannian manifolds [32].
Particularly, the natural gradient descent (NGD) is well-studied since its proposal for
perceptrons [33] and later for deep neural networks [34]. Essentially, it lifts standard
gradient descent from Euclidean space to operating on the Riemannian manifold of
probabilistic models by equipping the parameter space with the FIM. In practice, the
gradient is preconditioned by the inverse FIM, making it it approximately invariant to
reparameterizations [35]. A study by Kerekes, M├йsz├бros, and Husz├бr [36] questioned
whether the specific parameterization-dependency of standard GD impacts the induc-
tive biases to e.g. find better generalizing optima. They found empirical examples of
Euclidean GD obtaining better generalization performance than NGD, thus deeming
the reparameterization-invariance of NGD somewhat counterproductive.
2.2.1
The Monge metric
The Monge metric has gained attention for a handful of applications for the past
years due to its simplistic form; it was e.g. comprehensively studied by Hartmann,
Girolami, and Klami [37] in context of geometric Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
sampling. They replaced the FIM with the Monge metric for improving the eямАiciency
of geometric MCMC sampling and argued that the proposed metric has an inherent
notion of curvature. A different study conducted by Bergamin et al. [38] applied the
metric in context of Laplace approximations for Bayesian neural networks (BNN) for
better capturing the underlying geometry of the intractable posterior. Their modified
approach works well on a variety of problems, yet a study performed by Yu et al. [39]
revealed that the sample distributions of this Riemannian Laplace approximation are
not exact, even for Gaussian targets; it is conservative as it samples closer to the
mode. Instead Yu et al. proposed replacing the Monge metric with a metric based on
the FIM, resulting in improved sample quality.

CHAPTER 6
General discussion
This study sheds light on a novel, geometry-aware approach to sharpness-aware min-
imization that is reparameterization-invariant. After establishing the optimizer, we
examined our research questions through 1) theoretical studies regarding how the
geometry-awareness impacts properties of SAM and 2) considering how the method
behaves in practical deep learning scenarios, compared to other optimizers.
We theoretically showed that the Riemannian SAM perturbation ranges between be-
ing practically ineffective or exactly the SAM perturbation in the extreme cases; we
also saw implications of this trade-off between SAM with gradient descent behavior
when considering generalization performance of practical examples on deep neural
networks, however, with Riemannian SAM behaving more like SAM than gradient
descent. Theoretically, the fact that the Riemannian SAM perturbation is a mix of
gradient descent and SAM had the implication of making Riemannian SAM conser-
vative, meaning that it is more prone than SAM to exploit the valley it is currently
located in rather than escaping it and allowing for exploration of the full parameter
space. Experimentally, we see this exact behavior through the training performance in
the fine-tuning experiment of ResNet-18 in Section 5.1.1 where SAM is more affected
by large perturbation radii, suggesting that it can escape the bad global minimum val-
ley and find a solution that generalizes better. Though we did not directly run deep
learning experiments on Riemannian SAMтАЩs ability to better avoid saddle points - or
other suboptimal equilibria - than SAM, our findings from the alignment experiment
clearly revealed that SAM oftentimes diverge to extremely bad locations in parame-
ter space where Riemannian SAM succeeds due to its conservativeness, revealing that
the hypothesized robustness to hyperparameter tuning holds - at least for some tasks.
The alignment experiment revealed that sharpness-awareness gave better alignment
scores. We also qualitatively saw that the approach helped separate the latent space
into slightly more conceptual regions, suggesting that flatness in the parameter space
correlates positively with conceptual alignment in the latent space of the multi-modal
CLIP model. It would be interesting to further examine the relation between flatness
and representational alignment for multi-modal models, e.g. through a large-scale
experiment on pre-training multi-modal models in a similar way to what Liu et al. [9]
did for LLMs. As future work, we also suggest trying to merge Riemannian SAM with
the sharpness-aware perturbation algorithm. This could potentially improve practi-

cal use of the latter method as it currently requires specifying whether the gradient
norm is low enough to do a sharpness-aware update. Contrarily, Riemannian SAM
handles this by smoothly scaling the perturbation according to the gradient norm.
Though the SAM-based optimizers do incorporate a notion of sharpness, it is rather
simple, yet, we have restricted ourselves to rely on this inherent difference-based
sharpness in the deep learning experiments.
Another and more explicit approach
would have been to compute and report flatness in terms of the trace of the Hessian.
We want to emphasize that Riemannian SAM requires two backward passes per up-
date, thus for training large networks to find flat solutions, we propose training a
two-stage training approach of first applying Adam or so until convergence, followed
by an adjustment phase using Riemannian SAM.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this MasterтАЩs thesis, we motivated why establishing a reparameterization-invariant
version of the sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) optimizer is important. With
the aim of constructing a version of SAM that works under any model formulation,
we defined a manifold for general loss landscapes and considered the Monge metric
that was naturally induced by the training loss surface. Similarly to the Fisher SAM
approach, we searched for a worst-case perturbation using the inverse metric, thereby
proposing the Riemannian SAM optimizer. Due to the simplicity of the proposed
metric, the Riemannian SAM perturbation turns out to have a closed-form solution
whereas Fisher SAM requires explicit approximation and regularization.
Through theoretical analyses, we discovered that Riemannian SAM is a scaled ver-
sion of SAM, that diminishes perturbations in steep parameter space regions. This
effectively makes Riemannian SAM function as a mix of SAM and gradient descent,
with the ╬╖-parameter directly controlling the loss-awareness and thereby how much to
penalize steepness. Comparative analysis in a symmetric loss setting further revealed
that Riemannian SAM is conservative, robust to the hyperparameter configuration
and converges to lower loss values than SAM, indicating that it finds a better solution
than SAM if both methods end up in the same valley of the loss function. In more
complex loss landscapes, Riemannian SAM and SAM might be less likely to identify
the same valley as SAM is more capable of escaping suboptimal regions and explore
the parameter space while Riemannian SAM is limited in its exploration.
We further uncovered that SAM-based optimizers exhibit notable tendencies to get
attracted to suboptimal equilibria such as saddle points and even local maxima. By
deriving a differential equation of slight variations across parameter space, we found
that the regions of attraction depend on the interplay between the effective pertur-
bation radius and local curvature. Again, the conservativeness of Riemannian SAM
- showing itself through the effective perturbation radius - plays a crucial role, as it
shrinks the regions of attraction around non-mimima equilibria for Riemannian SAM,
compared to SAM.
Our theoretical analyses revealed the trade-offs of Riemannian SAM; while it might
settle for less optimal solutions than SAM, it reduces the chances of getting stuck
at saddle points and appears to be more robust to the hyperparameter setting. As

such it is potentially easier to tune, making it a promising optimization technique for
practical machine learning challenges depending on how the developer prioritizes the
tuning/performance trade-off.
Through experimental studies of applying Riemannian SAM to deep learning con-
texts, we verified that the method is a good choice for fine-tuning to find a better
generalizing solution. We saw this when fine-tuning from a bad global minimum of
ResNet-18 as well as on a larger study on representational alignment using CLIP.
Specifically, we found that searching with Riemannian SAM for flatter regions in pa-
rameter space gave regions in the latent representation space that were conceptually
better aligned, which was not obtainable to the same extent when using standard
optimizers like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and Adam. Though standard SAM
found conceptually better aligned regions than SGD and Adam, Riemannian SAM
was more robust to hyperparameter optimization - agreeing with the theoretical in-
dications - and obtained approx. 4% better alignment than SAM. On the downside,
Riemannian SAM performed slightly worse than SGD and SAM on average when used
for training a ResNet-18 from scratch to solve an image classification task. We lastly
proposed ideas for future experimental work and briefly discussed minor limitations
of the method and analyses.

Relation between the hyperparameters ╧Б and ╬╖
The scale of the Riemannian SAM perturbation depends on the gradient norm, per-

pendence reveals how Riemannian SAM shifts between SAM and GD behavior.
In Figure A.1 the relation between ╧Б, ╬╖ and their impact on the scaling term is
shown for a range of gradient norm values. Closer to equilibria, e.g. тИетИЗтИе2 = 0.2, the

the scaling function decreases with ╬╖ for higher norm-values revealing the quadratic

setting the perturbation radius of Riemannian SAM. If we fix the hyperparameters to
(╬╖, ╧Б) = (9, 0.9) (shown as ), Riemannian SAM behaves like SAM close to stationary
points (e.g. local minima) and takes relatively large steps as ╧Б is high, while the
high ╬╖ gives low scaling values (GD behavior) for larger gradient norms even though
╧Б is large.

bad equilibria without loosing stability away from equilibria (by jointly increasing ╧Б
and ╬╖), while the fixed perturbation radius of SAM limits how large ╧Б can be before
becoming unstable when away from equilibria. Nonetheless, high values of ╬╖ gives
GD-like behavior for Riemannian SAM which empirically generalize worse than SAM.
