Abstract
In November 1940, the well known Tacoma Narrows Bridge (TNB) collapsed due to large
torsional oscillations. Recent research presents wind conditions with yawed ﬂow direction of
β = 45◦and elaborates the effect of this on vortex formations leading to aerodynamic instability
in the form of torsional oscillation. Previous experimental investigations only consider ﬂow at
β = 0◦.
The present thesis will focus on the ability to recreate these ﬂow conditions and study the
vortex topology and strength, through numerical CFD simulations in Star CCM+. A series
of assessments are made to study the ability to model vortex formations around and in the
near wake of sharp-edge bluff bodies exposed to yawed ﬂow conditions, as well as validating
Strouahl number and drag coefﬁcients in relation to the Independence Principle (IP). This is
for use in the ﬁnal simulations of an elastically suspended TNB model in torsional oscillation.
From the assessments, yaw ﬂow around a square cylinder provided reliable IP validity up to
β = 75◦, while periodic coherent vortex formations was found in the near wake with a wave
length that relates to the bluff body height. Further, for an assessment of yaw ﬂow around
a stationary TNB model, similar periodicity of vortex structures were observed, again with
periodicity related to model height. Three turbulence models provided by Star CCM+, being
the k −ω STT, k −ε Realizable and Large Eddy Simulation (LES) model, were tested in their
performance on providing accurate Stourhal number and mean drag coefﬁcients, as well as
capturing coherent vortex structures. While accounting for requirements to mesh resolution at
present Reynolds numbers, the k −ω STT model provided best performance.
The ﬁnal dynamic simulations of the torsional oscillating TNB model, at yaw angles β = 0◦
and β = 45◦, showed that vortex formation at the leading edge of the bridge model was very
two-dimensional during the ﬁrst quarter oscillations from horizontal to maximum oscillation,
at both yaw angles. This oscillation ﬁrst quarter oscillation period was where the expanding
vortex model by (Larsen, 2016) predicts a dominating effect of this vortex on the onset of
oscillations. The fact that this vortex formation was very two-dimensional at yaw ﬂow was
to the authors knowledge not observed before in previous investigations. The strength of the
vortex formation was found to be close to factor 3 higher at β = 0◦than β = 45◦, which
supports previous observations Malthe et al. (2017) and predictions by Larsen (2016).

Preface
The project in the present report is the ﬁnal thesis in fulﬁllment of the Master degree programme
in Engineering Design and Applied Mechanics within the Mechanical Engineering faculty at
Technical University of Denmark. The project has been developed over a period from October
2016 to April 2017.
Main parts of the project are based on numerical simulations with the commercial CFD software
Star CCM+ v. 12.02.011, while data is processed using Matlab R2016b. As the report contains
a large amount of tables and visualizations, these are placed at the each chapter to provide a
smooth ﬂow when reading the reports content.
A special thanks goes to my supervisors Jens Honore Walther, Allan Larsen and Henrik Juul
Spietz for providing guidance and constructive feedback whenever needed. Finally, I would
like to thank my family for the support given throughout the project period.
Daniel Sand Christensen (s073422)

Chapter 1
Introduction
In November 1940, the well known Tacoma Narrows Bridge (TNB) collapsed due to self-induced
torsional oscillations as a result of the aerodynamic forces present for the wind condition on the
day of the collapse. The torsional oscillations of the bridge deck reached amplitudes +/- 35 ◦
until collapse of main span between the bridge towers. Immediately after the collapse, several
investigations were initiated to ﬁnd explanation for the wind dynamics causing this collapse.
Among these investigations, wind tunnels experiments by the US Federal Work Administrations
and The University of Washington concluded that the TNB should have collapsed already at
half the wind speed in comparison to the one measured on the day of the collapse (Malthe
et al., 2017). However, these investigations considered a wind direction perpendicular to the
bridge span, while resent research presents the wind direction as yawed 45 ◦to the upstream
girders of the bridge span, lowering the required wind speed for collapse (Malthe et al., 2017).
After the collapse of the main bridge span, the side side-spans between the bridge towers and
land experienced a short drop due elastic deﬂection of the bridge tower. Once rising again, one
of the side-spans was lifted while in a state of torsional deﬂection amplitude. Due to dust from
cracked concrete in the bridge deck, the formation of a vortex expanding across the bridge
width became visible as shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Visual formation of the vortex expanding across the width of the TNB deck
(University of Washington, 2017).

1.1
Project speciﬁcation
The main goal of the current project is to investigate the vortex behavior on the deck and in
the wake of the Tacoma Narrows bridge section model, similar to the one build and tested
at Force technology by Maja Rønne and Marlene Grundvig Malthe with Jens Honore Walther
and Henrik Juul Spietz from DTU, as well as Allan Larsen from COWI as supervisors. The
bridge model has been experimentally tested for vortex induced oscillations in a wind tunnel to
investigate the effect of vortex shedding behaviour on the aerodynamic damping of the bridge
model at different inﬂow velocities. Furthermore, the experiment investigated the effect of yaw
angled inﬂow, also on the aerodynamic damping of the bridge model as function of the inﬂow
velocity. This was to support the analytic model for self-induced torsional oscillations of bluff
bridge sections, presented by Larsen (2016). The current project focuses on CFD simulations
in Star CCM+ to investigate three-dimensional vortex shedding behaviour effects at similar
yaw angles and hereby determine the effects on the bridge vibrations. At the same time, the
CFD simulations are used to validate the results of the experimental model. As reference to the
yaw angled ﬂow, the simulations are also performed at perpendicular wind ﬂow for comparison.
To realize this, the following main objectives are to be completed during the project:
1. Simulation of yawed stationary square cylinder to validate results for Strouhal number
and drag coefﬁcients based relevant literature and previous investigations. Further,
the three-dimensional vortex behavior around the cylinder and in the wake ﬁeld is
investigated in order to validate the simulation model for use with the Tacoma model.
2. Simulation of yawed stationary Tacoma bridge section model to investigate the vortex
behavior on the bridge deck and in the wake ﬁeld. Strouhal number and drag coefﬁcients
are compared to relevant literature concerning geometries similar to the Tacoma model
experiments.
3. Simulation of yawed dynamic Tacoma bridge section model, in torsional vibration, to
investigate the three-dimensional vortex behavior on the bridge deck and in the wake ﬁeld.
In addition to this, the vortex strength is studied to support predictions by experimental
investigation by Malthe et al. (2017) and the expanding vortex model by Larsen (2016).
The three objectives listed above simultaneously constitute the methodology of the phases
elaborated throughout the present project.

Chapter 2
Background Theory
2.1
Bluff body aerodynamics
In civil engineering, most structures present bluff bodies to wind. The understanding of bluff
body aerodynamics is therefore of essence to understand vortex formations. As the ﬁeld of
aerodynamics is rather comprehensive, only important features concerning bluff body ﬂow
are presented in this report. In general, from an aerodynamic point of view, structures within
civil engineering are dealing with atmospheric natural wind. This makes it possible to limit
the scope of investigation to low-speed, in-compressible ﬂow phenomena. For the assumption
of in-compressibility to hold, a ﬂuid portion is said to be constant in density throughout its
motion in ﬂow. Although gasses such as air are compressible ﬂuids, they can however as an
approximation be modeled as in-compressible ﬂuids when the density changes are less than 5%,
which is the case at low Mach number < 0.3. Further, any compressible effects for air at room
temperature can be neglected for ﬂow speeds under 100 m/s (Cengel et al., 2010). Another
common assumption for the mentioned ﬂow type, is that of in-viscid ﬂow, implying that the
kinematic viscosity of the ﬂuid i zero (µ = 0). For this assumption to be valid, the inertial
forces need to by much greater than the viscous forces, hence yielding a large Reynolds number.
In external ﬂow, the viscous shear forces are negligible and in-viscid ﬂow can therefore be
assumed. In viscous regions, such as boundary layers and to some degree the wake downstream
of a body, the viscous shear forces are present and in-viscid ﬂow can therefore not be assumed.
2.1.1
Boundary layer, separation and Reynolds number
To get trustworthy simulations of vortex structure formations in ﬂow around bluff bodies, a
correct simulation of boundary layers and separation is crucial. Although viscosity of air is
small, it plays a signiﬁcant role in the formation of boundary layers. When a ﬂuid ﬂows past a
smooth surface, all experimental observations show that the ﬂuid comes to a complete stop
and "sticks" to the surface. This retardation of the ﬂuid is formed due to viscous shear forces
and friction induced by the presence of the surface. The result is zero relative velocity of the
ﬂuid directly at the surface, a condition in general referred to as no-slip condition for viscous

is reached. As mentioned before, two very inﬂuential force effects are present in the ﬂow of a
ﬂuid, the ﬁrst being the inertial forces dominating in the external ﬂow and second being the
viscous forces dominating in the ﬂow close to the bluff body. The relation between these two
forces is given by the non-dimensional Reynolds number:

forces dominate, which gives a more laminar ﬂow with smooth and steady motion. Vortex
induced vibration of structures occur at high Reynolds number in the presence of turbulence
where vortex formation is present. One should however be careful to use Reynolds number as
direct comparison of dynamic behavior of ﬂuid ﬂow since factors such as surface temperature
and roughness, as well as geometrical shape have equally dominant inﬂuence on the ﬂow
behavior. The geometrical factor is especially of important inﬂuence in the present studies as
the focus of investigation is made on a square cylinder and an H-proﬁle cross section, where
sharp edges introduce high amount of separation. At separation due to sharp edges, reversed
ﬂow may occur, creating a turbulent shear layer region. For a rectangular cross section, the ﬂow
may reattach if the downstream length of the body is large enough. In reality, the Reynolds
number differs along a bluff body depending in where the ﬂuid motion is considered, this is
due to the fact that velocity varies in places where the ﬂow is interrupted by e.g. sharp corners.
The same goes for the ﬂow wake of a body. However, the Reynolds number based on the mean
free-stream velocity is dominant and is therefore in general considered.
2.1.2
Fluid motion and vortex shedding
When bluff bodies are exposed to ﬂow, the motion of the ﬂuid changes. At Reynolds number
above a certain range, depending on the geometry, vortex shedding will take place in the wake
of the body. When ﬂuid approaches the body it will separate at a stagnation point, this point is
located in the upwind surface of the body as seen in Figure 2.1. At this point local pressure will
be relatively large compared to the rest of the body. A viscous shear ﬂow will develop in the
transverse vertical direction of the upwind surface. At low Reynolds number, the viscous shear
forces will be dominant and the ﬂow will in general follow the curvature of a body. At high
Reynolds number however, the ﬂow will separate depending on the increase in velocity and
turbulence in the ﬂow. For a continuous-curvature body like a circular cylinder, the separation
point oscillates at the downstream side of the cross-section. For bodies with sharp edges the
separation points are instead ﬁxed at these edges and the ﬂow will detach from the body.
Taking the example of a square shape as in Figure 2.1, the ﬂuid will separate at the upwind
sharp edges, creating a shear layer and leading to a low pressure at the upwind end of the

close to those mentioned above.

2.1.4
Independence principle
When a cylinder is placed in a yawed angle with respect to the main ﬂow direction, the
aerodynamic forces and the response of the cylinder body may change, compared to the case
where ﬂow is perpendicular on the cylinder. According to Hoerner (1965), for cylinder bluff
bodies yawed at angle β, ﬂow pattern and ﬂuid-dynamic pressure forces of such a bodies

axis. This phenomena is generally known as the Independence Principle (IP) or alternatively
the cross-ﬂow principle or cosine law. Another effect of IP is that the axial velocity component
may affect the vortex shedding characteristics. Several studies have shown that over a certain
range of yaw angles for a circular cylinder, the Strouhal number and the drag coefﬁcient,

2.2
Aeroelasticity
When a body is immersed in a ﬂow, ﬂuctuating surface pressures will induce forces leading to
oscillatory motion. The oscillatory motion of a body will in turn affect the ﬂuid forces. The
study of phenomena where aerodynamics forces and structural motions interact signiﬁcantly
with each other is known as Aeroelasticity. When the body in a ﬂuid deﬂects under the
mentioned forces and the initial deﬂections give rise to succeeding deﬂections of oscillatory
motion of diverging character, a phenomena known as Aerodynamic instability is produced.
All aerodynamics forces that act upon a body as consequence of its motion are refereed to as
self-exciting forces. Suspension bridges, such as the Tacoma Narrows bridge, with an H-shaped
cross section, are susceptible to aeroelastic effects in the presence of self-exiting forces. Such
effects are known to be ﬂutter and galloping, which can be studied by experimental or numerical
models. Theories such as stall ﬂutter and galloping have been discussed by Malthe et al. (2017)
and were found to be inadequate to describe the torsional self-induced vibration of the actual
event of the TNB collapse, as they relied on respectively static forces and high reduced velocities.
A model presented by Larsen (2016) to describe the vortex induced torsional motion of the
TNB is given later in this section, after an introducing of the governing equations for torsional
dynamics of structures.

2.2.2
Expanding vortex model
A model to describe the dynamic torsional motion of an H-proﬁle body such as the TNB is
given by Larsen (2016). The model describes the motion in relation to the vortex formed at
the upwind bridge girder. This vortex acts with an outward pointed force on the bridge deck
creating a torsional moment. When an H-proﬁled bridge deck section in torsional motion
moves through a horizontal line parallel to the wind direction, referred to as zero position,
vortex is formed at the upwind leading girder of the proﬁle. If the torsional direction is nose
up, the vortex will be formed at the upper leading girder, rotating clockwise and generating a
suction pulling in the nose up direction. At the lower leading girder, a counter rotating vortex is
formed. If the torsional direction is nose down, the vortex will be formed at the lower leading
girder and equally creating a suction puling in the nose down direction. The suction created on
the bridge deck can by presented by a outward pointing force from the bridge deck, creating a
torsional motion when the vortices are formed alternately. The vortex formation is shown in
Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of expanding vortex model in equilibrium state (Larsen, 2016).
The vortex created at one of the leading girder will grow in size until the center of it reaches
the mid chord of the deck (center of rotation). Hereby the outward pointing force moves with
the center of the vortex. While expanding, the source of circulation is assumed to be ﬁxed at
the leading girder until the vortex center reaches mid chord where the source of circulation
detaches. From here, the vortex travels to 3/4 chord point where it will be lifted of the bridge
deck by recirculating ﬂow at the trailing bridge girder.
As seen in Figure 2.3, when the vortex travels towards 1/4 of the chord length, the vortex force
and deck velocity will point in the same direction. Reaching 1/4 of the chord, the bridge is in
maximum deﬂection amplitude θmax and the velocity is zero. At this point the bridge deck will
start rotating toward the zero position. While doing so, the vortex will travel towards the mid
chord, with the force direction still pointing outward but the velocity vector pointing in the
opposite direction. Reaching the mid chord, the deck velocity is zero and as the vortex creates
no moment as it has no "arm". As it is seen that velocity is zero when the vortex is at 1/4, 1/2
and 3/4 chord length, hence no work is exerted at these points.

2.3
Previous related work
This section tried to outline previous work related to experimental and numerical ﬁndings on
bluff body aerodynamics in yawed ﬂow as well as work related to the ﬂow phenomena leading
to the collapse of the TNB. Both experimental and numerical ﬁndings are of interest as they
can support each other where uncertainties of one can be uplifted by the results from the other.
Of special interest are yawed circulars and square cylinders as well studies of ﬂow around an
H-shaped proﬁle.
2.3.1
Bluff body ﬂow investigations
As mentioned in Section 2.1.3, an experimental wind tunnel study by Okajima (1982) was made
for square and rectangular cross-section of varying width-to-height ratio. It was found that
the Strouhal number of a square cylinder remained largely constant at approx. St = 0.13 for
varying Reynolds number in the range Re = 100 −20000. Some variation is seen in the range
Re = 70 −500 and is attributed to the transition of 2D to 3D ﬂow structures. For rectangular
cross sections with B/H > 2, the ﬂow is seen reattach to the upper and lower surfaces parallel
to the free-stream direction, lowering the Strouhal number. For simulation of a square cylinder,
the Strouhal number is expected to lie close to St = 0.13 in the aforementioned Reynolds
number range.
Ramberg (1983) investigated the wake behind a yawed circular cylinder of ﬁnite length. The
study was made experimentally in a wind tunnel for a stationary and vibrating state (vibration
transverse to free-stream direction). Investigated yawed yaw angles were in the range of
0◦≤β ≤60◦for Reynolds number range Re = 160 −1000 and for length/diameter ratios of
L/D ≈20 −100. Furthermore, variation of end conditions were made with different conﬁgur-
ations of end plates. Ramberg (1983) concluded that for yawed stationary cases, the vortex
shedding frequency was always higher than predicted by the IP, however the results for normal
incidence ﬂow matched those of previous studies.
A numerical investigation was made (Zhao et al., 2009) to investigate the validity of IP for
yawed stationary circular cylinders with DNS in CFD analysis at Re = 1000. Results showed IP
validity for yaw angles in the range of 0◦≤β ≤60◦. As mentioned in Section 2.1.4, Zhao et al.
(2009) also compared results those by Ramberg (1983) and argued that cylinder end effects
may inﬂuence the validity of IP. Further, Zhao et al. (2009) used periodic boundaries in the span-
wise simulation domain direction to simulate a cylinder of inﬁnite length. The use of periodic
boundaries is chosen for domain conﬁguration in the present project as well to avoid end effects.
Lou et al. (2016) made an investigation of a yawed square cylinder in an experimental wind
tunnel analysis at yaw angles of 0◦≤β ≤60◦and Re = 3600. This study showed IP validity

for yaw angles in the range of 0◦≤β ≤40◦. At higher angles the Stouhal number and drag
coefﬁcients gave respectively too high and too low results. Furthermore, comparing with Zhao
et al. (2009) it was found that vortex shedding formation was suppressed faster at large angles
for a yawed cylinder with square shape than circular shape. This was believed to be related to
stronger three-dimensional effects in the former case (square cylinder).
The results from Lou et al. (2016) will in the current project be used as main source for com-

A thesis made by Andersen (2014) investigated the vortex induced vibrations and lock-in effect

thesis focused on testing the simulation domain setup for a well deﬁned bluff body such as

tested turbulence models were Spalart-Allmaras, k −ε Two layer Realizable and k −ω SST

were overestimated except for a case with an inlet distance of xinlet/B = 10.

focus on the k −ω SST model. Furthermore, a larger domain was used to reduce inﬂu-

will be used as inspiration in terms of turbulence model, domain size and physics conﬁguration.

2.4
Governing equations
The mechanisms behind CFD are the governing equations describing the mathematical state-
ments of the conservation laws of physics. As all CFD is based on these equations, it is important
to understand their form and each term within them. The following section introduces the
conservation of mass and momentum, of which the latter is conserved by using Newton’s second
law. A third conservation law also exists; the conservation of energy (based on ﬁrst law of
thermodynamics) which considers energy change and heat transfer in a ﬂuid. However, in the
present project this law is not applied and therefore not elaborated. In general, the equations
for conservation are presented in strong form as partial differential equation (PDE) in literature.
For use in CFD however, the equations are used in weak form allowing integration over mesh
cell volumes. Both the continuity and momentum equation can be derived based upon the
Reynolds transport theorem (RTT) for conservation of scalar in ﬂuid ﬂow (Walther, 2013).
When including assumption of Newtons viscosity law in RTT the result is the Navier-Strokes
(NS) equation.

2.5
Turbulence modelling
The purpose of deriving the incompressible (and isothermal) Navier-Strokes equation is to be
able to express the ﬂuid ﬂow in terms of change in pressure and velocity components alone
(pressure and three velocities in an orthogonal coordinate system, giving four unknowns in
total). This is now possible for laminar stationary ﬂows with the constitutive equations which
are the continuity (one equation) and momentum conservation equations (three equations, one
for each coordinate direction). Hence four unknowns are solved by four equations. However,
as many ﬂows are turbulent in steady or unsteady form, both mean and ﬂuctuating values of
the unknowns need to be solved the ﬂow. These unknowns can be solved directly, which can
be extremely resource consuming, or solved using turbulence models.
Common known turbulence models categories are:
1. Algebraic models
2. Turbulence-energy equation models based on Reynolds Averaged Navier-Strokes (RANS)
3. Simulation models such as Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), Large Eddy Simulation
(LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation (DES)
In the current project the RANS based two equation models k −ω and k −ϵ will be applied,
of which the former is widely used within the ﬁeld of aerodynamics where it is proven to
give good results. Both models mainly model the turbulent ﬂow instead of actual resolving.
Another applied model is the LES model, which compared to the latter two resolves the large
scale vortices while only small scale turbulent vortices below a threshold ﬁlter are modelled.
This model however in general requires a very ﬁne mesh grid to resolve ﬂow. The reason for
including the LES model is the fact that ﬂow validation on a square cylinder bluff body will be
made at relatively low Reynolds number which lowers the requirement for the scale of the ﬁne
mesh grid. It therefore serves as a good comparison for validation of the model later chosen for
simulation of the dynamic TNB. For illustrative purposes, the amount of resolved turbulence
is presented in the form of turbulent kinetic energy in Figure 2.6. For ﬂow around simple
geometry and at very low Reynolds numbers, the turbulent ﬂow can be completely resolved
using DNS which solves the Navier-Stokes equation without averaging or approximation other
than numerical discretization. However, this requires a much higher number of grid points and
computational resources than available for the current project.

3.1
Modelling in Star CCM+
The simulations carried out through this project are generated using the latest version of
the commercial ﬁnite volume CFD tool from CD-adapco called Star CCM+. When setting
up a simulation in Star CCM+, the governing physical conditions and a simulation domain
containing the ﬂow has to be decided upon. It is important to apply great attention to how
settings of the physical model treats the governing equations in terms of ﬂow behavior and its
interaction with boundaries. Well applied physical models and simulation domains increase the
plausibility of the solution and the make it possible to draw realistic and reasonable conclusions
from the results. As mentioned before, the physical model and simulation domain in the current
project are based upon inspiration from the work of Andersen (2014) and Rüegger (2016) as
is has been tested upon bluff bodies exposed to similar conditions as those in a wind tunnel.
Furthermore, best practice recommendations by CD-adapco (2015) are applied when necessary.
3.2
Setting up physics
Setting up the physical model applies the assumptions, limitations and ﬂow conditions applied
to the governing equations. Only the settings applied in the current project will be described
in this section.
Spacial setting.
In order to capture three-dimensional and study the topology of ﬂow behavior
around the bluff bodies, the option of three-dimensional ﬂow model setting is chosen. For simpler
ﬂows a two-dimensional spacial setting may be applied, but this does not allow development
of the desired ﬂow behavior as need in the current project where the ﬂow direction is yawed
upon the bluff body.
Time.
When calculating a solution, the choice can be made between a steady or unsteady
option, essentially determining how the governing equations are averaged over time. As a
==PAGE:44==
26
3 Numerical Modelling in Star CCM+
transient solutions is necessary to capture the turbulent vortex behavior in the current project,
the unsteady model option is applied. The choice furthermore requires selection between and
implicit or explicit unsteady model. As this choice however depends on the ﬂow model, the
implicit unsteady model is automatically chosen when using a segregated ﬂow solver model as
explained next.
Flow model.
For a ﬂow model, the choice can be made between segregated- or coupled ﬂow
solver. The difference between them lies in how their linear algorithms solve the governing
equations. As shown before, the continuity and momentum equations are deﬁned by linear
and non-linear terms in the form on pressure and velocity components. This makes them
coupled to each other, requiring an iterative process to reach solutions. The coupled model
solves for the pressure and velocity components in simultaneous manner and hereafter the
remaining variables in the equations. The convergence of the coupled solver is independent of
mesh size. However, the memory usage may increase due to data storage during its solving
process. The coupled solver has a robust and accurate performance for compressible ﬂow, high
speed- and Mach number and in the presence of shocks (CD-adapco, 2015). The segregated
model solves the governing equations in an uncoupled manner by ﬁrst solving the velocity, then
(correction) pressure and hereafter the remaining variables. The linkage between the continuity
and momentum equations is achieved using a corrector-predictor approach (CD-adapco, 2015).
This solver uses less memory than the coupled model, however the number of iterations
increases with mesh size. Furthermore, CD-adapco (2015) recommends the segregated solver
for incompressible ﬂows, such as for the cases in the current project. Segregated ﬂow solver is
therefore chosen.
Material.
Throughout the project, considering any bluff body ﬂow type (square cylinder and
TNB), the ﬂuid material is set to gas (air). It is possible to also specify a material for the bluff
body itself, yielding options of simulating stress and deformations of the body. However, since
the simulated TNB model is only a section of the bridge, the deformation has little inﬂuence
on the ﬂow and vortex formation behaviour. For simplicity, it is also of interest to study the
vortex formation topology independent of deformations, which also lowers the computational
resource required. In all simulations the bluff body itself is therefore set as boundary condition
instead of a solid body. Two properties of the gas (air) are speciﬁed in the current project: the
density and the dynamic viscosity. For advantageous reasons, the density, inlet free-stream
velocity and chord length are set unity (ρ = 1, U = 1, B = 1), and the Reynolds number from
Equation (2.1) can now be set by adjusting only the dynamic viscosity. With unity values, the
Reynolds number is now also inversely proportional to the dynamic viscosity, but only for the
cases presented in the current project.

Equation of state.
The equation of state model computes the density and the density de-

the continuum, hence incompressible ﬂow is achieved.
Viscous regime.
As viscous forces are present in the boundary layers and in the turbulent
wake of the body, a turbulent viscous regime is chosen. This is also needed for turbulent ﬂow in
a state of continuous instability, exhibiting ﬂuctuations in both space and time (CD-adapco,

Turbulence model.
When selecting turbulent ﬂow, a suitable turbulence model must also
be chosen. As described in Section 2.5, the approaches to turbulence modeling in the current
project are based on RANS and LES. For the modeling in RANS approach, the k −ε and k −ω
models are chosen and for the LES approach, the WALE sub-grid model is chosen. Only the
basic form have been described in the mentioned section. However, variations of the models
exists with improved modiﬁcations and wall modeling methods, the chosen variations will be
explained in the next section.
3.2.1
Turbulence models
The chosen turbulence model are selected based inspiration from the previous simulation
results by Andersen (2014) and Rüegger (2016). A additional simulation model, the LES
model, is also chosen when possible due to its ability to resolve large parts of the turbulence.
This model however is costly due to the need for ﬁne mesh resolution.
RANS, k −ε Realizable Two Layer model.
The basic form of k −ε model is a two-equation
model solving transport equations for the turbulent kinetic energy k and its dissipation rate ε.
The Realizable version replaces the transport equation for dissipation rate ε with an updated
version, while Cµ is a function of mean ﬂow and turbulence properties, instead of being
assumed a constant. This lets the model satisfy certain mathematical constraints on the normal
stresses, consistent with the physics of turbulence, hence is it called Realizable (CD-adapco,
2015). The Two Layer approach divides the computation domain into two layers, one close to
walls in the viscous sub-layer and one far from the walls. In the layer close to the wall, the
turbulent dissipation rate and ε and turbulent eddy viscosity µt are formulated as functions of
dimensionless wall distance y+. The dissipation rate near the wall is blended smoothly with
==PAGE:46==
28
3 Numerical Modelling in Star CCM+
the values computed far from the wall, while the turbulent kinetic energy is solved for the
entire ﬂow.
RANS, k −ω SST Menter model.
The basic form of k −ω model is a two-equation model
solving transport equations for the turbulent kinetic energy k and its speciﬁc dissipation
rate ω, which is the rate per unit turbulent kinetic energy (ω ∼ε/k). The k −ω model
is advantageous over the k −ε model in its ability to be applied throughout the boundary
layer under adverse pressure gradients. However, k −ω is sensitive to inlet conditions and
free-stream ﬂow. Therefore, Menter introduced the Shear Stress Transport (SST) formulation
which adds a non-conservative cross-diffusion term which lets k −ω achieve similar results to
k −ε in the free-stream. This approach blends the k −ω model near the wall with k −ε model
in the free-stream, hereby obtaining the best from the two models. It is to be noted althogh,
that the k −ω model in general has a slight tendency to over-predict separation (Siemens,
2016).
LES, WALE model.
As described in Section 2.5.2, the LES model resolves turbulent scales
above a threshold deﬁned by spacial ﬁltering. Turbulent scales below the ﬁlter are amused
universal in behavior and with negligent inertial effects. The model resolves approx- 80% of
the turbulent ﬂow, which provides more realistic results than the RANS models, but is however
costly in terms of requirements for a ﬁne mesh. The scales below ﬁlter are handled by the
WALE model which uses an algebraic formulation to model the sub-grid scales stresses. Other
sub-grid models exist, but the WALE model is the most improved version with best results
under various ﬂow conditions.

ﬂow resembles inviscid ﬂow and hence inertial forces dominate. The overlapping layer is also
known as logarithmic layer due to the behavior of the ﬂow within (Kundu et al., 2001). To

3.5
Mesh settings
The mesh generation process divides the ﬂow domain into cells where the discretized transport
equations are applied. The quality of the mesh has direct inﬂuence on the accuracy and
convergence of results. A balance is needed between ﬁne mesh (small cells) in regions with
steep spatial gradients and coarse mesh in regions regions dominated by uniform mean ﬂow.
The regions with steep spatial gradient are those containing strong shear layers, rapid change
in ﬂow direction and adverse pressure gradients. If the general domain mesh is too coarse,
then the results may be inaccurate, however if the mesh is too ﬁne, the consequences are high
computational costs. As mentioned before, he number of iterations used by the segregated
solver also increases with mesh size, hence the mentioned balance is needed.
Parts-based meshing strategy
A parts-based meshing strategy is chosen for the volume mesh-
ing. This meshing strategy executes mesh operations directly on the geometry parts and allows
ﬂexibility for design changes in simply changing the input geometry, this meshing strategy is
also recommended whenever possible by (CD-adapco, 2015). The meshing models applied
within the parts-based meshing strategy are display in Figure 3.2

Surface remesher.
The surface remesher improves the quality of existing surfaces and optim-
izes them by retriangulation for best use with the volume mesher models. This is needed for an
optimal mesh as the surface mesh forms the base from which the volume mesh is generated.
Trimmed cell mesher.
Star CCM+ offers volume meshers based on tetrahedral, polyhedral
and hexahedral shapes. For the study of external aerodynamics such as study of bluff body ﬂow
CD-adapco (2015) recommends this model, ensuring fast and high quality mesh generation.
The trimmer cell mesher works with hexahedral shapes and generates a volume mesh by cutting
a hexahedral template mesh with the geometry surface. An advantage of this model also comes
from the hexahedral cells being aligned with the ﬂow and thus provides improved accuracy
and increased convergence rate. Furthermore, when using a periodic boundary condition, an
interface of 100% matching cells is generated on the two boundary planes that are cyclically
connected, giving accurate data transfer between them.
Prism layer mesher.
The prism layer mesher adds prismatic cell layers next to wall boundaries
by projecting the core mesh onto to the wall boundaries to create prismatic cells. As mentioned
earlier, steep spatial gradients such as the velocity proﬁle in the vicinity of wall, require ﬁne
mesh to be captured accurately. This is critical in determining wall forces, stagnation and
separation, of which the latter in turn effects results such as drag. A prism layer mesh allows
the viscous sub-layer to be resolved directly if the applied turbulence model supports it (low y+
< 5). For coarser meshes, a wall function is applied for more accurate results (high y+ > 30).
As mentioned in Section 3.2.2, both cases are handled by the All-y+ wall treatment.
Surface- and volumetric controls.
In turbulent ﬂow past a bluff body, the vortex shedding
in the near wake of the body also requires adequate mesh resolution. With the trimmer cell
mesher is it possible to create volumetric control mesh reﬁnements based on predeﬁned geo-
metric parts. This allows custom reﬁnement of speciﬁc regions in the wake of the bluff body.
Several volumetric control regions may overlap each other with different mesh reﬁnements,
however the ﬁnest mesh control will dictate the reﬁnement of overlapping control regions.
Another tool available with the trimmer cell mesher is the surface control mesh reﬁnement, this
allows reﬁnement of different surfaces on a body depending on the demands set by the ﬂow,
e.g. if one regions has higher boundary layer formation compared to another. Both control
methods are applied in the current studies.

around a sharp edge bluff body, with multiple ﬁxed separation points, is essential due to the

are as mentioned in Section 2.3.1 based on inspiration from previous work by Andersen (2014)
and Rüegger (2016), with the k−ω SST Menter turbulence model as ﬁrst choice. By increasing
inlet distance and domain size to reduce inﬂuence of boundaries, Rüegger (2016) found well
ﬁtting results compared to overestimated results from the domain used by Andersen (2014).
In order to capture effects of three dimensional vortex behavior, two variations of mesh grid
reﬁnements are furthermore tested to minimize inﬂuences of spatial discretization.

The applied computational domain inspired from Rüegger (2016) can be seen in Figure 4.1.
With a body height equal to unit chord length (B = 1), the square cylinder yields a blockage
ratio of 5% in the domain, hence no effect is of boundaries affecting the ﬂow are expected.
This corresponds well will a blockage ratio of 5% in studies by Lou et al. (2016). Although

4.2.1
Force Coefﬁcients, Vortex Shedding and IP validity
For IP validity to be optimally valid, the ratio between normalized drag at yaw and drag at
zero yaw (β = 0◦) needs to be approx. equal to one, the same applies to the Strouhal number.
Hence, StN/St0 ≈1 and CDN/CD0 ≈1 implies IP validity. From the results for Case group 1 in
Table 4.3 (page 44) and time histories in Figures 4.5 (page 47) and 4.4 (page 46), tendencies
were found as described next.
Looking at the force coefﬁcient time histories, both mesh grids produce realistic force signal
for β = 0◦, as the overall lift signal has a regular periodic tendency, while the ﬂuctuation
of the drag signal remains within an approx. bandwidth. At β = 45◦, the ﬁne mesh lift
signal shows a slightly disturbed behavior around 110 < t∗< 150, although this may just be
an artefact. In both yaw and mesh cases, the mean lift coefﬁcients were approx. zero as desired.
The mean drag for the two mesh grids decrease with increasing yaw β as expected. No differ-
ence in CD at β = 0◦between the two meshes is seen, with only slight deviation β = 45◦. The
Stouhal number shows same tendency.
Comparing mean drag values with Lou et al. (2016), it is seen that higher values are achieved
for the present cases. Although present results at β = 0◦are close to those by Rüegger (2016),
the k −ω SST model is known to slightly over-predict separation (Siemens, 2016). In Lou et al.
(2016) comparison is also made with results at β = 0◦from Kim et al. (2004) with CD = 1.97
at Re = 3000 stating the their own values may be lower due to "imperfect sharp corners", hence
reducing separation.

The Strouhal numbers for both mesh grids are seen to remain relatively constant with only
slight decrease at β = 45◦. This is in contrast to Lou et al. (2016), which shows an increase
with yaw angle, but also in general has higher values. However, looking at the StN/St0 and
CDN/CD0 ratios, current results comply very well with IP validity for both yaw angles. In

To sum up, both meshes grids perform equally well in the determining the mean drag and
Strouhal numbers for yaw angles β = 0◦and β = 45◦. The results also show that the setup with
periodic boundaries, implying inﬁnite domain depth, provides a ﬂow that allows validation of
the IP at β = 45◦. Although Lou et al. (2016) has lower drag values in general, the k −ω SST
turbulence model in itself may be source of slight over prediction of mean drag.
4.3
Variation of Turbulence Model
The high mean drag results from Case group 1, may as stated be correlated with slight over-
prediction of separation by the k −ω SST model. Therefore, the LES and k −ε Realizable
model are tested on both mesh grids for β = 45◦to see any variation in performance. The LES
is applicable as Re = 3600 is relatively low. Since no signiﬁcant deviation was seen between
the meshes, the coarse mesh is tested further at β = 0◦with LES and at β = 45◦with k −ω
SST using 2nd order temporal discretization and with Quadratic Constitutive Relations (QCR)
applied. The QCR introduces new non-linear constitutive relations in addition to existing linear
relations, which may give improved accuracy, particularly for anisotropic ﬂows. It is well suited
for ﬂows with strong swirls and may reduce number of iterations to convergence (Siemens,
2016). The results from cases with varied turbulence models are presented in Table 4.3 (page
44), under Case group 2. All the cases are continued from Case group 1 with clear vortex
shedding at β = 45◦. Therefore, time-histories plot in Figures 4.7, 4.8 and 4.9 (page 50 - 52)
do not start from zero. Further, velocity plots for ﬂow around the cylinder in a section plane
normal to the span-wise direction are compared in Figures 4.6 - 4.11 (page 49 - 55). From
results in Case group 2, observations were made as described next.
Using LES, results in slightly increased Strouhal number and reduced mean drag compared to
k −ω SST, however difference is less than 6% and 3% respectively. Both meshes show almost
same results. The corresponding time-history plots in Figures 4.7a, 4.8a and 4.8b (page 50 -
51), show realistic force signal as k −ω SST. The matching results between the two is a good
indicator that k −ω SST models the yaw ﬂow well.
Looking at the results from k−ε Realizable, overly regular periodic force signal are seen Figures

to β = 45◦, this model is further used to investigate validity at one lower and three higher
yaw angles β = [30◦,60◦,75◦,80◦]. The coarse mesh is used, as no signiﬁcant difference was

From the listed results in Table 4.3 (page 44), it seen that for β = 30◦, both mean drag and
Strouhal follow the IP. For higher yaw angles, it is seen that IP validity holds very well for
Strouhals number up to β = 75◦, while mean drag gives an error of 13% at this angle. At
β = 80◦the results deviate with errors of 15% for Strouhal and 37% for mean drag. However
calculating the Reynolds number at this angle, based on the ﬂow component normal to span-
direction it is seen that ReN = 625 at β = 80◦. This value is close to the transition region from
to 2D to 3D ﬂow at Re = 500, (Okajima, 1982), indicating that laminar effects may inﬂuence
the results. Therefore a case was made, with ReN,corrected = 3600 by dividing the dynamic
viscosity with cos (80◦). As seen in the results, this moves the values towards IP validity, with
decreased errors of now 10% for Strouhal and 23% for mean drag. The IP validity can however
be concluded to be most reliable up to β = 75◦from the current results. The high IP validity
range corresponds well with the conclusions by Zhao et al. (2009), where a circular cylinder in
an inﬁnite domain was simulated using a periodic boundary interface, providing IP validity up
to β = 60◦.

4.5
Variation of Span-wise Depth
When investigating the vortex shedding topology in the near wake of the cylinder for D/B = 6
it is seen that three-dimensional vortex formations are present both for β = 0◦and β = 45◦.
These formations are seen in Figures 4.17a and 4.17b (page 61 - 61). At β = 0◦, coherent struc-
tures with stream-wise and transverse components are clearly seen. These become smeared
β = 45◦, and instead a wave like roll is formed. To study the extend of the wave like formation,
cases with variation of domain depth are investigated for D/B = [9, 12, 15, 30] for the coarse
mesh. The reﬁnement regions are kept the same, and thus due to depth increase, the cell
counts increase to approx. [2.65, 3.52, 4.40, 8.76] · 106 respectively, hence requiring more
computational resources. To display the vortex structures, an iso-surface with a threshold
vortex magnitude of 0.5s−1 is used, while ﬂow direction is represented by tangential vectors in
a plane normal to the y-axis. Further, to display change in stream-wise vorticity i-components,
an overlayer is added to the iso-surface displaying the scalar values of these.
From the results in Table 4.3 (page 44), under Case group 4, there are no signiﬁcant changes
in Strohal and mean drag values when varying the depth. For D/B = 6, a sinusoidal wave
like roll is present in the near wake with a span-wise wave length proportional to the domain
depth of D/B = 6. When increasing the depth, it is seen that periodicity of the waves increases
approx. proportionally with domain depth as seen in Figures 4.17, 4.18 and 4.19 (pages 61 -
4.19). Comparing D/B = 6 and D/B = 30, the ﬂow is assumed to have enough free space to
self-regulate its periodic wave formation, which suggests that the wave length is independent
of any possible effects imposed by the periodic interface. Further, it is seen that for each half
period in the sinusoidal waves, vorticity i-components shift in circulation direction.

corners" (corners are in the present report referred to as edges). As the geometry in simulation
of the cylinder is deﬁned numerically, the edges are approx. mathematically perfectly sharp.

4.7
Sub-conclusion
From the results in the present chapter in is concluded that the k −ω SST turbulence in
combination with the coarse mesh, provides realistic mean drag and Strouhal values giving
IP validity up to β = 75◦. Increasing the domain depth had little effect over D/B = 6, and
the wave length of the sinusoidal wave like vortex roll was proportional to this domain depth.
Further, at this domain depth no inﬂuence of effects imposed by the periodic interface was
found. The main inﬂuencing factor on difference between mean drag and Strouhal values
between present results and Lou et al. (2016), was found to be the effect of imperfect sharp
edges in the latter.

Chapter 5
Flow around Stationary Tacoma
Narrows Bridge Deck
Following the cases from the square cylinder ﬂow, the simulation model and assumptions
are now applied to the H-proﬁle cross section of the TNB in a stationary case, before testing
dynamic torsional vibration. This study is made to test how the k −ω model performs in terms
of modeling vortex shedding formation in and around the cavity of the H-proﬁle and near
wake. Further, investigation of 2D and 3D vortex formation topology around the H-proﬁle
is made, as it is of interest for predicting general ﬂow behavior for such bridge proﬁles. For
the simulations, yaw angles of β = 0◦and β = 45◦are chosen, of which the former angle is
used for comparison with a similar numerical studies by Larsen et al. (1998) and experimental
studies by (Nakamura et al., 1986). The second yaw angle was by (Malthe et al., 2017) found
to be the one present at the day of the TNB collapse. Results for mean drag and Strouhal for
ﬂow at this angle will also be studied in regards to IP validity.
5.1
Mesh and Flow Setup
The TNB model cross-section used for stationary simulations is obtained from Malthe et al.
(2017) and can be seen in Figure 5.1, where it is scaled to have a unity bride chord length
B = 1. By keeping the chord length unity, the correct depth/chord ratios are obtained using
the previously tested meshes. The model used by Malthe et al. (2017) is a simpliﬁed bridge
section model that represents the most governing geometry of the TNB cross section, hence
no railings, ﬂoor beam, stiffeners etc. are included. This makes the results from current
simulations comparable to theirs.
The computational domain and boundary conditions used for the square cylinder are reused
for the TNB model, as seen in Figure 5.2, since both proved to work well through all cases.
With a bridge girder height of H = 0.2B, the blockage is 1% and therefor no blockage effects
imposed by boundary conditions are to be expected. Although height 5 times smaller than for

the square cylinder case, the domain is later in the project to be used for torsional vibration at
pitch amplitude angles that may require a high domain. Both (Malthe et al., 2017), (Larsen
et al., 1998) and (Nakamura et al., 1986) used an H-proﬁle model with chord/height ratio of
B/H = 5. As default, the domain depth/chord ratio is kept as D/B = 6 as no signiﬁcant change
was seen in varying the depth for the square cylinder.

5.2
Force Coefﬁcients, Vortex Shedding and IP validity
To ensure that that simulation model and setup ﬁts the new geometry and higher Reynolds
number, the k−ω SST model it tested at Re = 3600 and Re = 60000 for yaw angles β = 0◦and
β = 45◦at ﬁrst. The results for mean drag and Strouhal number are seen in Table 5.4 (page 73)
under Case Group 6. As with the square cylinder, the ﬂow cases at β = 45◦are perturbed pitch
angled ﬂow to accelerate development of vortex shedding. Further, the ﬂow cases at β = 0◦, are
again started from a case with β = 45◦where fully developed vortex shedding is already present.
From the results in Table 5.4 (page 73), it is seen that the IP validity is very satisfactory for
ﬂow around TNB model for both low and high Re number. However, when going from low to
high Re, the mean drag decreases with approx. 4% while the Strouhal number increases with
approx. 5%. Further, comparing result with those for 2D ﬂow by Larsen et al. (1998) with
C D = 0.27 and St = 0.11, and 3D ﬂow Nakamura et al. (1986) with St = 0.12, is seen that the
results for Strouhal are very close to the 3D case, while mean drag for high Re is 15% lower
than the 2D ﬂow case. The large mean drag deviation may be explained by insufﬁcient mesh
resolution in the current cases and to some degree by 3D ﬂow effects.
To have more basis for comparison, cases are run with k −ω SST at Re = 46000 for yaw angles
β = 0◦and β = 45◦, and LES at Re = 60000 for β = 45◦. Both cases yield similar y+ and
Courant C values as for k −ω SST at Re = 60000. The results of these cases are also under
Case group 6. For k −ω SST at Re = 46000, no change is seen for the mean drag and Strouhal
values once in this Reynolds range, and therefore values are insensitive to Reynolds number in
this range. From the results of LES, the mean drag values come much closer to those by Larsen
et al. (1998), however as Reynolds number is high for LES, comparison should be made with
caution and further study needs to be made to determine whether the mesh resolution is ﬁne
enough. In terms of IP validity, all the models in Case group 6 are seen to perform well.

5.5
Sub-conclusion
Overall, the k −ω SST model with ﬁne mesh and domain depth of D/B = 6 seems to provide
the best conditions to adequately model the yaw ﬂow around the TNB model in terms of
resolution of vortex behavior and resulting mean drag and Strouhal numbers. Further, is is
observed that for constant chord length of B = 1 , the wave length of the periodicity is approx.
inversely proportional to the height for the square cylinder and TNB model. In continuation
of this ﬁnding, a domain depth D/B = 1 for the stationary TNB cases is seen to be enough
to capture the periodic vortex formation behavior, however due to strong span-wise parallel
ﬂow in the H-proﬁle cavity, the domain depth of D/B = 6 is maintained for use in the dynamic
simulation cases presented in the next Chapter.

6.1
Dynamic Properties
When investigating elastically suspended bridges, this is often done with a combinations of
down scaled and simpliﬁed spring based experimental models. Under these conditions, it is
often most practical to keep the shape, size, weight and natural frequencies constant, and
instead change ﬂow conditions by varying the wind speed to obtain desired reduced velocities.
In numerical modeling however, it is easier and more advantageous to vary the bridge model
properties and keep ﬂuid ﬂow properties constant. This also ensure less impact on the numerical
ﬂow setup, once it has been validated for certain ﬂow conditions. In the present cases, the
reduced velocity will therefore be controlled by the natural frequency of the bridge model in
terms of applied torsional spring stiffness. Further, numerical modeling allows setting many of
the applied ﬂow and model parameters to uniﬁed quantities. As mentioned in Section 2.2.1, to
conserve the physics of motion when re-dimensioning the TNB bridge model, the mass and
mass moment of inertia ratios per unit length must be kept equal to that of the real TNB and
hence also for the simpliﬁed model present by Malthe et al. (2017), which is used in the current
project. Although the torsional motion of the model is only described using the mass moment
of inertia, Star CCM+ requires an input for mass as well, therefore the mass per unit length is
also calculated and used for safeguarding. The mentioned ratios for the TNB were found by
Malthe et al. (2017), are given in Table 6.1.

Pre-liminary dynamic response tests
Prior to the chosen reduced velocities, a few test cases are at ﬁrst made for Vr,β=45◦= [2, 5, 8],

to no development is observed. A hypothesis for this behavior attributes the responses to a
correlation between Von Kármán vortex shedding at the trailing edges and the natural torsional
frequencies of the model, as these are controlled by the applied spring stiffness. For Vr = 2, the
ratio between the vortex shedding frequency of 0.424 Hz and natural frequency of 0.5 Hz, is
approx. 0.9, indicating likely inﬂuence from the shedding. For Vr = 8, with natural frequency
of 0.125 Hz, the ratio is approx. 3.5, indicating that fast vortex shedding might be preventing
the development of torsional oscillation.
In response to these ﬁndings, it was decided to perturb the oscillations by adding an extra
torsional moment for short duration of t∗= 0.5, forcing the starting torsional amplitude to
θ = 10◦. This resulted in desired incremental development amplitude for Vr = 8. However,
for Vr = 2 the oscillations remained at θ = 10◦, in contrast to expectation of decreasing
development as the reduced velocity is well below the onset of instability, again indicating
that behavior may be due to inﬂuence of Von Kárman vortex shedding. Therefore, a ﬁnal
test was made for Vr = 3 at same starting amplitude, which showed expected decremented
development, and was therefore chosen for further simulation. Plots of the dynamic responses
for the pre-liminary tests cases are found in Appendix A

simulations in Table 6.2 (page 92) with starting amplitude of θ = 10◦, except for Vr = 5 which

6.6.1
Work of Expanding Vortex
To ﬁnd a better agreement with the factor 3 in vortex strength for β = 0◦compared to β = 45◦,
as indicated by Rønne et al. (2017) and Larsen (2016), another approach is used. When
considering the oscillations of the TNB model from the view of energy and work principle, it is
obvious that energy is added to the oscillations while amplitudes increase. From this it can be
stated that the increase in potential energy stored in the spring of the simulated model, as the
amplitudes increase, can be deﬁned as energy added by the ﬂuid ﬂow, hence:

(6.8)

balance between the vortex strength and the energy dissipated by the viscous damping of the
surrounding ﬂuid ﬂow. If the viscous damping is larger than the vortex strength, the right term
in Equation (6.8) becomes negative. When the viscous damping balances the vortex strength,
the term becomes zero and thus equilibrium oscillation is achieved. The potential energy stored
in a spring is given as:

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The main purpose of the present report is to simulate and investigate the vortex shedding be-
havior and topology around the elastically torsional suspended Tacoma Narrows Bridge model
subjected to yawed inﬂow and to determine the effect of vortex induced oscillation. Results are
achieved using the the numerical software Star CCM+. The research and investigations within
the present report tries to support and build on observations from a project for a similar model
in wind tunnel experiments presented by (Malthe et al., 2017). Further, study of the vortex
induced oscillations are compared to the predictions from expanding vortex model presented
by (Larsen2016). Before the ﬁnal investigations of the elastically suspended bridge model,
ﬁrstly a case for the ﬂow around a yawed square cylinder was studied, and secondly for a
yawed TNB model, in order to ﬁnd the appropriate turbulence model and simulation setup. To
the authors knowledge, no numerical studies have previously been made for the yaw ﬂow in
any of the mentioned cases.
Simulation of the ﬂow around the yawed square cylinder served mainly for testing turbulence
models and simulation setup. Using periodic interface boundaries effectively implied an inﬁnite
ﬂow domain dept, hence allowing undisturbed yaw ﬂow within the domain and space for
the formation of three-dimensional vortex structures. Three turbulence models were tested,
namely URANS based k −ω SST and k −ε Realizable, as well as the LES model. Performance
was measured in terms of ability to determine force coefﬁcients, upholding IP validity at yaw
and to simulate vortex formations around the cylinder walls and downstream near wake. The
k −ω SST model in combination with a coarse mesh, provided best results for mean drag
and Strouhal values with IP validity up to β = 75◦. The effect of increasing the physical
domain depth was furthermore studied, allowing vortex structures to form independently of
any effect imposed by the periodic interface boundaries. This showed no variation for domain
depth/chord length over D/B = 6, where a sinusoidal wave like vortex roll was found in the
near wake with wave length proportional to domain depth of D/B = 6. In comparison to
literature and a similar wind tunnel experiment by Lou et al. (2016), deviation was found
between mean drag and Strouhal values, however this was attributed to the effect of imperfect
==PAGE:132==
114
7 Conclusion
sharp cylinder edges in the wind tunnel experiments and partly to the end effects of a cylinder
with ﬁnite length.
In order to validate the simulation setup from the square cylinder case to be used for simulation
of the elastically suspended TNB model, the simulation model was tested on stationary cases
of the TNB model. Again, performance was measured in terms of ability to determine force
coefﬁcients, upholding IP validity at yaw angle and to simulate vortex formations around in
the H-proﬁle cavity and downstream near wake. The k −ω SST model with a ﬁne mesh and
domain depth of D/B = 6 provided the best conditions to adequately model the yaw ﬂow
around the TNB model in terms of resolution of vortex behavior and resulting mean drag
and Strouhal numbers, when compared to literature. In the H-proﬁle cavity and near wake,
sinusoidal wave like vortex roll pattern was found, like in the wake of the square cylinder.
However it was observed that for constant chord length of B = 1 , the wave length of the
periodicity is approx. inversely proportional to the height for the square cylinder and TNB
model. In continuation of this ﬁnding, a domain depth D/B = 1 for the stationary TNB
cases is seen to be enough to capture the periodic vortex formation behavior. Within the
H-proﬁle cavity, a strong span-wise parallel ﬂow was furthermore found, implying that the
domain depth of D/B = 6 needed to be maintained for use in the ﬁnal dynamic simulation cases.
In the ﬁnal simulations of the TNB modeol, the model was elastically torsional suspended
by allowing 1DOF torsional motion in Star CCM+ and adding a torsional spring acting on
the rotation about the span-wise z-axis normal to the cross-section of the H-proﬁle. For the
simulation setup, the k −ω SST model and domain settings was used that provided best
performance from the stationary TNB cases. Three pair of reduced velocities were selected for
investigation for β = 0◦and β = 45◦respectively, inspired by the results and observations on
onset of aerodynamic instability found by Malthe et al. (2017). Comparing the resulting time
histories of the dynamic responses for the pairs of reduced velocities for β = 0◦and β = 45◦
respectively, a relation to cos(45) was found for the time taken to reach approx. same oscillation
amplitudes, with the time at β = 45◦being the longest. However, as the spring stiffness for
the two yaw angles differed with a factor 1/2 going from β = 0◦to β = 45◦, the vortex in the
case for β = 45◦is determined to have less strength than just a factor cos(45) = 0.71. When
instead comparing time histories of V rr,β=0◦= 5.66 with V rr,β=45◦= 5 for same interval of
oscillation amplitude, and accounting for the difference in spring stiffness, a factor 3.3 was
found in the vortex strength which support predictions presented by Malthe et al. (2017) and
Larsen (2016).
When visualizing the vortex formation at the leading edge in the ﬁrst quarter oscillation from
zero position, where the expanding vortex was assumed to have largest effect on oscillations,
the formation of the expanding vortex was clearly seen to be two-dimensional. Investigating
only the vorticity k-component (rotation about span-wise z-axis), showed similar circulation
==PAGE:133==
115
area but a factor cos (45) in difference for vorticity circulation rate. Again, the low ratio may
be attributed to the comparison of reduced velocities, which indicates that comparison should
be made at same velocity magnitude of free stream velocity.
In order to support and elaborate the ﬁndings from time histories of the dynamic responses,
increment in potential energy store in the spring for increasing oscillations at β = 0◦and
β = 45◦was calculated. When normalizing this incremental energy by the scale of the spring
constants at compared yaw angles, the result was a factor of approx. 3 in vortex strength for
the two yaw angles. However, a case needs to be run for Vr,β=0◦= 5 for conﬁdent conﬁrmation
of the approach with normalization of the energy by the ratio between the spring stiffness at
the two yaw angles.
Investigating the visualization the vortex formation from the leading edge in the quarter
oscillation period from max amplitude to zero position, it was furthermore found that the
formation of the expanding vortex develops from being a two-dimensional roll, to form periodic
transverse and stream-wise components with like those seen in the near wake for the stationary
case, however with a shorter wave length of the periodicity. This suggest that a domain depth
of D/B = 1 is sufﬁcient to capture three-dimensional vortex formation at yaw angled inﬂow.
This lowers requirement for computational recourse, which is beneﬁcial and recommended, as
the time for simulating the dynamic cases was high with each ∆t∗= 100 requiring approx. 250
hours alone to run. Comparing this with all simulations and run time, the total time consumed
by simulation is quite high.
