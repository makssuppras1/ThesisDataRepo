Abstract 
For the past many years, there has been growing concern about the presence of fungi and their 
effects on human health in indoor environments. Today, it is widely known that exposure to 
fungi and their produced compounds, especially mycotoxins, may result in adverse human 
health effects. This demands that the fungal species responsible for such effects are detected 
in indoor environments. However, most of the existing methods for assessment of fungal 
growth in buildings have limitations and require further development. Moreover, limited 
knowledge is available on the growth requirements of some of these fungi. Thus, the 
objectives of this study were to: 1) investigate the interacting effects of water activity (aw), 
solute, pH-value and temperature on the growth of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi; 
2) develop a new growth medium intended for the detection of these species in indoor 
environments; 3) investigate the mycotoxin production potential of these species; 4) evaluate 
the occurrence of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi in moisture damaged buildings.    
To investigate the growth requirements of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi, 
experiments were designed in order to obtain a better understanding of the interacting effects 
of water activity (aw), solute, pH-value and temperature. This was achieved by growing the 
fungi on a variety of different growth media, including both standard media and new media. 
Based on these findings, a new growth medium was selected for further research. To meet the 
third objective, the mycotoxin production potential of these fungi was analyzed, by the 
involvement of analytical chemistry. Lastly, to study the occurrence of these fungi in moisture 
damaged buildings, air and surface sampling were performed with the application of different 
collecting media, including standard media and the newly developed medium.  
The experiments revealed that Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi had very unlike 
requirements in regard to aw, temperature and solute/carbon source. However, both species 
had a preference towards the same new growth medium. Both fungi did produce various 
mycotoxins, especially Aspergillus versicolor. All case studies indicated that Aspergillus 
versicolor is present in most moisture damaged buildings, and that the choice of growth 
medium is important. Furthermore, Wallemia sebi was not found in any of the air samples.  
These results might help to understand the underlying causes for the occurrence of Aspergillus 
versicolor and Wallemia sebi in moisture damaged buildings. Eventually, this could help to 
reduce the fungal-induced human health implications, especially caused by the selected 
species in this study.

1   Background 
Since the observation that people could suffer adverse health effects that appeared to be 
linked with the time spent indoors, indoor air quality has become an important public health 
concern. This phenomenon became known as the “sick building syndrome” (SBS), and many 
causes have been known to attribute to this condition, such as outgassing from building 
materials or tobacco smoke (Wolkoff & Nielsen, 2001).  
However, the presence of fungi in indoor environments and their contribution to SBS has not 
received the proper attention until recently. Nowadays, fungal growth in buildings has 
become a common problem around the world, and it is widely known that exposure to fungi 
and their produced compounds may have potential health impacts (Portnoy et al., 2005).  
Over the past decades, energy efficiency in buildings has been introduced and this trend will 
likely continue to influence the building industry. As an example, the European Commission 
has set a 20 % energy saving target by 2020, which should reduce the energy consumption in 
the building stock (European Commission, 2012). Therefore, increased efforts to reduce the 
energy consumption have been undertaken, such as implementation of internal insulation and 
more tightly sealed building envelopes. The outcome, however, has been a reduction in 
internal air change rates, leading to aggravated moisture problems, which is associated with 
fungal growth (Howieson, 2017). Consequently, it is likely that fungal growth in buildings 
will take on even greater prominence in the future. Since people spent up to 90 % of their time 
indoors, this issue deserves continious consideration (Hänninen et al., 2004). 
1.1  
WATER AND FUNGAL GROWH    
Living organisms depend on various factors for growth. Nutrient availability, air and water 
are some of the main factors. Fungi are especially dependent on water for their nutrient uptake 
and normal metabolic activity (McGinnis, 2007). Therefore, water is considered a key factor 
for fungal growth (Adan, 1994).  
Water, or moisture, can originate from many sources, often as a result of four basic 
mechanisms (Bornehag et al., 2001):  
1.   Outdoor sources: Leakage of rain and snow into building constructions or moisture 
from the ground. 
2.   Indoor sources: Moisture from indoor activities, e.g. bathing, cooking, laundering etc. 
3.   Building sources: Moisture within building materials/structures from the construction 
time.  
4.   Accidents: Water leakage from pipes or flooding.

In buildings, moisture parameters may include assessments of moisture within a material, at a 
surface, or moisture in the air. Some variations exist between these parameters, which makes 
moisture-induced fungal growth a complicated case.   
1.1.1   Moisture in the air and water activity  
1.1.1.1   The relative humidity 
The relative humidity (RH) is used to describe the amount of moisture in the air (Ayerst, 
1969). At a given temperature, the RH is defined as the ratio between the actual water vapor 
content per m3 of air (xactual) and the maximum amount of water vapor per m3 of air, also 
referred to as saturated content (xsaturated) (Adan, 1994). This is expressed as:

The saturated water vapor content is strongly dependent on temperature, and rises with 
increasing temperature. In other words: warm air can contain more water vapor than cold air. 
This relationship can be illustrated in a water vapor saturation curve, as seen in Figure 1. As 
an example, if air with a temperature of 29°C and a RH of 50 % is cooled down, the RH will 
increase. At some point, the dew point (Td) is reached, which is the temperature to which air 
must be cooled before the actual vapor content equals the saturated vapor content. This means 
that the air will be saturated with water vapor and the RH will be 100 %. Below the dew 
point, and forward, water vapor turns into liquid water, a process called condensation (WHO 
report, 2009).

Typically, condensation occurs on the inside of external walls, when the warm moist air 
comes into contact with a surface that has a temperature around the dew point (Nielsen, 
2002). This is often due to thermal bridges, which are thermal zones in the building envelope, 
showing an increased outflow of heat, allowing surfaces to become cooler than the 
surrounding air (Adan & Samson, 2011). Thermal bridges can appear at several locations 
within the building envelope, but most commonly, thermal bridges occur due to insufficient 
insulation or at junctions between two or more building components. Also, if the dew point 
temperature is moved inside the structure, for example due to a missing damp proof barrier or 
by insulating an exterior wall, condensation can then happen inside building structures 
(Biseniece et al., 2018). 
1.1.1.2   Water activity  
The moisture requirements for fungal growth on building materials is often expressed as water 
activity (aw), which is the ratio of the vapor pressure of water in a material (p) to the vapor 
pressure of pure water (po) at the same temperature (Institute of Medicine, 2004). This is 
expressed as:

Generally, the water activity denotes the amount of free (rather than bound) water available 
for fungal growth. The water activity ranges between 0 and 1, where 1 is pure water (Tsongas 
& Rioroan, 2016). When a given building material is in equilibrium with the surrounding air, 
the water activity of a material equals the relative humidity of the air above it (Adan & 
Samson, 2011). Thus, multiplication of water activity by 100 gives the equilibrium relative 
humidity (ERH) in percent:

1.1.2   Moisture in building materials 
Water requirements of indoor fungi may also be considered in terms of the moisture content 
of a material. Generally, building materials are porous and can contain a certain amount of 
moisture, physically bound in the pore system. Also, water can be chemically bound; 
however, this is most often neglected in building physics (Janz & Johannesson, 2001). 
The physical binding of water is mainly due to adhesive forces between a building material 
and the water molecules. A process called adsorption. The amount of adsorbed water depends 
on the RH of the pore air and on the porosity of the material. As an example, fine-porous 
materials have a larger inner pore surface area and can therefore contain larger amounts of 
moisture. At a lower relative humidity (below 35 % RH), the adsorbed molecules will form a 
monomolecular layer of water molecules on the surface of the pores. As the relative humidity 
increases, multi layers of water will be formed in the pores. At some point, when the relative 
humidity approaches the saturated relative humidity, the water layer on the two opposite pore 
walls will come into contact and small droplets of liquid water begin to appear within that 
==PAGE:14==
 
4 
 
pore. When this point is reached and onwards, water vapor will rapidly condense within the 
pores by a process called capillary condensation (Kumaran, 2009).  
The connection between moisture content, and relative humidity at a given temperature, can 
be graphically expressed in a so-called sorption isotherm (see Figure 2). At equilibrium, a 
relationship will exist between the moisture content of the material and RH of the surrounding 
air, at a given temperature. As the figure shows, there exists a difference between the physical 
binding of water (adsorption) and the release of water from the pores during drying 
(desorption). This phenomenon is called hysteresis, and implies that the moisture content is 
not a single valued function of the RH, but also depends on the earlier history of the material 
(Adan, 1994). The effect of hysteresis on fungal growth is not fully understood. However, 
studies have shown that fungi may produce more heat during the desorption than during the 
absorption process, at the same RH (Li & Wadsö, 2013).

However, most often physical parameters such as relative humidity and temperature are not 
constant in indoor environments, and those fluctuations greatly influence the fungal growth 
(Nielsen, 2002). In order to deal with this, the concept of time-of-wetness (TOW) was 
introduced, describing the amount of time during a day in which the relative humidity exceeds 
a 80 % value  (Adan, 1994). The TOW has a strongly influence on fungal growth, however, 
the value and frequency of the relative humidity during dry periods (<80 % RH) is of nearly 
no influence (Adan, 1994; Sedlbauer, 2001) . The same findings have been reported in a more 
recent study, supporting the existing guidelines that the RH on interior surfaces should never 
exceed 75 % RH (Møller et al., 2017).    
When the right moisture conditions are present, fungal growth in buildings will be inevitably. 
Many different fungal species may be present in indoor environments, some more important 
than others.

Most indoor fungi have their optimal aw at 0.96-0.98, however, some fungi prefer much lower 
water activity (<0.8 aw), referred to as xerophilic species (Nielsen, 2002). Based on water 
requirements, indoor fungi can be divided into: (1) primary colonizers, which can grow at a 
water activity, less than or equal to 0.80 (e.g. Penicillium spp., Wallemia sebi and Aspergillus 
versicolor); (2) secondary colonizers which grow at a water activity between 0.80-0.90 (e.g. 
Cladosporium spp., Phoma glomerata and Ulocladium chartarum); and (3) tertiary 
colonizers, which require a water activity greater than 0.90 to germinate and start growth (e.g. 
Stachybotrys chartarum) (WHO report, 2009).  
Moisture requirements for fungal growth also depends on the type of building material (see 
Table 3). As an example, the moisture requirements for fungal growth on wood and wood 
based materials are considerably lower than on concrete or expanded polyester (EPS). This is 
due to the fact that wood and wood-based materials (e.g. plywood, particleboards) have a 
higher content of organic compounds that can be utilized as nutrients by the fungi (Grant et 
al., 1989). Furthermore, associations exist between indoor fungi and specific building 
materials (Andersen, et al., 2011). For instance, growth of Stachybotrys chartarum is 
particularly related with gypsum wallboards, whereas Cladosporium spp. grow well at painted 
surfaces (Nielsen, 2002).

1.2.1   Aspergillus versicolor 
Before the introduction of DNA-based methods for species identification, strains reported 
from the Aspergillus section Versicolores were reported as Aspergillus versicolor (Engelhart 
et al., 2002). However, other species such as Aspergillus jensenii and Aspergillus creber, 
which also belongs to Aspergillus section Versicolores, have also been identified in indoor 
environments (Despot et al., 2017). Phylogenetically, Aspergillus versicolor belongs to the 
Ascomycetes and produces spores (conidia) as dispersal units (McGinnis, 2007).  
Aspergillus versicolor is one of the most common fungi found on different building materials 
and from air samples in moisture damaged buildings (Andersen et al., 2011). Aspergillus 
versicolor can grow at a large temperature range of 4-40oC having the optimum temperature 
of 22-26oC (Pasanen et al., 1997). Aspergillus versicolor has xerophilic characteristics in 
being able to germinate, grow, and produce spores on agar at a water activity as low as 0.74-
0.81 (Mislivec et al., 1975). The optimum aw for growth varies between 0.96-0.98 (Grant et 
al., 1989). Aspergillus versicolor has different morphological characteristics depending upon 
growth conditions. As an example, colonies on CYA tend to be white or orange, and 
sometimes pink to wine-red exudate is produced (see Figure 4). Generally, colonies are 
typically white in the beginning of growth, and turn more green as growth proceeds (Pitt & 
Hocking, 2009).

1.2.3   Stachybotrys chartarum  
Stachybotrys chartarum is often found on wet gypsum wallboards in buildings with 
significant moisture damage, but is rarely detected in air, because the spores are born in sticky 
slime heads (Andersen et al., 2017). S. chartarum has an optimal temperature and aw for

1.2.4   Chaetomium globosum  
Chaetomium globosum, among other Chaetomium species, is one of the most common species 
in buildings, and may already be present in new gypsum wallboards (Andersen et al., 2017). 
Ch. globosum requires a aw higher than 0.90 for growth  (Andersen & Nissen, 2000), and thus, 
Ch. globosum is regarded as a tertiary colonizer, like Stachybotrys.  
1.2.5   Penicillium spp.  
Species belonging to the genus Penicillium are often found in indoor environments, of which 
Penicllium chrysogenum and P. brevicompactum are among the most common (Andersen et 
al., 2011). Penicillium spp. are more rapidly growing than other species, and some 
Penicillium spp. can grow at a aw below 0.80 (Scott et al., 2004).

1.2.6   Cladosporium spp.  
Cladosporium spp. are considered some of the most predominant species growing in 
buildings. The most frequently reported species are C. herbarum, along with C. 
cladosporioides and C. sphaerospermum (Pitt & Hocking, 2009). Generally, the minimum aw 
for germination is around 0.85, however, C. sphaerospermum can germinate at a minimum aw 
of 0.82 (Hocking et al., 1994). Cladosporium spp. are capable of growth at temperatures as 
low as -6oC, but prefer to grow at a temperature range of 18-28oC (Gill & Lowry, 1982).   
Fungi are able to grow in buildings under the right moisture conditions. Some of those fungi 
are capable of producing compounds, which may have negative health effects on humans.  
1.3  
HEALTH IMPLICATIONS OF INDOOR FUNGI  
Indoor fungi are known to produce allergens and various compounds, including mycotoxins 
and microbial volatile organic compounds (MVOCs), which are associated with a range of 
negative health effects (Portnoy et al., 2005) (see Table 4). Due to the possible fungal-
induced human health implications, much effort has been put into elucidating the 
epidemiology and underlying mechanism of action (Bush et al., 2001). Many studies have 
shown consistent associations between indoor fungi exposure and negative health effects, but 
still no causal links have been found (Mendel et al., 2011). Therefore, it is important to 
evaluate the existing research in details, and thereby recognize which future analysis that may 
indicate which, if any, of the compounds that are responsible for the development of human 
health effects.

1.3.1   Microbial organic volatile compounds 
Microbial organic volatile compounds (MVOCs) are produced by fungi during their primary 
and secondary metabolism. Thus, the production of MVCOs is highly dependent on factors 
such as pH, nutrients, temperature, and humidity, which all influence fungal growth (Korpi et 
al., 2009). MVOCs are characterized by a low molecular weight, low boiling point and high 
vapor pressure, which facilitate distribution to the surrounding air (Lemfack et al., 2014). 
Therefore, MVOCs are widely distributed in indoor environments, although no specific role 
==PAGE:20==
 
10 
 
in adverse health outcomes has been confirmed (Portnoy et al., 2005). However, studies have 
shown that 3-methylfuran, a MVOC produced by a broad spectrum of fungi, may have 
mucosal reactive properties against the eyes, nose, and airways (Wålinder et al., 2005). The 
mechanism behind is still not fully understood, but it may involve stimulation of the 
trigeminal nerve (Korpi et al., 2009). Nevertheless, the concentration of 3-methylfuran in the 
previous mentioned study was up to 1000 times higher than the common level encountered in 
indoor environments. However, it has been proposed that a mixture of MVOCs might have 
synergistic interactions, leading to a greater effect than what each compound could elicit 
alone (Korpi el al., 1999). 
Although it is believed that exposure to MVOCs may result in adverse health effects; further 
epidemiological studies on exposure are needed. Also, effective methods for the detection of 
MVOCs are not currently available, since many VOCs emitted from synthetic building 
materials may be similar to MVOCs released from indoor fungi (Schuchardt & Kruse, 2009). 
1.3.2   Mycotoxins  
Many indoor fungi are known to produce secondary metabolites called mycotoxins (Nielsen 
& Gravesen, 1999). Those compounds are not directly involved in the initial mycelial growth 
phase, but may play a role in sporulation processes, interactions with other organisms and 
stress tolerance. Approximately 400 mycotoxins have been identified, but this is only a small 
fraction of the presumed total number (Täubel & Hyvärinen, 2016). 
The effects of mycotoxins upon dietary exposure have been intensely studied, because 
mycotoxins may be found in foods that are colonized by fungi (Andersen & Thrane, 2006). 
Mycotoxins have some chemical and physical properties, which may lead to toxicological 
effects on humans when consumed. As an example, aflatoxins, produced by some Aspergillus 
species, are quite stable in many foods and may result in hepatotoxicity (liver-damage) after 
consumption (Egbuta et al., 2017). Also, recent concerns have been raised about respiratory 
inhalation of mycotoxins from the air, since mycotoxins have been found in airborne dust and 
in spores and fragments of fungi (Fischer & Dott, 2003). For instance, Aspergillus versicolor 
is known to be a major producer of the liver-damaging and carcinogenic mycotoxin 
sterigmatocystin, which has been detected in indoor carpet dust (Engelhart et al., 2002). Thus, 
many studies have looked at the quantification of mycotoxins at the surface of building 
materials or settled dust inside buildings (Bloom et al., 2007). However, the potential airborne 
toxic load of spores and fragments of fungi has not undergone sufficient analysis. Generally, 
many factors affect the aerosolizing potential of indoor fungal sources, such as the type of 
fungal species and texture of the surface. Though, the detachment and dispersion by air 
currents are probably the principal mechanism  (Górny et al., 2001). A recent study has 
investigated the potential airborne toxic load caused by indoor fungi (Aleksic et al., 2017). 
The results of this study has shown that mycotoxins, produced by Penicillium 
brevicompactum, Aspergillus versicolor and Stachybotrys chartarum, can be aerosolized from 
mouldy materials at air velocities that can be encountered in buildings (Aleksic et al., 2017). 
As an example, satratoxins (SG) were found in smaller particles released from the surface of a 
building material. These compounds have been linked to several health issues at a 
==PAGE:21==
 
11 
 
concentration of 5 µg, including acute neutrophilic rhinitis and olfactory nerve atrophy of 
rhesus monkeys (Carey et al., 2012). However, the total quantity of airborne SG in the study 
was only 0,102 µg, making the possible toxicity associated to inhalation questionable.  
Similar studies have concluded, that the dose of mycotoxin received through inhalation is 
unlikely to cause a toxic response (Kelman et al., 2004; Täubel & Hyvärinen, 2016).  
1.3.3   Allergens 
Fungi have the potential to produce allergens that can induce the production of antibodies in 
humans, leading to hypersensitivity to subsequent exposure (Zureik et al., 2002). Generally, 
hypersensitivity can be divided into different types of reactions based on the underlying 
mechanism, however, the most prevalent form caused by fungi is type I hypersensitivity, also 
termed allergy (Simon-Nobbe et al., 2008). Type I hypersensitivity is triggered by the 
exposure to a fungal allergen, after which the body will increase the production of specific 
IgE antibodies that can bind to mast cells (Horner et al., 1995). This will lead to allergic 
responses such as allergic rhinitis (e.g. sneezing) and allergic asthma (e.g. coughing), whereby 
especially asthma exacerbation in children is strongly suggestive of causality (Mendell et al., 
2011). However, it is only a small percentage of exposed people who actually develop 
allergies to fungi (Taskinen et al., 1997).  
Around 150 individual allergens from approximately 80 fungal genera have been isolated 
(Green et al., 2006), with Alternaria alternate, Aspergillus fumigatus, and Cladosporium 
herbarum being the most allergenic (Simon-Nobbe et al., 2008). However, also more than 20 
allergens from A. versicolor have been identified, making it an important allergenic source in 
the indoor environment (Benndorf et al., 2008). Many fungal allergens are found in spores, 
hyphae and fungal fragments and are released primarily during mycelial growth and spore 
germination (Mitakakis et al., 2001). However, non-viable fungal spores and hyphal 
fragments also contain allergens and may still be important for allergenic expression (WHO 
report, 2009).  
Still, the contribution of fungal allergen exposure to allergic diseases is still not fully 
understood and requires further research (Bush et al., 2001). 
1.3.4   Glucans  
Exposure to fungal structural cell wall components, called β-(1.3)-d-glucans, have been linked 
to non-allergic responses, such as coughing and wheezing (Douwes, 2005). These symptoms 
are caused by an inflammation, which is characterized by a production of cytokines and 
inflammatory cells, due to the interaction of β-(1.3)-d-glucans with epithetical cells in the 
airways (Portnoy et al., 2005).  
In a study of two schools with different concentrations of β-(1.3)-d-glucan (15.3 vs. 2.9 
ng/m3) the number of respiratory symptoms were higher among the pupils in the school with 
the highest concentration (i.e. 15.3 ng/ m3) (Rylander et al., 1998). However, difficulties with 
confounding factors, such as other fungal compounds (e.g. mycotoxins, allergens, MVOCs), 
could also account for the symptoms. Furthermore, the presence of fungal growth in the 
==PAGE:22==
 
12 
 
school with the highest concentration was known prior to the investigation, which could 
influence the subjectivity. Although studies have shown that β-(1.3)-d-glucans can lead to 
inflammation, evidence of a causal relationship still remains undemonstrated (Beijer et al., 
2002).  
1.4  
METHODS FOR SAMPLING AND DETECTION 
To understand the potential adverse health effects induced by indoor fungi, it is important to 
perform a thorough indoor assessment for fungi. However, many of the sampling and 
detection methods available are liable to produce both false positives and false negatives 
(Bonderup et al., 2016). This makes it difficult to evaluate the effect of indoor fungal 
contamination in buildings (Portnoy et al., 2004). Furthermore, the diversity of fungi in house 
dust, indoor air and on buildings materials may vary considerably when they are compared to 
each other (Wickman et al., 1992). Thus, there is a need of improving existing methods.  
1.4.1   Sampling methods 
1.4.1.1   Air sampling 
Air sampling for fungal aerosols is a widely-used method for the investigation of buildings 
with suspected fungal growth (Zorman & Jeršek, 2008). Generally, air sampling favors the 
detection of fungi that produce air-borne spores, such as Cladosporium, Penicillium and 
Aspergillus. Contrary to this, fungi such as Chaetomium, Stachybotrys and Trichoderma that 
produce spores in closed structures or slime heads are rarely seen in air samples (Andersen & 
Nissen, 2000). However, since inhalation is considered the main route of exposure (Khan & 
Karuppayil, 2012), air sampling provides important information.  
One of the limitation of the air sampling method is the relative short sampling time, which is 
not representative for assessing long-term exposure to airborne fungal spores. Also, factors 
such as activity in a room or outside spore concentration may influence the results 
(Sivasubramani et al., 2004). Most conventional air sampling methods are based on 
cultivating techniques, which also possess some limitations (see 1.4.2.1).  
Air sampling can be done either passively or actively. Passive sampling uses settle plates or 
culturing media, which are exposed to the air for a given time. In active monitoring, a defined 
quantity of air is drawn through a machine onto a culturing medium (Napoli et al., 2012). As 
indicated, passive sampling is not aggressive and may miss important fungal species, 
especially for situations with low fungal concentration. Active sampling is more ideal for such 
situations, and requires a lower sampling time. Ultimately, active air sampling can be a valid 
and precise tool for determining the extent of fungal growth inside buildings (Miller et al., 
2000).  
1.4.1.2   Surface sampling  
Surface sampling is used as a supplement to air sampling, and provides a more detailed 
depiction of the fungal growth on a given surface (Bonderup et al., 2016). Several surface 
based methods are available, but contact plates are most often used to gain information about 
==PAGE:23==
 
13 
 
the fungal growth on a surface (Samson et al., 2010). These plates are pressed against a 
surface and cultured until visual inspection can be performed. Also, swab sampling can be 
performed by using sterile swabs that can be rubbed over the affected surface and streaked 
directly onto an agar medium. Another widely used surface method is MycoMeter, which is 
based on quantification of the fungi specific enzyme β-N-acetylhexosaminidase (NAHA). The 
procedure of the method is the same as for swab sampling, but requires no cultivation step. 
Instead, the MycoMeter swab is analyzed in the lab by adding a fluorogenic enzyme substrate 
solution, which can be measured after 30 minutes of incubation (Bonderup et al., 2016; 
Rylander et al., 2010). The results are given as a MycoMeter value, which indicates the 
quantity of fungal biomass on the swabbed area. Therefore, this method provides no 
qualitative information.  
General, the main limitation of surface sampling is that it cannot identify hidden fungal 
growth, and do not provide direct measures of exposure to other fungi which are not directly 
on the sampled surface (Vesper, 2011).  
1.4.1.3   Dust sampling  
Dust sampling is used to evaluate long-term respiratory exposure to fungi, since dust samples 
are considered to reflect human inhalation exposure (Chew et al., 2003). However, studies 
have shown, that dust may not adequately reflect the inhalation exposure, since only a small 
amount of re-aerosolized dust particles is of respirable size (<10 µm) (Sivasubramani et al., 
2004). Furthermore, measures of airborne- and dust borne fungi most likely represent 
different types of fungal exposure (Chew et al., 2003) and, thus, dust sampling may not 
accurately reflect the respiratory exposure to indoor fungal propagules. Also, recent home 
alternations (e.g. remodeling, new carpets etc.) may also affect the dust deposition and 
retention (Sivasubramani et al., 2004). If all of these things are taken into account, dust 
sampling can be used in the assessment of indoor environments.

1.4.2   Detection    
1.4.2.1   Cultivation techniques 
Culture-based techniques have dominated the field of fungal identification and quantification 
for many years, and will possible continue to do it in the future (Vesper, 2011). In general, 
samples for culture analysis are inoculated on agar media and incubated at room temperature 
(20-25oC) for five to ten days. After incubation, the assessment of fungi is based on the 
determination of the number and type of colony forming units (CFU), providing qualitative 
and quantitative data on viable and cultivable fungi.  
The cultivation method lacks the ability to detect non-cultivable fungi, which may bias the 
picture of fungal diversity both quantitatively and qualitatively (Pitkäranta et al., 2008). 
Furthermore, research has pointed out, that the choice of agar medium is important, since 
different species have distinct growth requirements (Stetzenbach & Buttner, 2005). As an 
example, V8 juice agar has been shown to support good growth and sporulation of most 
indoor fungi (e.g. Alternaria, Aspergillus, and Cladosporium species); however, it does not 
allow growth of certain species like Wallemia spp. and Eurotium spp  (Andersen et al., 2011). 
Especially samples for airborne and surface-associated fungi have been analyzed by culturing, 
but dust sampling may also rely on cultivation techniques (Samson et al., 2010).  
1.4.2.2   Biomass techniques 
Different analytical approaches that analyze cellular constituents have also been developed 
and widely used in the analysis of indoor fungi. These methods are typically used to measure 
surrogates of total fungal biomass (Huttunen & Korkalainen, 2017), including cell wall 
components (e.g. ergosterol and β-glucan), fungal extracellular polysaccharides or specific 
enzymes (Hippelein & Rügamer, 2004). Commonly, all of these assay methods are highly 
specific and have a high sensitivity. However, the major limitations are that none of those 
methods allow for an identification of the fungal species involved (Rylander et al., 2010). 
1.4.2.3   Molecular techniques  
Recently, the application of DNA based techniques for the analysis of fungi in the indoor 
environment has increased, providing many advantages over the traditional cultivation 
technique (Vesper, 2011). The DNA-based method detects both cultivable and uncultivable 
fungal fragments, making it suitable in indoor studies since health hazards relating to indoor 
fungi may not require viability (Huttunen & Korkalainen, 2017). Another advantage of DNA 
methods is, in comparison with phenotypic methods (e.g. cultivation), that it is accurate and 
reproducible (Ettenauer et al., 2014).  
The molecular methods that are most often used include conventional or quantitative PCR 
(qPCR), which is very specific for fungal species or genus (Haugland et al., 2004). This 
method requires several steps and may lead to biases in DNA extraction and PCR 
amplification (Herrera et al., 2009).

1.5  
AIM OF THIS STUDY  
The main factor for fungal growth in buildings is water. Generally, most fungal species have 
high moisture requirements. However, some are capable of growing at relatively low moisture 
levels. Those species include, among others, Wallemia sebi and Aspergillus versicolor.  
The medium recommended for general detection of fungi indoors is V8, which should 
accommodate growth of most fungi. However, because of the high-water activity (aw), this 
medium favors the more hydrophilic fungi, usually at the expense of slower growing 
xerophilic species such as Wallemia sebi (Samson et al., 1994). As a supplement to this, 
DG18 has been introduced, which has proved to be excellent for isolation of xerophiles 
(Verhoeff et al., 1990). Nevertheless, DG18 medium has the disadvantage that Eurotium spp. 
may overgrow the more slower-growing fungi like Aspergillus versicolor  and Wallemia sebi 
(Reenen-Hoekstra et al., 1991). Furthermore, many of the sampling methods that are used to 
assess fungal growth in buildings have limitations. However, tests have shown that properly 
done air sampling can be a valid and precise tool for the investigation of indoor fungi  (Miller 
et al., 2000).  
The aim of this study was to investigate the interacting effects of water activity (aw), solute, 
pH-value and temperature on the growth of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi strains 
in vitro. Based on those findings, the overall aim was to develop a growth medium that 
supports large growth of slower growing xerophilic species like Aspergillus versicolor and 
Wallemia sebi. Another aim of this study was to elucidate the mycotoxin production potential 
of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi strains under different conditions.  
Finally, an aim was to investigate if Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi could be 
detected in air samples from moisture damaged buildings, and to comment on factors that 
might alter this relationship.  
1.6  
HYPOTHESES 
The main research question of this study is divided into four overall hypotheses, which are 
proposed for further research: 
1.   The media recommended for detection of indoor fungi are ineffective towards 
Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi. 
2.   Air sampling can be a useful tool for the identification of Aspergillus versicolor and 
Wallemia sebi in buildings with minor water damage. 
3.   Wallemia sebi is mostly found in buildings with plastered walls. 
4.   Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi produce various mycotoxins under different 
conditions.

SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS 
The delimitations of this study are based on decisions made during the development of the 
study plan, and from limitations that arose during the investigations.  
Regarding the case studies, the main focus was to investigate the fungal species composition 
in moisture damaged buildings, with focus on Wallemia sebi and Aspergillus versicolor.

2.1.2.2   pH 
To test the effect of pH on fungal growth, sodium hydroxide (NaOH) was added to each 
medium (medium 9-13) and adjusted to a pH-value of 9 (see Table 7). The pH-value was 
measured with a PHM210 Standard pH Meter (±0.25) and compared to universal pH test 
strips provided by MERCK. 
All of the Aspergillus versicolor strains and Wallemia sebi 32220 were each inoculated as 
three-point inoculations onto the media. Samples were inoculated in darkness at 25°C and 
colony diameter (in mm) was recorded after 7 days of incubation. New samples were made if 
no fungal growth was present after 14 days of incubation. 
2.1.3   Mycotoxin production  
All Aspergillus versicolor strains and Wallemia sebi 32220 were each inoculated as nine-point 
inoculations onto the following media: YES, DG18 and CYA. YES is often used for 
mycotoxin production (Samson et al., 1994), whereas DG18 and CYA represent two different 
carbon sources, i.e. glycerol and sucrose, respectively. The samples were grown at 25°C for 
10 days in darkness.  
The secondary metabolites were then extracted using the plug extraction protocol provided by 
DTU Bioengineering (see Appendix 2: Plug extraction protocol).    
2.1.3.1   Plug extraction  
Initially, 8 plugs were taken from each sample using a 6-mm straw (plug drill). The plugs 
were then transferred to Eppendorf tubes and 1 ml of organic solvent (ethyl acetate/2-
propanol (3:1) with 1 % formic acid) was added to each Eppendorf tube. The Eppendorf tubes 
==PAGE:30==
 
20 
 
were stored in a freezer for two days, after which the tubes were placed into an ultrasonic bath 
for 60 minutes at full effect. Then the extracts (solvent with metabolites) were transferred to 
new Eppendorf tubes using a glass pipette and placed to dry overnight. After the solvent had 
evaporated, 300 µl of methanol was added to each Eppendorf tube and the metabolites were 
re-dissolved in an ultrasonic bath for 10 minutes. Eventually, each sample was centrifuged at 
10 RCF for 5 minutes to spin down insolvable components and 200 µl of the supernatant from 
each sample were transferred to labeled HPLC insert vials ready for further analysis.   
2.1.3.2   HPLC 
All samples were analyzed on an Agilent 100 system HPLC with a Luna C18 II column and 
detected using a photo diode array detector (DAD). Also, samples were detected using mass 
spectrometry (Agilent 6545 Q-TOF LC-MS). 
 Eventually, all samples were evaluated using a standardized method adopted from Frisvad & 
Thrane (Frisvad & Thrane, 1987). In addition to this, data obtained from mass spectrometry 
were analyzed in Agilent Mass Hunter, which compared the fragmentation patterns with 
existing databases. The databases were provided by the “Natural Product Discovery” research 
group at DTU, and contained various mycotoxins isolated from different fungi and bacteria.

2.2.1   Methods for assessing fungal growth   
2.2.1.1   Air sampling 
Air samples were taken from each case study with an air sample device (MAS-100 Eco). The 
machine impacted a defined quantity of air (100 L/min ± 4 %) through a plate perforated with 
400 holes (0.7 mm in diameter) onto a growth medium (Meier & Zingre, 2000). Active air 
==PAGE:31==
 
21 
 
sampling was chosen based on the advantages compared to passive sampling (see 1.4.1.1). 
Generally, the air sample device was placed in the middle of the room without further 
precaution. However, the indoor air was circulated with a small leaf blower prior to the air 
sampling in case 6, which was a room without visible fungal growth and no moisture damage.   
All samples were incubated at 20°C for 7 days, except samples from case 1 and case 2, which 
were incubated for 14 days. After incubation, all cultivable fungi were counted as colony-
forming units (CFU) per plate. The upper limiting value for legibility was set to 400 colonies 
per plate. Furthermore, results of the MAS sampler were adjusted using a “positive-hole” 
correction table (see Appendix 3: Positive correlation table), since the probability of the 
next particle going into an “empty” hole decreases as the medium becomes filled (Engelhart 
et al., 2007).   
2.2.1.2   Surface sampling 
To test the prevalence of fungal species present on surfaces, additional measurements were 
carried out either by contact plating, MycoMeter surface swabs or regular swabs (see 1.4.1.2). 
Contact plates (V8 and/or DG18) were incubated at 20°C for 7 days, and the regular swabs 
were streaked directly onto an agar medium (V8 and/or DG18). The MycoMeter surface 
samples were analyzed in a laboratory by a consultant firm.  
2.2.1.3   Moisture measurements  
The amount of moisture in building materials was measured with a Gann Hydromette UNI1 
connected to a B50 active electrode. The instrument was based on a non-destructive high-
frequency measuring method that gave displayed values in Digits, which correspond to a 
percentage of dry weight (see Appendix 4: Digits in relation to the moisture content (in 
weight %) of a material). The instrument was chosen as the best method for determining 
high moisture content in materials, which could ease the comparison between moisture levels 
in different materials. Ultimately, the most absolute method for determining the moisture 
content would have been to measure the difference in weight (%) between a wet and dry 
material.  
The instrument had a measuring range from 0 to 199 digits. In the range between 30 and 70 
digits, the signal tolerance was ± 2 digits, and in the range between 80 and 140 digits ± 3 
digits (operating instructions).  
Generally, the amount of moisture was measured in the walls and floor near the location of air 
sampling. The values obtained from those measurements were compared to guiding values, 
which were provided by a specialized consultant firm (see Table 9).

2.2.3   Fungal identification  
Fungi were identified based on macro-morphology (e.g. colony color, texture etc.) and/or 
micro-morphology (e.g. spores, conidiophores).  
A stereoscopic (Olympus CO11) was used to study structures on the surface of fungal 
colonies, such as fruiting bodies (e.g. ascomata, cleistothecia). Generally, fungi were 
identified to genus level by looking at macro-morphology.  
The fine-level structures were observed using an Olympus BX50 microscope. Accordingly, a 
piece of clear sticky tape was used to pick up and remove fungal biomass from the cultivation 
media, after which the tape was transferred to a microscope slide with a drop of Shears liquid. 
The fungi were identified based on (Samson et al., 2010).

4   Discussion  
4.1  
LABORATORY STUDIES 
4.1.1   Standard media 
4.1.1.1   The effect of water activity, temperature and carbon source 
The optimal temperatures for growth of Aspergillus versicolor, according to Smith & Hill 
(1982), are between 25 and 30°C (Smith & Hill, 1982). Furthermore, in the experiments of 
Leggieri et al. (2018) this species was affected by low temperatures, with a very limited 
growth at 10°C (Leggieri et al., 2018). All of the Aspergillus versicolor strains grown on 
various standard media in this study had a temperature optimum between 25 and 30°C and 
limited growth at 10°C, which are in agreement with these findings. Generally, growth 
increased with increasing temperature in the measured interval 10-30°C on all media, except 
on CYA and V8, on which the highest growth occurred at 25°C. Furthermore, the results 
presented suggest that growth of Aspergillus versicolor is influenced strongly by temperature. 
As an example, the optimum aw for growth was lower at 30°C compared to 15°C. Ayerst 
(1969) emphasized that the greatest tolerance to low water activity is found at temperatures 
close to that of the optimum temperature (Ayerst, 1969). This corresponds well with the 
findings of this study; however, it does not explain why growth on V8 and CYA was 
decreasing above 25°C. Both of these media have a rather high aw compared to CYAS and 
DG18. One thinkable hypothesis is that Aspergillus versicolor actually prefers to grow at a 
lower aw at the optimum temperature for growth, assuming that 30°C is the optimum 
temperature for growth, which seems reasonable according to literature (Smith & Hill, 
1982).This might explain why growth of Aspergillus versicolor was higher on DG18 and 
CYAS at 30°C for all strains, except one. Another hypothesis for the decreased growth on V8 
and CYA above 25°C is a reduced ability of Aspergillus versicolor to tolerate high aw at 
temperatures above 25°C. Until now, these observations have not been demonstrated or 
studied. Thus, further studies are needed.  
Growth rates of Aspergillus versicolor were highest after 7 days of incubation on all standard 
media, except on DG18. Although it has been stated that Aspergillus versicolor often 
sporulates poorly after one week of incubation on czapek agar medium (Samson et al., 1994), 
this does not explain the low growth rate on DG18. Since DG18 is the only medium that 
contains glycerol, this could indicate that Aspergillus versicolor is affected by solute/carbon 
source. Pitt & Hocking (1977) also noted that at the optimum aw for growth, the growth rate 
on glycerol media was lower of that on salt or glucose/fructose meida (Pitt & Hocking, 1977). 
However, Aspergillus species were reported to be very little affected by solute type at which 
germination and growth occurred. Based on the findings of Pitt & Hocking (1977), this 
present study did not consider the effects of germination time, early exponential growth and 
late suboptimal growth on growth rates. Eventually, the unsolved question of whether solute 
type is important for growth rates of Aspergillus versicolor requires further experimental 
work, especially in the first week of incubation.  
==PAGE:55==
 
45 
 
Furthermore, the results from this study have shown that there are some variations among 
Aspergillus versicolor strains with respect to growth on standard media. In a recent study, it 
has been shown that environmental isolates of section Versicolores species exhibit variation 
both in macro-phenotypic appearance, but also in growth characteristics (Jurjevic et al., 
2012). Jurjevic et al. (2012) showed that Aspergillus jensensii and Aspergillus creber, which 
both have been identified in indoor environments (Despot et al., 2017), grew 20-27 mm and 
18-26 mm, respectively, on CYA at 25°C after 10 days of incubation. Contrary to this, 
Aspergillus versicolor grew 28-36 mm under the same conditions. In this present study, 
Aspergillus versicolor strain 33558 grew higher on CYA and CYAS compared to the other 
strains. This could indicate that the variation between strains is due to the involvement of 
different species belonging to the Aspergillus section Versicolores. However, all strains did 
show the same tendency in growth. Furthermore, when growth of all strains was compiled, 
the standard deviations were rather acceptable.  
This study has shown that the optimal temperatures for growth of Wallemia sebi were 
between 20 and 25°C. Wheeler et al. (1988) also reported optimum growth of Wallemia sebi 
at 25°C, with lower temperature limits between 5 and 10°C and an upper limit near 30°C 
(Wheeler et al., 1988). All of the Wallemia sebi strains in this study had limited growth at 
10°C, which are in agreement with these findings. However, high growth did occur at 30°C, 
suggesting a higher upper limit for growth. Some authors (Gock et al., 2003) have reported 
that Wallemia sebi can grow and sporulate at temperatures around 37°C, supporting this 
hypothesis. The results from this study also show an interestingly consistent interaction 
between growth medium and temperature on the growth of Wallemia sebi. Out of the standard 
media evaluated, growth of Wallemia sebi was generally highest on CYAS and lowest on V8 
at most temperatures. Thus, the optimum aw for growth was in general 0.964 at all 
temperatures. The present observations do not agree with those of most previous studies in 
that the greatest tolerance to low water activity is found at the optimum temperature. 
Furthermore, growth of Wallemia sebi was generally higher on CYA (aw = 0.986) compared 
to V8 (aw = 0.980), indicating that the nature of the growth medium (solute/carbon source) 
influences growth. Scott (1957) and more recent authors have also noted that the nature of 
solute strongly influences the rate of growth of Wallemia sebi, supporting these findings 
(Scott, 1957).  
The results from this study have shown that there are variations among Wallemia sebi strains 
with respect to tolerance of temperature, aw and growth medium. Previous studies have shown 
that variations exist within the genus Wallemia, indicating the existence of at least two cryptic 
genera (Zalar et al., 2005). Lately, it has been emphasized that the genus Wallemia 
exclusively comprises seven species, which all have different morphological and 
physiological characters. As an example, Wallemia sebi is the only species of Wallemia 
capable of growth on media without additional solutes (Zalar et al., 2005). In this study, 
Wallemia sebi strain 33220 grew on all standard media and was the only one of the tested 
Wallemia strains that also grew on V8, which contains no additional solutes. Therefore, these 
results could indicate that the variation of physiological behaviors among strains is due to the 
involvement of different Wallemia species.  
==PAGE:56==
 
46 
 
4.1.2    New media 
4.1.2.1   The effect of water activity, temperature and carbon source 
The results of this study show that Aspergillus versicolor has xerophilic characteristics in 
being able to germinate, grow, and sporulate on growth media at aw ranging from 0.884 to 
0.997. Grant et al. (1989) suggested that Aspergillus versicolor is able to grow at aw as low as 
0.75-0.81 in the optimal temperature range (i.e. 25-30°C), and that the optimum aw for growth 
varies between 0.965-0.980 at 12-25°C (Grant et al., 1989). This was also observed in the 
present study. Furthermore, the results presented suggest that the optimal aw for growth of 
Aspergillus versicolor is influenced by temperature. This is comparable to the findings on the 
standard media, indicating that growth of Aspergillus versicolor is indeed very dependent on 
the temperature. From this experiment, it is difficult to conclude whether Aspergillus 
versicolor actually prefers to grow at a lower aw at the optimum temperature for growth. 
However, growth was in fact higher at 20°C compared to 30°C at the highest aw. Again, this 
interesting relationship requires further research.  
On the other hand, Aspergillus versicolor grew similar on media containing additional amount 
of various solutes/carbon sources. Furthermore, this observation was also found at similar aw, 
suggesting that growth of Aspergillus versicolor is little affected by solute/carbon source at 
the same aw. It should be noted, however, that the comparison between solute/carbon source 
and growth of Aspergillus versicolor was on purpose simplified. As an example, it was 
assumed that medium 4, 5 and 8 had the same aw. Furthermore, growth of Aspergillus 
versicolor was remarkable low on medium 1, which contained glycerol. However, high 
growth was observed on medium 4, which also contained glycerol. General, the optimum aw 
for Aspergillus versicolor at 25°C was between 0.953-0.965. Taken all of this into account, it 
seems reasonable to suggest, that growth of Aspergillus versicolor is truly little affected by 
solute/carbon source and more effected by aw, which is in agreements with the findings on the 
standard media. Again, this same relationship has also been noted by Scott (1957) and more 
recent authors (Gock et al., 2003; Scott, 1957).  
Wallemia sebi has been reported to have an obligate requirement for salt. However, several 
authors have showed it to be equally capable of growth in other solutes (Pitt & Hocking, 
1977). Under favourable conditions growth has been observed to occur from 0.997 to 0.69 aw 
(Wheeler et al., 1988). The results from this study indicate that Wallemia sebi is capable of 
growth at aw between 0.884 and 0.997. Furthermore, the results show that Wallemia sebi 
grows best at sucrose-rich media but has no requirement for salt. On the contrary, growth was 
actually twice as low on salt-containing media. These findings indicate that Wallemia sebi is 
dependent on the type of solute/carbon source. Wheeler et al. (1988) also reported that growth 
on glucose-fructose media was significantly higher than on salt media (Wheeler et al., 1988), 
supporting this conclusion.  
Generally, all of the Wallemia sebi strains could tolerate growth over a range of aw; however, 
only one strain grew on medium 3, which had the highest aw (i.e. 0.997) and no appreciable 
additional solute (i.e. gypsum). This strain also grew on V8, which again suggests that this 
strain might be the only actual Wallemia sebi. Again, this is based on the assumption that 
==PAGE:57==
 
47 
 
Wallemia sebi is the only species among Wallemia that can grow without additional solutes 
(Zalar et al., 2005). 
4.1.2.2   The effect of pH  
The finding that Aspergillus versicolor is capable of growing on a pH-value of 9 is consistent 
with previous studies (Ebada, El-Neketi, Ebrahim, Kalscheuer, & Proksch, 2018). While 
studies report that Wallemia sebi can grow under very acidic conditions (Deschuyffeleer et al., 
2015), there has been no evidence that Wallemia sebi can tolerate alkaline conditions as well. 
This study, which is the first of its kind, shows that growth of Wallemia sebi is also capable of 
growing under alkaline conditions. Eventually, this could support the hypothesis that 
Wallemia sebi is often found in air samples from buildings with plastered walls, which may 
possess an alkaline environment (Adan & Samson, 2011). 
Generally, there is little information on the interactive effect of pH with aw on fungal growth. 
However, it has been presented that some Aspergillus species can grow at lower aw under 
optimal pH conditions. Both Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi did show different 
variation in response to pH and aw, however, no consistent relationship could be found   
(Adan, 1994).  
DG18 and V8 are the two most recommended detection media for fungi in indoor 
environments (Andersen, et al., 2011). The results from this study indicate that those media 
are not suitable as cultivation media for Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi. Especially 
V8 is ineffectual, because only one of the four Wallemia sebi isolates grew on it. Furthermore, 
the results showed that Wallemia sebi is very dependent on solute/carbon source, whereas, 
Aspergillus versicolor is more affected by aw and temperature. However, both species did 
have a preference towards the same new medium, which had the optimal aw for growth (i.e. 
0.953 aw) and contained the preferable solute/carbon source (i.e. sucrose). Ultimately, this 
study has found a new growth medium with improved recovery of both Aspergillus versicolor 
and Wallemia sebi.  
4.1.3   Mycotoxin production  
This study shows that all strains of Aspergillus versicolor were able to produce the 
carcinogenic mycotoxin called sterigmatocystin in vitro. This was not unexpected since the 
proportion of sterigmatocystin-producing strains varies from 60 to 100 % (Engelhart et al., 
2002). Generally, all strains of Aspergillus versicolor produced high quantities of 
sterigmatocystin, especially on YES media. The observation that Aspergillus versicolor is 
generating high quantities of sterigmatocystin on sucrose-rich media is in agreement with the 
findings of Hitokoto et al. (1982) and Nielsen (2003), sometimes representing up to 1 % of its 
biomass  (Hitokoto et al., 1982; Nielsen, 2003). However, the ability of Aspergillus versicolor 
strains to produce sterigmatocystin varied considerably in this study. This variation is likely 
due to two reasons. First, the process of plug extraction for secondary metabolite analysis may 
influence the quantity of mycotoxin observed. As an example, secondary metabolites, which 
includes mycotoxins, most often accumulate beneath the centre of the fungal colony (Moore, 
Robson, & Trinci, 2012). If plugs were taken from areas with low accumulation of secondary 
==PAGE:58==
 
48 
 
metabolites, this may explain some of the variation. In support of this, one of the strains that 
did produce low quantities of sterigmatocystin also had limited growth. Secondly, this 
variation among strains could be explained by genetic differences. As an example, all of the 
strains did produce different mycotoxins, and generally no consistent relationships were found 
between strains. However, the strains behaved more or less the same in the other experiments 
of this study, which makes this hypothesis questionable. In addition to sterigmatocystin, 
metabolites such as stephacidin A, notoamide A and versicolamides were produced. All of 
these compounds have been characterised before in Aspergillus versicolor extracts (Despot et 
al., 2017).  
Species belonging to the genus Wallemia has been known to produce a lot of secondary 
metabolites, however, only a limited number of mycotoxins have been detected (Frank et al., 
1999). In summary, this study found that a strain of Wallemia sebi produced a range of these 
compounds, which included walleminone, wallimidione and walleminol. Both walleminone 
and wallemidione were produced on YES medium by the strain, whereas, walledione and 
walleminone were only produced on DG18 and CYA, respectively. This indicates that the 
potential of mycotoxin production of Wallemia sebi might be dependent on the growth 
medium. In a recent study, it has been emphasized that the secondary metabolite production 
of Wallemia species is indeed very dependent on the growth medium (Jančič et al., 2016). It 
should be mentioned however, that the experiments were made under different concentrations 
of salt. It remains unclear if high-sugar conditions will also induce the production of 
mycotoxins.   
4.2  
CASE STUDIES 
The present results from air samples in case 1 demonstrate that the main detected species in a 
building with high moisture damage were Aspergillus versicolor, several species of 
Penicillium plus Cladosporium and Stachybotrys chartarum. Aspergillus versicolor and 
Stachybotrys chartarum also occurred frequently on surfaces without visible growth. 
Moreover, Aspergillus versicolor was found in the highest quantity on V8, although DG18 
has been found as the most suitable medium for growth of common xerophilic fungi, such as 
Aspergillus versicolor (Hocking & Pitt, 1980). Several studies have shown a strong 
association between Aspergillus versicolor and moisture damaged buildings (Andersen, et al., 
2011) and some authors have even named Aspergillus versicolor as one of the indicator fungi 
for moisture damage (Samson et al., 1994). In this case study, the high moisture levels that 
were observed was due to a leakage from a water pipe underneath the floor, which also was 
confirmed by the appearance of Stachybotrys chartarum which demands a aw above 0.90 to 
grow (Samson et al., 2001). Eventually, the high prevalence of Aspergillus versicolor on V8 
could be caused by an overload of Aspergillus versicolor in the indoor environment, due to 
the newly discovered water damage. Furthermore, the samples were incubated for 14 days 
instead of 7 days, which could make colony counts misleading. However, some authors have 
suggested that Aspergillus versicolor is a late primary colonizer, which is not commonly 
found immediately after water damage. Nevertheless, it will appear if the water-damage is not 
dried out (Yang & Heinsohn, 2007). Similarly, a corresponding high quantity of Aspergillus 
==PAGE:59==
 
49 
 
versicolor was found from V8 air samples in case 4, which also had substantial moisture 
damage. Altogether, these results indicate that Aspergillus versicolor is found in high 
prevalence on air samples collected from buildings with water damage that has not dried out. 
Furthermore, the results of this study show that the new medium that was developed in the 
laboratory studies seems more suitable for the detection of Aspergillus versicolor, especially 
in buildings with minor or medium moisture damage.  
The data presented in this study, show that Wallemia sebi plays a small role in moisture 
damaged buildings. Wallemia sebi was not to be found in any air samples, indicating that the 
performance of air sampling regarding the collection of this fungus is insufficient. However, 
Wallemia sebi was neither found on wall surfaces. Wallemia sebi has been reported as 
ubiquitous in buildings. However, growth is rare on building materials at >95 % ERH 
(Nguyen et al., 2015). Dannemiller et al. (2017) also stated that the underreporting of 
Wallemia sebi may also be due to a decrease in relative abundance despite the increase in 
concentration at increasing relative humidity (Dannemiller et al., 2017). This might explain 
why this fungus did not appear in case 1 and 4, which both had high moisture levels and high 
fungal abundance.  
Although, Wallemia sebi has been found in both air and on surfaces, it is most often 
encountered in high concentrations in house dust (Nonnenmann et al., 2012). According to 
previous studies (Chew et al., 2003; Cox et al., 2017), little association exist between fungi in 
indoor air and dust. From these case studies, it is unlikely that dust particles could be 
aerosolized merely by the inflow of active air sampling. Since Wallemia sebi was not found in 
any of the samples, it is difficult to determine if associations exist between Wallemia sebi and 
different types of materials, such as plastered walls. However, a recent study by Vesper et al. 
(2016) showed that no difference exist between growth of Wallemia sebi in homes 
constructed with gypsum drywall compared to plaster (Vesper et al., 2016). Whether the 
hypothesis that Wallemia sebi is mostly found in buildings with plastered walls should be 
retained or rejected would still require further research.

5   Conclusions and Further Research 
5.1  
SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS 
This study has demonstrated that Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi have different 
growth requirements in terms of solute/carbon source, temperature and water activity. The 
conclusion from these observations is that Aspergillus versicolor is little affected by type of 
solute/carbon source and more dependent on aw and temperature. On the contrary, Wallemia 
sebi can grow on media without additional solutes; however, the type of solute/carbon source 
can be important for growth. These findings have contributed to the development of a new 
growth medium for the detection/cultivation of both Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia 
sebi. In conclusion, the results suggest that this medium is more suitable than the general 
detection/cultivation media, such as V8 and DG18. Such data reported above have important 
implications for the detection of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi in indoor 
environments. Furthermore, from this study, it is concluded that strains of Aspergillus 
versicolor and Wallemia sebi isolated from indoor environments are able to produce 
mycotoxins under different conditions in vitro. Further research is needed to provide an 
understanding of the possible outcomes in indoor environments.   
The case studies carried out in this study have suggested that Aspergillus versicolor is present 
in high quantities in buildings with undried water damage and in buildings with minor 
moisture damage. In conclusion, the results from these investigations have showed that the 
recommended detection media for fungi in indoor environments, such as V8 and DG18, are 
suitable in buildings with undried water damage suspected of growth with Aspergillus 
versicolor. On the other hand, the data from this study support the conclusion that a new 
collecting growth medium seems more suitable for the detection of Aspergillus versicolor in 
buildings with minor moisture damage. Furthermore, this study concludes that Wallemia sebi 
is not present or is a minor component of the indoor air in moisture damage buildings.  
The results from these investigations support the hypothesis that properly done air sampling 
can be a useful tool for the detection of Aspergillus versicolor in buildings with different 
moisture damages. However, this method is not suitable for the detection of Wallemia sebi 
and should never be used alone. These findings provide valuable information for investigating 
the occurrence of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi in moisture damaged buildings. 
Ultimately, the conclusions expressed in this study could help to understand and reduce the 
fungal-induced human health implications.  
5.2  
SUGGESTIONS FOR FUTURE WORK  
For future studies, it is recommended to address the effect of other fungal species on the 
growth of Aspergillus versicolor and Wallemia sebi on the newly developed growth medium. 
Is the medium more suitable for other species? How is the competition among species? 
Furthermore, multivariate statistical analyses of fungal responses and medium composition 
could be interesting to perform. Also, experiments should be made on the optimal conditions 
==PAGE:61==
 
51 
 
for growth on the newly developed medium. What is the optimum incubation time? What is 
the optimum incubation temperature? For future case studies, it is recommended to address 
the involvement of dust sampling for the detection of Wallemia sebi in indoor environments. 
Ultimately, the hypothesis that Aspergillus versicolor actually prefers to grow at a lower aw at 
the optimum temperature for growth requires further research.
