Abstract
There is a high incentive to reduce the emission of CO2 from the combustion of fossil fuels due
to the adverse effects on the climate. One soultion is the electrochemical conversion of CO2 to
fuels using co-electrolysis of CO2 and water.
This project consisted of the design and construction of a single cell setup for co-electrolysis of
H2O and CO2 at intermediate temperatures (200-400 ◦C) and elevated partial pressures of H2O
using a KH2PO4/SnP2O7 composite electrolyte and gas diffusion electrodes.
The thesis starts with a theoretical part giving an overview of the theory and results of
electrochemical reduction of CO2 and of the components of the composite electrolyte. Then is
given an experimental part describing the design and development of the setup, fabrication
of electrolyte and electrodes and characterisation of these, and electrolysis and co-electrolysis
experiments using various electrodes and operating conditions.
It was found that the KH2PO4/SnP2O7 composite electrolyte was not stable under the operation
conditions and a hydration reaction to form a mixed potassium and tin phosphate was inferred.
The properties of this resultant electrolyte have not been investigated.
Cu and Inconel (Ni alloy) were promising electrocatalysts for the CO2 reduction reaction and
the oxygen evolution reaction, respectively, but both materials were found to be unstable when
in contact with the electrolyte under the operating conditions.
Current densities as high as 2 A/cm2 were obtained initially in a single experiment with 2.5 V
at 250 ◦C and 6 bar H2O pressure (20 bar total) with a Ag foam cathode and a Pt/C anode.
The results have given an indication of the possible challenges and beneﬁts of the proposed
system and point the way for continued research.

Resumé
Der er et stort inticament for at reducere udledningen af CO2 grundet den negative effekt på
klimaet. En mulig løsning er elektrokemisk reduktion af CO2 til brandbare hydrokarboner ved
co-elektrolyse af CO2 og vand.
Dette kandidatspeciale beskæftigede sig med design og konstruktion af en testcelle til co-
elektrolyse af CO2 og H2O ved mellemhøje temperaturer (200-400 ◦C) og øget partialtryk af
vanddamp med brug af en KH2PO4/SnP2O7 komposit elektrolyt og gas diffusions elektroder.
Første del af opgaven er teori vedrørende elektrokemisk reduction af CO2, de to komponenter
i elektrolytten, og komposit elektrolytter generelt. Anden del beskæftiger sig med de eksperi-
mentielle aspekter af projektet: metoder, opsætning, syntese af elektrolyt og elektroder samt
karakterisering af disse, og elektrolyse og co-elektrolyse forsøg med forskellige elektroder
under varierende forhold.
Projektet viste at komposit elektrolytten ikke var stabil under forsøgsforholdene og en mulig
hydreringsreaktion, hvor kompositten danner en blanding af kalium og tin phosphat, blev
foreslået. Den resulterende elektrolyt er ikke undersøgt.
To elektrokatalytiske materialer, Cu og Inconel (Ni legering), der ellers var lovende ved brug til
henholdsvis CO2 reduktion og oxygenudvikling, viste sig ikke at være stablie under kontakt
med elektrolytten ved de givne forhold.
Strømstyrkedensiteter på 2 A/cm2 blev opnået i starten af et enkelt eksperiment ved 250 ◦C, 6
bar vandamp og 20 bar total tryk ved brug af en Ag-skum kathode og en Pt/C-ﬁlt anode.
Resultaterne giver et indblik i de mulige problemer og fordele ved det undersøgte system og
lægger vejen for videre forskning.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The emission of CO2 is an increasing problem for the environment and much effort is going
into reducing CO2 levels. One solution is the conversion of CO2 back into valuable chemicals
or fuels using renewable energy resources such as solar and wind energy using CO2 captured
from the atmosphere or at a CO2 emission point-source. A number of chemicals including
methane, ethylene, methanol, CO and formic acid can be formed from the reduction of CO2 by
means of homogeneous catalysis, heterogeneous catalysis and electrocatalysis [2]. CO2 capture
from air is costly due to the low atmospheric concentration. The CO2 supply should not be
from ﬂue gas from combustion processes as it would be better to use the renewable energy
directly instead of the fuel combustion. A better concentrated source is CO2 as a component of
biogas which results from the anaerobic digestion of waste from sewage, manure and landﬁll
sites [3]. After puriﬁcation the biogas can be upgraded by converting the CO2 content to
methane thus raising the energy content of the biogas to natural gas levels [1, 3].
This project consisted of the design and development of a single cell setup for co-electrolysis
of H2O and CO2 at intermediate temperatures (200-400 ◦C) and elevated partial pressures of
H2O using a molten KH2PO4 electrolyte immobilised in a SnP2O7 matrix. Molten KH2PO4 had
previously exhibited very high conductivities under pressurised conditions [4], and SnP2O7
had shown promise as a solid electrolyte for intermediate temperature steam electrolysis [5].
The thesis consists of 6 chapters. Chapter 1 is the introduction. Chapter 2 describes the
theory of water electrolysis above 100 ◦C and then CO2 electrolysis with the main focus on
products and reactions on Cu. Chapter 3 discusses the two components of the electrolyte:
KH2PO4 and SnP2O7, other results from similar electrolyte combinations and other electrolyte
concepts for CO2 electrolysis. Chapter 4 deals with all the experimental aspects of the project:
development of the setup, fabrication of the electrolyte and electrodes and characterisation
of these, and the electrolysis experiments. Chapter 5 discusses the results from chapter 4 in
relation to each other and to the theory. Chapter 6 gives the conclusion of the project and a
suggestion for further work.

the electrochemical reduction of CO2 based on a study of the relevant literature and focuses
mainly on the reactions on Cu.

2.1.2
Kinetics
Besides the reversible potential a certain overpotential ηcell is required to pass a current and
drive the reaction in the desired direction at a certain rate. The overpotential can be divided
into contributions from different parts of the system, mainly: electrolyte ηe and activation of
the electrodes ηact.
Ecell = Erev + ηcell
(2.4)
ηcell = ηe + ηact = IRohm + ηa + ηc
(2.5)
The electrolyte overpotential is linear (ohmic) whereas the anodic and cathodic overpotentials
(ηa and ηc) are logarithmic with the current. Limiting the mass transfer by not supplying
enough reactants will make the cell potential go towards inﬁnity. So the cell potential at a
certain current is seen to be deﬁned by the reversible potential and the overpotentials. This is
illustrated in the polarisation curve in ﬁgure 2.2. The anodic overpotential is seen to be much
larger than the cathodic and this is because the oxygen evolution reaction on the anode is much

Products on various metal electrodes

• Au, Ag, Zn, Pd, Ga: CO.

2.2.2
Products on Cu
Due to the special ability of Cu to catalyse reactions involving more than two electrons, Cu has
been studied in depth and will also be the focus in the following. Reviews on the reduction of
CO2 on Cu are given in [9] and more recently in [10].
The number of compounds formed on Cu were seen to be much greater than originally
assumed when Kuhl et al. used GC and NMR to detect the products [11]. The Faradaic
efﬁciency and partial current density for the products at different potentials are shown in
ﬁgure 2.3.
With respect to Faradaic efﬁciency (ﬁgure 2.3, left) it is clear that at low overpotentials, besides
H2, mainly CO and formate are produced, with CO efﬁciency steadily declining with increasing
overpotential and formate reaching a maximum at ca. -0.9 V vs. RHE and then declining. CH4
and C2H4 start to increase in efﬁciency around -0.9 V and C2H4 peaks around -1.05 V, whereas
CH4 continues to increase and maybe reach a maximum at the most negative potential of -1.18
V. The hydrogen efﬁciency is seen to reduce from around 80 % at -0.7 V to around 20 % at -1.10
V and back up to 50 % at -1.18 V. Thus there seems to be an optimum potential for reducing
the fraction of H2 in the products.
The right hand side of ﬁgure 2.3 is a Tafel plot as the current axis is logarithmic and will thus
give a better mechanistic insight than the Faradaic efﬁciencies. Looking at the partial currents
we see that CO and formate rise slightly but are fairly steady until they drop slightly around
-1.0 V. CH4 rises steadily with increasing overpotential until -1.0 V, where a change in the slope
occurs, indicating a change in reaction mechanism. H2 increases only slightly until -1.0 V
and then follows the CH4 curve. It is interesting to note that a change occurs for all these C1
products around -1.0 V, which supports the idea that all the reactions are interconnected.
Looking at the C2 and C3 products we see that they all track ethylene indicating that they
are formed from ethylene or the same intermediate as ethylene. It makes sense that CO and
formate are produced at lower overpotentials as they require only a two-electron transfer and
indicates that they have low activation barriers. Further reduction requires more activation and
thus a higher overpotential.

2.2.3
Effect of reaction conditions and surface structure
A study of the effect of CO2 pressure, current density and CO2 ﬂux on a Cu electrode in
0.1 M KHCO3 was performed by Hara and coworkers [13]. They showed that increasing the
ﬂux of CO2 to the electrode surface, either by increasing CO2 pressure or by stirring, resulted
in changes in the main products as H2 →hydrocarbons →CO and/or HCOOH. Increasing
the current density resulted in CO and/or HCOOH →hydrocarbons →H2. From this they
concluded that the products were determined by the balance between the CO2 ﬂux to the
electrode and the current density. Low current density to CO2 ﬂux ratios gave mainly CO and
HCOOH, comparable current density to CO2 ﬂux ratios gave hydrocarbons and high ratios
resulted in H2 formation.
Some success has been seen for reducing CO on oxide-derived copper (OD Cu), where
polycrystalline Cu is ﬁrst oxidised to Cu2O and then reduced back to Cu either in situ or by
hydrogen [14, 15]. Adsorbed CO is generally agreed upon as an intermediate in CO2 reduction

and simpliﬁed investigations therefore often use CO as the reactant rather than CO2. With
OD Cu there was a high efﬁciency for ethanol and acetate (57 % Faradaic combined) whereas
normal polycrystalline Cu gives mainly gasseous hydrocarbons (in aqueous KHCO3). The
required overpotentials were also much less than for polycrystalline Cu (lower by more than
0.6 V). The high efﬁciency was attributed to active sites at the grain boundaries rather than
morphology or nanocrystallite size. It was observed that annealing of the OD Cu resulted
in a loss of the gained activity as the surface attained a higher equilibrium state with less
grain boundaries [15]. The optimal overpotential conditions (-0.3 V vs RHE) with respect to

active catalyst surface may be made but is not necessarily stable and particularly not at higher
temperatures where the surface can restructure.
2.2.4
Reaction mechanism on Cu using DFT
The exact reduction reaction mechanism is very difﬁcult to determine due to the many inter-
mediate steps and multiple possible pathways, all depending on the reaction conditions. To
help with this problem much research has been done using computational quantum chemistry
calculations, mainly density functional theory (DFT), on the reaction intermediates on the
various crystal facets of relevant electrode metals. This can give the binding energy/stability of
the intermediates on a particular site, the potential dependent reaction free energies of each
step and also the activation barrier of the transition states. Thus both the thermodynamics
and the kinetics of a reaction can be estimated giving both the onset potentials of the reaction
and the ratio between different reactions [16–22]. This can then be coupled to experiments on
single crystals [23].
There is however much disagreement on the reaction path even using DFT as different assumed
conditions and choice of exact method for the calculation will give different results. Particularly
it is worth noticing that the studies using not only thermodynamics but also activation energies
of the transition states get different preferred pathways due to kinetic considerations. Also
including solvation effects (aqueous solution) have a large impact.

2.2.6
Economics and key metrics
Durst et al. compared estimated production prices of various products from CO2 electrolysis
with the corresponding current market price for the same product by other routes [6]. Based
purely on price they concluded that CO and formate production were the only meaningful
processes with CO being by far the most used product.
From a renewable energy stand point the current sources of the other products must also be
taken into account as they are mainly coal and gas based.
Key metrics for a CO2 electrolysis process were listed in the review by Jhong et al.
as:
energy efﬁciency, current density, faradaic efﬁciency, catalyst stability, and process cost [2].
Energy efﬁciency is the amount of spent energy going towards the desired product(s). The
current density is the current per geometric area of the cathode and gives a measure of how
big a cell needs to be for a certain demand. The faradaic efﬁciency or current efﬁciency for
a speciﬁc product is the amount of current out of the total current that went to that product
and so is a measure of selectivity. The total current being used for each product is simply the
faradaic efﬁciency times the total current. The process cost includes the electricity, capital cost
and reactant cost.
It was stated that a main problem of CO2 electrolysis is that some products can be formed with
high Faradaic efﬁciency (formate) but at very low current densities and other products can be
formed at high current densities but with low Faradaic efﬁciency. A few cases show reasonable
Faradaic efﬁciencies and current densities but very low energetic efﬁciencies (high overpotential
required). This is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.5, where Faradaic and energetic efﬁciencies are plotted

2.2.7
Conclusion
The main problems with co-electrolysis on Cu is the high overpotential required as well as the
low selectivity towards any single compound. To make the concept of electrochemical CO2
reduction commercially feasible a catalyst that is selective for one high-energy or high-value
product at lower overpotentials must be discovered. Reaction conditions are seen to have a
large inﬂuence on the products. The higher temperature range, higher pressures and different
electrolyte used in this project are expected to also result in very different product distributions.

frequency ranges. When a sinusoidal potential signal is applied to a system it will result in a
sinusoidal current response. The impedance Z is deﬁned as the ratio of these two signals and

Problems with cell and system design and consequent development
Developing a system that could function at both high temperature and pressure proved a
challenge. Below is given the development of version 1 and 2 of the high pressure cell as seen
in ﬁgure 4.5, followed a section on the back pressure valves.
High pressure cell version 1
The cell is seen in ﬁgure 4.5 (top). When assembled it consists of two halves; the anode and the
cathode side. These function as current collectors (electrode arms placed under gas outlets) and
contain the gas ﬂow channels and will be referred to as the cell plates. Both cell plates were
coated with tantalum for high corrosion resistance (performed by Tantaline). The pattern of
the gas ﬂow channels is lowered 5 mm from the plane, allowing for insertion of the respective
electrode (the low pressure cell does not have this depression). The depth of the pattern is also
5 mm. A 1.00 mm thick stainless steel disc with a 20.00 mm Ø central hole is used to place the
electrolyte tablet in and will be referred to as the electrolyte disc. On each side of the cell is an
end plate with holes for six screws placed around the actual cell. The end plates, the cell plates
and the electrolyte disc are all equipped with a hole on each side of the center allowing for the
use of guide rods when assembling the cell.
Between the end plates and the cell plates are 3 layers of 0.075 mm kapton for electrical
insulation. There is also at least one layer of gasket material between the cell halves and the
electrolyte disc. The amount of layers was adjusted to ﬁt the thickness of the electrodes and
electrolyte. The regular gasket materials such as viton and even teﬂon are not stable at 300 ◦C.
Like many tested gasket materials Kapton was not completely tight at high temperature but
was very convenient otherwise as it did not melt and was dimensionally stable.
The cell plates were hard to get completely ﬂat due to the machining technique used (milling
was used to get the electrode arms as part of the design). The high temperature meant that
teﬂon tape could not be used to make the gas entrace and exits tight. Silver brazing was
ﬁrst attempted but turned out not to adhere on the tantalum coating. Finally the tantalum
coating was removed from the entrance and exit areas and the ﬁttings were welded on. This
introduced new problems, as the high temperatures required for welding resulted in both a
stronger oxidation of the tantalum layer (turned from dark grey to white and non-conducting)
and made the cell plates warped, making the cell practically impossible to get tight. The cell
plates were made plane again by milling and subsequent polishing to a mirror ﬁnish. This
meant that the tantalum coating was only present in the actual ﬂow pattern leaving the internal
faces of the cell plates vulnerable to corrosion.
High pressure cell version 2
In order to improve the cell a new design was made (see ﬁgure 4.5, bottom). Firstly the cell
plates were made without the electrode arms as part of the design, allowing the use of a solid
circular steel rod and therefore also turning with a lathe instead of milling. This should result
in much planer plates. Secondly the gas connections and electrode arms were welded on before
27
==PAGE:36==
Intermediate Temperature Co-Electrolysis of Water and Carbon Dioxide
the tantalum coating. The position of the electrode arms on top of the cell rather than under
the gas exits also allowed for easier assembly of the cell and attachment of wires.
The new cell also featured a groove at the perimeter of the anodic cell plate allowing for the
use of an O-ring. This O-ring was made from Kalrez to withstand the extreme temperature
requirement.
Furthermore the holes for the guide rods were modiﬁed to not penetrate the cell plates as
without this modiﬁcation an O-ring would be pointless. When the O-ring was tested it was
discovered that the grove was not appropriate for the O-ring and that the O-ring was destroyed
during operation. Thus the O-ring conﬁguration was not used for any experiments.
Back pressure valves
The setup had to be ﬁnely tuned in order to function. A constant ﬂow of steam had to pass the
electrolyte as it would otherwise dry out (transformation to pyrophosphate). This required
constantly letting out a little bit of steam on the anode side. The steam passing the O-ring
in the back pressure valve transfers a high amount of heat. As the O-ring is not stable at the
steam temperature, cooling was employed using a cooler that looped water through in-house
made alumina blocks attached to the back pressure valves. Allowing the steam to exit too
quickly, however, still resulted in mechanical failure of the O-ring and consequently in blowout.
It was decided to exchange the internal components of the back pressure valves (Swagelok
SS-3K-RL-IV) with Kalrez for higher temperature stability. These O-rings were not delivered in
time. Finally a different kind of back pressure valve (Tescom, 44-1700 series) was taken into
use and these could better stand high temperature and liquid.

Assemby and disassembly of the setup between experiments
Generally a cell would be assembled ﬁrst starting from the anode cell plate placed on top of the
anode side end plate (connected via guide rods in the case of version 1 and with short ceramic
rods in the case of version 2) with kapton between. On the anode cell plate the electrode
and surrounding gasket would be placed and then the electrolyte disc and electrolyte pellet,
followed by electrode and gasket and ﬁnally the cathode side cell plate and end plate. The nuts
and bolts were tightened with increasing moments in a star pattern usually to around 3 Nm
for each bolt.
The cell would then be attached to the inlet pipes of the system and the outlet pipes attached
to the cell. Disassembly after letting the system cool down occurred in the reverse order. After
a run the used electrolytes and electrodes would usually be saved for possible later analysis.
The tank was emptied and reﬁlled between each run. First the plug at the bottom of the tank
would be unscrewed and a rubber hose was attached that led to a glass container. When
unscrewing the ﬁtting at the top of the tank (the one with the thermocouple threaded through
it) the vacuum holding the water in place would be released and the water would ﬂow to the
container. The bottom plug could then be screwed on again and the tank ﬁlled from the top
with demineralised water from a water bottle (usually 400 mL). Finally the top plug would be
screwed on again.
Heating and cooling of the system
In order to avoid ﬂooding of the cell by condensation of steam, the cell is kept at a higher
temperature than the rest of the system (also the case for the low pressure setup). At the
ﬁnal operating conditions the vapour pressure from the boiler should correspond to or exceed
the vapour pressure of the KH2PO4 in the electrolyte, so as not to allow dehydration to the
less conductive pyrophosphate form. In order to avoid too much mechanical strain on the
electrolyte, a pressure difference between the anode and cathode side had to be avoided. In
the start up phase this was done by letting steam in on both sides. As the steam was from the
same boiler this ensured equal pressure on both sides. The exact procedure depended on the
required temperature of the cell and the boiler pressure and whether or not CO2 was used.
Below is given an example of a heating and cooling procedure of the system (in this case with
cooling of back pressure valves.
Heating:
1) start cooling of back-pressure valves and open H2O valve
2) cell, boiler, pipes in, pipes out = 150, 180, 100, 100 ◦C (until boiler is 150 ◦C)
3) cell = 200 ◦C (until boiler is 180 ◦C)
4) cell = ﬁnal temp (250 to 320 ◦C)
5) boiler to temperature corresponding to ﬁnal pressure (example: 184 ◦C ≈11 bar tot)
6) close H2O valve and open CO2 valve also at ﬁnal pressure
Cooling:

4.2.3
Risø setup
Due to time limitations it was decided to do measurements on a setup at Risø. The setup was
designed for measuring button-type cells at different pressures and temperatures. The cell
was contained in an autoclave, where the atmosphere and temperature could be controlled.
Steam was supplied by mixing hydrogen and oxygen in catalytic burner also placed in the
autoclave. A speciﬁc gas could be supplied to the working electrode and a capillary tube would
then suck gas from the working electrode gas exit channel. This gas was allowed to cool (1 m
capillary tube exposed to ambient temperature) to condense water before reaching a liquid gas
separator. The dry gas was then let through a pressure reduction valve, into a ﬂow controller
and into a gas chromatograph. The ﬂows of gas to the autoclave were controlled using mass
ﬂow controllers. The total pressure was controlled by a pressure valve on the autoclave.
The cell holder could hold 4 button-cells at the same time. Each button cell sat in a de-
pression (Ø 12 mm) with two holes, the gas inlet and outlet for the working electrode. The
holder was made from Inconel as the setup was designed for alkaline experiments. To protect
it from the acidic conditions of the KH2PO4, a Ø 12 mm expanded Teﬂon disc was placed in
the depression. The disc was equipped with holes for the working electrode gas. A wire for
current collection was threaded through the working electrode gas outlet and part way in to
the working electrode gas inlet. On top of this was placed a ring of expanded Teﬂon with a
6 mm inner Ø. In the hole of this ring 4 pieces of 1 mm thick silver foam discs were placed
in a stack and compressed slightly, so the top disc was slightly higher than the surrounding
Teﬂon ring. On top of this was placed a 10 mm Ø and 2 mm thick electrolyte pellet. On top of
the pellet was placed an anode consisting of Pt sprayed on carbon cloth, and on top of this a
Pt mesh current collector. The anode and pellet sides were exposed to the atmosphere in the
autoclave, whereas the cathode could be exposed mainly to a gas of choice (in this case CO2).

Fabrication and investigation of electrolytes
This section describes the synthesis of SnP2O7, the production of electrolyte pellets and how
the electrolytes were investigated by XRD and particle size distribution analysis.
4.3.1
Synthesis of SnP2O7
SnP2O7 was synthesised from SnO2 (Inframat Advanced Materials, 80 nm) and 85 % H3PO4
(Sigma Aldrich) with a molar P/Sn ratio of 2.8. An example of the procedure is given here:
10 g SnO2 are weighed in a 40 mL beaker. 21.43 g 85 % H3PO4 is added. 25 mL demineralised
water is added. The suspension is stirred with a disposable pipette and ultrasonicated twice
for 7 min with intermittent stirring
The mixture is heated at 95 ◦C for 90 min with a stirring rod. The temperature is raised to
270 ◦C (reached after approximately 25 min). The mixture is removed when the liquid has
evaporated and a thick clinging paste is obtained (approximately after an additional 25 min).
The mixture is scraped into an alumina crucible and heated in a furnace to 650 ◦C (ramp for 3
1
2 hr and dwell for 3 hr) and left to cool to room temperature.
In order to seperate the conductivity of SnP2O7 from KH2PO4 some of the amorphous phase
of SnP2O7 is removed by washing the product in demineralised water (dispersion of sample in
20 ml of water and centrifugation for 8 minutes at 9000 rpm followed by removal of water with
pipette, repeated three times). The acidity of the washing water was tested with pH paper.
Two batches of SnP2O7 were produced.
The ﬁrst batch experienced a malfunction with
the furnace (the temperature was still 650 ◦C the morning after when left overnight). The above
synthesis description is for the second batch. Particle size distribution analysis was performed
for both batches (see section 5.2).
4.3.2
Production of electrolyte pellets
Pellets were produced by mixing different ratios of KH2PO4 (Sigma Aldrich, Japan, CAS
#7778-77-0) and the synthesised SnP2O7 and pressing them uniaxially in a hydraulic press.
Initially, the main problems were cracking, uneven surface, unsufﬁcient cling, and holes. It
was found that cracking was a result of too high pressure when pressed (elastic vs inelastic
deformation). The 20 mm diameter pellets were pressed with 10 tonnes initially, which was
then reduced to 6 tonnes. It was difﬁcult to distribute the powder evenly in the press. Uneven
distribution meant different densities in the pellet, making it unstable and not smooth when
pressed. To avoid this a lubricant was used. At ﬁrst water was tried. This resulted in tablets
with better cling, but the water did not distribute evenly when pressed, resulting in pellets
with holes in the middle. The best results were obtained with ethanol as a lubricant and a

Electrochemical deposition of IrO2
IrO2 was electrochemically deposited. The procedure was inspired by the article by Lee et al.
[43], where IrO2 was electrodeposited on carbon paper. The deposition is thought to proceed
as follows:
[Ir(COO)2(OH)4]2−−−→IrO2 + 2CO2 + 2H2O + 2e−
(4.2)
The procedure used is given here:
A 50 mL 0.1 M [Ir(COO)2(OH)4]2– solution was prepared by dissolving 2.395 g of dihy-
drogen hexachloroiridate tetrahydrate (H2IrCl6 · 4 H2O, M = 479.01 g/mol) (Alfa Aesar, Ger-
many) in demineralised water and stirring magnetically for 30 min. 0.250 g of oxalic acid
((COOH)2 · 2 H2O, M = 126.07 g/mol) (Svetle) and 0.583 g of 30 % peroxide (H2O2, M=108.68)
(PerdrogenTM, Sigma Aldrich, Germany) were added and stirred for 10 min. The solution pH
was adjusted to ≈10.5 by addition of anhydrous potassium carbonate (K2CO3, Sigma Aldrich,
Japan) using indicator paper. The solution was allowed to stabilise while stirring for more than
3 days.
Electrodeposition was performed using a 3 electrode setup. It consisted of a substrate electrode
placed on a Ag spiral current collector as working electrode, a Ni foam counter, and a saturated
calomel electrode as reference (see ﬁgure 4.8).
The electrodeposition was performed at 0.7 V vs SCE for 5 min for good results as described in

The electrodeposition method turned out not to be reproducible as the deposition was not

much darker than electrodes with a higher weight gain. This could possibly be explained by
differences in contact between the current collector spiral and the substrate electrode.
According to Lee et al. [43] the faradaic efﬁciency was 15.6 % for the IrO2 deposition with the
rest of the current being due to oxygen evolution on the newly formed IrO2 layer/particles
(1.162 mg C−1 for pure IrO2 formation vs. 0.181 mg C−1 measured). By using this efﬁciency,

ﬁltered between depositions. 4 depositions of 5 minutes was estimated to result in a reduction
of molarity of the deposition solution from 0.1 M to 0.86 M. This change in molarity was
deemed to be acceptable.

bottom of a ﬂat-bottomed container. This would diminish the amount of IrO2 deposited on the
current collector and also focus the deposition on one side of the electrode. This side would
then be turned towards the electrolyte. This setup might also ensure better contact between
current collector and electrode.

5.3.3
Instability of electrodes and electrolyte
Electrodes
When the cell from the experiments of 24.02 was disassembled it was seen that the Cu cathode
had partly dissolved leaving ﬂecks of Cu in the electrolyte (see ﬁgure 5.7 left). The Inconel
on the anode side was green at the edges and the electrolyte was colored green as well (see
ﬁgure 5.7 right). This indicates serious instability.
The instability of Cu was conﬁrmed by testing it in pure molten KH2PO4 with a similar
procedure as in [44, 45].
The instability of Inconel was surprising as it had previously been identiﬁed as a promising
electrocatalyst for the oxygen evolution reaction in molten KH2PO4 [45]. The experiments were
however conducted under atmospheric conditions with only a condenser to reﬂux evaporated
water from the melt. The higher pressure conditions of the electrolysis experiments conducted
here could be more corrosive, or possibly the SnP2O7 is to blame.
The instability of Cu is a serious problem if the electrolyte is to be used for co-electrolysis
of CO2 and H2O, as Cu is the most promising pure metal electrocatalyst. Possibly other
Cu-containing catalyst are stable or other more stable catalyst will work in the intermediate
temperature range.
Electrolyte
Looking at the electrolyte itself it is also clear that there is a problem of cavities forming. The
cavities could be formed by bubbles of steam forming inside the pellet if some phosphate
is converted to pyrophosphate and releases water. Alternatively they form as ﬂowing steam
dissolves KH2PO4. It should be noted that the cavities also appear outside the area of the
ﬂow pattern in areas that were covered by gaskets. Similar cavities were observed in other
electrolytes of Lyngby experiments. Cavity formation was not observed in the Risø electrolyte
but here both setup and conditions were different. Formation of cavities in the electrolyte is a
major issue that must be solved.

5.6
Discussion
This section is an overall discussion of the results of the project and outlines the main challenges
and successes and suggests possible further work.
System design
The design of a pressurised sytem was only partly successful as no gasket material could be
found that was truly tight at high operating temperatures. A gasket material consisting of PBI
with closed pores was considered and may be tested in future experiments at the department.
Also an autoclave system similar to that at Risø is under construction at the time of writing.
This should ensure less leakage of gas as there will not be a pressure gradient across the cell.
To further improve the current system, steam should be supplied continuously and not as a
batch. This could be done by pumping water into the boiler using for instance an HPLC pump.
Also the system should be integrated with an online gas sampling system.
In later stages of the project it will be necessary to up-scale the cell to resemble commercial
size.
Electrolyte
The chosen conﬁguration of a KH2PO4/SnP2O7 composite electrolyte was seen not to work as
intended, as the components reacted at the imposed reaction conditions. Possibly the resultant
electrolyte has good properties. It is also possible that the composite does not require as high
surrounding vapour pressure as the pure electrolyte to maintain its conductivity. Alternatively
other ways of immobilising KH2PO4 should be considered.
It was also observed that cavities had a tendency to form in the electrolyte which would be
unacceptable in the ﬁnal design.
Electrodes
As EIS was unsuccessful it was not possible to separate the electrolyte and electrode resistances
64
==PAGE:73==
Intermediate Temperature Co-Electrolysis of Water and Carbon Dioxide
and therefore also hard compare electrodes. The lack of data on products also hinders an
evaluation of the activity towards CO2 reduction for the cathodes (Ni, Cu and Ag). The
instability of Cu and Inconel poses a problem and questions the corrosive conditions of the
electrolyte composite and particularly pressurised KH2PO4. Possibly an alternative electrolyte
must be selected. The low selectivity of Cu described in literature poses a problem in regard
to a feasible co-electrolysis setup. A more efﬁcient and highly selective catalyst, that is stable
under the reaction conditions, is required. The intermediate temperatures used should improve
the activity but not necessarily the selectivity and are most likely detrimental to the stability of
a catalyst material.
Electrolysis experiments
Due to the very high performance seen in the Risø experiment it is worth investigating if
the reduction in performance seen there was due to electrode degradation or the change in
the electrolyte. This and other investigations of the system would be aided by successful
EIS measurements. The reason for the very low success rate with EIS in this project is so far
unknown. Further investigations of particularly the performance and stability of the sprayed
IrO2 anodes in the system are being conducted by Thierry Chen.
Project structure
All the experimental work performed here was limited by the available time for the project and
also by the relatively wide scope of the project, meaning that no one part of the project was
studied in great depth. Choosing one smaller part of the project to focus on would however be
difﬁcult and to some degree pointless as all the parts are interdependent. The performance of
electrodes could not be tested without the electrolyte and neither electrodes or electrolyte could
be tested at the correct conditions without the experimental setup. All three parts - electrolyte,
electrodes and setup - should work together in the ﬁnal system design. The results obtained in
this project show that getting these 3 parts to work together is not easy and will most likely
not work in the ﬁrst attempt. The challenges of this project should guide the way for further
investigation.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
A pressurised intermediate temperature setup for co-electrolysis of CO2 and H2O using a
KH2PO4/SnP2O7 composite electrolyte was designed and constructed. It was tested with dif-
ferent electrode materials and at different temperatures and pressures for both co-electrolysis
and pure water electrolysis.
It was discovered that the SnP2O7 matrix was not stable under the high water pressure
reaction conditions, and a conversion to a mixed tin and potassium phosphate was proposed.
Cavity formation in the electrolyte was also observed.
Cu and Inconel 625 electrodes, which were believed to be promising electrocatalysts for
CO2 reduction and the oxygen evolution reaction respectiviely, were found not to be stable
with the electrolyte composite at the reaction conditions.
Measurement of gasseous products was not successfull and thus evaluation of the efﬁciency of
the cathode materials towards CO2 reduction was not possible.
Current densities as high as 2000 mA/cm2 were obtained with 2.5 V cell potential at 250
◦C, 6 bar partial H2O pressure and 20 bar total pressure, using a Ag foam cathode and Pt/C
anode. Cell performance was however seen to degrade drastically under the applied conditions.
Further investigation of this initial high performance is warranted.
