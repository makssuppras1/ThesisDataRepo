1 
Abstract 
A Life Cycle Assessment of the management of 1 tonne of household waste was conducted in 
accordance to the ISO 14044:2006 for 7 arbitrary selected European countries: namely Germany 
(DE), Denmark (DK), Greece (EL), France (FR), Italy (IT), Poland (PL) and United Kingdom (UK). 
The purpose of this study was to compare different environmental impacts of household solid waste 
management systems as well as to highlight the environmental “hotspots” and the main differences 
in the waste management systems in each country. Furthermore, the European waste hierarchy was 
analysed in order to assess whether it is always a reliable indicator of the environmental performance 
of a system. The following impact categories were considered: Climate Change, Freshwater 
Eutrophication, Marine Eutrophication, Terrestrial Eutrophication and Terrestrial Acidification, Human 
Toxicity, carcinogenic, Human Toxicity, non-carcinogenic, and Particular matter, Depletion of abiotic 
resources, fossil and Depletion of Abiotic Resources, elements (reserve base). Each country was 
characterized by its own waste composition, household sorting efficiency, collection, waste 
treatments, electricity and heat composition and technological efficiencies. Recycling processes and 
transport distances were modelled in the same way for all the countries due to the uncertainty about 
where and how the collected materials are actually recycled.  
The results showed that the processes can cause both environmental savings and environmental 
loads. The order of magnitude is comparable in all the impact categories studied and the countries 
have a similar trend in all the categories. Whenever recycling takes place it leads to significant 
environmental savings. Metals recycling in WtE and MBT plants lead to equal or greater savings than 
the actual energy recovery. The exception is Denmark which has the largest environmental savings 
in almost all the impact categories due to heat and energy crediting. Heat recovery has a greater 
importance than electricity recovery because of the higher percentage of fossil fuels in the heat 
composition modelled. Landfilling causes significant environmental loads due to methane emissions 
to air and heavy metals to water. 
Weighting the normalised values with a factor equal to 1, the results of all the impact categories 
were aggregated into 1 score. Considering the unique factor, three groups of countries can be 
identified. Germany and Denmark have a high quality waste management from an environmental 
point of view (high recycling, high incineration and negligible waste to landfill); France, Italy and UK 
have an average quality management (medium recycling and different technologies to divert waste 
from landfilling). Poland and Greece appeared to have the worst system and almost all the waste is 
landfilled. Detailed ranking within each group of countries is not recommended, in particularly due 
to the uncertainty of the data.  
The environmental impacts of each country does not show completely that respecting the waste 
hierarchy leads always to lower environmental impacts. The EU waste hierarchy appears to be a 
simplified tool which do not consider the differences between countries. In general, landfilling should 
be avoided, but in case of low technological level and relative clean energy substituted, waste 
recycling has a better environmental performance than WtE. In case of very high technological 
efficiency, energy recovery can lead to greater savings than recycling.

1 
Introduction  
In 2012, the total waste generated in the EU countries by all economic activities and households 
amounted to 2 515 million tonnes (European Commission 2015b). Even if municipal waste represents 
only 10% of the total waste generated in Europe, it has a strong political value due to its direct link 
to the behaviour of citizens (European Commission 2015a). The EU Waste Framework Directive 
(2008/98/EC) highlights the waste hierarchy as general principle for a sound waste management: it 
should start with waste prevention, preparing firstly for reuse, and then for recycling, recovery and 
disposal (European Commission 2014). However, due to the complexity of the systems, Life Cycle 
Thinking (LCT) can be used together with the waste hierarchy to identify the best environmental 
strategy (European Commission 2011). Several studies have already analysed Life Cycle 
Assessments (LCA) of municipal solid waste management (MSWM) systems to quantify the 
environmental loads and savings (Arena et al. 2003; Eriksson et al. 2005; L. Rigamonti et al. 2009). 
In general, they conclude that reducing landfilling of waste in favour of energy and material recovery 
leads to lower environmental impacts (L. Rigamonti et al. 2009). The main limitations of using the 
results of previous LCA at a country level is that they usually investigate micro-scale systems, as 
regions or cities. The few studies that compared European MSWM systems analysed only the 
environmental impacts of greenhouse gases emissions only on Climate Change (Gentil et al. 2009; 
Smith et al. 2001).    
This study aims to compare the household waste management of 7 European countries in order to 
analyse how results are affected by the national differences, and which are the national differences 
which affect the results most. This comparison would be useful to see whether the waste hierarchy 
is a sufficient tool to minimize environmental impacts in all the impact categories. Household waste 
is chosen instead of municipal waste because data are easier to compare.   
 
2 
Methods and Materials 
2.1 
The LCA approach  
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) can be defined as “a technique to evaluate the environmental aspects 
and potential impacts associated with a product, process, or service, by compiling an inventory of 
relevant energy and material inputs and environmental releases, evaluating the potential 
environmental impacts associated with identified inputs and release and interpreting the results to 
help you make a more informed decision” (EPA n.d.). In order to conduct a consistent, robust and 
quality assured life cycle data and study, The International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) 
provides a common guidance conforming to the 14044:2006 standards. Based on the ILCD, LCA is 
divided in 4 sections: 1) Goal and Scope, 2) Life Cycle inventory (LCI), 3) Life Cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) and 4) Life Cycle Interpretation. The LCI is the data collection portion of the LCA, 
where the inputs and outputs of entire systems are introduced. The LCIA presents characterized, 
normalized and weighted results. Finally, the Interpretation includes the identification of significant 
issues (e.g. most relevant processes and impact categories), their influence on the results (through 
sensitivity, completeness and uncertainty check) and the conclusions that can be extracted by the 
overall study (European Commission 2010). 
Together with many positive aspects of the LCA approach, some of its limits need to be highlighted. 
LCA does not cover issues of public health, hygiene and site safety. It also excludes pathogenic and 
eco-toxicological factors, odours, visual pollution, risk of accidents in the transportation etc. (Arena 
et al. 2003). Finally, it generates outputs only related to environmental impacts, thus decision 
makers should assess its results in the light of economical and social analysis.  
2.2 
Goal  
The intended application of this study is to compare different environmental impacts of household 
solid waste management systems and to highlight the environmental “hotspots” in selected seven

European countries, including which are the main differences in the waste management systems in 
these countries.  The countries studied, namely Denmark, France, Germany, Italy, Poland and UK 
are meant to represent the whole EU. An indirect output of the study is an analysis on how much 
the results are affected by the national context. Finally the results from different impact categories 
are aggregated into one value assuming a weighting equal to 1 in order to compare the outputs from 
this paper with the ranking based on the quantity of waste landfilled, recycled and recovered made 
by the EC (2015). 
The software used is EASETECH, a specialised LCA model developed by DTU (Clavreul et al. 2014). 
The target audience of the deliverables (that in this case coincides with the commissioner of the 
study) is the department on Environmental Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark which 
has a long history of LCA in the waste sector. Regarding the impacts coverage, the results are limited 
to Global Warming, Freshwater Eutrophication, Marine Eutrophication, Terrestrial Eutrophication, 
Terrestrial Acidification, Human Toxicity carcinogenic, Human Toxicity non-carcinogenic, Eco-
Toxicity, Particular Matter, Depletion of abiotic resources (fossil), Depletion of abiotic resources 
(reserve base). The limitations of the model include the lack of impurities in the source-separated 
fractions (e.g. in the glass fraction, there is only glass), the lack of modelling of waste prevention 
due to its high uncertainty, and the lack of the hazardous and Waste Electrical and Electronic 
Equipment (WEEE) handling. Finally, the main issue of the model is represented by the limited and 
uncertain data available for each country. 
According to the ILCD, the decision-context is essential to determine the scope and the type of LCI 
model. There are four different decision-context situations: A, B, C1 and C2. Situations A or “Micro-
level decision support” and B or “Meso/Macro-level decision support” include the LCAs based on 
which decisions are taken in order to improve environmental impacts of the studied product or 
service. If these decisions have any large-scale consequences on any process of the background 
system, this LCA will be categorize as Situation B, otherwise it will be categorize as Situation A 
(European Commission n.d.). Due to the complexity of the systems and to a potential use of the 
recommendations, this model falls into situation B.  
2.3 
Scope  
The Functional Unit is the management of 1 tonne of household waste in seven European countries. 
To simplify the comparison among the countries, garden waste, hazardous waste and WEEE are 
excluded from the composition together with the treatment of collected wood, textiles and batteries. 
The composition of the waste explored can be seen in Figure 2. The types of treatment included are 
Waste-to-Energy (WtE) and Mechanical Biological Treatment (MBT) plants, landfills, aerobic and 
anaerobic digestion for source separated food waste and recycling of dry materials (glass, ferrous 
and non-ferrous metals, HDPE, PET, soft plastic, paper and cardboard). 
According to the recommendation given by ISO 14044:2006, system expansion is privileged over 
allocation to handle multi-functional processes. In agreement with the goal definition of the study 
and with ISO 14044:2006 recommendations for system in situation B, attributional modelling should 
be used with the exception of “processes that have been identified as being affected by big changes 
as consequence of the analysed decision shall be modelled as mix of the long-term marginal 
processes”(European Commission 2010). For this reason, electrical and thermal energy credited and 
utilized in the processes are modelled as a long-term marginal mix. Regarding the system expansion 
for crediting material recovery, substitution of virgin material has been modelled utilizing different 
substitution ratio (SR) for each fraction. The SRs modelled depend on both the recycling technology 
and the quality of the secondary product.

factors are calculated can be found in Damgaard (2014). Both non-toxic and toxic impact categories 
are included. Land and water use are excluded because they heavily depend on the geographical 
location and the results would have been affected by a great uncertainty.

The description of the system and the system boundaries are crucial to understand the choices and 
assumptions made throughout the study. The system boundaries of the model are shown in Figure 
1. The waste enters the system boundaries of the model after being produced by the households. 
The system includes waste collection, transport, waste treatments, utilization of compost, digestate 
and treatment of residues from Material Recovery Facility (MRF), WtE and MBT. It has to be noted 
that all the source-separated fractions do not include any impurities, and this explains the lack of 
residuals from composting and AD facilities. Dry recyclables are directed first to a MRF and then to 
recycling plants. Furthermore, the capital goods are included for the transport and for all waste 
treatment plants (landfill, MRF, recycling facilities, WtE, MBT, composting and AD). Capital goods 
are excluded for bins and collection because they are considered to be the same in all the countries; 
therefore they can be neglected during the comparison. The treatment of the wastewater and bottom 
ash from WtE plants is excluded, the former because it is assumed to be reutilised internally, and 
the latter because of minor importance (explained later).

2.4 
Inventory analysis  
The Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) includes data collection and calculation of inputs and outputs of the 
life cycle.  
A more detailed explanation of each process can be found in the Supporting Information (SI), 
including waste collection (SI 5), waste transportation (SI 10.3) and capital goods (SI 10.4) and It 
needs to be noted that capital goods are added for all the processes, except for bins and collection 
trucks because they are modelled similarly in all the countries and the impacts would have been the 
same.  
2.4.1 
Household composition and sorting efficiencies

of disaggregated inputs, but the data available from national reports and EUROSTAT are often 
aggregated and it is not possible to part them easily. Some of the main parameters that vary from 
country to country are:  quality and sources of data, methodology and robustness of compositional 
analysis and degree of inclusion of commercial waste (Gibbs et al. 2014). 
The simplified household waste composition and source-separating efficiency for 6 fractions

indicate the percentages collected as a separate collection in each country. A more detailed

material, soft plastic, plastic bottles, other plastic packaging, diapers and tampons, wood, textiles, 
clear glass, green glass, brown glass, non-ferrous metals, ferrous metals, ash, batteries, 
combustibles and non-combustibles.

2.4.2.1 
MRF 
All recyclables are collected in a Material Recovery Facility (MRF). MRFs are characterized by the 
consumption of electricity, wire mass and diesel as well as by the recovery efficiency. The 
consumption of electricity and materials depends on the collection schemes based on the data 
collected by Pressley et al. (2015). Pressley studied the recovery efficiencies depending on the 
collection schemes, but his results were considered too high compared to the other values found in 
literature(L. Rigamonti et al. 2009; Lucia Rigamonti et al. 2009; ADEME and Eco-Emballages et 
Ecofolio 2014). For this reason, average efficiencies are used for all the countries in this paper. All 
residues from MRFs are modelled to be disposed in an inert landfill. Even if in the reality some 
countries send the plastic and paper residues to WtE plants, the difference of impacts due to the two 
types of disposal is negligible.  
2.4.2.2 
Recycling processes 
Nowadays, the destination of dry recyclables depend on the market prize. For this reason, generic 
European recycling processes are modelled for all the countries.  
The recycling processes for metal, plastic and glass are modelled following the information collected 
by Lucia Rigamonti (2007). Paper and cardboard recycling are based on EASETECH and EcoInvent 
databases, respectively. Composite materials recycling is excluded due to the low amount in the 
waste and to the potential uncertainty of its modelling.  Energy consumption is modelled as an 
average of the European long-term marginal electricity/heat. Rigamonti studied the specific fossil 
fuel (e.g. methane, oil, etc.) used in each process, but it was specific only for Italian plants. Different 
studies have highlighted that modelling recycling processes is affected by a great uncertainty, 
because the impacts strongly depend on the assumptions (Merrild et al. 2008 and Brogaard et al. 
2014).  
The substitution ratio describes how much primary material is avoided by recycling; it is calculated 
as the product between the recovery efficiency and the market ratio (Table 3), for example 1 kg of 
Aluminium entering in the recycling plant substitutes only 0.93 kg of primary Al.  The recovery 
efficiency (or reprocessing efficiency) refers to the recovery activity in itself (e.g. efficiency of 
furnace), while the market ratio refers to the quality of the secondary material produced (Rigamonti 
et al. 2010).

2.4.2.3 
Waste-To-Energy plant 
The incineration facility is modelled based on the average Danish incinerator and the flue-gas 
cleaning system on Vestforbrænding in 2011 (EASETECH database). Transfer coefficients are used 
to trace pollutants. In different countries, the average emissions and the ancillary materials vary 
substantially. Unfortunately,  the different methodology used (types of emissions measured, daily 
average, yearly average, half-hour average, etc.) made it very difficult to compare them. All the 
facilities recover metals due to the high value of these material in the market (Andreasi Bassi 2015) 
and 50% of Aluminium scraps and 80% of ferrous scraps are sent to recycling. All fly ashes are sent 
to inert landfills because modelling the impacts derived from a different utilization is not robust 
enough and bottom ashes treatment is neglected since in the reutilization for road construction 
negative and positive impacts are of minor importance (Birgisdóttir et al. 2007).  This assumption 
was tested sending all the bottom ash to inert landfills and no significant change was observed in 
the overall results. Both the produced electricity and produced heat are assumed to substitute long-
term marginal electricity and heat (explained in chapter 2.4.3). The electrical and thermal 
efficiencies are shown in Table 4 (Andreasi Bassi 2015).

2.4.2.4 
Composting 
The majority of household source-separated food waste is usually composted in-vessel (ADEME 
2013; WRAP 2013). The process is modelled based on a datasets available in the EASETECH 
database; the dataset is built on data measured in a vessel composting facility in Treviso (Italy), 
where the degradation of volatile solids (VS) and carbon (C) were estimated to be 73.5% for kitchen 
waste and 54.6 % for garden waste, whereas 71% of the total N is degraded during the process.  
All the emissions are treated in a bio-filter which has a substantial abating effect on the two 
compounds. Before entering in the bio-filter, 2.2% and 83% of the degraded C and Nitrogen (N) are 
emitted as CH4 and NH3. The water content of the compost is set to 70.5 % on wet weight. Compost 
is modelled as being used in three different ways and three types of use of compost are modelled 
(see Appendixes): fertilizer substitution in agriculture, peat and fertilizer substitution in gardens, 
and soil amendment (e.g. used in landfill, for maintenance, for landscape, etc.). Since it is assumed 
that no impurities are sent to the plant, there is no output from the plant but compost. 
2.4.2.5 
Anaerobic Digestion  
Anaerobic Digestion is modelled based on the unit process inventory of an hypothetical “wet” plant 
treating source-separated organic household waste (Møller et al. 2011). The degradation of each 
fraction is built as percentage of VS content, about 70% VS degradation for organic waste; the 
methane content in the biogas is 63%; the engine efficiencies for gas reutilization is 39% and 46% 
for electrical and thermal energy, respectively and 2% of CH4 is emitted as gas leakage from the 
digester. Since no impurities are sent to the plant, there is no output from the plant but digestate. 
The digestate is then composted and applied to agricultural soil.

2.4.2.7 
MBT 
Due to the lack of information on the detailed functioning of the MBT plants in Europe, several 
simplifications are made. Only two types of MBT plants are modelled: Mechanical Biological Pre-
Treatment (MBP) and the Mechanical Biological Stabilization (MBS). MBP is constituted by a 
mechanical treatment followed by a biological treatment to stabilize the organic material and meet 
the requirement for an MBT landfill, while MBS is composed of a short biological treatment, to dry 
the waste, and a mechanical separation of the remaining waste. The mass balance and the energy 
and materials consumption is based on Erikssen and Damgaard (n.d.).  
2.4.3 
Energy  
The results of the LCA are usually highly dependent on the composition of electrical and thermal

Sensitivity analysis  
Sensitivity analysis is conducted to investigate sensitive inputs (Clavreul et al. 2012) and to analyses 
how much the assumptions made in the model inputs influence the results (Laurent et al. 2014). In 
this paper, the method described in Clavreul et al. (2012) is used and perturbation analysis is 
conducted based on the results of the contribution analysis. Due to the complexity of the system, 
the parameters that could be tested are countless. Therefore, the first step is to choose the 
parameters based on the processes that contributes the most to the results and on the uncertainty 
of the assumptions made. The list of parameters tested is shown in the Results chapter. Then, 
perturbation analysis and scenario analysis are conducted.  
The perturbation analysis calculates a sensitivity ratio (SR) that is necessary to observe the effect 
of a small variation of a parameter on the final results. SR is the ratio between the relative change 
of the result and the relative change of the parameter. The SR are calculated as the below equation 
and the simplification is possible since all the parameters were raised or lowered by 10%. In this 
paper, all the SRs are calculated as absolute value.

Two limitations of the use of SRs were observed during the study and they need to be kept in mind 
during the interpretation: 
 
The first limitation is observed when the overall results are close to zero (as in the case of 
France or Poland for GWP100. In this case, even changing small parameters that did seem 
not relevant from the contribution analysis, caused a noticeable change of the results. This 
is because the ratio between very small numbers, close to zero, could give an unrealistic 
high value. All the cases for which excessive SR were observed without any reason when 
the impacts were close to zero, are excluded from the discussion of the sensitivity analysis.  
 
Another limit of SR is that raising 10% a parameter depend on the initial value of the 
parameter. For example in the baseline, French landfills collect 70% of the gas produced in 
the first 45 years, while Greek landfills collect only 30%. Raising the gas collection efficiency 
of 10% in both the countries would end in having 77% gas collection in France and only 
33% in Greece. Obviously, the effect of the change in France will be much higher than in 
Greece on the overall results. 
Finally, the scenario analysis simply “consists in testing different options individually and observing 
the effect of these changes on the final results.” (Clavreul et al. 2012).

3.1 
Main outcome from the LCA model 
This chapter presents the general results of the LCA modelling. The detailed results are shown in the 
SI 4.1. It is important to highlight that the results of an LCA should be analysed as potential 
environmental impacts, more than prediction of the actual effects (European Commission 2010). As 
each impact category has its own unit, it is not possible to make any comparison to rank the impact 
categories on basis of the characterised results. Therefore, normalisation and weighting are 
necessary in order to compare different impact categories and different categories in the systems. 
A unitary weighting for all the impacts categories is always assumed in this paper.  
Figure 3, Figure 4 and Figure 5 show the normalised results for the baseline scenario where the 
countries are listed according to the amount of landfilling as a percent of total waste management 
as listed in European Commission (2014). The impact categories presented in this paper are divided 
in three groups: the first group includes the impacts categories commonly used in the LCA, Climate 
change, acidification and eutrophication; the second group includes Human (carcinogenic and non-
) and Eco-Toxicity and Particular Matter; the third group Depletion of abiotic resources (fossil and 
reserve base). 
Each colour represents a sum of processes and Table 8 explains how processes are grouped. To 
avoid confusion, the groups of processes are always mentioned with the inverted commas (“”). It 
has to be noted that Transport always includes capital goods of the trucks if not specified otherwise.

detailed data are available in SI 4.2).

 
“Collection” is negligible compared to the other processes only in FEP, HT-C and HT-NC. 
Impacts due to the collection of material overtake impacts due to the transport of waste 
from households to first treatments in GWP100 due to CO2 emissions, and in MEP, TEP, AP 
due to NOx emissions, while it is lower in the other impact categories. The main loads of 
transport can be seen in ET and ADP-E, both caused by the CG of the transportation trucks.  
 
Where recycling takes place, it mainly leads to savings (negative impacts). The only 
exception is constituted by HT-NC, while the greatest savings can be seen in ET and ADP-E. 
The impacts caused by MRF and inert landfilling of residues are negligible. Furthermore, the 
magnitude of savings depends on the materials and on the quantity recycled in each country 
(sees SI 4.2.2). In general, the material recycling which contribute the most to GWP100, MEP, 
TEP, AP are Paper and Aluminium as well as to FEP are Paper and Steel. The three toxic 
impact categories and the ADP-E are driven by Paper, Steel and Glass, while PM and ADP-F 
by Paper and Aluminium. Glass recycling represent a load in GWP100 (due to the emissions 
of CO2 from the production of thermal energy and from the process-specific emissions) and 
Steel recycling is a load in HT-NC (due to the emissions to air of Mercury, Cadmium and 
Zinc), respectively. 
 
Danish WtE plants have the greatest savings compared to the other systems in Europe due 
to their high electrical and thermal average efficiency and to the very developed district 
heating system. Danish WtE leads to the highest savings in GWP100, AP, ET, PM and ADP-F. 
In all the other countries, the environmental savings from heat and energy crediting are 
mainly balanced by plant emissions (see SI 4.2.3). Waste incineration is usually a saving, 
but in few cases it represents a load as in France and UK for GWP100, MEP and TEP: both 
countries have low thermal recovery efficiency and in the case of France the produced 
electricity substitutes a lot of nuclear energy, which is considered a “clean” source. Heat 
crediting is always more important than electrical crediting and in Denmark it contributes 
between 50% (in ADP-E) and 95% (in PM) of the total saving due to energy crediting. The 
importance of heat recovery compared to electricity recovery is based on higher fossil fuel 
percentage in the composition of heat respect to electricity used in the baseline (see Table 
6 and Table 7). 
 
Recycling of metals from WtE plants is equally or more important than energy recovery for 
many of the impacts in all the countries excluding Denmark. Transport of fly ashes and 
recyclables, CGs and inert landfills are negligible in all the impact categories. Sending bottom 
ash to inert landfills leads to negligible impacts as well. 
 
MBT is relevant to the overall scenario only related to the recycling of metals. MBT plant 
consumption and the incineration of RDF seem to be negligible. For this reason and because 
it is not the focus of this paper, the assumption of modelling the plant for RDF as WtE is 
considered acceptable.  
 
Bio-waste treatment via composting and AD has a small net impact. Analysing the single 
processes, the impacts are mainly caused by the use of compost and composted digestate 
on land. The major loads are in MEP (due to the emissions of nitrate to surface and ground 
water), HT-NC and ET (due to the emissions of heavy metals in soil) for both composting 
and AD and the only saving is in FEP due to the avoided Phosphorus.  
 
Landfill is central in GWP100 for Greece and Poland and in MEP and ET for all the countries 
that have landfilling of bio-waste (FR, UK, IT, PL and EL). GWP100 is due to the methane 
emissions, while MEP and ET are caused by the discharge of ammonium and zinc from the 
leachate treatment to the surface water, respectively. Carbon sequestration is a fundamental 
parameter in the GWP100 impact category because it balances the GHG emissions from the 
landfilling neutralizing them. In case of Greece, it has to be noted that the small negative 
impact for landfilling in FEP is due to the anaerobic digestion of primary and secondary 
sludge from landfills. This excessive environmental saving is due to the fact that “lignite” 
was found in Ecoinvent as source of coal in Greece for the electricity composition instead of 
hard coal as in the other countries. Due to lack of WtE plants, this distortion does not cause 
any other unexpected results.

A separate discussion is added for waste transport, including CG of the trucks, and CG for all the

the impacts excluding FEP, HT-C and HT-NC. In SI 4.3 waste transport is shown separated from the

materials are recovered after the decommissioning of the buildings in the processes based on

3.3 
Comparison with the Waste Hierarchy 
The Waste Hierarchy is a simple tool that has been used in the European legislation to drive the 
technologies that are considered the most environmental friendly. This paragraph compares the 
results in this model and the ranking made by the European Commission (2015) in which countries 
are ranked based on the quantity of waste landfilled, recycled and recovered. In order to combine 
multiple scores into one aggregated value, weighting needs to take place. In this paper, the weighing 
is assumed to be equal to 1 for simplicity’s sake. This strong assumption has to be clear in order not 
to give the wrong interpretation of the results. In particular this weighting does not include the 
different level of uncertainty that affect the normalisation factors. For example, the ILCD considers 
the method used for GWP100 much less uncertain than the method for human and eco-toxicity 
(Hauschild et al. 2013).  
Each country is given a score from 1 to 7 in each impact, with 1 indicating the best environmental 
performance. In case two countries have a difference of value lower than 10%, the countries are 
given the same value. The single values given for each impact are available in the SI 4.8. Table 10 
shows the comparison of the overall scoring of this paper and the EU hierarchy. The generic 
observation from both the results is the division of the 7 countries studied in three groups: the first 
group includes Germany and Denmark, the second France, UK and Italy and the third Poland and 
Greece. The ranking between the countries in the same group should not be considered strictly, but 
it should be interpreted  in light of the assumptions and the data modelled into the model. 
The savings in Germany are mainly due to the high recycling rate of all the materials, and to the 
negligible amount of bio-waste sent to landfills and treated in other plants, while Denmark combines 
high recycling and WtE of the residual waste. The great savings from Danish plants derive from the 
very high efficiency of the WtE plants and from the composition of heat modelled in this paper. 
In the middle are France, UK and Italy. They have an average recycling rate, they start collecting 
and treating bio-waste and they diverge a portion of the bio-waste in the residual from the landfill 
through the use of WtE and MBT plants.

Conclusion 
The environmental impacts of management of 1 tonne of household waste were calculated for 7 
European countries, namely Denmark, Germany, Greece, France, Italy, Poland and UK. The results 
show how materials recycling in general leads to the largest environmental savings, especially 
regarding metals, paper and glass. The impacts/savings from energy recovery in WtE plants strongly 
depend on the efficiency of the energy recovery and on the composition of the long-term marginal 
electricity and heat. Due to the very specific WtE plants, Denmark is the only country in which energy 
recovery (especially due to the very high thermal efficiency) leads to significant environmental 
savings in almost all the impact categories studied. In other countries that consider incineration or 
MBT as a treatment for the residual waste (all but EL and PL), metals recycling is equal or more 
important than energy crediting. Another consideration is a low value resulting from food waste 
treatment via composting and AD. In conclusion, landfilling has a very relevant impact on GWP100 
for Greece and Poland, and on MEP and ET for all the countries that have landfilling of residual waste.  
By conducting a sensitivity analysis, paper recycling appears to be the most sensitive parameter for 
the countries that have high paper recycling, especially regarding the modelling of the substituted 
material. Landfilling modelling is mainly affected by the choice of the recipient from leachate 
treatment (surface water or ocean). Food waste treatment modelling does not appear to affect the 
overall results.  
Changing the type of long-term marginal mix for the electricity used and credited mainly influences 
the results of countries utilising WtE and recycling. The country mostly affected by this change is 
Denmark, and the more the electricity moves to a clean type of energy, the lower is the 
environmental saving due to electricity crediting respect recycling.  
In order to compare the results of this paper to the ranking made by the European Commission of 
the European countries based on the waste recycled, recovered and landfilled, the impact categories 
are aggregated in only one score with a weighting equal to 1. The results show how in both cases, 
the countries are divided in three groups: Germany and Denmark have both high recycling and 
incineration rate and almost negligible landfilling; Italy, France and UK have an average recycling 
rate and use a mix of technologies to reduce the amount of food waste sent to landfills; at the very 
bottom there are Greece and Poland that use landfilling as main treatment. The ranking inside each 
group is not recommended due to the overall data uncertainty and assumptions in the modelling. 
Respecting the EU waste hierarchy does not alywas lead to better environmental performance. The 
only certain result is that diversion of bio-waste from landfilling leads in general to better 
environmental impacts. The choice between energy and material recovery should be made on a 
basis of the technologies present and material or energy substitued: in case of low technological 
level and relative clean energy substituted, recycling appears to have a better environmental impact 
than incineration.  
In order to apply the results of this study, further studies are suggested in order to collect 
comparable data especially regarding landfilling and recycling.

tonnes of batteries and 26 000 tonnes of wood packaging. Wood and batteries are subtracted 
because it would be incorrect to leave these quantities in the composition since they follow a different 
treatment than the residuals.

used for Italy was used as a proxy.

France 
The composition of French waste derives from a municipal waste characterization campaign

The only hazardous waste considered were batteries, and this as well as garden waste were

The assumptions made to model the recycling processes are explained and justified as following: 
- 
GLASS. Three different coloured glass from EcoInvent are modelled as substituted material 
for 1t of glass as input: “packaging glass production, brown”; “packaging glass production, 
green” and “packaging glass production, white”. The normalized results do not show any 
difference, thus, green glass is used in the model.  
- 
ALUMINIUM. Two different processes are modelled for 1t of Al cans input: one from 
Rigamonti, (2007) and one from EASETECH database. No differences are noted, thus, the 
recycling process from Rigamonti is used.     
- 
STEEL. Two different substituted materials from EcoInvent are tried for 1t of Aluminium cans 
as input “steel production, converter, unalloyed” and “steel production, converter, low-
alloyed” No relevant differences were noted, thus the low alloyed steel is used as primary 
avoided production.  
- 
CARDBOARD. Three different substituted materials from EcoInvent are tested for 1t of 
cardboard input: “Core board production”, “Corrugated board box production” and “lineboard 
production, kraftliner”. “Corrugated box production” is used for the baseline. Important 
differences based on the choice of these materials were found, but due to the low importance 
of cardboard recycling in the overall results, there is no need to carry a sensitivity analysis.    
- 
PAPER: Different substituted materials from EcoInvent database are tested in the process 
for 1t of paper input: “Graphic paper production, 100% recycled RER”, “kraft paper 
production, unbleached RER”, “paper production, newsprint, virgin RER” and “paper 
production, newsprint, recycled RER”. “paper production, newsprint, virgin” is used in the 
baseline because newsprint is the most common second material from paper recycling (CEPI 
- Confederation of European Paper Industries 2013). Due to the differences noted between 
substituting virgin and recycled newsprint, a sensitivity analysis is carried out.  
- 
PET/HDPE. Three different recycling processes are modelled for both PET and HDPE: the 
publications used are Rigamonti (2007), Rigamonti et al. (2014) and Franklin Associates 
(2011). Furthermore, two substituted materials from EcoInvent were utilized for PET 
recycling: "PET granulate amorphous" and "PET granulate bottles" from EcoInvent. In both 
the cases,  no significant difference in the normalised impacts was noted, the process from 
Rigamonti (2007) and “PET granulate amorphous” are chosen. 
- 
SOFT PLASTIC. The soft plastic need to be separated from the other plastic, to avoid 
problems in the recycling. In this paper, the recycling modelled from Rigamonti (2007) is 
used. Due to the lack of information regarding the composition of the soft plastic in the 
different countries, it is assumed that 1kg of soft plastic is equal to 1 kg of the mix used in 
Rigamonti’s paper. Different substituted products from EcoInvent are tested for 1t of soft 
plastic input (Table 53). The density of the material tried is set to 540 kg/m3. Since soft 
plastic recycling does not show significant impacts in any category, “Particle board 
production, for outdoor use” is used in the baseline without running a sensitivity analysis.

3.4.5.1 
Use of compost 
Three types of use of compost based on EASTECH database are modelled: 
- 
In agriculture: the process is based on EASETECH database for Use-On-Land in plant farming 
on loam soil, where the fate of carbon (C) and nitrogen (N) is simulated by Daisy, a 
deterministic agro-system model (Table 64). The rate of N substitution is based on Danish 
regulation that defines the amount of compost that can be applied on farmlands: 20%, 
100%, 100% are the substitution factors for N, K, P respectively based on the Danish 
farming practice (N needs to be mineralized before being absorbed by plants). The avoided 
application of heavy metals to the agricultural soil was estimated based on the heavy metals 
content of N, P, K fertilizers, while the heavy metals introduced with the compost are 
equivalent to the amount contained in the organic waste calculated by EASTECH. The process 
for compost application is built for Danish conditions, but since it was difficult to find such 
detailed analysis for other European countries, it is used in all Europe. Loamy soil is chosen 
for the baseline, but a scenario analysis is made in case the compost is applied on sandy 
soil.  
- 
In gardens: the compost is used in gardens and avoids utilization of chemical fertilizers and 
peat as soil amendment. The process is based on Boldrin et al. (2010). The amount of N, P, 
K is not calculated based on the waste composition, but according to the average content in 
green waste (1.76, 4.67, 5.86 g/kg ww input, for N, P, K respectively). The substitution ratio 
is further adjusted to keep into consideration people’s behaviour. In fact, Andersen et al. 
(2010) studied the individual behaviors of private citizens, showing that people would, to

4.6 
Scenario analysis 
In order to assess the impact of the modelling of certain processes on the overall results, several 
scenarios are run for all the main assumptions: 
 
Changing sandy soil instead of loam soil in  the Use-On-Land of compost and digestate; 
 
Sending all the compost produced to agriculture; 
 
Using recycled newsprint instead of virgin newsprint in the paper recycling; 
 
Using ocean as final recipient after the treatment of leachate from the landfill instead of 
surface water. 
Furthermore, all the bio-waste was modelled to be sent to AD plant in Italy and Germany in order 
to assess the relevance of this process.  
Table 99 shows the net normalised results for each scenario. 
The parameter that affects mostly the overall result is the substituted type of paper. The final 
recipient for the leachate after the treatment is a significant for MEP and ET, which are significantly 
caused by the leachate from a landfill.
