Abstract
With the iterative upgrading of autonomous
driving technology, traffic sign recognition systems
face more stringent performance requirements, and
achieving fast and accurate traffic sign detection has
become a key technical challenge in the field of in-
telligent driving. As the mainstream architecture in
the current object detection field, YOLOv8 has been
successfully applied in multiple scenarios such as in-
dustrial detection and medical imaging due to its high
efficiency. Although this model has improved in com-
prehensive performance compared to its predecessors,
it still has dual limitations in traffic sign detection
scenarios: the detection accuracy and inference effi-
ciency are difficult to meet the strict requirements of
real-time performance and accuracy in high-dynamic
road environments; conventional lightweight strate-
gies can compress model complexity, but they eas-
ily lead to accuracy loss.
How to strike a balance
between model simplification and performance main-
tenance has become a research focus.
In addition,
although the optimization of complex model struc-
tures can improve detection performance, it signifi-
cantly increases training costs, which restricts prac-
tical engineering deployment. Aiming at the above
problems, this study carries out multi-dimensional
improvements based on the YOLOv8n network:
(1) In the lightweight optimization stage of
the traffic sign detection model, a dual strategy
is adopted to transform the YOLOv8n architec-
ture: first, the original backbone network is replaced
with the MobileNetV3 lightweight feature extraction
architecture, and at the same time, a Slim-Head
lightweight detection head is introduced in the de-
tection head part. Although these two improvements
effectively reduced model complexity, the detection
accuracy decreased. Therefore, the C2f module was
further replaced with the FW-C2f structure to en-
hance feature fusion capabilities while maintaining
the parameter scale. The MW-YOLO model formed
through the above combined optimization achieves
significant lightweight effects, but with some loss in
accuracy.
(2) In the lightweight model accuracy enhance-
ment stage, aiming at the problem of reduced accu-
racy of the MW-YOLO model, through comparative
experiments on various attention mechanisms and
loss functions, the CA coordinate attention mecha-
nism and Focal-EIoU loss function were finally se-
lected for integration.
By fusing these two com-
ponents, the detection accuracy was effectively im-
proved while maintaining the lightweight characteris-
tics of the model. The final optimized model is named
MW-YOLO-CF.
(3) In the model deployment and verification
stage, a cross-platform transplantation process was

adopted: first, the PyTorch model of MW-YOLO-CF
was converted into an ONNX intermediate represen-
tation through serialization, and then the TensorRT
inference engine was used on the Jetson Orin Nano
development board to complete format optimization
and accelerated deployment. Through inference tests
in the actual hardware environment, it was verified
that this lightweight model can effectively support
embedded platform deployment and meet the needs
of real-time traffic sign detection tasks in edge com-
puting scenarios.

Introduction
2.1
Research Background and Signifi-
cance
In recent years, with the accelerated iteration of
intelligent transportation technology worldwide, the
research on traffic sign detection and recognition has
become an international frontier topic for improving
road safety and optimizing traffic governance. Fac-
ing the common challenges of the surge in global mo-
tor vehicle ownership and the frequent occurrence of
traffic accidents, how to break through the bottle-
neck of detection accuracy and response efficiency has
become a common focus of researchers in this field.
Its core technology lies in the precise positioning and
semantic analysis of sign symbols in multi-national
traffic scenarios through artificial intelligence-driven
visual perception technology. The innovative value of
this technical system for the global traffic ecosystem
is mainly reflected in three strategic dimensions:
(1) Constructing a global traffic safety barrier:
As an internationally recognized road information
carrier, traffic sign detection technology can signifi-
cantly enhance the adaptability of human drivers and
intelligent systems to complex road conditions, in-
cluding extreme climate scenarios such as polar bliz-
zards, equatorial strong light, desert dust, and the
mixed traffic flow characteristics brought about by
the rapid urbanization of emerging economies.
(2) Driving the intelligent upgrading of global
traffic governance:
Relying on the technological
breakthroughs of deep learning and edge comput-
ing, the new generation of sign detection systems is
building a dynamic perception network covering ur-
ban roads and transnational highway networks.
(3) Laying the foundation for the global ap-
plication of autonomous driving: In the context of
the transnational deployment of autonomous driving
technology, the traffic sign recognition module has
become the core interface for vehicles to understand
regional traffic rules. From the EU ECE regulations
to China’s GB standards, the system needs to be
compatible with the spatial characteristics and se-
mantic differences of sign systems in multiple coun-
tries, which puts forward strict requirements on the
cross-cultural generalization ability of the algorithm.
However, due to the limitation of hardware re-
sources in current in-vehicle intelligent transporta-
tion systems, strict requirements of high real-time
performance and low memory occupation are put
forward for traffic sign detection algorithms.
Tra-
ditional methods have the problems of insufficient
accuracy and model redundancy, while deep learn-
ing promotes lightweight design as the mainstream
direction by balancing model compression and ac-
curacy maintenance. Its core lies in optimizing the
model architecture, reducing the number of parame-
ters, improving inference speed, and maintaining de-
tection performance to meet the deployment needs
of low-computing power terminals such as embedded
devices. Through practical hardware adaptation ver-
ification, such models can significantly improve edge
computing efficiency, which has important applica-
tion value for promoting the landing of intelligent
transportation systems.

2.2.1
Traditional Traffic Sign Detection Al-
gorithms
The research on traffic sign detection technology
can be traced back to the end of the last century.
Limited by the insufficient performance of early com-
puters, the technological iteration was relatively slow
[1]. The early algorithm system was mainly based on
traditional image processing methods of color thresh-
old segmentation and shape feature extraction, and
some studies tried to integrate pattern classification
algorithms to enhance discrimination ability [2]. Al-
though these explorations were restricted by hard-
ware computing power and algorithm complexity,
they provided a technical foundation for the evolu-
tion of subsequent intelligent detection systems.
As the most versatile visual data carrier, RGB
images contain color information in channels that can
be directly used for feature extraction. Scholars such
as Ruta [3] designed an image enhancement algorithm
based on RGB extreme value transformation, which
strengthens the color features of red, blue, and yel-
3
==PAGE:4==
low signs through the difference calculation of max-
imum and minimum values. However, this method
is susceptible to the convergence of blue and green
channel values in low-light scenes, leading to the fail-
ure of blue sign feature extraction. In contrast, the
HSV model reduces the impact of brightness changes
on color discrimination by decoupling hue (H), sat-
uration (S), and lightness (V). In addition to RGB
and HSV, color models such as HSI, Lab, and YUV
also expand the color analysis dimension through the
chrominance-luminance separation mechanism. For
example, the mixed segmentation framework pro-
posed by the Lillo-Castellano team [4] integrates the
advantages of Lab and HIS models, effectively dis-
tinguishes color/monochrome sign elements, and im-
proves segmentation quality through symbol region
fusion and overlapping element separation. However,
the threshold segmentation method based on a fixed
color space needs to adjust parameters for different
scenarios, resulting in limited algorithm generaliza-
tion ability. Therefore, some researchers have turned
to pixel-level classification strategies, transforming
color feature extraction into pixel label classification
tasks, and achieving more robust color discrimination
through machine learning algorithms.
The geometric shapes of traffic signs (such as
circles, triangles, rectangles, octagons) are highly re-
lated to their functional semantics. This standard-
ized design not only strengthens the visual recogni-
tion efficiency of drivers but also constructs a uni-
fied road information coding system. In automatic
detection technology, shape features (including edge
contours, geometric regularity, internal structure) are
often used as the core discriminant basis. Compared
with color features susceptible to light, shape fea-
tures have stronger stability in low-light, fading, and
other scenes. For example, the Yakimov team [5] im-
proved the generalized Hough transform framework
and combined with a noise suppression strategy to
achieve real-time traffic sign detection, achieving a
balance between accuracy and speed.
In addition,
by extracting the periodic features of the contour
through Fourier transform [6-8], a frequency domain
shape descriptor can be constructed. Jimenez et al.
designed a dual-branch detection architecture, which
respectively performs shape classification based on
connected domain signatures and symbol position-
ing through two-dimensional homography transfor-
mation, verifying its robustness to scale scaling, ro-
tation deformation, local occlusion, and noise inter-
ference.
Single-feature detection has limitations,
and
the fusion strategy combining color and shape bi-
modal features can significantly improve model per-
formance. Liu et al. [9] proposed a fast detection
method based on high-contrast region feature ex-
traction, whose SFC tree detector supports simulta-
neous recognition of multi-category signs; Yuan et
al. [10] constructed a graph structure-driven detec-
tion framework, integrating multi-dimensional fea-
tures such as color distribution, visual saliency, and
spatial correlation, and cooperating with a multi-
threshold segmentation strategy to effectively deal
with complex backgrounds, dynamic lighting, and oc-
clusion problems, enhancing the model’s adaptability
in real scenes.
2.2.2
Deep Learning-Based Traffic Sign De-
tection Algorithms
Traditional traffic sign detection methods based
on color and shape need to rely on manual parame-
ter setting to adapt to environmental changes. Their
parameter configuration is based on empirical tuning
or sample statistics, with the defect of limited cross-
scene adaptability. When facing unknown environ-
ments, it is necessary to repeat the parameter opti-
mization process, leading to a decline in algorithm
robustness and an increase in maintenance costs.
Therefore, introducing a machine learning framework
to realize the automatic learning mechanism of fea-
ture representation and optimize the color-shape fea-
ture extraction process through a data-driven ap-
proach can significantly enhance the model’s environ-
mental adaptability and detection robustness [11,12].
Machine learning-based traffic sign detection
methods mainly include three technical routes: Ad-
aBoost, support vector machine (SVM), and con-
volutional neural network (CNN). In the AdaBoost
framework, the quality of feature engineering directly
affects algorithm performance.
Researchers im-
prove feature expression capabilities through multi-
4
==PAGE:5==
dimensional optimization: such as introducing non-
adjacent dipole structures to enhance feature diver-
sity, constructing multi-block local binary patterns
(LBP) for texture representation, and designing a
multi-level normalized feature encoding mechanism
[13]. Although this model can improve detection real-
time performance, when processing high-resolution
images, the more abundant fine-grained features lead
to a surge in computational load, thereby restricting
algorithm efficiency. As a classic classifier, SVM com-
bined with histogram of oriented gradients (HOG)
features has formed a mature solution in the field of
object detection [14-20]. For example, the Park team
[17] constructed a detection framework that does not
rely on color information, suppresses background in-
terference through a Gabor filter bank and extracts
shape features, and finally completes sign discrimina-
tion in combination with SVM.
Although traditional machine learning methods
have improved the efficiency of traffic sign detec-
tion, their limited feature expression capabilities are
difficult to meet the high-precision requirements in
complex scenarios.
Deep convolutional neural net-
works (CNN) have significantly optimized real-time
performance while improving detection accuracy by
virtue of hierarchical feature extraction capabilities
[21-23]. For example, the Zhu team [24] proposed a
text-oriented traffic sign detection framework, which
uses a fully convolutional network to generate candi-
date regions (RoI), and then uses a lightweight net-
work to recognize the text information in the region.
This two-stage strategy effectively balances detec-
tion speed and accuracy by reducing the number of
candidate regions and integrating multi-scale analy-
sis.
In addition, Zhu et al.
[25] designed a dual-
network architecture, using a fully convolutional net-
work to optimize the EdgeBox region proposal al-
gorithm and enhance the discrimination of candi-
date targets.
Deep learning-based traffic sign de-
tection methods continue to improve performance
by optimizing network architectures.
For example,
the Zhu team integrates a candidate region screen-
ing mechanism in the CNN classification stage to
achieve fast and accurate detection. Aiming at the
problem of missed detection, Zhang et al. [26] de-
signed a multi-scale cascaded R-CNN framework, in-
tegrated a multi-scale feature pyramid and a self-
attention module, and generated weight parameters
through the dot product operation of feature maps
to strengthen the representation ability of key fea-
tures. Wang et al. [27] optimized the RPN network
through a region proposal screening strategy, used
Res2Net with multi-granularity feature extraction ca-
pabilities as the backbone network, and achieved si-
multaneous improvement of accuracy and recall rate
in TT100K dataset experiments. The Yang team [28]
improved the Faster R-CNN architecture, introduced
an attention network to guide the model to focus
on color salient regions, combined with a coarse-fine
two-stage candidate box generation strategy to re-
duce small target missed detection, and embedded
a deconvolution layer to enhance the detection abil-
ity of small-size signs. Han et al. [29] reconstructed
the Faster R-CNN network framework, added a small
target feature extraction module, and increased the
average precision (mAP) of the model by 12.1% com-
pared with the baseline by removing the pool4 layer
and expanding the ResNet network structure.
Although the two-stage detection framework
improves recognition accuracy, the delay problem
caused by high computational complexity is diffi-
cult to meet the needs of high-real-time scenarios.
The single-stage detection paradigm, through end-
to-end architecture design, significantly improves in-
ference efficiency while ensuring detection accuracy.
Among them, the YOLO series of algorithms domi-
nate the real-time detection tasks in agriculture, in-
dustry, medical care, transportation, and other fields
by virtue of their high efficiency [30-37]. For exam-
ple, the Zhang team [38] improved the YOLOv2 net-
work for the characteristics of Chinese traffic signs,
replaced the original top structure with multiple 1×1
convolutions to compress the number of parame-
ters, and enhanced the resolution of the feature map
through a dense grid division strategy, thereby opti-
mizing the detection ability of small-size signs. Dewi
et al.
[39] proposed to use a generative adversar-
ial network (GAN) to synthesize high-reality and di-
versified traffic sign data, construct a mixed training
set, and verify it on YOLO series models (v3/v4).
Experiments show that the mixed dataset effectively
improves the model’s generalization and detection ac-

algorithms promotes the development of traffic sign
detection technology. The Liu team [40] proposed an
improved YOLOv4 scheme: integrating the MLP ar-

the detection capability, building cross-connections
at the same layer inside the PANet network to en-

an angle constraint term in the loss function to ac-

robustness requirements of autonomous driving per-

researchers continue to optimize the YOLOv8 frame-

The Kumar team [44] trained the YOLOv8

datasets, significantly enhancing the sign detection
ability in extreme scenarios such as rain, snow, and

discriminative ability for sign semantic attributes.
2.3
Main Content and Innovations
Aiming at the problems of limited device com-
puting power and tight memory resources in real-
time traffic condition detection scenarios, this pa-
per adopts a strategy of coordinated promotion of
model lightweight design and accuracy optimization.
First, the network structure is simplified based on
the YOLOv8n architecture to construct a lightweight
detection model; then, the detection accuracy is en-
hanced without increasing model complexity; finally,
the optimized model is transplanted to the Jetson
Orin Nano embedded platform to complete deploy-
ment verification. The research focuses on three core
links: the lightweight reconstruction of the traffic sign
detection model, the accuracy improvement technol-
ogy under the lightweight base, and the engineering
landing verification in the edge computing environ-
ment.
(1) In the lightweight optimization stage of
the traffic sign detection model, a dual strategy
is adopted to transform the YOLOv8n architec-
ture: first, the original backbone network is replaced
with the MobileNetV3 lightweight feature extraction
architecture, and at the same time, a Slim-Head
lightweight detection head is introduced in the de-
tection head part. Although these two improvements
effectively reduced model complexity, the detection
accuracy decreased. Therefore, the C2f module was
further replaced with the FW-C2f structure to en-
hance feature fusion capabilities while maintaining
the parameter scale. The MW-YOLO model formed
through the above combined optimization achieves
significant lightweight effects, but with some loss in
accuracy.
(2) In the lightweight model accuracy enhance-

racy of the MW-YOLO model, through comparative
experiments on various attention mechanisms and
loss functions, the CA coordinate attention mecha-

lected for integration.
By fusing these two com-

Basic Theories
3.1
Object Detection Algorithms
Object detection algorithms can be divided into
two-stage and single-stage types according to the
process architecture.
Two-stage methods (such as
Faster R-CNN [48]) first generate region proposals,
and then perform fine classification and regression on
candidate boxes, with higher detection accuracy but
greater computational overhead; single-stage meth-
ods (represented by the YOLO series) directly output
target positions and category information through an
end-to-end architecture, with significant speed ad-
vantages. In recent years, the YOLO series has sig-
nificantly improved detection accuracy while main-
taining high-speed inference through continuous it-
eration, and its technical evolution path focuses on
network architecture innovation and computational
efficiency optimization. With the research focus shift-
ing to the collaborative optimization of accuracy and
lightweighting, the YOLO series has made break-
throughs in model compression, multi-scale feature
fusion, etc., gradually becoming the preferred solu-
tion that balances real-time performance and accu-
racy.
3.1.1
Common Deep Learning Algorithms
Deep learning realizes the autonomous learning
of features from data and completes decision-making
tasks by constructing computational models of brain-
like neural structures.
Its core architectures in-
clude typical paradigms such as deep neural networks
(DNN) [49], recurrent neural networks (RNN) [50],
and convolutional neural networks (CNN) [51]. DNN
enhances model expression capabilities by stacking
multiple hidden nodes, while RNN breaks through
the limitation of static modeling and specializes in
capturing dynamic features of time-series data. CNN
7
==PAGE:8==
bionic visual cortex processing mechanisms, showing
significant advantages in two-dimensional image fea-
ture extraction, and is widely used in image classi-
fication [52], target localization [53], semantic seg-
mentation [54], and other tasks.
Taking YOLOv8
as an example, its network architecture consists of
three parts:
the backbone network extracts high-
order semantic features of the image through multi-
level convolution operations; the neck network ag-
gregates feature maps of different layers of the back-
bone to achieve cross-scale information fusion; the
detection head performs bounding box regression and
classification based on the fused features, and its
structure dynamically adjusts the number of output
branches according to task requirements.
In deep learning algorithms based on CNN, op-
erations such as convolution, pooling, and activation
functions are often included:
(1) The convolution operation is shown in Figure
1. This process uses a learnable convolution kernel
(filter) to perform sliding window traversal on the
input image or feature map, with each window cor-
responding to the pixel values in the local receptive
field. In specific implementation, the weight matrix
of the convolution kernel is multiplied element-wise
with the pixel values in the corresponding window
area and summed to generate the response value of
a single pixel in the output feature map. The sliding
stride (stride) controls the interval of window move-
ment, while zero padding (padding) can adjust the
spatial size of the output feature map. By stacking
multi-channel convolution kernels, primary features
such as edges and textures in different directions of
the image can be extracted.
This parameter shar-
ing mechanism significantly reduces model complex-
ity. The depth of the final output feature map is con-
sistent with the number of convolution kernels, and
each channel corresponds to the response of specific
types of local features.
(2) Pooling is a key operation in convolutional
neural networks for feature compression and infor-
mation screening, and its main role is reflected in
the following aspects: by reducing the spatial resolu-
tion (width and height) of the feature map, the com-
putational complexity of subsequent network layers
is reduced; at the same time, the original pixel val-
Figure 1: Convolution Operation Diagram
ues are replaced by the statistics of the local area to
suppress noise interference and retain key features.
As shown in Figure 2, common pooling methods in-
clude two types: max pooling and average pooling.
Max pooling selects the maximum value in the local
area covered by the sliding window as the output.
This strategy enhances the model’s robustness to mi-
nor deformations by retaining significant feature re-
sponses; average pooling calculates the mean value
in the window area as the output, and reduces fea-
ture sensitivity through smoothing operations, which
is suitable for scenarios where the overall distribution
characteristics need to be retained. The window size
and sliding stride of the pooling operation need to be
set in advance. Usually, a 2×2 window with a stride
of 2 is used to halve the size of the feature map, so
as to achieve hierarchical abstract expression of the

In the evolution of convolutional neural net-

ability to extract and fuse discriminative features.
3.1.2
YOLO Series Algorithms
The YOLO (You Only Look Once) object detec-
tion framework was proposed by the Redmon team
in 2015, and its initial version YOLOv1 was officially
released at the CVPR computer vision top conference
in 2016, marking the rapid development stage of this
series of algorithms. The iteration history is shown
in Figure 2-3. As a representative of real-time detec-
tion technology, the YOLO series integrates the tar-
get positioning and classification tasks into a single
network through an end-to-end regression detection
framework, directly outputting the bounding box co-
ordinates and category probabilities, significantly im-
proving the inference speed.
Compared with two-
stage methods such as R-CNN, the architecture de-
sign of YOLO makes it occupy an advantage in sce-
narios with strict real-time requirements such as in-
dustrial detection and security monitoring.
In ad-
dition to the core versions shown in Figure 3, re-
searchers have also derived improved variants such
as YOLOF, continuously expanding the application
boundaries of the algorithm.
Figure 3: YOLO Development
YOLOv1, YOLOv2 [59], and YOLOv3 [60] were
all proposed by the Redmon team. Each subsequent
version optimizes performance based on the previous
one, among which YOLOv3 significantly surpasses
the first two generations in terms of detection ac-
curacy and multi-scale adaptability. The algorithm
uses a 416×416 resolution three-channel image as in-
put, adopts a batch processing mode during training,
and the input tensor dimension is Batch × 3 × 416
× 416 (Batch represents the number of samples in a
single batch). The backbone network leads out multi-
scale features through Res8, Res8, and Res4 lay-
ers, and realizes the transmission and integration of
high-dimensional features to low dimensions through
the cross-layer feature fusion module. Finally, three
scales of detection results are output: 52 × 52 × 255,
26 × 26 × 255, and 13 × 13 × 255, where 255 is calcu-
lated by (4 + 1 + C) × 3.
Although YOLOv3 lagged behind subsequent it-
erative versions in terms of detection accuracy, infer-
ence speed, and deployment convenience when it was
proposed in 2018, the multi-scale detection frame-
work it established (such as the feature pyramid
structure and multi-level prediction mechanism) has
9
==PAGE:10==
become the core architecture paradigm for the evo-
lution of subsequent YOLO series models. This ver-
sion lays the technical foundation for the balanced
optimization of accuracy and speed in object detec-
tion tasks by introducing the anchor box mechanism
and multi-scale prediction module.
Its core design
concepts (such as end-to-end regression strategy and
grid detection unit) continue to influence the devel-
opment path of YOLOv4 to YOLOv8 and other ver-
sions, becoming an important reference benchmark
for the continuous optimization of the series of algo-
rithms.
The YOLOv4 and Scaled-YOLOv4 [61] pro-
posed by the Bochkovskiy team, compared with
YOLOv3, one of their core innovations is the intro-
duction of online data enhancement strategies (such
as CutMix [62], Mosaic [63], etc.). These methods
improve model generalization ability by dynamically
generating diversified training samples: for example,
CutMix randomly cuts the local area of the image
and replaces it with other sample fragments, forcing
the model to learn the combination relationship of
local features; Mosaic stiches and fuses four random
images to simulate the multi-target distribution char-
acteristics in complex scenarios. Compared with the
traditional offline enhancement method, this strat-
egy synthesizes samples in real-time during the train-
ing process, which not only avoids the overhead of
large-scale enhanced data storage and preprocessing
but also enhances the model’s adaptability to occlu-
sion and scale changes through randomness injection.
This technical route has been widely adopted by sub-
sequent YOLO series, verifying its effectiveness in im-
proving detection robustness.
YOLOv5 [64] was developed by the Jocher
team and maintained by Ultralytics LLC. Its open-
source code continues to be iteratively updated on
the GitHub platform, with more than 200 devel-
opers contributing cumulatively.
Compared with
YOLOv3/YOLOv4, which uses an offline cluster-
ing algorithm to precompute anchor box param-
eters, YOLOv5 innovatively integrates the anchor
box size calculation process into the training pro-
cess (adaptive anchor box algorithm), eliminating the
tedious step of manual adjustment of anchor boxes
by users for specific tasks. The algorithm optimizes
the network architecture through high-frequency it-
eration.
Its core modules CBS (convolution-batch
normalization-activation function stacking) and C3
(cross-stage local network) have structural fine-
tuning in different versions (see Figure 4). The oper-
ation rules inside the module are addition and splic-
ing operations. The former superimposes the element
values at the corresponding positions of the feature
map, keeping the number of channels and spatial di-
mensions unchanged; the latter concatenates the fea-
ture maps along the channel dimension, expanding
the number of channels but maintaining the width
and height dimensions.
Figure 4: C3 Module
In January 2023, Ultralytics officially released
the YOLOv8 [65] algorithm, whose detection accu-
racy and inference efficiency both exceed the previ-
ous YOLO series models.
The algorithm includes
five predefined scale variants (n/s/m/l/x), where n
is the minimal lightweight version and x is the high-
precision extended version. The network backbone
continues the cross-stage local connection (CSP) ar-
chitecture concept, but optimizes the C3 module in

dundant calculations. The module is shown in Figure

of accuracy and efficiency by sorting out the archi-
tecture evolution from YOLOv3 to the current latest
generation YOLOv8. The iteration focus of the se-

The F1 curve is used to measure the comprehen-

and recall when detecting targets in an all-around

precision (Precision) and recall (Recall). The curve
takes the confidence threshold as the horizontal axis
and the F1 score as the vertical axis, and the val-
ues of both range from [0,1]. The PR curve is a key
indicator for evaluating model performance, where
the horizontal axis corresponds to recall (Recall), and
the vertical axis corresponds to precision (Precision).
Usually, the intersection of the PR curve and the

3.2.3
mAP
AP (average precision) is a quantitative index
of the area under the PR curve, reflecting the com-
prehensive performance of the model in reconciling
precision (Precision) and recall (Recall). The larger
the value, the higher the model accuracy. mAP [64]

is used to comprehensively evaluate the overall effec-

nition.
The improvement of mAP directly corre-
sponds to the optimization of the model’s compre-
hensive performance.

Aiming at the visual application requirements
of mobile and embedded devices, MobileNetV1 [66]
innovatively introduces depthwise separable convolu-
tion, which achieves efficient computation and pa-
rameter reduction by decomposing traditional con-
volution operations.
Its core idea is to decompose
the standard convolution into two independent and
complementary stages.
The depthwise convolution focuses on spatial di-
mension filtering, and each convolution kernel only
acts on a single channel of the input feature map.
If the input feature map has M channels, M inde-
pendent single-channel convolution kernels are used,
and the size of the convolution kernel is DK×DK×1,
which respectively filters each channel. This design
avoids cross-channel information interaction and only
requires
1
N of the computation amount of the tradi-
tional convolution, where N is the number of output
channels. Assuming that DW and DH are the sizes of
the feature map, the calculation formula of its com-
putation amount is shown in Equation 3.
Computation = DK × DK × M × DW × DH
(3)
The pointwise convolution is used to achieve
cross-channel feature fusion through 1×1 convolution
after the depthwise convolution. In this stage, N con-
volution kernels (size 1×1×M) are used to map the
M-channel feature map to the N-channel, and finally,
the output feature map has the same dimension as
the standard convolution. The calculation formula of
its computation amount is shown in Equation 4.
Computation = M × N × DW × DH
(4)
The process of depthwise separable convolution
is shown in Figure 8. When a 3×3 convolution ker-
nel is used, the total computation amount of depth-
wise separable convolution is only about one-eighth
of that of the traditional convolution. This decom-
position method reduces the number of parameters
while retaining the feature expression ability of the
standard convolution, providing a balance between
accuracy and efficiency for mobile devices.

MobileNetV2 [67] is an improved version of Mo-
bileNetV1, with the following two major improve-
ments:
13

(1) Linear Bottleneck
MobileNetV2 removes the ReLU activation mod-
ule at the end of the depthwise separable convolu-
tion of MobileNetV1.
Since the traditional ReLU
will cause the negative features to be zeroed af-
ter low-dimensional feature compression, resulting
in information loss.
Experiments have shown that
when the number of output channels is low, the non-
linear transformation of ReLU will destroy the fea-
ture expression ability of the low-dimensional mani-
fold. Therefore, MobileNetV2 uses a linear activation
at the end of the bottleneck layer to retain the infor-
mation integrity of the compressed features.
(2) Inverted Residual Structure
ResNet adopts a hourglass-shaped path of ”di-
mensionality reduction →convolution →dimension-
ality increase”, while MobileNetV2 is reversely de-
signed as a spindle-shaped path of ”dimensionality
increase →convolution →dimensionality reduction”.
The input channels are expanded by 1×1 convolu-
tion, then 3×3 depthwise separable convolution is
used to extract high-dimensional features, and finally,
the channels are compressed by 1×1 linear convolu-
tion. The ReLU6 is used in the depthwise convolution
stage to limit the maximum output value to 6, avoid-
ing quantization errors caused by too large numeri-
cal ranges during low-precision calculations (such as
FP16/INT8).
The comparison between the residual structure
and the inverted residual structure is shown in Figure
9. In the residual structure, the main purpose of us-
ing 1×1 convolution to reduce the channel dimension
is to avoid the too high computational overhead of the
intermediate layer traditional convolution operation.
The design logic of the inverted residual structure is
the opposite: since the depthwise separable convolu-
tion greatly reduces the computation amount of the
intermediate filtering layer, it uses 1×1 convolution
to expand the channel dimension to enhance the fea-
ture expression ability in the high-dimensional space.
At the same time, since the number of input and out-
put channels is low, 1×1 convolution will not signifi-
cantly increase the computation complexity whether
it is used for dimensionality increase or reduction.

4.2
Lightweight Detection Head
The detection head of YOLOv8 adopts a de-
coupled structure, which independently processes the
classification task and the positioning task.
This
design abandons the dependency relationship of the
traditional anchor box and instead uses an anchor-
free prediction mechanism to directly generate de-
tection boxes through the center point coordinates
and width-height parameters of the target, thereby
eliminating the confidence loss and only retaining the
classification loss and bounding box regression loss.
However, the dense convolution operations in its head
network, such as multiple stacked 3×3 convolution
kernels, lead to a significant increase in the number of
model parameters and the complexity of the network
topology. This poses a challenge to computational re-
source efficiency and model lightweight deployment.
As shown in Figure 12, this study constructs
a lightweight detection head Slim-Head architecture
with shared multi-branches based on residual connec-
tions to compress the number of parameters. Aiming
at the limitations of the batch normalization layer
in the original network CBS structure, such as inac-
curate estimation of statistics in small batches and
excessive normalization in large batches that inhibits
model convergence, the group normalization is used
to replace the BN layer to form the CGS feature en-
hancement module.
The processing flow of this module includes three
stages: first, the feature maps output by the four
feature layers are input into the CGS network for
basic feature extraction; then, the feature maps are
divided into three groups through channel blocking
operations, among which the first part extracts local
features through partial convolution, the second part
strengthens the deep feature representation through
the CGS network, and the third part retains the orig-
inal channel information; finally, the three groups of
features are superimposed and fused in channels. In
order to eliminate the influence of the size difference
of the input feature maps of the multi-scale detec-
tion head, a learnable dynamic scaling factor is in-
troduced to adaptively adjust the size of features at
different levels. By using the parameter-shared Slim-
Head structure to replace the independent convolu-
tion layers in the original detection head, a significant
compression of the number of parameters is achieved
while ensuring the detection accuracy.

5.1
Introduction of Attention Mecha-
nisms
In the target detection scenario, a single frame of
image often contains multiple targets to be detected
and interfering background elements, which can af-
fect the model’s recognition accuracy of core targets.
The introduction of attention mechanisms aims to
suppress the interference of background noise, guide
computational resources to be preferentially allocated
to the learning of target features, and thereby en-
hance the model’s ability to extract key information.
The core idea originates from bionic principles,
as the human visual system has the ability to quickly
focus on core areas and filter out secondary informa-
tion. Based on this characteristic, attention mecha-
nisms have been derived in the field of deep learn-
ing to simulate the biological cognitive process. In
this study, three types of attention modules, CBAM,
EMA, and CA, were sequentially tested for the MW-
YOLO architecture, and their performance differ-
ences were evaluated through comparative experi-
ments.
5.1.1
CBAM Attention Mechanism
The CBAM attention mechanism adopts a col-
laborative design of channel and spatial dual-path at-
tention, enhancing feature expression efficiency by fo-
cusing on key information in the channel and spatial
dimensions in stages.
As shown in Figure 23, the
process begins with the input feature map undergo-
ing global average pooling and global max pooling
respectively to generate channel-level statistical de-
scription vectors.
The two vectors share the same
multi-layer perceptron for non-linear mapping, and
after element-wise addition, a channel weight ma-
trix is generated through the Sigmoid function. This
matrix characterizes the importance distribution of
each channel, and performs channel-wise multiplica-
tion with the original feature map to achieve channel-
dimensional feature recalibration.
Subsequently, on the feature map processed by
channel attention, max pooling and average pooling
are performed along the channel dimension to gener-
ate two spatial position-sensitive feature maps. The
two are concatenated and then a standard convolu-
tional layer is used to extract spatial correlation, fol-
lowed by a Sigmoid function to generate a spatial
weight matrix for attention weighting of each posi-
tion in the feature map in the spatial dimension.
Figure 23: Schematic Diagram of CBAM Process
The dual-path attention module works in a
cascaded manner with channel priority followed by
space, strengthening the feature map’s expression
capabilities in channel sensitivity and spatial con-
text relevance through the collaborative mechanism
of prior channel screening and posterior spatial focus-
ing.
5.1.2
EMA Attention Mechanism
The EMA attention mechanism can improve the
detection performance of the network without in-
creasing the model’s computational parameters. As
shown in Figure 24, the EMA attention mechanism
generates a global description vector through feature
compression in the channel dimension, with its di-
mension maintained as 1×1×C (C is the total num-
ber of channels). Different from conventional chan-
nel attention mechanisms, EMA innovatively uses a
single-dimensional convolution kernel operation di-
rectly on the channel description vector, and the con-
volution kernel size k is dynamically adjusted through
an adaptive mechanism to adapt to different task re-
quirements.
This design effectively captures cross-channel in-
teraction information: the one-dimensional convolu-
tion kernel maintains the integrity of the channel di-
mension while modeling the correlation weights be-
tween channels through a local receptive field, avoid-
ing the risk of feature information loss caused by
channel dimensionality reduction in traditional meth-
ods. Since the convolutional kernel parameters are
24
==PAGE:25==
only related to the k value (usually set as a very small
integer), the parameter scale increment of the module
as a whole is negligible. This lightweight optimiza-
tion strategy significantly enhances the network’s fo-
cus on key features while ensuring that the model’s
computational efficiency and parameter efficiency are
not affected.
Figure 24: EMA Attention Mechanism Module
5.1.3
CA Attention Mechanism
The CA (Coordinate Attention) mechanism con-
structs a feature enhancement solution for lightweight
networks by fusing channel correlation and spatial
positioning information. As shown in Figure 25, its
core process includes three stages: first, performing
one-dimensional global feature compression along the
horizontal and vertical directions on the input feature
map to generate dual-path feature descriptors con-
taining vertical and horizontal spatial statistics; then
converting the spatial statistics into direction-aware
attention weight matrices through independent en-
coding paths; finally, re-calibrating the features with
the dual-path attention weights to achieve precise
spatial focusing on the target area.
This axial decomposition-based attention model-
ing method effectively establishes long-range depen-
dencies across pixels while maintaining precise coor-
dinate positioning capabilities. Due to the use of a
lightweight encoding structure with parameter shar-
ing, this mechanism can be seamlessly embedded into
various mobile network architectures, and the addi-
tional computational load brought by its modular de-
sign is minimal.

5.2
Introduction of Loss Functions
In the field of target detection, the loss function,
as a core evaluation tool, is used to quantify the devi-
ation between the model’s prediction results and the
real annotations, and its role runs through the entire
process of model training and inference. During the
training phase, the loss function corrects the predic-
tion error through a dynamic feedback mechanism,
driving the iterative optimization of model param-
eters. For the YOLOv8 algorithm, its loss calcula-
tion is mainly composed of bounding box regression
loss and classification loss: the former uses Complete
Intersection over Union (CIoU) combined with Dis-
tribution Focal Loss (DFL), and the latter is imple-
mented based on the cross-entropy loss function.

5.4
Summary of this Chapter
This study implements structural improvements
to the MW-YOLO algorithm in two key links: atten-
tion mechanism and loss function, significantly im-
proving detection performance while maintaining the
model’s lightweight characteristics. First, the impact
of three attention modules on model accuracy and
computational efficiency was experimentally evalu-
ated, and the CA attention mechanism was finally se-
lected for algorithm optimization. Secondly, by com-
paring the optimization effects of HCIoU Loss and
Focal-EIoU loss functions, it was determined to use
Focal-EIoU with stronger gradient focusing mecha-
nism as the training optimization strategy.
The MW-YOLO-CF model constructed by in-
tegrating the above improvements, ablation experi-
ments show that: compared with the basic version
of MW-YOLO, the improved model maintains the
number of parameters and the amount of calculation
basically unchanged, while the mean accuracy is im-
proved by 1.9%; compared with the original bench-
mark model YOLOv8n, while the mAP is increased
by 0.5%, the model parameters and floating-point op-
erations are compressed by 63.3% and 72.8% respec-
tively, and the FPS is increased by 14.4%, success-
fully achieving the optimization balance of detection
accuracy and computational efficiency, and verifying
the effectiveness of the lightweight design and algo-
rithm improvement strategy.
In the follow-up, hardware deployment tests will
be carried out to verify the actual engineering appli-
cability of this scheme.

6.1
Introduction to Edge Devices
The operation of deep learning algorithms re-
quires hardware platforms to have efficient computing
capabilities to support the model inference process.
Traditional solutions mostly use desktop worksta-
tions equipped with high-performance GPUs, which
have sufficient computing power but suffer from lim-
itations such as large volume and high deployment
costs.
With the progress of embedded technology,
current mainstream embedded development boards
(such as NVIDIA Jetson TX series, Jetson Nano,
etc.) have computing performance close to that of
microcomputers. As shown in Figure 32, Jetson Nano
is selected as the deployment platform in this paper,
mainly based on its multiple advantages: in terms
of physical characteristics, the device has a compact
structure design, which is easy to carry and oper-
ate; in terms of computing performance, its inte-
grated GPU can meet the real-time requirements of
the target detection algorithm; in terms of deploy-
ment adaptability, the device can operate stably with
only basic power supply, without relying on a network
environment; in addition, the platform has the advan-
tages of high cost performance and low maintenance
cost, requiring no additional debugging fees and hav-
ing rich peripheral interfaces to support subsequent
functional expansion needs.
The hardware parameter configuration of the Je-
ston Nano development platform used in this study
is detailed in Figure 33. Its core processor adopts an
8-core NVIDIA Carmel Armv8.2 64-bit CPU, with a
maximum base operating frequency of up to 1.5GHz.
The graphics processing unit configuration includes
1,024 NVIDIA CUDA parallel computing cores and
32 deep learning-specific Tensor Cores. When run-
ning in 15W power mode, the platform can provide
up to 40TOPS of heterogeneous computing perfor-
mance, fully meeting the computing power require-
ments of edge-side deep learning inference.

YOLOv8n network architecture:
(1) In the lightweight optimization stage of

adopted to transform the YOLOv8n architecture:
first, replace the original backbone network with the
MobileNetV3 lightweight feature extraction architec-
