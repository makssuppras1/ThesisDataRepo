Abstract
This master thesis contains the primary analysis of the second part of the FloatStep test campaign
carried out between May 11th and June 23th at DHI, Hørsholm. The experiments were conducted
in a deep water wave basin with a cylindrical structure (D = 25 cm) with a heave plate (D = 50
cm) representing one of the columns of a made up Froude scale 1:60 semi-submersible ﬂoating wind
turbine foundation design.
The structure was tested with two diﬀerent drafts and two diﬀerent
inclinations in three diﬀerent JONSWAP sea states. The structure was attached to an actuator
which moved the cylinder with diﬀerent amplitudes at the natural heave frequency and the ﬂexible
mode frequency as described in the primary analysis of the ﬁrst part of the FloatStep test campaign
in Vincent (2020). The damping in terms of linear and viscous drag has been assessed and two
diﬀerent models have been utilized to remodel the data.

Introduction
This master thesis is titled:
Experimental and numerical study of the damping eﬀect from heave plates on the response
of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines in operational sea states
It contains the primary analysis of the data from the FloatStep test campaign at DHI, Hørsholm
conducted between the 11th of May and 23th of June 2020. The experiments are divided into two
parts: A ﬂexible structure and a single cylinder structure with a heave plate attached to an actuator.
The ﬂexible part is analyzed in Vincent (2020) and the single cylinder set-up is investigated in this
thesis.
After this introduction the report will go over the relevant theory, the design and conduction of the
experiments. The main part of the report is the data analysis, which investigates the eﬀect of the
heave plate both in calm waters and in sea states representing three diﬀerent wave climates.

heave plates in operational sea states. As well as performing the primary data analysis to estimate
the damping and relevant model parameters.

Theory
This section will introduce the models used in this thesis. The models will be used to estimate the
loads in the design phase of the project in Section 3. Furthermore the models will be applied to the
data from the experiments for validation and optimization in Section 6.
First the linear wave theory will be introduced as well as a possible harmonic expansion and wave

Then the wave loads are explained from the perspective of a Morison-based approach

2.1
Waves
The wave theory is crucial to this thesis as all the experiments are carried out in a wave basin and
ﬂoating wind turbines are, in fact, operating in water. First the wave domain will be characterized
through the boundary conditions, then the JONSWAP spectrum is introduced. Lastly the linear
surface elevation is expanded through the Stoke’s perturbation. The harmonic expansion enables
a phase separation of the signal such that the time signal can be split into individual harmonics
through clever manipulation of the linear wave paddle signal. Some of the work presented in this
section is based on the work presented in the special course report Steﬀensen (2020).

2.2.2
WAMIT
The radiation-diﬀraction potential ﬂow solver WAMIT is the second method used to assess the
forces on the structure. WAMIT calculates the added mass, damping, restoring stiﬀness and force
amplitude transfer functions in the frequency domain. The panel method is used such that the source
strength of each panel is calculated to counteract the ﬂow so no ﬂow is passing through the structure.
Furthermore the interaction between the source strength of the diﬀerent panels are accounted for
too, thus the creation of an appropriate geometry ﬁle is important when considering computation
time.
For the cylinder part of the structure the kinematics near the free surface is the most signiﬁcant
contribution to the forcing and thus a cosine spacing of the panels with z-coordinate was applied.
As we move closer to the still water level (SWL) the ﬁner the panels get. The WAMIT geometry for
diﬀerent inclination is present in Figure 2.2.

Experiments
The test campaign was carried out at DHI, Hørsholm as a part of the FloatStep project. The testing
started the 11th of May 2020 and ended the 23rd of June lasting 44 days with 28 days of active
testing due to weekends and national holidays. The original plan was to start the testing the 11th
of March but due to COVID-19 the test schedule got postponed by two months. The experiments
were carried out with limited help from DHI personnel as a consequence of understaﬃng caused by
the pandemic. Furthermore DTU employees were not allowed on site for the ﬁrst couple of weeks.
The ﬂexible experiments have been carried out almost exclusively by the two Msc students Maude
Yvonne Madeleine Vincent (responsible for the ﬂexible tests) and Stefan Emil Steﬀensen (responsible
for the single cylinder test). The single cylinder tests were carried out by Stefan Emil Steﬀensen
with the assistance of the Hydro Group at DTU Wind Energy.
The experiments are divided into three parts. The ﬁrst part is the undisturbed waves before the
structures were placed in the water. The second part is the ﬂexible tests described in the brilliant
work of Vincent (2020). The ﬂexible structure was tested both in a ﬁxed conﬁguration which only
allowed the ﬂexible modes to be excited and a ﬂoating conﬁguration both with two diﬀerent ﬂexible
natural frequencies. The natural frequencies were tuned by changing the stiﬀness of the structure.
The third part is the single cylinder tests, which enables a closer look at the wave interaction with
heave plate separately by ﬁxing the structure i.e. ﬁxed scenario (diﬀraction problem). By having
the actuator forcing the single cylinder to oscillate at the heave natural frequency of the ﬂoating
structure or at the ﬂexible natural frequency in calm waters the eﬀects of the self-interaction of
the heave plate can be investigated i.e. calm scenario (radiation problem). A combination of both
incident waves and actuator motion will provide an insight in the interaction between the heave plate
and the wave kinematics i.e. mixed scenario (full problem).

4.3.1
Wave gauge
The wave gauges are used to measure the surface elevation. They work by measuring the voltage
through the circuit in the two metal rods. The resistance of the rods is proportional to the submerged
length of the wave gauge. The data acquisition system (DAQ) has a range of ±10 V and the expected
maximum surface elevation was 0.16 meters in EC 11. The bucket calibration procedure aimed for a
2 V drop over a change in surface elevation of 0.1 meters. This corresponds to a total range of ± 1
meter in the acceptable voltage range, this is longer than the length of the wave gauges themselves.
The bucket calibration procedure is an elaborate word for a rather simple process.
Figure 4.4: To the left is a picture of the white bucket with the brown sponge used as padding on the
inside of the bucket. The two metal rods from the red head of the wave gauges are partly submerged
in the bucket. The bronze slider is set in top conﬁguration corresponding to mean sea level. The
picture of the right depicts the metal tripod standing on the sea bed with roughly one meter above
sea level and 3 meters below. On this tripod there is both a single wave gauge and a thermometer
equipped.
The wave gauge is mounted to a metal rod with slider that can slide the wave gauge up 10 cm
(bronze slider on Figure 4.4 (LEFT)). The wave gauge is placed in a bucket, which has water in
it. The inner boundary of the bucket is padded with a sponge to suppress reﬂections from the edge
of the bucket to a minimum. The voltage measured by the wave gauge is measured three times by
averaging 30 seconds. Two data points are measured at the top of the slider corresponding to mean
water level, and one measurement point at the bottom of the slider submerging the wave gauge
10 cm corresponding to a positive surface elevation of 10 cm. With these three data points the

5
Preliminary Data Analysis
Data coming from experiments seldom looks like the model. There are several reasons for this. First
of all the signals from the sensors are noisy by nature. Secondly there are a lot of physical eﬀects
and couplings that are not taken into account by the models, some of these can be ﬁltered out using
frequency ﬁltering. Last and not least there might be elements in the signals that are associated
with faulty measurement equipment or other external inﬂuences that are not a part of the physical
system but still gets represented in the signal. The aim of this subsection is to assess these eﬀects
and ﬁlter them in a way that does not reduce the quality of or information contained in the data.
5.1
Representing Frequencies
Noisy time signals give rise to noisy spectra. In the process of carrying out a spectral analysis it
is important that the relevant frequencies stand out in a clear way and does not get drowned by
the noise. The purpose of the spectral analysis is to see signiﬁcant frequencies. The relative value
between the peaks can be relevant to assess the relative contribution of the diﬀerent eﬀects. Three
diﬀerent ways of representing the spectral energy is shown in Figure 5.1, it has both a large frequency
range (left) and a zoom in on one of the peaks (right).
Figure 5.1: Spectrum of load cell heave force for test with actuator in calm waters with f0 = 1.6Hz,
z0 = 6cm, conﬁguration 2 (d = 70cm and φ = 0◦). From 0 Hz to 20 Hz (left) and a zoom on 3 Hz
to 3.4 Hz (right).
The blue line is showing the unﬁltered signal which can be seen to contain a lot of noise. In this case
all the relevant frequencies are clear. On the zoom in it is seen that the peak is sharp, but noisy
on the adjacent frequencies. The red line represents a moving average using 20 samples. The shape
of the spectrum is well kept in regards to both the peaks and the ’dip’ seen around 7 Hz. When
zooming in on the peak, it is seen that the moving average is lowering the magnitude of the peak and
is ﬂattening the peak as well. This ﬂattening of the peak can make it diﬃcult to get an exact value
of the frequency causing the peak. The last method is the Welch’s power spectrum estimate (pwelch)
using the build-in MATLAB function pwelch∗. This speciﬁc conﬁguration of pwelch is dividing the
time series into 15 windows of equal size with a 50 % overlap and is applying a hamming window.
The power spectrum is calculated for all 15 windows and then averaged. This method is mostly
applicable to long time series or time series with insigniﬁcant sub-harmonic contributions since it
eﬀectively is lowering the duration of the tests causing a lower frequency resolution. In this case,
where a 10 minute test is conducted, the frequency resolution is reduced from dfregular = 0.0017 Hz
to dfpwelch = 0.0246 Hz. On the ﬁgure it is seen that the pwelch method is both lowering the noise
as well as keeping the peak frequency easily distinguishable at the cost of resolution. It is, however
in this case only due to the coarse resolution bin being placed in a lucky spot. If ﬁltering is needed
in the spectral analysis a moving average will be applied.

6.1.4
Fixed scenario sub-conclusion
The analysis has shown that the higher harmonics of the force are evident and important. This was
obtained by harmonic decomposition, which means that the use of the four-phased signals was a
success. If the inﬂuence of the bridge natural frequency is disregarded the third order contribution
is of the same order of magnitude or higher than the second order contribution. The inﬂuence from
the load cell pitch moment limitation being exceeded is present throughout the section. It prevents
any meaningful analysis of the peaks, an analysis which could potentially highlight the non-linear
eﬀects of the largest waves.
Both models are able to predict the general trend of the force signals. The polynomial approach is
a better match to the measured force than the WAMIT + drag approach. This is due to the fact
that the WAMIT model only has one parameter that can be optimized through the drag coeﬃcient
CD. There are clearly some eﬀects which the model does not account for, this is especially evident
in conﬁguration 2 (draft = 70 cm) where CD > 1000, which is an extremely unlikely value for the
drag coeﬃcient. The intermediate sea state EC 6, is modeled with most success and the conditioning
methodology to bring out a single wave event is creating the best ﬁts. This is not a surprise since
the parameters are tuned to a small time series of idealised events, with peaks in an intermediate
range. Thus a smaller amount of non-linearity than what would be expected if the largest peaks
were used.
The second order WAMIT loads failed to improve the results. Further investigation is needed to
assess why. It might be related to the coarse geometry of the cylinder used in the simulations.
6.2
Calm water scenario
In this scenario the actuator is turned on moving the cylinder with diﬀerent settings of frequencies and
amplitudes in calm waters for the three conﬁgurations. There are no waves. As already mentioned
the tests were conducted at two diﬀerent frequencies f = 0.4 Hz and f = 1.6 Hz. These correspond to
the natural heave frequency and ﬂexible mode frequency respectively for the ﬂoating conﬁguration
from the ﬁrst part of the test campaign. For the low frequency setting four diﬀerent amplitudes
were tested z0 = [0.015, 0.030, 0.045, 0.06] m for conﬁguration 1 and 2. The range of motion of the
actuator is the twice the amplitude. For the high frequency case and the inclined conﬁguration only
z0 = [0.030, 0.06] m were investigated.
This section will have an initial look at the force time series and calculate damping estimates from
a velocity/acceleration decomposition of the signal. Furthermore the equation of motion with drag
term as well as a polynomial model will be applied to remodel the force signal.
6.2.1
Initial look at vertical (longitudinal) forces
The scenarios with actuator motion contain a lot of higher harmonic force contributions, thus the
high-cut (low-pass) frequency has a great inﬂuence on the harmonics and thus forces visualized in
this section. To illustrate the inﬂuence Figure 6.16 presents a sample time series from conﬁguration
1 with f = 0.4 Hz and z0 = 6 cm for four diﬀerent high-cut frequencies. The spectrum reveals that
the higher harmonics from the actuator frequency is extremely prevalent across the whole spectrum
visualized. The contributions are, in fact, present up to the Nyquist frequency of ≈800 Hz (though
diﬃcult to see on the ﬁgure). The time signal of the force signal is naturally heave inﬂuenced by
the high frequency-content. The magnitude of the ﬁltered signals are much smaller than for the
full signal. The maximum force in the ﬁltered signal is 7 N whereas for the unﬁltered signal the
maximum force is almost 70 N i.e. ten times higher. The force signal can go from 40 N to -40 N
in less than a tenth of a second. These eﬀects are speculated to be related to a natural frequency
within the load cell and thus not physical.

the surface eﬀects which are considered to be the biggest contributor to the observed diﬀerence in
the linear damping between conﬁguration 1 and 2.

damping gets a horizontal force contribution by the inclined conﬁguration, the distance to the free

of the cylinder under water is 51 cm and thus not close to the 70 cm from conﬁguration 2 i.e. the
diﬀerence in damping is not related to draft alone. For the bow side the wetted length is decreased
by the same length as the stern is increased with. The horizontal contribution in the generated wave
ﬁeld will, however, not ’see’ the surface, so this could be a contributing factor to the larger linear
damping. The WAMIT results for linear damping predicts a larger contribution in conﬁguration 3

6.2.4
Calm scenario sub-conclusion
It has been found that the frequency ﬁltering criteria applied has a severe impact on the time signal
as higher harmonics are extremely dominating in the calm scenario. With a low-pass ﬁlter at 5 Hz
the 12 ﬁrst harmonics are included for the low frequency setting f0 = 0.4 Hz and only the ﬁrst three
harmonics for the high frequency setting f0 = 1.6 Hz.
From the analysis of the linear damping through the decomposition of acceleration and velocity it
was found that the damping is dependent on actuator amplitude and the correlation is linear for the
low frequency setting. Furthermore the radiation damping estimated by WAMIT is more than two
orders of magnitude lower than the calculated linear damping from experiments.
Vortices are present on the surface when the actuator is turned on and thus is it expected that some
energy is dissipated this way. In general surface interactions are a plausible explanation for the
diﬀerences in added mass and damping between the diﬀerent conﬁgurations.
The remodeling found that the added mass calculated through WAMIT is not large enough to
account for the forces in phase with the acceleration. The diﬀerence between the modeled added
mass and the remodeled added mass is in its most severe case a factor of two, which is signiﬁcantly
better than the modeled damping.
Both the polynomial model and the WAMIT based model is successful in remodeling the measured
force with high values of explained standard deviation E and using ensemble averaging improves the
results but not more than a couple of percentages.
It has been found that the inﬂuence of the misalignment of the accelerometer and the Qualysis
system (displacement and velocity) is signiﬁcant and larger than anticipated. Further investigation
is present in Section 7.

6.3.5
Mixed scenario sub-conclusion
In the mixed scenario both of the issue with the actuator stopping and the issue with the faulty
measurements of the load cell are present.
This makes the force model less trustworthy as the
parameters are optimized while attempting to model these non-physical phenomena.
The spectra of the measured signals indicate that the interaction between the wave kinematics
84
==PAGE:102==
generated by the heave plate and the heave plate motion itself at the second harmonic is important.
This eﬀect is dependent on the relation between the incident wave generated by the wave paddle
and the heave plate velocity. As the sea state gets more severe the heave plate self-interaction eﬀect
becomes smaller because the incident vertical particle velocity becomes larger and thus diminishes the
eﬀect of the self-generated wave ﬁeld local to heave plate. This is conﬁrmed by the second harmonic
being more prominent in conﬁguration 2 which has a lower draft and thus less wave kinematics at
the heave plate.
The linear damping term is very sensitive to actuator settings and less sensitive to sea state. The
damping is quadratic by nature as concluded for the calm scenario in Section 6.2.2.
The remodeling proved diﬃcult partly due to the issues related to the actuator suddenly stopping
causing a sinusoidal pattern in the frequency domain in some cases. Furthermore the poor perfor-
mance of the wave force model from the ﬁxed scenario is carried on in this model. The forces are best
modeled in the high frequency settings for two main reasons; The wave loads are small comparing to
the actuator loads when the actuator frequency is large, thus the results are less inﬂuenced by the
poor wave loads model. The cut-oﬀof frequencies higher than 5 Hz leaves the ﬁrst three harmonics
for the high frequency setting but 12 harmonics for the low frequency setting, this means that the
force time signal has less resemblance to a sinusoidal and thus more diﬃcult to model.

8
Concluding remarks
The purpose of this thesis has been to design, plan and execute a test campaign with the goal of
studying the damping eﬀects from heave plates in operational sea states. This section will summarize
the ﬁndings of the report as well as discuss the future exciting possibilities enabled by this test
campaign.
8.1
Main ﬁndings
The design phase was done iteratively in close collaboration and dialogue between DTU and DHI.
Models and calculations needed to be revised as new constraints were introduced or new information
was obtained during the test campaign. The ﬁnal design of the cylinder with heave plate has one
conﬁguration that is close to the known semi-submersible designs i.e. conﬁguration 1. The longer
draft of conﬁguration 2 was introduced to enable a better comparison between the ﬂexible part of
the test campaign and the single cylinder part. As the results from both test campaigns just have
undergone their primary analysis, a comparison between the two is yet to be made. Conﬁguration
3 can be used to understand how the physical behavior of the system changes during inclination.
The angle introduced is not to scale with the known mean pitch angle for ﬂoaters, but was chosen
to ensure measurable diﬀerences between this conﬁguration and the vertical conﬁgurations.
The planning of the test campaign and the work of this thesis has changed throughout the process
mainly due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Furthermore the test plans were changed e.g. the intro-
duction of conﬁguration 2 as the experiments were conducted. This was done to prioritise the most
important parts of the experiments. This is why there is no available harmonic decomposition for
the focused wave groups.
During the experiments and in the preliminary data analysis it was found that the applied wave model
was not estimating large enough surface elevations. As a consequence the data is marked by the fact
that the pitch moment limitations of the load cell, measuring the forces, were exceeded regularly. The
actuator, responsible for the movement of the cylinder, had suddenly and unpredictable interruptions
contaminating the data set with large load spikes outside the modeling domain. The structural
frequency associated to the actuator set-up was also found to introduce diﬃculties, especially in the
modeling eﬀort.
The ﬁndings from the primary analysis is compiled in the Section 6.1.4 on page 63 for the ﬁxed
scenario, Section 6.2.4 on page 76 for the calm scenario and Section 6.3.5 on page 84 for the mixed
scenario. The main ﬁndings in a summarized format are:
• Fixed scenario (Only waves. No actuator motion)
– The load cell limitations are causing un-physical load spikes for EC 11 in all conﬁgurations
and EC 6 for conﬁguration 3.
– Higher harmonic eﬀects in the force are clear and well visualized by the harmonic decom-
position.
– The WAMIT based model has very large drag coeﬃcients for conﬁguration 2. It seems
that a term could be missing from the model.
– The force amplitude transfer function from WAMIT does not provide good results. The
geometry ﬁle used might be too coarse.
• Calm scenario (No waves. Only actuator motion)
– The frequency at which the low pass ﬁlter is applied has a signiﬁcant consequence on the
force signal. It allows a diﬀerent number of harmonics for the two diﬀerent frequency
settings.
95
==PAGE:113==
– The damping is quadratic by nature and scales linearly with actuator amplitude for the
low frequency settings.
– Values for added mass, damping and drag coeﬃcient estimated by the WAMIT model
seem incorrect.
– Remodeling the sinusoidal force signals is successful.
– The accelerometer is slightly out of phase with the QTM-derived acceleration
• Mixed scenario (Both waves and actuator motion)
– Both the eﬀects of exceeding the load cell limitations and the eﬀects of the actuator
stopping are present in the signal to some extent.
– The forcing experienced at the second harmonic of the actuator frequency is due to self-
interaction between the heave plate and wave ﬁeld it generates. This eﬀect diminishes as
the vertical particle velocities becomes comparable to the heave plate velocity.
– The linear damping term changes with sea state.

In the reﬁnement of the data analysis some of the above observations were investigated further in
an attempt to improve the models.
It was found that the structural frequency at 3.3 Hz gives
rise to a sub-harmonic contribution as well. The estimation of the linear damping from the velocity-
acceleration decomposition proved robust. Using a moving mean to calculate the damping as opposed
to using a single value at the actuator frequency provided almost unchanged results. Truncating the
signals from the lower harmonic actuator settings improved the models signiﬁcantly. Furthermore
it was found that the added mass and damping were very sensitive to the small phase shift between
the accelerometer and the QTM-derived acceleration.
The ﬁndings have been used to create an enhanced model in the hope that the model results could be
improved upon. Speciﬁcally the low frequency settings in conﬁguration 1 were truncated at 1.4 Hz
and the QTM-derived acceleration used instead of the accelerometer. The results improved in terms
of explained standard deviation E. The values, however, for especially the added mass indicates that
the model is not suitable enough to model the physical system. Due to time constraints it has not
been possible to revise the enhanced model. As a consequence the model does unfortunately not live
up to its name.
The polynomial model applied throughout this report is promising. The methodology of tuning the
diﬀerent parts separately provides decent results.
8.2
Future analysis
The analysis carried out throughout this report has been primary and many aspects are yet to be
investigated. This section will address issues closely related to the work done in this report as well
as expand the perspective to comment on broader applications.
The main focus of this report has not been remodeling of the force. Further work should include a
force model that is able to accurately predict the forces from sensor data. For this model to work it
is necessary to include the cylinder-mounted accelerometer for an accurate measure of the structural
frequency at 3.3 Hz. For this to work there is a need to time-shift (delay) the accelerometer data
by 0.08 seconds.
Whether a band-pass ﬁlter should be applied on the accelerometer signal e.g.
at 3.3 ± 0.4 Hz or the full range within the global low pass ﬁlter needs to be investigated.
By
phase-shifting the accelerometer data and applying a suitable frequency ﬁlter I expect the structural
frequency to be modeled well. The sub-harmonic and intermediate range harmonic contribution
from the structural excitation seem to have a large inﬂuence on the overall quality of the model in
the wave scenarios. A better modeling and understanding of that structural frequency will improve
the model signiﬁcantly.
96
==PAGE:114==
The enhanced model was found to improve the results compared to the WAMIT model with Morison
drag.
The model, however, is not robust.
There seem to be some coupling of the parameters
estimated by the model. There are two noteworthy conclusions. i) The model needs to be revised
such that new terms are included that are able to account for the eﬀects that the model currently fails
to model. As the polynomial model is performing better in the mixed scenario it should be possible
to enhance the WAMIT based model further to reach an optimum and the best possible model with
the tools already at hand. The addition of new terms and a better modeling and understanding of
the excited structural frequency should yield satisfying results. ii) Because the optimization model
used in MATLAB fmincon has been treated as a black box the optimization is ill-deﬁned and the
model parameters are strongly dependent on initial guesses. A new attempt at modeling should
focus on building a robust and well-deﬁned modeling methodology with a focus on which parameters
might be coupled.
Large non-physical loads have been measured by the load cell due to the exceedance of its limitations.
Furthermore the excess loads coming from the actuator suddenly stopping are present too in the
data. Before further remodeling eﬀort is done, measures should be taken to mitigate some of these
eﬀects. Either the load cell limitations should be ﬁltered with some criteria based on either a speciﬁc
heave force or pitch moment, or EC 11 for all conﬁgurations and EC 6 for conﬁguration 3 should be
disregarded in the analysis. The actuator suddenly stopping in the calm scenario should be rather
straightforward to ﬁlter out of the data. Enough cycles are present to produce meaningful results
even if the measurements are disrupted by the actuator shut-downs.
If the above suggestions fail to improve the models satisfactory new terms can be added. As it
was found that the damping is not linear, an expansion of the model could include a new quadratic
damping term. The Morison drag term has currently been found insuﬃcient, if this is still the case
after the improvements this could be the next action in the modeling eﬀort. The harmonics of the
damping eﬀects can also be investigated by a harmonic decomposition of the QTM signal as a linear
phase shift of 90◦in the actuator signal simply corresponds to a time shift of
