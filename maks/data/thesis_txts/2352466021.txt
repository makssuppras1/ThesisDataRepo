Report Outline
This project is carried out in collaboration with Maersk Maritime Technology, and deals
with the development of a new hatch cover design for the Altair ship class. The target
audience of this report is twofold. The primarily target is Maersk Maritime Technology.
The secondary target is those interested in the application of large scale topology opti-
mization, with a background similar to the authors when the project was initiated. It is
assumed that a common interest for both target audiences are the resulting structures
from the topology optimization. In order to have a proper foundation for presenting the
results, a review of necessary and relevant introductory subjects is therefore included.
Furthermore, it is assumed that the reader has an understanding of the main principles
behind the ﬁnite element method, but not necessarily any exhaustive knowledge of the
principles and methods used in topology optimization.

Abstract
This report describes the development of a new hatch cover design for the container ves-
sel class Maersk Altair, by the use of topology optimization on a large scale. The design
requirements for a cover at a speciﬁc location of the vessel has been determined and
modelled through the use of ﬁnite elements. The topology optimization is based on the
fully parallel TopOpt program, Niels Aage et al., which utilizes the PETSc framework.
The report also contains a description and validation of the modiﬁcations made to the
program, including a robust design formulation and a p-norm stress constraint. A real-
izable and physically realistic hatch cover design has iteratively been made. The design
progression of the cover includes a determination of the dominant load cases, an improve-
ment of the boundary conditions in the ﬁnite element model, and an optimization with
a simpliﬁed robust design formulation, with and without a stress constraint. Finally, in
order to approach a design, which is manufacturable through the means of traditional
fabrication methods, an interpretation of resulting topology optimized structure is made
and analysed.

INTRODUCTION

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The ever increasing competition in the container shipping industry impose a pressure on
companies such as Maersk Maritime Technology to reduce operation cost, and increase
productivity. As a result, the size of the vessels developed within the last couple of years
has steadily increased. The competitiveness of older vessels, which have not yet reached
the end of their lifespan, is therefore reduced. It is therefore sometimes necessary to make
modiﬁcations and use newer technologies, also called retroﬁtting, in order for older ships
to stay relevant and viable.
Several factors inﬂuences the competitiveness of a container ship with one of main issues
being the turnaround time in port. Having the vessel at port is very expensive, which
is mainly due to the inactivity of the vessel and the cost of the harbour service. It is
therefore beneﬁcial for the competitiveness of a container vessel to reduce the time spent
at port. The majority of modern container vessels uses pontoon hatch covers. For this
type, containers are ﬁrst loaded into the cargo hold through large openings in the deck.
The hatch covers are then lifted onto the hatch coaming, by which the openings in the
deck are closed oﬀ. Hereafter, containers are loaded onto the hatch covers themselves. In
ﬁgure 1.1 a sketch of the principle of pontoon hatch covers are shown. For a vessel with
this type of covers, the turn around time can be reduced signiﬁcantly, by reducing the
number of hatch covers. However, this is not a straight forward task, as the maximum
allowable weight of a single cover is determined by the lifting capabilities of the harbour
equipment. Unless the harbour equipment is upgraded on a global scale, it is necessary
to rethink the design of the hatch covers in order to make them suﬃciently light, while
still fulﬁlling the various requirements dictated by the relevant classiﬁcation society.
Furthermore, the competitiveness can be increased by reducing the light-weight of the
vessel, by which the dead-weight can be increased. An increase of the dead-weight allows
for more goods to be shipped, which also increases the competitiveness.

1.2
Hatch Covers
When designing a hatch cover several load cases and regulations must be considered, as the
developed covers must comply with a rule set given by the relevant classiﬁcation society,
which for the Altair ship class is American Bureau of Shipping (ABS). This includes ship
movements, partial loading of the covers, weather loads etc. A thorough elaboration and
discussion of the rules and regulations is done in Section 6.
Here, a brief evaluation of the traditional way of designing hatch covers will be given,
followed by a short analysis of the hatch cover design on the Altair ship class.
1.2.1
Traditional Hatch Cover Design
The current approach to hatch cover design is similar to that used throughout the hull of
the vessel. The main principle is to strengthen the panels by a simple grillage structure,
which is made by a crossover of lifting members. Two diﬀerent types of lifting members
are used - beams and girders. The beams are evenly distributed, and their function is
to support the top plate. The girders are the primary support members, and are located
beneath the container supports, as large concentrated loads will occur at these positions.
The purpose of the girders is to transfer loads from the container stacks to the supports, as
well as supporting the beams. In addition to the shear forces generated by the container
stacks, the girder should also, just like the beams, be able to withstand bending.
When dimensioning a lifting member to withstand bending, the section modulus must be
calculated. The necessary elastic section modulus, Z, can be calculated from the maxi-
mum bending moment and the maximum allowable stress using the following equation,
where σmax < σy:

OBJECTIVE
2
Objective
This project is presented by Maersk Maritime Technology (MMT) and deals with struc-
tural optimization, using large scale topology optimization, of hatch covers for the Altair
container vessel. MMT wishes to reduce the number of covers from 4 to 3 per hatch,
by which the container arrangement must be changed. It is desired that the old 4-4-4-4
arrangement is replaced with a 6-4-6. The transition from 4 to 3 covers per hatch is shown
in ﬁgure 2.1 with respect to container arrangement. The blue boxes represent positions,
where both 20’ and 40’ containers can be stacked, either using Russian stowage, or consist-
ing solely of 20’ or 40’ containers. The green boxes represent 40’ container stacks, which
are partly placed on the covers, and partly on the ship. Besides reducing the number of
covers, it is also desired by MMT to reduce the overall weight of the covers.
Figure 2.1: Old and new container arrangement on hatch covers
The design criteria for the hatch covers, speciﬁed by MMT, are given as follows:
• The computed hatch cover designs should fulﬁl the requirements set by a classiﬁca-
tion society - in this case ABS [2].
• It should be possible to stack the covers, and the allowable stack weight should be
as before.
• The height and depth of a the hatch covers should be as is or lower.
• The covers should be able to withstand deﬂection of the hull.
• Two types of covers are to be designed - a center and a side cover. The center cover
should carry 4 container stacks, whereas the side covers should carry 6.5 stacks each.
• Maximum weight per cover is 40 tonnes.
• A realisable version, which is based on traditional fabrication methods, of the ﬁnal
topology optimized design should be made.

3.2

Gradient based topology optimization is often used on element based models, by assigning
a mathematical density, ρ, to each element. The density can vary from 0 to 1, and is used
to deﬁne the solidity of the element. When ρ = 0 the element is a void and the stiﬀness
close to zero, and if ρ = 1, the element will have full stiﬀness.
It is desired that the hatch covers are optimized to be as stiﬀas possible, as the weight
then can be kept at a minimum.

The objective of the optimization is to minimize compliance, f(ρ), i.e. the inverse of

allowable volume, the compliance minimization would, due to the nature of the problem,

minimum elasticity modulus and it is introduced in order to avoid singularities in the
stiﬀness matrix. The elasticity modulus of the material is set using Emax. The actual
values of Emin and Emax are irrelevant for the topology optimization as we only consider
linear deformations, but, due to numerical reasons, it is required that Emax/Emin ≈1·109.

Besides the necessity of solving the Lagrangian function iteratively, iterative methods are
also needed in order to solve the large scale FE problem. That is, the ﬁnite element

4
Method
As the problems considered, are physically large and with a desire for thin features, the
demand on computational power is high. It was therefore decided to use the open source
topology optimization framework, TopOpt, developed by N. Aage et al. [10].
The framework is based on well known and widely used solvers and optimization algo-
rithms used for topology optimization. However, the main beneﬁt gained by utilizing this
framework is, that the computations become fully parallel. The wall-clock time for large
scale topology optimizations is reduced drastically when the computations are parallel, as
super computers with multiple CPU can be utilized. The full parallelization is achieved by
using a suite of data structures and routines called PETSc (short for Portable, Expendable
Toolkit for Scientiﬁc Computations [7]).
Besides the features already included in the framework, some extensions and alterations
have been made for this project. These are all discussed in the following, alongside some
of the essential principles of the framework.
4.1

A modiﬁcation of the framework was necessary in order to be able to do topology opti-
mization using multiple load cases. This is done by calculating the objective function and
sensitivities by super positioning of the individual objectives and sensitivities from each
load case. It is implemented in a manner, so that a scaling of the objective of each load
case can be applied, which, in practice, is used to deﬁne the importance of each load case.

4.2
Index Sets - Voids and Solids
In some cases it is necessary to force the program to set certain parts of the design domain,
to either a void or solid, which will not be included in the topology optimization. This is
achieved by deﬁning the state of each element as either a void, solid, or active. Only values
concerning active elements will be passed on to the optimization solver. In practise, this
is done by creating a vector, which contains the state of each element, using the numbers
0-2. After the solver has optimized the active design variables, they are then returned to
a global vector, which contain densities for both the active, and the prescribed void and
solid elements.
Due to the design of the ﬁlter function, which is discussed in Section 4.5.1, the elements
marked as void or solid are aﬀected by it. It is therefore necessary to impose the correct
values for the non-active design variables each time after the ﬁlter has been applied. It
must be noted that this introduces a small error in the sensitivities.

4.5
Manufacturability
Topology optimized designs can be diﬃcult, if not in some cases impossible, to manufac-
ture. This is due to several factors which are inherent to the density topology approach,
such as non physical density distributions e.g. checkerboard patterns and undesired ele-
ment densities e.g. 0 < ρe < 1.
The ﬁrst problem is typically solved by implementing a ﬁltering of the solution. Many

diﬀerent ﬁltering approaches exists, but the most popular are sensitivity or density ﬁlter-
ing. Besides removing phenomenons such as checkerboard patterns, ﬁltering also ensures
a mesh independent design. A downside to any method of ﬁltering, when using the den-
sity topology approach, is that the solution contains grey (0 < ρe < 1) transition regions
between solids and voids. The grey transitions are undesirable, as these require some
sort of composite material, or, in the case when only utilising a single material, makes it
impossible to manufacture.
Grey transition areas can be diminished by implementing a projection method, which
ensure a 0/1 solution. This is, in many cases, done by implementing a Heaviside projec-
tion of the topology optimized solution. But, as discussed in [11], this does not always
ensure local mesh independence. A modiﬁed robust design formulation was proposed in
[11], which ensure local convergence, 0/1 solutions, and manufacturing tolerant designs.
The modiﬁed robust design formulation is a modiﬁcation of the original formulation,
which was proposed in [12], the modiﬁed formulation can be seen in Section 4.5.3.

6
Rules and Regulations
The American Bureau of Shipping has speciﬁed the rules and regulations [2] for the hatch
covers on the Altair ship class. It is decided to consider the four hatches forward of the
bridge, hatch 6F-7A, as seen in ﬁgure M.1, as these represent the majority of hatches
(3F-9A), while experiencing the largest possible loads.
6.1
Applied Loads
Depending on the position of the hatch cover diﬀerent rules apply. There is distinguished
between two main positions. The hatch covers in this project is located at a position 2,
as seen in ﬁgure C.1, which includes hatch covers located at least one standard height of
superstructure above the freeboard deck.
The covers will experience three main load types during operation. These are; static,
dynamic, and weather loads. When designing the covers the static loads are excluded, as
these are the smallest. It is therefore, as prescribed by ABS, only necessary to consider the
dynamic loads and those caused by weather pressure. In order to simplify the calculations,
the dynamic loads are applied as static loads, but with contributions due to the movement
of the ship. The weather pressure should not be applied simultaneously with any other
loads, as the weather loads are only present on unloaded covers.
The actual calculations of the applied loads are done in Section 7.3.
6.1.1
Weather Design Pressures
Unloaded covers will experience both vertical and horizontal weather loads during rough
sea. The weather design pressures are speciﬁed for ships above 100m in length.
The vertical weather pressure is speciﬁed in [[2], 3.3.2] as:
pV = 25.51kPa
(6.1)
The horizontal weather pressure is calculated in [[2], 3.5] as seen below, but it is required
that pH ≥25kPa, as prescribed in [[2], table 1].

where aH, cH, R, bH, cL, and f all are empirical values, which are determined on properties

is the vertical distance between the summer load line and the middle of the hatch covers.
The summer load line marks the water level on the ship, when it is loaded as designed,
and the water temperature is at certain point.

6.1.4
Load cases with Partial Loading
Apart from the loads mentioned earlier in this section, the strength of the hatch covers
should be tested for partial loading. ABS prescribes a simpliﬁed approach, where the
hatch cover is loaded without the outermost stacks. Partial loading is only relevant for the
load cases which include roll, as it will otherwise not have an impact on the performance

6.1.5
Loads due to elastic deformation of the ship hull
In [[2], 9.13] it is mentioned that one must account for the transverse loads on the covers,
which are caused by the deformation of the hull. The contribution to the loads from the
movement of the cover supports, should be added to the loads mentioned in the previous
subsections. Hull deformations are rarely accounted for during design of hatchcovers, and
some classiﬁcation societies does not require ship designers to account for it. In order to
keep the problem simple this regulation will therefore not be included in the optimization
process.
6.2
Strength Requirements
Besides the determination of the loads, strength requirements are also imposed on the
covers, as seen in [[2], 9].
The strength requirements discussed here, are relevant for
pontoon hatch covers ﬁtted with gaskets and clamping devices, as seen on the Altair ship
class.
6.2.1
Plate Thickness and General Cover Dimensions
It is stated in [[2], 9.3] that a minimum net plate thickness of 6mm is required. The
net plate thickness is determined by subtracting the corrosion addition of 1mm cf. [[2],
table 8], for each surface which is in contact with air. The typical minimum gross plate
thickness is therefore tmin = 8mm.
Besides the requirement of a minimum plate thickness, requirements to the net scantlings
of both secondary stiﬀeners and primary support members are given. These are based on
the traditional grillage construction, and therefore not directly applicable for the topology
optimized designs. Furthermore, requirements are also given for the spacing of primary
members, but these are not necessary to consider when FE analysis is carried out.
6.2.2

elements. The equivalent von Mises stress σV M must not surpass 0.9σy for calculations
based on the net thickness. In [[2], 9.1.1] it is stated how the equivalent stress must be
determined when plane stress or shell elements is used. furthermore, it is also stated that
the stress must be computed for the center of the elements.

6.2.4
Buckling
It is stated in [[2], 9.17] that a suﬃcient buckling strength is to be demonstrated for hatch
cover structures. A method, used to determine the buckling strength, is presented for a
hatch cover with a traditional grillage structure. This is not applicable to the topology
optimized hatch covers, which are presented in Section 8, and is therefore not considered
during the process of topology optimization. In order to evaluate the resistance against
buckling of the optimized covers it is necessary to solve an eigenvalue problem. This is
omitted, as it is not feasible to do so during the optimization, by which the optimized
covers would most likely have a low resistance against buckling. Instead, the buckling
strength of an interpreted topology optimized design, should be evaluated at a later stage
in the design process.

8
Design Progression
In this section, the progression of the topology optimization is reviewed.
The design
will, based on the experiences made throughout the project, gradually converge towards
a realizable and physically realistic design, which is in compliance with the rules and
regulations. In order to reduce the computational requirements, only the center cover is
considered during this process.
The design progressing consists of four design iterations:
• 1st design iteration: Determination of the dominating load cases, by which the
computational wall-clock time can be reduced for subsequent design iterations
• 2nd design iteration: Realistic boundary conditions are applied to the FEM
• 3rd design iteration: Simpliﬁed robust design formulation is utilized in order to
improve the manufacturability
• 4th design iteration: The maximum stress level is reduced by implementing a stress
constraint in the optimization
8.1
1. Design Iteration - Determining Dominating Load Cases
The purpose of the ﬁrst design iteration is to determine if any of the considered load cases
can be disregarded, while still obtaining a structure, that performs well for all load cases -
i.e. to determine the dominating load cases. The dominating load cases are determined by
computing a number of designs, each optimized for a single load case. The performance of
the computed designs are evaluated for each load case. The results hereof are compared
to the performance of a single cover, which has been optimized for all load cases.
The beneﬁt of determining the dominant load cases is that the computational wall-clock
time of the subsequent optimizations can be reduced, as redundant load cases can be
discarded, by which the number of FE problems (equation (3.1)) can be reduced.
8.1.1
Basis for Optimization
As a basis for the ﬁrst design iteration it is chosen to use the same amount of material
as an an average center cover. The average weight of the center cover, for the considered
hatches are Wavg = 32704kg. A volume fraction of 3.3% is therefore chosen, which results
in a cover weight of 32700kg. Furthermore, the optimizer is given as much freedom as
possible by choosing a small ﬁlter radius, such that the obtained designs can be used as
a benchmark, for the subsequent design iterations. A ﬁlter radius of 1.5el. is therefore
chosen.
The settings for the optimization can be seen in table 8.1. It must be mentioned that the
limitation of 120 iterations is imposed in order to reduce the computational wall-clock
time. After the optimization is done, a threshold is applied to the obtained solutions.
This is done in order to ensure designs, which can be manufactured using steel, and for
which computed stresses can be considered valid. The measure of non discreteness was
evaluated, in order to make sure that the impact of applying the threshold would be

eroded design. In both the blue-print and the dilated design it is seen that elements with
a thickness of 2 elements are present. These would be removed during the optimization,
as they are not present in the eroded design, which is used when the FE problem is solved.

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The main purpose of this project was to reduce the number of covers per hatch from 4
to 3 for the container vessel class Maersk Altair, as well as decreasing the light-weight of
the vessel.
Finite element models, based on design speciﬁcations and requirements, determined for
a representative hatch, were created. The method of compliance minimization through
topology optimization were then to be applied to these models, in order to obtain cover
designs with an optimized stiﬀness to weight ratio.
The topology optimization was carried out using a modiﬁed version of the TopOpt pro-
gram, which utilizes the PETSc framework to ensure fully parallel computations. Besides
the modiﬁcations, several additions has been made, such as the implementation of a sim-
pliﬁed robust design formulation, and a p-norm stress constraint. The simpliﬁed robust
design formulation was employed in order to ensure a more manufacturability tolerant
design, where as the p-norm stress constraint would reduce the maximum stress level.
Two cover designs where to be made. A side cover and a center cover. Due to high
computational demands, it was decided to focus the work on the center cover, as this is
the physically smallest. At the ﬁnal design iteration a design for the side cover were then
to be computed. This was however not possible due to lack of time and limitations on
the physical memory of the cluster.
In this report, the iterative progress of obtaining an easily manufacturable topology op-
timized hatch cover, which is in compliance with the rules and regulations, is presented.
The ﬁrst step was to the dominating load cases, such that subsequent designs could be
computed as eﬃciently as possible. This was achieved by comparing the performance of
designs optimized for individual load cases in a cross-check table. These results were then
compared individually, and to the results for a design optimized for all load cases. It was
concluded that the vertical weather load and the 20’ heel condition were representative
for all load cases, for this particular type of optimization.
By analysing the results from the ﬁrst design iteration, it was determined that the supports
applied in the FEM allowed for absorption of bending moment. As this is not physically
correct, an investigation was carried out in order to determine realistic supports. It was
determined that line supports would represent the physical problem better, and a new
design was then computed based on these ﬁndings. It was seen that the performance was
not impacted by the change in support, instead, the optimizer changed the principle of
the structure.
As the solver was given as much freedom as possible by choosing a ﬁlter radius of 1.5
elements, the complexity of designs obtained in iteration 1 and 2 were very high. The
high level of complexity made the designs impossible to manufacture using traditional
methods, as desired by Maersk Maritime Technology. A minimum length scale was there-
fore imposed on the solver in the third design iteration, by implementing the simpliﬁed
robust design formulation. As a result hereof, the complexity was reduced to a point,
where the cover could be manufactured using traditional fabrication methods.
When the performance of the 3rd design iteration was compared to that of iteration 2,
a slight decrease was seen for all load cases, except two. The decrease in performance
Technical University of Denmark,
Department of Mechanical Engineering

From the performance evaluation of the third iteration, it was seen that the stress criteria

An attempt to reduce the maximum stress was therefore made.

Outlook
As the competitiveness in the freight industry continues to increase, so does the demand
for more advanced structural designs, which serves to increase the eﬀectiveness.
The
method of topology optimization is already seen implemented in the aviation industry,
but it continues to expand to other industries, like the shipping industry. As the available
computational power increases, so those the use of large scale optimizations.
In this
project, topology optimization has been applied on a large scale, as a tool of designing
new hatch covers for the Maersk Altair ship class. Several problems has been encountered
during the process of utilizing topology optimization on a large scale, and then, through
iteration, converging towards a manufacturable design. More work is therefore required
in order to take the ﬁnal product of this project to a production ready state.
If this project had been provided with more time, the following tasks would have been
commenced:
• Investigate if the inclusion of LC 6 in design iteration 3 substantially aﬀects the
design. If so, it should be included in the optimization
• Compute design for side cover, where both the robust design formulation, and the
stress constraint has been utilized with all the necessary load cases
• Evaluate performance of topology optimized covers using commercial software, such
as COMSOL
• Based on the performance evaluation reduce or adjust the volume fraction used
in the optimizations, compute new designs, and evaluate the performance hereof.
Repeat until either the stress or deﬂection limitation has been reached, in order to
minimize cover weight
• Create design interpretations of both side and center cover based on the outcome of
the previous point, and evaluate performance of these. If necessary, make changes
to the design interpretations, such that they fulﬁl all of the rules and requirements
set by ABS
• Implement hold down devices, stacking feet, etc. in order to ensure proper func-
tionality of the ﬁnal cover designs
• Determine a simple way to support the top plate on the optimized design for the
weather load.
Apart from the tasks that were not completed, the work done in this project could initiate
some further studies:
• Implementation of Linear buckling in the PETSc framework, and optimization to-
gether with the robust design formulation and stress constraint.
• Vibration analyses of the top plate.
