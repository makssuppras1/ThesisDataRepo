Corporate Social Responsibility and Customer 
Loyalty in the Pharmaceutical Industry 
Christina-Anthoula Katsaiti 
 
Abstract 
Purpose: This study aims to explore the relationship between Corporate Social Responsibility 
(CSR) and customer loyalty of two counterpart groups, of patients with psoriasis and psoriatic 
arthritis, in Denmark and Greece and which are the main factors that are associated with these 
preferences. 
Design/methodology/approach: This paper used a survey to assess the perception of customer 
in CSR and the relation to customer loyalty in the pharmaceutical industry. The questionnaires 
were administered to patients of two counterpart organizations in Denmark and Greece. 
Regressions and t-tests were performed to test the hypotheses. Analysis was conducted with R 
statistical software. 
Findings: The results confirm that corporate CSR has a positive association with customer 
loyalty. Customers in Denmark and Greece showed diverse preferences in CSR activities, 
establishing the need for differentiated strategies. 
Originality/value: The novelty of this project is that it targets a specific group of customers of 
the pharmaceutical industry (patients of psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis) in two distinct 
countries attempting to understand and evaluate preferences of CSR activities and their 
correlation to customer loyalty. 
Research limitations/implications: Research limitation of this study is the size of the sample. 
More reliable results could be extracted if the sample had been bigger. 
Practical implications: Pharmaceutical companies could learn from patients how to form a 
valuable to society strategy. Patients could participate in co-creation and have an insight on 
corporate social responsibility strategies. 
Keywords: Social responsibility, Customer loyalty, Pharmaceutical industry, Psoriasis, 
Psoriatic arthritis. 
Paper type: Research paper. 
 
Introduction 
The last decades social responsibility has been drawing more and more attention from 
companies. The main reason for this is society’s expectations for companies to contribute to 
social and environmental development, and not only profiting (Filho et al., 2019a). Today, 
social responsibility is considered to be important for corporate survival. Social actions are part 
of corporate competitive strategies (Filho et al., 2019b). Corporate social responsibility (CSR) 
has a significant impact on the behaviour of customers’ purchasing habits, identity of products, 
loyalty and satisfaction (Chung et al., 2015). In gaining attraction from customers, CSR can be 
more efficacious than advertisements and has the ability to boost customer loyalty (Cole, 2017). 
This trend arises as a consequence of "generalized reciprocity", through which consumers 
compensate a company for the indirect advantages they receive when CSR activities have a 
positive impact on the community (Cole, 2017). In order for companies to perform CSR 
activities effectively, it is fundamental, to understand the expectations of customers regarding 
them, and identify to which of them, customers are probably to react (Lee et al., 2019). 
Customer loyalty is an essential focus point for the survival and growth of a business. Creating 
a loyal customer base has become not only a major marketing objective, but also an important 
foundation for the creation of a sustainable competitive advantage. Accordingly, recognizing 
loyalty maintenance or retention is considered a key element in providing long-term corporate 
profitability, because revenues can be boosted over a customer's lifetime by maintaining them 
==PAGE:3==
3 
 
(Chung et al., 2015).  Moreover, the highly competitive marketplace forces companies to put a 
continuous effort in order to cover the ever-growing customer demands and needs 
(Stanisavljević, 2017).  
As in other sectors, also in the pharmaceutical, the importance of CSR is well known, and a 
standard activity (Lee et al., 2019). On the global market, the pharmaceutical industry struggles 
with economic and political challenges (Smith, 2008). Pharmaceutical companies are often the 
target of social criticism and struggle with public acceptance. Although they represent a 
category of companies that save millions of lives, criticism and public disapproval about their 
practices is damaging their reputation (Kazazis, 2011). As loyal customers appear to purchase 
more from a company over time, pharmaceuticals embrace CSR philanthropic endeavours and 
try to communicate these actions to the public. Customer feedback could be of aid to 
incorporate social issues into CSR approaches and address the demands of key stakeholders, 
instead of implementing CSR programs without considering societal needs (Lee et al., 2019). 
Studies demonstrate the positive association between CSR and customer loyalty (Martínez & 
Rodríguez del Bosque, 2013). Even though research regarding CSR and customer loyalty 
exists, there is a gap in literature, as some critical issues have not been explored. For example, 
the effect of culture on the connection between CSR-customer loyalty relations. Preferences of 
customers (patients) of different cultures within the pharmaceutical industry hasn’t been 
explored thoroughly (to the authors knowledge). The purpose of this paper is to investigate how 
loyalty of 2 counterpart groups, of patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis, is affected by 
CSR practices of the pharmaceutical industry in Denmark and Greece and to cross examine the 
preferences of CSR activities between the two groups. 
 
Research framework and hypotheses 
Corporate social responsibility  
Various forms of corporate social responsibility exist for a long period of time. Evidence shows 
that businesses have been interested in societal concerns for centuries (Carroll, 1999). 
References on social responsibility appear in literature from the 20th century and on, mainly the 
last 60-70 years. Thus, it is fair to say that the modern era of CSR started in the 1950s (Carroll, 
2009). Kotler et al. define corporate social responsibility as the commitment of corporates to 
promote collective wellbeing through volunteering actions and contributions employing their 
own resources (Kotler & Lee, 2005). CSR activities include a range of strategies, such as a) 
contributions to national and local charities (philanthropy), b) performance of good deeds 
without expecting anything in return (volunteering), c) treatment of workers equally and 
ethically (ethical work practices) and d) introduction of greener business operations 
(environmental efforts) (Carroll, 1991). This commitment to common good should be 
publicized in order for a company to be considered socially responsible. Actions and practices 
(monetary or not) should be incorporated in their strategy and materialized by adopting new 
business practices. 
Customers expect companies to be socially responsible and take a stand on CSR issues such as 
sustainability, transparency or fair employment practices. Confidence of customers on 
companies is increased by their awareness of CSR actions that represent the organizational 
values (Martínez & Rodríguez del Bosque, 2013). They reward companies who align their 
strategies with common good. A survey that started in 1993 showed that 80 percent of the 
participants stated a more positive view for organizations that provided funding to causes of 
their interests. Mohr et al. (2001) conducted research to establish customers’ expectations on 
social responsibility of companies by using semi-structured interviews to best represent the 
variety of ideas and feelings of consumers about CSR. The results showed that most customers 
==PAGE:4==
4 
 
appreciate or would be interested in rewarding companies that choose to contribute to charitable 
donations (Mohr et al., 2001). 
Even though there is extended literature on CSR and international development there is only a 
small amount of literature dealing specifically with CSR activities in global health and 
pharmaceutics (Blowfield & Frynas, 2005). A few papers have studied whether pharmaceutical 
companies are meeting their human rights obligations, though, this literature lacks in 
considering CSR holistically (Droppert & Bennett, 2015). Even though consumers are 
interested in CSR, there is an ambiguity regarding what it entails. To understand why and how 
multinational pharmaceutics engage in CSR activities to improve universal health, Droppert et 
al. (2015) conducted a 2-phase study, the results of which revealed: a) the need to create explicit 
and standardized definitions of CSR in pharmaceutics and b) the need to carry out further 
country-level research in order to explore how CSR interfaces with national health systems 
(Droppert & Bennett, 2015). 
Volodina et al. (2009) stated that CSR is a matter of great significance for pharmaceutical 
companies because of the high number of ethical points that are associated with them and the 
high exposure of people to their products. For the pharmaceutical products, conventional 
market forces of supply and demand cannot always be practiced, as a) patients do not actually 
choose whether to purchase a drug or not, b) patients typically cannot make educated decisions 
on which medication to purchase. They must trust the opinions of health practitioners, such as 
physicians and pharmacists, c) the result of not being able to access medications or buy a drug 
is frequently a matter of life and death. As pharmaceuticals in most countries are mostly private 
enterprises, their core business is to profit for their stakeholders through production and sales 
of drugs. However, adoption of CSR principles can affect how medicinal products are priced,  
their pricing methods and fairness in particular countries (Volodina et al., 2009).  
 
Customer loyalty 
As mentioned Stanisavljević (2017), loyalty can be described as the absolute commitment of a 
customer to the company and its strong relationship with the brand, which under normal 
circumstances is unlikely to be affected.  Customer loyalty refers to the desire to apply a series 
of behavioural factors that signify the incentive to continue the relationship with a particular 
company, including increased spending on the company's products, positive word of mouth and 
frequent purchases (Stanisavljević, 2017). Indeed, she mentions that, customer loyalty 
increased by 5% will grow profits by 25% to 85%. Similarly, a rise in customer retention by 
2% has almost the same impact as a cost reduction of 10%.  
To assess customer loyalty three approaches are widely acknowledged: attitudinal, behavioural 
and combinational loyalty (Mandhachitara & Poolthong, 2011). Behavioural loyalty refers to 
repeating purchases of products or services, though does not guarantee satisfaction to the brand. 
Attitudinal loyalty is defined to be the predisposal of customers to a brand as an equation of 
psychological processes, which entails perspectival preference and commitment to a brand 
(Fitzgibbon & White, 2005). The combinational approach is the most fitting one to assessing 
loyalty as it includes the application of both attitudinal and behavioural aspects and has been 
widely used in the marketing literature to investigate customer loyalty (Mandhachitara & 
Poolthong, 2011).  
Customer loyalty, satisfaction, trust and commitment towards a company can be increased with 
the implementation of CSR policies (Servera-Francés & Piqueras-Tomás, 2019). Servera-
Frances and Piqueras-Tomas (2019), proposed and tested a model of causal relationships, by 
analysing a sample of 408 consumers. The variance-based method to estimate the structural 
model, predicted that CSR affected consumers, mostly through a range of variables, such as 
==PAGE:5==
5 
 
perceived value, trust and commitment, resulting in increased satisfaction and loyalty (Servera-
Francés & Piqueras-Tomás, 2019). 
The pharmaceutical industry is putting effort to listen to patients needs in order to be able to 
interpreter them, so to develop and adapt to effective strategies that could enhance customer 
loyalty. One of the few studies in exploring the relationship between CSR and customer 
reaction to it, in the pharmaceutical industry, illustrated customers’ responses to CSR practices 
with the use of empirical data. Lee et al. (2019) conducted a two group (healthy individuals and 
patients) 1298-respondent survey. They investigated public perception of CSR practices of 
pharmaceutics in Korea in order to identify the consumer attraction, CSR practices receive as 
well as to measure corporate performance in attracting public interest to their CSR activities. 
They also examined the various types of preferred CSR practices and the interlinked factors. 
The results showed that the majority of the respondents took an interest in the pharmaceutical 
companies’ CSR activities, although they reported high rates in lack of understanding and 
knowledge of CSR activities. Both groups showed the highest preference for "promoting public 
health" among the listed categories of CSR activities (Lee et al., 2019). 
Based on the above discussion, the following hypotheses were formulated:  
Hypothesis 1: CSR has a significantly positive influence on customer loyalty in the 
pharmaceutical industry. 
Hypothesis 2: Perceptions about CSR activities have a statistically significant influence on 
customer loyalty. 
 
Patients organizations, psoriatic associations  
The European Medicines Agency defines Patients’ organizations as: not-for profit 
organizations which are patient focused, and whereby patients and/or carers (the latter when 
patients are unable to represent themselves) represent a majority of members in governing 
bodies”(Schott et al., 2013). These organizations are striving to improve their constituencies’ 
organizational and governance systems with the intent of helping patient groups become more 
robust, sustainable and efficient. They are involved in building capacity for policy-makers, 
business, academics and the media (Sienkiewicz et al., 2017). In Europe, patients’ organizations 
play an active role that makes them, valued and trusted partners thus their opinion is valuable 
to the market (Sienkiewicz et al., 2017). Amongst patients’ organizations are the psoriatic 
arthritis and psoriasis patient advocacy organizations which are dedicated to raise public 
awareness and patient education; foster access to effective therapies partner with doctors and 
other groups to promote the creation of new therapies; and support research to shape successful 
treatments and cure psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis (Zimmerman et al., 2005).  
Psoriasis is a chronic disease of the skin which sometimes affects also the joints and has an 
adverse physical, psychological and social impact (WHO, 2016). Treatment of psoriasis has 
become increasingly effective, but, until now, physical symptoms can only be temporarily 
relieved. Therefore, dealing with psoriasis in everyday life is a struggle for patients (De Korte 
et al., 2004).  According to World Psoriasis Day consortium almost 3% of the population 
endures symptoms of psoriasis (WHO, 2016). In Denmark 2,2% of the population, almost 
126.000 people is affected by psoriasis (Egeberg et al., 2017). In Greece it is estimated that 2%-
2.5% of the population, 214.000-268.000 people is affected by psoriasis. Psoriasis drug 
treatments can be segmented into topicals, systemic, and biologics (Efthymiadis et al., 2015) 
In Denmark psoriatic and psoriatic arthritis patients have been represented actively since 1971 
from the Danish association of psoriasis, “Psoriasisforeningen”. Psoriasisforeningen, is an 
==PAGE:6==

active member of the International Federation of Psoriasis Associations, IFPA, and of the 
federation of psoriasis patients’ associations based in the geographic region of Europe, 
EUROPSO. In Greece an association for patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis is 
“Epidermia”. Epidermia, was established in 2014 and has been an active member of IFPA and 
EUROPSO.  
 
Effect of national identity in Corporate Social Responsibility. 
The culture of a country has been recognized as a central environmental trait causing systematic 
differences in consumer behaviour. It is a key driver which shapes the perceptions, personal 
temperament and behaviours of individuals. It affects the satisfaction of needs consumers fulfil 
when purchase and use products (Steenkamp et al., 1999). 
Institutional frameworks that have evolved traditionally from "national business systems", can 
interpreter differences in CSR in diverse countries. These frameworks consist of the political 
system, the financial system, education and labor and the cultural system. The way of 
integration into a national framework, stems from the "societal effects" and the "varieties of 
capitalism".  The capitalistic economy can be organized in numerous ways, within a range that 
varies between the poles of liberal market economies (USA, UK, Canada, Australia, New 
Zealand, and Ireland) and coordinated market economies (Germany, Japan, Sweden, Austria) 
(Habisch et al., 2005). 
The "varieties of capitalism" explain that socioeconomic performance is mostly better if 
countries fit in one or the other type of poles respectively. Nations, such as countries in the 
Mediterranean ring or small countries, generally lie between these extremes, thus are "hybrids" 
and their performance cannot be predicted as accurately (Hall & Soskice, 2001). This argument 
was questioned and revised in order to best fit the small countries paradox, e.g. Denmark or 
Switzerland. Even though the Mediterranean countries have highly developed domestic 
markets, small countries seem to be more flexible economically than big countries. 
Culture is one of the main factors shaping ethical decision-making. Therefore, the effectiveness 
of social responsibility can vary from country to country with different cultures. Moon et al. 
(2014) mention that collectivist-based consumers put greater emphasis on social responsibility 
initiatives than consumers from more individualistic cultures. In particular, the positive effects 
of social responsibility rely on the assumption that individuals evaluate themselves as part of a 
wider community. Individualist cultures emphasize the individual's short-term development, 
while collectivist cultures recognize group needs and the advantages of consensus (Moon et al., 
2015). 
Ioannou et al. (2012) stressed that cultural traits can influence CSR practices. A method to 
assess corporate responsible actions is by measuring corporate social performance (CSP). CSP 
is directly linked to CSR, as it measures the outcome of social performance. Ioannou et al. 
(2012) investigated, empirically the impact of nation-level institutions on firms’ corporate 
social performance. By using a 42 country, of approximately 2000 corporations’, sample, after 
combining 7-year data from different databases, they managed to design an annual composite 
CSP index of every company based on social and environmental metrics. The results indicated 
that cultural characteristics have an important role in explaining the heterogeneity in corporate 
social performance between companies (Ioannou & Serafeim, 2012). 
Organizational strategies are shaped by numerous factors; not only legal factors govern 
strategies but also a set of unofficial rules or common knowledge that is attained by participants 
due to their national identity, history and culture. Differences in market economies lead to 
diverse responses on shocks. Consequently, companies are trying to become socially stabilizing 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
actors, by adjusting to changing circumstances. A form to respond is by adjusting and 
implementing national CSR strategies that would boost the interest of all stakeholders, increase 
the competitive advantage and satisfy societal needs.  
Based on the above discussion, the following hypotheses were formulated:  
Hypothesis 3: Compared to Danish respondents, Greek respondents are expected to have 
different preferences for corporate social responsibility activities. 
Hypothesis 4: Compared to Danish respondents, Greek respondents are expected to have 
different expectations for corporate social responsibility activities. 
 
Research design 
A quantitative methodology is followed in this analysis. The quantitative phase entailed the 
collection of data of 142 respondents from which 126 valid responses were collected, after 
eliminating irrecoverable and invalid responses. The study was conducted from 24/04/2020-
12/06/2020. The valid responses accounted for an effective answer rate of 89%. A questionnaire 
was used in order to test the insight of Greek and Danish patients in pharmaceutical companies’ 
social responsibility activities as well as to investigate how CSR affects their customer loyalty. 
 
Questionnaire design and measures of constructs 
The questionnaire was a in the form of an online self-administered multilingual survey (Danish, 
Greek, English), that was distributed to patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis in 
Denmark and Greece, with the aid of the two associations, Psoriasisforeningen and Epidermia 
in order to ensure that only patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis were going to 
participate. It entailed three thematic sections, the first section addressed demographic 
characteristics of the sample, sex, age, education as well as nationality of the sample. The 
second section addressed CSR. Finally, the third part addressed customer loyalty in connection 
to CSR. The present questionnaire is the combination of two pre-existing questionnaires that 
were developed by Lee et al., (2019) (second section) and Stanisavljevic, (2017) (third section) 
and was developed to address the research question properly. Some questions have been 
modified to reflect policies that have been in use in Europe and address the European sample. 
Statistical data analysis was performed with the use of R statistical software. 
Demographics showed that, the majority of respondents were female (52%). The age group that 
participated the most were people of 25-34 years old (23%). The larger part of the participants 
has completed tertiary education (53%). The nationality with the highest involvement level is 
Danish (55%). A description of the sample is illustrated in Table I.

The second section of the questionnaire presented a concise overview of CSR activities, in order 
to aid respondents, have a clear understanding of CSR. The assessment of the attitudes of 
respondents towards pharmaceutical CSR activities was conducted by inquiring about 
participation, knowledge, and personal experience with pharmaceutical companies’ CSR 
activities. The participants were also asked to evaluate pharmaceutical involvement to CSR 
compared to other industries to assess corporate involvement in implementation of CSR 
initiatives. The survey included a total of thirteen types of CSR activities that Lee et al.(2019) 
designed based on the concept of CSR by the International Organization for Standardization, 
preceding literature and findings of the investigation of CSR activities of pharmaceutical 
companies in Korea (Lee et al., 2019). For this reason, some activities were modified according 
to European standards. Respondents were also asked for their preferences and expectations 
regarding social contribution of CSR by using a 5-point Likert scale where higher scores 
suggested a higher preference or expected effect. 
The third section is focused on customer loyalty. For this paper, the hybrid approach to measure 
customer loyalty, involving both attitudes and behaviour is considered the most fitting as it 
incorporates the two key factors of customer decision taking. This section of the questionnaire 
includes four elements that are commonly used in customer loyalty research and was developed 
by Stanislavitc (2017). A fifth element was added to address the unconventional market forces 
of supply and demand that exist in the pharmaceutical market. The respondents indicated their 
level of agreement or disagreement on all issues using a 5-point Likert scale, where higher 
scores suggested higher significance. The questionnaire consisted of 7 questions with sub-
questions measuring 8 main variables.

Research analysis and results 
Variation analysis of nationality οn CSR 
First the analysis of the influence of nationality on CSR was carried out to define any 
statistically significant differences in CSR perceptions among respondents of different 
nationalities. Analysis of frequency distribution of all questions that reflected respondents’ 
attitudes toward CSR activities in the pharmaceutical industry was conducted. For every CSR 
activity, the mean and standard deviation score for respondents’ preferences for and 
expectations of social contribution effects were computed and compared between the Danish 
and Greek groups. T-test was performed to identify significant differences. Moreover, CSR 
activities were ranked with a descending order for both countries according to preference. 
 
Attitudes towards CSR 
The interest of respondents (Danish and Greek) to CSR, their experience of CSR activities, 
their perception to other industries and how familiar the CSR concept was measured. Moreover, 
descriptive analysis was carried out to test Hypothesis 3, Hypothesis 4 and to assess the 
significance of CSR preferences of activities and CSR expectations of activities.  
The results showed that both patient groups of respondents were interested in CSR equally 
(58%). 42% of the Greek patient group and Danish patient group expressed awareness of 
pharmaceutical companies’ CSR activities. 65% of Danes stated that they have never 
experienced CSR activities from pharmaceutical companies. For the Greek sample the 
percentage of patient that haven’t experienced CSR activities was found to be lower (56%). 
33% of Danes believe that the pharmaceutical industry is more active than other industries and 
23% characterizes CSR activity equal to other industries. The Greeks answered the same 
question with 26% and 27% respectively. Regarding CSR preference for activities and CSR 
expectation of activities it is depicted that Greeks scored higher in all activities showing a 
difference in the response pattern compared to Danes. Figures 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5 below illustrate 
the results of the survey.

Preferences on CSR activities 
The mean scores for preferences on all CSR activities are found to be positive. Mean score is 
higher of 3,00, for every type of CSR activity that was included in the survey as it is depicted 
on table III. The mean scores of all six categories that include the activities are also found to be 
higher than 3,00. The top three categories for the Danish respondents are found to be category 
A “promoting public health”, category E “environmental protection” and category B 
“improving work and welfare environments for employees” (ranking 1, 2, 3 respectively). For 
the Greek respondents, even though the choices are the same, ranking differs. The first 3 
preferences on categories of activities are found to be category A “promoting public health”, 
category B “improving work and welfare environments for employees” and category E 
“environmental protection” (ranking 1, 2,3 respectively). 
CSR activities, between the 2 groups have statistically significant differences for most 
categories. For activity 1, activity 2, activity 3 and activity 12 no statistical significance is 
observed in responses. Activity 1, activity 2 and activity 3 are identified to be the most preferred 
CSR activities for both groups. Both groups ranked activity 11 as their least preferred. Both 
Greeks and Danes characterize the most preferred CSR activity to be “Support for research on 
new drug development”. 
The results of the conducted research showed that for 69% of CSR activities, the counterpart 
groups had statistically significant differences, thereby confirming Hypothesis 3.

The effects of CSR on customer loyalty 
Multivariate linear regressions were calculated to predict customer loyalty based on CSR 
awareness to verify Hypothesis 1, and to determine the effect of CSR preferences on customer 
loyalty in order to verify Hypothesis 2. 
In multiple regressions, adjusted r, explains how much of the variance in the dependent variable 
(customer loyalty) is explained by the model and beta variable justifies how strongly each of 
the independent variables contributed to R2 (Rencher, 2005).  
 
The effects of CSR awareness on customer loyalty 
To investigate respondents’ awareness of CSR in the pharmaceutical industry and to identify a 
possible correlation to customer loyalty, regression analyses were conducted for the 2 groups 
separately. The first regression was performed on CSR awareness (CSR awareness on the 
==PAGE:16==

survey was measured with two items, questions 1 and 2) to explain customer loyalty for the 
Danish group. The second regression was performed on CSR awareness to explain customer 
loyalty, for the Greek sample.  
The results showed that for the Danish respondents adjusted R2 was 0,3259, which is an 
indicator that questions 1 and 2, explain 32,59% of the total variance or loyalty. P-value was 
8,319e-07 (<0,05). From the analysis it is depicted that standardized coefficient of question 1 
contributes by 60,72% to the relationship of customer loyalty and CSR. As significance of 
question 2 is higher than 0,05, makes its contribution to the relationship unreliable. Table VI 
summarizes the outcome of regression.

Discussion and Conclusion 
Overall, the results of this study contribute to a comprehensive theoretical and empirical 
foundation for understanding the effects of pharmaceutical industry’s CSR on customer loyalty. 
This study examines CSR activities and attitudes that can play a role in enhancing customer 
loyalty of patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis. It also investigates the preferences and 
expectations in CSR activities of the pharmaceutical industry of the 2 counterpart groups of 
patients in Denmark and Greece to test national differences in choices.   
To begin with, the pharmaceutical industry can realize CSR activities that can be appealing to 
their customers (patients). Results presented here regarding preferences and expectations, are 
consistent to findings of Lee et al. (2019) which highlighted that the most preferred CSR 
activities (of pharmaceutical companies) for customers were related to new drug development. 
This stresses patients’ beliefs that pharmaceutical companies should above all satisfy their 
unmet needs by creating new medicines (Lee et al., 2019). 
Moreover, this study proved that environmental protection activities and improvement of work 
and welfare environments for employees scored higher compared to other activities for patients

Additionally, the analysis confirmed statistically significant differences between the 2 groups 
regarding CSR preferences and expectations, thus diversity in strategic approach of 
pharmaceutical companies for different countries is recommended. The findings are aligned 
with the results that were presented by Moon et al. (2014), which explained that national culture 
moderated the influence of CSR associations on social-concept connection. 
The correlation of CSR activities- customer loyalty provided limited results and only for the 
Danish group where it was evident that only “improvement of working conditions and welfare” 
was significant. This could derive from the demand of customers to buy products that are made 
under good working conditions as nowadays customers are not only satisfied with low prices; 
but they are interested in products that are manufactured ethically and sustainably (Neumann 
et al., 2014).  
Furthermore, the results showed margin for improvement of current strategies of 
pharmaceutical companies, as most of the sample (58% of Danish and Greek patients) weren’t 
informed of CSR activities in the pharmaceutical industry. Likewise, 65% of Danes and 56% 
of Greeks mentioned that they have never experienced pharmaceutical companies’ CSR 
activities. Thus, raising awareness of CSR and intensifying CSR activities should be a priority. 
Pharmaceutical companies must recognize that investing in differentiated CSR initiatives is a 
key strategic step which can contribute to maintaining customer loyalty in different countries. 
Pharmaceutical companies should invite patients and patient association in order to shape 
together effective and useful CSR strategies that could be beneficial to all parties. Publicizing 
these activities is crucial as it can boost company’s reputation among consumers, build trust 
and thus ensure their loyalty. 
 
Limitations and future research 
Results of this study should be interpreted with caution. This study delivers a useful first 
approximation to the correlation of CSR and customer loyalty in the pharmaceutical industry. 
The sample that is presented here is limited. Further studies should be conducted on a larger 
sample. A European sample of patients with psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis, to cross examine 
differences, in a European level could be very useful to patients and companies. Global research 
could be conducted to cross examine continental differences if applicable. Moreover, research 
could be carried out for different patient associations.  
More reliable results could have been obtained if survey had been conducted in a national level, 
for different pharmaceutical companies so to have results for each company. Since all 
participants were members of the 2 associations, there is the possibility that the responses are 
under clustering bias. As associations are supported by pharmaceutical companies, there is the 
possibility of the sample to be predisposed towards specific brands. Additionally, the survey 
appealed to people’s voluntary participation thus resulting in the sample being characterized as 
voluntary response sample, hence biased.
