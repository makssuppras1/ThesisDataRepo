Abstract
In this thesis the author identiﬁed in oﬀshore wind turbines support structures an interesting area
of cost reduction, which can be achieved through structural optimization. Structural optimization’s
aim is to ﬁnd the optimal lay-out of a structure in order to carry the load in the best way. A
two-steps method is proposed. The ﬁrst step deals with topology optimization of ground structures,
which concerns ﬁnding the optimal design starting from discrete structures consisting in a potentially
high number of members by removal of unnecessary members. Classical minimum compliance
topology optimization problems are solved using Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisation (SIMP)
method, for several load cases. It is observed that topology optimized designs are not suitable
for industry, because they may fail under the applied load or they may not satisfy structural
requirements and post-processing is required. The second step concerns sizing optimization of the
topology optimized designs under strength, buckling and eigenfrequency constraints.
In the last part, a case study was carried out for the INNWIND reference jacket supporting the
10 MW DTU reference wind turbine. Results show that 50% mass reduction can be achieved
through sizing optimization. The optimized INNWIND reference jacket is then compared to realistic
designs obtained with the two-steps method. In general, it is observed that topology optimized
structures are initially infeasible with respect to stress constraints and buckling, and performing
sizing optimization of these structures results in a mass increment that matches the ﬁnal optimal
mass of the INNWIND reference jacket after sizing optimization.
Finally, three and four legged innovative designs obtained with this method present internal
connectivities and might be taken into consideration for further analysis, which will include fatigue
analysis and dynamic loading condition.

1 Motivation
In this chapter, ﬁrst the reasons that led the author to undertake this project are listed. Then the
thesis outline and the main objective are presented.
1.1 Oﬀshore Wind turbines
European Wind Energy Association (EWEA) projections suggest that the wind power sector will
continue to expand fast [1]. One of the bigger issues that experts are asked to deal with, is whether
or not oﬀshore wind turbines will play a major role in the next future. In order to answer, the
technical potential in 2020 and 2030 for both onshore and oﬀshore scenarios is analysed. That is,
after assessing wind resources across Europe and determining projections of wind turbine technology
development, calculate the maximum amount of wind energy that could be generated. Onshore wind
energy potential appears to be located mostly in agricultural and industrial areas of North-Eastern
Europe, whereas oﬀshore potential is found to be mainly in low deep water in the North Sea, the
Baltic Seas and the Atlantic Ocean. The results of the analysis estimate an oﬀshore technical
potential in 2030 at 30000 TWh for all EEA countries, compared to 45000 TWh for onshore. While
the oﬀshore technical potential is only two-thirds of onshore, the total area considered in the study
accounts for 5000000 km2 land area and only 750000 km2 sea area. This data means that more
electricity might be generated from oﬀshore wind turbines for equal area. This can be explained by
looking at the main diﬀerences between the two environmental scenarios. Oﬀshore wind turbines
experience higher wind speeds respect to onshore wind turbines, meaning that the former are
characterized by larger load hours. Load hours refers to the amount of time that a wind turbine
operates, and more interesting are the full load hours, which is the amount of time that a wind
turbine would spend at full load if it always operates at that level. Figure 1.1 shows that only 10%
of onshore potential is actually realized in areas with more than 3000 full load hours, while more
than 40% of oﬀshore technical potential occurs for the same amount of full load hours.
Figure 1.1: Technical potential realised in diﬀerent full load-hour classes, see EEA 2008 [1]
Among the advantages that oﬀshore wind turbines have compared to onshore, it is worth to
emphasize that they are designed for higher wind speeds with bigger rotor swept area. Taking
advantage of steadier winds, the generation capacity results to be larger. Furthermore, transport of

huge wind turbine parts tends to be easier for ships as they can carry heavier and bigger components
than trucks on land. However, the analysis until now is neglecting any costs related aspects. In
fact, even though this ﬁrst analysis seems to make a compelling case in favour of oﬀshore wind
turbines, some constraints should be included in the study, in order to assess the feasibility of wind

it is assumed that only 4% of the oﬀshore areas at a distance within 10 km from the shore would
be available for wind farms, leading to only 3500 TWh in the 2030. Finally, when production costs
are compared to the PRIMES baseline electricity generation costs, the competitive potential for
onshore wind turbines is expected to be around 27000 TWh, while oﬀshore competitive potential is
slightly aﬀected at around 3500 TWh.

From 1.2, it can be seen that at average production costs (6.9 cents/kWh), 5800 GW could be

the top in ﬁgure 1.2.
In conclusion, it is clear that oﬀshore wind turbines will play a major role as long as engineering
will allow a shift from oﬀshore shallow water designs to deeper water feasible projects.

INTRODUCTION
1.2 Oﬀshore wind turbine support structures
The overall goal of a wind turbine design is to maximize AEP (annual energy production) while
minimizing LCoE (levelized cost of energy). LCoE is deﬁned as the “levelized” unit cost of 1 MWh
over the lifetime of the wind farm by taking into consideration the sum of the discounted lifetime
costs relative to the discounted energy production for every year of the wind farm lifespan [9]

INTRODUCTION
It is now clear that design of support structures represent an interesting area of study. It could be a
driving factor towards a new generation of oﬀshore wind turbines if designs were generated in a more
eﬃcient way in order to reduce costs. Cost reduction might be achieved by reducing material use
and reducing fabrication costs. Although the latter depends mostly on the manufacturer’s machines
costs, material costs and labor costs, reduction of material is strictly related to the conceptual
designs which stand at earlier stages than fabrication stages.
Since support structures are an important costs driver, there is wide room for structural optimization
techniques. One structural optimization techniques with high potential because of its design freedom
is Topology Optimization. Topology optimization is a technique that allows to move from initial
designs with many design elements to ﬁnal designs characterized by a reduced set of design elements.
The ensemble of these elements represent the optimal lay-out of the structure in order to carry the
load in the best way, while the unnecessary elements are removed by the algorithm.
In this thesis, the application of topology optimization is studied for the conceptual design phase of
oﬀshore wind turbine support structures, in particular jackets structures.

2.1 Structural Optimization
Structural optimization can be traced back to Michell’s work on truss structures (1904), when
optimal criteria were derived to ﬁnd a “limit of material economy” on frame structures [11]. As
deﬁned in [12], “structural optimization is the subject of making an assemblage of materials sustain
loads in the best way”. Thus, the overall goal is seeking the optimum lay-out of a structure. The
concept of lay-out may be related to shape, sizing or topology of the structure, resulting in three
diﬀerent optimization classes. The main diﬀerence between this three optimization methods can
be found in their design parametrization. In the ﬁrst class, the goal of the optimization process
is to ﬁnd the optimum shape in terms of geometry of the design domain, which is considered as
the design variable of the problem. Sizing optimization problems deal with ﬁnding an optimal
distribution of a certain design variable set (thickness of a plate, cross sectional area of a member) in
order to minimize a physical quantity such as compliance or mass of the structure, while respecting
equilibrium and other constraints on the design variables set, that cannot be changed or reduced.
In the last class, which can be considered the most general method within structural optimization,
one seeks for an optimal topology of the structure, involving the connectivity and distribution of
material, number and location of holes in the domain. Figure 2.1 illustrates the eﬀect of the three
diﬀerent structural optimization method on a very generic structure.
Figure 2.1: A comparison of the three diﬀerent structural optimization method. a) sizing optimiza-
tion b) shape optimization c) topology optimization [5]
When it comes to deal with naturally discrete problems such as truss structures, the concept of
sizing and topology optimization are slightly diﬀerent. In both problems the design variables
considered might be the cross sectional area of the members. However, the main diﬀerence between
the two categories of problems is that sizing problems deal with a design domain and state variables
that are already known a priori and cannot be changed throughout the optimization process [5].
Therefore only topology problems allow a more general and complete optimization analysis, having
more design freedom allowing members to vanish, leading to diﬀerent optimal connectivity designs

2.2 Topology Optimization
Topology optimization can be considered the most general class of structural optimization. As
described in [13], topology optimization is “essentially a systematic design methodology, which
involves a numerical process to iteratively re-distribute a given amount of material inside the
design domain subject to loads and boundary conditions, until a prescribed design objective is
optimized under speciﬁed design constraints”. It is possible to distinguish between two broad
categories of problems within topology optimization: continuum problems and discretized problems.
Traditionally, structural optimization problems have been solved a continua formulation using
the Homogenization method [13]. However, the method used in this thesis is known as Ground
Structure Method, which is more appropriate for discrete jacket structures (see ﬁgure 2.3).
8
==PAGE:20==
LITERATURE REVIEW
Figure 2.3: A comparison of Homogenization and Ground Structure methods, [6]
The Ground Structure Method (GSM) was introduced ﬁrst by Dorn et al.(1964) [14] to reduce the
complexity of a topology optimization problem. The idea is to consider a ﬁxed set of nodes and to
establish connections between them, depending on the desired degree of connectivity. Figure 2.4
shows three diﬀerent ground structures with increasing degree of connectivity from left to right.
Figure 2.4: Diﬀerent ground structures generated in a rectangular domain with ﬁve by three nodal
layout, with increasing degree of connectivity from left to right, [5]
Every connection between two nodes is a potential member and can be switched on or oﬀdepending
on the topology problem deﬁnition. Two considerations follow. Firstly, the number of nodes in the
initial ground structure inﬂuences the optimal topological design. Secondly, the higher is the level
of complexity, and therefore the number of connections allowed, the higher is the chance to result
in a realistic optimal topology. However, computational time costs are seen to be strictly related to
the complexity of the problem and to the degree of connectivity established.
There are also some limitations to the method.
Unrealistic optimal designs can be obtained
performing topology optimization on the ground structures. For instance, too many structural
members might disappear in the same region leading to critical structures. This might be due
to wrong formulation of the optimization problem or to the generation of critical initial ground
structures. A critical point is represented by overlapping members, which may be unwanted in
some cases. Furthermore, it may occur that too long and slender members generated may vanish
because of buckling constraints, leading again to unrealistic designs. Finally, another important
issue which should be investigated is how to enforce symmetry, which is often necessary for the
9
==PAGE:21==
LITERATURE REVIEW
application to reduce the number of diﬀerent members. Overall, the method is seen to be very
sensitive to multiple load cases.
In conclusion, the generation of the initial ground structure seems to be a crucial part of the design
process.
2.3 Optimization methods
For several years, diﬀerent numerical methods have been studied in structural topology optimization.
This ﬁeld deals with nonlinear constrained optimization problems, where constraints playing an
essential role or the objective function might be nonlinear functions [15]. Two classes of optimization
methods are commonly used in topology optimization, namely gradient based and heuristic methods.
This thesis is focused only on gradient-based methods.
Heuristic methods are also called evolutionary methods because they are inspired by biological
evolution ideas. Among them, the well known Genetic Algorithm uses the same process seen in
the natural selection, in order to ﬁnd an area around the optimal point eﬀectively. However, the
computational expenses are high for these methods because of the many design variables and
expensive function evaluations. Consequently, for this application these methods are considered
inappropriate. Refer to [16] for a deeper insight into the Genetic Algorithm.
Methods that use the partial derivatives of the design variables vector (gradient) are called gradient-
based methods. These use the information given by the gradient vector to compute the search
direction. In fact, the main diﬀerence between gradient-based optimization algorithms can be found
mostly in how the search direction is chosen and how the step size determined. See paragraph
2.4.1 in reference [17] for a clearer explanation on gradient-based techniques. More speciﬁcally, the
topology optimization solver method used in this project is a primal-dual interior-point algorithm
with a ﬁlter line-search method for non-linear programming. See [18] for a more detailed explanation.
The open source interior point solver IPOPT [19] was used in this thesis.
2.4 Structural optimization of oﬀshore wind turbine support structures
The design of oﬀshore wind turbine support structures is a non-trivial task, because of the complexity
of the wind turbine system. Complexity comes mostly from nonlinearities due for example to
nonlinear waves loads, wake inﬂuence and structural nonlinear eﬀects [20]. Another complex
factor is the turbulent nature of wind which varies highly both in time and space. Furthermore,
support structures are highly dynamic, being excited from both wind turbine rotor and waves [21].
This excitations cause a large number of quasi-periodic structural motions, meaning that support
structures are subjected to fatigue failure. Fatigue assessment is therefore mandatory and it is
determined by the fatigue lifetime of joints of the structure.
For these and other reasons, design of oﬀshore wind turbine support structures consists in non-linear
dynamic response constrained structural optimization problem which is prescribed to be performed
in time domain, accounting for nonlinearities and time-history dependency [22]. A study case
of structural optimization of oﬀshore wind turbine support structure is provided in [22], where
an analytic gradient-based method is used to mass minimize the oﬀshore wind turbine jacket
substructure used within the IEA Task 30 OC4 Project [23] supporting the 5 MW NREL oﬀshore
10

wind turbine [24]. Results show that an optimal jacket design with 52 percent structural mass
reduction was obtained in 27 iterations, while satisfying sizing, eigenfrequency, extreme load and
fatigue load constraints.

2.6 Buckling
When dealing with tubular steel frame structures, an important failure mode is buckling. Buckling
refers to the loss of structural stability of a component of the structure under axial compression
forces. This failure mechanism is not related to the yield strength of the material, but it is governed
mostly by both material and geometric stiﬀness. In fact, even before reaching the yield strength, a
tubular member subjected to compressive stresses might experience non-linear displacements and
buckling might occur.
The classical Euler Buckling theory can be used to derive the critical load Pcr above which the
beam element will buckle. In this thesis, the buckling assessment as deﬁned in the Recommended
Practice DNV-RP-C202 is used [28]. It is worth to emphasize that this approach does not consists
in a buckling analysis. But this criterion is suggested by the standards as a check in order to avoid
a buckling analysis. This can eﬃciently be solved in optimization since this criterion does not
depend on the state of jacket, but only on the design.

3.4 SIMP method
Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisation (SIMP) was introduced ﬁrst by Bendsoe in 1989 [31].
This method was thought ﬁrst for topology optimization problems of continuum structures where
the discrete (0-1) design variables (usually densities) indicate the presence or absence of material.
Traditionally, a classical topology optimization problem for continuum structures consists in ﬁnding
the optimal material distribution in a given design domain, by minimizing compliance under a
volume constraint. The result is a black and white solution where the former has meaning of
material and the latter has the meaning of void. SIMP method deals with the unwanted “gray”
solutions, which corresponds to the design variables that end up with values between 0 and 1. To
enforce the black-white solution, the SIMP method uses a power law relation between stiﬀness
and density, to penalise intermediate densities and to ensure that the design variables are pushed
towards the binary limits 0 and 1. A general deﬁnition of problem (3.4) with SIMP method is

4 Ground Structure Method
In this chapter, the ground structure method introduced before, is used to generate initial designs.
The methodology used for this purpose is described and short examples are used to illustrate the
concepts.
4.1 General overview
The following approach is used in this thesis. First, an initial ground structure is set up in a
MATLAB script, which creates input ﬁles needed for the optimization analysis implemented by
Frame Analysis and Optimization Software (FAOS). The model, the optimization problem and the
loading condition are deﬁned in the input ﬁles. In the model input ﬁles, the nodes coordinates,
the connectivity, as well as the boundary condition are speciﬁed. The optimization problem input
ﬁle states the objective function that undergoes the optimization algorithm, and the constraints,
for instance compliance minimization under mass constraint. Input ﬁles for each load case are
also created, where the magnitude and direction of the load is deﬁned, specifying the point of
application of the load. After the input ﬁles are created, FAOS reads the data and performs a
frame analysis of the initial structure. Using 3D beam element theory, it calculates the state of the
system by solving the state equation. In all the analysis performed, the structures are assumed
to be made of structural steel S355, with the density, Young’s modulus, Poisson’s ratio and yield
strength set to 7850 kg/m3, 200 GPa, 0.3 and 355 MPa respectively.
Afterwards, the optimization process can start. Several approach here could be taken in order to
achieve an optimal design. The one chosen in this project is to perform topology optimization of the
ground structure by applying SIMP method allowing members to vanish [31], and then performing
sizing optimization on the remaining elements, formulating and implementing buckling constraints.
Introducing stress constraints in topology optimization arises diﬃculties that prevent the direct
solution of the optimization problem. These are mainly associated with the presence of singular
optima and the large number of constraints, due to the fact that the stress is a local state variable
and it is not known a priori where the critical stress will occur [32].

4.2 Ground structure method: general approach
For discrete truss structures such as jackets a continuum formulation of the topology problem would
be inappropriate and extremely expensive from a computational point of view. By discretizing the
domain of interest, it is possible to solve the problem by using beam ﬁnite elements. The main idea
developed in the MATLAB script GeneralJacket.m is to create a ground structure starting from
a truncated cone reference design domain.The algorithm implemented to generate initial ground
structures is shown in ﬁgure 4.1 and consists in 4 steps:
• nodes generation
• member generation
• member remotion (long members and overlapping members)
• simpliﬁcation: external mesh and simmetry
4.3 Nodes Generation
The ﬁrst step is the generation of the nodes of the ground structure. It has been seen that jacket
structures present a truncated pyramid shape from a geometrical point of view, with real world
jackets limited to triangular and quadrilateral bases. Independently of the number of sides, any
2D polygon can be drawn inside a circumference, with all vertexes standing on the circumference.
Using this principle, it is possible to create any truncated pyramid inside a truncated cone domain
by choosing the two radius of top and bottom circumferences and by selecting the height. The
truncated cone domain in shown in ﬁgure 4.2, where 4 points were chosen on both bottom and top
circumferences, which are highlight in red. The two radius chosen for all the jacket analysed in this
thesis are ﬁxed to be Rbottom = 20 m and Rtop = 12 m, in order to simulate realistic dimensions.
The height is 70 m.

4.5 Members Remotion
When dealing with discrete structures, the number of design elements is strictly correlated to
the number of design variables. It is desirable to reduce the complexity of the problem in order
to reduce computational costs. Two limitations are therefore considered to limit the number of
connections:
• Maximum length of the members
• Non-overlapping connections between each pair of nodes
The ﬁrst limitation is applied in order to avoid long members to be included in the ground structure,
between for example node 1 at the base and the transition node N at the top. The presence of
long member may arise instability issues. This limitation is easily implemented by calculating the
length of each element and removing all the elements with length larger than a maximum value,
such as Li > Lmax. Secondly, in order to avoid overlapping members, the following algorithm is
applied. The idea is to connect node i only to all the other nodes with index bigger than i. When a
new connection is created, the unit vector along the member e1 is calculated, and the starting node
is checked. For instance, if the connection [i (i + 1)], (i.e connection from node 1 to 2) appears to
have the same unit vector as [i (i + 2)], (i.e connection from node 1 to 3), this means that the two
elements will be overlapping on the same segment line, having the same starting node i. Thus, the
longest element is deleted, leading to a clear ground structure with no overlapping members.

6 Sizing optimization: Second Step
In this chapter, an additional sizing optimization step is introduced, specifying the problem formula-
tion and the new non-linear constraints implemented at this stage.
6.1 Post-processing of topology optimized designs
The general idea behind the analysis until now was performing topology optimization of ground
structures using SIMP method to let members vanishing and ensure tubular elements and symmetry
while minimizing the sum of compliances for several load cases, under mass constraint. However,
topology optimization is generally used as initial stage in the design process and it is not suﬃcient
in order to establish reasonable optimal designs. In fact, the topology optimized designs may not
satisfy local failure criteria and they may fail under the applied loads [32]. For this reason, a
subsequent additional stage consisting in either shape or sizing optimization is implemented to
adjust the optimal designs in order to satisfy structural criteria in the standards.
In this thesis, an additional sizing optimization step has been carried out on the topology optimized
designs obtained in the ﬁrst step. In order to move from topology optimized design to consistent
initial designs for the sizing optimization process, an intermediate step is needed. In this step, new
input ﬁles are required by FAOS for the sizing optimization step, where the model, optimization
problem and loading cases are speciﬁed.
Model input ﬁle
In the model input ﬁle the connectivity, nodes coordinates and boundary conditions are speciﬁed.
After topology optimization, most of the design elements will have a SIMP density value xi close to
bounds 0 and 1. However, it is also possible that some design members will have an intermediate
SIMP density value between the limits. Therefore, all design members with SIMP density value xi
smaller than a certain threshold are removed. The threshold value is chosen to be xi = 0.1. Equally,
all design nodes that are seen to be ﬂoating because they are not being connected with any other
nodes, are removed as well, in order to avoid singularity issues in the stiﬀness matrix calculation.
Load cases input ﬁles
The load cases do not diﬀer from the loading conditions aﬀecting the topology optimization problem,
in order to be consistent. The load cases are speciﬁed in section 3.2
Optimization problem input ﬁles
Here the sizing optimization problem is deﬁned. The optimization problem consists in the minimiza-
tion of the sum of compliances for several load cases subjected to mass and symmetry constraints
as before, plus a new non-linear constraint to check buckling.

7.1 Problem deﬁnition
In order to achieve more realistic results, a new formulation of the sizing optimization problem is
required. Since the aim is to reduce the use of material as much as possible, mass is taken as the
new objective function. When dealing with light structures consisting in slender tubular members,
preventing column buckling and instability is essential. Buckling constraints formulated in section
6.2 are still valid.
Furthermore, it is important to control the dynamic response of the structures, and limitations
on the natural frequency domain should be imposed, in order to avoid resonance. First natural
frequency can not exceed the minimum 3P rotor natural frequency ω1 < 0.3 Hz and it should avoid
the 1P region as well, ω1 > 0.15 Hz. Applying a 10% safety margin, the ﬁrst natural frequency ω1
should lie in the free range 0.176 −0.273Hz.
Finally, strength constraints are implemented by calculating stresses in four points situated on the
external diameter of each tubular element. When dealing with tubular joints, the recommended
practice [34] suggests to use parametric equations to derive stress concentration factors (SCF) in
order to obtain hot spot stress for the actual geometry, depending on the load type and ﬁxity
condition. Then, this hot spot stress is entered a relevant hot spot stress S-N curve for tubular
joints. However, since fatigue analysis is neglected in this problem, and stress concentration factor
is not included in the analysis of the stresses at the tubular joints, too light structures would be
obtained as a result of the sizing optimization process. Therefore, it is imposed a safety factor
SF = 10 that reduces the maximum stress allowed σmax = 355
SF MPa = 35.5 MPa.

CONCLUSIONS
8 Conclusions
The main goal of this thesis was performing structural optimization of oﬀshore wind turbine support
structures. It was observed that jacket support structures represent a potential area for costs
reduction. One way to reduce costs can be achieved by limiting use of material through structural
optimization. The approach that was used in this thesis was: i) ground structures generation ii)
topology optimization of ground structures using SIMP method and then iii) sizing optimization of
the topology optimized designs minimizing mass under stress constraints.
First, ground structure method was selected as the topology optimization methodology more
appropriate when dealing with naturally discrete structures like jackets. A general algorithm to
generate ground structures was created. The algorithm allows to generate any type of jacket
structure starting from a reference truncated cone domain and two examples of the process were
illustrated. Generation of ground structures is seen to be a crucial step and it highly inﬂuences the
outcome of topology optimization. Errors during deﬁnition of nodes, for example ﬂoating nodes,
result in issues when it comes to deﬁne the global stiﬀness matrix. Inappropriate generation of
members results in a number of design elements which does not reﬂect the actual ground structure
design. For example, overlapping members might create conﬂicts during topology optimization.
The algorithm gives the user the possibility to simplify the problem reducing the number of design
variables by removing all internal connections and limiting the element length. It was seen that the
topology optimized design is highly dependent on this two options, leading to diﬀerent optimal
designs for diﬀerent initial conﬁgurations. Furthermore, symmetry is very important in order to
obtain realistic designs. However, only one symmetry plane has been considered in this thesis and
more symmetry planes should be included in future work.
Topology optimization using SIMP method seemed to work adequately for ground structures. For
some reasons, it appeared that the method converges better when the whole ground structure is
considered compared to when the internal connections are neglected. One possible reason might
be that it is easier to identify unnecessary members that do not carry any load when more and
more elements are included. Realistic designs were obtained after topology optimization of initial
ground structures with a relatively low number of design elements and therefore design variables.
When more connections are included in the initial ground structures increasing the complexity of
the problem, the outcome of topology optimization problem as deﬁned in this thesis is most likely
an innovative design, characterized by unfeasible joints from a geometrical point of view. This
issue could be solved by adding some geometrical constraints in the problem formulation derived
from the structural standards of tubular joints. In general, there is high potential for improvement
at this stage. In conclusion, it was seen that topology optimization does not result in optimal
designs that can be used from manufacturers directly. For this reason, post-processing of topology
optimized designs is crucial.
To obtain more realistic results, an additional sizing optimization step was performed on a complete
wind turbine model, which consisted of the reference wind turbine supported by the optimal
design jacket structures. In the new sizing optimization problem, mass was minimized under
stress constraints, geometrical constraints and eigenfrequency limitations. In particular, buckling
constraints were derived and implemented to avoid column instability. Firstly, it was proved that
50 percent mass reduction can be achieved by performing sizing optimization on the INNWIND
reference jacket supporting the wind turbine reference model in 55 iterations. The beneﬁt of sizing

optimization was also noticeable in the small shift experienced by the ﬁrst natural frequency, from
a region initially close the 3P rotor frequency to a safer free allowable area after optimization. Also,
the initial model was seen to violate both tubular structural requirements and buckling, which were
sorted out through sizing optimization.
Then, sizing optimization of topology optimized designs was carried out, extending the process to
other diﬀerent jacket structure concepts. Three and four legged jackets were investigated. Realistic
designs were obtained after topology optimization of ground structures with external conﬁguration
and a limited number of design variables. Overall, the sizing optimization problem seems to
be governed by stress constraints and infeasibility of the initial topology optimized designs with
respect to buckling constraints leads to a mass increment. In general, the mass of the ﬁnal optimal
designs appeared to be coherent compared to the mass of INNWIND reference jacket after sizing
optimization (around 650-700 tons). The ﬁrst natural frequency of the total structure appeared to
be governed by the tower natural frequency. Furthermore, diameters of the optimal design members
are maximized while thicknesses follow two behaviours: elements with initial thickness at lower
bound keep this behaviour while the rest of the members tend to maximize their thickness.
Overall, topology optimization is more computationally expensive compared to sizing optimization.
This is due to mostly the high number of design variables that enter the topology optimization
problem, while sizing optimization problems accounts for a reduced set of variables. Although
the number of iterations can be equal among the two process, Sizing optimization can converge
quickly without the solver recognizing the local minima, resulting in no variation experienced by
the objective function for several iterations. On the other hand, sizing process can be demanding
because of the large number of stress constraints and it was seen that basically the entire CPU
time was spent on NLP functions evaluations such as objective, constraints, jacobian calculation.
Finally, innovative conceptual designs of three legged and four legged jackets were achieved through
the proposed method after topology optimization of ground structures including internal connections
and might be taken into consideration for further analysis.
The methods presented in this thesis might be used from stakeholders to evaluate the feasibility
of the project in the early stages of the design process chain. Diﬀerent simulations should be
performed in order to take into consideration the eﬀect of water depth, site characteristics and
meteocean conditions. Furthermore, the methods should be followed by the economical assessment
of the applicability of the design, estimating over costs and LCOE, given AEP.
Future work will account for a full dynamic analysis of the turbine system, including realistic DLCs
which will include the eﬀect of fatigue loading and lead to more accurate designs.
