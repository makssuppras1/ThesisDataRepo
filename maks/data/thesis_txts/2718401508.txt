Abstract
The number of electric vehicles in Denmark has increased by more than 68% during the last year. As the
ambition of the Danish Government is to reach 1 million electric vehicles by 2030, the development of charging
infrastructure needs to be accelerated. The EU recommendation is to have 1 charging point per 10 electric
vehicles resulting in a national need of 100,000 charging points by 2030. Optimizing the locations of charging
points is a challenging task, as a high number of stakeholders with different and contradictory agendas are
involved. The current approach regarding optimization of charging point locations in Denmark primarily con-
siders technical and economic aspects. The purpose of this thesis is to develop a charging point infrastructure
optimization method, where charging points are located mainly based on user needs and preferences.
The factors influencing charging point locations from a user perspective are identified through a literature re-
view, a questionnaire answered by 1884 respondents, the Danish National Travel Survey, and exhaustive data on
Danish households and the demographics of the Danish population. An algorithm is developed to determine the
optimal locations and the number of charging points per location based on the factors and the data collected.
The algorithm consists of two elements; a heuristic and a mathematical optimization problem. Denmark is
divided into square cells of 500 and 1000 meters and data is extracted for each square cell. The purpose of the
heuristic is to eliminate half of the square cells outputting the ones identified to be most beneficial locations
for charging points. The mathematical optimization problem is developed to identify the optimal number of
charging points, if any, for the square cells outputted from the heuristic.
The concrete results of the method developed in this thesis are clear recommendations with regard to where
charging points should be located according to user needs, habits, and preferences as identified through the
project. The optimal charging point locations determined by the algorithm shows a strong tendency to locate
charging point in and around cities, as the concentration of charging points increases with the population and
the apartment density in an area. The output yielded from data based on square cell sizes of 500 meters favors
the largest cities, while 1000 meter square cell data yield a larger spread of charging points across the country.
This thesis presents a method to identify optimal locations and number of charging points from a user perspec-
tive, which based on the promising results can be used to guide decision makers when planning future charging
infrastructure. The method provides recommendations relevant to consider in a decision making process or inte-
grated with other methods considering additional aspects of charging point locations. In regard of the latter, it
is suggested to combine several methods to make an integrated method including the perspective of all relevant
stakeholders. The electric vehicle market is immature and still in an emerging phase where the charging demand
is bigger than the charging supply. Along with the market maturing, the competition among charging providers
is presumed to become more intense, and it is thereby expected that the user-centric approach developed in
this thesis will become increasingly relevant.

Introduction
The number of Electric Vehicles (EVs) in Denmark has increased by 68% from 31,900 in 2021 to 100,000 in
October 2022 (Bilimportører 2022a). The number of plugin hybrid EVs has also increased significantly from
29,700 in 2021 to 77,900 in August 2022 (Bilbrancheråd 2022). These two types of passenger vehicles accounted
for 2.4% of the total amount of passenger vehicles in Denmark in the beginning of 2021, while the number had
increased to 5.4% in the beginning of 2022 (Danmarks Statistik 2021). In addition, the sale of rechargeable
vehicles constituted 10% of the total vehicle sale in March 2020, while constituting 36% in March 2022 (Rørvig
2022).
These statistics illustrate the significant increase in the number of EVs in Denmark, and a tendency that more
Danes prefer a sustainable alternative to Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) vehicles. Furthermore, the increase
has been so rapid that it has created an unfulfilled need for Charging Points (CP) (Anel 2022). While an
increase in the number of EVs is a positive development seen from a sustainability perspective, the charging
infrastructure is falling behind. The inadequate charging infrastructure is especially challenging in larger cities
such as Copenhagen, Aarhus, and Odense (Goodstein 2021). The overall EU recommendation is to reach 1
public CP per 10 EVs (European Court of Auditors 2021). Currently there are approximately 14 EVs per CP
(Ryming 2022) in Denmark. The former Danish government launched a goal of reaching 1 million EVs in 2030
as a part of the plan to reduce the emission of greenhouse gasses by 70% in comparison to 1990 (Danmarks
Statistik 2020). In September 2022 the number of public CPs in Denmark reached more than 6,700, but to
reach the recommendation of 0.1 public CPs per EV, about 93,300 CPs need to be set up during the coming 8
years expecting 1 million EVs in 2030 (Bilimportører 2022b).
The transition to EVs will increase the charging need and consequently the demand for CPs will increase as well.
The inadequate infrastructure can turn out to be a barrier for the entire EV transition. A survey conducted in
December 2021 with 2000 participants provided the insight that 49% of the participants consider the distance
between Charging Stations (CS) as a large or very large barrier, while 45% consider the lack of charging options
in their neighborhood as a large or very large barrier to transition to EVs (Haun 2022).
The expected increase in demand for CPs is widely recognized, and both public and private actors are preparing
for the "race" of installing CSs and CPs. In August 2021, the Danish Parliament decided to invest 475 million
DKK in development, optimization and preparation of charging infrastructure in Denmark until the end of
2022. The purpose of the agreement is to support green domestic travelling and to provide subsidies to set up
CPs. Already during 2021, 275 million DKK had been spent on co-financing CPs by housing associations and
by publicly available sites (even if privately owned). The remaining 200 million DKK are to be used in 2022 to
establish CPs in municipal, regional and private areas (Dansk Bilbrancheråd 2021). Private actors are racing
to find and purchase land to place CSs. As an example, in April 2022 Norlys announced that they will invest
1 billion DKK in public CPs to be used during the coming 3 years (Dam 2022). To find the optimal locations,
extensive analyses on traffic flows, proximity to other CPs, driver behavior etc. need to be conducted.
1.1
Problem Statement
The high increase in demand for CPs creates a highly attractive business opportunity for electricity suppliers.
As explained through an interview with the Energy Director at Norlys A/S, it is likely that households and
companies decide to use the same supplier for CPs as they use as energy provider. Beside the sustainable
gains from using EVs, there is consequently also an economic motivation for energy suppliers to enter the
market of CPs. To succeed in the fast-growing market, it is however important to do it in a "smart" way.
It is common to distinguish between CPs based on the usage situation. A frequently used categorization is

home, work, urban and travelling. First, for a company to enter the CP market in a "smart" way, it must be
decided which usage situation to focus on. Second, it is of high relevance where new CPs are placed. It is thus of
great importance for companies to analyze possible locations, to decide where new CPs ideally should be located.
The optimization of CP locations can be investigated from a macro perspective relying on huge data bases,
e.g., BBR data containing information about buildings, CVR/VAT data about companies, data from the Dan-
ish Motorregister, the number and type of cars per municipality, traffic habits, already existing CPs etc.
On the other hand, the location of CPs can also be investigated from a micro perspective, which is the scope of
this project. The micro perspective refers to considering the decision processes, concerns and preferences of the
users to identify the base of data relevant to use in locating CPs such that these are available when and where
EV users need them. Considering the optimization problem from a micro perspective, focus is thereby shifted to
a user perspective and the overall purpose is for the users to be able to drive their EVs with reduced concerns,
Range Anxiety, and questions of doubt. Investigating the optimization of CP locations from a micro perspective
also relies on large amounts of data, but the relevant data is identified based on a user-centric approach. To
make an insightful investigation, the following research questions are raised and will be answered throughout
the thesis:
• Which factors influence EV users’ choice of CPs?
• Which data can be used to optimize CP localization to address user concerns and make it more convenient
to be an EV user?
• How can an algorithm be developed to assist in optimal location of CPs?
• How can the developed algorithm in combination with already existing CP location methods strengthen
the decision-making process regarding CP location?

Literature Review
With the massive growth in the number of EVs in Denmark as explained in Section 1, the demand for CPs is
increasing accordingly. Optimizing the location of CPs is a very complex challenge - due to the large number
of influencing factors including traffic patterns, existing charging infrastructure, a fluctuating charging demand
and load on the power grid, but also due to the objectives of different stakeholders. A reduction of Range
Anxiety is of high importance for EV users while cost is the main objective for CP providers. The problem
of locating CSs and CPs is referred to as the Electrical Vehicle Charging Station Placement Problem (EVCSPP).
The purpose of this literature review is initially to map out the current and future state of the charging
infrastructure in Denmark and investigate how the location of CPs has been decided. Literature within the
field is reviewed to obtain an overview of which criteria are used (Section 2.2) and which and how the methods
are implemented (Section 2.3). Finally, the findings of the literature review are summarized in Section 2.4.
The aim is to analyze the existing methods and used criteria to gain an understanding of the field and the
perspectives from which charging load, demand, and behavior can be explored and to investigate if there is a
lack of information and investigation in certain areas within the field.
Figure 2.1 visualizes the project process with the purpose of giving an overview of the process steps and
how outputs from every step serve as input for one or more other steps. As every single process step is input
to another step, the figure further highlights the importance of all steps - or in other words that no step can be
left out.
Figure 2.1: Visualization of the project process illustrating the different steps of the project and their inputs

The literature review is based on literature found through The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) Findit.
To find articles about optimization methods the key words "charging stations placement optimization" are used,
while "site selection charging stations" and "criteria charging station selection" is used to search for articles
focusing on criteria identified to impact the decision of CP locations. Only articles from the past five years
(from 2017) are used, as older literature is considered obsolete since the field is in rapid growth. Also, articles
focusing only on buses, taxis and freight vehicles are discarded as this project is concerned about passenger
vehicles.
Further, studies made in countries and cities too dissimilar from Denmark and Danish cities, for
example New York, Pakistan and India, are also left out to focus the search. Additionally, when reviewing
optimization methods, studies looking exclusively at home chargers or focusing on optimizing the load on the
power grid are discarded, as they are not relevant for this project. Accepted literature has been used to find
further relevant literature by backward search assessing the referenced literature.

2.1.1
Future Charging Infrastructure
Although the process of setting up CPs needs to be accelerated in Denmark, many actors have already planned
to set up new CPs throughout the country from 2022-2026. Among the planned CPs are:
• 84 CPs distributed on four CSs along the highway in Zealand and on Funen (Vejdirektoratet 2022).
• 12-14 CPs on Bornholm (Clausen 2022).
• 1000 new CPs from E.ON across 19 different municipalities Ritzau 2022.
• 600 CPs from Clever by 17 different shopping centers.Breinstrup 2022
• 8 Super Chargers (SC) in Western Jutland (Arent 2022).
• 31 CSs along the highway throughout Jutland and across Funen and Zealand (Søgaard 2022) and also in
the larger cities as Copenhagen (Københavns Kommune 2021).
As seen from the planned installment of CPs, there is not a specific area around which the planned charging
infrastructure is centered. Plans are made both along highways, in both small and large cities, and around
shopping locations.
2.1.2
Charging Prices
Several factors influence the charging prices. The prices depend on whether the EV user has a charging sub-
scription, but the electricity prices are also a factor to consider. A relevant factor to consider in that regard is
the daily fluctuations in the prices of electricity. In Denmark the electricity prices are generally lower during
the night hours (Elberegner 2022).
Due to different factors such as the COVID-19 pandemic and the war in Ukraine, the electricity prices are in
2022 historically high. In January 2019 the price per kilowatt-hour (kWh) was 2 DKK, while it was 3.95 DKK
in July 2022 (Clever 2022a). Increasing electricity prices have affected EV users with and without a charging
subscription. Users without subscriptions are affected directly by the increasing prices, while users with sub-
scriptions are affected indirectly.
There are different types of subscriptions offered by different charging operators. With some subscriptions the
users pay a relatively high fixed monthly price to be able to charge unlimited, while the fixed price is lower with
other subscriptions where the user will pay a price per kWh. Both subscription models are from many providers
subject to a temporary energy supplement due to the increasing electricity prices, where the price per kWh has
also increased for the latter. Prices on these subscriptions are however not dependent on daily fluctuations but
only dependent on whether a Normal Charger (NC), a Fast Charger (FC), or a Super Charger (SC) is used
(Clever 2022b).
2.2
Criteria
Existing literature has been reviewed to obtain a comprehensive overview of factors influencing EV usage and
CP locations. The factors used to estimate CP locations in some situations depend on which methods are
implemented, and in other cases the methods are implemented dependent on the available data.
In a literature review conducted by Pagany, Camargo, and Dorner 2019, a comprehensive selection of previous
studies has been evaluated to categorize factors used to optimize CP locations. Three different orientations are
identified being: User, route, and destination. The orientations depend on the perspective from which the CP
requirements are analyzed in a model. Some of the reviewed studies focus on user data, e.g., age, social status,
daily activities, spatial distribution etc. Others focus on destinations by analyzing the destination, dwelling

times, frequency of visits etc. Many studies focus on routes where network data for simulating the movements
of traffic in the road network is used. There is also a focus on driver behavior in the route-orientated methods.
Driver behavior is descriptive of deciding a route as well as the destination. The empirical data used in the
route-oriented studies are data on travel paths including starting points, end points, and conditions during the
trip such as elevations, speed, and driving time. The review groups the results in three: Demand density, parti-
tioning and network optimization. With the demand density, the desired outcome is the charging requirements
for fixed sub-areas making it possible to compare areas and identify suitable CP locations. Partitioning also
includes density calculations and identifications of hot-spots, but with partitioning the sub-areas are not divided
into specific patterns. Instead, a calculation tool is used to generate partial homogeneous areas. With regard
to the last result category, the desired outcome is to minimize the total costs, number of CPs, or transportation
costs or to maximize the coverage demands.
Another literature review, conducted by Adenaw and Krapf 2022, presents an extensive analysis of publica-
tions regarding the influencing factors in a process of choosing optimal CP locations. A comprehensive search
for influencing factors was performed to collect and cluster these into overarching categories called core aspects.
The core aspects are defined as:
• Accessibility
• Centrality
• Connectivity to Public Transport
• Coverage
• Dwell Characteristics
• Land Use
• Other Charging Infrastructure
• Point of Interest (POI)
• Parking Situation
• Residential Population
• Safety
• Surrounding Street Network
• Traffic Flow
• Visibility
• Working Population
Each of the core aspects serve as a category of criteria used as a metric for the specific aspect of CP locations.
Without clear definitions some of these aspects seem to be overlapping or ambiguous. These are written in
bold and will be briefly elaborated on. Some of the ambiguous and possibly overlapping terms are further
elaborated and defined by the criteria within the category. Accessibility is used to describe the public access
and unrestricted access to a potential CP location.
Centrality refers to the position within the main road
network and regional centers. The distance between destination and charging stations, the distance between
POIs from parking, the charging station range and the like are described by the core aspect Coverage. The core
aspect Safety is described by the safety of the user, the car and the ease of use of CPs.
In addition to the core aspects, Adenaw and Krapf 2022 derive three main arguments describing site-specific
charging demand from the literature and the identified core aspects. These are the availability and the quality of
a CP and the activities connected to the location. The quality of a CP is dependent on Reachability, Redundancy
and Attractiveness as explained below:
• Reachability refers to how easy and quick it is to reach the CP.
• Redundancy refers to the number of CPs at a CS. Users are more likely to choose a CS with multiple CPs
as that provides a back-up option in case other users are charging at the same desired time.
• Attractiveness is determined by low parking pressure, culture, and recreational services.
The review provides a detailed overview of factors and derived core aspects that could be used to streamline
future work. The core aspects are further grouped into main arguments providing aspects that need to be
considered when assessing locations for CPs. The example metrics mentioned for assessments of the activity
argument include the number of parking spaces, and the number of visitors. For quality the metrics include

the mean walking distance and the sum of other CPs within a 500 meter radius. Lastly, for the availability

The two following studies focus on the aspect of sustainability in the process of assessing locations. Hosseini
and Sarder 2019 developed a Bayesian Network Model for optimal CP site selection. The study investigates
the location selection from a sustainability perspective. As the concept of sustainability is considered in an
economic, environmental, and social dimension, each of these serve as criteria; each with sub-criteria identified
as the main contributing factors for the particular criteria. Furthermore, a technical aspect is considered. All
aspects and the sub-criteria are:
• Economic aspect: Land cost, construction cost, total maintenance cost
• Environmental aspect: Waste discharge, air quality, destruction degree on water
• Social aspect: Population density, service level, traffic convenience
• Technical aspect: Time to power outage (failure)
The other study seeking to find optimal locations for CPs based on sustainability is Lee et al. 2021.
The
purpose of the paper is to analyze the optimal CP locations based on criteria from the perspective of economic,
environmental, and social sustainability as well, where the sub-criteria are:
• Economic aspect: Construction cost, annual operation and maintenance cost
• Environmental aspect: Destruction degree on vegetation and water, waste discharge, air pollutants reduc-
tion
• Social aspect: Harmonization of CPs with the development planning of urban road, network power grid,
traffic convenience, service capability, adverse impact on people’s lives
The focus on environmental and economic criteria is recurring in multiple approaches. In Guler and Yomraliogl
2020 the focus is on three main categories: Economy, environment and accessibility, each of which have sub-
criteria:
• Economic aspect: Slope and land values
• Environmental aspect: Green areas
• Accesibility aspect: Population density, income rates, shopping malls, transportation stations, park areas,
roads and petrol stations
The majority of the sub-criteria are self-explanatory while the environmental sub-criteria green areas and the
economic sub-criteria slope needs a short explanation. The criterion green areas describe how locations far from
green areas will be more suitable as the environmental impact of green areas in close proximity will be higher
as compared to green areas further away. Slope in turn has an effect on the construction cost and feasibility of
a CP location. Areas with non-stable and high slope are less suitable as these areas require more preparation
and maintenance.
In a study by Franco et al. 2020 the focus is concentrated on the transportation network characteristics and
the user mobility patterns. These categories include aspects such as network topology, vehicle ranges, the most
common origin and destination locations, possible driving routes and potential CPs.
Viswanathan et al. 2015 in their own words expand the analysis of optimal CP locations by incorporating
a temporal aspect. An approach is developed to evaluate both spatial and temporal aspects of CP locations
based on modelling and simulation of real-world traffic data. The development of models and simulations is
based on previous studies revealing that considering the driving style of individual drivers and traffic factors
like congestion can impact the estimation of energy consumption significantly, also influencing the assessment of
optimal CP infrastructure. In this study, a simulation method is proposed. The simulation can be utilized when
data about the actual energy demand are not available. The energy demand is estimated by simulating single
vehicle energy demands and summing these. The single vehicle energy demands are based on many factors:

• Traffic conditions (e.g., time spent waiting at traffic lights and stop-and-go traffic during peak hours)
• Terrain (e.g., higher energy consumption when the terrain is inclining)
• Driving behavior (e.g., acceleration and deceleration aggressiveness and speed)
• The vehicles (having aggregated energy consumption determined by their components)
Even if data about the actual energy demand are available and simulations therefore are not necessary, the
factors described above are still of interest as these describe whether or not potential CP locations are suitable.
An additional approach is to consider the time spent at different locations by users in different age groups to
find possible locations. This approach is developed in Pagany, Marquardt, and Zink 2019. A data analysis
is conducted to investigate at which destinations different user groups spend their time to analyze where CPs
could be set up such that it benefits the user groups in their daily activities. The destination and amount
of time spent at the destination along with data on user ages are utilized to predict the CP demand at each
location within an activity area. The Point of Interest (POI) destinations are then weighted according to the
share of users of one user group in the total number of inhabitants, the share of vehicle users, and the share of
potential EVs.
Under the assumptions that life style patterns and walking behavior is similar in industrialized countries, Pa-
gany, Marquardt, and Zink 2019 also consider the walking distance between a potential CP and POIs for the
customers when assessing CP location. The paper refers to studies of walking behavior in which distances up
to 800 meters or a walking duration of between 10 to 15 minutes are both considered acceptable. The ages of
the customers are taken into account, e.g. some CPs might be placed in areas where the main customer group
are people of older age which then will have an impact on the acceptable walking distance between CPs and
POIs. Further, if the POI is of type “shopping” people often carry bags which also affects the acceptable walking
distance.
Csonka and Csiszár 2017 takes on yet another perspective. They assess potential candidate CP locations de-
pendent on whether or not it would be in accordance with the city development plan requirements to install
a CP. They start by identifying to demand types: Inter- and Intra-city charging demand. Inter-city charging
demand is when the charging process interrupts the journey, while intra-city charging demand refers to when
the charging process is performed at the end of the journey or at locations where the driver would have had a
stop anyway. They consider candidate sites for location of CPs for inter- and intra-city CP location by different
criteria. The inter-city CP locations are considered at sites already used for different levels of services. These
services are divided into different levels according to their offerings of e.g., parking lots, restrooms, and dining
options. The installation potential of inter-city CP locations are calculated based on the following criteria:
Traffic volume on near roads, service level and the negative effect of near fast CPs. The intra-city charging
demand is to be satisfied when the vehicles are parking. The number of parking spots therefore needs to be
considered when assessing intra-city CP location. Then number of parking spots is influenced by the available
services at the site (daytime parking) and the population (nighttime parking). The installation of potential
intra-city CPs is calculated by considering the negative effect of nearby CPs, the service provided at the site,
the parking time spent, and the total population.

2.3
Optimization Methods
Since the rapid increase in the number of EVs started around 2019 (Juarez 2022), many studies and researches
have been made with the purpose of developing a method for optimizing the locations of CPs. This section will
cover the present work within optimization methods of CP location to create an overview of current methods
and to determine areas in which further analysis is necessary. Overall, the methods developed can be divided
into six areas:
• Heuristics.
• Mathematical optimization.
• Algorithms.
• Machine Learning Methods.
• Genetic Algorithms.
• Game Theory-based Methods.
To understand this grouping, it is important to understand the difference between an algorithm and a heuristic.
Algorithms are step-wise procedures with predictable, reproducible and optimal outputs. On the other hand,
heuristics are searching methods with well founded, not reproducible, and not necessarily optimal outputs.
Heuristics are in that way able to generate a solution within shorter time, but it is not guaranteed to provide
the optimal solution (Bioinformatics Organization 2022).
Mathematical optimization is a commonly used method. Some of the studies include mathematical optimization
in their algorithms and heuristics but due to simplicity such studies will be grouped with algorithms or heuristics
in this literature study.
This section will first introduce the findings of the studies within the six areas and finally the findings will
advantages and disadvantages of the different methods will be presented in Section 2.3.7.
2.3.1
Heuristics
As explained, heuristics are algorithmic frameworks or methods providing strategies to solve problems differing
from algorithms by solving problems relatively fast with the trade-off being no guarantee of finding the optimal
solution but only a sufficient approximation.
Lima and Castro 2022 has implemented the heuristic Teaching-Learning-Based Optimization (TLBO) to solve
the EVCSPP and compare the results with solving the problem as a conventional mathematical optimization
method. TLBO is inspired by the learning and teaching process in a classroom where both the teacher is spread-
ing knowledge but the pupils are also sharing knowledge with other pupils. It is assumed that all pupils have
the same probability of learning. The heuristic is initialized by setting and evaluating the population based on
a desired variable. This variable is usually the pupil grades but in the case of CP infrastructure it is installation
costs and distances between the different CPs which is wanted to be minimized. The heuristic keeps seeking for
better solutions until the termination criterion is met. The termination criterion is not described in the paper.
The method was tested and the the results compared with solving the same problem as a mathematical opti-
mization model and the same results were obtained even though the TLBO only sought for an approximation
and not necessarily the optimal solution. However, the execution time of the two methods are not mentioned.
The data used in the research is also not actual but simulated data, and the data set is quite small. Therefore,
the authors highlight the next steps of the analysis to be testing the system on a larger and real data sets and
to include capacity limitations.
In Chakraborty, D. Das, and P. Das 2022 the Symbiotic Organisms Search (SOS) heuristic is used to optimize
the location of CPs with the objective of minimizing the power loss and the voltage drop level. SOS mimics

==PAGE:17==

the symbiotic interaction technique that is used by organisms to survive in an eco-system.
The organisms
constitutes the solution of the heuristic. The heuristic consists of three phases: Mutualism, commensalism
and paratism; in each phase new solutions are investigated and evaluated. In the mutualism phase, randomly
selected organisms are chosen and modified based on their relationship. In the commensalism phase randomly
selected organisms modified in the mutualism phase are further modified, and in the paratism phase the organ-
isms from the mutualism phase are randomly chosen and slightly changed in order to find the optimal solution.
The paper concludes that the optimal solution is to locate CPs more or less equally spread across the entire
country for maximum accessibility as it is a consumer-oriented service. As mentioned, the objective of the
model is to minimize the power loss and the voltage drop level, but the authors of the paper highlight that
other objectives must also be taken into consideration.
Dait et al. 2018 propose a third heuristic being the Ascent Heuristic Algorithm to solve the EVCSPP. The
objective of the study is to maximize the profit considering waiting time, travel distances, and an elastic de-
mand. The demand is considered in different zones, where the demand within a zone is considered the same.
A non-linear integer optimization problem is created and the optimal CP location and capacity is determined
using the Ascent Heuristic Algorithm. The heuristic examines all possible stations by choosing a number of
CPs and then exchange CPs until the largest possible profit is obtained. Simulations are made with different
random demand rates in the zones to illustrate the effectiveness of the approach and the proposed method is
performing better than the baseline set in the paper.
2.3.2
Mathematical Optimization
Mathematical Optimization is a method where a mathematical optimization model is created and solved. The
output of such models is an objective value and the value of the chosen decision variables. Such models can
have one or multiple objective functions where multi-objective models have an increased complexity but are
also more detailed and nuanced. Constraints are created to define and limit the problem in both cases.
Arayici and Poyrazoglu 2019 propose a mathematical model which is an improvement of a model from 2012
for solving the EVCSPP. Their model has one objective being to minimize the installation costs. The model
presented in this paper is very simplified and assumptions that may not hold are made, i.e. that all EVs are
charged once a day. The model is tested on an enormous data set and shows promising results if minimizing
the installation cost is the only objective. In Battapothula, Yammani, and Maheswarapu 2019 a model for min-
imizing the costs is also developed. However, in this paper other costs are considered as well, e.g., development
costs and costs related to energy consumption. Further, the presented model is a multi-objective model, and it
is also wanted to minimize the voltage deviation in the electrical distribution network. This research has a high
focus on technical aspects related to charging of EVs and to improve the voltage profile. Simulations are made
in order to test the model. The simulations show a reduction in both the voltage deviation and the costs, of
which the last is also both beneficial for the EV users and the CP providers.
A multi-objective model is also created in Sadhukhan, Sivasubramani, and M. Ahmad 2019. This model differs
from the ones in Arayici and Poyrazoglu 2019 and Battapothula, Yammani, and Maheswarapu 2019 by not
having cost as an objective. The objectives are to minimize the electricity distribution loss and to maximize
the utilization of the CPs. All constraints are related to technical limitations. A fuzzy optimization technique
is used to determine the best solution. This means that approximations are accepted and the goal is not nec-
essarily to find the optimal solution but a good solution. In this way uncertainties are addressed when making
the optimization. Tests are primarily made to test the technical requirements and show that an increase in the
number of CPs can reduce the power loss.
Finally, Dong et al. 2019 considers the EVCSPP from a perspective of wanting to maximize the CP coverage. It
is highlighted that the lack of realistic data on CP usage makes it difficult to predict the charging demand, and

models. In practice, spatial patterns of existing CPs are considered and the higher the charging demand in an

The model is tested and shows that the employed daytime population and traffic flows are highly related to

2.3.3
Algorithms
Algorithms are procedures and instructions to be followed to solve a particular problem. The output of an
algorithm is unambiguous.
In Deb et al. 2020 the EVCSPP is formulated as a multi-objective problem solved using a Pareto dominance
based Chicken Swarm Optimization and Teaching Learning Based Optimization (CSO TLBO). The objective
functions seek to minimize the cost and the voltage stability, reliability and power loss index and to maximize the
accessibility index (the latter with the purpose of reducing Range Anxiety). The Chicken Swarm Optimization
(CSO) algorithm mimics the behaviour of chicken swarms to search for the optimal feature combination for
maximizing classification performance and minimizing the number of features. The output of the algorithm
is a hierarchy of all possible CP locations. The Teaching-Learning-Based Optimization (TLBO) algorithm is
described in Section 2.3.1. In this study CSO is used for ranking while the TLBO is used to determine the
utilization rate. In combination, the CSO TLBO ranks all potential CS locations based on the rank and the
utilization. The method is tested in combination with different heuristics to reduce the solving time against the
Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) algorithm on several data sets. The solutions obtained
with CSO TLBO are more diverse while the run time is longer.
F. Ahmad et al. 2022a use the Grey Wolf Optimization (GWO) algorithm to solve the EVCSPP. They consider
three objectives; to minimize the grid stress, the power loss cost, and the installation cost. The number of
EVs, charging time of battery, and service time of CPs are parameters used to model the number of CPs. The
GWO is an algorithm inspired by the nature of wolves in a crowd. The crowd consist of alpha wolves being the
leaders of the groups, grey wolves classified as secondary, beta wolves, delta wolves counted similarly compared
to alpha and beta wolves and in the end omega wolves being the wolves with lowest importance as they must
obey grey wolves. The goal is to dominate omega wolves while submitting alpha and beta wolves. A special
case of GWO algorithm is the binary GWO algorithm applicable for some problems, among these the EVCSPP.
The algorithm is tested and shows promising results reducing the power loss and costs.
The primary author of F. Ahmad et al. 2022a is also the primary author of F. Ahmad et al. 2022b. In F.
Ahmad et al. 2022b an algorithm combining GWO, described above, and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
is proposed. The objective of the study is to minimize the land cost and the power loss cost and to maximize
the EV flow. The algorithm consists of two steps:
1. The Charging Station Owner Decision Index (CSODI) is formulated to reduce the search space. The
objective is to minimize the land cost and to maximize the EVs flow with the purpose of investors gaining
more profit with less investment.
2. A model with the single objective of minimizing the power loss cost is formulated in order to determine
the optimal CP location. The hybrid Grey Wolf Optimization-Particle Swarm Optimization (GWOPSO)
algorithm is used to solve this problem.
The PSO is based on the movement of swans which can be used to determine optimal location and has shown
promising results on many real life problems, but a problem with the algorithm is the risk of getting trapped
in a local minimum. For this reason it is combined with the GWO. The GWOPSO algorithm is initialized

2.3.4
Machine Learning
Machine Learning covers algorithms in which outcomes can be predicted based on historical data. Machine
Learning algorithms are used to identify patterns in the input data, make decisions, and optimize the output
through experience.
Wahl et al. 2022 highlight reduction of Range Anxiety to be one of the most critical
factors when considering the location of CPs. However, it is difficult to determine the optimal location because
of practical factors as the road network, traffic patterns, already existing CPs and due to local differences in
charging demand. This paper proposes a formal model for optimal charging infrastructure location and considers
the problem as a reinforcement learning problem. The model seeks to maximize the benefits covering the CP
capacity, meaning how many EVs the CP can provide service for at a time, the costs, and how close a potential
CP is to be placed to an already existing CP, where the benefit is higher if the CP is not close to existing CPs.
The model is used as input for the reinforcement learning problem solved using a deep Q network reinforcement
learning combining a neural network with an experience replay mechanism addressing the data correlation and
non-stationary distributions. The method is tested and evaluated on a real data set and compared to existing
charging infrastructure. Results show that the method achieves a benefit of up to 497% compared to existing
charging infrastructure and a reducing in waiting time up to 97%. The authors highlight evaluation of the
charging plans with respect to the underlying power grid as a factor that should be incorporated into the model
in future work.
2.3.5
Genetic Algorithms
Genetic algorithms differ from traditional algorithms by being based on the principal of natural selection when
solving optimization problems, while traditional algorithms is a step for step procedure. Genetic algorithms are
a part of machine learning. In Chandra, Lalwani, and Jajodia 2022 the genetic algorithm quantum annealing is
proposed to solve the EVCSPP. The algorithm creates a population of individuals each carrying the necessary
information to calculate the fitness indicating how good the solution is. The fitness is changed until a good
enough solution is found. More specifically, quantum annealing makes it possible to investigate multiple candi-
date solutions in parallel which is very efficient. A limitation of the study is the use of hardware being D-Wave
computers which is the currently available quantum hardware but is however not optimal. The objectives of
the algorithm are: 1) To minimize the distance to the POIs, 2) To maximize the distance from existing CPs,
and 3) To maximize the distance between new CPs. The study concludes that the quantum annealing has
room for improvements. The possibilities of combining this method with evolutionary genetic algorithms are
investigated, as research have shown good results by combining these.
Danping et al. 2020 propose the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) as a solving method
for the EVCSPP. The objectives are to maximize the average usage rate of new CPs and to minimize the con-
struction costs. The algorithm is made based on an estimated usage rate of the CPs and traffic flow data based
on a heat map, charger usage data, and parking station data. The algorithm generates a parent population
consisting of CP locations from which a progeny population, also consisting of CP locations, is made. The
populations are merged, the fitness of each location is determined, and the locations are ranked. The best
n locations are selected, and the algorithm is continued until an acceptable solution is reached. Experiments
are made on a specific area on 16 square kilometers, and the results are promising and show that the queuing
problem and low usage rate of chargers can be improved.

2.3.6
Game Theory-based Methods
Game theory is a part of applied mathematics studying strategic and optimal interactions among rational actors
under the assumption that the costs and gains depend on other actors’ choices; where the actors’ decisions and
choices are interdependent. In that way, such methods consider different optimal solutions for different CP
providers. Luo, Huang, and Gupta 2018 use game theory, more specific the Bayesian Game, to formulate and
solve the EVCSPP. Bayesian probability is used to determine the outcome of the problem. Exactly a Bayesian
Game is chosen for modelling the problem, because a Bayesian game is special compared to other areas of game
theory as it allows solutions to a game with incomplete information, which can be used as EV charging service
deals with the problem of rivals in terms of more than one CP provider. Simulation software constructing a
virtual digital city is developed consisting of a four step process:
1. The problem is initialized by creating the road network, infrastructure data, and travel pattern statistics.
2. EV movement is set up by determining a random route through all possible CPs using Dijkstra’s shortest
path algorithm.
3. The Bayesian Game is carried out, and costs are calculated and a search strategy for each CP provider is
determined based on the costs.
4. The cost and search strategies of each provider are reported.
The output of the simulation is the optimal location policies for the different providers, and the results show
that CP location is consistent with traffic flow. Further, it shows that CP providers prefer clustering compared
to separating CPs.

Summary
The criteria mentioned through the reviewed literature differ both in range and perspective. The range and
perspective of the criteria and the methods for evaluating CP locations are dependent. In some cases where
a wide base of data is available, the criteria are chosen dependent on which method is needed, but in other
cases the methods are chosen based on the available data. The main purpose of reviewing the literature is to
investigate the wide range of different criteria suitable to describe and assess charging demand and behavior
and location of CPs.
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the optimal location of CPs seen from the perspective of the EV
users. There will always be a need to consider the technical specifications of the potential site being evaluated
for installment of CPs. These are seen in the literature to include metrics regarding economy, environment and
accessibility.
The reviewed literature did not investigate potential CP locations from a user perspective. Data about drivers
were included indirectly using origin and destination data, and information about activities and services pro-
vided near CPs, but no studies included behavior data or more user descriptive data.
Thus, this is a gap
within the field of optimizing CP locations. It is relevant to investigate whether including this perspective in
optimization methods will result in new insights regarding CP locations. Further, it can be argued that the
17 of 104
==PAGE:22==
Master Thesis
Kathrine Hille & Nanna Damgaard
perspective must be investigated to ensure that that perspectives of all relevant stakeholders are considered
when planning future CP locations.
Looking at the optimization methods reviewed in Section 2.3, both similarities and differences are identified.
First, the complexity of the methods are very diverse. Some of the papers propose simple models with only one
objective and a few constraints, while models in other studies have multiple objectives an many constraints.
The complexity of a model is however not related to the performance or the robustness, as a very simple model
can show promising results for solving a specific problem. However, it is in general seen that a model with
increased complexity gives a more varied and detailed basis on which the optimization of CP locations is made.
On the other hand, the more complex the model is, and the more data the model requires to solve the problem,
the more time consuming the method will be to execute. In that way complexity shall be seen as a trade-off
between solving time and a detailed and precise basis for decision-making. In that regard it is important to
determine the application of the model. For instance, some of the simple models proposed in the review study
should be seen as a first step in a process of making a final model. In such cases it makes sense to make and
test the simple model in order to see if it performs as expected.
The range of location optimization methods in Section 2.3 is broad, and several different methods are showing
promising results. However, it is very difficult to compare the quality and performance of the different methods,
as both the objectives of the studies and the nature of the models are very different. In some papers different
methods are compared, but since the data set across the studies are not the same, the basis of comparison is very
small. A point for discussion could however be if benefits would be gained from combining different methods -
for disadvantages of one methods to be off-set out by advantages of another method. A proposal could be to
create an algorithm combining a heuristic with a mathematical optimization model. Such an algorithm might
show great results as the heuristic would narrow down the search space and in this way make it faster to solve
the mathematical optimization model and get an exact and optimal solution which would not necessarily be
possible with the heuristic alone.
A last topic for discussion regarding the optimization methods is the objectives. Regardless of the method,
most of the studies have the objective of optimizing the power usage, the load on the power grid, grid stress,
energy consumption or something related, optimizing the CP utilization and diversity as well as optimizing the
costs for the CP providers. Common for these objectives is that there is no or only a very small focus on the EV
users. Some of the studies mention that a reduction of Range Anxiety is wanted, but there is a lack of focus on
the EV users in general. Range Anxiety is a problem and a concern for many EV users (Feil 2022), which makes
it an important factor to consider. Further, a lack of focus on EV users also means a lack of focus on demand.
Without consideration of user preferences, it is also not considered where the demand for CPs is high and low.
This is not only negatively influencing the EV users but can also be a disadvantage to CP providers as it is
likely that they could earn more profit by considering the demand for CP when deciding where to place new CPs.
To conclude on the reviewed literature, the user perspective regarding criteria and potential CP locations
was not investigated, and no studies included behavior data or more user descriptive data. This is needed to
consider the user preferences and will both be relevant on a general level but also for different user groups with
different needs.
From the review regarding optimization methods it can be concluded that model complexity shall be seen as a
trade-off between solving time and a detailed and nuanced basis for decision-making, which must be considered
whenever developing or using a method. In addition, the possibility of combining different methods might be
interesting to investigate how to eliminate disadvantages of one method by advantages of another. Some studies
touch upon this but it is not a well researched area. Finally, as the review about criteria influencing CP location
concluded, there is a lack of knowledge and investigation of the EV users’ preferences, needs and CP demand
when determining the optimal CP location. These criteria are either not or only on a small scale included as
parameters in the proposed methods. User preferences should be a topic for further investigation as ease of use

a focus on user preferences and CP demand when choosing criteria to determine the optimal location of CPs
and the use of multiple methods in combination represents two gaps in the literature which are interesting and

Scope
CP location is a complex problem with many different stakeholders having different perspectives, preferences
and opinions in terms of the most important factors to consider. Some of the perspectives are already commonly
used and widely described in literature and thereby not of interest in this thesis. In addition, within the fixed
time limit of this project it is not be possible to consider all perspectives thoroughly. The purpose of this section
is therefore to define the scope and the limitations of the project.
This thesis is focused on optimizing the CP locations from a user perspective, meaning that user behavior,
preferences, and concerns will be considered. The technical requirements as e.g., power grid load, costs related
to installation and operation of CPs and profit are features which are widely investigated and considered in
existing literature as described in the literature review in Section 2. Thus, these features will not be explicitly
investigated in this thesis, and will only be included when these are side effects of features considered important
when viewing the problem from a user perspective. The range of EVs also differs from car to car, and it is
expected that the range will increase in the future. However, in this project a range of 300 km is used as this
is the average range of current EVs (Andersen 2022). Furthermore, the user scenarios of interest are those
occurring in the daily life of EV users and therefore not behavior when e.g., travelling in EV on holiday.
The project is only concerned about optimizing the charging locations in Denmark, and other countries are
thereby not considered. The primary reason for only including Denmark in the project is data accessibility and
the fact that it would have been impossible or at least very time consuming to get data on several countries.
Further, it is assumed that transport habits and patterns are different from country to country, meaning that
the complexity of the algorithm to be developed would increase dramatically. Lastly, a larger data set would
require more computational power making it very time consuming to both process data and run the algorithm.
Thus, this project can be seen as an investigation of whether including a user perspective when considering the
CP locations shows promising results and should be included in larger projects in the future.
Simplifications are made to make a mathematical model of the real world. These are:
• Denmark is divided into square cells, and the output of the developed algorithm is the number of CPs to
place in each square cell. Thus, the precise locations of the CPs within the square cells are not considered.
• It is not considered if it is practically possible to locate the number of CPs determined by the algorithm
in the square cells. This means, that power grid load or settlements are not considered.
• Traffic flow is not considered.
• The CP type is not considered (if it is a NC, FC, or a SC).
• Only electric passenger vehicles are considered. Thus, electric buses, taxis, and freight vehicles are not
investigated.
In continuation of the above, it must be highlighted that the algorithm is to be seen as a tool to be used in
combination with knowledge within the field and "common sense". Thus, the algorithm is not intelligent and is
thereby not able to give precise coordinates for CP locations but only the optimal number of CPs within each
square cell.
Further, an assumption made in the project is that there needs to be 100,000 CPs in Denmark to reach the
recommendation of 0.1 public CPs per EV expecting 1 million EVs in 2030 (Ryming 2022).
In conclusion, the main focus of this project is to develop an algorithm optimizing CP localization in Den-
mark seen from the perspective of the EV users. The findings of this method are to be compared with the
findings from other methods considering the problem from other perspectives to examine if the solution seen
from different perspectives is the same, or if combining several methods with different focus points is advan-

of the problem will only increase.
More stakeholders will be interested in the problem and have opinions

decision-making basis, which makes the need for a user perspective towards the problem indispensable.

Data Management
In the literature review in Section 2, state-of-the-art regarding charging infrastructure was mapped focusing
on which location methods are widely used, and which criteria are included in the analyses. Looking at the
methods, three things were concluded:
1. It is in general difficult to compare different optimization methods, as the objectives, the nature of the
models created, and the data used across the methods are very different. However, it is identified that
all methods have both advantages and disadvantages. Thus, it might be relevant to investigate if several
methods can be used in combination for disadvantages of one method to be off-set by advantages of
another.
2. The complexity of current methods vary a lot, and the complexity can roughly be seen as a trade-off
between accuracy and execution time.
3. The objectives of current methods are mostly related to load on the power grid, power consumption,
CP utilization, and costs.
In general, the preferences of the users are not taken into account.
Some
studies mention Range Anxiety as something that should be sought to minimize, but this is not a well-
investigated area. It is therefore identified that there is a lack of knowledge within this field. This is
neither advantageous for the EV users nor the CP providers, as a lack of focus on user preferences also is a
lack of focus on demand. Seen from the perspective of the CP providers, a higher focus on user preferences
- and thereby demand - might increase revenue.
Looking at the criteria included in current literature, the same lack of focus on EV users is seen. Table 2.1
summarizes the criteria investigated across the included studies. From this it can be concluded, that most
criteria lay within the categories of economics, environment, transport network and technical specifications in-
dicating the lack of user focus. However, some studies look at the accessibility of CPs, traffic conditions, driving
behavior and so-called social factors indicating that user preferences are investigated. However, the perspective
from which these criteria are included does not accommodate the EV users, as the main focus is a high CP
utilization and for the CPs to be as profitable as possible. Thus, a lack of focus on the EV users’ preferences is
identified.
Having mapped state-of-the-art regarding charging infrastructure and identified gaps, it is desired to create
a method with the following properties and characteristics:
• The method must consist of more than one method in combination - referred to as an algorithm - for
disadvantages of one method to be off-set by advantages of another.
• Complexity and time consumption for executing the models set up in the algorithm must be balanced in
a way such that it is possible to run the algorithm within a reasonable amount of time and the level of
detail is sufficient for the results to be adequate and not too superficial.
• The algorithm must be developed from the perspective of the EV user.
This will be the only focus
when developing the algorithm, and no other perspectives will be considered. Thus, for the final CP
infrastructure to be realistic and feasible, it might be relevant to combine this method with other methods
to create a holistic view of the problem and to accommodate as many stakeholders as possible.

can afford to switch out their current car or is motivated to prioritize buying an EV. Further, the concept of

respondents with the characteristics of the entire population, as it is assumed that the EV users have somewhat
higher technical competencies than the average Dane and therefore will belong to a certain group of the Danish
population. Thus, the aim is not that the characteristics of the respondents in regard to these features match
the characteristics of the Danish population considering the same features.

4.2
The Danish National Travel Survey (TU)
The Danish National Travel Survey (TU) is an interview survey with the purpose of documenting travel patterns
and transport behaviour of the Danish population. The survey tries to answer the questions: How, how much,
were, when and why do people travel?. The survey is thoroughly described in the documentation created by
DTU (Christiansen and Skougaard 2019), and only the most important details relevant for this project will be
described here.
The purpose of including and analyzing TU data in this thesis is to investigate EV and ICE users’ transport
habits and patterns in terms of how far they drive, where they drive, where they stay at their destination, and
whether the characteristics of typical EV and typical ICE users differ according to the data. The travel distance
and the distance from home to the place of primary occupation provide important information, as this is utilized
in the determination of the optimal distance between CPs. The duration and purpose of the primary stay are
important factors to consider to determine potential destinations optimal for CP locations. Furthermore, it can
also be relevant in determining activities the CPs must be placed close to and possibly the type of CP for future
work.
4.2.1
TU: Data Collection
DTU has been responsible for the survey since 2001, which is however supported both in terms of founding and
survey development by a large number of partners. Among these are The Danish Ministry of Transport, The
Danish Road Directorate, Danish Regions, several municipalities etc. The selection of potential respondents,
the creation of an introductory letter and the conduction of the interview is performed by Epinion (Baescu
2022a). The data set used in this thesis is the latest containing data from May 2006 to June 2022 including
201,313 interviews and 591,434 journeys (Baescu 2022b).
The sampling is based on a so-called 208 strata system (2 genders x 8 age groups x 13 geographical groups) of
the age between 10 and 84. The sampling is stratified to ensure that the number of selected samples within each
strata is determined in a way such that the expected number of respondents in each strata is proportional with
the population in each strata. In practice this means that the sample is representative of the Danish population
38 of 104
==PAGE:43==
Master Thesis
Kathrine Hille & Nanna Damgaard
(Christiansen and Skougaard 2015).
Figure 4.20 illustrates how the surveys are planned.
Figure 4.20: The TU data collection process (Baescu 2022a)
From Figure 4.20 it appears that 28,000 respondents are chosen every year, which is a large sample size even
though only 40% actually are participating. The error margin of this sample size is with 95% certainty below
±1 percentage points as the sample is randomly chosen from the target population (Bækgaard et al. 2020).The
sample size is thereby considered sufficient.
20% of the interviews are conducted as online questionnaires, while the remaining 80% are done via telephone.
Both methods have advantages and disadvantages. When answering the questions online as a questionnaire,
the respondents might interpret the questions differently, it is not possible to ask questions if in doubt, and
no one can ask follow-up questions if needed. On the other hand, the interviewer might ask the questions in
different ways with emphasis on different words etc., when performing telephone interviews. In that way the two
methods might lead to different answers which must be considered when analyzing the results. However, when
using several methods it can be argued that advantages and disadvantages to a certain extend are cancelling
each other out.
Lastly, error correction and post-processing is carried out. It is not described how this is done, but the purpose
is data reformatting enabling quantification of the data.
TU data is divided into 6 data tables with different focuses. Each interview is assigned a session ID being
the primary key of the interview, which makes it possible to combine data across different tables. For each
interview, data on 267 features are collected. The relevance of all features in regard to this thesis is assessed,
and only the ones considered relevant are used.
Each interview is based on one travel day, with the entire data set covering 365 days a year to get a complete
picture and to cover both work days and weekends. The phrase "journey" is used to describe the aggregated
trips for one day. Each journey is split into so called "trips" being a tour from one place to another, while each
trip is broken further down into "partial trips", where a new partial trip is considered as a switch in transport
mode. In this analysis, only the behavior of respondents driving passenger cars is relevant, as the purpose is
to define transport habits and patterns of current and potential EV users. 99.7% of the respondents using a
passenger car as the first transport mode does only have 1 trip in their journey, and only journeys and not trips
or partial trips are therefore considered in this analysis.

Geodemographic data on the Danish population as well as Building and Housing Data (BHD) from Denmark is

The MGBHD will be used in the algorithm. The extraction of relevant data from the MGBHD is based on

4.3.1
Geodemographic Data and Building and Housing Data: Data Collection
The geodemographic data received from LIFA contains statistical variables from Statistics Denmark, which is
the central authority of Danish statistics. The BHD is from the Danish Building and Housing Register.
The geodemographic data contains 27 features describing age, occupation, car availability, daytime population
etc. for 3,798,730 Danish households. The features are offered as factors representing the most probable out-
come of a given variable for a household (LIFA 2022). Each data point contains the factors for one unique
Danish address. The addresses are disclosed by municipality and street codes as well as house, floor, and door
numbers.

4.3.2
MGBHD: Data Processing
The primary goal of the data processing is to create a unique identifier for the addresses in each of the data
sets enabling a merge of the data sets and to divide data into geographic square cells preparing it to be input
in the algorithm. Reformatting and alignment of the data is also a part of the process.
Merging the data sets yields one source holding all information across data sets for all addresses present in both
data sets. This also enables geographic plotting of the demographic data as the BHD provides latitude and
longitude coordinates not originally available in the geodemographic data.
Creating LocID for Data Set Merging
By exploration of the features in both data sets it is seen how some features are of the data type object. Feature
content of the type object can be technically challenging to alter, concatenate, and otherwise reformat. The
features holding information about the addresses in both data sets are the municipality code, street code, house
number, floor number and door number features. These features are to be used together to create a unique
address identifier and are therefore converted in both data sets. The denotation of municipality codes and street
codes in the two data sets are already aligned, leaving only the three features representing house, floor and door
numbers to be reformatted for alignment.
These three features are checked for none-value content. The house number feature contains no none-value
fields, while the floor and door number features contains many none-values. The floor and door number features
contain a high number of none-values because these are not necessary for many buildings. The empty fields
in the floor and door numbers are filled with the strings ’NoNE’ and ’NoNSD’ respectively. The purpose of
replacing empty fields with different values for floor and door number is to not risk identical ID’s for different
adresses, which is further explained in Table 4.11. The feature types are converted to strings making the up-
coming process of reformatting and combining the features simple.
There are slight differences between the data sets in how the house, floor, and door numbers are stated. The
difference lie in how one data set has a different amount of 0’s in the beginning of their house, floor and door
numbers, while the other data set does not contain redundant 0’s.
The alignment of house numbers is done by removing all ’0’s to the left of the string converting for example
’004’ to ’4’. ’0’ is then added in the beginning of the string making it ’04’. Because the house numbers initially
have a different number of ’0’s in the beginning of the string, all ’0’s needed to be removed before adding one

4.3.4
MGBHD: Discussion
The MGBHD are a large amount of data relevant to use in the optimization of CP locations. However, the
MGBHD do also have some limitations, which will be discussed and assessed in the following.
When using the MGBHD it is important to be aware of how the data is descriptive of Danish households and not
individuals. Some of the factors are summed, some are averaged, some are descriptive of a single person in the
household etc. Further, some factors are stated for each household while others are given by geographic square
cells. Extracting information from the data set without handling this yields sums, averages or proportions
which are not based on the true data and will therefore be wrongful input to the algorithm. As an example, the
daytime population is given as sums for square cells in the data set, while the nighttime population is given per
household. If both features were summed on either square cell or household level it would yield a much lower
nighttime population or a much higher employed daytime population.
Extracting the households of typical EV and typical ICE users is done by sorting according to age, educational,
and occupational factors as well as the type of residence and the car availability factor of the households. From
the questionnaire and TU data the characteristics of typical EV and typical ICE users are derived. Based on
these characteristics the households of typical EV and typical ICE users are extracted from the MGBHD. The
MGBHD is not sufficiently detailed to sort strictly according to the insights from the analysis. According to
table 4.9 the age of a typical EV user is between 40-59 while the age of a typical ICE user is between 40-49, but
when extracting the household of each of these user types it is not possible to distinguish between these age
groups which are already overlapping. The age factor in the MGBHD is given by intervals that are to wide to
distinguish within the group of 40-59 year old people. Furthermore the age factor is as already mentioned only
descriptive of the oldest member of the households.
The educational and occupational factors also used to extract typical EV and typical ICE users are descriptive
of the average length of education for persons in the household and the number of employed persons in the
household as well as the tendencies for the type of employment.
The conditions mentioned above present a risk of excluding households that fit the characteristics of typical
EV households, and also the risk of including households that might not fit the characteristics exactly. As the
characteristics of the typical EV users will transition more and more towards the characteristics of the typical
ICE users, the detail level of the data is considered sufficient for the purpose.
The MGBHD make it possible to extract data serving as input to the algorithm, but it is crucial to pay attention
to the nature of the data when extracting specific factors for households in specific areas. The specific extraction
of data is described in detail in Section 5 for both parts of the algorithm.
The creation of square cells to sort data by is done based on the MGBHD. Therefore, square cells are created
for all areas in Denmark with buildings present in them, but there will be a few locations not covered by square
cells. These are square cells without buildings or existing CPs. It is expected that these locations would not
generally pose as great options for placement of CPs, because no relevant activities, buildings or households
are located in these areas. The square cells from the MGBHD will become input to the heuristic and the
mathematical optimization problem. The optimal locations and amount of CPs at these locations will be based
on the inputted square cells. This entails that areas of Denmark not covered by the square cells calculated
from the MGBHD can not be chosen as CP locations. The areas which are at risk of not being represented as
56 of 104
==PAGE:61==
Master Thesis
Kathrine Hille & Nanna Damgaard
input to the algorithm are not considered beneficial in regard to the factors identified as impacting the optimal
locations. It is therefore accepted that these areas will not be assessed in the algorithm.
The above mentioned uncertainties are important to mention and take into account. As the factors are handled
carefully and according to how the data are collected and stored, the only factor imposing uncertainty is the
age factor. The result of the age factor detail level is overlapping groups of typical EV and typical ICE users,
meaning that some households might be present both in the group of typical EV users and in the group of
typical ICE users. A higher number of EV and ICE users for each square cell will contribute to the location of
more CPs in the particular square cell. The overlap is not skewing the amount of CPs chosen to be set up in
the square cells as the groups of typical EV and ICE users are weighted equally.

To get the same square cells for both the MGBHD and the existing CP data, the latitude and longitude coordi-

conversion, the latitude and longitude coordinates are converted to northing and easting coordinates in UTM

that they can later be converted by the modulus process to square cells of 500 and 1000 meters respectively.
When converting coordinates of a different location than one within UTM zone 32, the parameters must be
changed to consider the Earth curvature and the reference system used for the coordinates at the given location
to enable correct calculation of the northing and easting coordinates.

5
Optimization Algorithm
This section describes the process of developing the algorithm used in this thesis. As described in the literature
review in Section 2 and in Section 4, there are several different methods and approaches towards optimizing CP
locations. The advantages and disadvantages of the different methods are summarized in Table 2.2. The main
consideration is how execution time and complexity of the model should be balanced, as a higher level of detail
requires a longer execution time. This trade-off is always to be considered when working with optimization
problems, and due to the limited time available in this project, a reduced execution time is of high priority.
However, if the findings of the project shall be able to genuinely affect how CP infrastructure is planned, a
certain level of detail is required. The CP location problem is very complex, as there is a high number of
stakeholders with different interests. In this project, the problem is only investigated from a user perspective
which decreases the level of complexity. The chosen methods must be executable within a reasonable time
frame and it must be possible to create a model with a reasonable level of detail. First, it is decided to create
an algorithm consisting of two elements, as it allows for the overall problem to be broken down into smaller
pieces making it easier both to design, implement, and test. As the data sets used in this project are large,
and a wide range of different data are used, the execution time will tend to be very long. To cope with this
it is decided to create a heuristic with the purpose of decreasing the search space. This is the first part of the
algorithm. However, a heuristic does not necessarily find a global optimal solution but rather a local optimal
solution. Thus, another method is required in combination with the heuristic. It is decided to use mathematical
optimization, as the level of detail can be adjusted, and as constraints are taken into account. Multiple objec-
tives are required for the CP location problem, as EV users have several habits and preferences which must be
considered as explained in Section 4.1. Second, constraints are needed to ensure that the desired amount of CPs
are located with a distance suitable for the EV users to always be able to reach a CP etc. A final advantage is
that it is possible to significantly vary the level of detail. In that way it is possible to create different versions
of the model and monitor when a sufficient level of detail is reached within a reasonable amount of time. The
mathematical optimization model, more specifically an Integer Program (IP), is the second part of the algorithm.
The other methods investigated in the literature study are machine learning, genetic algorithms and game
theory-based methods.
Machine learning and mathematical optimization have many similarities. They are both used across many in-
dustries, running on data requiring extensive computing resources, and based on mathematics. However, there
are some differences making mathematical optimization more applicable to use in this project. First, machine
learning is a predictive tool, while mathematical optimization is a prescriptive tool. This means that machine
learning can, if implemented correctly, predict likely future outcomes, while mathematical optimization can
guide in decision making. The latter is exactly what is needed in this project. Second, the adaptability of the
two methods is different. When running a mathematical optimization model, it is easy to change the data on
which the model runs, meaning that it is easy to adjust the model due to changing conditions. On the other
hand, machine learning models suffers from model drift. Such models rely on historical data and will therefore
lose their predictive power over time. Thus, models will need to be re-trained on new data, and the adaptability
is consequently lower than for mathematical optimization. In this project, adaptability is important for two
reasons: 1) The EV industry is rapidly growing, and it is therefore favorable to be able to change data easily,
and 2) In this project only the Danish CP infrastructure is investigated, but with a model with a high level of
adaptability it will be easy to use the same model to optimize the CP infrastructure in other countries as well
(Rothberg 2021). Lastly, utilizing machine learning on the location optimization problem raises a few challenges.
To apply supervised learning to find the optimal locations the training of the machine learning algorithm will
require training data. Training data is labelled as input and output data. As there is no simple truth about
the optimal way to locate CPs, and thereby no useful output data for the input data used in this project,

Heuristic
Figure 5.1: Visualization of the project process illustrating the different steps of the project and their inputs

As explained, a heuristic is a strategy trying to find a good solution to a given problem in a short time.
Heuristics do not guarantee to find the optimal solution which should be seen as a trade-off between time
and the importance of finding the exact best solution. Heuristics are therefore useful when seeking a solution
that is not necessarily the optimal solution within a short amount of time. This is the case in this thesis. As
explained in Section 4.3.2, Denmark is represented using respectively 109,555 500 meter square cells and 39,741
1000 meter square cells, each for which the optimal number of CPs must be determined. As these numbers
are high, an exact algorithm would have to run for a very long time in order to determine the optimal CP
location. A heuristic is therefore developed to decrease the number of square cells which must be considered
when determining the optimal CP location.
5.1.1
Heuristic: Data Management
The data input for the heuristic is the employed daytime population, the nighttime population, the number of
households where the housing type is apartment or house respectively, the two last only where the household
has access to at least one car.
The daytime and nighttime population are summed differently as they have been gathered and stated differ-
ently in the data set. As mentioned in Section 4.3.4, the daytime population is stated per 100 meter square

Employed Daytime Population and Nighttime Population Data
For the 500 meter square cells the extraction of the employed daytime population needs to be done in two

Number of Apartments and Houses with a Car Available
The MGBHD contains a car availability factor for each household and the type of households. To weigh the
nighttime population, these factors are considered together. First, the car availability factor describes the likely
number of cars in the household. With a car availability factor above 3, the household has at least one car.
Second, based on the usage codes provided for each household as stated in the MGBHD, residence types can
be used to extract data. For each 500 meter and 1000 meter square cell, the number of households with a
car availability factor above 3 is summed for both apartments and houses. Usage code 140.0 is used to denote
Residence in Apartment Building, Multi-Family House or Two-Family House. For each 500 meter and 1000
meter square cell, the sum of households with the usage code 140.0 and a car availability factor above 3 is
extracted to get the population residing in apartments with at least one car.
The sum of houses per square cell is in the same way extracted by counting the number of households with
usage code 120.0 and a car availability factor above 3. Usage code 120.0 denotes Detached Single Family-Houses.
The nighttime population can thereby be weighed and influence the recommendation of CP locations based on
whether they are mainly house or apartment residents, and whether they have a car available.

5.1.2
Heuristic: Method Creation
This section describes the implementation of the Local Search heuristic used to halve the number of square cells
to consider. Local Search is an approach, where the starting point is a certain position in the search space. The
solution is then modified by moving from one solution to a neighbor solution to obtain the optimal solution. As
the neighborhood is not searched systematically, there is no guarantee to find the optimal solution.
The Local Search heuristic consists of the following elements:
• A solution representation
• An initial solution
• A neighborhood function
• A step function
• Acceptance criterion
• Termination criterion
To create a solution for the problem, the solution representation must be defined. It is decided to represent
the solution as an array consisting of all square cells included in the solution, an array consisting of all square
cells not included in the solution, and the objective value of the solution. Each square cell has the following
attributes:
• A key identifying square cell i represented by a string.
• The employed daytime population in square cell i represented by an integer (EDPi).
• The nighttime population in square cell i represented by an integer (NPi).
• The number of apartments where the household has at least one car in square cell i represented by an
integer (ACari).
• The number of houses where the household has at least one car in square cell i represented by an integer
(HCari).
The attributes of a square cell create the base for determining the objective value of the solution. The objective
value is determined using Equation 1.

This objective function is chosen, as the purpose of the heuristic is to eliminate square cells in which it is not
beneficial to locate CPs. These are square cells in which only a few people either live or stay during the day.
Thus, square cells with a high nighttime and employed daytime population are prioritized. Further, the number
of houses and apartments with households having at least one car is included in the objective for two reasons.
First, only households who own a car are included, as CPs are only needed in areas where people have cars.
Second, people living in apartments have a higher need for public CPs than people living in houses, as it is
possible for the latter to install a CP on their own property. The fraction, ACari
HCari in Equation 1 is only multiplied
with the nighttime population and not the employed daytime population, as the nighttime population is only of
interest if they do not have a private CP installed by their house. The need is biggest in areas where residents
need charging possibilities because they are unable to install their own. By multiplying this fraction with the
nighttime population, the nighttime population is practically disregarded if the population consist mainly of
households living in houses, whereas it becomes of greater weight when consisting mainly of households living
in apartments. Further, insights from both the questionnaire and TU data show how EV users wish to charge
at their primary stay during the day, and for this reason the daytime population is used directly, included in
Equation 1 as EDPi. If either the number of apartments where the household has at least one car or the
number of houses where the household has at least one car in a square cell is 0, ACari
HCari is 0, meaning that only
the employed day population is considered for this square cell.

The initial solution must be created. In this case, two approaches towards creating an initial solution are tested.
The first approach is to generate the initial solution by selecting the square cells with the highest number of
people living in apartments to include in the solution. The remaining square cells are excluded from the solution.
This initial solution is created with a "greedy" mindset, as a high number of people living in apartments yields
a high objective value due to the nature of the objective function. The other approach is to create a random
initial solution. The advantage of the first approach is that a relatively good solution is guaranteed, as the
initial solution is good. For Local Search heuristics, solutions very close to the initial solution are investigated,
meaning that if the time limit is low, the best solution will be very similar to the initial solution. For the
random approach, the initial solution will be better in some cases but worse in other cases. Both approaches
are tested when testing different neighborhood functions as explained later.
A neighborhood function is a function generating possible solutions in the neighborhood of the current so-
lution performing local changes to the current solution. From the performance of the neighborhood function,
the neighborhood of the solution is in this way reached. The neighborhood function used in this heuristic is a
swap-function (Stidsen 2021) as illustrated in Figure 5.3.

A usual disadvantage associated with Local Search heuristics is that they tend to get trapped in a local optima
as a consequence of the fact that only neighbor solutions are investigated. To reduce the risk of getting trapped
in a local optima it is therefore decided to create a so-called random restart, if a better solution is not obtained
within a predefined number of iterations aiming for a solution in a new neighborhood. In practice this is done
by creating 10 new, random solutions after a defined number of iterations without improvements and continuing
with the best. However, if the new solution is not better than the currently best, the currently best is of course
still considered as the best solution. For Neighborhood function 1 and 3, the random restart is performed after
10 iterations without any improvements, while it is performed after 10,000 iterations without improvements in
neighborhood function 2 as the nature of the second neighborhood function makes it possible to perform more
iterations within a shorter period of time compared to the other neighborhood functions. For all three functions,
two random element exists being 1) Which square cells are tried to be swapped, and 2) The random restart.
This makes it possible to obtain the same solution more than once, which can be seen as a waste of time.
These neighborhood functions are simple to implement and have a low degree of complexity.
However, a
disadvantage with the approaches is that it is possible to get the same solution multiple times due to the

amount of time, as the number of square cells to loop through will differ from iteration to iteration. This
uncertainty will be more significant if the heuristic is run for a short amount of time, while it is expected that
it is more likely that the number of iterations with few and many swaps will cancel each other out with a longer

implementation of the heuristic, the neighborhood function and the step function are combined to shorten the
computation time, as both functions need to go through the whole neighborhood.

minutes as expected. High-quality data is of high priority to obtain a good solution from the IP. However,
the difference between the objectives obtained from executing the model in respectively five and 60 minutes is

solution and the other not being a part of the solution. When a swap resulting in a higher objective value that

then determined.

An IP is a mathematical optimization program, in which all variables must be integers (including binary
variables). The purpose of the IP is to optimize the location of CPs in Denmark based on the findings gathered
from the data management process described in Section 4. In this section it is described how the model is
created, how data included in the model as parameters are extracted and processed, and lastly the results from
executing the model will be presented.
5.2.1
IP: Method Creation
The elements of an IP and how it is created is described in this section. The IP model consists of four elements
being:
• Parameters being data inputs to the model with known values.
• Decision variables for which optimal values must be found.
• An objective function which is to be optimized.
• Constraints being a set of equalities and/or inequalities setting the boundaries within which all feasible
solutions lie.
Problem Sets
The following problem sets are defined and used in the model:
• Square cells: i ∈Squarecells = {1, ...., I}
• Square cells: j ∈Squarecells = {1, ...., J}
• Square cell sizes: k ∈Squarecellsizes = {500, 1000}
Parameters
The following parameters are used:
• ai: The number of relevant activities in square cell i.
• di: A weight encapsulating the number of people respectively living in and having square cell i as the
location of their primary stay.
• evi: The number of typical EV user households (as defined from the questionnaire) living in square cell i.
• cui: The number of typical ICE user households (as defined from the TU data) living in square cell i.
• cpi: The number of existing CPs in square cell i.
• fwi: The proportion of apartments measured against all other building types in square cell i.
• disti,j: A distance matrix with a value of 1 indicating that the distance between i and square cell j is
between 1 and 141 kilometers.
• maxcpk: The maximum number of CPs to be located within a square cell of size k.
In the following, the restructuring, extraction, and formatting of the needed data contained in the parameters
will be thoroughly motivated and described.
Activities per square cell (ai)
The TU data analysis yielded insights regarding the purpose of the primary stay. Figure 4.32 shows the most
frequent purposes. To extract the activities present and available in the 500 and 1000 meter square cells, the
activities are used to identify the building types facilitating the activities. Two examples are ’workplace’ and
’shopping’, which means that office buildings and grocery store buildings should be summed for each square
cell to count the activity possibilities. The building codes for desired building types are identified to create a
subset of the data set holding only these buildings. The activities per square cell are retrieved by summing the
number of activity buildings in the subset for each 500 and 1000 meter square cell.

the mathematical model accommodating areas where most people are during daytime and nighttime. Even

practically feasible. To a certain extend, practicalities can be left out and the real world can be simplified with
the argument that the purpose of the model is to give rough estimates and recommendations, and that simplifi-

unrealistic to locate 92,000 CPs in the centre of Copenhagen. To ensure that the model isn’t suggesting to

sizes is determined. Currently, the highest amount of CPs within one CS is 34 (Elbilviden 2022). Considering
the need from a purely mathematical perspective and based on the goal of 1 million EVs in 2030 and the current
number being 100,000 (Bilimportører 2022a), it could be argued that the largest number of CPs within a CS
should be at least 34 · 10 = 340 as the number of CPs must be more than 10 times as large in 2030 to reach the
EU recommendation. First, logically this seems as a very large number. Second, the CS with 34 CPs is located
in Nykøbing Falster. The nature of this model prioritizes areas where most people are during day and night and
will not result in a high number of CPs to be located in Nykøbing Falser. This model will in general prioritize

The objective function (4) indicates that the problem is a maximization problem. The overall goal of maximizing
the objectives is to create a CP infrastructure being as advantageous for the users as possible. In the following,
the different parts of the objective function will be explained.
First, it is sought to prioritize square cells as CP locations where the number of so called "relevant activities" are
high, and where more people are staying during the day than living. Activities are considered relevant if they
79 of 104
==PAGE:84==
Master Thesis
Kathrine Hille & Nanna Damgaard
are the typical purpose of driving to destinations. This is included in the model as it makes the accessibility for
CPs high, since it increases the chances of a CP being available for the EV where the user needs it. Further, it
is convenient for the EV users to charge their EVs at activities so the charging time is not seen as wasted time.
Thus, activities are weighed higher if people are staying in a square cell during the day compared to if they live
in the square cell. This, since people can either have a CP at home or be at home when charging publicly if
the CP is located in the same square cell as where they live making the need for activities smaller. In this way,
the demand for CPs is considered by looking into the number of people having a square cell as the location of
their residence or primary stay. It is thereby sought to balance the number of CPs with the number of people
staying in a square cell. Thus, a square cell where the number of people living and being as primary stay is
high will result in an equivalent high number of CPs. This is mathematically expressed as ai · di · xi making it
advantageous to locate CPs in square cells with a high number of relevant activities and many people staying
during the day.
While the first part of the objective function is considering buildings containing relevant activities and the
number of people respectively living and staying in a square cell during the day, the next part - expressed as
(evi + cui) · fwi) · xi - considers the number of respectively EV and ICE users living in apartments in each
square cell. More concrete, the model is trying to increase the number of CPs in areas where the concentration
of respectively EV and ICE users is high. This is included in the objective function to ensure a balance between
the number of CPs with the number of EV and ICE users, such that CPs are available at the place where the
users need them. Further, both EV and ICE users are considered, as it is expected that more and more ICE
users will be EV users over time. However, EV and ICE users living in apartments are prioritized higher than
EV and ICE users living i houses. Most people living in apartments will not be able to install a CP where they
live, and it is therefore considered of high importance to ensure that these people will have access to public
CPs. In practice, this is ensured by location most CPs in square cells with a high proportion of apartments (fwi).
Besides the objective function, the model consists of some constraints described below:
• Constraint 5 ensures that the solution consist of exactly 100,000 CPs as wanted.
• Constraint 6 ensures that the current CPs are included in the solution by forcing the number of CPs in
square cells where CPs are already located to be at least the current number.
• Constraint 7 ensures that at least one CP is located in sqaure i if yi is set to 1 by ensuring that xi is
greater than or equal to yi.
• Constraint 8 forces yi to be 1 if xi is greater than 0. This a so-called "Big M" constraint, where M must
be sufficiently large. In this case, M is therefore chosen to be 300, as it is not possible for xi to exceed
this value with the square cell sizes used in this project.
• Constraint 9 ensures that the total number of CPs within a square cell of size k does not exceed respectively
180 and 300 for 500 and 1000 meter square cells as explained earlier.
• Constraint 10 ensures that the distance between two CPs don’t exceed 141 kilometers. This constraint
is included in the model to ensure that all EV users can reach a CP within a distance that should make
the deviations from their habits and patterns as small as possible and thereby relieve stress. Technically,
yi equals one in square cells where at least one CP is located. Thus, the constraint ensures that for all
square cells at least on CP must be located within 141 kilometres.
• Constraint 11 and 12 defines the decision variables. xi is defined as a positive integer while yi is binary.
It can be seen that the model is an IP, as all decision variables are integer or binary.

Validation and Comparison of Methods and Results
In this section, the CP location optimization method developed in this project is compared to the method
of a large Danish charging operator, while the obtained results are compared to the results obtained from
the Frederiksberg Urban Smart Electromobility (FUSE) Project. The purpose is to conclude if the method
developed by the charging operater and the results from the FUSE project are similar to what is obtained in
this project to validate the method and the results.
7.1
Method Comparison with Large Danish Charging Operator
What differentiates the method used to optimize the location of CPs in this project from other methods is
the focus on user preferences. Consequently, it is interesting to compare this method with other approaches
to identify similarities and differences. Further, it is of relevance to investigate if benefits can be drawn from
using the method developed in the project in combination with other methods. With the regard to optimize
value from a user perspective, which is the purpose of this project, it is expected that this method is better
than other methods, as investigating the problem from a user perspective is only being superficially examined
by other methods. A hypothesis could therefore be that if the optimal location of CPs should be investigated
from an overall perspective including as many factors as possible, a combination of the method developed in this
project and other method(s) would be a powerful tool. Such a combined method would require deep insights
and a time consuming development process. Also, the different methods constituting the algorithm should be
weighted differently according to their importance. This is not within the scope of this project, and the primary
purpose of this section is therefore to compare methods and identify similarities and differences.
Through an interview with a project manager within research and analysis focusing on CP infrastructure from a
large Danish charging operator, their approach towards location of CPs is mapped out. The following is thereby
based on his statements. The analysis and research department within the charging operator is grouped in three
teams with different focuses towards location of CPs. The focus within the three teams is on:
1. Location of Super Charger.
2. Location of Normal Charger in municipalities.
3. Location of CPs at public roads and partner agreements.
For team 2 and 3, the charging operator is not highly focused on the location, as it is mostly pre-defined by other
stakeholders. However, they do conduct some rough analyses to indicate if it is a good investment to make an
agreement with the external stakeholder. A substantial and reliable location method is however crucial to team
1. They are using a mathematical tool developed by an external firm, which can estimate the optimal location of
CPs. This mathematical tool is obviously not available for the public, and it is therefore not possible to compare
this method directly to the method developed in this project. However, the charging operator highlights the
main focus of the tool being an analysis of the highway network and exits. This analysis is focused around
how far from an exit it makes sense to locate CPs. The size of the building plot is included in the analysis,
as it may be okay to drive longer if the building plot is sufficiently large making at possible to locate activities
as restaurants, play grounds etc. on the building plot as well. Previously the number of CPs was exclusively
determined from the available space. However, in the summer 2022 they have included optimization in terms
of the number of CPs per CS in their analysis.
The charging type is also considered by the charging operator. This is not included as a part of their math-
ematical tool but is however an important business factor. It is much more expensive to install a SC than a
NC due to the used technology. The strategy of the charging operator is therefore that about 30% of their
CPs must be SCs, while the remaining 70% has to be NCs. This strategy underlines the lack of focus on user

Discussion
The purpose of this section is to discuss the obtained results based on the applied methods.
The process
carried out in this project consists of four steps being a literature review, a data processing process, and the
development of an optimization algorithm consisting of a heuristic and an IP. The literature review maps the
current situation regarding criteria and methods applied when optimizing CP infrastructure to find gaps. The
data processing is carried out to prepare the data for analysis to obtain insights and findings necessary to
develop the algorithm. Thus, the two first parts of the project process can be seen as necessary steps making
it possible to develop the actual algorithm. The applied algorithm, more specifically the heuristic and the IP,
will be discussed in this section, as this is the actual optimization tool in the project.
As explained in Section 4, the findings from the literature review resulted in the ambition to create an algorithm
with the following properties and characteristics:
• The algorithm must be developed from the perspective of the EV user.
• The algorithm must be a combination of at least two methods for disadvantages of one method to be
off-set by advantages of another and vice versa.
• The balance between execution time and the importance of reaching the optimal solution must be consid-
ered carefully.
Investigating the heuristic and the IP in terms of the inclusiveness of an EV user’s perspective, it is in general
sought to develop the two elements for the user benefits to be as high as possible. It is not discussed if the
investigation of the best CP infrastructure seen from a user perspective is exhaustive, as the validity and
the representativeness of the data sources and the handling of data are already discussed through Section 4.
However, it is relevant to discuss the following:
1. Is it actually beneficial for the EVs users to base CP locations exclusively on user needs and preferences?
2. Is it realistic to to base CP locations exclusively on user needs and not consider what is practically feasible?
From the results in Section 6 it appears that most CPs are to be located in the cities and not along the road
network. This is a consequence of the nature of the heuristic and the IP accommodating locations with a high
population density at all times during the day. In many situations it is beneficial for the EV users that CPs are
located in the cities and close to other activities than work places, shopping centres, sports facilities etc., such
that they are able to do other things while charging their EV. However, it can be argued that it is not always
beneficial for the users if most CPs are located in the cities. When going for longer rides or not travelling to
or from cities, it will increase the travel time significantly if the only opportunity to charge an EV is to drive
into a city. Thus, it should investigated if it is advantageous to locate a certain number of CPs along the road
network to also consider the EV users travelling longer distances along the road network. Leading to question
2 above, the consideration of the practical feasibility of locating the recommended number of CPs within the
square cells is discussed. As described in Section 5.2.1, the practical aspect in terms of space is intentionally
only superficially included in the IP as the focus of the project is the EV user perspective. However, it can
be discussed whether the inclusion of practicalities is too sparse. Considering the output of the algorithm as a
practically unrealistic picture of the optimal CP infrastructure serving the purpose to draw the decision making
process regarding CP infrastructure planning in the direction of a more user oriented argumentation, it can
be argued that it is unnecessary to include a practical aspect of CP location. Following that argumentation,
it would be necessary to either use this method in combination with other methods to get a more nuanced
recommendation of the optimal CP infrastructure or it would require extra work to find the exact location of
each CP. On the other hand, if the method shall be seen as something from which the result can be applied, it
can be argued that it is necessary to include practicalities. Thus, it is important to define the application of the
method; more specifically whether it shall be seen as a guideline highlighting the importance of including the
92 of 104
==PAGE:97==

Kathrine Hille & Nanna Damgaard
EV user perspective when considering CP infrastructure, or if the purpose is to provide a location tool. It can
be argued that the first application is most realistic, as district plans and political agendas make it impossible
to determine the exact locations from an algorithm.
The second property of the algorithm is that it must be a combination of at least two methods for disad-
vantages of one method to be offset by advantages of another and vice versa.
In practice this is done by
combining a heuristic with an IP. The rationale behind this is closely related to the third property being the
balance between execution time and the importance of reaching the optimal solution, which is a tradeoff to
be carefully considered. The discussion of whether the usage of exactly a heuristic and an IP is endless, and
as described in the literature review in Section 2, many different methods are used for optimization of CP
infrastructure with great success. It is well-argued in Section 5 why exactly these two methods are chosen in
this project, and it will thereby not be further discussed. The potential of other methods showing as least as
good results as the results obtained in this project is however acknowledged. Thus, it is interesting to discuss
the balance between execution time and how close to the optimal solution the obtained solution is. This balance
is sought by using the heuristic to reduce the execution time of the IP, where the purpose of the latter is to find
the optimal solution based on the results from the heuristic. The combination of the two methods is thereby an
attempt to get as close to the optimal solution as possible within a short period of time. It is chosen to execute
the heuristic for five minutes, while the execution time of the IP is 5613.13 and 186.22 seconds for the 500 and
1000 meter square cells respectively. In terms of balancing execution time and performance, three tings can be
discussed:
1. Is five minutes the optimal time limit for the heuristic?
2. Is it necessary to have a heuristic?
3. Is the chosen square size optimal?
Regarding question 1 above, tests with different time limits are made, see in Section 5.1.2. In this section it is
also argued why a time limit of five minutes is chosen, and this will thereby not be further discussed.
If the heuristic was excluded from the algorithm as suggested in question 2, the obtained solution would be the
optimal solution. However, the execution time would have been much higher. As explained in Section 6, the
execution time of the IP is 30 times as high for the 500 meter square cells compared to 1000 meter square cells,
even though the number of square cells is only four times as high. This illustrates the importance of having a
heuristic as a part of the algorithm to decrease the execution time of the entire algorithm, as the heuristic is
only executed for five minutes. However, it is not known how much better the obtained solution would have
been if the heuristic was left out. Thus, as explained in Section 6 and 7, the method seems valid compared to
other methods, and the obtained results are considered of high quality and as expected with the focus of the
project. It is therefore not expected that it would be possible to obtain a much better solution that it would
weigh up for the much higher execution time.
Looking at question 3 above, it is explained in Section 4.3.2 why square cell sizes of 500 and 1000 meters are
tested in this project. A smaller square cell size would have resulted in a greater level of geographical detail,
while a larger square cell size would have resulted in a shorter execution time of the IP. A smaller square cell
size would not have been beneficial in this project, as the purpose is to create overall guidelines regarding CP
location rather than advising about exact locations. Thus, a greater level of detail would just have increased
the execution time without an expectation of adding additional value. On the other hand, it can be discussed
whether value would be lost by increasing the square cell size and thereby decreasing the execution time. As
explained in Section 6, the results show that the same overall tendencies are obtained independent of the square
cell size. Thus, it is relevant to discuss if it would have been valuable - in terms of a decreased execution time -
to use even larger square cells. On one hand it can be argued that it will be more relevant to apply the results

detail and accuracy will be too low. In conclusion, it is of great relevance to consider the square size to reach a
low execution time, a suitable level of detail, and a solution that is practically possible to implement.

Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a methodology to optimize CP locations in Denmark strictly from a user
perspective making CPs available where and when EV users need them. Research question 1-4 are presented in
Section 1.1 and listed below:
1. Which factors influence EV users’ choice of CPs?
2. Which data can be used to optimize CP localization to address user concerns and make it more convenient
to be an EV user?
3. How can an algorithm be developed to assist in optimal location of CPs?
4. How can the developed algorithm in combination with already existing CP location methods strengthen
the decision-making process regarding CP location?
The research questions are answered throughout this thesis, and the main findings will be presented in the
following.
Considering research question 1, the literature review reveals that there is an actual lack of knowledge and
investigation of the EV users’ preferences, needs, and charging demand when determining the optimal CP lo-
cations. Investigation of the user perspective through analysis of the questionnaire and TU data yields insights
regarding EV user characteristics, needs, preferences, concerns, transport habits, and behavior resulting in iden-
tification of the factors influencing EV users’ choice of CP. These are identified as SOC, activities and services
available at charging sites, the additional travel distance required to reach the CP, and the type of CP. The first
three factors in combination with the characteristics of the typical EV and the typical ICE users as derived from
the questionnaire and TU constitutes the base on which the optimization algorithm is developed. The input for
this algorithm is data chosen based on the factors influencing EV users’ choice of CP and the characteristics
of the typical EV and ICE users. An exhaustive list of the applied data and their data sources are shown in
Figure 9.1. The figure serves as an overview of the entire project process including data in- and outputs.

by advantages of another. Second, it is concluded that when creating an optimization problem the balance
between accuracy and execution time must be considered. Consequently, a heuristic and an IP is developed.
The purpose of the heuristic is to decrease the execution time of the algorithm by halving the data input for

The algorithm is tested using both 500 and 1000 meter square cells showing similar tendencies across square

The results of the IP illustrate that the concentration of recommended CPs increases with the population

the method used by a larger Danish charging operators confirms the importance of including a user perspective
in the optimization of CP infrastructure. For the results to be practically feasible and relevant for most stake-
holders, the method could be used in combination with other methods. To answer research question 4, it is
relevant to include factors descriptive of CP provider profit, installation costs, power grid load, available space
etc. By combining methods considering different factors, the basis for decision-making will be more nuanced.
In conclusion, a CP location optimization method investigated from a user perspective is developed as illustrated
in Figure 9.1. The purpose of the method is to provide decision-makers with insights and considerations to
take into account when considering the CP location problem. SOC, activities and services available at charging
sites, the additional travel distance required to reach the CP, and the type of CP are identified as the most
influential factors regarding EV users’ choice of CP. This is included in the optimization model by processing
and analyzing data from the questionnaire and TU. Combined with MGBHD and data on existing CPs, said
data are used in the optimization of CP infrastructure. In practice, an algorithm consisting of a heuristic and an
IP is developed. The heuristic decreases the execution time of the algorithm by halving the data input for the
IP, while the IP recommends the optimal number of CPs within each square cell showing very promising results.
Finally, it might be advantageous to combine the developed method with other CP optimization methods to
consider the preferences of all relevant stakeholders and practicalities to make the decision-making process as
nuanced as possible. However, with the primary goal being to consider the CP optimization problem from a
user perspective to provide decision makers with a broader basis for decision making and thus to ensure that
a user perspective is included in future CP infrastructure planning processes, the results strongly indicate that
the project goal has been achieved.

Perspectivation
Based on the discussion in Section 8 and the conclusion in Section 9, this section will describe how the optimiza-
tion method developed through this project advantageously can be further developed to obtain better results
and to expand the application area of the method.
As stated in the conclusion in Section 9, it will be advantageous to use the developed method in combina-
tion with other CP optimization methods. As this method only focuses on EV users, it is not realistic that
other stakeholders will accept using this approach as a stand alone tool. Thus, it will be relevant to consider how
this method can be combined with other methods viewing the problem from other stakeholders’ perspectives
and considering additional factors. Such factors could be CP provider costs and profit, power grid load etc.
In that way it would be possible to create a model accommodating as many stakeholders as possible with the
utopian goal of making a model accommodating all stakeholders equally.
Besides considering the perspective of other stakeholders, it is relevant to include more aspects determining
whether the solution is practically feasible. As previously stated, the method developed in this project shall
be seen as a guidance tool for decision makers to be used in combination with other methods.
Thus, the
method cannot provide the decision makers with exact coordinates on where to locate CPs. A combination of
methods would have to include the current location of buildings, current parking lots, if there is room parking
lot expansion, if it is feasible to establish the required power infrastructure etc. In combination this would
create a more informed basis on which the decision makers can make decisions. Further, they would have to
consider less practicalities compared to the current method. However, this would accommodate the user needs
and preferences less than the method developed in this project.
When considering if a CP can be located at a certain location, it must be considered if it is possible to either
establish the required power infrastructure, or if the infrastructure is already there. Currently, most charging
operators in Denmark establish their own power infrastructure when installing a new CP as explained in the
interview by the large Danish charging operator in Section 7. However, it would be beneficial if current power
infrastructure could be used. This is e.g., seen in the UK, where they are currently using existing street light
infrastructure in terms of lamp posts for public CPs (ubitricity 2022). Besides the fact that it is elegant and
effective to use already existing power infrastructure, the cost of installing a CP is also reduced. Thus, it might
be relevant to consider how existing power infrastructure can be utilized for new CPs and include this in the
method as well.
Another important factor to consider when looking at CP localization is the type of the chargers. In that
regard, it is indicated from the questionnaire as explained in Section 4.1.3 that most respondents find the type,
meaning whether it is a NC, FC or SC, of the CP the most important factor when choosing which CP to use.
This thesis only investigates where to locate CPs and is not considering the type. However, this is both very
important to the EV users but also relevant to include in the analysis when determining the number of CPs
required, as the charging time is highly dependent on the CP type. If the determination of charger type is
included in the model, this must be related to local differences. For instance, it might be optimal to locate more
NCs in residential areas and at workplaces where people mostly stay for a longer time, and more FCs and SCs
in areas with activities such as shopping centres, grocery stores, sport facilities etc. In terms of local differences
it is further relevant to consider how user needs and preferences might differ across the country, and how these
impact the interpretation of the optimal location of CPs. The method would become more directly applicable
by including this.
Lastly, in this project it is assumed that the current ICE users will be EV users in the future if the growth in
EVs is as expected. However, this assumption could have been more nuanced by investigating exactly which of

the ICE users it would be most likely who will drive an EV in some years. One way to nuance this analysis could
have been to include the sustainability factor available in the geodemographic data provided by LIFA describing
how environmentally conscious a household is. This would be useful under the assumption that people who
are more conscious regarding the environment and sustainability are more likely to buy an EV. Incorporating
this in the method would nuanced prediction about who future EV users are, where they live, their driving
patterns etc. Summing up, different suggestions to make the output of the method developed in this project
more realistic and directly applicable have been suggested. These can in general be summarized in two points:
1. The purpose of this project is to consider optimal CP infrastructure purely from the perspective of an EV
user. However, for the results to be feasible, the method must be used in combination with other methods
to consider the perspective of all relevant stakeholders.
2. The method developed in this project is a guideline highlighting the importance of including the EV
user perspective when considering CP infrastructure and not a location tool. Thus, many of the points
mentioned for further development is pulling the method towards being more a tool than a guideline. This
might not be a bad thing, but it is however important to consider and decide what the usage situation of
the method is.
Currently, the perspective of EV users is not present in the majority of CP location optimization methods.
However, given the fact that this market is highly immature and still in an emerging phase where demand
generally speaking is bigger than supply and with significant investments put on all operators, the (short-term)
profit-maximization approach is quite understandable. As the market matures and the competition becomes
more intense, it would nonetheless be expected that the user-centric approach developed in this thesis will
become more and more relevant.
