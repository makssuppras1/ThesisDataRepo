Abstract 
IX 
 
Abstract 
The aim of the present thesis is the design of a novel trigeneration system (TG) based on Municipal Solid 
Wastes (MSW) gasification, Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) and an ammonia-water absorption chiller. The 
system is suitable for medium scale applications such as hospitals and hotels, where there is continuous 
and significant need for electricity, heating and cooling supply. The base case model resulted in a total 
system efficiency of 64% taking into consideration the initial fuel of 190 Kg/hour MSW supplied to the 
system. The efficiency of the SOFC was 30%, the respective one of the absorption cycle 23%, whereas 
the heat duty of the heat exchanger responsible for the hot water production added the remaining 11%. 
After performing a sensitivity analysis the total system efficiency increased to 82%. Parameters such as 
AER, gasifier and SOFC operating temperatures as well as the amount of waste heat available for the 
absorption cycle are considered crucial parameters for the system´s performance. A techno-economic 
analysis revealed as the most expensive components regarding the purchase cost the burner with a 
purchase cost of 126.579 € followed by the SOFC with a cost of 76.914 €. Metal sorbents ZNO and 
NAHCO3 were utilized for the removal of H2S and HCL concentrations in the syngas composition. The 
results showed that 99% removal can be achieved resulting in the reduction of the contaminants to <1 
ppm levels, when using these sorbents. The possibility of whether this system can cover the demand 
profiles of a selected building was also studied, revealing that the system could fully cover the electricity 
and cooling demand, whereas the heat demand could be satisfied in a percentage of 57%. Furthermore 
in a time framework of 20 years system´s life expectancy, the payback period (PBP) was 3,2 years and 
the net present value (NPV) amounted to 5015.505 €. Finally an analysis was conducted in order to 
locate which component is characterized by the highest exergy destruction within the system. SOFC 
displayed the largest value of 271 kW due to the irreversibilities caused during the chemical reactions. 
 
Key words: Gasification, SOFC, Trigeneration, absorption chiller

Chapter 1. Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
Nowadays, waste management represents a matter of crucial importance for all humanity. The vast 
increase in the production of waste throughout the last century is followed by negative consequences 
in the society and in the environment. Particularly greenhouse gas emissions constitute a serious 
threat for public health,  whereas there is an important amount of raw materials that are lost due to 
the lack of efficient treatment strategies. Thus, there is an urgent need for establishing efficient and 
innovative public policies concerning the handling and exploitation of waste (Being Wise with Waste: 
the EU´S approach to Waste Management, 2010). 
Traditionally waste disposal in sanitary landfills was the prevalent method related to waste 
management. Today this method is considered antiquated due to the negative environmental issues 
that arise, as well as because of the high demand of large fields available for the waste disposal. 
Furthermore, EU-legislation has significantly contributed to this direction since many landfill areas are 
set out of operation across Europe, resulting in 25% decrease of Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) ending 
up to landfill facilities (Commision, 2010).  
A very good alternative to waste landfilling comprise the waste-to energy plants (Klein, 2002). Currently 
the most mature technology is incineration, while gasification is still in the early stages of development. 
Focusing on gasification, this technology constitutes an advanced thermal treatment method, which 
rose significant attention lately due the potential benefits that may provide compared to incineration. 
The result of the process is the production of gas named “syngas” containing large amounts of hydrogen 
(H2) carbon monoxide (CO) and methane (CH4)  (Arena, 2011). This gas can then be utilized as a fuel into 
energy conversion technologies such as micro turbines and fuel cells for the production of electricity 
and heat. 
Fuel cells are electrochemical devices able to provide higher efficiencies compared to conventional 
power production technologies. Especially solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC), apart from their high electrical 
efficiency, are also capable of producing heat as byproduct, due to their high operating temperature, 
favoring in this way combined heat and power (CHP) applications (A. Boudghene Stambouli, 2002). 
Furthermore, SOFCs show high fuel flexibility, since they allow H2, CO and CH4 to take part in the 
electrochemical reactions, resulting in the production of electricity and heat. The latter feature together 
with the fact that the operating temperature range of gasification and SOFCs is similar makes the 
coupling of the two technologies ideal (Møller, 2010). 
==PAGE:24==
 
2 
 
Whereas CHP applications are well established and it is expected that they will continue growing in the 
upcoming years, there is not so much research still related to trigeneration systems (TG). The term 
trigeneration implies the simultaneous production of electricity, heating and cooling by the same fuel 
input (Al-Sulaiman, 2011). In a TG system the waste heat resulting from the exhaust gases exiting a 
power generating unit such as a gas turbine or a fuel cell, is supplied in both the heating and cooling 
systems. While the heat recovery is accomplished through a heat exchanger network, cooling is usually 
produced in thermal driven refrigeration systems known as absorption chillers.  
The operating mode of TG systems depends on their application. For example when they are installed 
in residential areas, they usually operate in CHP mode during the winter, while the absorption chillers 
are activated in the summer time. On the other hand, when they are applied in buildings such as 
hospitals where there is a continuous need for electricity heating and cooling they need to operate in 
TG mode.  
In general terms the efficiency of TG plants is characterized by much higher values, compared to the 
case in which electricity, heating and cooling are produced separately in conventional plants. The 
explanation stems from the fact that in TG systems the same amount of fuel is utilized in order to cover 
the electricity, heating and cooling demands. On the other hand, conventional plants would require 
more fuel resources for the same output (Al-Sulaiman, 2011). 
A research study related to the modelling of a TG system based on waste gasification coupled with 
SOFCs and an absorption chiller can investigate the potential benefits of such a configuration. 
1.2 Objectives 
The research study aims to investigate: 
 The performance of a TG system based on MSW gasification, SOFCs and a single stage ammonia-
water absorption chiller 
 Hot gas cleaning method  in order to meet SOFCs strict requirements 
 The influence of the most important parameters related to each subsystem and their impact on 
the whole system´s performance 
 The economic feasibility of the project when applied in a real case situation 
 Entropy generation and the components with the highest exergy destruction within the system

1.3 Aspen Plus Simulator 
The building of the complete TG model was developed in Aspen Plus simulator. Aspen Plus has been 
widely used in the modelling of complex steady state processes in the fields of chemical engineering 
and in the oil industry respectively. It consists of several building models such as compressors, heat 
exchangers and reactors where most industry processes can be simulated. Furthermore, it constitutes 
of a large property databank containing information about thermophysical properties of different 
chemical substances and mixtures. The calculations related to the chemical reactors are performed 
based on the assumption of minimization of Gibbs Free Energy (Sharmina Begum, 2013), (Jian-Hua Yan, 
2011). 
1.4 Thesis Outline 
The thesis is divided into 10 chapters. Following introduction, chapter 2 gives an overview of the current 
status of waste management in European Union, together with numerical information of the waste 
production per country as well as per capita. Chapter three deals with the gasification technology. 
Gasification process, technologies of gasifiers as well as gas cleaning methods are thoroughly discussed. 
In the end the description of the base case scenario together with the results related to gasification part 
are presented. Chapter four is about fuel cell technology with a special focus on the SOFCs. Again the 
description of the SOFC model is presented with the different input parameters and the occurring 
results. In chapter 5 the absorption technology is introduced with a detailed description of the 
ammonia-water cycles along with an explanation related to the system modeling. Chapter 6 encloses a 
conducted sensitivity analysis concerning various parameters and a comparison with the base case 
model. Chapter seven refers to the analysis regarding the entropy generation in the whole system and 
the identification of the component with the highest exergy destruction. Chapter 8 constitutes from the 
techno-economic analysis of the optimized system, together with a performed feasibility study which 
investigates any potential benefits if the system is to be implemented. Finally chapter 9 comprises the 
conclusion part about the project findings, limitations that occurred and suggestions for future work.

These methods differ by the air equivalent ratio (AER) which is defined as the amount of oxygen 
supplied to the process divided by the oxygen required for a stoichiometric combustion. Pyrolysis is 
characterized by zero ratio with 100% utilization of external heat. In gasification process this ratio

AER=1.5+ without using at all an external heat source (Ellyin, 2012). 
Production and characteristics of MSW are highly dependent on various factors such as climate 
conditions, population, socio economic status and level of industrialization. Thus in order to determine 
the energy value of the waste delivered to a waste to energy plant the  energy content has to be 
calculated. There are two methods for determining the energy content: a) The higher Calorific Value

an initial state of 25 °C and the return of the combustion products in the initial state. In this way the

heating value. On the contrary LCV refers to the complete combustion of a fuel from an initial state of 
25 °C and the return of the combustion products in the temperature of 150 °C by cooling (U.S 
Department of Energy, 2015). The heat of water vaporization in this case is not taken into account.

3.1 Theoretical Background of Gasification 
Gasification is a thermo-chemical process during which a carbon based feedstock is dissociated in a high 
temperature environment and is converted into a combustible gas by the supply of a gasification agent 
(V belgiorno, 2002). Another interesting definition describes gasification as the partial oxidation of 
waste by an oxidant whose amount is lower than the one needed for stoichiometric combustion (Arena, 
2011).  
Compared to the traditional method of incineration related to thermal waste treatment, gasification 
shows a number of significant advantages. Firstly, gasification is considered to be much cleaner 
technology regarding the level of emissions produced. More specifically in the incineration process, the 
combustion environment favors the formation of oxides of sulfur (SOx), oxides of nitrogen (NOx) and 
heavy metals  which are considered to be major air pollutants (Wilson). Furthermore, the ability of 
implementing gasification systems close to the waste source and to the end users saves significant costs 
regarding transportation and hauling of wastes as well as it contributes to the promotion of 
decentralized heat and power production (Wilson). In Figure 3.1 another major advantage of 
gasification is illustrated. Compared to the gas product of incineration technology which can only be 
utilized in a boiler in order to produce steam destined to fuel a steam turbine for the production of 
electricity, syngas can be utilized in various applications. Particularly, syngas can supply steam turbines, 
internal combustion engines, as well as fuel cells if it is totally pure, for the generation of heat and 
electricity. Furthermore it can be the base for the production of valuable chemicals, fuels and fertilizer 
products.

3.2 Processes Inside a Gasifier 
Gasification process consists of three different stages: “Drying”, “Devolatilization” and “Reduction”. 
During the drying process a portion of the moisture contained in the fuel entering the reactor is removed 
in order to improve the gasification efficiency. The heat needed in order the drying to be accomplished 
is provided by the exothermic reactions occurring within the gasifier reactor. Based on the gasifier 
design pre-drying of the fuel before it gets into the gasifier may also be required. This usually happens 
by an external heat source such as steam or air. The process of drying takes place in temperature range 
between 150-200 °C. 
What follows the drying process is the de-volatilization (pyrolysis) of the fuel in temperatures ranging 
between 200- 600 °C in a complete oxygen free environment. De-volatilization implies the thermal 
decomposition of the carbonaceous feedstock into gaseous, liquid and solid products through 
endothermic reactions (Nagel, 2008). Among the gaseous products are H2, CO, CO2, CH4 and H20, 
whereas the liquid products include vaporized liquid hydrocarbon elements known under the general 
term of tars (Nagel, 2008). Presence of tars in the syngas composition can cause the malfunction of 
reforming catalysts and sulfur removal systems, thus an effective cleaning system is essential for 
maintaining the system´s performance. Solid products refer to volatile matter of carbon, hydrogen and 
oxygen together with unreacted carbon and traces of ash which are known as char (Klein, 2002).  
After the decomposition of the solid feedstock the reduction stage takes place where the gasification of 
the fixed carbon occurs through a gasification agent which can be air, steam, mixed air and steam or 
pure oxygen. The main reactions happening during the gasification stage are described below:

3.3 Design of Gasifiers 
3.3.1 Fixed Bed Gasifiers  
This type of gasifiers is divided into two subcategories: updraft and downdraft gasifiers. In the former 
type fuel is inserted in the top of the gasifier, whereas the gasifying medium enters the reactor from 
the bottom. As shown in Figure 3.2, the oxidation reactions take place in the bottom of the gasifier, 
providing in this way heat for the decomposition of hydrocarbons, the gasification of fixed carbon an 
finally the drying process. The produced gas is cooled in the drying zone and as a result it´s temperature 
exiting the gasifier is between 200-300 °C (Nagel, 2008). An important aspect to be mentioned is the 
high content of tars in the produced gas composition. Tars are formed in the pyrolysis zone and thus 
they pass only through the drying zone where the temperature is low and thus thermal cracking is 
unlikely to occur. 
In the downdraft design the fuel is also fed from the top, while air and is inserted either to the top or 
from the sides. The sequence of the processes based on the specific gasifier design is drying, pyrolysis, 
followed by the oxidation reactions and finally the gasification of the fixed carbon. As a result, the exit 
temperature of the produced gas is high between 500-900 °C and also it is characterized by small 
amounts of tar due to the effective thermal cracking in the pyrolysis zone. The heat needed for the

3.3.2 Fluidized Bed Gasifiers 
The main reason for the development of this kind of reactors rely on the fact that fluidized bed gasifiers 
are able to handle a vast variety of feedstock even containing large amounts of ash. In general the fuel 
to be gasified is introduced from the bottom where it mixes with the bed material which can be coarse 
sand or limestone. The bed material is firstly preheated from an external source and when the fuel is 
inserted in the gasifier it reaches very fast the temperature of the bed material.  The temperature 
conditions are isothermal in the range of 700-900 °C. Afterwards the oxidant medium moves upwards 
penetrating the bed material and makes it fluidize. In this kind of reactor the different gasification 
processes are not distinguished in different zones but instead they take place in the whole reactor 
volume (Nagel, 2008). In Figure 3.3 the configurations of a bubbling and a circulating fluidized bed 
gasifier are presented. In the former kind of gasifier the velocity of the oxidant medium is such that the 
formation of bubbles takes place in the bed material. Usually the only particles that can be drown out 
from the reactor together with the gas flow are ash particles and not the rest of the bed material. On 
the contrary the high velocities developed in the circulating fluidized bed cause the drifting of many 
solid particles within the gas flow. Consequently a cyclone is utilized for the removal of particles which 
are reintroduced again in the gasifier providing heat via partial combustion (Nagel, 2008).

3.3.3 Indirect Gasifiers 
This type of gasifiers is used for the indirect steam gasification of the feedstock and is divided in two 
categories according to the external heat that is utilized. Gas indirect gasifiers are steam fluidized 
gasifiers with bed heat exchange tubes. Part of the combustible gas is burnt in a combustor producing 
the necessary heat for the procedure. Char indirect gasifiers are composed with two reactors: a) a 
circulating fluidized bed gasifier where the syngas is produced and b) a circulating fluidized bed 
combustor where residual char is burnt in order to provide the necessary heat in the process. The main 
advantage of indirect gasification is the high quality of the produced gas. Typical values for LCV of the 
syngas produced from indirect gasification range between 15-20 MJ/m3 while the respective one from 
direct gasification with air as a medium is much lower approximately 4-7 MJ/m3 (V belgiorno, 2002). On 
the other hand it requires high investment and maintenance costs concerning the reactors.

3.4 Syngas Cleaning Methods 
3.4.1: Cold and Hot gas Cleaning 
Syngas produced from gasification process apart from its high energetic value, contains also different 
kind of impurities such as particles, tars, Sulphur compounds, alkali metals and hydrogen chloride. These 
impurities can cause a detrimental effect on systems such as the fuel cells if syngas is utilized as fuel. 
Thus pretreatment of syngas with effective cleaning methods is a matter of vital importance related to 
the system´s performance and life expectancy (Ayala RE, 1998). 
A convenient way for defining the cleaning methods is to specify them according to their operating 
temperature range. Thus the pretreatment of syngas can be divided into cold gas cleaning and hot gas 
cleaning. The temperatures related to the former method are below 200 °C while regarding the hot gas 
==PAGE:42==
 
20 
 
cleaning method the temperatures can exceed 500 °C (L.C. LAURENCE, 2012). Cold gas cleaning is a 
mature technology although it may present energy inefficiencies, as well as it can create waste streams 
that need further treatment. On the other hand most of the hot gas cleaning methods are in the early 
stages for development. Since the scope of the present thesis comprises the coupling of solid waste 
gasification together with a solid oxide fuel cell the hot was cleaning method is considered more 
advantageous for the syngas pretreatment. More specifically the temperature of syngas exiting the 
downdraft gasifier aligns with the operating temperature of the SOFC and thus there is no need for 
cooling. On the contrary if the cold gas cleaning method is to be applied the syngas exiting the gasifier 
must be cooled down first in order to reach the temperature that favors the cleaning and then heat up 
again to the fuel cell operating temperature. Naturally the rejected heat resulting from syngas cooling 
may be valorized in other parts of the process. 
Based on the assumptions that will be defined in section 3. 6 compounds of hydrogen sulfide and 
hydrogen chloride are the only contaminants that will be taken into consideration related to the 
cleaning of the syngas before it is utilized in the fuel cell. 
3.4.2 Sulfur Removal 
Sulfur constitutes a small percentage in the composition of syngas usually in the form of hydrogen 
sulfide (H2S) (P.V. Aravind, 2012). This small percentage is nevertheless capable of poisoning the nickel 
based catalysts which are often used in the high temperature fuel cells (Nagel, 2008). H2S is easily 
adsorbed from the anode surface, hindering in this way syngas molecules to take part in the oxidation 
reactions. According to extensive research of H2S effects on the fuel cell surfaces it is concluded that the 
concentration of H2 should be lowered in levels of few ppm in order a stable performance of the fuel 
cell to be achieved (Joseph Lee). Hot was cleaning method for the removal of H2S comprises the 
adsorption of H2S into a metal oxide such as Ba, Mo, V, Zn and Co at elevated temperatures. Currently

an alternative option can be the combination of ZNO with other metal oxides such as zinc ferrite or zinc 
titanate. It has been reported that zinc titanate which is formed through the reaction of ZNO and TIO2

3.4.3 Hydrogen Chloride Removal 
Chlorine mixtures are usually present in the form of hydrogen chloride (HCL) within the composition of 
syngas. Similarly to hydrogen sulfide they can poison the active surface of the anode catalyst but the 
intensity of deterioration is less compared to the effects Of H2S (Sharmina Begum, 2013). There are two 
ways of removing HCL from the syngas composition in the framework of hot gas cleaning: 
 Formation of salts by introducing a sorbent in the gasifier reactor. The salts then can be 
subtracted by a device suitable for particles control such as cyclones, filters, etc. Some examples 
of sorbents are elements NA2CO3 and CACO3  
 Adsorption of HCL onto a chemical compound such as NAHCO3, NAOH or K2CO3 (Toonssen, 2010) 
It has been reported (Joseph Lee), (P.V. Aravind, 2012) that the performance of cleaning in a separate 
reactor is more desirable since the addition of salts can create an increase in alkali concentration of the 
syngas. Thus hot gas cleaning comprising the adsorption process will be applied in this study. The 
sorbent will be NAHCO3 since it has proved to be very effective in HCL removal within a temperature 
range of 350- 500 (°C) (Toonssen, 2010); (P.V. Aravind, 2012). The reaction describing the adsorption

Chapter 4. Fuel Cells Technology 
4.1 General Features of Fuel Cells 
Fuel cells are energy conversion devices where electricity and heating are produced through 
electrochemical reactions between the oxygen and the supplied fuel. Most of the energy 
conversion technologies today such as internal combustion engines and gas turbines 
transform the energy which is contained in the fuel first to mechanical work which then moves 
a generator in order to produce electricity. Unlike these conventional technologies in the fuel 
cell electricity is directly produced from the fuel oxidation, something that enhances the 
overall electrical efficiency (Toonssen, 2010). More Particularly if the fuel cells work only for 
the production of electricity efficiencies of 40-55% can be achieved, whereas if they operate 
in  combined heat and power mode (CHP)  efficiencies up to 80% can be obtained (Chris 
Rayment, 2003). In the same moment the efficiency of internal combustion engines is 
approximately 30%, thus there is huge space for improvement if fuel cell technology is to be 
introduced in the power generation. Apart from higher electrical efficiencies fuel cells are 
characterized by total absence of noise since they do not require any moving parts (Møller, 
2010) as well very low emissions compared to the other conventional technologies (Chris 
Rayment, 2003). 
There are 5 main types of fuel cells: 
 Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC) 
 Phosphoric Acid Fuel Cell (PAFC) 
 Alkaline Fuel Cell (AFC) 
 Molten Carbonate Fuel Cell (MCFC) 
 Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) 
In table 4.1 the different kinds of fuel cells and their characteristics are illustrated. As it can be 
observed the first three types of fuel cells operate in a low-middle operation temperature 
range and the only fuel that can be utilized is pure hydrogen. On the contrary MCFC and SOFC 
can operate in high temperatures and show greater flexibility regarding the supplied fuel. 
These diverse operating characteristics make the different fuel cell types suitable in a wide 
range of applications. More specifically PEMFC and AFC are suitable for mobile and portable 
applications such as mobile phones and personal computers but their utilization can be 
extended as well in the automotive industry. On the other hand MCFC and SOFC due to their 
==PAGE:50==
 
28 
 
high operating temperature are favorable for centralized and distributed power generation or 
CHP applications (Bellomare, 2011); (Chris Rayment, 2003).

4.2 Solid Oxide Fuel Cells 
Nowadays, SOFCs have become a very promising option regarding power generation due to 
their high efficiencies. Furthermore the high operating temperature favors the production of 
heat which can supply bottoming cycles such as gas and steam turbines, In the framework of 
simultaneous generation of power and heat (CHP) as mentioned above. (Nagel, 2008). 
Another major advantage of this specific kind of fuel cell is the ability to utilize different fuels 
other than hydrogen. Particularly carbon monoxide or methane can be directly oxidized or 
internally shifted and reformed through the reactions described in equations (3.6) and (3.8) 
due to the high temperature of the fuel cell and the nickel material of the electrolyte 
(Toonssen, 2010), (Nagel, 2008). Consequently syngas generated from gasification process or 
natural gas which contains large amounts of methane can be supplied to a SOFC without any 
prior reforming needed. On the other hand one major drawback which applies as well in the 
other fuel cell types are the high investment costs related to the manufacturing process. 
Particularly for the SOFC, the materials related to the sealing and to the interconnection of 
the stacks require still high costs in order to avoid the thermal stresses resulting from the high 
operating temperature (Toonssen, 2010). Other disadvantages comprise the long start up 
times as well as a very low tolerance in impurities such as HCL and H2S (Toonssen, 2010).

Chapter 5. Absorption chillers 
5.1 General Characteristics of Absorption Systems 
Refrigeration systems based on vapor absorption cycles are a well-known technology which 
has been extensively studied for years. Nevertheless, their market share is still limited 
compared to the vapor compression systems. The fundamental reasons for this aspect are the 
relatively low efficiency in delivering cooling needs as well as the high initial capital costs. 
Regarding the coefficient of performance (COP), which is defined as the ratio between the 
achieved cooling capacity and the heat input to the cycle, typical values for absorption cycles 
range between 0, 5 and 1, 5. On the contrary vapor compression cycles display value higher 
than 3 based on the electrical input (Somers, 2009). 
Albeit their disadvantages, the utilization of absorption cycles is significantly favored when 
waste heat is available. More specifically it is very often the case when hot exhaust gases 
resulting from industrial processes are released in the surroundings. Thus the integration of 
absorption chillers which will utilize this heat that otherwise would be wasted, can lead to an 
increase in the overall efficiency of the plant. 
The driving force of an absorption cycle is a solution consisting of a refrigerant and an 
absorbent. In most cases the combination of water-lithium bromide either the one of 
ammonia-water is utilized. Furthermore the cycles can be single, double or triple effect, 
depending on the available waste heat temperature and the potential investment. In general 
multistage cycles need higher temperature heat sources and are characterized by higher 
values of COP compared to the single stage ones. On the other hand the installation is more 
complex since larger number of components is required, something that results also in higher 
capital costs. 
The basic design of a single stage absorption chiller is illustrated in Figure 5.1. The liquid 
solution of the refrigerant and the absorbent is pumped to the generator which is also known 
as desorber where the waste heat is added. 
==PAGE:56==
 
34 
 
 
         Figure 5.1: Basic operating principle of absorption cycle (Somers, 2009) 
As a result the refrigerant converts into a gaseous phase, separates from the liquid solution 
and is heading towards the condenser. In the condenser the refrigerant transforms into liquid 
by rejecting heat to a heat sink which is either air or water in a lower inlet temperature. The 
liquid refrigerant now pass through an expansion valve where it´s pressure decreases and 
continues to the evaporator where it is evaporated by subtracting heat from a higher 
temperature source and thus creating the cooling effect. As the cycle continues refrigerant 
vapor meets in the absorber, the liquid solution coming from the outlet of the desorber and 
they form again the liquid solution which is further pumped in order the cycle to be continued. 
The implementation of a solution heat exchanger between the solution pump and the 
desorber can enhance the cycle efficiency and will thoroughly analyzed in the coming 
chapters. 
In the framework of the thesis the combination of ammonia-water working fluids will be 
implemented in the modelling of a single stage absorption process coupled with the 
gasification and solid oxide fuel cell systems. 
5.2 Ammonia-Water Refrigeration Cycles 
In ammonia-water absorption systems ammonia is the refrigerant and water the absorbent. 
Due to the low boiling temperature of ammonia in atmospheric pressure (T=-33 °C) the system 
is both applicable in air conditioning as well as refrigeration applications. The manufacturing 
of the system is based on steel since ammonia shows incompatibility to copper and brass. 
==PAGE:57==
 
Chapter 5. Absorption chillers 
35 
 
The working principles of ammonia-water and water-lithium bromide absorption systems are 
similar except for two major differences. Firstly, the operating pressure levels for ammonia-
water cycles are much higher than the atmospheric pressure while on the other hand water- 
lithium-bromide systems work at very low pressure levels. As a result, the problem of air 
leakage into the system disappears as well the crystallization phenomenon which occurs in 
high pressures within the absorber and affects negatively the water-lithium bromide cycles. 
Nevertheless, ammonia has the drawbacks of being toxic and flammable, thus safety 
measures should always been taken into account. The second important difference is related 
to the boiling temperatures in ammonia-water systems. More specifically, since the boiling 
temperatures of the operating fluids do not differ much, ammonia and water evaporate both 
when heat is added in the desorber. Consequently the amount of pure refrigerant going into 
the condenser is between 90-95% compared to the water-lithium bromide cycles where the 
mass flow after the desorber is almost pure refrigerant (Somers, 2009).This fact has a 
deteriorating effect since only a small temperature glide is acceptable within the evaporator. 
For this reason a rectifier is implemented in the system aiming to eliminate the quantity of 
water vapor inside the condenser to less than 1% (Vapor absorption refrigeration systems 
based on ammonia-water pair). 
5.3 Description of the Single Stage Ammonia-Water Cycle 
In Figure 5.2 a flowsheet diagram of the absorption process modelled in Aspen Plus is 
illustrated. A general overview of the process will be discussed, whereas a more detailed 
description of all the components is given in (Appendix E). A strong solution of ammonia- 
water (high concentration of ammonia) is pumped to the DESORBER, having exchanged heat 
before with the hot weak solution (poor concentration of ammonia) coming from the outlet 
of the DESORBER through a solution heat exchanger (SHEX). In the DESORBER heat is added 
coming from the burner´s exhaust gases resulting in the vaporization of ammonia and part of 
the water. The outcome of the DESORBER which is hot vapor containing 5-10% water is driven 
to the RECTIFIER where water is removed through cooling and condensation. Almost pure 
refrigerant after the rectification is led to the condenser where it is transformed to liquid form. 
A vapor liquid heat exchanger (VLHEX) afterwards is used in order to sub cool the condensed 
liquid by transferring heat to the low pressure and temperature vapor coming from the outlet 
of the EVAPORATOR. The high pressure liquid refrigerant is then driven to an expansion valve, 
(VALVE1) where it´s pressure is decreased to the low pressure level of the cycle and enters 
the evaporator. There it vaporizes by exchanging heat with the refrigerated space creating in 
==PAGE:58==
 
36 
 
this way the cooling effect. The weak ammonia-water solution coming from the outlet of the 
DESORBER mixes with the condensed water coming from the RECTIFIER. Afterwards this 
mixture exchanges heat as mentioned above in the SHEX and enters the expansion valve 
(VALVE2) where the pressure is decreased in the low pressure level of the cycle. Finally it is 
led to the ABSORBER where it meets the refrigerant vapor coming from the evaporator and 
the cycle is completed.

6.2 Air Equivalence Ratio (AER) 
Air Equivalence Ratio implies the ratio between the air which is introduced in the gasifier and 
the necessary amount of air in order stoichiometric combustion to be achieved. In Figure 6.6 
the effect of AER on the chemical composition of syngas is illustrated. CO shows a large 
decrease of 80% (from 27,4 to 5, 3 %) while CO2 rises from 16% to 20% (26% increase) as the 
AER varies from 0, 2 to 0, 6. The trends of CO and CO2 can be attributed to equations (3.1) and 
(3.2). Practically increasing AER means higher amounts of oxygen are present inside the 
gasifier, a fact that favors the oxidation reactions. Nevertheless oxidation reaction (3.2) is 
favored more than the respective one in equation (3.1), something that explains the increase 
of CO2 and the decrease of CO respectively. H2 on the other hand descends slightly from 3% 
to 0,7 % (79% decrease) whereas H20 increases 30% (from 10,3% to 13,4 %.). The downward 
trend of H2 can be ascribed again to the dominance of oxidation reaction (3.3) which causes 
also the rise of H2O fraction as the AER increases. The percentage of methane is also lowered 
but it´s percentage is very low and thus it is not possible to show it in Figure 6.6.

7.1 Theory of Entropy Generation 
The term entropy refers to an extensive property used to identify and also quantify possible 
irreversibililies happening in the system as well as heat transfer (Adrian Bejan, 1995). The 
determination of entropy generation in all the components of a system can be a very useful 
tool, since it helps identifying in which component the most irreversibilities take place and 
consequent to investigate any potential measures for improvement. Equation (7.1) describes 
the entropy balance in a steady state open system.

𝑔𝑒𝑛=∑𝑚̇ 𝑜𝑢𝑡𝑆𝑜𝑢𝑡−∑𝑚̇ 𝑖𝑛𝑆𝑖𝑛−∑

𝑇𝑆𝑦𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑚
𝑆𝑦𝑠𝑡𝑒𝑚
                        (7.1)                                                                 
In order to get a better understanding of how entropy generation is calculated in Aspen Plus, 
an example of determining the entropy generation of the SOFC is given. The inlet streams in 
the SOFC are syngas and air and the outlet streams, depleted air and used fuel respectively.

In Figure 7.21 the exergy destruction of all the components is illustrated. The values related 
to the exergy destruction are stemming from the entropy generation results of each 
component multiplied by the ambient temperature, thus the units are in Watts.

Entropy generation is totally related to the irreversibilities caused by the chemical reactions, 
heat transfer and friction which have different significance depending on the specific 
component (Adrian Bejan, 1995). Particularly the highest entropy generation observed in the 
system is the respective one of the SOFC and stems mainly from the irreversibility caused by 
the nature of chemical reactions. Preheating the air and the reactants respectively can 
contribute to a small decrease in the entropy generation, but in general terms this kind of 
irreversibilities are unavoidable. The same remarks apply as well to the gasifier and burner 
reactors which also display an important share of system´s entropy generation. Regarding 
HEATEX5 which comes second after SOFC, the entropy generation is highly associated with 
irreversibilities related to heat transfer. Changing the specification of the heat exchanger by 
applying a small temperature difference between the hot and the cold streams can enhance 
the thermodynamic performance but on the other hand will cause an increase in the entropy 
resulting from friction factor. Desorber´s entropy generation stems not only from issues 
related to heat transfer but also from the entropy of mixing which is produced when 
substances with different compositions are mixed together as in the case of ammonia and 
water mixture. Finally the entropy generation in compressors and pumps are basically due to 
friction.

8.2 Techno-Economic Analysis 
A comprehensive analysis of an energy system apart from the thermal design shall include as 
well as the total capital investment (TCI) of the plant in order to have a realistic impression 
regarding the validity of the project. In Figure 8.1 the flowsheet regarding a methodology 
described in (Adrian Bejan, 1995) aiming to calculate the TCI of a plant is illustrated. As it is 
observed TCI is the sum of Fixed Capital Investment (FCI) and other outlays which are referring 
to the start- up costs and the licensing of a project as well as to the working capital. Working 
capital relates not only to the payment of salaries but also to the costs needed for materials, 
fuels and different kind of prerequisites regarding the operation. FCI is the sum of Total Direct 
Costs (TDC) and Total Indirect Costs (TIC) respectively. TDC are further divided in Onsite and 
Offsite Costs. The former costs are associated with the purchased cost of each component 
(PEC), the installation costs and the equipment needed for system controlling. The latter costs 
refer to the land which is required for the project installation, together with service facilities 
such as water and electricity utilities and the civil and architectural work. Finally TIC relate to 
the oversight of the system, the arrangement of any uncertainties or risks that will arise during 
the project and the costs that are destined for the contractor´s payment.

Chapter 9. Conclusion 
9.1 Summary of Findings  
The objective of the present thesis was to investigate the performance of a novel TG system 
based on MSW gasification coupled with a SOFC and an ammonia-water absorption chiller. TG 
systems are a considerable option when implemented in buildings such as hospitals, hotels 
and airports, where there is a continuous and large demand for electricity, heating and 
cooling. Based on this argument the composition of MSW stems from the wastes produced in 
the National University Hospital of Taiwan.  
The variations of different parameters related to each subsystem were varied in order to 
investigate their impact on the total efficiency of the system. AER proved to be a very 
important parameter regarding the gasification process since it greatly influenced the LCV of 
the fuel, which decreased sharply in higher AERs, resulting in deteriorating system efficiency. 
Moreover, higher operating temperatures in the gasifier increase the overall efficiency since 
they promote the endothermic reactions needed for the gasification process. Operating 
temperature of the SOFC is considered also a vital parameter. Specifically lower temperature 
values benefit the overall performance since less air is needed for cooling the cell, a fact that 
reduces the auxiliary power needed from the compressors. Furthermore, fuel Utilization 
Factor UF should be as high as possible but it is recommended a small portion of fuel to remain 
unburnt in order to avoid high concentration losses within the cell. Regarding the absorption 
cycle it was observed that there was a maximum in the desorber temperature after which the 
cooling effect started to decrease. The explanation was that a portion of water was also 
vaporized together with ammonia, resulting in smaller cooling effect in the evaporator. Finally 
the analysis related to condensation and evaporation temperatures, revealed the need to run 
the cycle in higher temperatures regarding the evaporator and lower ones in the condenser. 
Of course, both parameters are strongly dependent on the temperature of the heat sink.  
The utilization of hot gas cleaning method by metal sorbents ZNO and NAHCO3 showed very 
good performance since syngas was cleaned from H2S and HCL concentrations to < 1-ppm 
levels before entering the SOFC. An investigation of whether it is feasible to implement the 
system on a selected building was also conducted.  Since it was not possible to acquire the 
energy demand data from the same hospital that the waste composition was taken into 
consideration, a demand profile from a building found in literature was taken into account. 
The techno-economic analysis showed that the system could successfully cover the electricity 
==PAGE:90==
 
68 
 
and the cooling demand, whereas the heat was satisfied to a percentage of 57%, with the 
remaining demand was covered from natural gas boilers. The payback period was 
approximately 2,5 years. 
Summing up, the studied system showed interesting results related to different operation 
conditions as well as regarding the economic feasibility of the project. As mentioned in the 
beginning of the discussion the system is quite novel, since gasification, fuel cell technology 
and hot gas cleaning methods are not yet in the stage of commercialization, whereas 
absorption cycles are not the preferable option related to cooling demand. Thus, there are 
challenges for all the technologies, which need to be addressed and are discussed below. 
9.2 Limitations and Recommendations 
 The IDEAL property method was used during the modeling of the complete system in 
Aspen Plus. This method is appropriate when there are no complex chemical substances 
within the process, which is the case in the gasification and the fuel cell part, since 
elements such as H2 and CO can be considered as ideal gases. In the absorption cycle the 
mixture of ammonia-water can have different behavior compared to an ideal gas in 
different temperature and pressure levels. Thus, it would be recommended to try another 
property method which encloses more complex correlations in order to calculate the 
thermo physical properties of the specific mixture. Of course it will be less computational 
efficient since it will require more time to converge but on the other hand it may offer 
higher accuracy in the results. 
 The lack of a fuel cell building block in the Aspen Plus library made difficult the 
implementation of the equations related to voltage and current density calculations. 
However there are some published articles found in literature (W. Zhang, 2005); (Wayne 
Doherty, 2010) in which they used empirical correlations implemented in Aspen Plus in 
order to calculate the voltage losses and to perform a sensitivity analysis by changing 
different parameters in order to investigate the relations between electrical efficiency, 
voltage and current density. In this way it is believed that a better insight of the different 
phenomena happening within the cell can be achieved. 
9.3 Outlook 
Gasification technology can be proved a doable technology in the near future towards to a 
more sustainable and productive waste management. Summing up all the advantages of 
gasification, it is considered more environmental friendly than the traditional method of 
==PAGE:91==
 
Chapter 9. Conclusion 
69 
 
landfill disposal but also compared to the most commercial thermal treatment method of 
incineration. Furthermore when coupling with energy conversion technologies higher 
efficiencies can be achieved. On the contrary, electricity production stemming from steam 
cycles produced in incineration facilities display lower efficiencies, approximately 25%. 
Moreover, gasification can be a valuable option related to the development of distributed 
power generation. Centralized power plants suffer from energy losses due to the large 
transmission lines, as well as from high fuel transportation costs. Particularly the distributed 
power generation resulting from MSW gasification is highly favorable, since there is an urgent 
need for effective handling of waste production, which is naturally unavoidable. MSW 
feedstock by itself has a zero purchase cost and if additionally the transportation costs are 
eliminated due to the “close to end-users” production, then the result can be highly 
advantageous from both a cost and an environmental perspective. Moving forward, the 
obstacles that gasification technology has to overcome should now be addressed.  First and 
foremost there are not many examples of proven steady successful operation of gasifiers. 
Currently the number of gasification plants are around 100 worldwide, most of them in Japan, 
thus there is no clear evident if the technology is ready to pass from demonstration level to 
complete commercial. Another significant barrier comprise the fact that there is a lack of 
knowledge related to which kind of gasifier is suitable for the highly diversity observed in the 
compositions of the different feedstock. Focusing on MSW, they are characterized by high 
heterogeneity, which can be different from place to place since it is dependent on various 
parameters such as consumer´s habits and income. Thus usually pretreatment and sorting of 
MSW before entering the gasifier is a prerequisite for an efficient operation, a fact that 
increases the costs significantly. Countries who have adopt recycling in their waste 
management policy can be easily address the fuel pretreatment costs. For example, presence 
of metals or glasses, which are not characterized by valuable energy content, can be recycled 
before they reach the gasification plant. Finally the high costs related to the syngas cleaning 
constitute also a significant barrier. Concentrations of contaminants HCL and H2S, as well as 
solid particles, are highly undesirable when syngas is destined as a fuel to energy conversion 
technologies such as turbines or fuel cells. Currently hot gas cleaning methods have gained a 
lot of interest when coupling gasification and SOFCs. Compared to the mature technology of 
cold gas cleaning, hot gas cleaning provides better heat management of the process since the 
temperature of the produced syngas is in similar levels with the respective operating 
temperature of the SOFCs. On the contrary, it requires higher capital costs and it is not yet 
much reliable, due to the lack of adequate successive demonstration projects. In conclusion, 
==PAGE:92==
 
70 
 
more R&D needs to be performed on gasifier´s design related to fuel flexibility as well as on 
less costly cleaning methods, together with enough evident of successful operation of 
gasification plants in order the specific technology to penetrate the market. 
Similarly to gasification, fuel cell technology is a very promising option regarding the 
production of electricity and heat, which is also in the early stages of development. Features 
such as high electrical efficiencies compared to conventional technologies, very low emissions 
and quite operation constitute the specific technology very attractive. The most important 
barrier for the fuel cells to penetrate the power market is their significant costs. The current 
cost of fuel cells is estimated to 4000€/kW. If fuel cells want to compete with power 
production technologies for stationary applications the cost must be reduced in the range of 
750-1500 €/kW. In the automotive sector the challenge is even harder since the fuel cell price 
must get down to 60-100 €/kW in order to prevail against internal combustion engines. The 
efforts regarding the cost reduction should focus on finding new less expensive materials 
regarding the catalyst and the electrolytes. Particularly for SOFCs, research is nowadays going 
on, aiming to reduce the operating temperature inside the cell to around 650 (°C), a fact that 
will eliminate the thermal stresses as well as the necessity for expensive materials able to 
handle such a high temperature environment. Furthermore, since SOFCs are able to utilize 
different kind of fuels except for hydrogen, this aspect make them exposed to contaminants, 
which can have deteriorating effects on the fuel cells performance. SOFC´s are extremely 
sensitive to Sulphur and Chlorine poisoning, thus alternative materials with high resistivity in 
these two chemical elements, are highly desirable. This aspect will also cause a reduction in 
the cost for cleaning equipment. In general terms SOFC´s penetration in the market can also 
be benefited from economies of scale if the production increases. This aspect may also 
contribute to identifying and elimination, any inefficiencies related to the production process.  
In contrast to gasification and fuel cells, absorption cooling is more mature technology, which 
is however on the sideline of the cooling market due to the high prevalence of conventional 
vapor compression cycles. The most commercial combination of operating fluids water-
lithium bromide and ammonia-water suffer from crystallization and corrosion issues and the 
values related to COP compared to vapor compression cycles are relatively low. Nevertheless, 
the fuel utilized in the power plant for the production of electricity which will drive the 
compression cycles as well as the transmission losses should be taken also into consideration 
in order to estimate the ratio between the COPs of absorption and vapor compression cycles 
at which they are comparable. Absorption cycles are as mentioned in chapter five quite 
favorable option when waste heat is available, mainly stemming from industrial applications, 
==PAGE:93==
 
Chapter 9. Conclusion 
71 
 
since it is essentially free and can contribute to the reduction of electricity costs. An interesting 
configuration is the coupling of vapor compression cycle and absorption chiller in a hybrid 
cycle. Particularly, the vapor compression cycles can satisfy the base load demand for cooling 
while absorption cycles can be exploited during peak hours where electricity prices are 
significantly higher. Further research aiming to improve the absorption cycles should focus on 
finding new combinations of working fluids that can achieve higher COPs as well as on the 
reduction of installation and maintenance costs. 
Summing up coupling of gasification, SOFC and absorption chiller constitute a novel 
configuration that should be further investigated. The most important barriers that have to 
be addressed are the costs related to each technology that will make them competitive to the 
existing market technologies. Furthermore a sufficient number of successful demonstration 
projects can add reliability to the system, contribute to a better overview of the system´s 
performance and finally detect any challenges to be addressed.

away from the air used for cooling. Then the depleted air enters HEATEX4 and preheats the 
air going to the cathode as stated above. 
Based on the UF some fuel containing leftovers of H2, CO AND CH4 (stream TOBURN2) mixes

generating combustion reactions. As a result the exhaust gases from the burner in high 
temperature (400-500 °C) represented by stream TOCOOLER enters the block named COOLER 
which models the heat transfer provided to the absorption cycle and more specifically to the 
component DESORBER by cooling the stream TOCOOLER in a specified temperature. 
Subsequently the exhaust gases now in a lower temperature enter the heat exchanger 
HEATX5 where they heat up water coming from the ambient. The mass flow of the water is 
determined by a design specification block which varies the mass flow until the desired 
temperature of 55 °C based on the available heat coming from the stream GASIN.

RECTIFIER 
As mentioned above one of the major drawbacks of ammonia-water absorption cycles is the 
relatively small temperature difference of boiling regarding the two operating fluids. As a 
result water vapor coexists with the ammonia vapor in the stream leaving the desorber, a fact 
that can negatively affect the cooling effect in the evaporator. Thus a rectifier is inserted in 
order a significant portion of water vapor to be condensed along with some ammonia 
particles. As a result the stream leaving the rectifier is almost pure ammonia refrigerant vapor. 
The rejected heat to a low temperature heat sink is represented by stream QRECT. Rectifier 
modelling in Aspen plus is illustrated in Figure E.4. A cooler block is inserted, where the water 
vapor is condensed as well as a flash separator block where vapor refrigerant (stream 
TOCOND) and liquid absorbent together with a small amount of condensed ammonia are 
splitted (stream LIQUID). Concerning the cooler block the given input parameters are the

The vapor liquid heat exchanger (VLHEX) is placed between the outlets of the condenser and
