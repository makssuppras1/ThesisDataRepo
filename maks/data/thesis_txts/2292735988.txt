ABSTRACT 
 
The mitigation of unwanted frequencies such as harmonic and resonance frequencies in 
modern wind power plants, is a common and challenging issue for the power engineer-
ing field. The Wind Energy sector is using a wide range of different strategies in order 
to attenuate the unwanted frequencies injected in the power grid and many research pro-
jects that are focused especially on wind power applications are investigating possible 
improvements and optimization of the already existing solutions. 
 
The goal of this master thesis project is to investigate the possibilities of mitigating the 
resonance frequencies in a wind power plant by converter modulation control. The idea 
is to utilize the high switching harmonics produced by the grid-side converter of the 
wind turbine generators, for the active damping of the resonance frequencies.

INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Background 
 
As the share of wind power production in the global energy market is increasing, wind 
power plants become larger and more complex power systems. In modern wind farms 
and especially offshore, variable speed wind turbine generators are used almost exclu-
sively along with an increased number of nonlinear equipment such as power electron-
ics and active components in general.  
 
Components with non-linear operation introduce harmonic frequencies in their output 
signals. Unwanted frequencies such as harmonic and resonance frequencies are there-
fore very common in wind power systems. Since unwanted frequencies are causing dis-
tortions in the sinusoidal waveforms of voltage and current, there are essential require-
ments for their control.  
 
A typical solution for mitigating certain harmonic frequencies is the use of passive fil-
ters which absorb the harmonic content of the harmonic order to which they are tuned, 
thus preventing their flow into the power grid. Passive filters can also be used for damp-
ing power system resonances of known frequency, however they can introduce addi-
tional unwanted resonances [1]. 
 
With modern power electronics and controls, the damping of unwanted frequencies can 
be achieved by active power filters. In Wind Power Plants, the application of power 
converters with advanced and fast control strategies leaves plenty of room for affecting 
or controlling the higher frequency components [2]. By implementing additional har-
monic controllers in the main control scheme and by manipulating the converter modu-
lation, there are possibilities for reducing harmonic emission into the power grid or even 
for the mitigation of some resonance frequencies[3].

1.2 
Problem formulation 
 
This project is focused on building an algorithm in simulation software Simulink, for 
the active damping of the resonance frequency in a sample Wind Power Plant through 
modulation control of the grid-side converter. The main approach is to manipulate the 
modulation of the converter in a way that the switching frequencies are produced at the 
same frequency as the resonance. Consequently, by setting the phase of the switching 
frequencies to the appropriate angle, a low impedance path through the converter reac-
tor should be created for the mitigation of the resonance frequency. 
 
During the procedure, the following objectives are considered: 
 
 Building a sample Wind Power Plant model with realistic parameters and efficient 
steady state operation. 
 Accurately detecting the resonance of the system and determining its frequency, 
phase and amplitude.  
 Implementing the modulation control algorithm into the model and investigating the 
results. 
 
1.3 
Outline of thesis 
 
The outline of the thesis is presented below: 
 
Chapter 1 provides a short description and the objectives of the project. 
 
Chapter 2 describes the concept of harmonic and resonance frequencies, the Voltage 
Source Converter with Pulse Width Modulation and general damping techniques. 
 
Chapter 3 presents the Simulink model design of the sample Wind Power Plant. 
 
Chapter 4 presents the algorithm for detecting and measuring the characteristics of the 
resonance frequency. 
 
Chapter 5 presents the modulation control algorithm and discusses the results of the 
simulations.

The presence of harmonic voltages and currents has a detrimental effect on power sys-

Theoretical framework 
25 
Harmonics are mainly caused by the nonlinear equipment used widely in today's power 
systems. Common nonlinear loads are current regulators, switched-mode power sup-
plies, low power consumption lamps, all equipment with built-in switching devices and 
many other electronic devices. These components have nonlinear voltage-current char-
acteristics, meaning that the current is not proportional to the applied voltage and as a 
result it is distorted. Harmonic currents produce harmonic voltage drops across the im-
pedance of the power network resulting into harmonic distortion in the voltage as well. 
 
Other components related to generation of high frequency harmonics are the power 
electronic devices used in modern electronic control of power consumption. Such com-
ponents are power converters, HVDCs and STATCOMs which are classified as active 
components [6]. 
 
A typical source of high frequency harmonics is the Voltage Source Converter with 
PWM. In this case, the harmonic frequencies are integer multiples of the grid frequency, 
the carrier frequency or a mixture of both. In modern Wind Power Plants, a typical con-
figuration is the variable speed wind turbine with full power converter. The DC-AC 
Voltage Source Converters of each individual wind turbine are the main contributors to 
the total harmonic emission of the wind farm [7]. 
 
The total harmonic impact at the Point of Common Coupling of the Wind Power Plant 
must be monitored and controlled in order to comply with the power quality standards 
of the grid operator. 
 
Internally, within the wind farm, the main effects of harmonics are the reduction in the 
efficiency of power generation and transmission, the increased thermal stress of the 
electrical components and possible disturbances in the control systems. Externally, the 
main issue is the injection of unwanted harmonic currents into the power grid [7].

2.3 
Electrical resonance  
 
In electric circuits, the phenomenon of resonance occurs when the circuit stores energy 
in a form of oscillating voltage and current. This particular ability of electric circuits 
and the resonance phenomenon that stems from it, is due to the attributes of the two 
basic reactive components, the capacitor and the inductor. 
 
Capacitors store energy in the form of electric field, electrically interpreted as voltage. 
Inductors store energy in the form of magnetic field electrically interpreted as current. If 
a capacitor and an inductor are directly connected to each other and are momentarily 
subjected to an applied voltage, the energy injected into the system will be stored as an 
oscillating voltage and current due to the charging and discharging of the capacitor and 
the inductor respectively. 
 
C

Figure 2-2: Tank circuit, the energy momentarily provided by the voltage source is 
stored in the circuit in a form of oscillating voltage and current. 
Of course, these oscillations are propagating with a decreasing amplitude due to the 
resistive losses in the circuit, until they are eventually completely dissipated. However, 
the frequency of the oscillations is solely dependent on the size of the reactive compo-
nents   and   and independent of the amount of stored energy. This natural frequency 
of the system is also called resonance frequency.  
 
Almost all AC circuits with a combination of capacitive and inductive components have 
one or multiple natural frequencies. Therefore, resonance phenomena may occur in all 
power systems resulting into unwanted oscillations of voltage and current at the reso-
nance frequency.

into a high voltage level distortion between the inductance and the capacitance of the

In large offshore Wind Power Plants, resonance issues are almost inevitable since their 
power network consists of long transmission lines and cables, shunt compensators and 
step-up transformers. As resonance frequencies are determined by the size of the induc-
tive and capacitive components, the resonance frequencies in Wind Power Plants are 
essentially dependent on the length of the lines and cables, the shunt reactive compensa-
tion equipment and the operating state of the wind farm in general.  
 
The resonance frequencies may vary from a few hundred to a few thousand     accord-
ing to the configuration of the system at each operating state (e.g. the number and loca-
tion of the operating wind turbines). Based on theoretical studies in simplified networks, 
it is concluded that the resonance frequencies are decreasing with an increasing size of 
shunt capacitors and cable length [9]. 
 
Resonance frequency studies and analysis in wind power applications are of major im-
portance in order to combat, by means of passive filters and active damping, the reso-
nance distortions caused by harmonic excitation.

2.6 
Passive and active filtering  
 
As discussed in the previous sections, harmonic and resonance frequencies in power 
systems are in most cases unwanted frequencies that cause distortions in voltage and 
current waveforms.  
 
In wind power plants, significant resonance frequencies may be introduced by the wide-
spread MV cable system and the park transformers. The resonance effects are seen as 
significant harmonic distortion at the PCC [12]. The power converters and other nonlin-
ear electronic devices used in the control systems are also generating harmonic frequen-
cies. Careful design of the WPP and correct assessment of the behavior of the power 
network, is the first line of defense against harmonic and resonance frequencies. There-
fore, the evaluation of the harmonic emission and the identification of possible reso-
nance conditions are mandatory during the design process of a WPP. However, due to 
the complexity of modern WPPs, it is impossible to determine or predict all possible 
sources of harmonic and resonance frequencies. Hence, the application of passive and 
active filtering in WPP is considered mandatory. 
 
A simple, cost effective method for attenuating harmonic frequencies is the use of miti-
gating equipment such as Delta-Delta and Delta-Wye Transformers and reactors. Delta-
Delta and Delta Wye transformer configuration shifts the phase relationship to two-level 
converters through cancellation techniques. Reactors work as filters for harmonic cur-
rent due to their characteristic of inducing voltage at their terminals which opposes the 
rate of change of current.  
 
Passive filters are linear filters based on combinations of passive components. In power 
systems, shunt passive filters are connected in parallel to nonlinear loads and are tuned 
to provided especially low impedance paths to ground at certain frequencies. Thus, they 
can be used in WPP in order to eliminate frequencies of certain harmonic order. Differ-
ent filter configurations are used for filtering low order and higher order harmonics. 
They are installed in the WTG level circuit and at the onshore or offshore substations of 
the WPP [13]. Since they are designed to be capacitive at fundamental frequency, they 
are sources of reactive power.  
 
The disadvantage of passive filters in WPPs is that they attenuate only specific harmon-
ics to which they are tuned to. Also, they may introduce additional resonances due to 
their interactions with other passive components in the system. Therefore, their integra-
tion into the power network demands an extensive knowledge about the system. Anoth-
er complication is that passive filters are often oversized in order to cover uncertainties 
due to lack of information.  
 
==PAGE:39==
 
Theoretical framework 
37 
Active filters are filters which use active components and power electronics in order to 
implement the same transfer functions and achieve the same results as passive filters. 
Unlike passive filters though, active filers do not present potential resonance to the sys-
tem and are not affected by changes in the source impedance.  
 
The general principle of a shunt active filter realized by a converter, is to measure the 
distortion current and pass it to the control system. In return, the control system deter-
mines the correct pulse pattern for the converter gate signals in order to generate an am-
plified, anti-phase compensation current for mitigation of the harmonic components. 
 
Active filtering is realized as a secondary function of the power converters and control 
systems of WTGs in order to attenuate harmonic and resonance frequencies. By apply-
ing additional active filters such as band rejection filters, band stop filters and notch 
filters in the main control chain, the harmonic content produced by the converters can 
be actually reduced in order to comply with the grid standards [3]. Another possibility is 
the active damping of the resonance oscillations of the passive     filter by means of 
virtual resistor [14].  
 
In this project the active damping of the system's resonance frequency is implemented 
by controlling the frequency-modulation index of the grid-side PWM converter in an 
effort to generate the necessary "compensation" current.

3.5.1 
Submarine cables 
 
For harmonic studies in offshore WPP with submarine cables, the cable modeling is 
extremely important in order achieve sufficient accuracy in the harmonic frequency 
range.  
 
The parameters of a power transmission cable are distributed along the length of the 
cable as seen in Figure 3-13: 
 
Figure 3-13: Cable equivalent circuit with distributed parameters.  
 
 The series resistors   represent the distributed resistance of the conductors ex-
pressed in Ohms per unit length. 
 The series inductors   represent the distributed reactance of the conductors ex-
pressed in Henries per unit length. 
 The shunt capacitors   represent the distributed capacitance between the conductor 
and the ground/shield, expressed in Farads per length. 
 The shunt conductances   represent the distributed conductance between the con-
ductor and the ground/shield, expressed in Siemens per length. 
 
The impedance of passive components and therefore the impedance of cables depends 
on the frequency. However, in contrast to DC networks, in AC networks, an additional 
phenomenon occurs. The so-called skin effect is the tendency of AC current to flow 
through the conductor in a way that current density is largest close to the conductor's 
surface and near zero close to the center. This means that the effective AC resistance of 
a conductor increases and the increase is higher at higher frequencies.  
==PAGE:54==
 
simulink model design 
52 
The same applies to all electrical parameters ( ,  ,  ,  ) of the cable, therefore the ac-
tual parameters are frequency dependent [16]. Another interesting phenomenon in-
volved in submarine cable modeling is the proximity effect related to the wave propaga-
tion speed which depends on the   and   parameters of the cable. 
 
Based on the above, the cables are modeled with the Simulink Distributed Parameters 
Line model where wave propagation phenomena and line end reflections are represent-
ed with much better accuracy than the π-equivalent section model [15].

resonance frequencies are introduced into the system. In this chapter, the resonance fre-

quency, the amplitude and the phase of the resonance oscillations in the grid phase volt-

MODULATION CONTROL AND ACTIVE DAMPING 
 
The active damping method investigated in this project, is based on controlling the 
modulation of the grid-side converter. The goal is to control the high frequency switch-
ing harmonics produced by the PWM scheme in order to match the frequency of the 
detected resonance oscillations.  
 
The phase of the switching harmonics must also be controlled in order to be      re-
versed from the calculated phase angle of the resonance frequency. The switching har-
monics that have to be controlled are the high frequency harmonics in the voltage output 
of the converter.  
 
If the converter voltage harmonics at the resonance frequency, are in anti-phase with the 
grid voltage resonance oscillations at the terminals of the converter reactor, then a low 
impedance path is created across the reactor. As a result, the harmonic currents can can-
cel out some of the energy of the resonance oscillations which is dissipated in the resis-
tors of the WTG circuit.

5.4 
Limitations and future work 
 
Apart from the conclusion that the switching harmonics have to be somehow amplified, 
there are also some other matters of concern. First, the proposed method is focused in 
the active damping in phase  . Even though, the detection algorithm is developed for all 
three phase, the modulation control is implemented only in phase  . Obviously, the 
same modulation has to be implemented in all three phases, meaning that the PWM 
modulation must have a separate carrier signal for each phase.  
 
Another important issue is the fact that the switching harmonics occur in double pairs. 
By increasing the switching frequency of the carrier, one switching harmonic is set to 
match the resonance and be phase shifted by     . At the same time though, a switch-
ing frequency very close to the resonance is also produced. The effect of this harmonic 
depends on the internal damping of the system and how easily it could trigger additional 
resonance oscillations.  
 
Regarding the amplification of harmonics, a parallel capacitor would be a rather simple 
approach. A more sophisticated plan could be to use two parallel converters that cancel 
out their switching harmonics under normal operation and amplify them when a reso-
nance frequency is detected.   
 
Lastly, as explained in section 5.1, a three dimensional look up table must be built in 
order to compute the correct characteristics of the switching harmonics at a given opera-
tion state.

CONCLUSION 
 
The goal of this master thesis project is to investigate the possibilities of mitigating un-
wanted frequencies in Wind Power Plants by modulation control of the Wind Turbine 
Generator grid-side converter. The proposed method is based on controlling the modula-
tion scheme of the converter in order to manipulate the high switching frequencies.  
 
The idea is to increase the switching frequency of the converter in order for the high 
frequency voltage harmonics to coincide with the resonance frequency of the power 
system. Provided that the phase angle of the harmonics is appropriately adjusted to be in 
anti-phase with the resonance, a low impedance path is created through the converter 
reactor. As a result, the harmonic currents have an opposite phase from the resonance 
and are contributing in the mitigation of the resonance oscillations.  
 
The method is tested on simulation level with simulation software Simulink. First, an 
algorithm is built in order to detect the resonance and determine its frequency, ampli-
tude and phase. The algorithm seems to accurately measure the frequency and the phase 
of the resonance but the amplitude computation becomes problematic if sharp edges are 
present in the oscillations. Furthermore the frequency detection algorithm assumes a 
constant resonance frequency. However, it is very possible that multiple resonances at a 
very close frequency may appear in a power system. In order to handle such situations, 
the detection algorithm should be developed further.  
 
By examining the converter output voltage, it is derived that the amplitude and phase of 
the switching harmonic voltage has a linear relation with the modulation index. This 
means that the characteristics of the switching harmonics can be determined according 
to the operation state of the converter and can be used in a form of a look up table in the 
modulation control. The amplitude and phase of the switching harmonics are dependent 
on the frequency of the carrier as well, so in order to accurately implement the active 
damping method, a three dimensional look up table is required.  
 
Finally, the main conclusion of the project is that the generated harmonic currents at the 
resonance frequency have a limited impact in the mitigation of the resonance oscilla-
tions. The most probable reason is that the magnitude of the harmonic current is de-

creased by the step-up transformer of the wind turbine and therefore, it does not con-
tribute significantly in the mitigation of the resonance. In order to increase the effec-
tiveness of the method, the harmonic currents have to be amplified. A simple proposed 
solution is a modified WTG circuit with a parallel capacitor in order to allow harmonic 
current to bypass the transformer. With further investigation, other configurations can 
also be tested both in simulation and in system level.
