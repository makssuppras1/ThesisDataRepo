Abstract
Numerical Modelling of Piston Ring Wear
The main objective of this project was to create a numerical model which predicts the
behaviour of piston rings in large marine two-stroke engines under a range of conditions.
Piston rings in combustion engines have to withstand very adverse conditions, from
high pressures and temperatures to friction against the liner of the combustion chamber.
Therefore, they will suﬀer a large amount of wear, and in order to predict how and when
this wear will occur it is necessary to create models able to simulate these conditions and
the piston ring’s response.
To create the model initially the problem was assumed as static, in order to simplify
the solution of the Reynolds equation. Using this model as a starter point, a further
and more complex one was developed which considered the dynamic part of the problem.
Other aspects taken into consideration in the model were the behaviour of the lubricant
throughout the cycle as a result of temperature changes, the amount of wear due to the
roughness of the surfaces and the distribution of said wear as a result of the twisting of
the ring and the deformation of the liner.
The results given by the numerical model are presented and compared as each new
feature is introduced into the model. Finally, it is possible to obtain an estimation of the
behaviour of the piston ring under operating conditions.

1. Introduction
The life of machine components has been a very studied ﬁeld in the world of mechan-
ical engineering. The importance of investigating this phenomenon stems in the fact that
the premature demise of a certain component can bring greater problems other than its
own substitution. When a component breaks in a machine it can provoke the untimely
destruction of many other pieces, and the entire mechanism can even cease to work.
Therefore, its is important for designers to predict the behaviour of the components over
long time periods. In the case of combustion engines one of the pieces which suﬀers a
large amount of wear are piston rings.

These components are mounted on the piston and are separated from the liner by a
thin ﬁlm of lubricant as seen in ﬁgure 2. The width of said ﬁlm will depend on factors
such as the viscosity of the oil, the relative velocity between surfaces and the pressure.
When this ﬁlm becomes too thin due to a change in these conditions, wear will occur and
as a result the geometry of the piston ring will be modiﬁed.
Figure 2: Cross section of a mounted piston ring
Piston rings are placed along the piston of internal combustion engines, ﬁtted tightly
into grooves on the piston with the objective of sealing combustion gases. Furthermore
the oil separates the moving parts. This is essential to maintain the eﬃciency of the mo-
tor and to prevent oil leaks. Also, another function of piston rings is to ensure the heat
transfer from the piston to the cylinder wall. Other important aspects of piston rings are
to minimize the frictional power loss and to, where possible, minimize the amount of wear.
These rings have an annular shape and the surface in contact with the chamber walls
can have either a curved edge or a straight one, whether they are mounted at the top or
at the bottom [2]. This proﬁle will determine the pressure distribution along the contact
and are designed to ensure the correct amount of oil ﬁlm thickness between the wall and
the ring. In this project the top ring will be considered. The ring used will have a curved
proﬁle.

These rings are subjected to several forces, from the pressure in the combustion cham-
ber, the preloading of the ring and the pressure in the oil, as well as the friction from
the contact. The lubrication of said contact can be studied through the Stribeck curve,
which relates the relative velocity of the parts in contact with the coeﬃcient of friction.
With very low velocities the value of this coeﬃcient is high, this is caused by the contact
of the two surfaces and an insuﬃcient oil ﬁlm thickness. As the velocity is increased the
friction coeﬃcient lowers and the contact enters a lubrication regime called hydrodynamic
lubrication, where the two surfaces will be separated by a ﬁlm of oil.
As it is possible to see in ﬁgure 4 there are three main lubrication regimes: boundary
when the two surfaces are in contact, mixed when there is some contact and some lub-
ricant ﬁlm separating the surfaces and hydrodynamic when a complete ﬁlm is separating
both surfaces.

The lack of correct lubrication with low velocities leads to an increase in wear and it is
because of this that the most worn out areas of cylinder liners are in the top dead center,
which corresponds to crank angle 0o.
Wear in piston ring is of great importance, depending on how the piston is worn the
pressure distribution will change slightly and this will aﬀect the contact between surfaces.
Modelling wear is generally a complex problem and many assumptions must be made.
Many experiments have also been made in order to study this, such as that in [2]. Here
the piston ring geometry is studied after several hours of operation, varying the amount
of lubricant so that both starved conditions and full ﬂooded lubrication are considered.
Figure 5: Variation of ring proﬁles with time [2]
In ﬁgure 5 it is possible to see how wear will occur asymmetrically on the surface of
the ring. This is due to a series of factors which aﬀect the ring. On the one hand, the ring
is twisted as a result of the diﬀerence of pressures above and underneath it, which vary
throughout the combustion cycle. The twisting of the ring will vary and will therefore
favour a larger amount of wear on one of the sides of the ring. The unequal wear is also
a result of the deformation of the liner surface as a result of the thermal dilatation.
These phenomena must be implemented into any numerical model which aims at pre-
dicting the behaviour of piston rings under operation, since it is clear to see from the
experimental results that twist plays an important role.
There are many other factors which inﬂuence the wear found in piston rings such as
the temperature of the oil, which inﬂuences its viscosity, and the materials of the liner
and the ring. All these diﬀerent aspects of the problem make it quite diﬃcult to model

3.3
Introducing wear
As a result of the changes in the the ﬁlm thickness during the turn of the crankshaft
there are moments when it becomes too small and there is contact between the piston
ring and the liner and therefore wear occurs. This happens near the top and bottom dead
centres, therefore the greatest amount of wear is seen at these two points.
Wear is a very complex process and it is generally diﬃcult to model. In this project
a very simple wear model has been chosen. To introduce wear into the program it is
necessary to determine a limit ﬁlm thickness where the lubrication would cease to be
hydrodynamic and would be mixed, with therefore some contact between surfaces. Also,
it is necessary to determine the amount of material taken away after a cycle with wear.
When the ﬁlm thickness becomes lower than the limit value the program takes away a
certain amount of material from the ring, therefore changing the original parabolic height
distribution. This is done by calculating the value of the speciﬁc ﬁlm thickness λ [2]:
λ = hmin
σ
(50)
Where hmin is the minimum ﬁlm thickness in the oil distribution and σ is the composite
surface roughness. This value is given by the square root of the surface roughnesses of
the liner and the piston ring squared [11]:

In [2] the value of λ which marks the limit between full ﬂuid lubrication and mixed
lubrication is at around 4, whilst the limit between mixed lubrication and boundary con-
ditions is at λ = 0.5.
When it is assumed that the ring does not twist the wear proﬁle will be a truncated

surface. This result is unrealistic and can cause false pressure peaks, and when twisting

3.6
Ring twisting
The ring can be deformed as a result from the forces acting on it. The pressures above
and under the ring as a result of the combustion and the movement of the piston can cause
the ring to twist either downwards, if the pressure above is higher, or upwards when the
pressure in the combustion chamber becomes smaller than that between the rings. Also,
the ring can be twisted as a result of the moment exerted by the friction force of the ring
against the liner.
To model this situation initially a simpliﬁcation was make: it was assumed that the
twisting of the ring was a result of the bending of the walls of the crook where it rests.
This was given by the diﬀerence in pressures and the friction force multiplied by a spring
constant which depended on the stiﬀness of the walls.

An approximate maximum angular deformation of the piston ring landing of 4 · 10−4
radians was initially assumed, as an approximate value provided by MAN, in order to
calculate the stiﬀness constant K. This maximum was reached when the friction force
and the diﬀerence in pressures was maximum, at the top dead center. By using the value

the ring the maximum force per metre is calculated. It is important to remember that all
the forces mentioned, as the case is axisymetric, are considered in N/m.
The friction force was calculated by assuming that the normal force acting on on the
ring towards the liner wall was given by the pressure from the combustion chamber along
the side of the ring facing away from the liner. This was then multiplied by a very low
value of friction coeﬃcient, µ = 0.001 [4], due to the presence of hydrodynamic lubrication.
Depending of the crank angle the twist given by the friction force could have a posit-
ive or a negative sign, according to the reference system in ﬁgure 26, whilst the pressure
diﬀerence was always given by the pressure from the combustion chamber minus that

force depended on the sign of the velocity.

the geometric center of the ring’s cross section. After establishing said point, two sets

the ring as shown in ﬁgure 26. The local coordinate system, with subscript 1, would be
located in the center of rotation and would be ﬁxed on the ring. This means that in the
case of twisting, the system would turn too.

It is also important to note that the factors mentioned here are not the only ones

from the temperature gradient along the ring. The highest temperatures are found in the
interior part of the ring, on the top, where it is nearest to the top of the piston, whilst
the coldest part of the ring will be that which faces the liner. These temperatures will
result in a certain thermal dilatation, however their measurement is so complex that as a
result of a lack of data, they have not been taken into account in this model.

3.8
Load variation
In the real operating life of an engine there will be changes in the load, according to
the needs at each moment. Hitherto the data used corresponds to a load of 100%, however
in real operation of these engines this load will be reached rarely.
In order to take this change into the program it is necessary to consider how many
aspects of the operating conditions are aﬀected by the load used. As the load is reduced
so do the temperature, the pressure and the speed.

A lowering of the temperature will have as a consequence the rise in the viscosity, as
well as less deformation of the liner, due to a decrease in the thermal dilatation. This will
therefore change the angle at which wear will occur, if the ﬁlm thickness is lowered under
the aforementioned limit. To apply the temperature variation to the liner deformation
it was assumed that the relation between the deformation and the temperature was linear.
When the pressure is reduced there will be an eﬀect on several aspects. One of them
will be the angle by which the ring will be twisted, this will be due to a change in the
pressure diﬀerence on either side of the ring and on the friction force, which depends on
the pressure from the combustion chamber. The external force exerted on the ring also
depends on the maximum pressure and this has a direct impact on the ﬁlm thickness.
Additionally, by changing the pressures on the sides of the ring the boundary conditions
for the resolution of the Reynolds equation change and therefore the overall pressure dis-
tribution will change.
To estimate the value of the pressure between rings, as a result of a lack of data, a
reasonable assumption was made: the pressure is scaled down as a fraction of the pressure
at 100% load.

4. Conclusions
Piston ring wear is a very complicated phenomenon, which depends upon many vari-
ables and physical aspects.
This makes its modelling a very complex problem.
This
project has attempted to take into account many of the factors which play a role in this,
however there are many more which would require an extremely intricate model to incor-
porate them all.
The results obtained with the ﬁnal program were able to predict with a good degree
of accuracy the ﬁnal shape of the ring. Also, the construction of the model from scratch
showed how each new aspect had an inﬂuence on the overall result.
Overall, it is interesting to note that when it comes to determining the shape of wear
liner deformation plays a key role. When the model applied only the diﬀerence of pres-
sures and friction, therefore a ’ﬂat’ liner, the results did not match those found in real
engines. When considering the liner the results varied considerably. Previous to creating
the model the role of liner deformation was not expected to be so important, nevertheless
from the results it is interesting to note how it does indeed inﬂuence the ﬁnal results.
Regarding future work there are any aspects in which the model could be reﬁned and
improved. Many of these improvements would be centered on conﬁrming or contradicting
the accuracy of the assumptions made throughout the development of the program. This
would possible include experimental work, however, since the model is designed for large
two stroke engines this might pose a hindrance not only in the setup of experiments but
also in the availability of equipment.
One of these assumptions would be for example the temperature of the ring remaining
constant throughout the cycle for each given load, which in reality changes as the piston
moves away from the top dead center.
An important simpliﬁcation which was made at the beginning of the project was to
consider the problem as axisymmetric, which simpliﬁed considerably both the solving of
the Reynolds equation as well as the programming. This was a reasonable assumption,

nevertheless there is a slight variation in the wear found at diﬀerent points of the ring.
The rings are made with an opening in them to allow their installation, and as a result the
conditions in the vicinity of the opening will be diﬀerent to those in the regions further
away. In real experiments it has been found that the highest wear rates are found in the
region furthest away from the opening.
Another possible line of work would be the introduction into the model of other aspects
which have been overlooked or deemed unimportant in the present project. An example
would be the operation of the engine under starved conditions and how this would have
an eﬀect on ﬁlm thickness and lubrication. Other considerations would be the thermal
dilation of both the piston and the ring as a result of the higher temperatures on the top
side of the piston in comparison with the bottom.
An interesting aspect of load variation was also left out, and that is the eﬀect of the
transitory period from one load to the other. This means that, for example, when switch-
ing from a load of 100% to 25% there is a time while the temperatures of ring and liner,
and therefore oil, are decreasing. The study of how this could aﬀect the distribution of
wear could be of use, since in normal operations engines undergo many load changes.
Unfortunately this is diﬃcult to model and has not been considered in this project.
On the whole the project has taken into account most of the variables relevant in
piston ring wear and eventually yields a solution which is quite close to the experimental
data. The results of the program are certainly encouraging but as with any model there
is always room for improvement and ﬁne tuning.

5. Social and environmental impacts
Nowadays, one of the pressing concerns of our society is protecting the environment
and attempting at reducing our impact upon it. This concern takes shape in many ways,
from reducing the harmful emissions of combustion engines to minimizing the waste of
materials.
Two stroke marine engines use immense amounts of oil in order to function, therefore
the study of the lubrication process is of great importance. If optimized it would mean
a great reduction in the costs of operation and in the escape of oil into the combustion
chamber and therefore in the emissions.
Other reasons why the study of wear and lubrication in piston rings is important to
reduce our carbon footprint are the indirect emissions of carbon dioxide in the production
of steels and the power loss due to friction. If it were possible to optimize the amount of
wear produced in piston rings in order to ﬁnd the best ratio of material worn and power
lost through the contact there could be a reduction in the overall energy used both for
production and operation of these engines.
Marine engines have been reﬁned and improved through the thorough study of their
components. These have been perfected in order to minimize the costs of producing and
operating the engines which in turn means a reduction in the costs of transportation of
goods across the sea. In a rapidly evolving world, where globalization is becoming the
principal trend, something like this can have a considerable economic and social impact.
It is therefore of paramount importance to better the design of said ships and their en-
gines. This project makes a modest attempt at helping to move towards this goal and
will hopefully contribute somewhat to the study of piston rings in operation.
