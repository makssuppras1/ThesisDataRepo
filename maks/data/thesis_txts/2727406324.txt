Abstract
At present, there is a large demand for cleanrooms in the world, the common cleanrooms are designed to pro­
vide ACH much higher than unclassified rooms, resulting in excessive energy consumption. The objective of
this study is to investigate saving energy for the ISO7/8 pharmaceutical cleanroom by optimizing the airflow
organization at the low air change rate (10ACH). The study utilizes the RNG K −ϵ model to simulate the air
distribution and particle distribution. In this thesis, the hybrid ventilation (supplied by a non­unidirectional dif­
fuser and a unidirectional diffuser) was newly proposed to be equipped in an ISO7/8 pharmaceutical cleanroom,
compared to the traditional fully mixing ventilation (supplied by non­unidirectional diffusers). The results show
that the hybrid ventilation pattern can effectively meet more stringent cleanliness level requirements even at
10(ACH). It is worth noting that the study highlights the limited impact of increasing the air change rate (ACH)
on improving cleanliness levels. The outcomes advocate that using different diffusers improved the air pattern
to achieve the highest efficiency of contaminant control. In addition, this thesis also provides a qualitative and
quantitative analysis of the relationship between air Distribution and particle distribution, aiming to provide an
index that can be used in the future to evaluate cleanroom contaminants performance through the results of air
Distribution.

Introduction
1.1
Research background
Cleanrooms emerged in the early 1960s, and their evolution has been closely intertwined with advancements
in the precision industry. Originally designed for industrial purposes, cleanrooms have now expanded their ap­
plications to various sectors, including pharmaceuticals, biotechnology, food, and biochemical industries. The
demand for cleanrooms has surged in recent times, especially in response to the global pandemic caused by the
novel coronavirus. Pharmaceutical rooms hold significant importance within the medical industry, as they pro­
vide a controlled environment for manufacturing and assembling pharmaceutical products. The establishment
of pharmaceutical rooms is a common requirement for numerous pharmaceutical companies worldwide.
The fundamental objective of a cleanroom is to create an environment that minimizes the presence of particles
by limiting their introduction, generation, and retention [1]. This goal is crucial for ensuring product quality,
safety, and reliability in industries that rely on precise manufacturing processes.
Partical is an unavoidable topic for cleanrooms. As reported in their publication, Strauss et al. [2] conducted
a series of measurements in a cleanroom to verify that humans are the primary source of particles in such an
environment. It was first mentioned in the book on cleanroom technology [1] that the human body wearing
regular protective clothing in the cleanroom per minute will produce 2 ∗106 particles with a diameter larger
than 0.5 microns. The particle diameter distribution of the particles released by the human body is between 0.5
and 5 microns [3], particles in this range will remain in the air or be removed on exhaust flow [4]. Particles
and microorganisms floating in the air will affect the safe environment required by the cleanroom. Hence, to
achieve and maintain the desired level of cleanliness, the ventilation system plays a pivotal role in cleanroom
technology. The ventilation system within a cleanroom is responsible for supplying clean air, which undergoes
filtration by high­efficiency filters. This filtered air is essential for diluting and removing particles and bacteria
that may be present in the environment or generated by personnel [1].
Clean rooms are typically ventilated by two types of air distribution patterns: laminar flow and mixed flow
distribution. For clean rooms with ISO7 and ISO8 classifications, non­unidirectional flow (NUDF) diffusers
which create a mixed flow environment are commonly used. This ventilation system is dilution the particles by
achieving full mixing of air. Ideally, particle concentration can be regulated by adjusting the airflow to maintain
the desired level. For cleanrooms with higher cleanliness requirements, a large amount of air is supplied with
the unidirectional flow (UDF, laminar flow) to replace contaminated air with fresh air and improve overall
cleanliness. According to the Pharmaceutical CGMPS guide [5] show in table 1.1, a minimum of 20 air changes
per hour (ACH) is generally considered sufficient for achieving the required airflow in ISO8 cleanrooms. the
amount of air volume required often results in high energy consumption in clean rooms. In the journal Clean
Technology [6], it is noted that clean rooms consume a lot of energy compared to non­classified rooms; scientific
literature and experience in the field show that clean rooms use up to 25.3 times more energy (1.25kW/sqm vs.
0.06kW/sqm). Due to the high airflow rates required for specific ISO grades, the energy requirements of HVAC
systems typically account for 50­75% of the power consumption of clean production spaces. High ACH is
therefore the reason to cleanroom energy consumption [7].
. This study examined the demand for pharmaceutical rooms at the cleanliness level of Grade C (Table 1.1). To
address the challenges of global warming and the energy crisis, the study focuses on evaluating the performance
of different air diffusers units in a low air changes per hour (ACH) scenario, specifically ACH 10. The study aims
to provide insights into the suitability and limitations of different air supply units in maintaining the required
cleanliness standards under low ACH conditions. This research contributes to addressing energy challenges in
cleanroom operations and fosters the development of sustainable practices in the pharmaceutical industry.

1.2
Current research direction
Loomans [8] has identified three effective strategies for achieving energy­efficient ventilation in pharmaceu­
tical cleanrooms, namely fine­tuning, Demand Controlled Filtration (DCF), and optimizing airflow patterns.
The control aspects of the ventilation system relate to the first two strategies. Therefore, there are two main
approaches to improving cleanroom energy efficiency: ventilation system control and optimization of air orga­
nization. This thesis aims to investigate the potential for energy savings in cleanrooms by optimizing airflow
patterns.
The available reports proposed various solutions for optimizing cleanroom indoor airflow. These include ad­
justing the arrangement of the air inlet and outlet, varying the number of air changes per hour, and utilizing
different types of air supply equipment. An investigation was conducted by A Agirman [9] on the unidirec­
tional diffuser at a rate of 30 air changes per hour by utilizing the CFD simulation. The study included four
different cases with exhaust positioned in varying locations. The findings indicated that placing the exhaust near
the critical area in the cleanroom significantly reduced the risk of infections; Yun­Chun Tung [10] conducted
a study on the influence of different inlet and outlet arrangements in rooms with unidirectional flow diffusers
and 21ACH by CFD. The results showed that the arrangement did not have a significant impact on ventilation
effectiveness. However, a locally balanced ceiling­return ventilation arrangement was found to be effective
in contamination control. The study by Cetin et al. [11] investigated the comprehensive impact of different
ventilation strategies and particle source positions on the dispersion and removal of fine particles. The authors
conducted both experimental and simulation studies to find out that adjusting the position of the inlet and outlet
has a considerable effect on the airflow organization. They also found out that when the contaminant source
is located close to the outlet opening, the particles were removed easily without any dispersion to the majority
of the room. Fujen Wang[12] analyzed a non­unidirectional flow ventilation system with different ACH, using
CFD to optimize the air distribution by adding the exhausts at the area with low ventilation effectiveness. In
a recent report by Cetin [13], the impact of air changes per hour (ACH) on particle decay in a cleanroom was
investigated using CFD modelling. It was found that 10 ACH is sufficient to meet the recovery time standard
in GMP (bring the room to ’at rest’ level within 15­20min). This study highlights the potential of 10 ACH to
effectively remove particles in cleanrooms, making it a potential solution for meeting cleanroom requirements.
Whyte (2010)[14] examined the influence of diffusers on contamination control in cleanrooms. Specifically,
the study compared the performance of the cleanroom with a 4­way diffuser (NUDF) and the cleanroom with
an open inlet. The research findings highlight the importance of utilizing diffusers with a mixing effect in
cleanrooms to effectively control contamination throughout the entire space. The study conducted by Rouand
[15], proposed a methodology for designing the air distribution in cleanrooms by utilizing computational fluid
dynamics (CFD) simulation. The study was investigated the situations varying from 20.1 to 80.2 ACH with a
unidirectional flow diffuser. The report suggested a method for evaluating the level of cleanliness in a room by
using the age of air as a reliable indicator.
The above literature indicates that Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has emerged as a highly credible re­
search method in this field. Many studies have employed a combination of CFD simulations and experimental
measurements, utilizing existing experimental data to validate the accuracy of the CFD models. The integration
of CFD with experiments allows for a broader range of research possibilities and provides guidance for various
research objectives in the field of ventilation. Cetin [11] emphasized the importance of effective airflow distri­

Eleonora Bottani et.al [16] compared the accuracy of two important numerical models in CFD: k­ω and k­ϵ for

In cleanroom research, the assessment of room cleanliness level is a crucial aspect. The primary regulatory

the indoor airflow within a cleanroom and assessing particle performance across different ventilation system.
In recent research, Whyte.W introduced the indicators to evaluate the ventilation effectiveness in cleanrooms
under non­unidirectional flow ventilation system [21]. It was determined that Performance Index (PI) and Air
Change Effectiveness (ACE) were the most suitable parameters for assessing cleanroom performance. Cetin
[13], in a report, looked at the decay curve of particles and referred to the time it takes for particles to decrease
from 100 to 10 magnitudes as the recovery time. the GMP [19] standard also defines the ideal recovery time
should be between 15 and 20 minutes. Rouand [15], in their study, employed the concept of ”age of air” to
analyze the air cleanliness level within specific areas of a room.
1.3
Knowledge gaps and objectives
The existing literature predominantly focused on studying the airflow organization with different inlet and out­
let positions and the influence of different air change rates (ACH) on contaminant control in cleanrooms. The
location of inlet and outlet has been extensively studied, and this study will adapt previous research results, the
outlet location should be as close to the source of pollutants as possible. These studies primarily investigated a
kind of air diffuser, either focusing solely on unidirectional flow or non­unidirectional flow. To make up for the
lack of existing literature on diffusers exploration. The primary objective of this study is to examine the impact
of different air diffuser configurations on air distribution and particle distribution within the ISO7/8 cleanroom.
It specifically investigated the combined effects of UDF and NUDF air diffuser in the pharmaceutical room,
and examines the air pattern at different ventilation mode (hybrid ventilation and fully mixing ventilation).
Moreover, previous studies primarily concentrated on investigating high air change rate (ACH) scenarios. In
contrast, this thesis work focused on evaluating the energy­saving potential of different diffuser configurations
for cleanrooms under a lower ACH condition of 10.For this study, the selected air supply inlets included a swirl
diffuser, commonly employed in ISO7 and ISO8­grade cleanrooms, and a 4­way diffuser, typically used for
achieving fully mixed indoor environments. Additionally, a newly developed UDF diffuser was chosen as a
device capable of providing downward air in the chamber. By combining these air supply devices and consid­
ering practical implementation in the chamber, the study explored the air distribution and particle distribution
of different ventilation modes in a pharmaceutical cleanroom.
This study utilized Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) as the primary tool to integrate numerical simulation
of air distribution and particle distribution. The aim is to make reasonable predictions of cleanroom partic­
ulate matter distribution using CFD and to quantify the assessment of cleanroom contaminants by utilizing
existing ventilation effectiveness indicators. This approach enables the identification of a direct link between
ventilation efficiency and contaminant levels within the room. The analysis can provide insights into adjusting
specific aspects of ventilation efficiency to directly reduce overall contaminant levels or improve the efficiency
of contaminant removal.
Through these innovative studies, this paper aims to contribute to the field of cleanroom ventilation design,
supporting energy­saving initiatives and sustainable development in cleanroom environments.

Methodology
The study was developed to examine airflow distribution and contaminant distribution in a pharmaceutical clean
room. An investigation was conducted on various diffusers to optimize the airflow pattern within the cleanroom
and achieve better contaminant control at low Air Change Hour (ACH) rates.
The computational investigation utilized the Ansys Fluent software. The fundamental model’s results were
validated with chamber data which was investigated in the DTU campus chamber. The research process was
divided into the following stages: geometry model building, mathematical model building, model validation,
results evaluation, and results discussion.
2.1
Geometry model building
The geometry of the cleanroom model was based on the size and geometry of a climatic chamber located in
the laboratory of the International Centre for Indoor Environment and Energy (ICIEE), DTU. This was done in
order to validate the model with the experimental results obtained during a study on cleanroom ventilation in
the climatic chamber. The plan view is shown in Figure 2.1. The room size was 64 m2, with a 2.6m height
and a 5.3 x 4.7m floor area. There was a work area on the side of the room and two dummies were set near
a work table. The diffusers were placed on the centerline of the ceiling, and the air was extracted through the
displacement outlet at the corner of the room. The cleanliness level of the cleanroom under consideration was
classified as ISO 8 in the chamber.
Figure 2.1: The floor plan of chamber
To be able to effectively investigated the air distribution and particle distribution in a cleanroom. By using
the controlled variables method, a base model (Case 1) was used in the report for model validation, and in
subsequent studies, only the study factors were changed, keeping the other conditions the same as the base
conditions for the computational simulation study. The study factors in this study primarily revolved around
the ventilation parameters, specifically focusing on the diffusers and air change rate of the air diffusers. These
factors was recognized a significant impact on the ventilation effectiveness at the experimental setup.

2.1.2
Futher Case study
Moreover, to ascertain the ventilation mode that yields the highest contaminant removal efficiency at lower Air
Change Hour (ACH) levels, the present thesis delved into a comprehensive examination of particle distribution
and air distribution across varying ACH settings. As representative cases, Case 1, was extensively examined in
the study, particularly at ACH (air change hour) of 10, 15, and 20 Air Changes per Hour. This analysis provided
insights to examine the optimal air change rate required to achieve the desired ventilation performance and
maintain ideal levels of contaminant control.
2.2
Mathematical model building
The case investigation was using the Ansys Fluent 2021R1 version to do the simulation study. CFD simulations
have proven to provide valuable insights into complex airflow patterns and the movement of contaminants
within a given environment. In this study, due to computational limitations, all cases were initially subjected
to steady­state numerical simulations. Once the case met the convergence criteria, which was set at 10−3, and
the monitor plane exhibited stability, the case proceeded to transient numerical simulations using the Discrete
Phase Model (DPM). The simulation involved the tracking of particles over 1620 seconds.
2.2.1
Turbulence Model
Based on the pertinent literature [16], the k−ε model can accurately reproduce the velocity profiles observed in
experimental measurements for a cleanroom. The turbulent flow model for both steady and transient scenarios
was established utilizing the renormalization group (RNG) of the k −ε model with enhanced wall functions.
The enhanced wall function exhibits automatic optimal calculation performance in scenarios characterized by
low Reynolds numbers [24], thereby rendering it suitable for hybrid ventilation configurations in the case study
section 2.1.1.
The model turned on the energy equation and surface­to­surface radiation, allowing the model to consider the
impact of the surrounding environment on the occupant’s body temperature. The fluid in the room was air, and

Boundary Layer
The treatment of the y+ (non­dimensional distance between the wall and the flow) in the buffer layer remains
a challenge for CFD simulations. Figure 2.9 illustrates a comparison between the utilization of enhanced wall
functions and standard wall functions concerning the exact results obtained from DNS (Direct Numerical Sim­

ensuring efficient and uniform air mixing throughout the cleanroom space.

calculation was based on the results of a simulation that represents the room in a ”clean up” stage. During this
stage, the ventilation system operated to remove contaminants from the room and achieved a desired clean state.
The recovery rate quantified the speed at which the room recovered from the initial contaminated state to the

There was a need for quantitative analysis to understand further the impact of air supply volume (ACH) and
air diffusers on the effectiveness of contaminant removal and the level of contamination control achieved in the
cleanroom.
The main indicators analyzed in this study include the Air Exchange Efficiency (AEE), which was calculated
based on the age of air, to understand the ventilation pattern of the room (0.5 is a metric value to check whether
the air is fully mixed). The recovery rate is used to assess the speed of particle removal in the cleanroom. The
Air change efficiency (ACE), as proposed by Whyte.W [21], is employed to evaluate the ventilation efficiency
of the cleanroom ( ACE = 1 is the ideal value). Additionally, the Performance Index (PI) is used to compare the
performance of contaminants in critical area.
AEE, ACE, and PI were calculated using the equations outlined in section 2.3.4. ACE utilized data from C900s
and C1620s (Appendix A.8), while PI investigated a steady state situation under the DPM model. Therefore,
the average data from 720s to 900s were used to determine the contaminant of airborne concentration (no/m3).
The AEE results are shown in Appendix A.7 of the report.

Interestingly, based on the results displayed in Figure 5.4, Case 3 has a recovery rate of around 13. This means
that Case 1 had an excellent ability to remove contaminants in the critical area, which was a contradictory
conclusion with the critical area in Case 1 was contaminated seriously in contour analysis. The only variable
was whether pollutants in the environment were emission. Therefore, in the actual analysis, it is necessary to
pay attention to not generalizing the contaminant removal efficiency by overall results, and the critical area
needs a special analysis. At the same time, the state of the environment is also a factor.
Another perverse phenomenon is that case 1 had a better recovery rate in critical areas than case 3. The critical
areas in Case 3 have been transported clean air by a Unidirectional flow air supply, which should be better
contaminant removal efficiency. After considering the calculation method of recovery rate, it can be found that
the recovery rate is not suitable for evaluating the clean room which used UDF diffuser, because the UDF diffuser
was located in a critical area. The critical area remained unpolluted, rendering the recovery rate (calculated
by the decay of particle concentration at the critical area) cannot be suitable to evaluate the particle removal
efficiency of UDF cleanroom. Whyte.W also pointed out in the report that the calculation formula of recovery
rate applies to NUDF (fully­mixing) cleanrooms. This limitation will be explained further in the section 5.3.

Limitation
Limitations are inherent to any research study and provide insights into the potential constraints or weaknesses
of the research. In the context of the present study, it is important to acknowledge certain limitations that may
have influenced the findings and interpretations. By understanding these limitations, future researchers can
Effect Of Airflow Distribution on The Contaminant Distribution in A Pharmaceutical Cleanroom
47
==PAGE:54==
build upon the current study and address these aspects for further advancement.
The first concern about the findings is the model was designed to be consistent with the laboratory situation in
order to be validated. The same inlet and outlet air volume was selected, and there was no pressure monitoring,
so this is a limitation of the design of the model itself. However, maintaining positive pressure in the cleanroom
is a must. Changes in pressure also lead to changes in the movement of the airflow. It is recommended that
future studies of cleanroom ventilation need to be designed with the pressure in the control.
The model validation of this thesis lacks research verification of particles. The computation simulation which
was conducted under steady­state conditions was verified by tracer gas monitors. However, the transient simu­
lation was not further verified due to the lack of particle matter experiments. The results of sampling of particle
concentration can be enhanced in future experimental areas.
For the case study, in case 3 the air was equally supplied by the UDF diffuser and a swirl diffuser. Observing the
airflow on the side of the swirl, the air went flow back to the diffuser. This may be due to the improper placement
of the air supply inlet in the case of hybrid ventilation. It is possible to optimize air volume distribution ratios
between unidirectional and non­unidirectional diffusers in hybrid ventilation systems or incorporate more swirl
diffusers in combination with UDF diffusers to make sure the surrounding environment of the unidirectional
diffuser can achieve a good ventilation effect.
During the particle distribution research phase, this thesis focused solely on the study of 0.5 microns particles.
The human body naturally emits particles that vary in size from 0.5 to 5 microns [3]. Particles that are 0.5
microns in size tend to follow the flow of air in simulation results might be because of the light mass and
suspension remaining in the air because of buoyancy. Further research is required to investigate the behavior of
particles with different diameters and determine whether they are still influenced by air movement. Hence, to
better evaluate the results of the study in this thesis on ventilation methods, different particle diameters should
be taken into account.
In the phase of particle track, it was found that there is no further tracking of how many particles were trapped
in which plane. This is the limitation of this paper. At the same time, it needs to be known that for case 3, when
the particulate matter remained in the room was only 7% of the total release, the distribution between escape
and trap is very interesting to study. In addition, it is also necessary to combine the specific characteristics of
the pollutants, and whether traps definitely occur when they touch the table. The research will further improve
the development of hybrid ventilation applications in cleanrooms.
Furthermore, the current study did not simulate the sudden entry of a person into the room or the movement
of the occupants. Other locations of the pollution source were also not studied. All these factors would have
affected the ventilation efficiency of the studied cases.
In the results section, the analysis of the existing data does not provide conclusive evidence regarding specific air
distribution prone to the accumulation of particles. Exploring the commonalities between particle accumulation
and airflow movements across different cases could be a valuable direction for future research in order to identify
the particle accumulation position by air movement.
In the previous discussion, the recovery rate calculated in this thesis is not suitable for the evaluation of par­
ticulate removal efficiency under UDF diffuser. Because case 3 is always in a situation that is not heavily
contaminated compared to other cases. Therefore, there are limitations to looking at the decay in this area or
observing the specific particle removal capacity of the UDF diffuser. So if want to compare the contaminant
removal efficiency of UDF diffuser with a swirl or 4­way diffuser. It needs to set the same conditions for them
separately. For example, put a certain amount of particles in a limited space, and then observe the recovery rate
of the limited space in a period of time.

6
Conclusion
In this study, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) was employed to analyze a 64.8m3 pharmaceutical room
with an upward air supply and downward air exhaust. There were two dummies in the room to be the pollutant
source. Various scenarios involving different diffusers and different ACH were simulated. The k −ϵ method
of continuous term steady­state and discrete term transient is used to simulate air distribution and particle dis­
tribution. The simulation results can be verified with the experimental results. The main concluding remarks
are listed below:
• At an air change rate of 10, in Case 3, which utilized a hybrid ventilation system consisting of a UDF
diffuser and a swirl diffuser, the cleanroom achieved ISO7 cleanliness level. In Case 2, where a 4­way
diffuser was employed, and Case 1, using a swirl diffuser, both with the non­unidirectional flow, achieved
ISO8 cleanliness level.
• In Case 1, the cleanliness classification remained the same at ACH rates of 10, 15, and 20.
• With the increase of ACH, the contaminant removal efficiency increased. The air movement changed
from 10ACH to 20ACH. The overall particle concentration was slightly reduced by increasing ACH than
changing diffusers.
• When comparing the fully­mixing air pattern (Case 1 and Case 2), it was observed that the 4­way diffuser
exhibited superior performance in terms of contaminant control at the critical area. On the other hand,
the swirl diffuser demonstrated high efficiency in removing contaminants after particles stop releasing.
• The age of air changed with the different diffusers and different ACH. The age of the air is corresponding
with the cleanliness level.
• The AEE can be an indicator to examine the particle mixing status in the cleanroom. The PI value can
show the contaminant control at the critical area. The recovery rate presents the contaminant removal
efficiency in the NUDF cleanroom.
In conclusion, it was found that a hybrid ventilation mode can provide outstanding contaminant control ability in
critical area. This unique diffuser configuration enables the cleanroom to achieve the cleanliness levels that are
typically unattainable with conventional non­unidirectional flow systems at 10ACH. The results contribute to
optimizing airflow organization in cleanrooms and offer significant potential for energy savings. Additionally,
the study concludes that analyzing particle distribution through air distribution allows for a deeper understanding
of cleanroom contaminant control through ventilation. This provides new perspectives and quantifiable param­
eters for future research in the field, opening avenues for further investigations and advancements in cleanroom
design and operation.
