Abstract
This project investigated the chemical interactions of two fungal species, Penicillium exôÙ
pansum and Monilinia fructigena, which are the two most common and destructive apple
pathogens throughout Europe. Fungi were inoculated directly from infected apples, strain
isolated, and identified to species level via morphology and chemical profiling. Secondary
metabolite production was analyzed by UHPLCôÙDADôÙMS using a combination of massôÙ
guided automatic detection of known compounds and manual dereplication of major chroôÙ
matogram peaks. Chemical interactions of P. expansum and M. fructigena were studied
in a laboratory setting in order to gain insights into the natural role of fungal secondary
metabolism in the apple ecosystem. M. fructigena and P. expansum were cocultivated on
YES medium, and a semiôÙquantitative comparison of regions far and close to the other
fungus was carried out for each. The agar in the region of no growth between the two
fungi was also extracted and analyzed. In all cocultures, a region of orange pigmentation
with minimal sporulation developed in the area of P. expansum close to M. fructigena.
In experiments where M. fructigena was inoculated with a one week head start (HS) relaôÙ
tive to P. expansum, M. fructigena grew more robustly than P. expansum. Fewer known
Monilinia sp. compounds were found in regions close to P. expansum compared to the far
region, however, one of the known compounds, 4ôÙhydroxybenzoic acid, which is known
to be antiôÙmicrobial [1ã3], was upôÙregulated. P. expansum and M. fructigena were also
inoculated at the same time at distances of 4 cm, 2 cm, and 1 cm and incubated for two
weeks. At distances of 2 cm and 1 cm, M. fructigena was nearly completely overtaken by
P. expansum. At 4 cm, the fungi grew more equally, but compared to the M. fructigena
HS coculture, growth of P. expansum was more robust, with minimal space of no growth
left between the fungi. Significantly more known Monilinia sp. compounds were detected
in the region of M. fructigena close to P. expansum compared to the region far from P.
expansum on the nonôÙHS plate.
The same P. expansum compounds were found as major peaks in plugs taken from P.
expansum in both the HS and nonôÙHS cocultures. Some of these compounds are likely
involved in or coôÙoccurrent with sporulation, as they were found as major peaks in sporuôÙ
lating areas of P. expansum monoculture, but not in nonôÙsporulating orange regions. In
the HS coculture, these compounds were both upôÙ and downôÙregulated in the region of
P. expansum close to M. fructigena, while in the nonôÙHS coculture, they were all downôÙ
regulated. This was consistent with observations that the nonôÙsporulating orange region
was significantly larger in the nonôÙHS coculture.
In both monoôÙ and cocutures, many unknown compounds were detected, especially in
the case of M. fructigena. Interestingly, many of these unknown M. fructigena compounds
are suspected to contain nitrogen, while all currently known Monilinia sp. compounds are
nitrogenôÙfree.

known compound, M.U.1, and elucidate its structure using NMR. M. fructigena was streaked
onto 300 PDA plates, and after a two week incubation, plate contents were extracted with
acetone. Next, a liquidôÙliquid extraction with ethyl acetate was carried out, followed by a
partition with nôÙheptane. The resulting extract was fractionated via flash chromatography
on a NP silica column. Fractions were combined based on TLC, and M.U.1 was purified
via semiôÙpreperative HPLC equipped with a RPôÙC18 column. OneôÙ and twoôÙdimensional
NMR experiments were performed on 1.5 mg of purified M.U.1. Based on mass, M.U.1
was predicted to have the molecular formula: C22H20N2O7. The 1H NMR spectra conôÙ

the 13C, HSQC, and HMBC spectra confirmed the presence of 22 carbons. A complete
structure is not yet determined, however, resulting spectra suggest that the compound has
a diketopiperazine backbone with two symmetrical monoôÙsubstituted aromatic rings, two
carboxylic acid groups, and two methylene groups. BioôÙassays of purified M.U.1 against
P. expansum are proposed as a future experiment.
Overall, this project has demonstrated that many known bioactive secondary metabolites
produced by both P. expansum and M. fructigena are either downôÙ or upôÙregulated due
to their cocultivation, clearly indicating that the play key roles in the chemical ecology of
these appleôÙassociated fungi.

Introduction
1.1
Microorganisms
Modern human lifestyle would not be possible without microorganisms, as they have conôÙ
tributed to industrial science for over 100 years. Microorganisms include bacteria, fungi,
protists and archae, which exist in all types of environments ã even extreme habitats
such as arid deserts and deep sea thermal vents [8]. They make up approximately 60%
of biomass on Earth and over 90% of the cells present in a human body [9]. Additionally,
they are important for medicine and other industries, as they produce economically imôÙ
portant compounds known as secondary metabolites (SMs), including antibiotics, other
pharmaceutically active compounds, toxins, pesticides, and animal and plant growth facôÙ
tors.
Fungi are unique eukaryotic microorganisms that play an important role in degradation of
organic matter. Fungi produce a huge variety of SMs, however very little is known about
the role of SMs in natural systems, particularly their role in fungalôÙfungal interactions.
1.2
Classification of Life on Earth
1.2.1
Taxonomy and The Tree of Life
Fossil records show terrestrial life dating back to 3.5 ôñ 109 years ago. Over the last 3.5
billion years (approximately a quarter of the age of the universe) organisms have evolved
and adapted to almost all environments to provide the diversity of Earth as we see it today.
Evolutionary relationships between all organisms are organized using taxonomy, which
functions to describe, identify, classify, and name all living things. Based on morphoôÙ
logical and molecular characteristics, organisms are grouped into taxa of specific rank ã
species, which are grouped into genera, and then higher taxa: families, orders, classes,
phyla, kingdoms, and domains. In phylogenetic taxonomy, organisms are classified by
clades, which are based on evolutionary grouping by ancestral traits, which can be used
to separate and categorize taxa into unranked groups, creating cladograms. The tree of
life describes the evolutionary relationship of all organisms (Fig. 1.1) [10, 11].
An early attempt at constructing a phylogenetic classification was made by Ernst Haekel
in 1866, where organisms were divided into three categories: the Plantae, the Animalia
and the Protista, or microorganisms.
1.2.2
The Three Domains
Constructing an accurate tree of life required the development of molecular techniques
to determine evolutionary relationships of presentôÙday organisms. All organisms possess
genes encoding small subunit rRNA (SSUrRNA), which have a mosaic of conservation
patterns with rapidly evolving regions interspaced with almost invariant regions [12, 13].
This led to the recognition of three primary lines of evolutionary descent called domains
ã the Eubacteria, Archaea, and Eukaryota (Fig. 1.2). Eukaryota contains all eukaryotic
organisms [14].
Animals, plants, and fungi belong to the domain Eukaryota. Prokaryotes and eukaryotes
are distinguished by major differences in cell structure and genome organization: eukaryôÙ
otic cells have a membraneôÙbound nucleus, an extensive and complex endomembrane
system, and a cytoskeleton, while prokaryotes do not. The prokaryotic genome is smaller
(600 kb to 9.5 Mb) and is organized in a circular DNA molecule. In eukaryotes, DNA is

1.3
Fungi
Fungi are an integral component of terrestrial habitats and have tremendous value in
food, biotechnology, and pharmaceutical industries. They are categorized as one of the
five eukaryotic kingdoms, and are the great recyclers of organic matter in ecosystems.
Filamentous fungi secrete a variety of enzymes to externally digest and absorb nutrients,
which makes them ideal for industrial enzymatic production. Fungi produce a variety of
species specific SMs to interact and compete with other organisms. Fungal SMs are a
source of novel pharmaceuticals, such as cyclosporine and lovastatin, which have great
value to human health [16]. Additionally, many fungi are pathogenic to plants and animals.
1.3.1
The 5th Kingdom
For many decades, living things were classified as either a plant or animal, and fungi
were grouped with bacteria and algae in a group called the Thallophyta that formed part
of the kingdom Plantae. By the 1950s fungi were still classified in the Plant Kingdom and
were separated into four classes: the Phycomycetes, Ascomycetes, Basidiomycetes, and
Deuteromycetes [14]. By the 1970s, living organisms were classified by a system of five
kingdoms: bacteria, plants, animals, fungi, and protists. Fungi are therefore known to be
distinct from plants, comprising a unique kingdom of eukaryotic organisms.
Kingdom Fungi is recognized as one of the oldest and largest clades. Approximately
800 to 900 million years ago, fungi diverged from a common ancestor shared with aniôÙ
mals, meaning fungi are in fact more closely related to animals than plants [14, 17ã20].

Diversity
A previous conservative estimate of the number of fungal species on earth was 1.5 milôÙ
lion ã an estimate made by comparing amounts of fungal species and species of vascular
plants for certain regions. For example, in the British Isles there are about six times more
species of fungi than species of vascular plants. Extrapolating this ratio to the 270,000

to this old estimate have been made based on discovery of regions with extreme biodiôÙ

Developments in molecular phylogeny have revealed many cryptic species (species recôÙ

estimated to be 11.7 to 13.2 million fungal species on earth [21, 22]. Only 120,000 fungal
species have been isolated or described, meaning that the vast majority of fungi are likely
undiscovered or not described [17].
Ascomycota
The three main fungal phyla are Ascomycota, Basidiomycota, and Zygomycota (Fig. 1.4).
Ascomycota is the largest phylum, comprising about 65,000 species in 6400 genera, and
is over twice the size of the second largest phylum, the Basidiomycota (about 32,000
species in 1600 genera). Together these two phyla make up the subkingdom Dikarya.

Sporulation and Growth
Fungi are prevalent across diverse ecosystems ã their ecological success in terrestrial enôÙ
vironments is related to their ability to produce mass amounts of asexual spores. Spores
contain genetic information and can be dispersed in a variety of mechanisms, such as beôÙ
ing carried by wind or animals. If spores settle on an appropriate substrate under favorable
environmental conditions, which depend on factors such as temperature and humidity,
spores germinate to form a new fungal mycelium. Upon germination, the spore swells
in diameter (mainly due to water uptake) and metabolic activity (i.e. synthesis of DNA,
RNA, and proteins) increases markedly. Next, one or more germ tubes, or young hyphal
tips, emerge and extend away from the spore in a polarized manner (Fig. 1.6a). Rate
of extension varies between species, and depends on environmental conditions such as
temperature, pH, and nutrient availability. As the hyphae continue to grow, they form latôÙ
eral branches, which results in the characteristic ãmapôÙlikeã morphology of the mycelium
(Fig. 1.6b) [14].

Different regions of a fungal colony are at different physiological ages. The mycelium
develops and changes with age, and different parts of the colony therefore may exhibit
different phenotypes. The outermost mycelia is the youngest, and this region is often
exploratory, with rapid extension and growth. Fungi typically explore their habitat with
rapidly growing, sparsely branched hyphae, and when a nutrient source is found, extenôÙ
sion decreases and branching increases. This forms a highly branched network which
efficiently exploits the nutrient source. The center of the colony is oldest, and as nutrients
become depleted and metabolic products accumulate, spore production may be induced
and fruiting structures formed [14, 25, 26].
1.3.3
Fungi in Ecosystems
In all terrestrial ecosystems, filamentous fungi carry out the essential function of nutrient
recycling and breakdown of complex molecules. Fungi that form symbiotic relationships
with plants (mycchorizal fungi) are also a fundamental component of terrestrial ecosysôÙ
tems [28]. The filamentous nature of fungi allows them to colonize substrates rapidly and
grow away from nutrient poor areas, meaning they can be highly successful in their natural
environment. This success is exhibited by the extreme diversity of fungal species, secôÙ
ond only to insects, and their distribution in basically every habitat on Earth ã on average,
approximately 1 km of mycelium is found per gram of soil [14, 28]. Fungi obtain nutrients
via external digestion of nutrient substrates, which is a characteristic specific to Kingdom
Fungi. They secrete enzymes to break down substrates such as wood, dead animals, celôÙ
lulose, and tannins in soil. They also contribute to food webs, as mycelium is consumed
by small organisms, and fruiting bodies are major food source for some mammals [14].
1.3.4
AppleôÙInfecting Fungi
Many fungi are pathogenic to animals and plants, and thus contribute to disease in huôÙ
mans as well as crop damage and loss. Fungal infection of fruit is prevalent around the
world and causes fruitôÙrot diseases which pose a threat to industries and food security.
In general, crops are especially susceptible to fungal infection in the time after harvest.
Rate of loss of fresh fruit and vegetables post harvest is estimated at 25% in developed
countries, and as high as 50% in developing countries [29].
Apples and processed apple products are consumed in high levels throughout most counôÙ
tries, making up an industry worth billions of USD, according to the U.S. Apple Association
[30]. There are many different species of apples, which differ in molecular composition
and produce an array of SMs.

PostôÙharvest infection poses a threat to the apple industry by degrading product quality,
contributing to loss of food and sales, and increasing likelihood of mycotoxin consumption
by consumers. Currently, postôÙharvest fungicides are used to combat infection, with four
currently registered for fungal disease management. However, the emergence of fungiôÙ

pathogen species have been reported to cause decay in apples during storage. Several
genera are most problematic, including Penicillium, Geotrichum, Rhizopus, Phytophthora,
Monilinia and Botrytis [29, 33].

1.4
Secondary Metabolism
Over hundreds of millions of years, microorganisms have evolved the cellular machinery
to produce SMs to communicate, compete, and defend. SMs, also referred to as natural
products, are lowôÙmolecular mass molecules that are nonôÙessential for basic growth, deôÙ
velopment, and reproduction of the producing organism. As a general definition, primary
metabolites describe a metabolic intermediate or product found in most living systems that
is required for survival and biosynthesized by a limited number of biochemical pathways.
SMs describe a metabolic intermediate or product, found in restricted taxonomic groups,
that is nonôÙessential and is biosynthesized from one or more compounds produced by
primary metabolism [47, 48]. Since SMs are nonôÙessential and are energetically taxing
to produce, an organism may only synthesize a given SM under specific environmental
conditions.
There is enormous chemodiversity among SMs, and they possess a variety of functional
roles in cellular processes such as transcription, development, and communication. Many
SMs are bioactive, and have important applications as pharmaceuticals. A famous exôÙ
ample of this is the use of penicillin, derived from P. chrysogenum, as the first broadôÙ
spectrum antibiotic [49]. Often, the functional role that SMs play in ecosystems is not
fully understood, but they may confer important ecological advantages, such as eliminatôÙ
ing competing microbial species, chemical communication, habitat defence, and growth
inhibition of competing organisms [35, 50]. Microorganims, such as bacteria and fungi,
produce a wide range of natural products, and the most prolific produces are typically
those organisms which are competition selected and interact with complex and diverse
ecosystems [51]. For example, soilôÙdwelling bacteria and fungi live in a complex ecosysôÙ
tem where they compete and interact with a variety of organisms ranging from bacteria,
fungi and algae to protozoa and metazoa, and therefore produce a plethora of SMs [50,
51].
Secondary metabolism consists of a relatively small number of enzyme driven processes
which convert specific intermediates of primary metabolism into a wide range of products.
Complexes of enzymes assemble the core structure from simple building blocks, while
tailoring enzymes are responsible for carrying out further modifications after synthesis of
the core structure [52]. The genes encoding these enzymes are clustered together in the
genome of the producing organism along with genes encoding transport and regulatory
proteins ã transport proteins are particularly important for export of SMs which are harmful
to the producing organism, for example production of antiôÙfungal compounds by a fungus.
Regulatory proteins are responsible for regulating activation of the biosynthetic machinery
so that energy is only used on SM production under specific conditions [52]. Collectively,
the genes encoding the proteins needed to produce a SM are referred to as a biosynthetic
gene cluster (BGC). The later stages of secondary metabolism vary significantly between
organisms, meaning that production of a given SM may have a narrow species distribution.
Every organism has its own characteristic set of SMs produced under various conditions.
Some of these SMs may be shared across closely related organisms or produced by
totally unrelated organisms [47].

Fungal Secondary Metabolism
Secondary metabolism is a common feature of fungi. Over one third of microorganismôÙ
derived natural products are of fungal origin [51, 58]. Fungi can produce an impressive
variety of SMs, though most fungal SMs are NRPs and/or PKS derived. Genome mining
efforts indicate that their ability to produce natural products has been underestimated, as
many of BGCs encoding the enzymatic machinery needed to build a given SM are silent
under standard cultivation conditions [49].
In nature, fungal SMs serve a variety of functions: some are used for communication,
some are used for defence or competition for resources, such as antibacterial and antiôÙ
fungal compounds. For example, a given SM may inhibit growth or sporulation in another
fungus. Many SMs are involved in pathogenicity [59].

Analytical Challenges of Metabolomics
The metabolome describes all metabolites produced by an organism at a given time under
certain conditions. Monitoring and measuring the metabolome of an organism presents
an analytical challenge due to size and complexity [77].
Another challenge is chemical diversity. Polarity of metabolites can vary greatly ã from
extremely nonôÙpolar lipids to polar ions. Additionally, many different functional groups are
possible ã alcohols, amines, organic acids, aromatic alcohols, esters, aldehydes, ethers,
phosphates (etc.). Some metabolic subôÙgroups are so large and important that they have
their own classification such as the lipidome and the glycome.
Isomers ã different molecules with the same molecular formula ã present a challenge,
particularly when interpreting mass spectrometry (MS) data. Since the metabolome genôÙ
erally encompasses a relatively narrow band of molecular weights (most metabolites have
molecular weights below 1,000 Da), numerous isomers of a given compound may be
present. Compound chirality is a big challenge as enantiomers have identical physicoôÙ
chemical properties. Another challenge is low concentration: physiological concentrations
of SMs are typically in the millimolar to picomolar levels, making detection difficult [78].
Some analytical methods, such as nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy,
provide important structural information, but require concentrations which greatly exceed
those typical to most metabolites. For low concentration metabolites, a sequential apôÙ
proach of concentration, separation, and detection methods are used.
The metabolome is transient nature: biochemical pathways are sensitive to changes and
environmental stressors such as changes in pH, temperature, and concentration of other
compounds, possibly from another microorganism. This means that a metabolome meaôÙ
sured in a certain place and time will not be the same as another place and time, making
it difficult to fully characterize a biological system.

1.7
Chromatography
In chromatography, sample components are separated based on relative differences in
distribution between a stationary phase and a mobile phase. In gas chromatography
(GC), which is used for separation of small, volatile compounds, an inert gas comprises
the mobile phase. Separation is primarily based on interaction of sample molecules with
the stationary phase in GC. In liquid chromatography (LC), sample constituents interact
with both the mobile and stationary phase, meaning there is more room for variation and
optimization. LC is used for semiôÙvolatile and nonôÙvolatile compounds, and is primarily
used in the separation of SMs [80].
1.7.1

LC used extensively in metabolomics and in quality control of pharmaceuticals. In LC,

Silica (Fig. 1.21a) forms the bases of most stationary phases in LC, and has large surface

Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) separates sample constituents based on molecular

pores of an extremely small size. Separation occurs when different analyte molecules

the column with low to no pore penetration, and thus are retained for a shorter time. Small
molecules are transported by the mobile phase into the pores of the stationary phase, and
travel a long path length through the pores, resulting in a longer RT. Therefore, sample
constituents are separated on the bases of decreasing molecular size [80].

1.8.3
Ion Mobility Separation
Ion mobility separation (IMS) is a gas phase electrophoretic analytical technique, first
introduced in 1970 under the name plasma chromatography. IMS provides a means for
separation of ions of interest via a linear electric field applied to a drift tube containing a
buffer gas [91, 92]. The buffer gas resists ion progression through the tube, counteracting
the acceleration of the ions in the applied electric fields, resulting in ions which travel at
their terminal velocity proportional to the inverse their collision cross section (CCS). This
parameter, CCS, describes the orientational average of the collision rate, and is smaller for
more compact, spherical molecules in comparison to more extended molecules (planar,
extended chains, helices, etc.). Therefore, the CCS provides information about the overall
shape of the molecule, and has been used, for instance, to determine whether carbon
clusters exist as chains, rings, planar graphiteôÙlike sheets, or fullerenes [92].
The basic components of an IMS spectrometer consist of: a sample inlet system, an

33
==PAGE:42==
ionizer, a reaction region, an ion gate or gates, a drift region, and an ion collector. Ion
identifications can be obtained by coupling IMS to MS [91].
IMS is useful in enhancing the separation of components found in complex mixture, such
as biological samples, especially when coupled to other separation techniques such as
LC. The separation time of IMS (milliseconds) interfaces well between that of LC (secôÙ
onds to minutes) and MS (microsecond timescale). By coupling IMS with MS, a range of
separation parameters can be influenced, providing enhanced peak capacity, reduction
in total analysis time, reduction of chemical noise, decreased detection limit, and valuôÙ
able structural information [92]. Because IMS is a gas phase separation technique, it is
orders of magnitude faster than LC. However, the overall sensitivity of IMSôÙMS is limited
by efficiency of the interface between the mobility and the mass spectrometers [78].

MALDIôÙImaging MS
Matrix assisted laser desorption/ionization imaging mass spectrometry (MALDIôÙIMS) is a
powerful analytical tool in natural product research used to determine the spatial distriôÙ
bution of metabolites within a biological sample. MALDIôÙIMS combines the throughput
capabilities of MS, where up to hundreds of different ions can be measured simultaneôÙ
ously, with a surface sampling process which enables rapid twoôÙdimensional mapping
and imaging of metabolite content [93].
MALDI is a soft ionization technique where ions are generated using a laser. MALDI samôÙ
ples must be very thin, typically 5ã20 ö¥m thick, and flat. The sample is sprayed with a
MALDI matrix composed of small, acidic molecules, which are subsequently evaporated
to give a dry spot of fine crystals composed of matrix and analyte. Laser bombardment
is directed to this spot and matrix molecules absorb the laser energy, which is then transôÙ
ferred from the matrix to analyte molecules resulting in desorption into the gas phase and
ionization. In this way, the sample is ionized, but also protected from the disruptive enôÙ
ergy of the laser. Charged analyte molecules are accelerated through an electrostatic
field, then drift through a fieldôÙfree mass analyzer flight tube until they reach the mass
spectrometerãs detector. Ions are separated according to TOF, which is proportional to
m/z.
Spectra are acquired at discrete locations on the sample surface according to a predefined
rectangular x,y grid. Ion intensity is plotted as a function of these x,y coordinates on
the sample, generating an image of that signal over the sample surface. In this way,
twoôÙdimensional distribution maps of many different ions in the sample are generated,
meaning that the spacial distribution of metabolites of interest can be seen [85, 94, 95].
Resolving the spatial distribution of SMs helps to understand their biological function [93].
For example, the location of a metabolite of interest within a fungal coculture at a given
point in time may provide important information about how that metabolite is involved in
interactions with the other fungus. If a given metabolite known to be produced by one
of the fungi is found in the agar between the two fungi, it suggests that the metabolite is
secreted, and therefore may be involved in chemical interactions with the other fungus.
MALDIôÙIMS imaging has many advantages over other imaging methods commonly used
for visualizing and identifying molecular targets (chemical stains, immunohistochemical
tags, radiolabels, etc.). These methods are limited in throughput and specificity, and
typically require prior knowledge of target molecule chemistry, which limits their utility in
molecular discovery. In contrast, MALDIôÙMS is label free ã it does not require moleculeôÙ
specific tags or chemical modifications to enable detection. No prior knowledge of the
sample metabolome is required, and hundreds of unknown compounds can be obtained

Dereplication
Characterizing SMs requires dereplication of LCôÙMS data to identify known metabolites
from databases and compound libraries. LCôÙDADôÙMS data provides a total ion chroôÙ
matogram (TIC), which shows a sum of intensities for all ion peaks for a single scan. A
base peak chromatogram (BPC) can be extracted from the TIC, which shows the most
intense ion for each scan. If the exact monoisotopic mass of a compound is known, an
extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) can be generated. The monoisotopic mass of a known
compound can be found in databases, such as Reaxys, or calculated from molecular forôÙ
mula using certain MS analysis software, such as Isotopic Patters (IsoPat) and the ãMass
Calculatorã function in Agilent MassHunter. A target list of known SMs and their monoisoôÙ
topic masses can be used to automatically detect these compounds and generate EICs
for each compound that is found. If two compounds have similar retention, they may apôÙ
pear as highly overlapping peaks in a chromatogram. By generating a EIC for each ion
within an overlapping peak, it can be seen which ion corresponds to which part of the
peak, a process known as deconvolution.
1.9.1
Retention Time
RT can provide insights about the physicochemical properties of a compound, such as
relative polarity. If standard run conditions are used on a given instrument, RT may be
used to detect compounds from a database obtained with the same standard conditions.
This is only possibly if the same method is used in the collection of library data, and RT
alone is not enough to definitively identify a compound, mass spectra must also be used.
Selecting a peak from the chromatogram will give the mass spectrum for the compound(s)
found within that peak.
1.9.2

High resolution instruments capable of determining m/z values up to four decimal places
are essential for dereplication. Unlike nominal masses, there are a limited number of
formulas that correspond with an exact mass. [86].

2.2.2
Extraction of SMs
Acetone Extraction
The fungus and agar were extracted twice with acetone. First, 3.0 L of acetone was
added to each flask (enough to completely cover fungal material) and placed on a shaker
for 1 hour. Next, the fungus/agar was vacuum filtered. Acetone was removed from the
resulting filtrate via rotary evaporation, and the remaining liquid was poured into a large
beaker and stirred overnight to remove any excess acetone.
Remaining solid from the first extraction was transferred back into the 5 L Erlenmeyer
flasks, 3.0 L of acetone were added, and the extraction was left overnight. The extract
was then vacuum filtered. The resulting filtrate was placed on the rotary evaporator, then
combined with the product of the first extraction and left to stir overnight.
LiquidôÙLiquid Extraction with EA
The product of the acetone extraction was next partitioned EA. After acidifying to pH =
2.8, the acetone extraction product was added to a seperatory funnel along with an equal
ratio of EA and shaken. The aqueous layer was drained from the bottom of the seperatory
funnel, and the organic layer was poured off the top of the separatory funnel. The aqueous
material was then extracted again with EA, and the resulting organic layer was combined
with the first extraction. EA was removed via rotary evaporation and the product was
transferred to a scintillation vial using EA and dried under nitrogen.
nôÙHeptane partition
A partition with nôÙheptane was carried out to remove lipids. The extract was dissolved
in 450 mL MeOH and 5 mL DI H20 and added to a separatory funnel along with 500 mL
nôÙheptane. The separatory funnel was shaken, and the nôÙheptane layer was removed.
The MeOH/H20 layer was transferred to a roundôÙbottom flask and placed on the rotary
evaporator, then transferred to a scintillation vial using MeOH and dried under nitrogen.

3.7
Overall Conclusions
It can be confidently said that M. fructigena and P. expansum were isolated from moldy
apples and identified to species level. In coculture, many compounds ã both known SMs
and unknowns ã were upôÙ or downôÙ regulated in response to the other fungus, suggesting
that they may be involved in the chemical interactions of these appleôÙassociated fungi.
Spacial distribution of known metabolites in the HS and nonôÙHS cocultures indicate that
inoculation time plays a role in intensity of chemical response to the other fungus, parôÙ
ticularly in the case of M. fructigena, which is challenged by slower growth relative to P.
expansum on YES substrate. In the HS coculture, M. fructigena growth exceeded that of
P. expansum, and less Monilinia sp. compounds were detected in the region close to P.
expansum relative to the region far from P. expansum. This suggests that when given a
one week HS, M. fructigena is not as challenged by P. expansum, and a robust response
of chemical defense is less necessary.
When inoculated at the same time, M. fructigena appears to be under significantly more
pressure, indicated by plate appearance. In the nonôÙHS coculture, more Monilinia sp.
compounds were detected from M. fructigena plugs, and a greater number of these comôÙ
pounds was detected in the region close to P. expansum relative to the region far from P.
expansum. Additionally, more Monilinia sp. compounds were detected in the the regions
of P. expansum close to M. fructigena compared to the region far from M. fructigena on
the nonôÙHS plate, indicating that these compounds may be secreted by M. fructigena. In
the HS coculture, the same number of M. fructigena compounds were detected in both reôÙ
gions of P. expansum close to and far from M. fructigena. This suggests that the pressure
of inoculation at the same time results in a more robust chemical response from M. fructiôÙ
gena. It was observed that in both HS and nonôÙHS cocultures, 4ôÙhydroxybenzoic acid,
a Monilinia sp. metabolite with reported antiôÙfungal properties [1ã3], was upôÙregulated in
regions of M. fructigena close to P. expansum.
Several compounds were found in the agar between M. fructigena and P. expansum.
Patulin, M.U.1, 4ôÙhydroxybenzoic acid, chloromonilinic acid B, and chloromonilicin were
detected between the fungi in both the HS and nonôÙHS cocultures, and chloromonilinic
acid A was detected only in the agar between the fungi in the nonôÙHS coculture. Patulin, a
wellôÙstudied P. expansum SM, is known to be antibacterial, and is not reported to be antiôÙ
fungal [107]. Chloromonilicin, chloromonilinic acids A and B, and 4ôÙhydroxybenzoic acid
are known Monilinia sp metabolites. In addition to 4ôÙhydroxybenzoic acid, chloromonilicin
has reported antiôÙfungal properties [5ã7], while chloromonilinic acid B has reported phyôÙ
totoxic and insecticidal properties [6].
M.U.1, an unknown compound suspected to be produced and secreted by M. fructigena
was extracted from a largeôÙscale cultivation of M. fructigena on PDA, isolated and purified,
and partially elucidated by NMR. As it is consistently found in the agar between M. fructiôÙ
gena and P. expansum, this compound is an interesting candidate for future bioôÙassay
experiments.
Known P. expansum and Monilinia sp. metabolites and several mycotoxins were comôÙ
piled into a target list and used to automatically detect compounds based on mass. IdenôÙ
tification based on mass is not sufficient to confirm presence of that compound, as comôÙ
pounds with the same constitutional formula are indistinguishable. In the case of P. expanôÙ
sum, massôÙbased detection was supported by matches in a database of MS/MS spectra.
Therefore, detection of P. expansum compounds is more likely accurate, however, to be
definitively sure, a standard must be run, and RT and MS/MS compared.
