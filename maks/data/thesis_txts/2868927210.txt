Abstract
Listening in noisy environments can be fatiguing, particularly for individuals with hearing impair-
ments. Compared to normal hearing listeners, people with hearing impairments may expend
additional cognitive resources to comprehend and attend to stimuli in degraded auditory condi-
tions. To assess in which situations listening effort may be increased and how it can be reduced,
it is essential to identify methods for evaluating listening effort. One promising approach for ob-
jective measurements of listening effort is electroencephalography (EEG). However, most EEG
studies have limited ecological validity due to the use of stationary setups to minimize muscular
artifacts. Therefore, this study aimed to estimate listening effort in more ecological listening
scenarios using mobile EEG.
Mobile EEG was recorded for 15 normal-hearing participants while they listened to audiobook
excerpts in a simulated living room environment in the presence of background noise. The
signal-to-noise ratio was varied during the test, and participants switched between sitting and
standing positions. The assessment included questionnaires regarding subjectively perceived
listening effort, and content comprehension to evaluate cognitive performance. Local connec-
tivity and power, the existing methods used in the literature to estimate listening effort, were
extracted from the EEG data and compared to subjective ratings, performance, and previous
data from controlled setups.
Results indicated that subjectively perceived listening effort decreased linearly with increasing
SNR and that cognitive performance, measured by questionnaire accuracy, exhibited a more
quadratic increase with SNR, rather than a linear one. In addition, statistical trends of SNR
effects on local connectivity and power were observed in various brain regions. However, a
non-linear relationship between task difficulty (SNR) and local connectivity was only found for
local connectivity in the parietal region in the alpha frequency band, which is assumed to be
related to listening effort. Furthermore, only local connectivity in the parietal alpha band was
shown to be linearly related to subjectively perceived listening effort. No significant effect of body
position on local connectivity or power was found, suggesting successful artifact reduction in
the EEG data.
This study demonstrates the potential of local connectivity as an objective indicator of listening
effort in more ecological environments using mobile EEG. Future research should explore more
dynamic and realistic conditions, such as walking or audiovisual stimulation.
Keywords: listening effort, mobile EEG, phase synchrony, local connectivity

Introduction
Nowadays, people are increasingly exposed to complex listening situations where background
noise might be present, such as sitting in a café while attending to a conversation, conducting
a meeting in an open office environment, or listening to an audiobook on the train while com-
muting. For instance, a study found that noise levels in cafés and restaurants often exceed
70 decibels, making it difficult to concentrate and to maintain conversations (Legge et al., n.d.).
What these situations have in common is that the listener is intending to attend to a target signal
while masked by competing speech or noise signals. Sustained listening under such conditions
increases listening effort and may lead to fatigue. The reason for this is that the input stimulus
is degraded due to the masking of the target signal and the listener will have to expend more
cognitive resources to segregate target and noise (McGarrigle et al., 2014; Pichora-Fuller et al.,
2016). With increasing demand, the listener expends more energy on retrieving information. If
the listening situation becomes too difficult, the listener might give up due to lower motivation
(Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016). To assess in which situations and why listening effort may be in-
creased, when people tend to give up and how listening effort can be reduced to free up people’s
cognitive demand to minimize negative effects of high demand such as fatigue, it is essential to
evaluate listening effort and methods for its assessment. This is even more crucial for people
with hearing impairment as their neural representation of the input signal is already degraded
which can lead to even more fatigue (e.g. Wang et al., 2018, Hornsby, 2013) and even less
available cognitive resources for other cognitive tasks (Kramer et al., 2006; Pichora-Fuller et al.,
2016; Rönnberg et al., 2013).
However, listening effort is rather a construct than a physiological parameter. That is why it is
difficult to measure and why there is a lot of ongoing research towards it. For example, there
have been found only weak correlations between physiological and subjective measures due to
the different nature of these methods (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024). Nevertheless, listening effort
is important for research but also for industry to understand listeners’ needs and difficulties in
everyday life. That is why Oticon is interested in the project presented here. For many years,
Oticon has tested new technology not only on purely audiological outcomes, such as speech
intelligibility, but also on cognitive outcomes such as listening effort to improve people’s auditory
and cognitive processing.
Listening effort has been studied with a variety of subjective and objective methods. The latter is
particularly important as objective measures provide more comparable data across subjects and
studies as the self-perceived effort may differ between subjects, be dependent on the task diffi-
culty and it can be also based on different criteria such as the subject’s performance (McGarrigle
et al., 2014). Furthermore, they offer an opportunity to get data from people who are not able to
answer subjectively, for example because of age or disabilities such as cognitive decline. There
are different approaches to measure listening effort objectively using for example physiological
measures like pupillometry, heart rate or skin conductance (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024), as well

as neurophysiologically by looking at how brain activity is modulated by differences in listening
effort (Baboukani et al., 2022). Based on findings from Dimitrijevic et al. (2019), Fiedler et al.
(2021), and Seifi Ala et al. (2020) and Wisniewski et al. (2021), Baboukani et al. (2022) highlight
electroencephalography (EEG) as a promising measure to assess listening effort because of
its non-invasive nature and high temporal resolution.
There are different ways to estimate listening effort correlates from EEG. They include, among
others, time-frequency analysis regarding alpha- (e.g. Seifi Ala et al., 2020, Fiedler et al., 2021)
and theta band power (Wisniewski et al., 2015, Wisniewski et al., 2018), as well as functional
connectivity (Baboukani et al., 2019, Baboukani et al., 2021, Baboukani et al., 2022), which
refers to statistical dependencies between neurophysiological data. One way to assess func-
tional connectivity is to measure phase synchrony, indicating neural synchrony and interactions
across and within different brain networks, between scalp level electrodes (Baboukani et al.,
2022; Sanqing Hu et al., 2010). One promising approach to estimate correlates of listening
effort from local connectivity, which is defined as the functional connectivity in a specific region
of interest, was developed and proposed by Baboukani et al. (2019). They used a new mul-
tivariate measure of phase synchrony called circular omega complexity (COC). This measure
has only been studied before in a laboratory environment designed to minimize EEG artifacts.
Therefore, one of the interests of this thesis is to evaluate if COC can still serve as a good esti-
mator of listening effort when EEG is measured in less-controlled environments, as it would be
in an real-world situations where EEG data is more prone to artifacts.
EEG’s sensitivity to artifacts, such as muscular activity (Reis et al., 2014), has traditionally lim-
ited its use to stationary settings where participants remain still to minimize noise. Traditional
wired EEG setups further restrict mobility, making real-life applications challenging. While stud-
ies (e.g.,Damian et al., 2015, Reiser et al., 2021, Straetmans et al., 2021) have explored mobile
EEG, its potential for estimating listening effort through alpha power or phase synchrony remains
underexplored. Advancing mobile EEG for such purposes would enable more ecologically valid
measurements, bridging the gap between laboratory research and real-world applications, with
significant industry benefits, such as identifying customer’s needs and their difficulties in ev-
eryday life. Therefore, this project includes the development of a test set-up in which listening
effort can be studied under more realistic conditions. This is valuable for Oticon, as such a test
set-up can contribute to the development of new hearing aid technology.
The goal of this project is to estimate listening effort using the COC measure and a mobile
EEG set-up in two different body positions to better resemble more realistic listening conditions.
The study builds on a recent, unpublished experiment similar to Baboukani et al. (2022) and
examines the impact of a mobile EEG and less-controlled environments on COC measures.
By comparing phase-based and amplitude-based EEG measures, this project seeks to explore
whether these measures provide differing insights into listening effort estimation using EEG
signals.
To summarize, the project presented in this report aims to explore whether we can also estimate

Scientific Background
This chapter provides an introduction to EEG (see section 2.2) and listening effort (see sec-
tion 2.1), as these measures are investigated in the thesis to offer some background knowledge
necessary for understanding the content. Additionally, the relationship between EEG and lis-
tening effort is explained in section 2.3. This section also presents the advantages of EEG over
other listening effort measures such as pupillometry. In the final part of this section, the results
of an unpublished study are reported to compare them to the results obtained in this project.
2.1
Listening effort
This chapter introduces the concept of listening effort, defines it and provides an overview about
different measures of listening effort.
2.1.1
Listening effort definition
Listening in certain environments, such as very noisy ones, can be perceived as effortful, which
may be indicated by symptoms of fatigue (Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016). Listening effort typically
arises in the context of speech understanding, especially when auditory or cognitive processing
is limited. This is often the case for individuals with hearing impairment, who must expend
additional cognitive resources to focus on the target speech signal (Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016,
McGarrigle et al., 2014). Pichora-Fuller et al. (2016, p.10) define listening effort as the “mental
effort as the deliberate allocation of mental resources to overcome obstacles in goal pursuit
when carrying out a task ”. From this, it can be concluded that the more cognitive processing is
involved, the more cognitive resources have to be allocated, leading to a higher perception of
listening effort (Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016).
This concept is based on the “Capacity Model of Attention” by Kahneman (1973), which posits
that mental capacity is limited and that attention is therefore allocated to different processes.
This is why it is assumed that mental capacity is distributed between cognitive processing and
listening during effortful situations and the simultaneous execution of cognitive tasks (Pichora-
Fuller et al., 2016). How listening effort and cognitive demand are linked is further explained in
the next section 2.1.2.
Hearing-impaired individuals need to exert more effort to follow target signals due to degraded
auditory processing of the input signal. This increased effort can lead to greater fatigue (e.g.
Wang et al., 2018, Hornsby, 2013), which can negatively impact mental health by increased
stress (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024). Therefore, it is especially important for hearing-impaired
individuals to assess and reduce listening effort in order to make listening situations less de-
manding.
2.1.2
Factors influencing listening effort
Listening effort is influenced by demand, which can be affected by factors such as task difficulty
or changes in signal quality (e.g., SNR), and by motivation. This is explained in the Framework

of Understanding Effortful Listening (FUEL) by Pichora-Fuller et al. (2016), which is an adap-
tation of the model from Kahneman (1973). An illustration of the model is shown in fig. 2.1. It
demonstrates how listening effort is assumed to vary with demand and motivation. If the moti-
vation is generally low, the perceived effort is assumed to stay low despite rising demand, as
no or only a few cognitive resources are allocated to understand the stimulus. However, if the
motivation is high at the beginning of the task or in the situation, the model and the framework
suggest that listening effort increases with rising demand. When the demand becomes too high,
motivation decreases, and effort diminishes because the listener is unwilling to invest more en-
ergy and thus gives up. Therefore, Kuchinsky and Vaden (2020) propose that listening effort

point of highest effort, the task becomes easier, and listening effort decreases again, as shown
in fig. 2.2. Kuchinsky and Vaden (2020) also describe that listening effort and performance are
linked: listening effort is low when performance is low, but in easy conditions when performance
is high, listening effort is low as well.

2.1.3
Measures of listening effort
Listening effort is assessed using both objective and subjective measures. An overview of a
subset of different assessment methods is provided below.
2.1.3.1
Subjective Measures
Subjective measures of listening effort are investigated using questionnaires or subjective rat-
ings (Klink et al., 2012a). They can be assessed for example with the rating scale ACALES from
Krueger et al. (2017). Subjectively perceived listening effort, which might differ from objectively
estimated listening effort, reflects the listener’s subjective experience of effortful listening. This
experience depends on the questions and experimental design (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024).
According to McGarrigle et al. (2014), subjective ratings can also be influenced by individual
factors such as differences in effort perception, the definition of effort, the subjects’ performance
and perceived task difficulty. Another drawback of subjectively estimated listening effort is that
it is difficult to rate listening effort at high levels of demand when subjects tend to give up, and
subjective effort is often still rated as high (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024).
2.1.3.2
Objective Measures
As described in the previous chapter, subjective measures have some disadvantages. To make
measures of listening effort more comparable across subjects, objective measures are used.
These are considered to be indirect indicators of listening effort as they investigate correlates
to subjectively perceived listening effort.
Objective correlates of listening effort include physiological parameters that track changes in
the nervous system (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024) and changes in behavioral data, also known
as cognitive performance (Klink et al., 2012b). Physiological measures cover among others
electroencephalography (EEG, see section 2.2), functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI),
functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS), heart rate or heart rate variability, pupillometry,
skin conductance and endocrine factors (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024). Different physiological
measures can vary in their sensitivity to cognitive performance and changes due to auditory
task demand on listening effort. Therefore, observed relationships to listening effort might differ
between different methods (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024). Additionally, physiological parameters
can vary based on individual characteristics such as age, BMI, fitness level, health, and per-
sonality (Stemmler and Wacker, 2010, Johannes and Gaillard, 2014, as cited in Keur-Huizinga
et al., 2024).
Changes in cognitive performance, which can also vary between individuals, are evaluated in
terms of reaction times or measures of performance and accuracy on tasks such as working
memory, single or secondary tasks while listening (Klink et al., 2012b).
To summarize, listening effort is a construct, as seen in section 2.1.1, and it is not a direct physio-
logical measure but it is influenced by various factors. When looking at the relationship between
objective and subjective methods, it was found to be weak or absent (e.g Keur-Huizinga et al.,
2024). According to Keur-Huizinga et al. (2024), that can be due to the different factors which

measures which is also dependent on the measure.
2.1.3.3
Pupillometry as a measure of listening effort
Pupillometry, which measures pupil dilation by its diameter during task execution, has been
widely used in the past decade to study cognitive demands and listening effort (Kramer et al.,
2016). With increasing task difficulty, pupil dilation was found to increase, due to the activa-
tion of the sympathetic, autonomous nervous system in response to stress and thus arousal
(Samuels and Szabadi, 2008 as cited in Kramer et al., 2016). The amount of arousal, which
refers to the level of physiological and psychological activity, induced by fro example stress, is
assumed to reflect the amount of mental effort spent on a specific task , e.g. listening (Kah-
neman, 1973). As pupil dilation was seen to increase with the allocated effort, pupillometry is
suggested to measure cognitive load or effort (Kuchinsky and DeRoy Milvae, 2024). Like EEG,
pupillometry is a non-invasive method and it can be easily installed with little preparation and
simple equipment in various settings. However, the set-up is not very flexible as participants
have to wear glasses or sit in front of a pupil camera. Additionally, another limitation of pupillom-
etry is Kuchinsky and DeRoy Milvae (2024) also mention that pupillometry is sensitive to both
acoustic and cognitive demands, providing information about how effort and arousal change
within and across experimental trials.
2.1.3.4
Advantages of EEG vs. pupillometry in assessing listening effort
Measures from electroencephalography (EEG) and pupillometry can be both used to estimate
listening effort, both having its own strengths. EEG provides direct measures of brain electrical
activity (Teplan, 2002), which potentially can offer insights into the neural processes underlying
listening effort as for example in studies from Baboukani et al. (2022), Damian et al. (2015)
and Debener et al. (2012). However, there is some disagreement across findings how EEG
measures are related to changes in auditory task demand, as some studies found changes
in alpha power (e.g. Seifi Ala et al., 2020 ), while others did not (e.g. Fiedler et al., 2021).
Still, if variations in EEG measures with SNR are observed, this can provide information about
the complexity of the task and the findings can be used to assess changes in listening effort.
Another advantage of EEG is its high temporal resolution (Teplan, 2002), which can be used to
study how listening effort changes over time.
Keur-Huizinga et al. (2024) found no significant link between EEG alpha power and pupillometry
measures, which is probably due to physiological and methodological differences between those
measures. The authors suggested that pupil dilation reflects arousal related to different tasks
such as focusing on the target sound, while alpha power might indicate the brain’s effort to
ignore irrelevant noise (Keur-Huizinga et al., 2024). It was also assumed that alpha power and
pupil dilation use different timescales and have different response times. While EEG is good at
detecting changes in task demand, it might not be as consistent as pupil dilation results (Keur-
Huizinga et al., 2024). The main disadvantage of pupillometry is however, that it cannot be
used in real-life conditions as it is related to the light level (Winn et al., 2018). Furthermore, it
is not clear whether pupillometry shows arousal in general or listening effort, as listening effort

pupillometry as a measure of listening effort.
In summary, both EEG and pupillometry have different advantages for assessing listening effort.
The choice between these methods depends on the specific research goals and the context in
which listening effort is being assessed. Especially when designing experiments closer to real-
life communication situations, mobile EEG has the advantage of being very flexible in usage and
can be used outdoors as it does not require a preinstalled system, unlike pupillometry. That is
why it is focused in the following on the explanation of EEG measurements and its relation to
listening effort evaluation.
2.2
EEG
This chapter introduces EEG measurements, some of its applications and different analysis
methods.
2.2.1
Definition of EEG and its importance
One method for measuring neurophysiological activity is electroencephalography (EEG). As
defined in Teplan (2002), this technique is measuring electrical activity from different brain areas
recorded by electrodes placed on/around the scalp. Specifically, in EEG, the voltage potential
between various electrodes is measured. As the electrical field spreads through tissues such as
skin and skull between the electrodes and the brain (volume conduction), the detected voltages
at different locations measure the combined postsynaptic potentials generated by groups of
neurons in various brain layers and regions.
EEG has a wide range of applications and is a popular objective measurement method for neu-
rophysiological activity because of its high temporal resolution and non-invasive characteristics
(Teplan, 2002). Areas of investigation include cognitive processes such as attention, percep-
tion, and memory (Teplan, 2002).
2.2.2
Measures within EEG analysis
Electrical activity varies over time in amplitude, phase, and frequency, which are also dependent
on the location of origin and the function related to different brain processes.
There are different measures by which EEG signals are analyzed. Firstly, different investiga-
tions evaluate the presence, latency, and amplitude of evoked potentials, also called event-
related potentials (ERPs). ERPs are time-locked voltage fluctuations in EEG to sensory, motor,
or cognitive processes or stimulations (Teplan, 2002, Sur and Sinha, 2009). In the study de-
scribed in this thesis, ERPs are not considered. The reason is that they rely on the average
of EEG data collected in response to many, simliar short stimuli, which typically last only a few
seconds (McWeeny and Norton, 2020). Since this project uses long speech stimuli, resembling
more realistic conditions where speech is continuous and varies, it is difficult to use time-locked
measures, such as ERPs, for investigating listening effort. This limitation also affects their real-
life application in general.

2.2.3
Advantages and limitations of mobile EEG set-ups compared to
stationary EEG
Until recently, most EEG measurements and related research have been based on stationary
set-ups, as mobile EEG systems haven’t been available that long, especially high quality and
affordable systems, and therefore they haven’t been used as much as the stationary ones.
Problems in the development might have been to establish a wireless connection between the
recording software and electrodes, and the need for sufficient noise reduction algorithms to
reduce muscular artifacts in the EEG data from moving participants. As the goal of many ex-
periments in research is to study certain behaviors and processes in real-life settings, mobile
EEG systems are becoming increasingly important. To study more ecologically valid situations,
mobile EEG systems should be small and lightweight (Debener et al., 2012). With these char-
acteristics and being wireless, one can measure cognitive processes during complex move-
ments like walking (e.g., Reiser et al., 2021). Furthermore, wireless systems are not affected
by cable movements, which can lead to electromagnetic interference (Usakli, 2010) and more
electrode displacement compared to wireless systems, decreasing the signal quality (Debener
et al., 2012).
However, all EEG measurements are generally exposed to artifacts, especially muscular ones
(Reis et al., 2014). This drawback of EEG systems can become even more important when
measuring in conditions that involve complex movements, such as walking. However, it was
demonstrated in Straetmans et al. (2021) that good signal quality could be achieved with mobile
EEG using proper artifact attenuation methods in realistic conditions such as walking.
Shaw et al. (2018) observed that alpha power decreased during conditions involving complex
movements and that also ERP components were lower in such conditions (Reiser et al., 2021)

and in outdoor recordings generally (Debener et al., 2012). This could be due to residual noise

2.3
Relation between EEG measures and listening effort
This chapter gives a short overview on the relation of EEG measures and listening effort, sum-
marizing how changes in EEG measures as alpha power and local connectivity refer to changes
in listening effort.
2.3.1
Power as a measure of listening effort
Previous research has shown that alpha power changes with varying cognitive demand, for
example, when an auditory signal is degraded (Obleser et al., 2012). As listening effort is
also related to cognitive load and the availability of cognitive resources, variations in alpha
power can be associated with varying listening effort (Obleser et al., 2012, Fiedler et al., 2021).
Studies have shown that alpha power in the parietal brain region can follow different trends,
including increments, decrements, and nonlinear behavior, depending on the task and study
design (Fiedler et al., 2021). This variation depends on which aspect of listening effort was
focused on and how different cognitive resources are modulated (Herrmann and Johnsrude,
2020).
An increase in alpha power is associated with the inhibition of task-irrelevant processes (e.g
Klimesch, 2012, Marsella et al., 2017, Paul et al., 2021). Though in very demanding situations,
a decrease in alpha power is assumed to be correlated with the involvement of other sensory
networks to sustain attention and the need to constant updating working memory resources,
necessary for long stimuli (Seifi Ala et al., 2020, Baboukani et al., 2022). However, Rajagovin-
dan and Ding (2011) did not see only a decrease in alpha power but an inverted U-shape pat-
tern in relation to attention, suggesting that listening effort estimates may follow the same trend
(Baboukani et al., 2022, Fiedler et al., 2021). This aligns with the proposal by Kuchinsky and
Vaden (2020) that listening effort follows this shape. Thus, the contradictory results regarding
how alpha power changes with listening effort might be due to different levels of demand. De-
pending on how hard the task is, only one side of the inverted U-shape may be explored, and
alpha power may rise or decrease (Fiedler et al., 2021).
Additionally to alpha power, frontal theta power was found to increase with working memory
demands during effortful listening as a response to demands on internal attentional processes
and to non-speech stimuli (Wisniewski et al., 2018).

2.3.2
Local connectivity as a measure of listening effort
Similar to the assumed nonlinear trend of listening effort following an inverted U-shape (Kuchin-
sky and Vaden (2020)), Baboukani et al. (2022) found a similar relationship between parietal
alpha local connectivity and listening difficulty in terms of SNR level. Based on this result,
Baboukani et al. (2022) concluded that local connectivity can be used as a correlate of listen-
ing effort, as it is influenced by top-down cognitive functions like attention and working mem-
ory, which are assumed to modulate phase-locking (Peelle et al., 2013) and phase distribution
(Bernarding et al., 2017). Additionally, local connectivity is proposed to coordinate information
transfer in brain networks (Paul et al., 2021), which, when considering alpha local connectivity,
can be related to the inhibitory mechanisms and constant updating of working memory associ-
ated with listening effort (Baboukani et al., 2022).
Listening effort is assumed to be low in ”giving-up” situations (Kuchinsky and Vaden, 2020). In
these situations, local connectivity and phase synchronization were shown to decrease (Baboukani
et al., 2022) due to disengagement in the listening task (Marsella et al., 2017). Another reason
may be that in such difficult listening situations, information is transferred to other brain areas, in-
volving other sensory networks to facilitate speech understanding (Paul et al., 2021, Baboukani
et al., 2022). Towards easier but not too easy conditions, phase synchrony was observed to
increase with rising listening effort (Baboukani et al., 2022). This can be explained by the fact
that more effortful situations require more sustained attention and updating of working memory
(Baboukani et al., 2022). This was seen in alpha power behavior as well (Seifi Ala et al., 2020).
Furthermore, Baboukani et al. (2021) observed an increase in working memory load with in-
creasing SNR levels, indicating a more difficult listening situation, in frontal delta activity.

2.4
Results from unpublished related study
This project builds upon a previous, yet unpublished study that investigated EEG correlates of
listening effort by examining the effect of SNR and local connectivity measured by COC, as
used in studies like Baboukani et al. (2019, 2022). The new study expands on the earlier work
by increasing the number of SNR levels and incorporating subjective ratings alongside auditory
performance and questionnaire accuracy. Key findings from the earlier study are summarized
here to provide a basis for comparison with the results obtained in this project.
In the experiment, participants were presented with two competing audiobooks from frontal
loudspeakers, while 16-talker babble noise was played from background loudspeakers at six
different SNR levels. COC was calculated for Delta (0.5–4 Hz), Theta (4–8 Hz), Alpha (8–12.5
Hz), and Beta (12.5–25 Hz) frequency bands and eight different regions of interest (ROIs) using
linear and quadratic models. The results are presented as follows:
Firstly, it was shown that subjective listening effort ratings decreased with increasing SNR (see
fig. 2.4), indicating that the listening situation became easier. Supporting these results, ques-
tionnaire accuracy increased with SNR, although it saturated at high SNR levels when modeling
the relationship quadratically. In this study, the linear model showed a greater effect of SNR
on perceived listening effort, likely because participants were listening under challenging con-
ditions and did not experience an easy listening condition, as indicated by ratings that did not
drop below medium effort. This suggests that the task difficult was still high for the condition
with the highest SNR.
For the relationship between local connectivity and SNR, it was found that the linear model
produced a better fit of the data than the quadratic model (see fig. 2.5). This finding suggests
that only one side of the inverted U-shape, seen for local connectivity in Baboukani et al. (2022),
was explored, because very easy conditions are not reflected in this experiment and indicated
by a downwards trend of the curve. This aligns with the observed medium effort ratings at the
highest SNR.
In addition, a linear relationship between local connectivity and subjective ratings was found,
showing higher local connectivity with decreasing ratings (see fig. 2.6). This supports the pre-
vious findings and potentially indicates that local connectivity, estimated by COC, measures
listening effort, as also seen in Baboukani et al. (2022).

Research question and hypothesis
The research question of this project is to investigate if we can estimate listening effort correlates
using the COC measure also in less controlled environments and using mobile EEG recordings.
For evaluating the measures’ effectiveness in more realistic conditions, the body position is var-
ied between sitting and standing. As muscle activity might be higher in the standing condition,
this is a first step to develop a test set-up in more real-life conditions, and for more complex
movements which were not investigated here, the muscle activity should be even higher. Com-
pared to stationary EEG, the mobile EEG data, gathered in this study, might be more prone to
artifacts due to wireless connection issues. Furthermore, there might be additional noise in the
data as muscle activity is suggested to vary between both positions. Nevertheless, it is hypoth-
esized that we will obtain similar results as in the previous study on the relationship between
local connectivity and listening effort, since the same analysis method was used. Therefore, lo-
cal connectivity is suggested to follow a quadratic/ inverted U-shape trend, as seen in Baboukani
et al. (2022). That is given, if the null hypothesis is rejected.
From the previous study’s findings, the following hypotheses are formulated:
1. SNR influences subjectively perceived listening effort and questionnaire accuracy.
2. Local connectivity, as measured by COC, changes across localized brain regions with
SNR, especially for alpha activity in the parietal region.
3. Local connectivity reflects changes in listening effort, correlating linearly with subjectively
perceived listening effort.
4. Body position should not affect local connectivity or power measures if EEG data is cleaned
carefully. It has to be mentioned here that it was seen in previous research from Shaw
et al. (2018) that alpha power is decreasing in walking conditions assuming that ore com-
plex motoric movements are leading to fewer availability of cognitive resources (Reiser
et al., 2021). That indicates that differences in at least alpha power during more complex
movements would not be necessarily indicate that the reason for the decrease on power
is more noisy EEG data. As standing cannot be labeled as a very complex movement,
it is assumed that the position is not affecting the subjective perceived rating and ques-
tionnaire accuracy , especially because those metrics are not assumed to be sensitive to
small changes in the availability of cognitive resources.
Furthermore, alpha power is analyzed and compared to local connectivity to evaluate whether
both amplitude-based and phase-based measures can reflect changes in listening effort to the
same extent. This is because contradictory results on alpha power changes with task difficulty
were found as Seifi Ala et al. (2020) found significant changes, while Fiedler et al. (2021) did
not.

Methods
This chapter summarizes the experimental setup and methods used in this study, explaining
the procedure and chosen parameters. The methods are adapted from the ones used in the
previous study, described in section 2.4, as this investigation aimed to replicate and validate the
results in a less controlled environment with mobile EEG.
4.1
Participants
15 native Danish speakers (8 females and 7 males) with self-reported normal hearing partici-
pated in the study. On average, their age was 29.3 years (SD = 4.9), ranging from 24 to 41 years.
All participants gave informed consent before the experiment, and the study was approved by
the ethics committee for the Capital Region of Denmark (journal number H-1-2011-033).
4.2
Experimental set-up
The experiment was conducted in a simulated living room environment at Oticon A/S, Konge-
bakken 9, Smørum. This environment was chosen to mimic a real-life scenario. A picture of
the room and its set-up is shown in fig. 4.1, and the loudspeaker set-up, as well as the room
dimensions are presented in fig. 4.2. The room, furnished to resemble a living room, provided
a cozier atmosphere compared to a typical laboratory. Additionally, the room was not acousti-
cally shielded from surrounding noise, reflecting potential real-life conditions. However, unlike
a normal living room, there was noise from ventilation and people walking by, rather than back-
ground noise from other rooms. The background noise level, measured over a working day,
was 45.3 dB(A). The room was also perceived to be more reverberant than a typical living
room, which has a reverberation time of approximately 0.5 s (White, 1998).
Participants were either seated on a sofa chair or standing close in front of it, facing the target
loudspeaker (T in fig. 4.1; Genelec 8040A, Genelec Oy, Finland) at 0° and a TV screen, dis-

Audiobook recordings of 60-second excerpts about a biography of Simon Spies, narrated in
Danish, served as target stimulus. For the masker signal, 8-talker babble was used, created by

and investigate its effect on listening effort correlates, four different signal-to-noise-ratios (SNRs)
were used in the experiment.
The SNR was defined as the ratio of target speech level to masker level. The target stream was
always presented at 62 dB(A). This level was chosen to match normal speech levels (American

The experiment lasted approximately 2.5 hours, including EEG preparation, acquisition of con-
sent, and breaks. It consisted of eight conditions: four SNRs (-12, -7, -2, and 5 dB) and two
positions (sitting and standing). Each condition was repeated eight times which resulted in 64
trials. Additionally, there were four training trials at the beginning, with two trials in the sitting
position and two in the standing one, leading to 68 trials in total.

Each trial had a duration of 83 s and consisted of different parts (see fig. 4.3):
• 3 s of background noise only (i.e. masker)
• 60 s of simultaneous target and masker presentation
• 20 s for responding to a short questionnaire.

Data acquisition and analysis
The following sections provide information about the EEG data acquisition, as well as about the
methods for EEG and questionnaire data analysis.
4.5.1
EEG acquisition and pre-processing
EEG data was recorded using a wireless amplifier (Smarting PRO X, mBrainTrain, Belgrade,
Serbia) and a customized electrode cap (EasyCap GmbH, Hersching, Germany). The cap
was based on the international 10-20 EEG system with 44 electrodes, shown in figure fig. 4.5.
The driven right leg (DRL) and common mode sense (CMS) electrodes served as references.
Conductive gel was applied to the electrodes of the cap to ensure good conductance between
the scalp and electrodes, verified by impedance measurements below 10 kΩ.
EEG data was recorded at a sampling frequency of 2000 Hz. Audio stimuli were aligned with
EEG recordings by streaming event markers, indicating stimulus start times, to the record-
ing software (Smarting Streamer, mBrainTrain, Belgrade, Serbia) using the labstreaming layer
protocol (Swartz Center for Computational Neuroscience, Institute for Neural Computation,
University of California, San Diego, USA, adapted by Chadwick Boulay, https://github.com/
labstreaminglayer/liblsl-Matlab). The timestamps of the event markers were saved in .xdf files
20
Evaluating mobile EEG outcomes for real-world listening effort estimation
==PAGE:33==
and were aligned with the timestamps of the EEG data, saved in .bdf files.
EEG data pre-processing was performed in MATLAB (Mathworks Inc., Natick, MA), using the
FieldTrip toolbox (Oostenveld et al., 2011) and EEGLAB (Delorme and Makeig, 2004), utilizing
scripts adapted from Baboukani (2024). Firstly, the EEG data was separated into trials of 63 s
where the beginning of each trial was defined by the start of the audio stimulus. To minimize
filter transient artifacts, the first trial in the first recording session was defined to start 10 s before
the actual stimulus start and the last trial in the last session ended 10s after the last stimulus
presentation. The data was then downsampled to 512 Hz. After concatenating all files from all
sessions, a notch filter between 49 and 51 Hz was applied to suppress electrical noise compo-
nents. Additionally, bad channels, characterized by unexpected patterns of voltage fluctuations
and excessive noise, were identified by visual inspection and removed. This was followed by
an independent component analysis (ICA) which was performed on the bad channels removed
data to identify artifacts resulting from eye blinks, muscular activity, heart beats and single-
channel noise. To optimize the identification of relevant artifacts, the dataset was duplicated
and high-pass filtered at 1 Hz. The IC weights from this decomposition were then applied to
the unfiltered data. The components which were visually identified as artifacts were removed.
Bad channels were reconstructed by interpolation over neighboring electrodes, and the cleaned
data was filtered between 0.5 and 30 Hz to remove low-frequency noise artifacts and to focus
on the relevant frequency range. Finally, all data was saved into different conditions based on
the different SNRs and positions used in the experiment.

4.5.2
Power and local connectivity analysis
It is assumed that different cognitive processes are involved in effortful listening (Wisniewski
et al., 2021). As these processes can affect different brain regions and frequency bands, power
and local connectivity might change accordingly (Baboukani et al., 2022). Therefore, power
and local connectivity were calculated in eight predefined regions of interest (ROIs) and for
four different frequency bands. Using FIR band-pass filters, the EEG data was filtered into
Delta (0.5–4 Hz), Theta (4–8 Hz), Alpha (8–12.5 Hz), and Beta (12.5–25 Hz) frequency bands.
Additionally, the EEG data was re-referenced to the average over all channels to reduce the
influence of volume conduction (Baboukani et al., 2022).
The used ROIs are shown in fig. 4.5 and included the frontal, right and left frontal, central, right
and left frontal, parietal and occipital region. An overview of the electrodes corresponding to
the ROIs are reported in table 4.1. The ROIs were chosen based on the ones used for local
connectivity analysis in Baboukani et al. (2022) who used that eight ROIs based on a previous
study from Mehta and Kliewer, 2017. As the electrode cap layout in Baboukani et al. (2022)
contained 64 electrodes and the current study utilized only 44, the ROIs have been adapted.
There, the left and right temporal ROI was kept the same to be comparable to those in Baboukani
et al., 2022. The electrodes ’FT9’, ’TP9’, ’FT10’ and ’TP10’ were not included in the temporal
ROIs as they showed noisy data for most of the subjects. ’P3’ and ’P4’ were not included in the
analysis of the parietal area as those channels are located more laterally on the skull, resulting
in a larger spatial area which could affect the assessment of ’local’ connectivity.
To facilitate reading, ROI - frequency band combinations localizations are indicated in the re-
sults’ analysis by their regions and band names only, such as for example ’parietal alpha’ for
the parietal region in the alpha frequency band.

4.5.3
Questionnaires
Questionnaire accuracy for the comprehension questions was calculated by dividing the num-
ber of correctly answered questions by the total number of questions per condition, excluding
non-responses. For the calculation, both comprehension questions were evaluated together,
counting the total number of answered questions. The questionnaire accuracy was analyzed to
evaluate the subjects’ cognitive performance.
4.5.4
Statistical analysis
A two-way linear mixed-effects (LMM) ANOVA was used to evaluate the fixed effects of SNR
and position, as well as their interaction on the behavioral data (ratings and questionnaire accu-
racy), COC and power measures. It is assumed that listening effort behaves non-linearly (see
section 2.1.1, Kuchinsky and Vaden, 2020) contradictory results have been found in the litera-
ture regarding the linear or non-linear relationship between SNR and EEG estimates of listening
effort (see section 2.3, e.g., Fiedler et al., 2021). Therefore, the data for this study were mod-
eled using both linear and quadratic relationships (see eq. (4.2) and eq. (4.3)) to investigate the
non-linearity in the relationship between SNR and local connectivity, as well as power. Subjects
were treated as random effects to account for inter-subject variability. The effect of trials was
not included in the model as it was averaged over them per subject.
The linear model was defined as:

respectively. β0 represents the intercept, and β1...3 are the fixed effects for SNR, position, and
their interaction. The random effects of subjects are included as uk, and the residual error as
ϵijk.
Although subjects were included as a random effect, gender and age were not included in
the analysis as they were not part of the research question (see chapter 3). Additionally, age
differences were small, and gender distribution was almost balanced.
The linear model (eq. (4.2)) was adapted to model the COC values using subjective ratings as a
fixed effect. This approach aimed to relate objective and subjective measures of listening effort
and investigate hypothesis 3 in chapter 3. To ensure that the relationship between subjective
listening effort and local connectivity was not coincidental, the COC values were shuffled in the
final step. The model was then rerun to evaluate the effect of the ratings on the COC measure,
and consequently, on local connectivity.
Furthermore, local connectivity (COC values) were correlated with power, using Pearson cor-
relation.
The statistical analysis was performed in RStudio (RStudio Team, 2023) using the packages
lme4 (Bates et al., 2015) and lmerTest (Kuznetsova et al., 2017). The significance level for all
tests was set at α = 0.05. Corrections for multiple comparisons were not applied to identify
statistical trends. As the p-values were not extremely small, applying corrections would have
rendered most comparisons non-significant after 32 comparisons for the local connectivity and
power evaluation. Consequently, when significant effects are described in the results, they refer
to observed trends rather than strict statistical significance.

6
Discussion
The aim of this thesis is to evaluate EEG measures, especially local connectivity estimated
by the circular omega complexity (COC) measure, for their usability to evaluate listening effort
in more realistic environments using mobile EEG. For this investigation, data from 15 normal-
hearing participants were evaluated in terms of local connectivity and power from EEG, perfor-
mance, and subjective ratings as indicators of self-perceived listening effort.
The results are discussed in section 6.2 to 6.4. Furthermore, the limitations of this investigation
are explained in section 6.5 and based on those, future work to better understand the current
findings is elaborated on in section 6.6.
6.1
Behavioral data: Subjectively perceived listening effort and
performance
It was hypothesized (see chapter 3), that subjectively perceived listening effort and cognitive
performance, expressed by questionnaire accuracy, are influenced by the SNR. For both met-
rics, no effect of the body position was found, which aligns with the hypothesis and suggests
that the position does not influence experienced listening effort and cognitive performance.
Subjectively perceived listening effort, indicated by subjective ratings, was found to decrease
with increasing SNR. Although the quadratic model was significant as well, the effect of the lin-
ear one was much higher, suggesting that the subjective listening effort decreased linearly with
increasing SNR and thus easier listening conditions. In addition, the quadratic model prediction,
seen in fig. 5.1d, showed increased ratings at the highest SNR which does not align with the
expectations as the effort rating is unlikely to increase at the easiest listening condition. Com-
pared to the results of this study, the findings from the previous study (section 2.4) indicated
a stronger quadratic relationship at higher SNR levels, leading to a floor effect at these levels.
The discrepancy between the current study’s results and those of the previous study is likely
due to the different choices of SNR levels. The observed lowest variance at -12 dB SNR is
probably due to the participants’ general inability to comprehend the target content under this
condition. The higher variance in ratings at higher SNRs can be explained by the fact that in-
dividuals perceive effort differently, which also depends on their task performance (McGarrigle
et al., 2014).
Furthermore, listening effort is theoretically assumed to follow an inverted U-shape (Kuchin-
sky and Vaden, 2020, section 2.1.2), indicating a non-linear relationship with task difficulty. It
is suggested that rated listening effort at very low SNRs is low due to participants exhibiting
giving-up behavior. However, this pattern was not observed in the current results. This discrep-
ancy may be attributed to the limitations of the rating scale and the questions used to evaluate
self-perceived listening effort, which did not account for situations where participants might give
up. This resulted in participants rating the most challenging condition at -12 dB SNR with very
36
Evaluating mobile EEG outcomes for real-world listening effort estimation
==PAGE:49==
high effort. Based on the results of performance, it is however assumed that most participants
gave up in that condition. This was also reported by some of the participants. Therefore, future
investigations should incorporate questions that assess self-perceived listening effort in sce-
narios where participants might give up. Alternatively, using a different scale, such as ACALES
(Krueger et al., 2017), which allows participants to indicate when only background noise is per-
ceived, could be beneficial.
In the analysis of questionnaire accuracy, accuracy increased non-linearly with SNR, corre-
sponding to easier listening conditions. The quadratic model provided a more accurate fit to the
data, although the linear term was also significant. This suggests that accuracy, reflecting sub-
jects’ cognitive performance, is non-linearly but not only quadratically related to listening task
difficulty, as indicated by SNR. Instead, it is assumed to be a combination of linear and quadratic
terms. This observation is logical, as performance does not decrease under very easy condi-
tions, as seen in the modeled results for the SNR of 5 dB. The mean data follow a plateau
pattern, where questionnaire accuracy saturates at higher SNR levels. This finding aligns with
the ones from the previous study (section 2.4), which also reported a plateau effect for accu-
racy, and it supports the proposal by Kuchinsky and Vaden (2020) that higher performance is
achieved in easier listening situations, leading to lower perceived listening effort.
To support the analysis of questionnaire accuracy, the number of unanswered questions per
SNR and position was evaluated. It was found that the number of answered questions increased
with higher SNR levels. This finding suggests that participants found it easier to understand the
audiobook excerpts under easier listening conditions, thereby indicating higher performance in
those conditions.
Summarizing the findings from the assessment subjectively perceived listening effort and accu-
racy, the hypothesis, that the SNR has an influence on both metrics, was confirmed and, that
the position did not have any effect on those.
6.2
Local connectivity as a measure of listening effort
Significant effects of SNR on local connectivity were observed in frontal delta, frontal theta, left
frontal, right frontal, parietal alpha, and parietal beta for both the linear and quadratic models.
This confirms the first part of the second hypothesis (see chapter 3) that local connectivity, and
thus EEG phase synchrony, changes across localized brain regions depending on the SNR.
However, this was not only true for parietal alpha, as the highest effects of SNR on local con-
nectivity were also observed in right frontal delta and theta for the linear model and parietal beta
for the quadratic model, including parietal alpha for both models.
High effects in frontal delta can be explained by increased task difficulty at higher SNRs and an
increase in working memory load, as seen by Baboukani et al., 2021. In addition, phase-locking
values during speech tracking were found to change with SNR in the delta band (Mohammadi
et al., 2023). This finding supports changes in phase synchrony and thus local connectivity with
task difficulty as well. Interestingly, significant results were observed for frontal theta as well.

37
==PAGE:50==
However, theta activity is typically associated with experiments using non-speech stimuli and
involving internal attention processes (Wisniewski et al., 2018). Thus, it is suggested that the
difference in the nature of attentional processes and stimuli is why no significant change in frontal
theta power was found in other studies, such as those by Marsella et al. (2017) and Baboukani
et al. (2022). In these investigations, increasing task difficulty was induced by external factors,
like background noise, which involved external attentional processes (Wisniewski et al., 2018).
As the current study is assumed to involve external attention processes and speech stimuli
were used, it is unclear why local connectivity was found to be significant in right frontal theta
for the linear model as well. However, also Mohammadi et al. (2024) used speech stimuli and
they observed changes in frontal theta phase synchrony with varying listening effort as well,
which supports the findings from this study. But another question is why the local connectivity
change was only seen here in the right frontal area and not frontal in general. Furthermore,
the significant effects seen in parietal beta were not expected. It might be that the explanation
of the seen effect is that beta generally is assumed to relate to integrated cognitive processes
(Lundqvist et al., 2024).
In the previous study, significant effects were only seen in parietal alpha (see section 2.4) and
most research focuses on alpha band investigations (section 2.2.2). As parietal alpha was also
in this study shown to have the biggest significant effect, the discussion focuses in the following
especially on this ROI - frequency band combination. The effect of SNR on local connectivity in
parietal alpha was significant for both the linear and quadratic terms. This suggests that local
connectivity follows a function including both linear and quadratic terms. This is particularly the
case as the quadratic term was more significant than the linear model before the exclusion of
one subject, but afterwards, the linear model showed a higher effect. Still, as the relationship
between local connectivity and SNR, and thus task difficulty, includes a non-linear term, this fits
the non-linear nature of listening effort (Kuchinsky and Vaden, 2020) and the results of the pre-
vious study (see section 2.4, fig. 2.5). In that study, local connectivity was seen to change with
SNR more linearly, indicating that only one side of the inverted U-shape was explored. These
results and the observed ceiling effect in the performance data suggest that the predicted local
connectivity for this study exhibit a plateau pattern when excluding the subject with the strong
quadratic pattern. However, the modeled data without the subject’s exclusion show slightly de-
creased local connectivity towards the easiest listening condition with an SNR of 5 dB. Based on
results from Seifi Ala et al. (2020), this could be explained by the decreased need for sustained
attention, working memory update, and the decreased inhibition of task-irrelevant processes
(e.g., Marsella et al., 2017, Paul et al., 2021) due to less experienced effort in that condition.
As the increase of those processes is necessary in more difficult listening situations such as
-2 dB SNR, high local connectivity and thus high phase synchrony were observed in that condi-
tion. Towards even worse SNRs, local connectivity and phase synchronization decrease. One
explanation could be disengagement and giving up behavior in very difficult listening conditions
(Marsella et al., 2017, Baboukani et al., 2022). Another assumption is that in very difficult listen-
ing conditions, the need for sustained attention and working memory updating is even higher
(Seifi Ala et al., 2020), which is why other brain networks are getting involved to facilitate speech
38

==PAGE:51==
understanding (see section 2.3.2, Paul et al., 2021, Baboukani et al., 2022).
For the mean data excluding one subject, the assumed plateau pattern is seen in fig. 5.5. One
reason why there is evidence for a higher linear effect on local connectivity after the subject’s
exclusion, without indicating a decreased need for sustained attention at 5 dB, could be that
the listeners still allocate cognitive resources to listening during that condition. Furthermore, a
decrease in sustained attention might occur only at even higher SNRs, as indicated by subjec-
tive ratings at 5 dB SNR with an average rating of 3, suggesting that the perceived effort in that
condition was not minimal either. Thus, this data might also show only one side of the inverted
U-shape. The decrease of the mean data in local connectivity already towards -2 dB could then
be attributed to the involvement of other brain networks for speech understanding facilitation,
as described above. It is, however, controversial that in the excluded dataset, the mean COC
at -7 dB SNR is lower than at -12 dB. The explanation might be the high variance in the data.
This might be also the reason why the predicted data still indicate the inverted U shape pattern
with a slight decrease in local connectivity from -2 dB SNR towards 5 dB.
Regarding the effect of position on local connectivity, no significant effect was observed, which
aligns with the fourth hypothesis. The effect of the interaction between SNR and position was
not significant either, except for alpha left temporal in the quadratic model. However, as the
effect of position was not significant for this ROI - frequency band combination and the effect of
the interaction was very small, it can be concluded that this observation is not important for the
current analysis and can be neglected. The results, that the position did not significantly impact
local connectivity, indicate that artifact reduction on the EEG data was successful. Furthermore,
they provide evidence that the standing position does not involve a complex movement, as
no change in the availability of cognitive resources was observed due to neural interactions
between cognitive and motor processes (Reiser et al., 2021).
6.3
Power as a measure of listening effort
Highest effects of SNR on power were observed in parietal and occipital theta and parietal
beta. This finding was not expected. Firstly, frontal theta activity is assumed to be related to
listening tasks. Secondly, as discussed in section 2.3.1 and section 6.2, it is associated with
non-speech stimuli and internal attention processes (Wisniewski et al., 2018) but in this study
speech stimuli are used and it is assumed that external attention processes were involved. So
it remains unclear why parietal and occipital theta were showing an effect. The same holds true
for parietal beta. But as beta activity generally is assumed to be related to integrated cognitive
processes (Lundqvist et al., 2024), that could explain the results.
For parietal alpha activity, power was observed to decrease linearly with decreasing SNR but no
quadratic relationship was found. The decrement can be explained with the need of sustained
attention and working memory update involving other brain networks towards very difficult lis-
tening conditions at low SNRs (Seifi Ala et al., 2020). Here, it is unlikely that the explanation
for the not seen inverted U shape pattern is that only one side of it is explored as not even an
indication of a plateau pattern was observed. However, one reason could be the differences in

39
==PAGE:52==
individual power, which then influenced the model prediction and average power per condition.
Compared to the insignificant results for power, the effect of local connectivity showed significant
results in parietal alpha for the quadratic model. This suggests that local connectivity may
be more robust to individual differences and consistently reflects changes with varying task
difficulty, providing evidence that it is a reliable estimator of listening effort.
An effect of position on power was only found in occipital delta for the linear model. As the
analysis result was close to the threshold for significance, showing that the effect was very
small, this result can be neglected. No effect was found in the alpha frequency band either,
confirming the hypothesis that the body position is not affecting power.
6.4
Relationship between EEG measures and subjectively
perceived listening effort
Subjectively perceived listening effort, determined by subjective ratings, increased with de-
creasing SNR. That indicates that the listening situations became more difficult. Those findings
are supported by decreasing performance in those conditions, suggesting that the subjects had
more difficulties to understand the content. As local connectivity was also dependent on the
SNR in parietal alpha, it was assumed that local connectivity is related to subjectively perceived
listening effort, indicated by subjective ratings. In addition, Paul et al., 2021 saw a relationship
between alpha power and self-reported effort ratings. This finding suggests that local connec-
tivity and self-perceived effort are related as well, which was confirmed in the previous study
(see section 2.4) on which this project is based. Therefore, it was evaluated whether local
connectivity and subjectively perceived listening effort were related in this study as well.
Significant effects of subjective ratings on local connectivity were among others seen in right
frontal delta, right frontal theta and parietal alpha. This aligns with the effect of SNR seen on
local connectivity, suggesting that changes in local connectivity in these regions may indicate
changes in listening effort.
Regarding the results in especially parietal alpha, they indicate that local connectivity is linearly
correlating with subjectively perceived listening effort. It is found that local connectivity is de-
creasing with increasing subjectively perceived listening effort. This was already seen in the
previous study. These results, along with those from Baboukani et al., 2022, demonstrated
that the COC measure is a valid indicator of listening effort. Based on these findings, the cur-
rent study suggests that listening effort can also be estimated through local connectivity, as
measured by COC, using mobile EEG in less controlled environments.
The linear relationship between local connectivity and subjectively perceived listening effort was
confirmed by rerunning the analysis with shuffled COC values, which gave no significant result.
Thus, hypothesis 3, see chapter 3, was confirmed that local connectivity reflects changes in
listening effort.
On the contrary, the relationship between power and subjective ratings was not significant in

parietal alpha. This aligns with the results showing that the effect of SNR on power was not sig-
nificant either. Therefore, it could be concluded that local connectivity might be a more reliable
measure of listening effort than power. Another reason for this is that some other studies on
alpha power in relation to listening effort did not show significant differences, such as Fiedler
et al., 2021. Nevertheless, it was discussed previously in section 6.3 that power may not have
been significant in this study due to individual differences. Furthermore, a significant correlation
between power and local connectivity was observed in parietal alpha and other ROI - frequency
band combinations, suggesting that local connectivity and power might not originate from the
same but from related cognitive processes. Additionally, it was noted that changes in power,
especially in parietal theta, should be further investigated.
6.5
Limitations
One of the aims of this study was to estimate listening effort in more realistic environments.
Therefore, a living room environment was chosen instead of a soundproof laboratory. Although
the room gave the impression of a living room, and it is assumed that participants felt like they
were in a more realistic environment, the room differed from a typical living room in a few im-
portant aspects. The reverberation was perceived as higher, and the walls were not connected
to the ceiling, allowing background noise from the surroundings to be recognizable.
On one hand, the testing environment can be related to a realistic environment as it included
factors such as background noise and reverberation that were not controlled for. Indeed, the
study’s aim was to evaluate listening effort measures with mobile EEG in less controlled en-
vironments. Additionally, background noise could influence people’s listening effort in normal
living room environments. On the other hand, the nature of the background noise in this study
might differ from typical household noises, such as those from a nearby kitchen or appliances.
Furthermore, the high reverberation in the room could have affected the participants’ listening
effort. To assess the influence of reverberation, it would have been beneficial to compare the
reverberation time of the simulated living room to a normal one. However, due to limited time
resources, this comparison was not conducted.
To summarize, there is a concern about how realistic the chosen environment was. Neverthe-
less, the influence of background noise from the surroundings is assumed to be minimal, as the
measured average background noise was 45.3 dB(A), which is 12 dB lower than the masker
level in the condition with the highest SNR and 29 dB lower in the lowest SNR condition. Addi-
tionally, participants reported that they did not consciously perceive the background noise and
that it did not distract them during the listening task.
When evaluating the similarities of the listening environment in the living room to a real-life
condition, there were limiting factors as well. By designing the experiment so that participants
were seated between two loudspeakers playing babble noise and listening to an audiobook,
the idea was to simulate a situation where people are, for example, sitting relaxed on a sofa
with people around talking, which could be a realistic scenario. However, during the sitting
and standing situations, participants were still asked to look at the screen and move as little as

==PAGE:54==
possible to minimize the influence of muscular activity artifacts in the EEG data. Therefore, it
is still unclear how the investigated local connectivity from COC measures would look in even
more ecological situations, such as lying on a sofa or walking conditions. Additionally, it might be
that a situation where people are sitting on a sofa and watching TV is more likely than listening
to an audiobook. The overall listening task was kept as close as possible to the one in the
previous study to minimize the influence of more variables to be able to compare both study’s
results. Thus, it was decided not to include an audiovisual condition as listening effort was
shown to change in audiovisual conditions compared to audio-only (e.g., Sommers and Phelps,
2016). Furthermore, there was no visual material available which would have matched the used
audiobook in this study.
Another limitation of the real-world applicability of this study was the decision not to include a
walking condition due to the difficulty in defining and controlling different SNRs in such condi-
tions. Investigating brain activity in listening situations with mobile EEG devices has already
been explored in outdoor walking conditions using inserted earphones (e.g., Debener et al.
(2012), Reiser et al. (2021), Straetmans et al. (2021)). However, especially outdoors, the sound
pressure level can change due to weather conditions, and indoors, the SNR will also change
depending on the listener’s position in the room and its distance to the target and masker signal,
as well its distance to reflective surfaces. This implies that the control over stimulation and noise
sources, including parameters such as the SNR, is reduced as the test situation becomes more
dynamic. Therefore, there is a trade-off between replicating real-life dynamics and maintaining
a high degree of experimental control.
Furthermore, predefined SNRs were used in this study. Although a small pilot was conducted
to determine different SNRs and the perception of listening effort at those levels to use an SNR
range leading to very low to very high effort, it should be noted that different individuals have
different speech reception thresholds, which were not controlled for in this study. Therefore, it
was not directly ensured that the speech stimulus was audible for all participants in the lowest
SNR condition. However, this study only investigated listening effort in participants with self-
reported normal hearing, and as the participants were quite young with a limited age difference,
it was assumed that their speech reception thresholds were close to each other. Nevertheless,
when studying the effect of SNR with hearing-impaired individuals, it would be beneficial to
adjust the SNR based on people’s speech reception thresholds.
Regarding the evaluation of local connectivity, based on previous studies, it was assessed in
eight different ROIs. To provide complementary information, it could be considered to investi-
gate local connectivity in more ROIs. Additionally, the results could have changed depending
on the analysis method used. COC values were calculated in time windows of 4 seconds, but
it would be interesting to see how and how much the results would change using longer time
windows. The same holds true for the calculation of power. It might also be interesting to see
if the method of power calculation affects the results. For example, Fiedler et al. (2021) used
the p-welch method for power calculation (eq. (2.3) in eq. (2.2)). As the focus in this study’s
results discussion was the investigation of listening effort in parietal alpha, future work could in-

clude a closer evaluation of local connectivity and power in the delta and theta frequency band.
Furthermore, the implications of the results presented in this study are difficult to assess, as
the local connectivity and power evaluation were not corrected for multiple comparisons for the
number of investigated ROI - frequency band combinations. Therefore, only a trend is shown
that local connectivity in parietal alpha indicates listening effort.
Moreover, this study investigated time- and trial-averaged local connectivity and power. How-
ever, the listening effort might have changed during the experiment due to its length and the
continuous listening task, which likely caused participants to become tired. Therefore, future
work should include an assessment of listening effort over trials. The audiobook excerpts were
randomized to ensure participants’ attention remained stable and did not increase due to en-
hanced engagement with the story. Nevertheless, the randomization could also lead to a drop
in attention if participants were not sufficiently interested in the excerpts. Thus, investigating lis-
tening effort over time would help determine whether the randomized nature of the experimental
design was a good choice.
Lastly, it should be kept in mind that the sample size used in this study included only 15 par-
ticipants. It can be thus argued whether the exclusion of the subject with the strong quadratic
pattern was an outlier and if a bigger sample size would lead to either the effects of SNR on
local connectivity seen with or without that subject’s exclusion.
6.6
Future work
Based on the limitations explained in section 6.5, possibilities for further investigations are sum-
marized as follows:
Firstly, it would be interesting to investigate the influence of different parameters in COC and
power calculation, such as the time window length and choice of ROIs. Secondly, it is aimed
for evaluating changes in local connectivity and power over time to be able to assess how
listening effort changed over time. Moreover, future work should incorporate models combining
the linear and quadratic term as the evaluation of the separate linear and quadratic models
suggest that power and local connectivity are dependent on both terms. Additionally, it would
be advantageous to compare the cleaned EEG data in terms of the remaining artifacts and noise
estimation to the data before ICA removal, and to compare both sitting and standing conditions
to assess the influence of noise on the data used for local connectivity calculation.
Furthermore, for real-world applications of mobile EEG, it is important to include more dynamic
and/or realistic conditions in listening effort assessment, such as lying down, walking, and au-
diovisual stimulation. Future studies could explore methods to control SNR in dynamic envi-
ronments to better understand listening effort in real-world conditions. Moreover, it would be
valuable to investigate other physiological measures of listening effort simultaneously. Depend-
ing on the setup and environment, either pupillometry or heart rate assessment could be used.
The evaluation of heart rate would be especially useful when testing in non-laboratory environ-
ments, as only a wristband is needed.

Conclusion
Modern life increasingly exposes people to complex auditory environments. In addition, listening
in noisy environments can be particularly exhausting for individuals with hearing impairments.
Unlike those with normal hearing, people with hearing impairments may need to invest extra
cognitive effort to understand and focus on auditory stimuli in challenging conditions. To pre-
vent the negative effects of high demand, it is crucial to develop methods for assessing listening
effort. Objective measures are especially valuable because they are not influenced by subjec-
tively perceived effort, making them more comparable across subjects. Various physiological
measures have been used to study listening effort objectively. Pupillometry has been shown
to be a good indicator of listening effort, but its real-life application is difficult as it depends
on luminosity and might be a more indirect measure reflecting people’s arousal. Electroen-
cephalography (EEG) may offer a more direct approach. One promising method is estimating
local connectivity from EEG, measured by circular omega complexity (COC), which has previ-
ously been tested only in controlled setups. Therefore, the goal of this study was to evaluate this
method in less-controlled settings using mobile EEG, as stationary EEG systems limit ecological
validity.
To investigate the potential of local connectivity for listening effort estimation, this phase-based
approach was compared to amplitude-based power measures, subjective ratings of listening
effort, cognitive performance and previous data from controlled setups. Fifteen normal-hearing
participants listened to audiobooks under various signal-to-noise conditions in a living room-like
environment. Mobile EEG data were recorded while participants were either sitting or standing.
The results revealed that as the SNR increased, participants reported a linear decrease in per-
ceived listening effort. Additionally, cognitive performance, assessed through questionnaire
accuracy, was shown to increase more quadratic than linearly with SNR. Furthermore, findings
indicated that local connectivity and power change across different brain regions depending on
the SNR, suggesting that EEG phase synchrony and power metrics vary with task difficulty.
However, local connectivity was only shown to vary non-linearly with task difficulty (SNR) in the
parietal region in the alpha frequency band, which is assumed to be associated with listening
effort, but this was not the case for power. This finding aligns with the result that only local con-
nectivity was shown to be linearly related to subjectively perceived listening effort, indicating
that local connectivity is a valid indicator of listening effort even in less controlled settings. The
effect of body position on local connectivity was insignificant, suggesting that artifact reduction
on the EEG data was applied successfully, even though mobile EEG was used and participants
were standing in one condition.
Summarizing, the results validate that local connectivity as measured by COC can serve as an
objective listening effort estimator in more ecological settings and may be more reliable than
power measures. This study also demonstrates the ability to measure listening effort in real-

life situations using mobile EEG. However, there is still space for further research. While this
study has shown promising results, even more real-world scenarios need to be explored to
confirm these findings. Future research should include more dynamic and realistic conditions,
such as walking or audiovisual stimulation, to enhance the ecological validity of the results.
Additionally, investigating other physiological measures of listening effort, such as heart rate or
pupillometry, could provide a more comprehensive understanding of listening effort in real-life
situations. Still, it is important to note that real-life mobiles EEG studies for assessing listening
effort will be a trade-off between allowing a relevant degree of movement or dynamics and
extracting meaningful information, for which it is important to have experimental control over
the conditions such as the signal-to-noise ratio.
In conclusion, this thesis not only contributes to the academic understanding of listening effort
but also provides value for companies such as Oticon. By developing a test setup to objectively
investigate listening effort in a more realistic environment, this research can contribute to the
understanding of their customers’ difficulties in everyday life and the benefits hearing aids pro-
vide for them. By demonstrating the feasibility of using mobile EEG to measure listening effort
in realistic environments, this research provides a valuable foundation for future studies and
technological advancements in the field of auditory research.
