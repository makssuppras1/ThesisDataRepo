Abstract
Balcony structures from the period 1890-1950 have proved to be in very poor condition due
to corrosion. Therefore, the need for a non-destructive method to evaluate the severity of the
damage is needed.
The present thesis investigates, if vibration-based methods can be used to detect structural
deterioration in traditional balcony structures. Studies of two states are included in this project,
an undamaged state and a damaged state. The damaged state is introduced by controlled sys-
tematic corrosion of the steel beams carrying the balcony deck. The deterioration is applied by
an electrolytic corrosion process exposing the steel beams to accelerated corrosion.
An experimental approach by means of Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) is used to iden-
tify the dynamic properties of a full-scale balcony test setup. The dynamic properties of the ﬁrst
four global modes of the structure are considered. Two diﬀerent identiﬁcation techniques respec-
tively Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD) and Time Domain Poly Reference (TDPR),
are used to analyse the responses in order to identify the modal properties. The responses are
obtained by exciting the full-scale balcony by means of a shaker, which generates a random
loading on the structure. 20 tests are performed for each of the two states, in which accelerations
are measured via attached senors placed at points of interests along the balcony.
Concurrently with the experimental work, a Finite Element (FE) model of the balcony struc-
ture is established in Ansys ADPL software, serving as a digital twin. The aim is to create an
FE-model, which replicate the characteristics of the physical test structure.
From the performed analysis, changes in the dynamic properties, i.e. modeshape and corre-
sponding frequency, can be detected for both the FDD and TDPR techniques during OMA. The
digital twin of the balcony is improved to have an accuracy within the predetermined bench-
mark of [5-10%] compared to the experimental results, for the investigated modes in both the
undamaged- and damaged state.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Balcony collapses during the past years have emphasised the need for a non-destructive method
to assess if the structure is safe [1]. A speciﬁc type of balcony construction, which is very
common for buildings in Denmark from the period 1890-1950 [2], is of special interest. These
balconies are constructed with continuous cantilevered steel proﬁles going through the bearing
brick wall and clamped into the wooden ﬂoor deck. The steel proﬁles are embedded in concrete,
constituting the balcony base plate. Since these balconies have served for many years, diﬀerent
deterioration mechanisms have inevitably developed in the structure. Deterioration happens
due to nature impacts in terms of wind and weather. Especially moisture, chlorides and air are
parameters that causes degradation over time. Degradation of a structure is not always visible
and therefore it can potentially constitute a major risk of failure and cause extensive damage.
Corrosion is one of the mechanisms which can be very severe for the structural integrity. The
steel beams in cantilevered balconies are likely to corrode in the area were they penetrates the
brick wall. At this place the steel proﬁles are more vulnerable due to often insuﬃcient casting
around the proﬁles all the way through the thickness of the facade [2]. This gives rise for a
thermal bridge, where the unprotected steel is exposed to air and moisture from condensation
due to the thermal bridge. Furthermore, the risk for presence of chlorides from the brickwork
causes further acceleration of the corrosion process [2] eventually causing signiﬁcant reduction
of the steel cross-section. Because the area of corrosion is located inside the facade wall, space
for the corrosion product to develop and expand is present. Therefore, it may not cause any
visual signs of deterioration going on, as for instance cracks in the brickwork. Since the balcony
is a cantilevered structure, the largest section forces also occurs where the beams are going
through the facade, once again making this area the main point of interest.
In order to investigate this highly present subject, a research project has been initiated at DTU
in collaboration with Technological Institute and funding from Grundejernes Investeringsfond
and Realdania. The present master thesis is a continuation of the previous work by Saleh [3].

1.2
Literature Review
As mentioned, this thesis is an elaboration of a previous master thesis by Saleh [3] with the
title "Experimental investigation of traditional balcony structures". His work made the basis
for further study of traditional balcony structures. In the thesis an investigation of the static and
dynamic behaviour were conducted with respect to corrosion degradation. Diﬀerent FE-models
were made in Ansys APDL© with purpose of comparing the results from the physical tests with
the numerical model. Displacements were measured at six diﬀerent places on the balcony during
quasi-static loading of the undamaged structure and compared with the numerical results from
Ansys having the same load conditions. The results showed that the numerically calculated
displacements did not ﬁt fairly well with the actual displacements of the balcony. It is stated, that
the deviation might be due to the reﬁnement of the FE-model. Static tests with respect to corro-
sion eﬀects was not performed. Dynamic investigation of a small steel specimen was conducted
by Operational Modal Analysis (OMA). The modal parameter in terms of natural frequency
was studied for the ﬁrst two modes of the test specimen at three stages namely undamaged,
exposed to electrolytic corrosion after 4 days and lastly after 22 days. Results showed, that the
corrosion process had a signiﬁcant impact on the eigenfrequencies, which were lowered for both
the 1st and 2nd mode. The OMA approach was not elaborated to investigate the physical balcony
test-model. Furthermore, two FE-simulations of the vibration test were made with respectively
2D beam elements and 3D volume elements constituting the test specimen. The aim was to
investigate, if the numerical model could detect the same changes in dynamic properties, when
the steel beam had been exposed to corrosion as for the experimental tests. The study showed,
that the model with 3D volume elements was better to predict the dynamic properties of the
corroded steel beam, compared to the model with 2D beam elements. Using more elements in
the FE-model led to a better estimation of dynamic properties compared to the experimentally
determined eigenfrequencies. Basis for further and more comprehensive study through OMA
on structural changes and dynamic responses is prevailing.
The following paper also deals with degradation phenomena in balcony structures: Risk of failure
in existing RC balcony and strength veriﬁcation under degradation phenomena by Campione
and Cannella [4]. The study examines a common balcony type in Italy, made of light-aggregate
concrete with embedded T-beams of reinforced concrete (RC). Several balcony structures which
have been exposed to carbonation, presence of chlorides and water inﬁltration are studied and
discussed. Due to carbonation and corrosion, the balcony structures will get a reduction of steel
area in the reinforcement bars, therefore the ductility and safety of the balcony is reduced. Other
eﬀects of material degradation that is considered, are loss of bond strength between reinforce-
ment bars and the concrete, together with concrete compressive strength degradation. Static

An example of using Operational Modal Analysis to determine the dynamic properties of a
structure, is given in the paper: OMA identiﬁcation of a wind turbine rotor hub for monitoring

Estimation of the modal parameters are performed with three diﬀerent algorithms, Enhanced
Frequency Domain Decomposition (EFFD), Time Domain Poly-Reference (TDPR) and Stochas-
tic Subspace Identiﬁcation (SSI). A numerical model of the rotor hub is established in Ansys
to compare with the experimental ﬁndings. The paper determines from the various investigated
identiﬁcation techniques, 15 modes and corresponding modeshapes for the structure, of which
closely spaced modes were detected. A mismatch between the measured and the numerically
determined frequencies was observed from the analysis. The relative frequency error of the
estimates were found to be around 0.5 % and the comparison of the modeshapes were performed
by the Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC). MAC values, in the diagonal of the MAC-matrix,
were found to be 0.8 or better for mode 5 to 10, whereas for the lower- and higher modes, a
modelling problem seems to be present. The article argues that the diﬀerence is most likely to
be explained by the complex boundary conditions of the experimental setup, which is diﬃcult
to model accurately.
Another study where OMA has been used to identify the modal properties of a structure, is
in the paper: Ambient response analysis of the Heritage Court Tower Building structure by
Brincker and Andersen [6]. The study deals with a 15 story building in Vancouver, tested
during operational conditions from ambient excitation, meaning the loading on the building is
unknown and not measured. Six sensors were placed in the tower, measuring the response of the
structure in terms of accelerations. Post-processing of the data was analysed using two diﬀerent
identiﬁcation techniques, a frequency domain method and a time domain method namely, FDD
and SSI. From the analysis, 11 modes were identiﬁed by both techniques, with three very closely
spaced modes in the range 1.1-1.4 Hz. The modeshapes, of the three closely spaced modes, were
seen to be the ﬁrst bending mode, a torsion mode and a combined bending and torsion mode of
the tower. The paper concludes that for all modes, natural frequencies determined with the two
identiﬁcation techniques showed very good agreement. The modeshapes were estimated nearly
identical for the two methods, and the damping ratio was determined reasonably close around
1 −2% in most cases.

A paper which investigates the dynamic properties by Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA)
is: Theoretical and experimental modal analysis of a cantilever steel beam with a tip mass by
Miguel et al [7]. The article determines the ﬁrst ﬁve natural frequencies and damping ratio
for a cantilever steel beam with a tip mass, and compare them with theoretical and numerically
obtained values for the frequencies. Excitation of the test specimen happens by applying an
impulse load in terms of a hammer-force. The approach for processing the response of the
steel beam to determine its dynamic properties, is the Stochastic Subspace system Identiﬁcation
method (SSI), which is a time domain algorithm. The numerical model was created using the
software program Ansys ©. Results of the EMA experiment using the SSI approach shows with
comparison of the theoretical- and numerical analysis, that the highest deviation between the
determined natural frequencies was 2.81 % which was for the 2nd mode. The coeﬃcient of vari-
ation for the determined natural frequencies obtained by SSI was between 0.10 % and 0.58 %.
Therefore, the paper states that EMA is a good method to determine the natural frequencies of a
structure. The damping ratio for the ﬁve modes were also determined by EMA. The coeﬃcient
of variation was signiﬁcantly higher than for the natural frequencies, namely between 12.71%
and 55.91%. The article however states, that such variation is acceptable since the damping
ratio is the most diﬃcult dynamic parameter to quantify.
In relation to the paper presented above another study, where EMA also has been used to
determine the natural frequencies of a steel beam, is: Theoretical and Experimental Modal
Analysis of a beam by J. Sharma [8]. The diﬀerence in relation to the previous paper is that
this article post-process the data in the frequency domain instead of the time domain using Fast
Fourier Transformation (FFT). The study determines the ﬁrst three natural frequencies for a
steel beam test specimen with diﬀerent boundary conditions, respectively free in both ends and
simply supported. During EMA testing, the beam is excited with an impact hammer at diﬀerent
locations along its length.
Measurements are obtained using a laser vibrometer measuring
displacements as response, which also diﬀers from the previous paper. Comparison of results
for the natural frequencies obtained via EMA with theoretical values and a numerical FE-model
made in Ansys © is conducted. The ﬁndings from the study shows a very good correlation
between the EMA results and numerical and theoretical values, which is stated to be within the
reasonable error margin. The paper describes that the minor diﬀerences is due to deviations for
the physical test specimen in terms of geometrical dimensions and stiﬀness modulus used for
the analytical FE-model and the theoretical calculations.

1.3
Objectives for the study
Based on the papers presented in the previous section, it is the authors opinion that vibration-
based methods are useful in terms of structural health monitoring (SHM) and determination of
the dynamic properties of a structure. The scope of this thesis is to investigate, if changes in the
dynamic properties and responses can be determined through vibration-based methods in order
to evaluate structural deterioration. The structural degradation will be asset for a traditional
balcony structure exposed to damage at the bearing steel proﬁles. More speciﬁc the deterio-
ration will be applied as controlled systematic electrolytic corrosion. The limitations of the
study concerns for the dynamic experimental investigation to be performed with OMA, using
two diﬀerent identiﬁcations techniques, one in the time domain and the other in the frequency
domain. The reason not to choose EMA for the study, is due to the enhanced post-processing
of the data afterwards, which would be too time consuming within the time frame of this thesis.
Simultaneously with the experimental investigations a numerical study will be conducted. The
FE-model will serve as a so-called digital twin of the balcony test setup. The numerical model
is created with Ansys ADPL software due to prior knowledge of the interface for both authors.
Moreover, the software is acknowledged to be suitable for engineering problems within structural
research, which also was a part of the decision.
To summarise, the present thesis aim to investigate the following question.
Is it possible to detect artiﬁcial introduced corrosion in a traditional balcony structure through
vibration-based methods both experimentally and numerically?
To answer the question requires theoretical knowledge, useful test results, computational skills
and veriﬁcation of the data, which all are addressed in this project outlined in the overview of
the diﬀerent chapters:
Chapter 2) contains all the theoretical background required for the dynamic analysis as well as
knowledge about the processes during electrolytic corrosion.
Chapter 3) deals with the experimental investigation of the balcony test setup. Comprising ma-
terial property determination, corrosion application for the steel beams, and the dynamic
testing in damaged and undamaged state of the balcony.
Chapter 4) introduces the numerical model of the balcony and presents the ﬁndings hereof.
Chapter 5) compares the results between the experimental analysis and the numerical model.

2.2 Operational Modal Analysis
9
2.2
Operational Modal Analysis
Operational Modal Analysis (OMA) is a method to determine a structures dynamic properties
based solely on the measured responses. Thereby, the loading acting on the structure is unknown
as opposed to experimental modal analysis (EMA), which makes OMA a very nice tool since it
can be used during operational conditions of the system. The responses in OMA are generated
from normal operational conditions e.g. natural loading such as wind and waves, or from traﬃc,
machinery and so on. These loads can be classiﬁed as ambient loads, which introduces vibrations
in the structure measured as either accelerations, velocities or displacements. Since the loading
is unknown, the essence is that the load needs to have white noise characteristics, meaning it
has to be random in time and space [10]. This is one of the key assumptions for OMA in order
to acquire useful measurements. For large civil engineering structures, the natural excitation is
most often suﬃcient, but for smaller structures artiﬁcial excitation might be necessary to excite
the structure properly.
The theory behind OMA applies for both continuous time series as well as discrete time se-
ries. When performing experiments, the data output is always in the form of discrete time
series, i.e. measured responses at a time step ΔC. The number of sensors placed on the struc-
ture each corresponds to a degree of freedom (DOF). The total number of sensors gives the
amount of modes, which can be identiﬁed for the system. OMA testing requires extensive
know-how and preliminary work on the modes of interest to determine the sensor conﬁguration
and sampling parameters properly. Furthermore, the identiﬁcation algorithms used for post-
processing the data have to be capable of separating the physical modes from the noise modes.
Thus an FE-model of the investigated system is often made in order to support the OMA analysis.
In the following sections, the main elements of OMA will be presented. The theory is primarily
obtained from Introduction to Operational Modal Analysis by R. Brincker & C. E. Ventura [10].
2.2.1
Correlation Functions
Correlation Functions (CF) is a central concept in OMA theory. All information regarding the
dynamic properties of a system are hidden in the correlation functions. The ﬁrst step in OMA
theory is therefore to calculate the correlation functions of the responses [10].

2.2.4
Identiﬁcation Techniques
The modal identiﬁcation can either be performed in the time domain or in the frequency domain.
The frequency domain has the advantage of considering a small frequency band of interest, where
some modes dominates, making it easy to identify due to the appearance of the PSD plot. The
drawback of the frequency methods is, that it suﬀers from more bias compared to the time
domain methods from estimation of the spectral density functions. For the time domain, the
methods relies solely on the correlation functions, meaning that it deals with free decays of
which the physical information about the structure has to be extracted.
In this thesis, two
diﬀerent identiﬁcation techniques are used, which will be presented in the following. The ﬁrst
is a frequency domain method named Frequency Domain Decomposition (FDD) and the other
is a time domain method called Time Domain Poly Reference (TDPR).

2.3
Electrolytic corrosion
Corrosion in general is an unintended deterioration of metals. In order for corrosion to happen,
a metal, oxygen and water needs to be present. Corrosion of steel causes rust, which thereby
gives a reduction in cross-section of the proﬁle. This will lead to a reduction in bearing capacity
and also a reduction in stiﬀness, which is the main concern for this project.
Electrolytic corrosion is a process of accelerated corrosion. It requires two electrodes respec-
tively an anode and a cathode, as well as a common electrolyte. Each electrode constitute an
electric conductor in which the current transport the negatively charged electrons. The common
electrolyte secure the ﬂow of metal ions between the electrodes, now forming a closed circuit.
When the current passes through the system, this implies respectively oxidation and reduction of
the electrodes. The oxidation always happens at the anode meaning that electrons are released,
and for the cathode the reduction takes place meaning the electrons are obtained [12]. The
presence of an oxidation and a reduction, i.e. transfer of electrons, together constitutes what is
called a redox process. The most noble metal is determining, which of the two metals that are
going to corrode, speciﬁed by their potential. The metal with the lowest potential i.e. lowest in
the Reactivity Series is the one that will corrode [13]. The concept of electrolytic corrosion is
illustrated in Figure 2.8.

Dynamic tests
The main part of this thesis is to investigate dynamic methods for detection of structural dete-
rioration. This section will examine the experimental testing of the full scale balcony setup by
means of random excitation and OMA analysis. The experiments will be conducted for balcony
test setup 2 just as the static test by Saleh [3]. Initially the tests are performed for the undamaged
state. The dynamic characteristics i.e. modes shapes and eigenfrequencies are determined for
the undamaged state to serve as a reference for damage detection after deterioration. Subse-
quently, controlled artiﬁcial corrosion from section 3.4 is applied to the steel proﬁles at selected
points of relevance. Afterwards, the same dynamic test is performed to see if any structural
changes can be detected due to the deterioration. The ambition is to see from the OMA analysis
for the two states that the dynamic properties of the system has changed.
As described in the theory from section 2.2, OMA analysis relies on random loading of the
structure, i.e. from the ambient environment. In order to be able to excite the full scale balcony
structure suﬃciently, a load cell is used as an exciter. The load cell is a shaker, which can be
programmed to load with white noise characteristics meaning completely random. To capture
and depict the modeshapes as good as possible, the sensors have to be placed adequately. The
sensor conﬁguration on the test setup as well as location of the load cell is indicated in Figure
3.20. By knowing the displacement of the wooden beams in both ends, the sensors are placed
in the quarter points over the length. For the balcony plate, all sensors are placed at the front in
the third points of the balcony plate width in order to be able to detect twisting in the plate.

Full-scale FE-model of the balcony
A numerical model of the tested balcony structure treated in the previous sections is created,
to determine the dynamic properties of the balcony. The purpose is to create a model, which
reproduce the ﬁndings from the experimental tests, a so-called digital twin. A digital twin is a
numerical replica of a system, which can be updated and tuned to provide a better representation
of the physical system.
The FE-model is created with the Finite Element software Ansys
APDL©, an illustration of the model is shown in Figure 4.1.

As shown in Table 4.1 the wooden beams and steel proﬁles are modelled with 2D beam elements
called BEAM188 (2-node). This element type is a two-node linear element, with one integration
point along its length. Thus it evaluates all element solutions as constant over the element length
i.e. the same value in both ends/nodes [19]. The element can obtain tension, compression
and bending and has six Degrees Of Freedoms (DOF) at each node. They are respectively the
translation in all three directions x, y and z plus the rotation around all three axes x, y and z.
The concrete plate is modelled with shell elements of the type SHELL181 (4-node). This ele-
ment is a four-node element with three integration points over the thickness, which is suitable
for thin to moderately thick shell structures [19]. The element has six DOF at each node namely
translation in the x, y and z direction as well as rotation about x, y and z-axis. In order to get
the steel beam and concrete plate to work as one unit, the two structural elements must share
nodes. Thus the discretization in the FE-model is formulated so that all elements have an edge
length of 0.05 m. This entails that the diﬀerent structural elements have coinciding nodes and
are thereby connected to each other.
The bolted connections between the steel beams and wooden beams are represented in the FE-
model by Coupling Equations. They deﬁne coupled static and kinematic relations between two
nodes, making them behave in the same way, so they have the same displacement and rotation
in x, y, and z direction. A rigid link between the wooden beams and steel proﬁles at the point of
the two aﬀected nodes, is thereby established. There are three bolts between each wooden- and
steel beam, causing a total number of 54 DOF shared among nine, bolts which has been coupled
to form a rigid connection.
The last element to be deﬁned is the Combin14 element. This element is a two-node element,
which only has longitudinal or rotational spring capabilities.
In the present FE-model the
element is utilised as rotational spring, having three DOF at each node namely rotation about the
x, y and z direction. The element has no mass and thus only contribute with rotational stiﬀness
deﬁned by the rotational spring constant :B?A8=6.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This thesis aim to investigate, if vibration based methods can be used to detect structural dete-
rioration. In the previous sections, diﬀerent experimental methods and a numerical model have
been studied. In the following, diﬀerent elements of the analysis are discussed.
The reasons for the deviations between the numerical model and experimental methods can be
many. Starting with the material properties of the structural elements the variation in these
contribute signiﬁcantly. The more accurate the input parameters are in an FE-model, the better
are the results. Thus it is of great importance, in terms of creating a digital twin, to estimate the
material properties of the structural elements appropriately. The largest uncertainty with respect
to the output of the FE-model is assigned to the wooden beams. These was seen to have huge
variations in mass and cross-sectional area and thereby in density and moment of inertia, which
has signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results.
Another cause of the deviations between the numerical model and the experimental results could
be the reduced cross-sections of the steel beams after corrosion in Figure 3.18 which is a sim-
pliﬁed estimate. As it was shown, the corrosion of the cross-sections had happened completely
random. In order to quantify the amount of area, that has been removed during the corrosion
process, average values of the dimensions were used to estimate a reduced cross-section, which
could be implemented in the numerical model. This is of cause an approximation, and if one
should consider to do anything diﬀerent, the part of the steel beams that are corroded should
be modelled with volume elements instead. A more accurate representation of the damaged
cross-section could thereby be implemented from advanced camera technology.
As an explanation for the diﬀerences might also by the modelling of the support between the
balcony plate and the brickwork. This line support is modelled with rotation springs in Ansys,
tuned with a spring stiﬀness :B?A8=6 determined on basis of the static test made by Saleh [3] as
presented in section 3.3. This connection is very diﬃcult to incorporate into a numerical model,
==PAGE:94==
84
Investigation of Structural Deterioration
as the rotational resistance has a contribution from the degree of ﬁxing over the brickwork, but
also from friction in the connection, making things much more complicated.
The bolted connection between the steel proﬁles and the wooden beams are also subjected with
a potential source of error. The connection is modelled to be fully rigid, which most likely is
not completely correct, but yet is a fair assumption. There might be some slip between the steel-
and wooden beams, when the structure is loaded and friction forces may appear at the bolts.
Making a friction connection would be very diﬃcult and require a very time consuming eﬀort,
which not necessarily would beneﬁt equivalently.
Another important factor to the deviation is, that damping of the structure is not considered
in the FE-model, which of cause is present in the real balcony structure. This will naturally
inﬂuence the results of the model, but it is an assumption that is chosen, because it is a complex
quantity to estimate. The TDPR technique estimates the damping ratio for each mode, but it
requires further study to determine, which value of the damping ratio to be used in the FE-model
since each mode has diﬀerent damping and contributes diﬀerently to the total response of the
balcony.
Lastly, a cause of explanation to the deviations might be, that non-linearities are not considered
in the FE-model. It is however not expected, that these are of much inﬂuence. This is assessed
based on the comparison plots between numerical results and the experimental results in Figure
5.1. Since they are fairly close to each other for all modes, non-linear behaviour can not be that
pronounced for the real balcony structure.
Considering the experimental results, a possible explanation of why any signiﬁcant change in
frequency could not be detected, might be due to temperature variations between performing
the two tests. Table 3.2 shows, that the test for the damaged state was made six weeks after
the initial test for the undamaged state. Thereby, it is possible that the balcony structure in the
damaged state has increased in stiﬀness due to a signiﬁcantly lower air temperature in December
than October in Denmark, which the FE-model does not take into account.
Finally it should be kept, in mind that the above discussed deviations between the numerical
results and the experimental ﬁndings are relatively small as quantiﬁed in section 5, and falls
within the benchmark of [5-10%] for the relative deviation.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project, deterioration has been investigated for a traditional balcony structure by means
of OMA and a numerical model. The results from the experimental tests revealed that a minor
change in frequency could be detected before and after corrosion were applied. Even though
the change in frequency was not as pronounced as the authors would have hoped fore, slightly
changes was observed for both the FDD and TDPR identiﬁcation technique. However, it was
found that the diﬀerence in frequencies between the two states was within one standard deviation
of the obtained data. Thereby the small diﬀerence in frequency can not be assigned solely to the
corrosion of the steel beams, since it falls within the statistical uncertainty of the experiment.
On the other hand, it was possible to detect a diﬀerence in the modeshapes between the two
states, where the amplitude became more distinct compared to the initial undamaged state.
The quality of the digital twin was quantiﬁed by the deviation between the experimental ﬁndings
and the results from the model. The FE-model ended up calculating the frequencies of the
balcony, with a relative deviation within the predetermined benchmark of [5-10%] for all modes
in both states, with exception of Mode 3 before corrosion. Thus, the digital twin of the balcony
is assessed to be successful, with potential for further improvement by the mentioned subjects
in the discussion chapter.
Another point of interest relates to the electrolytic corrosion process applied on the steel beams.
The approach worked well, with signiﬁcant corrosion developed for two of the proﬁles, although
the last one did not experience any noticeable change. Implementation of the damaged state in
the numerical model from the corroded cross-sections also showed, that the change in frequency
due to corrosion would be minor, as found from the OMA tests.
Thereby, it can be concluded that, OMA and numerical modelling provide the possibility of
detecting changes in the dynamic properties due to structural deterioration, however it is fairly
little. To fully prove the potential of this methodology as basis of a warning criteria and if the
detected change is suﬃcient enough to asses whether the balcony is in such a poor condition,
that safety precautions have to be enforced, requires further studies to answer.

CHAPTER 8
Future Perspectives
Future work in continuation of the preceding thesis provides further eﬀort to improve the digital
twin of the balcony structure. This might involve modelling of friction in the support con-
dition for the interaction between the balcony plate and the brickwork. The inﬂuence of the
very distinct variation in material properties for the wooden beams should be implemented in
the model, since those parameters were found to impact the results of the analysis to a great extent.
It could also be relevant to simulate the responses of the dynamic experiment. The advantages
of doing a simulation before an actual dynamic test is, that no external parameters such as
measurement uncertainties, weather conditions and etc. will inﬂuence the results. It would thus
be possible to simulate the response of the balcony structure exposed to diﬀerent degrees of
corrosion for the steal beams. This approach is obviously much faster compared to the exper-
imental work, but on the other hand requires a high level of accuracy in representation of the
physical structure in order to provide a trustworthy and useful output.
As mention in the discussion part of the report, considerably temperature variations in weather
between the dates for the dynamic experimental tests before and after corrosion has been applied,
which might play a role regarding the experimental results. It could be interesting to study the
inﬂuence of temperature variations during the year, in relation to the dynamic properties of the
balcony. One could imagine, that when the temperature decreases, the stiﬀness of the system will
increase, and potentially have an impact on the frequency of the balcony. A way to investigate
this, would be to perform the dynamic tests of the structure continuously over longer period of
time for a year.
No actual reason could be identiﬁed for the diﬀerence in water temperature as well as the
amount of corrosion for one of the corrosion cells. Therefore, further investigations of the
process and the mechanism behind the electrolytic corrosion process is required. An analysis
of the corrosion sediment as well as the water contained in the cells can reveal some potential
cause of explanations to the experienced challenge.

Finally, a diﬀerent sensor conﬁguration at the balcony plate could be considered to possibly
obtain an indication of damage from the modeshapes. Instead of only placing sensors at the
front of the balcony plate, there could be placed 2-3 additional sensors from the brickwork and
towards the front of the deck. This will facilitate a more accurate illustration of the modeshape
for the balcony plate. The claim is, that when the steel beams are undamaged, the modeshape
should deﬂect as a cantilevered beam. However, when damage has been introduced at the ﬁxed
end at the brickwork, the deformation of the balcony plate would be expected to take form as if
a yielding point has develop, meaning that a linear shape of the deformation should be detected.
In other words, a change in slope going from varying to constant, between the undamaged- and
damaged state ideally is to be seen.
