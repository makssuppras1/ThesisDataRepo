Abstract 
 
 The challenge of keeping up with protein demand both for animal feed and human 
consumption has become even greater as global population is expected to grow from 7.2 billion 
people to 9.6 billion by 2050. This large increase in population will lead to a dramatical increase 
in global meat production. Seaweeds and plant biomass are considered as very promising 
alternatives which through biorefinery technologies can provide additional protein thus 
reducing the gross feed demand. Furthermore, the need for new and highly efficient, plant-
biomass degrading enzymes is greater than ever, in order to make biorefinery processes 
economical viable. 
 In the present work the extractability of proteins from the brown seaweed S. latissima and 
plant biomass namely, rye grass and red clover pulps was evaluated. Protein recovery in these 
studies was determined as the yield of solubilized protein with regard to the total protein in the 
biomass. Solubilized protein was determined with the Bicichronic assay (BCA) and Kjeldahl 
analysis for comparison of methods. Different strategies were considered for the recovery of 
protein from S. latissima. Proteases enhanced extraction, aqueous/alkaline extraction as well 
as alkaline extraction combined with an ultrasonic or a carbohydrolase pre-treatment were 
employed. Protease assisted extraction using Alcalase (Novozymes) resulted in protein 
recovery of 72 g/ 100g total protein as determined by BCA. The effect of the physical pre-
treatment (freeze drying, alginate extraction), extraction solvent, extraction temperature and 
duration on the recovery of protein was assessed. Optimal extraction occurred with alkaline 
conditions when using untreated seaweed, NaOH 0.1M, and incubation temperature and 
duration of 55°C and 1h respectively. This resulted in a protein recovery of 68 and 38 g/ 100g 
total protein as determined by BCA and Kjeldahl analysis. Ultrasonication or enzymatic pre-
treatment using carbohydrolases (Alginate lyase, Sigma- Aldrich), (Cellulase, Megazymes), 
(Laminarinase, Sigma-Aldrich) did not improve the optimized alkaline extraction. For the 
recovery of protein from plant biomass rye grass and red clover pulps, the optimized alkaline 
extraction mentioned previously was employed. Also, the effect of ultrasonication pre-
treatment on the protein extraction yield was assessed. Alkaline extraction from rye grass led 
to significantly higher protein recovery (56 g/100 g total protein) compared to extraction from 
red clover (7 g/100g total protein. Ultrasonication pre-treatment improved significantly the 
protein recovery from both ryegrass and red clover pulps leading to an increase of 80% and 
124% for ryegrass and red clover respectively.

1. Introduction 
 
As current global population is expected to grow from 7.2 billion people to 9.6 billion by 2050 
the challenge of keeping up with adequate protein supply both for human consumption and 
animal feed is becoming even greater. The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United 
Nations (FAO), has estimated that approximately a seventh of the world’s population do not 
meet adequate diet requirements and an even greater number have protein malnutrition. This 
increase in human population combined with increase of income and demand for more nutrient-
dense foods, will cause the global meat production to be dramatically increased by 206 million 
tons per year during the next 35 years [1]. Therefore, alternative protein sources must be found 
in order to meet world protein demand for food and feed. Seaweeds and plant biomass are 
considered as very promising alternatives. Some species of seaweed and microalgae as well as 
various plant biomasses are known to contain protein levels similar to those of common protein 
sources, such as meat, egg, soybean, and milk[2]. Despite that, they still remain unexploited as 
far as protein is concerned. 
A major factor limiting their use as protein sources is the and poor extractability and bio-
accessibility of protein. The bio-accessibility and extraction of protein from these sources is 
greatly hindered by the presence of cell wall polysaccharides, namely alginate and carrageenan 
in the case of seaweeds and cellulose and hemicellulose in the case of plant biomass. Aqueous 
and alkaline extractions are the most common methods reported throughout the literature for 
recovering protein from algal and plant biomass. Furthermore, various cell wall disruption 
methods and inclusion of chemical reagents have been employed to improve algal and plant 
biomass protein extraction efficiency[2], [3],[4]. Most common methods being used is osmotic 
shock, mechanical grinding and ultrasonic treatment. One method that has been identified as a 
promising cell disruption method for aiding the macroalgal and plant protein extraction is 
polysaccharidase digestion [5],[6]. Employment of enzymes has long been used in biorefinery 
processes to degrade lignocellulosic substrates and convert them to fermentable sugars which 
are later utilized by microorganisms to produce bioethanol or other chemical products[7]. 
Nevertheless, the efficiency of enzymatic processes differs according to the biomass cell wall 
structure which makes it very difficult to establish a standardized method. In addition to that, 
enzymatic processes are very costly due to the fact that multiple types of enzymes are needed 
in most biorefinery processes. Therefore, highly efficient biomass degrading enzymes with

1.1 Brown seaweed  
 
Algae refers to large group of photosynthetic, oxygen-producing, unicellular or multicellular 
organisms. Seaweeds are complex marine algae which can be divided into three main 
taxonomic groups based on their colour; Phaeophyceae (brown algae), Chlorophyta (green 
algae) and Rhodophyta (red algae). Brown algae represent a diverse group of multicellular 
organisms and consist of approximately 1800 species. They are mainly found in marine habitat 
and brackish waters although fresh water species of brown algae have also been found[8]. A 
carotenoid photosynthetic pigment, fucoxanthin which is present in their chloroplasts is 
responsible for their distinctive olive-brown colour which they derive their name from. 
Phaeophytes differ from most other algae for several seasons.  Their cell walls are mainly 
composed of alginate as oppose to carrageenan and agar found in other seaweeds. Also, they 
have cellular inclusions of polyphenolic polymers, known as physodes and chloroplasts with 
thylakoids in stacks of three, enclosed by a girdle lamella. Finaly, their main storage product 
is laminarin, a β-1,3-glucan. Some species are known to also produce mannitol, sucrose, 
glycerol, or oils as storage reserves [9].  
In the present work, the brown seaweed Saccharina latissima (Figure 1) was used for the study 
of protein extraction from algal biomass which will be thoroughly discussed in sections 3 and 
4. S. Latissima, also known as Laminaria saccharina is a common kelp in north Atlantic and 
Pacific. In Europe, it can be found from Norway and as far as northern Spain at depths down 
to 10 meters.

1.2 Brown seaweed cell wall structure 
 
The brown seaweed cell wall is mainly composed of alginic acid, cellulose and various 
polysaccharides. Sulfated fucans and alginates comprise the main portion of the brown algae 
cell wall (up to 45% algal dry weight) with cellulose only accounting for a small part between 
1-8% of the algal’s dry weight. Other components of the brown algal cell wall include proteins, 
halogenated and/or sulphated phenolic compounds (phlorotannins) and halide compounds such 
as iodide (Figure 2)[11] .

Sulphated containing polysaccharides (FCSP) 
Sulphated fucans are sulphated polysaccharides that contain a-L-fucosyl residues. The 
sulphated fucans from the cell wall of brown algae are divided into three main categories: 
homofucans, fucoglucuronans and glucuronofucoglycans. Homofucans (or fucoidans) are 
primarily composed of consist of a backbone of (1→3)- and (1→4)-linked α-l-fucopyranose 
residues. The l-fucopyranose residues may be substituted with sulfate (SO3−) on the C-2 or C-
4 (rarely on C-3), with single l-fucosyl residues and/or with short fucoside (fuco-
oligosaccharide) side chains. Fucoglucuronans (sargassans and ascofihyllans) consist of a 
polyuronide backbone, branched with short chains of neutral or sulphated residues of galactose, 
xylose and fucose Glucuronofucoglycans consist largely of linear chains of galactose or 
glucose branched with fucose and glucuronic acid. [12][13][14].The structure of algal fucans 
varies greatly among species and sometimes among different parts of the seaweed as it is shown 
in Figure 3.[15][16]

Alginates 
Alginates are linear copolymers comprised of (1→4) linked b-D-mannuronic acid (M) and a-
L-guluronic acid (G) residues with varying composition and structural sequence[11] (figure 2). 
Different algal sources, extraction techniques and harvest times can result in the uronic acids 
irregular arrangement in a blockwise pattern of varying proportions of MM, MG, and GG 
blocks. The Mannuronic acids gives the MM-blocks a linear and flexible conformation while 
guluronic acid provides a rigid and folded structure which gives the polymer chain stiffness 
[17]. Alginate in brown algae is believed to be a structure-forming component which gives 
them mechanical strength and flexibility. Their physiological properties can be compared to 
those of cellulose in terrestrial plants. Alginate content in seaweeds used in commercial 
applications reaches up to 40% of the dry weight.[18]

1.4 Amino acid composition of algal proteins 
 
The quality of proteins can vary significantly depending on factors such as digestibility and the 
availability of essential amino acids[22]. Animal proteins are considered complete due the fact 
that they are rich source of all essential amino acids (EAA) which the body is unable to 
synthesize. Plant derived proteins, are considered incomplete as they usually lack one or more 
of the EAA’s. Also, their high content in polysaccharides and fibers and the presence of anti-
nutritional factors makes them harder to digest compared to animal proteins [23]. Despite that, 
the amino acid composition of seaweed proteins has been extensively studied and found that 
algae represent a viable source of protein, with EAA composition being on parallel with that 
of other protein sources such as ovalbumin and soy bean [2], [19]. However, tryptophan and 
lysine are usually limiting amino acids in most algae[24]. A comparison of the amino acid

 In many macroalgae, the concentrations of glutamic acid and aspartic acid together represent 
40% of the total amino acid content[3]. Galland-Irmouli et al. (1999) evaluated the nutritional 
value of proteins from the edible red seaweed Palmaria palmata (Dulse) and found that

abundant amino acids, whereas β-alanine, citrulline, histidine and methionine-histidine were

1.5 Algae consumption and potential applications of algal proteins 
 
1.5.1 Algae consumption and current uses 
Protein is an essential component of humans diet, and has been found to play a significant role 
in regulation of body composition and bone health, gastrointestinal function and bacteria flora, 
glucose homoeostasis, cell signalling and satiety[30]. The world health organization (WHO) 
proposed a protein requirement of 0.75g/kg per day for both young and elderly people[31], 
while higher intakes, of 1.6-1.8 g/kg are recommended for athletes[32]. 
As mentioned previously, algae are considered a rich source of proteins, with a nutritional value 
comparable to common protein foods such as eggs, soya etc. This could be beneficial for people 
who have restricted diets (vegans) or allergies to animal derived proteins. Seaweeds and 
microalgae have been consumed for thousands of years. Human consumption of algae, varies 
by nation, with annual per capita consumption in Japan ranging from 9.6 in 2014 to 11.0 g in 
2010 of algae per day. Moreover, they are been increasingly marketed as ‘’functional foods’’ 
[33] due to the presence of several bioactive compounds[3] [13]. 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
One the most consumed species of seaweed, is P. tenera (nori) which is commonly used as 
wrap for sushi in many Asian countries. Arthrospira platensis most commonly known as 
‘’spirulina’’, is a species of microalgae that contain up to 70% of dry weight protein [3]. It has 
gained, big attraction in the past few years with organizations such as WHO labelling it a 
‘’superfood’’ due to its high protein content and other nutritional benefits, such as anti-
hypertension, renal protective, anti-hyperlipidaemia, and anti-hyperglycaemic properties with 
large scale-production occurring throughout the world for use as food additive (salad dressings, 
beverages etc) and health supplement. Hainan Simai Enterprising Ltd., which is located in the 
Hainan province of China is the largest producer of spirulina with annual production of 200 
tonnes dried biomass which is represents 10% of the global production .[34] [2][35].

Bioactive peptides 
Bioactive peptides extracted from various sources have been extensively studied for their 
potential health benefits and have been reported to exhibit drug like activities or mimic 
hormones. Furthermore, they could modify the physiological functions or promote a positive 
effect through binding to specific receptors or inhibition by enzyme actions [36][37]. Bioactive 
peptides extracted from algae have been reported to exhibit antioxidative, antihypertensive, 
anticoagulative, antitumor and immune-stimulative properties [38].  
Angiotensin I-converting enzyme (ACE), a zinc containing metalloenzyme, plays a significant 
role in the control of blood pressure by the rennin angiotensin system. It catalyses the 
conversion of the peptide angiontensin-I to the peptide angiontensin-II which is recognized as 
potent vasoconstrictive molecule. Therefore, ACE inhibition has been used as a treatment for 
hypeternsion. A number of synthesized drugs, Captopril®, Enalapril®, Alacepril® and

reported to exhibit high ACE inhibitory effect with IC50 value of 0.3 μg/mL compared to the 
commercial drug Captopril®,  which showed an IC50 value of 0.05 μg/mL [38].

1.7 Protein extraction methods from algae and green biomass 
 
1.7.1 Conventional extraction methods 
Algal and plant cells are surrounded by a rigid cell wall, which acts as a structural support and 
protection. As mentioned previously, the algae cell wall constituents are mainly anionic 
polysaccharides such as alginate and carrageenan while in plant cell walls the main constituents 
are cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. These constituents  hinders their protein extractability 
as well as their bio-accessibility [46]. For this reason, it is crucial to establish extraction 
methods for aiding the protein recovery from these sources, as it has the potential to be on 
parallel to those of animal origin.  
The most common methods of protein extraction from seaweeds and green biomass, are 
deionized water treatments, acid and alkaline or combined acid-alkaline treatments which are 
followed by recovery/precipitation treatments using pH or heat adjustment, salting out 
techniques such as ammonium sulphate precipitation and ultrafiltration. Additionaly, reducing 
agents such as N-acetyl-L-cysteine (NAC), or 2-mercaptoethanol may also be used to increase 
the extraction yields. Furthermore, in order to increase the extractability of proteins, various 
cell disruption methods such as mechanical processes (grinding, pressing), enzymatic pre-
treatments with proteases or carbohydrases, osmotic shock and ultra-sound treatment are 
usually employed[28] [2][47][48]. A review of the most common pre-treatments methods 
reported throughout the literature will be presented in the following sections. 
 
1.7.2 Pretreatments 
Mechanical  
Physical processes have been used for disrupting the cell wall of plants, macro algae and 
microalgae, cereals etc. The disruption of the cell walls leads to the solubilization of the 
proteins which are located within the cell wall resulting to their better extraction. Mechanical 
homogenization which can be achieved usually by two types of equipment, namely rotor–stator 
homogenizers and open blade mills is one of the most common physical pre-treatment used in 
processes for protein extraction from seaweed and green biomass[4]. Mechanical pulping and 
pressing is another pre-treatment/extraction method used to produce leaf protein concentrates. 
==PAGE:20==
13 
 
The procedure involves pulping and pressing of pulps in one operation to separate the fibrous 
portion from the green juice which contains the majority of proteins [43] . 
High pressure homogenization, is another type of physical pre-treatment that is used for 
improving the protein extraction from various types of biomasses. In this method, cells in 
media are forced through an orifice valve using high pressure. Disruption of the membrane 
occurs due to high shear force at the orifice when the cell is subjected to compression while 
entering the orifice and expansion upon discharge It is mostly used for disruption of bacteria 
cells for extracting nucleic acids and proteins that are located within the cell wall[49]. Parimi 
et al. (2015), reported a 83.5% of protein recovery using high pressure homogenization as 
opposed to to 69.9% using ultrasonication [50]. 
 
Physical  
Osmotic shock which implies the suspension of biomass in a hypertonic solution, has also been 
extensively used as a cell wall disrupting method for seaweeds. The method relies on the 
osmotic pressure which is developed inside the cell due to the concentration difference of 
solutes across the membrane [40]. Veide & Undeland et al. (2016) reported a 46% total protein 
extraction yield from brown seaweed S.latissima using 1hr osmoshocking preatreatment 
combined with alkaline extraction whereas extraction without osmoshocking resulted in 34% 
yield. 
Cavitation is a method which is used for the formation and subsequent rupture of cavities or 
bubbles. These cavities can be formed by reducing the local pressure which can be done by 
increasing the velocity, ultrasonic vibration, etc [40]. Most reported cavitation method used  
for extraction of proteins from algae is ultrasonic cavitation[38][2][[28].Ultrasonic Cavitation 
is a widely known laboratory based technique for disruption of cells. Ultrasonic vibration can 
be used to generate a field of bubbles which grow to a critical size and collapse violently, 
generating extreme temperatures and pressures inside the collapsing bubble. This leads to 
rupture of the cell wall and release of endocellular material[51] Kadam et al. (2016), reported 
a 59% protein recovery using ultrasound pre-treatment combined with alkali extraction[47]. 
 
 
==PAGE:21==

Enzymatic 
Enzymatic degradation of cell wall polymers has received attention for many years and is 
becoming a more attractive alternative to chemical and mechanical processes. It is currently 
being used in many applications including fermentations, biogas production, recovery of 
bioactive compounds, lipids etc. Enzymatic pre-treatments are favoured in many processes as 
they consume less energy due to lower reaction temperatures. Furthermore, they can selectively 
degrade various polymers such as cellulose or alginate which can lead to the recovery of fully 
functional products such as proteins, antioxidants etc compared to traditional 
methods[18][52][53]. 
As far as protein recovery is concerned, enzymes can aid the recovery in several ways. 
Carbohydrases, which can attack the cell wall components, may increase protein extraction 
yield by liberating more protein from the polymeric matrix. Fleurence et al. (1995) using a 
combination of carrageenase and cellulase on algae Chondrus crispus and a combination of 
agarose and cellulase on algae Gracilaria verrucosa reported a ten-fold and three-fold increase 
in protein yield respectively compared to the non-enzymatic treatment [54]. Guan and Yao et 
al. (2008) reported a 56.2 % protein extraction yield from defated oat bran using a combination 
of cell-wall hydrolysing enzymes (Viscozyme L) and alkaline extraction while 14.76% yield 
was obtained by the alkaline treatment alone[55].  
In recent years, the use of proteases for improving protein extraction has also been evaluated 
as they can partially hydrolyze proteins, thus increasing their solubility and making them easier 
to extract. Dotsenko & Lange (2016) reported a 95% protein recovery using a carbohydrase 
pre-treatment followed by protease extraction. It should be mentioned that carbohydrase 
treatment did not release any protein [43]. 
 
1.8 Protein quantification methods  
 
Protein quantification is an integral part of any laboratory process involving protein extraction, 
purification labeling or analysis. Researchers rely on protein quantification methods, to 
elucidate the composition and structure of proteins, determine functions under various 
conditions and compare similar proteins isolated from different organisms or proteins isolated 
from wildtype and mutant plants etc. Quantification methods are also used to determine the 
protein content of foods, biomasses such etc. One of the basic requirements for a protein 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
quantification method is to have the possibility to be used in the presence of diverse 
intracellular components and to exhibit insensitivity to the components of the buffer mixtures 
used for protein extraction. Thus, ideal method should be characterized by a high specificity, 
sensitivity, reproducibility and absence of nonprotein component effects but also be simple and 
rapid. No method can be considered as the ideal as each one has its advantages and 
drawbacks[56]. The most frequently and widely used methods of protein quantification are: the 
Lowry method, the Bradford method, the bicinchoninic acid (BCA) method, UV 
spectrophotometry, Kjeldahl nitrogen content and amino acid analysis [53] [54], [55] [56]. 
 In the present work BCA and Kjeldahl analysis were chosen for determination of protein 
content of extracts and biomasses. In the next section, these methods will be briefly discussed. 
 
Lowry method 
The Lowry assay is based on a two-step procedure. The first step is known as the Biuret reaction 
which involves the reduction of copper (Cu2+ to Cu+) by proteins in alkaline conditions. The 
second step is called the enhancement stage, where it involves the reduction of the Folin–
Ciocalteu reagent (phosphomolybdate and phosphotungstate) in which it interacts with the  
cuprous ions and the side chains of tyrosine, tryptophan, and cysteine producing a blue green 
color that can be detected between 650 and 750[57] [58]. The method is relatively sensitive but 
requires longer incubation than other methods, and it is prone to interference from many 
compounds such as detergents, carbohydrates, glycerol, tricine, EDTA, Tris, potassium salts, 
sulfhydryl compounds, disulfides, phenols, guanine, xanthine, magnesium, and calcium 
interfere with correct assay. Many of these compounds are usually used in buffers for 
homogenization or protein sample preparation. This is one of the main disadvantages of this 
method. In addition, the method is not very suitable for determination of hydrophobic protein 
content or the content of protein in membrane fractions. The Lowry method is also sensitive to 
changes in the proportions of tyrosine and tryptophan residues in proteins[56]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:23==

Bicichronic acid method (BCA) 
The bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay, is similar to the Lowry assay, since it also depends on the 
conversion of Cu2+to Cu+ under alkaline conditions. The Cu + is then detected by reaction 
with BCA. The sensititivity of the assays is similar, but since BCA is stable under alkali 
conditions, this assay has the advantage of being able to be carried out as a one-step process 
compared to the two steps needed in the Lowry assay. The reaction results in the development 
of an intense purple color with an absorbance maximum at 562 nm. Protein concentration of 
unknown samples can be determined spectophotometrically by comparison with known protein 
standards, since the production of Cu+ in this assay is a function of protein concentration and 
incubation time. One more advantage of the BCA assay compared to the Lowry assay it is its 
tolerance to various compounds which can interfere with protein determination in the Lowry 
method. Furthermore, it has high tolerance to various detergents and denaturing agents such as 
urea, guanidinium chloride and SDS, although it is more sensitive to the presence of reducing 
sugars compared to Lowry. The range of determination lies between 0.1-2.0 mg protein/mL for 
standard assays and 0.5-10 μg  protein/mL[59], [60].

Kjeldahl analysis 
The Kjeldahl method is a method of determining the nitrogen content of organic and inorganic 
substances. Although the technique and apparatus have been altered significantly over the past 
100 years, the basic principles introduced by Johan Kjeldahl are the same until today. The 
Kjeldahl method can be divided in three main steps. The first step is the digestion in which the 
nitrogen in organic samples is decomposed using a concentrated acid solution. This is 
accomplished by boiling a homogeneous sample in concentrated sulfuric acid. The end result 
is an ammonium sulfate solution. The next step is the distillation   in which excess base is added 
to the acid digestion mixture to convert NH4 + to NH3, which is received in a solution by 
boiling and condensation. The final step is the titration where the amount of ammonia in the 
receiving solution is quantified. The amount of nitrogen in a sample can be calculated from the 
quantified amount of ammonia ions in the receiving solution[61]. In order to calculate the 
protein content of foodstuff or other types of biomasses the nitrogen content should be 
multiplied by a N-to-Protein factor. The most common factor used especially in food is set at 
6.25. This historical factor (dating back to the 19th century) assumes the nitrogen content of 
proteins to be 16%. It is crucial to use correct N-to-protein factors as the common 6.25 factor 
could result in over or under estimation due to, presence of non-protein nitrogen compounds 
or from differences in amino acid composition of the proteins [62]. The reactions involved in 
the Kjeldahl analysis are shown below. 
 
Digestion:  Protein + H2SO4                  (NH4)2SO4 (aq) + CO2 (g) + SO2(g) + H2O(gas) 
 Distillation: (NH4)2SO4(aq) + 2NaOH               Na2SO4(aq) + 2H2O(l) + 2NH3(g) 
Titration: H3BO3+ NH3                 NH4 + + H2BO3-

1.9 Enzymatic activity discovery 
 
As petroleum-based energy sources are radically depleted it is of great importance to find new 
alternative sources to ensure a reliable future source of energy and raw materials. Biorefinery 
is a concept which has received big interest over the decades, and relies on the utilization of 
sustainable biomass. The purpose of biorefinery is to integrate biomass conversion processes 
and equipment to produce fuels, power, and chemicals from biomass, instead of conventional 
oil refinery processes. The great advantage of this concept is that can utilize inexpensive 
lignocellulosic agricultural byproducts and industrial waste. These byproducts comprise a rich 
and renewable source of sugars, which can be used as feedstock for the production of liquid 
fuels, chemicals and other products through biorefinery processes[63]. Degrading enzymes 
play a very crucial role in biorefinery processes. They responsible for the degradation of the 
biomass or industrial waste to fermentable sugars which are utilized by microorganisms and 
converted to desired chemical products, including alcohols, fatty acids, organic acids through 
microbial fermentation. Bioethanol which is produced through biorefinery processes is 
considered as one of the most promising alternatives to traditional petroleum-based fuels[64]. 
Despite that, the complex structure of lignocellulosic materials, makes the biorefinery process 
and particularly the bioconversion step, a big challenge compared to traditional sugar- substrate 
fermentations. Lignocellulosic materials are mainly composed of cellulose, hemicellulose and 
lignin (figure 8) Cellulose forms microfibrils of high crystallinity consisting of homopolymers 
of b-1,4-linked glucose units embedded in a hemicellulose, pectin and lignin matrix[65]. This 
polysaccharide conformation makes the structure resistant to saccharification by hydrolytic 
enzymes. Furthermore, the hydrolytic enzymes endoglucanase, exoglucanase, and b-
glucosidase which are required for releasing sugars from cellulose and hemicellulose, are 
inhibited by their intermediary and final products, such as cellooligosaccharides and 
glucose[64][66]. To overcome this, simultaneous saccharification and fermentation (SSF) 
where enzymatic hydrolysis is combined with sugar consumption in one step is preferred. 
However, the large difference in optimum temperatures between saccharification and 
fermentation requires the need for large amounts of saccharification enzymes, which greatly 
impacts the cost efficiency of a biorefinery process[7].  Therefore, new and more efficient 
hydrolysing enzymes are needed to overcome the challenges associated with lignocellulosic 
bioconversion processes.

To identify new polysaccharide-degrading enzymes, namely glycosyl hydrolases (GHs) with 
desired properties, activity measurement assays are employed. Several techniques exist for 
assaying GH enzymes such as oligosaccharide analysis using high performance 
chromatography, or mass spectrometry for obtaining information about the product identities. 
GH activities can also be monitored by measuring the generation of reducing ends, for example, 
using the 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid [67] and phydroxybenzoic acid hydrazide (PAHBAH) [68] 
method. However, these reducing end assays also suffer from limited throughput and can be 
prone to side reactions. 
Enzymatic activities can be also measure using chromogenic substrates. These substrates are 
chemically modified forms of natural substrates such as cellulose which release soluble or 
fluorescent groups when their glucosidic bonds are being cleaved. There are various 
chromogenic substrates depending on the enzyme activity being measured. Para-nitrophenyl 
(pNP) glycosides are being used for rapidly assaying GH activities their use is limited for the 
study of high molecular weight and sometimes crystalline substrates such as chitin, cellulose 
and some arabinoxylans[69].

P. salina with P. arenariae are extremely widespread species which have been found in all of

Extracellular GH enzymatic activity of P.salina was assesed using azurine cross-linked 
(AZCL) polysaccharides. This type of chromogenic substrates is produced by cross-linking 
polysaccharides to make them insoluble and then inserting dye to them. When exposed to an 
enzyme with appropriate activity, small dyed oligosaccharide products are released changing 
the colour of the reaction. There are two ways to measure activity using AZCL substrates. The 
first way, is by performing the enzymatic reaction with the substrates in multi-well (usually 
96) plates, and measuring the change in colour of the reaction supernatants 
spectrophotometrically. The second is, by incorporating the substrates into agar plates, and 
measuring the diameter of the formed halos caused by the diffusion of the released 
oligosaccharides into the gel. Despite their speed and relatively low cost they do have some 
disadvantages with most important being the colour of the substrates. They are available only 
in blue colour which limits the throughput since one substrate per reaction can be tested[72].

2.2 Enzymatic extraction of algal proteins 
 
Biomass proteolysis reactions were performed in 2 mL Eppendorf tubes under continuous 
shaking (1400 rpm) (Figure 1). In brief, lyophilized, grinded biomass (22.5 mg dry matter, 
0.5mm) was mixed with 1 mL enzyme solution. Biomass samples were incubated in a 
thermomixer at optimum conditions of the respective enzymes with varied enzyme 
concentrations as shown in table 1, for 1 h. After 1 h, each sample was clarified by 
centrifugation at 13,000 rpm for 30 min to remove the non-hydrolyzed biomass. Supernatants 
were thereafter collected and heated to 100 °C for 10 min to deactivate proteases. Finally, the 
protein concentration of the supernatants was determined and then samples were stored at -20

enzyme were used as controls. The values of protein concentration contained in the enzyme 
solutions were subtracted from the protein concentration of the samples and results were 
presented as mean ± standard deviation. Biomass proteolysis procedures were performed in 
independent triplicates (n=3).

Fungus and Culture conditions 
105 spores/mL of P. salina CBS112865 were collected in 3ml 10% glycerol solution from 
oatmeal agar plate consisting of 2% oats (Finax, Denmark) and 1,5% agar and inoculated in 
250 L Erlenmeyer flasks containing 50 mL of defined medium adapted from Ramada, M.H.S 
et al. 2016 [74]. It was composed of: 0.1% (w/v) bactopeptone (Bacto, Australia), 0.03% (w/v) 
urea, 0.2% (w/v) KH2PO4, 0.14% (w/v), (NH4)2SO4, 0.03% (w/v), MgSO4.7H2O, 0,06% 
Tween80, 0,1% glucose and 2% (v/v) trace elements solution containing 0.025% (w/v) FeSO4, 
0.0085% (w/v) MnSO4, 0.007% (w/v) ZnSO4 and 0.01% (w/v) CaCl2. Unless otherwise stated 
all chemicals were from Sigma, Germany. The medium was suplemented with either 2% 
granulated freezedried S. latissima or 2% wheat bran as major carbon source and autoclaved at 
121C for 20minutes. Three biological replicates were grown in a shaking incubator at 180rpm 
and 23C for 7 days. On the last day of the fermentation the culture was filtered in miracloth 
and spun down at 10.000g for 10 min. The supernatants were immediately used for enzymatic 
assays. 
Biomass degrading enzymatic activity determination 
Azurine-Cross-Linked substrates (Megazyme, Ireland) were used in a downscaled protocol for 
96 well microtitter plates. 0,1% agarose was dissolved in miliq water and cooled to 60C before 
adding 0,01% of substrate. The buffers consisted of 0,07M phosphoris acid, 0,07M acetic acid 
and 0,07M boric acid adjusted to desired pH with 5M sodium hydroxide. Each well in the 
microtiter plates were filled with 100 μL substrate, 100μL buffer, 100μL sample (culture 
supernatant), covered and placed in incubation at 30C for 20 hours. After the incubation, the 
plates were spun down at 3000g for 15min., and the absorbance of the supernatants containing 
released blue dye were read at 590nm in a Tecan microplate reader. The background controls 
consisted of fermentation medium without inoculum added to AZCL substrates. These were 
deducted the sample absorbance results.

4. Discussion 
 
4.1 Alcalase can enhance protein recovery from S.latissima 
The extraction of compounds from seaweed can be greatly hindered by the various cell wall 
polysaccharides which limit the active compound accessibility. Enzymatic degradation of cell 
wall polymers has received great attention in the past few years and is becoming an attractive 
alternative to chemical and mechanical processes. Degradation of plant cell wall 
polysaccharides is one of the most common route as far as extraction of compounds is 
concerned hence the frequent use of polysaccharidasses [2]. 
The first aim of the present work was to test whether the use of proteases instead of 
polysaccharidases could assist in protein release. As mentioned in the theory part, the brown 
seaweed cell wall comprises of mainly sulphated fucans and alginates. Despite that, it has been 
discovered that cell wall proteins comprises a significant amount of the algal dry weight around 
(5-9%) [14] and are tightly associated with fucose-containing sulfated polysaccharides 
(FCSPs)  and cellulose within the walls.[11]. This knowledge, led to the assumption that 
proteases could be used to cleave proteins that are embedded within the cell wall by selectively 
hydrolysing proteins. 
Extraction using Alcalase resulted in significantly higher protein release 72.04 ± 0.07 % (w 
protein extracted/w total protein) compared to papain 31.3 % (w/w) and pepsin 33.37 % (w/w). 
The significantly higher protein release observed using Alcalase might have been caused due 
to the higher extraction temperatures (enzyme temperature optima) 55 °C versus 37 °C which 
was used for the papain and pepsin extraction schemes. Also, differences in proteolytic activity 
can be a possible explanation although the activity of the enzymes was not assessed. However, 
only Alcalase treatment showed significant increase in extraction yield compared to the control 
(no enzyme) with yields reaching up to 72% of total protein compared to 31% of the control. 
To the best of the author’ knowledge, there has been limited research on protease assisted 
protein extraction from algae. Sari et al. (2012), demonstrated that commercial proteases 
(Protex 40XL, Protex P, Protex 5L, Protex 50FP, and Protex 26L) increased protein recovery 
from microalgae compared to the control extractions (no enzymes added). More specifically, a 
5% (v/w of protein in sample) dosage of Protex 40XL resulted in 60% extraction yield 
compared to 20% achieved without enzymes[6].  
==PAGE:47==
40 
 
Besides protein extraction, the use of proteases for extraction of bioactive components from 
brown algae such as polyphenols, phlorotannins, polysaccharides, carotenoids and peptides has 
been thoroughly investigated [36][52]. Furthermore, it has been reported that brown seaweed 
protease extracts show significant antioxidant [75][42] and immunomodulatory effects[76]. 
Heo et al.,[77] investigated the protective effect of brown seaweed Ecklonia cava enzymatic 
extracts on hydrogen peroxide-induced cell damage. Proteases (Protamex, Kojizyme 500 MG, 
Neutrase 0.8L, Flavourzyme 500 MG, and Alcalase 2.4L FG) extracts exhibited hydrogen 
peroxide activity ranging from 40-68%.  
By evaluating the results of the protease assisted protein extraction, it can be concluded that 
Alcalase can be used for the extraction of proteins/peptides from S.latissima. Bearing in mind 
that peptides from seaweeds have been found to exhibit a variety of biological activities, it 
would be interesting to study the potential of S.latissima peptides as health promoting products.  
 
4.2 Aqueous/ alkaline extraction 
 
Extraction solvents 
The second aim of this work was to investigate the extractability of intact proteins from the 
brown seaweed S.latissima. Aqueous and alkaline processes were chosen for investigation and 
various parameters were tested to find optimum extraction conditions. The first step was to 
evaluate the extraction efficiency of a number of extraction solvents. For aqueous extraction, 
deionized water was selected as it has been reported that hypotonic solutions, can create an 
osmotic shock effect which leads to the disruption of the seaweed cell wall and subsequently 
to the release of protein in the extraction solvent [48][28].For alkaline extraction NaOH was 
selected at concentrations ranging from 0.1mM to 100 mM as it has been reported that alkaline 
solutions (NaOH) effectively solubilise and aid in the extraction of highly water insoluble 
proteins from macroalgae and microalgae[25][78]. Furthermore NaOH is a food grade solvent 
which has been extensively used for the extraction of protein from plant sources (soy)[79] and 
fish muscle proteins[80]. Extraction with 100 mM NaOH gave the highest extraction yields 
compared to the other solvents used which is in agreements with findings from other authors.

NaOH concentrations, from ~5 (g/100g dry weight) at 0.08 M NaOH to ~7 (g/100g dry weight)

Biomass pre-treatment, extraction duration and temperature 
The next step of finding the optimum extraction process was to assess the effects of the biomass 
pre-treatment, extraction duration and temperature on both aqueous and alkaline protein 
extraction procedures.  
 
Biomass pre-treatment 
A factor that can greatly influence the extractability of proteins from seaweeds it’s the biomass 
pre-treatment, eg. by drying methods, mechanical methods etc. In the present work, 
lyophilization pre-treatment was tested and showed that had little effect on the aqueous protein 
recovery, as far as the highest protein yield was concerned. The same was observed for the 
alginate extracted fraction (table 7). In the case of the alkaline extractions, lyophilization had 
a negative effect compared to the raw biomass which is in agreement with the findings of 
Barbarino & Lourenco (2005) [25]. As mentioned earlier, extraction of algal proteins is 
difficult because of the presence of large amounts of anionic or neutral polysaccharides as well 
as phenolic compounds within the cell wall [28]. These polysaccharides can form highly 
viscous solutions and hinder the extraction and purification procedures for proteins [5]. 
Furthermore,  phenolic compounds can also negatively affect and limit  the protein extraction 
efficiency as they may reversibly complex with proteins via hydrogen bonding or be oxidized 
to quinones and combine irreversibly with protein molecules [84]. The negative effect of the 
phenolic compounds on algal protein recovery was confirmed by Wong et al (2000), who 
studied the effect of drying pre-treatment on protein extractability from three brown seaweeds 
using oven and freeze drying. He found that oven-drying improved significantly the protein 
extractability compared to the freeze-drying method. Also, he showed that the overall yield 
was negatively correlated with the total phenolic contents in the seaweed samples. This led to 
the assumption that the significantly higher overall yield achieved with oven-drying might be 
closely related to the significantly lower total phenolic contents in the oven-dried seaweed 
samples[85].  
 
 
 
==PAGE:50==

Duration and Temperature 
 Two other significant factors that can greatly influence the extractability of proteins is the 
duration and temperature of the extraction. It was shown that in the case of the aqueous 
extractions using lyophilized seaweed, extending the extraction duration past the 4hr time 
frame had a negative effect regardless of the extraction temperature. In contrast, for the aqueous 
extraction using raw homogenized seaweed, extending the extraction period up to 24 h resulted 
in higher protein release at 25 °C and 40 °C while it decreased significantly at 55 °C. Also, a 
24h extraction at 55 °C using the alginate extraction residue showed to give higher yields 
compared to extractions at lower temperatures and shorter extraction durations.  For the 
alkaline extraction, extending the extraction duration from 1hr to 4h and 24 h using lyophilized 
seaweed had a negative effect regardless of the extraction temperature. In contrast when using 
raw homogenized seaweed extension of the duration from 1 to 4h had a positive for all 
temperatures while extension to 24h had a negative effect regardless of the temperature. 
As far as the extraction temperature was concerned, higher temperatures (55 °C) showed to 
have a positive effect on the extraction yield for all types of starting material (freeze-dried, 
raw) at 1 and 4h durations while extending the extraction to 24 h resulted in decrease of yield. 
The positive effect of high temperature on the extraction yield observed in the present work is 
in agreement with the findings of Mæhre et al., (2016). In brief, the author reported an increase 
of protein recovery from red seaweed P. palmata using alkaline extraction (NaOH 0.1M) when 
the extraction temperature was increased from 23 °C (28.9 % w/w DM) to 60 °C (39.1% w/w 
DM) [46] High temperatures have also been used for extraction of proteins from other 
biomasses as shown in table 13. 
By evaluating the results, it was decided that the optimum extraction process regarding the 
extraction yield, duration and economic viability was alkaline extraction at 55°C, 1hr using 
raw homogenized seaweed which resulted in extraction of 62.07 ± 0.99 % (1.99 ± 0.02) of the 
total protein. It was concluded that a combination of high, pH (13) and temperature (55°C) and 
low extraction duration (1-4 h) are the optimum conditions for protein extraction from 
S.latissima as far as the conditions tested in this work are concerned. Furthermore, freeze-
drying as a pre-treatment does not improve the protein extractability. Despite that, it should be 
noted that extraction of proteins at high temperatures combined with alkaline treatment could 
have a negative impact on the functionality of the protein which is something that must be 
taken into consideration when extracting proteins [86].

4.3 Effect of ultrasonication on extractability of algal and green biomass 
proteins 
 
When optimum extraction conditions were determined, the extraction was repeated on larger 
scale (40 ml) to assess the effect of ultrasonication on the extractability of proteins. The same 
extraction method was used for extraction of protein from rye grass and red clover pulps. 
Extraction of algal proteins with deionized water was also performed for comparison of the 
two extractions.  
 
Algal protein extraction 
Various factors such as the sonication duration, amplitude and the temperature of the biomass 
suspension during the sonication, should be taken into consideration when determining the 
efficiency of the ultra sound pre-treatment. This was apparent as the ultrasound pre-treatment 
test which lasted 30min with no control of the temperature of the suspension (eg. with ice) 
resulted in an increase of extraction yield from 57.45 ± 2.74% (untreated) to 82.96 ± 1.18% as 
measured by BCA (results not shown). This higher protein release was probably attributed to 
a combination of the sonication’s efficiency in breaking the algae cell wall as well as the big 
increase of the suspension’s temperature caused by the sonication. Because it was not clear 
whether the sonication itself or the temperature increase caused the high protein release 
compared to the untreated sample, it was decided to run the sonication for 3 min, split in 1 min 
segments and incubating the falcon tube with the biomass suspension on ice to reduce heat 
increase. The ultrasonication treatment (3min) had a slight effect on the protein extractability 
using aqueous extraction while for the alkaline extraction it resulted in lower or same protein 
release compared to the untreated as measured by BCA and Kjeldahl respectively. Alkaline 
extraction yield with and without sonication, as determined by Kjeldahl analysis, was 38.86 ± 
2.49 % and 34.94 ± 6.38 % of total protein respectively (table 9). 
The results on the effectiveness of ultrasonication on the extractability of algal proteins 
reported in literature are scarce and is some cases contradicting which makes it difficult to 
determine whether it’s a suitable method for aiding the protein release from algal biomass. 
Kadam et al., (2016) reported that use of ultrasound as a pre-treatment for improving the 
alkaline extraction of proteins from brown seaweed Ascophyllum nodosum. Using ultrasound 
at amplitude levels of 22.8 μm and 68.4 μm resulted in protein recovery of 26.27 ± 1.74% and 
==PAGE:52==
45 
 
57.23 ± 2.31 % respectively while alkaline extraction with no sonication led to a protein 
recovery of 51.80 ± 1.74% [47]. Alternating two counter-current frequencies has also been 
evaluated as a viable method for improving protein extraction, which resulted in a 50% increase 
in yield and 18% reduction in extraction time using 15 and 20 kHz in Porphyra yezoensis 
compared to mono-frequency ultrasound-assisted extraction [2].  
 
Green biomass protein extraction 
Highest protein recovery was observed in the rye grass extractions, 105 ± 7.90 %, 56.52 ± 
8.45% w/w of total protein as measured with BCA and Kjeldahl analysis respectively while 
protein release from red clover was only 46.84 ± 1.81%, 6.84± 0.02 % w/ w of total protein as 
BCA and Kjeldahl analysis showed (table 12). Ultra sound pre-treatment showed to be an 
effective method to aid the extraction of proteins as it resulted in approximately 80% and 124% 
increase in protein extraction yields from ryegrass and red clover pulps respectively compared 
to the non-treated samples. Dotsenko & Lange.; (2016) using extraction conditions of 50°C, 
pH 8.0 and 72hr duration, reported an extraction yield from rye grass of 31% without and 40% 
with addition of Tween 80[43]. By taking into account the findings of Dotsenko & Lange as 
well as the findings of the present work it can be assumed that increase of pH can improve the 
extraction of proteins from rye grass pulp and shorten the duration of the process. This is in 
agreement with the findings regarding the extraction of protein from S.latissima mentioned 
previously. 
 
4.4 Effect of combined alginate lyase-cellulase- laminarinase pre-treatment 
followed by alkaline protein extraction  
 
The aim of this process was to investigate the use of a combined alginate lyase-cellulase- 
laminarinase pre-treatment for extracting intact or semi-intact proteins from S. latissima. It was 
hypothesized that the use of a carbohydrase pre-treatment to degrade the cell wall would 
improve the alkaline extraction of high molecular weight proteins. The rationale behind the 
selection of the particular enzyme combination for the pre-treatment was the fact that cell walls 
consist mainly of alginate (45 % of dry algal weight) and 1-8% cellulose hence selective 
degradation of these polysaccharides could improve protein extraction. Laminarinase was also 
==PAGE:53==
46 
 
included as it has been reported that brown algae can contain laminarin up to 35% of their dry 
weight [87]. 
To evaluate the effect of the 24hr enzymatic pre-treatment combined with a sequential 1hr 
alkaline extraction, a control sample without enzymes was extracted at the same conditions 
followed by an alkaline extraction. The enzymatic pre-treatment resulted in release of ~27% of 
total protein content (table 10). This is in accordance with Manns et al.; 2013 findings which 
they reported a 71% protein by weight recovery in the solid residue by using a combination of 
alginate lyase (Sigma) and a cellulase preparation (Cellic®CTec2) on S. latissima for 24hrs 
[88] . 
Enzymatic pre-treatment resulted in higher protein release compared to the control sample, 
however the protein release in the subsequent alkaline extraction was higher in the case of the 
control sample compared to the enzymatically pre-treated. Also, a comparison of the total 
protein yields obtained from the enzymatically pre-treated and control samples shows that 
enzyme pre-treatment does not increase protein extraction which leads to the conclusion that a 
combination of only a buffer pre-treatment and alkaline extraction can be an effective protein 
extraction process. Finally, a comparison of the extraction yields obtained with the enzymatic 
pre-treatment method (52.57 ± 3.92 % w/w of total protein) and the 1hr alkaline extraction 
(68.29 ± 4.84 % w/w of total protein, table 7) indicates that the latter is a more efficient method 
than the former. This finding is in contrast with the results reported by Hanne K. Mæhre et al., 
(2016) [48] which by using an enzymatic pre-treatment containing xylanase and cellulase prior 
to alkaline extraction increased the yield by 160% compared to the standard alkaline extraction. 
The use of xylanases for extracting proteins from P. palmata has also been reported by 
Fleurence et al (1995) [54]. In the case of P. palmata, cell walls have been reported to be 
essentially composed of neutral mixed linked xylans and a minor amount of cellulose 
associated with β-(1,4)-linked d-xylans [89]. 
 
4.5  Variances between yields measured with BCA and Kjeldahl 
 
Determination of the protein content of algae is critical when developing protein extraction 
methods and assessing the efficiency of the method. The most common methods used for 
quantification of algae proteins are (i) the alkaline copper method (Lowry et al., 1951) [90]: 
(ii) the Coomassie Brilliant Blue dye method (Bradford , 1976) or (iii) determination of crude 
==PAGE:54==
47 
 
protein by measuring nitrogen content and multiplying it by a nitrogen N-to-Protein factor, eg. 
N × 6.25 [25]. The calculation of protein content by N × 6.25 should be used carefully as plants, 
fungi and algae have high concentrations of non-protein nitrogenaceous substances such as 
pigments (chlorophyll and phycoerythrin), nucleic acids, free amino acids and inorganic 
nitrogen (nitrate, nitrite and ammonia)which can cause an overestimation of the actual protein 
content [91] [73][92].  
In the present work, total (crude) algal and green biomass protein content was quantified using 
Kjeldahl analysis for total nitrogen determination and then multiplying it by a N-to-protein 
factor. For algae, a factor of 5.6 (N×5.6) was used, as suggested by Bogolitsyn et al. 2014.; 
who determined the amino acid composition of 25 samples of four commercial Arctic brown 
algae including Saccharina latissima [73] and a factor of 6.25 (N×6.25) for green biomass. 
Soluble protein in the extracts was quantified using the bicichronic assay (BCA) since it 
exhibits greater tolerance to many commonly used buffers and detergents compared to the 
alkaline copper method( Lowry) [93] and also Kjeldahl for comparison of methods. The BCA 
method was found to overestimate the protein content in almost all samples by >100% 
compared to the Kjeldahl method (table 9&12). This overestimation could have been caused 
by the possible presence of lipids in the extract as various pre-treatments such as 
osmoshocking, ultrasound and enzymatic can aid in lipid extraction [94].  Morton & Evans 
(1992) investigated the lipid interference in determining lipoprotein protein content using the 
BCA method and found that the standard BCA assay overestimated the protein content of very 
low-density lipoprotein by -70% and low -density lipoprotein by -30% compared to a standard 
modification of the Lowry assay which is the most used method reported in relevant literature 
[93]. One common method that is used to help avoiding these interferences is the precipitation 
of protein with TCA (trichloroacetic acid) or cold acetone [25]. Despite that, protein estimation 
could be underestimated because of un-precipitated protein or amino acids. It should also be 
noted that, yields in the present work were calculated as the ratio of protein in extract measured  
by BCA or Kjeldahl to the biomass initial protein content measured by Kjeldahl. This could 
lead to wrong yield estimations and can be avoided if the same quantification method was used 
for both biomass and extract. Therefore, to counter the problems mentioned in this section, 
amino acid analysis should be used as it is considered the most accurate protein quantification 
method.

4.6   Molecular weight of algal and green biomass proteins 
 
The SDS-PAGE analysis of the aqueous extraction proteins from S. latissima revealed several 
bands ranging from low molecular weights ~16kDa to high molecular weights 100 kDa. This 
was in contrast with the SDS-PAGE analysis of the alkaline extracted proteins which revealed 
mostly high molecular weight proteins ranging from 65-280 kDa. A single 250 kDa protein 
was also revealed from the SDS-PAGE analysis of the green biomass pulps. The differences in 
the molecular weight of the proteins extracted by alkaline and aqueous extraction could be 
explained by the Osborne theory. Osborne described the fractionation of plant proteins based 
on differences in solubility. In brief, he categorized protein in four groups. Albumins, which 
are water-soluble. Globulins which may be enzymes or storage proteins and are extractable by 
salt-solutions and glutelins, which are structural proteins, and can  be extracted with dilute acid 
or alkali or with detergents[4]. Despite the fact that seaweeds are not considered as plants and 
therefore they might not have the same types of proteins, the findings of Osborne or the idea 
behind it that different types of protein can be solubilized with different solvents can explain 
the results found in the present work. It should be also mentioned, that higher molecular weight 
bands in the case of the alkaline extraction,  might be a result of the  alkaline conditions which 
can cause protein cross-linking through the formation of lanthinonine, lysinoalanine, or 
dehydroalanine [95].  
 
4.7 Plant biomass-degrading extracellular activity of P. salina 
 
Fungi are eukaryotic microorganisms that are highly successful in survival because of their 
great plasticity and physiological versatility. They can be found in any type of habitat and can 
thrive under extreme environmental conditions because of their efficient enzyme system. Their 
ability to produce extracellular enzymes is of great survival value. This ability has been 
extensively used for the biodegradation of undesirable materials or compounds and conversion 
into harmless, tolerable,or useful products. P. salina also known as dendryphiella salina is an 
under-exploited saprobic or parasitic fungus which is found in marine habitats. It belongs to 
the genus Paradendryphiella, which comprises only two species: the marine P. salina and P. 
arenariae. In the present work, evaluation of glycosyl hydrolase extracellular activity of 
P.salina using twelve AZCL polysaccharides showed enzymatic activity with positivity to 
xylanase, amylase, mannase, cellulase and β-glucanase. Similar findings were reported by 
==PAGE:56==
49 
 
Gessner et al (1980). He evaluated the ability of twenty higher filamentous fungi including 
D.salina to produce degradative enzymes when grown on their corresponding substrates( eg. 
grown on medium containing lipid as the primary carbon source for determination of lipase 
activity). D. salina exhibited the highest cellulase, β-glucosidase, carboxymethyl cellulase, 
lipase and amylase activities compared to all the other fungi and showed positivity to xylanase 
activity [96]. Various authors have also confirmed the fungus ability to degrade algal 
polysaccharides such as alginate and laminarin [97][98][70] [99]. These findings show that P. 
salina is a very promising fungus that can produce a wide range of degrading enzymes and 
therefore can be utilized in biorefinery processes for aiding degradation of both plant and algal 
biomass.

Conclusions and future perspectives 
With the expected to grow of global population from 7.2 billion people to 9.6 billion by 2050, 
and therefore the large increase in protein demand, the need for alternative protein sources is 
greater than ever. Seaweeds and plant biomass are promising protein sources with protein 
content on parallel or even greater than conventional protein rich foods such as soybean, 
cereals, eggs and fish. Despite that, little attention has been placed on exploiting these sources 
for production of sustainable protein.  
In the present work the extraction of proteins from the brown seaweed S. latissima and plant 
biomass namely rye grass and red clover pulps was investigated. For the recovery of protein 
from S. latissima, protease enhanced extraction, aqueous and alkaline extractions as well as 
alkaline extractions combined with a polysaccharidase pre-treatment were investigated. 
Proteases employment and particularly Alcalase (Novozymes), was proven to aid protein 
recovery with yields reach up to 72g/100g of total protein. Furthermore, the present work 
identified number of critical parameters, in particular the extraction temperature and duration 
as well as the extraction solvent which affect the yield of aqueous and alkaline extractions.  It 
was found that extraction at high pH (13), combined with low duration and high temperatures 
facilitate the highest protein recovery as far as the conditions used in this work are concerned. 
Furthermore, it was shown that physical pre-treatments (freeze drying, 3 min ultrasonication) 
as well as the polysaccharidase pre-treatment are not effective methods for improving the 
extraction of alkaline soluble proteins from S.latissima. 
Alkaline protein extraction of plant biomass, namely ryegrass and red clover pulps using the 
optimum conditions mentioned before, proved to be an effective method for recovering protein 
only for rye grass pulps. Ultrasonication pre-treatment (3 min) was found to significantly 
improve the protein extractability from both rye grass and red clover pulps. 
Finally, the evaluation of the biomass-degrading extracellular activity of the fungus P. salina 
using AZCL polysaccharide substrates, demonstrated the ability of the fungus to produce a 
wide range of polysaccharide degrading enzymes. 
The present report has proven the extractability of proteins from both S.latissima and rye and 
red clover pulps using different approaches. Despite that, there are several factors, not 
examined in this work, that can affect the protein extractability such as solvent to biomass ratio 
==PAGE:58==
51 
 
[100]and pre-treatment methods (defatting, oven drying etc)[85] which should be investigated. 
Sequential aqueous and alkaline extractions should also be studied as it has been reported that 
proteins can be categorized in different categories according to their solubility [4]. The ability 
of the fungus P.salina to produce a wide range of polysaccharide degrading enzymes was also 
proved. This finding as well as the reported ability of the fungus to degrade algal 
polysaccharides should be investigated in regard to improving current plant and algal protein 
biorefinery processes.
