Abstract
In this report we explore some of the near-field properties of dielectric nanopillar
cavities with the intention of using them for localizing single photon emitters. We
outline the fabrication and characterization of several physical pillar arrays and com-
pare them with theoretical measures of optimization, such as cavity resonance, field
enhancement and purcell effect. All our fabricated pillars are found to be lower than
estimated in the fabrication process. We numerically find field enhancements in the
range ~0.5 to 4.2.
Likewise we find purcell enhancement ranging from ~1.8 to 2.
We also characterize scattering from several pillars using dark-field spectroscopy and
compare them with numerical simulations. It is concluded that a broader white light
light excitation source would be needed to resolve potential scattering features above
2.9 eV.

Introduction
Solid state single photon emitters (SPEs) have come to play an important role in
many emerging quantum technologies, such as quantum communication [1–3], quan-
tum computing [4–6] and quantum sensing [7].
Many of these quantum emitters
only work at cryogenic temperatures [8], while hexagonal Boron Nitride (hBN) hosts
luminecence centers that have been demonstrated as SPEs at room temperature [9].
The microscopic nature of these emitters remain illusive however – and various works
have attributed their emission to atomic structure defects such as nitrogen vacancy
sites [10] and, more recently, carbon atoms replacing atoms in the structure [11].
Recently our group has developed a novel method of generating luminescence centers
in high-quality hBN through oxygen irradiation and subsequent annealing [12] how-
ever their locations remained random. In transition metal dichalcogenides such as
WSe2, it has been shown that SPEs can be generated at local strain maxima [13–15].
This poses the question if an effect like this also appears in hBN and could be used
to promote site-selectivity among generated luminescence centers. The overarching
project this report is part of explores this idea by transferring high-quality, few-layer
hBN on top of dielectric nanopillar arrays to induce strain in the material. In this
report we aim to better understand the near-field effects between defects localized on
top of the pillars and the pillars themselves. This is done through simplified analytic
derivation as well as numeric simulations.
Throughout the project several batches of dielectric pillars were fabricated with dif-
ferent dimensional parameters. These were made to try and strike a balance between
maximizing strain and avoiding piercing or washing away the hBN flake during trans-
fer to the pillars. This is covered in more detail in [16]. In this report we focus on
verifying the physical dimensions of the fabricated pillars through SEM and AFM
measurements.
Finally we explore the scattering characteristics of several pillars using dark-field
spectroscopy and numerical simulation.

CHAPTER 1
Fabrication of physical
pillars
Throughout the project several batches of SiO2 pillars were fabricated in DTU’s
cleanroom. The dimensions of these pillars were chosen to cover a fairly wide range
– Particularly in height since it potentially affects the amount of strain imposed on a
2D material deposited on top.
In this chapter we quantitatively investigate the dimensions of the available pillars
and the divergence from fabrication specifications. These results will help us perform
more accurate numerical predictions in a later chapter.
All cleanroom activities, as well as SEM measurements, were performed by Aurimas
Sakanas1.
1.1
Fabrication Process
The pillars were fabricated by spin-coating the negative resist hydrogen silsesquioxane
(HSQ) onto a silicon wafer. When exposed using electron beam lithography (EBL),
HSQ turns into cross-linked silicon oxide which is resistant to solvents [17].
The
angular frequency of the spin-coater as well as the type of HSQ solution determines
the thickness of the resist – and by extension the height of the pillars.
Figure 1.1 shows the mask layout used for the lithography step. The diameter of the
pillars is determined by the mask as well as the EBL dose [18]. The base mask consists
of 5x5 pillar arrays of varying radius and pitch (pillar-to-pillar distance). This pattern
is repeated four times on each chip, with a total of 8x8 chips per batch/wafer. The
specified diameters were 100, 150, 200, 250 and 300 nm. However, in many cases only
the 200-300 nm pillars remained after the fabrication process. The specified pitches
were 1, 2.5, 5, 7.5 and 10 µm. Selecting a pitch to deposit a hBN flake on top of is a
trade-off between probability of ”tent” formation versus amount of pillars covered by
hBN2. This range of pitches allows for a good deal of experimentation in finding the

1.3
AFM measurements of the pillar heights
To get a more accurate idea of the height of our pillars we did atomic force microscopy
(AFM) measurements on all but the 1690 nm and 1140 nm batch as these were never
used due to their extreme height. The measurement was done in a 20x20 µm2 area on
the 250 nm diameter, 2.5 nm pitch array when it was available, giving us roughly 8x8
pillars per measurement. Due to the comparatively quite large height variation in the
map artifacts tend to occur along the scan lines. An example of a scan line with this
phenomenon is shown in figure 1.3c. These lines were corrected using a second order
polynomial in the context that the ”floor” should line up with the neighbouring lines.
The map before and after correction is shown in figure 1.3a and 1.3b respectively.
The mean heights as well as the calculated standard deviation of the pillars are
shown in table 1.2. For all batches, we obtain an actual height that is smaller than
the ellipsometry estimated height. For the HSQ solution FOx-16 with an estimated
height of 930 nm we observe an actual height of 904.9 nm resulting in a deviation of
25.2 nm. For the 550 nm FOx-15 batch, this deviation is 142.8 and 111.9 nm for the
chips G3 and G7, respectively – a much larger deviation than for the 930 nm batch.
Generally it appears that the FOx-15 batches have larger deviations than those made
with FOx-16 and XR-1541-006.
During the initial ellipsometry measurements it was noticed that the resist was not
even across the wafer after spin coating. This is in agreement with what we see across
the different chips measured on the 550 nm and 800 nm batch. To get a better picture
of this variation one would have to perform a measurements across several chips on
the wafer, however we only had a limited number of chips available (With the rest
being in use or still in the clean room).

In this chapter we extend our model to 3D and use numerical solution1 to predict the
best dimensional design parameters for our pillars.
In the last chapter we used resonance to try and get an idea of how to pick good pillar
dimensions. Practically we would like to establish some more concrete measures of
what makes a ”good” pillar than just ”resonance”. To this extent we will explore two
different measures in this chapter and discuss their implications.
3.1
Field enhancement
As shown in figure 2.1b we predict a field maximum along the edge of the pillar.
Conveniently this is also where we expect any luminescence centers to appear in the
treatment process as this is where the strain of any deposited hBN will be highest [21].
We can predict interband transition rates in a quantum mechanical system using
Fermi’s golden rule with the dipole approximation:

of measurement. The location of this point is chosen where the field enhancement is
the largest – along the axis of polarization. We can easily see that this is the case
in figure 3.1b which shows the simulated field enhancement for an XY-slice at the

CHAPTER 4
Dark field
spectroscopy
In previous chapters we have primarily been focused on excitation light at zero angle
of incidence (AOI). A real excitation field will naturally cover a range of incidence
angles. This chapter will explore some of the effects of oblique angle fields on our
pillar.
4.1
Methodology
The easiest way for us to experimentally investigate this behaviour is through dark
field spectroscopy.
Using a confocal microscope we can illuminate our sample at
incidence angles higher than the numerical aperture of the objective. If this light hits
a flat surface it will simply be reflected and not be collected by the objective, however
if the light hits a rough surface it can be scattered into the numerical aperture and
collected.
To gauge the amount of light our pillars scatter at a given wavelength we use a white
light source (Nikon C-HGFI in our case) and measure the scattered spectrum from
the pillars. The spectrometer splits the light by wavelength using a grating. The light
can then be recorded using a CCD, where one axis will be the wavelength and the
other a physical position. One such measurement is shown in figure 4.1a. To treat
the data we integrate the spectrum across the physical dimension in the CCD. This
region is marked with white horizontal lines. The resulting spectrum is labeled I0.
To avoid artifacting from background sources in the scattering spectrum we attempt
to subtract any spectral contribution that might come from the substrate.
This
contribution is estimated by measuring the scattering spectrum in close proximity on
either side of the pillar. The edges of the measured physical range is marked in blue
on figure 4.1a. This background spectrum is labeled Ibg.
Finally as the white light source has a characteristic profile of its own we need to
normalize the final spectrum over the white light reference spectrum, Iref. This spec-
trum is obtained by mounting a diffuse reflector in the confocal microscope instead
of a sample.

Conclusion and
Outlook
We conclude that our method for pillar fabrication works well for our purposes. For
future batches it is recommended to fabricate the pillars slightly taller than designed,
since all measured pillars turned out shorter than expected. Measuring the variation
in pillar height across the wafer would also be a good idea. One could imagine using
this variation to our advantage, since it allows for finer adjustment of the height.
Through Maxwell’s equations we model our pillars as step-index fibers and predict
that only the m = 1 mode will be present for our physical pillars. Modeling the pillars
as a Fabry-perot cavity we predict resonances at excitation wavelength for different
heights/diameters of our pillars. For use with localized emitters we conclude that
a better measure sees the pillar heights shifted down by a quarter wavelength from
resonance. This measure was compared with numerical results, where we observe a
slower variation in optimal pillar height as a function of diameter than found analyt-
ically – Most likely due to the field being a mix between the m = 1 mode and the
plane wave mode.
Numerically modeling an oscillating dipole in proximity to the pillar we find a purcell
enhancement factor of ~1.8 to 2 in our existing pillars with ~2.1 being the maximum
achievable with our current fabrication options.
Regarding the dark-field measurements we can conclude that due to the limited col-
lection angle of the objectives a better comparison point than the scattering cross
section is needed. One could imagine calculating the far field emission and integrat-
ing this over the numerical aperture, however due to the way COMSOL does far-field
calculations this is both troublesome to set up and very computationally expensive.
Another more simple measure would be to evaluate the far-field pointing in the z-
direction (towards the objective). This would still disregard the numerical aperture
of the objective, but compared to the scattering cross section which takes into account
scattering in all directions, this might still be a better measure. In the future a better
light source would also be needed to resolve features at higher photon energies.
During the course of this project we have faced a number of issues. Prior to the start of
the project some promising results were obtained that seemed to show localization of
luminescence centers in hBN. We were confident that these results could be replicated
quite quickly, giving us time to characterize the localized centers. As such two projects
==PAGE:38==
32
Conclusion
were started in parallel; One for formalizing and improving the localization method
[16] – and this project for understanding light-matter interaction between eventual
quantum emitters on top of the pillars and the pillars themselves, modeled as nano-
antennas/cavities. Unfortunately replicating these prior results proved troublesome.
Quite late in the project it was shown that the results we were most likely due to
Raman scattering and not localized quantum emitters. This result together with the
lack of success even after many fabricated samples brought into question whether
strain is a viable mechanism for promoting site selectivity in hBN at all. Another
group (Li et al. [30]) also failed to find evidence for strain as a localization mechanism.
As for this project a lot of time was spent looking into scattering- and eigen-modes
of the pillars. This turned out to be a challenging task due to the lossy and quasi-
infinite nature of the substrate on which the pillar was placed. Instead we turned
to parameter optimization of the pillars, where we identified field enhancement and
the Purcell factor as two candidates for a measure of goodness.
We limited the
optimization parameters to only the diameter and radius of the pillars such that we
would be able to manufacture them in the span of the project. In hindsight it would
have been interesting to turn this project in a more theoretical/hypothetical direction
where we could really try and push how far we could optimize potential pillars for
field-/purcell enhancement and even far field directivity. Other studies have already
shown promising results in localizing and enhancing emission from transition metal
dichalcogenide SPEs using various nano-antenna schemes such as GaP dimers [21] or
hyperbolic meta-material antennas [31]. Through topological optimization and meta-
material design one could imagine nano-antennas with very good near-field properties
and far-field directivity.
In the future if we manage to localize single photon emitters on top of the pillars,
either using hBN or TMDCs, one could do lifetime measurements on the emitters to
gauge the purcell effect.
