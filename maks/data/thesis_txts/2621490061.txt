Preface
This report serves as the Master’s thesis for Peter Holmsø. The thesis is developed from the 24th of Au-
gust to the 24th of January at DTU Management. It is the ﬁnal project of the Master of Science (MSc.)
in Business Analytics (Optimization and data design).
The report deals with optimal life cycle planning in pension funds. The objective is to ﬁnd optimal
pension plans based on speciﬁc asset properties and personalized needs. Pension plans from the Danish
insurance and pension company Topdanmark are used as a reference throughout the thesis.
It is assumed that the reader is familiar with stochastic simulation and life cycle planning. It furthermore
assumes that the reader knows basic ﬁnancial concepts such as risk and market returns.
The thesis was developed together with Kourosh Marjani Rasmussen, from the Department of Techno-
logy, Management and Economics at DTU, who served as the project supervisor.

Abstract
This thesis analyses optimal life cycle planning for pension funds on a 40-years basis. The objective is to
analyze if personalized pension plans will generate a higher return than general pension plans. 3 different
pension plans from the Danish insurance and pension company Topdanmark is used as a reference to the
general pension plans.
The ﬁrst part of the report deﬁnes the models and methods used to make optimal life cycle planning.
Stochastic simulation is used to simulate asset returns. Market expectations from a Danish expert group
is used as input parameters in the simulation. The risk of the pension plans is controlled by changing
the ratio between global stocks (risky asset) and government & mortgage bonds ("safe" asset). From a
normality test of stock and bond returns, it is fair to assume that these assets’ price ﬂuctuations follow
a normal distribution. The pension plans are based on 4 different mathematical functions, which all use
different parameter settings. This gives different and diversiﬁed risk proﬁles, which ensures that every
aspect in regards to risk is simulated.
Case 1 analyses a simple pension plan where 5000 DKK is saved each month for 40 years. When ana-
lyzing the optimal risk curves, it can be concluded that they are quite risky. The reason is that portfolios
with an early decrease in stocks will be too affected by the negative return of the government & mortgage
bonds. The most optimal pension plan is the medium-risk plan, with a Net present value of 4.167.000
DKK. The best performing curve from Topdanmark is the high-risk curve with a Net present value of
3.216.000 DKK. Therefore, the simulated medium-risk pension plan is having a 30% higher return than
the best performing Topdanmark curve.
Case 2 analyzes a pension fund where the monthly contribution depends on the average monthly salary
in Denmark. The optimal risk curves are very similar to those in the previous case - only with minor dif-
ferences. The best performing pension plan has a Net present value of 4.999.721 DKK. This is 18−19%
higher than the high-risk curve from Topdanmark, which has a Net present value of 4.213.761 DKK.
From a sensitivity analysis, it can be concluded that the pension plan’s lifetime inﬂuences the optimality
of the curves. The longer the pension plan’s lifetime, the later the risk curve should decrease its propor-
tion of stocks.
Case 3 considers 4 different groups of individuals. It will analyze if there is a difference between the op-
timal pension plans when using personalized monthly pension contributions. The initial analysis shows
minor differences between the optimal risk curves for the private and public sectors, but no differences
between the genders within the sectors. A sensitivity analysis can conclude that both the amount paid and
the trend of the monthly pension contribution does not affect the optimal risk curves. However, changing
the underlying assets will signiﬁcantly impact the optimal risk curves’ shape.
Therefore, the overall conclusion is that personalized pension plans will not affect the optimal risk curves.
However, the lifetime and the underlying assets will signiﬁcantly change the curves’ optimality.

Chapter 1
Introduction to the problem
Many people are dependent on a ﬁxed income to have a good living standard. To uphold the same stan-
dard after retirement, people choose to save money throughout their professional careers into a pension
saving. The savings depend on people’s individual needs and expectations for their retirement. The pen-
sion savings is mostly funded by the employers, where the amount saved is between 8-18% of the salary.
The public employee pension plans are often more generous than the private, why employees sometimes
choose to fund the pension saving from their own salary. Even though Denmark’s pension system is one
of the best in the world, estimations say that towards the year 2060, there will be 200.000 people with no
pension savings at all[13].
It is often hard for individuals to make optimal life cycle planning. Therefore, many people are using
pension funds to control their pension savings. Pension funds are often privately controlled companies
with a large amount of money to invest. They play an important role in the ﬁnancial market because
many pension funds invest a large amount of money into stocks and bonds. Many pension funds offer
different pension possibilities, depending on their individual needs. For example, the Danish pension
fund Topdanmark gives their customers the possibility to choose between low, medium, and high-risk
pension plans and the overall asset classes. However, many of these pension plans are generalized, which
does not consider individual needs.
This thesis will analyze if personalized risk curves can yield a higher return then more generalized curves
from Topdanmark. Stochastic simulation is used to generate return curves based on market expectations
from Forsikring & Pension and Finans Danmark. A broad range of risk curves is simulated to ﬁnd the
most optimal curves for low, medium, and high-risk curves. The overall research question is:
Will personalized risk curves yield a higher return than generalized risk curves?

1.1
Thesis outline
The thesis begins in chapter 1 with a general introduction to the problem. The chapter starts with the
overall thesis goals, where different sub-questions are deﬁned to answer the overall research question. A
description of different risk curves from Topdanmark will then be presented, which serves as a baseline
for the problem.
Chapter 2 will present the most important literature. The theory is the foundation of the thesis and is
used to analyze the risk curves.
In chapter 3 the method and general model will be deﬁned. It starts with an introduction to the risk
curves used in the simulation. The method of simulating asset returns is deﬁned, which will be used in
the simulation of pension portfolios. A normality test is done to test if changes in asset prices are nor-

Thesis goals
Having the correct pension plan can make a big difference in the amount of money available at retire-
ment. When doing life cycle planning, the optimal pension plans can be analyzed by different methods.
The most common method is either by using deterministic models or stochastic simulation. The deter-
ministic models focuses on systems of non-linear equations. These systems are hard to solve and often
need certain start conditions. However, they can be quite accurate in ﬁnding the most optimal risk curves.
The stochastic simulation uses random number generation to simulate different outcomes. The random
numbers are often following a normal distribution when simulation ﬁnancial markets. For stochastic
simulation to be efﬁcient, many simulations need to be made to make sure that the complete solution
space is covered. When using simulation for optimal life cycle planning, different risk curves need to be
simulated to ensure that a broad risk proﬁle is analyzed.
This thesis will analyze 1600 different risk curves to ﬁnd optimal pension plans. All simulation scenarios
will be based on a 40 years pension plan, where market expectations for stocks will serve as the risky
asset, and market expectations for government bonds will be the "safe" asset. The goal is to use the
scenarios to answer different sub-questions to answer the overall research question.
The ﬁrst scenario will focus on a case where 5.000 DKK is saved into a pension fund each month. This
will be the most simple of the three because it does not consider any personalized factors. The second
scenario considers the amount saved as a function of age, where the pension contribution is derived from

1. Will the rate and amount of money saved into the pension fund inﬂuence the optimal risk curves?

The net present value (NPV) is measuring an investment’s proﬁtability. It considers the difference in
earnings and losses compared to a certain discount rate for a speciﬁc time period. The discount rate

to inﬂation. An investment is proﬁtable if the NPV is positive, and it can be used to ﬁnd the proﬁtability

2.2
Volatility
Volatility is a statistical measurement deﬁning the dispersion of returns for a given asset. It is used for
measuring the risk, where higher volatility means higher risk. Because most asset returns follow a normal
distribution, where the prices swing around a mean, volatility is often measured in standard deviation or
variance. One way of deﬁning an asset’s volatility is to quantify the percentage change in the price from
one time to another [18].
2.3
Value at Risk
Value at Risk (VaR) can be used to quantify the ﬁnancial risk in a portfolio. It measures the worst-cases
of a portfolio beyond a certain probability. When simulating portfolio returns, VaR can quantify the
outcomes, giving the lowest return. The metric is widely used in investments banks, where it is used to
determine the potential losses in portfolios [33].
2.4
Conditional Value at Risk
Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) is another risk measurement, analyzing the worst-case portfolios be-
yond the VaR. CVaR is, therefore, quantifying the amount of tail risk a portfolio has. The metric is
calculated by taking the average of all simulation outcomes beyond the VaR. When using CVaR as a risk
measurement in stochastic simulation, it is important to ensure that CVaR is stable by simulating enough
portfolio outcomes. The number of simulations can be found by doing a stress test for the speciﬁc case
since it will vary dependent on the speciﬁc case. When interpreting CVaR, it is important to decide
whether the expected loss or expected return is analyzed. If expected losses are analyzed, a higher CVaR
means a higher risk. If expected returns are analyzed, a lower CVaR means a higher risk. CVaR will be
used as risk measurement throughout the thesis [14].

2.6
Efﬁcient frontiers
Efﬁcient frontiers are used to ﬁnd optimal portfolios. The portfolio with the highest expected returns
at a given level of risk is called the efﬁcient frontier. The portfolios that are having a lower return at a
given risk or have the same return at a higher risk are therefore less optimal because the ratio between
return and risk is lower. When working with efﬁcient frontiers, an assumption is that higher risk means
higher returns. However, adding more risk will not necessarily gain the same amount of return because
the relationship is not linear. Therefore, the optimal portfolio is the one with the best balance between
risk and return [2].

2.8
Stochastic simulation
Stochastic simulation is used when wanting to generate outcomes based on random variables. It is of-
ten used for predicting possible events in the future, which contains some uncertainty. Therefore, most
stochastic simulation systems simulate an event as a function of time. The random variables are often
generated based on a certain distribution [27].
Stochastic simulation can be used in ﬁnance to help investors make correct decisions. It can generate
different scenarios for the future, which will help understand the possibility of certain returns, risks, etc.
Portfolio managers can beneﬁt greatly from using the tool because it let them understand their portfolio
in detail. Using stochastic simulation will not give an exact result about the future but only an indication.
However, doing a sufﬁcient number of simulation-runs will give good predictions [17].
One big disadvantage of using stochastic simulation is that these methods fall into a single "true" estimate
without considering other mechanisms, such as expert opinions. This can lead to unrealistic simulation
runs, affecting the overall results. Therefore, it is important to make "enough" simulation runs to make
these outliers less meaningful. Another disadvantage is that these models often use historical data to gen-
erate random numbers, e.g. the Bootstrap method or the Monte Carlo method. Therefore, the simulation
is dependant on the mechanisms that have happened in the past, even though the future can be different.
This report tries to accommodate that by using market expectations from an expert group [1].
The stochastic simulation will be used to simulate the 40 years pension plan. The random numbers will
follow the normal distribution of government and mortgage bonds and global stocks based on the market
expectations made by the 3 experts.

7.1
Discussion of results
From the analysis’ in the previous chapters, it can be concluded that the lifetime of the pension plan and
the assets used for controlling the risk has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the efﬁcient frontiers. Chapter 6
showed a small difference in the optimal risk curves between the public and private sectors. However,
a sensitivity analysis could conclude that personalized pension plans do not affect the optimality of risk
curves. The small differences in the optimal risk curves could be because of the stochastic simulation
itself. The random numbers used to simulate stock and bond returns are different for the public and pri-
vate sectors. Therefore, the average return and CVaR will be slightly different due to the randomness of
the numbers. Therefore, the results are approximations, which is also one of the disadvantages of using
stochastic simulation. However, since stock and bond predictions will never be an exact representation of
the future, the deviation in returns due to the random numbers has no signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results’
accuracy.
The risk curves for the efﬁcient frontiers in chapter 4,5 and 6 have a higher Sharpe ratio than the risk
curves from Topdanmark. However, when looking at the sensitivity analysis’, where the stock and bond
asset was changed, the performance of the Topdanmark risk curves increased signiﬁcantly. This could
indicate that the risk curves from Topdanmark are based on other market expectations than those used
in this report. Therefore, it is important to assess the expected return for the pension fund’s assets when
making life cycle planning. One way of doing it in practice could be to update the expected return and
standard deviation of the assets every half year and change the risk curve accordingly. However, updating
the asset properties at the beginning of the pension funds lifetime is not that important since most of the
risk curves will contain 100% stocks. Nevertheless, as the pension fund is approaching retirement, the
risk curves’ variation starts, why correcting the asset expectations is more important at this stage.
The sensitivity analysis in chapter 5 established that the pension fund’s lifetime is also an important fac-

7.2
Discussion of assumptions
The risk curves from Topdanmark has been used as a reference throughout the thesis. Section 1.4 showed
a noticeable difference between the pension funds risk curves. Extending the comparison of the results to
other funds than Topdanmark could maybe inﬂuence the optimally of the curves. However, it was clear
that the optimal curves decreased their risk later than any of the curves from the 4 funds. Therefore, none
of the pension funds curves will probably be more optimal than the simulated efﬁcient frontiers.
The pension contributions in chapter 5 and 6 were ﬁxed for all 40 years to 13%, 15%, and 17%. It is
realistic to imagine that people change their percentage contribution throughout their careers. This could
be tested to see if it changes the curves’ optimality. However, the sensitivity analysis where the pension
contribution was changed did not change the optimal risk curves. Therefore, it can be expected that it
will not signiﬁcantly change the results.
Another underlying assumption is that only 1 "safe" asset and 1 risky asset can be used to control the
risk. However, pension funds can use multiple assets, such as real estate, futures, swaps, etc. Adding
this to the simulation framework could give a deeper understanding of each asset type’s inﬂuence on the
optimal risk curves.
The cost of having a pension fund is not taken into account in the simulation framework. The cost varies
depending on the company used. As an example, PFA is having a yearly cost of approximately 1% of
the total savings for their high-risk plan [23]. Adding this would have a greater inﬂuence on the good
performing pension plans because they have higher returns and therefore higher savings. Adding this
kind of cost would maybe inﬂuence the overall picture.

7.3
Future work
The stochastic simulation is based on the assumption that market returns follow a normal distribution.
From the normality test in chapter 3, it was clear that returns deviate from the tails’ distribution. There-
fore, other methods of predicting returns could be used, such as predicting algorithms that use machine
learning or methods that are taking the skewness and kurtosis into consideration.
All risk curves used in this report stops at the retirement date. However, the savings are paid over time
once people retire - often 10, 20, or 30 years. Therefore, it could be interesting to extend the analysis to
cover the years after retirement. An analysis of the optimal risk curves after retirement could be made
where different lifetimes are tested.
The Geometric Brownian motion (GBM) was used to simulate asset returns. However, other methods
could be used, such as mean reversion models. These kind of models ensures that the simulated returns
revert towards a long-term mean. The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process is a broadly used method for simulat-
ing asset returns. The degree of reversion can vary and needs to be calibrated for the speciﬁc case. Here
different ﬁtting methods can be used such as least squares ﬁtting and maximum likelihood ﬁtting [19].
An implementation plan of the optimal life cycle plan could also be investigated further. This thesis
has established the important factors to determine the optimal risk curves but has not considered how
it should be done in practice. However, the focus should probably be to determine the expectations for
asset returns.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis has analyzed life cycle planning for pension funds. Stochastic simulation has been used to
simulate market returns, based on market expectations from Forsikring & Pension and Finans Danmark.
Chapter 3 deﬁned the different risk curves used in the simulation. From a descriptive analysis, it was
concluded that the risk curves cover a broad risk proﬁle. Furthermore, a normality test showed that asset
returns are nearly following a normal distribution. Therefore, the geometric Brownian motion method
was used to simulate returns for government and mortgage bonds and global stocks. A Kolmogorov-
Smirnov test showed that the simulated returns follow a log-normal distribution.
Chapter 4 analyzed a case where 5000 DKK was saved into the pension fund each month. From the
stochastic simulation, 5 efﬁcient frontiers were found covering different risk levels. The conclusion was
that the optimal risk curves decreased their risk close to retirement with a high steepness. The reason
was that the bond asset had a negative average return. Therefore, having stocks until close to retirement
was more beneﬁcial. The curves were decreasing their risk towards the end to avoid market crashes close
to retirement. By comparing the Sharpe ratio between the efﬁcient frontiers and the risk curves from
Topdanmark, it was clear that the efﬁcient frontiers were more optimal. The medium-risk curve was the
most optimal and had a Net Present Value of 4.167.000 DKK.
Chapter 5 considered a life cycle plan for the average Danish citizen. The optimal risk curves were very
similar to those in chapter 4, only with small differences. The risk curves from Topdanmark were again
underperforming, where the lower-medium-risk curve had the highest Sharpe ratio, with a Net Present
Value of 4.999.721 DKK. From sensitivity analysis, it was concluded that the lifetime inﬂuences the
curves’ optimality. A shorter lifetime of the pension fund would result in lower risk curves being the
most optimal ones.

Code for simulating return curves
#Mean return of stocks
mean = 1.059**(1/12)-1
#standard deviation of stocks
std
= 0.135/np.sqrt(12)
#Time in months
time = 40*12
#Simulations
simulation = 50000
#Return matrix for the simulations
Returns = np.zeros([time,simulation])
#Fix the seed
np.random.seed(42)
#Loop for each simulation
for j in range(simulation):
#Initial portfolio value
Returns[0,j] = 100
#Loop for all years
for i in range(1,time):
#Making random number based on the standard normal distribution
r = np.random.normal(0, 1)
#Calculating the change in return
