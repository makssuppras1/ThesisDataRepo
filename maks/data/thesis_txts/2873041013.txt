Abstract
This project investigates the performance of module­level power electronics (MLPE), fo­
cusing on the static DC/DC conversion efficiency and conversion ratio dependency of two
commercially available power optimizers: the Huawei SUN2000­450W­P and the Zerun
Z8S­D. As distributed PV systems expand in urban and residential environments, design­
ers face increasing challenges from shading, mismatch, and orientation variability. MLPE
are heavily deployed in some regions for distributed photovoltaic (PV) systems [1] to im­
prove energy harvest under suboptimal conditions such as partial shading and mismatch.
However, manufacturers often report weighted efficiencies close to 99%, which are ob­
tained under idealized test conditions. Using a controlled experimental setup, both opti­
mizers have been tested across different input voltages and conversion ratios to evaluate
their influence to static DC/DC conversion. Results demonstrate that real­world efficien­
cies are lower than datasheet values, particularly as the conversion ratio increases. The
study highlights the importance of considering conversion ratio and voltage level in eval­
uating MLPE performance and proves the need for a more detailed efficiency estimation
including conversion ratio and voltage levels.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The global energy landscape is undergoing a rapid transformation year by year, driven by
increasing energy demand and evolving regulatory frameworks. In particular, distributed
energy technologies are becoming more widespread, with solar photovoltaics (PV) playing
a central role in this shift. Advances in technology and decreasing manufacturing costs
have made PV systems more accessible for both utility­scale and especially distributed
applications, such as residential and commercial rooftops. Policy initiatives like the EU
Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD, EU/2024/1275) which mandates the
installation of solar panels on all new buildings are also a big stakeholder in this rapid
development of distributed PV systems. In this context, the performance optimization
of distributed PV systems has become increasingly relevant, especially with the growing
role of module­level power electronics (MLPE) in enhancing system efficiency, safety, and
monitoring.
1.2
Growth of distributed PV and problem statement
While utility­scale PV plants continue to grow steadily, a big portion of recent expansion
is also happening in the distributed generation sector. Distributed PV systems ­typically
installed in residential rooftops, commercial or industrial buildings ­ now account for a
considerable portion of solar annual additions. Figure 1.1 taken from [2] shows a fore­
cast from 2021 about the annual capacity additions of utility­scale PV, distributed PV and
accelerated cases from 2020 to 2026.
Figure 1.1: Annual capacity additions of solar PV.
As distributed PV systems grow, they are increasingly exposed to not optimal conditions
such as partial shading, inefficient tilt and orientations. MLPE have been developed with
the aim of addressing these challenges. MLPE operate at module level performing MPPT
tracking and secondary features like monitoring and voltage regulation or rapid shutdown
safety commands. MLPE manufacturers claim that the use of these devices substantially

improve the energy harvest under non­optimal conditions. However, there are few quanti­
tative studies that evaluate the actual benefits of these devices under realistic conditions.
Moreover manufacturers often report weighted efficiencies of 98% ­ 99%, however these

ratio is equal to one. In this scenarios the DC/DC converter inside the optimizer experi­

contribute to this goal, by characterizing the performance of two power optimizers (PO)
comercially available, the Huawei SUN2000­450W­P and Zerun Z8S­D.
1.3
Research Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to characterize the performance of two power optimiz­
ers. The project aims to quantify the difference between manufacturer stated efficiency
and real efficiency under actual operating conditions and compare the influence of the
current ratio ”k” on their efficiency.
By adressing these objectives, this thesis will contribute in a better understanding of the
behaviour of MLPE in practical PV systems and will help inform PV designers and stake­
holders of the true performance of these devices.
1.4
Structure of the thesis
This project is divided into 6 chapters, covering progressively the context, methodology
and analysis of the study:
• Chapter 1 ­ Introduction: Presents the background, motivation, and scope of the
thesis. It also introduces the problem statement, research objectives, and outlines
the structure of the project.
• Chapter 2 ­ Literature Review: Reviews existing studies and technologies related
to power optimizers, MPPT techniques, converter topologies, and their reported
performance in distributed PV systems.
• Chapter 3 ­ Zerun Z8S­D characterization: Describes the methodology and ex­
perimental results obtained from testing the Zerun Z8S­D power optimizer. Focuses
on its static efficiency across various current ratios and input voltages.
• Chapter 4 ­ Huawei SUN2000­450W­P characterization: Describes the methodol­
ogy and experimental results obtained from testing the HUAWEI SUN2000­450W­P
power optimizer. Focuses on its static efficiency across various current ratios and
input voltages.
• Chapter 5 ­ Discussion: Analyzes and compares the results from both devices.
Highlights performance trends, deviation from datasheet claims, and the influence
of the current ratio and input voltage on efficiency.
• Chapter 6 ­ Conclusion: Summarizes the key findings, addresses the research
objectives, and suggests potential areas for future work, including modeling and
broader testing of MLPE performance

2
Literature Review
2.1
Module Level Power Electronics
MLPE are a class of electronics designed to improve the performance, safety and moni­
toring potential of PV systems at the individual module level. MLPE mainly include power
optimizers and microinverters. Power optimizers are basically DC/DC converters that also
perform MPPT at the module.
On the other hand, microinverters are DC/AC converters that replace the necessity of a
string inverter, converting the DC power to AC power at a module level. Both technologies
aim to tackle mismatch losses, a relatively common problem in distributed PV systems
such as residential and commercial systems. Common topologies are presented in figure
2.1.
Figure 2.1: Schematic of conventional PV system (top); DC optimizer­equipped PV sys­
tem (middle); and microinverter­equipped PV system (bottom) [3].
Despite their advantages, MLPE introduce additional cost, more complexity and potential
points of failure into the system. The presence of electronics at module level can raise
concerns about long term reliability, particularly in extreme environments. As such, the
actual performance and efficiency of these devices needs to be critically evaluated under
real operation conditions.
2.2
Mismatch losses and partial shading in PV systems
Distributed PV systems hardly ever operate under perfect conditions. Real world installa­
tions often experience mismatch between modules due to orientation, partial shading, tilt,
temperature variation, aging etc. This mismatches can cause significant losses especially
on traditional PV systems where modules in the same string are electrically dependent
on one another.
Mismatch losses occurs when the current, voltage or power of PV modules in a string do

==PAGE:18==
not align. The most common causes are:
• Partial shading (trees, chimneys, buildings etc)
• Orientation mismatch
• Tilt mismatch
• Manufacturing tolerances
• Soiling and degradation
Figure 2.2: Shading due to a) different orientation, b) a chimney and c) a tree
When modules are connected in series in a string, they have to operate at a common
current. If an underperforming module is connected, it will limit the current of the entire
string causing a bottleneck effect. This phenomenon is known as mismatch loss [4].
Partial shading is one of the most critical form of mismatch. Only if one or two cells are
shaded, it can cause a reduction of the output due to the nature of series­connected cells
in a PV module. This effect can cause significant drops in the power output of the entire
string. Studies indicate that partial shading on just 10­20% of a PV module can reduce
the the total output of the string by 30­40% if no remedy action is taken [5]. The use of
buck­based power optimizers will reduce the output voltage and increase current to match
string requirements, thereby extracting maximum power from underperforming modules.
2.3
Power optimizers and Submodule power optimizers
Power optimizers are typically installed on a per­module basis. These devices perform
local DC/DC conversion and maximum power point tracking, allowing each module in a
string to operate independently of the others. This is an effective approach for handling
mismatch losses caused by partial shading, soiling or different orientations between mod­
ules in a PV array.
On the other hand, Submodule power optimizers are embedded within the PV module
itself and operate at the level of cell­substrings, typically operating on one third or half
of the module’s internal cell groups. Therefore, these devices integrate DC/DC control

directly into the module, allowing MPPT that can respond to shading of cell­string level,
rather than only at the module level. The key advantage is the ability to adress intramod­
ule mismatch, such as when only a part of the module is shaded. By dynamically adjusting
the load on each substring, the Submodule optimizer will help prevent energy losses.
2.4
Power optimizers as DC/DC converters
The main functionality of a power optimizer is based on a DC/DC converter, that will adjust
the input voltage of the PV module to ensure that maximum power is extracted at all time.
This approach will ensure that one single underperforming module is not limiting the entire
string.[6]
2.4.1
DC/DC converter topologies
Power optimizers rely on DC/DC converter circuits to transform the electrical output of a
PV module in order to match the voltage and current of the downstream inverter or string.
Among the commercial available power optimizers, we can find the following topologies:
buck, boost and buck­boost converter. Each design serves a specific need, depending
on the difference between MPP voltage and the desired output voltage.
• Buck converter ­ Step down
A buck converter or step­down converter is a DC­to­DC converter which decreases the
output voltage, while increasing current, from its input (supply) to its output (load) [7].
A simple buck converter design consists of a MOSFET switch, diode, inductor and a
capacitor. When the switch is on, energy is stored in the inductor, when the switch is off,
energy is released from the inductor to the load. The duty cycle determines how long

In DC/DC power conversion, the current ratio or conversion ratio plays a critical role when
determining the device’s efficiency. For power optimizers used in PV systems, this ratio
is not fixed, it changes during time depending on the module’s operating point and the
current required by the downstream string or inverter. The current ratio ”k” is defined as:
k = Iout
Iin
(2.4)
Where:
• Iin is the current drawn from the PV module (at its MPP)
• Iout is the current delivered by the optimizer to the string.

Pin = Pout ⇒Vin ∗Iin = Vout ∗Iout

In real world buck converters there are power losses, and higher conversion ratio means
higher losses. Higher current ratios lead to:
• Increased ohmic losses: Ploss = I2 ∗R
• Higher switching losses
• Greater thermal stress
On the other hand, lower current ratios are more efficient, as current will remain moderate
and there will be less stress on switching components. As a result, manufacturers report
high efficiencies, values obtained when the current ratio is close to 1 ­ i.e., when the
duty cycle is near 100% and 0 or minimal conversion is required. Figure 2.4 from the

2.6
Maximum Power Point Tracking
The performance of PV systems is heavily constrained by the nonlinear current­voltage (I–
V) characteristic of PV panels. Each module has a unique Maximum Power Point (MPP),
the combination of current and voltage at which it delivers maximum output power. Be­
cause the MPP varies continuously with changes in irradiance, temperature, and aging,
PV systems require active control strategies to continuously adjust operating conditions.
This process is known as Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT).
MPPT is usually implemented with power electronic devices such as inverters, microin­
verters, or power optimizers. These devices will use algorithms that adjust the operating
voltage or current to track the module’s MPP in real time. The goal of MPPT is to extract
the maximum possible power from a PV source under varying environmental conditions,
and its efficiency is critical to the overall system performance.
• Centralized MPPT
In conventional string inverters systems, MPPT is centralized. A single tracker is ruling
all the modules in a string. This approach is effective but can introduce mismatch losses
when the modules in the same string are subject to different orientations, tilts or irradiance
levels. According to [9], for conventional MPPT, the technique works very well if all mod­
ules have the same irradiation, meaning no shading and the same orientation. However,
if irradiation changes on one or few modules due to partial shading, the curve will change
as shown in figure 2.5

7
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.5: Power voltage curves with and without shading
As stated in [9], the conventional MPPT might now only find the ”local maximum with
partial shading” shown in figure 2.5, therefore not delivering the maximum possible energy.
Advanced MPPT will perform a sweep over the complete voltage range to ensure that the
”MPP with partial shading” shown in figure 2.5 is found. This sweep will need to be done
from time to time due to dynamic changing conditions on irradiance.
• Descentralized MPPT
As the power optimizers are installed at a module level, the MPPT now will ensure that
each module is always working at their individual MPP. On the other hand, submod­
ule power optimizers will perform MPPT at cell­string level, allowing for optimizing intra­
module mismatches.
• MPPT Algorithms
Different MPPT algorithms have been developed over time, ranging from simple to com­
plex models. The most widely adopted commercial algorithms include:
• Perturb and Observe: It will incrementally adjust the voltage and then observe the
power variation.
• Incremental conductance: Uses derivative of power with respect to voltage; better
performance in dynamic irradiance but requires more computation [10].
• Constant voltage: Operates the PV module at a fixed voltage, typically a set per­
centage of its Voc, based on the assumption that the maximum power point occurs
near that voltage under most conditions.
Finally, MPPT efficiency can be calculated using standardized procedures such as EN
50530 or IEC 62891, which will simulate both static and dynamic irradiance profiles. In
this project, static MPPT efficiency will be calculated according to the standard IEC 62891.

2.7.1
California Energy Comission (CEC) Weighted Efficiency
The CEC efficiency assigns more weight to higher loads, aiming to reflect operating pro­
files of converters in sunnier climates like California. It is calculated using the formula:
ηCEC = 0.04∗η10% +0.05∗η20% +0.12∗η30% +0.21∗η50% +0.53∗η75% +0.05∗η100% (2.6)
2.7.2
European Weighted Efficiency
On the other hand, the European Efficiency will reflect colder climates and more temperate
conditions, found in most parts of Europe, where converters frequently operate at partial
load. It is calculated using the following equation:
ηEU = 0.03 ∗η5% + 0.06 ∗η10% + 0.13 ∗η20% + 0.10 ∗η30% + 0.48 ∗η55% + 0.20 ∗η100% (2.7)
Weighted efficiencies such as CEC and European Efficiency are typically understood as
the product of conversion efficiency and MPPT tracking efficiency. Static MPPT tracking
efficiency can be calculated using standards such as IEC 62891 or EN 50530. Since
MPPT efficiency is generally very high, (above 99%), EU or CEC efficiencies will focus
mostly on converter performance at different loads.

2.8
Performances of power optimizers
The IEA­PVPS Task 13 report titled ”Performance of Partially Shaded PV Generators
Operated by Optimized Power Electronics” investigates the impact of partial shading
on photovoltaic (PV) system performance and evaluates the efficiencies of various power
electronics solutions, including string inverters, module­level power electronics (MLPE),
and shading­tolerant PV modules. Through a combination of indoor measurements, out­
door testing, and annual performance simulations, the report provides detailed insights
into the technical behavior, advantages, and limitations of these systems under different
shading scenarios.
Independent measurements detailed in the IEA report reveal that the real­world perfor­
mance of DC/DC power optimizers often is lower than the manufacturers’ claims. While
datasheets typically show efficiencies above 98.5%, lab tests conducted by ZHAW show
that these figures are only achieved under ideal, passthrough conditions where input and
output voltages are nearly equal. Under practical shading scenarios, where optimizers
operate in buck or boost modes, efficiencies are notably lower, often 1–2% lower. In a
setup where all the modules have a power optimizer, this efficiency drop in each power
optimizer can outweigh the energy gains from individual MPP tracking, especially in sys­
tems with light to moderate shading. On the other hand, the report confirms that power
optimizers do offer real benefits when there are heavily shaded arrays ( with a shading
index >5%) or complex rooftop layouts where modules face different orientations or tilts.
Consequently, the report concludes that while power optimizers are beneficial in heavily
shaded or irregularly oriented PV arrays, conventional string inverters or systems using
shade­tolerant modules can offer superior performance and simplicity in less shading con­
ditions.
2.9
Summary and research gap
The literature reviewed in this chapter highlights the growing importance of MLPE, es­
pecially power optimizers, when enhancing the performance of distributed photovoltaic
systems. It also provides a general understanding on what are power optimizers, why are
they used, and what strategies they implement in order to extract the maximum power
possible at all time. Finally, standardized procedures are presented showing how the
efficiency of a power optimizer can be calculated. On the other hand, key performance
insights have also been provided by the ”2024 IEA PVPS Task 13 Report”[8]. This the­
sis aims to quantify how the power optimizers efficiency varies under real world operation
scenarios by comparing real performance with manufacturer stated values and evaluating
how static conversion efficiency varies according to the current ratio, and not just simply
looking at the efficiency when none or minimal conversion is actually done.

5
Discussion
The experimental results from the two tested optimizers reveal clear trends in how cur­
rent ratio ”k” and operating voltage affect the power optimizer performance. In both cases,
measured efficiencies were lower than manufacturer reported efficiencies, especially as
the current ratio ”k” increased. The Zerun Z8S­D showed important deviations, with CEC
efficiency falling from 93.76% at k=1 to 87.02% at k=1.6, and a corresponding EU effi­
ciency drop from 94.37% to 88.41%. for the 10V experiment. The Vmp=13V showed a
better performance, however it is important to remark that a unity current ratio ”k” was
not achieved, further investigation needs to be done to know the cause of the problem.
On the other hand, the Huawei optimizer has demonstrated a better overall stability with
efficiency reductions of 2% across the tested current ratio range.
These trends confirm that the power optimizer static conversion efficiency is highly de­
pendent on converter’s duty cycle and their associated conduction and switching losses,
which scale with current ratio. At low current ratios (i.e minimal conversion) the optimizers
operate near peak efficiency, as claimed by the manufacturers. However, [8] suggests
that power optimizer only offer real overall benefits when there are heavily shaded ar­
rays(with a shading index >5%). This highlights the importance of evaluating their effi­
ciency under higher current ratios, which are common in non­ideal operating conditions
Interestingly, the results also suggest that operating at a higher input voltage (e.g., 13V
or 55V) mitigates some of the losses, likely due to lower currents and less stress on the
switching components, improving overall conversion efficiency. Additionally, the static
MPPT efficiencies remained consistently high across all operating points, confirming that
most losses originate in the DC/DC conversion process rather than in the tracking algo­
rithm itself.
44
Performance characterization of module­level power electronics for PV
==PAGE:59==
6
Conclusion
This thesis provided a comprehensive anlaysis of the performance characterization of two
power optimizers using controlled experimental setups. It has been shown that the actual
weighted efficiencies of both the Huawei SUN2000­450W­P and Zerun Z8S­D optimizers
can be lower than manufacturer­stated values, particularly at higher current ratios where
DC/DC conversion losses become bigger.
The Zerun Z8S­D showed a bigger efficiency decrease compared to the Huawei device,
underscoring the variability in performance across different MLPE products. While the
MPPT tracking efficiency remained consistently high (>99%) across all conditions, static
conversion efficiency proved to be highly sensitive to current ratio.
These findings underscore the need for PV designers and installers to critically assess
optimizer performance under real operating conditions rather than rely only on datasheet
values.
Future work could explore dynamic MPPT behavior, long­term performance under varying
irradiance, and comparisons with systems using string inverters under similar mismatch
conditions.
Another continuation of this work would be to complete the fitting of the Driesse inverter
performance model by incorporating input voltage as an explicit variable. This would allow
the use of a single set of empirical parameters to describe conversio efficiency across
varying input voltages and current ratios, thereby improving model general usefulness.
