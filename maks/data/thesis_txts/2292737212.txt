Abstract
In this thesis, a thorough investigation of a simple patch antenna operating at 2.4 GHz has been conducted
regarding patch substrate size, ground plane size, support structure and dome inﬂuence. Analysis is made using
a high frequency tool (HFSS) where the main focus has been on reﬂection coefﬁcient and radiation pattern
characteristics. The radiation pattern is compared to that of an analytical model of the patch antenna and its
diffraction from the edges of the ground plane, designed using geometrical theory of diffraction. Results for the
ground plane and support structure shows higher order diffraction at the edges of the ground plane together with a
change in the reﬂection and radiation pattern due to the support structure effects.
Other patch conﬁgurations are also introduced, where the main focus is on folded patch antennas. Especially the
Inverted F antenna is investigated, both folded in the W and L dimension of the patch to see how the size of the
ground plane compared to the antennas size inﬂuences the reﬂection coefﬁcient and radiation pattern. The matching
shows no signiﬁcant change between the three conﬁgurations, the same is found for the radiation pattern however
the H-plane cross polar component is as expected increased due to asymmetry in the setup.
The diffraction is desired to be reduced by using a lossy metasurface. Several possibilities have been investigated,
Electric Band Gap (EBG) structures were found to be relatively big in size compared to wavelength and have a small
bandwidth. Furthermore the lossy structure for the EBG structures would only be realized using CMS resistors
between the patches or a lossy substrate. The lossy substrate beneath the antenna showed great possibilities both in
optimization towards avoiding support structure effects but also cheap production costs. Three conﬁgurations where
considered, where Conﬁguration 1 and 2 were designed using decreasing conductivities towards free space. However
the radiation patterns did not show a clear reduction of the diffraction effect. The ﬁnal design was Conﬁguration 3,
which was designed for easy prototype manufacturing using slots in the ground plane. The slots were implemented
in the patch ground plane and the antenna was backed by a support structure. The radiation pattern showed good
results for the free space case, however the inﬂuence of the support structure on the current characteristic was not
well understood. In order to investigate this effect a Perfect Electric Conductor (PEC) wall (VIA’s) was introduced
between the edges of the ground plane and support structure, which together with a ground plane without slots
backed by the support structure showed the expected results. However, standing waves in terms of interference
was still seen for the ﬁnal conﬁguration in the H-plane. This effect requires a more detailed investigation and it is
planned to be included in a future work.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Measurements have shown that placing an antenna in the vicinity of a support structure changes its characteristics
such as the reﬂection coefﬁcient and the radiation pattern (as determined when the antenna is situated alone in free
space). In the aeronautic and shipping industry the placement on a support structure can include a fuselage on a
plane, side on a cargo ship or placement on the front of a windscreen on a car. However, since the antenna is highly
inﬂuenced by the support structure, regarding excitation of surface waves on the patch ground plane and altering the
antennas radiation pattern. These undesired surface waves is of great interest to ﬁnd a mean of reducing its effects
on the antenna performance. In recent years a great deal of effort has been made to investigate High Impedance
Surface (HIS) and so called Electric Band Gap (EBG) structures [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] (most common are the square and
hexagonal patch), especially regarding an increase in gain and matching for a better reﬂection coefﬁcient. However,
several HISs have also been shown to be capable of reducing the excited surface waves on the ground plane and
associated coupling effects to a backed support structure. This is realized using a lossy substrate [6] or by including
resistors between the EBG structures patches to suppress surface waves [7]. Anisotropic materials have also been
investigated to avoid minima in the radiation pattern [8] or lossy materials [9] designed for absorbing purposes with
high bandwidth compared to that of a Salisbury Screen, which is close to 1/4 λ of a wavelength in size (hereby the
low bandwidth).
The support structures substrate gives rise to high coupling and diffracted ﬁelds from the antenna if the surface
wave is not suppressed at the edges of the antenna ground plane for example by using a HIS. However the HIS is
challenging to realize at low frequencies such as 2.4 GHz, which is a free band, since the effective wavelength is
long, and the patch is designed to resonant at half of a wavelength. Furthermore, the working bandwidth is small
(a reﬂection phase of ±90◦) and it only holds for normal incidence. However, since the lossy metasurface from
previous works showed great promising effects regarding surface wave suppressing, a new design was implemented.
The purpose of this work is to devise a mean of suppressing surface waves in a conﬁguration consisting of a
simple patch antenna of W × L = 37.4 mm × 29.2 mm for a free band such as 2.4 GHz, with a patch substrate of
FR-4 with a permittivity of ϵr = 4.4 and height og 0.8 mm. Simulation is preformed in a high frequency simulation
tool, High Frequency Structural Simulator (HFSS), where the patch substrate and ground plane inﬂuence was
investigated. The investigation includes reﬂection coefﬁcient, -10 dB bandwidth and radiation pattern. It was shown
that the reﬂection coefﬁcient would be inﬂuence for a substrate decrease with a detuning as well as the radiation
pattern with a ground plane decrease. The numerical results for the radiation pattern is compared to an analytical
model, which was shown to give a good comparisons for ground plane above 4λ0 × 4λ0. Next, backed support
substrate and dome effect is obtained where also the near-ﬁeld currents are analysed in the support substrates. Since
it was shown that the ground plane and substrate decrease was inﬂuencing the patch antenna, an folded patch was
also investigated to see if the antenna characteristics would be more inﬂuenced. Several folded patch antennas are
presented, which is using the fact that the electric ﬁeld is zero in the middle giving one open radiating slot including

CHAPTER 2
Patch Antenna Conﬁgurations
This chapter presents a detailed investigation of a patch antenna with the aim of determining the inﬂuence of the
change in its ground plane size, the addition of an extra support structure as well as a protective dome. The latter are
likely to occur in the applications intended in the present work. The dimensions of the patch antenna are determined
by use of transmission line formulas and the extensive numerical analysis are conducted in the commercial software
package HFSS.

2.2
Numerical Investigation
The numerical investigation is made using HFSS from Ansys, which is a ﬁnite element method solver for
electromagnetic structures. The patch was investigated using a coax and a lumped port feeding. However, since
the presence of the coax probe was seen to give unwanted inﬂuence in the reﬂection coefﬁcient, (S11) the lumped
port was used instead to generate the presented results. The major investigation was how the patch substrate and
ground plane size would inﬂuence the antenna performance, regarding reﬂection coefﬁcient and radiation pattern.
In addition the support structure and dome is investigated as well. The analysis involves the following main aspects:
• Substrate variation
• Ground plane variation
• Support Structure variation
Height and permittivity of structure
• Dome variation
Permittivity, height and thickness of dome
2.2.1
Patch with Substrate & Ground Plane Variation
The simple patch is designed with a PEC sheet, with dimensions as described in Section 2.1, with a width
W = 37.4 mm (0.30λ0) and a length L = 29.2 mm (0.24λ0). Together with a FR-4 substrate with a relative
permittivity of ϵr = 4.4 and a PEC ground plane of size 3λ0 × 3λ0. The substrate of FR-4 with a height of 0.8 mm
is chosen, from the perspective of easy fabrication from a manufacturer where FR-4 is cheap and a size of 0.8 mm
is standard. An air box is inserted around the conﬁguration with edges half a free space wavelength, λ0/2 away
from the structure, giving a size of 4λ0 × 4λ0 and good convergence in the entire band 2 −3 GHz. Next Perfectly
Matched Layer (PML) is inserted around the entire air box to absorb all radiation from the antenna and reﬂection,
working as an anechoic chamber. A frequency sweep of 2 −3 GHz is chosen over the investigated band by using
the interpolating function (discrete sweep was used to check if the simulation had converged). Finally, a ﬁne mesh
for the S-parameters was set with ∆S = 0.001 as the maximum change of ∆S between each pass for the reﬂection
coefﬁcient. These criteria are set for the entire simulation routine shown below. The combined conﬁguration under
investigation is seen in Figure 2.2, with dimensions described above.
The feeding position was calculated to y0 = 10.8 mm. However, the ﬁrst investigation for the position showed
that this was not the optimal position for a 50 Ωfeeding. But a position of 6 mm was shown to give an input
impedance of the desired 50 Ω.
The patch ground plane was ﬁxed with a size of 3λ0 × 3λ0 and the patch substrate was decreased in size from
3λ0 × 3λ0 to W × L. The reﬂection coefﬁcient when the substrate was decreased is shown in Figure 2.3, from
2.4 −2.6 GHz and a S11 level of −40 −0 dB. A shift in frequency is seen when the substrate is decreased below
0.35λ0 × 0.35λ0. The -10 dB bandwidth and the center for matched reﬂection coefﬁcient is seen in Table 2.1.
This size reduction gives rise to a shift towards higher frequencies, which agrees with a smaller antenna size.
When the size is very close to the patch size i.e. 0.31λ0 × 0.24λ0, the shift is 20 MHz. When the substrate is
decreased to the patch dimensions of W × L the shift is 70 MHz and the -10 dB bandwidth is shifted entirely out of
the original frequency band with substrate size 3λ0 × 3λ0. Furthermore, the radiation pattern is seen in Figure 2.4 at
2.4 GHz as a function of θ ∈[−180; 180] and directivity level −25 −10 dB. The far-ﬁeld radiation pattern shows
no difference when the substrate is decreased, therefore only the largest difference in size is shown. A comparison
of the radiation patterns is shown in Table 2.2. As expected, the H-plane is much smaller than the E-plane, which
agrees with the radiation pattern for a magnetic dipole with zero radiation at the ends.

2.3
Summary
• The substrate size reduction affects only the reﬂection coefﬁcient when the substrate was decreased to the
size of the patch, while no effects on the radiation pattern was observed. However when the ground plane
size was decreased a frequency shift of 10 MHz for the reﬂection coefﬁcient was introduced from 3λ0 × 3λ0
to 0.31λ0 × 0.24λ0. The radiation pattern was affected as well: a decreasing ground plane gave a 25 dB
difference in ﬁrst side lobe to main (ground plane of 3λ0 × 3λ0) to an almost omni directional E-plane and
dipole pattern for the H-plane (0.31λ0 × 0.24λ0).
• When the support structure was inserted the radiation pattern caused a wider main beam for the H-plane
and also the E-planes minima were greatly decreased due to the wider ground plane. Therefore the support
ground plane was clearly inﬂuencing the patch. However, the support substrate permittivity value was not
affecting the resulting radiation pattern. Nevertheless, when studied in the near-ﬁeld, a higher permittivity
gave a smaller electric ﬁeld strength level.
• The dome protection close to the antenna did not show any inﬂuence to the radiation pattern or the reﬂection
coefﬁcient in any signiﬁcant way.

The simple patch from [10] can also be modiﬁed into a folded patch or an IFA conﬁguration. The folded

4.1
Folded Patch
The patch antenna exhibits low proﬁle, compact size and low cost in production. However there are some
problems to overcome when designing an electrically small patch antenna such as its size at resonance (λFR−4 =
60 mm at 2.4 GHz), which can be solved by applying a high dielectric material. Size reduction is also possible using
the folding or Short Circuit (SC) techniques. A cut-out in the patch antenna has been done in [15, 16] to create a
longer electrical length in order for the current to run. Furthermore the patch has a rather narrow bandwidth, which
can be solved by increasing the distance from the patch to the ground plane (increasing h), making the radiating
slots larger in size.
The folding patch antenna and SC technique exploits the fact that the electric ﬁeld is zero in the middle of
the patch [10, Figure 14.7b]. Several authors have presented this type of antenna with only one radiating slot in
[17, 18, 19, 20, 21] also called meanderly folded patches. The down side to this technique is lowering the bandwidth
due to the reduced antenna size. To increase the bandwidth a folded feed technique is used to increase the inductive
coupling [22] and a cut-out in the patch is created to introduce a capacitive effect [23, 24, 25]. For comparison,
previous results at 2.4 GHz is shown in Table 4.1.

The reﬂection coefﬁcient for Conﬁguration 1 indicated that the inner sheet was dominant when designed for

conﬁguration is not able to give the desired results. Since Conﬁguration 1 and 2 are not realisable, Conﬁguration 3
with slots in the ground plane was designed.
Initial simulation for the metasurface in free space showed a great similarity to an inﬁnite ground plane for sizes
above nslot = 5. When the support structure was introduced the radiation pattern was not altered. However a great
inﬂuence was seen in the surface current were a standing wave in the E-plane was introduced. The standing wave
was suppressed by inserting a PEC wall (VIA’s) at the edge between the patch ground plane and the support ground
plane. However, the metasurface is still not able to avoid high diffraction at the edges, on top of that causing a
diffraction at the edges in the H-plane. The support structure effect even though not seen to appear in the radiation
is clearly seen in the current plots.

CHAPTER 6
Summary and Conclusion
In the present work some different implementations of the general patch antennas at 2.4 GHz have been
investigated using HFSS. The investigation consists of substrate and ground plane reduction together with the
inﬂuence of support structure and dome effects. The substrate decrease showed no signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the
radiation pattern however for the reﬂection coefﬁcient a clear tuning towards higher frequencies was found from
2.41 −2.48 GHz from largest to smallest ground plane size. Which was indicating that the patch should be matched
for the new size of substrate given a new feed distance y0 of 6.75 mm for a substrate size of W ×L. The ground plane
size reduction did maintain equal reﬂection coefﬁcient, but a more evident difference in the radiation pattern was
found. A more smooth pattern in the E-plane was observed and the smallest ground plane size of 0.31λ0 × 0.24λ0
showed radiation close to dipole radiation possibly due to the feeding. Therefore it was concluded that the substrate
and ground plane size were highly important when designing an antenna for a speciﬁc working band and far-ﬁeld
characteristics. The support structure effects was also investigated, where the main results were the detuning in
frequency for permittivity values from ϵr = 1 to 10 due to longer electrical length for higher permittivities. Dome
effects did not show any change in antenna characteristics. Numerical and analytical results were supported by the
use of geometrical theory of diffraction, where the far-ﬁeld radiation was combined by the contribution from the
magnetic line sources and the edges. A good agreement was found for ground plane sizes above 4λ0 and for a size
of 6λ0 in the forward hemisphere for θ = ±55◦. Furthermore, a higher level of ripples were seen for the smaller
ground plane due to surface wave and diffraction at the edges, but the frequency of the ripples increased for a larger
ground plane.
Folded patch conﬁgurations were also investigated, were the IFA was folded in the dimensions of L and W to
observe effects of a reduced ground plane size. Some detuning in the reﬂection coefﬁcient was found compared to
the full patch. However, the -10 dB bandwidth was maintained of 1.6%. Radiation patterns were insigniﬁcantly
changed, but high cross polarisation levels were introduces for the IFA’s, due to the asymmetry of the feeds.
A major investigation was made into the possibility of introducing a lossy substrate beneath the patch antenna to
suppress surface waves causing high diffraction at the edges as seen in the analytical model. An EBG structure
was ﬁrst analysed both using with and without VIAs, giving a bandwidth of 24 MHz and 25 MHz, respectively.
Due to the long wavelength the structure either needs a large size (because of resonance at half a wavelength) or to
have a high permittivity substrate and thus increase manufacturing costs. Thus this structure was not used in the
following investigation, but the possibility of a lossy substrate was introduce. The lossy substrates were deﬁned for
conductivity values calculated using power transfer relations, where values below 30 S/m showed a power increase
for the calculated dissipated and accepted powers at the material transition region. Three designs were analysed,
Conﬁguration 1 implemented as two sheets, showed that the inner sheet was dominant when designing for good
matching. Radiation patterns below 58 −30 S/m showed lowered ripple effect in the E-plane and this was used for
further analysis. In Conﬁguration 2 implemented as 5 sheets, a reduction of the surface wave for some distances of

d = 2.25λ0 and 1.75λ0 was observed, but nothing signiﬁcant for other distances. Thus it was not clear if the lossy
substrate reduced the surface wave or if it had another effect. The low values of σ were not found in nature and
therefore not realizable so Conﬁguration 3 was designed using slots in the ground plane (of d = 1λ0) to create the
lossy effect. In free space smoothing of the radiation pattern in the E-plane was observed and radiation close to that
of an inﬁnite ground plane in the forward hemisphere were found for slot dimensions nslot = 1/10, 1/15, 1/20λ0.
When the support structure was implemented and backed by the antenna, standing waves were present for the
current plots in the E-plane with a peak at a distance of 1/3 λ0. This effect was suspected to be caused by a parallel
capacitor effect created by the second ground plane below. It has been shown that this effect can be suppressed by
introducing PEC walls (VIA’s) at the edges. The ﬁnalised design indicates high diffraction in the E-plane and some
ripple effect which was even seen in the H-plane for slot distances 1/15λ0 and 1/20λ0 (not the smoothing effect as
for the free space design). Standing waves were present in the color plots in the H-plane with a peak distance of
1/4 λ0 which was not investigated further due to time limitations.
Further studies would include implementation of the Uniform Theory of Diffraction using the Kouyoumijan
function to avoid discontinuities in the transitions region due to the division of cos(π/2). For the metasurface,
diffraction in the E- and even in the H-plane was found for the ﬁnalised Conﬁguration 3 and the design should be
altered to avoid this unwanted inﬂuence. Furthermore, standing waves were presents in the H-plane which should be
further investigated because it is very strange why this occurs in this dimensions since the excited and high currents
are seen to be present in the E-plane.
