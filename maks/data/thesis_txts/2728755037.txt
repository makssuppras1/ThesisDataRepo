Abstract
The widely used manufacturing process to produce plastic parts is Injection Molding.
In recent years micro manufacturing are highly used to produce parts with great
quality. The Industrial need for the digital twin in micro injection molding is in high
demand. This requires Data Driven approach by simulating the process parameter
effects in plastic Injection molding.
In this project, Design of Experiment(DOE) for a particular geometry with three
input process parameter such as Melt Temperature, Mold Temperature and Injection
Speed are varied for different material grades and the corresponding responses of
Part Weight, Cavity Injection Time and Maximum Injection Pressure are recorded
using commercially available FEA Simulation software. Data Driven Modelling is
achieved by taking Viscosity and pvT coeﬀicients of each material with the key process
parameters mentioned above. Modelling by Statistical method and Machine Learning
methods are used. Statistical method uses regression analysis by fitting the results
and the machine learning method uses Supervised learning with regression based
Artificial Neural Network(ANN).
From the results, it is clear that the part weight and Maximum Injection Pressure is
based on all the three input parameters and the Cavity Injection Time is mostly based
on the Injection speed of the machine. Both Statistical and Machine learning models
are performed well with the selected materials.
Both Models able to predict the
results of the materials which are also not selected which concludes the Data-Driven
Modelling is achieved for a particular geometry.
Keywords: Data Driven Modelling, Micro Injection Molding, DOE, FEA, Statistical
Modelling, ANN

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With the widespread use of algorithms in the engineering sciences, lots of additional
costs associated with time-consuming and expensive tests have been eliminated from
the product design and production development cycle. Today’s modeling, prediction,
and optimization methods have extremely reduced the need to use traditional ex-
perimental trials and measurements for product and process improvement. These
techniques include statistical methods (such as ANOVA), machine learning methods
(such as artificial neural networks, ANNs), as well as optimization methods using
meta-heuristic algorithms [5]. Nowadays, the use of FEA methods combined with
modern optimization methods has helped manufacturers select the optimal levels of
input parameters and achieve the highest quality products [20]. Due to the complex
behavior of polymers, particularly when processed by injection molding, many param-
eters can affect the product quality. Hence, monitoring and controlling each of these
parameters as well as their interactions is vital to prevent injection defects.
1.1
Background and Motivation
The quality of injection molded products is dependent on a large number of process pa-
rameters during injection, packing, and cooling phases[3]. To exploit the advantages
of the injection molding process, many of these parameters should be monitored, con-
trolled, and if necessary, optimized[15]. Several studies are available in literature[23,
8], but they are usually either limited to one or two materials [11]. What is more,
many different machine learning methods have been employed in the literature to
predict the quality characteristics of the injected parts [2]. In this project, a large
dataset including numerous materials will be employed to create a general prediction
model for injection molding with different materials.
1.1.1
Project Scope
The aim of the project is to establish a clear correlation between the process param-
eters of melt temperature, mold temperature, and injection speed and the objectives
which are part weight, maximum injection pressure, and cavity injection time.
In this regard, several finite element analyses will be carried out using state of the
art injection molding simulations technology. The results of the simulations need to
be extracted. Thereafter, these results will be employed for data-driven approaches
to see the effect of process parameters on the objectives. Hence, different prediction

CHAPTER 2
State of Art
Plastic injection molding includes four phases:
1. Injection: During this phase, the Injection Machine pushes molten plastics into
the closed mold to be nearly filled (mostly up to 98% of the cavity volume).
2. Pressure: The Pressure decreases (usually to 80% of the maximum injection
pressure) and the mold fills. The pressure will be held to avoid the material
exit the mold until solidification of the gate.
3. Cooling: After filling the mold, cooling phase starts. A coolant fluid (usually
water) will pass through the cooling channels of the mold to cool down and
solidify the melt.
4. Ejection: After cooling and solidification of the melt, the mold opens, and the
part ejects from the mold.

Factorial designs are the best way to interpret an experiment to get a better under-
standing of the experiment. The factor is the input parameters and the objectives
are the possible outcomes. If there are ’a’ level of factor A and ’b’ level of factor
B, each run has ’ab’ treatment combinations. The effect of a factor is the change in
response produced by a change in the level of a factor. The change of response of a
single factor with a single response is the main effect of the factor. The treatment
combination of several factor that affects the results of the response is the interaction
effect of the factor. The experiment is quantitative and it is easy to interpolate the
data if a series of readings are taken based on the design of experiments principles.
The equation generated in this process is called empirical model. General approach
to fitting empirical model is called regression analysis. While fitting, linear fit and
quadratic fit are widely used. Quadratic fit provides better results than the linear
fit[13].

regions[22].

3.3.2
Input Process Parameters
The Input process parameters are the set of inputs given at the certain conditions.
It is normally recommended by the manufacturer to give certain conditions for a
particular material grades.
3.3.2.1
Melt Temperature
Increasing or decreasing the melt temperature will leads to affect the viscosity of the
material. If the melt temperature is lowered, the viscosity increases and for increasing
melt temperature will lead to decrease the viscosity of the material. This viscosity
variation will also affect the injection speed of the material. Thats why Injection
speed is also considered as an input parameter[6].
3.3.2.2
Mold Temperature
The Mold temperature is also an important parameter. An appropriate mold tem-
perature will reduce the defect of the part. Since the filling, packing and cooling
stages are mainly happened in the mold, the appropriate mold temperature has to
be maintained. Hence it is considered as one of the input parameter.
3.3.2.3
Injection speed
For most of the material, the injection speed is the crucial parameter to reduce large
amount of defects in the plastic part. The Injection speed is a critical parameter to
reduce the time, avoid short shot and many other things. The Injection speed has
to be minimum, such that it must be suﬀiciently high to avoid premature freezing
and short shot.
The Maximum Injection speed is to be in a level that the melt
shear rate does not exceed the critical share rate of the plastic being moulded. An
appropriate Melt Temperature, Mold Temperature and Injection Speed is required
for each material grade to get a defect free product[4].
3.3.3
Objectives
The Objectives are the parameters that are relative to the variation of the above
mentioned input parameters. It is not easily predictable to see that the below men-
tioned effects will be purely based on the single input parameter. There is also a
possibility that the variation of responses also based on the interaction between the
input parameters.
3.3.3.1
Part weight
In general, the part weight of the material is the product of the material density
and the volume of the geometry. Since the geometry is fixed Dog-bone two cavity
specimen, the volume is fixed.
Hence the part weight varies by material density
based on each material grades. However this variation of part weight is subjected
to variation by the melt temperature, mold temperature and injection speed of each
material grades which leads to different part quality across specimen.
3.3.3.2
Cavity Injection Time
The Cavity Injection Time is the time taken by the machine to fill the cavity ge-
ometry. Since we need time, we may assume that the distance is constant for the
fixed geometry. The Cavity Injection Time is inversely proportional to the injection

3.3.5
Uncontrollable Factors
The Uncontrollable factors are the flow coeﬀicients associated with each material
grade. The flow coeﬀicients are determined by means of viscosity, pressure, volume
and temperature of the material during flow. The Viscosity is the flowing resistance
offered by the fluid. The viscosity usually shows shear rate dependent properties.
With the shear rate increasing continuously, polymeric chains are much more well
oriented. The Viscosity is also sensitive to temperature of the fluid. Increase the
temperature, the decrease in fluid viscosity will occur. But when consider the pressure,
the viscosity increases with increase in pressure. There are many theoretical models
are available to find the viscosity based on the shear rate as viscosity model and the
dependence of specific volume on temperature and pressure as pvT model[6].
3.3.5.1
Viscosity Model
There are different mathematical models to represent the relationship between the
viscosity and shear rate for thermoplastics. They are
• Newtonian Fluid
• Power-law Model
• Modified Cross Model(1)
• Modified Cross Model(2)
• Modified Cross Model(3)
• Carreau Model
• Carreau-Yasuda Model
• Herschel-Bulkley Cross Model(2)
• Herschel-Bulkley Cross Model(3)
Among them, Modified Cross Model(2) and Modified Cross Model(3) are used widely.
Modified Cross Model(3) is one of the frequently used model to simulate the plastic
flow which is capable to describe both Newtonian and shear thinning region and width
range of temperature dependence. However, Modified Cross Model(2) is also used in
some of the thermoplastic material to describe the shear rate dependence only on
Newtonian and shear thinning region[6].

4.2.1.3
Regression Model
Regression model is expressed in terms of regression equation. To form the equation,
the factors are interpreted as
• A - Melt Temperature
• B - Mold Temperature
• C - Injection Speed
• AB - Melt Temperature and Mold Temperature Interaction
• AC - Melt Temperature and Injection speed Interaction
• BC - Mold Temperature and Injection speed Interaction
The regression coeﬀicients are the coeﬀicients of the above mentioned factors. Those
coeﬀicients are expressed as β1, β2, β3, β4, β5, β6. The Regression equation for main
effects with second order interaction is given by
M[g] = µ+β1∗(A)+β2∗(B)+β3∗(C)+β4∗(A∗B)+β5∗(A∗C)+β6∗(B∗C) (4.1)
T[s] = µ+β1 ∗(A)+β2 ∗(B)+β3 ∗(C)+β4 ∗(A∗B)+β5 ∗(A∗C)+β6 ∗(B ∗C) (4.2)
P[MPa] = µ+β1∗(A)+β2∗(B)+β3∗(C)+β4∗(A∗B)+β5∗(A∗C)+β6∗(B∗C) (4.3)
In the above equation, there is a new term µ which is the intercept/mean of the
responses. The notations M, T and P refer to the objectives Part Weight, Cavity
Injection Time and Maximum Injection pressure, respectively. The Part weight pa-
rameter estimates for PLA is shown in the below figure 4.15.
Figure 4.15: Part weight Parameter Estimates for PLA Natureworks 7000D in JMP
In Parameter estimates, the values shown in the estimate column for each input
parameter is the coeﬀicient explained above. In PLA Natureworks 7000D, the melt
temperature ranges from 200◦C to 220◦C. Within this range, all the levels are able
to calculate with the above mentioned equations. Since there are 36 materials taken
for study, there will be 108 equations which leads to 756 coeﬀicients. To make the
comparison, the above mentioned results are replicated in python.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
With the consideration of statistical model, it is evident that the part weight has
better prediction. Whether the material grades in the list as well as the materials
not in the list provides a good results. The Materials from Modified Cross Model(3)
has better prediction of part weight than materials from Modified Cross Model(2). It
is also same for the machine learning model in terms of part weight, but the trend
varies.
The Cavity Injection time statistical model predictions are having atleast 10% error
within the material grades in the list. This also reflects in the materials which are not
in the list of 36 materials. On the other hand machine learning model has a better
predictions of cavity injection time when compared to the statistical model.
Both statistical model and machine learning model, Maximum Injection Pressure
response performs better for most of the materials in the list. The material grades
in the high performance engineering plastic and advanced engineering plastic also
have good predictions. The material grades not in the list have bad predicts with
output output responses where the absolute error is higher.
On the other hand,
the modified cross model(3) combination has better predictions than modified cross
model(2) combination in terms of maximum injection pressure.
Overall, the possibility of obtaining a data driven modelling is possible by means of
both statistical methods and Machine learning methods. However, statistical model
requires less ranges of injection speed, and both statistical and Machine learning
model requires appropriate Injection Molding machine suitable for all material grades
will provide more accurate predictions. Apart from that both model requires more
amount of data to accurately predict the results for all the material grades. More than
5000 material grades from different manufacturers are available in Moldex3D. With
the data of 36 materials, the prediction models perform better in all the responses.
Hence the Data Driven Modelling is achieved by simulating the process parameters
in Micro Injection Molding.

CHAPTER 7
Future Works
7.1
Dataset
It is impossible to predict a large set of material grades from different manufacturers
with just 36 material grades. Hence the dataset needs to be increased. The range of
Injection speed has to be reduced. Since the melt temperature and mold temperature
input levels are directly taken from Moldex3D, the range of injection speed are to
be minimized to get a better prediction model. Either the selected material will be
either 23 or 33 design. The combination of both provides an impossible way of finding
root cause for the deviation.
7.2
Plastic defects as a Response
There are multiple defects in the plastic parts. If the plastic defects are quantifiable,
then the defects are also considered as response. This will also predict the defect of
the part for particular input process parameter setting.
7.3
Geometry
The Geometry taken for this project is Dog-bone specimen with two runners suitable
for micro injection molding. Once the dataset is prepared, it is possible to try for
other complex geometries for Micro as well as conventional Injection molding and
achieve Data Driven Modelling with minimal errors
7.4
Comparison of Experimental and Simulated
Responses
The Simulated responses provide single output for one input parameter setting. Whereas
in real time each replicates has a variation for single input process parameter setting.
With the same material grade from the manufacturer and the same injection molding
machine the experimental test are carried out to visualize how much the responses
deviated from the simulated value
