I. Introduction 
a. Opening statement 
In 2015, during the United Nations Climate Change Conference, the Conference of Parties adopted the 
Paris Agreement, which aims to “[hold] the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 
°C above pre-industrial levels and [pursue] efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above 
pre-industrial levels”. This quantified mitigation target at the global level is accompanied by the 
recognition of “the need for an effective and progressive response to the urgent threat of climate change 
on the basis of the best available scientific knowledge” (UNFCCC, 2015). This position was taken after 
numerous global political gatherings, that have taken place for decades (the Stockholm Conference in 
1972, the Earth Summit in 1992, the various COPs), aimed at creating a governmental approach to 
mitigate greenhouse gas emissions, which ultimately reached a consensus among the various actors 
present. (Barrett, 2015)   
This global commitment is then followed by near- and long-term GHG emission reduction targets from 
unions of countries, countries and even cities (Hale et al., 2021). The funnel narrows to reduce the scale 
increasingly, until the question of local actors arises with an encouragement of the Paris Agreement 
which “Welcomes the efforts of all non-Party stakeholders to address and respond to climate change, 
including […] the private sector.” (UNFCCC, 2015). Non-states actors are the last granularity to must 
commit, especially as their importance is not to be neglected. Indeed, they emit 10 GtCO2e, which 
represents approximatively 20% of the global GHG emissions. (‘Climate Commitments of Subnational 
Actors and Business’, 2016) 
The world of scientific research must take into account the 20% of greenhouse gas emissions in current 
writings, and their reduction will require help from the academic world. For this reason, this thesis 
tackles the problem of managing the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions on an organisational scale, 
via decarbonization tools: this support is necessary to be in line with the trajectories defined by the 
IPCC. (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2022)

b. State-of-the-art 
In the last three decades, this sub- and non-state actors commitment development has resulted in actions 
with the objectives to mitigate GHG emissions and adapt to climate risks, based on sciences (Aggeri 
and Cartel, 2017; Iken, Aggeri and Morel, 2019). This period began with organizations commitments in 
order to find alternatives scientific studies wishing an highlighting of the lack of climate change 
scientific evidence and the high economic costs for an organization to quit fossil fuels, with the Global 
Climate Coalition in 1989, to minimize the impact of climate change (Brulle, 2022). However, as the 
academic literature evolves (as we can see with IPCC publications evolution, from first Assessment 
Report in 1990 to sixth Assessment Report in 2021 and 2022), so do businesses, and in 2010, the World 
Business Council for Sustainable Development, a coalition of 190 multinational corporations, 
recognized businesses responsibility and the need for private sector commitment in international climate 
negotiations, and encouraged self-governance and voluntary approaches for climate action from 
organizations (WBCSD, 2010). Specifically on the climate perspective, the private sector plays a key 
role in the transition to a low carbon society. To support organizations in this major challenge, 
regulations have taken place (for example, in France, there are multiple regulation laws implemented 
since a decade : Loi Grenelle II, 2010 ; Loi TECV, 2015 ; Loi Energie-Climat, 2019) and 
decarbonization tools developed (SBTi, 2015, 2019; ABC (Association Bilan Carbone), 2017b, 2017a) 
(non-exhaustive list of them) constituting regulatory or voluntary reporting and steering of GHG emitted 
by their actions. 
Grey literature also supports organizations, by introducing tools to deal with climate issues at 
organization level and to integrate climate into organization strategies (CBI, 2021; CDP, 2021). These 
tools may relate to measure GHG emissions, set mitigation targets, design low-carbon strategies or 
giving guidelines (Eleftheriadis and Anagnostopoulou, 2017; Hale et al., 2021). However, no academic 
literature has been identified to compare and evaluate these different decarbonization tools. Academic 
literature in regards with low-carbon strategies for organizations has one main axis: carbon accounting 
tools (Kolk, Levy and Pinkse, 2008; Le Breton and Aggeri, 2018, 2020; Marlowe et al., 2022). There is 
==PAGE:7==
3 
 
a second and more recent focus in regards to organization climate strategies, focusing on the Science-
Based Targets tools or other ways to downscale the global climate change issue to the organization level 
(Bjørn et al., 2017; Ryberg et al., 2018; Faria and Labutong, 2019; Walenta, 2019; Andersen et al., 
2021; Giesekam et al., 2021; Rekker et al., 2021).  
Based on a systematic literature review, Marlowe et al highlights that though organization climate 
strategies are much broader than measurement, since much of the current research is still focused on this 
step (Marlowe et al., 2022). In terms of organization climate transition tools, numerous articles have 
been focusing on carbon price, but as Aggeri and Cartel shows, economic tools are not the only ones for 
organization climate transition and they must be considered as complementary to others, such as carbon 
accounting, carbon management actions, or low-carbon transition strategies (Aggeri and Cartel, 2017). 
Indeed, very few articles are focusing on strategic planning, transition pathway or organization climate 
action. There is a need for further research on how climate can be integrated into decision-making 
processes, on link between city-level and organizational level GHGs measurement, on budgeting, on 
organization climate action scenarios, on low-carbon transition strategies and on the integration of social 
equity to ensure that climate action does not result in furthering social concerns (Matsumura, Prakash 
and Vera-Muñoz, 2014; Lippert, 2015; Vesty, Telgenkamp and Roscoe, 2015; Aggeri and Cartel, 2017; 
Griffin, Lont and Sun, 2017; Le Breton and Aggeri, 2018; Marlowe et al., 2022).  
c. Objectives of the study 
As a result of these multiple commitments, as explained on the previous sub-section, a new demand has 
emerged, with a need to create low-carbon strategy tools for these different actors (‘Climate 
Commitments of Subnational Actors and Business’, 2016). The response has been the development of 
different benchmarks, methodologies, regulations, and recommendations to measure emissions, create 
reduction targets, define a transition trajectory, or create action plans, and sometimes several of these 
objectives at the same time (CDP, 2021). This multitude of tools, all different in their objectives as well 
as in their mode of operation, are sometimes misunderstood by organisations or analysts, which seems 
==PAGE:8==
4 
 
to not be able to respond to a climate transition challenge by a precise strategy and defined targets (Faria 
and Labutong, 2020). The efficiency of these decarbonization tools and tools to tackle climate change 
from a business perspective is also questionable (Hale et al., 2021). Synergies, complementarities, and 
barriers between decarbonization tools need to be understood to ensure consistent and credibly corporate 
low-carbon transition plan. 
Thus, by comparing these multiple tools, a highlighting of a method with the best characteristics in each 
step of defining a low-carbon strategy is possible. However, tools have a lot of precise specifications, 
and no peer-reviewed literature on this subject exist (CDP, 2021). This MSc thesis therefore aims to 
create a scientific comparison referential of different existing tools on low-carbon strategies 
implementation at the organizational level; to develop a framework to assess decarbonization tools for 
organizations, clear in its choices and precise in its criteria; to test a consolidated decarbonization tool 
based on previous results with existing case studies. To define these objectives, in its globality, a 
separation of the thesis work in four steps is made, as it follows:  
- 
Literature review, with the aim of clarifying the work done about low-carbon management for 
organisations. 
- 
Decarbonization tools identification and review matrix development, with the aim of collecting 
the previous work done by the scientific and professional community. 
- 
Decarbonization tools assessment, scoring and ranking systems development, with the aim of 
deepening and evaluating of the work done by decarbonization tools. 
- 
Consolidated decarbonization tool development and application, based on knowledge and 
maturity brought by the first three phases of work

a. Methodological approach 
i. Semantic  
During this MSc thesis, vocabulary will be used on many topics, and to avoid misunderstanding of these 
ones, a definition of important and potentially confusing terms is provided: 
- 
Organisation: Term used to define an organization, regardless of its size, sector, and country. 
- 
Methodology: Body of methods, that can be defined as the research methodology applied 
during this entire thesis. The term “methodological” will be used to define a principle linked to 
this methodology. 
- 
Method: A particular process used for a particular task, as part of a set that is the methodology 
- 
Decarbonization tool: Tool used in organizations to decarbonise their activity, which will be 
studied here in the context of this review. 
- 
Criterion: Indicator created and defined in this thesis, allowing the evaluation of a 
decarbonization tool. 
- 
Cluster: Grouping made of a certain data, for example a criterion or a decarbonization tool. 
- 
Colour assessment system: System that assess an indicator with a colour translation of defined 
levels, with 3, 4 or 5 colours. 
- 
Review matrix: Matrix correlating criteria and groups of criteria with decarbonization tools. 
- 
Scoring system: System that establish a numerical score, based in that thesis on a previous 
colour assessing system. 
- 
Ranking system: System that establish a ranking, based in that thesis on a previous colour 
assessing system or a scoring system. 
Some new terms will also be defined throughout the MSc thesis, at their creation.

c. Identification of existing decarbonization tools 
In this section, the objective is to define and develop the methods used to identify the existing 
decarbonization tools, and among these the decarbonization tools to be taken into account in this study. 
A decision was made to have the largest scope of choices possible, in order to obtain a complete 
overview of the existing decarbonization tools for organisations. This scope is therefore defined as a 
goal, to which each decarbonization tool must answer. This goal scope is defined as “Every tool used 
by an organisation in order to develop a low-carbon strategy, in at least one step of this strategy 
development1”. Low-carbon trajectories at global or national scales have also been regarded, as it is 
common that organizations align their carbon budgets with these in terms of annual GHG emissions 
reduction rate (Aubert et al., 2021). 
Moreover, in terms of geographical scope, the objective is to obtain an international representativeness 
by not taking this dimension into account in the search for decarbonization tools. This means that we

d. Review criteria: Criteria choice and definition 
In order to assess these selected decarbonization tools, a choice was made to create a review matrix, 
with the decarbonization tools as vertical inputs, and criteria as horizontal inputs. These criteria 
have been divided into 6 main groups and a single secondary group (for criteria outside the study scope 
but allowing a complete overview of carbon management), which will be referred from now as criteria 
clusters. These 6 main criteria clusters are: 
- 
Adaptability: Decarbonization tool adaptability to all organisations 
- 
Decarbonization tool goal: Objectives set to which the decarbonization tool responds3 
- 
Completeness of tool extent: Scope of action and measures of the decarbonization tool 
- 
Scientific robustness: Decarbonization tool ability to be science-based, with quantitative 
criteria and objectives, and to translate qualitative information into technical data 
- 
Accessibility and transparency: Clarity of the decarbonization tool and its outputs, as much

Most of the 38 criteria are graded on 3 steps, except for 6 criteria graded on 2 steps and one on 4 steps. 
This choice of three steps was made with the objective to answer the question "Does this decarbonization 
tool fully meets this criterion?". The colour green is used when the decarbonization tool fully meets the 
criterion, red when it does not meet the criterion at all, and orange when it addresses issues related to 
the criterion but does not go into sufficient detail to consider that it fully meets it. 
Criteria scored on only 2 levels are those that are yes-no criteria, totally quantitative and without any 
intermediate response, either the decarbonization tool meets this criterion, or does not. On the other 
hand, the 4-level rate for a criterion is made when a single intermediate level is insufficient, and it is 
necessary to add one more level to reflect the diversity of solutions that are not equal (in this case, this 
criterion deals with the monitoring of the trajectory over time, and the difference between a solution in 
the form of a table, a digital monitoring solution and a human monitoring solution is deep). 
A choice was made to exclude the possibility to give relative importance to criteria in comparison to 
each other. This relative importance may have been done in other work (ILCD, 2010), but the 
development work carried out in this study was based on a view of criteria all having an equal 
importance. This was done in order to not bias the work by imposing a personal importance on criteria 
developed without any underlying scientific judgement.  
e. Consolidated decarbonization tool 
The objective of this section is to describe the decarbonization tool used to develop and test the 
consolidated decarbonization tool. 
i. Ranking and choice of relevant decarbonization tools 
The development of the consolidated decarbonization tool is based on a decarbonization tools 
ranking, itself based on the review matrix. Once a ranking is made, an analysis study will be carried 
==PAGE:24==
20 
 
out to investigate the best decarbonization tools in each step of a low carbon strategy4. It will then be 
used with a structure for the consolidated decarbonization tool, and its shortcomings will be highlighted. 
If several decarbonization tools can fill these steps, the ranking carried out previously will be reused to 
choose the most efficient decarbonization tool in the specific step. 
The ranking is based on the assessment of the decarbonization tools developed in the review 
matrix. However, with a colour rating system, a ranking appears complicated. Indeed, a colour average 
is complicated, and would mean digitally translating the colour, averaging it, and then translating it back 
into colour, using a colour-to-digital translation scale. Another solution is not to use an average colour 
but to use a colour ranking directly, which is retained in one of the three ranking methods used. 
This ranking will therefore be based on three different rankings systems, and then to obtain a 
classification not truncated by methodological biases such as colour scanning. These three ranking 
systems are draw on the data developed in the review matrix: 
- 
Olympic games ranking: a ranking system based on the Olympic Games ranking system to 
classify countries. This system ranks the best decarbonization tool as the decarbonization tool 
with the highest number of green criteria. If two decarbonization tools have the same number 
of green criteria, then they are tied by their number of orange criteria, and if they also have the 
same number of orange criteria, they are tied by their number of red criteria. If they have the 
same number of red, orange, and green criteria, they are ranked at the same row n, and the 
ranking continue from rank n+2 (row n+1 is empty). 
- 
Mean numeric grade ranking: A ranking system based on a numeric translation of the colour 
grading system of the review matrix. Each colour has a number, equivalent to its level (a green 
cell is equal to a 3, an orange cell is equal to a 2, a green cell is equal to a 1 in the 3-colour 
grading system ; a 2-colour grading system criteria will be graded with 3 or 1 only and a 4-
colour grading system criteria will be graded with 3, 2,33, 1,66 or 1, with the same way of 
 
4 Cf. sub-section II.e.ii. “Structure development” 
==PAGE:25==
21 
 
thinking than the 3-colour grading system numeric translation). After this translation, a mean 
grade is calculated to obtain a numeric ranking. This numerical value is taken to two decimal 
places. 
- 
Weighted mean numeric grade ranking: A ranking system based on the same way of thinking 
than the mean numeric grade ranking, but with another way of calculation the mean grade. This 
system calculates firstly the mean grade of each criteria cluster, and then it calculates a mean 
grade of these 5 mean grades, to obtain a finale mean grade and a numeric ranking based on this 
last grade. With this rating method, criteria clusters have the same weight. This numerical value 
is taken to two decimal places. 
These choices are made to create a ranking and make a choice of the best decarbonization tool based on 
the review matrix. This ranking of the best decarbonization tool can evolve, in particular to become the 
ranking of the best tool only among the tools fulfilling a specific indicator (for example, criterion n°XX 
is green), making it possible to define, for example, the best tool among the tools enabling carbon 
accounting. By keeping the colour grading system, the main issue is to translate into the ranking system 
the acceptance interval of each colour, which seems to be at odds with a single numerical score for each 
colour or an equality given to all identical squares, but it remains the only user-friendly way to translate 
these colours into a ranking system. 
This choice of a colour grading system is based on the scoring system used in assessment of diverse 
tools from the academic literature, according to steps not rated by colours but by the ability to comply 
with the definition of the criterion (e.g. from Full compliance to No compliance) (ILCD, 2010). The 
choice of translating these steps into a colour grading system was made with a view to obtain a review 
matrix where visualization allows an initial understanding.

iii. Choice of organisation studied 
After the choice of decarbonization tools forming the consolidated decarbonization tool comes the 
choice of the organisation that will be the illustrative example of this new decarbonization tool. The 
objective is to choose a unique organisation; thus, the choice must be made well enough to prove that 
the use of the consolidated decarbonization tool is possible with every organisation, or at least a large 
majority. This choice of the organisation studied was based on two criteria: organisation size and sector.  
Firstly, based on the idea that a decarbonization tool that works on a large organization will work on a 
small organization, but the opposite doesn’t work, as scaling up is easier in one direction than in the 
other (several geographical and organisational entities, slower decision making, high hierarchy, high 
carbon emissions), the choice was made to take a large organization, with at least more than 250 
employees, several departments, and several geographical sites. 
==PAGE:28==
24 
 
Secondly, an organisation with a sector too specific would not make an almost general case. However, 
no organization can define itself as working in all professional sectors. The aim is therefore to find a 
organization working in several sectors or having a link with many sectors. A production organization 
would therefore be complex to study from this point of view, so we will concentrate on a service 
organisation with links to many professional sectors. 
Finally, the research of the organization with these two criteria will be done with the collaboration of 
Toovalu. Thus, the Toovalu database will be used, with these two criteria, to find the organisation that 
will be used. This organization will be anonymised, for data confidentiality reasons. 
In view of these three criteria, the choice fell on a French service organisation with between 600 and 
700 employees, 5 different sites distributed throughout France, which work on the integration of digital 
solutions, the outsourcing of information systems and the publishing of management software for 
different sectors (Transport, Industry, Health, Retail, Service, social housing, administration). 
Moreover, this organisation is a Toovalu customer, thus a lot of data is available.

IV. Discussion 
a. Discussion of decarbonization tools assessment results 
Based on these previous graphics, some comments on the set of results from the review matrix are 
possible to do.  
i. Scoring and ranking systems 
Some comments are to be made about the three different scoring systems.  
The Olympic Games scoring system uses the full medal ranks (i.e. all types of "medals" were used at 
least once to decide between two decarbonization tools), which proves the usefulness of this ranking 
methods. In comparison, the two other numerical scoring methods have equally ranked decarbonization 
tools because of the rounding to the hundredth of their numerical grade. 
In addition, some decarbonization tools (such as CA100+ or TPI, which are respectively 7.5 and 4.5 
places higher in the Olympic games scoring ranking than their average numerical ranking) rank very 
well with the Olympic Games scoring method compared to the two numerical methods. This is due 
to the "Manicheism" of these two decarbonization tools assessment compared to closely ranked 
decarbonization tools, i.e. the fact that they have a lot of green or red criteria, but very few orange 
criteria, thus these two decarbonization tools have a mean numerical score that is close to many other 
decarbonization tools but a marked difference when the ranking is mostly based on the ability of a 
decarbonization tool to have a maximum of green. By analysing the ranking of the Olympic games 
scoring system, it is possible to see that other decarbonization tools are also very "Manichean" but do 
not have a very different ranking in the other scoring systems (such as OCARA, CDP-Q or FF55 which 
have 1, 2 and 3 orange scores respectively, but which have a difference in rank between the Olympic 
Games scoring system and the numerical scoring systems of -0.5, -2 and 0 respectively), this is explained 
by their position in the ranking, which is lower but above all further away from the other decarbonization 
==PAGE:39==
35 
 
tools, it is therefore more complicated to gain many places. This information on the number of 
decarbonization tools in the ranking can be seen in Figure 5 - Numerical scoring systems distribution. 
The solution to make the scoring systems more equal would be to take into account the standard 
deviation in the ranking of numerical scoring systems, which would translate the Manichaeism in a 
numerical way, and which would make it possible to distinguish between the decarbonization tools 
ranked equally. 
In the opposite, some decarbonization tools are very poorly ranked with the Olympic Games 
scoring method compared to numerical scoring methods (such as IPCC, TCFD or NZI which have 
respectively a ranking difference of -7, -9 and -13.5 between the Olympic Games scoring method and 
numerical methods) This is due to a large number of grey criteria (which reduces the absolute number 
of marks distributed in the coloured rows (red, orange and green) and thus inevitably the ranking of this 
system, but which does not influence the average numerical mark (the grey marks having no influence 
on them)), coupled with a large number of orange scores and an average score of the numerical systems 
in the intervals with a high density of decarbonization tools, making the evolution of rankings easier. 
The solution to this problem would be to make the Olympic Games ranking with a percentage of red, 
orange, and green marks in relation to the total-coloured marks of the assessment of the decarbonization 
tools, which would remove the influence of the grey marks in this ranking and thus make the system 
fairer. 
In view of these various criticisms, it can be said that both numerical and coloured scoring systems 
are not perfect and could have improvements to make them more accurate and revealing of 
reality. However, the sensitivity remains high, the cases of fundamental difference in ranking (more 
than 3 places difference, i.e. 10% of the total number of places) between the different methods are low 
compared to the cases of little or no difference. Keeping an average of all these scoring and ranking 
systems therefore seems to be the best solution to have the highest possible mathematical sensitivity for 
this ranking system.

ii. Criteria and criteria clusters 
Some comments are also to be made on the different criteria and criteria clusters, in view of the dataset 
created in this review matrix. As shown in Figure 3 – Graphical visualisation of numerical scoring 
systems, the different criteria clusters do not score equally well, and some stand out strongly. The 
average of the mean scores for each criteria cluster is 0.58 but with a standard deviation of 0.16, a 
relative standard deviation of 27.6%. This therefore numerically highlights the divergence in scoring 
between the different criteria clusters, which can be explained by the definition of the clusters itself.  
Indeed, the highest rated criteria cluster is the one judging the adaptability of the decarbonization 
tools, to the professional sector of the organisation but also to its size. This criteria cluster is rated at 
0.84 on average, which is 0.26 points higher than the average grade. This is due to the need to separate 
the different occupational sectors in low carbon trajectory studies, as it is currently the case in the current 
scientific scenarios used as a global reference, as the 6th Assessment Report of the IPCC 3rd Working 
Group does on the Figure SPM.5: Illustrative Mitigation Emissions Pathways (IMPs) and net zero CO2 
and GHG emissions strategies  (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2022), and to separate the 
different organisation sizes in low carbon trajectory studies, as shown by scientific research in the field 
in recent years (Hampton et al., 2019), in order to be able to specify priority actions and improvement 
areas, and to adapt to the environmental and legislative context of the organisation according to these 
two characteristics. 
Then, there is a group of 4 criteria clusters, which have an average score in the range 0.58 ± 0.12. 
These 4 criteria clusters are, in descending order of average score, Accessibility and transparency (0.69), 
Stakeholders acceptance (0.59), Scientific robustness (0.51), and Completeness of tool extent (0.46). An 
explanation for this mean score centred on the mean for each of these clusters is also possible: 
For the Accessibility and transparency cluster (+0.11 compared to the average), this above-average 
grade reflects an important fact: this cluster judges the accessibility and transparency of the information 
and data related to the decarbonization tool, however methodologically when information is missing the 
==PAGE:41==
37 
 
criterion is assessed grey. There are therefore a lot of grey (20.6% of all scores in this cluster) and green 
(47.9% of all scores in this cluster) scores but very few red scores (17.6% of all scores in this cluster), 
which is due to a lack of transparency. Indeed, this lack reflects an impossible access to existing data 
(e.g. the criterion of the number of organisations that have used the decarbonization tool, the red note 
being no organisation, the grey note being a lack of information, if no communication has been made 
then it will be noted grey, and therefore the criterion is always noted green, orange or grey, because no 
organisation communicates on the fact that it has no organization that uses it) This red criterion is not 
well defined, and would need to be redefined, or grey scores taken into account in the numerical score, 
as grey has sometimes become the equivalent of a fair red criterion, and so this gives a particularly high 
score. 
For the Stakeholders acceptance cluster (+0.01 compared to the average), the average score which is 
extremely close to the overall average reflects a distribution of scores which is more or less identical to 
the overall scores of the criteria, so there is no difference between this distribution and the generality of 
the scoring system, so this cluster is devoid of explanation for divergence from the average. 
For the Scientific robustness cluster (-0.07 compared to the average), this below-average score reflects 
the conclusions of the state of the art: there is a lack of scientific research on the subject, and a 
decorrelation between the work done in organisations and that done in academic research. There is some 
scientific rigour, however, including a consensus on quantifying climate performance, but this 
quantification is limited to the single criterion of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, with no broader 
environmental criteria relating to different planetary boundaries. 
For the Completeness of tool extent cluster (-0.12 compared to the average), this rather low score, the 
first below 50%, highlights major shortcomings that seem essential, showing a lack of commitment by 
managers and organisations to a credible and consistent low carbon trajectory, rarely requiring a full 
scope of the organisation or a commitment to targets linked to the objectives that are now the norm. This 
score is not lower, and remains close to the average of the criteria clusters, because it is based on criteria 
that deal with the essentials of a low carbon approach, in particular considering the initial situation.

Ultimately, a last cluster, with an average grade of 0.39, is the Decarbonization tool goal cluster. 
This very low score compared to other clusters, with a negative offset of 0.19 from the average, is 
explained by the functional unit of this decarbonization tool research scope. As a matter of fact, some 
decarbonization tool clusters have a very specific objective (Environmental assessment tool, Carbon 
accounting tool, National or sectoral low carbon trajectories), and therefore only tick one box out of 5, 
which gives these decarbonization tools an average score of 0.2 on this criteria cluster. Some 
decarbonization tools allow this average score to be raised, but this shows that on average, about two 
objectives are met by the decarbonization tools. 
These criteria clusters therefore highlight the fact that this functional unit biases the average score 
of the criteria cluster Decarbonization tool goal (but highlights decarbonization tools that meet all 
objectives, so this grouping remains important), that decarbonization tools need to be scientifically more 
precise and compelling, and generally more accessible on their tools and clearer on their communication.  
However, there are still criteria whose comments are not visible at the cluster level, but which need 
to be commented on all of these decarbonization tools: 
- 
The criteria Require envy from governance (2 green scores, 1 orange score, 30 red scores) 
demonstrates that the decarbonization tools do not need to be integrated into an existing 
approach, with a CSR manager and/or GHG emissions accounting already in place. 
- 
The criteria Reputational impact assessment (7 green scores, 2 orange scores, 24 red scores) 
demonstrates that quantitative or simple to answer indicators are very often taken into account, 
as shown by the criterion of quantifying climate performance, but that more predictive and 
qualitative criteria are rarer in these tools, because they bring a strong complexity for a non-
zero but weak technical contribution. 
- 
The criteria Support for organizations (5 green scores, 5 orange scores, 23 red scores) 
demonstrates that the organizations developing the decarbonization tools do not aim to actively 
accompany organizations in this process but only to offer them a framework and a tool enabling 
them to respond to their needs in autonomy. This would be a positive indicator if only the criteria

of ease of handling and use were sufficiently good to consider that the tools can be used, which 
is not the case with an average score of 0.58 and 0.61 respectively. 
- 
The criteria Uncertainty for responses (8 green scores, 1 orange score, 1 grey score, 23 red 
score) demonstrates that the scientific approach of taking into account uncertainties in the 
various calculations and numerical approaches is not taken into account, in particular when the 
scientific character of these tools is already questioned, and accentuates this criticism, 
uncertainty being a primary and indispensable stage in scientific reasoning. 
- 
The criteria Legal administrative use of output (3 green scores, 30 red scores) demonstrates that 
in addition to the detachment of these decarbonization tools from scientific research, there is a 
real detachment between the current national and global legal obligations and the tools 
developed, leading to two consequences: a need for organizations to actively undertake this 
process because there is no legal obligation to encourage them; and an inertia in the political 
and legal environment that is out of step with the reality of the climate deadlines that are the 
subject of a consensus in the scientific world. 
- 
The criteria Important barriers raised (1 orange score, 3 red scores, 29 grey scores) demonstrates 
that little feedback is received from users of the various decarbonization tools, and that existing 
feedback is not communicated by the organisations behind these decarbonization tools. There 
is therefore a real lack of publicly available feedback and continuous improvement processes. 
- 
The criteria Relevance with current policy targets (24 green scores, 9 red scores) demonstrates 
that, despite a disconnect between the political and legal world and that of GHG emissions 
management in organizations, the major political decisions, and in particular the Paris 
Agreement, make it possible to put forward a simple numerical objective accessible to all and 
taken into account as a reference objective to be followed at all levels. 
- 
The criteria Recognised authority behind the tool (26 green scores, 2 orange scores, 5 red scores) 
demonstrates that all of these decarbonisation tools have technical shortcomings and important 
criticisms, but that they are largely developed or supported by recognised organisations and 
considered reliable.

low carbon trajectories are therefore below or at the level of 50%, which shows that they meet few of 
the criteria but are not totally opposed to them either. The Carbon accounting tool and Low carbon

with this reference framework. Finally, the Environmental assessment tool cluster is the one with the

the small number of environmental assessment tools (only 3) which, out of these three tools, are quite

A major problem of communication of results, progress and overall development of the tools is

it has an added value compared to the set of decarbonisation tools already developed and reviewed in 
this thesis. Indeed, no decarbonisation tool currently has the capacity to meet all the criteria with green 
scores, and none of them consistently fulfils all the steps defined in this structure. This consolidated 
decarbonation tool therefore manages to meet the full scope required for a complete decarbonisation 
tool, with scientific robustness and operationality to the maximum of what has already been developed. 
Indeed, this consolidated decarbonisation tool has a green score on each criterion, as can be seen in the 
assessment of this decarbonisation tool, with the same assessment template that has been used for all 
the decarbonisation tool assessments in this review: 
Therefore, this new consolidated decarbonisation tool seems to be perfect, meeting almost each criterion 
defined before, however limitations and uncertainties are to be put forward on the development work of 
this tool, and recommendations to limit them are to be highlighted: 
- 
The consolidated decarbonisation tool has as a basis the methodological choices (mapping, 
scope of the study, etc.) of the GHG emissions accountment step. This step then takes on a 
strong importance, especially on the definition of the work scope, and will strongly influence 
the following steps. For example, the prioritisation of the improvement areas will depend to a 
large extent on the organisational scope studied and the greenhouse gas emissions taken into 
account. A separation of this methodological choice phase, which will define all the choices and 
hypotheses made on the whole of this decarbonisation tool, and the greenhouse gas emissions 
accounting phase should therefore be envisaged, in order to allow for a lower relative 
importance of this second stage and a function which is then only to account for greenhouse gas 
emissions according to what has been defined beforehand and for what is going to be decided 
later, and nothing more. 
- 
The decarbonisation tool developed starts directly with the carbon accounting phase, without 
any commitment from the organisation to implement a coherent, rigorous, and ambitious low 
==PAGE:53==
49 
 
carbon strategy. An improvement possibility would therefore be a prerequisite, similar to the 
ACT-S and ACT-A prerequisites, which require from organisations to demonstrate their 
commitment to this approach through compulsory training, modelled on didactic tools such as 
the Climate Collage, which allows rapid discovery of energy-climate issues for all employees 
of an organisation. 
- 
Each criterion is scored green, except five criteria which are: 
o Scored as orange for two of them (“Pluralism of environmental indicators” and 
“Recognised authority behind the tool development”). For the first one, it’s explained 
by the fact that there isn’t a decarbonization tool with a green score of “Pluralism of 
environmental indicators” criterion and a green score of “Factuality” criterion. A choice 
was made to prioritize “Factuality” more than “Pluralism of environmental indicators” 
in the decarbonization tool choice for the Indicators and targets step7, thus the chosen 
decarbonization tool doesn’t allow a green score for this criterion. For the second one, 
it's explained by the fact that the responsible person behind this tool development is a 
master student in environmental management and can’t be considered as a responsible 
authority. However, every step is developed by a recognised authority, only the 
structure and the choice of decarbonization tool is developed by this non-recognised 
authority. 
o Scored as grey for three of them (“Number of organizations that have already used it”, 
“Existing public case studies” and “Important barriers raised in case of study”) due to 
the fact that the decarbonization tool is still in development. These three criteria can’t 
be scored with another colour in this case, then no recommendation is done.

made, influencing the development of this consolidated decarbonization tool:

- 
There are a lot of uncertainties in the entire methodological approach of this thesis, but they are 
never taken into account. These uncertainties are particularly present during the decarbonization 
tools assessment, which influenced the next steps of the thesis and especially this consolidated 
decarbonization tool by the ranking system based on the decarbonization tools assessment and 
scoring. Colour grading system takes uncertainties into account in the fact that the colour leaves 
room for a response interval, which therefore reflects this uncertainty, but it is never expressed. 
Moreover, qualitative, or semi-qualitative assessment by a human also brings uncertainties, 
which must be expressed. This uncertainties add-on in the assessment system, and thus in the 
scoring and ranking systems, can bring a consolidation of these systems and data, and thus of 
this consolidated decarbonization tool. 
- 
Along the entire working time on this thesis, choices were influenced by numerous sources: the 
work already carried out by the co-supervisors and external experts; the work already carried 
out by Toovalu, and the methodological choices made; the papers selected and read which 
highlight certain decarbonization tools; the author's personal knowledge of some of the 
decarbonisation tools and therefore a preconceived opinion prior to the work of evaluating them. 
These influences are difficult to quantify, and the changes induced by them cannot be put 
forward, but it is necessary to put this work into context to understand the full picture. 
After having seen these different problems in the development of the consolidated decarbonisation tool, 
its evaluation and results, recommendations are also to be made on the further work to be given to the 
whole thesis and on the review of decarbonization tools for the implementation of low carbon strategy 
in companies. These recommendations will be made in the following section Conclusion.

V. Conclusion and recommendations 
In conclusion, this thesis takes place in a scientific environment where there is a real lack of academic 
research, and where reconnecting scientific and academic research to the professional world is becoming 
a priority. GHG emissions management are, since 2015 with the Paris Agreements and targets that 
emerged, the only vision of environmental management in companies, and the implementation of low-
carbon strategies with tools has not yet reached this level of maturity, notably because of a lack of 
development and research on the subject. 
Based on this observation, a selection of all decarbonisation tools and a review of these ones, using an 
assessment system developed within this thesis, was carried out, in order to, based on scoring and 
ranking systems, develop a consolidated decarbonization tool, with the best decarbonization tool for 
each step that are considered as the structure of a consistent, complete and operational decarbonization 
tool. This work enabled to create a tool which, despite the obstacles caused by the form of this scientific 
research, seems to synthesise in a single tool most of the research done on the subject.  
Consolidated decarbonization tool use having been shown succinctly with the help of an illustrative 
example, the general conclusion of this review and consolidation of decarbonisation tools tends to be 
that entities closely linked to the world of research but unconnected to the academic world (e.g. the CDP 
and ADEME) are the primary actors in the development of the decarbonization tools that best meet our 
criteria of credibility, scientific rigour and operationality in organizations. 
This study has three different objectives: to create a scientific comparison referential of different existing 
tools on low-carbon strategies implementation at the organizational level; to develop a framework to 
assess decarbonization tools for organizations, clear in its choices and precise in its criteria; to test a 
consolidated decarbonization tool based on previous results with existing case studies. These three 
objectives have therefore been addressed, as stated in this conclusion, and have led to the creation of 
this consolidated tool, which finally brings together these three objectives into one. 
==PAGE:56==
52 
 
However, the research work on this topic does not end with this MSc thesis, and recommendations can 
be made on how to proceed. First, even if the structure and the steps have been developed and shown 
with the help of an illustrative example, a proof of concept as well as the operational development of 
this tool seems to be necessary in order to introduce it properly in the professional world and within 
companies. Secondly, questions were raised about many of the methods used, including ranking 
systems, scoring and evaluation of methodological tools, which in themselves are questions that should 
be addressed individually to consolidate the different steps of this MSc thesis. Finally, apart from this 
consolidated tool, the major current challenge is to create a dynamic for reducing greenhouse gas 
emissions within companies, in particular by training them in energy and climate issues. Many solutions 
already exist, but during the development of this thesis two decarbonisation tools, the CDP 
Questionnaire, and the B-Impact Assessment, caught the attention of the people involved in this work 
for their ease of implementation within organizations. Without going into its development, this 
questionnaire was thought of as 10 questions that could judge the maturity of the company, whose 
objectives are introduced by the statement: "To judge the maturity of a company on the implementation 
of a low-carbon strategy within the company using the developed consolidated decarbonisation tool, the 
company must answer these 10 questions: ...". This ease of response, particularly through the small 
number of questions, must also be reflected in the questions themselves, by being rigorous and credible 
but understandable and comprehensible to everyone.
