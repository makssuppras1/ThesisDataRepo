Abstract
This report expands on the design evolution and perception of mobility aid, and the market ex-
ploration of the “Scandinavian Butler,” an innovative indoor rollator developed by byACRE. A
Danish company at the vanguard of transforming and challenging conventional perceptions of
assistive products. The research focuses on a new target market: hospitals and nursing homes
and identifies and addresses the limitations of the existing product in these environments.
The methodology adopts universal design, design thinking and system-oriented thinking, holis-
tic approaches that ensure accessibility, usability, and convenience for a diverse user base. By
involving users in the design process, and empathizing with their context, the project adopts a
user-centered approach. It considers the product as an integral component of a broader ecosys-
tem, considering its interactions and impact within its environment, stakeholders, and society in
general.
The research evaluates a spectrum of design alternatives, examining their feasibility, desirability,
and practicality in the market. Conducting a comprehensive user and product analysis to under-
stand the needs, preferences, and behaviors of the target user audience, as well as to assess the
product’s current market status.
The culmination of this research is a strategic proposal for the redesign of the rollator, aimed at
increasing its adaptability, attractiveness, and practicality for the specified segment. The report
further analyzes the implications and makes recommendations for byACRE, paving the way to-
wards the introduction of a new product that repurpose mobility aids at hospitals and nursing
homes.
Keywords: mobility aids, rollator, Scandinavian Butler, well-being, aesthetics, system-oriented
thinking, universal design, product redesign.

Introduction
Assistive devices’ role in life and society is profound, demanding insight on a topic that is highly
sensitive. These devices are recognized not only for their functional advantages, but also for
their social implications. They are products that enhance and facilitate daily activities, but carry
an underlying stigma, often associated with a perceived decline or diminished joy of life. In an
evolving landscape, aesthetics are increasingly being prioritised by the industry, recognising that
appearance and functionality can coexist to combat stigma. [1] Therefore, designing aids can
be understand as a socio-technical system [2], and it is important to analyze the factors that
influence the system and what affects the user experience, in order to engage empathy, connect
with them and offer a solution beyond the traditional approaches.
Figure 1 below, corresponds to a systematic presentation of those factors and provides an initial
insight into a better understanding of the topic. Five key elements were highlighted, shaping the
basis of this research. It is based on personal observations on the surrounding community and
professional experience.

Following the representation, this report explores how social perceptions of disability influence

Methodology
The methodology for this project was guided by the principles of system-oriented thinking as
described in the book “No More Muddling Through” by Rainer Züst and Peter Troxler[2]. This
structured approach helped address the complex challenges associated with redesigning the Scan-
dinavian Butler rollator. Here’s how each phase of the project aligns with this methodology:
1. Search for Goals
Situation Analysis: The project began with a comprehensive analysis to understand the
current state of mobility aids and the specific challenges faced by users. This included task
analysis, examining existing products, market conditions, and user needs through interviews
and on-site observations. The system demarcation was also performed to define the project’s
boundaries, stakeholders, and key areas of focus.
Goal Definition: Based on the situation analysis, clear goals were established to guide the
project. These goals included improving the design and functionality of the rollator, reducing
stigma associated with its use, considering manufacture process and cost-effectiveness. The
goals were aligned with byACRE’s mission to innovate and destigmatize mobility aids.
2. Search for Solutions
Concept Synthesis and Analysis: This staged included benchmarking against popular models
in the market, assessing the current rollator’s features and performance, and selecting
critical requirements based on user feedback and market analysis.
Concept Development: Three conceptual proposals were created, each addressing the iden-
tified requirements and incorporating aesthetic and functional improvements. These pro-
posals were evaluated using a three-dimensional design framework to ensure they met the
project’s goals.
Prototyping: To provide fidelity to the three concepts, 3D printing were prepared for the
evaluation process.
3. Selection
Evaluation and Decision: The concepts prototypes were evaluation according to byACRE
interests.
User Feedback: A comprehensive market strategy was developed to introduce the redesigned
rollator to the healthcare market. This involved creating mood boards and client materials
to present the product effectively, and gathering user feedback to evaluated their reaction.

4.1
Historical overview
Medical assistance products have been around for centuries, their need arises from a variety of
social factors, primarily the constant search for self-improvement, empathy, and human dignity.[4]
These products reflect a collective commitment to improve the quality of life for all individuals.
During the Middle Ages, society largely ignored people with reduced mobility or other disabilities.
Many of these people were marginalized living on the edge of a society immersed in superstition.
It was not until the 15th century that mobility devices began to be developed in response to
compassion and practical necessity.[5] This era marked the beginning of a paradigm change, in
which philosophical movements, revolutions, wars and scientific advances catalyzed the evolution
of society. The inherent value of the human being was increasingly recognized, transcending the
mere physical capacity to contribute to society.
An example of such a contribution is the intellectual legacy of Stephen Hawking, whose theoretical
work in physics was not constrained by his physical condition. With his profound perspective of
life he constantly sent the message with great authority and sense of humor, that life should be
appreciated with fervor and that human mind possesses an extraordinary capacity to transcend
barriers.[6]
 “Sometimes I wonder if I'm as famous for my wheelchair and 
disabilities as I am for my discoveries.”
Stephen Hawking
Theoretical phycisist
Today, in 2024, a collective effort to promote equality and social inclusion has been normalized,
and understanding of human beings is deeply integrated with the concept of mental health,[7]
changing the perception of disability from an impairment to a facet of human diversity. This
reflects a broader societal recognition that mental well-being is as crucial as physical health. Per-
ceptions of disabilities have evolved, recognizing that mental resilience and psychological diversity
are integral to the human experience. This progressive approach has significantly influenced the
development of products designed to assist people with disabilities, ensuring that they support not
only physical needs, but also promote mental and emotional well-being. From this perspective,
is possible to state that the evolution of personal mobility devices is linked closely to the growing
recognition of people with physical disabilities and the diverse value they bring to their societies.

Shifting perceptions
A powerful symbol of transformation, not only because of its physical design and utility, but
also because of the social perceptions it represents, is the walking cane. Its origins date back
to primitive human society, made from sticks and staffs to assist with walking, hunting, and as
protective devices. Following its path through history, see Figure 2, the cane has undergone a
remarkable metamorphosis in both its form and its meaning.

From practicality to status, canes emerged, originally, as an indispensable tool for travelers, made
of easily accessible materials such as wood or bone.[8] Its main purpose was functional, as it
served as a support for those who undertook long and arduous journeys. Over time, the walking
stick became a symbol of wisdom, recognition, and status. The elite began to personalize their
canes with elaborate carvings and selected materials, transforming them into a symbol of wealth
and authority.
The cane’s journey did not end with the aristocracy. As a change in style and identity, it moved
into the fashion industry, becoming an accessory no longer serving its original purpose. Public
figures and celebrities adopted it, rather than as a support, but as a statement of style and in-
dividuality. Within the average population, canes were also used as a symbol of protest, to hide
things like weapons and even alcohol during prohibition.[8]

==PAGE:17==
Today, the cane continues to redefine itself in an industry where the distinction between func-
tionality and aesthetics is becoming more and more blurred. Products that serve similar purposes
merge, offering alternative appearances that change public perception. Certain people who need
the physical support of a cane, for example, prefer ski poles, looking for a more active and modern
image.
Within the ongoing dialogue between utility and identity, the evolution of the cane is emblematic.
By adapting to changes in both style and functionality, the cane remains a statement of the
ability to reinvent and reshape tools to fit the narrative of its time.
4.3
Addressing stigma
By definition, stigma is: "A mark of disgrace associated with a person, a personal quality, or a
personal circumstance".[9] It is an important topic that bridges many disciplines, such as sociol-
ogy, clinical psychology, and public health.
Social psychology offers insights into how individuals and society perceive stigmatized products.
The identity threat model[10] suggests that stigma can lead to identity threat, which in turn af-
fects the user’s self-esteem and psychological well-being.[11] In relation to design, when a product
is specific to an already stigmatized group, it will often carry the burden of negative associations,
which can then determine biases of both consumers and society in general. When designing aids,
it is important to empathize with the end users, and to work on understanding the individual and
social processes behind the different experiences they might have when needing such devices. In
this way, it is possible for the industry to relieve product users from the potential burden of using
these stigmatizing products.[11]
The article "Contending Stigma in Product Design” suggest three strategies to address product-
related stigma:[11]
• Understanding Context: Recognizing the social and societal context in which products
are perceived. This involves being mindful of the potential stigma and emotional impact
products can have.
• Reshaping Meaning: Efforts directed towards changing the meaning of the product. This
could involve redefining the narrative around the product to transform its perception from
one of dependency to one of empowerment.
10
==PAGE:18==
• Empowering Users: Designing products that enhance the user’s self-esteem and self-
worth, ultimately contributing to their psychological well-being and helping them contend
with stigma.
While strategies to combat product-related stigma are multifaceted, they serve as key reference
points for understanding the progressive approaches taken by the industry. Companies like by-
ACRE are at the forefront, recognizing the stigma surrounding mobility aids and actively working
to change these perceptions through a user-centered design approach.
A belief that design is a powerful agent of change is at the core of byACRE’s mission. The
company stands for innovative design that challenges conventional aesthetics and functionality,
setting a turning point from traditional mobility aids. The company’s commitment to this phi-
losophy is clear in its products, which are designed not only to fulfill their intended function, but
also to empower users. By prioritizing a positive user experience, byACRE’s designs aim to break
down stigma and foster a sense of pride and self-esteem among users.

4.4
The Scandinavian Butler
Designed in 2015 on an effort to create an indoor rollator that would hit the market while
transforming the way consumers perceived mobility aids, byACRE launched the Scandinavian
Butler, a sophisticated and functional indoor rollator designed with a focus on stability, style and
ease of use. With the main goal of integrating with home furniture, the rollator offers a set of
features with a user-centered approach.
• Blends in with your furniture 
• One-hand-only operation of handle and brake
• 360° maneuverability
• Designed to get close to your furniture, 
counter tops and appliances in your home
• Maximum responsiveness and easy turning 
with 8 independently turning wheels
• Stylish and stable
Figure 3: Scandinavian Butler main offer Source: byACRE’s webpage[12]
Under the slogan, "The world’s most reliable rollator", the Butler provides a stable structure for
people with mobility impairments within their own homes. Its one-handed operation simplifies
use for people with limited hand control, while offering 360° maneuverability through eight inde-
pendently rotating wheels, and smooth navigation around furniture and tight spaces. It places
the user in the center to provide a sense of security and makes it easy to transport items around
with an integrated tray and basket.
Through a conversation with Anders Berggreen, byACRE’s CEO, it was possible to understand
the Scandinavian Butler concept, and how the company wanted to generate a change in the way
mobility aids were perceived.
The initiative was driven by a request from a market leader at the moment, aimed to fill a gap
in a market saturated with functional aids. The Scandinavian Butler emerged from a design
philosophy that prioritizes aesthetics to destigmatize mobility aids, drawing inspiration from the
recognized tradition of Scandinavian furniture. The creation of a rollator that is not just a support
device, but an elegant accessory for the home, was guided by iconic furniture.

During the review of byACRE’s customer feedback archive 3 key points have been identified that
are fundamental in guiding the redesign of the rollator.
1. Benefits of ergonomic design: Reviews repeatedly highlight the functional advantages
of rollators with ergonomic features. Users claim to be less tense and more comfortable,
which facilitates longer periods of use and greater independence. Customers have expressed
a clear appreciation for the ergonomic design of the rollators, which has not only facilitated
their mobility, but also fostered a sense of well-being.
2. Emotional impact of thoughtful, modern design: Users express a positive emotional
response to the modern aesthetics of the rollators. The design not only serves a practical
purpose, but also inspires a sense of pride and confidence, countering the stigma often
associated with mobility aids. This emotional impact is evidence of design’s success in
improving users’ quality of life.
3. Personal connection: Users often develop a personal connection with their rollators. This
connection is not only due to the functionality of the product, but to its role in improving
their well-being and enabling a more active lifestyle. A great number of users chose to
name their walking aids.
Naming their mobility aids reflects a bond that goes beyond
functionality; the rollator has become a trusted companion in their life.
These findings present a notable contrast to the interviews, in which aesthetics and the potential
stigma associated with the use of rollator were considered irrelevant to the sector. However, for a
significant number of users, the rollator is more than just a product: it is an extension of their
identity. This difference highlights the importance of considering users’ individual experiences in
the design process. This approach will link the practical needs identified in the interviews to the
emotional connections revealed in customer feedback, creating a walker that is both a reliable
aid and a valuable part of the user’s life.

While price is an important factor in the selection process of medical equipment, based in the

following reasons:
• Comprehensive Cost-Benefit Analysis: The selection criteria go beyond purchase price
to include long-term considerations such as maintenance and cleaning. These factors rep-
resent ongoing costs that, particularly for hospitals, can significantly impact the total cost
of ownership.
• User Preferences and Priorities: Nursing Homes: When residents have the autonomy
to choose their rollators, they tend to prioritize functionality and ease of use over cost. The
decision-making process is driven by the product ability to meet their specific needs rather
than its price. Hospitals: In a hospital setting, the selection process is often influenced by
a range of benefits that extend beyond cost.
• Long-Term Benefit Justification: The prioritization of requirements is based on the
demonstrable long-term benefits to the users. When these benefits are clearly supported by
evidence, the importance of price diminishes. The focus shifts to the value provided over
time, rather than the immediate financial outlay.

10.2
Further Development
The New Scandinavian Butler remains in a conceptual stage, the final proposal has been evaluated
in terms of concept, user acceptance and eliminate a big uncertainty regarding one of the main
components, as it is the carbon frame. Thus, in order to turn this concept to reality certain
stages need to be completed before the final user can actually have a real product and enjoy its
benefits.
• Detailed Design and Engineering: The first stage involves finalizing the design details
and ensuring all components meet the required specifications. This includes refining the
design based on feedback, conducting detailed engineering analysis, and creating compre-
hensive CAD models and technical drawings. Specifically this concept has uncertainties on
the folding mechanism and attachments of the seat and tray rotation system. The outcome
from this stage is expected to be a fully detailed design ready for prototyping.
• Prototyping: Physical prototypes are created to test the design in real-world conditions.
This stage involves manufacturing prototypes using the finalized design, with the real ma-
terials, to perform functional testing. The goal is to have physical prototypes that can
be evaluated for performance and usability. This stage is also expected to require several
iterations.
• Testing and Validation: This stage ensures the product meets all functional, safety, and
regulatory requirements. Starting for the tests specified in the Danish Standard. Rigorous
testing, including load testing, durability testing, and also user testing are required to
validate the product against the overall market expectations and regulations. The outcome
is a validated design ready for production.
• Supplier and Manufacturing Setup: This stage involves selecting and negotiating with
suppliers, finalizing manufacturing processes, and setting up production lines with quality
control measures in place. As the rollator was design under the three-dimensions framework,
the benefits from the ’Heritage’ dimenesion can be seen here, over the rest of the process,
because a great percentage of the rollator consider well-known parts and process fro the
company. Components made in PU for example, will represent a bigger effort in this stage.

11
Conclusions
This project started with the belief that design can significantly impact healthcare settings, a
sector that often prioritizes functionality over the overall user experience. Recognizing the impor-
tance of emotional well-being, the project aimed to improve rollators, by integrating aesthetics
and functionality. This approach aligns with byACRE’s initiative to destigmatize rollators and
enhance the users quality of life.
The design and acceptance of mobility aids are influenced by society’s perceptions and the stigma
associated with disabilities. Historically, disabilities were often viewed as impairments, leading
to the development of functional but unattractive mobility aids. However, as perceptions have
shifted towards recognizing disabilities as aspects of diversity, there has been a growing emphasis
on integrating aesthetics into the design of these products. This shift not only enhances user
well-being but also helps reduce stigma, particularly in healthcare settings where the appearance
of mobility aids can significantly impact user confidence and acceptance.
Aesthetics play a crucial role in the design of mobility aids, transforming them from mere tools
into accessories that users can feel proud to use. By focusing on design elements such as color,
form, and material, designers can create products that are both functional and visually appeal-
ing. This approach not only improves the user experience but also helps to normalize the use of
mobility aids and in consequence reducing the stigma associated with them.
The essential features that define the functionality and success of rollators in institutions like
hospitals and nursing homes have evolved too. It is expected from users that rollators not only
served as mobility aids but that could be integrated as part of their identities.
The redesign of the Scandinavian Butler aims to meet the specific needs of the target market
while contributing to the evolution of the industry. By a user-centered approach and close col-
laboration with stakeholders to close gaps between markets, transferring the benefits provided to
the private to institution, while moving the stigma away from the product.
Marketing strategies play an important role in shaping the perception and use of mobility aids
in institutions. Effective marketing can help shift the focus from the condition to the product,
encouraging acceptance and normalizing the use of mobility aids. By highlighting the design and
functionality of products like the New Scandinavian Butler, marketing efforts can help reduce
stigma and promote a positive image of rollators.
61
==PAGE:69==
The project highlights the importance of integrating aesthetics and functionality in the design
of rollators to improve user well-being and reduce stigma. By addressing both the practical and
emotional needs of users, the redesigned Scandinavian Butler not only meets market demands
but also sets a new standard for mobility aids in healthcare settings. The insights gained from
this project can guide future developments, ensuring that mobility aids continue to evolve to meet
the diverse needs of users in their own specific time.
