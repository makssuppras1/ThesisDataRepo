Abstract 
 
The aviation industry offers the biggest challenge to reach the carbon reduction targets by 2050. 
Since airplanes cannot run on batteries or hydrogen for long hauls in the near future, sustainable 
aviation fuel plays a key role in reaching these targets. With the technological advancements in 
solid oxide electrolysis and carbon capture technologies, the cost of SAF has drastically reduced 
over the past decade. However, to make SAF cost competitive with fossil-based jet fuel, major 
improvements are needed in how we optimize the energy use from variable renewable energy 
sources like wind and solar. One key factor to reduce the intermittency and variability is by the use 
of energy storages.  
 
This study delves into the process modelling of SAF production, optimization of the production 
using CO2 and heat storages and comparison of the results based of the cost of SAF estimated using 
an economic model for different wind farm sizes and carbon capture technologies. 
 
The inclusion of energy storages has a positive impact on the cost reduction for all the cases 
discussed. The integration of CO2 storage and CO2 plus heat storage offers the lowest cost of SAF 
at 449 €/MWh using a combination of direct air capture and point source capture of carbon dioxide 
and by oversizing the wind farm by 30% than the SAF plant for this specific case. The preferred 
choice is having just a carbon dioxide storage since it is relatively simple to integrate into existing 
systems, is cost effective and also a more matured technology than heat storage. The cost of SAF 
can be further reduced if carbon tax is put into play, however, that case has not been considered in 
this project.

1.  Introduction 
 
The global aviation industry has long served as a cornerstone for economic growth and 
global connectivity, enabling the seamless movement of people and goods across the world. 
However, in a world increasingly conscious of the ecological repercussions of fossil fuel 
consumption, aviation is under mounting pressure to curtail its carbon footprint and transition 
toward more environmentally sustainable alternatives. In response to this imperative, the 
development and adoption of Sustainable Aviation Fuels (SAF) have emerged as a promising 
solution. 
 
SAFs represent a class of aviation fuels derived from renewable and sustainable resources, 
such as biomass, waste materials, and synthetic processes. They hold the potential to 
significantly diminish greenhouse gas emissions and reduce the overall environmental impact 
of aviation, as their production has a negative carbon emission compared to traditional fossil-
based jet fuels. To fully unlock the potential of SAFs and ensure their sustainable integration 
into the aviation sector, it is imperative to address several key facets of their production and 
implementation. 
 
One of the pivotal domains that requires thorough exploration and innovation is the balancing 
and optimization of production processes based on variable renewable energy (VRE). The 
production of SAF entails many energy-intensive processes, which would require a continuous 
source of energy and raw material to maximize the SAF production. In order to achieve this 
with variable energy sources like wind and solar, it is inevitable to have energy storages within 
the process chain to compensate for the variability.  
 
This thesis is a comprehensive investigation into "The Effects of Energy Storage and Process 
Integration into SAF Production." It delves into the nuances of SAF production, energy 
management and storage, and process optimization, scrutinizing on how these elements effect 
the SAF production chain. This research endeavors to furnish invaluable insights into how 
energy storage solutions and adept heat integration can substantially contribute to the cost 
reduction of SAF, consequently increasing its adoptability and fostering a greener aviation 
sector. 
 
The production of SAF involves a series of complex processes, including carbon capture, 
electrolysis for syngas production, Fischer Tropsch fuel synthesis, and refining. Each of these 
processes is examined in detail in the following sections, covering their respective limitations, 
assumptions, and reasons for choosing them over alternative methods. An integrated Aspen 
Hysys model is then presented, outlining the production chain. Additionally, the impact of 
CO2 and heat storage is evaluated, discussing how these factors affect the cost of SAF 
production and exploring strategies to minimize costs while adhering to process limitations. 
The results and discussions also include a comparison of a modelled case with similar studies 
found in the literature.

2.   Literature Review 
2.1 Conventional jet fuel production 
 
Conventional fossil-based jet fuel, also called aviation turbine fuel (ATF) is derived from crude 
oil that is extracted from the ground through drilling. Crude oil is a mixture of various hydrocarbons 
from C1 to infinitely long hydrocarbon chains along with some other compounds like sulfur, salts 
etc. ATF is a crucial component for powering aircrafts and undergoes a complex production process 
to be ready for use in an aircraft.  
1. Crude oil extraction: Crude oil is a naturally occurring fossil fuel and is the starting point of 
almost every petroleum-based transportation fuel that we see today. It is extracted from 
underground reservoirs through drilling and brings a mixture of hydrocarbons and other 
compounds to the surface. [1] 
2. Distillation: The extracted crude oil is heated to a high temperature of around 400°C and sent 
to a distillation tower, where it is separated into fractions based on boiling points of the 
hydrocarbons. The resultant products are light hydrocarbon gases (methane, ethane etc.), 
naphtha or gasoline, kerosene or jet fuel, diesel and heavy waxes. [1], [2] 
3. Hydrocracking and Oligomerization: To maximize the production of jet fuel, the heavy long 
hydrocarbons (diesel and waxes) are cracked into smaller hydrocarbons in a catalytic reaction 
in the presence of hydrogen. This process is called hydrocracking. Similarly, the light 
hydrocarbons (gases and naphtha) are transformed into long chain hydrocarbons in the presence 
of a catalyst. This process is called oligomerization. [3], [4] 
4. Testing and Quality Control: There are several standards set by the American Standard of 
Testing and Materials (ASTM) that the produced jet must pass in order to be commercially 
usable. Tests include assessments of flash point, density, freezing point and other critical 
properties. This quality control process is paramount to guarantee the safety and efficiency of 
the fuel in flight. [5] 
 
2.2 Fischer Tropsch synthesis 
 
Fischer Tropsch (FT) was developed during World War II by German chemists Franz Fischer 
and Hans Tropsch to produce liquid fuels from coal. It is a collection of reactions that convert 
syngas (H2 and CO) into crude, where the syngas is derived from the gasification of coal. Crude oil 
can be readily refined into a number of liquid transportation fuels like gasoline, jet fuel and diesel 
as mentioned in Section 2.1.  
 
The reaction to convert syngas into crude takes place in a Fischer Tropsch reactor at high pressures, 
usually in the range of 20-40 bar, in presence of a catalyst. FT synthesis using iron or cobalt-based 
catalysts can be represented by the set of chemical reactions presented in Table 1. Commercial FT 
reactors have three distinct temperature ranges, high temperature FT (HTFT) 300–350°C, medium 
temperature FT (MTFT) 250–300°C and low temperature FT (LTFT) 200–250°C. HTFT often runs 
on iron-based catalysts and favors the production of olefins and naphtha, whereas LTFT runs on 
iron- or cobalt-based catalysts and favors diesel and wax fractions.  
 
The FT process is highly exothermic, hence a rapid heat removal is a major focus during reactor 
design. Different reactor types are available for use in different applications: a) fixed-bed reactors 
for LTFT synthesis aiming at high average molecular weight product, b) fluidized-bed reactors for 
HTFT synthesis aiming at low molecular weight olefinic hydrocarbons and c) modern LTFT slurry 
phase reactors to produce hydrocarbon wax, offering improved temperature control and high per-
pass conversion. [6], [7], [8]

2.2.1 Selectivity 
The selectivity of  FT synthesis is influenced by various parameters, including reaction temperature, 
syngas composition, and the type of catalyst used. High-temperature operations around 340 °C 
typically favor the production of short-chain hydrocarbons, while lower temperatures around 230 
°C lead to the formation of longer-chain and linear hydrocarbons. The syngas composition, 
particularly the hydrogen to carbon monoxide (H2/CO) ratio, also plays a crucial role in determining 
the product selectivity. A hydrogen-rich syngas tends to increase alkane formation but shortens the 
chain length.[9] 
 
For the production of jet fuel, which consists of medium to heavy hydrocarbons, a syngas ratio 
(H2/CO) of 2 is considered ideal. This ratio helps in achieving a good balance between the formation 
of alkanes and maintaining the desired chain length for jet fuel components.[10] 
 
The complexity of the FT synthesis and the factors affecting selectivity are discussed in various 
research papers. For instance, the paper "Effect of Temperature, Syngas Space Velocity and 
Catalyst Stability of Co-Mn/CNT Bimetallic Catalyst on Fischer Tropsch Synthesis Performance" 
explores how temperature influences the selectivity for long-chained molecules [11]. Another study, 
"Jet Fuel Synthesis from Syngas Using Bifunctional Cobalt-Based Catalysts," examines the 
production of jet fuel under specific FT synthesis conditions [12]. Additionally, "Kinetics and 
Selectivity Study of Fischer–Tropsch Synthesis to C5+ Hydrocarbons: A Review" provides insights 
into how the catalyst design can enhance syngas conversion and selectivity towards desired 
hydrocarbons. [13] 
 
These studies contribute to a deeper understanding of how to control and optimize the FT synthesis 
process for specific product selectivity, such as jet fuel, by adjusting the reaction conditions and 
catalyst systems.

The Fischer Tropsch products follow the Anderson-Schulz-Flory (ASF) distribution during the 
polymerization reactions. CO undergoes dissociative or hydrogen assisted dissociative 
chemisorption on the surface of the catalyst (Co, Fe, Ru etc.), forming CHx intermediates as the 
monomers for polymerization. These CHx monomers couple and lead to a chain growth, forming 
CnHm intermediates. These intermediates then undergo hydrogenation or dehydrogenation to give 
paraffins or olefins as the final product. The C-C coupling of CHx monomers on the catalyst surface 
in undefined and random leading to statistically distributed hydrocarbon products, which is 
described by the ASF model. The model states that the molar fraction (Mn) of the hydrocarbon 
products with a carbon number n, is only dependent on the chain growth probability (α), which is 
the probability that a chain will propagate. It is given by the equation[14] : 
 
Mn = α n-1.(1- α) 
 
The parameter α plays a crucial role in influencing the dynamics of polymerization reactions. 
Specifically, it determines the balance between two key processes: termination and propagation. In 
polymerization, monomers join together to form polymer chains through repeated addition of 
monomer units. The smaller the value of α, the more dominant the termination process becomes 
relative to propagation. 
 
Termination refers to the halting of chain growth, usually occurring when two reactive sites on 
different polymer chains come into contact and combine. A smaller α implies a higher probability 
of termination events compared to propagation events. As a result, polymer chains are more likely 
to terminate before achieving significant length, leading to the formation of shorter polymer chains 
on average. [14] 
 
Conversely, a larger value of α tilts the balance in favour of continued chain growth during 
polymerization. With a higher α, there is a greater likelihood of propagation events compared to 
termination. This increased probability of continued chain growth results in longer polymer chains 
being formed on average.

reactions. A smaller α favors termination over propagation, leading to the production of shorter 
polymer chains, while a larger α promotes continued chain growth, resulting in the formation of

2.3  Power-to-X 
 
The two conventional processes mentioned earlier for producing liquid fuels involve fossil-based 
sources such as crude oil, coal, or natural gas. However, a more recent and environmentally 
sustainable approach to liquid fuel production utilizes renewable sources, a concept known as 
Power-to-X. This method involves the conversion of electrical power into liquid fuels through the 
use of electrolysers and carbon capture units. The following sections delve into the specifics of this 
process. 
 
2.3.1 
Alkaline or PEM Electrolysis 
 
One pathway for producing syngas in the Power-to-X concept involves the use of alkaline or 
PEM electrolysers. This process begins with water electrolysis, where water (H₂O) is split into 
hydrogen (H₂) and oxygen (O₂) through the application of electrical power (Equation 1). The 
produced hydrogen is then subjected to the water gas shift reaction, in which hydrogen and captured 
carbon dioxide (CO₂) react to form carbon monoxide (CO) and water (H₂O) (Equation 2). This 
approach requires two separate pieces of equipment: an electrolyser and a reverse water gas shift 
reactor (RWGS). However, this also provides with an opportunity to store the hydrogen which can 
help with dynamic operation of the system, although storing hydrogen can be expensive.

While PEM and alkaline electrolysis technologies are commercially available, they have drawbacks 
related to efficiency and material costs. PEM electrolysis, for instance, employs expensive materials 
like iridium and platinum as catalysts, leading to efficiency levels of around 60% and 50% for PEM 
and alkaline electrolysers, respectively [16]. The downstream RWGS reactor requires some extra 
energy because the RWGS reaction is endothermic, which makes this pathway less efficient. 
Moreover, the RWGS reactor must be sized according to the electrolysers' production capacities, 
limiting dynamic operation possibilities, especially when power sources are variable. This 
constraint significantly impacts capital costs as equipment must be oversized for continuous syngas 
production.

2.4       Carbon Capture 
 
2.4.1 Point Source Capture (PS) 
 
Carbon capture from a concentrated stream of flue gas usually from an industry like cement, 
fossil-based power plants or from a CO2 rich stream within the process is termed as point source 
capture. Amine carbon capture has been the preferred choice for PS capture in our case since it can 
operate on waste heat as a major source of energy. Amine carbon capture technology is based on 
cyclic absorption and desorption processes. The CO2 (which is an acidic gas) is absorbed from a 
stream containing CO2 by a circulating aqueous amine solution (alkaline solution) and released as 
a concentrated CO2 stream through thermal regeneration of the amine solution, that is applying heat 
to the solution, in a desorber. The CO2 capture process is thus driven by thermal energy. Carbon 
capture from point source is relatively less energy demanding than Direct Air Capture (DAC) since 
the concentration of CO2 in a flue gas stream is higher [19].

2.4.2 Direct Air Capture (DAC) 
 
Direct air capture units (DAC) capture CO2 directly from air and are a key technology in the 
carbon removal portfolio. DAC holds the potential to decarbonize the atmosphere, and for providing 
a CO2 feedstock for functional industrial applications, that is, e-fuels production. There are two 
types of commercial direct air capture units [19]:

a. Solid absorption and low temperature regeneration: The solid adsorption process (S-DAC) 
operates by sucking air from the atmosphere and directing the flow through a solid activated 
filter material that chemically binds with the CO2. The solid CO2 rich absorber is then heated

b. Liquid absoption and high temperature regeneration: The technology involves capturing 
CO2 from ambient air by chemical interaction with a capture solution, potassium hydroxide

precipitated as limestone (CaCO3), which is then regenerated in a calcinatory at high 
temperatures 800-900 °C.

2.5 Solid Oxide Co-Electrolysis 
 
Solid oxide electrolysers are the heart of a power-to-X operation and provide the syngas which 
is the raw material for e-fuel synthesis. The advantage of using solid oxide electrolysis over PEM 
or alkaline is the possibility of co-electrolysis of H2O and CO2 together, as discussed in Section 
2.3.2. The most common cell configuration is Ni-YSZ for the fuel electrode, a YSZ electrolyte and 
a LSM oxygen electrode. It is also common to have a CGO barrier layer between the electrolyte 
and the oxygen electrode to reduce the risk of short circuit due to ions passing over to the other side. 
The reduction reactions proceed on the surface of the fuel electrode where Ni, YSZ and the fuel 
meet. Such a junction is termed as a triple phase boundary (TPB). Nickel acts as an electronic 
conductor and a catalyst on the TPB, suppling electrons from an external power, YSZ electrolyte 
permits only the transport of oxygen ions and restricts the permeability of the reduced species in 
the fuel. The overall reaction on the fuel side can be summarized by:

SOECs are high temperature electrolysers typically operating between 600-900 °C. There are two 
major reasons for operating at high temperature; first, YSZ is an inexpensive ceramic material that 
is used as an electrolyte for such cells and is only conductive at such high temperature. The higher 
the temperature, the lower the resistance to the transport of oxygen ions from the fuel side to the 
oxygen side which leads to better efficiencies. Secondly, a part of the energy needed to split water 
and carbon dioxide for syngas production in a SOEC can be supplied by heat. The total energy for 
the electrochemical reaction is ΔH, which is composed of two contributions, the Gibbs free energy, 
ΔG, and the reaction entropy, Q (Figure 5). ΔG is the electrical demand while Q is supplied in the 
form of heat. As can be seen from Figure 5, the total energy demand remains almost the same for 
the given temperature range. However, as we go higher in temperature, ΔG decreases and Q 
increases due to the positive entropy change, ΔS. This effect can be beneficial if some of the heat 
is provided by waste heat sources or by heat recovery from the hot outlet gases from the SOEC 
itself.

While the mechanism for the reduction of H2O is well known, the reduction of CO2 had been a grey 
area. It was believed that CO2 was also reduced electrochemically. However, after further research 
it was found that CO2 is reduced through the RWGS reaction (2). The participation of reverse water 
gas shift (RWGS) has been identified by comparing the area specific resistance (ASR) of the cell. 
From experiments conducted in literature [22], [23], the ASR for sole H2O reduction was reported 
to be 1.36 Ωcm2 and 1.38 Ωcm2 for H2O/CO2, which are very close. But for sole CO2 reduction it 
was reported to be around 3.84 Ωcm2 [23]. This suggests that nearly no CO is formed due to 
electrochemical reduction of CO2 but only because of RWGS reaction. This information is 
important to model the co-electrolysis process on Aspen Hysys.

3. Methodology  
 
The main objective of this project was to optimize the SAF production process by maximizing 
the running time of all the processes involved when the power source is from a variable renewable 
energy source, which, in this case is power from a wind farm. It also includes the possibility of 
energy storages like CO2 and heat storage to maximize the productivity. The complexity arises when 
we further want to size the equipments for off-design conditions (partial loads), keeping in mind 
the operational limits of each technology and the cost incurred when using those technologies. The 
processes, modelling and optimization has been described in detail in the following sections.  
 
Solid Oxide Electrolysis and Fischer Tropsch, both being complex processes needed to be modelled 
in detail on Aspen Hysys to get an idea about the energy consumptions and the possibility of heat 
recovery within the process. Hourly wind data from the year 2021 is considered as the power source 
for the model and the processes are optimized with respect to this data.  
 
3.1 Power Source: Wind and Grid 
 
For any power-to-X process, the starting point is the source of power. For the final products to 
qualify as green and sustainable, the power should come from renewable energy sources like wind, 
solar or grid (depending on the renewability). Power is needed for the SOEC, carbon capture units, 
compressors, heaters and pumps.

3.2 Solid Oxide Electrolysis Model 
 
The modelling of SOEC co-electrolysis on Aspen Hysys is a relative complicated process, 
considering the thermodynamics and kinetics of the reaction. To model the SOEC in detail and as 
close to actual as possible, it was divided in two reactors. First, a conversion reactor to simulate the 
H2O reduction reaction and then a Gibbs reactor to simulate the RWGS reaction for CO2 reduction 
(Figure 7). An operating temperature of 800 °C and an arbitrary size of 90 MW was chosen for the 
SOEC, which is expected to be a reasonable size in the near future. The operating current density 
cannot be varied directly in the Aspen model, however, the effect of current density can be simulated 
varying the percentage conversion in the conversion reactor named “Electrolysis”.  
 
The input for the SOEC is only H2O, CO2 and electrical power. The feed gases pass through a 
network of heat exchangers to absorb the heat from the hot outlet gases (at 800 °C) of the SOEC 
itself. This makes the temperature of the feed gases reach around 370 °C and the remaining heating 
to reach 800 °C is provided by an electrical heater, “SOEC Pre-heater”. Cooled outlet gases from 
the SOEC fuel side then go through the point source carbon capture unit, “PS”, to capture the 
unreacted CO2 and recycle it back to the CO2 storage. The CO2 storage stores CO2 from both DAC 
and PS and acts as a buffer for times when there is not enough electrical power to run the DAC.  
 
The sweep gas on the oxygen side is just ambient air and is also heated using a heat exchanger and 
an electrical heater (E-100) to reach 800 °C before entering the SOEC. The flow on the air side and 
fuel side is the same to reduce any pressure differences across the cells, which may otherwise lead 
to cracks.  
 
The syngas H2/CO ratio for the FT reactor has to be around 2, which is a requirement to maximize 
the production of middle hydrocarbons, that is from C8-C20. However, after the PS capture, we 
obtain clean syngas with a H2/CO ratio of 1.52. This is done because there is recycling of unreacted 
syngas in the downstream FT reactor, which has a high H2 content. When these two syngas streams 
from the SOEC and recycling are mixed, it balances out the low H2 syngas from SOEC to make the 
syngas ratio 2.04 before entering the FT. Around 10% of the syngas produced is also recycled back 
to the SOEC to have some reducing gases in the feed. This is important to not oxidize the nickel in 
the fuel electrode and maintain a slight reducing condition. The inlet and outlet gas compositions 
are summarized in Table 4. Rest of the syngas (SYN GAS COLD) proceeds to the compressor and

3.3 Fischer Tropsch Model 
 
The Fischer Tropsch model consists of multiple chemical reactions (as described in Table 1) for 
the formation of hydrocarbons. Parameters like temperature, syngas ratio, pressure and type of 
catalyst affect the chain growth probability (α), which in turn affects the selectivity of hydrocarbons 
products. However, to simplify the chemistry, it is generally a good assumption to only include the 
formation of paraffin (CnH2n+2) compounds since the proportion of olefins and alcohols are really 
small. The model here is simulated to be a low temperature (220 °C) Fe-catalyst based slurry bed 
reactor operating at 25 bar.  
 
When using a single chain growth probability factor (α), the mass fraction of methane is usually 
lower than the actual value. Thus, a stoichiometric model for the FT reactions was developed based 
on the triple α Anderson-Schulz-Flory (ASF) theory. ASF theory is often used to estimate the FT 
product distribution according to the chain growth probabilities factor (α) [37], and the triple α 
corrected ASF theory can provide even more accurate estimation. Triple values of α were used to 
explain the high methane yield and change in the chain growth probability at certain break point 
due to the vapor-liquid equilibrium in the reactor, which could not be accounted for by the 
conversional single α value method. Detailed explanations of the triple α ASF theory can be found 
in [38], [39]

& (14). For the scope of this project hydrocarbons till C30 are considered and modelled.

recycling loop.

3.4 Refining Model 
Refining model consists of a pre-heater and a distillation column. The pre-heater heats the crude 
oil to 360 °C, which then enters the distillation column from the bottom. As the hot vapors rise to 
the top, they start to cool and condense according to their boiling points. Heavy hydrocarbons 
condense close to the bottom while lighter hydrocarbons stay in the vapor phase until they reach 
the top. This segregates the hydrocarbons at different levels of the distillation column, which can 
then be extracted.

it is acceptable for use. The selectivity of jet fuel can be increased further by processing of LHC, 
naphtha and waxes by oligomerization and hydrocracking. However, it has not been included in 
this project.

Firstly, the effect of just heat storage is analyzed without CO2 storage in Figure 18. Unlike CO2 
storage, the effect of heat storage does not compensate for the lower wind farm capacity. When the 
wind farm is sized equal to plant size of 167 MW, the costs remain high, even for larger storage

even with a small storage capacity but after a storage capacity of 100 MWh, it starts to plateau and 
begins to rise gradually after that for all wind farm sizes.  
 
The impact of a storage also depends on where the output of the storage is used. The effect of heat 
storage is not as impactful as CO2 storage because the heat is used in an intermediate process, that 
is DAC, to produce CO2 which does not contribute directly to increase the production of SAF. 
Without a CO2 storage, the DAC can only run when there is sufficient power to run the whole 
system, no matter how much heat is stored for utilization. Whereas, the output of CO2 storage is 
CO2, which directly adds more operational hours to the plant. Even when there is not enough power 
to run the DAC, the downstream system can run since the output of DAC is already stored. To put 
it simply, heat storage acts as a battery for the DAC, but CO2 storage acts as a battery for SAF 
production plant. This is the reason the costs for different wind farm sizes in Figure 21 do not 
converge like in Figure 17. A heat storage lowers the cost for their respective wind farm sizes but 
does not add much to the production of SAF. Another proof of this theory lies in comparing the 
maximum possible operational hours for a 220 MW wind farm with CO2 storage vs heat storage. 
For a CO2 storage, the maximum operational hours are 4999 whereas with heat storage its 4606 
hours. So, although both the storages serve the same purpose of storing energy, the impact of these 
storages depends upon the end use of these energies.

5. Conclusion 
 
Aviation industry is one of the biggest emitters of greenhouse gases and reducing emissions for 
this sector is difficult since airplanes would not be able to run on batteries or hydrogen for long 
distance hauls in the near future. Sustainable aviation fuels present an opportunity to make air travel 
carbon neutral, but the cost of SAF is a barrier in its widespread adoption. Technological 
advancements and government policies can provide some support to make SAF cost competitive 
with fossil-based aviation fuel, however the bigger challenge is to optimize the process to be able 
to run on variable renewable energy sources like wind and solar.  
 
After a detailed modelling and analysis for the SAF production chain and the intermediate processes 
involved, some results are drawn for the maximization of SAF production while also reducing the 
production cost when wind is the primary source of power. The type of storage and the source of 
CO2 have a big impact on the production cost of SAF. The economic evaluation gives an insight on 
what type of storage and at what conditions would lead to the lowest possible cost.   
 
It is safe to say that out of the four cases discussed in this report, Case 1 with grid connectivity 
would lead to the lowest cost of SAF at 319 €/MWh. However, it is not the most sustainable case 
since the source of power might not be completely renewable. Having any kind of storage has a 
positive impact on the total production in all cases (except case 1). The cost of SAF follow a “U” 
shaped curve, that is there is an exponential drop when going from zero to a small storage size, 
either heat or CO2, and keeps dropping up to a certain point and then starts to increase again when 
the cost of storage begins to outweigh the gain in production from it.  
 
For Cases 2,3 and 4, the CO2 is sourced 50% from PS and 50% from DAC. The impact of CO2 
storage can be seen when comparing Case 2 with Case 3, where the power profile remains the same, 
however, Case 3 with a CO2 storage leads to a cost of 449 €/MWh with a wind farm size of 250 
MW and in Case 2 the lowest cost is 533 €/MWh which is seen at a wind farm size of 310 MW. A 
larger wind farm would also require more land and higher lead times for construction and thus is 
not the preferred choice. 
 
While comparing Case 3 and Case 4, the lowest price achieved were for a wind farm size of 250 
MW in both the cases. For Case 3, this point was at a CO2 storage of 300 tons resulting in a price 
of 449 €/MWh, whereas for Case 4 the point was at a heat storage capacity of 50 MWh with a 
CO2 storage of 50 tons with a similar price of 449 €/MWh.

consideration. The storage can be either a large CO2 storage or a combination of small heat and CO2

Section 4.5) and is relatively simple to store in pressurized tanks, CO2 storage would be the 
optimal storage option. CO2 storage technology is also a more matured technology than heat

Heat integration provides a solution to reduce the energy consumption for DAC units, however, 
sufficient heat is not generated in Fischer Tropsch based fuel synthesis pathway to cover the DAC’s 
heat demand completely. The availability of waste heat from other processes like thermal power 
plants would not only reduce the energy consumption, but also act as an excellent source of point 
source carbon capture.  
 
Further cost reduction is possible if all the CO2 is captured using PS carbon capture. From the model 
it was calculated that the price of SAF in such a case would be 375 €/MWh. For comparison, the 
current cost of fossil-based jet fuel is around 100 €/MWh. For SAF to be competitive with this price, 
further advancements are required in solid oxide electrolysis technology and DAC to reduce the 
replacement cost and over CAPEX. Carbon tax could potentially have a big impact in making SAF 
cost competitive with fossil-based fuel.  
 
In conclusion, this study gives a deep insight on how a storage unit can enhance the production of 
a fixed plant under certain constraints. There also lies great potential in integrating such facilities 
with solar power plants and possibly other storage options like syngas or crude oil storage. 
Moreover, a more detailed optimization model could improve the results by include the ramping 
rates for technologies, idling operation and the losses from storages.
