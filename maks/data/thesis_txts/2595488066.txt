Abstract
Structural robustness is the ability of a structure to withstand abnormal events without experi-
encing disproportionate damage or collapse. Structural robustness can be achieved by several
means of design methods, many of which are prescribed by national or international standards
and regulations. One of these methods to ensure robustness in a structure is by a notional
removal of elements, such as columns, and show the structure can develop alternative load
paths and redistribute gravity loads.
This thesis aims to study the possibilities of using numerical analysis with the finite element
method to model precast concrete structures in a column removal scenario.
For this purpose, a thorough review of the current Eurocode regulations and design guide-
lines on robustness is made, with a detailed examination of available alternative load paths in
framed precast concrete structures.
Techniques for modelling precast structures in a finite element environment are proposed
along with the software specific modelling approach used in this thesis. Smaller-scale finite
element models of sub-structures are examined to study the behaviour of precast beams and
slabs, and to attempt to validate the model from the generated results. A larger-scale structure
is modelled with the notional removal of columns to examine the alternative load paths devel-
oping in a precast concrete structure.
Catenary action of beams and slabs is able to be depicted through decreased deflections
and introduction of axial forces. The redistribution of loads was generally found to be through
catenary action and torsion of the slabs. Resulting twisting moment and tensile forces should
be considered in a design situation of the precast elements. Findings showed the Eurocode
provisions for structural ties are sufficient for the obtained tensile forces under certain circum-
stances and that loads on remaining columns can increase by up to a doubling of the original
load. Shear walls in wall panel buildings can effectively reduce total deformations at the cost of
introducing large horizontal forces in the slabs.
Precast concrete structures are generally able to be satisfactorily depicted in normal de-
sign scenarios using finite element software. Employing the notional member removal method
in such models is achievable, but should always be done with careful assessment of obtained
results and their applicability in a design situation.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Building structures are designed to withstand the load actions occurring on the structure during
its lifetime, whether it be permanent actions from the self-weight of the structure, variable ac-
tions, such as imposed load from expected usage or accidental actions from unexpected events,
such as explosions. Accidental actions are unlikely to occur but can have severe impacts on
the structure, unless they are either designed against or measures have been taken to limit the
consequences of such actions.
Designing all structural members of a building to withstand all possible abnormal events is not
possible nor feasible, so the consequences of an accidental action will likely be the failure of a
single structural member of the building. This is not disastrous if the damages are limited to the
directly affected member. If, however, the failure of one member leads to the failure of adjoining
structural elements of the building, which leads to further failures, a total collapse of the building
is ensuing. This is also known as a progressive collapse, where a local failure leads to a total
collapse.
There are numerous examples of such events, such as the well-known progressive collapse of
the 22-storey apartment building in London, Ronan Point, where the failure of a load-bearing
wall on the 18th floor, due to a gas explosion, lead to the collapse of one entire corner of the
building as seen in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Progressive collapse of Ronan point, (The Daily Telegraph, 1968)
Designing buildings to withstand such progressive collapses is key to reduce the consequences
of accidental actions and local failures. The robustness of a building is central in ensuring this
and has become a part of most modern standards and building codes.

A buildingŌĆÖs robustness and resistance towards progressive and disproportionate collapse are
highly dependent on the structureŌĆÖs load carrying system. A building structure consisting purely
of cast-in-place concrete elements are considered to have higher robustness than prefabricated
concrete structures, which are assembled on-site, particularly due to the continuity and redun-
dancy of the cast-in-place joints.
There are multiple ways and strategies for providing acceptable levels of robustness and one
of the design strategies in the Eurocode is the notional column removal, where the extent of
damage from the removal of a column should not exceed a certain limit.
In a design process, the structural engineer then has to analyse the present structure in each of
the notional column removal scenarios and show alternative load paths can be developed. This
analysis can be cumbersome and time-consuming to make using traditional hand calculations.
With finite element analysis software becoming more accessible and computer power is improv-
ing, these software is a valuable tool which can be employed, especially in such analyses.
1.2
Scope and limitations
The scope of this thesis is to investigate the possibility of using finite element software in
analysing precast concrete structures in a column removal scenario. The aim is to determine
the behaviour of a precast concrete structure and the methods for developing alternative load
paths, including an evaluation of the current Eurocode provisions with a comparison to numer-
ical results.
The thesis will be limited to studying smaller-scale FE sub-structures and a single larger-scale
case study model.
1.3
Outline of the thesis
The work is divided into background information on robustness and finite element modelling in
the first part and analysis of sub-structure models and case study model in the second part.
Chapter 2 provides a thorough review of regulations on structural robustness in the Eurocodes
and the design methods available to achieve this. Evaluation of different alternative load paths
is made with a focus on framed structures along with an analysis of the typical failure modes
experienced in framed precast structures.
Chapter 3 provides an introduction to the analysis methods used with the finite element method
along with how the finite element modelling is done in this thesis.
Chapter 4 provides finite element models of several progressing precast concrete sub-structures
including analysis of these in support removal scenarios.
Chapter 5 investigates the finite element model of the case study in column removal scenarios
along with proposed measures to enhance the robustness and decrease deformations.
Chapter 6 provides an overview of the major work in this thesis and conclusions obtained from
the finite element modelling.

2
Robustness in structures
2.1
Introduction
This chapter serves as an introduction to the work done in this thesis by providing theory and
background on structural robustness in buildings. A small introduction to robustness with the
historical development of the building regulations is given along with the current regulations on
robustness in the Eurocodes and the recommended design methods for achieving structural
robustness. Then a more in-depth specification on the alternative load path mechanisms avail-
able in the notional removal of a load-bearing element is laid out. Finally, a specific focus on
the failure modes in precast framed structures in case of a column loss is presented.
2.2
Conceptual de’¼ünitions
2.2.1
Structural robustness
The term robustness has no standard accepted definition regarding its use in connection with
building structures.
Most modern building codes have adopted some kind of robustness definition in their guidelines.
In the Eurocode, robustness is defined as: ŌĆØthe ability of a structure to withstand events like fire,
explosions, impact or the consequences of human error, without being damaged to an extent
disproportionate to the original causeŌĆØ [1].
Structural robustness is, in general, a structures ability to withstand local failure without suffering
disproportionate damage to its system. It is a function of the properties of the structure, such
as redundancy which is the structures ability to redistribute loads after a local member failure.
As such, a robust structure must be able to develop alternative load paths by use of structural
ties, general ductility and strength.
2.2.2
Progressive collapse
The concept of progressive collapse is often used to describe the type of collapse sequence
typically happening when a chain reaction of local failures leads to the total collapse of a struc-
ture.
The concept is comparable to the term of disproportionate collapse which is where the extent
of a collapse is disproportionate to the damaging event or initial local damage.
So while their definitions are not relating to the same measure (mechanism of collapse and
extent of damage), they can often be used to describe the same event.
Multiple types of progressive collapse have been defined. Starossek [2] has identified six types
of progressive collapse, specifically the pancake-, zipper-, domino-, section-, instability-, and
mixed-type collapse. The pancake- and domino-type collapse is characterized by continuation
of the failure chain reaction by impact forces of the failed elements. Zipper- and section-type
collapse is caused by redistribution forces from failed elements to intact elements. Instability-
type collapse is when a stabilizing element fails and causes destabilization of the affected part
of the structure, and mixed-type is a combination of two or more types of collapse.
2.3
Historical background
The focus on structural robustness and prevention of progressive and disproportionate col-
lapses has increased after numerous catastrophic building collapses throughout the last 50
years.

2.3.1
Ronan Point
The event that first brought the attention to the need for structural robustness was the collapse
of one corner of the 22-storey Ronan Point apartment building in 1968. A gas explosion blew
away a load-bearing wall on the 18th floor, causing the above floors to collapse due to support
loss. The impact of the debris from the 18-22nd floor caused all the subsequent floors below
to collapse. Depiction of the pancake-type progressive collapse of Ronan Point is shown in
Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Progressive collapse mechanism of Ronan point [3]
The building was composed of precast concrete elements, lacked structural redundancy and
provided no alternative load paths in the design for the upper floors, in case of failure in a
supporting lower level. Investigations into the cause of the collapse revealed bad workmanship
in the construction with poorly executed joints between the precast elements, which might have
been a contributing factor to the scale of the collapse [4].
The incident and subsequent investigations brought focus on the need for research into the
susceptibility of progressive collapse of precast buildings. This lead to changes in the British
building codes and the first guidelines for resisting progressive collapse by providing alternative
load paths by the use of structural ties, provided by suitable reinforcement [5].
2.3.2
Alfred P. Murrah Federal building
The next large scale building collapse that caught the attention of the world, was the bombing
of the AP Murrah federal building in the USA in 1995. The AP Murrah building was a 9-storey
reinforced concrete building. A planned terrorist attack including a large truck bomb caused half
of the building to collapse and the death of 168 people. While the explosion had a large impact
on the closest columns and floors, investigations on the collapse of the building report that the
total collapse was caused by the failure of only a few columns [6]. The progressive collapse
spread both upwards and horizontally causing a large disproportionate collapse. The building
after the collapse is shown in Figure 2.2.
2.3.3
World Trade Center
Another recent event which can be categorised as a progressive collapse, is the total collapse
of the World Trade Center in 2001, where a terrorist attack involving two aeroplanes initiated
the collapse of the building. The initial impact from the aeroplanes did not cause the building to
collapse, despite the large impact forces, but caused local collapse of columns on the affected
floors. The subsequent fire then caused the failure of the intact columns around the zone of

This event, along with the bombing of the AP Murrah building, led to a significant increase in

2.3.4
Modern day
In the following years, the design provisions on robustness were incorporated into most coun-
tries building codes in various degrees, ranging from the minimum required tying requirements
to more systematic approaches for designing against progressive collapse [8].
With the implementation of the Eurocodes, a reorganisation of current design requirements were
made although the design methods remained largely unchanged [5].
2.4
European standards and regulations
This section will mostly centre on the Eurocodes and the Danish national annexes, but will also
shortly touch on other relevant publications.
2.4.1
General
The Eurocodes are the European Standards (EN) for the design of buildings within the European
Union developed by the European Committee for Standardization (CEN).
The current standards on robustness regulations include EN 1990 - Basis of structural design
[9] and EN 1991-1-7 - Actions on structures - Accidental actions [1]. EN 1991-1-7 contains spe-
cific guidelines on accidental design situations and sustaining localised failure from unspecified
causes. Annex A to EN 1991-1-7 provides design strategies for consequences of localised fail-
ure in buildings from an unspecified cause. It should be noted, this annex is informative and
not normative and national choices can complement or provide replacing guidelines to the infor-
mation in annex A. The design standard for concrete structures, EN 1992 - Design of concrete
structures [10], include further guidelines for designing tying systems in concrete structures.
Verification and designing for robustness is done in the accidental limit state with the combina-
tion of actions for accidental design situations as given in EN 1990 - Equation 6.11b, with the
design value:

2.4.2
Design strategies
EN 1991-1-7 presents two main strategies for accidental design situations, illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.4.

The direct design methods involve a specific analysis of the structure to ensure it is capable of
resisting local damage and disproportionate failure. This done by two methods:
1) Is the alternative load path method, which simulates the notional removal of a structural
member. The structure should then be able to develop alternative ways of carrying the
loads of the now removed column.
2) Is the specific load resistance or key element method, which is recommended when the
loss of a structural member cannot be tolerated due to extent of damage or it is not possible
to develop alternative load paths.
The indirect design methods aim at increasing the resistance of a structure towards collapse by
providing minimum levels of robustness through prescriptive measures of tie forces, continuity
and ductility without consideration of specific hazard scenarios.
In the following, these design methods will be described in detail.
2.5.1
Prescriptive tying measures
Prescriptive tying systems are recommended by the Eurocode to ensure minimum levels of
robustness in the structure by enabling alternative load paths to develop in case of local failure.
This is particularly important in precast concrete structures where continuity is lacking.
The tying systems consist commonly of reinforcement bars and continuous elements and are
placed in both the horizontal and vertical direction, covering the whole structure. The horizontal
ties are placed in both the longitudinal and transverse direction, internally and peripherally of
the structure, typically in the floor construction. Vertical ties are typically placed within vertically
load-carrying structural elements such as columns and walls and are continuous throughout the
structure. Connecting every element is important to increase the structures ability to redistribute
the loads. An illustration of these tying systems is shown in Figure 2.6.
The core function of tying systems is to enable redistribution of loads. The principle of this is
mostly by catenary action or vierendeel action in internal frames, which can be seen in Fig-
ure 2.7, or cantilever beam action in peripheral corner frames. These mechanisms are clarified
in Section 2.6.
Horizontal ties
Horizontal ties at the levels of floor slabs, consisting of internal and peripheral ties, is commonly
achieved by putting in reinforcement bars in the joints of precast floor slabs. Other acceptable
ways may consist of rolled steel sections, steel mesh reinforcement or profiled steel sheeting
in composite floors [1]. Horizontal ties should be provided in buildings in consequence class
CC2a and higher as described in Section 2.4.2.

2.5.2
Alternative load paths
The alternative load path method includes the notional removal of a load-bearing element such
as a column or a wall section. The nominal length of the wall section is given in Annex A.7 to EN
1991-1-7 and is for reinforced concrete walls a length not exceeding 2.25H, where H is the story
height in metres. This method is prescribed for buildings in consequence class CC2b and higher
as an alternative to the tying method. When using the alternative load path method, the building
should remain stable and the local damage not exceed the limits described in Section 2.4.2. If
this cannot be obtained, the element should be designed as a key element, which is elaborated
on in Section 2.5.3.
Although there is no detailed specification on how to obtain these alternative load paths in the
Eurocode, there are multiple ways of achieving them. In Section 2.6 the most common and
recognized methods used in the analysis of framed structures will be described.
2.5.3
Key element design
When a structure is unable to develop alternative load paths in the notional element removal
scenario, leading to damage exceeding the agreed limit, such elements should be designed
as key elements. According to Annex A.8 to EN 1991-1-7, key elements should be capable of
sustaining an accidental action Ad applied in the horizontal and vertical direction (one at a time)
to the element and any attached components having an influence on the strength of the element
and its connections [1]. The Eurocode recommends the value of Ad for building structures to be
34 kN/m2. In the scenario with identified accidental actions, key elements should be designed
to withstand these specific actions. EN 1991-1-7 presents accidental design loads for design
situations involving impacts and internal explosion.
The Danish national annex, DS/EN 1990 DK NA, proposes robustness through the key element
method to be achieved by applying a material partial factor ╬│M, which has been increased by a
factor 1.2, in the design of the selected key elements.
2.5.4
Segmentation
A less common method of ensuring resistance to progressive collapse is the method of segmen-
tation, or compartmentalisation, which aims to reduce the consequence of failure or collapse
of a structure. It consists of limiting the damage spread throughout the building by designing

collapse in a part of a compartmentalised structure, the collapse will be confined to that part of

where the ongoing collapse came to a halt at joints at both sides of the collapsing section. The

2.6
Mechanisms for alternative load paths
This section focuses on the different mechanisms to achieve alternative load paths in case
of the loss of a column in framed structures. In wall-panel structures, additional load transfer
mechanisms can be utilised such as the arch effect, strut and tie method or deep beam model.
2.6.1
Beam and cantilever action
The concept of bridging over a failed column is essential in developing new load paths. One
way to achieve this, is by having the affected beams double their span length and bridging over
the failed column with normal bending action. However, it is unlikely the beam will be able to
transfer the loads in this manner unless the beams are over-designed for their normal span.
In case of a corner column removal, the affected beams will no longer be simply supported,
having lost one of their two supports. Instead, they will act as cantilever beams provided they
have a degree of continuity in their joints, making the support condition fixed. This is obtainable
either through the use of continuous beams or ties, which is critical in precast concrete struc-
tures. An example of using ties to develop a cantilever beam in a precast structure is shown in
Figure 2.11. This assumes horizontal compression forces can be supported in the lower flange
of the beam, creating a moment resistant connection from the force couple.

Suspension
Vertical load-carrying members, such as columns, losing their lower support will no longer be

joining horizontal structural members through suspension action, leaving them suspended from
their top support. The horizontal structural members are then able to transfer the loads through
the various mechanisms described earlier in this section. This requires the columns to be tied
to the horizontal members.

Finite element modelling
3.1
Introduction
This chapter gives a summary of the analysis methods available when performing a finite ele-
ment analysis. It is assumed the reader has a basic knowledge of the finite element method,
while the characteristics of the different analyses using the finite element method are described
in short, for reference to further work carried out in this report. The basis for how the modelling
is done during the work for this thesis is then described in detail. All finite element modelling is
done using the software Robot Structural Analysis Professional by Autodesk.
3.2
Analysis methods
3.2.1
Linear static analysis
Linear static analysis is the simplest form of analysis to perform using the finite element method.
In the linear analysis, a linear relationship between applied forces and displacements is imple-
mented and is based on the assumption of small deformations. Furthermore, it is assumed
these deformations do not impact the behaviour and stability of the structure. In finite element
modelling of linear static problems, the global stiffness matrix of the model, which describes the
stiffness of the model and how it deforms under external forces, is constant and thus the solving
process is relatively short when compared to a non-linear problem of the same model.
This method can be applied to structures where stresses are assumed to remain in the linear
elastic range of the material. It is therefore used in problems where the material is not entering
a plastic stage and deformations remain small. However, in problems with large deformations,
such as the loss of an intermediate support of a beam, the outcome of a linear static analysis
will be unrealistically high stresses and wonŌĆÖt account for non-linear behaviour such as buckling
and membrane forces, which might be a critical failure mode.
For such problems, a non-linear static analysis takes into account these shortcomings of the
linear approach.
3.2.2
Non-linear static analysis
A non-linear static analysis refines the linear static analysis by including material and geometric
nonlinearity in the analysis. This allows for a more accurate representation of the elasto-plastic
stress-strain relationship in the material and membrane action in horizontal structural members
from large deformations. These effects cause a stiffness matrix that is not constant anymore as
load is applied. Thus an iterative analysis is required, where the load is applied in incremental
steps and the resulting displacements, stresses and stiffness matrix of a load step is adopted for
the next step in the analysis. This is can be achieved through the Newton-Raphson algorithm by
equilibrium iterations, where displacements are adjusted until the internal and external forces
are within a relative pre-set tolerance. This principle is illustrated in Figure 3.1.
Material non-linearity
Linear-elastic material models provide a linear relationship between forces applied and defor-
mations of the material, but this is not an accurate depiction for larger deformations when the
material exhibits non-linear, inelastic behaviour. Therefore, non-linear material models, such as
elastic-perfectly plastic or multi-linear elastic-plastic models with changes in the stiffness of the
material at different plastic regions, are often used. The stress-strain curves of such material
models is illustrated in Figure 3.2.

To implement material non-linearity in a finite element analysis, an appropriate material model
has to be made and incorporated in the stiffness matrix of the element. The non-linear behaviour
of the material causes the stiffness matrix to change as the material deforms.
Geometric non-linearity
Non-linearity problems in static analysis can also occur from a change in geometry in the struc-
ture. This is assumed not to be the case in a linear static analysis where deformations are as-
sumed to be small enough to be neglected. If, however, a structure undergo large deformations,
as when a beam suddenly experience twice its original span length, the stiffness of the structure
will change and membrane forces will occur in the elements. The concept of membrane actions
is discussed in Section 2.5.2 and will reduce the deflection of horizontal load-carrying members
by allowing axial forces to develop, increasing rigidity of the element.
3.2.3
Dynamic effects
In a static analysis problem, the structure is considered in a static state with no variation in time.
Dynamic effects, such as impacts or sudden acceleration of loads, are hard to accurately depict
using conventional static analysis methods. For such scenarios, non-linear time history analysis
is the most accurate, but are however difficult to model and calculate, is very time consuming
and require a certain expertise to use and interpret correctly.
Dynamic ampli’¼ücation factor
A way to account for dynamic effects without resorting to time history analyses, is through the
application of a dynamic amplification factor of the loads. This is done in either the linear or non-
linear static analysis where the applied loads are simply increased by a specified factor. Using

an appropriate factor is important since a larger factor will overestimate the dynamic effects and
lead to over-sizing of the structural elements, while a smaller factor can lead to under-sized
elements.
A load increase factor is not specified in the Eurocodes for the notional column removal scenario,
but is recommended by the Department of Defense, U.S.A. [24] for a linear static analysis as
╬®LF = 2. Since ductile structures experience reduced dynamic effects, the value of ╬®LF = 2

3.3.6
Non-linear analysis
When doing a non-linear static analysis in Robot, two methods of solving the equation system
exists; The arch-length method and the incremental method. The arc-length method is a dis-
placement steering method and is good for highly non-linear behaviour such as buckling but is
not used in this project, as it is meant for analysing collapsing structures and will run indefinitely
if the analysis is convergent, meaning no collapse of the structure. The incremental method is
discussed in Section 3.2.2 as the Newton-Raphson method and is the one used in this project.
As discussed earlier in Section 3.3.1, Robot canŌĆÖt model material non-linearity for concrete and
thus only geometric non-linearity is used in the non-linear analyses. Two options to take into
account higher-order effects is present; P-delta analysis and large displacements analysis.
P-delta analysis considers second-order effects such as the influence of the structure deforma-
tion on the equilibrium state and the change in bending rigidity depending on the axial forces
[26].
Large displacement analysis considers second- and third-order effects such as the influence
of bending on the element elongation and the additional lateral rigidity and stresses resulting
from deformation and rotation [26]. This is the resulting axial forces and decreased deflection
in structures, such as beams with fixed supports, loaded by a vertical load.
The parameters used for non-linear analysis in this project is shown in Figure 3.6 along with
the stop criteria. When solving non-linear problems, three algorithms are available in Robot de-

Structural idealisation
When having to analyse a structure in a scenario, such as a column removal scenario, modelling
the entire structure can be greatly time-consuming. For such purposes, levels of structural
idealisations can be carried out by reducing the structure into sub-structures with appropriate
boundary conditions to accurately depict their interaction with the remaining structure. The
responses of sub-structure models can also be used to assemble the responses at higher levels
of structural idealisation.
This model reduction simplifies the problem making the analysis easier and faster and can
greatly reduce the number of scenarios to be considered in a robustness analysis involving
notional column removals. The level of structural idealisation depends on the regularity of the
structure, where highly irregular structures will have to be analysed at higher levels of structural
idealisation. The difference in strength and stiffness of different floors can also influence the
optimal level of structural idealisation, and how much the analysis will over- or underestimate
the actions on the structure.

4.6
Summary
In this chapter, several sub-structures were modelled with the intent of examining the modelling
methods to be used for modelling precast concrete structures in Robot and achieving under-
standable results through discussion and validation.
In model 1, a simple double-span beam configuration was modelled to investigate the non-linear
behaviour. The non-linear behaviour of the displacements and normal force was seen to be in
good agreement with the proposed analytical method at lower loads.
In model 2, a single bay slab with two simply supported beams was modelled. The refinement
of the mesh of the finite element panels was examined with regards to accuracy and desired
behaviour. As a result of smaller mesh sizes leading to undesirable effects, a mesh size of 1.2
m was chosen. Furthermore, simple validation of shear and moment forces in the slabs and
beams were made.
In model 3, two sub-structures with two bay slabs and simply supported beams were examined
to determine the behaviour of the slabs in a support removal scenario. Several mechanisms
were discussed such as transverse shear forces and twisting moment forces, which are both
believed to aid in a reduction of the deflections. Membrane and shear forces in the slabs were
studied and resulting forces obtained through panel cuts. The general mechanisms for transfer
of loads and magnitude of panel forces were tried validated, but considering how the exact load
paths in such panel structures in support removal scenarios are difficult to determine, a critical
judgement of such models should always be employed.
In model 4, a larger-scale sub-structure consisting of 4 slab bays supported by beams and
columns were modelled. This model investigated the behaviour and impact of different column
removal scenarios on the structure. Mainly membrane and twisting moment forces were taken
into account, as these were believed to be the main contributors to the alternative load paths.
Twisting moment was seen to always be present in directly affected bays, and tensile membrane
forces were present in the slabs which were able to transfer them to the horizontal supports.
Increased loads on some columns and decreased loads on others, show how the slabs redis-
tribute the vertical loads from the lost support. While ties were modelled between slabs in the
floor, they were not seen to develop any internal forces and, consequently, the panel forces are
decisive for evaluating the total horizontal forces.
In general, the panels contribute considerably to developing alternative load paths, and the aris-
ing panel forces should be checked against their resistance to these. Precast hollow core slabs
may not be designed for resisting such arising torsional moment forces, and their connecting
joints to beams or contiguous slabs have to be capable of transferring the membrane forces in
the slabs. The slabs may also not be designed to resist such large tensile forces, so these will
likely be designed to be concentrated in structural ties which should then be designed for these
equivalent concentrated tensile forces. Lastly, the columns should be designed for increased
loads as they may experience larger loads in a column removal scenario, than designed for in
the normal scenario.

5.6
Summary
A case study of a finite element model of a suitable larger-scale structure was performed by
analysing the deformations and load paths in multiple column removal scenarios.
The removal of a column introduces large increases in deflections. The alternative load paths for
transferring loads from the lost support to intact columns is primarily through torsional moment
action of the slabs and longitudinal catenary action of the slabs in their span direction. The
possibility of transferring loads through the torsional moment action in hollow core slabs is not
investigated in this thesis, but needs to be evaluated if the application of the provided results in
a design situation is desired.
The catenary action introduces large horizontal forces, present through tensile membrane forces
in the slab panels. Though structural ties are modelled as steel bars, the actual forces in these
are close to zero and not usable in a design situation. Because of the slabsŌĆÖ much larger stiff-
ness compared to the ties, all the horizontal forces are transferred in the slabs and evaluation
of these forces has to be done for the membrane forces in the slabs. For such, panel cuts were
made and the sum of the tension forces was evaluated against Eurocode provision. The design
provisions for ties in EN 1991 were seen to cover the forces found in the model, while the EN
1992 might underestimate the needed design force, depending on the consequence class, in
this current structure.
The loads on existing columns were seen to increase by up to 100 % in column removal sce-
narios, making it reasonable to design the columns for such a load increase in the accidental
limit state, while loads on other columns decreased due to the twisting action of the slabs.
Using smaller-scale models of sub-structures from a larger structure can be modelled, if the right
support conditions are employed. Elastic supports should be modelled to accurately depict the
stiffness of a full-scale structure and avoid overestimation of the resulting horizontal forces.
Different alterations to the original model were made to investigate methods to reduce deflec-
tions and to examine arising loads in walls. A transverse shear wall above the removed column
reduced the deflections considerably by effectively transferring the loads to the intact columns.
This reduced the twisting moment forces in the slabs, but instead generates larger horizontal
forces in the slabs.
Facade beams reduced the deflections by a small amount, which are primarily thought to be
through the additional stiffness of the floor structure provided by the beams. It reduced the
tensile forces in the slabs, however, by transferring the horizontal forces in the beams instead.
By putting walls in the facade and removing facade columns, the effect of column removals on
the forces in the wall could be investigated. Horizontal forces were introduced in the walls when
columns were removed, with the largest forces generated when column 10, closest to the wall,
was removed.

6
Conclusion
The main purpose of this thesis has been to investigate the possibilities of modelling precast
concrete structures in a finite element software and analysing them in column removal scenar-
ios.
Background on structural robustness is provided, including a detailed explanation of the cur-
rent regulations in the Eurocodes and the different design approaches for achieving structural
robustness. This includes the notional removal of a column, where alternative load paths have
to be developed in case of a lost column. There are many ways of establishing alternative load
paths to the original, many of which require a degree of continuity in the structure which is usu-
ally obtained through the use of ties. The ties can be designed on the basis of structural analysis
during the notional removal of an element, or through the prescriptive requirements to the ties
given in the Eurocode. Failure modes typical for precast concrete structures are characterised
by slabs slipping off their supports, cracking of the longitudinal joints between slabs and torsion
induced cracking of the slabs and structural topping.
For modelling precast concrete structures in a finite element environment, proposed modelling
techniques are presented, which includes necessary releases of beams and slabs for correct
precast behaviour. Methods for using non-linear analysis in the used software, Robot Structural
Analysis, is described, which is necessary to model the non-linear behaviour of catenary action
and induced horizontal forces.
Preliminary finite element models are investigated to gain an understanding of the non-linear
analysis and modelling choices in Robot. Numerical results for a simple beam sub-structure is
compared to a proposed analytical method for obtaining axial forces from vertical displacements
in horizontally restrained elements. The numerical results proved to be in good agreement with
expected results, with low relative deviations in low load conditions. The optimal mesh size of
panel elements for traditional hollow core slabs was examined for the desired behaviour and
results, which includes reducing singularities in the finite elements.
In a two bay slab structure, the non-linear analysis showed the introduction of horizontal mem-
brane forces in the slabs in a support removal scenario. These forces were high due to complete
lateral restraint of the structure, which is not an accurate representation of such a sub-structure.
The load transfer of the slabs is primarily through torsional bending action and catenary action.
The membrane forces are analysed through panel cuts to obtain a total value of the tensile
force, which can be used in a design situation. The modelling of structural ties through solid
steel bars was made to investigate the effect and forces of these. It was found the ties had no
effect on the model, as the much larger stiffness of the slabs caused all the horizontal forces to
be transferred in the slabs.
A full-scale structure was modelled as a case study and analysed in two column removal sce-
narios. The general behaviour of torsion and catenary action was representative of the sub-
structure models, but with more realistic lower membrane forces. The membrane forces were
compared to the Eurocode provisions, and the design provisions for ties in EN 1991 were seen
to cover the forces found in the model, while the EN 1992 might underestimate the needed
design force, depending on the consequence class, in this current structure. Using provision
values for CC3 when designing using EN 1992 might be suitable. The loads on existing columns
were seen to increase by up to 100 % in column removal scenarios, making it reasonable to
design the columns for such a load increase in the accidental limit state.

Providing transverse shear walls above removed columns reduce deflections considerably, but
instead introduces larger horizontal forces in the slabs. Facade beams can also reduce deflec-
tions by additional stiffness of the floor structure and by providing better capacity for transferring
horizontal forces from catenary action. Horizontal forces in walls from column removals were
investigated and seen to develop partial uplift forces in some scenarios.
The behaviour of the models showed to be representative of real-life precast structures in some
instances, while other behaviours such as the twisting moment in hollow core slabs should be
assessed carefully. The capacity for catenary action in the floor structure should also be consid-
ered, as the typical failure modes might not allow for such behaviour under large deformations.
A design situation should focus on the transfer of forces in the connections of elements, as
these are the critical areas in precast concrete structures.
