Abstract
This thesis focuses on the design and evaluation of a customized virtual reality (VR) hous­
ing for stroke rehabilitation, specifically targeting the game of ping pong. The goal is to
create an immersive and engaging gaming experience that can facilitate motor skill re­
covery and enhance the rehabilitation process. The thesis follows an iterative design ap­
proach, exploring various concepts for the VR housing, handle shapes, and attachment
mechanisms.
Quantitative and qualitative data were collected to assess the usability, immersion, and
gameplay experience of the VR housing. The results revealed significant changes in
participants’ perceptions of immersion, absorption, and effort exerted after engaging with
the VR housing. The qualitative findings highlighted the enjoyment and engagement of the
participants, emphasizing the potential of the VR housing design for stroke rehabilitation.
The significance of the VR housing design lies in its potential to provide a personalized and
immersive rehabilitation experience. By leveraging 3D printing technology, the housing
design can be customized to meet individual needs and preferences. The thesis also
discusses future directions, including the incorporation of feedback from clinicians, the
expansion to cover additional sports, and the involvement of stroke patients in clinical
studies.
Overall, this thesis contributes to the field of VR­based interventions in stroke rehabilita­
tion, showcasing the potential benefits of immersive gaming experiences. The personal­
ized VR housing design holds promise in improving the effectiveness and enjoyment of
stroke rehabilitation programs, ultimately enhancing the quality of life and recovery out­
comes for stroke patients.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Stroke rehabilitation is an area of great importance due to its significant impact on indi­
viduals and society. A stroke happens when the blood flow to the brain is interrupted and
can be caused by a clot or a rupture in a blood vessel, resulting in a range of physical,
cognitive, and emotional consequences. In the European Union (EU), stroke is the sec­
ond leading cause of death and a major contributor to adult disabilities [1]. Every year,
there are approximately 1.1 million stroke cases [2] and 440,000 stroke­related deaths in
Europe alone [3]. With the steady increase in life expectancy and the aging of the pop­
ulation, the number of stroke survivors is projected to increase 27% between 2017 and
2047 [3].
In addition, the COVID­19 pandemic was a very challenging time for stroke patients, as
they were expected to continue their rehabilitation journey from their homes, due to lock­
downs and reduced access to healthcare facilities. This sudden shift from supervised
to unsupervised rehabilitation highlighted the lack of preparedness within the healthcare
system and it was clear that some changes needed to be made to provide stroke patients
with the necessary support, guidance, and resources [4].
Given these compelling statistics, it is natural to assume that the demand for quality stroke
rehabilitation services is expected to rise. It is crucial to look into technology to develop
innovative methods that can help stroke survivors regain their physical and functional
ability, and improve their quality of life. The utilization of virtual reality (VR) gaming for
rehabilitation seems like a very promising avenue that could enhance the motivation and
engagement of stroke patients, increasing their adherence to the therapy program. There­
fore, this thesis seeks to explore the effectiveness of VR­based interventions, their impact
on immersion and motivation levels, and their potential to revolutionize the rehabilitation
experience for stroke survivors.
The findings of this research hold the potential to enhance future rehabilitation practices,
leading to better patient outcomes through more engaging rehabilitation programs. By
addressing the pressing needs of stroke survivors, this thesis strives to contribute to the
field of stroke rehabilitation and pave the way for advancements that can significantly
enhance the lives of those affected by stroke.
1.2
ReHyb Project
This thesis project is carried out in close collaboration with the ReHyb project, which is
funded under the EU Horizon 2020 initiative. The main objective of the ReHyb project is to
develop an upper­limb exoskeleton solution for stroke patients to continue their rehabilita­
tion at home. These exoskeleton measures serve two purposes: monitoring the patients’
upper limb movements and facilitating their movement to perform training exercises [5].
Furthermore, the system is capable of predicting the intended actions of patients through
the utilization of a digital twin [6].
As part of the ReHyb project, the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has established
a dedicated and multidisciplinary research group focusing on exploring innovative ap­
proaches to improve stroke rehabilitation. One of the key areas of focus in the ReHyb
project is the importance of providing engaging and immersive VR experiences so that

1.3
Thesis Focus & Added Value
The focus of this thesis is on the design and evaluation of a 3D­printed VR controller
housing for stroke rehabilitation to improve game immersion and patient motivation. The
primary objective is to create a controller housing (also referred to as an accessory) in the
shape of a sports racket and investigate its impact on the rehabilitation experience com­
pared to using a regular racket and body tracker via a study. The impact of the housing
is analyzed taking into account the following key aspects: game immersion, motivation,
and engagement. Enhancing game immersion and motivation levels can make the reha­
bilitation process more engaging and enjoyable for stroke patients, leading to improved
outcomes. These findings can potentially impact patients’ overall well­being and quality
of life during and after the rehabilitation process.
The contribution of this thesis to research has the potential to improve the stroke rehabil­
itation system. By successfully designing and integrating a 3D­printed housing with VR
technology to enhance stroke rehabilitation, this research opens doors for future devel­
opments of different add­on modules, providing patients with virtual sports activities that
are tailored to their preferences and needs.
1.3.1
Research Questions & Objectives
The overarching research question that serves as the starting point of this project is as
follows: ”Does a 3D­printed racket with an embedded controller improve game immersion
compared to using a regular racket with a body tracker?” This research question can be
split into the following specific objectives:
• Understand game immersion, motivation, and user experience in rehabilitation: The
objective is to identify the relationships between game immersion and motivation,
and how the overall performance and outcome are influenced.
• Explore the potential of 3D printing technologies to create rehabilitation accessories:
This focuses on exploring different sports accessories through an iterative design
process.
• Identify and select the concept that meets the requirements: This objective involves
assessing different design concepts and evaluating their potential and alignment
with the predetermined specifications.
• Test and evaluate the end product in terms of user experience: The final goal is
to assess the usability, comfort, and overall satisfaction of the chosen 3D printed
housing compared to a different approach from the perspective of users.

Methods & Approach
In this project, a User­Centered Design approach has been followed. User­Centered De­
sign involves putting the user’s needs front and center and follows an iterative design
process that focuses on the user’s needs every step of the way. The main goal of User­
Centered Design is to create a product that is highly desirable and functional to the user.
This is achieved by a clear understanding of the contexts of use and user needs, involve­
ment of end users, and regular collection and analysis of user feedback. Therefore, an
adaptation of Design Thinking methodology was employed to guide the methods, pro­
cesses, and tools to align with User­Centered Design principles.
2.1
Design Thinking
In this fast­paced world, technology is advancing rapidly, and this brings new expectations
and requirements for product design. Designers face challenges in keeping up with these
changes and innovating at a faster rate. To address these challenges, it is important
to shift away from traditional step­by­step design processes. Instead, designers should
adopt iterative approaches that prioritize the needs of the users above everything else.
By embracing this user­centric approach, designers can create products that better meet
their needs and keep up with the ever­changing technological landscape.
Design Thinking is an iterative user­centered methodology for innovation and problem­
solving, commonly used in fields such as product design, service design, and business
strategy. This approach encourages a deep understanding of users’ needs and chal­
lenges and aims to generate innovative solutions that address those needs practically
and innovatively. The term was popularized in the 1990s, when the design company
IDEO [7] was formed. This company was one of the first design companies to showcase
their design process, based on design methods and design thinking [8].
The Design Thinking process typically involves several phases including empathizing with
users to gain insights and understand their perspectives, defining the problem or opportu­
nity to be addressed, ideating potential solutions, and prototyping and testing those solu­
tions. This last phase is a very essential part of the process since it provides the designer
with valuable insights and user feedback that can be used for continuous improvements
through iteration.

2.1.1
Universal Design Principles
Universal Design is the process of creating products that are accessible to people with
a wide range of abilities, disabilities, and other characteristics. Typically, products are
designed to be most suitable for the average user. In contrast, products that are designed
according to principles of Universal Design are designed to be usable by everyone, to the
greatest extent possible, without the need for adaptation or specialized design [10].
To guide designers towards more universal solutions, the Seven Principles of Universal
Design [11] were developed, which are described as follows:
• Equitable use: The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.
• Flexibility in use: The design accommodates a wide range of individual prefer­
ences and abilities.
• Simple and intuitive use: The design is easy to understand regardless of the user’s
experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level.
• Perceptible information: The design communicates necessary information effec­
tively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user’s sensory abilities.
• Tolerance for error: The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences
of accidental or unintended actions.
• Low physical effort: The design can be used efficiently and comfortably and with
a minimum of fatigue.
• Size and space for approach and use: Appropriate size and space are provided
for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of the user’s body size, pos­
ture, or mobility.
Figure 2.2: The Seven Principles of Universal Design provide a framework for creating
inclusive and accessible designs for all individuals.
The principles of Universal Design must be taken into account when creating the design
requirements for the solution because many different kinds of people may use the prod­
uct. These principles will influence the idea generation and concept development phases,
using them as an evaluation tool to see if the solutions are usable for everyone.
The main objective of Universal Design is to develop designs that are accessible and
inclusive, doing away with all types of prejudice based on factors like age, nationality, dis­
ability, or other characteristics. It is important to recognize that this thesis project’s target
audience may not have the same degree of physical and cognitive capacities as other
people because stroke patients are often older than 65 years old. Because of this, it’s
essential to approach the design process from several angles to make sure the final solu­
tion supports a variety of users and addresses their unique requirements. The objective

2.2
Process
Different models have been introduced to better define the Design Thinking process, but
one of the most common ones is the Double Diamond Model which was developed by
the British Design Council in 2003 [12]. This model is based on divergent exploration and
convergent refinement, and the methodology is divided into the following four phases:
• Discover: This phase helps designers understand what the problem is. It involves
researching information about the problem and speaking to people who are affected
by the problem.
• Define: The insights gathered from the discovery phase are used in the define
phase to clearly define the challenge and the user requirements and needs.
• Develop: The fourth phase encourages the designer to come up with different ideas
to solve the defined challenge, either on their own or seeking inspiration from else­
where, or co­designing with different people.
• Deliver: The last phase involves testing out the different solutions at a small scale
to select one and reject the ones that do not meet the requirements.
Figure 2.3: The Double Diamond by the Design Council [12]. A visual representation of
the iterative design process, emphasizing discovery, definition, development, and delivery
stages for user­centered innovation.
It is important to mention that even though the Double Diamond is presented as a linear
model, it’s a very iterative process in which it is necessary to go back and forth between the
phases when new information is discovered. While the second diamond encompasses
the divergent thinking of generating ideas and the subsequent convergence of selecting a
solution, it is essential to extend the process beyond this point. This thesis project builds
on the Double Diamond by adding a third diamond to carry out the testing and evaluation
phase, where the ideas and solutions are tested in real­world scenarios. Therefore, the

following two­phase diamond is added to the Double Diamond, creating a Triple Diamond

To consider the logistical aspect of the Triple Diamond Model, a Gantt chart was used to
provide a structured plan for the project, outlining tasks and timelines. However, due to
unforeseen changes, new findings, and time limitations, the progress deviated from the
original plan. This required constant adaptation and adjustments to the timeline, follow­
ing the iterative nature of the process. The following are overviews of the Triple Diamond
phases however they will be presented in greater detail in subsequent chapters.
2.2.1
Research & Analysis
The divergent phase of the first diamond consists of the research and analysis phase,
which involves ’discovering’ all the necessary knowledge of this project. This phase fo­
cused on gathering data through extensive research on stroke, stroke rehabilitation, and
the importance of patient motivation. Additionally, different VR controllers and acces­
sories were explored to get an idea of what was available in the market. The outcome of
this phase was a deep understanding of the context and a list of identified themes and
problems in the VR stroke rehabilitation system.
2.2.2
Problem Scoping
The convergent phase of the first diamond consists of the problem scoping phase, which
involves ”defining” the problem to create a common understanding of the problem space.
Throughout the research and analysis, the data that was collected was synthesized and
filtered to come up with the main focus of the project. The outcome of this phase was a
defined problem scope, a target persona, and a list of design requirements.
2.2.3
Idea Generation
The idea generation phase corresponds to the divergent phase of the second diamond
and involved generating ideas through brainstorming sessions and sketching different
concepts until valid solutions were achieved. The outcome of this phase was a cluster of
six ideas to solve the defined problem.

3.1
Stroke & Stroke Rehabilitation
In the European Union (EU) specifically, stroke stands as the second most common single
cause of death, accounting for over 176,000 male deaths (7%) and just under 250,000
female deaths (10%) annually [1].In the EU, stroke incidence is a very concerning topic,
given that by 2047, it is estimated that 2.4% of the population will have experienced a
stroke, indicating a significant increase of 28% compared to 2017 [3].
These projections can be attributed to a change in demographics, as over time, a larger
proportion of the EU population will be constituted by older people. Consequently, this will
lead to an increase in the number of stroke patients, who will have a greater chance of
surviving, due to two reasons. First, most of these strokes will have milder consequences
because of ongoing advances in medicine and the implementation of prevention strategies
[13] Additionally, patients in the future will receive better and improved care in both the
acute and long­term stages after a stroke.
The economic impact of stroke on the EU is substantial, with an estimated annual cost
of €45 billion. This amount accounts for approximately one­fifth of the overall cost of
cardiovascular disease. Within the total expense of stroke in the EU, direct healthcare
costs amount to €20 billion (44%), productivity losses account for €9 billion (22%), and
the provision of informal care for stroke survivors represents €16 billion (35%) [1].
These statistics highlight the importance of creating effective stroke prevention and reha­
bilitation strategies. Furthermore, it is necessary to address these to mitigate the impact
of stroke, improve patient outcomes and stabilize the healthcare system as a whole.
3.1.1
Definition, Types & Effects of Stroke
A stroke is a medical condition that occurs when something blocks the blood supply to
part of the brain or when a blood vessel in the brain bursts. In either case, portions of
the brain become damaged or die because the brain requires oxygen to function properly,
which is delivered through oxygen­rich blood. When blood flow is obstructed, brain cells
begin to die within minutes due to a lack of oxygen, resulting in a stroke [14].
There are two main types of stroke: ischemic stroke and hemorrhagic stroke. Ischemic
strokes are the most common, arising when blood clots or other particles block the blood
Hand Controllers and Accessories for Stroke Rehabilitation at Home
11
==PAGE:20==
vessels that supply to the brain. This blockage can also be caused by plaque, which
consists of fatty deposits, that accumulate in the blood vessels.
On the other hand,
a hemorrhagic stroke occurs when an artery in the brain leaks blood or breaks open,
putting excessive pressure on brain cells and causing damage. High blood pressure and
aneurysms, which are bulges in an artery that can stretch and burst, are conditions that
can lead to a hemorrhagic stroke. Additionally, a transient ischemic attack, often referred
to as a ”mini­stroke,” is caused by a temporary clot [14] [15].
Figure 3.1: Ischemic Stroke vs Hemorrhagic Brain Stroke. A visual comparison of the two
main types of strokes [16].
The consequences of a stroke can be long­lasting brain damage, chronic disability, or
even fatality. The effect of a stroke depends on various factors, including the specific
location of the blockage and the extent of damage to the brain tissue. Since each hemi­
sphere of the brain controls the opposite side of the body, a stroke affecting a particular
side will manifest neurological complications on the corresponding side of the body. For
instance, if a stroke occurs in the left side of the brain, it will affect the right side of the
body, leading to conditions such as right­sided paralysis, speech/language problems, a
slow and cautious behavioral style, and memory loss. Conversely, if the stroke occurs
in the right hemisphere of the brain, the left side of the body will be affected, resulting
in left­sided paralysis, visual impairments, a quick and inquisitive behavioral style, and
memory loss [15].
3.1.2
Stroke Rehabilitation
Stroke rehabilitation is a highly individualized process because every individual is differ­
ently affected by a stroke [17]. Recovery time after a stroke can take weeks, months, or
even years. Some stroke victims achieve full recovery, while others have long­term or
lifelong disabilities [14]. It is also very common for stroke survivors to experience another
stroke, given that 1 in 4 stroke victims have another stroke within 5 years [18].
The John Hopkins Medicine University analyzed the rehabilitation process and estab­
lished it to begin approximately 24 hours after the stroke. In this early stage, the patient
receives therapy 6 times a day for 5 to 7 days while staying at the hospital. The intensity of
the therapy exercises provided to the patient depends on the severity of the stroke. These
exercises are mainly focused on activities of daily living, which include tasks like bathing
or preparing food. When the patient is discharged, the care team prepares a rehabilita­
tion plan for the patient to follow either at home or at a designated clinic [17]. Physical
therapy is the main form of rehabilitation for most stroke survivors. As mentioned before,
a common after­effect of stroke is movement impairment and the aim of physical therapy
is to help people relearn simple motor activities like walking or standing, through training

and exercises. Another form of stroke rehabilitation is occupational therapy, which takes
a broader approach by considering the overall well­being and functional independence of
individuals in their daily life. These therapists can recommend home or workplace modi­
fications to better help the person regain their independent or semi­independent lifestyle
[18].
Many stroke survivors report that achieving independence at home is their main personal
goal, and almost all patients desire to leave the hospital as quickly as possible. This de­
sire is a strong motivating factor to engage in rehabilitation exercises [19]. Conventional
physical therapy has shown positive results over time [20]. However, once patients return
to their home environment, the challenges and barriers to adhering to the therapy become
apparent. The level of commitment to an in­home rehabilitation regime tends to be low,
as homes typically lack the specialized facilities available in the hospital setting. Tradi­

and current game­based systems designed for rehabilitation often lack the entertainment
value found in recreational games [21].
Several studies have estimated that approximately 65% of patients do not adhere to their
rehabilitation programs, and this rate further decreases when rehabilitation continues at
home without regular therapist observation. Training at home is clearly perceived as
unattractive and patients frequently describe it as boring and monotonous [20][22]. While
gamification can support motivation to some extent, it cannot fully compensate for strong
internal motives such as the fear of pain, the avoidance of boredom, or general human
laziness [22], which will be later discussed in section 3.3 when explaining both flow theory
[23] and Self­Determination Theory (SDT) [24].
3.2
Serious Games & VR Stroke Rehabilitation
In recent years, the concept of serious games has gained popularity as a way of keeping
users motivated and engaged. Serious games are a genre of games that are designed
for purposes other than pure entertainment and have found applications in various fields
including education, industry, and healthcare. The double mission of serious games is
to simultaneously achieve an intended goal (serious part) without compromising the ex­
perience of playing a game (game part) [25]. Lately, these serious games have been
increasingly implemented in virtual reality as a form of rehabilitation exercise to immerse
patients in the experience and enhance their motivation and engagement.
Studies have shown that virtual reality tasks in serious games are often perceived as
more interesting and enjoyable by both children and adults, resulting in an increased
repetition of exercises [26]. For instance, one study demonstrated that stroke patients
who performed additional VR­based training for hand rehabilitation in the early sub­acute
phase after a stroke exhibited greater improvements in motor performance [27].
Using VR for serious games offers several advantages, including a personalized experi­
ence, increased engagement and motivation leading to behavioral change, and the pro­
vision of appealing and context­based training environments [28]. Currently, most VR
applications in rehabilitation simulate real­life activities such as object manipulation or
daily tasks, but the physical simulations of object animations in these systems are often
pre­rendered and lack realism [29].
In one study by the University of Derby in the United Kingdom, several VR games were
created to encourage patients’ physical activity. A couple of examples, of those serious
games are the cath­the­orange game and the fishing game. In the first one, cath­the­
orange game the user holds a real basket that is virtually replicated with which they need

3.3
Motivation & Immersion in Stroke Rehabilitation
The word motivation, etymologically, concerns what ”moves” people to action [24]. Mo­
tivation plays an important role in stroke rehabilitation as it directly influences a patient’s
engagement and willingness to participate in the rehabilitation process. Repetitive ex­
ercises can be perceived as tedious and patient motivation can be negatively affected,
leading to low engagement with home rehabilitation. To avoid this problem, rehabilita­
tion exercises should be gamified and provide a captivating gameplay experience while
seamlessly adapting to the users’ needs [21]. Therefore, exercises must be meaningful
and relate to the patient’s interest. Patients are usually motivated by the goal of fully re­
covering and regaining their identity, such as returning to their previous occupations and
hobbies.
One of the biggest challenges in stroke rehabilitation is maintaining patient motivation,
and games have shown potential in addressing this issue. While some games are specif­
ically designed to support rehabilitation plans, it is also beneficial to allow patients to play
games they are already familiar with. In a study by the Scandinavian Journal of Occu­
pational Therapy, it was found that the incorporation of Wii Sports [33] into conventional
rehabilitation was beneficial for patients as it provided motivation for alternative leisure ac­
tivities. During this study, an overarching theme was found, ”Connecting to past, present,
and future occupations”, and many patients were pleased by the opportunity to engage in
simulations of valued past or current occupations. Even so, some patients had used Wii
prior to the stroke and remembered positive experiences of playing with their friends, chil­
dren, or grandchildren [31]. Giving people the opportunity to reconnect with their previous
lifestyles and activities provides them with a purpose and motivates them to continue with
their rehabilitation exercises.
In the Beyond Boredom and Anxiety study by Csikszentmihalyi [23], the concept of moti­
vation is deeply examined to try to find what makes playing enjoyable. During this study,
the author sought to learn why people engaged in ”autotelic” (self­purposed) activities in
which they received no extrinsic reward, like money, status, or power. He found that all
14

==PAGE:23==
autotelic activities reward the participants intrinsically, with the flow experience, which is
defined as ”a merging of action and awareness, ego and environment that temporarily
negates consciousness of an outside observing self”. In other words, flow describes an
optimal experience where individuals become fully absorbed in an activity, losing track
of time and forgetting about their surroundings. Csikszentmihalyi also divided flow into
eight components: clear goals, high degree of concentration, loss of the feeling of self­
consciousness, distorted sense of time, direct and immediate feedback, sense of personal
control and balance between skill level and challenges [34].
Related to the latter, studies suggest that flow theory mainly occurs when there is a bal­
ance between the challenges presented by the activity and the individual’s abilities to meet
those challenges. If the person’s skills are higher than the challenges faced, boredom oc­
curs, and if they are lower, anxiety takes over [23]. To maintain motivation, rehabilitation
tasks should be set at an appropriate level of challenge. Additionally, for patients to re­
main engaged in the process, they should experience a sense of immersion in the virtual
environment. Striking a balance between activities perceived as trivial and those consid­
ered excessively difficult is vital to sustaining motivation [29].
Figure 3.2: The Flow Model by Csikszentmihalyi, 1975 [35]. Illustrating the dynamic re­
lationship between anxiety and boredom in the flow experience, showcasing the optimal
state of engagement and focus where skills match challenges, leading to a state of height­

The term ”immersion” does not have a common definition, but can very much be related
to Csikszentmihalyi’s concept of flow theory [23]. Generally, immersion is considered a
somewhat fragile psychological state of the user, that can relate to focusing one’s attention
”on something other than the immediate surrounding reality”, meaning that the user is
captured by this reality while they experience a story that makes them shut their real­world
out [36]. Researchers Daniel Pietschmann, Georg Valtin, and Peter Ohler carried out a
study in which they tested ”authentic” controllers to see if they positively affected game
immersion [37]. The authors’ experimental study compared the immersion attained when
using a Wii [33] Remote controller (being this the more ”authentic” or ”realistic” controller)
to the immersion measured when using a traditional game controller for the same game.
The results indicated that there was a positive correlation between the authentic input
device and a higher immersion, but it is worth noting that that effect was to some degree
influenced by the gaming experience of the players. Overall, immersion is understood as

an essential element of the pleasure of playing a digital game, and it is closely connected
to motivation.
Another interesting framework to analyze is the Self­Determination Theory (SDT) [24]
which is a theory that grew out of researchers Edward L. Deci and Richard M. Ryan in the
1970s. It supports that intrinsic motivation leads to optimal performance and well­being,
and it is formed by six mini­theories. For this analysis, the following two most relevant
theories will be described.
• Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET): this is the first of SDT’s theories and focuses
exclusively on intrinsic motivation, supporting that it leads to optimal performance
and well­being. The theory further argues that events that positively affect a per­
son’s experience of autonomy or competence will enhance intrinsic motivation [24].
This theory is often linked with the concept of rewards, given that when people are
intrinsically motivated for an activity, they derive personal rewards directly from do­
ing it, and are fully willing to carry out the activity even if they receive no other reward.
In other words, acknowledging people’s feelings and performance tends to enhance
their intrinsic motivation [38].
• Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT): the fourth mini­theory, argues that
psychological well­being and optimal functioning are based on autonomy, compe­
tence, and relatedness ­ the three basic psychological needs. Within BPNT, it is
worth mentioning the Player Experience of Needs Satisfaction concept [39], which
is an evaluation instrument used to assess the factors that support intrinsic motiva­
tion in games. This questionnaire has served as an inspiration to create the study
in section 7.4.
In conclusion, motivation and immersion are fundamental aspects of stroke rehabilitation,
as they directly impact patients’ willingness to participate in the rehabilitation process. By
gamifying rehabilitation and providing activities that are meaningful and tailored to the
patient’s interests, motivation can be enhanced and their enthusiasm for recovery can
be maintained. Additionally, the concept of immersion plays a vital role in enhancing the
pleasure of playing digital games and virtual experiences, providing a sense of absorption
and flow where patients become fully engaged in the activity, contributing to optimal per­
formance and enjoyment. Striking a balance between challenges and individual abilities,
acknowledging intrinsic rewards, and providing a sense of autonomy and competence are
essential components to sustain motivation and foster immersion in the rehabilitation jour­
ney. These factors not only enhance patient outcomes but also contribute to the overall
well­being and quality of life for individuals recovering from a stroke.

3.8
3D Scanning & 3D Printing Technologies
The fundamental concepts of 3D scanning and 3D printing, which have been substantially
used in this thesis, are introduced in the following section. While 3D printing allows the
creation of three­dimensional items from digital models, 3D scanning makes it possible to
create digital representations of real­world objects. Marquardt and Zheng [59] highlight
that the early roots of these technologies came from the field of sculpture. In 1860, the
French artist François Willème patented a photo­sculpturing method in which a subject
was placed in the center of a circular room and was surrounded by 24 cameras that took
photographs simultaneously. Then the artist traced the 24 profiles using a cutter attached
to a pantograph that cut the wood. When computers started to become more powerful in

and 3D printing [60], and nowadays, the combination of both provides a new tool for the

3.8.1
3D Scanning
3D scanning is the process of analyzing an object from the real world, by digitally capturing
the object’s shape to recreate its shape and appearance. After this process, the three­
dimensional data is used to create a digital 3D model of the object that consists of a
polygon mesh that replicates the surface of the object. In 3D scanning technology, some
many different machines and methods can be used, but the following main processes can
be distinguished:
• Photogrammetry: This is a method in which the 3D model is created using many
photographs of the object from different angles [61]. These pictures are then ana­
lyzed from all angles and the pixels that correspond to the same physical point are
detected. This technology does not require special hardware and can be achieved
using a normal camera that can be moved around the object or, on the contrary, the
object can rotate on itself. This technology has been used for hundreds of years in
fields like topography, architecture, engineering, or manufacturing [62][63].
• Structured light: 3D scanners using structured light project a series of linear light
patterns onto an object which distorts these patterns of light. The system then ex­
amines the deformations of each line and calculates the distance between the 3D
scanner and the object’s surface to then build a 3D model of the object [62][63]. This
technology is commonly used by police forces for facial recognition or to photograph
fingerprints in a 3D scene.
• Laser 3D scanning: This is the most common and used 3D technique and consists
of digitally capturing the shape of the object with a laser light that is projected onto
the object’s surface. The laser shines on the object and a camera looks for the loca­
tion of the laser dot and then calculates the distances and angles between the laser
dot, the laser emitter, and the camera, which form a triangle and is why this tech­
nique is also called triangulation [64]. The accuracy of the laser scanner is high and
therefore, it can measure complex geometries [65]. This technology is commonly
used for machine vision because of its balance between resolution, precision, and
high speed, but also in commercial 3D scanners.
3.8.2
3D Printing
3D printing, also known as additive manufacturing, is a process in which a physical object
is created by layering material based on a digital model. The process of 3D printing
usually involves several steps. Firstly, a digital model of the desired object is needed and
that can be created using Computer­Aided Design (CAD) software or obtained through
3D scanning, as mentioned in the previous section. This model has to be exported as
a Stereolithography file format (STL) and imported into a 3D printing software that slices
the model into thin layers. Lastly, the slicer software produces a G­code file that the 3D
printer interprets and then proceeds to extrude material to build the object layer by layer
[59].
There are several additive manufacturing methods but, the most commonly known and
easily accessible 3D printing technique is Fused Deposition Modeling [66]. FDM operates
on the principle of material extrusion in which a solid material in the form of a filament is
extruded and melted through a nozzle onto a build platform. The nozzle traces out the
geometry of the design and deposits material moving in an X­Y direction. After that layer
is done, the nozzle moves up in the Z direction and proceeds with the next layer, and this
entire process repeats until the desired object is formed [67].
3D printing encompasses many forms of technologies and materials and is used in a wide

6.1
Concept Evaluation
In the evaluation chapter, the focus shifted to assessing the racket concepts created dur­
ing the ideation phase. Before delving into the evaluation process, it is important to clarify
that certain design decisions were already made regarding handle shape and attachment
mechanism. These decisions were made based on considerations of familiarity, user
comfort, and feasibility within the scope of this thesis project.
Firstly, it was decided to design the handle in a Flared shape, which is narrower on the top
and wider at the bottom, as seen in Figure 5.7. The Flared handle is a commonly used grip
style in racket sports, including ping pong. By opting for a Flared handle, stroke patients
who have prior experience with racket sports were more likely to be familiar with this grip
style. Additionally, the Flared handle is known to create less tension in the forearm, which
aligns with the goal of promoting a comfortable and ergonomic design for stroke patients.
Another important decision was to attach the handle to the paddle using glue. This choice
was driven by practical considerations and the convenience it offers within the context of
this thesis project. Utilizing glue for the attachment simplifies the manufacturing process
and reduces the complexity of 3D printing. Printing objects with protrusions or intricate
snap­fit mechanisms would probably introduce challenges in terms of print quality, as­
sembly, and durability. By opting for a glued attachment, the focus could be directed
toward refining other aspects of the design, such as the attachment of the controller to
the paddle.
To evaluate the previously generated six concepts, a Weighted Objectives Method was
employed, seen in Figure 6.1. The evaluation process involved comparing the six racket
concepts based on several criteria: easy assembly, robustness/tightness, lightweight, re­
alism, easy to clean, and easy to manufacture. These criteria were chosen to align with
the design requirements and were inspired by the project’s goals.
To assign weights to each criterion, a total weight cap of 50 was established. The cri­
teria were then ranked based on their importance, with different weight values assigned
accordingly. Easy assembly, robustness/tightness, lightweight, and easy to manufacture
were considered very important and were given weights of 10 each. Realism and ease to
clean were deemed less important and were assigned weights of 5 each.
The decision to prioritize easy assembly was driven by the aim of enabling stroke patients
to independently assemble the racket in their own homes. Robustness and tightness
were crucial to ensure the controller was securely attached to the housing, preventing
any accidents or risks during gameplay. The lightweight design was prioritized to minimize
arm strain and enhance usability for stroke patients.
Realism, although considered less important, still played a role in the evaluation. The
main factor contributing to realism was the shape of the handle, as the virtual racket
would be displayed in the game while users wore the VR gear. Easy cleaning, while
important, was considered a secondary priority compared to other factors. Finally, the
ease of manufacture was deemed highly important, given the focus on 3D printing the
housing, and the preference for designs that could be accurately printed without relying
on small parts.

Final Design
After carefully considering the feedback provided by the clinicians, further iterations were
carried out to refine the design and arrive at the final solution. The ultimate solution entails

of two distinct parts: the paddle and the handle.

through the two holes on the handle support, providing an additional layer of stability and
fastening. This arrangement effectively secures the controller, preventing any unintended
movements during gameplay.

7
Testing
To evaluate the efficacy of the 3D­printed housing a study was conducted focusing on
players’ perception of motivation, game immersion, and overall game experience. The
goal was to compare how playing with the Pico Neo 3 Eye controller attached to the 3D­
printed housing would affect participants’ experience compared to playing with the con­
troller alone, or the HTC Vive paddle with its tracker. Hence, a within­subject design was
applied with the type of device used as the independent variable. To capture participants’
responses, a questionnaire was designed that can be found in section A.1. During the
study, the participants got the chance to learn about the ReHyb project and the goal of
this thesis, and they tried out the different devices to play a VR ping pong game. Their
feedback was then collected and used for future analysis.
7.1
Participants
Twenty­one healthy participants were recruited to test and evaluate the three game modes.
In total, 21 females (60%) and 9 males (40%) participated in the experiment. The average
age of the subjects was 23.95 [standard deviation (SD)=1.75]. The vast majority of the
participants were right­handed, given that 18 participants were right­handed (85.71%), 2
were left­handed (9.52%), and only 1 participant was ambidextrous (4.76%) All of them
were familiar with ping pong in general and virtual reality to some extent, but only 11
participants (52.38%) had experienced a VR game before.
7.2
Procedure
The study took place in the virtual reality lab at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU).
After informing the participants about the ReHyb project’s goal and the study’s proce­
dure, participants were asked to sign an informed consent form which included informa­
tion about the data management and privacy plan of the study. After signing the consent
form, a first questionnaire was administered to assess the participants’ age, gender, and
hand dominance (right­handed, left­handed, or ambidextrous). They were also asked
some questions about their prior experience with virtual reality, VR accessories, and ping
pong in general.
Subsequently, participants were introduced to the three different game devices that they
would have to use to play: the Pico Neo 3 Eye controller, the 3D­printed housing that
attaches to the latter, and the HTC Vive paddle with its tracker attached. Then, the par­
ticipants answered some questions about their initial state of motivation and engagement
and their perceptions and expectations of the study. These questions were assessed us­
ing a Likert scale ranging from 1 to 7, where participants chose their responses on the
scale indicating their level of agreement or disagreement.
The order of these three game modes (VR controller, VR controller + 3D­printed hous­
ing, HTC Vive paddle + tracker) was randomized for every participant to avoid possible
sequence and learning effects. This randomization was done when participants were
presented with a question where they were asked to select one option from the three
available choices without prior knowledge of the specifics of each option. The chosen
option determined the sequence in which they played the game modes. Before each of
the three gameplay sessions started, the participants had to answer a pre­test question­
naire to gather data on their initial perception of motivation and expectations regarding
that game mode.

9
Discussion
Based on the quantitative and qualitative data obtained from the study, the discussion
section provides an in­depth analysis and draws conclusions. Through the correlation of
these data sets, a comprehensive understanding of participants’ experiences and prefer­
ences can be gained, thus shedding light on the impact of VR housing design on stroke
rehabilitation.
Increasing scores from pre­ to post­test results indicate an increased awareness of the
importance of immersion in the gaming experience. It is likely that participants were more
aware of the immersive nature of the game and the importance of fully engaging in it. This
finding aligns with the goal of VR­based rehabilitation, which aims to create a sense of
presence and absorption for optimal therapeutic outcomes.
It was surprising to find that there was a slight decrease in participants’ confidence fol­
lowing the game experience. In part, this can be explained by the challenges encoun­
tered during gameplay, such as the difficulty in detecting movement and the need to react
quickly. It is possible that new technology and novel gameplay mechanics initially unset­
tled participants, resulting in temporary confidence declines. However, it is important to
note that this decrease in confidence did not overshadow the positive experiences and
enjoyment expressed by participants during gameplay.
The challenge level of the game was carefully considered and tailored to the capabilities
of stroke patients. The VR housing design facilitated the integration of challenging game­
play elements, such as precise paddle movements and reaction times, while ensuring
that the level of difficulty remained manageable and engaging for participants. The pos­
itive experiences reported by participants regarding the challenge level underscore the
importance of striking a balance between providing an engaging experience and avoiding
overwhelming frustration.
Participants perceived an increase in the level of effort exerted during gameplay. This
suggests that the VR housing design, with its physical interactions and motor skill de­
mands, effectively challenged participants and required them to exert effort and focus.
The integration of the housing with the controller provided a natural and intuitive inter­
face, allowing participants to fully engage with the game mechanics and experience a
sense of agency and control.
The game experience did not significantly impact participants’ perceived level of fatigue.
This suggests that the gameplay duration and intensity were well­tailored to the capa­
bilities of stroke patients, ensuring that they were not overly exerted or fatigued during
the study. This finding is crucial in stroke rehabilitation, as maintaining an optimal bal­
ance between challenge and fatigue is essential for sustained engagement and effective
recovery.
The findings also highlight the significance of immersion, as participants recognized and
valued the immersive qualities of the VR gaming experience. The sense of presence and

9.1
Limitations
While this project’s study showcases the great potential of the VR housing design in stroke
rehabilitation, several limitations should be acknowledged. These limitations highlight the
challenges encountered throughout the project and provide insights for future research
and development.
Firstly, the need for 3D scanning to obtain accurate measurements of the controller posed
a significant challenge. The complexity of the controller’s contours and shape required
precise measurements to ensure a proper fit and alignment with the VR housing design.
Although 3D scanning provided a reliable solution, it added an additional step to the design
process, requiring access to specialized equipment and expertise.
Another limitation stems from the difficulty in accessing stroke patients for the study. Due
to logistical constraints and the limited availability of stroke patients, the study was con­
ducted with healthy participants. While this provided valuable insights into the usability
and gameplay experience of the VR housing design, it is important to acknowledge that
stroke patients may have unique needs and considerations that were not fully explored in
this study. Further research involving stroke patients would provide a more comprehen­
sive understanding of the effectiveness of the VR housing design in stroke rehabilitation.
Furthermore, the 3D printing process presented challenges in terms of time and equip­
ment availability. Some 3D printers experienced technical issues or were occupied, lead­
ing to delays in the production of prototypes. The lengthy printing time of each racket
prototype, ranging from 10 to 15 hours, also contributed to the overall time­consuming
nature of the 3D printing process. These limitations impacted the efficiency and timeline
of the project, requiring careful planning and allocation of resources.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis aimed to design and evaluate a personalized VR housing for
stroke rehabilitation, specifically targeting racket­based games such as ping pong. Through
a user­centered design approach, the Design Thinking methodology was modified to em­
ploy an iterative Triple Diamond Model to drive the design process. This process led to
the development of various concepts that featured design considerations such as handle
shapes, attachment mechanisms, and user comfort. The final version of the housing de­
sign concept was compared against a racket with a body tracker attached to play a ping
pong game using a virtual reality headset during a study. The study produced quantitative
and qualitative results that led to the identification of valuable insights into the usability,
immersion, and gameplay experience of the VR housing design.
The quantitative data revealed significant changes in participants’ perceptions of immer­
sion, absorption, and effort exerted after engaging with the VR housing. There was a
heightened recognition of the significance of immersion, with participants expressing a
stronger sense of being absorbed in the game. Additionally, participants perceived an
increase in the level of effort exerted during gameplay. However, the game experience
did not significantly impact their perceived level of fatigue.
The qualitative findings shed light on the participants’ experiences, showcasing the poten­
tial of the VR housing design for stroke rehabilitation. Many participants expressed a high
level of enjoyment and requested additional playtime, emphasizing the fun and engag­
ing nature of the VR gaming experience. Challenges such as movement detection and
confidence levels were identified and provide insights for future design improvements.
The VR housing design presented in this thesis has the potential to contribute significantly
to stroke rehabilitation. By providing stroke patients with an immersive and engaging
gaming experience, the VR housing design may facilitate motor skill recovery, increase
motivation, and enhance the overall rehabilitation process. Moreover, the use of 3D print­
ing technology allows for the customization and personalization of the housing design to
meet the specific needs and preferences of individual patients.
In terms of future work, further development of the VR housing design is warranted. With
more time and resources, improvements can be made in various aspects, including using
higher­quality 3D printers for more accurate and efficient production, expanding the scope
to cover additional sports and activities, and conducting studies with stroke patients to
assess the effectiveness of the VR housing design in a clinical setting. The incorporation
of feedback from clinicians, such as interchangeable handles, different sizes and shapes,
and the addition of a cord for secure grip, can enhance the usability and adaptability of
the VR housing design.
