Abstract 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is amongst the most favorable biomass converting methods due to its 
robustness, simplicity and wide substrate applicability. It offers a solution for converting waste into 
energy, which reduces pollution and contributes to nutrient recirculation. Ammonia (free ammonia plus 
ammonium ion) has been found to be one of the most inhibitory compounds to AD and in particular to the 
methanogenic archaea. Its presence in anaerobic digesters is related with the degradation of substances 
such as proteins that are present in some of the residues that are most commonly used for biogas 
production. Bioaugmentation with acclimatized methanogens is one of the most novel approaches for 
relieving a digester from ammonia inhibition. In the present study, an investigation of the minimum 
biomass concentration (weight/working volume) necessary for bioaugmentation strategies that release 
manure fed CSTR reactors from an ammonia-induced inhibited steady-state was developed. Therefore, 
first a preliminary batch assessment of the dosage (bioaugmentation volume/working volume) was 
performed by testing different volume ratios in several batches. It was concluded that 2% was the optimal 
ratio (v/v) for both tested temperature conditions (mesophilic and thermophilic). Furthermore, four CSTR 
reactors were inhibited with 8 g NH4
+/L (mesophilic) and 4 g NH4
+/L (thermophilic) and different 
bioaugmentation strategies were tested by varying the biomass concentration in the bioaugmentation 
inoculum. It was concluded in the mesophilic case, that the critical biomass for a 61% improve in 
methane yield reached in 30 days was 1.27 mg/L. As for the thermophilic case, the results of the 
continuous assay were inconclusive.

 Introduction 
Chapter 1. 
Background 
1.1. 
Energy is fundamental for heat and electricity generation which fuel industry, transport and the 
modern way of living. Nowadays, energy derived from fossil fuels (coal, oil, petroleum and natural 
gas products) represents more than 80% of the world energy consumption (World Bank, 2014). 
However, fossil fuels are a non-renewable resource that is becoming more and more scarce and their 
combustion is extremely harmful to the environment (Shafiee & Topal, 2009). Global warming has 
been documented as one of the most serious issues that the Earth’s environment has been facing with 
over the past century. Studies have shown that the increasing temperatures are leading to unbalances 
in many of the Earth’s systems (IPCC, 2014). The main causes of the evident climate change are 
anthropological and the most significant one is the release of greenhouse gases (GHG), such as carbon 
dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide (Höök & Tang, 2013). According to the climate change report 
elaborated by IPCC (2014), 78% of the increase of the total GHG emissions from 1970 to 2010 was 
classified as carbon dioxide emissions derived from fossil fuels and industrial processes. 
This reality has led to the development of national and international policies that aim to prevent and 
reduce GHG emissions. The European Commission, for instance, has established several initiatives 
such as the 2020 climate and energy framework that targets the reduction of the GHG emissions by 
20%, the increase of energy consumption from renewable sources by 20% and the improvement of 
energy efficiency until the year 2020 (European Commision, 2017). Denmark, more specifically, has 
also self-proposed a series of even more ambitious goals since the national Climate Policy Plan 
intends to reduce 40% of the GHG emissions by 2020 (Danish Government, 2013). Considering the 
==PAGE:20==
2 
 
present framework, the necessity of developing the renewable energy sector is undeniable to reduce 
our environmental impact whilst maintaining economic and population growth.  
One of the most promising renewable energy sources in the world is biomass. Unlike other renewable 
sources, such as wind and sunlight, biomass can produce fuel diversity (gaseous, liquid and solid), is 
less likely to be intermittent and it can be easily stored (Holm-Nielsen, et al., 2009). Additionally, this 
resource includes virgin biomass (all vegetation and trees, including algae) and waste biomass (animal 
manure, agricultural, forestry, industrial and organic household waste and wastewater) (Acaroglu, et 
al., 2005); making it the fourth main energy resource worldwide (Mao, et al., 2015). Also, considering 
that residual resources require treatment in any case, using them as biomass couples energy generation 
with waste management (Appels, et al., 2011). There are several methods – physicochemical, 
thermochemical and biochemical - by which biomass can be converted into energy. Anaerobic 
digestion (AD) is one of the biochemical methods and is amongst the most favorable biomass 
converting methods due to its robustness, simplicity and wide substrate applicability (Appels, et al., 
2011).  
Anaerobic digestion 
1.2. 
AD is a biological process through which organic matter is decomposed in the absence of oxygen by 
the combined action of different microorganisms (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). It can be applied to a 
series of different substrates that range from dedicated energy crops, to biodegradable solid residues 
(agricultural waste, manure, slaughterhouse waste, organic fraction of household waste, etc.) and to 
wastewater (Zhang, et al., 2015). The products of this process are biogas (mixture of methane and 
carbon dioxide) that can be used for cogeneration, heat production, or as vehicle fuel; and a nutrient-
rich digestate widely used as a fertilizer in the agricultural industry (Evans, et al., 2014). Therefore, 
this technology offers a solution for converting waste into energy, which reduces pollution and 
contributes to the return of nutrients back to the soil.

Microbial processes 
1.2.1. 
AD can be divided into four main phases: hydrolysis, acidogenesis/fermentation, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis (Figure 1, Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). Each of these steps is performed in a 
sequential order and is carried out by the action of a different group of microorganisms (Melville, et 
al., 2014). 
 
Figure 1: Stepwise degradation of organics to methane and carbon dioxide  
Hydrolysis 
In the hydrolysis step, the complex molecules that compose the substrate (proteins, hydrocarbons and 
lipids) are converted into soluble monomers (amino-acids, sugars and long-chain fatty acids plus 
glycerol) by the action of primary fermenting bacteria (Evans, et al., 2014). These bacteria produce 
hydrolytic extracellular enzymes that catalyze the hydrolysis of the biopolymers in order to generate 
smaller molecules that can be transported through the cell wall (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). 
==PAGE:22==
4 
 
There are several primary fermenting bacteria involved in this step, since each biopolymer requires 
specific enzymes. The hydrolysis of lipids originates both long-chain fatty acids and glycerol by the 
action lipases produced by the lipolytic bacteria (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). The hydrolysis of this 
biopolymer depends mainly on the size of the molecules, on the pH and on the surface tension of the 
surrounding media (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). In the case of proteins, the proteolytic bacteria produce 
proteases able to catalyze their hydrolysis into amino-acids (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). The 
degradation of proteins depends on their structure, since globular proteins are highly biodegradable 
and fibrous are relatively more complicated (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). In any case, generally this 
biopolymer is rapidly hydrolyzed (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). The cellulytic and xylanolytic 
bacteria are responsible for the secretion of cellulases and xylases, respectively; these enzymes 
catalyze the breakdown of carbohydrates into simple sugars (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
Carbohydrates can be distinguished into two different groups according to their degradability: simple 
or recalcitrant (Evans, et al., 2014). The simple carbohydrates, such as sugars and starch, are easily 
hydrolyzed. However, the lignocellulosic material (mixture of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin) 
that composes the cell wall of plants is extremely hard to hydrolyze (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011) 
and may require additional pretreatment. 
Acidogenesis/Fermentation 
In this step, some of the products of hydrolysis (sugars and amino acids) are converted into volatile 
fatty acids (VFA), alcohols, carbon dioxide and hydrogen (Melville, et al., 2014). The acidogenesis 
can be carried out through different mechanisms depending on the substrate and on the conditions of 
the surrounding environment. Therefore, this process has several intermediates and end-products. 
Nevertheless, the acidogenesis is a fast process (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). The long-chain fatty 
acids that result from the previous step are not converted through fermentation; instead these 
molecules are directly oxidized during the acetogenesis stage (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
The fermentation of monosaccharides can be performed following two different pathways: the Acetyl-
CoA or the Emben-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP). The first generates a series of different products, such as 
acetate, butyrate and ethanol, and is generally electron producing (net). The latter (EMP), that is 
==PAGE:23==
5 
 
normally electron consuming and originates solely C3 products (propionate and lactate) is very 
uncommon unless the reactor is overloaded (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). In the case of electrons being 
produced during overall reaction, the extra electrons will be used for hydrogen or formate production 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011).  
Unlike the monosaccharides, the amino acids degrade in pairs (oxidation/reduction) in a reaction 
denominated the Strickland reaction. The degradation of amino acids during the acidogenesis stage 
leads to the release of ammonium molecules (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
During this process, if the reactor is operating under stable conditions, the substrate is directly 
converted to acetate and hydrogen, instead of generating more reduced intermediates. On the other 
hand, if the system is out of balance and there is a surplus of hydrogen and/or acetate, or extreme pH 
levels, the fermentation of monosaccharides will lead to the formation of less oxidized products 
(propionate, butyrate and ethanol) (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011). 
Acetogenesis 
The acetogenesis step is associated with two different families of bacteria: the obligatory hydrogen-
producing acetogens (OHPA) and the homoacetogens (Massi, 2012). In this phase, acetate is 
synthetized either by the reduction of carbon dioxide (acetyl-CoA pathway) or by the oxidation of 
organic acids (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). 
The homoacetogens use the acetyl-CoA pathway to produce acetate from the reduction of carbon 
dioxide (Equation 1, Angelidaki, et al., 2011).

Consequently, these bacteria only have sufficient energy for metabolism if the concentration of the 
products is kept low. Thus, a dependence of the OPHA on methanogens or other hydrogen consuming 
microorganisms is created for product removal. This syntrophic relationship is denominated 
interspecies hydrogen transfer (IHT) (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). As a result of this syntrophic

facilitates the degradation of long-chain fatty acids that is otherwise impossible for acetogens and 
methanogens (Angelidaki, et al., 2011). 
Methanogenesis 
In the final step of the AD, water, carbon dioxide and methane are formed (Evans, et al., 2014). This 
process can be performed mainly through the degradation of acetate and by two different 
mechanisms: the acetoclastic methanogenesis and the syntrophic acetate oxidation (SAO) coupled 
with hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis (Fotidis, et al., 2013). There are several orders of 
methanogens that have been recognized: Methanobacteriales, Methanococcales, Methanosarcinales, 
Methanopyrales, Methanomicrobiales and Methanocellales (Angelidaki, et al., 2011).

Operational parameters 
1.2.2. 
There are several parameters that can greatly influence the efficiency of AD. In order to maintain 
process stability and maximize the production yield it is important the control and adjust the following 
key process variables: feedstock characteristics, pH, temperature, organic loading rate (OLR), 
retention time, carbon to nitrogen ratio (C/N) and mixing. In the following section, these parameters 
are explained in further detail. 
Feedstock 
Biogas is composed mainly by methane and carbon dioxide. In the total production of an anaerobic 
digester, the methane portion is influenced by the degradability of the feedstock as well as its 
oxidation state (Angelidaki, et al., 2011); therefore, the characteristics of the substrate are highly 
significant in this process. The potential of each substrate component can be predicted theoretically 
through stoichiometry. Per gram of volatile solids (VS), carbohydrates ((C6H10O5)n) can produce up to 
==PAGE:26==
8 
 
415 NmL CH4, proteins (C5H7NO2) up to 496 NmL CH4 and lipids (C57H104O6) can produce a 
maximum of 1014 NmL CH4 (Angelidaki, et al., 2011).  
Originally, AD was used mostly for the processing of manure and of sewage sludge (Weiland, 2010). 
Nowadays, several other substrates are also considered, such as the organic fraction of municipal solid 
waste, waste oils and animal fats derived from food processing industries (slaughterhouses, dairy 
farms, etc.), energy crops and agricultural waste (Appels, et al., 2011).  
Manure is a very common substrate used in AD due to its ready availability, its abundance and the 
fact that when left on the fields uncontrolled, it is the cause of several environmental problems 
(Appels, et al., 2011). Unattended animal manure may lead to methane emissions to the atmosphere, 
odor issues, infestations, ground and surface water contamination and pathogen release (Sakar, et al., 
2009). Additionally, manure is known to contain recalcitrant matter which is difficult to degrade in 
AD (Angelidaki, et al., 2000). The methane potential of manure is influenced by the species and 
growth stage of the animals, type of bedding, feed and the degradation processes that may occur 
during storage (Ward, et al., 2008).  
pH 
Numerous parameters in AD are influenced by the pH level: the relative amount of microbial species, 
the VFA composition, the hydrolysis rate constant, the amount of free ammonia nitrogen (FAN) in the 
system and the growth rate of the microorganisms, as well as their activity (Mao, et al., 2015). The 
optimal pH in anaerobic digestion is considered to be within 6.5 and 7.5 (Liu, et al., 2008). However, 
the different microbial groups have different optimal pH ranges. In the case of the methanogenic 
archaea, the preferable pH level should be between 6.5 and 8.2, given that their growth rate is 
extremely reduced at pH lower than 6.6 and that their activity is decreased at pH level outside the 
described optimal range (Mao, et al., 2015). As for the acidogens, the ideal pH range is between 5.5 
and 6.5 (Mao, et al., 2015). Nevertheless, the hydrolysis of total solids (TS) and VS demonstrated to 
be maintained at high extents both for pH 7 and 8 (Dinamarca, et al., 2003).  
==PAGE:27==

Temperature 
There are several temperature ranges under which the microbial processes associated with AD can be 
performed: psychrophilic (< 20 °C), mesophilic (20-40 °C), thermophilic (45-60 °C) and 
hyperthermophilic (> 60 °C) (El-Mashad, et al., 2004). Nonetheless, the most commonly used 
conditions are mesophilic and thermophilic (Massi, 2012).  
The reactors operating under thermophilic temperatures have the advantage over the mesophilic since 
the reaction rates are faster (Mao, et al., 2015). Therefore, the thermophilic reactors can operate under 
reduced hydraulic retention times (HRT) and, thus smaller sized reactors are necessary for digesting 
the same volume of substrate (Massi, 2012). Furthermore, the probability of successful elimination of 
pathogens is also higher compared to systems with lower temperatures (Melville, et al., 2014). 
Nevertheless, thermophilic reactors have tendency for acidification that can cause inhibition in the 
system. Also, the thermophilic microorganisms are extremely sensitive to various other operational 
parameters (pH, organic loading rate and substrate characteristics) (Mao, et al., 2015) and these 
microbial communities are rarer (Evans, et al., 2014). In the specific case of Denmark, most of the 
centralized biogas plants are currently operating under thermophilic conditions (Seadi, 2000). 
Organic loading rate 
The OLR is the rate at which organic matter is fed into a continuously fed reactor. It is usually 
expressed per day and in terms of volatile solids (VS) for solid wastes and in terms of chemical 
oxygen demand or biological oxygen demand for wastewater per reactor working volume (Wang, et 
al., 2009). The OLR is positively correlated with the methane production of AD until a certain degree. 
However, an overloading of the system with organic matter may occur, as the high OLR will lead to 
increased hydrolysis and acidogenesis that the methanogenic archaea is enable to match. This 
situation creates accumulation of VFA followed by an irreversible acidification of the system (Mao, et 
al., 2015).

Retention time 
In order to achieve sufficient organic matter degradation, the microorganisms present in the AD must 
be in contact with the substrate for a certain amount of time – retention time (Melville, et al., 2014). 
This parameter is, therefore, dependent of the microbial growth rate that can vary according to the 
feedstock characteristics, the temperature of the reactor and the OLR (Mao, et al., 2015). There are 
two retention times to consider: the solids retention time (SRT) and the HRT. The first describes the 
average amount of time solids (microbial biomass) reside in the system, whilst the latter is related 
with the average time liquid sludge remains in the digester (Melville, et al., 2014). The HRT is 
calculated based on the ratio between the working volume of the reactor and the influent flow rate. 
This parameter can drastically influence the performance of the system given that if it is shorter than 
ideal will result on VFA accumulation and if it is longer will lead to suboptimal utilization of the 
reactor (Mao, et al., 2015). The optimal HRT in mesophilic conditions is considered to be between 30 
and 60 days, while for high rate reactors, like thermophilic, it is usual to use an HRT of 15 days 
(Wang, et al., 2009). 
Carbon to nitrogen ratio 
The C/N translates the nutrient availability in an anaerobic digester (Mao, et al., 2015). If this 
proportion is low, it means that there will be nitrogen in excess which will accumulate as ammonia 
and consequently result in inhibition of methanogens (Evans, et al., 2014). On the other hand, if this 
ratio is exceptionally high, there will not be sufficient nitrogen to maintain cell biomass and the effect 
will be lower methane production. The ideal C/N ratio is considered to be within 20 and 30 (Mao, et 
al., 2015). 
Mixing 
Mechanical mixing is essential for maintaining substrate and inoculum homogeneity, to maximize the 
contact of the microorganisms with undigested substrate, to eliminate specific increased concentration 
or temperature areas and to help the dispersion of byproducts (Melville, et al., 2014). However, 
==PAGE:29==
11 
 
mixing should be kept below certain speed in order not to disrupt microbial communities that rely on 
small distances to maintain essential syntrophic relationships (Evans, et al., 2014). 
Reactor configurations 
1.2.3. 
There are several parameters that influence the design of an anaerobic digester, such as: HRT, mixing, 
temperature, number of tanks, feedstock digestibility (Nizami & Murphy, 2010). The reactor 
configurations that are currently used can be divided into three categories: batch digesters, one-stage 
continuous digesters and two-stage (or more) continuous reactors in which the different AD processes 
are separated (Ward, et al., 2008). The two designs that were used throughout the experimental 
procedure of the present thesis are the continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) and the batch reactor. 
Batch reactors 
The batch reactor is a closed system in which chemical reactions occur and during this process, no 
material is added or removed. These reactors are the most commonly used systems in research 
considering their practicality; however, this configuration is considered outdated for industrial 
purposes (Mann, 2009). The batch reactor consists of a sealed container composed mainly by 
inoculum, organic substrate and distilled water. These batches are usually flushed with nitrogen gas in 
order to guarantee anaerobic conditions and kept in incubators at constant temperature. It can be used 
for microorganism acclimatization to extreme conditions and inoculum cultivation for 
bioaugmentation strategies. 
CSTR 
The CSTR reactor is designed based on two assumptions: the system is in steady-state and inside the 
reactor the parameters (temperature and concentration) are uniform due to the continuous or 
periodical mixing (Mann, 2009). It is usually connected to an influent, to an effluent and to a biogas 
exhaust. The simplicity of this configuration and uniformity of system conditions makes the CSTR the 
most commonly used reactor for research purposes. Also, the full-scale digester configurations always 
share similar fundamental design characteristics with the CSTR reactor; therefore, it is suitable for 
most preliminary evaluations (Usack, et al., 2012).

Inhibitors 
1.2.4. 
An extensive variety of compounds has been reported to be inhibitory to the AD process. These 
substances are characterized by being the cause of unbalances in the microbial community, or by 
inhibiting the growth of the microbes (Chen, et al., 2008). An inhibited state is normally associated 
with a decrease in the methane yield, leading it to perform under the expected theoretical yield value 
(Chen, et al., 2008). In this section a succinct explanation of the main inhibitory compounds for the 
AD is given. Nevertheless, the most relevant inhibitor for the present study is ammonia, which is 
described in detail in a separate segment. 
Ammonia 
The origin of this chemical in anaerobic digesters, apart from already existing in the AD feedstock 
(Yenigün & Demirel, 2013), is related with the degradation of substances such as urea and proteins 
that are characterized by having nitrogen in their molecular structure (Chen, et al., 2008). Some of the 
residues that are most commonly used for biogas production, such as the organic fraction of municipal 
solid waste and slaughterhouse waste, are protein-rich (Yenigün & Demirel, 2013). Hence, AD 
inhibition due to high total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) levels (free ammonia plus ammonium ion) is 
particularly common (Angelidaki & Ahring, 1992).The ammonia form that is the main cause of 
inhibition in AD is the FAN. The relative amount of this molecule is amplified by increasing the TAN 
concentration in the system, by increasing the pH and/or by increasing the temperature (Fotidis, et al., 
2014d). The inhibition induced by ammonia in non-acclimatized microorganisms has been evidenced 
to occur when the TAN concentration is above 3 g NH4
+-N/L or above 0.15 g NH3-N/L in the case of 
the FAN concentration, independently of the experimental conditions (Chen, et al., 2008). 
These compounds affect the balance between acidogens and methanogens, in which the stability of 
AD relies upon (Rajagopal, et al., 2013). The presence of free ammonia impacts the shift between the 
two methanogenesis mechanisms due to the fact that the most affected archaea are the acetoclastic 
methanogens (Westerholm, et al., 2011) and that the hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis is more robust 
(Fotidis, et al., 2014b). Considering that the methane formed through the acetoclastic pathway 
==PAGE:31==
13 
 
accounts for most of the total methane production (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011), the inhibition 
caused by ammonia is associated with a drop in the methane production rate. Also, given that the 
methanogens fail to convert the same amount of VFAs as before, an increase of their concentration is 
verified (Rajagopal, et al., 2013). The result of this acid accumulation consequently leads to a drop in 
pH, which in turn derives to a decrease of the FAN concentration and a relief of its inhibitory effect 
(Angelidaki, et al., 1993). The combination of these effects usually stabilizes the AD at a determined 
pH and VFA concentration (Angelidaki, et al., 1993). In this situation the reactor is considered to be 
operating under “inhibited steady-state” (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008). 
Currently, the potential of full-scale anaerobic digesters of biogas plants is highly affected by the 
presence of ammonia. It has been reported that numerous plants operate up to 30% below their 
potential CH4 yield because of the previously described “inhibited steady-state” (Fotidis, et al., 
2014d). This scenario represents a considerable efficiency reduction and results on substantial 
economic losses for the biogas suppliers. 
Heavy metals 
Heavy metals are characterized by not being biodegradable, which facilitates their accumulation 
(Chen, et al., 2008). The most commonly found heavy metals in wastewaters and municipal sludge are 
copper, zinc, lead, mercury, chromium, cadmium, iron, nickel, cobalt and molybdenum (Chen, et al., 
2014). The toxicity of these compounds affects greatly different microbial groups, such as acetogens, 
acidogens and methanogens (Chen, et al., 2014). Heavy metals contribute to the disruption of 
enzymes by binding to different functional groups of protein molecules or by replacing naturally 
occurring metals in enzyme prosthetic groups (Chen, et al., 2008). 
Light metal ions 
These substances, when found in high concentrations, contribute to the increase of the osmotic 
pressure in bacterial cells, causing them to eventually dehydrate (Chen, et al., 2008). Light metals 
such as sodium, potassium, calcium and magnesium are commonly originated from the deterioration 
of organic matter, or might be added for pH adjustment (Chen, et al., 2008). 
==PAGE:32==
14 
 
Organic compounds 
Organic compounds that are characterized by having a low tendency to solubilize in water or that can 
likely be adsorbed to the surfaces of flocs can easily accumulate in anaerobic digesters (Johnson & 
Young, 1983). Several organic compounds have been found to inhibit the anaerobic digestion process, 
such as chlorophenols, halogenated aliphatics and long-chain fatty acids (Chen, et al., 2014). The 
inhibitory effect of these toxicants varies according to different factors, such as toxicant and biomass 
concentration, exposure time, cell age, acclimation and temperature (Chen, et al., 2008). 
Sulfide 
Sulfate is a compound that is commonly found in wastewaters originating from the industrial sector 
(Chen, et al., 2008). When this compound is found in anaerobic digesters it is reduced to sulfide by 
sulfate reducing bacteria (SBR) (Koster, et al., 1986). This product is responsible for two different 
inhibitory effects. The first is direct inhibition due to its toxicity towards several bacteria groups. The 
second is caused by the competition of the SBR for organic and inorganic substrates for sulfate 
reduction, leading to a reduction in the methane production (Chen, et al., 2008) 
Bioaugmentation 
1.2.5. 
Bioaugmentation is based on the insertion of specific microbes or of an enriched consortium in a 
microbial community to amend targeted process operations (Westerholm, et al., 2012). The microbes 
used in this technique are commonly obtained through adaptation to extreme conditions, selection and 
mutation methods (Stephenson & Stephenson, 1992). Nevertheless, despite the fact that the 
microorganisms used for bioaugmentation are supposed to resist harsher conditions, they may still fail 
to thrive in a bioreactor. The following reasons have been proven to cause bioaugmentation to fail: 
competition with the already established microbiome, lack of acclimatization, presence of 
bacteriophages, unsuited microorganisms for the system’s conditions, temporary effect caused by the 
eventual washout of the enhanced culture and insufficient volume (Herrero & Stuckey, 2015, El 
Fantroussi & Agathos, 2005 and Nielsen, et al., 2007). 
==PAGE:33==
15 
 
This method has been applied to several different process phases of AD. Some studies used 
bioaugmentation of hydrolytic bacteria in a pretreatment step in order to increase the methane yield of 
lignocellulosic material (Nielsen, et al., 2007; Lü, et al., 2013; Cater, et al., 2015; Zhang, et al., 2015). 
Bioaugmentation with hydrogen producing bacteria has also been used to increase methane 
production by regulating the hydrogen concentration in the anaerobic digester (Ács, et al., 2015). 
Others use this method to remove or relieve inhibition generated by the presence of toxicants (Tale, et 
al., 2015; Fotidis, et al., 2014d).  
The bioaugmentation method represents one of the most novel approaches developed for releasing 
anaerobic digesters from ammonia induced “inhibited steady-states”. A successful bioaugmentation of 
ammonia-tolerant hydrogenotrophic methanogens in a mesophilic CSTR with manure as feedstock 
has been reported (Fotidis, et al., 2014d). In this experiment, a 31.3% increase in the methane 
production yield was reported by inserting a final bioaugmentation ratio of 11% of the working 
volume of the reactor.

 Aim and objectives 
Chapter 2. 
The AD process can become inhibited by several compounds, however, it has been found that the 
unionized free ammonia (NH3) and the ammonium ion (NH4
+) are the most significant inhibitors. 
Therefore, numerous methods have been developed with the goal of addressing this issue. Performing 
bioaugmentation of ammonia-tolerant methanogens is one of the most novel approaches to this issue. 
Therefore, there are still several parameters that influence the success of this method or that limit its 
application on full-scale digesters that have not been studied.  
Critical biomass 
2.1. 
The critical biomass is the minimum amount of ammonia-tolerant methanogens (in terms of biomass 
weight over working volume of reactor) that can reestablish an inhibited reactor to its full potential. This 
parameter is extremely significant given that if the amount introduced is insufficient, the biomass will be 
washed out before becoming established in the system. On the other hand, if the amount is too high there 
is the possibility of removing too many of the other essential microorganisms in the system and/or of 
wasting bioaugmentation inocula for obtaining the same improvement. Furthermore, bioaugmentation 
would be a more attractive approach, if the benefit of enhancing the methane production yield would 
cover the expense of the necessary amount of acclimatized microorganisms.  
The critical biomass concept is considered to be influenced by three different parameters: inoculum 
dosage (bioaugmentation volume/working volume), biomass concentration in inoculum and frequency of 
dosage. 
==PAGE:36==
18 
 
Aim of the study 
2.2. 
The main aim of this MSc. thesis was to determine the critical biomass for recovering the methane 
production of a lab-scale CSTR reactor after inhibiting it with extreme ammonia concentrations. The 
applied bioaugmentation strategies focused on two of the parameters – dosage and concentration - while 
fixing the third – frequency. Considering that in the current industrial context, most biogas plants are 
working either under mesophilic or thermophilic temperatures; both cases were investigated. 
Furthermore, a change in temperature conditions affects several parameters that may have impact on the 
success of the bioaugmentation. Thus, the experiment was developed also with the objective of specifying 
the critical biomass for both the mesophilic and the thermophilic case. 
The present study was divided into two distinct stages. The first was a preliminary dosage batch 
assessment focused on the determination of the minimum bioaugmentation dosage. The objective of this 
part was to establish a result-based starting point for the design of the bioaugmentation strategies applied 
in the following stage. The second and main experiment was based on the performance of the mesophilic 
and thermophilic CSTR reactors inhibited by extreme ammonia concentrations. This stage focused on the 
variation of the biomass concentration, while maintaining the dosage obtained from the preliminary 
assessment constant.

Before each measurement, calibration standards were introduced in the GC with at least three different 
methane/carbon dioxide ratios.

Statistical analysis 
3.7.5. 
All statistical analysis were performed using MS Excel 2010. All batch and VFA measurements were 
done with a minimum of three replicates. Statistical outlier determination was performed for all the 
measurements to exclude the results significantly (p = 0.05) different from the other replicates. The test 
used for this purpose was the Grubbs’ test (or extreme studentized deviate method) (Grubbs, 1969). The 
significance of the difference between the µmax of the different inocula was analyzed with a regression 
slope student’s t-test (p = 0.05) (Paternoster, et al., 1998). To compare different steady-states in the 
CSTR, a student’s t-test for testing the mean values of samples with different variances was performed (p 
= 0.05).

 Conclusions 
Chapter 5. 
The present study was focused on the determination of the critical biomass for relieving CSTR reactor 
from an inhibited state caused by the presence of extreme ammonia concentrations. Overall, the main 
conclusions of this project are as follows: 
 
The 2% (bioaugmentation volume/working volume) dosage was the most successful dosage 
tested in the preliminary batch experiments in both thermophilic and mesophilic conditions. The 
maximum expected methane production was reached in both cases. 
 
61% increase in relative methane production was reached 30 days after the bioaugmentation was 
performed in mesophilic conditions. It can be concluded that the bioaugmentation dosage of 2% 
(bioaugmentation volume/working volume) determined in the preliminary batch assay is 
sufficient for successful bioaugmentation strategies. The critical biomass is 1.27 mg biomass/L. 
 
Bioaugmentation strategy B in mesophilic conditions demonstrates to reduce the reaction time 
until an improvement in relative methane yield is visible from 21 to 15 days. 
 
The maximum specific growth of the methanogenic community after bioaugmentation strategy A 
in mesophilic conditions improved but remained below the value preceding the inhibition. 
 
In thermophilic conditions, the bioaugmentation strategy A was not sufficient for the relieve of 
the inhibited steady-state with a TAN concentration of 4 g NH4
+/L. The critical biomass in this 
case is higher than 1.43 mg/L.

 Future perspectives 
Chapter 6. 
The present study established a base for additional critical biomass and bioaugmentation studies. Further 
analysis are recommended to support the findings and to decrease the uncertainties of the effect of the 
bioaugmentation. Additionally, some suggestions are given for further experimental development and 
approximation to reality. 
 
After this study, it is still unclear what is the critical biomass for the thermophilic conditions. 
Thus, the continuation of the present experiments with the CSTR reactors is necessary. 
Additionally, it would be advantageous to test other parameter combinations by either increasing 
the volume or the dosage frequency. Also, using an acclimatized inoculum of a higher generation 
could be tested. 
 
To complete the results analysis, further studies on the composition of the microbial communities 
in the system should be developed after identifying the key changes in the past performance of all 
the CSTR reactors. 
 
The mesophilic critical biomass has been defined in the present thesis for CSTR reactors fed with 
manure. However, further studies to approximate the experiment to a full-scale anaerobic digester 
are required in order to test the obtained value. Therefore, it would be advantageous to change 
some parameter, such as substrate and/or reactor dimension.
