Abstract
The purpose of this project is to design and develop a software solution that improves implementation of new
regulatory initiatives. The improvement comes in two ways: through automated processes that reduces amount of
generic tasks and functionality that improves the maintenance of the entire portfolio of regulatory initiatives.
To achieve this the design science methodology and software engineering will be used to craft an artifact, that
delivers on those goals.
Danske Bank UK serves as a case for the project and provides material on how Regulatory Initiatives are currently
being implemented and what processes they currently have in place to do so. The material and data provided will
shape the design of the solution and reveal how the developed software can enhance the daily operation in Danske
Bank UK.
The evaluation reveals three major beneﬁts of using such a software system. Firstly it improves the chances of
avoiding non-compliance during implementation of Regulatory Initiatives. Secondly it increases eﬃciency by reduc-
ing the amount of workhours spend on generic and trivial tasks. Thirdly the system provide better management of
the entire portfolio of Regulatory Initiatives leading to overall better maintenance for Danske Bank UK.
It is concluded that these beneﬁts combined, that are derived directly from the software solution I have created,
provide signiﬁcant value for any company seeking to improve their handling of Regulatory Initiatives.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Each year regional and national legislators formulate new regulatory material that eventually turn into regulatory
initiatives. Regulatory initiatives cover all types of regulation. A regulatory Initiative can have many forms ranging
from the most signiﬁcant called directives to the least signiﬁcant such as a Q&A. Most readers of this paper will
remember the regulatory initiative General Data Protection Act (GDPR) [5] which was created by the European
Parliament. Often European authorities will create an act or directive for the entire region which is then adopted
by national authorities to translate the intends of the regional regulation into regulation that can be applied in a
speciﬁc country and under it’s law.
The scope and ramiﬁcations of these regulatory initiatives vary a lot, some only impact a few companies while
others like GDPR aﬀect most companies signiﬁcantly. Additionally some larger Directives or Acts like GDPR often
come as several regulatory initiatives in various formats. When a company is aﬀected by a regulatory initiative it
has to change it’s processes and procedures to stay compliant with the law - this is known as the implementation
of a regulatory initiative.
The topic of how to handle regulation eﬃciently has become more important due to the fact that the amount of
new regulation being passed through legislators are increasing in these years. This puts a large ﬁnancial burden
on larger companies as the cost of implementing the new regulatory initiatives usually scales with the size of the
company.
The consequence of not implementing all regulatory initiatives is that the company will not be compliant with the
Law and thus risk severe penalties like ﬁnes, loss of reputation or sanctions against it’s markets and operation that
will reduce proﬁts long term. Additionally the increase in new regulatory initiatives means that the company has
to keep track of increasingly more deadlines, respond to hearings and consultations and invest more in staﬀwith a
Legal educational background.
One such company that is feeling the burden of increased ﬁnancial regulation is Danske Bank which will be used
as case for the thesis. In particular I will focus on a branch of Danske Bank known as Danske Bank UK. This
branch was previously a separate company named Northern Bank before being acquired by Danske Bank in 2004 to
expand it’s operation into the UK and Northern Ireland. Regulatory authorities in the UK has been known to be
years ahead of it’s fellow european countries when it comes to protocols and penalties which make Northern Bank
a prime case for the topics I want to investigate through this thesis.
Regulatory Initiatives is a term that cover all new regulation from relevant legislators, however not all of them
aﬀect Danske Bank. Take for example an act that impose new restrictions on the production of chocolate - while
it might have a huge impact for certain companies it does not aﬀect Danske Bank.
One can quickly get the idea that new regulation is clearly divided into categories and that a company automatically
only get the one or two categories relevant for them however this is far from the reality. New regulation form almost
a spider web where regulatory themes are intertwined and overlap. Cybersecurity heavily aﬀect Danske Bank even
though it is not a tech company and the same goes for Tax regulation even though the company has nothing to do
with public institutions handling taxes. To make things more complicated a regulatory initiative can be phrased in

a way that eﬀectively involves several themes.
To identify which regulatory initiatives aﬀect Danske Bank a manual assessment is required. Generally an Attorney-
At-Law or a person of similar background is assigned and reads through the entire text.
In Danske Bank UK the assessment is handled by compliance oﬃcers through a team called Risk Management.
They refer to themselves as the second-line of defense because they usually distribute responsibility of individual
regulatory initiatives to employees from other teams.
Danske Bank UK is divided into 10 so called Business Units:
Figure 1.0.1: An overview of Danske Bank UK
With the current setup the Risk Management team is responsible for discovering all incoming regulatory initiatives
they deem will impact Danske Bank UK. This process is called the screening phase and if a regulatory initiative is
not discovered the sole responsibility falls back on the Risk Management team.
The screening phase is not as simple as it might seem since initiatives come from many diﬀerent sources and is
collected diﬀerently depending on how the legislator operates. Some legislators publish their Initiatives on a website
where a compliance oﬃcer can fetch the data while in other instances the initiatives are send directly to mailboxes
in Northern Bank. A legislator is an institution capable of passing new regulation and includes the likes of the
Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) that handle local ﬁnancial legislation in the UK and the European Commission
that handle regulation on a regional level.
Once the regulatory initiatives has been discovered they will be distributed to the business unit that will most
likely be aﬀected the most by that particular initiative. This phase is the assignment phase where ownership for
each regulatory initiative is being established.
With the ownership in place this is where we get to the core regulatory process where the implementation has to
take place. This involves several processes like:

==PAGE:9==
• Perform a Conduct Risk Assessment.
• Perform an Impact Assessment.
• Initialize an Implementation Project.
A conduct risk assessment is the process of analyzing the consequences of non-compliance, meaning that if Danske
Bank UK fails to implement the initiative they know in advance what scale the penalties will be. Such penalties
include ﬁnes, restrictions in operations etc.
Impact Assessment is an assessment where the impact is rated. A higher impact rating means more resources and
often involves multiple units of responsible persons while smaller impact ratings lead to lower priority.
Once the two main assessments are out of the way implementation can begin. This is where Danske Bank UK
gather resources, create a project an start adapting their business, IT-systems and practices to the rules outlined
in the speciﬁc regulatory initiative.
So to summarize a bunch of regulatory initiatives pass through Danske Bank UK regularly. Some aﬀect the com-
pany and require implementation leading to several implementation projects.
This leads us back to the Risk Management team and their role because on top of all these implementation projects
they handle the governance and track each regulatory initiative from the start (screening) to the end (implementa-
tion complete).
This is where the term second-line of defense come into play as they perform a huge amount of reporting which is
conducted on a monthly basis. This is done so that the Risk Management team and upper management in Danske
Bank UK can intervene and act proactively should an implementation project fail to deliver the necessary changes
in due time.
To mitigate the increase burden of regulatory Initiatives I will during this project explore how Computer Science can
be applied to build a software Solution that streamline the regulatory processes in Danske Bank UK and improve
the overall handling of new regulation.

2.1
Problem Definition
The core idea and theory that this project seek to address is that the creation of a software solution carefully crafted
to improve handling of new regulation will provide signiﬁcant value to any company aﬀected by regulation.
To achieve this I have set two primary goals for the project:
1. Increase eﬃciency through automation of regulatory processes so that employees in Danske Bank UK have to
spend less time on trivial and generic tasks.
2. Create a platform for the Regulatory Portfolio that streamlines the overall handling of new regulation through
transparency and tools that improve reporting and control.
To create such a software solution I will do research on similar products available on the market today. I will
also investigate the current trends in the legal industry to see if there is an appetite for software systems like this.
Finally I will analyze the case of Danske Bank UK and build a system based on their daily operation and current
setup of regulatory processes.
The ﬁrst goal requires a deep understanding of the processes used to handle regulation today. Afterwards each
process has to be analyzed and any potential beneﬁt of automation has to be identiﬁed.
This will enable the
development of a software solution that can reduce manual tasks through automation. In theory such a software
solution should be able to increase the eﬃciency of implementing new regulation into Danske Bank.
The second goal of the problem is all about controlling and maintaining the new regulation. To make sure that all
stakeholders involved in implementing new regulation make progress and stay compliant according to deadlines the
software solution will have to provide statistics and reports. The system will have to provide a solid platform that
uniﬁes all regulatory processes.
In theory a system that delivers on those two goals will provide signiﬁcant value to Danske Bank UK and if
that is the case it should demonstrate that there is room for Software Tools in all companies seeking to improve
handling of new regulation.

2.2
Scope
The primary source for this project will be the Risk Management Team and Danske Bank UK. Material from them
will be supplemented with research on state of the art, similar software systems etc. To keep things simple and
reasonable within the amount of time available I will build the solution as an internal tool meant to be deployed
inside a walled oﬀcompany LAN network. This means that without contact to the Web of third party software I
will have to worry less about security and risks as opposed to common IT systems.
One topic that would be applicable to a software solution aiming to solve the problem outlined in the previous
section is Artiﬁcial Intelligence. The problem is that for AI to enhance anything it needs a foundation and right
now there are only a simple database system setup in Danske Bank UK. Then there is the scope creating an AI for
regulatory initiatives would easily turn into separate theses. Therefore the ﬁrst constraint for this project will be
to ignore the use of AI and heuristics and instead to focus on getting the core regulatory management framework
up and running.
The second constraint is to create a system for internal use only. This entails than any servers and database clusters
will be walled oﬀagainst the outside world and only be accessible from the internal network within Danske Bank.
This is again to narrow down the scope as security measures would turn into a huge topic if a software solution
were to be build for public access. This constraint also removes the topic of working together with legislators about
creating a broader system that connects the legislators directly with the companies aﬀected even though such a
global system would be highly beneﬁcial for all parties.
The third constraint is to ignore any machine learning algorithms that would track user patterns in the system,
this could for example be a feature that tracked search histories of all users and could come up with suggestions.
This certainly adds value to any software system however it is deemed nice-to-have and not mandatory in order to
solve the two goals I highlighted earlier.
Due to the limit of time and the conﬁdentiality of Danske Bank material and processes the only company used for
evaluation will be the feedback received from the Risk Management Team in the UK. They will be the only ones
allowed to test the system, however multiple persons will be involved to give a better foundation for the evaluation
chapter in the end of this report.

Chapter 3
Methodology
This report will combine three diﬀerent methodologies to solve the problem. For research purposes I will apply
design science to create an artifact that solves the problem. To create this artifact I will be applying the agile
development method.
Finally I will evaluate the created artifact through the use of empirical research on the
persons working within the problem environment.
3.1
Research Method
The problem deﬁned in the previous chapter is best handled as a design problem. Natural and social science is
problem oriented while design science is solution oriented which makes design science the ideal methodology in this
case.
Design science is all about design and investigation of artifacts in context [6]. Here the context is the handling of
new regulatory initiatives in a ﬁnancial institution. The artifact in question is a software solution that improves
the implementation and reporting of the regulatory initiatives.
Figure 3.1.1: A Diagram of the Design Science Cycle
Design science diﬀers from professional design[6] where you take best practices and a deﬁned context within a
known problem environment and build an artifact on top of that knowledge.
Design science involves a ton of
research on best practices within the problem context but diﬀers greatly in nature because the goal here is to
solve something that has not been solved before. Professional design is based on existing knowledge to solve a
problem that has already been solved or to improve the existing solution. Design science on the other hand uses
existing knowledge to collect existing knowledge and design an artifact that expands the knowledge in the form of
a scientiﬁc contribution. In the State of The Art chapter I will be going through existing knowledge within the
problem environment and outline similar artifacts to the one I want to design and create. Later in the report I
Page 11 of 82
==PAGE:13==
will follow up on the state of the art chapter and conclude what scientiﬁc contribution the created artifact will entail.
Talking of artifacts, in this problem context the artifact will be a software solution. But to keep within the domain
of the design science methodology I will simply use the term artifact.
The ﬁrst step of design science is to translate the project goals into a design problem.
Improve the implementation and reporting of regulatory initiatives
By using design science to create an artifact
Such that the processes of implementation and reporting is automated
In order to improve the overall handling of regulatory initiatives
Looking at the problem one could ask why Danske Bank UK would not just apply professional science, contact
suppliers of similar solutions and simply buy an existing artifact to solve this problem. That strategy is certainly
viable but would force Danske Bank UK to alter their internal processes and procedures to accommodate whatever
product or artifact they were to ﬁnd on the market. The goal of this project is to design an artifact based on their
existing procedures and thereby shed light on whether or not it is possible to take their current setup and simply
design an artifact around it.
First challenge is to understand the Environment. Here the environment is the handling of regulatory initiatives
and speciﬁcally how the Risk Management team in Danske Bank UK tackles them. For this I travelled to Danske
Bank UK in early November 2018 to sit down with members of the team and go through all of their processes.
They walked me through each of their process templates, their reporting and everything in between when it comes
to regulatory initiatives. This gave me hands-on experience with the problem environment.
The second pillar needed to create the artifact is of course knowledge. Here I will be using scientiﬁc papers, google

Banking Regulation
In year 2000 Kenneth Spong published his ﬁfth version of his book Banking Regulation - Its purposes, implemen-

in Risk Management at the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City and shares the same background as the team in

Mr.
Spong found that above all new regulation serves the purpose of promoting a stable and competitive ﬁ-
nancial system in any country[4]. The reason regulation plays such a prevalent role in banking is the fact that most
banks is commonly treated as a matter of public interest. The book concludes that regulation mostly is created
as a response to ﬁnancial crisis, historical events and political events. This was also the case for the most recent
GDPR Act that is a direct response to the threat of social media hoarding massive amount of personal information.
Additionally he points out an important fact about the authorities responsible for creating new regulation - that no
single architect or person have been assigned to layout the regulatory system and a set of principles for the process.
This means that many people with many diﬀerent views, objectives and experiences are responsible for the current
supervisory framework in each regulatory authority.
This notion underlines the feeling that new regulation can feel a lot like the wild west, however they can usually be
assigned to one of the following common goals for regulation of banks:
• Protection of depositors.
• Monetary and ﬁnancial stability.
• Eﬃcient and competitive ﬁnancial system.
• Consumer protection.
Protection of depositors are the most frequent form of regulation in any country as most citizens are forced to have
large portions of their wealth locked away in bank accounts. In recent years with cash being less frequent this
protection of digital funds are more important than ever before.
Monetary and ﬁnancial stability is about forcing banks to protect themselves against external factors like a new
ﬁnancial crisis, fraud, money laundering etc. If a Bank is not prepared well for such threats and focuses entirely on
proﬁt the bank might face bankruptcy in dire circumstances costing the society jobs, money and a large amount of

Eﬃcient and competitive ﬁnancial system concerns the fact that citizens in any country should be free to choose

Consumer protection can be summarized by looking at the GDPR act that forces Banks to treat customer infor-
mation with great care when they do business with the bank.

speciﬁc branches of Danske Bank UK.
The regulatory initiatives are the main entity of the software solution I am designing and they have many as-
sociated entities which can be divided into two parts: Owners and Internal Assessments.
Owners cover the employees that will be assigned to assess and implement a regulatory initiative. There are usually
3 primary owners for each initiative:
• Accountable
• Responsible
• Second-Line Responsible
The Accountable person is an employee that will be held accountable if Danske Bank UK fails to implement a reg-

the role of the accountable person is to ask who within Danske Bank UK will take the blame if something goes
wrong with a regulatory initiative. Given the nature of this responsibility the accountable person will usually be
an executive from the Executive Committee (ExCo). In some cases where the initiative is deemed non critical a
manager or employee of lower status can be assigned as an accountable.
The Responsible person is the employee that will be held accountable internally if Danske Bank UK fails to imple-
ment the regulatory initiative in due time. In general this person will be the main driver behind the implementation
of a regulatory initiative. Sometimes the person will carry out every process himself or in other instances he will
assemble a team of employees to carry out sub tasks and sub processes during implementation.
The Second-Line responsible is an employee from the Risk Management Team that is assigned to supervise and
monitor progress on implementation of the regulatory initiative. The amount of time the second-line responsible
spends on an initiatives varies a lot depending on the scope of the regulatory initiative. It is also the responsibility

Optimization Analysis
Any reader paying attention to the presented diagram will notice that there are a whole lot of generic tasks like
completing various assignments and sending them to the Risk Management Team through email. I will now go
through each one and discuss which ones are suitable for automation.
Due to the constraints I put in place earlier about creating a system for internal use and access only the process of
screening for new regulatory initiative cannot by nature be automated. However for the system to work the found
initiatives needs to be uploaded to the system.
Analyzing the initiative by hand is still necessary unless the system deploys and AI to automatically parse the
initiatives and detect where they belong and who should be assigned. This process is again hard to change due to
scope restrictions.
Assigning ownership should be performed through the system, this way the system can partially help along by
sending out an email notiﬁcation to the owner automatically and also add another layer through email reminders
in case the owner is slow - optimizing respond time.
The ownership template cannot be ﬁlled out automatically however it can be integrated into the system so that the
user just logs on, ﬁll it out and afterwards the system automatically sends the full ownership template to the risk
management team removing one full task from the user.
If an initiative has no impact it should be archived through the system, here we can again add another con-
trol layer by sending out an automatic email alert to the risk management team notifying them that the initiative
was rated with no impact and archived allowing them to review it.

By now I have outlined the why and the what aspects of the artifact I want to create - this chapter will explore the

6.1
Design Influence
There are several factors to keep in mind when designing a system to solve the requirements obtained in the Analysis
chapter. These include:
• System should be easy to access.
• There are multiple roles so the system need authentication.
• Employees should be able to use the system concurrently.
• System need an intuitive interface and to be user-friendly.
• System need a persistency layer.
• System should be sealed oﬀfrom the public to preserve conﬁdentiality and integrity of data.
Since regulatory initiatives aﬀect the entire company of Danske Bank UK it is important that all business units
and all employees can access the system. Additionally there is a need for diﬀerentiated rights based on the nature
of the task an employee has to complete. The diﬀerent users are:
• Employee that uploads Regulatory Initiatives.
• Employee that assign ownership of Regulatory Initiatives.
• Employee that has been assigned and need to ﬁll out assessments.
• Employee that want to browse the entire Regulatory Portfolio for information.
• Employee that want to manage the Regulatory Portfolio and pull data from it.
To achieve this the system will have to be designed in a way that support multiple roles and thus require authenti-
cation. This is an important topic that I will cover later in the designated Authentication section.
It is also important that multiple users can use the system concurrently as the system will have to support many
diﬀerent Regulatory Initiatives some of which has to be assessed all in the same day of business. A system that
allow users to work concurrently will make that using the system will not delay the ﬁlling of assessments and other
important tasks associated with the implementation of Regulatory Initiatives.
Since the system has to support employees from all business units and the entire Danske Bank UK employees from
all types of backgrounds and with very diﬀerent technical skills will come in touch with the system. Therefore it
Page 36 of 82
==PAGE:38==
is important that the system provide an easy-to-use graphical user interface preferable an interface that resembles
other Danske Bank systems so it feels familiar and approachable for even the most skeptical employees.
The goal of the system is to support a majority of all the processes associated with Regulatory Initiatives thus
making it the backbone for the entire Portfolio. This means that the system will store critical information that
cannot be allowed to be lost no matter the circumstances. Data integrity is essential here as the loss of data can
lead to non-compliance and sabotage the entire Risk Management Team.
Finally it is important that the system is walled oﬀfrom the outside as assessments made by Danske Bank UK are
conﬁdential at all times. This means that the system cannot be allowed to be accessible from the public and more
importantly the system should not be connected to the outside world either.
Running through each factor I see three common approaches to create an appropriate software solution:
1. A Standard Application.
2. A Cloud Application.
3. A Web Application.
Building a standard application in Java, C# or similar will allow users to work remotely and without the need
for internet access. However concurrency is a major concern here as this can lead to inconsistency if work on the
same Regulatory Initiatives is not synchronized accordingly. I also feel that this system is hard to maintain as it
will require all employee computers to be updated everytime an update for the system is made. How the system
should access data is another concern, without internet access it will be hard to coordinate the Regulatory Portfolio.
Overall not a very viable approach to building a good system with the factors I have outlined here.
A cloud solution would be interesting as it lends itself well to the very accessible system I want to create however
the discussion ends here as Danske Bank UK has a ban in eﬀect on all cloud based solutions. This decision is to
protect Danske Bank systems and to make sure that Danske Bank has ownership of all data ﬂowing through the
company. For example this means that cloud providers like Microsoft with it’s Azure powered applications are not
allowed either as this would allow Microsoft to process data that Danske Bank wants to keep conﬁdential.
This leads to my favorite approach - a Web Application. Such an application provide universal access to all em-
ployees through a web browser. It also is easy to maintain as changes to the client and the database take eﬀect
immediately without interrupting service notably. Finally it allow me to store all data in a Database that can be
equipped with automatic backups and thereby protect the integrity of all stored data at all times.

10.3.1
Digital distribution of Initiatives
A big challenge for this kind of system is how the regulatory initiatives themselves are imported into the system.
As it stands there are multiple legislators in both the EU as well as in local countries like United Kingdom or
Denmark. The process of proposing new regulatory initiatives is not streamlined or digitized meaning that each
legislator simply publishes the initiatives as pdf or text ﬁles on their respective websites. This manual and old
school way of handling regulatory initiatives mean that companies must rely on manual screening like it is the case
in Danske Bank UK or hire a third party company to do the screening.
The screening phase is naturally a place where a software solution like the one I have developed should be expanded
and automate the process of screening for initiatives by automatically collecting all initiatives and importing them
seamlessly into the system. This would require that each legislator ﬁlls out a digital form for each initiative and
saves it in a database. The ideal setup would be a global database that all legislators use making the digital form
for initiatives identical for all legislators. The form would include details like:
• A title of the regulatory initiative.
• A summary of the regulatory initiative.
• The publication date.
• The legal stage, has the initiative been proposed, adopted etc.
• The application date, how long before a company has to be compliant.
The global database should then allow public access or grant access to all companies that apply for insight into
upcoming regulatory initiatives. This way the risk of a ﬂawed screening process where an employee fail to detect all
initiatives is mitigated or removed entirely. Another beneﬁt would be the ability for software tools to automatically
connect to the global database and fetch a pull request to collect all initiatives. For example the system could
be scripted to automatically fetch all new initiatives at a speciﬁc time each week and present the new initiatives
on the front page of the system. Furthermore you could have the system send out email notiﬁcations to relevant
stakeholders each time new initiatives are detected. The distribution for regulatory initiatives could look something
like the following diagram:
A setup like the one above would allow Danske Bank to store all initiatives locally to ensure oﬄine access to
initiatives at all times during the week. Additionally the local database would be used to store assessments and
other internal data created by Danske Bank for each initiative.

• Aid the user and provide assistance if the user is stuck or seems inactive.
The ﬁrst beneﬁt is obvious and would allow for more optimal implementations since initiatives could be batched
together. There could be a related box attached to each initiative where the owner of the most recent one can look
back at how similar initiatives were implemented and assessed in the past. This sharing of relevant knowledge can
increase eﬃciency and speed up the assessment process for each initiative.
The second beneﬁt is again to speed up the assessment process. By providing suggestions like:
User X did this for Initiative Y, you want to do the same?
This will reduce redundancy and still provide integrity since an actual employee is approving each suggestions before
the selections are applied in the system.
The ﬁnal beneﬁt is more broad but it is common that not all employees ﬁnd the use of systems like RIS easy
to navigate and operate. Older employees might not be used to using such systems and will need additional assis-
tance if you want to avoid that the system actually slows down their handling of initiatives and end up hurting the
overall implementation. No matter how intuitive a graphical user interface is some people will experience problems,
here some pop ups linking to guides or videos of how to use the system can greatly improve the user experience.

Chapter 11
Discussion
This chapter will discuss three important outcomes of this project, that is how the results align with the project
goals in a broader context, how the results materialize to a scientiﬁc contribution and ﬁnally what lessons I have
learned through the time I worked on the project.
11.1
Results
Looking at the results presented in the evaluation chapter I think it is important to look at them in a broader
context. The three major results are:
• Better chances to avoid non-compliance.
• Increased eﬃciency in tasks related to regulatory initiatives.
• Improved maintenance of the regulatory portfolio.
I think all can agree that these results are positive for any company working with Regulatory Initiatives, that said
I think it is important to recognize how these results were obtained.
The Regulatory Database System is based entirely on processes and procedures used in Danske Bank UK resulting
in a software solution that is almost tailor made to one speciﬁc team from one speciﬁc company. It is important to
discuss what lead to these results: is it because the system solves the fundamental issue of improving the handling of
Regulatory Initiatives or because the system assist with the processes placed on top. The ﬁrst scenario is preferable
as this mean that the software I have developed can be used in any company and provide immediate beneﬁts from
day one, while the second is far less glamorous.
Having a tailor made system to assist in the daily operation of a company can deﬁnitely be a good thing in itself
and I acknowledge that the results provide tangible and concrete value to Danske Bank UK. That said I worry that
by building a software solution based on one company in a very close agile collaboration has created a solution that
provide tremendous value to Danske Bank UK but little to no value to any other company that want to tackle the
same challenge of implementing Regulatory Initiatives in a better way. To use an analogy one could say that this
software wins a battle, but does not win the war, meaning that there is a very real concern that the solution has
limited or no appeal to any other company.
An assessment like the ownership template is unique for Danske Bank UK, yes it touches on some fundamental
topics like risks something that is universal for all Regulatory Initiatives, but the way companies handle risks might
be entirely diﬀerent. Another example is how ownership is assigned, in Danske Bank UK this involves three speciﬁc
employees, while another company might want to assign an entire business unit or branch as owner. These examples
goes to show that the system is in many ways build upon some unique internal processes that are only applicable
for Danske Bank UK.

The problem with Model A is that you build the software on top of some custom processes and thereby create a
solution that is so customized and tailor made, that it become to rigid to be used anywhere else. To make things

ment etc. making the software either obsolete, or reduce the value it provide signiﬁcantly. In other words this

Model B is much more preferably as this create a software solution that provide value to all companies widening

in Model A as a software solution focused entirely on the fundamental problem will require much less changes in
the future.
Luckily the reality is looking less black and white: some parts of the software solution has broad appeal and
touches with the fundamental issue of handling Regulatory Initiatives while other parts of the system are process
oriented that only provide value to the Risk Management Team in Danske Bank UK. An example of this is how
the entire Regulatory Portfolio is stored in a big database accessible for all employees, creating one platform for all
Regulatory Initiatives. This part of the system provide universal value to any company seeking to improve handling

11.2
Scientific Contribution
Scientiﬁcally in a more broad perspective I believe this project demonstrates several points:
1. That Regulatory Processes can be automated and augmented by software just like any other Business Pro-
cesses.
2. A Signiﬁcant amount of resources can be saved by using Software to handle Regulatory Initiatives.
3. Design science is an applicable methodology to use for this type of project.
The ﬁrst point goes to show that not only can the software I have developed during this project provide beneﬁts,
but a whole bunch of software applications can actually be used and applied here. I see no reason why tools aimed
at business process management could not be used here to streamline many of the processes in the same way as I
have done. This is probably also why third party solutions from Enablon and Predict360 oﬀer their solutions for
Regulatory Initiatives as a part of a full software suite that also include management and workﬂow tools found in
Business Process Management Tools.
Second point is derived directly from the evaluation chapter as participants responded that they can save an esti-
mated 28% or more of their time on generic and trivial tasks. This result is in line with the current trends I covered
in the State of the Art chapter, where a survey from Business Insider ﬁnds that two thirds of professionals expect
demand for RegTech software to increase untill 2020. It also explains why the Regtech industry has dramatically
increased investment in this category of software solutions. Any company wants to increase eﬃciency and if Regtech
solutions can achieve anything like the 28% I have achieved during this project it will be seen as a tremendous
business opportunity for any company working with compliance and Regulatory Initiatives.
Finally I want to address the choice of Design Science to solve this problem. The methodology suggests that the
problem context and domain has to be analyzed in detail before an artifact that solves the speciﬁed problem can
be created. This approach has lead me through an extensive research phase and analysis, where I spend weeks
understanding every little detail of the processes in the Risk Management Team. As proven in the analysis section
this creates a strong foundation for any software development and I believe, that this approach and methodology
contributed positively to the good results derived from the survey.

11.3
Realization
Earlier I touched upon the fact that the approach of only attacking this topic with one case and one company
has lead to some negative ramiﬁcations for the overall outcome of the project. If I were to do a similar project in
the future I would awknowledge early on that building a software solution on top of one case gives a biased view
of the fundamental problem, one that should be mitigated by analyzing multiple companies with totally diﬀerent
approaches to handling Regulatory Initiatives. This would give me a much better understanding of the fundamental
issues that need to be solved for all companies and not only the issues Danske Bank UK has with their speciﬁc
setup. This in turn would lead to a more universal solution that not only provide value for Danske Bank UK but
could be integrated into all companies around the world with little to no customization required. This is however
not a regret as a I recognize that this approach of analyzing multiple cases and companies would require even more
time than what I had available here. It would lead to a much larger scope or to a project where I did not understand
processes to the tiniest detail as I had the opportunity to do here. I strongly believe that a strong analysis of the
Regulatory Processes are the foundation for all the positive results obtained during this project and if the scope
were to be increased with multiple companies and cases all the new processes would have to be analyzed with the
same care.
Finally I want to address the collaboration with the Risk Management Team and the tremendous eﬀort they have
put into this study. Besides the visit in early November 2018 I have been in contact with them almost daily and
since December they have tested my software with such a great interest and passion, that I strongly believe that
the success and positive results achieved can be tracked directly back to their eﬀorts. Working with them in this
close agile collaboration has been a privilege and one I would be happy to repeat anytime in the future.

Conclusion

party RegTech companies play a vital role in handling Regulatory Initiatives both today and more importantly in
the future where the burden of new regulation might increase even further.

Chapter A
Glossary
Implementation Date
The date where a Regulatory Initiative have to be fully implemented. Usually the legislator behind the Regulatory
Initiative will set a deadline once a Regulatory Initiative has been ﬁnalized and adopted into force. If a company
is not compliant with the content of the Regulatory Initiative before this date it is subject to penalties.
Regulatory Initiative
A label that cover all types of regulatory material. A directive would be considered a regulatory initiative on the
same level as other material like hearings or drafts.
Implementation
The process of altering day to day operation within in a company to satisfy a set of regulatory requirements. In this
context implementation may involve technical changes but the term diﬀer greatly from software implementations.
Implementation Project
A project inside the company that carries out a regulatory implementation and takes care of all tasks associated
with it.
Horizon
This is the ﬁrst phase in the lifecycle of regulatory initiatives. This is the stage where drafts and proposals are
thrown back and forth. These papers will later materialize into actual directives or regulations.
Screening
The process of screening legal authorities and their websites for upcoming regulatory initiatives. This is done to
identify trends or catch initiatives with large impact early.
