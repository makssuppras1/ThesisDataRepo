Abstract 
Microbial secondary metabolites, such as antibiotics, have been explored for decades. 
Their antimicrobial properties against pathogens have been intensively studied. 
However, limited knowledge about their ecological role in nature where they are often 
produced sub-inhibitory concentrations. In order to investigate the ecological role of 
secondary metabolites in complex bacterial communities, simplified synthetic 
communities (SynCom) are being developed.  
In this study a four-membered SynCom consisting of Algoriphagus sp., Muricauda sp., 
Mameliella sp., and Marinobacter sp. isolated from the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica 
was used to investigate how the marine bacterium Phaeobacter piscinae S26 
producing tropodithietic acid (TDA) – or its TDA-deficient mutant – affects the bacterial 
growth and taxonomic composition of the SynCom.  
CFU counts and qPCR quantification were used for bacterial enumeration where the 
matrix effect on qPCR was investigated. Compared to CFU counts qPCR 
quantification resulted in an underestimate. This underestimate could have been 
caused by the purity of the samples or by the data processing constructing the 
standard curve used for estimation. 
All members of the SynCom were present for the duration of the experiment. Neither 
the composition nor the maximum cell density in the SynCom was influenced by the 
presence of S26. It was not possible to detect TDA by LC-ESI-HRMS/MS. The reason 
for this could be that TDA was below the limit of detection, catabolized by the SynCom. 
Another possible explanation is of course that TDA was simply not present. 
The divergence of the global metabolome was mainly driven by the presence of S26. 
However, the abundance of chemical features was different in the two setups. Further 
investigation is needed to elucidate whether this was caused by the deletion of tdaB 
or the presence of TDA. 
It can be concluded that the SynCom is stable in the presence of a TDA-producer and 
that the divergence in global metabolome observed in the presence or absence of S26 
is mainly driven by the presence of Phaeobacter and not its TDA-producing 
capabilities.

Resumé 
Mikrobielle sekundære metabolitter, såsom antibiotika, er blevet udforsket i årtier. 
Deres antimikrobielle egenskaber mod patogener er blevet studeret nøje. På trods af 
dette er der kun en begrænset viden om, hvilken rolle de har i naturen hvor de ofte 
produceres ved ikke-hæmmende koncentrationer. For at undersøge den naturlige 
rolle af sekundære metabolitter i komplekse bakteriesamfund udvikles simplificerede 
syntetiske bakteriesamfund.  
I dette studie er et syntetisk bakteriesamfund, bestående af fire forskellige bakterier; 
Algoriphagus sp., Muricauda sp., Mameliella sp. Og Marinobacter sp., isoleret fra 
mikroalgen Tetraselmis suecica blevet anvendt til at undersøge hvordan den marine 
bakterie Phaeobacter piscinae S26, som kan producere det antimikrobielle stof 
tropodithietsyre (TDA) eller dens ikke-producerende mutant, påvirker 
bakterievæksten og sammensætningen i det syntetiske bakteriesamfund. 
Til at estimere bakterieantal blev der brugt to metoder; kolonidannende 
enhedsestimering og qPCR, hvor matrixeffekten på qPCR blev undersøgt. Ved 
sammenligning af de to metoder blev der konstateret at qPCR underestimerede 
celleantal. Denne underestimering kunne være forårsaget af urenheder i prøverne 
eller på grund af programmet, der blev anvendt til at generere standard kurverne, 
som blev brugt til at estimere bakterieantal.  
Alle medlemmer af det syntetiske samfund var til stede under hele eksperimentets 
varighed. Hverken sammensætning eller den maksimale cellevækst blev påvirket af 
tilstedeværelsen af S26. Det var ikke muligt at påvise tilstedeværelsen af TDA ved 
LC-ESI-HRMS/MS. Årsagen til dette kunne være, at TDA var under 
detektionsgrænsen eller blev kataboliseret af bakteriesamfundet. En anden mulig 
forklaring er, at TDA ikke blev produceret. Forskellene i det globale metabolom var 
primært forårsaget af tilstedeværelsen af S26. Dog var der en forskel i mængden af 
kemiske produkter når vildtype eller mutant var til stede. Yderligere undersøgelse er 
nødvendigt, for at kunne fastsætte hvorvidt dette var forårsaget af sletningen af 
tdaB-genet eller tilstedeværelsen af TDA. 
Det kan konkluderes at det syntetiske samfund er stabilt ved tilstedeværelsen af en 
TDA-producerende bakterie, og de forskelle der blev observeret i det globale 
metabolom primært var forårsaget af Phaeobacter  og ikke dets TDA-producerende 
evner.

1 Introduction 
Secondary metabolites (SM) are small organic molecules that are not considered 
essential for cell growth. These molecules have been extensively studied for their 
bioactive potential. Antibiotics such as penicillin and streptomycin have shown 
antagonizing effects against different pathogens which have been beneficial for 
human health [1], [2], [3]. Because of the antibiotic activity observed in the laboratory, 
it was long assumed that SM had the same role in nature: to improve survival of its 
producer and give a competitive advantage in nutrient acquisition by killing or reducing 
growth of other microbes inhabiting the same niche [4], [5]. However, the 
concentrations required for SM to exhibit antimicrobial effects are higher than those 
observed in nature, where SM are often found at sub-inhibitory concentrations [6], [7]. 
Studies have demonstrated how SM at sub-inhibitory concentrations can alter gene 
expression in communities and promote biofilm formation or motility [8]–[10]. SM has 
also been found to act as a signal molecule mediating quorum sensing (QS), altering 
gene expression in its producer to promote balanced colonization and to signal a shift 
from mutualism to pathogenesis in its host [6], [11], [12]. Furthermore, SM can act as 
activators for silenced biosynthetic gene clusters (BGS) and promote production of 
otherwise repressed compounds. Buijs et al. also demonstrated how sub-inhibitory 
concentrations of the antibiotic andrimid could induce transcription of BGC in P. 
galatheae and act as an activator for antibiotic holomycin production [13].  
It is apparent that SM play a variety of roles in nature, and it is of interest to investigate 
their ecological role in microbial communities where they may play a pivotal role in 
interspecies interactions and be crucial in shaping the composition of these 
communities.  
 
1.1 Synthetic communities as model systems  
Synthetic communities (SynCom) have become of increasing interest [14]. These 
model systems are of intermediate complexity compared to environmental samples 
and it is possible to investigate species-species interactions that would be absent in 
mono- and co-cultures 
Development of SynComs should be based on natural systems in order to make the 
microbial interactions as relevant as possible from an ecological perspective. 
Furthermore, the system should contain species that are tractable in a laboratory 
==PAGE:9==
1 Introduction 
 
 
Page 8 of 67 
setting. When such prerequisites are met these model systems make it possible to 
test hypotheses in a controlled setting with a community of intermediate complexity of 
the ecological niche of interest [14], [15].  
The assemblage of microbial communities can be influenced by several factors within 
the community: the host, spatial distribution, interspecies interaction, and the niche. In 
order to attempt to understand the microbial interactions that occur in nature several 
SynComs have been developed for various environmental niches. 
 
1.1.1 Synthetic communities 
The altered Schaedler flora, consists of eight species, this model system mimic the 
normal gut structure and immune system development in mice [16]. This model system 
makes it possible to address questions regarding the human gut microbiome [17]. 
Weiss et al. also studied the Oligo-Mouse-Microbiota model to investigate growth and 
metabolic interactions by inoculating all 12 strains together and determining relative 
abundance by qPCR after 10 days. The most dominating species of the SynCom was 
identified and the SynCom was tested in the absence of these. Here it was reported 
how Enterococcus faecalis producing the antibiotic enterocin was a key driver of the 
community composition due to its ability to reduce the most abundant species in the 
community. Moreover, it was reported how specific media support growth of certain 
species and thereby shape the community composition [18].  
Spatial distribution of community members can be of importance when stabilizing a 
SynCom. In nature soil bacteria often coexist in microcolonies separated by 
micrometers where it is only possible to exchange exometabolites [19], [20]. To study 
these interactions microfluidics devices and microtiter plates with bottom filters into a 
shared media have been used [21], [22]. These models can be used to investigate 
metabolic cross feeding that can shape microbial communities by promoting 
cooperative interactions [23].  
In Arabidopsis thaliana it has been demonstrated how the composition of a seven-
membered SynCom was affected by its host genotype [24]. It has also been 
demonstrated in A. thaliana how microbiota from different niches – leaf and root – was 
able to relocate even though they were specialized to their respective niches [25]. 
The soil SynCom THOR (The Hitchhikers Of the Rhizosphere) [26] derived from 
soybean roots which consist of three species showed how the antibiotic koreenceine 
==PAGE:10==
1 Introduction 
 
 
Page 9 of 67 
produced by Pseudomonas koreensis was able to change gene expression in the 
community and modify the expression of BGCs in Bacillus cereus – thus 
demonstrating how community interactions can regulate secondary metabolism [27], 
[28]. 
Niu et al. developed a representative seven-species maize root SynCom. To 
investigate the ecological role of this community they were adding, replacing, or 
removing species in the community. They demonstrated the importance of 
Enterobacter cloacae in community assembly. Furthermore, they also demonstrated 
how the host benefitted from the SynCom because it was able to reduce maize 
seedling blight by delaying the colonization of the fungus Fusarium verticillioides [29]. 
De Boer et al. also demonstrated how four non-antagonistic bacteria when mixed were 
able to suppress growth of the plant pathogenic fungi Fusarium culmorum, Rhizoctonia 
solani, and Trichoderma harzianum as a side-effect by the interspecific interactions 
between the community that triggered antibiotic production [30]. 
Kiesewalter et al. also revealed how the addition of the NRP-producing Bacillus subtilis 
to a 13 species soil SynCom had no major effect on the microbial composition [31]. 
With the increasing understanding of SM and their ecological role in microbial 
communities one relevant question is whether we can benefit from these versatile 
properties.  
 
1.2 Challenges in aquaculture  
In aquaculture rearing occurs on a large scale and in high densities, and difficulties 
with controlling introduction of opportunistic pathogens, such as Vibrio species, are of 
concern [32], [33]. The challenge often starts at the larval stage. Here vaccines are 
inefficient due to an undeveloped adaptive immune system and antibiotics only have 
limited success [32], [34]. In order to produce larvae live feed as rotifers and copepods, 
microalgae are often used and it is at this stage opportunistic pathogens are 
introduced [33], [35], [36]. Different mitigation strategies have been suggested such 
as phage therapy and probiotics [34], [37]–[39]. Previous studies have proven that 
probiotics can mitigate disease outbreaks and decrease mortality in aquaculture [39]–
[41]. Species from the Roseobacter group have been found in several marine niches 
from open waters to harbors around the world [42]–[45]. They have been widely 
==PAGE:11==
1 Introduction 
 
 
Page 10 of 67 
studied because of their ability to antagonize fish pathogens without the pathogen 
developing resistance to their bioactive compound tropodithietic acid (TDA) [45], [46]. 
 
1.2.1 Phaeobacter sp. as probiotics 
Present studies have isolated bioactive Phaeobacter sp. from aquaculture units and 
proven how they can antagonize fish pathogens such as Vibrio anguillarum [45]–[48]. 
Furthermore, studies have demonstrated how P. inhibens and P. piscinae S26 can 
reduce mortality in fish larvae by introducing them to the live feed while maintaining 
the inhibitory effect towards vibrios  [45], [46], [48].  
Rasmussen et al. have also demonstrated how V. anguillarum after 90 days of 
exposure to sub-inhibitory concentrations of TDA did not develop a tolerance or 
resistance [49].  
 
1.2.2 Tropodithietic acid 
TDA was first isolated in Pseudomonas sp. from soil and subsequently isolated from 
marine bacteria including members from the Roseobacter group [50], [51].  
The antimicrobial property observed in certain Phaeobacter sp. is due to the SM TDA. 
Wilson et al. demonstrated the mode of action of TDA was similar to polyether 
antibiotics like salinomycin, nigericin, and monensin where it disrupts the proton 
motive force by a proton antiport mechanism [52].  
 
1.3 Ecological role of TDA 
The studies on TDAs are not exclusively focused on their antimicrobial properties. The 
ecological role of TDAs in sub-inhibitory concentrations have also garnered 
considerable attention. Here it has been suggested that TDA coordinates colonization 
behaviors for successful adaptation to new niches [10]. TDA has shown to have iron 
chelating activity and that metal metabolism is affected TDA deficient mutants [10], 
[53]. In P. inhibens TDA can act as a quorum sensing molecule in the same manner 
as the signaling molecule N-acyl-homoserine lactone (AHL) and reduce biofilm 
formation and motility [11]. The same has been observed in P. piscinae S26: DtdaB 
became hyper-motile through an upregulation of flagellar proteins [10]. Will et al. 
demonstrated how curation of the plasmid encoding the TDA biosynthesis resulted in 
==PAGE:12==
1 Introduction 
 
 
Page 11 of 67 
increased growth in P. inhibens though this effect has not been observed in P. piscinae 
S26 [10], [54].  
 
1.3.1 Impact of TDA-producer on host microbiome 
Before TDA-producing bacteria can be safely introduced to aquaculture as probiotics 
it is necessary to study how they affect the natural microbiomes they would be 
introduced to. Studies have shown how pure TDA can affect the composition of the 
microalgae Nannochloropsis salina [55]. The limit of detection of pure TDA in T. 
suecica has been estimated to be <50 nM. When the TDA-producer was co-cultured 
with T. suecica it was not possible to detect. It has therefore been suggested that 
natural concentrations is lower than those possible to detect [48]. P. inhibens has been 
introduced into live feed and microalgae without causing major imbalances to the 
microbial community. It has, however, been shown to cause changes in the low 
complexity microbiome of E. huxleyi compared to the more complex O. edulis 
microbiome [56], [57]. It has also been studied how axenic T. suecica was unaffected 
by P. inhibens roseobacticides which did not result in algal bloom [58]. Furthermore, it 
has been demonstrated by Henriksen et al. that P. inhibens had no major impacts on 
the microbial composition of non-axenic T. suecica and how the colonization of its host 
was independent of the ability to produce TDA [59].  
 
1.4 Tetraselmis suecica synthetic community 
A SynCom isolated from T. suecica has been developed in order to study the impact 
of TDA-producer P. piscinae S26 on its host microbiome. Based on morphological 
differences and cultivability in the laboratory, five strains from different genera 
Algoriphagus; Muricauda; Mameliella; Oceanicaulis; Marinobacter were chosen to 
represent the microbiome of T. suecica [60]. All genera have been found in association 
with microalgae [61], [62]. It was investigated how the SynCom – when grown in axenic 
T. suecica – was affected by P. piscinae S26. Certain strains in the SynCom were 
initially affected. However, this observed effect disappeared after growth for eight 
days, and growth of the host was not influenced [60]. These observations were similar 
to those of other studies investigating the impact of P. inhibens on its host and 
microbial community composition [58], [59]. The SynCom was later reduced to a four-
member community excluding Oceanicaulis sp. due to difficulties in differentiating two

1.5 qPCR  
Molecular methods using Real Time Quantitative PCR (qPCR) have become more 
standard in the research laboratory. When working with microbial communities this 
approach can be used to overcome difficulties with the traditional plate count when 
quantifying several species at a time. Instead of relying on morphological differences 
qPCR use amplification of a specific DNA/RNA fragment for quantification. The 
fragment is stained using fluorescent dye. The fluorescence is measured after each 
thermal cycle. Specific primers are used for individual strains and the qPCR can 
determine the amount of DNA/RNA. This makes it possible to estimate bacterial count 
by the use of standard curves makes it possible [65]. Studies have used qPCR to 
estimate bacterial abundance in several niches such as sea water, sediment, plants 
and humans [66]–[75]. qPCR can result in an overestimation of bacterial abundance 
compared to those obtained by plate count. Such overestimation might be caused by 
a higher sensitivity in qPCR or by the possibility of quantification of dead and nonviable 
cells [68], [72], [76]. Furthermore, the accuracy of qPCR quantification depends on the 
standard curve. The standard curve can be influenced by a number of factors that can 
interfere and make enumeration difficult. One such factor consists of problems that 
may arise when using samples with a low concentration of DNA. Another factor is the 
matrix effect which can be affected by the presence of polymerase inhibitors, reagents 
from DNA extraction, and the presence of other species. These considerations should 
be taken into account when using qPCR [66], [71], [74], [77]–[80].

1.6 The project 
The purpose of this project was to assess how the bioactive marine bacterium 
Phaeobacter piscinae S26 producing tropodithietic acid affects the bacterial growth 
and composition of a four-membered synthetic community isolated from the 
microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. 
Through ecological microbial experiments it was analysed how the SynCom changes 
in composition when grown together with a wildtype P. piscinae S26 or its TDA-
deficient mutant DtdaB. The SynCom matrix effect on the qPCR standard curve for 
each strain was evaluated. Both quantification methods CFU counts and qPCR 
quantification was used for bacterial enumeration. Furthermore, analysis of untargeted 
metabolomics was used to determine how the presence or absence of P. piscinae S26 
affects the global metabolome of the SynCom.

2.5 DNA extraction 
1 mL of O/N pure culture or mixed community were transferred to 1.5 mL Eppendorf 
tubes and centrifuged for 2 min. at 8,000 x g. The supernatant was discarded and 400 
µl Lysis buffer[82] (400 mM sodium chloride, 750mM sucrose, 20 mM EDTA, 50 mM 
Tris-HCl) was added before storage at -20 °C. Lysates stored at -20 °C were thawed 
and fresh lysozyme [82] (stock: 100 mg mL-1) was added to a final concentration of 1 
mg mL-1. After 15 min. of incubation at 37 °C fresh Proteinase K (stock: 20 mg mL-1, 
VWR Life Science, E195-5ML) was added to a final concentration of 0.5 mg mL-1 and 
sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS; stock: 10%) was added to a final concentration 1%. 
Lysates were vortexed and incubated for 20 hours at 55 °C and 300 rpm. DNA 
extraction of incubated lysate samples was performed on Promega Maxwell 16 
AS3000 instrument using Maxwell 16 LEV Blood DNA kit with parameters seen in 
Appendix 3. After extraction, samples were stored at -20 °C. 
Quality control of extracted DNA was done using Denovix Nanodrop and Qubit 
(dsDNA BR, lot: 2061432) assay kit. 
The purity ratios 260/230 and 260/280 should be in the range of 2.0-2.2 and 1.8-2.0, 
respectively.

4 Discussion 
Model systems such as synthetic communities have received increasing interest – 
especially with regards to elucidating the ecological role of bacteria in microbial 
communities. This is due to their ability to reduce the complexity of the community 
while still preserving some of the interspecies interactions compared to monocultures. 
This study investigated the TDA-producing bacterium Phaeobacter piscinae S26 
impact on a synthetic community isolated from the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. 
The synthetic community consisted of four different species: Algoriphagus sp., 
Muricauda sp., Mameliella sp., and Marinobacter sp. all genera have been found in 
the microbiome of the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica [61], [62].  
All members of the SynCom were able to grow for the whole duration of the 
experiment. The presence of S26 did not influence growth rate or maximum cell 
densities of SynCom. The observed divergence in global metabolome was primarily 
driven by the presence of S26. However, differences between S26 WT and S26 DtdaB 
global metabolome was observed whether this was caused by the deletion of the tdaB 
gene or by the TDA-producing capabilities of the WT remains unanswered. 
 
To estimate bacterial count in the SynCom several different quantification methods of 
cell densities could be used [63]. In this project both standard CFU counts and qPCR 
quantification were used for bacterial enumeration. CFU colony counts were based on 
different colony morphology whereas qPCR was based on design of unique primers 
for each individual strain [64]. All the primers had specificity towards the desired strain 
with no cross-reactivity between strains [64]. Previous studies have mentioned the 
importance of testing the matrix background when developing qPCR standard curves 
so both standard curve and tested sample are exposed to the same interference since 
small differences in efficiency can result in significant differences in absolute 
quantification [71], [77], [78], [89]. In the comparison of standard curve developed from 
pure culture and matrix the efficiency for all strains increased in the matrix setup.  
However, it was observed that the matrix standard curves underestimated cell 
densities compared to the standard curves derived from pure cultures which should 
be taken into consideration if used for further experimentation and there would be a 
need to develop an over/underestimation factor for the qPCR standard curve derived 
from a target strain in a matrix solution.  
==PAGE:37==
4 Discussion 
 
 
Page 36 of 67 
 
The matrix standard curve also underestimated the cell count compared to CFU count. 
This was surprising since qPCR has a tendency to overestimate cell count because of 
the possibility of including DNA of dead or dying cells in its measurements compared 
to CFU count that rely on viable cells [66], [90], [91].  
 
It has been reported that the amplification efficiency can result in imprecise qPCR 
quantification and it was speculated that this was the cause for the observations [79]. 
The underestimation observed in this study could be explained by a number of issues. 
From purity ratio 260/230 of the DNA extracts, it was seen that there was a high 
variance in the individual samples used for the pure culture standard curve. This could 
have an impact on the amplification efficiency of some of the dilutions and decrease 
the efficiency thus resulting in decreased average amplification efficiency. 
Furthermore, all 260/230 ratios were <1.8 which could indicate excess salts from the 
lysis buffer in the extracted samples and it is speculated that extra washing steps 
before DNA extraction should be included which might also improve 260/280 ratio. 
The efficiency could also have been impacted by the purity of extracted DNA and 
inhibitory compounds interfering with the qPCR [71]. When inhibitory compounds are 
present, a shift in Cq value is expected since the presence of inhibitors will be less in 
more diluted samples [92]. However, this was not the case for Cq values in either pure 
culture or matrix dilutions.  
 
A solution to overcome the observed bias from the matrix standard curve could be 
using different software to process qPCR data, like Full Process Kinetics-PCR [93] 
that can calculate the individual efficiency from cycle to cycle. Or LinRegPCR [94] that 
also by-passes the assumption that efficiency is equal in all dilutions by using the 
fluorescence measured during each cycle for better whole reaction efficiencies and 
thus more accurate quantification [95]. Another approach would be the one-point 
calibration that uses a single independently defined standard amplified in the same 
qPCR run as the unknown samples [96]. This method would also remove systematic 
errors in pipetting and reduce the error with possible PCR inhibitors when preparing 
the standard curve [94], [95], [97]. It would be of interest to compare the data acquired 
with Bio-Rad CFX Maestro and the web-based LinRegPCR [98]. 
==PAGE:38==
4 Discussion 
 
 
Page 37 of 67 
 
Further investigation into an extra washing step or data processing with another 
software is needed to assess if this would remove the qPCR underestimation observed 
in this study. A third option would be development of an estimation factor that could 
be multiplied to the qPCR data. 
 
Previous studies have suggested that the secondary metabolite TDA has antimicrobial 
properties by disrupting the proton motive force [52], [54] and that it can antagonize 
fish pathogens such as Vibrio species [45]–[47]. TDA also has iron chelating properties 
[53] and can in some strains act as a quorum sensing molecule [11], [99]. Also, it may 
decrease motility and promote biofilm formation [10], [44]. All non-Phaeobacter strains 
of the SynCom were on a spot assay inhibited by the TDA-producer while in co-culture 
all non-Phaeobacter strains – except for AS02 – were inhibited by the TDA-producer 
[60]. 
 
When the SynCom strains were grown individually as pure cultures, S26 WT and AS16 
reached levels of ~108 CFU/mL and S26 DtdaB, AS01, AS02, and AS07 reached levels 
of ~109 CFU/mL. In the SynCom, the strains reached maximum cell densities of ~106 
cells/mL for AS01 and AS07 and ~108 cells/mL for AS02, AS16, and S26 based on 
the qPCR quantification. Based on CFU counts, the densities were approx. 1 log 
higher. The differences observed in maximum cell density for AS01 and AS07 
compared to the other strains are most likely due to the difference in growth rate [60] 
whereas the differences in the two quantification methods are believed to be caused 
by the amplification efficiency or software used to generate the standard curve.  
 
The presence of the TDA-producer S26 did not influence maximum cell density or the 
community composition in the SynCom. However, Geng et al. demonstrated that pure 
TDA at different concentrations affects the taxonomic composition over time in the 
microbial community associated with the microalgae Nannochloropsis salina [55] 
which was not used in this study. It was not possible to detect TDA or any TDA-
associated metabolites with the targeted metabolomics. It was speculated whether this 
was due to a TDA concentration below detection limit or that the compound was not 
==PAGE:39==
4 Discussion 
 
 
Page 38 of 67 
produced. However, it is possible to detect TDA in monocultures at the same cell 
densities as achieved in this study but not in co-cultures [10], [100]. 
 
D'Alvise et al. also demonstrated how addition of pure TDA to the microalgae 
Tetraselmis suecica had a detection limit of 50 nM but TDA was not detectable in co-
cultures of T. suecica and a TDA-producer even though tdaC was expressed [48]. 
Henriksen et al. also demonstrated in a complex non-axenic T. suecica model that 
tdaC transcripts were present. This detection of TDA expression could also be applied 
to this setup by the extraction of RNA followed by qPCR.  
 
All non-Phaeobacter strains in the three variants of the SynCom reached similar cell 
densities. Based on the CFU counts these were similar to those achieved in pure 
culture. From these data it can be concluded that the TDA-producer has no impact on 
the SynCom composition. However, it would still be of interest to investigate the 
expression of tdaC to determine if TDA is produced. 
 
A possible explanation behind the absence of TDA  
A study by Chevrette et al. demonstrated how the open ring form of the antibiotic 
lokisin decreased in abundance when P. koreensis was cultured in co-culture and a 
three-member community versus a monoculture. They hypothesized that lokisin was 
either produced in different versions in a response to the present community or was 
modified by the community [28]. One could speculate that TDA is metabolized in the 
SynCom. However, a spot assay from another study did not show any SynCom strains 
having the ability to catabolize or modify TDA [60].  
To investigate this a spot assay could be done in which the TDA-producer is 
embedded in agar and combinations of the SynCom strains are spotted on said agar. 
The presence of an inhibition zone could indicate SynCom tolerance whereas 
decreased brown pigment in the agar could be TDA catabolism. 
 
The experimental setup could also influence the TDA-producer. Previous studies have 
reported that certain Roseobacter strains capable of producing TDA were influenced 
by the growth conditions. Here TDA production was most pronounced under stagnant 
growth conditions [44], [101]–[103] whereas P. inhibens DSM 17395 produced 10-fold 
==PAGE:40==
4 Discussion 
 
 
Page 39 of 67 
higher amounts under shaken conditions [99]. A study by Linqvist et al. proposed that 
TDA plays a pivotal role for its producer in successful surface colonization. When TDA 
accumulates it acts as a signal molecule to decrease motility and rate of horizontal 
gene transfer [10]. Because of this, it would be of interest to investigate if shaken or 
stagnant growth conditions influence the SynCom and the TDA production of the 
Phaeobacter strain used. The initial inoculum could also have an impact on TDA 
expression. Hurley et al. observed how expression of koreenceine was downregulated 
compared to wildtype when initial inoculum was reduced 50-fold to the other strains in 
the community [27]. However, D’Alvise et al. have demonstrated with tdaC::gfp 
reporter fusion how Tritonibacter express TDA at single cell level when attaching to a 
surface [48] 
 
All three variants of the SynCom were similar in composition and the presence or the 
absence of S26 did not influence the community structure. 
 
It has been suggested that community composition and microbial interactions can 
regulate secondary metabolism and alter the global metabolome [14], [28]. Previous 
studies have reported how P. koreensis producing the antibiotic koreenceine was able 
to reduce growth of F. johnsoniae in co-culture. In a three-species community, 
however, B. cereus was able to protect F. johnsoniae by reducing koreenceine 
production [26]–[28]  
In an on-going experiment by PhD student Peter Bing Svendsen, it has been shown 
through the use of metabolomics analysis of co-culturing of Phaeobacter sp. and 
Pseudoalteromonas piscicida that certain metabolites were only detectable in co-
culture and not in monoculture [100].  
 
As the three variants of the SynCom entered the stationary phase a principal 
component analysis (PCA) showed divergence for the setup without S26 whereas the 
setup with S26 converged. In spite of the convergence, however, it should be noted 
that differences in chemical features remained which were clearly detectable when the 
SynCom was exposed to either S26 WT or S26 DtdaB. A study by Lindqvist et al. 
reported that the PCA of S26 WT and S26 DtdaB diverged after 72 hours and 
==PAGE:41==
4 Discussion 
 
 
Page 40 of 67 
suggested that this could be due to the S26 DtdaB possibly having the ability to affect 
both TDA-related metabolites and the secondary metabolome [10].  
 
Since TDA was not detected in this study it can only be concluded that the presence 
of S26 is the main driver for the global metabolome change. It is speculated whether 
the differences in chemical features between S26 WT and S26 DtdaB are caused by 
the tdaB deletion. However, if TDA is produced in such low quantities as to remain 
below the limit of detection the question becomes: Why? Is this because of the 
possible catabolism of TDA by the SynCom? Or is it due to the shaken growth 
conditions mentioned above? [10].  
 
For future perspectives, it would be of interest to replicate the experiment at different 
initial inocula S26 has been able to coexist when inoculated at the same cell density 
as the SynCom. However, if TDA provides a competitive advantage in successful 
colonization of new niches it would be expected that Phaeobacter is the less abundant 
species [10]. Furthermore, in nature, the species in the microbiome are not present in 
the same abundance and a different inoculum ratio of the SynCom strains could 
change the species-species interactions [104].  
 
With regards to the use as a probiotic, studies have reported how cell densities of 106-
107 CFU/mL can antagonize Vibrios in complex systems [45], [46], [48]. In this study 
Phaeobacter was inoculated at 105 CFU/mL. Therefore, it would be necessary to test 
the ability of S26 to antagonize Vibrio at this cell density or ensure the stability of the 
SynCom when Phaeobacter is inoculated at a higher cell density. 
 
It would also be important to study the SynCom in an algal media to mimic the natural 
ecological niche better. Studies have reported how the algal host and Phaeobacter 
have a mutualistic relationship that changes when the algae enter senescence and 
elicits the production of algaecides [12], [105]. However, Henriksen et at. 
demonstrated in a 70-day co-culture experiment that P. inhibens could colonize non-
axenic T. suecica with no major impact on the community composition or its host [59].

Moreover, it would also be interesting to further investigate the global metabolomics 
briefly touched on in this study by comparing metabolomics of individual strains to the 
SynCom. Another interesting perspective would be to determine TDA production when 
the TDA-producer is grown with the SynCom either by using transcriptomics of tdaC 
[59] or by the use of reporter fusion as D’Alvise et al. [48].

5 Conclusion 
 
 
Page 42 of 67 
5 Conclusion 
Secondary metabolites have been shown to have several properties apart from 
bioactivity. They can shape microbial communities, act as signal molecules to 
coordinate colonization behavior, and activate silenced biosynthetic gene clusters by 
altering gene expression. 
 
This study investigated the impact of the TDA producing Phaeobacter piscinae S26, 
on a four-membered marine microbial synthetic community isolated from the 
microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. This was done in order to determine the ecological 
role of the secondary metabolite TDA and elucidate its versatile nature on microbial 
communities beyond its antibiotic properties.   
 
It was possible to develop a stable multispecies synthetic community in the presence 
of S26 WT or its TDA-deficient mutant S26 DtdaB. Here it was demonstrated that 
neither the WT nor the mutant had any impact on the community composition. It was 
not possible to detect TDA or TDA-associated metabolites using LC-ESI-HRMS/MS. 
However, untargeted global metabolomics analysis showed that the metabolome 
diverged over time when S26 was present unlike when it was absent. 
 
The SynCom matrix effect on qPCR quantification was investigated. It became clear 
that the use of qPCR resulted in a minor underestimation of bacterial enumeration and 
that further calibration is needed for more accurate quantification.  
 
It can be concluded that the SynCom community composition was not influenced by 
the presence of a TDA-producer. 
Further investigation into the changed metabolome between S26 WT and S26 DtdaB 
should be conducted to elucidate the origin before it can be concluded that S26 does 
not cause significant changes to the T. suecica microbiome and can be safely 
introduced as a probiotic in aquaculture. Furthermore, this also contributes to previous 
research that the ecological role of TDA is a multifunctional antimicrobial.
