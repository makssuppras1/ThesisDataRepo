Abstract 
The present study examined the effects of sodium nitrite freshness, sausage storage, meat types, 
cooking methods, meat freshness, liquid smoke flavouring and ascorbic acid on N-nitrosamine 
(NA) formation in sausages. N-nitrosodibutylamine (NDBA), N-nitrosopiperidine (NPIP), N-
nitroso-2-methyl-thiazolidine-4-carboxylix acid (NMTCA), N-nitrosoproline (NPRO) and N-
nitrosothiazolidine-4-carboxylicacid (NTCA) were detected in the sausage samples. Positive 
correlations between the detected NAs and sodium nitrite freshness, sausage storage, sausage 
cooking and liquid smoke flavouring were observed. Ascorbic acid was confirmed to have an 
inhibitory effect on the non-volatile NAs. Target and non-target profiling were employed to 
confirm the presence of NAs detected in target analysis and to identify compounds formed from 
sausage making, respectively. Compounds related to black pepper, paprika, pyrrolidines, 
protein breakdown, prolines, fatty acids were discovered from non-target profiling.

1. Introduction 
Consumption of red meat and processed meat has shown positive associations with various 
cancers including in the pancreas, prostate, stomach and colorectum, of which colorectal cancer 
is the second death-leading cancer in the United Sates (International Agency for Research on 
Cancer Monograph Working Group, 2015; Carr, et al., 2016; Siegel, et al., 2020). Furthermore, 
the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) has concluded the consumption of 
red meat and processed meat as probably carcinogenic to humans (Group 2A) and carcinogenic 
to humans (Group 1), respectively (IARC, 2018a). Red meat is defined as “fresh unprocessed 
mammalian muscle meat, which may be minced or frozen, and is usually consumed cooked” 
(IARC, 2018b, p. 33), while processed meat refers to any meat that has undergone one or more 
processing methods to either improve flavour or for preservation purposes. Carcinogenic 
compounds can arise from meat processing, such as N-nitrosamines (NAs) from nitrite curing, 
polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons from smoking and heterocyclic aromatic amines from high-
temperature cooking. 
Nitrite curing is commonly employed in meat processing to create multifunctional beneficial 
properties, including the characteristic curing flavour, pigment fixation, inhibition on 
pathogenic growth and prevention on lipid oxidation (Heinz & Hautzinger, 2007; Rivera, et al., 
2019). However nitrite in cured meat can produce nitrous anhydride, which acts as a nitrosating 
agent and interacts with secondary amines to form NAs. The formation of NAs is dependent on 
factors such as nitrite concentration, presence of nitrosation catalysts (e.g. chloride anion) and 
inhibitors (e.g. ascorbic acid), cooking temperature and time, storage conditions and presence 
of microorganisms that degrade proteins to amines (Rath & Reyes, 2011). Biogenic amines 
present in food products high in protein can also act as precursors and react with nitrite to form 
NAs in foods. Biogenic amines are generated by the decarboxylation of amino acids and can 
be formed during the storage of meat, especially if the storage condition favours the growth of 
microorganisms and decarboxylase (Stadnik & Dolatowski, 2010). This mechanism also 
explains how storage condition and presence of specific microorganisms affect NA level in 
foods.  
NAs are categorised into two groups: volatile NAs (VNAs) and non-volatile NAs (NVNAs). 
VNAs are a group of relatively non-polar, low molecular weight NAs that have higher 
carcinogenic potential and NVNAs are in general polar and have high molecular weight (Rath 
& Reyes, 2011; Šimko, 2011). VNAs such as N-nitrosodimethylamine (NDMA), N-
nitrosodiethylamine (NDEA), N-nitrosopyrrolidine (NPYR), N-nitrosopiperidine (NPIP), N-
nitrosodibutylamine (NDBA), N-nitrosomethylethylamine (NMEA) and N-nitroso-di-n-
propylamine (NDPA) are considered to be mutagenic and carcinogenic, whereas NVNAs 
including N-nitrosoproline (NPRO), N-nitroso-2-methyl-thiazolidine-4-carboxylix acid 
(NMTCA) and N-nitrosothiazolidine-4-carboxylicacid (NTCA) are generally considered to be 
non-carcinogenic (EMA, 2020). NDMA, NDEA, NDBA, NPIP and NPYR are commonly 
detected in processed meat products, of which NDMA and NDEA are classified as Group 2A 
probably carcinogenic to humans and the latter three are classified as Group 2B possibly 
carcinogenic to humans (IARC, 2018c; EMA, 2020).  
Ascorbic acid is sometimes used in the curing process as a cure accelerator to shorten the curing 
colour development time (Heinz & Hautzinger, 2007; Rivera, et al., 2019). Various studies 
(Mottram, et al., 1975; Pourazrang, et al., 2002; IARC, 2010; Rath & Reyes, 2011) have 
reported ascorbic acid as an inhibitor for NA formation by reducing the nitrosating agents or 
scavenging nitrosonium ions. However no specific efficacy of ascorbic acid’s inhibitory effect 
has been concluded by European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) (Codex Committee on Food 
Additives, 2019).

Traditionally, smoking is employed to meat products to enrich the organoleptic profile and to 
achieve a longer shelf-life by inactivating enzymes and microorganisms. Smoking is applied 
both at industrial scale and domestically to aromatise and heat treat meat products such as ham, 
bacon and sausages (Šimko, 2011). However the smoking process generates nitrogen oxides 
and can induce the formation of nitrosable amine, thereby enhancing the NA level in meat 
products (Rath & Reyes, 2011). In recent years smoking flavour alternatives such as liquid 
smoke flavouring has been developed and are available to consumers. Liquid smoke flavouring 
is produced from smoke condensates and provides the advantages of control over process 
contaminants and reduction in time to develop the desired organoleptic profile. Limited studies 
have provided insight on the effect of liquid smoke flavouring on NA formation in meat 
products. 
Since processed meat has a stronger connection with cancer than red meat, it is therefore more 
relevant to investigate the carcinogenic compounds present in processed red meat via chemical 
profiling, so that a better overview on carcinogens in processed meat can be attained and 
interventive actions can be suggested to reduce the risk of cancer.  
The aim of the present study is thus to examine carcinogenic compounds, in particular NAs, in 
processed red meat using chemical profiling based on target and non-target method. 
Furthermore, various factors such as sodium nitrite freshness, storage condition, meat type, 
cooking method, preparation method, meat freshness, ascorbic acid and liquid smoke were 
investigated to evaluate their influence on NA formation. Factorial design was applied to obtain 
information of the significance of each factor and interaction between the factors. 
2. Theory 
2.1 Extraction  
QuEChERS (Quick, Easy, Cheap, Effective, Rugged, and Safe) methodology is commonly 
employed for the extraction for a wide array of analytes and matrices. The principle behind 
QuEChERS is solid-liquid extraction followed by liquid-liquid extraction, in which the analyte 
is separated in two immiscible solvents base on difference in solubilities (Socas-Rodriguez, et 
al., 2017). A simplified flowchart for QuEChERS extraction process is illustrated in Figure 1: 
 
Figure 1 Simplified flowchart for QuEChERS extraction process. 
The matrix is first homogenized, followed by extraction using an organic solvent and an 
aqueous solvent. The homogenizing process is employed to increase surface area hence a more 
efficient extraction can be achieved. Water is added to matrices with water content < 80% to 
draw the analyte of interest from the matrix and to ensure partitioning. Polar organic solvents 
such as acetonitrile (ACN) are ideal for the extraction of polar compounds such as NAs. 
==PAGE:8==
3 
 
Furthermore, NAs are observed to have great stability in ACN (Lehotay, et al., 2015). It has 
also been shown that acidic condition, for example by the addition of formic acid, is favourable 
for NA extraction (Herrmann, et al., 2014). 
Inorganic salts are typically added to the aqueous-organic polar solvent mixture to induce phase 
separation by creating a shift in the ionic strength of the aqueous layer. The addition of 
inorganic salt also helps drive the target analyte into the organic layer (Pavkovich & Bell, 2019). 
After addition of the inorganic salt, the mixture is vigorously shaken. A ceramic homogenizer 
can be added to the mixture to facilitate homogeneity and efficiency. 
A cleanup step is usually carried out after the initial QuEChERS extraction to remove matrix 
interferences. Dispersive solid phase extraction with SPE sorbents e.g. primary secondary 
amine (PSA) removes interference from fatty acids, organic acids, sugars and pigments. 
Freezing out is another technique used for the cleanup step. By freezing the extract in a freezer, 
the lipids, sugars and other matrix interferences can precipitate out and be removed by 
centrifugation and/or filtering (Pavkovich & Bell, 2019).  
2.2 Liquid chromatography  
Chromatography refers to a separation method where the sample components are separated 
based on their differences in travelling rate through a system that consists of a stationary phase 
and a mobile phase. In liquid chromatography (LC), the sample components (solutes) are 
carried by a liquid mobile phase and passes through a stationary phase held by a solid support 
in a column, where separation of the solutes takes place (Li, et al., 2016). Figure 2 shows the 
schematic diagram of a LC system and the UPLC system used for the present study. 
 
Figure 2 (A)A schematic diagram for LC (Moldoveanu and David, 2013a, p. 21) (B)The UPLC system 
used for the present study. 
In a LC system, the sample is injected by an autosampler in small volumes to the mobile phase, 
to which a pressure is applied to pump the mobile phase through the system. When the eluted 
solutes exit the column, detection and measurements based on their specific physiochemical 
property are made by a detector. The detector transforms the detected physiochemical property 
into electrical signals and generates a graphic output of the signals (chromatogram). 
Chromatogram provides qualitative and quantitative information such as retention time, amount 
of the analyte, resolution and so on (Moldoveanu & David, 2013a).

The primary separation principles for LC include polarity, electrical charge, affinity and 
molecular size (Ismail, 2017). The separation process in polarity-based LC depends on the 
partition between the solute in the stationary phase and that in the mobile phase; if at 
equilibrium the concentration of the solute is higher in the stationary phase due to a stronger 
interaction with the stationary phase, then the solute will be retained. Likewise, if the 
concentration of the solute is higher in the mobile phase at equilibrium, the solute will then be 
eluted with the mobile phase (Moldoveanu & David, 2013a).  
In high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), the column is typically packed with small 
porous particles with diameter equal to or less than 10 µm. The small diameter provides better 
mass transfer by reducing the distance required for the analytes to travel through the pores, 
hence a better efficiency (Li, et al., 2016). An extension of HPLC using even smaller particles 
and higher operating pressure is known as ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC). 
Revered-phase chromatography is the most commonly employed HPLC technique and is used 
for separating compounds with hydrophobic moieties and are not dominantly polar. In reversed-
phase LC (RP-LC), the stationary phase is hydrophobic while the mobile phase is either polar 
or partially polar. Polymers and porous silica covered with a non-polar bonded phase, for 
example C8 and C18, are usually used for the stationary phase of RP-LC. The mobile phase for 
RP-LC is typically a blend of aqueous (e.g. water) and miscible organic (e.g. methanol) solvents 
that is more polar than the stationary phase (Moldoveanu & David, 2013b). As NAs are polar 
compounds (Nawrocki & Andrzejewski, 2011), it is thus preferable to use RP-LC for the 
analysis of NAs so that NAs can be eluted with the polar mobile phase. Furthermore, it has 
been shown that the addition of 0.1% formic acid to water increases the signal intensity for the 
majority of NAs (Herrmann, et al., 2014). 
Another important aspect of LC is the mode of elution. Most RP-LC separations are conducted 
using gradient elution. Gradient elution refers to when there is a change in the composition of 
the mobile phase during a chromatographic run. At the beginning of the gradient programme, 
a short section of isocratic hold is included to retain the least retained solutes by using a higher 
proportion of the weakest solvent. The concentration of the organic modifier then increases 
over time, thus increasing the eluting power of the mobile phase, and is held constant for a 
period of time at the highest organic modifier concentration to flush out all sample components 
from the stationary phase. Afterwards the mobile phase composition is reset to the initial 
condition and equilibrated for the next injection (Moldoveanu & David, 2013c).  
2.3 Mass spectrometry  
Mass spectrometry (MS) is an analytical technique in which the analytes are ionized and 
subjected to electric and magnetic fields in vacuum. The technique is used for the identification 
and/or quantification of analytes by measuring the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of the ions. MS 
is commonly coupled with LC to provide analyte-specific detection based on retention time and 
structural information. A mass spectrometer primarily consists of an ion source, a mass analyser, 
and a detector (Przybylski, 2014; Rockwood, et al., 2018).  
2.3.1 Ionization methods 
It is necessary for analytes to be in the charged state or ionized prior to entering the mass 
analyser, so that the analytes can be transported through the electric and magnetic fields in the 
mass analyser. Furthermore, the ions are transferred to the gas phase in the mass spectrometer. 
Various ionization methods are available and are categorised into hard and soft ionization 
methods. Hard ionization methods often produce fragment ions, whilst soft ionization methods 
provide little or no fragmentation of the analyte, allowing analyses for intact molecular ions. 
Unlike hard ionization methods which operate at very low pressure, soft ionization methods 
operate at atmospheric pressure and are often used in conjunction with HPLC.  
==PAGE:10==
5 
 
Electrospray ionization (ESI) is a soft ionization method where the sample solution is subjected 
to a high voltage (1-4 kV) and sprayed from a capillary into the orifice of the mass spectrometer. 
The analyte is sprayed as charged droplets under atmospheric pressure and a nebulizing gas is 
applied at the capillary to assist droplet formation. As the charged droplets travel across the 
spray chamber, solvent evaporates from the droplet and the charged droplets become desolvated, 
resulting in a decrease in droplet size. Once the Coulomb repulsion force exceeds the surface 
tension (Rayleigh limit), the droplets explode (Coulomb explosion) and are transformed into 
gas phase ions (Przybylski, 2014). ESI is suitable for compounds of moderate to high polarity 
and is applicable for a wide range of molecular weights (Gergov, 2008).  
Another soft ionization method is atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI), where the 
sample solution is vaporized by the heater in the capillary and ionized by a potential created by 
the corona discharge. Similar to ESI and as the name suggests, APCI takes place under 
atmospheric pressure. APCI also involves nebulization and desolvation and uses the same ion 
extraction cone as ESI. However, unlike ESI where ionization takes place in the liquid phase, 
ionization of APCI method takes place in the gas phase (Przybylski, 2014). APCI is suitable 
for compounds with low to medium polarity and has the advantages of less susceptibility to 
matrix effects and tolerance to higher flowrates (up to 2 mL/min) (Gergov, 2008).  
ESI and APCI are the two most widely used ionization methods for LC-MS and are considered 
as complementary methods. Their similarity in instrument design allows switching between the 
two ion sources for LC elution, hence making it convenient to select a more suitable ionization 
method for different compounds using the same elution and analysis method (Rockwood, et al., 
2018).  
2.3.2 Mass analyser 
MS can be employed to analyse specific known compounds (target analysis/target screening) 
or to identify unknown compounds in the sample (non-target screening). Several classes of 
mass analysers are available. Here the focus will be on the two beam-type mass analysers: triple 
quadrupole and time-of-flight (TOF) mass spectrometers. 
Triple quadrupole mass spectrometer is also known as tandem mass spectrometers and is 
denoted as MS/MS. MS/MS consists of three quadrupoles, each a set of four parallel electrically 
conductive rods, in sequence. More specifically, it consists of two mass spectrometers arranged 
in series (denoted as Q1 and Q3) with a collision cell (denoted as q2) in between as shown in 
Figure 3. Depending on the mode of operation, different ranges of m/z can be selected in the 
first and the third quadrupoles by adjusting the applied radio frequency (RF) and direct current 
(DC) voltages. Under selection mode where a narrow range of m/z is set, only ions of the 
specified m/z can travel through the quadrupoles; under scan mode the applied RF and/or DC 
voltages are varied continuously to scan the full mass spectrum (Rockwood, et al., 2018). For 
target analysis, multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) function is typically used (Gergov, 2008; 
Rockwood, et al., 2018). In MRM the precursor ion from the compound of interest is targeted 
in Q1, and is subsequently fragmented in q2, producing a range of product ions that are 
measured in Q3. Only compounds that fit the selected precursor and product ions’ m/z will be 
isolated and further used for quantification. The results are often confirmed by monitoring a 
second transition in the same run. When used in conjunction with LC, the detected compound 
can be verified based on retention time (RT), precursor ion and product ion. This way the 
selectivity and specificity are increased. Other advantages of MS/MS include high sensitivity, 
good reproducibility and relative moderate costs (Rockwood, et al., 2018). The advantages 
make LC-MS/MS the primary choice for target analysis and quantitative analysis.

Figure 3 A schematic diagram for a tandem mass spectrometer linked with HPLC (Creative Proteomics, 
n.d.). 
In TOF-MS the m/z is measured based on the ion’s flight time to travel through the flight tube. 
When entering the TOF mass analyser, ions are accelerated by an electric field and have the 
same kinetic energy. The lighter ions will thus travel faster than the heavier ions, and the flight 
time is used to determine the m/z of the ions. Traditionally TOF-MS is coupled with matrix-
assisted laser desorption ionization due to the compatibility in pulsed technique. Other 
continuous ionization methods such as ESI and APCI can also be employed for TOF-MS. The 
orthogonal accelerator in TOF-MS helps overcome the mismatch between continuous 
introduction of ions from ESI/APCI and the pulsed detection in TOF-MS. TOF mass 
spectrometers are often used in screening due to its wide m/z range, high mass accuracy, high 
acquisition speed and good resolution. Its capability to provide accurate mass measurements is 
particularly useful for identification of unknown compounds in non-target screening.

3.2 Model development 
Sausages were selected as the representative meat product for the study because sausages are 
consumed at the highest volume both in Denmark and globally (Statista, 2021a; Statista, 2021b). 
Frankfurter style sausage, a raw-cooked product, was chosen and the recipe based on (Heinz & 
Hautzinger, 2007) was slightly modified to better fit the aim of the study. Both pork and beef 
were used individually for the lean meat component as the two red meats are the two most 
consumed red meats both in Denmark and worldwide (FAO, 2003; OECD, 2021). 
==PAGE:12==
7 
 
The prepared sausages consisted of lean meat (pork loin or beef flank, 61%), pork back fat 
(7.5%), water (25%), potato starch (3%), sodium chloride (2%), sugar (0.8%), pepper (0.15%), 
paprika (0.5%), sodium nitrite (0.05%), ascorbic acid (0 or 0.13%) and liquid smoke (Try Me 
Hickory Liquid Smoke, Reily Foods Company, 0 or 12.5%). Sodium nitrite was added at an 
enhanced ingoing level of 500 ppm to better observe NA formations. Lean meat and fat were 
minced separately with a meat mincer (Varimixer, Teddy) and then comminuted and mixed 
with the rest of the ingredients in a chopper (Kenwood, CH250), followed by sausage stuffing 
into a sheep casing. After the comminution and stuffing process, a reddening step was applied, 
where the sausages were exposed to moderate heat in a preheated oven at 50℃ for 30 min to 
accelerate the curing process. Afterwards they were submerged in an 80℃ hot water bath for 
10 min to achieve heat coagulation of protein, which gives rise to the characteristic elastic 
texture of frankfurter sausages. The sausages were marked as “raw sausage”, packed in zip-
closure plastic bags and stored at -80℃ at the end of preparation unless further heat treatment 
was applied.  
3.3 Experimental design 
3.3.1 Preliminary study 
The preliminary study was performed to evaluate the effect of sodium nitrite freshness. Two 
different batches of sodium nitrite, one that was opened and has been in use since 2011 (Set A) 
and the other recently purchased, newly opened sodium nitrite (Set B), were used in the pork 
sausage preparation.  
Blank samples (without sodium nitrite) were prepared for each set and spiked with NA 
standards at 0.5 ng/g sausage and/or 5 ng/g sausage to assess recovery of each trial. NA 
concentration was examined based on raw sausages for Set A and B, each analysed in triplicate. 
3.3.2 Storage study 
The storage study aimed to investigate (i) the effect of sausage storage time, (ii) meat type and 
(iii) cooking method on NA formation in the sausages. Pork and beef sausages were prepared 
to examine whether similar NA formation pattern would be observed in sausages of the two 
different meat types.  
An aliquot of each meat type was prepared then divided into two groups; one frozen at -80℃ 
after preparation (marked as “Day 0”), the other stored at 5℃ for 5 days then frozen at -80℃ 
(marked as “Day 5”). A portion of Day 5 sausages underwent two different cooking methods: 
oven-cooked at 190℃ for 20 min and pan-fried on a hot plate at 190℃ with a teaspoon of 
rapeseed oil for 20 min. The pan-fried sausages were turned frequently during the cooking 
process to imitate cooking procedure at home or at food stalls, and to ensure all contact surfaces 
were heat treated. The outline for the storage study design is shown in Figure 4. Recovery of 
each run was assessed by spiking the blank sample with NA standards at 5 ng/g sausage. The 
sausage samples were analysed in triplicate.

3.3.3 Factorial design I (reddening time and submergence in hot water) 
In sausage making a reddening step was incorporated to accelerate the curing process while 
submerging the sausages in hot water helped to achieve protein coagulation, giving rise to the 
elastic consistency of the frankfurter style sausages. It was suspected that the reddening time 
has an impact on NA formation since curing process takes place in this step. (EMA, 2020) 
reported high water solubility for NDMA and NDEA and lower water solubility for NDPA and 
NDBA. The effect of the protein coagulation via submerging the sausages in hot water was thus 
examined. 
This 2-level 2-factor factorial design (full resolution) was set up to examine the effect of 
reddening time at 50℃ (30 min or 120 min) and submergence in 80℃ water (0 min or 10 min) 
on NA formation in the pork sausages prepared with 500 ppm sodium nitrite. In total four 
sausages of each condition (listed in Table 1) and one control sample (0 ppm sodium nitrite, 
reddened for 30 min without submerging in hot water) were made. Recovery of each run was 
assessed by spiking the blank sample with NA standards at 5 ng/g sausage. The sausage samples 
were analysed in duplicate.

3.4 Extraction procedure 
The processed meat sample was first homogenized with a hand blender, 5 g of the homogenized 
sample was then transferred into 50 mL a plastic tube with 41.5 μL of internal standard (0.6 
μg/mL NDMA-d6 and NPYR-d8 in ACN) added to each aliquot. Spiked samples were prepared 
by spiking the blank sample at 0.5 ng/g sausage and/or 5 ng/g sausage in duplicate. 10 mL ACN 
with 1% formic acid and a ceramic homogenizer were added to each sample then shaken on 
Geno/Grinder at 900 rpm for 10 min. After addition of 10 mL purified water, the samples were 
shaken on Geno/Grinder at 1500 rpm for 10 min and centrifuged at 4500 g for 10 min. 2 mL of 
the supernatant was transferred to an Eppendorf tube and frozen (-80℃) overnight. To assist 
phase separation, 5 g ammonium formate was added to the rest of the centrifuged mixture, 
which was then shaken on Geno/Grinder at 1500 rpm for 1 min then centrifuged at 4500 g for 
10 min. 5 mL of the organic phase (top layer) was then transferred to a 15 mL plastic tube and 
frozen overnight.  
The frozen water and organic phase were defrosted the next day. The organic phase was first 
centrifuged at 4500 g for 5 min. 4 mL of the supernatant was then mixed with 400 mg of PSA 
in a new 15 mL tube, then shaken on Geno/Grinder (1500 rpm, 1 min) and centrifuged (4500 g 
5 min). Afterwards, 3 mL of the supernatant was mixed with 100 μL of purified water in a 5 
mL excelo glass and evaporated under a stream of nitrogen at 30℃ to approximately 100 μL, 
then adjusted to 0.5 mL with purified water. The mixture was vortexed and transferred to an 
Eppendorf tube, where it was frozen until solid, followed by centrifugation (14000 rpm, 5 min). 
Finally, 200 μL of the supernatant was added to 5 μL ACN and filtered by a filter vial. 100 μL 
of the filtered solution was transferred to a glass vial and analysed by LC-(APCI)-MS/MS.  
The water phase was thawed and evaporated under a stream of nitrogen at 30℃ to roughly 0.75 
mL, then adjusted to 1 mL with purified water and frozen until solid. The frozen solution was 
centrifuged cold at 14000 rpm for 5 min. 200 μL of the supernatant, 200 μL purified water and 
10 μL ACN was mixed together and filtered by a filter vial. 100 μL of the filtered solution was 
transferred to a glass vial and analysed by LC-(ESI)-MS/MS.

4. Results and discussion 
4.1 Preliminary study 
Mean values and standard deviations for each VNA and NVNA are shown in Table 4 and Table 
5. The presence of seven VNAs and three NVNAs in the pork sausages were investigated, but 
only NDBA, NDEA, NPIP, NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA were detected in the samples. As seen, 
most VNAs were either not detected or below the limit of quantification (LOQ). The most 
frequently found NAs in processed meat, NDMA, NDEA and NPYR (IARC, 2018d), were not 
detected in the sausage samples even though the ingoing amount was drastically higher than 
that in commercial products. This could indicate that the respective precursors were not present 
in the analysed matrix, or that the condition was not optimal for the formation of the VNAs. 
A slight increase in the average NPIP present in pork sausages with 500 ppm new sodium nitrite 
was observed, although the increase was not statistically significant (p > 0.1). For the NVNAs, 
variations in the mean values from using old and new sodium nitrite were seen. NPRO and 
NTCA in pork sausage with new sodium nitrite were approximately twice as high as that with 
old sodium nitrite. Using the t-test analysis it was confirmed that sodium nitrite freshness has a 
significant effect on NPRO and NTCA formation in the examined pork sausages (p < 0.1). 
Even though the shelf life of sodium nitrite is not specified and its chemical stability is noted 
as stable under room temperature (Merck, 2021), (Budavari, 1989) indicated that sodium nitrite 
oxidizes slowly to nitrate in air. Considering that the old sodium nitrite has been opened for 
almost ten years, it is possible that some of the sodium nitrite has been oxidized. As the 
nitrosating process involves nitrite instead of nitrate, the potentially reduced amount of nitrite 
available in the old sodium nitrite could be the reason why a lower overall amount of NA was 
observed.

Two timepoints, Day 0 and Day 5, were selected for the assessment of effect of sausage storage 
time on NA formation in pork and beef sausages. The change in each NA level between the 
two timepoints was evaluated. Furthermore, significance at 90% confidence level for each NA 
level was analysed using t-test: two-sample assuming unequal variances. The numerical results 
for all analysed NAs are presented in Table 6 to Table 9, the change in detected NA levels 
across the storage period are summarized in Figure 6 and Figure 7.

Mean NDBA level in the pork sausages increased from below LOQ to 5.7 ng/g throughout the 
5-day storage period, whereas a slight decrease of 1.8% in NPIP was seen across the same 
storage span in the pork sausages per displayed in Table 6. Increase in mean NMTCA and 
NTCA levels were observed for the pork sausages while mean NPRO level decreased by 10.1%. 
Mean NMTCA level in the pork sausages increased by 68.9% and mean NTCA level increased

Increase in mean NDBA, NPIP, NMTCA and NTCA levels were observed in the beef sausages.

determine the statistical significance of the growth in NDBA concentration in the beef sausages.

that storage time has a significant effect on NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA concentration in the

(Rywotycki, 2007) and (Mah, et al., 2004) suggested that nitrosation precursors are formed 
under microbial activities. Microorganisms assist protein degradation to secondary amines and 
provide a suitable nitrosation environment due to acidification. Furthermore, decarboxylation 
of amino acids by a microbial enzyme generates amine precursors for NPIP and NPYR. Even 
though refrigeration hinders microbial activities, storage of the samples still allowed prolonged 
microbial activities and nitrosation, which can be the contributing factor behind the elevated 
concentration of NDBA, NMTCA and NTCA. However, the decreased concentration of NPIP

study did not comply with the above-mentioned theory.  
It is important to note that the increase in NDBA and NPIP concentration over the 5-day sausage 
storage span is of concern, as the two VNAs are classified as Group 2B possibly carcinogenic 
to humans. Other detected NAs such as NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA are not classified as 
carcinogenic by IARC and are regarded as of low concern in relation to carcinogenicity. 
Therefore despite the high NVNA concentrations present in the sausages, it is not as alarming 
as the presence of NDBA and NPIP.

(Mirvish, 1975) pinpointed that nitrosation kinetics, i.e. reaction equation and rate constant, 
plays a factor in the amount of NA formed. The nitrosation rate of most secondary amines are 
first order to amine concentration and second order to nitrite concentration. Since the ingoing 
amount of nitrite was identical for both sausage matrices, the determining factor is therefore 
amine concentration. The precursors for NPIP such as piperine and piperidine can be found in 
black pepper (Sen, et al., 1973), the other NPIP precursor, lysine, is found in meat products 
(Walters, 1992). A higher amount of lysine is present in lean pork loin (2020 mg/100 g) 
(National Food Institute, Technical University of Denmark, 2019a) than in beef flank (1340 
mg/100 g) (National Food Institute, Technical University of Denmark, 2019b). The greater 
amount of lysine in pork loin explained the higher NPIP level in pork sausages than beef 
sausages despite having the same amount of ingoing nitrite and black pepper. Similarly, the 
precursor for NPRO, proline (Rath & Reyes, 2011), is found at higher concentration in pork 
loin (827 mg/ 100 g) (National Food Institute, Technical University of Denmark, 2019a) than 
in beef flank (721mg/ 100 g) (National Food Institute, Technical University of Denmark, 
2019b), which could be the cause of higher NPRO level in pork sausages. NTCA and NMTCA 
are formed by condensation of formaldehyde and acetaldehyde with cysteine (Walters, 1992). 
Cysteine is a protein component and can be found in meats (Kohlmeier, 2003). Therefore the 
observed higher NTCA and NMTCA concentration in beef sausages could suggest a greater 
level of cysteine in beef flank.

4.3 Factorial design I (reddening time and submergence in hot water) 
NDPA, NMEA and NPYR were not detected in the NA analyses. NDBA and NDEA were 
detected but were below LOQ, thus only indicative observations regarding these two VNAs 
were made. It was observed that the combination of 120 min reddening time without hot water 
submergence led to the highest levels of NDBA and NDEA. Longer exposure to moderate heat 
(50℃ in the reddening step) without hot water submergence also induced higher level of NPIP, 
even though the effect of reddening time and the interaction of the two factors were deemed to 
be insignificant based on statistical analysis results. Hot water submergence, however, had a 
significant effect on NPIP formation at 90% confidence level, as shown in Figure 10. This could 
indicate that NPIP is more sensitive to dissolving in water than exposure to heat, or simply that 
at 80℃ its water solubility is more dominant than the influence of heat. 
Reddening time had an inverse effect on NMTCA at 90% confidence level while hot water 
submergence had a positive effect on NMTCA level at 95% confidence level as demonstrated 
in Figure 10 and Figure 11. Similar to NPIP, a proportional relationship with reddening time 
and an inverse relationship with hot water submergence were noticed for NPRO. However only 
hot water submergence had a statistically significant effect on NPRO level. No significance 
was discovered in relation to reddening time and hot water submergence on NTCA level, but it 
was indicated that the two factors both had a positive influence on NTCA formation in the 
studied matrix per illustrated in Figure 11. Interaction between reddening time and hot water 
submergence had no significant effect on the level of NPIP, NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA.

4.4 Factorial design II (meat freshness, liquid smoke and ascorbic acid) 
Among the analysed VNAs, no detection was made for NDPA, NMEA and NPYR, whilst 
NDEA was detected but was below LOQ. Partial detection was obtained for NDBA, NDMA 
and NPIP, results of some of the factor combinations were either below LOQ or not detected. 
For these three VNAs, t-test: two-sample assuming unequal variances from Excel was 
employed to determine the effect of individual factors where possible. Quantitative results were 
obtained for all three analysed NVNAs for all factor combinations. JMP Pro (version: 15) was 
applied to evaluate the main effects, interactions and desired condition for minimum NVNA 
formation. The summary of main effect and interactions between the factors for NVNA levels 
are presented in Figure 12, with the significance of each factor shown in descending order. 
Prediction Profilers (Figure 13) demonstrated the relationship between each individual factor 
and NVNA level, as well as the desired combination of the factors to achieve the lowest NVNA 
level.   
The sausages prepared with less fresh minced meat seemed to result in higher VNA levels. For 
NPIP the effect of meat freshness (Combination 1 vs Combination 5) was evaluated by t-test 
analysis, and it was confirmed that the increase of NPIP level due to less fresh meat was 
significant at 99% confidence level. Meat freshness displayed high impact on NVNA levels, 
but surprisingly fresher minced meat induced higher NVNA level in the pork sausages. The 
similar phenomenon was observed for pork sausages from the 5-day storage trial, where a 
higher amount of NPRO was found in the fresh sausages.  
Liquid smoke was seen to interfere with the detection of NPIP, as no NPIP could be detected 
in the sausages where liquid smoke was added. Liquid smoke had a strong positive effect on 
NVNA levels, at 99% confidence level for NMTCA and NPRO, and at 95% confidence level 
for NTCA. The addition of liquid smoke induced roughly 9, 3 and 4 times higher NMTCA, 
==PAGE:25==
20 
 
NPRO and NTCA level in the pork sausages, respectively. NTCA and NMTCA are known to 
be formed by condensation of cysteine and aldehydes, which can be found in wood smoke (Rath 
& Reyes, 2011). As liquid smoke flavourings are manufactured by smoke condensates, it is 
highly likely that aldehydes were also present in liquid smoke flavourings, leading to the 
formation of NTCA and NMTCA when reacted with cysteine in meat. (Herrmann, 2014) also 
demonstrated an increase in NTCA with an addition of 2000 mg/kg liquid smoke.  
Ascorbic acid has been reported to have inhibitory effect on NA formation by reducing nitrous 
acid or by acting as a nitrite scavenger (Walters, 1992). (Pourazrang, et al., 2002) demonstrated 
500 ppm and 2000 ppm ascorbic acid had a significant effect on the decrease of NA-induced 
mutagenicity in sausage samples prepared with 150 ppm and 600 ppm nitrite, respectively. 
(Mottram, et al., 1975) proposed a minimum 0.5 molar ratio between ascorbate and nitrite for 
maximum suppression of NDMA formation in cured pork slices at pH 5.6. The role of ascorbic 
acid as a NA inhibitor in vivo has also been reported in various publications and concluded in 
(IARC, 2010). A significant increase in NPIP at 95% confidence level was seen when 
comparing sausages prepared with fresh minced meat added with ascorbic acid, however the 
same effect was not observed when comparing sausages prepared with less fresh minced meat 
added with ascorbic acid. This could suggest interaction between meat freshness and ascorbic 
acid, but due to the lack of data from all combinations, it was hard to draw a conclusive 
observation. On the other hand, ascorbic acid at equimolar ratio to sodium nitrite was proven 
to have a significant effect at 99% confidence level on NVNA inhibition. NMTCA and NPRO 
levels were reduced by approximately 81% with the addition of ascorbic acid at equimolar ratio 
to sodium nitrite, whilst a reduction of about 34% was seen for NTCA level.   
It was seen interaction between liquid smoke and ascorbic acid had a significant effect on 
NVNA formation in the studied matrix, and interaction between meat freshness and liquid 
smoke had a significant effect on NMTCA and NTCA level but not NPRO level. Similarly, 
interaction between meat freshness and ascorbic acid also had a significant effect on NMTCA 
and NTCA level but not that of NPRO. 
Based on JMP analyses the combination of 3-day old minced meat, 0% liquid smoke and 0.13% 
ascorbic acid was predicted to result in minimum NVNA formation in pork sausages. Per 
indicated by the Prediction Profiler results, the corresponding NVNA levels were predicted to 
be 1.1, 17.4 and 63.0 ng/g for NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA respectively in pork sausages 
prepared with 500 ppm sodium nitrite.

Figure 15 Chromatographic peak subtraction diagram (minced meat sample subtracted from blank

but not in minced meat supports the presence of piperidine and N-cis-Feruloyltyramine after

pork back fat, whereas peptides could indicate protein breakdown resulting from sausage 
making. When subtracting blank sausage samples from cured sausage samples, the main 
distinction was the possible presence of lincomycin, which belongs to the class of proline and 
derivatives.  
Compounds belonging to classes of fatty acids, pyrrolidines and amines were found when the 
Day 3 cured sausage samples were compared with Day 0 cured sausage samples. The higher 
level of amines found in Day 3 samples was speculated to be the reason why higher levels of 
VNA was seen in the sausages made with minced meat that had been stored for three days. It 
was interesting that even though pyrrolidines were observed, no NPYR was detected in any of 
the cured sausage samples. This could be because the formation of other NAs was more 
competitive than that of NPYR in the examined matrix.

5. Conclusion 
Use of fresh sodium nitrite was found to lead to higher NA formation in sausages. Sausage 
storage time had a significant effect on NPIP, NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA level in both pork 
and beef sausages. Significant difference in NPIP, NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA levels were 
observed between pork and beef sausages; higher NPIP and NPRO levels were found in pork 
sausages while higher NMTCA and NTCA were detected in beef sausages. Cooking of the 
sausages resulted in significant increases in NPIP, NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA levels but a 
decrease in NDBA level was noticed. The heat-induced protein coagulation stage in sausage 
preparation had a significant inverse effect on NPIP and NPRO formation and significant 
positive effect on NMTCA and NTCA formation. The reddening stage in sausage making had 
a significant inverse effect on NMTCA level. Increased NPIP level and decreased NVNA levels 
were associated with less fresh meat. Liquid smoke was found to interfere with NPIP detection 
and had a proportional relationship with NVNA formation. Ascorbic acid was proved to have 
inhibitory on NMTCA, NPRO and NTCA at equimolar ratio to sodium nitrite. Target profiling 
confirmed the presence of NDBA, NPIP, NPRO and NTCA in the sausage samples, while 
compounds corresponding to black pepper, paprika, pyrrolidines, protein breakdown process, 
prolines, fatty acids were identified from non-target profiling.
