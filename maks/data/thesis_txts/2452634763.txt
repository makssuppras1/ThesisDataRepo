Abstract
Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) are completely biodegradable polyesters with interesting
characteristics for a signiﬁcant number of industrial applications representing a very promis-
ing alternative that can substitute petroleum-based plastics. The current PHA production
scheme is based on a 3-step process, using crude glycerol, a by-product of bio-diesel indus-
try as a substrate. Crude glycerol can be converted to Volatile Fatty Acids (VFAs) and 1,3
Propanediol (1,3 PDO) by fermentation followed by a selective conversion of VFAs to PHA
while leaving 1,3 PDO in the supernatant. Previous research within the group has proved
the capability of mixed microbia consortia to accumulate PHA using this strategy.
Target of the current project was to study PHA accumulation at different concentrations
of VFAs and 1,3 PDO and evaluate their possible inhibitory effect. First of all, Sequential
batch reactor (SBR) feed’s composition was examined during the mixed culture enrichment
phase. Different VFAs composition in the feed of SBR were tested suggested that butyrate
concentration was of highest importance for the performance of the enriched culture during
the accumulation phase. Under this scope, three different concentrations of VFAs (190 CmM,
290 CmM and 390 CmM) and three different 1,3 PDO concentrations (10 g
L, 20 g
L, 40 g
L) were
tested in accumulation phase. The already tested concentration of 90 CmM VFAs and of
4.5 g
L 1,3 PDO was used as a reference in order to evaluate the performance of the high
concentrated batches. The evaluation was based on the speciﬁc consumption rates of VFAs
and speciﬁc PHA production rate and on PHA yields.
The reference batches proved to be very stable with an average VFAs speciﬁc consumption
rate equal to 0.49 CmolVFAs
CmolX·h
and average speciﬁc PHA accumulation rate equal to 0.57
CmolPHA
CmolX·h . The VFAs concentration increase (up to 390 CmM) suggested that no inhibition
was observed at the range tested, since it did not had any signiﬁcant effect on substrate
speciﬁc consumption rates, PHA speciﬁc accumulation rates and PHA yields. 1,3 PDO
concentration increase, however affected negatively the process resulting generally in lower
VFAs speciﬁc consumption rates and lower PHA speciﬁc accumulation rates. Additionally,
1,3 PDO speciﬁc consumption rate was increased, that is detrimental to our process target (
PHA accumulation with a simultaneous recovery of 1,3 PDO) suggesting in total that the
increase of 1,3 PDO higher than 4.5 g
L can be inhibitory to PHA productivity.
In conclusion, the results suggest that PHA accumulation fed-batch process is preferable
working at the reference case concentration of 90 CmM VFAs and 4.5 g
L 1,3 PDO, due to the
inhibitory effect of 1,3 PDO to PHA productivity.

Introduction
Over the last decades up until now, most of energy, raw chemicals and plastics are pro-
duced from fossil-fuel sources, which could be depleted in the following years. This use of
these non-renewable sources also results in extensive carbon dioxide accumulation in the
atmosphere, leading to greenhouse effects. Moreover, the uncontrolled use and disposal of
plastics in the modern society burden the problem of earth’s environmental pollution. In
order to counter these environmental impacts, and prepare for supplement of energy and
chemicals for the future when the non-renewable sources are depleted, substantial steps
have been taken in the recent decade towards obtaining the needed energy and products
from renewable sources. During the last years, ﬁrst steps towards sustainability have been
achieved with the use of renewable energy solutions such as wind, solar and hydro-power
energy. Further steps towards the establishment of an alternative economy should be the
use of waste and biomass as raw materials to produce fuels, chemicals and plastics. The
concept of bio-reﬁnery is getting more and more widespread [1], aiming at the transition
from the fossil-based economy to the bio-based one. A remarkable research is being done
on the ﬁeld of plastics, which up until now is one of the greatest source of waste that has
huge environmental impact on the ecosystem, with a focus on bio-based and bio-degradable
plastic.
In 2017, the global production of plastics reached 348 million metric tons, with 64 million
metric tons produced in Europe alone [2]. Plastics are used in a wide variety of products
and have displaced other materials, such as wood, metal, and glass. Plastics are an essential
part of almost all industries, nevertheless their very desirable properties, such as resistance
to degradation and durability, have now become their greatest problem [3]. Plastic products
usually have single-use applications, especially in food packaging and medical materials.
Since these plastic products are not biodegradable, they are extremely persistent and ac-
cumulate in the ecosystem, resulting in a signiﬁcant burden on solid waste management
[4]. Recycling was suggested to diminish the problem but is very tedious, since the sorting
of the wide variety of discarded plastic material is also a very time-consuming process. In
addition, plastics contain a variety of additives such as pigments and coatings that limit the
possibility of recycling. Since, it is very difﬁcult to reduce the production of plastics due to
their versatile properties [4], the scientiﬁc community focuses on replacing petroleum-based
plastics with bio-based biodegradable plastics. Biopolymers provide the dual advantage
of conservation of fossil resources and reduction in CO2 emissions, which make them an
important innovation of sustainable development.
The most promising biodegradable plastics are poly-lactic acid (PLA), polybutylene
succinate (PBS) and polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA). The ﬁrst two are produced upon poly-
merization of lactic acid and succinic acid respectively, while the latter is produced naturally
by bacteria [5]. PHA are microbial polyesters produced by a wide range of microorganisms
mostly as a energy storage under conditions of unbalanced growth [6]. PHA have properties

2.1
Biodegradable and bio-based plastics
The predictions about the depletion of fossil-fuel sources has lead the scientiﬁc research
towards bio-based plastics. Bio-based plastics are plastics produced by renewable resources
instead of petroleum based ones. Nevertheless, the huge environmental problem that the
earth is experiencing also requires research on the ﬁeld of biodegradable plastics. Biodegrad-
able plastics are plastics that can be degraded through the exposure to naturally occurring
microorganisms.
Bio-based plastics are not necessarily biodegradable.
Biodegradable
plastics have attracted big interest recently between the scientiﬁc community due to the
world-wide problem of petroleum based plastics that have been accumulated in the envi-
ronment. Few examples of biodegradable and bio-based plastics are :PLA, PHA, PBS and
starch-based biodegradable plastics (e.g starch-polyethylene). Polyethylene Terephthalate
(PET), polyamide (PA), polypropylene (PP) are some examples of plastics that are not
biodegradable but can be produced by biomass (bio-based).
2.2
Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) biosynthesis and properties
Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) are biologically synthesized polyesters of hydroxyalkanoates
and they are completely biodegradable to water and carbon dioxide and therefore considered
among the most promising substitutes of oil- based synthetic polymers [8]. Polyhydroxyalka-
noates are polyesters consisting of hydroxy acid monomers (HA) linked by an ester bond.
The general structure of PHA can be seen in ﬁg. 1. The alkyl group R varies from methyl
(C1), or simply hydrogen to tridecyl (C13) [9]. However, PHA with aromatic, halogenated
and branched monomers have been reported as well [9].
Figure 1: Structure of PHA [3]
PHA are synthesized by various bacteria as an energy and carbon source either under
3
==PAGE:12==
limiting conditions of an essential nutrient (phosphorous or nitrogen), or lack of electron
acceptor or intracellular factor (insufﬁcient amount of RNAs or enzymes necessary for
growth) [10, 11, 12], while carbon source is in excess. However, several bacteria can produce
PHA during their growth [5]. PHA is accumulated in the cytoplasm of these bacterial cells
and thus can be considered as an intracellular product. This carbon storage source is been
used for the cells survival under stress conditions. Most of the bacteria are able to accumulate
PHA from 30 % up to 85 % of their cellular dry weight [13]. The average molecular weight
of these polyesters varies from 105 to 106 Da depending on the microorganisms produced
and on their chemical structure [14]. PHA exist as a form of intracellular granules with a
typical diameter of 0.2 ±0.5 µm and can be visualized with a phase contrast microscope
[15]. In ﬁg. 2, PHA under the microscope can be seen. PHA can be divided into two groups
according to the carbon atoms of the monomers units:
• Short-chain length (SLC) (3-5 carbon atoms)
• Medium-chain length (MLC) (6-14 carbon atoms)
Figure 2: PHA granules in various bacteria cells [16]
The chemical synthesis of PHA depends on various factors such as microbial strain,
substrate composition, accumulation conditions and presence of inhibitors [9]. The most
common polymers are polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) and polyhydroxyvalerate (PHV), al-
though there are more than 150 monomers that have been found in more than 300 different
microorganisms [10]. In addition, some bacteria can form copolymers such as poly(3-
-co-3-hydroxyvalerate) or poly(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-4- hydroxybutyrate) when mixed sub-
strate is used [17]. Biosynthesis of the most common form of PHA (PHB) begins with the
condensation of two molecules of acetyl-CoA by β-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase (phaA) that gives
acetoacetyl-CoA [9] .Acetoacetyl-Co is then reduced to hydroxybutyryl-CoA by NADPH-
dependent acetoacetyl-CoA dehydrogenase (phaB). At the end, this compound is used as
the monomer for PHB polymerization by the PHA synthase (phaA) [18]. As discussed
4
==PAGE:13==
before, PHA synthesis is achieved mostly under nutrient limitation conditions. This is
because, during balanced growth conditions, acetyl-CoA is used in the tricarboxylic acid
cycle and then CoA works as an inhibitor to β-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase and therefore PHB
synthesis[9]. In the case of nutrient limitation though, NADH oxidase activity is decreasing
and NADH increases. Therefore, it inhibits citrate synthase and isocitrate dehydrogenase,
and ﬁnally β-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase is not inhibited as before and begins the condensation
of acetyl-CoA necessary for PHB synthesis [9]. The pathway used for the conversion of the
carbon source to acetyl-CoA at the ﬁrst place is dependent mostly on the carbon source
available. Moreover, the carbon source available determines the type of PHA produced. PHA
can be converted again to soluble components by PHA depolymerases and the degraded
materials enter various metabolic pathways [9] in order to be converted to carbon source
available for consumption at conditions of starvation.
2.2.1
PHA properties
PHA are thermoplastic polymers and appear different properties depending on their com-
position (homopolymer or copolymer). Therefore, by controlling the conditions of PHA
synthesis and thus PHA composition, their physical properties can be adjusted depending
on the needs. PHA are less sticky when melting than the traditional petroleum-based
plastics. They are resistant to hydrolytic degradation, but not to acids and bases [18].
These bio-polymers are non-toxic and bio-compatible and therefore can be used in medical
applications. PHB is brittle and stiff and due to its crystalline nature presents difﬁculties
in the processing [5]. However, when it is produced as a co-polymer (e.g P(3HB-co- 3HV),
it becomes less crystalline and more elastic [9]. The rest of its properties though resemble

2.4
Suitable substrates for PHA production
Most of the microbes that are able to accumulate PHA can use a wide range of carbon
sources. Therefore, the choice of the most suitable carbon source depends on the process (the
use of pure or mixed cultures) and its economic requirements [4]. It should be noted that
not only the price of the raw material is important, since sometimes it requires additional
pre-treatment in order to be used as a carbon source from the microbes. In addition , the
ﬁnal product puriﬁcation should be always taken into account, that in the case of waste
streams used as substrates could require more steps and thus be more expensive. One of
the most suitable and available carbon source that can be used for large scale production
is sugars ( glucose, sucrose etc.) [4]. Starch is also suitable. It has been reported thought
that the productivity often is low when using starch [4]. Moreover, pre-treatment of starch
(liquefaction and sacchariﬁcation) adds extra costs to the process. PHA accumulation has
been also reported from alcohols. Alcohols have the advantage of reducing the possibility of
6
==PAGE:15==
contamination in case that a pure culture is used. Nevertheless, the scientiﬁc community is
targeting towards the cost reduction of PHA production and more speciﬁcally of substrate
cost that according to economical evaluation of PHA production, accounts for 70% of the
total production cost [21]. For that reason, industrial by-products have been also used for
PHA accumulation such as molasses[22] and crude glycerol [7, 23]. Another advantage of
the use of industrial by-products as feedstocks is that there is no competition with food
industry. Waste streams have been used as well, such as cooking oil [24]. Finally, vegetable
oils and fatty acids have also characterized as potential substrates.
2.4.1
Crude glycerol
Crude glycerol is a by-product of the bio-diesel industry. Biodiesel is produced via transes-
teriﬁcation of fats and oils. This reaction results in a two-phase product: biodiesel and crude
glycerol. The average glycerol content usually found in crude glycerol accounts for 70%
[25], since it contains also various impurities (water, soap, free fatty acids, methanol)[26].
The increasing trend of the production of bio-based fuels and especially biodiesel, has
caused signiﬁcant surplus of crude glycerol on the market. The scientiﬁc community is
targeting either in crude glycerol puriﬁcation or in its direct use [26]. Henja et al, report
that almost 1 million tonnes of crude glycerol is generated through biodiesel industry [26].
Crude glycerol can be utilized in various applications, the most common of which are :
oligomerization and polymerization, esteriﬁcation and transesteriﬁcation, liquefaction of
biomass and fermentation of it to valuable products such as acids, alcohols and PHA.
2.4.2
Glycerol to PHA
As discussed in section 2.4, glycerol can be used as a substrate for PHA production. In that
case, glycerol is ﬁrst converted to dihydroxyacetone-P and then to glyceraldehyde 3-P . Then
through glycolysis (pathway that carbohydrates use) glyceraldehyde 3-P can be converted to
pyruvate and ﬁnally to acetyl-CoA. Consequently, PHA is formed through the same manner
as described in section 2.2. In ﬁg. 3, the schematic representation of the pathway leading to
PHA formation from glycerol can be seen.

Mixed cultures tend to store glycogen expect PHA as an energy storage when glycerol
or other carbohydrates are used as substrates [28, 29, 30] and thus the yield in this case is
lowered. This is the reason, why most of studies using MMC are focusing on transforming
crude glycerol to volatile fatty acids through an anaerobic fermentation step and then
use these fatty acids as a carbon source for the PHA production [7]. However, with pure
glycerol as a substrate high PHA contents (67%) and yields have been achieved (0.57 Cmol
PHB/Cmol glycerol) [31]. Nevertheless, the results when crude glycerol (by-product of
bio-diesel industry) is used as a substrate in some studies are not so encouraging due to
the presence of impurities contained in crude glycerol. The impurities can be inhibitory
to the bacterial culture cause there is a high amount of methanol, fat and salts [25]. There
are studies though that have achieved similar PHA content as with pure glycerol, having
though lower yields [32].
2.4.3
Volatile fatty acids to PHA
The metabolic pathway for the PHA production from volatile fatty acids varies depending
on the acid. However, all of them have as precursors hydroxyacyl-CoA compounds that
are polymerized to the respective PHA. The main and well-studied acids that lead to PHA
synthesis are propionate, acetate and butyrate. Butyrate is the substrate that is most preferred
for PHA production [33]. First of all, the theoretical yield of butyrate is 0.94 Cmol/Cmol
butyrate, higher than the one of others VFAs (acetate: 0.76 Cmol/Cmol acetate, lactate
0.71 Cmol/Cmol lactate) [34]. In addition, higher consumption rates have been observed
experimentally for butyrate. The reason for the higher consumption rate is probably the
higher afﬁnity of butyrate to membrane transporters than the one of the other VFAs [34]. In

can be seen.
Figure 4: Schematic representation of metabolic pathway for PHA production from VFAs (Modiﬁed
from [10, 12, 35])
More speciﬁcally, acetate forms acetyl-CoA, and then starting with the condensation
of two acetyl-CoA molecules, PHB is formed through the same manner as described
in section 2.2. Butyrate, ﬁrst enters the β-oxidation pathway and it is transformed to
acetoacetyl-CoA. Another option is that butyrate can ﬁnish β-oxidation to give acetyl-
CoA. Acetyl-CoA then can lead to PHB synthesis or to TCA cycle or to biomass synthesis.
On the other hand, propionate and lactate tend to form propionyl-CoA that can later be
converted to 3-hydroxypropionyl-coA and be polymerized to PHP. Alternatively, propionyl-
CoA can decarboxylate to acetyl-CoA. If a molecule of acetyl-CoA and propionyl-CoA
condense, the hydroxyacyl monomer will be 3-hydroxyvaleryl-coA and polymerize into
PHV (polyhydroxyvalerate) [35, 36]. Formic acid has not been reported to be used for PHA
formation. In detail, Hasssan et al. state that consumption of formic acid did not lead
neither to growth neither to PHA while the consumption rate of other acids were lowered
[37]. Temudo et al. state that formic acid at low pH is converted to CO2 and H2, while at
high pH this is not the case [38].

had not been studied thoroughly, but PHA accumulation using alcohols as substrates have
been mentioned [4]. It has been reported PHA accumulation using a Pseudomonas putida
[39] and Chromobacterium strain [40]. In both cases low PHA contents were achieved (not

2.6
PHA production by Mixed Microbia Consortia (MMC)
Another alternative for reducing production cost is the use of mixed cultures instead of
pure strains[18, 55]. In this case, there is no need for sterile conditions and therefore there is
a signiﬁcant reduction in cost. Mixed cultures can be selected from biological wastewater
treatment facilities. Biological wastewater treatment processes are characterized by the expo-
11
==PAGE:20==
sure of microorganisms to transient conditions, where biomass is submitted to alternating
periods of high and low substrate concentrations, or aerobic and anaerobic environments. In
these unbalanced conditions, it has been found that microorganisms respond by the produc-
tion of storage polymers [56]. PHA production from MMC usually is performed in two steps.
The ﬁrst step is a step of enrichment of the culture and its objective is to select and
maintain a microbial culture with high PHA accumulation capacity. In this step, transient
operational conditions (such as feast-famine regime) are imposed to the mixed culture in
order to maximize their ability to accumulate PHA. In the so called enrichment phase,
Sequential Batch Reactors (SBR) are used, and the Hydraulic Retention Time (HRT) and
Solid Retention time (SRT) play a crucial role in the maximum accumulation capacity of
the culture [5]. The microbes that have the ability to accumulate PHA are selected by
the conditions that are imposed on the biological system [18]. Therefore, this approach
combines environmental biotechnology principles with industrial biotechnology, as it uses
mixed cultures as producers of valuable products, sometimes even using waste streams as
substrates [57]. This way, a selection of strains tolerant to the waste streams can be done. The
second step is the accumulation step, where the selected culture from SBR is subjected to
conditions necessary for the maximum PHA accumulation. Finally, the culture is harvested
and PHA is extracted through a series of downstream processes.
In the case of pure cultures, the most suitable substrate is considered glucose and thus is
the most used industrially, however for MMC that is not the case. MMC tend to accumulate
also glycogen in their cytoplasm when they are fed with carbohydrates [29, 31]. As an
alternative in this case, a previous step is required that is the fermentation of the substrate
to volatile fatty acids that is one of the most suitable substrate for PHA production from
mixed microbia consortia. As aforementioned, the scientiﬁc community is targeting to the
use of waste streams as substrates in order to reduce production cost. The cost reduction
comes not only from the operational non-sterile conditions, but also from the use of waste
streams due to the ability of cultures to adapt to various substrate because of their diversity.
Nevertheless, in that case, the results obtained are not close to the ones obtained with
synthetic media and thus not suitable for industrial processes. To conclude, MMC have
achieved productivities and PHA contents similar to the pure cultures when the substrate
is synthetic, but the challenge for MMC is to use waste streams as substrates and achieve
yields and productivities similar to the ones with synthetic media.
2.6.1
Enrichment phase
There are various types of enrichments, the main types of which are brieﬂy described below:
• Aerobic Dynamic Feeding (ADF)
In this type of enrichment, the limiting factor is the carbon source. This process
includes repeated feast-famine cycles, in which the mixed cultures have access to an
excess of carbon source during the feast phase, while during the famine phase the
carbon source becomes limiting. The bacteria that can convert substrate to PHA have
an advantage against the ones that can not, because during the famine phase they can
utilize PHA in order to survive. Therefore, at the end, the mixed culture is enriched
with the bacteria that can accumulate the maximum quantity of PHA. There fave been
reported contents of PHA at around 60 % [5].
The operational conditions of ADF play an important role in the capacity of the mixed
12
==PAGE:21==
culture to accumulate PHA. The dissolved oxygen is an important parameter, and most
studies conclude that low air inlet ﬂow favours PHA synthesis, while high ﬂow favours
growth [10],[27]. Studies show that the pH during the enrichment or accumulation
should not be acidic, because in that case, more acids diffuse into the cells and substrate
inhibition can occur [10]. However, what is more important in the enrichment phase is
the cycle length and the feast-famine ratio. The longer cycle allows the microbes with
higher PHA accumulation capacity to survive [58]. Nevertheless, the substrate-microbe
ratio is also important during the cycle length, because the higher it is, there is more
competition between the microbes for the substrate. Therefore, microbes can have
higher uptake rates [27]. In conclusion, all these parameters combined affect the
feast-famine ratio, that according to most studies should be in the range of 0.1-0.2
[30, 55]. As for the nitrogen availability, excess of ammonia seems to give better results,
since it increases the selective pressure of organisms with a high cell growth rate [10].
In general, the ratio of carbon/nitrogen affect the growth of the microbes during the
feast phase or both in feast and famine phase.
• Anaerobic/Aerobic enrichment (AN/AE)
In this case, microbes during the anaerobic phase, because of the lack of electron
acceptor (oxygen), are able to accumulate PHA. During the aerobic phase then, they
start consuming the PHA stored during AN phase. However, the capacities reported
are low (around 20 % of total dry cell weight [5]).
• Variations of ADF
For example, there have been proposed enrichment types where the nitrogen is
depleting in the feast phase and is in excess during famine phase in order to enable the
growth of bacteria during the famine phase [59]. In addition , ADF enrichment have
been used in halophilic conditions, in the case where the bacteria culture is halophilic.
• No enrichment
Some researchers skip the enrichment step and perform directly the accumulation step
and have obtained PHA up to 70 % [60]. The main reason for skipping the enrichment
step is that even though PHA is formed during the enrichment, it is also allowed to be
consumed in order to drive the culture selection [5]. Therefore, they state that the total
PHA/substrate yields is lowered.
2.6.2
Accumulation phase
After the selection of the culture from the enrichment, the accumulation phase follows,
where nutrient limitation conditions exist in order to allow the maximum PHA accumulation
of the culture. Mostly, nitrogen limitation is used as it increases the PHA content in the
cells and the overall yield [30, 61]. The most important reason for the nitrogen limitation
is the avoidance of non-PHA accumulation microorganisms growth that can be present
in the culture [59]. As for the working temperature and PH most of the studies show
that the same temperature and PH should be kept as the ones used in the enrichment
phase. As in the enrichment phase, acidic conditions are avoided. The reason again is
the acid inhibition probability. There have been applied different strategies regarding the
accumulation phase. In general, batch is avoided in most of the times especially when
the available substrate is concentrated, because the selected culture may face inhibition
phenomena [30, 62] and the productivity can be decreased. As an alternative, fed-batch
13
==PAGE:22==
process is suggested. More speciﬁcally, pulse fed-batch has been proved very effective,
especially when using as indicator changes in pH or DO. When the substrate is consumed
by the mixed culture a change in DO is occurring and can be used as an indicator. As
for the pH, in the case of VFAs used as substrate, the consumption of them lead to an
increase of the pH and therefore it can be used as well as an indicator for the feeding strategy.
2.7
Downstream process
The process of PHA recovery and puriﬁcation is one the main reasons for the high PHA
production cost that rise the question of feasibility of this process. The downstream process
of PHA uses a variation of different techniques, but in general involves 5 steps [5]:
• Biomass harvesting
• Pre treatment
• PHA recovery
• PHA accumulation
• Polishing and Drying
The step of the biomass harvesting requires techniques as ﬁltration or centrifugation in
order to collect the biomass while getting rid of the fermentation broth. The next step of
pre-treatment focuses on facilitating the recovery of PHA from the cells by weakening the
bacterial cell . This can be achieved by lyophilization or heat drying, however these processes
are rather suitable for lab-scale than industrial. Industries are more focusing on either using
more suitable drying techniques, or recovering PHA from wet biomass. Alternative methods
are chemical such as NaClO digestion and physical methods such as high temperature
and ultrasonication methods [5]. PHA recovery can be achieved by several ways. Since
PHA is intracellular, bacterial cell wall has to be disrupted, either by chemical means (e.g
acid or NaOH treatment), either by enzymatic disruption (e.g proteases that can break the
cell wall), or by biological disruption (microorganisms or virus that can liberate PHA) [5].
Another alternative is the use of non-halogenated solvents, that is an expensive solution
and non-environmental friendly. The accumulation step can be done either by precipitation
using alcohols or by ﬁltration and centrifugation and mostly depends on the previous step.
Finally, a polishing step is required by washing with water, alcohols or detergent. The last
step is the drying. All these techniques need to be evaluated economically depending on
the ﬁnal product purity and yield of each process.
2.8
Inhibition phenomena
The three-stage process (crude glycerol fermentation, enrichment phase and accumulation
phase) that is used for PHA production from crude glycerol has already been used in various
studies. The concentration of the fermentation efﬂuent in VFAs is of crucial importance to
the ﬁnal stage of accumulation. Inhibition due to the high concentration of VFAs has already
been reported [30, 37, 62, 63, 64, 65]. In [62], substrate inhibition during the enrichment
stage is reported resulting in a PHB storage rate decrease. Total acids concentration over
90cmM seems to have inhibitory effect on the PHA storage capacity of the culture. In [64, 65],
14
==PAGE:23==
inhibition to PHA accumulation and growth rate by acetate is reported, while Hassan et
al. report inhibition of growth rate by formic acid [37]. However, the inhibition effect of
acids is dependent not only on their total concentration , but also on the pH [63]. The
inhibitory effect is caused by the dissociation of the free fatty acids inside the cytoplasm
[63]. The ratio of dissociated/undissociated acids depends on the pKa of each acid. At
pH equal to pKa the ratio of dissociated/undissociated is 1. At low pH, acids appear in
undissociated form. When acids exist in the media in the undissociated form and enter
the cytoplasm of the bacteria, the dissociation happens in the cytoplasm. The pH of the
cytoplasm will be disrupted and the increased acidity in the cytoplasm can affect negatively
purine bases [66] and most of enzymes that can be denatured [67]. The toxic effect of organic
acids concentration to the cell viability is related both to the concentration of protons and
anions in the cytoplasm. The change of pH is regulated by the transport of protons outside
cytoplasm through the membrane or by decarboxylation reactions [67]. In ﬁg. 6, a schematic
representation of the organic acid toxicity and tolerance mechanisms can be seen.
Figure 6: Schematic representation of organic acids toxicity and tolerance mechanisms in a gram-
negative bacteria [67]
Moreover, crude glycerol fermentation efﬂuent is rich in alcohols and especially 1,3
PDO. It is far well known that alcohols can cause inhibition on bacterial cell growth and
lower production yields [68]. Scientists are already targeting at developing strains that can
tolerate speciﬁc alcoholic compounds [68]. The reason for that inhibition is that alcoholic
compounds interact with the membrane directly and membrane ﬂuidity is affected by their
presence into the membrane [69]. These modiﬁcations in the membrane ﬂuidity can cause
conformational changes in the proteins of membrane [68]. In addition, alcohols can induce
15
==PAGE:24==
partial breakdown of the membrane function [68]. Subsequently, this can cause ions leakage
or loss of energy. The most common example of alcohol inhibition is ethanol production
by fermentation, when the concentration of the product (ethanol) can be inhibitory at
high concentrations for the cells. Even if PHA production by 1,3 PDO is not studied yet
thoroughly and inhibition has not been reported yet in our knowledge, 1,3 PDO has been
already reported inhibitory for cell growth in glycerol fermentation [70, 71].
2.9
Objectives
Intensive research on PHA production from crude glycerol using MMC has been held at the
AT CERE research group. The process ﬂow of PHA production used in the research group
can be seen in ﬁg. 7. As described in section 2.4, when glycerol is used as a substrate for
PHA accumulation, microbes tend to accumulate glycogen expect for PHA. Therefore, the
ﬁrst step for the process of PHA production from crude glycerol is a continuous anaerobic
fermentation which converts glycerol to VFAs and 1,3 PDO. An advantage that results from
this extra step is the ability to direct the PHA content of monomers through the variety
of acids and alcohols. Enrichment step is performed in SBR and operated in cycles using
Aerobic Dynamic Feeding. In the beginning of each cycle, substrate is supplied. MMC is
rapidly consuming the substrate in order to store PHA in their cells (feast phase). Then the
famine phase follows when no substrate is supplied and microbes use stored PHA as an
energy source. At the end of the famine phase a new cycle starts, where part of the reactor is
send to the next step (PHA accumulation) and replaced with fresh substrate. Enrichment’s
feed consists of crude glycerol and VFAs. The reason that the feed contains also crude
glycerol is to avoid any inhibition phenomena coming from crude glycerol when the culture
face the real waste efﬂuent in the PHA accumulation (the ﬁnal step of the production of
PHA) [7]. 1,3 PDO is not provided in the feed of SBR. The reason for that was the hypothesis
that the culture selected would not consume 1,3 PDO in the accumulation phase when
supplied with the real efﬂuent and 1,3 PDO would be recovered as it is a valuable product
[6]. The third and ﬁnal step is the accumulation of PHA that was done up to now in
batch mode. The efﬂuent of VFAs and 1,3 PDO from the anaerobic fermentation is used as
substrate with nitrogen limiting conditions.
Research group previous work was based on a 10 g
L crude glycerol efﬂuent containing
VFAs at 110 CmM (acetate, propionate and butyrate) and 4.4 g

concentrations were tested [72]. A 50 g

PHA monomers were trans esteriﬁed in its presence resulting in propyl-3-hydroxyalkanoates:
propyl-3-HB and propyl-3-HV, which were later quantiﬁed by gas chromatography accord-
ing to [76].
The procedure followed was based on [77] and is shown below:

conditions and the transeriﬁcation reaction. At the end, 100 µL of benzoic acid spike solution
were added as an internal standard solution. Benzoic acid spike solution was prepared by

partially neutralized the acid. Because of CO2 released due to the reaction happening, vortex

L
efﬂuent (excluding formic acid) but in equimolar concentrations. Finally, in the last SBR
(SBRG3), the total 45 CmM VFAs (excluding formic acid) in the feed followed a distribution
proportional to the 50 g
L efﬂuent’s. In the following sections, SBRs evolution will be discussed
and the performance of their enriched cultures in PHA accumulation batches.

4.1.2.4
Performance of SBRs in accumulation batches overview
The comparison of the three accumulation batches was based on the speciﬁc consumption
and production rates and yields. Butyric acid consumption was of great interest since
butyric acid had the highest consumption rate in all batches and during its consumption
the highest PHA accumulation rate was observed. Therefore, except from overall yields,
yields during the butyrate phase (duration of butyrate consumption) were calculated. Total
substrate, VFAs and butyrate’s rates and speciﬁc rates were also calculated because of great
37
==PAGE:46==
importance of VFAs and butyrate in our process.
The rates and speciﬁc rates of the 3 accumulation batches can be seen in Table 11. As
discussed in section 3.7, rates are not always indicating the performance of the culture since
they are inﬂuenced by variations in the biomass concentration. However, consumption
rates for SBRG_B90 were higher, with SBRG2_B90 following and SBRG3_B90 having the
lower rates. As for the speciﬁc consumption rates, SBRG_B90 had higher VFAs and butyrate
consumption rates, while SBRG2_B90 had higher total consumption rate. In fact, this is
due to the fact that in SBRG2_B90, 1,3 PDO was consumed at the same time with butyrate
(total 8 hours) and therefore the overall rate became higher. The total substrate speciﬁc
consumption rate though for SBRG_B90 is lowered since after 4 hours no more acids were
consumed. What is of most importance however, is VFAs and butyrate consumption, since it
is shown that these are contributing more to PHA accumulation, while 1,3 PDO not. Indeed,
this is reﬂected to the PHA accumulation speciﬁc rates that for SBRG_B90 is slightly higher
than the other 2 batches. Therefore, the high total substrate consumption in SBRG2_B90 did
not lead to higher accumulation rate of PHA .
Finally, 1,3 PDO recovery did not vary signiﬁcantly with SBRG_B90 and SBRG3_B90
having an 85 % recovery and SBRG2_B90 an 89 % during the total duration. However,
these numbers are also relative to the active biomass concentration. Therefore, speciﬁc
consumption rates of 1,3 PDO (table C3, table C4, table C5) indicate that SBRG2_B90 had
slightly higher speciﬁc consumption rate. This fact suggests that the highest recovery
percentage of SBRG2_B90 was observed because of lower active biomass present. Moreover,
the recovery rates of 1,3 PDO increase to 95 % for SBRG_B90 and SBRG3_B90, if only the
butyrate phase is taken into account, where the higher PHA content is achieved.

In conclusion, even though only slight differences existed between the 3 enrichments,
SBRG_B90 had the best performance compared to the other reactors, both in terms of
yields and speciﬁc consumption and production rates . Having in mind the difference
of the feed compositions in the enrichment stage of these reactors, one can conclude that
SBRG had better performance due to the presence of higher quantity of butyrate. From the
results discussed above it is shown that probably butyric acid is the one that contributes
more to PHA accumulation [33]. Butyrate high concentration in the enrichment’s feed
probably resulted in cultures that have higher butyrate consumption rates and therefore
PHA accumulation rates during accumulation. During the previous work in the research
group [6], low acetate and propionate consumption rates were observed. SBRG2 was
designed with a feed of equimolar VFAs concentrations based on the hypothesis that if all
acids were present at the same proportions in the enrichment’s feed, their consumption
rates would be similar. However, the presence of propionic and acetic acid in equimolar
concentrations, did not seem to have any signiﬁcant effect, since their consumption was
not signiﬁcant in all 3 cases. On the contrary, in all three accumulations, formic acid that
was absent in all of the 3 different enrichment reactors, had higher consumption rates than
propionic and acetic that were present. As for the absence of lactic acid in the enrichment
stage, it did not seem to affect its consumption rate during accumulation. Overall, the
presence of acetic, propionic and lactic acid in high concentrations did not favour PHA
accumulation rates, reinforcing the argument that butyrate is the acid that has higher uptake
rates and contributes more to PHA accumulation. Finally. since all reactors were stable
throughout their operation and SBRG was the one that had the best performance in the
accumulation experiment, SBRG was used for the further inhibition experiments.

initial media pH was 4, while the target was pH 8. Therefore the time needed for pH to reach
8 was not productive for PHA accumulation and led to lower speciﬁc consumption and
accumulation rates. Indeed, in the ﬁrst experimental set of VFAs 190 CmM, consumption
and accumulation rates of the concentrated batch (VFA 190) are signiﬁcantly lower than
the reference batch resulting in normalized speciﬁc rates equal to 0.7. This is the reason
why the accumulation of 290 CmM VFAs initial concentration was repeated. This is veriﬁed
comparing the results of VFA 290R with VFA 290 (appendix D). For the two remaining
experimental sets (VFA 290R, VFA 390) speciﬁc consumption and accumulation rates did
not vary, resulting in normalized speciﬁc consumption rates higher than 0.9. In fact, the
differences between 290 CmM VFAs batch and the reference batch (90 CmM VFAs) is
negligible with 0.99 normalized PHA speciﬁc accumulation rate and 0.96 VFAs normalized
speciﬁc consumption rate. 390 CmM VFAs batch presents slightly higher differences with
the reference batch but not considerably signiﬁcant.

To conclude, at high VFAs concentrations (up to 390 CmM) there was no inhibition
observed. In fact, the differences between references speciﬁc rates and concentrated batches
ones were negligible. The batch of VFAs 190 CmM initial concentration can not be taken
into account for the discussion, since pH regulation after inoculation seemed to be negative
for the speciﬁc rates (not for yields). As described in section 2.8, in the case of acids
present in the broth, operating pH combined with pKa of acids determines the ratio of
dissociated/undissociated acids that creates possible inhibition effects [63]. At this process
operating pH is 8 and all of the acids present in the media are in the dissociated form at
almost 100% due to their pKa value (Lactic acid: pKa=3.86, formic acid: pKa=3.77, acetic
acid: pKa=4.76, propionic acid: pKa=4.88, butyric acid: pKa=4.82). Therefore, it is unlike
that there is inhibition effect due the possible dissociation of acids inside the bacterial cells
(section 2.8) . A small percentage of undissociated acids however can be present that in
the case of high concentrated batches (as the batch of 390 CmM) it may lead to a higher
total number and therefore a slightly lower productivity. In [63], it is stated that pH plays a
crucial role and that the increase of pH from 7.5 to 8.5 minimized the toxic effect of organic
acids. Seraﬁm et al. that have reported inhibition (at the enrichment stage), have stated that
high pH was favourable for PHA accumulation [62]. As for the batches of VFA 190 and VFA
290 (appendix D) during the ﬁrst 10 minutes that pH was regulated, the presence of more
undissociated acids due to low pH could be a possible reason for the lower observed speciﬁc
VFAs consumption and PHA accumulation rates. Based on the current results, it cannot be
concluded that VFAs at the range tested can cause inhibition neither to PHA accumulation
neither to biomass growth and did not have any negative effect neither on yields nor on
speciﬁc rates.

normalized yields do not follow a linear relationship with increasing 1,3 PDO concentration.

responsible for the observed lower yields. In addition, the normalized yields follow exactly

In conclusion, increase of 1,3 PDO concentration proved to have a negative effect on the
whole process. Lower PHA production rates and PHA yield, lower VFAs speciﬁc consump-
tion rates and higher consumption of 1,3 PDO were observed. 1,3 PDO consumption is
negatively affecting the process since its purpose was the simultaneous PHA accumulation
and 1,3 PDO recovery. It cannot be concluded from the above results if there is a relationship
between 1,3 PDO concentration and PHA yields, VFAs speciﬁc consumption rates, and PHA
accumulation rates. However, it can be concluded that the increase of 1,3 PDO concentration
probably changed the physiology of the culture. When increasing 1,3 PDO concentration,
there was no signiﬁcant differences in the total speciﬁc consumption rates, but the fraction of
51
==PAGE:60==
compounds consumed changed ( higher 1,3 PDO consumption, lower VFAs consumption).
This also affected how the substrate is distributed resulting in lower PHA yields (because
of low VFAs consumption) and higher biomass yields (because of 1,3 PDO consumption).
Moreover, probably more substrate was used for energy and maintenance purposes. How-
ever this cannot be veriﬁed since substrate could be also used for CO2 or other possible
metabolites that were not measured.
The obtained results could possibly be attributed to changes in cells membrane by the
increase of 1,3 PDO concentration. There are not many available information about 1,3 PDO
inhibition and the possible inhibition mechanism. However, in general, alcohols in high
concentrations can allow leakage of essential cofactors and ions from the membrane and
increase permeability in the cells membrane [80]. The possible leakage of essential ions can
cause also loss of energy [68]. The fact that the needs for energy were probably increased
when increasing 1,3 PDO concentration can support this assumption. Therefore, 1,3 PDO
concentration increase could have resulted in the decrease of the effectiveness of membrane
as a semipermeable barrier[80]. Overall, 1,3 PDO concentration increase was not inhibitory
for biomass growth, neither affected signiﬁcantly the total consumption rates of biomass.
However, product inhibition was observed, since 1,3 PDO increase affected negatively PHA
accumulation rates, since MMC tendency to consume VFAs was decreased and 1,3 PDO
consumption was increased lowering thus PHA productivity.

5
Conclusion and future perspectives
In this project, all three sequential batch reactors used were stable during their whole
operation and the different distribution of volatile fatty acids (VFAs) in the enrichment’s
feed did not have any effect on reactor’s stability and PHA accumulation maximum capacity.
However, the effect of the different acids distribution was reﬂected on the enriched culture’s
performance on PHA accumulation phase, suggesting that the high fraction of butyrate in
VFAs feed is of major importance and seems to inﬂuence positively the performance of the
enriched culture.
The main objective of this project was to study the possible inhibitory effects of volatile
fatty acids and 1,3 propanediol (1,3 PDO) concentration on the PHA accumulation. VFAs
concentrations up to 390 CmM were examined and no inhibition was observed during the
accumulation. In fact, PHA accumulation rates and VFAs consumption rates did not vary
signiﬁcantly. As for 1,3 PDO, the increase of its concentration up to 40 g
L was found to
have a negative effect on PHA productivity. VFAs consumption and PHA accumulation
speciﬁc rates were decreased even at 10 g
L of 1,3 PDO, while speciﬁc consumption rate of 1,3
PDO was increased but 1,3 PDO was not contributed to PHA. Higher biomass yields were
observed when increasing 1,3 PDO concentration suggesting that increasing its concentration
affected only PHA production and not growth.
The ﬁnal goal of this study was to ﬁnd a threshold concentration of fermented crude
glycerol efﬂuent without compromising PHA productivity. The fermented crude glycerol
efﬂuent consists of 510 CmM VFAs and 23.3 g
L 1,3 PDO, concentration that would be in-
hibitory for the accumulation process. Therefore, using this efﬂuent at high concentrations
of 1,3 PDO (higher than the reference case of 4.5 g
L 1,3 PDO) would decrease productivity.
However, the fed-batch process has to be designed carefully, since almost non consumption
of 1,3 PDO will result in 1,3 PDO accumulation in the reactor and after 10 g
L of accumulated
1,3 PDO, PHA productivity will be affected negatively.
As a future work, since there were no replicates of the accumulation batches performed, a
ﬁrst step would be to perform so, in order to verify the obtained results. Additional steps
would require a fed-batch accumulation with real efﬂuent (not mix of real and synthetic) to
be performed to check for possible inhibitory effects because of the accumulated impurities
of crude glycerol. Finally, a economical evaluation regarding the negative impact of the
high 1,3 PDO concentration versus PHA productivity would determine the feasibility of the
process and possible next steps.
