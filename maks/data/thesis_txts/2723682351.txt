Abstract
This work designs and implements a scalable distributed controller for per-
forming leaderless formations of micro aerial vehicles (MAVs) in 3D space.
The design is capable of handling network changes in the form of MAVs that
are joining or leaving the network during online operation while maintaining
stability and feasibility. The architecture employs a distributed cooperative
model predictive control (MPC) to optimize the system performance while
satisfying various physical constraints of the vehicles and the desired for-
mation constraints. The proposed method for distributed MPC takes the
coupling dynamics into account in the form of separable terminal costs and
time-varying terminal sets to provide guarantees of global stability.
The
optimization problems are solved using ADMM as the distributed optimiza-
tion method. The architecture leverages relative sensing and benefits from
communication with the neighbors during the closed-loop operation. This
design has been validated in MATLAB simulations. The results not only
show the good formation performance of the proposed method while satis-
fying various constraints but also demonstrate that MAVs can be endowed
with plug-and-play capabilities in a distributed and safe manner.
Keywords: Formation Control, Micro Aerial Vehicles, Distributed MPC,
ADMM, Plug-and-Play control, MATLAB

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
With the advancements in autonomous systems technology and computer
science, robotics is undergoing a major transformation in scope and dimen-
sion. With the rapid development in communication technologies and com-
putational power, networked multi-vehicle systems have received increasing
attention over the last decades. A particular application hereof is the forma-
tion control of multi-vehicle systems, which forms the basis for applications
such as cooperative transportation by small automated guided vehicles or
cooperative surveillance. Therefore, it becomes crucial to develop intelligent
solutions to control spatially interconnected systems with state and input
constraints.
1.2
Motivation
Multirotor systems such as quadcopters have seen exponential growth in
their use in many applications due to their high maneuverability and low
costs. In the scope of Micro Aerial Vehicles (MAVs) that perform formations,
there is an extensive contribution to control algorithms with Model Predic-
tive Control (MPC) [1] being one of the most efficient ones [2]. Conventional
methods, like Proportional, Integral, and Derivative (PID) controllers or
Linear Quadratic Regulators (LQR), can also be used for formation con-
trol, but they do not support adding explicit constraints. MPC is good at
handling explicit constraints and also provides guarantees on the stability
and feasibility of the system. The design and deployment of a centralized
MPC controller is not scalable due to sudden changes in computation and
communication needs when dealing with a reconfigurable network. Hence,
it is desirable to design interacting local controllers which rely only on lo-
cal information and computing power. The downside of such controllers is
their dependency on the network topologies, as they are designed with a
prescribed structure, and one has to reformulate them anew if the network
were to be modified. Moreover, the rigid structures of the solvers prevent
the algorithms from revealing their full potential. The goal of this project
is to investigate, design, and analyze an MPC-based formation control algo-

rithm for MAVs that is both modular and scalable, allowing subsystems (or
robots) to join and leave with minimal supervision effort and performance
degradation.
1.3
Literature Survey
Model predictive control (MPC) is an optimization-based methodology that
is well-suited for constrained linear systems [3].
Despite recent advances
in computation and communication technologies, formulating and solving a
large optimization problem in real-time remains a challenging task. As a
solution to this, several methods leverage the distributed structure of the
network and decompose the global optimization problem into smaller cou-
pled subproblems.
Distributed model predictive control (DMPC) approaches can broadly
be categorized into non-cooperative and cooperative ones.
In the non-
cooperative DMPC approaches, each subsystem considers the coupling of
the neighboring subsystems as a disturbance in its own dynamics and con-
straints.
The most common approaches are tube-based methods, where
the effects of states and inputs of neighboring subsystems are confined to
a bounded set. Although computationally simple and effective in practice,
non-cooperative approaches can be conservative in the presence of strong
coupling. However, cooperative DMPC approaches require significant com-
munication and computational resources, since a system-wide MPC problem
is formulated and solved. These approaches generally involve the communi-
cation of planned control sequences or state trajectories between neighboring
systems. An advantage of cooperative methods over non-cooperative ones
is that they can guarantee convergence to the optimal solution of the global
optimization problem. In all these approaches, a terminal cost based on Lya-
punov stability concepts and an invariant terminal set satisfying the state
and input constraints is designed to guarantee the stability and recursive
feasibility of the closed-loop system.
In the scope of formation control, many researchers have proposed differ-
ent ways of implementing MPC techniques to control multi-vehicle systems.
An ADMM-based DMPC strategy to maintain formation while moving to-
ward a desired target is presented in [4]. The formation control problem
is converted into an optimization problem that allowed the splitting and
merging of agents while traveling in formation as proposed in [5].
Both
studies only included simulative experiments in 2D space. Formation con-
trol in 3D is achieved by combining consensus-based control with collision
avoidance control in MPC in [6] and by implementing a distributed MPC
control using relative-based sensing and estimation in [7]. Other interesting
and important references consist of [8] [9] [10].

There are also various contributions in the literature for multi-vehicle
formation control without employing an MPC framework. A decentralized
control methodology based on local sensing for the 3D encirclement problem
is presented in [11]. A rigidity-based bearing formation controller for a team
of quadrotors is presented in [12]. A real-time receding horizon optimization
of trajectories was applied to guarantee rendezvous for mobile robots in [13].
In all of the methods listed above, the controllers are designed for a fixed
number of vehicles in the network and do not handle network modifications.
In order to achieve such flexibility, the design of local controllers should be
scalable.

In the literature on scalable MPC, there is this concept of Plug-and-
Play (PnP) design [14] capable of adding or removing subsystems in a safe
manner. The idea of PnP design is depicted in 1.1. Existing approaches
are either decentralized [15] or distributed [16] MPC architectures. The for-
mer uses a tube-based MPC approach assuming state feedback controllers
and control-invariant sets to achieve robustness against coupling. This type
of PnP-MPC is applicable if the subsystems are weakly-coupled, thus not
suitable for formation control with strong coupling and system interactions.
Also, the computation of invariant sets is complicated and time-consuming
for a large network of interconnected subsystems. On the other hand, dis-
tributed PnP-MPC relies on a distributed invariance based on structured
time-varying terminal sets that are synthesized offline and updated during
online operation.
The main idea behind PnP schemes is to modify only

those subsystems affected by the network modification. The key problem
in scalable design is how to guarantee collective properties in spite of the
limited information available for local control synthesis.
Although there
are significant contributions to the theory of scalable MPC design, they all
validate their methods on numerical problems in simulation.
1.4
Proposed Solution
This project aims to design and implement a scalable Distributed MPC for
formation control of MAVs that allows them to be flexible to network mod-
ifications. More specifically, the control algorithm should be able to stably
adapt to any number of robots entering/exiting the swarm performing for-
mation without any supervision. The novelty of the proposed method is
the network-independent structure of the distributed local controllers that
perform formation based on relative sensing in the 3D space.
The con-
troller design can be broadly divided into two steps: 1) design distributed
controllers to be solved by individual subsystems using distributed optimiza-
tion, and 2) synthesize local control laws to ensure stability and recursive
feasibility of the closed-loop operation. During a PnP operation, as opposed
to the general notion of only modifying the affected subsystems, we redesign
and modify the local controllers of all the subsystems in the network for bet-
ter and optimal performance that more closely corresponds to the central
optimal solutions.
The flexibility of the control algorithms has a significant effect on the
performance outcomes of a distributed system. Consequently, in the end,
this thesis tries to briefly analyze the effect of various parameters on the
overall performance of the control algorithm with respect to the chosen
metrics.
1.5
Scope of the Project
The final goals of this project are as follows:
1. Review the main quadrotor theory (modeling, estimation, and control)
and MPC theory (design, stability, and feasibility).
2. Select a suitable scalable MPC with appropriate control architecture
for formation control using a group of quadrotors.
3. Execute a simple simulation in MATLAB for formation control with
canonical MPC methods.
4. Design and implement the selected scalable MPC approach in MAT-
LAB to validate the model.

ual rotors. The overall system is underactuated with translational velocities
controlled by the system dynamics.
We consider the full rigid-body dy-
namics of the quadrotor, including only the dominant effects due to the
propellers based on [17].

3.1
Nominal Linear MPC
For this project, linear MPC has been considered using the linearized quadro-
tor model as a prediction model.
Model predictive control has its roots in optimal control. The important
constituents of MPC are the system dynamics prediction model, the initial
state of the system, the constraints, and the objective function (cost). MPC
can be considered to be an improvement over the Linear Quadratic Regu-
lator (LQR) controller. LQR is a regulator in the form of an unconstrained
optimization problem that is solved for an infinite time horizon for a lin-
ear dynamical system. Extending this formulation by adding constraints to
the inputs and states of the system can lead to an optimal solution, as real
physical systems have limits. Unfortunately, for constrained problems, it is
impossible to compute the optimal control exactly. However, it is possible
to approximate the optimal control by replacing the infinite-time limit with
a finite-time N, named the horizon. Additionally, we define a terminal cost
to approximate the tail of the cost and terminal constraints to approximate
the tail of the constraints. For a nominal MPC, feasibility, and stability are
not guaranteed. Thus, these terminal conditions help explicitly in ensuring
these properties.

3.2
Distributed MPC
MPC can have different control structures depending on how the system is
connected and how it optimizes the problem. Firstly, there is the centralized
case where a single agent will compute optimal inputs and solve the global
problem, then there is the decentralized controller where every subsystem
is independent and solves a local problem without any communication with
other subsystems, and finally, there is the distributed controller that ex-
changes information to optimize the local control inputs. For the purpose
of formation control, a centralized structure is not ideal as it doesn‚Äôt scale
well with larger groups and a decentralized controller does not accurately
approximate the global objective due to a lack of communication. Therefore,
a distributed MPC was chosen for the project.
This section will describe the formulation of distributed MPC for a net-
work of dynamically coupled linear systems. The literature on distributed
MPC mainly distinguishes between non-cooperative and cooperative ap-
proaches.
In the former, neighboring subsystems generally communicate
once per time step and each subsystem then solves the local MPC prob-
lem. These approaches are robust against coupling to neighboring subsys-
tems and become very conservative in presence of strong coupling in the
local dynamics. Also, it does not provide global stability guarantees as ev-
ery subsystem selfishly solves its local problem. In cooperative distributed
MPC approaches, neighboring subsystems iteratively communicate typically
multiple times per time step to optimize the central MPC via distributed
optimization. Taking inspiration from [20] and [21], the following sections
will discuss in detail the formulation of the cooperative distributed MPC
approach employed in the project.

4.1
Cooperative Distributed MPC
Existing cooperative DMPC strategies typically solve an optimization prob-
lem in a distributed fashion in every update cycle and generally involve
multiple iterations which require substantial communication between the
neighboring agents. In practice, this often results in slower update rates as
individual agents have restricted computational power and communication
capabilities.
Taking inspiration from [4], we decouple the subproblems to reduce the
computational load and solve them using a modified ADMM approach which
only solves one ADMM iteration per time step to further reduce the amount
of communication during distributed optimization.

4.2
Plug and Play Operation
In this section, we discuss how to handle network modifications during
closed-loop operation and use the previously introduced techniques to guar-
antee stability and control of the modified network. It is assumed that the
desired modifications are not immediate and a PnP request is made to check
whether the topology change is feasible. The goal of the PnP operation is to
update the local controllers according to the modifications and to ensure the
modified system is feasible at the current system state. The PnP operation
can thus be divided into two steps:
1. Redesign Phase: Redesign local controllers and synthesize terminal
laws for the modified system.
2. Inspection Phase: Check for the feasibility of the modified system
under new control laws starting from the current system state.
Note that when a PnP request is made, we redesign the local controllers
of all the subsystems in the network to obtain optimal control laws, and
also since we do not perform formation control on a very large network of
drones.
We classify the network modifications or PnP requests into two types:
1. Plug-in request: When a new agent wants to join the network.
2. Plug-out request: When an existing agent wants to leave the network.
The proposed procedure is summarized in the Figure 4.1.

System Design
The controller structure employed in the simulation is as shown in Fig-

formation control based on MPC. This form of cascaded arrangement helps
to separate the high-frequency (attitude) control from the low-frequency
(formation) control.

The first step in the simulation is to perceive neighbors to calculate the
average relative formation displacement. Every drone has information about
the relative distances it has to maintain from its neighbors. It also receives
the neighbors‚Äô predictions, according to the Algorithm 2. This information is
then sent to the DMPC controller that performs one iteration of ADMM to
compute optimal attitude (tcmd) and thrust (Tcmd) references for the given
horizon. The corresponding control inputs (t‚ąó

This complete process is repeated at every time step until a PnP request is
initiated.

5.5
Discussion
From all the above simulation results, we can see how the performance of
the proposed method is quite comparable with the baseline MPC, which
is a non-cooperative distributed MPC for formation. In particular, from
Figure 5.4a, we can say that there is not much difference in performance
in terms of formation error, peak error, and fall time when the new drone
communicates only with one existing drone to maintain formation. We can
see in Table 5.4 that for every iteration our proposed method required almost
3 times more data to be communicated. Also, for every drone, this amount
increases linearly with its number of neighbors but is well within the range of
a wifi module that is generally available in practice. Though it is a drawback
of the method as using the baseline method one can get similar performance
without the cost of communication, it does not provide any guarantees on
the stability of the global system as at every iteration it selfishly solves the
local MPC. On the other hand, our proposed method relies on shared data
to solve the local problems that estimate the global objective. Additionally,
the local terminal costs and sets help provide stability guarantees. Now,
there are ways to improve the quality of the solutions of the baseline MPC
like increasing the horizon or manually adding terminal conditions. In the
former case, the solver times will increase, and in the latter case, it will have
to be manually tuned and might be network dependent which simply is not
scalable. Moreover, Figure 5.5 shows how sizes of the local terminal set vary
over time as it depends on its local states and the states of the neighbors
while satisfying the global terminal condition (PM
i=1 őĪi ‚Č§őĪ). This makes
the approach more flexible in principle without compromising stability.
The difference in the performance is noticeable in Figure 5.4b when the
new drone has 2 neighbors to maintain formation with. Evidently, case B
has a higher overall formation error than case A. This is due to the fact that
in case B, when the network is modified, it directly affects both drones 1 and
2, whereas in case A the modification only affects drone 1. Performance wise
it lags behind the baseline in terms of fall times. This can be explained due
to additional constraints like terminal sets making the overall performance
slightly more conservative than the baseline. This conservatism provides
us with stability guarantees without much compromise in performance. It
is interesting to note that with higher formation error we observe faster
convergence. This is justified as case B has a redundant connection between
drone 2 and drone P allowing drone 2 to actively reduce formation error,
while in case A drone 2 was reacting to the error propagated via drone 1.
This not only makes the system faster to respond but also makes it safer as
the system has to satisfy more constraints.
The comparison of the two methods to maintain formation with 4 drones
is shown in Figure 5.8. We observe behavior and performance similar to
those observed in the previous results. Here, it is interesting to note that

==PAGE:48==

the proposed method becomes more conservative when more drones are
modified in the network. Figure 5.12 shows the effectiveness of the proposed
method for a distributed network topology with 5 drones. Furthermore, from
Figure 5.9 and Figure 5.13 we notice how the starting local terminal set sizes
inversely vary with the total number of drones in the network. Resulting in
a stricter constraint at the beginning when the network is modified. This
is due to the averaging of neighbor states in the local controller model.
This suggests that the local relative states (‚ąÜxi) will be less affected by an
error due to a single neighbor as the number of neighbors increases. As a
consequence, we get a slower response to formation errors. The fall times and
maximum formation errors in Table 5.5 and Table 5.6 confirm this behavior.
Looking at the solver times from Figure 5.6, we can clearly see how in
the case of the baseline method, the drone with more number of neighbors
takes the longest amount of time to find the optimal solution. Whereas, in
our proposed method, the difference is not much apparent as they all took
comparable time solving the optimization problems. Similar is the case in
the scenarios with 4 and 5 drones performing formation. Note that for our
method, the reported solver times represent the total time taken to solve
both the optimization problems (primal and slack update). Still, we get
faster solving rates overall because by averaging the neighbor‚Äôs information
it sort of behaves like a single neighbor. Another observation is that the
solving times decrease for both methods as the drones converge to the desired
formation, which is intuitive as the inputs, and so do the states, will not
change much. Interestingly, this rate of decrease in solver times is higher in
our method compared to the baseline for all the cases and scenarios tested.
An important thing to remember is that we use MATLAB solvers fmincon
and quadprog as they are quick to implement and our objective is to show
the effectiveness of our method. In practice, we can use external solvers like
ACADO [26] that uses code generation to export the methods which can be
solved with much more accuracy and speed. This can reduce the solving
times by a factor of around 100, thus facilitating the practicality of our
proposed method.
To summarize the trends for the results, we can say that the communi-
cation rates increase with the number of neighbors for both methods. Re-
garding solver times, the baseline method has a strong positive correlation
with the number of neighbors, while the proposed method is much less af-
fected by it. The performance as a group depends on the network topology
with lower fall times for a lesser number of drones in the network or more
redundant connections in the topology. Also, our proposed method is more
conservative with a larger group size compared to the baseline method.

5.6
Challenges
During the development of the proposed method, we encountered a lot of
challenges. This section will briefly highlight them and discuss the solutions
if they were found.
Firstly, since we average the neighbors‚Äô information during online opera-
tions, we lose the sense of individuality among our neighbors. This inhibits
us from putting constraints on individual neighboring agents like adding a
minimum safe distance to avoid collisions during formation control. One
way to circumvent this with the current implementation is to compute the
weighted average by assigning higher weights to the immediate neighbors
than the ones relatively far away. Although this does not allow us to pre-
vent collisions, as we do not have dedicated states for each neighbor.
Secondly, during the synthesis of local terminal costs, the optimization
problem composed of LMIs 3.27-3.28 often fails as the variables Ei ap-
proaches singular values, thus making the problem infeasible. We increased
the tolerances (mentioned in the Appendix) in the solver‚Äôs configuration to
avoid this issue. It is a trade-off between the accuracy of the converged
solution and the overall performance.
Lastly, performing formation while in motion adds many complications
to the problem. There is difficulty in converting the tracking MPC to regula-
tion MPC as there exists no steady state for nonzero velocity references. One
alternative is to redefine local states such that they include relative velocities
instead of ego velocities. This, when linearized and discretized, results in an
input-coupled system which has to be converted to an input-decoupled sys-
tem by state augmentation. Due to time limitations, we restricted ourselves
to static formation in the scope of this project.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
and
Future Work
Designing a modular and scalable controller for multi-robot formation is a
significant challenge. This problem is of great interest due to its applications
to various use cases like surveillance, inspection, etc. This project presents
a scalable and distributed architecture for formation control that utilizes
strong coupling between subsystems and provides stability and feasibility
guarantees. In contrast to the literature, our approach can handle network
modifications online and provides stability guarantees for the global sys-
tem. The proposed method can be extended to any network of dynamically
coupled systems like a thermal power plant, water tanks, etc.
At the beginning of the project, we studied the theoretical concepts
of predictive control and its design in chapter 3. A distributed coopera-
tive structure was selected and implemented to carry out the formation of
MAVs. A linear model of the quadrotor was used to reduce the computa-
tional load online. A structured global MPC problem was formulated with
suitable objective functions and system constraints. This global problem
was decomposed and decoupled into subproblems for each subsystem. The
optimal values for the local terminal costs and sets were computed. We im-
plemented distributed optimization during closed-loop operations using the
ADMM method. In case of network modification requests, we first redesign
the local controllers accordingly and check their feasibility for the current
system state. If permissible, we modify the local controllers and perform
formation with a modified network.
Finally, we tested this approach in
MATLAB with various simulation scenarios and compared its performance
with an appropriate baseline method.
Due to time constraints, comprehensive tests with a high-fidelity sim-
ulator or with real hardware could not be conducted.
Nevertheless, the
MATLAB simulation results show that adding terminal conditions makes
the performance more conservative but benefits from inter-robot commu-
nications and provides feasibility assurances. By averaging the neighbors‚Äô
information, the size of our formation model remains the same and does not
increase with the number of neighbors. Also, we see slightly better solver
times that decrease at a higher rate as the formation converges. Although
in our proposed method we communicate extensively, the total amount of
50
==PAGE:51==
data is well within the permissible limits of the available hardware devices.
Finally, this thesis presents a scalable and distributed formation con-
trol design method with plug-and-play capabilities. The simulation results
demonstrate how, by adding more constraints and taking advantage of com-
munication, we can ensure the feasibility and stability of the global system
without compromising much on the performance.
There are various directions for future prospects of this project work.
Firstly, validate the performance of the simulation results by implementing
it in a faithful high-fidelity simulator like WEBOTS and then with real
hardware. Furthermore, perform formation control while moving at constant
velocity and test various plug-and-play scenarios. One interesting direction
for future work is heterogeneous formation. Lastly, to examine the effect of
sensor noise, model imperfections, and communication delays on the overall
performance.
