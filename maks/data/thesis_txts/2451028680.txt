Project formulation
Project formulation
Recently, 3D printing of liquid inks has been employed for fabrication of microphys-
iological devices.[1] The speciﬁc inks employed in this study permits preparation
of materials with a range of mechanical, electrical and biological properties. This
platform therefore provides a very attractive basis for materials preparation and
modular design.
However, the formulation of these inks were based on solubi-
lization of known polymers in diﬀerent organic solvents, which is not desired for
incorporation into processes working with living cells. The objective of this MSc
project is therefore to investigate the possibility to use emulsion processes to pre-
pare polymers that can be formulated into liquid inks applicable for 3D printing.
This should be done by combination of classical emulsion polymerization[2], ad-
vanced controlled radical polymerization methods such as e.g. reversible addition
fragmentation chain-transfer (RAFT) polymerization[3].
The objective of this project is to prepare a range of polymer emulsions for
3D printing of inks. An initial literature study should be conducted on emulsion
polymerization and particularly controlled radical polymerization methods applied
in emulsion polymerization.
The experimental focus will be on preparation of
diﬀerent polystyrene (PS) emulsions, where a simple PS based emulsion system
will be used as a model system. This should be used as a basis for preparation of
a range of diﬀerent copolymers, enabling latent cross-linking of the systems after
removal of the solvent from the emulsion. Depending on the literature study, a
range of attractive copolymers should be decided and targeted for preparation as
emulsions. As part of the project, controlling the viscosity of the systems either
through up concentration[4] or with ﬂow modiﬁers[5] should be investigated.
The prepared polymers should be isolated from the emulsion systems and char-
acterized by use of standard techniques such as FT-IR, SEC, DSC and NMR. Fur-
thermore, the possibility to control the viscosity and ﬂow properties of selected
prepared emulsions should be assessed by rheology, which should ﬁnally lead to
emulsion systems that could be tested in 3d printing.

Abstract
Abstract
The ability to formulate emulsion based DIW inks has through this study
been investigated for their use within biocompatible applications. Several
emulsion systems of hard and soft polymer properties were synthesized by
use of both classical emulsion polymerization and controlled radical polymer-
ization with reversible addition fragmentation chain-transfer, which allows for
tailoring of polymer properties based on their structural and chemical copoly-
mer composition. From formulation studies on hard polystyrene emulsions,
10wt% Texanol wrt. solid content were required to induce coalescence of
latex particles during water evaporation, but ultimately resulted in a brit-
tle material, unable to be used as a DIW polymer system. Instead, a hard
and ductile polymer system of poly(BMA-co-MMA) exhibiting no brittleness
with Texanol was utilized, and further formulated with organic and inorganic
thixotropic agents to achieve a ﬁnal ink exhibiting the required thixotropic
behaviour for DIW.
To achieve full dispersion of thixotropic agents without inducing latex
particle destabilization, low shear magnetic stirring at 50rpm was required
resulting in a homogeneous ink exhibiting thixotropic shear-thinning be-
haviour. From formulation series of methacrylate polymer emulsions with
various thixotropic agents, gelatin formulated inks exhibited the highest sta-
bility towards shear forces enabling them for DIW 3D-printing at printing
resolutions of 400µm.
From the gelatin formulated ink, three dimensional structures of sub-
sequently printed layers could be achieved, but suﬀered from ﬂow due to
gravitational forces. These formulated inks could subsequently be utilized
in biocompatible applications as an enzymatic screening platform, where li-
pase adhesion to the surface was achieved, exhibiting enzymatic activity 24h
after printing in a polymer platform of poly(BMA-co-MMA) with 1.9wt%
gelatin. This study serves as a proof of concept, as the polymer system can
be tailored, formulated and applied within biocompatible DIW applications,
where hazardous volatile solvents are incompatible.

Resumé
Resumé
Muligheden for at formulere emulsionsbaserede DIW inks er igennem dette
studie blevet undersøgt for deres videre anvendelse i biokompatible applika-
tioner. Forskellige emulsionssystemer af både hårde og bløde polymeregensk-
aber blev syntetiseret med klassisk emulsionspolymerisering samt kontrolleret
emulsionspolymerisering med RAFT, hvilket muliggør skræddersyede poly-
meregenskaber baseret på deres strukturelle og kemiske sammensætning. Fra
formulering af hårde polystyren emulsioner var det nødvendigt at inkorporere
10wt% Texanol med hensyn til faststof for at inducere sammensmeltning af
latexpartikler under fordampning af vand, men ultimativt resulterede det i
et sprødt materiale som ikke kunne anvendes. Et hårdt og duktilt polymer-

Texanol, blev i stedet for anvendt og formuleret med organiske og uorganiske

texpartikel destabilisering, blev lav shear magnetisk omrøring på 50 rpm an-
vendt, hvilket resulterede i en homogen ink, som udviste thixotropiske shear-
fortyndende egenskaber.

emulsioner med forskellige thixotrope agenter, udviste gelatin formulerede

studie egner sig som et proof of concept, da det anvendte polymersystemet
kan blive skræddersyet, formuleret og derefter anvendt i biokompatible DIW
applikationer, hvor giftige og ﬂygtige solventer ikke egner sig.

1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Three-dimensional printing has since its invention in the 1980’s seen major
improvements to both cost, material range, and precision.
The advances
in technology over the last couple of decades have drawn attention to the
ﬁelds of application, in which 3D printing oﬀer several advantages over con-
ventional mold-processing with reduced waste generation, complexity, and
precision. Direct ink writing (DIW) is a novel 3D printing method, which
enable direct printing of high precision three dimensional structures.[6, 7]
DIW has granted the possibility to manufacture microﬂuid devices suitable
as microchannel reactors, and in recent years been of interest in the ﬁeld of
biomedical devices.[8]
Several biomedical devices have been developed utilizing DIW 3D printing
of polymer with diﬀerent biosensors. One such system developed by Spence
et al.[9] is able to monitor dynamic pharmacokinetic parameters in vitro,
compared to an otherwise static environment. Recent advances within 3D
printing of biomedical devices, which allows for continuous monitoring, has
been investigated by Lind et al.[1] In this study, integrated sensors are printed
in combination with biocompatible inks. The microphysical device (MPS)
was engineered to continuously monitor the stress cardiac tissue experience
during contractions in vitro, which can be used to investigate cardiac drug
response.
For biocompatible applications, the printable inks consisting of polymeric
materials have to be inert towards biological tissue paired with good mechan-
ical and chemical stability. Emulsion polymerization is a method of achieving
such inks, where the medium for polymerization is water, compared to poly-
merization in solution, as most solvents for polymers are harmful in biological
applications. To achieve printable inks prepared by emulsion polymerization,
several techniques are available to modify ﬂow properties, as emulsions do not
by nature acquire a high viscosity. Furthermore, as complexity of applications
increase, so does the need for specialized inks. Combination of emulsions with
controlled radical polymerization (CRP) techniques such as reversible addi-
tion fragmentation-chain polymerization (RAFT) enables selective engineer-
ing of copolymers with variable molecular weights of low polydispersity index
(PDI). Copolymerization with RAFT enables latent crosslinking of printed
inks, which can enhance the mechanical properties and stability of printed
inks. The possibility of crosslinking layered inks when printed yields selective
mechanical properties of the ﬁnal product as type and density of crosslinking
can be tailored towards a speciﬁc application, whether in biomedical or other
complex 3D printed applications.

2.1
Emulsion polymerization
Emulsion polymerization is the most common method of industrial polymer-
ization techniques, as it oﬀers several advantages over both bulk and solution
polymerization techniques.[10] Advantages of emulsion polymerization arise
from an industrial point of view by utilizing water as the continuous phase
compared to otherwise hazardous solvents where complete removal is often
hard to achieve, time-consuming and expensive. For bulk polymerization,
industrial manufacturing and processing is often limited due to high viscos-
ity achieved during polymerization, which has severe complications. Aside
from removing the need for hazardous solvents, water, being an environmen-
tally good choice, possesses a high heat capacity, which enables dissipation
of heat generated from exothermic polymerizations. Disadvantages of us-
ing emulsions arise from low latex particle concentration, which leads to low
storage capacity, and emulsifying agents are often hard to remove entirely.
The mechanism of emulsion polymerization was investigated by Harkins[2]
in 1947, which is based on three separate phases. Phase I constitutes initia-
tion of radicals in the continuous aqueous phase, where they can react with
a minuscule amount of monomer present, due to low solubility. A theory
proposed by Maxwell et al.[11] in 1991 describes how entry of radical species
formed in the aqueous phase can enter micelles containing monomer for fur-
ther polymerization. For a radical species to enter micelles in the emulsion,
a critical degree of polymerization, z, must be achieved before entry into
micelles can occur. Maxwell et al. studied a range of monomeric species
where the critical length for hydrophobic styrene (St) was calculated to have
a DP=2, while more polar monomers such as vinyl acetate had a critical
length of DP=7-8.
Once the radical species have entered micelles, prop-
agation in the micelle proceeds, and coalesce with micelles containing no
monomer or polymer, until 10-20% conversion have been achieved. At this
point, micelles are disrupted and surfactant molecules adsorb to the surface
of polymer particles yielding latex particles as seen in Figure 1.
Phase II consists of further propagation of polymer particles which pro-
ceeds with zero-order kinetics by migration and diﬀusion of monomer from
monomer droplets to polymer particles, which are stabilized by adsorbed
surfactant. At 60-70% conversion phase III occurs, where monomer droplets
vanquishes, and propagation continues with respect to monomer concentra-
tion in solution and polymer/monomer particles.

2.1.1
Stabilization of emulsions
The principle of emulsion polymerization is to utilize emulsifying agents or
surfactants to create a dispersed phase of micelles in the continuous aqueous
phase, where hydrophobic monomers can reside and polymerize. Surfactants
used in emulsion polymerizations can either be ionic or non-ionic species,
but the common trait is they both possess the ability to lower the interfa-
cial surface tension between aqueous phase and dispersed phase in an oil in
water (O/W) emulsion. Emulsion stability can be achieved either through
electrostatic or steric stabilization, both of which reduce the eﬀect of Ost-
wald ripening followed by coalescence and phase separation.[12] Commonly,
the DLVO theory is applied to such systems to evaluate stability based on
particle-particle interactions in the system, and optimization of an emulsion
system stability can be conducted based on this theory.
Ionic surfactants, either anionic or cationic, such as sodium dodecyl sul-
phate (SDS) and cetrimonium bromide (CTAB) respectively, are some of the
most applied ionic surfactants for emulsion polymerization.[13] Extensive use
of these surfactants are due to high availability at a low cost, spherical micel-
lization above critical micelle concentration (CMC), and good amphiphilic
properties. Furthermore, isolation of polymeric product from emulsion is pos-
sible through breakage of emulsion system with either counter-ionic species
or acid, which destabilizes electrostatic repulsion.[14] Non-ionic stabilizers
which are not able to provide electrostatic stabilization between particles
can instead provide steric stabilization. Polyethylene oxide-derivatives are
most commonly applied in emulsion polymerizations as they provide good
environment for a variety of monomers during emulsion polymerization.[13]

2. Litterature study
Combination of electrostatic and steric stabilization can further increase
emulsion stability. Such system could be between ionic SDS and non-ionic
nonyl phenoxypolyethoxylethanol (NP-40) as investigated by Chern et al.[15].
A ratio between SDS/NP-40 (20/80) was optimal as the combination between
electrostatic and steric stabilization yielded least amount of ﬂocculation dur-
ing conventional emulsion polymerization of styrene with potassium persul-
phate (KPS) as initiator, while maintaining steady polymerization rates.
The aforementioned surfactants and combinations result in conventional
emulsion polymerization as described by Harkins[2], but as conventional
emulsions are thermodynamically unstable systems coalescence is impeding.
More stabilized emulsion systems known as miniemulsions, have the ability to
retard coalescence for longer period of time, and is characterized by decreased
particle and monomer droplet size. Miniemulsions can be prepared by use
of ionic surfactants in combination with a costabilizer, which mode of ac-
tion is to further stabilize the interface between water and monomer droplet.
Costabilizers such as cetyl alcohol[16] and hexadecane[17], have previously
been utilized in miniemulsions where droplet size decreased from 1-5µm[18] as
observed in conventional emulsions to 50-500nm with soniﬁcation.[19] Retar-
dation of coalescence is achieved in these systems, due to particle nucleation
in monomer droplets causing low retained polymer particle size.
2.1.2
High solid content emulsions
There are several methods for creating high solid content (HSC) emulsions,
which all have a solid content above 60wt%. HSC latexes prepared by emul-
sion oﬀer several advantages beyond reduced water content evaporation re-
quired for DIW inks, such as reduced shrinkage after drying and increased
viscosity. The latter is crucial during 3D-printing, as it reduces ﬂow and en-
ables layered printing. The limiting factor of HSC latexes is the eﬃciency of

2.2
Polymerization methods in emulsion
Utilizing emulsions as reaction condition enables large scale production of
latex particles, where propagation can be facilitated through diﬀerent mech-
anisms of polymerization.
The most common technique employed on an
industrial scale is free radical polymerization (FRP), due to cost, low com-
plexity, and how well deﬁned the system behaves.[10] The use of FRP in
industrial application has its merits when the formulated latex particles are
low in complexity and polydispersity index (PDI) not needing to be low.
A higher PDI for FRP are caused by termination occurring in the radical
species during propagation, which leads to polymers with disperse molecular
weights.
When applications are more complex and speciﬁc, the method of FRP
is often insuﬃcient as complex patterned copolymers can not eﬀectively
be achieved due to reactivity ratios seen for FRP, thus diﬀerent methods
of controlled radical polymerization (CRP) techniques can be applied such
as reversible addition fragmentation-chain transfer polymerization (RAFT).
RAFT is one of the most versatile CRP techniques compared to atom transfer
radical polymerization (ATRP) or nitrogen mediated polymerization (NMP)
in terms of monomer availability for polymerization. NMP has its limitations
on homopolymerization of methacrylates while ATRP are not able to poly-
merize acid containing monomers. These limitations are not found utilizing
RAFT, which is beneﬁcial for complex polymers with low polydispersity and
structural designs of copolymers.[25]

will evaporate from the 3D printed material, and to achieve a ﬁnal product

2.4.3
Latent crosslinking
For softer polymeric networks, increasing the mechanical and thermal prop-
erties is beneﬁcial for their incorporation in 3D-printed materials. A novel
method of achieving selective properties is to introduce latent crosslinking
induced by evaporation of water after printing of the ink. Latent crosslinking
can be attributed to interactions between polymeric chains, which can either
be done by hydrogen bonding interactions or chemical bond formation, bond
strength increasing in same order. The former possessing thermoplastic be-
haviour and latter possessing thermoset behaviour depending on crosslinking
density.
As seen in nylon, hydrogen bonding forces contribute to both mechanical
and thermal stability, which can be utilized by incorporating hydrogen bond
donors and acceptors into the backbone as copolymers. The most commonly
utilized hydrogen bonding species are carboxylic acid, amides, and alcohols,
as they possess both donor and acceptor ability, and can readily be incorpo-
rated as pendant groups through RAFT mediated emulsion polymerization.
As water evaporates, the intermolecular distance between pendant hydro-
gen bonding group reduces and intermolecular forces increase. By utilizing
hydrogen bonding forces, ﬁnal material properties will be dependant on ap-
plication temperature, as increased temperature breaks hydrogen bonding,
which subsequently can be formed by cooling. Pendant ionic groups exhibit
same behaviour, but equal for these systems are they possess self-healing
properties. A strongly hydrogen bonding moiety, ureidopyrimidine acrylate
(UPyA), investigated by Feldman et al.[42] possess the ability to hydrogen
bond four times on the same pendant group. UPyA’s ability to hydrogen
bond is illustrated in Figure 9.
In their study, random copolymerization of BA with UPyA (up to 15mol%)
was investigated. The resulting copolymer exhibited increase of glass tran-
sition temperature, storage moduli, and creep resistance as a function of
increased UPyA incorporation. All of these eﬀects could be attributed to

functional groups. One such application, which utilizes pendant functional
groups, is enzyme immobilization. Enzyme immobilization can be achieved
by anchoring, entrapment or adsorption, important for all is that the reten-

In biocompatible applications involving contact between printed material
and tissue, rheological properties between the two species need to be similar
to mimic biological function. Applications, such as MPS, are in direct con-
tact with soft tissue and thereby require mimicking of rheological properties
to aquire representative results. A MPS developed by Lind et al.[1] is able to
monitor stress induced in cardiac tissue during contraction, where a PDMS
based ink (Young modulus of 1.28 MPa) was utilized during DIW printing.
The material utilized compares to cardiac soft tissue, and thus mimicks rhe-
ological behaviour, which allows for more precise measurements by reducing
stress diﬀerence in the two materials. For harder polymeric materials such
as PS, which elastic modulus is higher by a factor 103, copolymerization can
be utilized to reduce diﬀerence between the two interfacial species.
The ability to tailor polymer properties shows great versatility, enabling
formulated inks prepared by emulsion polymerization to be utilized in various
biocompatible applications, without use of hazardous volatile solvents.

3
Experimental plan
Synthesis of inks which can be utilized for DIW 3D-printing are to be for-
mulated by both FRP and RAFT emulsion polymerization. Reactions are
seen in Scheme 9. Initially, PS inks will be formulated by conventional emul-
sion FRP, stabilized with ionic surfactants or biocompatible non-ionic sur-
factants, to achieve both monodisperse (1.1) and polydisperse (1.2) PSD
emulsions, the latter prepared by SCEP. In both systems, molecular weight
are to be controlled by chain transfer agents. Investigation of HSC emulsion
will be conducted based on these systems to achieve inks, with reduced water
content. (1.3) Flow modiﬁcation to achieve shear-thinning, high viscosity
behaviour of these systems will then be achieved by incorporation of fumed
SiO2 at low concentration. These systems will then subsequently be tested
for their rheological properties followed by water evaporation and creep test-
ing. Subsequently, these inks are to be utilized in DIW 3D printing. These
PS systems will be used as a model system for copolymer systems prepared
by emulsion RAFT polymerization.
Based on results from the PS model system, inks are to prepared by
RAFT as it enables copolymerization to achieve inks with varying types
of functionalization. RAFT mediated emulsion polymerization enables se-
lective tailoring of chemical properties and can be achieved by both ran-
dom or block copolymerization, such as di- or triblock, with functionalized
monomeric species either pendant or incorporated into the backbone. One
functionalization type is the incorporation of a strongly hydrogen bonding
moiety, such as ureidopyrimidine methacrylate (UPyMA), which inﬂuence
the behaviour of the ﬁnal material with self healing properties and reduced
creep. Incorporation of UPyMA into PS (2.1) yields a hard copolymer sys-
tem (A), while incorporation of UPyMA into polymers such as BMA, BA, or
isoprene (I) (2.2) yields soft copolymer systems of (B-D) respectively, which
attain thermoplastic behaviour with both hard and soft regions. Varying de-
grees of UPyMA incorporation will be investigated and its inﬂuence on the
ﬁnal material for all systems. (2.3) Subsequently, ﬂow modiﬁcation based
on the model PS systems, creep testing, and DIW 3D printing are to be con-
ducted for the individual systems (A-D) and for blended systems containing
(A) and (B/C/D).
If time permits, surfactant free RAFT polymerization with macro RAFT
agent are to be conducted, as inks prepared by emulsion polymerization
are well suited for biocompatible applications due to lack of small molecule
surfactants.

4. Results and Discussion
4
Results and Discussion
The following results and discussion is based upon experimental work con-
ducted as described in Section 7.
4.1
Synthesis of polymer systems
Within the ﬁeld of biocompatible applications, both hard and soft polymer
systems are utilized depending on the speciﬁc applications. Hard polymer
systems are often utilized in conjunction with proteins as they serve as a
platform for protein immobilization for further screening studies. For the
hard systems to be applicable in biocompatible applications they need to
be inert towards biological substrates, ie. not interfering with their biologic
functionality. Softer polymer systems in biocompatible applications are as
previously described in Section 2.4.4 utilized when a biological function is
mimicked (such as cardiovascular tissue), where ﬂexibility and material soft-
ness are required to avoid bioﬁlm formation between polymer and tissue. To
investigate the ability to produce both hard and soft biocompatible systems,
two hard systems and one soft system were synthesized. The two hard sys-
tems will be based on pure PS and a copolymer system of MMA/BMA as
these types of polymer systems are inert towards biological substrates, the
former commonly utilized as a platform in ELISA studies. The choice of two
separate hard systems will be discussed later in Section 4.2.
4.1.1
Hard PS system
Initial synthesis experiments were conducted on hard PS systems to investi-
gate the stability of the resulting batch emulsion polymerization. Emulsiﬁ-
cation of such conventional emulsion polymerization systems can be achieved
by numerous surfactant compositions, which determines the type of micelle
structure. To avoid complications during polymerization, a surfactant sys-
tem of anionic sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) was utilized as this surfactant
exclusively form spherical micelles above CMC, which at lower solid content
(SC) emulsions would not aﬀect viscosity and behave Newtonian. To further
stabilize the emulsions, sodium phosphate (dibasic) (SPD) was incorporated
to reduce head-group distance between individual SDS molecules resulting in
a reduced risk of micelle disruption. To initiate polymerization of styrene, a
water soluble initiator of potassium persulphate (KPS) was utilized and due
to its highly polar nature, initiation of styrene monomer would almost exclu-
sively occur in the aqueous phase. The reaction scheme for polymerization
of PS by FRP is shown in Scheme 10 for a 25wt% SC emulsion resulting in
PS(SC25).

Hard BMA/MMA system
Another type of hard polymer system of interest is methacrylic systems,
which similar to PS possess hard biocompatible polymer properties.
By
copolymerizing diﬀerent methacrylic monomers, physicochemical properties
such as hardness and glass transition temperature can be tailored towards a
speciﬁc application depending on the types of monomers incorporated and
their composition.
Initial experiments for hard methacrylic systems were conducted to in-
vestigate the thermodynamic properties of varying copolymer compositions
utilizing the same emulsifying system as previously investigated for PS emul-
sion polymerizations. Two methacrylic monomers, butyl methacrylate and
methyl methacrylate, were investigated as they have a glass transition tem-
perature of 20oC and 105oC respectively, where a resulting copolymer system
can be tailored within these boundaries. FRP of such system is well suited
to achieve a random copolymer composition as the molecular structure of
the two monomers are highly similar, resulting in equivalent reactivity ratios
as investigated by Brar and Kapur[47] for a copolymerization between MMA
and BMA at 70oC. In their study, the reactivity ratios for both MMA and
BMA was determined to be close to 1, indicating no preference for either
homo- or crosspolymerization of monomers, resulting in statistical random
copolymerization.

4.2
Formulation and 3D printing
For the synthesized polymer emulsion systems to be printable by DIW, for-
mulation is necessary to achieve a ﬁnal ink which possess the rheological
behaviour required; high viscosity at low shear paired with shear-thinning
behaviour. Several possibilities of formulation are available to achieve these
rheological requirements, such as increasing solid content to near theoret-
ical maximum packing capacity or by introduction of a thixotropic agent.
As high solid content emulsions were hard to achieve by conventional emul-
sion polymerization, rheological behaviour were achieved by formulation with
thixotropic agents. The mode of action which increases viscosity is the for-
mation of a three-dimensional network throughout the emulsion at a critical
concentration, which readily can be sheared into small agglomerates resulting
in shear-thinning behaviour. At cease of shearing, these smaller agglomerates
are able to regenerate their three dimensional structure as seen in Figure 16,
which enable their use in ink formulation for DIW printing.

4.2.1
Formulation of PS emulsions
Formulation of PS emulsions were initially investigated as PS has a wide
application area within biocompatible applications. Based on previous ob-
servations, PS(SC25) exhibited the highest stability during polymerization,
and was for this reason chosen initially as the system to be formulated into a
printable ink. To achieve thixotropic behaviour in the aqueous latex system,
hydrophilic thixotropic agents are required to ensure formation of a three-
dimensional network throughout the emulsion. One of the most common
thixotropic agents applied in aqueous systems within food and cosmetic in-
dustries are inorganic fumed SiO2, due to its hydrophilic, non-toxic nature
and was with high availability initially incorporated into PS(SC25). As a
critical concentration of fumed SiO2 are required to form a three-dimensional
network within the emulsion, a series of fumed SiO2 formulations from 2-
8wt% into PS(SC25) were investigated. To ensure adequate dispersion of
SiO2 particles, each sample was speedmixed at 3500 rpm for 90s to shear
SiO2 agglomerates from each other as seen in Figure 18, resulting in a well
dispersed, formulated system.
Figure 18: Speedmixing of SiO2 into PS(SC25) to ensure adequate dispersion of
fumed SiO2 particles represented by black dots.
From the series of formulated systems, a critical concentration at 6wt%
SiO2 was observed, where the viscosity transitioned from a viscous liquid to
an almost solid consistency at 8wt% SiO2. No signiﬁcant viscosity change
was observed below 6wt%, indicating that no complete three-dimensional
network was formed in these formulations. These observations correspond
to the results of Raghaven et al.[5] as described in Section 2.4.1, where vis-
cosity of formulated systems are highly sensitive towards small diﬀerences in
thixotropic agent concentration above the critical concentration.
For the formulated systems to be applicable in DIW, individual lay-
ers have to withstand the gravitational forces exhibited from subsequently
printed layers to prevent ﬂow prior to water evaporation. From the series of
SiO2 formulated systems, three systems with respectively 6.5, 7 and 8wt%
SiO2 were syringe printed as these exhibited viscosities in a range where ﬂow
of initial layers would not occur. The three formulated inks were syringe

The high fragility observed in the samples was hypothesized to be caused

causes could ultimately result in the lack of coalescence. rendering the for-

concentration of thixotropic agent, which could increase the inter-particle dis-

particles. To reduce the overall thixotropic agent concentration while retain-

To investigate the critical concentration of gel formation for NC, a formu-
lation series was conducted in which a threefold decrease in thixotropic agent
concentration exhibited equivalent viscosity and gel formation compared to
SiO2 formulated systems. By incorporating NC as the thixotropic agent, a
critical concentration was determined to 2.25% incorporation where a viscous
liquid was achieved, transitioning to near solid behaviour at 2.75wt% incor-
poration. Three formulations of PS(SC25) with 2.25, 2.5 and 2.75wt%NC
were then syringe printed to investigate if lack of coalescence was caused by
high thixotropic agent concentration. After water evaporation, the samples
exhibited brittle and fragile behaviour similar to SiO2 formulated inks, indi-
cating that the governing factor of no coalescence is a result of high interfacial
stability between PS particles.
To induce coalescence between PS particles, it is necessary to shift the
total energy balance towards attractive forces as the interparticle distance is
reduced during water evaporation. A method to achieve attractive interpar-
ticle forces is to introduce a coalescence aid (CA) to the formulation, which
mode of action is to swell and wet the surface of PS particles, which increase
the aﬃnity for coalescence between particles in close vicinity. For a CA to be
eﬀective it needs to fulﬁll two requirements, which is the ability to wet the
particles ie. the polymer system needs to be soluble in the CA, and possess
a boiling point higher than water to prevent evaporation prior to water from
the emulsion. To evaluate the coalescence in a CA formulated system, a ref-
erence of pure PS(SC25) was placed on a hydrophobic PVAc/ester surface
followed by subsequent water evaporation at ambient temperature for 24h as
seen in Figure 21.

for PS and possess a boiling point higher than water at 111oC. Introduction
of toluene into PS(SC25) was done by speedmixing to disperse and ensure
proper wetting of PS latex particles, and was done post-polymerization to
prevent coalescence of the latex particles during polymerization. Three sam-

4.2.2
Formulation of BMA/MMA emulsions
As PS was found too brittle for applications within DIW emulsion based inks,
other hard polymer systems were investigated. To ensure compatibility with
the previously investigated CA, hard methacrylate polymer systems were
chosen due to their systematic use with Texanol within the paint industry.
By utilizing a copolymer system of hard, yet ductile BMA/MMA copoly-
mer, the fragility of the previous brittle systems could be bypassed, resulting
in polymer systems able to withstand higher internal stress. Furthermore,
by employing a copolymer system, tailored properties can be achieved de-
pending on the application as previously shown in Section 4.1.2, where the
glass transition temperature could be modiﬁed depending on the copolymer
composition.
As the hard systems are required to retain their physical properties at
room temperature, BMA100 could not be utilized as the polymer system
possess a glass transition temperature of 23oC resulting in softening and po-
tential creep at room temperature. These hard properties were still retained
for the diﬀerent BMA/MMA copolymer systems, which enable them to be
utilized as an ink resulting in hard systems after water evaporation. To inves-
tigate and compare the three BMA/MMA copolymer systems in their ability
to coalesce, each system was formulated with equal amounts of thixotropic

agent, but suﬀered from poor resolution governed by printing head size and
uncontrollable material feed during extrusion, the latter being a result of the
nozzle size and yield stress exhibited by NC formulated inks. To circum-
vent the poor printing resolution, smaller printing heads are required, but
as the NC formulated ink exhibited destabilization at shear rates lower than
the approximated values for smaller nozzle sizes, other types of thixotropic
agents are required to formulate an ink exhibiting stability towards shear
rates exerted during the process of DIW 3D-printing.

Alternative thixotropic agents
To increase DIW printing resolution, ink stability was to be improved as NC
formulated inks of the previous type displayed insuﬃcient stability at higher
shear rates. Several hydrophilic thixotropic agents of both inorganic and or-
ganic nature were investigated in their ability to formulate a DIW ink, which
retain stability at higher shear rates. Initially, two other types of montmo-
rillonite NC’s (Delite HPS and 67g) were freeze dried and investigated to
observe whether higher resolution printing could be achieved with NC for-
mulated inks in general. From a series of critical concentration experiments,
only Delite HPS was able to adequately be dispersed by magnetic stirring at
50rpm, where Delite 67g would agglomerate excessively in contact with water
from the emulsion, resulting in poor exfoliation of the NC. From the series
of HPS NC formulations, a critical gel concentration was found at 5.8wt%
incorporation, which were then evaluated by optical microscopy as seen in
Figure 34.

was not achieved indicated by large agglomerates.

and polymer particles have a critical lower printing resolution of 800µm.

Biocompatible applications
For biocompatible applications, the hard DIW 3D-printed BMA80MMA20_1.9%Gel

5. Conclusion
5
Conclusion
Formulation of emulsion based DIW 3D-printing inks have through this study
been researched to achieve printable biocompatible polymer materials. Ini-
tially, two hard polymer systems of PS and poly(BMA-co-MMA) and one
soft polymer system of poly(BA-b-St) were synthesized by use of free radi-
cal and RAFT emulsion polymerization, in which polymer properties could
be selectively tailored towards biocompatible applications as conﬁrmed by
analytical techniques such as 1H-NMR, COSY, DSC, SEC and ATR-FTIR.
Formulation was initially conducted on PS(SC25) emulsions, as this sys-
tem is extensively utilized in biocompatible applications and exhibited the
highest stability during emulsion polymerization with an emulsifying system
of SDS/SPD. From syringe printing studies, lack of coalescence between PS
latex particles were observed, where incorporation of 10wt% Texanol wrt. SC
was required to induce coalescence during water evaporation, which resulted
in no high internal stress evaluated by optical microscopy. Further formula-
tion with thixotropic agents resulted in highly brittle materials, which would
collapse by the least exerted force. To circumvent the high brittleness of
PS, a ductile polymer system of BMA80MMA20 was investigated with var-
ious thixotropic agents. To fully disperse the diﬀerent thixotropic agents,
low shear magnetic stirring was required as speedmixing resulted in destabi-
lization of the formulated ink due to shear forces between thixotropic agents
and latex particles. From the investigated thixotropic agents, gelatin formu-
lated inks with 1.9wt% incorporation exhibited the highest stability towards
shear forces, resulting in a formulated ink which could be printed in multiple
layers at a resolution of 400µm by DIW 3D-printing. The 3D-printed ma-
terials exhibited slight ﬂow after printing, which stems from low viscosity of
initially printed layers not being able to withstand the gravitational forces
of subsequently printed layers.
This adverse eﬀect were caused by either
too low gelatin concentration or lack of gelatin three dimensional network
regeneration as the formulated non-Newtonian ink possess a time dependant
viscosity after shearing.
From the gelatin formulated polymer system, a biocompatible assay plat-
form could be achieved, which was investigated by an enzymatic lipase assay.
In the assay, surface activity of immobilized lipase was observed 24h after
printing, showing that the polymer matrix provides a suitable environment
for enzymes. From this study, it was shown that biocompatible emulsion
based DIW inks could be achieved, tailoring the polymer properties towards
a speciﬁc applications, in this case a platform for enzymatic assays.

6. Outlook
6
Outlook
To further investigate and develop the emulsion based DIW inks, higher solid
content systems would be of interest to obtain through eg.
SCEP, other
surfactant systems, or phase inversion, as it would result in a formulated
ink which would require a lower thixotropic agent concentration, or none
if close to the theoretical maximum packing, while having reduced shrink
during water evaporation. These higher solid content emulsions could then
be formulated with gelatin and characterized with an shear step analysis, to
investigate the recovery time of the gelated three-dimensional network, which
correlate directly to the rate at which subsequent layers should be printed
with to prevent ﬂow. Based on the investigations, formulation of the soft
copolymer system of poly(BA-b-St) could be conducted to further expand
the biocompatible applications, which for this system could be utilized as an
interfacial soft polymer to human tissue, but would require an increase in
Young modulus. This could be achieved with either a change in copolymer
composition and/or use of triblock copolymer systems polymerized by RAFT.
This study serves as a proof of concept, as the DIW inks polymer com-
position can be tailored to speciﬁc applications depending on the physico-
chemical properties required. To illustrate the wide application area, ther-
moset polymers could be achieved through incorporation of latent crosslink-
ing groups induced by water evaporation, or covalennt immobilization of
proteins could be achieved through pendant electrophilic groups incorporated
into the polymer backbone.

Methods
FT-IR spectroscopy
Analysis of polymeric material by FT-IR was done by attenuated total re-
ﬂectance fourier transformed infrared (ATR-FTIR) spectroscopy. Charac-
terization was performed on a Nicolet iS50 FT-IR, which did 32 scans per
measurement with a resolution of 4 cm-1.
Diﬀerential Scanning Calometry
Diﬀerential scanning calorimetry was performed on a DSC Q1000 from TA
instruments. Heating and cooling rates were set to 10oC/min from -90oC to
230oC for two cycles. Glass transition temperatures determined at endother-
mic discontinuity inﬂiction point.
Size Exclusion Chromatography
Size exclusion chromatography was performed on a Viscotek 200 instrument
with two PLgel mixed-D columns (Polymer Laboratories (PL)) assembled
in series with an attached refractive index detector. The mobiles phase was
THF (1 mL min-1) operating at room temperature. Molecular weights were
then calculated using PS standards from PL employing TriSEC software.
NMR
NMR spectroscopy was perfomed on a Bruker Avance 300 MHz spectrometer
utilizing deutorated chloroform (CDCl3) as solvent referenced according to
Journal of Organic Chemistry 1997.
Freeze drying
Freezedrying of nanoclay was performed by creating a dispersion of the chosen
nanoclay by mixing with a Turrax set at 20.000 rpm. The dispersion was
then frozen by liquid nitrogen while rotating to increase the surface area
for water to sublimate. The frozen dispersion was then put in a CHRIST
ALPHA 1-2 LDplus freezedryer with an ice condenser unit set to -60oC. A
two stage sublimation process was initiated by utilizing a pressure of 0.3
mbar overnight followed by subsequent post-drying for 4 hours at 0.01 mbar
resulting in a freeze dried nanoclay.
Optical microscopy
Optical microscopy was performed on a LEICA DMLB optical microscope
with variable zoom and a LEICA MC190 HD camera attachment. The cor-
responding lens for the light source was at bright ﬁeld.
