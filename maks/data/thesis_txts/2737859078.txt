II. Abstract  
This project is focused on the construction of a naïve human scFv-displaying phage library. 
Antibody phage libraries are the cornerstone of modern antibody discovery, seeing wide use in 
academia and industry. Prior to this project, library construction was initiated by harvest of 
antibody light and heavy chain mRNA from human lymphocytes. The mRNA was transcribed 
to DNA, which was amplified, and heavy chain DNA had restriction sites inserted.  
At the start of this project, restriction site insertion of light chain DNA was carried out by 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR), while pSANG5 vector was harvested from pSANG5-
containing Escherichia coli (E. coli) cells. Restriction enzymes NheI/NotI were used to digest 
light chain DNA and pSANG5, and Antarctic phosphatase (AnP) was subsequently used to 
treat the digested pSANG5. Ligation of the light chains into pSANG5 was succeeded by 
transformation of the ligation products into E. coli by heat shock and subsequently by 
electroporation. The electroporated light chain-containing pSANG5 library was evaluated 
through sequencing. 
Purification of light chain-containing pSANG5 was followed by digestion with restriction 
enzymes XhoI/NotI and AnP treatment. Heavy chain DNA was likewise digested and ligated 
into the light chain-containing pSANG5. Heat shock transformation of the ligation product and 
subsequent colony PCR revealed that about 50% of the colonies contained both heavy and light 
chains. However, gel electrophoresis analysis of samples from digestion, AnP treated, and 
ligation found that all these steps proceeded as expected. Future evaluation of the ligation 
products through sequencing could help advance the library construction.  
 
Upon completion, the naïve human scFv-displaying phage library will be used to discover 
binders against snake toxins. Snakebite envenoming, a neglected tropical disease, claims the 
lives of 100.000 people annually. Increased awareness through the Wellcome project has 
sparked interest in developing snake antivenoms using novel binders. The research conducted 
in this project has therefore been an aid in the discovery and potential treatment of snakebite 
envenoming.

1.  Introduction 
From their discovery in 1891 to modern days, antibodies have had a major impact on our 
understanding of the immune system, drug development and diagnostic methods. In 2023 the 
market of antibody-based therapeutics reached an estimated revenue of 247.3 billion $1, 
highlighting the critical role that antibodies play in developing innovative and effective 
treatments. Successful drugs such as Adalimumab (antirheumatic)2, Rituximab (anti-
lymphoproliferative)3 and Nivolumab (immune checkpoint inhibitor)4 are used extensively in 
clinic. In addition, several diagnostic antibodies including HercepTest (breast cancer)5 and 
Hybritech PSA (prostate cancer)6, have been developed. Comparing the present day range of 
antibody products to the theoretical limit of antibody diversity at ~1039(7) exemplifies that 
possibilities within antibody discovery are close to endless; one just has to find the needle in 
the haystack. This vast diversity, underscoring the enormous potential of antibodies for both 
therapeutic and diagnostic purposes, originates from the intricate processes of humoral 
immunity.

1.1 Antibodies 
1.1.1 Humoral Immunity  
Humoral immunity is the body’s defense system against pathogenic organisms, viruses, and 
toxins residing in the extracellular fluids, such as mucus and blood. Humoral immunity relies 
on antibodies to neutralize pathogens and potentially dangerous molecules8. Antibodies are 
globular glycoproteins in the immunoglobulin superfamily that specialize in binding antigens, 
which are molecules that trigger an immune response. These antibodies are produced by 
differentiated hematopoietic stem cells, named B-cells, which originate from the bone marrow. 
Initially naïve, meaning they have not been exposed to an antigen, B-cells are matured through 
a T-cell independent or a T-cell dependent response.9  
In the T-cell independent response, the B-cells interact directly with polysaccharides, 
glycolipids, and other non-protein antigens. The interaction involves crosslinking of the 
antigen with the surface-bound form of an antibody, known as a B-cell receptor (BCR), in order 
to produce low-affinity antibodies. In the T-cell dependent response, antigen specific T-cells 
and B-cells recognize identical antigens through interaction between the T-cell receptor and 
BCR. This interaction triggers a multistage process of cytokine expression and ligand 
interactions between the cells, differentiating the B-cell into a plasma cell capable of producing 
high titers of antibody10. Depending on the signals exchanged between T- and B-cells, the B-
cells may differentiate into memory B-cells that are able to initiate a rapid, secondary immune 
response upon encountering the antigen again, producing antibodies with high affinity and 
specificity in the process.11   
The antibodies secreted by plasma cells are identical to the BCR that resides on their surface, 
targeting specific antigens. These antibodies circulate freely in the bloodstream, until 
encountering an antigen12. Depending on the antigen, antibodies execute different effector 
functions to eliminate it: 
• Neutralization: Antibodies bind to viruses or toxins neutralizing their functions, 
creating an antibody-target complex. This complex is taken up by macrophages for 
degradation of the pathogen or molecule.  
• Opsonization: Antibodies interact with specific Fc receptors on phagocytes, facilitating 
ingestion of antibody-coated pathogens through phagocytosis. Inside the phagocyte the 
pathogen-containing phagosome merges with a lysosome, effectively neutralizing the 
antigen.  
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
• Antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC): When encountering pathogens 
that are not effectively neutralized by opsonization or upon intracellular infection, 
ADCC is initiated. Antibodies that bind the antigens, interact with Fc receptors on 
natural killer or eosinophil cells, triggering cytotoxic responses that lead to the 
destruction of the target pathogen or infected cell.13 
• Complement dependent cytotoxicity (CDC): Antibodies bound to surface antigens of 
viruses or bacteria, are bound by the C1q protein, triggering the classical complement 
pathway. C1q recruits more complement proteins, forming a membrane attack complex 
that lyses the antibody bound pathogen.14 
 
These antibody effector functions must encapsulate various aspects of humoral immunity and 
are mediated by different antibody isotypes.  
 
1.1.2 Antibody Isotypes 
The basic structure of all antibody isotypes contains four polypeptide chains: two heavy (HC) 
and two light chains (LC) linked by disulfide bonds, forming a Y-shape structure weighing 
around 150 kDa (Fig. 1). The four chains each feature a variable (V) region at the amino acid 
N-terminus, forming a part of the antigen-binding site, and a constant (C) region which defines 
the antibody isotype. HC and LC are connected through multiple non-covalent interactions and 
a disulfide bond. 
Digestion of the antibody Y-shape structure with the protease papain, yields two fragments: 
F(ab)2 (comprising one LC and part of an HC) and Fc (crystallizable fragment). Further 
digestion with pepsin separates the F(ab)2 into two individual Fab molecules that are each 
capable of independently binding to an antigen. This binding is facilitated by the Fv (variable 
fragment), which consists of a variable heavy (VH) and a variable light (VL) domain. These 
domains are termed variable due to three hypervariable loops residing on each domain, known 
as complementary-determining regions (CDRs). CDRs are highly susceptible to mutations, a 
characteristic tightly regulated during antibody maturation that contributes significantly to the 
vast diversity observed among antibodies8. 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
 
Figure 1: The five isotypes of immunoglobulins. Fv = Variable fragment, Fab = Antigen binding fragment, Fc = Crystallizable fragment. 
 
Although sharing a common Y-shape structure, five different antibody isotypes are found in 
the human immune system: IgA, IgD, IgE, IgM and IgG. These isotypes of antibodies are 
distinguished from one another and gain their effector function from the structure of their heavy 
chains15. From all these antibody isoforms, there are only two types of light chains: lambda (l) 
and kappa (k). When an antibody is produced, only one of these chains contributes to its 
structure, with no apparent influence on functionality, at a ratio of 2:1 of k to l. 
 
When it comes to the role of different antibody isotypes in humoral immunity, IgM is the first 
functional isotype produced during the B-cell lifespan and the main antibody of the primary 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
immune response. As seen in Fig. 1, IgM is a pentamer molecule, capable of binding to 10 
antigens, that plays a crucial role in immune regulation and activation of the complement 
system, which enables both opsonization and CDC16. IgM is expressed alongside IgD in mature 
B-cells, and although the definitive effector function of IgD has not been fully uncovered, it is 
relevant in the regulation of antigen responsiveness in mature B-cells.17 Following antigenic 
stimulation, either T-cell independent or dependent, a secondary immune response is initiated, 
triggering class switch recombination in memory B-cells and causing them to produce only a 
single antibody isotype18. 
One of the isotypes produced after class switch recombination is IgA, which exists both in 
monomeric form in the plasma of the body and in dimeric form as secretory IgA (SIgA). SIgA 
is primarily found in the gastrointestinal and respiratory tracts, where it acts as a first line of 
defense to maintain mucosal homeostasis by neutralizing and opsonizing antigens19. IgE, 
which is exclusively found in monomeric form, is the antibody isotype most closely associated 
with ADCC in relation to multicellular pathogens such as helminths. IgE recognizes Fc 
receptors on eosinophils and mast cells20, making it the main antibody isotype involved in 
allergic reactions. IgG on the other hand, is the main antibody isotype associated with the 
neutralization of toxins21, phagocytosis of opsonized bacteria22 and degradation of viral 
particles23. IgG is a monomeric immunoglobulin, the most common in the serum, comprising 
75% of the total antibodies in humans. IgG is highly stable with a half-life of around 3 weeks. 
IgG can develop high target specificity and affinity during maturation process in the secondary 
immune response, in which it has critical significance.  
 
1.1.3 Antibody Maturation 
The first matured antibody in the repertoire is IgM, achieving initial diversity through the 
process of V(D)J-recombination, where variable (V), joining (J) and diversity (D) gene 
segments are recombined seemingly at random to form a unique V-region of the antibody. In 
humans, these antibody genes are found in the immunoglobulin LCl locus on chromosome 22, 
the immunoglobulin LCk locus on chromosome 2, and the immunoglobulin HC locus on 
chromosome 14. Each of these loci contains a large number of different V and J gene segments, 
with various D genes being exclusively found in the immunoglobulin HC locus. Recombination 
of the genes is facilitated by recombination-activating gene (RAG1-RAG2) proteins, which 
form a paired recombinase complex. RAG1/2 are guided by specific recombination signals 
(RSS), which are conserved regions of V-, D- and J-gene segments. Upon binding to two RSS, 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
RAG1/2 creates a nick at the junction between the RSS and coding segment, thus forming a 
paired complex, joining the gene segments through a hairpin. The paired complex cleaves off 
the RSS ends, opening the hairpin and leaving the coding segments open, in order for non-
homologous end-joining (NHEJ) DNA repair pathway proteins to join them together. NHEJ 
joining is imprecise and results in several random nucleotides being added between the joint 
coding gene segments, which further adds to the diversity of the IgM antibody24.   
V(D)J-recombination of IgM, however, does not equate an efficient antibody by measure of 
specificity and affinity. To further mature, the gene of the antibody undergoes the process of 
somatic hypermutation, in which affinity is improved 10-to-5000-fold and specificity is 
significantly increased.25 Single base substitutions make up the bulk of somatic 
hypermutations, happening specifically in the CDRs of the V gene segments. Here, mutations 
happen at a rate 1 million-fold higher compared to regular genes26, carried out by the protein 
activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID). AID effectively generates deoxyuracil (dU) by 
deaminating deoxycytidine in the V-region DNA. The generated dU is either translated to 
deoxythymidine or changed by various repair mechanisms to generate other bases, resulting in 
mutations27.  
 
Somatic hypermutations produce antibodies with both decreased and increased affinity for the 
antigen. To ensure that high affinity antibodies are produced, follicular helper T-cells (TFH) 
carrying the corresponding antigen interact with the B-cells. In this interaction, survival signals 
are elicited by the TFH-cell to B-cells that express high-affinity antibodies, effectively 
outcompeting low-affinity binders28. The antigen carrying TFH cell will confer membrane 
ligand interactions and secrete several cytokines, triggering class switch recombination (CSR) 
in the B-cell, definitively changing the isotype of the produced antibody. Upon antigen 
exposure, repetitive switch regions of the B-cell residing upstream of their corresponding 
constant regions, are targeted heavily by AID. Base excision repair enzyme creates nicks in the 
AID affected switch regions, eventually producing double stranded breaks in these regions. 
Double stranded break repair proteins will then bring together the switch regions and excise 
the constant regions in between, leading to a class switch. With the variable region intact and 
only high-affinity B-cells surviving, an effective weapon against the antigen, and by extension 
the pathogen, has been developed to provide immunity for the body.26,29

1.2 Immunity 
Immunity is a state of the body that renders it immune to certain pathogens, infectious diseases, 
or dangerous molecules through the presence of antibodies. In this regard, two overall types of 
immunity exists, active immunity and passive immunity30. 
Passive immunity is gained by receiving antibodies from an external source, effectively 
transferring antibodies from one individual to another. Natural passive immunity is exemplified 
through the transfer of maternal antibodies from mother to child through the placenta (IgG) or 
breastmilk (IgA)31, resulting in instant immunity that can last between 1-4 months. Acquired 
passive immunity on the other hand, is typically gained as a concentrated cocktail of antibodies 
from an immunized individual, for the treatment of immediate inflammation or toxic molecule 
effects.  
The other overall type of immunity is active immunity, which is acquired when the body is 
exposed to an antigen, that is then targeted by antibodies generated through adaptive immune 
responses. These antibodies are kept by memory B-cells, to help protect the body against the 
target antigen, usually resulting in lifelong protection. Natural active immunity is acquired 
when the body is infected by an infectious disease such as influenza virus or smallpox. The 
process of natural active immunity has inspired the creation of vaccines, that introduce the 
antigens of a weakened pathogens to the body. This results in a controlled adaptive immune 
response, providing the body with acquired active immunity towards the pathogen. Although 
vaccines have been researched extensively for decades, advancing them and providing 
immunity for millions of individuals, the cells and antibodies involved take a long time to 
mature properly32. Pathogens that pose a swift and fatal threat to the immune system are not 
easily combated through natural or acquired active immunity. 
In such cases, acquired passive immunity becomes the primary solution. However, purposely 
immunizing animals or humans to harvest antibodies for passive immunity raises significant 
ethical concern. To address this issue, biotechnological methods have been developed to 
discover and produce antibodies with high affinity and specificity against a wide range of 
antigens. One crucial method for discovering novel antibodies is antibody display technology.

1.3 Antibody Display Technology 
1.3.1 Types of Antibody Display Technology 
At the core of antibody display technology is the ability to present antibody fragments in vitro 
on the surface of a carrier that retains the genetic information of the fragment. In antibody 
display, the fragments that are most commonly used are antigen binding fragments (Fabs), 
VHHs (nanobodies) and single chain variable fragments (scFvs, consisting of VH+VL). These 
fragments are acquired from antibody libraries, which will be described later, but in essence, 
consists of numerous antibody fragments. Antibody libraries are used by screening against one 
or multiple target antigens, to discover fragments that can be further characterized, optimized, 
and made into an effective antibody.  
Throughout the 1980’s and 1990’s, various displaying techniques using different carriers were 
developed33. One highly successful version of the technology is yeast display, which utilizes 
the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Having a eukaryotic cell for the expression of antibody 
fragments enhances the correct folding of the end-product, allowing successful expression of 
both Fabs, VHHs, and scFvs34. Yeast display leverages the surface glycoprotein a-agglutinin, 
specifically its subunit Aga2p, which binds to Aga1p situated on the surface of the yeast. By 
expressing Aga2p linked to the antibody fragment of interest through a vector, the fragment is 
displayed on the yeast surface35 and can bind to a target antigen. Despite its advantages, the 
technology suffers from generally low transformation efficiency and differences between 
human and yeast glycosylation36. 
 
To achieve more human-like glycosylation and post-translational modification, mammalian 
cell display can be used. From mammalian cells like Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells, the 
entire range of antibody fragments up to whole IgG can be displayed on the surface. The 
antibody displaying mammalian cell is created using the Flp-In system, initiated by transfection 
of a Flp recombinase vector and an expression vector containing the antibody of choice. The 
expression vector contains a Flp Recombination Target (FRT) site, from which the Flp 
recombinase catalyzes homologous recombination to an FRT site in the host cell, effectively 
inserting the expression vector into the host cell. Also contained in the expression vector, is a 
transmembrane domain, that assists in localizing the expressed antibody to the surface of the 
cell. From here, the cells expressing surface antibodies are screened by Fluorescence-activated 
cell sorting (FACS) against a target antigen.  
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
While mammalian display has the potential to display fully functional antibodies, it faces 
challenges such as sensitivity towards contamination and high development costs. 
Additionally, in the context of antibody discovery, mammalian display is limited by the 
relatively small library size, with a maximum of only 106 variants37–39. 
Ribosome display, on the other hand, offers the capability to screen up to 1012-14 variants, with 
a relatively straightforward and cell-free in vitro approach. An antibody library of scFv-mRNA 
lacking a stop codon is translated by a ribosome, withholding the mRNA at its tunnel. In this 
way, genetic information is retained via the mRNA, while an antibody fragment is produced 
from the ribosome, capable of binding to an immobilized antigen. By binding to the antigen, 
the mRNA-ribosome-scFv is immobilized on a surface, where non-bound targets are washed 
away40. After recovery of the mRNA-ribosome-scFv-antigen complex, mRNA is reverse 
transcribed to cDNA and amplified via error-prone PCR to introduce mutations, enchancing 
affinity in a second round of selection41. Despite its simplicity, the technology requires various 
stabilizing agents to sustain the conformation of the non-covalent mRNA-ribosome-scFv 
complex, and is susceptible to gaps in the spacer sequences that hinder mRNA translation42.  
Finally, there is phage display, which is a high-throughput, robust, and cost-effective discovery 
technology. Developed by George P. Smith in 198543, this extensively researched method 
remains the foremost technology in antibody discovery. Phage display harnesses 
bacteriophages (or simply phages), which are genetically modified to co-express and display a 
foreign peptide on their surface. A wide array of phages see application in phage display, 
including Lambda, T4, T7, and M1333. Among these, M13 stands out as the most researched 
and widely used phage in the phage display technology, making it the chosen phage system for 
this project.  
 
1.3.2 M13 Bacteriophage 
M13 is an F-pilus specific filamentous phage, belonging to the group of Ff-phages, that 
exclusively interacts with E. coli. It contains a single stranded DNA (ssDNA) genome of 
6407bp, encoding five proteins that make up the virion and six proteins involved in phage 
replication and self-assembly44. The virion structure is presented in Fig. 2, with the major 
capsin G8P coating the sides, and the minor coat protein pairs of G9P/G7P and G6P/G3P 
forming the ends of the M13 page.  
 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
Fig. 2: Illustration of M13 bacteriophage. Illustration is not to scale as 2700 copies of G8P is present on M13.  
 
G9P/G7P make up the remote tip of the phage, while G6P/G3P constitute the infective end45. 
In the presence of E. coli, the G3P-N2 domain binds to the F pilus residing on the surface of 
the E. coli. F pilus will naturally retract, bringing the phage close to the surface where G3P-N1 
domain can bind to the TolA receptor of E. coli, assisting in the translocation of viral ssDNA 
into the cell via TolR and TolQ receptors.  
Upon entry, a complementary DNA (-) strand to the ssDNA is synthesized from which 
translation and subsequent mRNA transcription is carried out for all 11 phage-proteins (G1P-
G11P). The accumulation of the replication protein G2P, nicks the DNA, initiating the process 
of rolling circle replication46. This allows for elongation of the DNA (+) strand that G2P cuts 
at the origin after 1 round, freeing the newly elongated (+) strand and recirculating the old. 
When sufficient levels of DNA binding G5P is acquired, it will dimerize on ssDNA (+) to 
create a conformation of the DNA fit for the rod-shaped M13 virion. The virion itself is 
assembled through 5 stages:  
• Preinitiation: 12-14 Monomeric G4P form a channel in the cell membrane. Assembly 
complex of G1P, G11P and G4P is formed. 
• Initiation: G7P and G9P interact with a hair-pin loop in the G5P covered ssDNA termed 
the packaging signal. The proteins form the tip of the budding phage. 
• Elongation: G8P exchanges G5P on ssDNA, translocating the DNA into the phage.  
• Pretermination: G3P and G6P make up the terminal end of the phage. 
• Termination: Phage is released as the G3P-G6P complex changes conformation.47

1.3.3 Antibody-displaying Phage Library 
The first step in using a phage such as M13 in antibody research was taken by McCafferty et 
al. in 199048, exhibiting the possibility of displaying antibody fragments (scFv or Fab) fused to 
G3P at the tip of the phage. This fusion was done by creating a phagemid vector with the M13-
G3P gene, a linker containing a trypsin cutting site next to the scFv gene, and a selection 
marker, either kanamycin or ampicillin. To then create the antibody presenting phage, E. coli 
is transformed with the G3P-scFv phagemid. The phagemid however, doesn’t contain the 
remaining proteins used in the replication and assembly of the M13 antibody-phage.49 To assist 
this task an engineered M13 helper phage named M13KO7 is employed to infect the same E. 
coli transformed with the phagemid. M13KO7 differs from the wild-type M13 by having an 
ampicillin selection marker gene, an ineffective origin of replication and a trypsin cutting site 
on G3P. The M13K07 chromosome produce all necessary proteins for phage assembly, and 
with G3P-scFv fusion proteins being expressed from the phagemid, these will take the place of 
some G3P proteins of the newly assembled phages50. Upon termination from E. coli, scFvs are 
then effectively expressed on the antibody phages. 
 
1.3.4. Single Chain Variable Fragments  
An scFv is a fusion protein, made from the variable regions of antibody LC and HC (VL and 
VH, respectively) connected by a linker peptide, to mimic the antigen-binding site of an 
antibody, as visualized in Fig. 3. The simplistic design with only disulfide bond formation as 
post-translational modification and the small size of ~30kDa has made scFvs one of the easiest, 
and hence most popular, antibody formats in antibody discovery51. 
 
Figure 3: Production of scFvs by insertion of a VL+VH-containing vector into Escherichia coli. 
 
scFvs are primarily employed in research for their potential medical applications and to 
discover, optimize, and study the properties of antibodies. Although their medical use is often 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
hindered by fast clearance from the blood and their tendency to aggregate when administered 
in vivo, they remain an attractive antibody format for both research and diagnostic purposes. 
One advantage of the scFv format is its high flexibility due to a simple design, allowing the 
antigen targeting diversity of a whole antibody in a less complex and smaller body. In addition, 
massive diversity can be achieved by combining VL and VH  genes from various donors. When 
displayed on the surface of a phage, diverse scFvs aid the discovery of novel antibodies through 
phage display selection.52  
 
1.3.5 Phage Display Selection 
By inserting a vast diversity of scFvs into phagemids, an antibody-displaying phage library 
containing 1011-1012 phages can be constructed. This library can be used in phage display 
selection, a high throughput method designed to discover target-specific and high affinity scFvs 
within the library. An overview of the phage display selection process is shown in Fig. 4. 
 
Figure 4: Phage display selection with scFv. 1) The scFv-displaying phage library is added to immobilized antigen. 2) Some scFv-displaying 
phages bind to the antigen. 3) Phages with nonspecific scFvs are washed away, leaving antigen-bound phages. 4) Trypsin digestion cleaves 
wild-type M13-G3P and scFv-G3P, eluting all phages. 5) Phages with intact G3P, which carry genes for scFvs bound to the antigen, infect E. 
coli to be amplified and either continue a new selection round or be evaluated for binding to the antigen in a binding assay. 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
The phage library is added to a container coated with the antigen of choice. Phages displaying 
a scFv capable of binding to the antigen, will bind it. Phages that are unable to bind the antigen 
will be washed away in the next step, leaving only antigen-bound phages. Adding the 
proteolytic enzyme trypsin to the container cleaves M13KO7-G3P off the phages, while it only 
cleaves the scFv off the G3P protein on the antigen-bound phages, which means only the 
antigen-bound phages will be capable of infecting E. coli. In this process, only phages 
displaying scFvs with specificity towards the target antigen are amplified and can either be 
used in another round of selection or be evaluated for their antigen binding in a binding assay 
(commonly ELISA53).  
This powerful selection process facilitates the rapid and efficient identification of antigen 
specific antibodies, significantly advancing the development of novel diagnostics and 
therapeutics for a wide range of diseases.

1.4 Naïve Human scFv- Displaying Phage Library  
Antibody-displaying phage libraries can be constructed by various methods, utilizing different 
antibody fragments. The construction of a naïve human scFv-displaying phage library 
described within this project, is done with inspiration from Schofield et. al (2007)54, who 
constructed a phage library of 1010 human scFvs. The protocol for antibody library construction 
that this project follows can be seen in Fig. 5.  
 
Figure 5: Construction of a naïve human scFv-displaying phage library. 1) Blood is acquired from donors and 2) centrifuged to sort out 
B-cells, from which 3) RNA and mRNA is harvested. 4) mRNA is translated to DNA by complementary DNA synthesis, using various 
primers, and subsequently 5) amplified by PCR. 6) Using primers designed with restriction sites, a second PCR is performed to insert the 
restriction sites into the DNA. 7) From these restriction sites, the VL-DNA is first inserted via complementary restriction sites into the pSANG5 
vector. 8) The construct is cloned into E. coli and 9) the VL-containing pSANG5 is harvested, digested with other restriction enzymes, and 
ligated with VH to generate the entire scFv. 10) The scFv-pSANG5 is cloned anew into E. coli, from which 11) the naïve human scFv-
displaying phage library can be purified and used in phage display selection. 
 
In the first step, lymphocytes are isolated from buffy coat donations using a Ficol gradient. The 
total RNA, and subsequently the mRNA, of the B-cells is extracted and mRNA obtained from 
every 5 donors is pooled together, creating 10 separate mRNA pools. These mRNA pools are 
used to synthesize cDNA by complementary DNA synthesis, using specific primers for VH, 
VLl, and VLk, resulting in 10 VH, 10 VLl, and 10 VLk cDNA templates. Initial PCR 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
amplification of these cDNA pools is performed using a mix of forward primers specific to VH, 
VLl and, VLk, together with 14 (VH), 15 (VLl), and 13 (VLk) different backward primers. This 
results in 140 VH, 150 VLl, and 130 VLk samples, generating the main diversity of the library.  
Following this, restrictions sites for VH (NcoI and XhoI) and VL (NheI and NotI) are introduced 
by a second PCR, using similar mixes of the forward primers, and the respective backward 
primers, but this time including the restriction sites. These restriction sites match the restriction 
sites of the pSANG5 vector, a pUC plasmid derivative, enabling the sequential ligation of VL 
and VH into the vector. The scFv-containing pSANG5 vector, demonstrated in Fig. 6, also 
includes a Lac promoter, Myc-tag, M13 Gene 3 coding for M13-G3P, and an ampicillin 
resistance gene. Once the entire scFv has been successfully inserted into pSANG5, E. coli is 
transformed with the scFv-containing pSANG5, and a naïve human scFv-displaying phage 
library is constructed. 
 
 
Figure 6: pSANG5 phagemid and scFv-containing pSANG5. pSANG5 contains the M13 gene 3 coding for M13-G3P, AmpR to enable 
ampicillin selection, a Lac promoter for induced translation and a Myc-tag to enable affinity chromatography. Prior to insertion of scFv, a  
FLAG-tag was located between sites for restriction enzymes NcoI and XhoI.  
2. Project aim 
The work described in this thesis is part of a greater research project, the Wellcome project. 
Wellcome is a global foundation that seeks to solve urgent healthcare challenges, one of these 
being snakebite envenoming, classified as a neglected tropical disease by WHO55. Yearly, 
snakebites cause the death of 100.000 people, with 400.000 suffering severe consequences of 
snakebites56. To combat this, the Center for Antibody Technologies at DTU is discovering and 
developing broadly neutralizing antibodies, that target the wide array of snake toxins. 
Discovering antibodies requires antibody libraries that inhabit a plethora of antibody fragment 
genes. However, antibody libraries are not widely available and expensive to acquire. This 
project therefore focuses on the construction of a naïve human scFv-displaying phage library, 
intended to be used in the discovery of snake toxin targeting antibodies.

3.2 Methods Used in Construction of Library  
This section will describe the methods used in the construction of a naïve human scFv-
displaying  phage library. To give a sense of the overall construction, as this project was 
established during library construction, a short walkthrough of what has been done up until 
now will be given. Human lymphocytes were extracted from the blood of 50 healthy and 
anonymous donors (no knowledge of sex, age, or race; under Ethical Committee approval 
22006822). This extraction yielded mRNA, which was used to synthesize cDNA using 
antibody primers based in the constant region of k and l LCs, as well as IgM HCs. After 
amplification by PCR and purification, the HC cDNA had restriction sites inserted via a second 
PCR. pSANG5 vector was acquired through collaborators and transformed in NEB® 10-beta 
Competent E. coli. This project resumes from here (Fig. 5, step 6).

Agarose 1% gels were cast with SYBRTM Safe DNA Gel Stain and loaded with 1 kb DNA 
ladder or 1 kb DNA plus ladder. Loading Dye 6X was added to samples in appropriate volume 
and pipetted into wells in full volume. Gel electrophoresis was performed in TAE Buffer at 
110V between 75-85min, at which point the gels were pictured and analyzed. Gel pieces were 
excised and stored in -20 oC freezer, after which DNA was purified using the GeneJet Gel 
Extraction Kit following the manufacturer’s protocol. For this, centrifugation steps were 
performed at 13.000g for 1 min. For elution, 60 oC NFW was added in 2 rounds, and the 
purified samples stored in a -20 oC freezer.

4.1 NheI/NotI-Digestion of VLs and pSANG5 
Insertion of restriction sites for NotI and NheI enzymes in the amplified VL genes was 
performed using PCR. For VLk, 13 individual backward primers containing a NheI restriction 
site, together with a mix of 5 forward primers, containing a NotI restriction site, were used in 
10 sample pools. This accumulated to 130 VLk samples containing NheI/NotI restriction sites.

highly usable to estimate how successful a ligation has been, compared to a known positive 
control (pUC19). However, to achieve high transformation efficiency (TE of 1010-11)57

efficiency entails higher end diversity.

similar to the ones presented Fig. 16 were expected.

For this project, a TE of 1 ⋅10! CFU / µg DNA was benchmarked as the minimum requirement

To ensure the transformed VL-containing pSANG5s were robust, sequencing of 10 colonies 
from each of the VL-containing pSANG5 plates was done. Requirements for the VL-containing 
pSANG5s were established to assert their robustness. Necessary genes have to be present and 
non-mutated for the library to work as intended. These include: AmpR, Myc-tag and Linker. 
Non-mutated restriction sites are equally important for the function of corresponding restriction 
enzymes and includes: NheI, NotI, NcoI and XhoI. Lastly, premature stop codons cannot be 
present in the coding regions of the genes. If all of these requirements are fulfilled, the VL-
containing pSANG5 is considered robust. 
99 out of 132 colonies  (75%)  met these criteria and a robust VL-library had been established. 
The library plates containing cells transformed with VL-containing pSANG5s were 
subsequently scraped with freezing media, transferred to cryogenic tubes, and stored in -80 oC 
freezer.

One might wonder, why the experimental strategy of VL insertion first, followed by VH 
insertion into pSANG5 is conferred in the first place. It is a valid method to ligate the chains 
via. overlap extension PCR, and creating scFv inserts that can then be ligated into pSANG566 
directly. This cuts back on the time spend on digestion, ligation, and transformation, but the 
diversity acquired from this method is not up to par with the strategy displayed in this project.  
The creation of a VL-library is in its own right, an argument for the use of this strategy. When 
the finished scFv-displaying phage library is eventually used for discovering effective scFv 
binders, the main part of the interaction between antigen and scFv happens at the VH. Upon 
discovery and further characterization, it may be apparent that the scFv VH is optimal for 
binding the antigen, but the VL is insufficient. Having an VL-library at the ready, the optimal 
VH can be exposed to a round of VL shuffling, effectively finding an optimal VL-VH pair, to 
create the best possible scFv binder towards the antigen, as exemplified by Ledsgaard, et. al. 
(2022)67.  
As the end-goal of this project is to create an scFv-displaying phage library for the discovery 
of snake-venom binders, discovering the optimal scFv is prioritized over ease of construction. 
This concludes the results and discussion section of this project.

7. Conclusion 
The goal of this project was to finalize the construction of a naïve human scFv-displaying phage 
library. Initially, restriction sites were successfully inserted into amplified VL DNA and 
pSANG5 vector was harvested. Ligations were done with NheI/NotI-digested VLs in 
NheI/NotI-digested and AnP treated pSANG5 vector, and ligation products were used in heat 
shock transformation. Gel analysis of ligation products and plate growth of heat shock 
transformed colonies, revealed that separation of NheI/NotI-digestion and AnP treatment 
pSANG5 is essential for successful ligation and insertion of VL-containing pSANG5 vector. 
VL-containing pSANG5 were used in electroporation, in which a cuvette size of 1 mm and 
incubation at 30 oC was found to be necessary to obtain growing electroporated colonies, with 
small, circular, and white morphology. VL-containing pSANG5 cells were confirmed robust 
via. sequencing, proving that a VL-library had been successfully made, from which VL-
containing pSANG5s was purified.  
Ligations were done with XhoI/NcoI-digested VHF DNA, and a pool of XhoI/NcoI-digested 
AnP treated VLkF-containing pSANG5. Heat shock transformation of ligation products and 
subsequent colony PCR gel analysis, indicate that ligation was not optimally conferred. 
Analysis of VLkF-containing pSANG5 by XhoI/NcoI-digestion, varying AnP incubation time, 
and ligation with VHF yielded consistent results, expected from optimal ligation. Further 
analysis of ligation conditions is therefore necessary before the naïve human scFv-displaying 
phage library can be finalized.  
8. Future Perspective 
The steps completed throughout this project, have taken the construction of a new naïve human 
scFv-displaying phage library to the brink of completion. To assert what hinders the optimal 
ligation of the VH families in the VL-containing pSANG5s, sequencing of cells heat shocked 
with ligation product and controls will be necessary. Due to the robustness of the VL-library, 
only changes to the processing or transformation of VL-containing pSANG5 should be made 
to complete the full library. After finalizing the library, it needs to be validated against a wide 
array of antigens, to assert the range of binders that can be found within the library.   
The research conducted within this project should be viewed as an aid in the construction of 
future naïve scFv-displaying phage libraries, showing the pitfalls that can be encountered in 
both VL-library and complete scFv-displaying phage library construction.

9. Innovative and Commercial Potential 
The aforementioned validation of the finalized human naïve scFv-displaying phage library will 
exhibit the range of the library and thereby its applications. A wide range of antigen binders is  
expected from the library, which will hopefully lead to discovery of novel binders against snake 
toxins. The IP of these snake-toxin binders would belong exclusively to the Center for 
Antibody Technologies. Furthermore, novel binders targeting non-snake-toxins binders can be 
readily patented as well, granting the library immense commercial potential.  
 
The range of the library opens up for the possibility of engaging with collaborators, such as 
research-groups that are looking for specific antigen binders, for the purpose of advancing 
research. Companies could also be engaged as potential collaborators for a more commercial 
approach. However, keeping the library in-house would be most beneficial, as this limits the 
the possibility of others discovering identical binders.  
Acquiring antibody phage libraries through commercial means is a significant expenditure, and 
the economic advantage of possessing such a library cannot be underestimated. Not only does 
it save a costly investment, as libraries would be required to conduct research either way, but 
it also equips our research group with a prominent resource.
