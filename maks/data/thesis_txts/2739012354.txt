Abstract
In this thesis, an ML-based approach is studied, using turbine sensors, which will aim to provide
more accurate estimates of wind turbine inflows than current methods. Conducted using LES
data with an aeroelastic solver in three wind farms with different layouts and atmospheric
conditions, the study evaluated the wind field reconstruction accuracy at an average hub wind
speed of 8 m/s. First, two different models with different input/output configurations were
developed and tested in terms of accuracy and computational eﬀiciency. A small investigation
on the impact of atmospheric conditions was also carried out.
A sensor correlation study
improved the prediction of wind inflow by 20 to 40% depending on the complexity of the flow
conditions. The results of these two tests are put through two types of performance evaluation:
metrics-based and frequency-based performance evaluation. The robustness of the output was
tested on different grid resolutions. Although the approach demonstrated great potential, its
drawbacks were also noted, among them the one that demands better quality input data and
issues with grid resolution. In short, results imply that such sensor data in turbines can be
used as an alternative that is reliable for inflow prediction, but more refinement is necessary
for higher optimization of model performance.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
One of the key challenges facing wind energy science is understanding and modeling wind farm
flows (Veers et al., 2019). This problem is hugely complex because wind farm flows are driven
by a number of interconnected physical phenomena that are not only hard to model but also
partially still poorly understood. Understanding the wind inflow field is vital to optimizing
turbine performance, improving energy production, and reducing structural loads using real-
time adjustments on the turbine. This knowledge can also enable better design, operation
of wind turbines, better yield estimates, and site suitability to be erected. Without precise
knowledge of the inflow, operators struggle to optimize the turbine during operation, leading
to reduced energy output, increased maintenance costs, and shorter lifespan.
Using any piece of information to reconstruct the wind conditions upstream of a wind turbine is
called Wind Inflow Field Reconstruction (WIFR), an up-rising aspect of wind energy research.
Wind Inflow Field Reconstruction is one of the powerful techniques that improves our ability
to understand and handle complex wind conditions that affect wind turbines. Wind turbines
typically rely on meteorological masts equipped with various anemometers and, more recently,
Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) systems to measure the turbine inflow, or the wind
conditions, at the turbine site. These masts provide point-specific high-accuracy data, but
are limited by their spatial coverage and the height to which they can extend, thus failing to
cover large areas or capture variations in wind conditions. Although LiDAR systems offer more
comprehensive spatial data and flexibility, they also come with high costs and limitations related
to line-of-sight measurements and reduced accuracy in certain weather and terrain conditions.
Wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of the wind into electrical energy, but can also serve as
sophisticated sensors for the wind conditions in which they operate. This hypothesis suggests
that by analyzing various parameters, such as the bending moments at the roots of each blade,
the speed of rotation, power output, and other operational data, it is possible to infer the
characteristics of the wind inflow field. The approach of using turbines as sensors is based on
the concept that changes in wind conditions at input will be reflected in the mechanical response
of the turbine; thus, this approach could reduce costs and improve the eﬀiciency of wind field
reconstruction without the need for additional and costly measurement devices (Bottasso et al.,
2018, Bertelè and Bottasso, 2020).
An alternative to using met masts or LiDAR is to estimate wind inflow using machine learning
(ML) models. This approach is attractive because ML can leverage data from multiple already
existing sources (e.g., turbine sensors, historical weather data, and environmental conditions)
to generate more accurate inflow estimates across a wider range of spatial positions. ML models
1
==PAGE:13==
can continuously improve by learning from new data, potentially providing detailed site-specific
wind assessments without the need for extensive physical measurements. This method offers a
cost-effective, scalable, and flexible solution, especially for large or complex wind farms.
There has been an increase in the use of machine learning methods in wind energy. Scientific
novelty includes advanced computational techniques in the research area: increasing accuracy in
the prediction of wind speed and power, optimization of the control strategies of a turbine, and
fault detection or maintenance. Stock-Williams et al., 2018 introduced a model using LiDAR
devices in order to train an ML model to produce the full 3D wind fields, robust to overfitting,
however, dominated by the disadvantages of LiDAR. Shao et al., 2021 proposed a wind speed
prediction model using optimized Long-Short Term Memory networks (LSTMs), using historical
wind speed data to predict the wind speed in the future. Dimitrov and Göçmen, 2022 have
studied the use of virtual sensors for wind turbines, using machine learning-based time series
models to estimate the main parameters such as blade tip tower clearance, blade root bending
moment prediction, and wake position. Xu et al., 2022 carried out an investigation to build
an artificial neural network (ANN) to estimate wind turbine loads, using field data. Chen and
Han, 2022 have developed a method for estimating effective wind speed using deep learning
techniques that utilizes turbine data to train two neural network models: one to estimate
the effective radius of the blade and reconstruct aerodynamic mapping, and another LSTM
model to estimate and predict wind speed in real time. Kim et al., 2023 published a paper on
estimating wind inflow for wind turbines in real time during operation, adding to the work of
Bertelè and Bottasso, 2020, where the ML model replaces the linear model for simplified tuning,
with improved performance at lower wind speeds. Beu and Landulfo, 2023 have presented the
application of LSTMs in their study to predict wind speed profiles (at different heights) on
complex terrain using an observational LiDAR study. An interesting approach is followed by
Wang et al., 2024 and Zhang and Zhao, 2021 where an ANN is constrained using the Navier-
Stokes (NS) equations, forming the so-called Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) along
with sparse LiDAR wind data. However, most of the uses of ML models assume that there exists
some prior knowledge of the flow conditions and do not solely rely on the turbine’s response to
estimate these conditions. This means that while these models improve accuracy in predicting
wind speed and power, they often depend on additional flow information.
The meaning of the approach in the use of wind turbines as sensors for wind field reconstruc-
tion has only recently been understood in the literature, showing that this innovation has the
potential to increase the precision of the measurement of wind and turbine performance. This
concept, commonly referred to as the “rotor as a sensor” approach, has been validated by
various studies and field tests, which have demonstrated its potential to provide accurate and
real-time estimations of integrated quantities such as wind shear and veer (Borraccino et al.,
2017, Bertelè et al., 2019, Bauweraerts and Meyers, 2021, Kidambi Sekar et al., 2022, Gräfe
et al., 2022, Braunbehrens et al., 2023 and Coquelet et al., 2024). Østergaard et al., 2007
presented a method that uses state and input observers to estimate the Rotor Effective Wind
Speed (REWS) from operational parameters and aerodynamic loads, indicating improvements
in the quality of the estimate. Bottasso et al., 2018 has introduced a novel approach using blade
load measurements to estimate local wind speeds in different rotor sectors, aiming to provide
detailed inflow information and detection of disturbances in the wake. Held and Mann, 2018
implemented the use of nacelle-mounted LiDARs to estimate REWS, showing the coherence
between LiDAR and turbine measurements, which can be crucial in the design of optimal pitch
controllers. A significant addition here is the research conducted by Schreiber et al., 2020,
which validates the model-based wind sensing technique to estimate the local wind speed at
the location of the blade through the use of out-of-plane bending moments of the blade. An-
other study by Conti et al., 2021 proposes two methods to improve the accuracy of wind turbine
2
==PAGE:14==
load assessments by combining LiDAR measurements with wake wind field reconstruction tech-
niques (Gaussian and Mann). Sagredo et al., 2024 presents a method that combines proper
orthogonal decomposition (POD) to reconstruct the full rotor flow, using LiDAR measurements,
which increases the uncertainty of the prediction. In conclusion, these studies focus primarily
on integrated wind characteristics rather than reconstructing the full 2D/3D flow field, and in
the case that the full field is estimated, the use of flow data is necessary. The limitations of
previous work highlight the need to develop methods to achieve a complete reconstruction of
wind inflow using the available infrastructure on wind turbines.
1.2
LES Data for Model Development
This study leverages Large Eddy Simulation (LES) to generate detailed synthetic data, enhanc-
ing machine learning models for improved wind inflow predictions. Conducting experiments in
the real world may be expensive, so this turbulence model allows the creation of synthetic data
that can be used to train and validate models.
LES offers more detailed and realistic simulations compared to other turbulence models such
as Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) or detached-eddy simulation (DES), a hybrid
version between RANS and LES. LES is capable of providing highly detailed simulations of
turbine dynamics and surrounding wind fields, effectively capturing complex interactions such
as wake effects and turbulence. These high-resolution data are crucial for advanced Wind Inflow
Field Reconstruction (WIFR), making LES the optimal choice for reconstructing the full flow.
The detailed insights provided by LES are particularly valuable when developing models from
scratch, where the precise information needed to optimize predictions is not predefined Jamshed,
2015.
However, the trade-off with LES lies in its computational expense, which requires significant
resources in terms of time and computing power. With the advent of modern supercomputers,
LES has become increasingly feasible, offering a significant advantage in capturing the intricate
details of flow around turbines. This cost is justified with providing the detailed information
necessary for accurate and reliable WIFR. By training neural networks on high-fidelity simula-
tion data generated by LES, this study aims to create an inflow model with high accuracy and
reliability of wind inflow predictions.
1.3
Motivation behind ANNs
The primary reason behind the section of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) is to minimize
the assumptions about the structure of the wind flow, which allows a more flexible analysis.
This flexibility is crucial when testing an inflow model in both free-stream and wake conditions,
where traditional decomposition methods might fail to capture local variations by scaling down
the wind field to some key parameters.
Although decomposition techniques provide valuable insight, they often struggle with the com-
plexity of the wind field and calibrating, leading to potential accuracy issues. However, ANNs
can utilize all available data and integrate information from multiple sources, as suggested in
the literature. Wind inflow prediction is a complex nonlinear problem that requires a more
robust and accurate reconstruction.
Overall, ANNs are more generic, as they do not depend on specific physical phenomena, which
allows them to adapt to various conditions without constraints. Although training ANNs can
3
==PAGE:15==
be more computationally expensive compared to calibrating decomposition models, the benefits
of accuracy and flexibility make them a compelling choice for the wind field estimation.
1.4
Problem Statement and Research Goals
This thesis hypothesizes that a turbine sensor-based machine learning (ML) method to estimate
wind turbine inflow is more accurate and comprehensive than traditional methods, such as those
based on met mast measurements. Using the turbine’s operational data and integrating it with
advanced ML models, it is expected that the reconstructed wind inflow field will provide greater
accuracy, especially in capturing the spatial and temporal complexities of the wind within the
atmospheric boundary layer and the induction zone.
Although previous studies have validated the concept of using turbines as sensors to estimate
integrated wind characteristics such as shear and veer, this research expands the concept by
aiming to reconstruct the entire wind inflow field using only the turbine response data, without
relying on external flow measurements. This represents a significant shift towards fully data-
driven models for wind field reconstruction. In this regard, the aim is to beat these limitations
by integrating LES data with neural network models to achieve a precise and comprehensive
wind field reconstruction.
The methodology of the thesis is based on a turbine-specific inflow model by incorporating
in-depth LES data for a wind farm in a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN). The wind turbines
are modeled using an actuator line/disk technique, and aero-elastic responses are captured via
an aero-servo-elastic solver; i.e. FLEX5 (Sessarego et al., 2017).
In this study, we investigated the following research questions:
RQ 1: What is the degree of correlation among the various sensors on a wind turbine, and how
does understanding this correlation contribute to predicting inflow details and assessing
the suitability of surrogate models?
RQ 2: What are the advantages and disadvantages of data-driven inflow models, particularly
in terms of their construction and training, and how effectively can physical principles
be incorporated into these approaches?
RQ 3: How accurate and robust are data-driven inflow models when evaluated in terms of error
and uncertainty, focusing on their performance and reliability in predicting wind turbine
inflow?
By answering these research questions and addressing the goals stated above, this thesis aims
to push the boundaries of current wind energy technology by providing a robust and accurate
method to reconstruct the wind field, leading to better turbine performance and eﬀiciency.

Understanding Neural Networks
2.1.1
Recurrent Neural Network
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are designed as a kind of artificial neural network (ANN)
to recognize patterns in data sequences, whether the target is speech recognition (Li and Wu,
2015), handwriting recognition (Graves et al., 2009) and time series forecasting (Tealab, 2018).
It is considered as one of the two broad types of ANN, as its characteristic relies on the direction
of the flow of information moving through its layers.
Figure 2.1 shows that the main difference between the classical feedforward neural network
(FNN) and RNN is that in the former the data/input travels in a single direction, as the
information goes through the input layer and exits through the output layer. RNNs can improve
such predictions, since the output of a layer can be fed back to the input. Until the first layer
of an RNN everything is quite similar between them. Following this layer, each unit retains
some information from the previous step, allowing it to function as a memory cell during
computation.

within each layer. These weights are adjusted using two techniques, called backpropagation

Traditional RNNs face serious challenges, especially when dealing with long-term dependencies
caused by problems such as gradient disappearance and gradient explosion. The former raises
the issue of diminishing gradients when training a deep neural network (DNN), specifically
occurs when the gradients that are propagated backward through the layers are very small,
which leads to the inability of the network to effectively update the weights. The activation
functions, such as the sigmoid and hyperbolic tangent, introduce non-linearity in a DNN model.
The gradients of such functions can become close to zero either for large or small inputs, leading
to the vanishing gradient problem. Given a DNN, the backpropagation that calculates the
gradients of the loss function as functions of the weights using the chain rule method also
exacerbates the problem. This can lead to slower learning, poor convergence, and weak capture
of the complex pattern in the data.
Gradient explosion refers to the case where an error
gradient calculated during training is very large, resulting in an unstable neural network. This
explosion can occur when the gradients through the layers exceed 1 causing an exponential
growth. Unfortunately, as Figure 2.4 suggests, as the gap between the input and output states
grows, the RNNs become very unstable and they are unable to learn how the information is
connected. Further exploration of the problem can be found in Bengio et al., 1994.
2.1.2
Long-Short Term Memory Network
An advanced type of RNN, namely Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, tries to solve
such issues by allowing mechanisms for long-term storage and retrieval of information. LSTM
networks were invented by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997 as a novel, eﬀicient and gradient-
based method to learn to store information over extended time intervals. LSTM neural networks
add cell state to store long-term information. A gate structure is introduced to achieve infor-
mation protection and control. This can solve the aforementioned issues with traditional RNNs
and deal with long-distance dependence (Gu et al., 2021).
Since this study concerns the estimation problem of the wind inflow field, LSTM is suitable
to deal with it as the predicted value is highly correlated with time series. In addition, the
estimation of the wind inflow has a strong non-linearity due to the complex phenomena that
occur around the wind turbine.
Furthermore, LSTM is robust enough to noise and fault
tolerance, guiding the model to better approximate the full complex nonlinear relationships
(Chen and Han, 2022).

This section will try and bridge this gap between the qualitative and quantitative by explaining
the computations that are necessary through the equations of an LSTM cell. It is important to
understand LSTMs from both perspectives. If the quality of these networks is not understood
well, then it is not easy to optimize this technique.
There are three main components in an LSTM cell, namely the input, output, and forget gates.
The main reason behind this is that, using this method, the cell can remember data for some
time, and these three gates act as regulators for the information going in and out of this cell.
Figure 2.5 shows how these gates coexist in an LSTM cell.
Figure 2.5: LSTM Cell Structure. This diagram illustrates the three main components of an
LSTM cell: the forget gate, input gate, and output gate.
In simple words:
1. Forget Gate (ft) can decide how much of the information within the cell state will be
“forgotten”. The sigmoid function is a nonlinear activation function, where it outputs a
value between 0 and 1 representing the amount of each component to be kept or forgotten.
8
==PAGE:20==
2. Input Gate (it) controls how the cell state is updated with new information. Combining
a sigmoid and a tanh activation functions, this gate determines which values should be
updated.
3. Output Gate (ot) determines the output of the cell, based on its state, which is regulated
by a sigmoid function.
Compared to an RNN that has a single neural network inside its cell, the LSTM cell consists
of four FNNs that interact in their own way. Figure 2.6 shows a detailed representation of
the chain-like structure of an LSTM. In this diagram, each line transmits a complete vector
from the output of one node to the inputs of other nodes. Yellow circles indicate point-wise
operations such as vector addition, whereas green boxes represent neural network layers. Lines
that merge signify concatenation, while lines that fork indicate the vector is being copied and
sent to multiple destinations.
Figure 2.6: LSTM Cell Structure.
This diagram provides a detailed representation of the
LSTM cell’s internal structure, showing how it processes sequences of data to capture long-
term dependencies. The yellow circles represent point-wise operations (such as addition and
multiplication), while the green boxes indicate neural network layers. Lines that merge denote
concatenation, and lines that fork represent copying the vector to multiple destinations.
The main idea behind LSTMs is the cell state, which is the horizontal line connecting ct−1 to
ct, as it runs on top of the LSTM block. The cell state is like a conveyor belt as it runs through
the entire chain of LSTM blocks, with only some linear interactions. Using the aforementioned
gates, the LSTM can add or remove information in the cell state. Inside the input gate, there
exists a tanh layer gt, which creates a vector of new possible values to add to the cell state, as
it decides which to update. The combination is where the update of the cell state takes place.
Mathematically, it can be written as:
ft = σ(W xf · xt + W hf · ht−1 + bf)
it = σ(W xi · xt + W hi · ht−1 + bi)
gt = tanh(W xg · xt + W hg · ht−1 + bg)
ot = σ(W xo · xt + W ho · ht−1 + bo)
ct = ft ⊙ct−1 + it ⊙gt
ht = ot ⊙tanh(ct)
(2.3)
9

where, W and b represent the weight matrices and bias vectors for the respective gates, and
⊙is the Hadamard product (also known as the element-wise product). The equations above
apply to a single time step, which means that they must be recalculated for each subsequent
time step. For instance, in a sequence of three time steps, these equations will be computed
three times, once for each time step. The weight matrices and biases remain constant over time.
This implies that the same weight matrices are used to compute the outputs at each time step.
Bi-LSTM (Bidirectional LSTM) is an LSTM that processes sequential data in both forward and
backward directions. This bidirectional approach allows the model to capture both past and
future context within the input sequence. It includes two LSTM layers: one for the forward
direction and one for the backward direction, each maintaining its own hidden states and
memory cells. The input sequence is fed into the forward LSTM layer from the first to the last
time step. The forward LSTM computes hidden states and updates memory cells on the basis
of current input and previous states. Moreover, the input sequence is fed into the backward
LSTM layer in reverse order, from the last to the first time step. The backward LSTM also
computes hidden states and updates memory cells in a similar way. The hidden states from
both LSTM layers were then combined at each time step, often through concatenation or
other transformations. This advancement will be an upgrade to the prediction model, as the
performance is better than traditional LSTMs. The disadvantages are high computational cost,
large amounts of data are needed, and they are diﬀicult to interpret. This process should not
be confused with stacking LSTM layers.

2.2.3

The purpose of this subsection is to introduce a way to scale down the wind inflow data in order
to build a simplistic inflow model initially, which can predict on a very coarse grid and flexibly
increase the resolution as needed. Additionally, since the rotor moves azimuthally, defining the
grid in a polar coordinate system allows for better integration of information about the blades,
such as the a bending root moment (BRM), along with the rotor position. This integration can
significantly improve prediction accuracy, especially in the azimuthal direction.
The original flow field data are provided on a Cartesian grid (y, z) with a spatial spacing of
approximately 0.077R. For the aforementioned reasons, this Cartesian grid will be transformed
into a polar grid. The polar grid is defined with coordinates (r, θ), where r is the radial distance
from the center of the rotor, and θ represents the azimuthal position around the rotor disk. The
new grid can be defined flexibly, with the number of sections nsec chosen as a power of two (2x)
in order to ensure a manageable and computationally eﬀicient grid resolution. The smallest
resolution starts with the four quadrants of the rotor, where one can easily distinguish the
effects of the horizontal and vertical shear. Each section (in this case the quadrant) is further
divided into a large number of smaller polar cells based on the polar grid. An interpolation is
then followed after the transformation to map the velocity data onto the polar grid. The result
is shown in 2.10b after transforming and interpolating the inflow data from 2.10a.

2.3
LSTM-Based Wind Field Reconstruction Model
In this subsection, a WFR model is presented based on LSTM neural networks. This model
leverages high-fidelity data from LES and integrates machine learning techniques to achieve
precise wind field estimates.
2.3.1
Workflow and Processes
Figure 2.12 shows the comprehensive workflow for reconstructing the field of turbulence wind
input. The workflow is divided into four main stages: LES-FLEX5 Simulations, Data Prepa-
ration, Data-Driven Learning (Training), and Prediction.
1. LES-FLEX5 Simulations: The process begins with these simulations, where high-fidelity
data are generated using the FLEX5 and EllipSys3D tools. For the purposes of this study,
these data were provided and ready to be used.
2. Data Preparation: This step involves SEWS estimation to preprocess the LES data,
followed by techniques to understand the correlations between various variables of the
turbine response and the turbulent flow field.
3. Data-Driven Learning: In this stage, input features are selected and preprocessed for the
LSTM neural network. This step focuses on utilizing the powerful learning algorithms of
LSTM networks in order to estimate a high-resolution grid.
4. Prediction: Finally, the trained model is used to predict the turbulent wind field on
the entire datasets of turbines that were not used for training. They are evaluated for
performance based on the label data available and the former is considered in order to
repeat the learning procedure (back to Step 3).

Sequence-to-sequence models
Sequence-to-sequence models are useful for tasks such as forecasting and virtual sensing. The
former refers to a model that predicts future values based on past data, since the input window
precedes the output window by a time shift of τ, as shown in Figure 2.13a. The virtual sensor
model predicts values that occur at the same time or earlier with the input data, shown in
Figure 2.13b.
(a) Forecasting model
(b) Virtual sensor model
Figure 2.13: Sequence-to-sequence models: (a) Forecasting model, where the input window
precedes the output window, predicting future values based on past data; (b) Virtual sensor
model, which predicts values occurring at the same time or earlier than the input data.
Since wind inflow is always ahead of the turbine response in time, real-time forecasting is then
not feasible. Instead, since the outputs are contemporaneous with the inputs, the concept of
a virtual sensor is used. An adaptation of the virtual sensor model to fit the purpose of this
study is illustrated in Figure 2.14, where τin represents the length of the input window, τout
represents the length of the output window, and τshift represents a shift applied to the output
window, justifying the mutual interaction between the turbine response and flow (Andersen
and Sørensen, 2018). In the implementation of the models, τout and τshift are not explored
deeply and are kept constant at 1 and 0 s, respectively. For example, if τin = 10 s, it means
that 10 seconds of input data are needed to predict 1 second of the time series.
The mathematical representation of this adapted model is as follows:
ˆy[t −τin + τshift, . . . , t −τin + τshift + τout] = g(X[t −τin, . . . , t])
(2.6)
After the data set is separated into these sequences, it is served in batches. The batch size is
the number of samples that are passed into the model at each iteration during training and
can affect the frequency with which model parameters are updated. Larger batch sizes require
memory and computation. Common sizes are 32, 64, and 128. This study uses a batch size of
32 for a good balance between eﬀiciency and generalization with the validation set. When the
batch size is very large, the network struggles to generalize well.

2.3.3
Network Architecture
The architecture of the LSTM-based model for wind field reconstruction is designed to capture
the temporal dependencies and complex patterns in the wind inflow data. The network consists
of multiple layers, including LSTM layers, dropout layers, and dense layers, which work together
to predict the wind field with high accuracy. In addition, the model incorporates a weight decay
rate as another way of regularization, an adaptive learning rate with a scheduler, and a suitable
optimizer for the training process.
The flow chart in Figure 2.15 combines the overall architecture of the LSTM-based model.
For a detailed view of the architecture, see Figure A.1, for a case where two LSTM and fully
connected layers are used.
Figure 2.15: Flowchart depicting the architecture of the LSTM-based model. The process starts
with the preprocessing of input data, which is then processed through LSTM layers, followed
by dropout layers, and finally through dense layers with a linear activation function to produce
the output.
Stacked LSTM Layer
In deep learning, deep hierarchical models are known to be more eﬀicient in representing func-
tions compared to shallow models Pascanu et al., 2014. In the case of RNNs, because of their
sequential nature, they are inherently deep in time. In the study of Graves et al., 2013, they
found how RNNs also benefit from depth in space when stacking multiple recurrent layers,
similar to stacking feedforward layers in conventional neural networks. A stacked LSTM layer
is characterized by its depth (nLSTM) and its hidden size (hLSTM).
19
==PAGE:31==
Fully Connected Layer
The final LSTM layer is then followed by a dense layer that predicts the output. This fully con-
nected layer (FC) combines the information from the LSTM layers, as a sequence is extracted,
and applies an aﬀine linear transformation to make the final prediction. The same as in the
LSTM layers, the depth (nFC) and hidden size (hFC) define the FC layers.
Dropout Layer
A dropout layer can be found in multiple neural networks as it prevents overfitting by adding
noise to neurons. In this case, dropout (d) is applied after the LSTM layers, as applying it
within the LSTM cells can disrupt their ability to remember important sequential data.
Output Activation Function
There are three activation functions commonly used in the output layer, linear, sigmoid, or
softmax. As the latter two functions are only appropriate for classification problems, linear
activation is the most suitable for the regression problem for which this study is concerned.
Weight Decay Rate
Another commonly used regularization technique is weight decay (w) which penalizes large
weights by adding a term to the loss function proportional to the squared weights. This weight
decay rate can be adjusted to control overfitting and underfitting. This can change depending
on the data, the model and the learning rate. There is a trade-off between regularization and
flexibility when selecting the weight decay rate.
Learning Rate
The learning rate (α) is a crucial hyperparameter as it controls the rate at which the model
weights are updated in response to the estimated error. It is beneficial that the learning rate
can be dynamically adjusted during training, i.e. if the model is well learned, then the learning
rate should decrease, so a learning rate scheduler is used. The 1cycle policy will be applied
that changes the learning rate after every pass through the data.
Optimizer
The model optimizer dictates how the model weights are updated on the basis of the computed
gradients. Common optimizers are Adam, RMSprop, and SGD. Each has their own ability to
handle gradients in noisy problems and large datasets. The article by Wirth, 2020 gives a nice
insight into how these optimizers work, detailing their benefits and cons, which helps to choose
the best optimizer for various machine learning tasks.
Epochs
When a complete pass is done through the training data, which are divided based on the batch
size, then this is called an epoch. If the number of epochs is very large, then the model is prone
to overfit.

Two separate approaches will be taken into consideration regarding the input and output con-

for predicting the entire wind field.
Model A: Predicting SEWS at a point
In this model, the input Xt is the turbine sensors and the output ˆyt is the SEWS at a specific
point. To accomplish this, the position of the section is required as an input, which means that
the coordinates r and θ are added as additional inputs.
• Input: Input X has a shape of (nsamples, nfeatures), where nsamples is the number of time
steps and nfeatures is the number of input features (e.g. wind speed at hub, power, pitch,
rotor speed).
• Output: Output ˆy is a one-dimensional array, with shape of (nsamples, ), representing
SEWS at (r, θ). The model duplicates X by the number of sections and r and θ are
constant through time. For example, predicting four quadrants, the input becomes as
shown in Figure 2.16.

along with coordinates (r, θ).
The relationship can be expressed mathematically as follows:
ˆyt = g(r, θ, Xt)
(2.7)

2.3.5
Performance Measurement Metrics
Training evaluation
There are various aspects of evaluating the model during training, with the main indications
appearing on the training and validation curves, addressing overfitting and underfitting, the
chosen loss function and backpropagation.
The training and validation curves typically plot the loss over each epoch, providing insights
on how well the model learns and generalizes to unseen data. A training curve that steadily
decreases indicates that the model learns patterns in the training data. In the case of validation,
the decreasing validation loss indicates how well the model generalizes to new data. Overfitting
occurs when the model learns the former too well, even noise and outliers, leading to poor
generalization. This is indicated by a low training loss and a high validation loss. On the other
hand, when the opposite happens, there is underfitting where the model is too simple that fails
to capture important patterns in the data. This shows that the model needs some better tuning
to fit the data adequately.
The main difference between the training and validation passes is that the gradients, subse-
quently the model’s weights, are not updated during validation, providing an unbiased evalua-
tion of the model’s performance on the validation set.

2.3.6
Hyperparameter study
Hyperparameter optimization is the process for choosing the optimal hyperparameters for a
neural network. A hyperparameter can be any parameter that can control the learning pro-
cess. By selecting the right hyperparameters, the network will be able to learn effectively and
accurately. Each set of data needs different sets of hyperparameters for an accurate prediction.
23
==PAGE:35==
There is no universal answer to the question of what the optimal architecture is, i.e. what the
optimal number of layers, number of neurons, or the most suitable optimizer is.
Common approaches in the study of hyperparameters are grid search, random search, Bayesian
optimization, gradient-based optimization, and population-based training. In this study, an
open source hyperparameter optimization framework called Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019) will
be used. Optuna is framework-agnostic, which means that it can optimize networks for any
architecture. A Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE), a Bayesian optimization method,
will be used to select hyperparameters for different trials. TPE is selected for its eﬀiciency in
handling complex search spaces and its ability to provide good results with fewer trials. The
optimization will run for a maximum of 1000 trials or until the validation loss stops improving.
The evaluation metric to judge the performance of a proposed set of hyperparameters is the
validation loss. This metric is chosen because it directly reflects the ability of the model to
generalize to unseen data.
The hyperparameters that will be optimized include:
• Number of neurons per layer, hLSTM and hFC
• Number of LSTM layers and Dense layers, nLSTM and nFC
• Dropout rate, d
• Learning rate, α
• Number of epochs, nepochs
• Weight decay, w
• Maximum learning rate for scheduling, αmax
• Optimizer
• Bidirectionality of LSTM cells, D
The results of the hyperparameter optimization will be presented, highlighting the best hy-
perparameters found and their corresponding validation performance. The search space for
each hyperparameter is defined in Table A.1. For consistency and feasibility, some parameters
remain constant in different cases, in order to ensure reliable and meaningful comparisons. In
other cases, the network architecture will be optimized.

In order to further assess any more information that might be available and ‘lost’ through the

feature independently, by measuring the relative difference to the model’s prediction error after
permuting any feature. The following steps are performed to complete this procedure:

Feature Selection Analysis
In the previous subsection, the basic features were used to train the inflow model. Although for
a free-stream turbine a good performance was achieved due to less complex wind conditions,
when going in the wake-affected regions, the turbines have higher RMSE values. For this reason,
in the feature selection analysis, all wind farms will be analyzed in order to enhance the model
with additional features to capture the complexities of wind behavior in wakes.
3.2.1
Correlation-Base Feature Selection
Figure 3.5 presents the results of the cross-correlation as a heat map for the three different
layouts of the wind farms, where the x-axis represents the turbines chosen for the analysis,
representative of the different flow conditions that occur on each farm. The turbines appear
in the results as they are in their farm layouts, i.e. from left to right along the streamwise
32
==PAGE:44==
direction. The heat maps illustrate the correlation coeﬀicients (ρxy) and their corresponding
time delays (τ) between various features and the REWS. Table 3.3 lists the selected features
based on the cross-correlation analysis, together with a description.
The features shown in Figure 3.5 are separated to three main categories: a) operational param-
eters and b) loads. Figure B.2 in the appendices shows the correlations of the wind speed to
the modes/deflections of the turbine. The analysis showed that the operational features consis-
tently show strong correlations across different farm layouts, indicating significant impact on
the REWS. It should be noted that in wake-affected turbines the pitch turns to have a negative
correlation with the wind speed, meaning that this feature is mainly useful in free-stream tur-
bines. Along with these features, shaft torsion (MZN, driven by aerodynamic torque) appears
to have a strong correlation with the wind even in free-stream turbines. The rest of the bold
features exhibit notable correlations, especially in the wake-affected turbines, suggesting their
relevance in capturing the high-frequency components of the wind on the turbine. Specifically,
turbine loads on the tower at H = 40.4 m (Tc) are also available, which can help predict lower
sections of the rotor. The bottom loads of the tower (Tb) in the y and z directions also have a
high correlation to the incoming wind. Features related to modes and deflections (e.g., tower
mode 1L, flap mode 1, shaft bend X) tend to be higher frequency signals compared to loads.
Although these features can provide valuable information, particularly in wake-affected regions
where dynamic interactions are more complex, they are prone to high uncertainty as a result
of the complexities of accurately measuring these quantities.
The time delay (τ) analysis highlights the temporal dependencies between these features and
the REWS, which is crucial to define τin for the model. If the input sequence length is large,
then the size of the dataset reduces as the input sequence dataset is smaller. However, if this
length is smaller than it should be, then the model will not be able to capture these temporal
dependencies accurately. For the selected features, their corresponding delays appear to be
within the time window of -20 to -10 s. For this reason, a value of τin = 20 s is suggested for
the preprocessing of the input sequences, to account for these temporal dependencies of the
features on the wind prediction.

3.4
Impact of Atmospheric Boundary Conditions
In the previous sections, it was assumed that the data from all turbines could be uniformly useful
for training, without considering the variability in atmospheric boundary conditions, specifically
the surface roughness length. However, one of the three wind farms under consideration has a
different roughness length boundary condition, set at 2e-4 m (30-degree layout), compared to
2e-3 m for the 0-degree and 90-degree layouts.
This difference can be crucial, as higher surface roughness results in more turbulence and
a thicker boundary layer, leading to increased turbulence intensity and more complex wind
profiles, where a lower roughness length can result to the opposite. In both cases, the surface
roughness is still considered relatively low, which results in a steeper boundary layer profile,
also known as the wind shear profile. This steep profile means that wind speeds change more
rapidly with height.
The goal of this section is to investigate whether blending data from the farm with a different
roughness length (30-degree layout) into the training dataset with the other farms (0-degree
and 90-degree layouts) is beneficial. If it turns out to be detrimental, it may be necessary to
consider the roughness length as an input feature to the model, to account for the different
atmospheric boundary conditions.
The performances of two distinct models will be compared, each using a different set of input
features:
• Model A: First, a model is trained using data from the 0-degree and 90-degree wind
farm layouts and evaluated on the four evaluation turbines from the 90-degree layout, as
previously established in Section 3.1.
• Model B: The same model is then trained using data from the 30-degree and 90-degree
layouts, with evaluation performed on the same turbines.
Note: The 90-degree layout is included in both models to maintain a consistent dataset size for
training, as it has fewer available turbines for training compared to the other two layouts. The
model architecture for all cases is listed in Table A.4 in the Appendices.

3.5
Grid Resolution Study
The last section of the results concerns a study on the impact of grid resolution on the accuracy
of wind inflow predictions. Grid resolution refers to how finely the rotor plane is segmented
(nsec) during the modeling process, with each grid representing a distinct section of the rotor.
As the grid resolution increases, the model must capture more detailed variations in the wind
field, which can significantly affect the accuracy of the predictions.
The models are trained with grid resolutions corresponding to nsec = 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, where each
grid requires a separately trained model, since the target dimension increases in powers of two
based on nsec. The model architecture for all cases is listed in Table A.5 in the Appendices.
The objective here is to determine how accurately the model can predict while maintaining
the accuracy of the model. By evaluating the performance of models trained on different grid
resolutions, the goal is to identify the optimal balance between grid resolution and prediction
accuracy, considering the computational complexity involved in higher resolutions.

3.5.2
Outcome
The findings of both visual and quantitative error analyses indicate that the predictive capacity
of the model declines as the resolution of the grid increases beyond nsec = 8. Although the
model performs adequately up to nsec = 8, accuracy decreases significantly at higher resolutions.
The best performance among the higher resolutions is seen in nsec = 32, where the model shows
some capacity to capture wind dynamics, especially in the lower part of the rotor. However, the
model completely fails at nsec = 64, highlighting the challenges of handling high grid resolutions
without suﬀicient input features or computational resources.
In summary, the model is best suited for lower grid resolutions. Although there is potential
for improvement with better optimization and more computational power, the current input
features are insuﬀicient to support high-resolution predictions effectively.

Chapter 4
Discussion
4.1
Summary
This thesis work initiated the process of implementing machine learning models for predicting
wind turbine inflow, using sensor data from the turbines, with a focus on different input/output
configurations likely to impact the model performance and eﬀiciency. Two main models were
developed: Model A - prediction of the SEWS at a point, and Model B - prediction of the
entire wind field throughout the rotor plane. As shown in Section 3.1, both models considered
were applied with a minimal feature set, and although a similar accuracy was achieved, Model
A has a high computational cost compared to Model B. However, a disadvantage of Model B
is that it depends on the grid configuration, so it needs different models for different grids.
Research Question 1 examined the correlation of different turbine sensors and their effects on
inflow prediction. As discussed in Section 3.3.1, cross-correlation analysis revealed significant
relationships between turbine response and inflow. For example, load-related features such as
blade root bending moments and tower loads have a high correlation with the inflow in wake-
affected turbines, which can be crucial for accurate predictions in complex wake interactions.
For free-stream turbines, the minimal feature set of operational parameters was suﬀicient for
a good prediction. Although load features are necessary for complex flow conditions, simpler
models can effectively capture inflow dynamics in more straightforward scenarios. The anal-
ysis through PFI identified additional features that could enhance the model’s performance.
However, the additional features resulted in marginal improvements over the correlation-based
features; thus, proving the fact that cross-correlation features themselves offer a good foun-
dation for making effective predictions. While load-related features like blade root bending
moments and tower loads can enhance inflow predictions, their practical use depends on the
availability and ease of obtaining such data. Most turbines will have operational parameters,
such as hub wind speed and rotor speed, that are relatively easy to collect. Unlike this, loads
often require more specialized, expensive sensors, which may be less commonly fitted to all
turbines. The benefits of accurately predicting the inflow of wind, including enhanced turbine
performance and more eﬀicient energy production are clear. The measured loads bring in a
trade-off: more detailed predictions can be improved on, but complexity and expense increase
in data collection. Understanding sensor correlations is vital to selecting the right features
and optimizing model complexity in order to provide a strong base for better predictions with
balanced computational cost.
Research Question 2 explored the advantages and disadvantages of data-driven inflow mod-
els, focusing on model development and training. Data-driven models show significant advan-
tages in adaptability and flexibility. They can be tailored to work perfectly under many different
operational conditions, such as below and above rated wind speeds, where the turbine operates
48
==PAGE:60==
in different states. This ability allows the model to maintain accuracy across these distinct
modes, making them valuable in dynamic environments. However, the study has also shown
problems, notably with increased grid resolution. Larger resolutions require more accurate and
higher-quality data; that is, more detailed measurements of blade loads and deflections in order
to properly model wind inflow and turbine behavior along the rotor span. In the absence of
detailed data, this holds back the models from providing high-fidelity predictions, especially
in complex flow conditions, as the model does not capture physical principles and is driven by
data. As more data and features are incorporated, understanding the model’s decision-making
process becomes more diﬀicult, which can impede validation and lower confidence in the model’s
predictions, in particular, for rare or extreme operational scenarios.
Research Question 3 addressed to the inflow accuracy and robustness of data-driven models
for the prediction of wind turbine inflow. Evaluating the two models with different input/output
configurations provided insights into their performance under varying conditions. This study
showed that data-driven models are accurate, more so when the models are developed by
considering sensor correlations in modeling the features, as the predictions were improved,
particularly in wake-affected turbines. For a wind inflow prediction, this study indicated that
using data-driven models with proper features can guarantee consistent performances under
different grid resolutions. To ensure robustness, at least two grid resolutions were tested during
each model selection to observe behavior under different levels of detail. These tests revealed
models work adequately at relatively low grid resolutions but clearly show significant loss of
accuracy as resolution increases, and the input features may not be suﬀicient to capture the fine-
scale dynamics of the wind at high resolution. However, it was observed that high-frequency
load data could be misleading, as frequency analysis showed that the predictions consists of a
much higher-frequency components in the inflow when compared to the actual data, showing
a noisiness in the predictions. Here, each feature could be studied separately by evaluating its
contribution to the model.
4.2
Limitations
Despite these promising results, the study revealed many limitations:
• Lack of Physical Modeling: The models are purely data-driven and do not capture phys-
ical principles, which might limit their ability to describe complex wind inflow behaviors
where the physical dynamics are important.
• Atmospheric and Terrain Influences: Wake recovery is influenced by atmospheric condi-
tions and also terrain roughness, that models are not capable of accounting for. Although
the thesis included a basic study on different surface roughness between farms, this area
could be further explored to better understand its impact on wind inflow predictions.
• Computational Constraints: This study was only limited to a maximum of 24 h compu-
tational time; hence, the computation restricted the exploration of higher grid resolutions
and more complex model architectures. While more features could be incorporated in the
model, on the other side, they also run the risk of overfitting where the model captures
noise in place of true patterns.
• Turbine Region-Specific Performance: Here, the above-rated and below-rated wind speed
regions are not separated even though the data volume is much higher in the above-rated
region. These regions represent fairly different operational states of the turbine. Focusing
the model into just one specific region (either below or above-rated) as a start could make
it have more accurate and specialized predictions.
49
==PAGE:61==
• Quality of Data: The prediction accuracy, especially for the outer parts of the rotor, may
be better with a more detailed input dataset, especially with respect to blade information.
With more information on how the wind varies in both the radial and azimuthal directions
across a rotor, this model could be developed to capture these complex dynamics of the
wind.
• Modes and Deflections: The modes or deflections of blades and tower were not included
in this study. Inclusion may have led to improvement in the accuracy, but loads are the
main concern since modes and deflections are highly uncertain.
• Permutation Feature Importance: The PFI analysis did not significantly improve model
performance, but this may be due to not fully utilizing its potential. Further exploration
and development of PFI might result in more value added to produce useful insights in
future feature selection studies. A PFI study can also be conducted in every single model
developed in this study in order to gain more insight on the most impactful features to
the prediction.
• Model Architecture and Hyperparameters: All models have been tuned with the same
architectural structure, with the same hyperparameters used across cases with many
similarities. A more flexible model architecture, along with a deeper understanding of
each hyperparameter, could allow for better optimization bounds with the Optuna solver,
leading to improved training outcomes.
• Preprocessing of Temporal Parameters: The preprocessing mainly focused on the input
sequence length τin, and not on the shift length τshift, and output sequence length τout.
With more improvement in ways related to these parameters, better temporal dependen-
cies could be captured within the wind inflow data, as the preprocessing is one of the
biggest aspects of data-driven modeling.
• Spatial correlation: The only spatial correlation considered in this study was what hap-
pens within the neural network model itself, with no explicit restriction in the model
output. This lack of spatial correlation limited the model’s ability to accurately predict
the inflow especially in higher grid resolutions.
• Comparison against other models or established benchmarks: Although the results were
promising, they were not at a level where they could be reliably compared against other
established benchmarks. Further refinement and testing are needed to bring the models
to a competitive standard.
These, therefore, form limitations of the models, which must be addressed in future investiga-
tions in order to enhance the operation of the models in an actual wind farm.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary of Findings
This study aimed to explore the capability of turbine response data in wind inflow prediction
based on data-driven models without the strong dependence on conventional inflow measure-
ments, such as LiDAR and/or met mast. The present study was guided by several questions:
What information about the inflow there is in the turbine response, the strengths and weak-
nesses of data-driven models, and how accurate and robust these models are in different wind
conditions.
The research questions were partially answered, and evidence was provided for potential work
on these models to provide important insights into this field.
Significant correlations have
been found among the turbine sensor data, which helps improve inflow predictions, especially
under complex wake-affected conditions. However, computational costs, grid dependency, and
limitations in data quality show scope for further refinement.
Therefore, this work will add to the literature and further demonstrate the viability of a turbine
sensor-based approach for forecasting wind inflow. The potential is surely there, but much more
work must be done to fully exploit the capabilities of data-driven models in this context.
5.2
Future Work
The following future research directions, which might serve to build on this study’s findings,
are offered as suggestions.
• Combining Decomposition Approaches with Machine Learning: Combining methods such
as empirical mode decomposition helps better capture complex patterns of wind inflow.
The model becomes much better in detecting and predicting different scales of turbulence,
which will increase its overall accuracy.
• Embedding Physical Laws into Neural Networks: The use of physics-informed neural
networks may lead to a model with the positive aspects of data-driven approaches and
high reliability in physics. This very method may yield even closer predictions, especially
in extreme or untested conditions.
• Transfer Learning: This would make it possible to extend models trained on one dataset
to deal with a new environment or condition, thus enhancing generalizability. This could
alleviate the requirement for complete retraining when deploying across geographies or
operational scenarios.
51
==PAGE:63==
• Load Validation by Means of Reconstructed Wake Fields: The use of reconstructed wake
fields in aeroelastic simulations, such as for the DTU 10MW turbine, would finally serve
toward the validation of model accuracy with respect to actual turbine loading.
• Impact from Atmospheric Boundary Conditions: A more detailed understanding of the
atmosphere conditions, including roughness and the terrain effects on the surface, will
improve the predictability of the models about the wind farms in different locations. The
development of further flexible models for a given type of environment will be based on
this.
• Improved Data Quality and Extrapolation: Data quality must be improved, especially
for these extreme-end sections of the rotor, and methods of extrapolation from the BRM
is considered in an attempt to improve the inflow prediction. It is only such extensive
information on radial and azimuthal changes in the wind that can make it possible to
properly model complex dynamics in the wind.
• CNN-LSTM Architectures: The use of a CNN in an LSTM model would enrich the
inference of both temporal and spatial data dependencies. In scenarios where the wind
patterns are dynamic, the above method can improve the overall accuracy of prediction.
5.3
Final Thoughts
This research work is believed to have contributed to this growing body of data-driven wind
inflow prediction and has demonstrated the enormous potential of turbine response data. The
research not only opens a novel line of development, but also highlights the necessity of further
development to unlock the full benefit of these models in practice.
