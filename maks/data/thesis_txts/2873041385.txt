Abstract
As a consequence of climate changes water levels are rising and weather conditions are
changing.
This increases the risk of floodings, making accurate bathymetry critical;
however, there are critical gaps in the current manual bathymetric survey practice.
The purpose of the HydroHex project is to make bathymetry surveys more accurate.
This thesis evaluates the combination of three SLAM algorithms; ORB-SLAM3, RTAB-
MAP, and Cartographer (2D LiDAR), for navigation in riverine conditions where the
GNSS signal might be insuﬀicient. The experiment was divided in two parts: Test in
an indoor pool using a known rectangular trajectory, and tests performed in a riverine
environment at Lyngby Sø using a Total Station for ground truth.
In the controled environment, Cartographer showed promising results producing the
most accurate trajectory. This continued in the field tests maintaining close alignment
with the total station ground truth. ORB-SLAM3 was having diﬀiculties in both the
controlled environment and in the field test accumulating drift over the trajectory length.
RTAB-Map achieved metric accuracy in the indoor pool but it was not able to reconstruct
the exact route. In the field tests it achieved reasonable trajectory reconstruction under
tree shade.
In conclusion, the Cartographer performed the best, followed by RTAB-Map. ORB-
SLAM3 had diﬀiculties in both environments. A combination of algorithms for use when
GNSS fails still needs more investigation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Manual bathymetry in Danish waterways requires surveyors to wade through rivers
with hand-held equipment. This method is dangerous, ineﬀicient, and often impossible
in areas with dense vegetation or unstable banks. The RiverWorks project addresses
this problem with an amphibious drone, called HydroHex, that automates bathymetric
data collection in riverine environments where traditional methods fail.
1.1
Project Motivation
Climate change drives more frequent and severe flooding, creating an urgent demand for
bathymetric data[55]. Manual surveys cannot provide the coverage or frequency needed
for accurate flood prediction. Current methods require surveyors to physically enter
waterways, limiting both the number of areas that can be accessed and the speed of
data collection.

1 Introduction
While GNSS (Global Navigational Satellite System) enables precise positioning in open
areas, riverine environments degrade signals through canopy cover and multipath effects.
Bathymetric surveys require precise position estimates that GNSS may not always be
able to provide.
This thesis investigates offline localization methods using RGB cameras, RGB-D
cameras, LiDAR, and inertial sensors to estimate the drone’s trajectory without rely-
ing on GNSS. These sensors enable SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)
algorithms that build environmental maps while tracking sensor position within them.
Offline processing removes computational constraints, allowing sophisticated algorithms
to extract maximum positional accuracy from recorded sensor data.
1.2
Technical Challenges
Amphibious drone localization in rivers faces three primary challenges:
• Environmental Complexity: Water creates dynamic reflections that disrupt
visual sensors, vegetation poses collision risks, and wave action induces constant
platform motion. These factors create a uniquely diﬀicult navigation environment.
• GNSS Degradation: Forest canopies and riverside vegetation severely degrade
satellite signals through attenuation, multipath effects and occlusion. Positioning
accuracy drops from centimeter-level in open areas to multi-meter errors under
canopy, with potential for complete signal loss in dense vegetation.
• Multi-Modal Localization: Visual, laser, and inertial sensors all fail under
different conditions. Creating robust localization requires algorithms that exploit
sensor strengths while compensating for individual weaknesses.
Besides these major challenges, there is also retrofitting the drone to fit and accommo-
date all the new sensors. Additionally, there is the ever-bearing challenge of working
with drones e.g. hardware failure and crashing. This can be especially troublesome
as the HydroHex is operating on or near water meaning that a flight failure can end
catastrophically.
1.3
Research Questions
Fundamentally, this thesis tries to answer the following:
How can offline processing of camera, LiDAR, and inertial data provide reliable position
estimates for amphibious drones operating in GNSS-degraded riverine environments?
The work addresses three specific aspects:
• Sensor performance: Which sensors maintain reliability when exposed to water
reflections, vegetation occlusion, and platform motion?
==PAGE:11==

• Algorithm selection: What localization algorithms exploit the complementary
strengths of visual, laser, and inertial data best?
• Accuracy bounds: What accuracy can offline methods achieve compared to
GNSS in degraded signal conditions?
By answering these questions, this thesis aims to enable autonomous bathymetric surveys
in the dense, vegetated waterways where manual methods fail and GNSS cannot provide
adequate positioning.

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter establishes the practical and technical context motivating this research.
Specifically, it explores bathymetry and flood risk management as the driving forces
behind the HydroHex drone’s development, then examines how GNSS signal degrada-
tion in vegetated riverine environments creates the fundamental need for alternative
localization methods.
2.1
Bathymetry and National Flood Risk
Management
Denmark faces increasing flood risk from both coastal and riverine sources.
Under
the EU Floods Directive, Kystdirektoratet serves as the national authority responsi-
ble for flood risk assessment and mapping, encompassing both coastal flooding (hav-
vandsoversvømmelser) and river flooding (vandløbsoversvømmelser) [28].
2.1.1
National Flood Mapping Framework
Denmark’s flood risk strategy centers on high-resolution hazard maps developed through
hydraulic simulations. These models incorporate:
• Statistical discharge data for 5-, 20-, and 100-year return periods (T5, T20, T100)
• Detailed cross-sectional bathymetry of rivers and streams
• Topographic data of surrounding floodplains
• Climate change projections for extreme precipitation events
As Kystdirektoratet states: ”Kortene viser oversvømmelse fra både hav og vandløb...
og bygger på modelberegninger baseret på vandføring og topografi” [The maps show
flooding from both sea and rivers... and are based on model calculations using discharge
and topography] [28]. This explicit requirement for riverine data in national risk models
underscores why accurate bathymetry remains critical.

2.1.2
Current Bathymetric Survey Practice
Flood model accuracy fundamentally depends on bathymetric input quality [34]. Danish
municipalities currently employ manual survey teams through consultancies like COWI,
NIRAS, and WSP. Standard protocols include:
• Surveyors entering waterways with 2-meter poles equipped with RTK GNSS re-
ceivers
• Cross-sections measured at 10-25 meter intervals [34]
• Coverage limited to 1-2 km per day with 2-person teams [10]
• Costs ranging from 6,000-10,000 DKK per hectare for bathymetric and river sur-
veying, with Naturstyrelsen’s detailed hydrological surveying documented at 6,364
DKK per hectare[54]

2.1.3
Critical Gaps in Current Practice
Three fundamental limitations compromise Denmark’s flood modeling capability:
• Coverage Gaps: Approximately 30% of Danish river segments remain unmea-
sured due to access constraints including dense vegetation, private property re-
strictions, and hazardous conditions [15]. These data voids create significant un-
certainty in flood predictions, particularly in smaller tributaries that can contribute
substantially to downstream flooding.
• Update Frequency: Budget constraints limit most municipalities to bathymetric
surveys every 3-5 years [10]. This interval fails to capture morphological changes
from extreme weather events, sediment transport, and human modifications. Cli-
mate change accelerates these processes, making historical data increasingly unre-
liable for future flood prediction.
• Safety Hazards: Danish workplace injury statistics document serious incidents
among surveying professionals, however, occupation-specific data for hydrographic
surveyors is not separately tracked1. Field crews face risks from unstable riverbeds
and unexpected depth changes, particularly during high-flow periods when mea-
surements are most critical for flood modeling.
2.1.4
Impact on National Flood Preparedness
Municipalities particularly vulnerable to river flooding demonstrate the consequences of
inadequate bathymetric data. Silkeborg and Holstebro, historically affected by flooding
from Gudenåen and Storåen respectively, rely on precise riverbed geometry for flood
predictions [Kystdirektoratet2021, 56]. Model uncertainty from outdated bathymetry
translates directly to:
• Inaccurate flood extent predictions affecting zoning and development decisions
• Miscalculated flood depths impacting infrastructure design standards
• Delayed warnings due to poor calibration of real-time flood forecasting systems
• Ineﬀicient allocation of flood defense resources
This situation establishes the critical need for innovative survey methods that can
provide frequent, safe, and comprehensive bathymetric data to support Denmark’s cli-
mate adaptation strategy.

2.2.1
Development and Design Innovation
The HydroHex emerged from collaboration between DTU Space’s GNSS research group
(now the Geopostitioning and Navigation Group) and DTU Environment’s hydrology
team, initiated by Alexander Rietz Vesterhauge, who experienced firsthand the chal-
lenges and dangers of manual bathymetric surveying.
Under Alexander’s leadership,
this collaboration evolved into RiverWorks, a DTU spin-off initiative aimed at commer-
cializing amphibious drone technology for bathymetric data collection.
The amphibious drone represents a novel design with no comparable platforms cur-
rently available in the commercial or research markets. This uniqueness led to a patent
application (currently pending) for the innovative pontoon and propulsion configuration
that enables seamless air-water transitions. While VTOL (Vertical Take-Off and Land-
ing) aircraft transition between hover and forward flight, no existing platform transforms
from a hexacopter to a surface vessel.

CHAPTER 3
Literature Review
This chapter examines existing approaches to navigation in GNSS-degraded environ-
ments, with particular focus on riverine and forested conditions. The review progresses
from general UAV navigation methods to specific challenges of amphibious platforms,
identifying the research gap this thesis addresses.
3.1
UAV Navigation in Structured
Environments
3.1.1
GNSS-Dependent Systems
Traditional UAV navigation relies heavily on GNSS for absolute positioning. Commercial
platforms such as DJI and Pixhawk1 implement Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) fusion
of GNSS and IMU data, achieving high positional accuracy in open environments [17].
However, these systems fail catastrophically when GNSS degrades below four visible
satellites.
Recent work has explored GNSS augmentation rather than replacement. [45] demon-
strated how forest canopies create severe positioning challenges, with horizontal errors
reaching 12-15m under dense coverage compared to sub-meter accuracy in open sky. [3]
characterized signal degradation under various canopy densities, showing positioning er-
rors of 2-20m in forested environments. While these approaches extend GNSS usability,
they cannot address complete signal outages common in dense riverine corridors.
3.1.2
Visual Odometry Evolution
Visual odometry emerged as the primary alternative to GNSS for mobile robot navi-
gation. Early monocular approaches suffered from scale ambiguity and drift accumu-
lation. [44] provided foundational work on feature-based visual odometry, establishing
the framework still used today. The introduction of PTAM [26] (Parallel Tracking and
Mapping) enabled real-time performance by separating tracking and mapping threads.
Modern visual SLAM systems build on these foundations. ORB-SLAM [35] intro-
duced robust loop closure and relocalization, while ORB-SLAM2 [36] added stereo and
1DJI produces commercial drones. Pixhawk is an open source standard for flight controllers

RGB-D support. The latest iteration, ORB-SLAM3 [7], integrates IMU data and sup-
ports multiple map sessions, achieving 0.1% translation error on benchmark datasets.
However, these benchmarks use indoor or urban environments with stable lighting and
abundant features—conditions absent in natural waterways.
3.1.3
Inertial Navigation Integration
Pure inertial navigation accumulates unbounded drift, but short-term IMU predictions
prove invaluable for handling aggressive motions and motion blur. [43] demonstrated
tightly-coupled visual-inertial optimization maintains tracking during rapid maneuvers.
For aquatic platforms, [33] pre-filters IMU data to remove wave-induced periodic ac-
celerations, improving feature tracking stability by 40% in marine environments. This
approach is referred to as Visual Inertial Odometry (VIO).
3.2
USV Navigation Approaches
3.2.1
Marine SLAM Limitations for Rivers
USV navigation research focuses predominantly on open water with radar-based sys-
tems [18]. These approaches assume wide areas with distant landmarks—fundamentally
different from narrow rivers bounded by vegetation. [25] presented the most relevant
work for narrow waterways, demonstrating autonomous navigation in 15m-wide canals
using camera and LiDAR fusion with deep learning semantic segmentation, achieving
root mean square lateral error below 0.45m.
3.2.2
Bathymetric Survey Platforms
Autonomous bathymetric surveying drives much USV research. [13] developed a catama-
ran USV for reservoir mapping, using RTK-GNSS for positioning. While achieving 5cm
bathymetric accuracy, their system required constant GNSS visibility, limiting operation
to open water bodies.
Recent advances in LiDAR for USV navigation have shown promising results. [20]
demonstrated effective use of LiDAR for inland waterway vessels during docking oper-
ations, while [14] evaluated multiple LiDAR SLAM approaches specifically for water
surface vehicles. Their key insight: riverbanks provide consistent geometric features for
scan matching when water returns are filtered. Field trials with LiDAR systems achieved
0.3m position accuracy over 2km trajectories.

3.3
SLAM in Natural Environments
3.3.1
Forest Robotics
Terrestrial robots navigating forests face similar challenges to riverine environments:
GNSS denial, visual feature scarcity, and geometric ambiguity. [53] combined visual
and LiDAR data for forest navigation, using tree trunks as persistent features. Their
approach switches between modalities based on environment: visual in canopy gaps,
LiDAR under dense cover.

Unaddressed Challenges
Despite extensive research in UAV navigation, USV autonomy, and SLAM, critical gaps
remain for amphibious platforms in riverine environments:
Environment Combination: Rivers combine the worst aspects of forest (GNSS

contains infrared that can wash out the projected pattern, while water absorbs these
wavelengths, creating measurement gaps.

surfaces provide strong returns: concrete walls and tree trunks appear clearly in scans.
Rough or angled surfaces scatter the laser energy, reducing return signal strength. Dark
materials absorb more laser energy, while highly reflective surfaces can cause saturation.

4.1.4
GNSS Fundamentals
Global Navigation Satellite Systems determine position by measuring distances to mul-
tiple satellites. The basic principle resembles trilateration; if you know your distance to
several known positions, you can calculate your own location.
Satellites continuously broadcast signals containing their position and the precise
time of transmission. Ground receivers measure when these signals arrive, calculating
distance through [17]:
d = c · (tr −ts)
(4.5)
where c is the speed of light, tr is the reception time, and ts is the transmission time.
To determine a 3D position using satelites requires solving for four unknowns: three
spatial coordinates (X, Y, Z) and the receiver clock offset. Each satellite provides one
pseudorange measurement, creating one equation [17]:
√
(X −Xi)2 + (Y −Yi)2 + (Z −Zi)2 = c · (tr −ts −δt)
(4.6)
where (Xi, Yi, Zi) is the satellite position and δt is the receiver clock error. These dis-
tances are called pseudoranges because they include errors from the receiver’s imperfect
clock.
Standard GNSS receivers measure pseudoranges using the coarse/acquisition
(C/A) code, a unique pattern broadcast by each satellite. The receiver correlates the
incoming signal with a local copy of this code to determine signal travel time. This code-
based measurement achieves 5-10 meter accuracy but remains susceptible to various
error sources.
This equation reveals GNSS’s fundamental vulnerability: timing precision directly
determines position accuracy. Light travels approximately 300,000 kilometers per sec-
ond, meaning even microsecond-level errors translate to hundreds of meters of position
uncertainty. A timing error of just 10 nanoseconds—roughly the time light takes to travel
3 meters—produces equivalent position errors. While satellite atomic clocks achieve re-
markable stability, the entire measurement chain from satellite to receiver must maintain
this precision.
Four satellites resolve position and clock bias; extra signals over-determine the system
and sharpen the solution. Modern multi-constellation receivers routinely track 60 space
vehicles, enabling a least-squares adjustment that tightens coordinates and suppresses
noise [37].

Standard GNSS achieves 5-10 meter accuracy using code-based pseudorange mea-
surements [17]. For applications requiring higher precision, methods such as RTK or
PPK are used:
Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) positioning uses a stationary base station at a known
location. The base continuously calculates the difference between its known position and
GNSS measurements, broadcasting these corrections to mobile receivers (rovers). Since
atmospheric delays and satellite clock errors affect both base and rover similarly, dif-
ferentiating their measurements cancels these common errors. RTK also utilizes carrier
phase measurements, tracking the phase of the carrier wave rather than just the code
modulation. This provides millimeter-level precision in the raw measurements, enabling
centimeter-level positioning when integer ambiguities are resolved [17].
Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK) applies the same mathematical principles but
processes data after collection.
Both base and rover record raw GNSS observations
during the survey. Later, specialized software performs forward and backward filtering
through the dataset, optimally combining all available information. PPK offers several

advantages: no real-time radio link requirement, ability to use multiple base stations,
and more sophisticated ambiguity resolution algorithms. The backward processing pass
particularly improves accuracy during signal outages, as future measurements help con-
strain position estimates [42].
Both techniques require a nearby reference station. In Denmark, the national GNSS
network provides base station data, such as from the BUDD station in Buddinge. The
effectiveness decreases with distance from the base due to spatially decorrelated errors,
limiting baseline lengths to approximately 10-20 km for good accruacy [9].

Having established the sensor capabilities and the basic theory for exteroceptive sensors

type while offering different trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and robustness.

ORB-SLAM3 processes monocular camera images to estimate camera motion through

The system extracts ORB (Oriented FAST and Rotated

ROS2 Framework Implementation
The software stack builds on ROS2 Humble[39] on Ubuntu 22.04 (Jammy) allowing us to
leverage existing sensor drivers. The realsense-ros[23] package publishes color images,

all sensor nodes and initiates recording. A ros2bag3, also referred to as a ros bag, captures
all sensor topics with both system and GNSS timestamps. This is useful in particular
because we can compare time-step by time-step from PPK, total station, and SLAM
trajctory. The system records sensor data at native rates: LiDAR at 10Hz, camera at
30Hz, and IMU at 200Hz. For algorithms using IMU data (visual-inertial ORB-SLAM3
and Cartographer), temporal alignment between sensors is handled during algorithm
processing. ORB-SLAM3 integrates IMU measurements between camera frames, while
Cartographer fuses IMU data between laser scans. RTAB-Map processes only RGB-D
data without IMU fusion.

5.2.2
SLAM Algorithm Integration and Data Pipeline
Each algorithm outputs trajectories in distinct formats requiring standardization for
comparison. ORB-SLAM3 produces TUM format files (timestamp tx ty tz qx qy
qz qw). RTAB-Map’s database viewer exports optimized poses in RGBD-SLAM format,
compatible with TUM structure. Cartographer’s compressed .pbstream files require
extraction using the cartographer _pbstream tool to generate pose lists. A Python
post-processing pipeline converts all outputs to TUM format at 10Hz, handling format-
specific timestamp conventions and coordinate frame transformations.
The critical transformation from local algorithm coordinates to global UTM coordi-
nates utilizes the first valid GNSS fix as the reference origin, enabling overlay visualiza-
tion with total station ground truth measurements. The pipeline interpolates trajectories
to 10Hz after coordinate transformation, ensuring consistent comparison metrics across
algorithms.
Data management uses DVC (Data Version Control) with Google Drive backend.
Raw rosbags push to cloud storage after each mission, protecting against hardware
failures and enabling processing on more powerful computers instead of the Beelink
N100. Processing occurs in Docker containers to ensure identical software environments
across machines.

Experimentation
This chapter presents the experimental methodology used to validate the three SLAM
algorithms in GNSS-degraded environments. The experiments progress from controlled
pool testing at ASTA to realistic field conditions at Lyngby Sø. Each test compares
ORB-SLAM3, RTAB-Map, and Cartographer performance against ground truth mea-
surements. Ground truth comes from GNSS in the pool environment and Total Station
tracking in the field.
6.1
Experimental Approach
The experiments follow a two-stage progression from controlled to realistic conditions.
ASTA pool testing establishes baseline algorithm performance with clear boundaries
and moderate GNSS coverage. Field testing at Lyngby Sø challenges the algorithms
with dense vegetation, dynamic water surfaces, and severe GNSS degradation. This
progression reveals how each algorithm handles increasing environmental complexity.
Simple trajectory patterns test fundamental motion capabilities. At ASTA, the drone
follows basic geometric paths like straight lines with 90-degree turns, allowing clear as-
sessment of drift and rotation handling. Field trajectories adapt to the river environment,
following the waterway while conducting cross sections perpendicular to the flow. These
cross sections mimic actual bathymetric survey patterns where the drone traverses from
bank to bank at regular intervals.
ASTA provides ideal conditions for initial validation. The indoor pool offers con-
sistent artificial lighting, concrete walls with distinct visual features, and a rectangular
boundary that produces clear LiDAR returns. Water remains calm without waves or
current. GNSS coverage stays adequate for PPK processing despite the metal roof struc-
ture. In contrast, Lyngby Sø introduces every real-world challenge: changing natural
light filtered through leaves, repetitive vegetation textures, irregular riverbanks, and tree
canopy that severely degrades GNSS. While PPK solutions were computed for the field
data, the poor satellite geometry under trees produced unreliable results. Thus, a Total
Station is used to obtain the position of the drone in the field. It tracks HydroHex as
there is a reflective prism attached.
Coordinate transformation enables comparison across all methods. Each experiment
begins with a 60-second stationary period where GNSS establishes the global position
and the compass provides heading. This initial pose defines the transformation from
each SLAM algorithm’s local coordinate frame to UTM coordinates.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
7.1
Algorithm Performance Analysis
7.1.1
ASTA
In the literature, the use of LiDAR for navigation in both inland waterways and forest
environments has shown promising results. It was especially found to outperform vision-
only options in settings with variable lighting and repetitive textures. Similar properties
were found in this study when using Cartographer, a 2D LiDAR based algorithm. When
testing in a controlled environment in ASTA the lighting is constant, but the edges of
the pool are straight and thereby repetitive. In this setting, the Cartographer shows
ability to visualize and map the edges of the pool. The Cartographer was also able to
reconstruct the trajectory with minimal drift and successfully captured the 90-degree
turns at each corner. This algorithm was the only one to successfully reconstruct its
trajectory.
The diﬀiculties associated with the straight edges and lack of distinctive features
around the pool in ASTA became evident when testing the ORB-SLAM3. This algorithm
is based on creating a distinctive fingerprint for each feature by sampling gradients
around those key points. However, it was clear that it lacked such elements in the pool
in ASTA. It was able to track in the beginning, but when it gets close to the other end it
loses track and is not able to recover its position. The only distinctive features available
in the pool for the ORB-SLAM3 to recognize is everything but the pool walls. Therefore,
as the HydroHex approaches the wall, it is left without any distinctive feature to track
and fails.
Theoretically, the RTAB-Map should function well under the controlled lighting con-
ditions in an indoor environment since there should be no interfering with the infrared
pattern. In the beginning it is able to reconstruct its trajectory, but as ORB-SLAM3
it fails when it gets closer to the other end. However, the RTAB-Map attempts to con-
tinue, but is failing to provide the correct positions. When getting closer to the end,
where it begun, it starts to find the correct position again. The positioning issues is
likely caused by the depth sensor encountering issues from the water surface at certain
angles. It succeeds to produce a metrically accurate trajectory that closely matches the
pool boundaries, which make this algorithm more successful than ORB-SLAM3 but less
successful that Cartographer in a controlled environment.
==PAGE:64==

7 Discussion
7.1.2
Field
In the field a lot more challenges are expected than in a controlled environment. When
operating in a riverine environment it is not possible to control the lighting, trees can
be causing shadows or be interfering with the signals from the drone, and you have to
navigate around them. Furthermore, the wind can cause the trees to move and change
the image when using visual recognition.
By theory the presence of trees and, in general, more distinctive features should be
of benefit to ORB-SLAM3. This was, however, not the case. The algorithm completely
failed when tested in Lyngby Sø and was not able to provide any useful information at
all. Several factors can possibly explain this: When the trees move in the wind it will
not be able to use those distinctive features to create a constant point. Using these as
basis for measurement of depth will give inaccurate results. Furthermore, sunlight may
make it possible for ORB-SLAM3 to detect features.
The RTAB-Map did also fail in the field test as it was not able to perform any kind of
localization. An important factor contributing to this is sunlight. The method is based
on projecting infrared light to the scene to measure depth. In direct sunlight it will not
be able to detect these projections. This indicates that this is not an applicable method
for this purpose.
In the literature, LiDAR has shown great results in use for navigation of forest
robotics. Since the Cartographer is based on 2D LiDAR it was expected to also function
well in riverine environments surrounded by trees. At the test done on Lyngby Sø the
Cartographer once again gave the best results. It was able to reconstruct its trajectory
closely following the reference trajectory.
7.1.3
Optimal Sensor Selection by Environment
While the project is unable to quantify the difference in performance between the sensors,
it is obvious that the LiDAR performed remarkably better in both environments. The
camera based methods suffer significantly in both instances. This is also the case of the
papers that compared the two types of sensors.
While quantitative performance metrics could not be established due to ground truth
limitations, qualitative observations clearly demonstrate LiDAR’s superior performance
across both test environments. Camera-based methods, monocular ORB-SLAM3 and
RGB-D RTAB-Map, suffered catastrophic failures in both environments. In ASTA, due
to the lack of features when getting close to the conrete wall both algorithm lost track of
relevant features and lost information about positioning. In the field environment, these
problems compounded with direct sunlight, more water and moving vegetation. Only
Cartographer’s LiDAR-based approach maintained continuous localization throughout
both experiments.
This performance hierarchy aligns with findings in the literature, where studies com-
paring visual and LiDAR SLAM in aquatic environments consistently report similar
outcomes. The fundamental issue lies in water’s interaction with different sensing modal-
ities: cameras rely on appearance features that become unstable over reflective surfaces,

while infrared-based depth sensing fails due to absorption and sunlight interference.
LiDAR, despite receiving no returns from water surfaces, succeeds by exploiting the

7.2
Limitations and Challenges
7.2.1
Cartographer Trajectory Extraction
Extracting trajectory coordinates from Cartographer proves much harder than other
SLAM systems. While ORB-SLAM3 and RTAB-Map save trajectories as simple text
files with position data, Cartographer uses a complex compressed format (.pbstream
files) that requires special tools to read. The system divides maps into smaller sections
called submaps, each with its own coordinate system. To get the actual drone trajectory,
these local coordinates must be converted to global positions, a process that requires un-
derstanding Cartographer’s internal structure. Additionally, when Cartographer detects
it has revisited a location (loop closure), it adjusts all previous positions to improve ac-
curacy. This means the final trajectory differs from what was recorded in real-time.
These design choices make Cartographer excellent for creating accurate maps but diﬀi-
cult for extracting precise position data. Unfortunately, there was not enough time to
implement a method for converting from pbstream to a global coordinate system.
7.2.2
Hardware Issues
One major issue during the thesis was the GNSS module. It resulted in a wasted field
experiment as it stopped working on the way to one of the earlier experiments. The
replacement still suffered poor results. The exact reasons for this quality is uncertain
however, it has been speculated that the antenna that is mounted on the side of the
drone simply is inadequate. It is low to the ground, does not have great line of sight,
and it is simply not a very strong receiver antenna.

7.3
Future Directions
This project demonstrates that reliable USV navigation in GNSS-degraded environments
is achievable with appropriate sensor selection. The clear success of 2D LiDAR opens
several paths for future research.
Given Cartographer’s strong performance with basic 2D LiDAR, investigating more so-
phisticated LiDAR sensors is a natural next step. Higher-resolution 2D LiDARs could
provide denser point clouds for improved feature extraction in sparse environments.
Additionally, 3D LiDAR systems would capture vertical structures like overhanging
branches and bridge undersides, potentially improving localization in complex riverine
environments. As seen in the literature review, most serious robotic navigation plat-
forms have converged on LiDAR-based solutions for good reason.
While this project evaluated sensors independently, operational systems would benefit
from intelligent fusion of GNSS with exteroceptive sensors. When GNSS quality is high,
it provides absolute positioning to correct LiDAR drift. As signal quality degrades un-
der canopy, the system could smoothly transition to LiDAR-based localization. This
approach would combine the global accuracy of GNSS with the local precision of Li-
DAR, creating a more robust navigation solution than either sensor alone.
The project’s conclusions are limited by the small number of experiments. Future work
requires extensive field testing across different rivers, weather conditions, and seasons.
Only through repeated experiments can the true reliability and failure modes of each
approach be quantified. A larger dataset would also enable statistical analysis of perfor-
mance metrics rather than the qualitative comparisons presented here.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In conclusion the Cartographer showed the best results both when testing in a controlled
environment (ASTA) and in the field. It was able to reconstruct its trajectury with high
accuracy in both environments.
It was not affected by lighting variations or water
reflections and therefore it shows great perspective for the use of LiDAR based models
in such riverine environments where GNSS is degraded. However, both ORB-SLAM3
and RTAB-Map failed in the field test and gave no useful information at all.
The
effect of sunlight on RTAB-Map suggests that this method is not the best choice for
navigation in such environments. ORB-SLAM3 already did not work in the controlled
environment, but it is suggested that different factors were causing the failure in the
different environments. RTAB-Map was not successful when tested in ASTA either but
it still managed to reconstruct the beginning and ending of the trajectory.
Overall LiDAR shows great potential for autonomous navigation in GNSS-degraded
riverine environments. However, ORB-SLAM3 and RTAB-MAP do not seem to be useful
in such environments.
