ABSTRACT 
 
Aquaculture is one of the fastest growing food industry, which shows an increased interest in the fulfilment 
of sustainability criteria set by UN. European aquaculture sector is strongly represented by salmonids 
farming both in term of production and value. One of the reasons behind the faster development of this 
sector is the adoption of innovative solutions, which increase efficiency and quality along the production 
chain. Concepts like precision farming and aquaculture 2.0, are integrative farming systems which can 
interface new technologic and informatic tools with farming practices. These concepts, already adopted in 
animal husbandry, are drawing more and more interest both in aquaculture industry and academia. The 
main area of focus for these innovative applications is the production planning which combines several 
parameters at the same time to have a detailed description of the production status and progress. 
Improving accuracy and efficiency of the production results in reduced losses and costs. Veterinary aspects 
are very important part of it, which are often overlooked in production settings. Since these aspects have 
major influences on management decisions, in relation to health condition of biomass, disease outbreaks, 
mitigation and preventative measures for disease controls. Notably Veterinary aspects have major 
economical impact, which affect the production and therefore it has essential importance to assess the cost 
of mortality to evaluate the efficiency of health management. Feeding programs and related aspects clearly 
represent a very important role in the management of the production and its economy also in relation to 
health management. In conclusion, taking into consideration the interaction between these described 
factors will contribute to a detailed analysis of the operating cost and condition of fish farming. 
Furthermore, the integration of the study in an informatics model will visually and practically describe the 
economical effect of the parameters interaction and, therefore, support decisions for the management of 
veterinary aspects and feeding programs along the production.

INTRODUCTION 
1. Freshwater trout farming: 
Denmark has been a pioneer among the trout farming (Onchorinchus mykiss) sector and it keeps 
developing to find new solutions in terms of innovation and productivity. Thanks to fast growing trend of 
the aquaculture industry, scientific and technologic progresses led farmers (and investors) to gradually 
abandon traditional farming in order to embrace new production systems based on water recirculation 
technology. Recirculated Aquaculture Systems (RAS) are gaining interest and importance for its high 
production potential and Danish aquaculture is leading this movement adopting these systems in different 
applications called ‘model type’ farms (EUMOFA, 2020). 
Based on water recirculation and technology level, it is possible to define these innovative farming systems 
as ‘Model type 1’, semi-extensive trout farms adapted to a recirculation level of minimum 70%, and ‘Model 
type 3’, intensive trout farms with minimum 95% water recirculation, higher density and more complete 
water treatment equipment (mechanical filter and bio-filters). Water sources can be either superficial 
water from a river or underground water from a well, but in any scenario the water intake can be up to 15-
20 times lower compared to a traditional flow-through trout farm (EUMOFA, 2020). 
Concerning veterinary aspects, these facilities includes high risk of biological contamination from inlet 
water especially if from rivers or lakes, from input of fries and juveniles and from external and 
environmental factors, since no barriers or water disinfection are included in the systems. The ideal water 
supply is well water, which it is theoretically free from biological hazard. However, it can present over-
saturation of gasses based because of the depth of the underground water source (high pressure). Massive 
presence of gasses like N and CO2 are harmful for fish health and for this reason the water flow undergo 
“stripping” or degassing process to guarantee ideal water quality. 
The case of study is based on data of three production cycles in a Danish model type 3 farm presenting an 
average production of 603,3tons Rainbow trout per cycle. 
 
1.1. Saltwater trout farming: 
The low salinity of Baltic Sea surrounding Danish coastline represented an interesting and unique 
opportunity for trout farming (Onchorinchus mykiss) to expand its horizon to saltwater sites. Sea cage 
farming occurs in the final stage of production for rainbow trout. Typically, adult fish of approximatively 1kg 
size are moved in sea cages during spring (Mar-Apr) to be afterwards harvested during autumn (Oct-Dec) at 
a final size range between 2 and 5 kg. Furthermore, this farming system adds roe (eggs) collection from 
slaughtered fish, which also represents a source of income for sea cage farmers (FAO, 2022). 
This farming system is highly affected by external factors, as the possibilities to control environmental 
parameters including environmental such as water quality, temperature, wild fauna potentially harbouring  
pathogens and parasites are very limited. Concerning veterinary aspects, treatments are often costly and 
non-efficient, while vaccines may result not effective when temperatures reach peaks during hot summers 
and cold winters (EUMOFA, 2020).

2. Fish mortality 
Fish health and welfare are regulated by a complex interaction between internal and external factors. 
Clinical disease is the outcome of interaction of three main elements, the pathogen, the host and the 
environment each one with several specific factors (Moreira et al., 2021). Each environmental factor has a 
direct effect on host resilience/resistance and pathogen virulence, furthermore each element is 
characterized by its intrinsic features. The dynamic balance of the three elements can push the outcome to 
disease or health.  
 
FIG 1. INTERACTIONS OF FACTORS DEFINING THE OCCURRENCE OF A DISEASE OUTBREAK. 
Within aquaculture, as in other animal husbandry practices, health aspects are of main concern since 
farmed stocks are often exposed to stressing conditions caused high density, intensive feeding and other 
technical operations (grading, movement, vaccination etc.). Unbalances in physiological health status of fish 
and high stress factors represent a critical condition for micro-organisms threat and disease outbreaks. 
Pathogenic agents are often present in the farming environment, mainly introduced through water or 
biomass.  
Rainbow trout is susceptible to several pathogens that can lead to high mortality and critical losses within 
the production. Diseases are often difficult to eradicate, prevent and treat, representing a critical point in 
aquaculture practice, in all its different features. 
Fish pathogens include bacteria, viruses, parasites and fungi. Focusing on Danish Rainbow Trout farming, 
the main infectous diseases are below listed and described.

2.1. Bacterial diseases: 
Enteric Red Mouth Diseases 
The Enteric Red Mouth (ERM) disease is caused by Yersinia ruckerii, a gram negative cocco-bacillum of 
Enterobacteriacee family (Kumar et al., 2015). It affects several fish species including salmonids, especially 
Rainbow trout, causing significant economical losses (Kumar et al., 2015). The disease can develop in fish of 
different ages and sizes, but it usually cause higher (acute) mortality in juvenile stages and chronic form in 
adults (Kumar et al., 2015). Environmental factors are also affecting the infections. In fact, the pathogen 
typically cause critical damages during spring-summer when the water temperature reach 15-18°C. 
Mortality varies from 10% to 60% but can peak 80% or higher values in 5cm fries at high temperatures.  
The name of this disease is given by the typical haemorragic lesions observed in the oral area that may 
degenerate in erosions and necrosis. Moreover, other external symptoms are the typical of bacterial 
disease such as haemorragic lesions at the fin base, darker skin colour, exophthalmia, lethargy and slowed 
growth. Internally, it is possible to observe swollen spleen together with petechial haemorrhage on liver, 
pancreas, pyloric caeca, swim bladder and in epiaxial muscle. Furthermore, it is often observed inflamed 
tissues and purulent exudates at enteric level where this bacteria operate its pathogenic effect (Tobback et 
al., 2007).  
The important economical loss caused by this diseases, led the industry to develop ERM vaccines as one of 
the first among fish diseases available vaccines. Several commercial vaccines are available against serotype 
O1 of Y.ruckerii, including monovalent vaccine protecting against biotype 1 and bivalent against biotype 1 
and 2 (Kumar et al., 2015). Vaccination can be performed by immersion for fries and juveniles (<10-15 
grams fish) 4-5g (Marana et al., 2019), while it can be given by intraperitoneal injection in adult fish 
showing longer protection (Chettri et al., 2013). 
 
Foruncolosis 
The responsible for Foruncolosis disease is Aeromonas salmonicida sub-specie salmonicida, a gram negative 
cocco-bacillum, widely spread in water environment especially in fresh/brackish water (Dallaire-Dufrense et 
al., 2014) where it can survive several days depending on the water salinity as observed by McCarthy 
(1977): 8 days in saltwater, 17 days in freshwater and 24 days in brackish water. Furthermore, A. 
salmonicida is observed to survive for 6 days on wet surfaces such as nets and up to 32 days in tissues of 
fish died of Foruncolosis (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). All salmonids are target species for this bacterium, 
which is globally affecting the salmonid aquaculture (Menanteau-Ledouble et al., 2016). Mortality starts at 
+7°C and increases with higher temperature, during summer, and it varies between 40% as chronic form in 
10 months adults and 85% as acute in juveniles and adults up to 5 months (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). 
The disease develops as a severe bacterial septicaemia in acute forms showing a-specific symptoms in 
fingerlings and juveniles, while it show chronic form in adults and larger fish, causing typical ‘furunculous-
like’ degenerative skin lesions at the base of the fins and mouth (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). These lesions 
starts as haemorragic and can grow with deep necrosis in the epithelial and muscular tissue (Cipriano and 
Bullock, 2001). Darker skin colour is commonly observed both in acute and chronic forms of the 
==PAGE:7==

septicaemia (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). Internally it is possible to observe swollen spleen and spread 
haemorrhage in the viscera (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). 
Commercial vaccine are available for this disease and they are usually containing adjuvant to prolong the 
protection after the administration, performed by injection in adult fish (Cipriano and Bullock, 2001). 
However, low immunogenicity of A.salmonicida and high temperatures reached during summer represent a 
limitation for the efficiency of the actual vaccination against Foruncolosis. Several virulence factors are 
defending the pathogen from the fish macrophages, showing less efficiency in Foruncolosis treatment 
(Dallaire-Dufresne et al., 2014) 
 
Rainbow Trout Fry Syndrome 
Flavobacterium psychrophilum is a gram negative bacillum responsible of two infections affecting rainbow 
trout: the saddleback disease (or cold water disease CWD) (Starliper, 2011) and the rainbow trout fry 
syndrome (RTFS) (Hoare et al., 2017). The second one is a visceral form that affect fries at start feeding 
stage but also larvae still with yolk sac are susceptible. As the name of the bacterium suggests, this disease 
can develop at low temperatures, giving mortalities between 10-30% and peaking 70% between 3-15°C 
(Nematollahi et al., 2003). A-specific symptoms such as lethargy, inappetence and disturbed swim are 
occurring in post-larvae and fries together with exophthalmia and spleen edema that confer it swollen and 
irregular aspect (Nematollahi et al., 2003). 
This bacterium is widely spread in the environment and therefore it is difficult to prevent and control and, 
on the other hand, treatment is also difficult since the infection may occur in fish before their start feeding 
and vaccination is difficult to perform at this stages (Hoare et al., 2017). Selective breeding is providing 
solutions against RTFS with eggs and larvae resistant to Flavobacteriae and immersion vaccines are object 
of studies (Hoare et al., 2017) even though they did not showed full protection yet (Whali et al., 2018). 
 
Bacterial Kidney Disease 
Bacterial kidney disease (BKD) is an infection caused by Renibacterium salmoninarum, a gram positive 
cocco-bacillum widely spread in salmonids farms (Jansson, 2002). All life stages and sizes are susceptible, 
but losses are typically happening in fish of  6-12 months age since this infection naturally develop in a 
chronic form (Wiens, 2011). The disease can spread horizontally from infect fish and also vertical 
transmission has been showed with the presence of the pathogen in eggs and yolk sac (Jansson, 2002).  
The kidney and its functions are compromised by the infection, where in fact is possible to observe necrosis 
and nodular lesions (Wiens, 2011). The infections can be observed at 12°C in autumn (Murray et al., 2012) 
but also at higher temperature toward end of spring (Delghandi et al., 2020). Due to chronic nature of the 
disease it has been difficult to study successful vaccination for BKD (Wiens, 2011).

Vibriosis 
Vibriosis is a disease caused by gram negative bacterium Vibrio anguillarum (Frans et al., 2011). It affects 
aquaculture production mainly in marine environments but also in fresh/brackish water sites, causing 
haemorragic septicaemia in farmed fish, crustaceans and molluscs: significant economical losses has been 
attributed to this diseases, worldwide spread in the aquatic environment (Frans et al., 2011).   
Concerning trout farming, Vibriosis represents a threat in sea cage culture in brackish water especially in 
warm season during summer, when peak of mortalities are reported (Thorburn et al., 1988). Prevention 
and control of Vibriosis are highly important in order to minimize the impact of the outbreaks (Hickey and 
Lee, 2018). Commercial vaccines are available in Europe for rainbow trout, protecting against Vibriosis by V. 
anguillarum (Shefat, 2018). 
 
2.2. Viral diseases: 
PRV-3 Infection 
Piscine orthoreovirus (PRV) is causing infection in farmed salmonids. Three subtypes have been studied and, 
in particular, PRV-3 subtype is giving an emerging disease in rainbow trout (Vendramin et al., 2019). It was 
observed for the first time in Oncoryhnchus mykiss juveniles in Norway in 2013 and furtherly reported in 
several EU countries including Denmark in 2017 (Sorensen et al., 2020) where it is an emerging pathogen in 
rainbow trout aquaculture (Cuenca et al., 2020) . The virus is shown to replicate in blood and to cause heart 
inflammation (Pancarditis) that lead to significant mortalities especially in juvenile stages of rainbow trout 
(Vendramin et al., 2019). The viral infection is often detected in association with other pathogens, and the 
interplay of different agents in co-infection is considered to be the main cause of mortality in these cases. 
 
Infective Pancreatic Necrosis 
The Infectious pancreatic necrosis (IPN) it is caused by a double strand RNA non-enveloped virus, belonging 
to the genus Aquabirnavirus (Roberts and Pearson, 2005). This viral infection is widely spread in the 
environment worldwide and it represents an important issue for rainbow trout hatcheries and farms 
causing relevant economical losses (Gomez-Casado et al., 2011). The transmission of the virus can happen 
horizontally from infected fish and vertically from broodstock to offspring and for this reason selective 
breeding plays an important role for the prevention against this infection (Roberts and Pearson, 2005).  
IPN typically affects rainbow trout fries within 3-4 weeks from start feeding and the disease disappear 
when fries grows in size (Roberts and Pearson, 2005). At optimal temperature (around 10°C) and high 
density, mortality can reach very high values (up to 90% in per acute form)(Roberts and Pearson, 2005) 
while it decrease below 10°C and over 16°C (Rimstad et al., 2014). Infected fries typically presents darkened 
skin, swollen abdomen full of liquid, ascites, swollen spleen, anaemic gills and white filament faeces with 
catarrhal exudate from the intestines (Geoghegan et al., 2007). The target organ is the pancreas, which may 
be completely degenerated and fibrous replacement of pancreatic tissue in survivors, causing lacking of 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
protein conversion (no growth) and mortality (Geoghegan et al., 2007). Detection methods for IPN are 
reported in OIE aquatic manual (OIE, 2003). 
Control method of IPN in Atlantic salmon is based on a combination of using eggs selected for resistance 
and vaccination. IPN infection still affects trout aquaculture causing important economical losses, for 
Rainbow Trout vaccines are not available and the administration of vaccines to early life stage is not 
currently available for this pathogen (Gomez-casado et al., 2011) 
 
Infective Hematopoietic Necrosis  
The first outbreak of this disease has been reported in 2021 in Denmark. Infective Hematopoietic Necrosis 
(IHN) is caused by Salmonid Novirhabdovirus, a single strand RNA enveloped virus widely spread rainbow 
trout farms (Dixon et al., 2016). This disease appears between 8-15°C and heavily affects fries and juveniles 
with mortality that peaks 90-95% (OIE, 2019a). Adult fish are often more resistant to the infection, large 
size fish may become asymptomatic carrier and act as reservoir of infection. On the other hand, they can 
result asymptomatic when they catch this disease and they can spread the virus horizontally (OIE, 2019a).  
The virus typically enter the organism through the base of fin and spreads in the hematic circle to reach the 
target organs: kidney and spleen hematopoietic tissue (OIE, 2019a). In hyper-acute form fries can die 
without visible symptoms, otherwise haemorragic skin lesions are present at the fin base together with 
swollen abdomen, exophthalmos, anaemia and lethargy (OIE, 2019a). In chronic form, the disease may 
present nervous symptoms such as looping. Fish who survive the disease may presents skeletal deformities.  
DNA vaccines given by intra-muscular injection are showed to be effective against IHN, but not yet available 
in Europe because of concern of considering DNA vaccinated fish as genetically modified organisms 
(Gomez-Casado et al., 2011). This disease is notifiable to OIE (OIE, 2019a). 
  
2.3. Parasites and Fungi: 
Relevant causes of infections are also these two biological categories that are commonly spread in aquatic 
environment and aquaculture. As reported by Buchmann et al., 2020 the main five parasitic infection in 
European salmonid aquaculture are caused by Lepeophtheirus salmonis known as salmon louse, 
Neoparamoeba perurans causing amoebic gill disease (AGD), Ichthyophthirius multifiliis causing fish 
whitespot disease, Saprolegnia parasitica a fungus-like oomycete, and Tetracapsuloides bryosalmonae, 
causing proliferative kidney disease (PKD).  
Concerning Fungi the main pathogen is Saprolegnia parasitica (Ozcelik et al., 2020), which is not a proper 
cause of mortality but it proliferates on dead fish and spreads easily to other fish causing damages in the 
production especially in fish which present wounds or other ongoing infections (Van den berg et al., 2013). 
Moreover, it is matter of concern in hatcheries because it affects eggs and can spread widely among eggs in 
the same tray and with water. Constant monitoring and removing of eggs affected by this fungus are 
operated together with antifungine treatment with several chemicals such as iodophores and Bronopol 
(Ozcelik et al, 2020).  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Parasitic infections represent a major limitation in global marine aquaculture counting relevant economical 
losses (Shinn et al., 2015). Regarding Rainbow trout farming, usually they are introduced in freshwater 
farms through inlet water and their persistence is often correlated to scarce tank cleaning. Parasites can be 
transported by infected and healthy fish (reservoir), ichtiophagous birds, water, wet surfaces and not 
properly disinfected clothes and tools. There are several species involved in Danish rainbow trout farms 
such as Chilodonella piscicola, Ichtiobodo necator, Trichodina nigra, and Ichthyophthirius multifiliis, which is 
associated with mortality (Buchmann and Bresciani, 1997). 
 
3. Bottlenecks in mortality management 
Aquaculture production is the result of the interaction between several parameters working together along 
the whole chain, from hatcheries to final harvesting. As mentioned in previous chapter, fish are often 
exposed to stressing factors during the production and for this reason the incidence of disease outbreaks 
increases causing important mortalities within the total biomass.  
Losses can be estimated in terms of biomass and number of fish lost, but it is also important to consider the 
economical impact of the mortality in the production. In fact, the economical loss is including several 
parameters such as the cost of the feed given to the biomass before the mortality. Infected fish generally 
refuses to feed and the uneaten feed sinking at the bottom of tanks, represents a cost. Furthermore, it 
represents a threat to water quality and a proliferation site for pathogen microorganism mentioned above, 
parasites and fungus. As well dead fish lying in grates or bottoms represent a reservoir and growing media 
for pathogens that can spread furtherly in the population. Therefore, dead collection represent a critical 
point in aquaculture practice, but it is not always possible or properly operated especially in big size farms 
with deep tank or sea cages. As a consequence, the lack of precise control on biomass and number of fish 
generate non-accurate feeding programs, lack of size homogeneity and extra costs. 
Treatment and vaccination against disease outbreaks also represents a stress factor for fish and may 
include mortality (even low). The balance between costs and results may be unstable or eventually not 
profitable for farmers. Based on these hypothesis, this study aims to analyse the economical impact of 
mortality in Rainbow Trout farming by expressing the interaction of the discussed parameters and the 
effects on the final profit of the production. In the following paragraphs, mortality management and 
mitigation tools are described. 
 
3.1 Egg disinfection 
Fish eggs represent the first matter of concern regarding health and diseases management in an 
aquaculture system. Fish eggs can be obtained from broodstock reproduction internal to the facility or 
usually from specialised egg suppliers, which are delivering fertilized eggs produced following specific 
protocols and breeding programs.  
For this reason, fertilised eggs can be considered the first biomass to access the rearing system and they 
represent the first hazard of introducing undesired and threatening pathogens in the production cycle. In 
order to ensure biosecurity and decrease risk of contaminations between facilities, hatcheries and further 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
compartments, eggs disinfection is an essential procedure to be applied at the beginning of the production 
cycle (OIE, 2021). 
Eggs disinfection can be operated with several products but most commonly iodine-based agents such as 
Buffodine. Iodophores, in fact, are shown to preserve integrity of eggs surface thanks to their neutral pH 
and low toxicity (manual). It is important to proceed to disinfection using eyed, hardened eggs, to ensure 
the efficiency of the procedure (OIE, 2021). 
Egg disinfection procedures remove or inactivate potential pathogens that can be transported on the 
surface of the eggs and spread horizontally to other incubators/tanks through water or tools. Vertical 
transmission of pathogens from broodstock to offspring cannot be fully controlled by eggs disinfection (OIE, 
2021). 
 
3.2 Vaccination 
Following the principle “prevention is better than the cure”, preventive methods for diseases control in 
aquaculture have gained an essential role in health management, leading the sector to benefit from the 
development of several commercial vaccines against infectious diseases (Gudding et al., 2014). Part of the 
success of salmon farming is the development of vaccination application and technology (Sommerset et al., 
2005) 
Vaccines consist in the immunization of the fish through the administration of non-active pathogen cells to 
stimulate the immune system of the fish, which will be resistant to future infections of specific pathogen. 
The administrated pathogen is non active for safety reasons, in fact attenuated and live vaccines, contrary 
to inactivated vaccines, may not cause diseases in the targeted fish but be released in the environment and 
infect other fish (Gudding et al., 2014).  
Vaccination can be performed in different methods based on vaccine type, desired immunity stimulation 
and fish size. Post-larval stages and fries are vaccinated by immersion in the vaccine solution, while 
juveniles and adult fish can be vaccinated by injection, which is more invasive methods and may create 
undesired damages in fishes smaller than 10grams (Press and Lillehaug, 1995). Vaccines are injected in the 
peritoneal cavity and are usually added with an adjuvant in order to prolong the duration of the 
immunization (Press and Lillehaug, 1995). DNA vaccines are administrated as intra-muscular injection and 
represents an interesting subject of studies (Gudding et al., 2014). 
Concerning salmonid aquaculture, vaccination has long time application and sees nowadays several 
vaccines available on the market in different forms and administration methods (Adams et al., 2019). 
Commercial vaccines are available in Denmark against infectious diseases of Rainbow Trout caused by 
Yersinia ruckerii, Aeromonas salmonicida and Vibrio anguillarum (Shefat, 2018).  
 
3.3 Antibiotic treatment 
Antibiotic treatments are a standard practice to control mortality due to bacterial disease in aquaculture. 
The trend see their utilization to decrease to avoid antibiotic resistant and thanks to development of new 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
vaccines to prevent diseases in aquaculture (Romero et al., 2012). Medication is typically given to the fish 
within the feed, which can be enriched with specific agents to treat specific diseases, but also be used in 
bath treatment or dilution in the tank water (Chen et al., 2020). It is important to evaluate conditions and 
target before to start an antibiotic treatment, prescript by a veterinarian.  
Concerning salmonid aquaculture, the most commonly allowed antibiotic are Oxytetracycline and 
Chlortetracycline, Florfenicol, Flumequin, Oxolinic acid and potentiated Sulfonamides and they are used to 
treat frequent disease such as Foruncolosis, Yersiniosis, Vibriosis and Flavobacteriosis (Smith et al., 2008).  
 
3.4 Antiparasitic treatment 
The impact of parasitic disease will grow together with aquaculture intensification and expansion and 
antiparasitic drugs are the main treatment to control parasitic infections (Shinn et al., 2012).  It is very 
important to identify the parasite to treat because of the diversity of agents that can be used (Shinn et al., 
2012). Similarly to antibiotic treatments, the risk of resistance is growing and the trend suggest to decrease 
the utilization of antiparasitic drugs in aquaculture (Shinn et al ., 2012).  
Among the most common drugs used to treat parasitic disease in salmonid culture there are acetic acid, 
Bronopol, chlorine, hydrogen peroxide and peracetic acid (Shinn et al., 2012). 
 
3.5 Eradication  
Eradication programs aim at removing the specific disease causing agent from a certain area as described in 
the European regulation EU 2020/689. The steps to follow in order to eradicate the disease and obtain the 
freedom status, require a surveillance system that can demonstrate absence of pathogen; in case of 
detection of the pathogen target of the eradication, infected stocks shall be culled, disinfection of farm 
environment, and equipment, fallowing and defined inactivity time before restocking with certified specific 
pathogen free material. All the facilities inside the epidemiologic unit interested in the eradication 
programs need to stop their production in order to ensure the eradication of the pathogen. The Aquatic 
Animal Health Code describe measures of fallowing in aquaculture (OIE, 2019b) 
Denmark has concluded eradication program against VHS in 2010 as described in “Technical and financial 
report on the Danish programme for the eradication of Viral Haemorrhagic Septicaemia in accordance with 
Commission Decision 2008/897/EC, Article 19 (c) for 2010.” By Animal Health Division of Ministry of Food, 
Agriculture and Fisheries of Denmark.  
 
3.6 Functional feeds 
The utilization of functional feeds is growing interest in aquaculture as a tool to improve growth and 
decrease diseases impact on the fish production (Dawood et al., 2017). The mitigating effect against 
infections outbreaks, in fact, is an important aspect as an alternative to antibiotic use, which is being critical 
topic in the sector (Dawood et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
Functional feeds consist in diet enriched with specific components chosen among a wide variety of 
additives, based on the specific fish demand and purpose. In particular, prebiotics and probiotics are added 
to improve health and response to stress and diseases (Encarnacao, 2016). Related to salmonids 
aquaculture, positive results has been showed in growth performances and reduced mortality from 
Aeromonas spp. Infection when including additives in the diet, such as prebiotics, probiotics, synbiotics and 
essential oils (Hayagtheib et al., 2020). The utilization of functional feed as a complementary tool together 
with vaccination and treatment is likely to show improved protection against viral diseases in aquaculture 
(Kibenge et al., 2012). 
 
3.7 Selective breeding vs disease 
Selective breeding plays an important role in aquaculture fast growth especially in salmonids, which are the 
most studied species within aquaculture genetics (Odegard et al., 2011). Several traits can be selected in 
aquaculture breeding programs and disease resistance is always among the priorities as shown by 
Chavanne et al., 2016. Innovation with new genetic tools such as genome editing (Houston, 2017) and 
machine learning, are being tested to predict disease resistance in aquaculture (Palaiokostas, 2021). 
The aim is to produce offspring immune to certain pathogens, which would reduce significantly costs and 
losses from diseases outbreaks along the production chain. The main results in selective breeding for 
disease resistance in salmonids had been achieved with the production of fish immune or high resistant to 
frequent diseases such as, CWD, IHN, IPN, BKD and ERM (Troutlodge, Hendrix genetics), IPN and BKD 
(AquaSearch ova Aps). Disease resistance is leading to a reduced utilization of antibiotic treatments and 
more efficient health management within the farming system. This results show higher productivity and 
profitability in fish farming (Gjedrem and Baranski, 2010).

METHODS 
4. Assessment of mortality: economic impact 
Infectious disease have a negative impact on production and economic revenue of fish farming. This 
economic impact of disease is characterized by direct and indirect effect. The main direct effect observed at 
sites is increased mortality, which require additional expenses to try and mitigate the adverse effect on 
production, such as visit of fish health inspector, treatment, dead removal and disposal. 
Additionally diseases cause indirect effects, which hamper growth performance, such as increased FCR 
(feed conversion rate), feed spill, increased susceptibility to other diseases. 
To be considered also, as indirect effect, the necessary changes in production plans such as delay in 
delivery of final product to the market, target market size and quantity of fish at harvesting, competition 
for niche market and increased production costs. Finally, also the withdrawal time indicated for antibiotics, 
which is required to avoid transfer of residues in the food chain of Antimicrobials, which also contribute to 
changing market plans of a site as further described in the discussion chapter. The delay of delivering fish 
causes extra costs for prolonged permanence of biomass in the system: usage of water, electricity, oxygen, 
manpower and feed increase and nonetheless the spaces (ponds, tanks, cages) are cannot be utilized to 
host new biomasses, causing delays of live stocking set-out and economic losses.  
It is very important to analyse both direct and indirect cost parameters to achieve an economic assessment 
of the mortality cost in a fish farming system. 
The collaboration with AquaPri made possible to set the experiment and analyse data from two farms, 
which will be furtherly reported as Saltwater site (SW) and Freshwater site (FW).

DISCUSSION 
The study reports one of the first costs assessment of disease outbreak impact based on a real-data. Up to 
date, a tool, capable to estimate correctly the impact of a single disease on a farm site, is not available.  
Determining the impact of a single disease on farm is virtually impossible, because of lack of proper 
diagnostics and monitoring systems to record cause of mortality, presence of co-infection (thereby making 
impossible to define a single agent as responsible of disease), and also some practical aspects including the 
non-accurate dead removal and quantification. The main parameter observed during production at farm 
level and reported is mortality, hence this study aimed at assessing the economic cost of mortality during 3 
production cycles on 2 different sites one in seawater and one in freshwater.  
Previous studies reported estimative of economic losses caused by diseases outbreaks in several areas. As 
per data and studies reported hereunder, economic impact of mortality has been calculated in different 
ways. It is possible to observe some broad range of impact depending on species considered, farming 
system, disease considered, etc. for example, losses by parasitic infections (argulosis) in carp pond culture 
in India estimated 0,146 USD per kg of fish (Sahoo et al., 2013) and in Bangladesh 444 USD/hectare per year 
(Monir et al., 2015). Losses by sea lice in marine salmonid farms globally estimated more than 100million 
USD per annum (Johnson et al., 2004), 4% value of the production in Canada (Mustafa et al., 2001), 6% 
value of the production (0,1-0,2 €/kg of fish farmed yearly) (Costello, 2009) and, 7-10% value of the 
production in Scotland (31-46million USD per year on 130.000tons production) (Rae, 2002). The potential 
value of the fish lost due to the 2006 IPN outbreak in Irish Salmon aquaculture was estimated at €26 - 31M 
according to Geoghegan, 2007. A study concerning the assessment of economical losses caused by total 
disease outbreaks was carried on a big scale for Brazilian freshwater aquaculture sector, estimating in total 
84million USD economic loss (Tavares-Dias at al., 2017). The estimative is taking into consideration official 
production data and labour costs. However, it is reported lacking of diagnostics and official data reporting 
outbreaks, mortalities and control methods, which do not appear in the cost analysis either indirect costs of 
mortality and effect on growth and feed conversion (Tavares-Dias et la., 2017). Instead, a study on 
economic losses caused by disease in catfish culture in East Mississippi region, provides a survey-based 
estimative of direct economic impact as total of production losses and income losses during one production 
year. Considering costs of medicated feed and chemicals used, it was estimated an economic loss of 
16.9million USD over a production of 39.500 tons (Peterman, 2019). A study on European Seabass 
aquaculture in the Mediterranean estimates an increase of total production cost ranging from 2.41€/kg to 
8.07€/kg (based on farm size) due to outbreaks of Viral Encephalopathy and Retinopathy (VER)(Sanchez et 
al., 2022). Moreover, a study on Asian Seabass estimates the cost of vibriosis outbreaks as 0,24USD/kg for a 
production cycle of 210 days (starting size: 20 grams, final size: 1079 grams) (Nor et al., 2019). 
Pettersen et al., 2015 estimates direct costs of 55.4million NOK (in a 1million salmon sea cage site) caused 
by Pancreas Disease (PD) as the sum of biomass loss and health management cost. The calculation are 
obtained using a stochastic model based on the one developed by Aunsmo et al., 2010, which estimates 
economic losses of 14,4million NOK (in a 500.000 smolt site) caused by Pancreas Disease in Norwegian 
Atlantic salmon aquaculture. The model presents national statistics as source for prices and production

The cost assessment elaborated in the study is based on a concept of total mortality, which includes several 
factors that causes biomass losses in a fish farm and not only a single specific disease. Multiple infections 
may occur simultaneously and the complexity of clinical situation leads to difficult diagnosis and disease 
control management. With specific reference to the seawater site, it is also estimated, thanks to the 
information gained from the fish health manager at the farm and the data gathered from the diagnostic 
laboratory, that mortality is primarily ascribed to Foruncolosis. However, fish are vaccinated against A. 
salmonicida prior to be moved into sea cages during early spring and show to be protected until August, 
when mortality reaches highest peak together with water temperature. Therefore, it is possible to assume 
a drop of protection coming with time (4-5 months) and water temperature rise, as observed among all the 
three studied production years. To stop the mortality increase, medicated feed is used (average 
39,3tons/year, 790.553dkk, for a final production of 887,3tons). The medicated feed includes an extra cost 
of 10dkk/kg, which has a negative impact on farm economy. Furthermore, when antibiotics are used, fish 
cannot be sold to the market for human consumption prior of a specific withdrawal time (EU 470/2009), 
causing delay in harvesting and extra costs to adapt the production planning (Esposito et al.,2007). 
One of the main symptoms of disease is reduced appetite of infected fish. The reduced feed intake during 
an outbreak needs to be taken into account when feeding programs are elaborated, especially when 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
medicated feed is given. In fact, a lower feeding ration is adopted for treatment in order to avoid feed spill 
and optimize the efficiency of the medication. Intensive feeding would represent an extra stress factor for 
the fish and cause oxygen-drops, especially considering that mortality peaks are mainly observed during 
summer with higher temperatures and lower dissolved oxygen level in the water. For this reasons, the risk 
is higher regarding infection for healthy fish and mortality for infected fish. In fact, non-consumed feed 
would significantly contribute to lower water quality level, especially in a closed system with low 
recirculation level, as in the case of freshwater site. Uneaten feed laying on the tank bottoms or dissolved 
in the water, also represent a substrate for growth of other pathogens that may cause secondary 
infections. In an open environment like the saltwater site, it could be possible to integrate the medicated 
feed ration with normal feed in order not to lose growth of the healthy individuals that would consume a 
larger amount of feed. As it is possible to observe in the SW case, when medicated feed is given, normal 
feed is given with a lower feeding ration than previous month before treatment. Size heterogeneity 
represent the main consequence of more consistent feeding during an infection treatment, enhancing feed 
consumption of partial population compared to general reduction of feed intake of infected fish stocks. 
Environmental parameters such as temperature of water and dissolved oxygen are also influencing the 
above described dynamic, leading fish farmers to opt for different strategy based on external conditions 
and personal experience. The excel model permits, for example, to opt for an intensive “feeding strategy” 
by inserting an higher amount of feed given in a month during medication treatment, assume an increase 
of the mortality rate of the correspondent month, and finally observe the impact of the “feeding strategy” 
on the final income and total mortality cost, fig 14 and compare it to real case fig 13.

It needs to be mentioned that the excel file requires a little adaptation (calculation of biomass as feed/FCR 
instead of number of fish*individual weight) in order to display this variation of “feeding strategy” showed

Mortality rates are shown to directly affect the economic impact of the total production. In fact, as the 
mortality rates increase, the mortality cost as loss of income rises and represents a growing portion of the 
total income and total cost. In the saltwater site case, 2020 production presents the lowest mortality rate 
and as well the smallest loss of income caused by mortality costs. 2019 production instead presents the 
highest mortality rate and the biggest loss of income by mortality. On the other hand, the study show how 
the variation of production costs and market values among different years can influence the economic 
performance together with impact of mortality. The recent drop of market value of trout flesh led 2018 
production to be the highest in terms of produced biomass and total income although the mortality rate is 
higher than 2020 production.  
Overall, this study shows how the mortality cost has a relevant impact on the production in the SW site 
while it appears less consistent in FW site where lower mortality rates are reported. However, the excel 
model can show how similar economic impact would be reported in both SW and FW, if the mortality rate 
was adapted to equal values, in specific, reduced in SW site. The main differences behind the results that 
are observed are the production costs, the smaller biomass produced at the FW site and the environmental 
factors that differ in the two farming sites. In fact, the risk of diseases outbreaks occurrence is reduced 
within a smaller biomass (most likely lower density too) and its management is easier regarding prevention 
and treatment, which result more efficient. Veterinary aspects within the bigger biomass present at the SW 
site results instead more difficult to manage and control (difficult manipulation and observation), giving 
more room for economic impact to mortality effects. In addition, the farming environment plays an 
important role regards to biosecurity. SW site displays sea cages in open environment, being exposed to 
physical and biological hazards that may cause higher incidence of diseases outbreaks and higher mortality 
rates. FW site works in a controlled environment where water quality is constantly monitored and 
recirculating through water treatment. Inlet water from a well theoretically prevents risk of contamination 
by several pathogens that would cause diseases outbreaks in the farming system and to higher mortality-
related direct and indirect costs. FW site reports higher production costs due to electricity use for water 
recirculation and oxygenation expenses.  
Concerning indirect effects of mortality, the study observe that during an infection outbreak the total 
production is not only decreasing by loss of biomass but also by reduced feed conversion. Average FCR 
values among three production years interested in the study, are shown to increase with the increase of 
mortality rate. The higher is the FCR the lower is the feed conversion, meaning less biomass growth per 
given feed amount. The reduction of feed conversion of infected fish represents a relevant economic issue 
and a significant bottleneck for feeding programs and production planning. In fact, there is not available 
tool to estimate and predict the variation of feed intake and feed conversion of infected fish in relation to 
progression of mortality rates. As a consequence, this problem is causing lack of control of the real 
dimension of biomass in the system and lack of accuracy in feeding programs. Rosaeg et al., 2019 estimated 
the impact on feed conversion rate caused by Pancreas Disease outbreak in Norwegian Atlantic salmon 
showing 0,7kg decreased growth and 0,07 increased FCR. The current study make possible to observe and 
quantify the variation of FCR among the three production years in relation to different mortality rates, 
reporting an average monthly increase of 0,3 to the FCR during 2019 (highest mortality rate) compared to 
==PAGE:28==
27 
 
2020 (lowest mortality rate), which is reflected in a slightly smaller final production starting from and 
higher initial biomass. This variation it is estimated to consist in loss of biomass growth for 72tons 
(potential sales 2.304.000dkk), and 115,2tons of feed (potential saving 1.163.520dkk). Based on the result 
achieved in this study, further analysis can be carried on to estimate more precisely the effect of mortality 
on FCR, representing interesting steps in the development of aquaculture sector toward precision farming 
direction. 
Technological development leads aquaculture industry to higher standards and new goals thanks to the 
adoption of innovative solution able to overcome certain bottlenecks of fish farming sector. Among these 
topics, the matter of focus of this study finds its own collocation. In fact, the assessment of economical 
impact of mortality and related direct and indirect effects, represents an interesting contribution to 
optimization of efficiency in rainbow trout farming. The application area of the study are important 
subjects such as costs reduction, health management, feeding accuracy and production planning, matching 
the objectives of precision fish farming PFF concept defined by Fore et al 2018:  
“1) improve accuracy, precision and repeatability in farming operations;  
2) facilitate more autonomous and continuous biomass/animal monitoring;  
3) provide more reliable decision support and;  
4) reduce dependencies on manual labour and subjective assessments, and thus improve staff safety.”  
The aim of the study and the development of innovative solutions according to PFF concept, represent an 
optimization of the efficiency and growth of the industry. The reduction of losses and waste leads to better 
control of the production and to parallel positive response in terms of animal welfare and ecological 
sustainability of aquaculture (Bang Jensen et al., 2020). Mortality in salmonids aquaculture involves millions 
of individual fish losses representing a major economic limitation and its reduction is essential to guarantee 
the sustainability of the aquaculture production (Oliveira et al., 2021). 
In conclusion, losses from mortality represents a serious limitation in aquaculture. For this reason, health 
management has a significant importance along the production cycle since it can mitigate the economic 
losses and contribute to the overall efficiency of the farming system. The development of the excel tool 
come as a support for decision making in rainbow trout aquaculture when infectious diseases occur. The 
study visually express the impact of mortality rates and its direct-indirect effects on the economical result 
of the production. Data collection concerning outbreaks and mortalities plays and important role for the 
developing of predictive tools to help farmers to deal with infections, treatment and vaccination. Also 
production planning and feeding programs represent area of improvement in relation to mortality effects 
on the production analysed in the study. Based on the result obtained, future elaborations can contribute 
to the study of fish farming accuracy and precision, essential goals toward the sustainable growth of 
aquaculture.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
The development of the excel spreadsheet includes feeding data directly from the producer’s database file. 
For this reason, it is possible to replicate the study and observe the cost analysis in new production cycles 
as new data are collected. With specific adaptations to future cases, the application of the model may 
potentially show the economical impact of mortality on the production in live-time, by progressively 
feeding with the real data. 
The cost assessment reveals another interesting field of study. The vaccination expenses and efficiency is 
reported in the model and thanks to the dynamic calculations elaborated in the study it is possible to 
observe how the final income and the mortality cost would be affected by different prices of vaccines and 
different protection level provided by the vaccination. In the appendix it is reported and excel spreadsheet 
showing a comparison of performances of the real vaccine used in the production and two scenarios with 
vaccines with higher protection and higher price and with lower protection and lower costs, according to 
the result obtained by Thorarinsson and Powell, 2006. This analysis show how, providing stronger 
immunization, the price of vaccine unit can increase being still profitable for the fish producer, representing 
interesting application for both pharmaceutic industry and fish farmers in a mutual growth.
