Abstract 
Bacteriophages, or phages, are ubiquitous bacterial and archaeal viruses with an estimated 
1031 total global population. However, only a relatively small fraction of phages has so far 
been identified, and even fewer have been classified according to host-phage interactions 
and relevant taxonomy. It is well-known that wherever there are bacteria, their phage 
counterparts will be found, aiding in shaping the bacterial population. The present project 
used data from a metagenomics study of global influent sewage to try and identify phages 
associated with bacteria to explore their potential role in antimicrobial resistance gene (ARG) 
dissemination. The low abundance of the phages identified in this project makes it difficult 
to draw any bona fide conclusions from the findings but may give some preliminary insights 
into the global and regional distribution of phages and their potential impact in the 
transmission of ARGs between bacteria. Nevertheless, the abundances may indicate, that 
phages in sewage, depending on various conditions, could have adopted a lytic lifestyle, 
meaning that most will not be associated with bacteria, and instead will be widely distributed 
throughout as free-living phages, who are known to persist longer in the environment than 
their hosts. It is found, that some phages are not equally distributed on a global scale, but 
that their distributions are potentially dictated by region, and the given socioeconomic status 
of the countries within. The most prominent observation is the allocation of phages infecting 
lactic acid fermenting bacteria, such as Lactococcus and Leuconostoc bacteria, in countries 
of high income, known to have a higher dairy intake in comparison to low- and middle-
income regions, while their potential hosts of lactic acid fermenting bacteria are thought to 
be reservoirs of antimicrobial resistance genes. In addition, a significant correlation between 
phages and ARGs is obtained, indicating that phages play a role in ARG dissemination, 
however, further analyses must be performed to establish the true relationship due to the 
low abundances.

1. Introduction 
Bacteriophages, or phages, discovered on two independent occasions by Frederick W. 
Twort (1915) [4] and Felix d’Herelle (1917) [5], are ubiquitous bacterial and archaeal viruses, 
with a total global population estimate of more than 1031, surpassing that of bacteria (1029), 
making them the most abundant biological entities in the biosphere. Their genomes, ranging 
from 2.4kb to 735 kb [3, 6], consist of either DNA or RNA which can be either single-stranded 
(ss) or double-stranded (ds). Archaeal and RNA phages (not known to encode any reverse 
transcriptase) are beyond the scope of this project and will not be discussed further.  
1.1 
 Taxonomy  
1.1.1 
 The challenge of mosaicism 
The taxonomical classification of phages has been revised a multitude of times [8], where 
different approaches have been adopted, some no longer relevant.  In 1966, what is now 
known as the International Committee on the Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV) was founded. 
Charged with the streamlining of viral taxonomical classification, the ICTV constitutes 
several subcommittees each responsible for their separate viral domains, with that of 
Bacterial and Archaeal Viruses Subcommittee (BAVS) being responsible for the 
classification of phages [9]. In the ICTV system, phages are classified on basis of some 70 
properties of which the nature of nucleic acid, morphology and physiochemical properties of 
the virion are the most important (Table 1). These properties are now largely completed by 
genomic data, however, an approach relying entirely on genomics is an almost impossible 
endeavour, as phages, even within those that infect the same hosts, are highly diverse, and 
often most show very little sequence similarity [10]. This diversity largely arises from the

highly variable polysaccharide modifications, these phages thus generally tend to have a 
narrower host range as compared to those targeting bacteria with rough LPS. In addition, 
the more exotic receptors, such as flagella and pili have only been identified in Gram-
negative bacteria, despite their presence in Gram-positive bacteria.

account for a substantial proportion of the bacterial genome, thereby accounting for the

variation is the cholera toxin (CTX) encoded by the CTXΦ phage1.

phage replication factors interplay. A pitfall of the conventional replication machinery is the

mechanism behind concatemerisation is proposed to be through recombination but has not 
been further elucidated [18].

1.3.1.2 
Virion assembly 
Assembly of virions, whom hitherto have been identified as the most densely packed 
entities, have most extensively been studied in the dsDNA tailed phages, but the processes 
still share substantial similarities to that observed in other viruses including viruses that are 
not associated with bacteria nor archaea [21]. However, phages, even within the same 
group, still have notable differences in the assembly pathway, size and symmetry of the 
capsid and tail proteins, and their gene structure. Here, the assembly of phages of 
icosahedral symmetry is briefly described.

The first event in virion assembly is the assembly of the procapsid, a premature capsid that 
contains no genetic material, around a protein scaffold. Typically, the procapsid and its 
internal scaffolding proteins are nucleated from the portal protein [22, 23], a protein residing 
on any one of the viral vertices and through which all DNA movement occurs. The protein 
scaffold is in most cases encoded by a separate gene, but has also been observed to be 
fused to the N-terminal of the major capsid protein [24, 25]. It forms a core inside the 
procapsid, and typically does not adopt the icosahedral symmetry like that observed for the 
procapsid. The release of the scaffolding proteins is in many dsDNA phages initiated by 
maturation proteases [25] (often a serine protease) packaged within the core, either as 
separate proteases or as an N-terminal domain of the scaffolding protein, but can also in 
some cases be released as an intact protein for reuse in another round of virion assembly 
[25]. Either way, the maturation process is often most accompanied by intricate structural 
rearrangements, that expands the procapsid [22, 24, 26], maturing it for DNA translocation. 
DNA translocation in dsDNA phages is mediated by an ATPase termed terminase (Ter); one 
of the most powerful molecular machines identified to date due to its capability of tightly 
packaging DNA within the capsid. The  most well-studied terminase protein, consisting of 
the large (TerL) and small (TerS) multimeric subunits, assembles on to the procapsid’s portal 
protein when DNA needs packaging. TerL possesses the ATPase activity necessary for 
translocation [27] while also acting as an endonuclease to cleave the concatemeric progeny 
DNA at initiation and termination (hence terminase) of DNA translocation. The packaging 
then proceeds by “headful packaging” whereby the amount of DNA packaged within the 
procapsid is determined by the interior volume of the mature capsid. Thus, if DNA molecules 
smaller than the capacity of the head is packaged within, the packaging machinery will 
continue to scavenge DNA to fill the head [28], making phages potential mediators of 
horizontal gene transfer. The proteins found together with the DNA after packaging are 
integral parts of the DNA-free procapsid and are only associated with DNA subsequent to 
DNA translocation and have proven to be important in the initial stages of infection. Virions 
can be divided into four different categories, dependent on their inner structure of the 
procapsid [29]:  
1. Those containing only naked DNA and few proteins required for initial infection 
2. Those with thousands of unstructured proteins dispersed within the DNA 
3. Those with a small number of localised proteins within the DNA 
4. Those whose internal structure is dominated by more than one type of protein that 
has a defined shaped and volume and which contains a reduced amount or no DNA. 
Finally, the terminase will dissociate from the portal protein and assembly of the tail proteins 
commences. 
1.3.2 
Temperate phages and the lysogenic cycle 
In contrast to virulent phages, so-called temperate phages can replicate through both the 
lytic cycle but also the lysogenic cycle which confers phage propagation either through 
integration by site-specific recombination or random transposition into the bacterial 
chromosome, thus becoming a prophage, or as residing circular or linear plasmids [30]. 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
Page 9 of 47

Here, the phage can modulate the bacterial genome (lysogenic conversion), which can 
reinforce bacterial fitness by supplying genes that enhance metabolism, antimicrobial 
resistance, stress responses and virulence. It has been reported, that as much as 20% of 
the bacterial genomes deposited in the NCBI genomes database consists of prophage DNA 
[31] that has gradually decayed over time by e.g. point mutations [32]. 
The lysogenic cycle progresses through two distinct stages, which at very low frequencies 
may abruptly and upon induction terminate in a lytic cycle (section 1.3.1). The stages 
comprise establishment and maintenance. During establishment, the phage needs to evade 
host resistance mechanisms such as CRISPR-Cas systems prior to integration or 
establishment as a plasmid, Integration can be site-specific, e.g. within tRNA genes [33], 
and driven by certain phage encoded recombinases, but can also be random, with no 
apparent site specificity. Once the phage is established, the phage will in the course of 
integration passively replicate with the bacterial chromosome, while certain systems such 
as toxin/anti-toxin plasmid systems will ensure inheritance and persistence of extra-
chromosomal plasmids with the next generation. 
Although the knowledge of mechanisms controlling phage dynamics in bacterial populations 
are very limited, some parameters have been explored that can influence the decisions 
made by phages for the induction of lysis (productive) from a lysogenic (latent) state. 
Amongst these are temperature dependence [34], where higher temperatures (37°C) 
promotes lytic lifecycles, and lower temperatures (25°C) generally promotes lysogenic 
lifecycles; modelling of the phage-bacteria interactions have shown that there are highly 
variable daily oscillations in bacterial densities that are either phage-free, lytic or lysogenic 
[35] dependent on daily temperature fluctuations. Furthermore, the presence of specific 
chemicals such as antibiotics, the density and metabolic state of the host bacterium, and 
UV irradiation may also induce the lytic cycle of temperate phages [32]. Finally, the activation 
of prophages has also been linked to biofilm formation [36]. Yet, phages within the same 
family or even with a greater degree of nucleotide sequence similarity, may still respond 
differently to the same conditions. 
1.3.3 
Chronic and pseudolysogenic infections 
While only occasionally described, the chronic and pseudolysogenic cycles are somewhat 
in-between states of the aforementioned cycles. The chronic cycle is observed in archaeal 
phages and in some Plasmaviridae which pertains to Acholeplasma. This type of cycle 
stably, or as the name refers, chronically, releases phage progeny with no harm to the host 
bacterium, whilst the latter, usually caused by unfavourable growth conditions of the host 
bacterium [37], denotes the entry of a phage into a host without stable integration or 
residence, in turn resulting in no phage reproduction, while no phage degradation is neither 
observed. It is fair to acknowledge, that regardless of the lifecycle, virions can occasionally 
capture bacterial DNA during assembly and thus effectively act in horizontal gene transfer.

1.4 
Phages as resistance gene vectors 
Phages can act in horizontal gene transfer through either specialised or generalised 
transduction [38]. In specialised transduction, typical for temperate phages, a small, flanking 
portion of the bacterial genome is excised with the prophage genome, as the temperate 
phage is induced to enter a lytic cycle, thereby mobilising a restricted part of the bacterial 
genome. Similarly, generalised transduction, observed in both virulent and temperate 
phages, occurs when the phage genome either needs packaging within the viral capsids, 
and as mentioned previously (Section 1.1.1), gets destabilised if too little DNA is packaged 
within, or if the phage genome undergoes recombination. To circumvent the destabilisation, 
the phage can capture a fragment of the bacterial genomic DNA usually becoming a 
multipartite satellite phage that is dependent on other phages for replication. In general, both 
transduction phenomena of ARGs occur at extremely low frequencies in clinical settings 
which have been estimated to approximately 10-9 and 10-7 transductants/pfu, for specialised 
and generalised transduction, respectively [32].  
The role of transduction for gene transfer within a clinical setting is well-established, and 
there is growing evidence, that phages through transduction, aids in the dissemination of 
ARGs in the environment [39-42]. However, this is still controversial as contrasting research 
also indicates that the number of phage encoded ARGs is grossly overestimated [43] due 
to too lenient parameter thresholds, with only a small fraction of phages carrying ARGs in 
human-associated viromes. 
Another interesting, and possibly important aspect, is the mobile genetic elements 
associated with phages denoted PICIs (phage-inducible chromosomal islands) [31, 32, 38]. 
PICIs were originally identified is Staphylococcus aureus (SaPIs), where they are associated 
with virulence and antibiotic resistance and were later discovered to also occur in both other 
Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. PICIs are so-called satellite phages, dependent 
on helper phages for infection and replication. They are characterised by a specific set of 
phage-related functions enabling them to use another phage’s reproductive cycle while 
inhibiting the phage reproduction in the process. In contrast to conventional phages 
described previously, PICIs appear to have a more conserved gene organisation. In 
addition, the rate of transduction observed within PICIs greatly exceeds that of conventional 
phages, reported as 10-1 transductants/pfu [32], suggesting that they play a key role in the 
dissemination of ARGs. However, PICIs, are not considered in the present project.

Metagenomic surveillance of ARG dissemination in sewage 
The emerging antimicrobial resistance is one of the biggest health threats globally; a report 
from 2016 [44] estimated that the total global death toll from antimicrobial infectious disease 
currently is at 700,000 while by 2050 this number will have risen to 10 million. Furthermore, 
it is predicted to have a huge economic impact with an estimated inaction cost of US$100 
trillion between 2016 and 2050. Therefore, one of the key recommendations set forth in 
prevention of deaths attributed to antimicrobial infectious disease and the resulting

Metagenomic shotgun analyses, where one aims to investigate the entire composition of

2. Objectives 
A global study from 2019 by Hendriksen et al. [45] found that there is a systematic regional 
difference in bacterial population and ARGs in urban sewage. The study included 79 
metagenomic samples from 60 different countries and investigated the global distribution of 
ARGs. However, a certain aspect of ARG distribution was not investigated, namely that of 
phages. Therefore, the present project aims to characterise phages associated with bacteria 
in the data from this study and see if there is a corresponding regional segregation of 
phages. For this purpose, some of the largest viral databases will be explored. Furthermore, 
the occurrence and diversity of phages across the globe will be examined in addition to 
whether socioeconomic status influence their distribution, and finally, a potential correlation 
between observed phages and bacteria in addition to ARGs will be determined.

3.6 
Filtering phages from viruses 
Phages were filtered from other viruses using the script phage_philter.py [53]. A visual 
representation of the filter can be seen in Figure 6. The filter works by reading a metadata 
file to a set of phage accession numbers, then querying each accession number of the first 
line of the query file against the set. The reason for using a set is due to its relatively fast 
look-up. If the header is observed in the set, the index of the header is stored in a list of 
phage indices, if not, the index of the header is stored in a list of other virus indices. The 
reason for choosing a list for this task is due to it being more efficient running iterations, 
which will be done, when the phage and virus datasets are each written to files. The index 
lists will simply provide an index on where to retrieve data from each line in a given input.

3.7.3 
Regional distribution of phages 
The number of phages contributing to each region was found by summing the relative 
abundances of a given phage across samples belonging to a given region. Phages having 
a sum greater than 0 were regarded as having been observed within that region. Their 
percentages of the total number identified for a given database were then subsequently 
calculated.  
The regional distribution of phages are visualised through a principal coordinate analysis 
(PCoA) of the relative abundances of phages, viruses, and bacteria. The PCoA plots are 
chosen over that of e.g. PCA due to the low abundances and many null values observed, 
making the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity more suitable for ordination, in turn making PCoA more 
suitable for viewing dissimilarities. The PCoA plots were generated on Hellinger 
standardised relative abundances to account for differences in abundances, and the 
dissimilarity between samples were, as mentioned previously,  computed using Bray-Curtis 
dissimilarities. The betadisper function of the vegan package was used to reduce the original 
non-Euclidean distances (or more appropriately dissimilarities) of  the Bray-Curtis 
dissimilarity matrix to principal coordinates, and the variance contributions by each principal 
coordinate were calculated using the eigenvals function of the vegan package.

3.7.5 
Diversity, richness and evenness 
To adjust for differences in sampling sizes across samples, all data was rarefied to lowest 
sampling size (Supplementary Figure 16), allowing for retention of all samples, prior to 
analysis using the R library vegan. Diversity, richness and evenness were then visualised in 
ggplot2 boxplots using Simpson, Chao1 and Pielou indices. The Simpson index is a good 
measure for the assessment of phage diversity and is commonly used in ecology studies; it 
takes into account the number of species present as well as their relative abundances. The 
Chao1 richness index is an appropriate measure in this context, as many phages are of very 
low abundance, and thus perceived as rare, why a weight will be placed on these using this 
index. Finally, the Pielou evenness is calculated using the diversity function of the vegan 
package in R.  
3.7.6 
Phage abundance  
Phage relative abundances were visualised raw for the top 15 most abundant phages per 
sample and pooled for all phages in each sample within each family (for NCBI phages) or 
clade (for huge phages) using the R package pheatmap. The top 15 most abundant phages 
were found using the script utopia.py, while all phages were pooled to family level and the 
number of phages contributing to the specified taxonomical level were calculated using the 
script group_phages.py. Due to the poor annotation of IMG/VR no data pooling was 
performed for this database. The very low abundances of phages made log-transformation 
more or less insignificant, thus raw values were used for visualisation. Instead, Colour 
intensities within the heatmaps were generated based on quantiles (Table 7) to show the 
distribution of the phages given the quantiles. Quantiles were determined based on the 
visual representation, with the smallest possible margins (percentages). The samples were 
clustered according to Bray-Curtis dissimilarities of Hellinger standardised relative 
abundances, while phages were clustered according to their Pearson correlation, to explore 
how phage abundances were associated.

3.7.7 
Phage phylogeny 
Only the most abundant phages were used for further phylogenetic analysis. The analysis 
was conducted using CSIPhylogeny [58] and phyML [59], and visualised using iTOL [60]. 
CSIPhylogeny was used to perform variant calling and subsequent sequence alignment by 
using the most abundant phages as reference genomes and sewage metagenomics raw 
reads as input files. The tree topology structure was generated using PhyML on basis of the 
HKY85 substitution model and SPR tree topology heuristic with 100 bootstrap replicates. An 
attempt to find the most optimal nucleotide substitution model was done using jmodeltest 
[61], but did not succeed, why the default substitution model was used. 100 bootstrap 
replicates were chosen, as this is the minimum number of replicates to give a significant 
result.

4. Results 
Most viruses within the NCBI dataset were of phage origin; of 719 total accession numbers, 
638 belonged to phages while 81 belonged to that of other viruses. For the IMG/VR 
database, a total 54,893 sequences were of phage origin of which 3,699 were more than 
90% completed, high-quality draft genome or entirely completed genomes (herein after 
collectively referred to as “IMG/VR genomes”); 24,348 sequences were of other viral origin. 
A total of 114 phages from the huge phage database were identified within the datasets. 
The mean coverage and depth of coverage, referred to as depth, of phages within the 
various databases were generally very low (Supplementary Table 2 and Supplementary 
Figure 3 to 5) with values ranging between 1.21 to 2.5 and 0.03 to 0.18, respectively.

4.1 
Mapping summary 
The vast majority of fragments were mapped to the NCBI database of bacteria, followed by 
that of IMG/VR (Supplementary Figure 1). Of the viral fraction, a larger proportion of reads 
were mapped to that of the huge phages (Figure 7, top), followed by that of IMG/VR. Only 
very few reads had been mapped to NCBI virus and rather negligible to that of KVIT. As the 
documentation for KVIT named only viral families of viruses infecting eukaryotic cells in 
addition to an unclassified branch, it would not be expected to observe any phages identified 
from this database. However, it was later discovered, that two phages with GenBank 
accession numbers in the KMA genomic database existed, and that one of these particular 
phages did not have an NCBI virus complete nucleotide accession number counterpart, 
suggesting it originated from KVIT (while the other had such an accession number and was 
likewise identified within the NCBI database). Regardless, the number of fragments mapping 
to genomes from KVIT was already diminished, thus recovering potential phages from the 
KVIT database would probably lead to insignificant discoveries.  
Five samples, i.e. one Asian sample (India, IND-11), two European samples (Germany, 
DEU-27 and Hungary, HUN-61) and two North American (Canada; CAN-22 and CAN-22a) 
had only very few fragments mapping to any of the databases, with most of their fragments 
generally mapping to phages of IMG/VR. 17 countries had more than 1 million fragments 
mapped in total to all viral database, and two of these countries (Pakistan, PAK-7 and Nepal, 
NPL-33), both within the Asia region, had more than 2.5 and 3 million fragments mapped, 
respectively, most prominently to that of huge phages followed by IMG/VR, of which most 
were of phage origin. The number of fragments mapping to IMG/VR for these countries 
seemed comparable to that of other regions. The number of fragments generally mapped to 
NCBI was rather few, with the most prominent in an African and South American sample: 
Senegal (SEN-8) and Peru (PER-35). The huge phage database had most mapped 
fragments, but also had not competed against any other databases.

4.4 
Diversity, richness and evenness 
Most samples except for a single sample in North America, appeared to have reached 
rarefaction (Supplementary Figure 16). Meaning that the true diversity of most samples 
could be interpreted. It appeared, that at least 50% of all samples had a Simpson diversity 
index of more than 0.80 across all databases (Figure 12, left panel), with IMG/VR having a 
Simpson index close to 0.99 in all regions except for Asia, which has a wider inter-quartile 
range (IQR) in comparison, indicating that the environments are generally diverse. However, 
for the NCBI database, the IQR of the Simpson diversity index of the Middle East, North 
America and Oceania regions are more widely distributed, indicating that not all samples 
within these regions share the same degree of diversity. These regions also tend to have a 
lower median Simpson index, indicating that the diversity is correspondingly smaller. 
With regards to phage richness, the overall median topology appears identical between 
NCBI virus and huge phages, except for Asia which has a greater median richness in NCBI 
in comparison to that of all other regions, while for huge phages Asia has a smaller median 
richness in comparison to Africa, Europe and South America (Figure 12, middle panel). The 
IQRs are also somewhat comparable, albeit Asia and South America have greater 
dispersion within 50% of the data and proportionately more outliers. For IMG/VR it appears 
that Europe has the highest median richness, followed by that of North America, Africa, Asia, 
Middle East, South America and finally Oceania. The dispersion is greatest within Asia, 
Africa and South America, consistent with the observations from both NCBI and huge

5. Discussion 
It is not uncommon nor a surprise that only a small fraction of phages has been observed 
within the data. Firstly, due to the high diversity within phage nucleotide sequences and the 
fact that there is a certain overlap in phage and bacterial DNA, owing to the integration of 
prophages, adopting a targeted approach only allow for a very limited identification, that may 
lead to false negatives with respect to phages. In addition, it is important to acknowledge 
the vast differences in database sizes when comparing that of NCBI virus to that of NCBI 
bacteria, with only 2925 (as of June  2020) and more than 370,000 assembled genomes, 
respectively, while that of IMG/VR contains approximately 760,000 viral sequences of which 
more than half are of phage origin and 23,500 of those are high-quality draft of completed 
phage genomes. The differences in database sizes were evident, as many more fragments 
had mapped to that of the IMG/VR database in comparison to that of the NCBI database 
even when IMG/VR had competed against a much larger collection of sequences. With 
regards to the huge phages, the larger number of mapped fragments were possibly due to 
the database not competing against any other databases and only internally had few, albeit 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
Page 41 of 47

Final version 1.0 
 
relatively large sequences. Furthermore, it has previously been established [65] that 
employing an untargeted (de novo) metagenomic approach, will allow for a much wider 
identification of phages within metagenomic samples. This of course, has its pitfalls, such 
as assembling an unbiased viral metagenome due to the vast diversity, while identifying the 
hosts of the assembled phages and performing annotations according to the ICTV 
classifications is not easily done, as many of the phages are not cultured and thus cannot 
undergo the necessary examinations. The wider identification is merely due to the very short 
evolutionary scale on which phages evolve, even phages belonging to the same group still 
show quite a significant nucleotide sequence diversity with little homology [3], whereby de 
novo assembly will more easily capture these differences. Consistent with this is the 
observation, that the large majority of phages identified within this study only exhibit low 
coverages (Supplementary Table 2). Other limitations are the inherent randomness of 
sequencing that is performed when undertaking a metagenomics approach, where bacterial 
DNA is far more abundant than that of phages [66], and the fact that the DNA purification 
protocol must be adapted to the given environment from which phages should be isolated, 
which is not the case for the data employed in this study. Phages are usually purified with 
the viral fraction of the samples, namely that of the supernatant, due to the general buoyancy 
of viral particles in a CsCl gradient. This, however, will bias towards phages that exhibit this 
buoyancy, potentially eliminating yet unknown phages. Briefly speaking, purification and 
identification of phages using a metagenomics approach require carefully considered 
protocols and pipelines bearing in mind the aim of the project. Thus, phages expected to be 
observed within this study are those in association with bacteria. However, as mentioned 
previously, the lifecycle through which a given phage progresses may be dictated by the 
external factors such as temperature, and chemicals like antimicrobial residues, both 
influencing municipal wastewater; the global sewage study from which the data is borrowed 
[45], detected antimicrobial residues, and another study has found, that e.g. the 
concentration of ciprofloxacin in wastewater is high enough to induce the SOS response in 
bacteria which in turn affects the activation of prophages [67]. It is therefore likely that many 
phages should be regarded as free-living and therefore most phages will not be in 
association with said bacteria. Other external factors and bacterial stressors that may 
influence whether a given phage adopts a lytic or lysogenic lifecycle, are not considered 
here, mostly due to the lack of data in this regard. 
In contrast to oceanic phages, which are the most studied environmental phages, some of  
the phages observed in this project appears to segregate regionally, nevertheless this 
appears also to be the case for other types of viruses, which is not surprising as the samples 
investigated are confined to local environments, as opposed to oceans where phages are 
likely spread via oceanic currents. However, when looking into the segregation of other 
viruses identified from NCBI, there is no apparent regionality, this may be contributed to the 
fact that the pellets of the samples analysed are not conventional for identifying other types 
of viruses, and consistently, only a small number of other viruses are captured within the 
samples; most likely those of high abundance. As mentioned previously, viral DNA is usually 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
Page 42 of 47

Final version 1.0 
 
purified from the supernatant, which in turn is reflected in the number of non-phage viruses 
identified from both NCBI and IMG/VR which are significantly less than that of phages. Yet 
the number of other viruses (and other viral fragments) within the IMG/VR database is still 
significantly high (24,348 viruses) to capture regional segregation. Interestingly, there is a 
remarkable segregation of huge phages (Supplementary Figure 8), where Europe tends to 
cluster with that of Africa, Asia and South America, while Middle East tends to spread widely 
across clusters. Yet the highest diversity of huge phages is observed within Africa and Asia, 
which is consistent with the higher diversity of phage clades observed in these two regions. 
It appears, that a country’s income (GNI per capita) plays a role in the distribution of phages, 
as many countries in Europe, North America and Oceania, tend to allocate to the high-
income class, where the low income-class primarily consists of countries from Africa and a 
single country from Asia, and the middle-income class consists of primarily countries from 
Middle-East and Asia, including some European countries. The middle-income class 
distributes somewhat between the two classes with regards to phage composition, but 
generally shares more phages with the low-income class, and both classes have 
Escherichia phages among their unique phages, while the low-income class only appears 
to share a small amount of phages with that of high-income class, albeit both classes have 
Lactobacillus phages (another lactic acid fermenting bacterial genus) among their unique 
phages. A possible explanation for this observation could be differences in dietary intake, a 
parameter not investigated in the present project, but that is shown to be – at least locally – 
affected by income [68], which in turn will affect the intestinal microbiota, and thus potentially 
phages. This is supported by the observation of a higher prevalence of phages infecting 
lactic acid fermenting bacteria such as Lactococcus and Leuconostoc phages in high-
income countries and certain regions associated with these, which are known to consume 
larger amounts of dairy products than countries in other income classes [69]. The majority 
of these phages are observed within Slovakia, which interestingly has had a decline in dairy 
intake up until at least 2014 after admission into the European Union as compared to other 
countries in said union [70]. Furthermore, antibiotics are usually consumed in larger 
amounts, within high-income countries in comparison to middle- and low-income countries 
[72], in which insufficient use, i.e. not covering a full course of treatment, both can lead to 
resistance. According to WHO amoxicillin and amoxicillin/clavulanic acid are the most 
frequently used antibiotics globally, consistent with the finding, that the abundance of 
amoxicillin resistance genes detected within the samples are some of the highest. This is 
interesting, since there is an increase in resistance towards these exact antibiotics in some 
Lactococcus species [73], and there is increasing evidence, that foodborne lactic acid 
species are overlooked with regards to the dissemination of ARGs [74]. Some broad-
spectrum antibiotics are by the WHO classified as Watch antibiotics, due to their high 
potential in causing antibiotic resistance phenotypes; these have been reported to constitute 
between 20% and 50% of antibiotics consumed in certain countries. Cefoxitin 
(cephalosporin), a Watch antibiotic, is one of the most abundant resistance phenotypes

observed from the Functional Resistance database consistent with this notion. With regards 
to cefoxitin, most Lactococcus sp. are intrinsically resistant towards this compound.  
Unfortunately, only SNP trees could be resolved for that of the uncultured crAssPhage and 
the Salmonella phage SJ46. It is not surprising that the crAssPhage appears to be the most 
abundant, as it has been reported to constitute a class of highly abundant phages infecting 
that of the genus Bacteroides [75], being of the most abundant bacterial genera in the human 
gut microbiota [63]. This finding is also supported by the fact that the regions appear to have 
dominant species, when looking at the regional evenness, while the regions still maintain a 
high degree of diversity; which is not surprising, since phages generally are highly diverse. 
This is interesting, as one of the predominant phage phyla of the huge phages is 
Bacteroidetes (Supplementary Figure 15 and Supplementary Table 3) while the genus 
Bacteroides is among the top bacterial genera with a higher prevalence in European and 
North American countries. The identification of the Salmonella phage SJ46 is somewhat 
surprising, as the Salmonella genus is not among the most abundant within the dataset. The 
occurrence of Salmonella phage SJ46 can possibly be explained by its relatedness to the 
E. coli P1 phage [76]; a temperate phage that does not integrate into the genome of its host, 
but merely resides as a plasmid. As some phages have a broader host range, Salmonella 
phage SJ46 could potentially also target E. coli strains, which are among the most abundant 
bacteria in sewage. Furthermore, a large proportion of phages observed within the 
presented data appears to be that of Escherichia phages.  
Whether there is truly a correlation between phages and dissemination of ARGs in 
wastewater is still an open question. Unfortunately, due to the low abundances of phages 
identified within this study, and to some degree only in very few numbers with respect to 
their estimated total global abundance of 1031, nothing conclusive can be determined. The 
observation that there is a significant correlation between other viruses, whom should not 
correlate to neither bacteria nor ARGs, could arise from the fact that other viruses are also 
present in only very low abundances and in very few numbers in comparison to that of 
phages, implying a false positive. On the contrary, it has previously been postulated, that 
there is a negative selection for resistance genes, as phages tend to select for genes acting 
in carbon flux pathways for their own benefit in reproduction [43]. However, this is mainly 
investigated for oceanic surface and deep water phages in which bacterial nutrition is limited, 
which is not the case for wastewater influent. If, however, there is a positive selection for 
genes acting in carbon flux pathways, then there is a possibility, that phages may not be 
significantly correlated to ARGs.  
Nonetheless, considered they should be significantly correlated to that of bacteria, and that 
bacteria are significantly correlated to that of ARGs while being relatively abundant, 
indicates that further research should be performed into this relation, possibly with the 
inclusion of PICIs.

6. Conclusion 
The fact that only a very small abundance of phages is observed within the data analysed 
makes bona fide conclusions difficult to ascertain. The observations made in this project 
could, however, serve as preliminary insights into phage distribution and further support the 
notion that wastewater treatment plants may be hotspots for the transduction-mediated 
exchange of ARGs between bacteria. As DNA purification was not optimised for viral 
identification it is not a surprise that only a small fraction of phages has been observed while 
the method employed for identification has its limitations due to the vast diversity in phage 
nucleotide sequences. Regardless, some interesting phages do exhibit a certain degree of 
regionality as those infecting Lactococcal and to a lesser extent Leuconostoc bacteria 
appear to be in greater abundance in regions of high income-countries, and Klebsiella 
phages tend to be of higher relative abundances in middle-income countries, indicating that 
the effect, or rather consequences, of income, could have an influence on phage 
segregation. Whether phages are truly significantly correlated to ARG dissemination is still 
an open question, as the numbers and abundances are far too low for any certainties, albeit 
some observations, namely that of the distribution of the aforementioned Lactococcal 
phages in relation to the increasing resistance in Lactococcus sp., indicate that there could 
be a significant correlation, especially considered the increasing resistance phenotypes 
observed in these bacteria, why they could act as ARG incubators and disseminators.

phylogeny 
[58] 
Mapping of reads to a reference sequence using BWA v.0.7.2. The depth at each 
position is then calculated using genomeCoverageBed from BEDTools. Individual 
SNPs are subsequently called using mpileup from SAMTools. SNPs at each position 
are filtered out if the depth at the SNP position is not at least 10x or 10% of the average 
depth of the genome mapping. SNPs are filtered out if the Phred-based qualities of the 
mapping or the SNP are below 25 and 30, respectively. These scores were calculated 
by SAMTools. Pruning is performed, where a SNP is filtered out, if the SNP is called 
within 10 bp of another SNP. Lastly, a Z-score is calculated using the formula:
