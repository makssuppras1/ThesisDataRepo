Summary
Human mobility prediction provides valuable information in fields like traffic manage-
ment, disaster response and more. Research in mobility of masses has been thoroughly
explored and now, with smart phones, smart watches, cars and other devices accom-
modating GPS, exploring mobility of individuals has been made possible. An upper
bound of predictability of human mobility was early on suggested to be 93% where
several studies were able to recognize this with prediction rates above 90%. However,
the temporal and spatial resolution of the data has been shown to be an important
input and this has called for research that explores the limits of next location data in-
stead of continuous location data. Using next location data state of the art networks
has gotten prediction rates up to 23.2%.
In this study we explore the predictability of human mobility using the Sensible
DTU data set. We show that the theoretical upper limit on mobiliy prediction in
this dataset is 65.9% and we train an artificial recurrent neural network model to
make actual predictions. Specifically, a long short term memory (LSTM) model is
constructed, which achieves an average prediction rate of 34.6%. This performance is
obtained by combining embeddings of generalized and individual information across
users and by using a novel prediction score that is able to generalize across users. The
result is a huge improvement on several baseline models provided in this thesis as well
as an improvement to results found in similar work in the litterature (23.2%). We
note, however, that a fair comparison of the methods found in the literature to the
methods provided in this thesis would require them to be tested on the same data.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Overview
As technology has advanced the last decades so has the amount of data collection
in many forms. We are in the age of ”big data”, as expressed in The Economist
[1], which opens up a new world of exploration when combined with the growth in
computing power. Many algorithms like artificial neural networks [2] were introduced
decades ago, but had limited impact for a long time. The success of these algorithms
in recent years is to a great extend driven by the immense leap in data and computing
power required to be efficient.
Nowadays technology is everywhere and we log nearly everything for safety, prac-
tical as well as economical reasons. This has allowed questions to be answered which
was previously nearly impossible to approach, e.g. the ”small world problem” which
asks about the minimum length of connecting chains in social networks. This prob-
lem was attempted to be solved by Milgram et al [3] , in the 1960s, but now with
social platforms like Facebook, which has 2.5 billion users[4], the problem was finally
answered, with six degrees of separation, i.e. there is a maximum of 6 social links
between any human on earth.
1.1.1
Human mobility
Human mobility is a term used to describe the dynamics of humans in space. It is a
central part of human behavior that is still poorly understood. With the development
of personal devices that has a GPS, such as smart phones, wrist bands, cars, etc, data
now exists to explore this challenge. With our society aiming at being as efficient
as possible, the ability to predict crowds of people is valuable in fields like traffic
management [5], disaster response [6], health monitoring [7], spreading of diseases [8],
smart cities [9] and many more areas. In contrast to earlier research, that primarily
were focused on mobility of masses, this project will focus on the individual. Different
types of data has been used in individual mobility predictions, usually collected from
phones. These types includes GPS, wifi network, call detail record (CDR) and travel
surveys. CDR is the location of the cell tower which a phone is connected to every
time a phone call is made. The different types have different temporal and spatial
resolutions with GPS being the most accurate since it is a ”continuous” stream of
data collection, whereas the other three depends on phone usage. In this study we

locations instead of latitude and longitude coordinates as explained in [10], which

1.2
Literature review
Human mobility on an individual level was first suggested to follow the patterns of
Levy flights, which is a random walk where the jump distance comes from a power law
distribution. This was discovered by following how bank notes traveled in the US [11],
and shortly after researched again with the use of cell phones data [12]. Here the jump
distances was found to follow a truncated power law distribution, which suggested
that human mobility was more restricted than the bank notes. The same patterns
were confirmed by Rhee, et al. with the use of GPS data [13]. They found that the
parameters of the truncated power law depended on the geographical settings, and
was not the same for everyone. Additionally they studied the waiting time between
each jump which was also suggested to follow a truncated power law. However, Zhao
et al. found that power laws may not be the best way to represent travel in space
by studying different transportation modes; walking, train, driving and biking [14].
They proposed a log-normal distribution as a better fit to travel distances compared
to the truncated power law.
Unsurprisingly, humans do not move around totally at random. We move some-
where with a purpose (e.g. work, social activities, sport, etc.). It was shown by Song
et al. that the probability of revisiting a location is proportional to the amount of
previous visits [15]. Furthermore, when looking at social activities the time between
meetings of acquaintances was found to follow a heavy tail distribution similar to a
truncated power law (we meet again) [16]. Not surprisingly it was found that those
who call each other tended to share the same location [17], i.e. people who share
locations are more likely to be related. Wang et al. turned the table and tried to
predict future friends by looking at the trajectory of individuals, which showed great
predictive power [18].
1.2.1
Upper bound on the predictability of human mobility
Presently, the prediction of human mobility is often separated into two challenges;
(1) finding a theoretical upper bound and (2) making actual predictions. Finding
an upper bound has been explored several times with varying results. It was first
estimated by Song et al. to be 93%, [8], followed up by Lin et al. who estimated the
upper bound to be around 90% [19]. Song et al. did this by logging the connected cell
tower of phones every hour, which gave an average resolution of 3 km2. This lead to
questions about the temporal and spatial resolution. An investigation into the spatial
resolution was explored and lowered the upper bound to around 81%-85% by Smith
et al. [20] and Lu et al. [6]. The temporal resolution however still raised a challenge

1.2.2
Predictability of human mobility
We now turn our focus towards the task of making actual predictions rather than just
finding upper bounds. Several models have been explored, e.g. non-linear time series
[24], naive Bayes [25], Markov chains [26], Gaussian mixture models [22], principal
component analysis (PCA) [27] and neural networks [28, 29]. Some reached prediction
rates over 90%, but as discussed earlier the temporal and spatial resolutions are at
play and makes the predictions differ between models and data sets. Next location
prediction has also been attempted and Gao et al. took the most recent suggested
algorithms, for next location prediction, and compared them against their own [28].
The better performing models were all neural networks. They used four different data
sets from New York, Singapore, Houston and California. The best Top@k prediction
rate for each data set is shown in Table 1.1, where a Top@k prediction rate is the rate
of which the true location is in the k highest suggestions from a model. These results
are all generated by Gao et al.’s best performing algorithm; their own variational
attention model. It should be noticed how different the prediction rates are depending
on the data set. This is crucial to keep in mind when comparing models.

CHAPTER 2
The Data Set
In this study we use data from the Sensible DTU project also known as the Copen-
hagen Networks Study [30].
In that project more than 800 smartphones (Google
Nexus 4) were given out to students at The Technical University of Denmark (DTU)
in September and during the following months in 2013. Around half of the phones
were handed out to freshmen, whereas the other half were handed out to undergrad-
uate students. A year prior Sune Lehmann, professor at DTU Compute, had been
granted 200 smartphones which provided an exploitative basis to sort our potential
technical and unforeseen difficulties before receiving the remaining smartphones.
Every participant receiving a smartphone had to fill out an online questionnaire
made by people at the University of Copenhagen from the departments of anthro-
pology, health, psychology, and economy.
In this questionnaire most participants
also agreed to let information from different apps, such as Facebook, to be recorded.
The purpose was to capture several factors about the individual which could prove
important for their behavior like the Big Five Inventory (BFI) [31]; Openness, Con-
scientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (OCEAN) and many more.
The Sensible DTU dataset includes information about calls, texts, GPS coordi-
nates, Bluetooth signals, and screen activity. Every instance of captured data includes
a timestamp as temporal interactions is an important factor for the experiment. Since
it is personalized data it is not public and authorization is needed to work with the
data set.
This thesis will focus on pre-processed data from the raw GPS data from individual
users. The raw GPS data includes a latitude and longitude coordinate for each time
step which can be converted into a series of locations when the user moves from one
stationary location to another.
2.1
GPS to stop location
The conversion from GPS data to locations was done by Cuttone et al., where they
tried to eliminate the problem of self-transitions, and only include data points when
an individual changed location [32]. Cuttone et al. defined a stop as a location where
the following location were not further away than some threshold, σ, in a given time
interval from time t to t + ∆t. The threshold was chosen to be σ = 50m since that
approximated the size of a building and if the location at time t+∆t were not within
the threshold, a new stop was then detected.

The centroid of each stop sequence was then set to the median of the locations’ lat-
itude and longitude readings. Furthermore to remove uninteresting stops, i.e. waiting
for the buss etc., they decided to only consider stops longer than 15 minutes. Lastly
they used the clustering algorithm DBSCAN, [33], to cluster the stops together with
the distance parameter ϵ = 50m and minimum amount of points min_pts = 2. This
length scale was used as an approximation for the size of a building.
2.2
Stop locations
Every user now has a sequence of stop locations with a corresponding sequence of
timestamps for arrival and departure of the given location. A random piece of user
data could be:
stop_loc = [1, 2, 3, 2, 5, 1, 2, 3, 1, 2, ...] ,
time = [(1572296587, 1572296598), (1572299587, 1572299887), ...] .
The time is shown in unix time which is the amount of second since the 1st of January,
1970 UTC time. Since the data only shows changes in locations the sequence will
never have sequentially repeated locations.
A hierarchical stop location sequence is also available where every location is
within some bigger cluster containing other locations. The amount of ”higher” cluster
differs from user to user, but is between 2 and 5. In other words; if we had an user
with 3 hierarchies of clusters, a sequence could be; [[1,3,5],[1,3,2],...] where the first
location could represent [Denmark, Copenhagen, Tivoli]. Each id is unique over the
higher orders of the hierarchical representation. This means that if Tivoli is id 5,
there will be no other id 5 even outside Copenhagen or Denmark. This conversion
was done by Alessandretti et al, [34], but the paper is unpublished as of the writing
of this thesis.

CHAPTER 3
Theory
This chapter will first introduce Shannon entropy which in short is a measure of order
in data. It has been proven that given sequential data this measure can be converted
into an upper bound of predictability of next state predictions, which is useful knowl-
edge when trying to do actual predictions [8]. Afterwards a throughout explanation is
made of the steps going from vanilla feed forward neural network, to recurrent neural
networks (RNN), and up til recent long short term memory (LSTM) neural networks.
Some extra notes on the optimization of the training is also mentioned.
3.1
Shannon entropy
This section introduces the definition and motivation of Shannon entropy, together
with an estimation algorithm. This is particular useful since it is proven that the
Shannon entropy can be converted into a theoretical upper bound of predictability
[8], which we can use to get an idea of the performance of our predictions. It is also
useful to see when/if the entropy of the individual sequences has converged, so that
we can remove the ones that are not converged from our data set, to make sure the
sequences contains enough information.
Entropy is a term used in many fields with different definitions, but represents a
common concept of how disordered a system is or how the energy is distributed [35].
The higher the entropy the more disordered the system and vice versa. The concept
of entropy was first discovered when investigating the loss of energy when studying
engines [36]. The concept of entropy in information theory was introduced by Claude
Shannon in 1948, and is the theory to be used in this project [37]. From now on we
will refer to Shannon entropy as just entropy.
Today information is stored in computers using bits which is either zeros or ones,
and since space is not infinite, the minimal way to represent data is important. The
entropy of a system may intuitively be understood as the average amount of bits
that needs to be shared to describe the system state. An equivalent formulation is to
view entropy as the average number of yes/no questions that needs to be answered
to obtain the system state.

3.2
Neural Networks
An estimation of the entropy has been introduced with a corresponding theoretical
upper bound of predictability. However, we are interested in doing actual predictions,
specifically using neural networks. This section will introduce the fundamentals of
neural networks. This includes how data affects the architecture and choices of the
specific network, how they are trained and tricks to avoid different ”traps”.
We
will specifically go from vanilla neural networks where the network does not care
about temporal structure in the data, to recurrent neural networks which takes time
dependencies into account and then specifically long short term memory models which
is an extension of the recurrent neural networks.
Deep learning in general has been growing immensely the last few decades due
to the advancement in computational power of machines and the expanding size of
data sets. This growth is especially due to GPU/TPU’s which are very efficient at
performing tensor operations. More data effectively means that models needs less
specificity since the data explores the space itself which allows models to have less
assumptions and just rely on the input data. More data however makes bigger models
possible which requires more computing power.
3.2.1
Feed Forward Neural Network
A neural network (NN) is often motivated by how a brain functions with neurons and
edges which carries the information through the system. The simplest NN is a feed
forward NN where information is only carried one way as in Figure 3.1. This network
assumes four input attributes and predicts two output attributes, e.g. given height,
weight, age and gender it predicts body fat percentage and muscle mass. From left
to right we have:
1. The input layer which is the data.

3.2.4.1
Optimizers
Doing backpropagation can be tough due to the immense amount of computing. Imag-
ine 1.000.000 data points running through a network with 10.000 weights. That means
that the network would have to compute 10.000.000.000 derivatives to take one step.
This is not feasible, and therefor optimizers have been developed with the purpose to
speed up the process. The most known optimizer is the stochastical gradient descent
(SGD), which basically only computes the derivatives for one data point and then
takes a step. SGD is often combined with batches which are subsets of the data with
a size defined by the user. The data is split into batches and then parsed one batch at
a time. The network will here compute the derivative for all the points in that batch
and then take a step based on the average gradient. The hope is that a subset of the
data is representative for all of the data, and that the model learns faster by taking
more steps with subsets of the data. Approximating the gradients with batches also
has the extra benefit that models can escape local minimas. Running through all
batches in a data set once is called an epoch.
One of the most used optimizers at the time of writing is the ADAM optimizer
[44]. ADAM combines RMSprob (explained in a moment) and SGD with momentum.
Momentum is used when dealing with a complex problem, since the gradient might
change a lot locally, which makes the steps feel random even though there is an overall
structure. Imagining a mountain side. It is clear which way is down, but with all
the small rocks on the way the gradient locally changes back and fourth. Therefor
momentum is introduced which basically keeps the momentum from previous steps
to ensure a smoother descent. Same goes if a flat surface is encountered. Here the
momentum should help coming out, instead of getting stuck and taking very small
steps. The math is

3.2.4.2
Embeddings
It is not possible for networks to work directly on non-numerical data. A computer
only understands numbers. It is therefor necessary to convert data which contains
words or classes into a numerical domain and this is where embeddings comes into
play. An embedding of a variable is a representation in the numerical domain. E.g.
a network does not understand the words ”yes”, ”no” and ”maybe”, but if projected
onto a line with ”yes” being 1, ”maybe” being 0 and ”no” being -1 it could be processed.
Often a one dimensional space is not enough and the network needs to find its own
meaning. Therefor, when using data like words, an embedding space is defined with
some fixed dimension, n, in which the available words is distributed. The embedding
of the data is then trained just like the rest of the network.

The initialization of the network weights can have a huge impact on how well the
network trains. One will typically initialize weights using some random distribution.
Initializing with very large values can be problematic since it can result in slow learn-

y = 1 for z >> 0. This means that when computing the derivatives of a(z) = a(xw+b)

To get an idea of a lower bound of predictability and see how other models perform we
fitted some simple models to the data. This was done after the data was preprocessed
by the removal of potential unconverged or short sequences. We selected six prediction
baseline models and evaluated how they performed:
1. Most common. The simplest model which always guesses on the most occur-
ring location.
2. Most common but not self. A model which guesses on the most occurring
location. If current location is the most occurring location, it guesses on the
second most occurring.
3. Markov order 1. A Markov model of order 1 guesses on the most occurring
transition away from the current location.
4. Markov order 1 with twist. A Markov model of order 1 with a twist, which
given a location guesses on the location from which the user switches to the most
but only if this location is visited more than n times. If it is not then guess on
the in general most common. This is due to the high amount of explorations in
which jumps could be random.
5. Most common given time slot. A model which given the hourly time slot
guesses on the most common location.
6. Markov order 1 given time slot. A model which combines the Markov order
1 and hourly time slot.

CHAPTER 6
The Network
The initial goal was to make a neural network which sole purpose was to make the best
predictions on next locations of human mobility. Here we had the choices of going with
the Long Short Term Memory (LSTM) network or a Gated Recurrent Unit network
(GRU), which are both extensions of the vanilla recurrent neural networks (RNN).
Both networks are quite similar with the GRU network being a bit more simple in
construction, which makes it more computationally efficient [42]. This makes it great
for processing huge loads of data with nearly the same performance as a LSTM but
since we did not have an overload of data we went with the more complex LSTM.
The implementation was done in python version 3.7.3 using PyTorch version 1.2.0
as the main tool.
6.1

relation to each other. To deal with this problem we embedded the data and trained
the embedding. Not all of the inputs needed to be embedded since they did have
relative meaning, like time of the day. As introduced in chapter 2 the data consists of
the location sequences, and time of arrival and departure. We suspected there were
information in both the sequences and the departure times, so we decided to split the
departure time into several inputs:

Ignoring batches for a second, we needed to define how to pass the data into the
network. Here we drew inspiration from natural language processing models with text,
and took all the sequences and extended them with a ”stop token” in between them.
This stop token is a token that is only shown whenever there is a switch in sequence.
The purpose for this token is to tell the network that a new sequence is coming, and
the hidden state of the previous sequence should ”reset”. We selected different stop
tokens depending on the sequence attribute. I.e. for locations we choose the number
925 since it was higher than any other location id, 859 for person id, etc. Basically it is
a token that is only shown between sequences, e.g. [x(1,1)..., x(n,1), ⟨token⟩, x(1,2), ...],
where x(n,k) is the nth value in sequence k. When using batches we took this long
sequence and just reshaped it into the desired size and cut off the remaining data
that did not fit the reshaping.

rate by itself and uses a combination of several methods which has been seen to
generally work better. Since it is not possible to shuffle our data set, due to temporal
information, we instead generated a random number, i, between zero and the sequence
length, i ∈[0, seq_length], which decides the initial index of the batches. E.g. lets

b1 = [xi, ..xi+20], b2 = [xi+21, ..., xi+40]. This way we ensured some randomness in
the training. However when we wanted to predict on a test set we needed to ensure
that the hidden state was warmed up. So there we needed to fix the index i such that
the last batch directly matched the following test data. E.g. lets say our data set is
100 points, and the test set is 10% of the data. This leaves us with a training set
of 90 points and 10 test points. If we have a sequence length of 7, and we want the
batches from the training set to align with the test set, we need the last point of the
last batch to be point 90. By selecting i = 90 mod 7 = 6 we achieve exactly this.
Lastly we needed to optimize the hyper parameters for the network, specifically:
1. Amount of LSTM layers.
2. Sequence length.
3. Hidden dimension.
4. Embedding dimension.

to see if anything stood out. However to find structure in a 13 dimensional space,
we needed to do some dimensionality reduction. Several methods is available and
already implemented in python like PCA, TSNE and UMAP. PCA however is a simple
transformation of the basis, where TSNE and UMAP are nonlinear dimensionality
reduction techniques.
The purpose of those algorithms is to find clusters in high
dimensions and then do a nonlinear projection down into two or three dimensions.
UMAP is the newer algorithm which tried to solve some problems with TSNE, and
we choose to go with that.
Since we wanted to compare the locations across users, we took the embeddings
of the type data and ran it through the UMAP transformation. We then colored each
location according to modulo 20, i.e. location 46 mod 20 = 6. We ended up with
the result shown in Figure 6.4. Unfortunately we did not see any useful patterns, and
neither when looking at the individual locations, see Figure 6.5.
We had especially hoped to see some structure around the first three locations,
but it seems like that the network have learned to predict through the LSTM layers,
and not the actual embedding. We tried to combat this problem by removing the
ranking data from the target embedding, however this proved a greater challenge for
the network since it had to learn which twenty range to guess in. The hope was that
this would force the type embedding to be trained and hopefully capture more of a
structure. After training, the network got a prediction rate of 31.7%, which was lower
than before (33.6%), but still a plausible attempt. Looking at the embedding however
we saw it had captured a bit more structure, compare Figure 6.6 with Figure 6.4.
Again we plotted each type location separately shown in Figure 6.7. Clearly there are
some kind of structure, but specifically what the structure is, is unclear. Only location
1 seemed to be more determined, where the rest of the locations switched from the
left to the right cluster. A guess for this behavior could be that the network learns
not to guess on itself by splitting even and odd numbers up. However just like the
previous network no geographical information seem to be present in the embedding.
Most of the actual learning seems to be stored in the LSTM layers.

6.2.1
Prediction vs entropy
Looking back at the entropy it would be interesting to know if the prediction rates had
any correlation with the upper bound of predictability that was computed from the
estimated entropy. Using the prediction rates on the validation data, we plotted the
individual prediction rates vs the corresponding estimated upper bound of prediction,
see Figure 6.9. A positive correlation is clearly present, with a Pearson correlation
coefficient of 0.47.
The outliers are likely due to the last 10% of trajectories not
always being representative of the whole trajectory, for example if the user was on
a vacation, etc. This was also the result we were hoping for since it shows that the
network follows the complexity of the data, i.e. the lower the entropy of the data, the
more structure should be present.
Figure 6.9: A scatter plot of the correlation between the estimated upper bounds

how they are positively correlated just as desired. This shows that our network uses

previous 90% due to the high amount of explorations etc. So if a user is on vacation

6.2.2
Exploration prediction
As mentioned several times one of the great challenges of predicting human mobility
is the amount of explorations to locations never seen previously.
From the data
exploration, chapter 5, we know that 21.6% of all locations are explorations which
effectively means that we cannot predict those 21.6% of locations. This does not
exclude us from trying to predict if someone is doing an exploration; not where they
are going, but just that they are in for an adventure. To do this we use the existing
network and expand on it.
We simply add another decoding linear layer at the
end which takes the output from the LSTM layers and transforms it down into a 2
dimensional output to tell us if it is an exploration or not. When computing the loss
we use cross entropy on the output and then does a weighted sum of the location
prediction loss. This way we try to balance the networks learning so that not one
prediction counts more than the other.
Training the network we quickly reached a prediction rate of ∼79%, which is
basically just that the network always guesses on ”not exploration”. After that the
prediction rate slowly increased to ∼81%, but we quickly decided that predicting
actual explorations were not that feasible. Instead looking at the probability of an
exploration made more sense. In reality what the network does is exactly outputting
two probabilities, one for an exploration and one for not an exploration. The network
is then trying to minimize the cross entropy ”error” from these probabilities, so looking
at the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) and computing the area under the
curve (AUC) made more sense.
In Figure 6.10 we plotted the ROC curve with
the corresponding AUC= 0.71. Depending on the rate of false positives, that we are
willing to accept, we can select a cutoff for our model. We will not select a cutoff since
it is an problem specific value. Now however the network will spit out a probability
of the next location being an exploration, together with the location that the network
predicts, given it is not an exploration.
6.2.3
Transfer learning
To see if we could increase the performance of the network, we tried to do some extra
training on each individual user, in which we knew from early testing very quickly
resulted in overfitting. However letting the network just get the feel of the individual
might improve the performance. To test this theory we ran every user through our
pre-trained network and saved the performance on their test set every second epoch
for 12 epochs in total. The pre-trained network had an average accuracy of 33.6%
which was increased with extra training as shown in Figure 6.11. Here it can be seen
that training the network after 6-8 epochs seemed to slightly boost the performance.
It went up from 33.6%, see Figure 6.3, to 34.2% after 6 epochs. This was tested
several times with different initial networks and the result was the same. Here we
just accumulated all the predictions for each epoch, but better and more flexible
measures of when the training began overfitting could be explored further.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis we have explored the next location predictability of the Sensible DTU
data set. Specifically the next location sequences constructed from continuous GPS
data by Cuttone et al [32]. To get the best quality of the data, we introduced Shannon
entropy and how it could be converted into an upper bound of predictability for time
series. The entropy was then estimated for every user to see which of the 851 user
sequences that had converged. A convergence criteria together with only looking at
location sequences longer than 1000 locations left us with 507 users which together
gave us an upper bound of predictability of 65.9%.
We then split our data into a training, validation and test set, and fitted 6 baselines
on the training set and tested them on the validation set. This gave us a lower bound
of predictability of 26.7% which came from a Markov model order 1 with a small
twist.
Our recurrent neural network model, based on long short term memory (LSTM)
models, was then introduced with all the choices and details of construction. The
first iteration of the model achieved an average prediction rate of 28.0%, which we
boosted to 34.0% after introducing a method of combining generalized and individual
user information with embeddings. Using transfer learning to continue training on
each individual (which quickly overfitted), for just a few epochs, we boosted the
average prediction rate up to 34.6%.
Since the data consists of categorical variables we had to embed them in a higher,
13, dimensional space for the network to comprehend. The network also trained the
embedding, so we hoped that the network would find some geographical or location
based structure (home, work, etc.). This however was not achieved. This could be due
to the network learning these connections in the LSTM layers, or that it is something
totally different learned. Further research is encouraged.
Lastly due to the high amount of explorations, 21.6% of the locations, we tried to
let the network predict if the next location was an exploration. Due to the skewed
distribution of explorations vs non-explorations the network mostly learned to never
predict exploration. We instead looked at AUROC which had an AUC of 0.71. So
depending on the application a trade-off could be chosen between the false positive
rate and true positive rate.
In total we achieved to create a LSTM neural network with a prediction rate
way above the baselines presented. We also achieved a higher prediction rate than
previous presented models, but due to the dependence of the data, see Table 1.1,

data set. The sensible DTU data mostly consists of students which might have more

Encouraged further work includes comparing our model against other models on
the same data, doing an ablation analysis (removing features of the model to see what
contributes the most), include geographical/relative information about distances, and
look into a better measure of when transfer learning starts overfitting.
