Abstract
The building sector accounts for 35% of all energy-related emissions in the EU, underscor-
ing the need for strategic initiatives to mitigate the emissions. One of the most significant
potentials for energy savings lies in renovating existing buildings or managing their energy
more efficiently, a recognition now reinforced in the recently strengthened Energy Perfor-
mance of Buildings Directive, EPBD. In response to the imperatives, there is a recognition of
the need to characterize the energy performance of the existing building stock. The clas-
sical energy signature methods offer a well-tested, interpretable way to extract intrinsic
energy performance indicators of the building at scale; however, they rely on assumptions
that do not always match with reality. This project builds on the classical approaches
in a Bayesian setting to explicitly include the uncertainties. It contributes to the energy
signature literature by proposing a new method to separate the heating seasons using a
Gaussian Mixture Model. This allows for a more interpretable, scaleable, and uncertainty
quantifying model needed in this context, where the physical properties of houses are to
be extracted from data with many unknowns. Compared to a baseline model often used
in the energy signature method literature, it improves the CRPS by 40% when the method
is combined with a dynamical ARX model. Without the dynamical ARX extension, the
improvement from the baseline model is 24% when evaluated on 4312 houses in Fredericia,
Denmark, using daily average heat loads, outdoor temperature, wind, and solar irradiation.
The ELPDloo is also drastically improved, which signifies enhanced calibration of the pro-
posed model to the data, a critical factor for robust uncertainty quantification. The study
highlights the optimistic bias in simple, non-calibrated models when estimating heat-loss
coefficient uncertainty. These simple models exhibit credibility intervals more than twice
as narrow as their well-calibrated counterparts, emphasizing the need for advanced model
structures to enhance calibration. Formulating the models within a Bayesian framework
facilitated a comprehensive analysis, as it seamlessly integrates uncertainty quantification
throughout the modeling process. This proves effective in identifying both structural issues
and deficiencies in the uncertainty quantification. The research also highlights a divergence
in energy efficiency within the sample: residences holding the best Energy Performance
Certificates, EPC, underperform surprisingly in reality, whereas those with worst EPC rat-
ings consume significantly less heat than their certificates suggest.

Introduction
The building sector accounts for 35% of all energy-related emissions in the European Union
[1]. On a global scale, the operations of buildings accounted for 30% of final energy con-
sumption [2].
Acknowledging the urgent imperative for action, governments and insti-
tutions are actively scrambling to implement initiatives and plans to reduce energy con-
sumption in the sector. While there has been notable progress in the energy performance
of newly constructed buildings, the bulk of the building stock in countries like Denmark
was established under less stringent energy performance regulations. Moreover, estimates
project that 85% of the existing building stock will still be occupied by 2050, highlighting
the importance of enhancing energy efficiency in the existing buildings [3]. Indeed, the
International Energy Agency has deemed energy efficiency the largest single measure to
avoid energy demand in the net zero by 2050 scenario, providing one of the fastest and
most cost-effective ways of mitigating CO2 emissions [2]. Aligning with that, the energy
performance of buildings directive, EPBD [4], was recently strengthened in an effort to boost
the energy performance of the buildings in the EU structurally. To meet the requirements
of these directives, it is imperative to characterize the energy performance of the building
stock at scale, and thus, there is a growing emphasis on data-driven methods to identify,
assess, and make informed decisions about building development initiatives. Though there
is an increased availability of data foundation facilitated by smart meters and sensor net-
works, challenges arise in extracting relevant information for decision-making [5]. These
smart meters can record all energy usage related to operations of all aspects of build-
ings: heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems. However, this study focuses only
on space heating and hot-water consumption, which also accounts for 25% of the energy
consumption in the geographical scope of this study; Denmark [3]. The models must be
interpretable to ensure that the physical properties of the houses can be extracted from
model parameters, can quantify the uncertainty because there are so many unknowns
in the data, and are scaleable, because the models are tested on the daily average heat
consumption of 5697 houses in the city of Frederica, Denmark. The main focus is to build
a comprehensive, principled methodology from raw data to the extraction of parameters
in a Bayesian framework, allowing for seamless integration uncertainties.
Energy performance can also be characterized by energy performance certificates, EPCs,
and thus, an additional focus in the study is to compare the energy performance with
the EPCs. Around 43.51% of the houses have an EPC issued by an energy consultant
that physically inspects the building and calculates a theoretical heat consumption. How-
ever, the industry recognizes a significant gap between actual and calculated consumption,
termed the energy performance gap. In 2018, the Danish Energy Agency found 23% of the
EPCs issued were wrong, with a major issue being that the consultant used data from the
BBR - the nationwide central register on property conditions and characteristics known for
not being up to date [6]. Consequently, this study examines the disparity between EPCs
issued by energy consultants and those derived from actual consumption data.

1.1
literature Review
Contributing to the energy efficiency in the built environment can take many forms. A
line of research focuses solely on forecasting energy use with applications in energy man-
agement and optimized predictive control [5]. The focus is to find the model with the best
predictive performance; thus, advanced statistical and black-box machine learning meth-
ods are often seen in the literature [7]. Artificial Neural Networks have successfully been
deployed on large databases of residential buildings over multiple years [8][9] with high
predictive performance and interesting new innovation each year [10][11] the latter using
a transformer network. Gaussian Process models, GPs, are often used when there is also
an element of control [12][13] or simply in prediction of loads [14]. Though some of the
GP models have a degree of interpretability, the commonality of the models with the best
performance in this field is that they are black box models, meaning that there is a lack
of interpretability [7]. Therefore, these models have no value in the second line of research
that focuses on characterizing the intrinsic energy performance of buildings. Here, the
objective is to formulate a model with a set of parameters with an interpretable physical
value. The parameter values are in the center; thus, it is of paramount importance that
the model is adequately calibrated to the observed data. The models should capture the
statistical characteristics of the data while ensuring that the parameters capture the in-
tended physical effect. The classical energy signature methods introduced decades ago in
papers like [15] are interpretable, simply, and would fall into the class of white-box models.
However, these white-box models rely on assumptions that might not match reality, and
the simple model structures cannot capture central statistical characteristics of the data
[16][17].
Capturing central statistical characteristics while maintaining a parsimonious
structure is a daunting challenge, complicated further by the many unobserved variables
that influence the heat demand. In general, the influential variables are partitioned into
occupancy and weather variables. Occupancy greatly affects the consumption profile given
different set-temperature preferences, occupancy presence, and opening and closing doors
and windows. Describing occupancy is an ongoing research field that is covered in great
detail in [18] with ideas like using Markov-Switching models and the CO2 levels of the rooms
to predict occupancy [19]. However, indoor temperatures and CO2 measurements are not
always available, and thus, the focus shifts to formulating models that adequately quan-
tify the uncertainty for the interpretable models. Greybox models explicitly include
uncertainty, errors, and unobservable variables to improve statistical inference about the
parameters. Models based on stochastic differential equations, SDEs, is a popular choice
[20] often based on resistor-capacitor models [21] which could also explain why GPs popular
in the field given the connection between GPs and SDEs [13]. However, when models are to
be deployed at scale, the principals of parsimony apply again, as capturing all statistical
characteristics of each individual building is computationally expensive, and some grey-
box models could be hindered by the need for human interaction in the model selection
and validation [16]. Therefore, practitioners still sometimes resort to simple yearly quan-
tities [22] when working at scale or methods like the classical energy signature method
[23]. Though these models can describe the level of accuracy and uncertainty needed to
fit industry criteria defined in the ASHRAE guideline 14, the models could be so inad-
equately calibrated that the extracted physical parameters would not represent the true
state of the building [5]. However, it has been shown that turning these white-box models
into better-calibrated grey-box models as in [24][16] is highly effective when deployed at
scale; thus, worth developing further. Fundamentally, the energy signature models assume
the house can be in two regimes; a weather dependent regime, WD, where the house needs

an innovative transition mechanism, and using ARX models within each regime.

Contributions
1. This study contributes to the field of energy signature models by proposing a new
transition mechanism between the WID and WD regimes, which increases the inter-
pretability of the model and the efficiency of sampling when formulated in a Bayesian
framework.
2. The study takes the classical energy signature models and formulates them in a
Bayesian framework to demonstrate their strengths and weaknesses.
3. The models are applied on 5697 houses in Fredericia to test their abilities when
deployed at scale. This includes a full pipeline from cleaning to posterior insights.
The model-building process involved setting up the necessary infrastructure to enable
model evaluation on the high-performance cluster at the Technical University of
Denmark, as one model takes around 1.5 minutes per house on an M2 2020 Macbook
Air with 8GB of RAM thus evaluating one model takes 5.5 days when tested on the
full sample.

Bayesian Data Analysis
Bayesian Data Analysis is a powerful approach in statistics that leverages Bayesian prin-
ciples for drawing conclusions from observed data. At its core, Bayesian analysis involves
updating prior beliefs or knowledge with new evidence to obtain a posterior distribution,
representing the updated beliefs in light of the observed data. The Bayesian approach
enables a way to seamlessly integrate the known physical details of a specific system into
the modeling process while adeptly handling uncertainties and making probabilistic infer-
ences. In the context of energy performance, a Bayesian model based on domain knowledge
would yield parameters directly linked to the intrinsic energy performance of the houses
and provide the uncertainty of the parameters, which boosts the confidence that the model
can be seen as a representation of the underlying physical process. The first part of this
chapter will start with section 2.1 and highlight some central concepts of Bayesian mod-
elling, which assumes some level of prior knowledge of the general idea introduced in more
comprehensive works like [27][28][29].
The fundamental difference between frequentist
and Bayesian statistics lies in their approach to statistical inference. Frequentist meth-
ods maximize the likelihood function, treating model parameters as fixed but unknown,
while Bayesian inference introduces a prior probability distribution for model parameters,
combining prior knowledge with observed data. In section 2.2, the foundational difference
between the approaches is described with a focus on understanding why it could be useful
to use the Bayesian approach over a frequentist approach in some applications. Rigorous
model checking, selection, and validation are crucial to ensure the accuracy and reliability
of the model; thus, section 2.4 described the process in a Bayesian setting. The models
considered will primarily be regression analysis; thus, section 2.5 focuses on understanding
regression in a Bayesian setting.

Evaluation of statistical procedures often involves quantifying the uncertainty of param-

approach, the definition of the related concepts of the frequentist confidence interval and
the the Bayesian credible interval will be examined. The deviations are adopted from [31]1.
Definition 2.3.1. Given a posterior distribution p(Œ∏ | x), a 100(1 ‚àíŒ±)% credible interval
for Œ∏ is an interval C(x) satisfying p(Œ∏ ‚ààC(x) | x) = 1 ‚àíŒ±.
Definition 2.3.2. A 100(1 ‚àíŒ±)% confidence interval for Œ∏ is a random interval C(x) if
for every Œ∏ ‚ààŒò, Pr(Œ∏ ‚ààC(x)) = 1‚àíŒ± where the probability is taken w.r.t. the distribution
of the data x.
Fundamentally, the Bayesian approach expresses the inherent uncertainty in the value of
the parameter estimates for the current sample, whereas frequentists represent uncertainty
in terms of the interval itself. The interpretation of the credibility intervals is straightfor-
ward; the probability that Œ∏ lies in C(x) given the observed data DN is a least (1‚àíŒ±)¬∑100%.
The Bayesian credible intervals are formulated after observing the data; based on the data,
there is a (1 ‚àíŒ±) ¬∑ 100% probability that the true Œ∏ is in the interval. In contrast, the fre-
quentist confidence interval is quite different. Recall that the frequentist approach assumes
that Œ∏ is unknown but not random, while the specific data sample, DN, is a random sample
of many possible data samples. Therefore, the interpretation of the confidence interval is;
if DN is sampled infinitely many times, the (1 ‚àíŒ±) ¬∑ 100%-confidence interval will contain
Œ∏ around (1‚àíŒ±)¬∑100% of the time. Conceptually, probability is involved before any data
is collected because the statement is related to the (1 ‚àíŒ±) ¬∑ 100% probability of collecting
data that produces an interval in which the true parameter value is contained. Bayesian
statisticians argue that the credible interval is more appealing as it is more straightfor-
ward to interpret. However, in the end, these intervals are just different ways of expressing
uncertainty.

Energy Performance Estimation
The primary objective is to extract intrinsic performance indicators of the buildings in
the sample using the average daily heat loads and meteorological variables. A secondary
objective is to compare the EPCs for the building using the actual consumption and the
EPCs issued by an energy consultant. Models that exhibit high interpretability, scalability,
and adequate uncertainty quantification are prioritized over models lacking these attributes
but having superior predictive abilities. Therefore, the following guiding principles were
formulated to form a coherent framework for the entire model-building process:
‚Ä¢ interpretable: stakeholders would want to understand why a house has a certain
energy performance. Suppose a house would need to be renovated based on recom-
mendations from the model. Then, a clear explanation linked to the characteristics
of the house and how it reacts to changes in weather conditions is needed. Within a
Bayesian regression framework in this context, it would be highly advantageous that
the posterior parameter distribution be uni-modal. If the model has two distinct
yet equally likely parameter settings, then that ambiguity hinders physical inter-
pretability as a house cannot simultaneously have two heat loss coefficients. Another
objective is to achieve a parsimonious model, utilizing only the essential predictors
for the desired level of explanation. If a predictor marginally benefits a few houses
but not the majority, it should not be included.
‚Ä¢ scalable: the model needs to be structurally adequate to be deployed across all
houses without altering the model structure or priors. Therefore, while more com-
plex models may capture additional statistical characteristics for a few houses, their
prolonged evaluation time renders them less suitable for widespread deployment. A
balance is needed to ensure both comprehensive modeling and efficient scalability.
‚Ä¢ uncertainty quantification: uncertainty quantification is instrumental in grasping
the validity of the insights from the model. There are many unknowns in the data, like
the number of occupants, their behavior, and the exact number of heated rooms; thus,
an adequate understanding of uncertainty throughout each model step is essential.
Adequate uncertainty quantification is also relevant for the EPCs comparison to grasp
if a house is a clear F label or is just as likely to be an E.
5.1
Energy Signature Models
Energy signature models represent a category of models designed to explain the heat con-
sumption of a building through parsimonious relationships with various weather variables.
These models are parsimonious and interpretable and facilitate extraction of valuable phys-
ical characteristics of a building, such as the heat loss coefficient, HLC. A distinctive charac-
teristic of these models is their transition between two regimes: a weather-dependent WD and

Results
In the upcoming section, the results of the models discussed in chapter 5 will be presented.
It includes a comprehensive selection process aimed at highlighting situations where spe-
cific models struggle to capture the statistical characteristics of the data. A significant part
of this process involves visually demonstrating when models exhibit limitations, providing
insights into why the struggle. It is crucial to underscore that the chosen models should
adhere to the principles introduced in section 5.1, emphasizing interpretability, scalability,
and the ability to quantify uncertainty. Given the impracticality of visually demonstrating
residuals and model limitations for each house, attention will be centered on the repre-
sentative example introduced in fig. 4.2. Following this, model insights will be showcased
across all houses for the selected model and the EPCs found using the actual consumption.
6.0.1
Technical Specifications
RStan [49] is used as the interface through R to access Stan. Three chains are used for
each house with 1000 samples warm-up and 1000 sampled parameter estimates; thus, 3000
posterior parameter samples for each house are obtained. It should be mentioned that a
criteria for a feasible model is that it is scaleable and can run on all models in a reasonable
amount of time. Specifically, the practical limit was set to abound 1 minute per house,
meaning that running one model would take a bit less than four days on a single computer,
in this case, an M2 2020 MacBook Air with 8GB of RAM. To combat this computational
challenge, the DTU high-performance computing cluster service was employed. This enabled
the concurrent execution of multiple models, although it necessitated navigating some
overhead for efficient implementation. Posterior parameter and posterior predictive insights
required careful handling of substantial data quantities and postprocessing.
6.0.2
Preprocessesing
Given that this project involved the analysis of 5697 houses with hourly heating loads and
BBR data, it was crucial to implement a thorough and systematic preprocessing approach,
mainly due to the inherent complexities due to measurement errors, missing values, outliers,
and inconsistencies associated with consumption data in this research domain [22]. The
following section offers a concise overview of the preprocessing methodology, with a more
detailed account available in appendix F.1. To prepare the heating load data, the hourly
observations were aggregated to daily averages, normalized by the living area of each
house, and scaled by 100 to address the proximity to 0, which sometimes hindered certain
models. It should be mentioned that all figures in the results section have been scaled
back; however, the evaluated metrics are still from the scaled domain. With the scaled
heat loads, many houses were excluded based on criteria such as more than 60 missing
days or many days with recorded heating loads of 0kWh/m2. A simple Kalman filter was
employed for each house to filter out days with unusually high consumption. Preprocessing

was essential for the meteorological data, given the considerable number of missing values
from one station. Therefore, the daily averages were found by pooling the values across

6.0.3
Postprocessing of the Found Parameters
The postprocessing focuses on the ARX-EWS model; however, it closely resembles the process
for related models. For this model, a subset of houses was removed directly when running
the model because the distribution of the heat consumption was far from most houses.
Additionally, houses with parameters that had a potential scale reduction of ÀÜR > 1.1 were
removed, adhering to the standard outlined in [50]; this led to the removal of 46 houses
based on this criterion. Given that many dwellings displayed parameter distributions signif-
icantly different from the majority, developing a screening methodology became important.
Going through the distribution for each house would be a daunting task. Thus, an outlier
detection module was developed based on the Mahalanobis distance. The technique, de-
scribed in appendix F.2, is scale-invariant and considers correlations of parameters useful
in this context, and removed 210 houses. As models can only be compared when they
run adequately, the comparison is limited to houses that performed well across all models.
This resulted in a dataset of 4312 houses, forming the basis for calculating the quantities
presented in the subsequent figures and tables.

6.1.2
The LSE Energy Signature Model
When the LSE model was implemented in a Bayesian framework, it immediately exhibited
sampling issues. Upon analyzing the posterior parameters, it became apparent that the
problem could be attributed to discontinuous degeneracy [47], as explained below.
In
the introduction of the LSE model in section 5.1.3, concerns were raised about potential
sampling problems due to the stepwise function in the dispersion parameter. When running
the model on houses in the sample, error warnings would appear, including divergent
iterations and large potential scale reduction, ÀÜR. The issue with that can be seen in the
posterior samples generated by the sampler. In fig. 6.3a the sequence of sampled UA0s can
be seen, with the red lines separating the three MCMC chains. There is a clear indication of
poor mixing between the chains, meaning that the ÀÜR is large. Within the first chains, there
is a strong autocorrelation, leading to a low effective sample size, Neff. The last chain seems
to have found a possible set of parameters where it can sample the heat loss coefficient more
efficiently. It hints that one could, on a house-to-house basis, regularize the prior heavily
or constrain the parameter space to make it work. However, it would involve an extensive
manual process that violates the scalability principle introduced in section 5.1. Similarly,
considering a longer warm-up period would increase evaluation time, violating scalability,
and even with the iterations doubled, the model still had convergence issues. The issue
is further evident in the joint distribution of parameter pairs of (UA0, UAw)(m) seen in
fig. 6.3b.
The distribution is multimodal with multiple high-density areas, surrounded
by regions with no or a minimal likelihood of possible parameter configurations - a clear
indication of issues caused by discontinuous degeneracies [47], likely introduced by rounding
the dispersion parameter in 5.1.15. To better understand that issue, consider only the
UA0 in fig. F.2, which is bimodal with a few high-density areas - quite different from the
baseline model with only one mode, fig. 6.1b. Some houses that closely resembled the
type of houses initially considered in [16] had better convergence; however, still higher
than ÀÜR < 1.1.
Moreover, the houses would still have multimodal posterior parameter
distributions, violating the interpretability principle. Therefore, the LSE model will not
be considered in further model selection procedures.

Discussion
7.1
Data
7.1.1
Limited Data Scope
The available data was limited to one year, raising the possibility that the inference and
parameters derived may be specific to that particular year. The use of standard weather
conditions by energy consultants when issuing EPCs could contribute to the observed dif-
ferences between EPCs based on actual consumption and energy consultant-issued EPCs.
Although 2021 presented a notably average weather year according to the Danish Meteo-
rological Institute [56], the model‚Äôs sensitivity to multiple years should be investigated for
a more nuanced understanding. Despite the data being from 2021, it did not immediately
reveal any impact of the energy crisis starting in late 2021. However, considering the surge
in energy prices, stringent temperature policies, and media attention, deploying the model
on 2022 data might significantly alter the conclusions. To comprehensively account for
the effects of the energy crisis, integrating policy dummies, monitoring energy prices, and
considering attention indicators like Google searches for energy crisis and energy prices
emerges as an interesting aspect for further research.
7.1.2
Occupancy
Incorporating occupancy information into the model could have enhanced insights and re-
duced uncertainty. Variables such as the number of occupants, their ages, economic factors,
weekly schedules, and the actual heated area of the house would have provided valuable
context. Potential ideas like using the number of bedrooms as a proxy for the number
of occupants, average income level in the area, and the latest price of the house were
considered during discussions. Additionally, data from Center Danmark was restricted to
residential buildings only. To extend the applicability of the models to a broader range
of occupancy types, additional effects should be considered. For instance, intra-week dy-
namics for workdays and weekends are relevant for offices, institutions, and restaurants,
while seasonal effects may impact certain types of businesses. Exploring these ideas could
be interesting for further analysis.

7.2.2
Inclusion of the Heating Capacity
Certain houses exhibit two plateaus in their heating load: one during the WID regime and
another at the maximum heating capacity of the heating device. This might partly explain
why the GM model incorrectly identified a WID regime at very high heat loads, as observed
in fig. 6.5b. One explicit approach to model this behavior involves introducing an S-shaped
Gompertz curve, as proposed in [58]. However, adopting this method would increase the
model‚Äôs complexity and reduce interoperability, as only an approximation of the HCL can
be obtained. Another potential solution is to extend the GM model to include an additional
regime for the plateau at maximum heating capacity. The three-regime GM model was not
considered to maintain model parsimony because the plateau was not universally present
for all houses.
7.2.3
B-Spline on the Probability of Turning on the Heating Device
While the EWS based models have improved interpretability and separability for the WID
and WD regimes, there is room for enhancement, particularly in houses where the heat load
appears actively managed, as illustrated in fig. F.9b. This observed phenomenon could be
attributed to people responding more promptly to a temperature decrease in the autumn

7.2.4
Gaussian Process for Temporal Autocorrelation
Drawing inspiration from [13] and [5], a model integrating a Gaussian Process to capture
temporal dependence in the residuals was implemented, utilizing the GM-EWS as a template.
An exponentiated quadratic function [27] was used for the covariance function and imple-
mented in Stan. However, the computational time for evaluating a single house exceeded
10 minutes. Thus, it would take more than a month to evaluate all houses in the sample
if trained sequentially, which would violate the scaleability principle. While exploring the
combination of multiple kernels for the Gaussian Process to capture temporal variations
in the data could be interesting, it would still be impractical for the current sample size.
For studies with fewer samples, it is an interesting idea to pursue.

7.3
The Bayesian Approach and Scaleability
In this study, the model-building process was guided by three central principles: inter-
pretability, uncertainty quantification, and scalability. While the Bayesian approach aligns
with interpretability and uncertainty quantification, concerns about scalability arise due
to the significant increase in computational demands for sampling. Although a frequentist
version could run faster, the relatively infrequent changes in dwellings‚Äô physical properties
diminish the urgency for rapid model execution, as the model would only have to run once
over multiple years. From a practical standpoint, the analogous frequentist representation
of the model could be considered for deployment at scale if the Bayesian counterpart is
deemed computationally costly. However, in the pursuit of model structures, the Bayesian
framework demonstrated notable efficiency in identifying issues with the model structure
and shortcomings in the uncertainty quantification. Even minor misspecification in the
model structure caused several convergence challenges with the sampler. This is logical
since it deploys three MCMC samplers to navigate the NPL space, significantly increasing
the chances of identifying problematic regions in the likelihood function compared to using
a single instance of an optimization algorithm, as in many frequentist approaches.

7.4
Extensions and Further Research
7.4.1
Classification Based on BBR data
In the sections appendix E.4, appendix E.5, appendix E.6, a comprehensive pipeline for
Bayesian classification is presented. The core idea involves utilizing Bayesian categorical
logit or ordinal logistic regression to predict EPCs based solely on BBR data. This could
be valuable for specific institutions seeking a method to estimate the most likely EPCs for
houses in Denmark. However, incorporating this into the main report would significantly
expand its scope.
The primary finding from fitting the model reveals that BBR data
provides minimal information for accurately determining the EPC of a house. Achieving
convergence in the classifiers proved to be time-consuming and often unattainable with
numerous potential parameter settings, which is also an indicator of ambiguity in the data.
The only parameter that consistently converged was the construction year. However, the
classifier often defaulted to predicting the most prevalent EPC, D. Attempts to balance
the training sample with respect to EPCs only worsened the convergence issues. Utilizing
the discovered parameters from the ARX-EWS model to predict the EPCs of houses with no
EPC could also be an investigation for further research.
7.4.2
Clustering
Various cluster-based techniques, including k-means clustering, principal component anal-
ysis, and distance-based clustering using the Bhattacharyya distance, have been applied
to the inferred parameters to categorize buildings into performance groups. For simple k-
means clustering, around 10 clusters seemed appropriate, closely aligning with the 9 EPC
labels. However, no strong relationship with the EPCs was observed upon investigating
the clusters. The methods and models have not been included as the impact of the insights
is limited. The cluster labels could be understood as a new digital EPC systems, but that
would be a bold statement. Given the limited interpretability of the cluster formation
process, transparent policy-making based on these clusters would also be challenging. An
alternative and more interpretable approach could involve setting thresholds on the HLC in
collaboration with industry professionals. This could be worth exploring in future research
to enhance the impact of the methods.
7.4.3
Retrofit Suggestions and Policy Compliance
It would have been interesting to make an optimization model to identify and calculate the
cost of retrofitting buildings to align with climate policies like the EPBD. This agreement
outlines a mandate for countries to achieve a reduction in the average primary energy use
of residential buildings by 16% by 2030 and 20-22% by 2035 and ensure that at least 55%
of the decrease of the average primary energy use is achieved through the renovation of the
worst-performing buildings. Such a model could also support European mortgage lenders
in navigating energy renovations and facilitating green mortgage financing, as discussed in
[59]. Exploring the introduced method for energy performance estimation as a decision-
making tool in the process could be interesting to consider for further studies. This could
also include post-retrofit savings as it is shown that the actual savings could be 52% lower
than the theoretically calculated a prior [60].

Conclusion
In response to the strengthened imperatives to structurally improve the energy perfor-
mance of buildings across the European Union, it has become increasingly important to
comprehensively characterize the energy performance of the building stock. This requires
adopting data-driven methods to effectively identify, assess, and inform decisions about
building development initiatives at scale. This study encompassed the analysis of daily av-
erage heat loads from 5697 houses in Fredericia, providing a testing ground for evaluating
the effectiveness of models in the domain. The classical energy signature methods were
reexamined within a Bayesian framework using Stan to gain deeper insights into the sta-
tistical characteristics captured by each model. It revealed that specific energy signature
models in the literature only capture a subset of the statistical characteristics, paving the
way for exploring a more diverse class of models in this research domain. This study intro-
duces a novel model structure for describing the transition between the weather-dependent
and weather-independent regimes.
This innovation employs exponential weights based
on past ambient temperature observations, resulting in a more interpretable and smooth
transition boundary. This enhancement improves interpretability and facilitates more ef-
fective sampling and better performance.
When compared to the baseline model, the
CRPS improved by 40% when the transition mechanism was integrated with the dynamical
ARX model. Without the dynamical ARX model, the improvement from the baseline is 24%,
when outdoor temperatures, wind, and solar irradiation are also included and evaluated on
the 4312 dwellings left after pre-processing and post-processing. The study highlights the
importance of precise model calibration to ensure reliable results, particularly in assessing
parameter uncertainty. The ARX model demonstrates superior performance across a mul-
tiple of visual assessments and scores, including the leave-one-out expected log posterior
density ELPDloo. The implication of proper calibration is demonstrated when examin-
ing the uncertainty of the heat loss coefficient in each model.
Inadequately calibrated
models exhibit overconfidence, with credibility intervals more than twice as narrow as the
more adequately calibrated ARX-based model. This underscores the imperative of employ-
ing accurately calibrated models when extracting physical indicators and their associated
uncertainties from heat load data, as the distinct uncertainties in each model could lead
to substantial variations in decision-making for retrofit and development initiatives. This
study also demonstrates a considerable discrepancy between the EPCs issued by an energy
consultant and the EPCs derived from the actual consumption. Houses with the best EPCs
use more than they should, while the houses with the worst EPCs use less heat than an-
ticipated. Several model extensions are suggested to enhance the capacity to capture more
statistical characteristics of houses, such as incorporating more occupancy information. An
impactful extension of this study could entail integrating a retrofit and policy compliance
model. The integration could guide decision-makers by proposing specific houses for tar-
geted emission reduction, ensuring a swift and cost-effective approach to meeting climate
objectives in an industry so core to emissions today.

B.3
Fundamental Principals for the Bayesian Paradigm
In the following, a couple of principals will be described that are fundamental to the
Bayesian paradigm and and different from the frequentist paradigm. The definitions and
are taken from [31]. First, recall the definition of a sufficient statistics:
Definition B.1. Let x ‚àºf(x; Œ∏). Then a function T of x is a sufficient statistic for Œ∏ if
the distribution of x conditional on T(x) does not depend on Œ∏.
Were it can be shown that under some regularity conditions the density of x will factor
into a product f(x; Œ∏) = h(x)gŒ∏(T(x)) where the function gŒ∏ only depends on x through
the function T(¬∑) [31][42].
This leads us to the first principal,
Definition B.2. The Sufficiency Principle: Suppose we have two data-sets x1 and x2
from the same distribution f with the same value of the sufficient statistic, T (x1) = T (x2).
Then both data-sets lead to the same inference about Œ∏.
This is widely accepted by both frequentis and Bayesians; however, the next one is more
controversial.
Definition B.3. The Likelihood Principle: After observing x, the relevant information
in any inference about Œ∏ is contained entirely in the likelihood function L(Œ∏; x). Moreover
if x1 and x2 are two data-sets depending on Œ∏ with L (Œ∏; x1) = cL (Œ∏; x2) for some constant
c, then they contain the same information about Œ∏ and must lead to identical inferences.
Definition B.4. The Conditionality Principle: If two experiments, E1 and E2, re-
garding Œ∏ are available and if one of these two experiments is selected with probability p,
then inference on Œ∏ should only depend on the selected experiment.

Appendix: Building Characteristics
Historical Anecdotes
A few historical houses were build prior to 1850 and then a steady growth until the be-
ginning of the Second World War in 1939. After the end of the war in 1945, construction
increased drastically from 1950 to the beginning of the 1970s, when the energy crises in
1973-1974 started to drastically affect the rate of construction from 1970 to 1980. The
rate of construction was worsened further by extensive fiscal policy reforms implemented
in 1986-1987. The reforms turned out to recover the Danish economy along with other fac-
tors, and as a consequence, the construction started to increase again from around 1995.
The growth is short-lived as the global financial crisis in 2007-2008 would drastically reduce
the growth again [22].

F.1
Preprocessing
In the following, the preprocessing methodology will be described. First, the weather data
from each of the three weather station is handled, then secondly the consumption data,
and then the preprocessing of the BBR data is described.
Preprocessing for the weather data
Notice in fig. D.1 that all the weather stations are a considerable distance from all of the
houses; thus assigning houses to specific weather stations based on the shortest distance
seems unnecessary. Additionally, it can be seen that many days of missing data for one
of the stations. Therefore, the average of the weather observations for each day across all
stations is used for all houses. A more elaborate framework, were we would use wind di-
rection and related meteorological variables to estimate which station might best represent
the weather at the location of the houses in a specific span of time. However, given that
the data is aggregate to daily observations, it was decided that the average of all station
would be more robust and good enough for the purposes in this project - especially given
the considerable distance from the stations to the houses.
Preprocessing for the consumption data
Initially, we find that not all houses in the house meta-data had any existing consumption
files removing 92 houses. With the remaining, the data filtered based on summary statistics
for each house over the year of available data. Then for each house a simple time series
model is used to remove daily means considered to be outliers using a Kalman filter.

remove a house.

houses has missing around 6 days; however, some houses had much more and thus
