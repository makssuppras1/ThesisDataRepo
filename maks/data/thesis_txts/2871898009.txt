Abstract
Manual evaluation of student reports is time­consuming, subjective and difficult to scale,
particularly in large university courses. This study investigates the feasibility of using deep
learning models to automate the evaluation of student­written reports from DTU’s 02450
Introduction to Machine Learning and Data Mining course. The dataset consists of around
4000 reports, graded on 19 criteria.
Two main research questions are addressed, firstly, whether additional contextual infor­
mation in the form of questions being answered by the report improves predictive perfor­
mance, and secondly, to what extent AI models can accurately predict binarized grading
outcomes on complex reports. Multiple neural network architectures are evaluated using
various input configurations, with report text represented through embeddings generated
by a compact language model. A third sub­research question is addressed based on
prior work on the dataset, exploring whether a Compact Language Model can provide
effectively representative embeddings of student reports required for classification.
Building on previous work highlighting challenges of this task, their results are difficult to
interpret due to massive class imbalance and light model evaluation. To improve inter­
pretability of results the problem is reformulated as a binary multi­label classification task,
prediction whether each individual question criterion receives a perfect score or requires
deduction, with a focus on attention mechanisms.
The study finds that the inclusion of contextual information does not significantly improve
performance, and that models tend to overfit early during training regardless of complex­
ity. Despite these limitations, both deep and transformer models marginally outperform
baseline methods, suggesting some potential for automated grading. However, the re­
sults also indicate that the dataset poses substantial challenges. The findings highlight
the limitations of embeddings generated by compact language models in capturing the
features necessary to distinguish between perfect and non­perfect reports. The study
concludes that improving embedding quality and greater computational power is essen­
tial for making automated evaluation a practical and reliable tool in educational contexts.

Introduction
The evaluation of student assignments is a core aspect of academic assessment and of­
ten serves as the primary source of feedback for students. However, manual grading is
time­consuming, subjective and challenging to scale, particularly in large courses, where
increasing grading capacity typically involves adding more teaching assistants. [1]
The course ”02450 Introduction to Machine Learning and Data Mining” typically receives
close to 300 student reports to grade each semester. Students hand in 2 reports, based on
free­text and figures. A team of teaching assistants collectively dedicates 30 minutes on
average per report, at a salary of 230 dkk per hour, representing a significant investment
of instructional resources, costing 69000 dkk per annum. Moreover the involvement of
multiple graders introduces the potential for considerable variability in grading outcomes
due to differences in interpretation and evaluation standards. Scalable automatic evalu­
ation methods could thus streamline this process greatly
The dataset used in this study is comprised of student reports evaluated using rubric­
based scoring from the course 02450 Introduction to Machine Learning and Data Mining,
totaling approximately 4000 samples across two distinct reports. The dataset includes
information protected under the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which re­
stricts all processing to local computational resources. This constraint significantly limits
access to more powerful models that require cloud­based infrastructure. Report 1 in­
cludes 19 evaluation criteria, while Report 2 includes 17, each scored on a Likert scale
from zero to four. The distribution of scores is heavily left­skewed across all criteria, re­
sulting in a significantly imbalanced dataset. Prior to model training, the report texts are
embedded using a compact language model to generate input representations.
Automatic evaluation has traditionally been limited to a narrow subset of assignment
types, such as multiple­choice questions, single­answer responses without required jus­
tification, and programming tasks. While these formats can effectively reduce grading
workload, multiple­choice questions in particular have been shown to risk overestimating
students’ mastery of the material [2].
These limitations underscore the difficulty of automating the assessment of text­heavy
assignments, such as written reports, which demand nuanced understanding and con­
textual reasoning. However, recent advances in natural language processing, particularly
the emergence of transformer­based architectures like BERT [3] and the development of
large language models such as GPT [4], have greatly enhanced the ability of machine
learning systems to process, comprehend, and generate human language. These devel­
opments open new possibilities for automating the evaluation of complex student writing
tasks, offering a more scalable and consistent approach to grading.
The project aims to predict grades based on report input, using multiple models to com­
pare performance relative to model complexity. For non­transformer­based models, the
input configurations are designed to mimic the behavior of an attention mechanism by
selectively controlling the information available to the model [5]. While for the transformer
model, the input configurations are the raw report text with and without every question an­
swered in the report. Figure 1.1 displays a simplified illustration of the prediction pipeline.

Prior work
This project builds upon prior work conducted using the same dataset, where two earlier
studies investigated the task of automated report evaluation. While both studies demon­
strate the effectiveness of their methods on synthetic datasets, their performance on the
real dataset is mixed. One fails to outperform the baseline [6] whereas the other does,
however with questionable use of the attention mechanism [7].
Given the findings of the previous studies, which suggest that automated evaluation on
this dataset is more challenging than initially anticipated, the present study adopts a
modified approach to the problem. Instead of framing the task as ordinal prediction, it
is reformulated as a binary multi­label classification task, distinguishing between perfect
scores and those warranting point deductions, combating the significant class imbalance
present within the dataset. This modification, combined with more rigorous experimen­
tation across varying model complexities and different report segments as input, aims to
shed light on the underlying issues within the dataset that contribute to the difficulty of
achieving reliable predictive performance.
1.1
Contributions
The primary objective is to further investigate the potential for developing an AI model
that can support and complement traditional evaluation methods. To this end, additional
contextual information, specifically the questions that each report is intended to address,
is incorporated into the input of a transformer­based model. This added context should aid
the attention mechanism, central to transformers, and thus allow for greater performance.
During the course of the study, a significant issue emerged when exploring simpler model
capabilities on the dataset: a consistent pattern of overfitting was observed across all
models, regardless of their complexity. Had the previous studies done more rigorous
evaluations of their models this issue would have been known from the beginning. As
a result, considerable effort was dedicated to investigating and understanding this phe­
nomenon. A way to test the attention mechanism using simpler models was implemented
by using input configurations of varying context.
Furthermore given the ambiguous results reported in the previous studies on this dataset,
this project adopts a more rigorous evaluation framework and incorporates stronger base­
line models for comparison. Finally, as neither of the previous studies made effective use
of the attention mechanism, this project aims to provide insights into how transformer
models interpret the dataset. By doing so, it seeks to shed light on the limitations of ear­
lier approaches and offer potential explanations for their suboptimal performance.
The following research questions are examined to assess the feasibility and effectiveness
of this approach.

1. Can an AI­based evaluation model achieve higher predictive accuracy when
provided with additional contextual information in the input data, compared to
a model that operates without such context? To address this question, models
are trained using various input configurations, some incorporating additional contex­
tual information and others relying solely on the report text. The predictive perfor­
mance of each configuration is then compared to evaluate the impact of contextual
input on model accuracy.
2. To what extent can an AI­based evaluation model accurately predict binarized
grading outcomes of student reports? This research question explores the fea­
sibility of using language model­generated embeddings to represent student reports
for the purpose of automated evaluation. The investigation focuses on the model’s
ability to predict binarized grading outcomes across multiple evaluation criteria. Ad­
ditionally, the study examines factors influencing model performance, such as ar­
chitectural complexity, and compares results via rigorous baseline evaluation.
3. Can a Compact Language Model provide embeddings that effectively repre­
sent student reports for classification? Iterating on the confusing results of prior
work on the dataset this question will be explored by spoofing an attention mecha­
nism for simpler models, and exploring the difference in performance of models with
separate and limited context available to them. Examining whether the embeddings
allow the attention mechanism to work.
Following the introduction, the thesis presents the data section, which describes the his­
torical dataset derived from the DTU course 02450 Introduction to Machine Learning and
Data Mining. This includes an overview of the collected student reports and evaluation
rubrics spanning several years, as well as the preprocessing steps and embedding tech­
niques applied. The subsequent methods section details the model architectures, training
procedures, and evaluation strategies employed in the study. The results section then ad­
dresses the two main research questions, while also highlighting key challenges encoun­
tered with the dataset. Finally, the discussion interprets the results and critically assesses
the limitations of both the data and the models.
1.2
Related work
The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) has significantly advanced automated
evaluation systems in education. While recent studies demonstrate that LLM­based ap­
proaches can be effective, several challenges remain. For example, Xie et al. [8] propose
a comprehensive LLM­driven grading system that encompasses the entire evaluation pro­
cess, ranging from rubric development to generating individual scores, personalized feed­
back, and post­grading review. Their work draws attention to the potentially high compu­
tational and financial cost associated with large­scale automated grading using LLMs.
Chu et al. [9] introduce GradeOpt a multi­agent framework for automatic grading of an­
swers, which leverages LLMs for both grading and self optimization through self­reflection
mechanisms. The authors point to key limitations of LLMs, including hallucination [10] and
misalignment [11], and emphasize the importance of incorporating human oversight dur­
ing the training process to mitigate these issues.
A comparative analysis by Seßler et al. [12] demonstrates that state­of­the­art closed­
source LLMs can deliver reliable essay evaluations, particularly in language­related as­
pects, with outputs that closely align with human judgments.
In the prior work done on the dataset Hjort focused on predicting rubric ratings and over­

In total there are 2231 submissions for report 1 and 2025 for report 2, after processing
the numbers end at 2017 and 1789 respectively.

2.2
Processing
2.2.1
Text extraction
Document loading was performed using the PyMuPDF library, allowing for data extraction
of PDF documents [13]. From the document the raw text as markdown was then extracted
via PyMuPDF4LLM [14]. The extracted text is then stored in dictionaries, identified by
either group number or student number based on document naming convention between
previously mention DTU platforms; Inside and Learn.
2.2.2
Processing of report evaluation
The evaluation data is extracted from multiple Excel files, one for each semester. Each file
contains two sheets, one each for reports 1 and 2 respectively. Two separate pandas data
frames were created surrounding these excel files, one for each report. With each row
consisting of a single report, and each column representing a criterion, with the first three
columns being standardized to student numbers as identifiers for each report’s group, to
be used for connecting samples to labels for model training.
2.3
Text embeddings
To convert textual report content into numerical representation suitable to machine learn­
ing models, the All­MiniLM­l6­v2 model from SentenceTransformers library was selected
for embedding. A lightweight transformer­based model balancing computational efficiency
and semantic accuracy, making it well­suited for large­scale or resource­constrained ap­
plications. It produces dense, 384 dimensional vectors that capture the semantic similarity
between sentences or documents [15].
Compared to larger transformer models such as BERT or RoBERTa, All­MiniLM achieves
comparable performance on standard semantic tasks, at a significantly faster inference

tations of DistilRoBERTa and Longformer were tested for comparison, there was not a
noticeable increase in classification performance such that the increased computational
cost would be justified.

Methods
This section outlines the methodological framework used to evaluate model performance.
First, baseline models are introduced to establish reference points for assessing the qual­
ity of the proposed predictive models. This is followed by a detailed description of the
model architectures and the evaluation metrics employed. Finally, the training procedures
and hyperparameter tuning strategies are presented.
3.1
Input configurations
A total of six input configurations were used: four for the non­transformer­based models
and two for the transformer­based models. Each configuration consists of a portion of
the report text. For the non­transformer­based models, the following input configurations
were applied:
• Embeddings of first half of the chunked report
• Embeddings of second half of the chunked report
• Embedding of the first chunk of the report
• Embedding of the last chunk of the report
For the transformer­based models, the input configurations were:

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of Results
The results highlight how different model architectures and input configurations influenced
predictive performance across the dataset. A clear and consistent pattern emerged: all
models, irrespective of complexity or input design, exhibited significant overfitting. The
primary distinction among models lay in the rate at which overfitting occurred, rather than
in their ability to avoid it entirely, as illustrated in figs. 4.1 to 4.6.
Despite the application of various regularization techniques, none were sufficient to elim­
inate this issue. In all cases, models began to overfit within the first 20 epochs. These
techniques served only to moderate the pace of overfitting rather than prevent it. Across
both reports, a consistent trend was observed: a validation macro AUC peaked early,
around 0.65 for report 1 and 0.7 for report 2, before gradually declining as training loss
continued to decrease. Despite this, models outperformed the baselines.
While the deep model statistically significantly outperforms all baseline models, and the
transformer outperforms all but the linear baseline, as seen in tables 4.4 and 4.5, the
observed performance gains from increased model complexity are marginal. These im­
provements are constrained by severe overfitting as demonstrated before, indicating that,
although the models exceed baseline performance, they still fall short of delivering reliable
or robust results.
When exploring Figure 4.7 it is apparent that the embeddings are not sufficiently capturing
the features required for prediction. Logically the first half of the chunks should be signifi­
cantly outperforming the second half for earlier labels, and vice versa. This however does
not happen, and similarly when only a single chunk is used for input, logically the results
should be significantly worse, yet the performance loss is relatively minimal. Given these
results it is unlikely that a Compact Language Models manages to create embeddings
that capture the difference in quality of answers required for evaluation.
The reformulation of the problem using spoofed attention in non­transformer models proved
effective in providing additional insight. The statistically significant difference in perfor­
mance between the first and second halves of the embeddings when used with the deep
model suggests the presence of some underlying structure within the embeddings that
enables one half to yield better predictive outcomes. However, this structure does not
appear to be sufficiently informative for the transformer’s attention mechanism to learn
meaningful distinctions at the chunk level as seen in figs. 4.8 and 4.9. Highlighting in­
sights into the limitations of the dataset not discovered in prior work.
5.2
Limitations
Given the systematic overfitting observed across all models, irrespective of architecture or
complexity, a reasonable conclusion is that the underlying issue may lie with the dataset
itself.
Regarding the issue with the dataset, two primary factors are likely contributors: the lim­
ited size of the dataset and the lack of sufficiently discriminative vector representations of
the reports. To address the limitation in data size, the only solution is to continue collect­
ing reports over time, adding approximately 400 samples to each report annually, and to
revisit the problem after several years, once a substantially larger dataset has been ac­
cumulated. Previous research has demonstrated that model accuracy tends to improve
AI Report Evaluation Using Compact Language Model Embeddings and Varied Context

==PAGE:37==
following a power­law relationship as the size of the training dataset increases [24], and it
could be interesting to see if the same is the case for this dataset. However, the practical
feasibility of this approach may be limited. If a substantially larger dataset is required, it
would effectively constrain the course from modifying any report questions the foresee­
able future, in order to maintain consistency and compatibility with the existing data, and
given that the current dataset consists of 7 years worth of data, it could very well take
many more to reach an acceptable dataset size.
The larger embedding models used for comparison with MiniLM did not yield a mean­
ingful improvement in predictive performance that would justify their increased parameter
count. Despite being relatively larger, these models, peaking at approximately 149 million
parameters, still fall within the category of small to moderately sized models. It is possible
that they lack the capacity to fully capture the nuanced information necessary to differen­
tiate report quality. In contrast, significantly more complex models, with parameter counts
in the billions, may possess the representational power required for this task; however,
such models are not feasible to run in a local computing environment.
5.3
Future work
Despite the significant limitations encountered during this project, there remain several
directions for future work that could help advance the task using the current dataset.
5.3.1
Improved embedding
Given that the current embeddings may lack sufficient discriminative power to effectively
represent the reports, a critical area for improvement is the development of higher­quality
embeddings. This could be achieved either through the use of larger pre­trained models
or by successfully fine­tuning models specifically on this dataset. Enhancing the quality
of the embeddings may be key to unlocking a predictive potential in the data.
5.3.2
Alternative chunking
In this project, standard chunking was performed based on token count. However, a po­
tential improvement would be to segment the text such that each chunk aligns with the full
content of a specific question associated with a label, ensuring no overlap between ques­
tions and preserving their completeness within individual chunks. This more structured
approach to chunking could enable further investigation into the representational capac­
ity of the current embedding model. For example, by framing the task as a multiclass
classification at the chunk level, predicting which label a given chunk corresponds to. Ad­
ditionally, such alignment would enhance the interpretability of attention­based models, as
each chunk would be directly associated with a specific label, allowing clearer attribution
through self­attention mechanisms.

6
Conclusion
This study examined the feasibility of using deep learning models of varying complexity
for the automated evaluation of student reports, with a primary focus on different input
configurations of report text. Additionally, the research aimed to identify and better un­
derstand existing limitations within the dataset, building on observations from previous
work.
The first research question asked: Can an AI­based evaluation model achieve higher pre­
dictive accuracy when provided with additional contextual information in the input data,
compared to a model that operates without such context? The findings revealed that in­
corporating additional contextual information, specifically, the questions being answered,
did not lead to a statistically significant improvement in predictive performance. It is plau­
sible that the context added did not sufficiently improve model understanding of the data
due to poor context choice. However it is likely, given the results across all models, that
the dataset itself does not hold the information required to evaluate the student reports
accurately.
The second research question asked: To what extent can an AI­based evaluation model
accurately predict binarized outcomes of student reports? Through rigorous evaluation
on baselines of varying strengths, both the transformer and deep models outperformed
the benchmark baselines across all input configurations in term of validation AUC, and the
deep model outperformed the much simpler linear model, though the improvements were
modest. These outcomes suggest that while more complex models offer some gains in
predictive power, their performance is heavily constrained by the limitations and quality
of the underlying dataset. Despite outperforming baseline models, the extent to which an
AI­based evaluation model can accurately predict on student reports in a practical setting
is limited, at least with the heavy constraints of the dataset allowing only for models run
in a local environment, heavily restricting the complexity ceiling of models.
The final research question asked: Can a Compact Language Model provide embeddings
that effectively represent student reports for evaluation? In order to explore this, context
given to a deep neural network was varied, between the two halves and a single chunk
of 512 characters at the beginning and end of the report respectively. The results did in­
dicate performance difference when limiting context by which half of the report was used
as input. Moreover, even when models were provided with as little as 5% of the report
(i.e. a single chunk), predictive performance did not degrade substantially. Indicating that
while the current embedding representations of the reports manage to capture the se­
mantic differences between the two reports, they may be severely lacking when it comes
to evaluating within the reports, failing to capture the features required to differentiate on
the prediction task. As a result, the current embeddings fail to effectively capture and
represent the necessary features of student reports for reliable evaluation.
A consistent pattern of overfitting was observed across all model complexities and input
configurations. Validation AUC typically peaked early in the training process, ranging from
approximately 0.63 to 0.67 for Report 1 and 0.65 to 0.70 for Report 2, before steadily de­
clining to values as low as 0.54 with continued reductions in training loss. The recurrence
of this trend across different model types and input setups suggests that the embeddings
produced by the simpler language models may fail to encode task­relevant features in a
form that the current architectures can effectively utilize. It is plausible that significantly
AI Report Evaluation Using Compact Language Model Embeddings and Varied Context
32
==PAGE:39==
more complex open­source large language models, if they could be run locally in a fea­
sible manner, would address the limitations of the current embeddings and potentially
enable the development of a practically viable tool for automated evaluation of student
reports.
Overall, this project builds upon previous work using the same dataset, offering additional
insight into its potential shortcomings, and further demonstrate the limited effectiveness
of regression models on the dataset. It highlights a significant issue with the dataset as
it stands, systematic overfitting regardless of model complexity and regularization. The
findings underscore that, while there is plausible potential for automated grading as seen
in related works, substantial improvement are required before such models can be con­
sidered for practical deployment with this dataset. In particular, enhancing the quality
of embeddings is crucial. With continued significant refinement, and greatly increased
computation capabilities in a local environment allowing for the use of Large Language
Models, automated grading systems may become a valuable tool for providing consistent
and scalable assessment in educational settings.
