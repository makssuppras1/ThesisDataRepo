PREFACE 
The present study is completed in the summer 2016 at Denmark Technical University, department of 
Civil Engineering. Work was conducted over a period of six months, from 25th of January to 5th of 
August and constitutes a workload of 30 ECTS. The present study represents an experimental project 
and all the performed tests have been done on the equipment provided by Geotechnical Laboratory 
from DTU. Main supervisor of the project is Gitte Lyng Grønbech and co – supervisor is Varvara 
Zania, professors at DTU. 
The thesis deals with an experimental investigation of the influence of structure and the importance 
of preserving/not preserving soil structure to be destroyed by compression. The investigation was 
done in two test setups, shear tests performed on a Direct Simple Shear apparatus and consolidation 
tests performed as an Incremental Loading Oedometer test. The aim of the investigation is to study 
the strength parameters and the consolidation properties of the Reconstituted Røsnæs Clay and make 
a comparison with the results obtained on the intact Røsnæs Clay samples reported in the 
Geotechnical Investigation Report (GIR, 2011), by Rambøll Arup Joint Venture. 
This project represents an experimental work and the test results, derived discussion and conclusions 
can be used for further investigations. The main focus of the thesis is on the experimental laboratory 
work.

experimental setups and for providing answers to all my questions. Furthermore, I would like to thank

1 INTRODUCTION  
Two major factors, having a significant influence on the behaviour of a soil, are the deposit geology 
and the later history. The landscape in Denmark is defined by the glaciers that covered the country 
during the Quaternary period.  
The focus of this study is on the Palaeogene Røsnæs clay, which is a highly plastic clay, located 
directly below the Quaternary deposits, lasting from 65 million years to 23 million years ago. 
Significant investigations on Palaeogene Clay have been carried out through advanced laboratory 
testing in order to study deformation properties during loading and unloading from the in – situ stress 
state, undrained shear strength after consolidation to define stress states and effective shear strength 
parameters. An important factor in studying the properties of soil is given by the soil structure 
(defined in section 2.3.2). 
In order to analyse the influence of structure, each soil type should be tested at two states: an intact 
state and a reconstituted state. In the intact state, the structure of soil is influenced by sedimentation 
and geological development. The reconstituted state is that in which the initial structure of the soil 
was destroyed and rebuilt by consolidation to the in situ stress of the intact soil. For separating the 
properties of the clay from the properties of the structure, all the tests performed during this study, 
have been done on reconstituted specimens. 
1.1 MOTIVATION 
During the past years, a major focus was on studying the behaviour of Danish Palaeogene deposits 
as they are present across whole Denmark. Many laboratory investigations have been performed in 
order to get a better understanding of these clays behaviour.  
This study has been carried out to supplement the existing ‘database’ of results and to contribute to a 
better characterisation and understanding of Røsnæs Clay behaviour. 
During this project, a series of nine Direct Simple Shear tests and five Incremental Loading 
Oedometer tests have been conducted and the results have been analysed with the aim of finding the 
consolidation properties and the strength parameters. 
The main goal of this work is to describe the results and conclusions of an experimental study 
developed to evaluate the properties of the reconstituted Røsnæs Clay. 
1.2 AIM OF THESIS 
The present study focuses on the properties and behaviour of reconstituted Paleogene Røsnæs Clay. 
This type of clay has a very high plasticity and this fact dominates its behaviour, which results in high 
compressibility and relatively low strength. The strength of Paleogene clay was found to be 
dominated by pre – existing shear planes within the fabric of the clay, resulting from its geological 
history (glacial actions). (Rambøll&Arup, 2012) 
A starting point for the project was to study the work done by Gasparre, et. al. (2007), who describes 
an intensive investigation into the London Clay units from Heathrow Airport, Terminal 5, London. 
In his study, the effects of the clay’s natural structure were identified at all depths by relating the 
behaviour of intact and reconstituted samples.

Taking into account the influence of structure on the behaviour of similar clays, one purpose of this 
work is to eliminate this factor and, for this reason, all the tests were performed on reconstituted 
Røsnæs Clay samples. 
The thesis deals with an experimental investigation of the influence of structure and the importance 
of preserving/not preserving the soil structure to be destroyed by compression. The investigation was 
done in two test setups, shear tests performed on a Direct Simple Shear apparatus and consolidation 
tests performed as an Incremental Loading Oedometer test. All the tests have been carried out in the 
Geotechnical Laboratory of Technical University of Denmark, in Building 119. It should be 
mentioned that three of five oedometer tests have been performed by Rene Bøgelund Pedersen and 
Anette Krogsbøll in 2014, as part of an unfinished PhD project and the result have never been 
published. 
The aim of the investigation is to study the strength parameters and the consolidation properties of 
the Reconstituted Røsnæs Clay and make a brief comparison with the results obtained on the intact 
Røsnæs Clay samples reported in the Geotechnical Investigation Report (GIR, 2011), by Rambøll 
Arup Joint Venture. 
1.3 STRUCTURE OF THESIS 
Chapter 2 consists of a literature review, in which the geology and properties of the Paleogene Clay 
are presented. This chapter also introduces the general concepts about the influence of structure, 
background to the lithology of London Clay and the influence of structure on London Clay behaviour. 
Chapter 3 presents the theory of the two test setups (DSS and ILO) and the methods for interpreting 
the data. The interpretation of data is split into two main parts: one – dimensional consolidation 
(consolidation parameters and methods for determination of preconsolidation stress) and strength 
parameters (undrained shear strength, effective parameters and SHANSEP method).   
Chapter 4 presents the soil characterisation and the results obtained from all the ILO and DSS tests 
performed on the reconstituted samples of Røsnæs Clay. 
Chapter 5 presents the analysis of all data. Similar tests are combined and discussed, as well as the 
results obtained from tests performed on different apparatus. The strength parameters from the 
undrained DSS tests are discussed in this chapter and a comparison between the intact samples and 
reconstituted samples of Røsnæs Clay is performed. 
Chapter 6 contains the final conclusion of the thesis. Based on this, a perspective future work starting 
from the present investigation is proposed.

2 LITERATURE REVIEW 
The main focus of this section will be on the geology and properties of Røsnæs Clay, but also on the 
behaviour of London Clay. The detailed discussion about the London clay behaviour is because, even 
though it is a less plastic material compared to the clays found in Denmark, it is the most known and 
investigated plastic clay. A lot of new engineering approaches were adopted for the new Terminal 5 
at Heathrow Airport in London, which required an advanced investigation of London clay, and for 
this reason it is used as a base of comparison in most of the works. 
2.1 GEOLOGY AND PROPERTIES OF RØSNÆS CLAY 
The Røsnæs clay is a Palaeogene Clay comprised in the Eocene formation. The upper Paleocene and 
Eocene deposits in Denmark are referred to six formations. The Eocene consists of Røsnæs Clay, 
Lillebælt Clay and Søvind Marl Formations, while the Upper Paleocene includes Holmehus, Ølst and 
Fur Formations. (Heilmann - Clausen, et al., 1985) 
The Palaeogene clay is located below the Quaternary deposits and locally as floes within the Lower 
Till Unit and includes the formations shown in lower part of Figure 2-1. 
The oldest formations described from the Danish Palaeogene, the Danian Limestone, the Lellinge 
greensand and the Kerteminde Marl, have never been found in the Fehmarn Belt area. The Lillebælt 
Clay represents the youngest Pre – Quaternary deposit found in the borings at Fehmarn Belt. The 
younger layers that have originally been deposited in the area, have later been eroded away. This 
situation can be indicated by the fact that floes of Miocene deposits are present in the coastal cliff at 
Heiligenhafen, right south of Fehmarnsund. (GEO, 2011) 
In the borings that were made in Fehmarn Belt, all the units from the Æbelø Formation to the Lillebælt 
Formation have been found. The deposits from Æbelø Formation seem to be silty or very silty clay, 
but all the others (Holmehus Clay, Ølst Clay, Røsnæs Clay, Lillebælt Clay) are very highly plastic 
clays. The clays have clear and bright colours and they vary down through the layers, but also from 
one area to another in Denmark. (GEO, 2011) 
In Røsnæs Clay, the dominated colors are olive brown, olive grey, patches of grey, shades of brown 
and olive also appears. Because there is a high content of micro – shells, the clay is almost always 
calcareous. Usually, the layer has no organic content, but in the uppermost part of the unit the 
presence of two thin bands with a characteristic black color and rich in organic matter, are present. 
The clay also contains many nodules or concretions (siderite and others), but dark green ash layers 
appear. (GEO, 2011) 
The dominating formation, lying directly below the Quaternary deposits, is the Røsnæs clay 
formation. It is known that in many parts of Denmark the thickness of the Røsnæs Clay layer is more 
than 15 – 20 m, but an explanation for A – boring which passed more than 65 m of Røsnæs Clay 
could be that the series of layers might be steeply dipping, so that the boring is almost parallel with 
the layering in the unit, giving a much to high apparent thickness. (GEO, 2011) 
Relevant engineering parameters should be considered when modelling the Paleogene Clay. In the 
report made by Ramboll//Arup in 2011, some important traits are discussed and some of them are 
listed below: 
- 
Paleogene Clay has a very high plasticity and this dominates the clay behavior, which results in 
a relatively high compressibility and low strength. (Ramboll//Arup, 2011)

- 
The unloading of clay in the presence of water, the clay expands and this leads to an increase of

-

- 
The permeability is varying with the void ratio. 
- 
The strength was observed to be dominated by pre – existing shear planes within the fabric of 
the clay that results from its geological history. (Ramboll//Arup, 2011) 
The mineralogical and chemical composition of Røsnæs Clay is presented in  
 
Table 2-1 and Table 2-2. In the north-western part of the Røsnæs Clay formation, the smectite content 
is very high, but to the south – eastern part, the content of illite and kaolinite is increasing comparing 
to the smectite content. A reason for this, can be that the supply of kaolinite is from more southerly 
land areas which were not very influenced by volcanism. The main chemical components of this type 
of clay are sodium, calcium and magnesium. (Heilmann - Clausen, et al., 1985)

2.2  INFLUENCE OF STRUCTURE 
The term ‘structure’ is used to define a combination of fabric (arrangement of the component 
particles) and bonding (combination of forces acting to connect the particles), which are not of a 
purely frictional nature. (Lambe & Whitman, 1969) 
The physic – chemical equilibrium between the soil particles constitutes the structure of clay. This 
equilibrium develops during the geological life of the soil as a result of water chemistry during 
deposition, mineralogy, temperature, organic content, electrostatic and magnetic interactions between 
crystals. In time, both the fabric and the bonding of the clay may change because of externally induced 
variations of the above mentioned factors or the development of chemical reactions within the 
sediments. The depositional (sedimentation) and post – depositional (post sedimentation) processes 
represent two major factors that influence the formation and evolution of soil structure, which 
represents the result of all processes that a soil has undergone during its geological history. (Gasparre, 
2005) 
The processes of sedimentation and post – sedimentation define the equilibrium of the particles and 
govern the soil response to subsequent changes in stress and strains. Clays that have a sedimentation 
structure are those which developed their structure during and after deposition as result of one – 
dimensional compression, while the clays with a post – sedimentation structure are those which 
developed their structure when geological processes alter the sedimentation structure after normal 
consolidation. In general, the sedimentation structure is typical for normally consolidated clays that 
have undergone diagenetic processes only while compressing to high stresses, while the post – 
sedimentation structure is typical for overconsolidated clays that have undergone erosion, ageing or 
diagenesis altering their initial sedimentation structure. (Cotecchia, 1996), (Cotecchia & Chandler, 
1997) 
 The ‘intact state of structure’ was defined as the state that occurs in natural deposits (Leroueil & 
Vaughan, 1990). There were distinguished four different states of structure: a destructured state (an 
intact clay subjected to large strains that destroy its original structure), an intact state, a remoulded 
state (obtained when sufficient mechanical energy is imparted to the clay mass to reduce its strength 
to a minimum) and a resedimented state (achieved by deposition of clay particles originally 
remoulded and remixed o  slurry and then consolidated under the self – weight of a soil column of 
increasing thickness   (Leroueil, et al., 1984).  
The concept of ‘state of structure’ (Leroueil, et al., 1984) states that the characteristics of soils 
represent a result of all the processes that a soil has undergone and that soils, regardless their state, 
are considered to have a structure which depends on their sedimentation processes. In general, the 
term ‘structure’ is used to define a clay in its intact state, while a destructurated state is assumed to 
be the state of a reconstituted soil that has been mixed at a water content equal or greater than the 
liquid limit and has been consolidated under one – dimensional conditions. (Burland, 1990) 
In the reconstituted state, the clay will still have a structure, but it will be comprised of a fabric and 
bonding that are stable as they do not change with disturbance (Cotecchia, 1996). Burland (1990) 
defined the reconstituted state as the state of a fully destructured material and it is considered as the 
reference to define the degree of structure of a natural material, which corresponds to the degree of 
enhanced strength.

2.3 PROPERTIES OF RECONSTITUTED LONDON CLAY 
An original engineering approach was used for Terminal 5 at Heathrow Airport, which required an 
advanced investigation of London Clay through stress path laboratory testing on high – quality rotary 
– cored samples and detailed in situ profiling. An intensive research was conducted at Imperial 
College on the collected samples and they were described in three companion papers, two by 
(Gasparre, et al., 2007a) and one by (Nishimura, et al., 2007). All these papers contain information 
obtained from the recent research, the commercial investigations, but also the previous studies in 
order to offer a better understanding of the geology and characteristics of this stiff clay type. The 
importance of structure and lithology were studied and the practical implications have been discussed. 
2.3.1 Background to the lithology of London Clay  
London Clay was deposited in Early Eocene, in marine conditions, covering much of northern and 
north – eastern Europe, around 30 million years ago, in a basin that was subsiding and in which sea 
levels were rising. In UK, the deposited soils were eroded from the surrounding Jurassic shales, Chalk, 
Greensand and lateritic Eocene soils. The mineralogy is spatially varying, Central London being 
dominated by illite and smectite, while the western parts are dominated by kaolinite. (King, 1981) 
During the Eocene period, sea level was generally rising, but there was also a series of transgressive 
– regressive cycles that took place, fact that led to water depth changes and sediment variability. 
Being close enough to the basin margin, Central London was affected by the sedimentation due to 
the changes in the sea level, but this was associated with a coarsening of the material being deposited. 
The changes in sedimentation, together with the cycles in the sea level changes, made possible the 
division of the London Clay into lithological units. This division was made by King, being mainly 
based on the content of fossil fauna and then on the contents of silt and sand. (King, 1981) 
Five principal transgressive – regressive cycles were identified, defining five divisions in the London 
Clay (from A to E) and marking the base of a coarsening – upward sequence. 
The lowest unit, A2 (≈ 12m thick), contains a high percentage of silt, and very rarely, fragments of 
wood and pyrite nodules can be found. Minor sea level changes are reflected by alternations of sandy 
and silty clay layers. This unit is generally non – calcareous and contains few claystone, being referred 
as the basal beds. Unit A3 (12m thick) contains the first silty clay layer and the main claystone layer 
is encountered near the base, being more or less continuous. (King, 1981) 
A thick sandy clay layer marks the boundary between units A and B. Unit B (≈ 12m thick) contains 
silty clays with weak silt and sand partings and numerous claystones. Units C, D and E can be 
observed in the full sequence of London Clay (up to 150 m in South Essex). At Terminal 5 Site (T5), 
Heathrow, only the lower part of the sequence is present (A2, A3, B1, B2 and lower part of C). 
Responsible for the increasing plasticity from west to east (Burnett & Fookes, 1974) is the increasing 
depth of deposition in moving from west to east across the London basin, fact that makes the 
lithological units to become less distinct in this direction. (King, 1981) 
The continuing deposition resulted in a deeply burying of the soils that constitute the London Clay 
Formation in Central London. Later, during the Alpine orogeny, they were subjected to mild tectonic 
loading, forming the syncline of the London Basin. Due to the erosion in the late Tertiary and 
Pleistocene times, all the overlying deposits and the London Clay itself, have been removed, 
especially from the Thames Valley. The amount of erosion was estimated based on the interpretation 
of the vertical yield stress in the oedometer tests on high quality samples, ignoring the effects of 
structure and the range was between 150 m in Essex (Skempton, 1961) to 300 m in Wraysbury district 
(Bishop , et al., 1965).

2.3.4 The Influence of Structure on the Behaviour of London Clay  
After an intensive investigation of the London Clay units from T5 – Heathrow, intrinsic properties 
and composition were established. In order to study the effects of the clay’s natural structure at all 
depths and the behaviour of intact and reconstituted samples were related. Structure contributes to 
the enhanced shear strength of intact unfissured clay and affects the initial stiffness relationships. An 
important impact on shear strength  that led to a very unusual pattern of directional dependence and 
particle orientation is represented by the natural fissures within the clay.  All the data from the relevant 
papers have been synthesized and the practical consequences of the obtained results have been 
discussed. (Hight, et al., 2007) 
 
2.3.4.1 Sampling, Tests and Procedures  
The studied samples were obtained throughout the depth of the London Clay from two adjacent and 
continuously sampled rotary boreholes. After being collected from the site, the samples were 
retrieved from their liners and 5 mm thickness was trimmed off around them before preserving the 
samples by wrapping them in plastic and wax. 
The tests were carried out in different stress path apparatus, oedometers, high – pressure triaxial 
apparatus and hollow cylinder column apparatus (HCA). Samples of different dimensions have been 
tested in order to investigate a possible influence of sample size and in all cases a 2:1 height to 
diameter ratio was used. (Gasparre, et al., 2007) 
The intact samples were trimmed by hand and the inner cavities of the HCA were cut on a metal 
working lathe. The trimmings of the intact samples were used in order to prepare the reconstituted 
triaxial samples, being mixed at water contents about 1.25 times the liquid limit and compressed to 
around 30 kPa vertical stress in a 38 mm diameter consolidometers. The reconstituted HCA samples, 
made from slurry with water contents about 1.5 times the liquid limit, were compressed to around 
730 kPa in a 90 mm diameter consolidometer. In all the above mentioned cases, the void ratios from 
the initial and final water contents were measured, bulk unit weight and dry unit weight, with an 
average being taken. (Gasparre, et al., 2007) 
Shearing of the triaxial samples was undrained and strain controlled, in compression or extension, 
because a drained shearing would have required a very long test duration. In order to examine the 
shape of the intact boundary surface, some of the intact triaxial samples were compressed at stresses 
higher than the in situ stresses. In order to examine the effects of destructuration through swelling, 
some of the samples were isotropically swelled to low stresses (10 kPa) before being recompressed 
at around 100 kPa. The reconstituted samples used in triaxial tests were isotropically compressed at 
different stress levels and then were sheared undrained, considering only behaviour in compression.

2.3.4.2 Nature and structure of clay 
The nature and structure of the clay from different strata were microscopically investigated and 
correlated with its small and large strain mechanical response. A more open structure and higher clay 
content were observed in the shallower units comparing with the deeper units. In samples collected 
from the same units, a similar mechanical behaviour and engineering parameters were observed, 
while the samples from different units presented many differences which reflected a variation in the 
nature and structure of clay from each stratum. (Gasparre, et al., 2007) 
The nature and structure of clay seemed to dominate the behaviour both in compression and shearing. 
The clay from the units having a more open structure showed a loose response and smaller strengths 
than the clay from units with a more packed and oriented structure. The elastic parameters obtained 
by investigating the clay behaviour in the elastic region confirmed the effects of lithology on sample 
behaviour. (Gasparre, 2005)

3 METHODOLOGY  
In this section, a systematic and theoretical analysis of the test setups and of the applied methods for 
interpreting the results will be described. There will also be presented some technical limitations 
encountered during test preparations. 
3.1 DIRECT SIMPLE SHEAR – TEST PROCEDURE 
In this study, a direct simple shear (DSS) apparatus produced by American producer Geocomp is 
used, Shear – Trac II device. A device for direct simple shear was first developed for testing by 
Kjellman in 1951 and The Royal Swedish Geotechnical Institute (SGI), (Kjellman, 1951). Based on 
the device invented by SGI, the Norwegian Geotechnical Institute (NGI) developed a similar device 
which was described by Bjerrum and Landva (1966), in order to test Norwegian Quick Clay. 
This method of soil testing defines equipment specifications and testing procedure for the 
measurement of constant volume strength and stress – strain characteristics of cohesive soils after 
one – dimensional consolidation using a constant rate of simple shear deformation mode of loading. 
The constant volume condition is equivalent to the undrained condition for saturated specimens. The 
undrained conditions are reached by maintaining a constant specimen volume during shearing phase 
and by performing the test at a sufficiently fast displacement rate. 
In this test method, the specimen is axially loaded and allowed to consolidate one – dimensionally. 
The maximum normal load is maintained until the sample is 100% consolidated or the maximum 
duration of the load step is reached. After the consolidation phase, the specimen is sheared at a 
constant rate of displacement and the resulting shear force is measured. The volume of the specimen 
is constant during shear in order to simulate undrained conditions. This is obtained by changing the 
normal load applied to the specimen to maintain constant the height of the soil sample. This test can 
be applied to field conditions where soils have fully consolidated under one set of stresses and then 
are subjected to changes in stress without time for further drainage to take place. The constant volume 
(undrained) strength is a function of stress conditions. This simple shear stress condition can be 
observed in many field situations, such as zones below a low embankment and around axially loaded 
piles. (ASTM D6528, 2007)

During samples’ preparation, several problems have been encountered, such as:

3.2 INCREMENTAL LOADING (IL) OEDOMETER – TEST PROCEDURE 
The first time attention was drawn to the problem of long term consolidation of clays was in 1925 by 
Terzaghi who proposed a theoretical approach to the consolidation process. By that time, he had 
already designed the first consolidation apparatus, named ‘oedometer’ from the Greek word ‘oidema’ 
(swelling). In 1930s, in USA, many consolidation test were carried out on specimen of different sizes 
and they were reported by Gilboy (1936), Rutledge (1935), Casagrande (1932) and many others. In 
1936, Teraghi and Frohlich published the mathematical theory of consolidation. (As described by 
(Head, 1994)) 
==PAGE:26==
 
RECONSTITUTED PROPERTIES OF RØSNÆS CLAY 
MASTER THESIS 
 
16 
August 5, 2016
In order to determine the consolidation of soils, the oedometer consolidation test is used. Normally, 
two parameters are required: 
- 
The soil compressibility, which is a measure of the amount by which the soil will compress when 
loading and allowed to consolidate (it is expressed in terms of the coefficient of volume 
compressibility/modulus of volume change); 
- 
The time related parameter, which indicates the time – period over which consolidation 
settlement will take place. 
Whenever the ground is loaded, some degree of settlement will occur even if the applied pressure is 
well within the safe bearing capacity of the soil. In clayey soils, due to their low permeability, 
settlements can take place over long time periods after completion of construction, comparing to 
settlements in sands and gravels which take place in a short time, usually as construction proceeds. 
That is why, the estimation of the time within which settlement will be complete represents an 
important factor in foundation design. (Head, 1994) 
The test is performed by applying the vertical load, in steps, to a laterally confined specimen having 
the height of about one quarter to one third of its diameter. The vertical compression is observed 
under each load step for a period of time of about 24 h. As there are no lateral deformations, this is a 
one – dimensional test from which the one – dimensional consolidation parameters are obtained. 
The consolidation cell consists of a mould for accommodating and supporting the test specimen, an 
upper and a lower drainage surface, an outer plastic casing filled with water for immersing the 
specimen and a top loading cap. A general arrangement of a typical oedometer apparatus is presented 
in Figure 3-3 and the procedural stages are listed below: 
(1) Prepare and check apparatus 
(2) Weigh and measure consolidation ring 
(3) Cut and trim specimen into ring 
(4) Determine moisture content and particle density from trimmings 
(5) Weigh specimen in ring 
(6) Assemble specimen in consolidation cell 
(7) Fit cell in load frame 
(8) Set up loading yoke

3.3 INTERPRETATION OF DATA 
3.3.1 One – Dimensional Consolidation 
Soils are formed of particles and between them there are voids which can be filled with gas, liquids 
or a combination of both. Usually the gas is represented by air and the present liquid in soil voids is 
water. In fully saturated soils the voids contain only water. 
When soils are subjected to a compressive stress, their volumes are decreasing and for saturated soils 
this can happen by three means: 
(1) Compression of solid grains 
(2) Compression of the existing water between the grains 
(3) Escape of water from the voids 
The theory of consolidation is based on the third mean from the above mentioned, the escape of water 
from the voids between the skeleton of the solid grains. In saturated sands the escape of water takes 
place rapidly, but in case of a clay, the water movement takes place very slow and a noticeable time 
is required to the excess water to be squeezed out to permeable boundaries. In the consolidation 
process, the change in volume occurs equally slowly and the settlement taking place under the applied 
load occurs over a long period of time. (Head, 1994) 
The total stress, σ, represents the stress induced by the externally applied load. The pore water 
pressure, u, is the pressure in the water contained by the voids between the soil particles. When a 
saturated soil is subjected to an external load, the load is first carried by the additional pore water 
pressure, named also excess pore water pressure, which is equal to the total applied stress. If the clay 
is bounded by surfaces from which water can escape, the excess pressure will cause water to flow out 
of the clay (Figure 3-4).

For analytical purposes, the compression of clays under applied load can be divided into three 
phases: 
1. Initial compression 
2. Primary consolidation 
3. Secondary compression 
The initial compression occurs with the application of a load increment in a laboratory test and before 
the drainage begins. It occurs due to compression of small gas voids within the pore spaces and 
bedding down the contact surfaces in the cell and in the load frame. This phase is responsible for a 
deviation from the theoretical curve near the beginning of a load increment. (Head, 1994) 
The primary consolidation represents the time – dependent compression and occurs due to dissipation 
of the excess pore pressure under loading. When the load is removed, swelling can happen if the soil 
has access to water. For most of the clays, the primary consolidation phase relates closely to the 
theoretical curve. The secondary compression phase continues after the excess pore pressure of the 
primary phase has dissipated. This secondary compression is not recoverable after the load is removed, 
but secondary swelling has been observed. (Head, 1994)

Rate Effects 
The rate effects influence the strength and stiffness of the soil and for this reason, they are taken into 
consideration in many areas of geotechnical engineering. Although it represents an important factor, 
the impact of rate effects is hard to determine and analyse. One of the most used assumption in terms 
of rate effects is that of Kulhawy and Mayne (1990) which states that for every increase in strain rate, 
a ten percent increase in shear strength is expected. This assumption doesn’t take into account any 
variation of rate effects with strain level (Brown, 2009), as it is based on a correlation using peak 
shear strengths. Only undrained testing was used which means that the impact of drainage on 
determining the rate effect was not taken into account. However, the effect of drainage is significant 
as the strain rate influences whether there is time for drainage to occur. This being the case, the rate 
effects tend to follow an ‘U shaped’ curve (Figure 3-15). (Robinson & Brown, 2013)

During all tests, the specimen was inundated in the beginning of the third load step (70kPa).  The 
water was poured before the stress level of 70 kPa was reached, in order to prevent swelling. The 
figures presented above show that for most of the samples the consolidation curves do not follow the 
theoretical consolidation curve (see section 3.3.1), especially for the 3rd and 4th loading steps, a stress 
level of 35, respectively 140 kPa. After observing this difference in the first four tests (samples 1, 2, 
3 and 4), the decision of increasing the consolidation time from 24 h to 48 h was taken, for the last 
loading step of all the upcoming tests. The decision was made with the aim to start the shear phase 
from a 100% degree of consolidation (U). Although the duration of the consolidation time was 
increased, no significant changes can be noticed in the behaviour of the soil during the consolidation 
stages. 
Observation: the DSS apparatus interpreted the consolidation steps as fully consolidated. The method 
used by the software to interpret the test results, during the consolidation phase, was the “Square – 
Root – Time Method”. 
A very strange response in the consolidation phase of samples 4, 5, 6 and 7 during the second stress 
level (35kPa) and on samples 1 and 2 during the third stress level (75kPa) was observed. These 
samples experienced very large deformations. A reason can be that in the DSS device there is a space 
between the sample and the Teflon rings, thus no % condition is established. This leads to the 
development of radial deformations. On the other hand, in the ILO tests, the % condition is ensured 
since he sample is totally restrained by the steel ring. The large deformations experienced by samples 
1, 2, 4, 5, 6 and 7 might also be explained by the fact that the samples were sheared during the 
consolidation process. Due to radial strains, the observed lateral deformations are not only from the 
consolidation process, but also because of radial deformations. 
In the present study, it is assumed that this is the case; it is known that all the deformation were not 
actual deformations, but due to time restrictions it is not considered and a method for compensation 
was not found, so further study is recommended in order to solve these uncertainties. 
From the above mentioned observations, the following effects can be observed on the obtained results 
and on the state of sample before shearing: 
(1) The samples were not fully consolidated before the shearing phase, influencing the water 
content at the end of the test. According to Terzaghi, “consolidation is any process which 
involves a decrease in water content of saturated soil without replacement of water by air”. 
This means a reduction in volume due to water expulsion under long term loading. From the 
obtained water contents presented in Table 4-1, it can be observed that the water content did 
not decrease. 
(2) Assuming shearing of the samples during the consolidation phase might result in a lower 
undrained shear strength during the shearing phase 
(3) Taking into account the radial deformations, it is known that the soil samples expand until 
getting totally restrained, this resulting in a larger diameter of the sample (Figure 5-5). This 
fact has an influence on the obtained results, as the area is changing, meaning that the values 
of normal effective stresses and shear stresses are overestimated.

5.2 DIRECT SIMPLE SHEAR – STRENGTH PARAMETERS 
The strength of an undrained soil is solely dependent on the undrained shear strength, >  (*. The 
effective strength parameters computed with Mohr – Coulomb failure envelope, maximum undrained 
shear strengths, shear rates, normalized velocities and overconsolidation ratios are presented in Table

From Table 5-3 it can be noticed that there is a difference between the undrained shear strength values 
for the NC samples at different shear rates. It seems that for samples 1, 5, 6, 2 and 7, at lower shear 
rates, the undrained shear strength is increasing and there is a difference of 3kPa between the obtained 
values, while the cohesion and friction angles give similar values, so it can be said that there is a 
repeatability between these samples as following any pattern cannot be the case when there are only 
few performed tests. However, an increasing of the undrained shear strength was observed with 
increasing the stress level. 
For the OC samples, it can be observed that for samples 3 and 8, at different shear rates, the undrained 
shear strength and the effective parameters give similar values. The same behaviour should be 
expected from the other two OC samples, 4 and 9, but in this case the undrained shear strength 
decreases with a decreasing in the shear rate and there is a very high cohesion and very low friction 
angles, so there is no repeatability observed in the OC samples. In case of the OC samples, it can be 
assumed that there is no rate effect, but maybe there is an effect of the overconsolidation. 
In the unloading of clay in the presence of water, the clay expands and the result is an increae in void 
ratio, while the undrained shear strength is decreasing. 
From section 5.1 it is known that shear phase might have started from different degree of 
consolidation (U), which might result in different shear strengths. However, from the results given 
by the DSS device, computed with “Square – Root – Time Method”, the samples were 100 % 
consolidated. 
Due to a small number of tests, the results on the strength parameters are inconclusive. For more 
precise results, the number of tests should be increased and a new investigation should be performed 
that will provide an increased chance for obtaining conclusive results.

6 CONCLUSIONS 
The work presented in this master thesis was performed in order to enlarge the knowledge on the 
behaviour of highly plastic Røsnæs clay. This chapter has the aim of presenting an overview of all 
the findings of this project. 
In the first part of the project, a characterisation of the soil was performed. The liquid limit was found 
with Casagrande method, although the Eurocode recommends the Fall Cone method, which is 
actually not recommended in case of highly plastic clays. A liquid limit of 80% was found, 
characterising the Røsnæs Clay as a highly plastic clay. The water contents have been measured in 
the beginning and in the end of each test. By assuming the soil to be fully saturated at the end of the 
tests, the final void ratio has been calculated at the end of the test and the initial void ratios have been 
back calculated for each load step. 
Five incremental loading oedometer tests have been analysed on different size samples. The ‘Log 
Time Method’ was used in order to determine the consolidation parameters. The preconsolidation 
stresses have been computed using Casagrande (1936) and Janbu (1969) methods. Both methods gave 
similar values, but it could be observed that Janbu method was considered more precise, showing 
values almost identical to the preconsolidation stress of 120 kPa. 
 Nine undrained direct simple shear tests have been performed at constant shearing rates. The 
consolidation properties have been computed in the same way as for the ILO tests and strength 
parameters have been obtained using Mohr – Coulomb failure criteria. Five of the DSS tests were 
done on normally consolidated samples, while the other four tests were done on overconsolidated 
samples. 
While interpreting the results from tests 1, 2, 3 and 4 in the DSS, it was observed that the consolidation 
curves do not follow the theoretical consolidation curve. The decision to increase the consolidation 
time was taken with the aim of starting the shear phase from 100% degree of consolidation. However, 
the DSS device interpreted the consolidation steps as fully consolidated, using the ‘Square-Root-
Time method’. 
From the time curves it was observed that some of the samples experienced very large deformations. 
A reason could be that in the DSS device there is a space between the sample and the Teflon rings, 
thus no % condition is established. This led to the development of radial deformations. The large 
deformations experienced by the samples might also be explained by the fact that the samples were 
sheared during the consolidation process. Due to radial strains, the observed lateral deformations 
were not only from the consolidation process, but also because of radial deformations. 
This behaviour influenced the results and the state of the sample as follows: 
- 
The samples were not fully consolidated before the shearing phase, influencing the water content 
at the end of the test. 
- 
Taking into account the radial deformations, it is known that the soil samples expand until getting 
totally restrained, this resulting in a larger diameter of the sample, meaning that the values of 
normal effective stresses and shear stresses were overestimated. 
- 
Assuming shearing of the samples during the consolidation phase might result in a lower 
undrained shear strength during the shearing phase. 
It could be noticed a significant scatter between all samples. This was due to large vertical 
deformations, homogeneity of the samples and that they were not laterally restrained, which resulted 
==PAGE:66==
 
RECONSTITUTED PROPERTIES OF RØSNÆS CLAY 
MASTER THESIS 
 
56 
August 5, 2016
in larger settlements. The scatter was significant for supposedly homogenous material. The 
inhomogeneity of the samples could be proved after the test or before the test, since the samples were 
consolidated, respectively they should have been destroyed prior to testing. These findings led to the 
assumption of inconsistency in the samples; even though the material was reconstituted, not all the 
samples were homogenous.  
The ILO oedometer tests were found to give more precise results on the behaviour of the clay, 
comparing to the results obtained during the consolidation phase of the DSS tests. The difference in 
the sample size during the ILO tests did not have any influence on the results. 
The results observed in the DSS tests at higher stresses seem to be more reliable when related to the 
ILO analysis. Thus, going further with stress levels higher than 280 kPa, would result in a better 
interpretation of data. 
The undrained shear strengths were found to increase with increasing stresses in the NC tests. Due to 
a small number of tests, the results on the strength parameters are inconclusive. For more precise 
results, the number of tests should be increased and a new investigation should be performed that will 
provide an increased chance for obtaining conclusive results. 
The SHANSEP parameters S and λ were found to be 0.213 and 0.707. 
6.1 RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK 
Some recommendations for future work are going to be proposed in order to get better results on 
highly plastic clays behaviour, when performing Direct Simple Shear tests. 
 If the Teflon rings will be changed or some others will be manufactured, the tests should 
be performed again on low stress levels 
 A significant reason for the large scatter was mainly due to radial deformations. In case 
of using the Shear – Trac II device from American producer Geocomp, new stack of rings 
or wire reinforced membrane should be used in order for the sample to be laterally 
restrained. 
 A step forward for this study would be to compare the intact properties to the reconstituted 
properties of Røsnæs Clay.

8. Calibration 
The “Calibrate” menu provides options for calibrating the transducers of the system as well as determining the machine compressibility.  
“Machine” opens the “Machine Calibration” window that is used to calibrate the compensation needed for the compressibility of the 
LoadTrac – II load during test.  
1. Use a standard consolidation test. Prepare a sample where clay is replaced with a metal block. Place the sample in the shear box, 
putting a porous stone on the bottom and one at the top and then place the top cap onto the porous stone. Only the vertical loading 
mechanism is used for calibration. 
2. Open “Machine Calibration” menu and in the first column enter the stress values that are to be applied for each step during the 
machine compressibility test. The value should be those from the first column of the Consolidation table. After entering the values 
in the increasing order, continue the entries by using the same values, but in decreasing order. 
3. Click the “Step” button. The crossbar will lower until the stress value you have entered has been reached. When the platen stops 
moving, click the Step button again. Values will appear in the second and third columns, and the cross bar will again move down. 
When it stops, click the Step button again to record values and start the next step. 
4. If, at the end of a step you click the Read button instead of the Step button, values will be recorded in the second and third columns 
but the next step will not begin. This allows you to look at the graph of the results (by clicking the Graph button) while the 
calibration is in progress. To start the next step, click the Step button. 
5. You may have to cycle the loading sequence several times to get rid of the plastic deformation in the frame, so that you only 
account for the elastic deformation. 
6. When you have completed the calibration, we recommend you view the graph of the values (by clicking the Graph button) and 
examine it for reasonableness. Figure 6 shows a typical machine calibration graph. You may edit the values in the two End of Step 
columns on the Machine Calibration window, making any changes you consider necessary. 
7. When you are satisfied with the compressibility calibration, raise the crossbar using the vertical keypad on the front of the unit, then 
close the Machine Calibration window. Finally, save the results to your setup file for use in future tests by pulling down the File 
menu and clicking Save.
