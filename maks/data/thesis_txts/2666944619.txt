Abstract
This project has quantified the derived health effects of indoor environmental im-
provements due to residential renovations in Denmark. Associations between indoor
environmental quality and its effects on health and well-being were determined. The
most burdensome sources of exposure in the residential indoor environment were
particulate matter (PM) and mold/dampness and their associated health responses
included all-cause mortality, asthma, allergic rhinitis, chronic bronchitis and respira-
tory diseases.
The increased risks of asthma, allergic rhinitis and chronic bronchitis attributable
to mold and dampness were modelled as an function of different mold exposures,
expressed as a mold index. The risk of asthma and allergic rhinitis was higher for
children than adults, and the risk of asthma increased proportionally with the severity
of exposure.
Reduced exposures to PM were associated with a decreased risk of all-cause mortality,
chronic bronchitis and health-care visits due to asthma, respiratory and cardiovascular
diseases.
Each health outcome was economically valuated as either a cost of illness or a willing-
ness to pay. The health outcomes with the biggest potential savings due to residential
renovations were identified as chronic bronchitis for mold and dampness and all-cause
mortality for PM.
The exposure-response functions and their valuations were integrated in a building
performance simulation tool named RIEM. The tool quantifies the potential health
benefits of an improved indoor residential environment as a result of renovation.
The tool was tested and validated using indoor climate measurement data from a
renovation project in Svendborg.
The validated tool estimated the health-related
savings of the renovation as up to 9,500 DKK per child and up to 37,000 DKK per
adult. The total health-related savings of the renovation project of 250 apartments
was estimated as up to 15 million DKK.

Resumé
Dette projekt har kvantificeret de afledte sundhedsmæssige effekter af indeklimatiske
forbedringer grundet renovering af boliger i Danmark. Forholdene mellem kvaliteten
af indeklimaet og dets indflydelse på sundhed blev kortlagt. Eksponering til partikler
(PM) samt skimmel og fugt blev udpeget som de mest sundhedsskadelige indeklima-
tiske parametre i hjemmet, og deres påvirkning på sundhed indebar øgede risici for
dødelighed, astma, allergisk rhinitis, kronisk bronkitis og luftvejssygdomme.
Forholdet mellem eksponering til fugt og skimmel og øgede risici for astma, allergisk
rhinitis og kronisk bronkitis blev modelleret som funktion af forskellige skimmel ek-
sponeringer, udtrykt ved skimmel-indekset. Risikoen for astma og allergisk rhinitis
var højere for børn end voksne, og risikoen for astma steg proportionelt med graden
af eksponering.
Reduceret eksponering til PM var associeret med reducerede risici for dødelighed,
kronisk bronkitis og kontakter med sundhedsvæsenet grundet astma, luftvejs- og
hjertekarsygdomme.
Ovenstående sundhedsbyrder blev økonomisk værdisat som enten en økonomisk byrde
til samfundet, eller som betalingsviljen for at reducere risikoen for en sygdomsbyrde.
Sygdomsbyrderne med de største økonomiske besparelsespotentialer som følge af ren-
overing af boliger blev udpeget som kronisk bronkitis for fugt og skimmel og døde-
lighed for PM.
Eksponerings-respons funktionerne og deres værdisætninger blev integreret i et simu-
leringsværktøj kaldet RIEM. Værktøjet kvantificerede de potentielle sundhedsmæs-
sige gevinster ved indeklima renoveringer i et boligbyggeri.
Værktøjet blev testet og valideret gennem indeklimadata fra et renoveringsprojekt
i Svendborg. Det validerede værktøj estimerede de sundhedsmæssige besparelser af
renoveringen til at være op til 9,500 DKK per barn og op til 37,000 DKK per voksen.
De totale sundhedsmæssige besparelser ved renoveringen af de 250 lejligheder blev
estimeret til at være op til 15 millioner DKK.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Danes spend on average 80-90% of their time indoors, and 66% of their time at
home. This means that by the age of 80, we’ve spent 64 years of our life inside a
building, 48 years in the confines of our home [9]. We’ve (d)evolved from an outdoors
hunter-gatherer species to what Velux has coined ”The Indoor Generation”. [124].
Because so much of our time is spent indoors, the quality of our indoor environment
has a significant impact on our environmental health. Environmental health is the
branch of public health concerned with all aspects of the natural and built environ-
ment and its effect on health [2]. The fundamental concept of environmental health
is the association between exposure and response; that exposure to an undesirable
environmental condition will result in an undesirable bodily response; that an action
will elicit a reaction.
The Danish Center for Indoor Climate and Health in Residences (CISBO) has esti-
mated that approximately 22.000 disability-adjusted life years are lost in Denmark
each year due to an unhealthy indoor climate [21].
By improving our indoor environmental quality (IEQ) through renovations, we may
reduce our exposure to undesirable environmental conditions, thereby leading to a
reduction in undesirable health responses. The aim of this report is to quantify the
associations between residential IEQ and environmental health, and to quantify the
health impacts and economic improvement potentials of residential renovations. The
purpose of this effort is to inform and incentivize renovation strategies and policy-
making to improve the built environment, thereby improving our health and well-
being.

1.1
Motivation
This study is part of a larger EU project entitled EERAdata. EERAdata aims to
accelerate the implementation of energy eﬀiciency (EE) in buildings across Europe
through data-driven assessment of the impacts of energy eﬀicient renovation actions
(EERA). In doing so, EERAdata aims to support and incentivize policy-making to-
wards an energy eﬀicient and decarbonized building stock. [31]
EERAdata aims to develop a decision support tool (DST), which will aid local admin-
istrations in the collection and processing of their building and demographic data. The
EERAdata DST aims to provide a holistic understanding of the impact of EE invest-
ments, which quantifies the derived benefits of EE in ecological and socio-economic
terms as well as on its direct interactions with the broader policy context and energy
market.
One of the derived benefits of energy eﬀiciency renovations is improved health. The
indoor environmental module (IEM) of the DST aims to quantify the effect that the
indoor environment has on the comfort, health and performance of its occupants.
Building renovations have the potential to improve our built environment and the
quantification and valuation of this potential health improvement is one part of the
holistic understanding of the impact of EE investments in the EERAdata DST.
Europe has a rich diversity of building types, climatic conditions, energy systems,
norms and regulations.
Eventually, the DST will encompass the largest possible
range of these diversities, but at the current stage of development, it is based on a set
of data, methodologies and algorithms that are limited to a few building typologies
and geographic locations.
This study aims to add to the existing database and methodology, by assessing and
quantifying the health burden and potential health benefits of residential renovations
in Denmark.

1.2
General Methodology
The general methodology to assess the health burden and identify potential health
improvements associated with IEQ is the Impact Pathway Approach (IPA). Origi-
nally developed for the Externalities of Energy (ExternE) project, the IPA provides
a framework for economically quantifying the environmental impact of a harmful
source, by tracing its pathway from source to cost [38].
For the purposes of this study, the generalized IPA has been adapted to model the eco-
nomic burden of health responses associated with inadequacies in the indoor environ-
ment, based on the exposure-response model of environmental health. The adapted
Health Impact Pathway Approach (HIPA) has four principal steps: source, exposure,
response and cost.
SOURCE
EXPOSURE
RESPONSE
COST
Figure 1.2: Health impact pathway approach, conceptual model.
Source: The source of a harmful indoor agent is the object, place or event from
which it was initially released into the environment.
Determining factors include
the source strength, which expresses the rate at which the agent is released, and the
environmental media, which express which physical medium the agent is released into.
An example of a source in the indoor environment would be burning candles, which
release harmful particles into the air.
Exposure: Once a harmful agent has been released, humans are at risk of exposure.
Determining factors include micro-environmental occupancy, which expresses where
we are exposed to the agent and for how long, and the route of exposure, which
expresses how the agent moves from the external environment into the body. In the
case of candle burning, exposure to particles chiefly happens in the micro-environment
of the home, and the primary exposure route is inhalation.
Response: The presence of harmful agents in the body risk eliciting internal biomark-
ers; these are the physical indicators of exposure, which signal that the bodily systems
are at risk. When internal biomarkers reach critical levels, they lead to an adverse
health response. When burning candles, internal biomarkers include irritation of the
eyes and throat, and long-term exposure can lead to cardiovascular diseases and lung
cancer.
Cost: In order to quantify the burden of this health response in a metric that is both

easily understandable and readily comparable with other improvement potentials, the
burden of disease of the health response is quantified by a monetary valuation.
By tracing the health impact pathway of harmful agents which we are exposed to
in the indoor environment, and quantifying the health impacts that they elicit in
the body in a unified monetary metric, we may quantify the total burden of disease
attributable to the insuﬀicient indoor environment and the improvement potentials
of policies and actions which break the impact pathway chain.
This methodology has been applied to evaluate the environmental health improve-
ment potentials of building renovations in Danish homes. The fundamental hypoth-
esis is that a building renovation results in an improvement of the IEQ, which has
the potential to mitigate our exposure to harmful agents, leading to a measurable
improvement in health and well-being. The effects of a building renovation on the
indoor environment, and the derived health effects that this results in, have been
modelled and quantified in an extension of the IEM called the Residential Indoor
Environmental Module (RIEM).

2 Literature
The study objective was first examined through a random search, where search terms
were evaluated based on the quality and relevance of the papers they produced. Search
engines were used to locate relevant literature in the fields of engineering and public
health, including DTU Findit, Scopus, Web of Science and PubMed.
The most
relevant search terms in the categories of IEQ, health, renovations and economic
valuation were identified based on the quantity and quality of relevant papers found.
Sources on the indoor environment were categorized into four categories: thermal
environment, air quality, light and acoustics. The initial search led to the discovery
that there was a very limited number of publications concerning the associations
between health and light or acoustics. On the other hand, several key sources [124]
[21] [98] found significant associations between health and the thermal environment
and indoor air quality of residential buildings in Denmark. These findings were used
to narrow the scope of the study, which ultimately led to the decision to investigate the
associations between health and the two indoor climate parameters with the largest
estimated attributable health burden in residencies: mold/dampness and particulate
matter.
Based on these results, a cited relevant search was conducted to increase the depth
of knowledge in this focused scope. A number of relevant meta-analyses were found
which quantified the burden of different diseases attributable to mold and dampness,
and their included studies were retrieved and investigated.
The cited relevant search for particulate matter located several studies and meth-
ods which quantified the association between outdoor ambient PM concentrations
and a range of health responses.
Relevant literature on modelling the concentra-
tions of mold/dampness and particulate matter in an indoor residential environment
was located under the guidance of Jørn Toftum (Professor, DTU) and Gabriel Bekö
(Associate Professor, DTU).
Relevant sources on the state of health were located from registries.
The Global
burden of Disease (GBD) was identified as the primary source on the global state of
health, and the primary sources on the Danish state of health were the National Pa-
tient Registry, Cause of Death Registry and the Registry of Selected Chronic Diseases
and Mental Illnesses [115].
Lastly, a quality assurance check was performed. Each study was evaluated based
on its relevance, year of publication, size of population etc. Literature which passed
the quality assurance was selected and used in the study.
Relevant findings and
associations between mold/dampness, PM and environmental health will be further
discussed in the Methods and Results section.

3.1.1
Indoor Air Quality
Indoor air quality (IAQ) refers to the purity of the air in an indoor environment.
It is determined by the concentration of harmful agents present in the air such
as dust, suspended particles and pollutants. [108]
Externally sourced harmful agents pollute the outside air, which then enters
the building through ventilation or infiltration. Combustion processes from motor
vehicles, solid fuel burning and industry are some of the primary sources of external
air pollution, along with natural sources such as dust suspension due to erosion, radon
gas emitted from the earth’s crust, and pesticides from agriculture.
Common internal sources of harmful agents include the products and materials in
the environment, which may release chemical compounds into the air through the
process of off-gassing, and occupant activities such as cooking, heating, cleaning,
candle-burning and environmental tobacco smoke, not to mention exhaled breathing.
The indoor air quality is regulated by the exchange between outdoor and indoor air.
This air exchange may occur either via infiltration through the building envelope, via
natural ventilation through open doors and windows, or via mechanical ventilation
through fans and filters. The Danish Standards recommend different design ventila-
tion rates for diluting emissions from the occupants and building based on how much
the building is polluting: These rates are listed in appendix A.1.
Figure 3.5: Most common means of air exchange: infiltration, natural ventilation
and mechanical ventilation.
3.1.2
Thermal Environment
The thermal environment refers to all aspects which affect the temperature and
humidity of the indoor environment, and the perceived thermal comfort of its
occupants. The quality of the thermal environment is a product of personal and
environmental parameters.
Personal parameters include clothing insulation
and activity levels. The human metabolism produces heat and moisture, and
human occupancy therefore affects the thermal environment.
==PAGE:25==

Environmental parameters include the outdoor thermal environment and indoor heat-
ing, cooling and moisture control, both from mechanical regulatory systems such and
boilers and air conditioners, and from occupant-driven sources such as window open-
ings and equipment use. The recommended indoor levels of relative humidity (RH)
and temperature are listed in appendix A.1.
3.1.3
Mold and Dampness
Building dampness refers to the unwanted presence and penetration of moisture
on and in the structure of a building, including walls, floors and roof. It results
in visible wetting of surfaces, blistering paint, bulging plaster and, under critical
circumstances, mold growth on surfaces and fabrics, which risk compromising
the health of the occupants [8].
Building dampness is the result of moisture intrusion from either the internal or
external environment. The most common causes of persistent dampness in buildings
are condensation, rising damp and penetrating damp.
Figure 3.6: Most common causes of dampness: condensation, rising damp and pen-
etrating damp.
Condensation occurs when moist air cools below its dew point, causing the water
in the air to condense. This may occur as surface condensation, or within the fab-
ric of the building as interstitial condensation. Condensation typically occurs when
warm air from the internal environment is cooled upon contact with colder building
structures.
Rising damp is caused by capillary action, whereby water in the ground travels up
through porous building materials such as concrete, brick or plaster.
Penetrating damp is caused by moisture from the external environment that pen-
etrates through the fabric of a building, typically as a result of leaking roofs and
pipework or cracks and openings in walls.

Dampness risks eliciting mold growth. While not all damp homes have mold growth,
all moldy homes have dampness. Mold is a fungus that grows by spreading spores
which grow into colonies. While not necessarily toxic, certain molds produce toxins
that risk eliciting negative health effects in humans, including asthma and AR.
Mold spores are a common component of household and workplace dust; nearly all
indoor air contains some quantity of mold spores. When these spores are present
in large quantities, they may become toxic under the right circumstances. The key
conditions required for mold growth are sustenance, appropriate temperatures and
moisture.
Molds will feed on any substance containing organic material. Many of the natural
materials found in the built environment provide suitable sustenance for mold growth.
Molds can survive and grow in temperatures close to freezing, and will thrive in
warmer environments.
The critical condition for mold growth in the built environment is moisture. Most
molds require a relative humidity of at least 80% to thrive. Maintaining a relative
humidity below 80% is therefore the most effective way of preventing mold growth in
buildings.
The prevalence of dampness and mold in European and Danish dwellings has been
examined by EuroStat, in their survey of European Income and Living Conditions
(EU SILC) [34]. In 2019, 14.9% of the surveyed Danish population reported they
were living in a dwelling with a leaking roof, damp walls, floors or foundation, or rot
in window frames of floor. This rate varied between 4.2 - 36.6% for other European
countries.

3.1.4
Particulate Matter
Particulate matter (PM) is a general term for microscopic solid or liquid parti-
cles which are suspended in the air. These particles vary considerably in weight,
shape and size, with diameters ranging from 2nm to 100µm - a spectrum of 5
orders of magnitude [50].
PM is categorized based on particle size. The two most common categorizations are
PM10 and PM2.5. PM10 encompasses PM with a mean diameter of 10µm or less
(coarse particles), while PM2.5 encompasses PM with a mean diameter of 2.5µm or
less (fine particles).
PM originates from both natural sources and human activities. In the ambient air,
coarse particles primarily originate from dust, and are produced by mechanical pro-
cesses such as construction activities and road dust re-suspension and natural pro-
cesses such as erosion. Fine particles primarily originate from combustion processes,
and are predominantly caused by human activity.
Exposure to PM is recognized as one of the main contributors to environmental
disease.
PM2.5 is the leading environmental risk factor for the global burden of
disease with an estimated 3 million attributable deaths in 2017 [55]. Inhalation of
particulate matter may cause cardiovascular and respiratory diseases, lung cancer and
premature death [46].
The primary route of exposure of PM is inhalation, and the size of the inhaled particles
is a key determinant of the severity of the resulting health responses. Particle size
determines where in the respiratory tract particles are deposited. Particles coarser
than PM10 are generally deposited in the nose and throat, causing minimal damage.
PM10 are typically deposited deep inside the lungs, and PM2.5 can even penetrate
the lung barrier and enter the blood stream. The fraction of particles deposited in

3.1.4.1
Indoor Particulate Matter
The indoor concentration of PM is a product of both external and internal sources.
The amount of ambient PM which enters the building is a product of the air change
rate, which may be regulated by infiltration, natural and mechanical ventilation [132].
Natural ventilation through window openings does not filter the ambient air, meaning
that any ambient PM concentration is transported directly into the building. The
same is not necessarily the case for infiltration. Depending on the size of the trans-
ported particles and cracks in the building, there is a chance that larger particles do
not penetrate into the indoor environment.
The rate of particle penetration through cracks is expressed by the penetration factor,
P, which depends on the number and size of cracks in the building envelope. The
empirical study by Ozkaynak et al. [83] measured the penetration factor in typical
residential homes in the US for both PM10 and PM2.5, and found that the factor
was equal to unity for both particle sizes (P = 1).
Mechanical ventilation has the potential to filter the incoming air. By doing so, it
is possible to significantly reduce the PM concentration of the air which enters the
building. As is the case for particle deposition in the lungs, the eﬀiciency of these

3.2.2
Exposure-Response Function
In the field of epidemiology, the exposure-response function (ERF) describes the
relative rate of response as a function of the magnitude of exposure to a stimulus or
stressor after a period of time, and is illustrated by an exposure-response curve (ERC).
Exposure-response functions are typically modelled using logistic regression, in which
the probability of a binary dependent variable (the health outcome) is modelled as a
function of an independent variable (the magnitude of exposure).
ERC vary in appearance, and can be either linear or non-linear, either with or without
a boundary threshold. The simplest ERC is the linear no-threshold model (LNT),
in which the relative risk of response is linearly proportional to the magnitude of
exposure with no lower threshold.
In the case of a log-linear exposure-response function, such as the linear no-threshold
model, the relative risk (RR) can be expressed as:

3.2.5
Burden of Disease
By determining the fraction of diseases attributable to environmental risk factors,
and quantifying the total prevalence of these diseases, it is possible to estimate the
total health burden attributable to the environmental risk factors, otherwise known
as the environmental burden of disease (EBD).
The EBD is a subcategory of the BoD. Burdens of disease may be calculated at
any scale in which representative ERFs, accurate measurements of exposures, and
prevalence data on health outcomes are available. This is done annually on a global
scale by the WHO, in their Global Burden of Disease Study (GBD), a systematic
analysis of the burden of 369 diseases and injuries in 204 countries and territories.
In the GBD, the loss of healthy life years due to mortality (death) and morbidity
(disability) is measured in the metric disability-adjusted life years (DALY). [129]

either the incidence or prevalence of the disability case. The incidence-based YLD
is calculated as the number of incident disability cases, multiplied by the average
duration of the disability, multiplied by a disability weight. The disability weight is a
factor used to quantify and compare the severities of different health effects. It ranges
on a scale from 0 (perfect health) to 1 (equivalent to death). The prevalence-based

year, and in this regard more closely resembles the YLL, which inherently takes an
incidence perspective. Its major disadvantages are that health data on prevalence is
far more common than incidence, that it does not reflect the current prevalent burden
of the disease, and that it does not accurately depict the age distribution at which
the disease is burdensome. For these reasons, the prevalence-based YLD approach
has been used by the WHO since 2010, and is the preferred approach in this study.
3.2.7
Diseases Associated with Mold/Dampness and Particulate
Matter
The impact of mold/dampness and PM on health has been extensively researched,
and associations have been observed with a multitude of health responses.
Mold/dampness in the indoor environment is primarily associated with respiratory
diseases, most notably asthma, allergic rhinitis and chronic bronchitis. Associations
have been found in infants, children and adults and some evidence for a dose-response
relationship has been demonstrated [122].
Prolonged exposure to PM2.5/PM10 has been researched in numerous epidemiological
studies, which have found associations with adverse health effects including premature
mortality, lung cancer, asthma, chronic bronchitis, upper respiratory diseases and
cardiopulmonary diseases [89] [3] [53].

3.2.7.1
Asthma
Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways. The inflam-
mation causes the airways to spasm and narrow periodically, resulting in
airway hyper-responsiveness and airflow obstruction. Common symptoms
of asthma include wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness and cough. In-
fants and children are particularly susceptible to asthma, because children
have smaller airways than adults [70].
The World Health Organization estimates that 300 million people suffer from asthma,
and that with current rising trends this will reach 400 million by 2025. This is because
the relative prevalence of asthma is higher in developed countries than in developing,
and as the global standard of living increases, so too does the relative prevalence of
asthma. An estimated 250.000 people die prematurely from asthma every year [129].
In Denmark, there were 430.620 registered cases of asthma in 2017 amongst Danes
5 years or older. The average relative prevalence of asthma in Danes aged 5 years
and above was 7.9%, and the relative prevalences in children (<15 years old) and
adults were 8.85% and 7.72% respectively [100]. The relative prevalence for children
is a conservative estimate, given that the relative prevalence is highest among infants,
and that this age group was not included in the data-set. Asthma is categorized as
either mild, moderate or severe, with corresponding disability weights of 0.015, 0.036
and 0.133 [55]. Mild asthma is characterized by wheezing and cough at least once
a month, which does not cause diﬀiculty with daily activities. Moderate asthma is
characterized by wheezing and cough once a week, and causes some diﬀiculty with

3.2.7.2
Allergic Rhinitis
Allergic rhinitis (AR), also known as hay fever, is an irritation and inflam-
mation of the mucous membrane inside the nose, which occurs when the
immune system overreacts to allergens in the air, such as pollens, animal
danders, dust mites and molds. Common symptoms of allergic rhinitis
include sneezing, a runny and stuffy nose, and itchy or watery eyes. Tired-
ness and concentration problems are also common [70].
Globally, the WHO estimates that 400 million people suffer from AR. As is the case
for asthma, epidemiological studies indicate that the relative prevalence of AR is
highest in developed countries, and that the global prevalence of AR is rising.
In Denmark, an estimated 1,264,730 people suffered from AR in 2017, at a a relative
prevalence of 21.9% in Danes aged 16 years or older [118]. This number is significantly
higher than the number of registered cases. A study from 2012 of 1277 Danes aged
18-69 years old found that 56% of AR cases had never been given a diagnosis, and
that 44% of cases had not received any form of treatment in the previous 12 months
[40].

3.2.7.3
Chronic Bronchitis
Chronic bronchitis (CB) is an inflammation and irritation of the bronchial
tubes, which is characterized by chronic coughing, hypersecretion of mu-

contributing to chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), defined as
a chronic inflammatory lung disease that causes obstructed airflow from

9 million of these have CB. Whether the same distribution applies to Denmark is
unclear, as danish medical statistics rarely divides COPD into subcategories.

3.3
Economic Burden of Disease
The DALY metric is a useful tool for quantifying and comparing the health bur-
dens of different diseases in a unified standard of measurement. For the purposes of
policy-making, an economic quantification of the burden of disease is similarly use-
ful. By quantifying the burden of disease in a monetary metric, policy-makers can
weigh the economic costs of health investments against the economic gains of health
improvements in a cost-benefit analysis, which may inform incentivized, prioritized
policy-making. There are two primary methods of economical quantification of health
burdens; these are the willingness-to-pay and cost-of-illness.
==PAGE:46==
32
3 Background
3.3.1
Willingness-to-Pay
Willingness-to-pay (WTP) is the term used to express the maximum price a person is
willing to pay for a product or service. In the field of public health, WTP is the main
method used to quantify the monetary value of a statistical life (VSL) and the value
of a statistical life year (VOLY). These values are determined in contingent valuation
studies, surveys in which respondents are asked to quantify their willingness to pay
for mortality risk reductions. By translating mortality risk reductions to saved lives
and life years, the WTP to reduce mortality and morbidity is estimated.
In the case of mortality, the WTP to achieve a marginal reduction in the risk of death
has been used to quantify the VSL. VSL is not a metric of how much a person’s life is
worth, but rather a unit of measurement used for risk assessment. VSL estimates are
often used in the transport sector to economically quantify the benefit of car accident
preventions.
In health economics, where diseases are often more predictable and treatable than car
accidents, resulting outcomes are typically less acute and morbid. For this reason, a
more commonly used metric of economically evaluating the burden of diseases is the
VOLY, a value assigned to each DALY lost, rather than to each premature death.
Both the VSL and VOLY are expected to vary from country to country due to cultural
and financial differences.
In Denmark, the Danish Ministry of Finance estimates
the VSL to be 4.6 million euros (34 million DKK), and the VOLY to be 190,000
euros (1.4 Million DKK) (2019 prices) [35]. This VOLY is significantly higher than
that determined by similar valuation studies in Europe. The Externalities of Energy
(ExternE) project, whose Impact Pathway Approach informed the methodology of
this paper, quantifies its economic burden based on a contingent valuation study
conducted in the UK, Italy and France, which found a median VOLY for the European
Union of approximately 74,000 euros (2019 prices) [38].
The WTP method represents an economic approximation of the value of health. Such
a quantification is undoubtedly associated with a high level of uncertainty, due to the
subjective nature of the hypothetical valuation surveys, and the conceptual diﬀiculty
in economically quantifying health and safety. In spite of this, WTP provides the
best estimate in our attempts to economically quantify the countless tangibles and
intangibles associated with a healthy life. For this paper, the valuation of a statistical
life by the Danish Ministry of Finance has been used. If the scope of the paper were
to be expanded to a European context, the valuation determined in the ExternE
study would be deemed more appropriate. With the VOLY determined, the WTP to
remove a BoD is given as:
WTP = V OLY · DALY

3.3.2
Cost-of-Illness
The second method of economic quantification is the Cost-of-Illness (COI). This
method aims to quantify the costs to society caused by an illness which are clearly
documented and inarguable. Where the WTP aims to estimate the value of the mul-
titude of intangibles associated with a healthy life, the COI focuses solely on the
tangible, measurable economic burden to society.
The COI are typically categorized into two components: direct and indirect costs.
Direct costs are those expenses incurred by the use of health care services, such as
visits to doctors, specialists and hospitals, as well as medical and pharmaceutical
expenses. Indirect costs are those expenses incurred from reduced work as a result of
illness, including reduced productivity, work loss days and early retirement.
The direct COI are based on registry data on the number and types of interactions
with the healthcare sector and sales of medicine attributable to the disease.
By
combining these figures with the average costs of the healthcare interactions and
medicinal expenses, the direct economic burden attributable to the disease may be
quantified.
The indirect COI are based on an economic quantification of the reductions in pro-
ductivity and learning ability incurred by the illness. Reduced work productivity has
both an immediate and long-term impact on the profit margins of the business at
which the patient works, and potentially an impact on the patient herself, should the
illness lead to an eventual firing. Reduced learning ability has a long-term impact
on children and young adults taking an education and society as a whole, as reduced
learning is associated with a less economically successful career.
Because the indirect COI are largely associated with activities outside of the home,
and because this paper aims to quantify the economic burden of disease associated
with residential buildings, the choice was made to exclude indirect COI from the
economic quantification. There are a multitude of environmental factors which influ-
ence work productivity and learning ability, and while the indoor environment of the
home is certainly a contributor, the IEQ of the workplace or educational institution
is assumed to be a significantly more impactful determinant.

4.1
Exposure-Response Functions of Mold/Dampness
To determine the increased risk of asthma, allergic rhinitis and chronic bronchitis
attributable to mold and dampness, results from relevant studies were gathered in a
literature review in order to perform a meta-analysis.
The aim of a meta-analysis is to determine whether and to what extent predictors are
associated with effect size differences in an observed association. In a meta-analysis,
the aggregated data of different studies which investigate the same association are
pooled into a combined data-set, which may then be categorized based on differences
in predictors between and across studies [48].
This pooled data-set may then be fitted using a regression model which accounts for
the effect size differences of relevant predictors. The aim is to find the regression
model which explains the largest fraction of the current variability in effect sizes, and
therefore most accurately describes the observed variability. This fraction of explained
variability is quantified by the R2 value or similar goodness-of-fit models. Common
models of meta-analyses include the fixed-effects model, random-effects model and
mixed-effects model.
Several literature studies and meta-analyses investigated the increased risk of diseases
attributable to exposure to mold/dampness. One of the most extensive and relevant
meta-analyses was performed by Urlaub and Grün in 2016 [122], which aimed to in-
vestigate the relationship between mold/dampness and respiratory diseases including
asthma, cough etc. A total of 170 studies were identified and a total of 1,250 effect
sizes were summarized. They found increased risks of both asthma and other respira-
tory symptoms due to different mold exposures. Quansah and Jaakkola [92] and Fisk
et al. [36] [37] also conducted meta-analyses, where the risk of developing asthma
and chronic bronchitis due to residential dampness was investigated.
The findings from these meta-analyses were pooled into a data-set containing informa-
tion about the exposure type (grouped into four categories: water damage, dampness,
visible mold or mold odor), morbidity, age of test subjects, and observed effect size
(and their confidence intervals). The included studies are listed in table 4.1 and a full
overview is listed in appendix A.8.

4.1.1.1
Random-Effects Model
The simplest means of determining a pooled estimated effect is to use either the
fixed or random-effects model. Both models express a weighted average of the effects
observed in the individual studies, assuming that the true effect is either fixed or
varying between studies. Due to the variability in experimental methods between the
pooled studies, the random-effects model is by far the more representative of the two
models in the context of this meta-analysis.
The random-effects model uses the observed effect and standard error of each study
to calculate the estimated pooled effect.
Studies are weighted based on precision
using the standard error, as studies with a larger sample size and lower variability in
observations have a smaller standard error and therefore greater statistical precision.
The relative weighting of the 133 included studies varied between 0.2% and 1.1%.
The random-effects model found that the pooled estimated effect of mold/dampness
on the increased risk of asthma was 1.5450 [1.3778; 1.7122].
The simplest model is however rarely the most accurate, or the most insightful. One
method of determining whether a statistical model is overly simplified is with between-
study heterogeneity, which quantifies the between-study variance in observed effects.
High heterogeneity may be due to a lack of correlation between exposure and effect,
large differences in true effects between studies or differences in sample characteristics
(e.g. age of subjects).
There are several metrics of between-study heterogeneity, one of which is the per-
centage of variability in effect sizes which is not caused by sampling errors, I2. The
rule of thumb for interpreting this variable is that an I2 > 75% is an indication of
substantial heterogeneity. The percentage of variability for the random-effects model
was I2 = 87.7%[85.8%; 89.2%], an indication of high heterogeneity which requires
improvement.
4.1.1.2
Subgroup Analysis
One method of potentially improving the model is to account for subgroup variability.
By splitting the pooled data into subgroups based on shared characteristics, and
comparing the effects of these subgroups, it is possible to identify which subgroups
have significantly different effects, and are therefore predictors of the pooled estimated
effect.
Based on the categorizations observed in the literature review, it was hypothesized
that two subgroups were significant predictors: age groups and exposure groups. The
age groups included in the analysis were infants, children and adults, while the ex-
posure groups included were water damage, dampness, visible mold and mold odor.

4.1.1.3
Multivariate Mixed-Effects Model
Based on the results of the subgroup analyses, the hypothesis that age groups and
exposure groups were significant predictors of the estimated effect was accepted. With
the predictors determined, a linear multivariate mixed-effects model was constructed.
This model expresses the increased risk of asthma (OR) as a function of the mold
index.
The model includes both age and exposure as predictor variables. Age was included
as a predictor with two categories (child and adult), while exposure was included as
a continuous predictor, by converting the different exposure types to their equivalent
mold indices.
This conversion was performed by matching the visual descriptions of the different
values of the mold index defined by Ojanen et al. [81] to the four exposure types,
taking into account their respective estimated effects.
• Mold indices 0 and 1 were assumed to correspond to ”no exposure”.
• Mold index 2 was assumed to correspond to water damage, described as ”mi-
croscopic growth of mold colonies on surface”.
• Mold index 3 was assumed to correspond to dampness, described as ”visual
findings of mold on surface, <10% coverage”.
• Mold index 4 was assumed to correspond to visible mold, described as ”visual
findings of mold on surface, 10%-50% coverage”.
• Mold index 5 was assumed to correspond to mold odor, described as ”plenty of
growth on surface, >50% coverage”.
It was estimated that none of the exposures in the included studies corresponded
to mold index 6, defined as ”heavy and tight growth, coverage about 100%”. The
conversion from exposure types to the continuous mold index scale is illustrated in
figure 4.1.

With the predictors of the estimated effect defined, a generalized mixed-effects linear
meta-regression model could be expressed as:
θk = θ + β1 · x1,k + β2 · x2,k + ϵk + ξk
(4.2)
where θk is the estimated effect size, β1 and x1,k are the fixed effect and predictor
variable of the age group, β2 and x2,k are the fixed effect and predictor variable of
the mold index, and ϵk and ξk are independent error terms accounting for fixed and
random errors.
The generalized model deals only with additive relationships, meaning that the con-
tributions of individual predictors to the estimated effect are independent of each
other. In order to account for interdependence between predictors, an interaction
term needs to be included. The interaction term expresses the relationship between
one predictor variable (x1, age) and the estimated effect size changes for different
values of another predictor variable (x2, mold index). This additional interactional
relationship is denoted by introducing a third predictor effect, β3, which describes
the interaction between the two variables.
A linear mixed-effects interaction model with two predictor variables and a single
interactional relationship may be expressed as:
θk = θ + β1x1,k + β2x2,k + β3x1,kx2,k + ϵk + ξk
(4.3)

4.1.2.1
Random-Effects Model
A total of k = 56 studies on AR were included in the analysis, for which a random-
effects model was constructed. The relative weighting of the studies varied between
0.5% and 2.0%, and the model found that the weighted average effect of mold/damp-

A total of k = 30 studies on CB were included, yielding a relative weighting varying
between 0.3% and 5.1%.
The random-effects model found the estimated effect of
mold/dampness on the risk of CB as an OR of 1.6441 [1.4443; 1.8439].
As was the case for asthma, the test of heterogeneity showed substantial heterogeneity
for both AR and CB at I2 = 97% and I2 = 83% respectively, which warranted a
subgroup analysis.

exposure categories (p = 0.707 and p = 0.292).

4.4
Exposure-Response Functions of Particulate Matter
Numerous epidemiological studies have documented that increased exposure to par-
ticulate matter is correlated with adverse health effects, including premature mor-
tality, reduced lung function and cardiovascular diseases [1][45][82][89]. One of the
first exposure-response methodologies were modelled in the ExternE project in 1999
[38]. ExternE developed an Impact-Pathway approach with four steps from source
of emission to monetary valuation, and formed the basis of several exposure-response
methodologies including the CAFE programme (Clean Air for Europe) [127] [53] and
the EVA model (Economic Valuation of Air Pollution) [16].
The ExternE, CAFE and EVA projects all employed the ExternE Impact Pathway
Approach (IPA) to model the estimated health impacts of air pollution. The general
approach of this methodology was to quantify a relative risk of disease associated
with increased ambient PM concentrations, documented in a reliable epidemiological
study, to express a relative change in health endpoints per 10µg/m3 change in ambient
PM10 or PM2.5 concentration, and to link this with (i) the background rates of the
health endpoint in the target population, expressed as new cases (or events) per year
per unit population (for example per 100,000 people) (ii) the population size and (iii)
the relative change of exposure, expressed in µg/m3. Results were then expressed in
an impact response function (IRF) as a change in the number of cases or events in a
given population that were attributable to a change in PM concentrations.
However, these methodologies were all based on ambient concentrations of PM. Han-
ninen [47] and Zuraimi [109] both stress that personal PM exposures correlate poorly
with ambient concentrations, and that indoor sources make remarkable contribu-
tions to personal exposure. This was also in line with Ozkaynak, whose empirical
data showed that outdoor PM concentrations could explain around 16% of personal
exposure, indoor home PM concentrations could explain about 50% of personal expo-
sure, and outdoor and indoor home PM concentrations could explain about 66% of
personal exposure [83]. The remaining was attributed to occupancy in other micro-
environments, and spikes in personal exposure that were not picked up by static
outdoor and indoor PM monitors.
The average residential personal exposure of a Danish resident was determined us-
ing a mass-balance model and time-weighted micro-environmental model, based on
which the potential reduction of exposure due to renovations could be evaluated. The
average residential personal exposure was determined using the occupancy pattern
profile in figure 3.12. The residential personal exposure was calculated as a function
of time and as a product of indoor exposure and occupancy.
The exposure-response functions of PM included in this study were based on the
Impact-Response-Function (IRF) methodology.

A meta-analysis by Ward and Ayres [126] reviewed 12 studies on the associations
between PM10 concentrations and lower respiratory symptoms (LRS) and cough in
children (5-15 years old).
The LRS included in the studies were wheezing, chest
tightness and shortness of breath. The meta-analysis found a significant association

4.5
Economic Valuation of Particulate Matter
The economic burden of air pollution has been studied extensively, including in the
projects by ExternE, CAFE and EVA. These projects all assessed the economic bur-
den of disease using a combination of WTP and COI, to economically valuate the
impact response functions. The same methodology has been used in this study, with
the identified valuations of the health responses listed in table 4.11.
4.5.1
All-Cause Mortality
The value of a statistical life year (VOLY) is defined by the Danish Ministry of Finance
as 1.4 million DKK [35]. This valuation was used to quantify the economic burden
of disease of chronic mortality attributable to PM.
4.5.2
Lung Cancer Mortality
The willingness-to-pay to avoid lung cancer mortality was included in the economic
burden of all-cause mortality. However, a study by Gundgaard et al. determined that
there was a significant cost-of-illness associated with the treatment of lung cancer, and
estimated that the average COI of a case of lung cancer was 942 DKK to the primary
sector and 112.898 DKK to the secondary sector (2002 prices).
Moreover, a study by COWI [102] quantified the terminal care costs of fatal lung can-
cer diseases (hospices and other institutions) based on data from Amtsrådsforeningen.
The study concluded that 49.7% of patients with a fatal lung cancer condition died
at the hospital, 24.5% died at a nursing home or institution and 22.2% died at home.
The study found that the average terminal cost of illness of a fatal lung cancer con-
dition was 29,845 DKK (2002 prices).
By summing the costs of illness from these two studies, the total COI of a fatal case
of lung cancer was estimated as 143,685 DKK (2002 prices). Adjusting for inflation,
the COI of lung cancer was estimated as 182,745 DKK per lung cancer death.
4.5.3
Chronic Bronchitis
The economic burden of disease of chronic bronchitis was quantified as a willingness
to pay, based on the contingent valuation study by Viscusi et al [62]. The study used
survey data from 389 subjects in the U.S. in 1991 to estimate a WTP to avoid a case
of severe CB as 2,380,610 DKK.
However, the results of this study were not directly applicable to this paper, which
aims to quantify the total burden of CB, rather than severe cases specifically. To
quantify the WTP for an average case of CB, Krupnick and Cropper [65] scaled this

severity of their relatives against the disease severity of severe CB as defined in [62].

Hospital Admissions
The economic burden of hospital admissions (HA) was quantified as a cost of illness

The database was used by Andersen et al. [3] to estimate the average COI of cardio-

COI was 89,362 DKK for a cardiovascular HA, and 52,229 DKK for a respiratory HA.
Adjusting for inflation, the estimated COI per hospitalization was 112,688 DKK for
cardiovascular HA and 65,862 DKK for respiratory HA.

4.5.5.1
Asthma
The average cost of illness of a consultation with a general practitioner (GP) was

a conservative estimate, the non-asthmatic estimated value of 498 DKK was used

estimated as 628 DKK.

The module aimed to quantify the potential health benefits of an improved indoor
residential environment resulting from a building renovation. It does so by simulating
the indoor environment before and after the renovation, and comparing the associated
health burdens for both scenarios.
The RIEM was based on the EeraData Indoor Environmental Module (IEM), which
aimed to measure the quality of an indoor environment and its effects on its occu-
pants in a model that reflected the dynamics of typical buildings, by accounting for
diurnal and seasonal variations along with variations caused by occupant behaviour.
It simulates the thermal indoor environment and indoor air quality, evaluates the
IEQ, and quantifies its derived socioeconomic impact.
The IEM was based on several existing and peer-reviewed models for infiltration,
ventilation, energy performance and mould growth, and was aligned with the recom-
mendations in DS/EN 16798-1 (2019) [22]. The methodology for the IEM is described
in detail in [121], and the methods of relevance to the RIEM are briefly described
below:
Air infiltration was modelled using the LBL simplified infiltration model, which com-
bines weather data and the tightness of the building (expressed as a leakage class
between A-J) to estimate the air exchange rate by infiltration.
Natural ventilation was modelled as either single-sided or cross-flow ventilation, based
on the empirical models by Phaff [87], and Karawa 2001 [59]. Both models used win-
dow opening areas and pressure differences induced by either temperature differences
or wind speeds to calculate the airflow rate through window openings.
Indoor temperatures and heating/cooling needs were calculated according to the
hourly method in ISO 13790 (2008).
The method made a simplified estimate of
air and operative temperatures and the corresponding heating/cooling needs based
on information on ventilation and transmission properties, internal and external heat
gains, weather data, building components and heating/cooling set points. Human gen-
eration of heat and moisture was modelled according to ISO 8996 (2004), in which
heat and moisture generation is a product of activity levels and surface area. Human
generation of CO2 was modelled according to ASTM D6245-98 (2002).
The RIEM is an extension of the IEM, and includes a number of additions and adap-
tations, including the addition of a mass-balance model for indoor particulate matter,
and the adaptation of occupant behaviour to reflect the behaviour of residential oc-
cupants.

4.6.1
Modelling Indoor Particulate Matter
The concentration of indoor PM was modelled based on the dynamic mass balance
equation 3.8. In the model, the airflow rates of mechanical ventilation, infiltration
and natural ventilation were calculated at each time step. The penetration factor of
infiltration was assumed equal to unity, and the deposition loss rates were assumed to
be 0.39h−1 and 0.65h−1 for PM2.5 and PM10 respectively [83]. There were therefore
three parameters in the mass balance which needed to be determined; these were the
filter eﬀiciency, outdoor ambient concentration of PM, and indoor source strengths.
4.6.1.1
Filter Eﬀiciencies
Filter eﬀiciencies for PM2.5 and PM10 was determined according to ISO 16890-1
2016, expressed as ePM2.5 and ePM10. The most typical filter installed in residential
mechanical ventilation is an F7 filter, which has eﬀiciencies of ePM2.5 = 0.68 and
ePM10 = 0.89.
4.6.1.2
Outdoor Particulate Matter
The outdoor ambient concentrations of PM was modelled based on outdoor PM mea-
surements performed by The Danish Centre for Environment and Energy (DCE) as
part of The Danish Air Quality Monitoring Programme [32]. Measurements from year
2018 were averaged to estimate annual PM concentrations across Denmark, both at
street level in Copenhagen, Århus and Odense and at suburban/rural levels in Copen-
hagen, Risø and Keldsnor. DCE also estimated the regional variations in background
PM concentrations based on results from the environmental air quality model Eco-
nomic Valuation of Air Pollution (EVA). The results of these annually averaged PM
concentrations are listed in table 4.12. Annual PM concentrations at street and sub-
urban/rural levels for regions without measurement stations were assumed equal to
concentrations measured at similar regions.
Outdoor ambient concentrations varied not only based on location, but also seasonally
and diurnally. Seasonal variations were generally minor compared to diurnal, wherein
the largest variations were observed on street level. For the sake of simplicity, seasonal
variations were not included in the model, but diurnal variations were deemed relevant
and therefore investigated.
To estimate the diurnal distribution in PM concentrations, PM measurements from
March and September 2020 were gathered from two DCE stations, one at H. C.
Andersens Boulevard (HCAB) in Copenhagen (street level), and one at Risø (rural
level). The Measurement station at HCAB measured both PM2.5 and PM10, while
the station at Risø only measured PM10.

4.6.1.3
Indoor Sources of Particulate Matter
Indoor sources of PM2.5 and PM10 were modelled based on the findings from two
studies conducted by Morawska et al. [49] and Nasir et al. [75], which measured the
source strength of PM2.5 and PM10 production (in mg/min) of various residential
activities including cooking, cleaning and general movement. A table of their findings
is included in appendix A.3. The residential activities resulting in the production of
PM included in the RIEM were:
• Cooking (1 mg/min PM2.5, 1.03 mg/min PM10)
• Showering and hair drying (0.04 mg/min PM2.5 and PM10)
• Dish-washing (0.04 mg/min PM2.5 and PM10)
• Vacuuming (0.016 mg/min PM2.5 and 0.69 mg/min PM10)
• Washing machine (0.04 mg/min PM2.5 and PM10)
General movement was also included as a source of PM production, as this results in
the re-suspension of otherwise deposited particles back into the air. The estimated
source strengths of general movement were 0.002 mg/min of PM2.5 and 0.18 mg/min
of PM10.
The indoor sources of particulate matter are not constant; they are the product
of occupant behaviour. In order to estimate the indoor production of PM, it was

4.6.2
Modelling Mold/Dampness
The indoor prevalence of mold/dampness was modelled based on the mold index. The
mold index was calculated based on the relative humidity, dynamic temperature and
surface materials of the building, and quantifies the probable extent of mold growth
due to indoor condensation as a result of excessive relative humidities in the indoor
environment.
The surface materials of the indoor environment were evaluated based on the sensi-
tivity of the material to mold growth. The default assumption in the RIEM was that
surface materials exhibit moderate resistance to mold growth.
The dynamic temperature and relative humidity were calculated in the RIEM for
each time step by solving the mass balance models of heat and moisture. They were
a product of the outdoor climate, indoor heating/cooling, the degree and type of
air change, and the indoor sources of heat and moisture. These indoor sources were
largely a product of occupant behaviour.
4.6.2.1
Indoor Sources of Heat and Moisture
The indoor sources of heat and moisture included heat and moisture generation from
human metabolism, heat gain from internal sources such as lighting and equipment,
and activity-driven sources such as cooking, showering and washing dishes/clothes.
Heat and moisture generation from human metabolism was modelled as a product of
the number of occupants in the indoor environment, along with their ages and activ-
ity levels. Activity levels of occupants were modelled as constant while in the indoor
environment, and the heat and moisture generation from occupants was therefore a
product of the estimated number of occupants present in the indoor environment.
Heat gains from indoor sources were similarly modelled as constant, and were calcu-
lated as a product of floor area based on the methodology in EroPhit 2015 (FIND
SOURCE!).
The residential activities resulting in the production of dampness in the RIEM were
cooking, washing dishes and showering, with the source strengths of these activities
based on the study by Pallin et al. [84]. The study modelled the indoor moisture
generation in U.S. Homes, and expressed the source strengths of different residential
activities either as a fraction of time, or as the total average moisture generation of
an activity. The residential activities resulting in the production of moisture included
in the RIEM were:

Occupant Behaviour
4.6.3.1
Occupancy Profiling
This distribution was used to model the occupancy profiling in the RIEM. The occu-
pancy patterns was used to determine an occupancy factor at an hourly resolution
for both weekdays and weekends. The occupancy factor represented the percentage
of occupants expected to be present in the home.
By multiplying the number of
residents in the building with the occupancy factor, the number of occupants that on
average are present in the home at a given hour of the day was estimated.
4.6.3.2
Occupant Activity
The same study also investigated how Danish residents spent their time in the home,
by analyzing the TUS data on occupant activities including their durations, starting
times and ending times. The results of the study were used in the RIEM to model
the average starting and ending times of typical occupant activities which influence
the indoor environment.
The residential activities included in the RIEM were sleeping, cooking/washing dishes,
cleaning/washing clothes and using the bathroom (showering).
The starting and
ending times were determined as the time of day with the highest number of recorded
activity starts/ends in the TUS. Based on the results of the study, the typical starting
times and durations of the activities included in the RIEM have been estimated for
both weekdays and weekends.
The starting times and durations of expected occupant activities for both weekdays
and weekends were listed in the occupant activity schedules in appendix A.4, along
with their associated production of moisture and particulate matter. These schedules
were implemented in the RIEM in order to model the average production of PM and
moisture due to occupant behaviour in residential buildings.

4.6.3.3
Window Openings
Window openings were modelled based on a survey of occupant behaviour and control
of the indoor environment in Danish dwellings described in [4]. In the study, a survey
group representative of the Danish population noted whether their windows were
open or closed at the time of survey completion. The survey was distributed during
both summer and winter months, and the time of completion was matched with
meteorological data to determine the association with outdoor climatic conditions.
The study found that windows were open for 22% of survey responses, and that out-
door ambient temperature had a significant impact on the window opening behaviour.
This conclusion was in line with similar research which found that the outdoor tem-
perature was the single most important explanatory variable of window openings,
accounting for over 70% of the observed variance.
Based on the results of these studies, the window opening behaviour in the RIEM was
modelled with a window opening percentage of 22% during the time of occupancy in
the residence, excluding time of sleep. This opening percentage was assumed repre-
sentative for a calendar year, and was adjusted on a monthly basis due to variations
in average ambient temperatures, such that window openings were shortest for the
coldest months, and longest for the warmest. This meant that the variability of win-
dow openings was assumed to be entirely explained by outdoor temperatures, which
was a simplification of the findings in [4].
Window openings was in RIEM modelled as hourly occurrences, meaning that win-
dows were opened at the start of each waking hour where residents were present, and
were open for a given fraction of the hour.

4.7
Case Study Validation
In order to validate the assumptions and methods in the RIEM, and to demonstrate
the ability of the module to quantify the health impacts of a building renovation, a
real-life renovation case was examined.
The RIEM was validated based on indoor climate measurement data from renovated
residential apartments both before and after the renovation took place. Using the
measurement data, the observed impact of the renovation on the indoor climate was
quantified.
By modelling the residential apartments and their renovations in the
RIEM, it was determined how closely the simulated renovation impacts matched the
observed ones.
Measurement data from renovated apartments was made available through the hous-
ing association Domea.
Domea had recently renovated 250 apartments from the
1960s in Svendborg, Denmark, at three locations: Toftemarken, Klintemarken and Fri-
landsvej from November 2018 to June 2019, and had during this renovation installed
IC-meters measuring temperature, CO2 concentration (CO2) and relative humidity
(RH) in the majority of these apartments [28].

and Frilandsvej in Svendborg [12].

apartments to improve the quality of life and comfort of its residents. The aim was
not to improve the energy eﬀiciency of the apartments, as renovation initiatives such

parameters independent of occupant behaviour needed to be validated, specifically
the mechanical ventilation and infiltration air flow rates.
The mechanical ventilation air flow rate after renovation was assumed to comply with
the regulations in the Danish Building Standards, which for an indoor air quality of
category II required an air change rate of at least ACH = 0.6h−1 [22].
The infiltration rate in the observed apartments was estimated by Domea using on-site
measurements of the exhaust airflow prior to the building renovation. The assumption
was that by closing all external doors and windows and keeping all internal doors open,
the airflow measured at the exhaust airway could be used to approximate the hourly
air change due to infiltration. According to these measurements, this air change due
to infiltration varied from 0-0.146 h−1. However, there was a considerable degree
of uncertainty associated with this calculation, as it was assumed that no additional
exfiltration occurred, which likely leaded to an underestimation of the infiltration
rate. [12]
Instead of using these calculations, the infiltration rate was estimated using decay
rates of CO2-concentrations prior to the renovation. The assumption was that when
occupants left their apartment, this would result in a decay in CO2-concentrations
caused by air change from infiltration. Based on the volume of the apartment and
the rate of decay, the infiltration rate could be estimated. For each apartment, CO2-
concentrations were analyzed at a daily resolution to identify reductions in CO2-
concentrations which seemed to plausibly correspond with exponential decay rates
caused by occupants leaving the apartment. An example of a CO2 decay is illustrated
in appendix A.7. The identified occurrences were investigated, and the decay rates
estimated using the following equation, under the assumption that the air in the
apartments was fully mixed (i.e. no closed internal doors):

Health Impacts Associated with Mold/Dampness
Regarding the estimated health improvements due to a change in mold index, no
observable change was found in the RIEM simulations for the three apartments. The
calculated mold index, which is based on relative humidity levels and the sensitivity
of the interior materials, resulted in an average annual mold index of MI ≈0, both
before and after the renovation in all three apartments. The simulation indicated

that the air change rate in the apartments was suﬀiciently high both before and after
the renovation to prevent relative humidities from reaching the critical levels at which
condensation would occur, and mold growth would begin.
However, as illustrated in figure 3.6, condensation is not the only cause of mold
and dampness. According to a mold inspection of 15 apartments at Toftemarken,
Klintemarken and Frilandsvej in 2011, 10 of the 15 apartments had visible mold,
either in the bathroom, window frames or in the corners of walls and ceilings [26].
The inspection was described in a report in which the level of mold was evaluated
based on the number of mold colonies found in samples taken from the apartments,
defined as either ”none”, ”minor” (1-10 colonies), ”moderate” (11-50 colonies) or ”ex-
tensive” (more than 50 colonies). The samples were also examined using microscopy
to determine whether the mold colonies were in growth, where it was found that
approximately 90% of the investigated samples had mold growth.
The report concluded that visible mold growth was present on walls, ceilings and
in wet rooms in two thirds of the observed apartments, and that this was likely
attributable to thermal bridges in the building facade and a lack of exhaust ventilation
in wet rooms. Any signs of visible mold were removed from the apartments during
the renovation.
Given the assumption that the visible mold reported in the inspection corresponded
to a mold index of MI = 4, the result of the renovation with regards to mold/dampness
was assumed to be a reduction of the average mold index from 4 to 0 in two thirds of
the apartments in Svendborg. This observed reduction in mold index was simulated
in the RIEM, and the resulting health impacts and their economic valuations were
quantified as listed in table 4.19.

The economic savings of asthma and AR prevention were higher for children than
adults, whereas the economic savings of CB prevention were substantially higher for
adults. Average cost-of-illness savings were highest for adult cases of CB, and lowest
for child cases of CB.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The importance of indoor environmental quality is often stressed in the field of archi-
tectural engineering; we learn that the indoor environment impacts our health and
well-being, but because of the complexities and variability of public health, it is rare
to take the next step and concretely quantify these impacts. This study aimed to
measure the effects of building renovations on the IEQ of Danish residential build-
ings, and the derived effects that this had on the health of the residents, expressed
as prevented health responses and associated economic savings. The intention was
that the quantification of these potential health benefits and economic savings could
inform and incentivize policy-making and strategies regarding the renovation of the
Danish building stock.
This project is intended as an addition to the Indoor Environmental Module (IEM)
in the European EERAdata project, which aims to quantify the effect that the indoor
environment has on the comfort, health and performance of its occupants. The long-
term ambition for the IEM is to include as many of the building typologies which we
commonly inhabit as possible. No building typology is inhabited more than residential
buildings; seen in terms of environmental health, the home is the micro-environment
in which we spend the majority of our time, and persistent exposure to harmful
agents in the home therefore poses a serious risk to the compromise of our health. A
residential extention of the IEM was therefore developed, called the RIEM.
It was discovered that the most burdensome sources of IEQ health problems in residen-
tial buildings were particulate matter (PM) and mold/dampness. To limit the extent
of this study, it was decided to focus on these indoor environmental parameters and
their health responses. There are however several other indoor environmental parame-
ters which are associated with significant burdens of disease, including radon, volatile
organic compounds, carbon monoxide and ozone. These indoor environmental pa-
rameters, and undoubtedly many others, could all just as well have been investigated,
and their associated burdens of disease quantified.
It is also worth noting that there is growing evidence to support the theory that
exposure to ultra-fine particles (PM < 0.1 µm) pose a serious risk to environmental
health, and that this particle size range may in fact be more burdensome than PM2.5
or PM10. Because this study was based on the quantification of health burdens based
on exposure-response functions determined in large-scale, long-term cohort studies,
==PAGE:108==
94
5 Discussion
and seeing as no such studies have currently been performed for ultra-fine particles,
it was not possible to include these particles.
The RIEM was validated using indoor climate data from before and after the ren-
ovation in Toftemarken. Unfortunately, data was only available for the months of
September and October. The mechanical ventilation rates and occupant activity ad-
justment factors were determined to match the measurement data with the simulation
for this time period, but it is unclear whether these adjustments accurately depicted
the measured indoor climate for the remaining months.
Based on the methodology implemented in the IEM, the degree of mold growth was
modelled based on the mold index, which quantifies the visibility and size of mold
growth on a numerical scale from 0 to 6. The mold index provides a reliable method
for quantifying the degree of mold growth caused by interior condensation, but does
not account for rising or penetrating damp.
This might explain why the simulations of mold growth in the RIEM did not match the
observations of mold growth in the renovation case study in Svendborg. The results
of the RIEM clearly indicated that no mold growth was present when, in reality,
inspection of the apartments had shown that two thirds of the observed apartments
had visible mold and clear signs of mold growth. This disparity between simulation
and observation may be due to external dampness penetrating the building, which the
model does not predict. In the mold report it was concluded that the mold growth was
due to a combination of water leakage, excessive thermal bridges in the building facade
and insuﬀicient ventilation in wet rooms. If the RIEM were to be developed further,
the model to predict mold/dampness should be expanded to include penetrating and
rising damp, potentially modelled as a function of the leakage class.
Another uncertainty in the mold/dampness model was the conversion of exposure
types observed in the literature study into mold indices. Because the degree of ex-
posure to mold/dampness was modelled continuously using the mold index, it was
attempted to also model the exposure-response model continuously, rather than with
binary or categorical associations as is more common in observational studies. To
model this continuous association, a meta-analysis was performed to determine the
most statistically accurate continuous exposure-response relation. The exposure types
were subdivided into four categories: Water damage, dampness, visible mold and mold
odor, as these were the most typical categories in the included studies. However, these
categorical variables were associated with a significant degree of uncertainty due to
their ambiguity; what is classified as visible mold may vary significantly from study
to study, but their observed effects on the prevalence of diseases were pooled all the
same.
The indoor concentration of particulate matter was modelled in the RIEM as a dy-
namic mass balance, accounting for outdoor sources of PM entering the building
==PAGE:109==
5 Discussion
95
through mechanical and natural ventilation and infiltration, and indoor sources of
PM produced by occupant behaviour. The indoor sources were modelled according
to occupancy and activity schedules, based on a standard occupancy scheme. Sim-
ulations in the RIEM indicated that these indoor sources significantly contributed
to the indoor PM concentrations, in particular combustion processes such as cook-
ing. The occupant activities included in the activity schedule were modelled as an
expected minimum; these were the activities that any occupant was deemed likely to
perform. There were several other activities which could have been included in the
model, including smoking and candle burning, which would likely have contributed
to even greater indoor concentrations of PM. Most other mass balance models for
the indoor concentration of PM neglect the inclusion of indoor sources, due to the
uncertainty associated with occupant behaviour. The results from this study indicate
that such an exclusion is likely to lead to a significant underestimation of the indoor
PM concentration in residential buildings.
The uncertainty of simulation models attributable to occupant behaviour is not iso-
lated to particulate matter; occupant behaviour is one of the most consistent sources
of disparities between simulated and observed indoor environments. Another source
of uncertainty in the RIEM attributable to occupant behaviour was window openings
and use of extractor hoods, which the case validation indicated may have differed
significantly between units. The occupant behaviour adjustment factors were intro-
duced to match the simulation results with the indoor climate measurement data, but
whether these adjustment factors were an accurate depiction of the actual occupant
behaviour is unclear. The observed differences in indoor climate measurements may
just as well have been due to inaccuracies in occupancy or activity schedules, or in
the estimated number of residents.
Simulations are consistent and predictable, whereas human behaviour is changing
and impulsive. The occupant behaviour in the RIEM was modelled according to the
Danish Time Use Survey, by identifying the most common starting and ending times
of occupant activities, and using these to define deterministic occupant behaviour. An
alternative to this would be to model the occupant behaviour stochastically, wherein
the starting times and durations of occupant activities were modelled as probabilistic
distributions. While such a simulation method better captures the randomness and
unpredictability of occupant behaviour, it also introduces a significantly higher degree
of complexity to the model, in particular because it means that the estimated health
improvement potentials will also be stochastically distributed.
Another example of occupant behaviour defying expectations was for the indoor cli-
mate measurements of unit 7532, in which the RH and CO2-concentrations measured
in the apartment had both increased after the renovation. Given that the volume of
the apartment had increased, that a mechanical ventilation system had been installed,
and that the number of occupants before and after the renovation was the same, it
was concluded that the occupants had either changed their behaviour after the reno-
==PAGE:110==
96
5 Discussion
vation, or that the renovation had somehow worsened the indoor climate, rather than
improved it. Because the measured increases could not be replicated, unit 7532 was
disregarded in the case validation, but it was an indication that the assumption of
unchanged occupant behaviour before and after the renovation may in fact have been
an inaccuracy.
The exposure-response functions used to quantify the health burden of PM were ex-
pressed as functions of either PM2.5 or PM10, and the indoor concentrations of each
of these particle sizes were therefore modelled using a mass balance equation. In both
of these models, penetration factors, deposition loss rates and filter eﬀiciencies were
assigned constant values determined in empirical studies. In reality, these factors
depend on the particle size distribution, and each of them are also influenced by a
number of external factors, such as the state of the building facade, the interior air
change rates and filter cleanliness. Inaccuracies in the penetration factors, deposi-
tion loss rates and filter eﬀiciencies may lead to incorrect calculations of the indoor
concentration of particulate matter.
Another source of uncertainty in the quantification of associated risks due to PM was
the particle size distribution. PM10 particles are deposited in the nose and throat,
but can also be deposited inside the lungs, whereas PM2.5 can penetrate the lung
barrier and enter the blood stream. Given the same change in exposure concentration,
the relative health impact of PM2.5 is therefore greater than PM10. This was seen
in the impact response function of chronic bronchitis, where the estimated change
in cases was twice as high for a change in concentration of PM2.5, compared to the
same change in concentration for PM10. The majority of epidemiological associations
between health and PM have been modelled based on the concentrations of ambient
PM10, as this is the more easily measured particle size range. The simulation results
in the RIEM indicated that the majority of the indoor PM was in the PM2.5 size
range, and modelling the health impacts of these concentrations based on PM10
exposure-response functions will therefore potentially lead to an underestimation of
the associated health burden.
For the economic valuation, two different methods were used: willingness-to-pay
(WTP) and cost-of-illness (COI). These two methods of valuation are very differ-
ent; where WTP expresses the economic quantification of a loss in quality of life
that occurs when life is shortened or worsened, COI quantifies the costs of medicine
and healthcare attributable to an illness. COI may therefore underestimate the true
value of a morbidity, as it does not express the intangible values associated with a
reduction in quality of life. Conversely, WTP may overestimate the true value of a
morbidity, and is highly dependent on the quality of the contingent valuation study
from which it estimates its valuation. The valuations of these two methods should
therefore not be directly compared, but can independently be used to express the
personal value of a healthy life (WTP), compared to the societal costs associated
with disease treatment (COI). One example of the significant differences in valuation
==PAGE:111==

97
between WTP and COI is seen in the valuation of mortality associated with exposure
to PM. All-cause mortality was evaluated as a WTP according to the Value of a Life
Year (VOLY) of 1.4 mio DKK. One subcategory of all-cause mortality is lung cancer
mortality, for which the COI of a severe lung-cancer patient was evaluated indepen-
dently. This valuation was based on the health care expenses due to hospitalizations,
medical treatments and hospice residency, and was valued at 182,745 DKK. In this
example, the COI of lung cancer mortality was approximately 1/7 of the WTP of the
same health outcome.
The importance of public health and the prevention of disease has perhaps never
been more apparent than during the COVID-19 pandemic. Data scientists around the
globe are tracking the impact of this pandemic in every metric imaginable. Health im-
pacts are being measured in mortalities, hospitalizations, symptoms and side-effects,
and indirectly in inactivity, loneliness and depression. Economic impacts are being
measured in GDP losses, healthcare expenses, unemployment rates and continuously
climbing state debts. Because the potential health impacts of the COVID-19 pan-
demic are so severe, the willingness-to-pay to contain and control its spread seems to
have no limits and, as a result, the costs of this illness to society have been astronom-
ical.
The importance of the home has also been made evident by the COVID-19 pandemic.
People are spending more time in their homes now than perhaps ever before, and
many have been forced to adapt their routines and ways of life, with the home en-
vironment as the new temporary replacement for oﬀices and schools. What many
people have perhaps also realized, is that their homes were not as pleasant as they
had thought. People are renovating and refurnishing, painting and building, to cor-
rect the deficiencies in their home environments that had otherwise gone unnoticed.
National restrictions and recommendations to socially distance and stay at home have
caused us all to slow down, and has likely given many people a newfound appreciation
for the value of having a comfortable, healthy indoor environment.
It is the hope and belief of the authors that one of the positive derived effects of
this otherwise terrible pandemic will be a sustained, heightened awareness of the
importance of the home and the quality of its indoor environment. Combined with
the heightened focus on the importance and value of public health to society, this
pandemic may well help to propel policy-making and strategies aimed at the informed
renovation of the Danish building stock, for the betterment of the built environment
and public health.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This project has quantified the derived health effects of indoor environmental improve-
ments due to residential renovations in Denmark. Associations between the indoor
environment and its effects on health and well-being were determined. The most bur-
densome sources of exposure in the residential indoor environment were particulate
matter (PM) and mold and dampness, and their associated health responses included
all-cause mortality, asthma, allergies, chronic bronchitis and respiratory diseases.
These associations were modelled as continuous functions, expressing the relationship
between exposure concentrations and health risks. The increased risks of asthma,
allergic rhinitis and chronic bronchitis attributable to mold and dampness were mod-
elled as a function of different mold exposures, expressed as a mold index. The risk of
asthma and allergic rhinitis was higher for children than adults, and the risk of asthma
increased proportionally with the severity of exposure. For particulate matter (PM2.5
and PM10) it was determined that a decreasing concentration of PM was associated
with an decreased risk of all-cause mortality, chronic bronchitis, and health-care vis-
its due to asthma, respiratory and cardiovascular diseases.
Each health outcome
was economically valuated as either potential societal savings (’cost-of-illness’), or as
a willingness-to-pay to reduce the risk of mortality or morbidity. The health out-
comes with the biggest potential savings attributable to the exposures were identified
as chronic bronchitis for mold and dampness and all-cause mortality for particulate
matter.
The exposure-response functions and their valuations were integrated in a building
performance simulation tool named RIEM. The tool quantifies the potential health
benefits of an improved indoor residential environment as a result of renovation. The
tool was tested and validated using indoor climate measurement data from a reno-
vation project in Toftemarken, Svendborg.The validated tool estimated the health-
related savings of the renovation as up to 9,500 DKK per child and up to 37,000
DKK per adult. The total savings of the renovation project of 250 apartments in
Svendborg were estimated as up to 15 million DKK.
The RIEM was developed as an extension to the Indoor Environmental Module (IEM)
developed by EERAdata, which aims to contribute to informed decision-making of
residential renovations, and the incentivization of energy eﬀiciency investments.
