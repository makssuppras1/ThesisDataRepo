Abstract  
Regular excessive intake of iodine might be associated with thyroid dysfunction and as a consequence several 
other serious adverse effects in some individuals. Recently, Seaweed consumption is becoming more common 
in the European Union (EU) countries. Since some species of seaweeds have a high content of iodine, the 
attention has raised to (i) possibilities in production processes to decrease the iodine and (ii) assess the risk of 
intake of the excess of iodine. 
In this study, by using ICP- MS technique the iodine reduction was determined at different production process 
steps in preparing two fermented seaweed salads and one seaweed juice using three different brown algae of 
Saccharina latissimi, Alaria esculenta, and Fucus vesiculosus. Data showed the same trend in reduction of 
iodine concentration in defrosting, blanching (83°C, 3min), and fermentation processes. In the first batch 
analysis of Salad I, iodine concentration was decreased significantly (p<0.01) by 59- 74% in blanching step. 
In the second batch analysis of Salad II, blanching caused 37% and 62% (p< 0.01) reduction of iodine. 
Moreover, the fermentation step caused 55-75% decrease in iodine concentration, due to the soaking of 
seaweeds in 35°C water for 10-20 hours. As a result, the iodine concentration of finished product of salad I 
and II were, 177±6 and 514±33 (µg/g) respectively. The pressing process for obtaining the seaweed juice 
caused 58% (p< 0.01) decrease in the amount of iodine.  
Assuming that the target individuals consuming seaweed are adults, the Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) of 
1000-1100 μg/day iodine considered to be reasonable for this study. The EFSA UL factors for different age 
groups (4-18), and pregnant and lactation women considered sensible in this study. To our best of knowledge, 
there have not been data regarding the seaweed consumption in EU. Thus, using our results together with a 
consideration of new scenario (low, moderate, and high consumption) of fermented seaweed salad and 
consumption of 1.5- 2% of seaweed juice as a supplement, the iodine intake (µg/day) were estimated. Besides, 
according to DANSDA the primary dietary iodine intake was milk and dairy products in all age groups. 
Moreover, the total exposure of iodine (µg/day) was estimated, based on the iodine dietary intake plus iodine 
intake from fermented seaweed salad and seaweed juice. Lastly, a risk characterisation was performed on the 
total consumption of fermented seaweed salad and seaweed juice plus dietary intake. 
Once a week a moderate single serving of fermented salad is considered to be acceptable for healthy adults. 
Due to seasonal variation of iodine concentration in seaweed, frequently intake of seaweed juice is not 
recommended for the healthy adults. In case of children, adolescent and individuals with the thyroid 
dysfunction, the low consumption, once a week, of fermented salad is not assessed to be associated with health 
concern. Likewise, rarely intake of seaweed juice is not associated with health risks for the age group of 4- 18. 
However, the consumption of fermented salad and seaweed juice should be avoided during the pregnancy and 
lactation, and for all children under 3 years old due excessive iodine adverse effect on the fetus, newborns and 
children organ development. Previously published studies recommend moderate intake of seaweed to address 
the iodine deficiency in individuals; however, our observation does not support this recommendation and it is 
advised to use standard dosage e.g. salt.  
 
Keywords: Iodine excess, Brown algae, Thyroid dysfunction, Iodine reduction, Processing, ICP- MS, 
Fermented seaweed salad, Seaweed juice, Tolerable Upper Intake Level, Iodine exposure, Risk assessment

1. Introduction  
1.1. 
Iodine  
The element iodine was derived from kelp 
ashes by chance two hundred years ago [1–3]. 
Iodine is a micronutrient that is essential for 
the production of the thyroid hormones in the 
human body [4]. Iodine exists in different 
types in the environment, such as simple 
inorganic form of iodide (I)- and iodate (IO3-), 
and 
more 
complex 
organic 
iodinated 
compounds such as thyroid hormones, 3,3ʹ, 
5,5ʹ- tetraiodothyronine (T4) and 3,3ʹ, 5-
triiodothyronine (T3) [5, 6]. 
Iodine enters the human body through the 
dietary intake and uses with the thyroid gland 
for the production of thyroid hormones. 
Thyroid hormones have several essential roles 
in the human body such as the growth and 
maturation of several tissues, skeleton, brain 
and neurodevelopment, for the body´s energy, 
and metabolism [7].For instance, during child 
development, pregnancy, or in a certain 
situation that body needs more energy, the 
thyroid gland is producing more hormones [8]. 
Especially, brain nervous system development 
during fetal life is dependent on adequate 
thyroid hormones [8].Both iodine deficiency 
and iodine excess lead to thyroid dysfunction 
which interferes with the thyroid hormone 
synthesis, causing several serious diseases [4, 
9]. Consumption of high amount of iodine may 
affect thyroid function and potentially lead to 
hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid that  
 
 
 
gland does not make enough hormones) or 
hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid that 
makes too many hormones) [4].  
Various types of edible macroalgal assume to 
have the high amount of iodine, but the whole 
amount of iodine is different between species 
and taxonomic entities [2]. The brown algae 
(Phaeophyta) has the highest amount of iodine, 
followed by the red algae (Rhodophyta), while 
the green algae (Chlorophyta) has the least 
iodine enrichment [10]. The brown seaweed 
characteristics are the largest group of thallus 
size, with the high total biomass produced, and 
with the brown color pigments among the three 
major 
groups[11, 
12]. 
Environmental 
conditions, such as duration and intensity of 
light, water temperature, and concentration of 
iodine effect on development and morphology 
of brown seaweeds [11, 13]. Generally, the 
brown seaweeds are common in cold water 
temperature (arctic or boreal) [14].  
Over the past decades, the number of iodine 
deficiency countries has decreased from 54 to 
30, and at the same time, iodine sufficient 
countries have been growing from 67 to 112, 
whereas the number of iodine excess countries 
have doubled from 5 to 10 [15]. Traditionally, 
East Asian and Pacific countries have used 
seaweed as a part of their daily dishes and 
currently, Asian countries such as Japan, 
China, and the Republic Korea are the largest 
seaweed consumers [2, 16]. In Europe, mostly, 
some coastal societies, like Ireland, France 
Wales, Iceland, and Scotland have used

Background 
1.2.1. Role of iodine in the environment 
and seaweeds 
The distribution, and conversion of iodine are 
caused by complex physical, chemical and 
biological processes in the environment [5]. It 
is suggested that the iodide concentrations is 
lower in southern Atlantic´s surface water than 
the lower latitudes, because of higher 
temperature, and solar radiation [18]. Iodine 
released in the form of sea- spray and gases in 
the environment, where micro and macroalgae 
play a key role in the transformation of iodine 
which mostly emitted by seaweed [5, 19].  
Brown kelp species such as Laminaria 
digitata are known as the highest iodine 
accumulators among all living systems which 
the iodine accumulation can be more than 
30,000 times higher than the surrounding 
environment [1, 20, 21]. Between 9 to 99% of 
iodine is mostly water soluble in common 
eatable seaweeds, depending on algal species 
[2], with maximum levels of 99.2% water-
soluble iodine in kelp (Laminaria) [22]. 
Chemical type of iodine in seaweed is mostly 
inorganic iodine I- up to 88% in kelp 
(Laminaria), Organic iodine reached to 10% in 
kelp and iodate IO3 is 1.4% in kelp [2, 22]. 
There are some factors that influence the 
iodine content in seaweed. Geographic 
variation and harvesting area influence the 
amount of iodine [2, 22]. For instance; iodine 
in wakame (undaria) is 23 times higher in 
China than the wakame in New Zealand, 
Australia, and Japan [22] and average of iodine 
content was 1542 µg/g in 10 species of 
Laminaria from Namibia to 5307 µg/g in the 
French kelp [22]. Moreover, iodine contents 
are significantly influenced with the seasonal 
variation and notably lower in spring and 
summer than autumn and winter and higher 
value in young blades [2, 17]. The iodine 
content would be decreased t0 50% or more 
during the summer months, when kelps expose 
to temperature stress and photooxidative stress 
[23]. It might be due to, iodide biological role 
which acts as an inorganic antioxidant in kelps 
upon oxidative stress [2, 23, 24]. Iodide is 
mostly stored in a unstable form, located in the 
apoplast of the cell wall space, and not into 
cytosol or in the vacuoles (Fig. 1) [23, 25]. This 
can be a reason that iodide mobilize fastly 
during oxidative stress [23].

1.2.2.2. 
TH synthesis 
The synthesis of thyroid hormones (THs) is 
complex and requires the high amount of 
iodine, 
which 
is

from the blood plasma into the follicular cells 
in the thyroid gland [31].  Due to this active 
transport, the concentration of iodine inside the 
follicular cells of thyroid tissue is much higher 
than in the rest of the body [29, 31]. Further, I- 
ions oxidized to I2 by Thyroid Peroxidase 
enzyme [27, 32]. Thyroid Peroxidase enzyme 
has several key reactions and present in the 
follicular lumen [33]. For further process 
tyrosine 
amino 
acids 
are 
needed. 
Thyroglobulin which is produced by the 
follicular cells in the thyroid gland contains a 
large number of tyrosine residues which bonds 
to I2. Tyrosine binding either single or double 
iodine 
molecules 
creating 
3-iodotirosine 
(MIT) and 3,5-diiodotyrosine (DIT) [27, 34]. 
By combining two DIT residue by Peroxidase 
T4 is generated, while by combining MIT 
residue and DIT residue, T3 is generated [27, 
29]. Thyroid secret 80% of thyroid hormones 
(TH) in the form of T4, while 20% release in 
the form of T3 in the human body [27, 29]. 
Peroxidase is much more efficient at 
combining of two DIT residues, and hence the 
thyroid gland primarily produces T4 rather than 
T3 [27, 29]. T4 is then transformed to the active 
form of T3 or the inactive form of reverse 
triiodothyronine (rT3) by the Peroxidase 
through 
the 
body´s 
tissues 
(Fig. 
5). 
(Carvalhoand Dupuy, 2017; van der Spek et

about 10-15 mg of iodine, which means 79- 
90% of iodine exist in thyroid gland [35]. 
Normal levels of iodine in the serum is 50-100 
µg/l-1 which 95% of iodine is in organic form 
and 5% in inorganic form [5].  
 
1.2.2.3. 
Thyroid Hormone Regulation 
Thyrotropin- Releasing hormone (THR) 
synthesized 
in 
hypothalamus 
stimulates 
synthesis and release of Thyroid-stimulating 
hormone (TSH) from pituitary gland, which in 
turn regulates the thyroid glands and hence 
THs synthesis (Fig. 6 ) [37, 38]. TSH is 
regulated by synthesis of T4 and T3 in the 
thyroid gland. It means when T3 and T4 
concentrations are high, the production of TSH 
is decreased, and conversely, when T3 and T4 
concentrations are low, the production of TSH 
is increased [37, 38]. The low and high level of 
TSH may indicate the hypothyroidism or 
hyperthyroidism which can happen in both 
iodine deficiency and excess [39].

1.2.3. Source of iodine intake 
In humans, the iodine would be digested 
from, food intake, drinking water and dietary 
supplements 
such 
as 
vitamin/mineral 
supplements which may contain hundreds to 
thousands higher level of iodine in the diet [5, 
41]. The most common sources of iodine in 
industrial countries are dairy products, eggs, 
grain and cereal products, poultry and meat, 
freshwater fish, and fruits [42, 43].  
Seaweeds, fish, and seafood are the richest 
sources of iodine; however, consumption of 
these products is low among many countries 
[44–46].Due to usage of iodine/iodophors as 
disinfectants on the dairy industry especially 
on the cows, and also due to the high iodine 
content in animal feed (including grass), milk  
and dairy products become a major sources of 
iodine [47, 48]. Moreover, mostly over 
iodization of salt and/or poor monitoring of salt 
iodization cause the excess intake of iodine 
[15, 44]. Recently, the consumption of 
seaweeds have been increased among the 
Western countries, and seaweed has become 
increasingly common on some people every 
days plates. It seems seaweeds are a great 
source of proteins [49], carbohydrates [50], 
fatty acids [51], minerals [52], and bioactive 
products [53]. Brown seaweeds can be a great 
source of polyphenols and antioxidant [54]. 
However, a high amount of iodine in seaweeds 
might be of health concern [24].

The frozen fluid samples stood in the lab

2.5. 
 Inductively 
Coupled 
Plasma 
Mass Spectrometry (ICP-Ms) 
ICP-MS is a technique for analyzing 
inorganic elements. It provides high sensitivity 
and low background signals ensuing in very 
low detection limits in the ppb. The ICP-MS 
instrument employs a plasma as the ion source 
and a mass spectrometer analyser to detect the 
positively charged ions produced in the 
plasma. By using a calibration curve, the 
concentration of the analyte on a sample is 
quantified. Several standard solutions would 
be measured in ICP-MS to obtain a calibration 
curve. Standard solution´s signal would be 
measured, 
and 
the 
signal´s 
intensity 
corresponds to the known concentration of the 
standard solution.  
Iodine is volatile, and because of that slightly 
unstable signal might be obtained [57]. 
Further, Ionization of iodine is low, cause the 
low sensitivity [57]. However, the sensitivity 
of ICP-MS is higher than other existing 
methods [57, 58]. By optimized microwave 
assisted alkaline (TMAH) digestion procedure, 
vaporization of iodine can be controlled [57–
60]. The matrix effect of TMAH is low and the 
stability of ICP-MS with it is high [57]. 
Moreover, to eliminate the matrix compound 
interference in ICP-MS measurement, the use 
of internal standard (ISTD), in this purpose 
Tellurium (Te), is recommended [59]. On the 
whole, ICP-MS is recommended for food with 
the low iodine level (less than 25 µ/100 g) and 
foods containing high sodium or iron 
concentrations [58].  
2.6. 
 Calibration standard solution 
Calibration curves were based on twelve 
standard solutions of different concentration of 
iodine (0, 1, 2, 5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 150, 200, 300, 
and 400) made from a 1000 mg/L of iodide 
(SS-11I, SpectraScan), different concentration 
of TMAH 0.5% and different concentration of 
10 ng/mL of ISTD (125Te).

3.2. 
Discussion 
3.2.1. Method validation  
For the analysis of total iodine content the 
samples were divided into Salad I, Salad II and 
Bladder wrack. The calibration curve with 6 
standard solution showed linearity in high 
concentration of standard solution of Salad I 
analysis (Fig. 16) and in low concentration of 
standard solution of Salad II analysis (Fig. 17). 
LOD and LOQ in both analyses was much 
lower than the iodine concentration measured 
in the seaweed samples. This indicated the 
high sensitivity of the instrument to quantify 
the low amount of iodine in seaweed. Although 
the high variation of standard deviation could 
be seen in some samples (Table 4), the analysis 
of the analytical replicates showed low 
standard deviation (Table 1, 2, and 3). Thus, 
the procedure was highly precise. The trueness 
of 100% in the second analysis presented the 
high accuracy of the methods. The low RSD% 
indicated that the method were highly precise 
and the samples were well homogenized. Thus 
the method carried out using ICP-MS to 
quantify iodine were highly precise and 
accurate. 
The reason that the proper mass balance could 
not be obtained might be, the exact analytical 
data of the fermented salad processes was 
lacking (Fig. 19 and Fig. 20). Besides the 
production is on the large scale and the system 
is not closed. Thus the weight of kelps and 
water measured approximately. Furthermore, 
it was important that the sampling step from 
water-tank was representative.  
 Although the measurement of the pressing 
step in the seaweed juice processes (Fig. 22) 
was more precise, appropriate mass balance 
could not be obtained. It might be due to, the 
BW juice contained high amount of pulp inside 
which could not be extracted by the pipet (it 
was more like smoothie than juice). This juice 
should be treated in the same way that solid 
samples such as chopped BW and pulp treated 
for the extraction of all iodine. The assumption 
was that the juice contains more iodine µg/g 
that could be obtained. 
3.2.2. Seaweed treatment and processes for 
reduction of iodine 
3.2.2.1. Production processes of fermented 
salad 
In this study, defrosting of the kelps of Salad 
II showed that the defrosted-water extract had 
iodine concentration (Fig. 29), though, no 
frozen samples of Salad II were available to 
compare 
and 
measure 
the 
defrosting 
effectiveness on reduction of iodine in the 
kelps. However, iodine presentation in 
defrosted-water extract proved that iodine 
could leach easily out of the cell. Iodine in 
seaweed is water soluble [61], and most of 
iodine exist as non-covalently bound I- on the 
surface and between the cell, thus it is may 
easily leach out rapidly [1]. Thus, with 
defrosting the kelps in the room temperature, 
iodine dissolved in defrosted water and 
leached out. 
The blanching step of both Salads (at 83°C 
for 3 min) caused 35-75% reduction of iodine 
content (Fig. 25 and 28). In preparation of the 
seaweed, 
after 
harvesting 
and 
before 
consumption, processes such as, rehydration 
(soaking) in freshwater, blanching (boiling), 
steam or deep fried have reduction impact on 
iodine concentration compare to the initial 
concentration  [2, 17, 22, 24]. The trend of high 
reduction of iodine in this study, is supported 
by the others reporting a reduction of almost 
70% after 2 min of boiling [24]. The blanching 
process was effective in decreasing iodine 
concentration, due to in higher temperature, 
iodine has and the weak bounds break easier 
and as a result, iodine leaches out faster. 
However, heating in 83°C for 3 min does not

statement is in line with other study that 
showed after 15 min boiling of kombu, 99% of 
iodine found in water [62]. Blanching 
temperature stays higher than 82°C for the

The fermented salad contains double amount 
of SK compare to WK, and as a result the 
reduction of iodine concentration in the 
fermentation step should be compared to

4. Human Risk Assessment  
4.1. 
Hazard identification  
4.1.1. Absorption and metabolism 
Iodine is ingested in many chemical forms [9]. 
Iodine and iodate are changed to the iodide in 
the gut and then it is totally absorbed by the 
small intestine [68]. Generally iodine is cleared 
from the circulation by the thyroid and kidney 
[9]. Furthermore, more than 90% of ingested 
iodine is excreted in the urine, with a small 
excretion in the faeces [28] (Fig. 31). The 
seven days studies in normal diets rich in dairy 
products showed that an iodine excretion of 
200 µg/d associates with excretion of 180 µg/d 
by urine and 20 µg/d by faeces [69]. According 
to high renal extraction on the seven days 
studies, it can be assumed a good availability 
of iodine in the food and mostly iodine 
presents as iodide in the food, due to high 
absorption [69].  
It is necessary to understand the biological 
behavior of iodine in the body. Under normal 
thyroid, iodine change in blood rapidly which 
plasma iodine has a half-life of approximately 
10 hours [9]. However, it is difference in case 
of iodine deficiency and excess, as in 
overactive thyroid it is shortened [9]. Studied 
on thyroid gland in patients showed that the 
average biological half-life of 
131 I in 
euthyroid, hyperthyroid and hypothyroid 
patients was 66.1±6.3, 38.2±8.6, and 29.3±8.8  
 
 
 
 
days respectively [70]. The half-life of 
circulating T4 is 5- 8 d, and for T3 is 1.5- 3 d 
[9]. The biological half-time of inorganic 
iodine ingested by food in all ages was 0.25 d, 
which 30% transfers to the thyroid gland and 
70% excrete with the urinary content [71]. 
Concerning the age group, biological half-time 
of iodine in the thyroid for the infant was 11.2 
d, age 1 yr was 15 d, age 5yr was 23 d, age 10 
yr was 58 d, age 15 yr was 67 d, and adults was 
80 d [71].

4.1.2. Consequence of excess of iodine 
The healthy thyroid gland of human adults 
releases approximately 80 µg of thyroid 
hormones per day, which means the thyroid 
gland should trap about 50-60 µg of iodine 
daily in order to stay in balance [73, 74]. It is 
expected with the dietary iodine intake 
between 100-150 µg daily, thyroid gland 
secrets the expected amount of hormones [73]. 
The EFSA Adequate Intake (AI) of iodine that 
essential for the synthesis of thyroid hormones 
is 150 µg/day for adults and the EFSA 
Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) is 600 
µg/day for adults [75]. While the UL for iodine 
is 1100 µg/day in united State [76], Australia 
and new Zealand [77], 1000 µg/day according 
to the WHO [78], and in Japan 3000 µg/day 
(Tokyo; 2005. Dietary reference intakes for 
Japanese).  
Most healthy adults tolerate 1 mg of intake of 
iodine daily [5], due to adaptation of thyroid to 
iodine excess by regulating the releasing 
hormones into the blood stream [79]. During 
initial iodine exposure, thyroidal iodide 
prevents the oxidation of iodide which is the 
essential first step to the iodination of tyrosine 
and moreover producing the thyroid hormones 
[4, 73, 79].. This mechanism known as acute 
Wolff- Chaikoff effect [4, 73, 79]. This 
adaptation to escape from the acute Wolff- 
Chaikoff effect is due to TSH- independent 
autoregulation results to decrease in the 
releasing the sodium- iodide symporter (NIS) 
which results to decrease the active transport 
of the iodide plasma and inhibit the synthesis 
of thyroid hormones [4, 73, 79].  In the 
euthyroid adult gland, the decrease in the 
expression of NIS happens after 24h of high 
iodine exposure and last for 48h until thyroid 
starts the normal function of synthesis of 
hormones [80]. While excess iodine exposure 
in persons with dysregulation of the thyroid 
follicular cell, may cause temporary or 
permanent thyroid dysfunction [4, 73].Patients 
with hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism, 
patients with autoimmune thyroid disease, and 
patients with grave disease might have a high 
risk of failure to escape from the acute Wolff- 
Chaikoff effect [81]. During fetal and neonate 
development, the hypothalamic- pituitary- 
thyroid axis is still immature and fetus might 
failing to adapt to the acute Wolff- Chaikoff 
effect  [4]. Due to lipophilic form of thyroid 
hormones, they can pass the placental barrier 
and as a result of maternal exposure, the fetus 
get thyroid hormones [82]. Due to not 
adaptation to excess of iodine, fetus and 
neonate may develop hypothyroidism or 
hyperthyroidism which may affect their brain 
growth and developmental skills [28]. The 
adequate iodine intake of mothers who breast 
feed is essential, due to, iodine excreted 
through the breast milk [83]. The excessive 
iodine intakes may cause the delayed 
developmental status between the children as a 
result of the thyroid hormone disturbance [28].

4.2.4.1. EFSA, USA 
The same approaches were used for the 
estimation of UL by EFSA and USA, while, 
consideration of different UF resulted in the 
different UL in the European Union and the 
USA (Table 8 and 9). Generally, based on the 
health based guidance value (HBGV) the UF 
of 10 uses for the contaminants [88] and 
smaller UF of 2- 3 uses for the nutrients. 
Therefore, both UF of 3 and 1.5 used by EFSA 
and USA respectively, is acceptable for the 
determination of UL.   
The limitation of both studies by Paul et al. 
and Gardner et al. that used as references for 
the estimation of UL by EFSA and USA, was 
that the duration of both studies were short and 
the small groups of healthy adults observed. 
However, the results of both studies were 
supported by the longer duration studies that 
used higher iodine dosages. Although by 
exposure of two high dosages of 1500 and 
4500 μg iodide/day significant decreased 
happened in mean serum T4 concentration, and 
TSH secretion, the T3 concentration remained 
stable on the both dosages. Furthermore, in 
EFSA estimation, both Paul et al. and Gardner 
et al. studies supported by the data of the 5 
years study on the 1800 μg iodide/day dosage 
which  no clinical thyroid observed. Under the 
high exposure of iodine, the thyroid gland of 
healthy adults showed an adaptation to the 
iodine load by decreasing the NIS, resulting in 
decreasing in the iodide transport into the 
thyroid, the mechanism called the escape or 
acute Wolff- Chaikoff effect [89]. Moreover, 
under the high iodine exposure, the iodine does 
not absorb in thyroid gland, and the excess 
would be excreted mostly by urine. Besides, 
NOAEL of 1,000 to 1,200 μg/day were 
observed for adult humans. Thus, the UL of 
1000- 1100 μg/day seems reasonable for 
healthy adults, and the EFSA estimation of UL 
for adults, seems conservative in term of 
healthy population (Table 8). While, the higher 
iodine exposure of 1000- 1100 μg/day is not 
advisable, since the higher exposure in a long 
term might have significant impact on the TSH 
and T4 with the slightly changes on T3 [90]. 
Besides, Wolff- Chaikoff escape phenomenon, 
does not occur in some individuals [4, 91]. 
Especially, 
in 
individuals 
with 
thyroid 
dysfunction which are more vulnerable to 
develop hypothyroidism than healthy ones, 
individuals 
with 
previous 
thyroid 
autoimmunity, or other underlying thyroid 
issues, when exposed to fairly high dose of 
iodine [79, 84]. Thus, the intake of 1000- 1100 
μg/day for the general population of adults 
considered in this study.   
The only source of iodine intake should be 
milk, formula, and daily dietary for the age 
below one. Thus, both EFSA and USA, did not 
consider the UL for this age group.  The 
estimation of UL of both approaches for the 
different age groups between (1- 18), were 
based on adjusting the adult factor with the 
body weight, and not on the observation on the 
children 
and 
adolescence. 
Mostly, 
the 
consequence of thyroid dysfunction, depends 
on the age of the infant or child. It is due to, the 
most 
thyroid 
hormone-dependent 
brain 
development is completed by the age of 3 years 
[81]. For instance, newborn infants with 
congenital hypothyroidism commonly have 
delayed on skeletal maturation, liver, and bone, 
with the risk of permanent mental retardation 
[81]. While, children above 3 years that 
develop 
hypothyroidism, 
might 
have 
predominantly delayed in growth and skeletal 
maturation, but not permanent impact on 
cognitive development [81]. According to 
studied on the school-aged children, the 
recommended UL for the age groups of 7-10, 
==PAGE:41==

11-14 were 150- 249 µg/d iodine and 150- 299 
µg/d iodine respectively, since with the higher 
iodine intake have impact on  thyroid volume,

and the approaches that EFSA and USA used 
as a reference is not clear. Adequate iodine 
intake is essential during pregnancy for 
keeping the appropriate thyroid function in the 
mothers and normal brain development during 
fetal and neonates, as a high amount of iodine 
in the mothers is transferred to the placenta or 
in the milk, hence the daily intake of iodine for 
the pregnant and lactation women should be

increased for the lactating mother up to 200 µg

UL is 600 µg per day (Table 8). Since iodine is 
excreted through the breast milk for breastfed 
infants and children, the adequate intake of 
iodine by mother´s is essential, [95, 99]. 
Excessive iodine intake by lactating mothers 
might be related to the thyroid disturbance and 
subclinical hypothyroidism in their infants and 
children [100, 101], and as the adverse effects 
mentioned earlier, thus the value of intake of 
iodine of 600 µg/d estimated by EFSA seems a 
sensible UL for the European lactating women.

the scientific observation, it seems the value of 
UL is acceptable for the Japanese, and not for 
the other races.

4.3.1. Estimation of daily exposure of 
iodine in Denmark 
The 7 days survey by DANSDA in 2011- 2013 
showed that from all the 23 subjects of food 
groups, the consumption of milk and dairy 
products related to the highest intake of iodine 
in all age groups (Appendix E). The daily 
consumption (95 percentile) for the age groups 
of 11-14, 15-17, and ≥ 18 were lower than UL 
of 600 µg/day established by EFSA (Table 11), 
while the age groups of 4- 6, and 7-10 
exceeded the UL.  
The average intake of iodine from milk and 
dairy products was 81.1 µg/day which means 
all age groups get 41% of their daily iodine 
from Milk and dairy products. Spices and other 
excipients, bread, grain and starch products, 
beverages, and fish and fish products with the 
average of 38.8 (19%), 29.1 (15%), 17.5 (9%), 
and 11.3 (6%) respectively are the highest 
dietary intake groups after milk and dairy 
products (Appendix E).   
For the age groups of 1 to 37 months, the total 
iodine intake was within the EFSA UL 
(Appendix F). The infant milk formula, breast 
milk, milk and dairy products, porridge and 
ready to eat products were the main sources of 
iodine intake for these age groups.  
 
4.3.2.  Estimation of exposure of iodine 
from intake of different portion of 
seaweed salad  
To our best of knowledge, detail data 
regarding the amount of seaweed consumption 
in Denmark or the rest of EU have not been 
published in literature search.  
In attempt to estimate the iodine exposure 
from seaweed, Phaneuf et al. used different 
cookbooks to report a maximum consumption 
of 12.5 gr DW in a single serving and 
furthermore, average daily intake of 3.3 gr DW 
of seaweed by Japanese based on the study by 
[113] considered. Recent published studies 
have used these references given by Phaneuf et 
al [66], as a reference to estimate the iodine 
exposure of seaweed [17, 114]. However, this 
approach did not considered in this study. 
Since, the Japanese intake rate is not reflecting 
the daily/weekly European consumption, as 
the Japanese seaweed consumption data has 
been available and has remained moderately 
constant during the years (4.3 g/day in 1955 
and 5.3 g/day in 1995) [115]. Moreover, 
seaweed is not accounting as the main food 
source, but rather as side-dish or ingredients in 
Denmark. 
Furthermore, 
the 
maximum 
consumption of 12.5 gr DW is equal to almost 
300 g WW (3 boxes) of fermented salad made 
==PAGE:43==
36 
 
by Nordisk Tang, which seems to be rather 
unusual intake for a single serving for one 
person in Europe. Lastly, Phaneuf et al did not 
specified the source of the cooking books nor 
described the method of calculating the 12.5 gr 
DW. Normally, cooking books have recipe of 
one dish for at least 2-4 people and hence 
difficult to calculate the exact single intake 
amount.  
Additionally, 
Food 
and 
Agriculture 
Organization of the United Nations (FAO) 
reported the amount of export and import of 
seaweed for food and feed between 2012- 2016 
[116]. Accordingly, the consumption of 
seaweed in 2015 was estimated at 34.2 gr per 
person yearly in Denmark (Statistics Denmark 
2016). Thus, the Danish consumption was 0.09 
gr/d/person. 
However, 
this 
report 
has 
following short comings (i) due to Danish 
cuisine culture, the whole Danish population 
could not be considered as seaweed eaters, and 
(ii) seaweed consumption should be estimated 
based on the dietary records, and food surveys 
[108]. Therefore, FAO estimation did not 
considered in this study. Hence, in this study a 
new scenario was considered to estimate the 
daily seaweed consumption per person, based 
on the Nordisk tang salad package size. A box 
of fermented salad weighing 85g (Fig. 8) had 
almost 25-30 g of dressing, hence the pure 
seaweed salad was estimated to have 50g 
weight (Figure XX). A low, moderate and high 
consumption scenario were: 
 
A low consumption: 
a. 5 gr (0.4 gr fdw) (one 
teaspoon),  
b. 15 gr portion (1.1 gr fdw) (1 
table spoon),  
c. 25 gr (1.8 gr fdw) (half of a 
box)  
 
 
 A moderate consumption; 
a. 50 gr (3.5 gr fdw) (one box, see 
figure)  
 
 A high consumption: 
a. 100 gr (7 gr fdw) (two boxes)  
Moreover, the results of the concentration of 
iodine in fermented salad I (section 3.1.2.1) 
and fermented salad II (section 3.1.2.3) (Table

intake form fermented salad.  
The dietary iodine intake was estimated base

Iodine Intake form fermented salad (µ/day) =

4.3.3. Estimation of total intake of iodine  
Since, the iodine intake sources for the 
children under the age of 3 recommended to be 
only milk, formula, and dietary daily intake, 
age group of 0-1 and 1-3 was not consider in 
the estimation of daily dietary plus fermented 
salad consumption.  
Total intake of iodine (µg/daily) were 
determined based on the equation below: 
Total intake of iodine (µg/day) = Dietary 
intake + Iodine Intake form fermented salad  
The total intake would be in a range of UL for

4.4. 
Risk characterisation 
In this study, regarding the estimation of 
potential human health risk from the total daily 
intake (consumption of fermented salad and 
seaweed juice besides the dietary intake), 
different scenarios considered (section 4.3.2 
and 4.3.4). The results of total iodine exposure 
were listed in table 13, 14, and 16 (section 
4.3.3). Based on the discussion of the hazard 
characterisation (section 4.2.4), UL of 1000-
1100 μg/day iodine for adults considered to be 
reasonable in this study, and  the EFSA 
tolerable upper limit intake of iodine for 
different age groups of 4-18, and pregnant and 
lactation women considered sensible in this 
study. 
Since the UL of 1000-1100 µg/d for adults 
were considered in this study, the daily intake 
of 25- 50 g (one box) of fermented salad I, and 
15- 25 g of fermented salad II might be 
acceptable. Further, due to the acute Wolf-
Chaikoff effect mechanism in healthy adults, 
high portion of  100 g (two boxes) of salad I, 
and moderate to high portion of  5o- 100 g of 
fermented salad II as a single serving once in a 
week might be acceptable. However, both 
fermented salads were contained the spring 
kelps. As the seasonal variation (Table 6 and 
7) effect the iodine concentration of the kelps, 
the fermented salad would have lower or 
higher amount of iodine. Thus, once a week, a 
moderate single serving of 50 g (one box) of 
fermented salad would be recommended to the 
healthy adults.  
According to table 11 (section 4.3.1), 
children and adolescent have high dietary 
intake of iodine, and as these age groups are 
under the body development, the adequate 
thyroid function is important. Thus, the low 
consumption of 5 g (one tea spoon) of 
fermented 
salad, 
once 
in 
a 
week 
recommended.  
As mentioned in hazard characterisation, the 
most vulnerable groups are children under 3, 
neonates, and fetus, as their thyroid gland is 
under development. Thus the fermented 
seaweed salad consumption should be avoided 
during the pregnancy and lactation, and 
children under 3 years old.  
According to table 16 (section 4.3.4), all the 
age groups except adults will exceed the range 
of UL by consumption of seaweed juice daily 
(total consumption). Based on the seasonal 
variation in seaweed (table 6), the seaweed 
juice might have lower or higher iodine 
concentration in different months. Thus, the 
daily intake of seaweed juice is not 
recommended for adults. Moreover, rarely 
intake of seaweed juice is advisable for the age 
group of 4- 18. Lastly, seaweed juice 
consumption should be avoided during the 
pregnancy and lactation, and children under 3 
years old.  
Despite of the studies recommendation about 
iodine deficiency treatment with seaweed, it is 
not recommended in this study. Iodine 
deficient individuals are more sensible on the 
excess of iodine. Small increase in dietary 
iodide intake of this group is likely to cause 
hyperthyroidism which widely documented in 
endemic goiter areas [117]. The standard 
dosage of iodine will be recommended to 
overcome the iodine deficiency.

5. Conclusion 
In the present study, the reduction of iodine 
concentration in the different production 
processes of fermented seaweed salad based on 
Sugar kelp and Winged kelp, and seaweed 
juice 
based 
on 
Bladder 
wrack 
were 
investigated. Based on chemical analysis 
results of the iodine concentration obtained 
from the finished products (fermented salad or 
seaweed juice), and the daily dietary intake in 
Denmark, the total exposure of iodine were 
estimated. 
Moreover, 
according 
to 
the 
estimation of the total iodine intake and with 
the consideration of UL, the risk assessment 
was performed.  
The processes of production of fermented 
salad had a major impact on reduction of 
iodine concentration in both Sugar kelp and 
winged kelp. The defrosting, blanching and 
soaking as a part of the fermentation steps were 
effective to decrease iodine amount in 
seaweed. Due to unknown factors, defrosted 
kelps of Salad II had 3 times higher iodine 
concentration compare to frozen kelps of Salad 
I which both kelps were harvested in the same 
month. However, both iodine concentrations 
were in line with previously published reports. 
The UL estimation of EFSA were assessed to 
be realistic for all different age groups, 
however, the UL of 1000-1100 µg/d iodine 
was considered to be more reasonable for the 
healthy European adults. Hence, once a week a  
 
 
 
 
moderate single serving of fermented salad is 
considered to be acceptable for healthy adults. 
Due 
to 
seasonal 
variation 
of 
iodine 
concentration in seaweed, frequently intake of 
seaweed juice is not recommended for the 
healthy adults. 
In 
case 
of 
children, 
adolescent 
and 
individuals with the thyroid dysfunction, the 
low consumption, once a week, of fermented 
salad is not assessed to be associated with 
health concern. Likewise, rarely intake of 
seaweed juice is not associated with health 
risks for the age group of 4- 18. However, the 
consumption of fermented salad and seaweed 
juice should be avoided during the pregnancy 
and lactation, and for all children under 3 years 
old due excessive iodine adverse effect on the 
fetus, 
newborns 
and 
children 
organ 
development.  
Previously published studies recommend 
moderate intake of seaweed to address the 
iodine deficiency in individuals; however, our 
observation 
does 
not 
support 
this 
recommendation and it is advised to use 
standard dosage e.g. salt.  
Finally, consideration of seaweed as a part of 
varied diet seems not to be of health concern.

5.1. 
Limitation 
In this study we lacked samples representing 
the whole process of producing fermented 
salad, hence it was not possible to calculate the 
exact mass balance. In order to estimate the 
effectiveness of the production processes in the 
reduction of iodine more data was needed. 
Furthermore, detail data on kelp e.g. harvesting 
area would have helped explaining variation 
that was seen between the kelps in this study.  
Due to the lack of published data on 
consumption of seaweed in Denmark and also 
generally in EU, it was difficult to consider 
exact intake scenarios and hence evaluate the 
risk exposure precisely. The understanding of 
determining the uncertainty factor by EFSA 
and USA was difficult since there was not 
enough explanation of how the uncertainty 
factor was considered. 
 
5.2. 
Perspective  
Appropriate 
product 
labeling 
for 
the 
consumer safety will be recommended for the 
future packaging of fermented salad and 
seaweed juice. Due to the seasonal variation, 
the optimal harvesting time of seaweed with 
lower iodine concentration for the production 
will be advised. From risk assessment point of 
view, the use of SK in fermented-salad 
production, should be limited to minimum in 
order to reduce the higher content of iodine. 
Additionally, 
efficient 
iodine 
reduction 
processes in industry needs to be investigated 
and implemented.  
Long-term studies in adverse effect of excess 
of iodine in both healthy general population 
and 
among 
individuals 
with 
thyroid 
dysfunction is needed in order to assess the 
impacts and define the UL. Currently the 
seaweed is not on the list of the food survey, 
and it is recommended to do so, since seaweed 
is becoming more customary. Both authorities 
and industry needs more data in order to 
capture the seaweed consumption in different 
age groups especially with regards to pregnant 
women, children under the age of 4 and people 
with thyroid diseases.
