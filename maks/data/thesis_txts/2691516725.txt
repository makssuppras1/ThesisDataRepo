Abstract
A 2D particle-in-cell (PIC) model of the DTU tokamak NORTH has been
developed and implemented in a Python script where simulations with
upwards of 34 million superparticles have been performed. From the
simulated data, we were able to study dynamics perpendicular to the toroidal
magnetic ﬁeld for 2 different plasmas. First we studied so-called seeded
plasma blobs which were evolved over time. Here we found a square root
dependence in both blob size and amplitude for the maximum centre of mass
velocity, in agreement with the literature. The second plasma studied was
generated by continuous electron cyclotron resonance heating similar to the
heating method of the NORTH tokamak. The dynamics of this plasma was
studied by analysing stream plots of E × B drifts and electron density proﬁles
for simulations with and without a Langmuir probe. We found that the
Langmuir probe had no signiﬁcant effect on the plasma beyond length scales
comparable to the Debye length, in agreement with the theory of Debye
shielding. The density proﬁles were compared to experimental results where
discrepancies which in part are due to modelling in 2D was seen.

1.1
Fusion Research Motivation
Fossil fuels such as oil are currently a major player in the global energy market-
place. Many believe that fossil fuels pose a threat to the global climate due to
their emission of greenhouse gasses and that their usage should be phased out
as soon as possible. The global energy demand must then be satisﬁed by other
energy sources. The beneﬁt of renewable energy sources such as solar, wind
etc. is that they don’t emit greenhouse gases but each of them has its drawbacks
compared to fusion power[1]. Solar and wind power for example require a lot
of land or sea area to produce a signiﬁcant amount of energy. Still, the energy
production of these energy sources is unreliable due to their dependence on
favourable weather conditions[2]. Contrary to this, fusion power plants will be
able to produce energy year-round without any dependence on weather con-
ditions and on the same amount of land as say ﬁssion power plants. Fission
power is a very good source of energy since it is reliable and doesn’t require a
lot of land. The drawbacks of ﬁssion power are however relevant and should
be considered. The fuel is rare, so mining it will become increasingly hard
as time goes on. Fission can therefore not be considered a renewable energy
source. Additionally, due to the risk of mishandling radioactive waste material
and reactor meltdowns, the general public considers ﬁssion power plants dan-
gerous and undesirable. Although rare, reactor meltdowns can cause signiﬁ-
cant long term consequences to the global environment by emission of radioac-
tive materials into the atmosphere. This is a relevant concern since radioactive
materials are highly carcinogenic to both humans and animals. While plasmas
in tokamaks are conﬁned magnetically, they are not self-sustaining as opposed
to ﬁssion reactions. Reactor meltdowns and similar catastrophes are therefore
inherently impossible in a tokamak power plant. There is also no radioactive
waste material left over except for the chamber itself which will only be ra-
dioactive for around 100-150 years. While fusion power is an excellent energy
source, like the other energy sources it has its drawbacks but they are mostly
technological or political. For fusion power plants to be viable, the gain factor

1.3
Tokamak Basics and NORTH Introduction
Regardless of their complexity, tokamaks can be boiled down to a device that
creates and maintains a plasma inside a torus-shaped vacuum chamber and
conﬁnes it with magnetic ﬁelds. Diagnostics are added to monitor the condi-
tions of the plasma. The vacuum chamber provides a high vacuum environ-
ment which allows control of the gases present. NORTH has a vacuum cham-
ber with a circular cross-section as opposed to the ”D-shape” chamber cross-
section often seen in large scale tokamaks. The plasma is created and main-
tained by continuous ionisation of a gas using a heating source. The heating
source is in NORTH a microwave emitter that creates and maintains the plasma
through electron cyclotron resonance heating. To conﬁne the plasma, a toroidal
and poloidal magnetic ﬁeld is applied but in NORTH, only a toroidal ﬁeld is
applied making the plasma inherently unstable due to radial plasma drifts.
The only plasma diagnostic considered here is the Langmuir probe which is
used to measure the electron temperature and density indirectly through I-V
curves.

1.4
Project Motivation
While NORTH is a small-scale tokamak experiment compared to larger-scale
experiments, physical mechanisms may still be explored. Relevant knowledge
of plasma dynamics may then be obtained by studying numerical models and
experimental measurements in NORTH. Since NORTH is operated at low tem-
peratures and densities, the plasmas observed can be assumed collisionless.
Fluid models which work well in collisional plasmas can therefore not be used
to simulate NORTH plasmas since the assumption of collisionality will not be
met. To obtain a valid model, a different approach is then to use a discrete
particle model. The project is therefore mainly motivated towards obtaining a
model and script that can simulate plasmas in NORTH. In this project, we con-
sider 2 different ways of generating a plasma. So-called seeded plasma blobs
are studied ﬁrst where results are compared to results of the mentioned ﬂuid
models. While the main focus of the project is the development, implemen-
tation and validation of the model, we also wish to understand dynamics ob-
served in the NORTH simulations. To this, we study how the electric potential
and particle orbits evolve and consider electron density proﬁles.
1.5
Project Description
This project aims to develop and implement a PIC model that simulates guid-
ing centre drifts of discrete charged particles. In this project, we consider 2 dif-
ferent plasmas in a vacuum chamber modelled after NORTH. The ﬁrst plasma
studied is known as seeded plasma blobs where an initial plasma density struc-
ture is evolved over time. Scans of the blob amplitude and size are performed
where the centre of mass velocity and compactness of the blobs are analysed
and compared to results from the literature. The second plasma is continu-
ously generated and modelled after plasma creation in NORTH. The dynamics
of the NORTH plasma are studied using stream plots and density proﬁles. The
stream plots and density proﬁles with and without the Langmuir probe are
compared to each other to see the effect the probe has on the plasma. The den-
sity proﬁles are ﬁnally compared to experimental results obtained in NORTH.
1.6
Simulating Discrete Particles
Simulating discrete particles is at its most basic level a task of keeping track
of the location and velocity of each particle between timesteps. This task can
be solved with the mentioned PIC method. Here, space is discretised into a
rectangular grid mesh. The charge of the particles are interpolated with a ex-
ponentially decaying function onto the gridpoints without changing the actual
positions of the particles. From the resulting potential, the E-ﬁeld is found
on each gridpoint. Electric and magnetic ﬁelds are then interpolated onto each
particle’s position to update their velocity using drift equations. The PIC model
is inherently collisionless since each particle only feels a collective Coulomb
potential rather than the potential of each particle. A plasma can be consid-

The NORTH tokamak has been modelled and simulated in a geometry that
includes a simpliﬁed Langmuir probe. We have studied the dynamics of the
plasma by considering potential structures, E × B drift stream plots and den-
sity proﬁles. The E × B drift was initially dictated by the ∇B drift and probe
potential but when enough particles have separated, the E × B was dictated
by the potential of the plasma. The results indicate that the external potential

since no signiﬁcant changes to the density occur with and without the probe
except at very close distances where the plasma is seen to be drained(the aver-
age density decreases dramatically). The measurements without the probe can
be thought of as the true density and it is of course important that the probe
doesn’t affect the plasma signiﬁcantly since that would make measurements
invalid. The probe should therefore measure the correct electron density of
the plasma given that the theory presented in Section 2.1.6 is correct. It would
then be very interesting to measure the probe current from particles lost on the
probe and see if the correct density comes out of the simulated I-V curves.
Comparisons between the simulated density proﬁles with experimental pro-
ﬁles were performed. We see a small peak in the experimentally observed den-
sity proﬁle which corresponds with the peak observed in the simulated density
proﬁle. The largest density was found near the outer walls which were not in
agreement with the simulation results and in fact the overall shape of the curve
didn’t correspond well with the simulated curve. This is likely caused by the
fact that we consider a 2D model where in the real 3D experiment, particles
are created at a different location than the probe. Then, while particles stream
along ˆΦ they will drift outwards which will cause the measured density pro-

model should therefore be considered and some aspects are discussed in the
outlook. We also note that the missing curvature drifts and polarisation drifts
must be implemented to achieve the full dynamics. The model must then be
validated again in the seeded blob study since the maximum centre of mass
scalings might change.

Conclusions
A simple 2D PIC model has been developed and implemented in a Python
script and is used to study plasmas in the DTU tokamak NORTH. Seeded
plasma blob studies validate the model since we ﬁnd that the maximum centre
of mass velocity scale with a square root dependence in both the initial blob
size σ and amplitude n0 which is in agreement with ﬂuid models[7, 8, 9] of a
similar nature. Meanwhile the time at which the maximum velocity occur was
measured with a √σ and 1/√n0 dependence. Compactness measurements are
also in agreement with the ﬂuid models as they decrease throughout the simu-
lations towards zero. The measurements shift to the right for larger blob sizes
and shift to the left for larger amplitudes. It is believed that the time depen-
dence of the compactness scale with √σ and 1/√n0 since the compactness at
maximum centre of mass velocity were all distributed around ˆIc = 0.4.
Dynamics of a continuously generated plasma modelled after plasma creation
in NORTH has been studied by looking at electric potential structures, E × B
drifts and density proﬁles. Through these studies we were able to see how
the E × B drifts develop and we are able to explain why density snapshots
evolve the way that they do. We ﬁnd that interesting potential structures arise
in which charge separation occur in the front of the plasma as expected with
negative charge density on top and positive charge density below. Newly cre-
ated particles then form a charge separation where the negative charge density
is on the bottom and positive charge is on top. These structure causes parti-
cles to be ejected from both the front and back of the plasma which causes the
plasma to become very turbulent.
The average and maximum density was measured on a line at y = 0 on the 2D
plane. The density proﬁle and the stream plots of the E × B drifts suggest that
the probe doesn’t have a signiﬁcant effect on the plasma beyond length scales
that are less than 3λD, in agreement with the theory of Debye shielding. The
density proﬁle is compared to an experimentally determined density proﬁle
which ﬁnd a small density peak at the same location as the simulated density
proﬁle. Signiﬁcant discrepancies between the proﬁles were seen and are be-
lieved to be an effect of reducing the 3D experiment to a 2D model. Because
the curvature and polarisation drifts aren’t implemented, the full dynamics
of the perpendicular drifts cannot be seen and should be implemented before
more well founded conclusions can be drawn.
