Abstract
Neutron scattering methods are essential for studying the structure and dynamics of mat-
ter, yet the tools available for manipulating neutron beams remain limited compared to
those for X-rays. Wolter mirror configurations, commonly used in X-ray telescopes, have
been demonstrated to focus neutron beams [1], [2]. Inspired by this potential, this project
seeks to apply expertise in X-ray optics from the Center for High-Energy X-ray Systems
(CHEXS) and DTU Space to develop coatings for neutron-focusing optics.
This thesis investigates the design and fabrication of a supermirror coating based on nickel
and titanium, utilizing multilayer thin film physics to enhance neutron reflectivity.
The first part of the project focuses on the design and optimization of the supermirror
coating, which is based on a power-law depth-graded multilayer stack. The goal of the
design optimization was to extend the high-reflectivity response of the mirror beyond the
natural critical angle of Ni at θ = 0.3◦(m = 1), reaching θ = 0.9◦(m = 3) at a neutron
wavelength of λ = 3 Å.
The second part focuses on the experimental fabrication of the supermirror using
DC magnetron sputtering at DTU Space. Preliminary experiments were conducted on
Ni and Ti single layers as well as Ti/Ni and Ni/Ti periodic multilayers to investigate
collimation methods and the influence of deposition order on surface and interface rough-
ness. Additionally, reactive sputtering with N2 was tested for Ni/Ti multilayers. The
preliminary samples were characterized using 8.048 keV X-ray reflectometry (XRR) at
DTU Space, while the supermirror samples were characterized using X-ray reflectometry
at DTU Space and BESSY II in Berlin, atomic force microscopy (AFM), and scanning
transmission electron microscopy (STEM) at DTU Nanolab.
A 256-bilayer supermirror coating design was optimized to successfully reach m = 3, achiev-
ing a simulated average reflectance of 98%, assuming a surface and interface roughness of
1 nm.
Experimental investigations of single layers showed that honeycomb collimation did not
significantly reduce roughness for Ni or Ti. The periodic multilayer experiments showed
that Ni/Ti, with Ti deposited first, is the preferred deposition order, as it resulted in lower
roughness, and that sputtering in pure argon is more effective than reactive sputtering with
N2 in minimizing roughness.
STEM measurements showed that the supermirror coating experiment successfully
produced a well-defined layer structure in which all layers are distinguishable, marking
an important milestone in supermirror fabrication at DTU Space. Characterization with
XRR and AFM indicated that the layer roughness ranges from 1 nm to 4 nm. By combining
experimental characterization with neutron performance simulations, this study highlights
the critical role of layer roughness in optimizing reflectivity performance. Future work
should focus on alternative roughness suppression strategies, such as exploring NiC and
TiV in multilayers, to further enhance supermirror performance.

Introduction
X-ray and neutron scattering methods are important tools for studying the structure and
dynamics of matter, spanning over an increasingly larger range of disciplines, including
physics, chemistry, biology, ceramics, and metallurgy [3]. Both methods exploit the wave
nature of X-rays and neutrons, with wavelengths on the order of an Ångström (10−10 m)
which matches the atomic spacings in solids.
While X-rays can be produced by laboratory-scale equipment, neutrons are only avail-
able at international large-scale facilities. Despite that, the intensity of neutron beams is
significantly lower. Compared to X-rays from synchrotron sources, the neutron facilities
produce intensities that are many orders of magnitude weaker [4]. This presents a signif-
icant limitation in neutron science, where data collection is constrained by low available
flux. As a result, optimizing neutron beam transport and focusing techniques are critical
to preserve the neutron flux from the source to the sample. However, the toolbox for
manipulating neutron beams is still relatively small compared to that for X-rays [1].
Manipulating and focusing X-rays and neutrons is inherently diﬀicult due to their weak
interaction with matter. This presents a challenge for producing eﬀicient reflective optics
such as mirrors. At normal incidence, their reflectance is essentially zero, causing the
beam to pass through rather than reflect on the mirror. As a result, conventional opti-
cal instruments designed for normal-incidence reflection do not work, and alternatives are
needed. Because of the refractive index of X-rays and neutrons in all materials, their reflec-
tion is restricted to extremely shallow grazing angles. In addition, the possible angles of
reflection significantly diminish as the wavelength decreases. This requires mirror surfaces
to be nearly parallel to the incoming X-ray or neutron beam for effective reflection.
Leveraging the physics of multilayer thin films, it is possible to design supermirror
coatings that vastly improve the eﬀiciency of X-ray and neutron mirrors, enabling them
to operate at higher angles and energies. These coatings consist of depth-graded stacks
of two alternating materials with distinct optical properties. Their functionality relies on
constructive interference of waves reflected at the interfaces within the layered structure.
A main challenge in multilayer thin-film production is to deposit smooth layers to minimize
interface roughness and diffusion, which can reduce reflected intensity.
Center for High-Energy X-ray Systems (CHEXS) is a company specializing in X-ray optics
for industrial applications, while DTU Space has world-leading expertise in X-ray optics
for high-energy astrophysical missions. Wolter mirror configurations, commonly used for
X-ray space telescopes, have been demonstrated to focus neutron beams [1], [2]. This
opens new opportunities in the field of neutron science and has driven the demand for
a neutron-focusing device, known as a neutron microscope, for the ODIN instrument in
development at the European Spallation Source (ESS) in Lund, Sweden. By leveraging
the expertise in X-ray optics of CHEXS and DTU Space, this project aims to advance
neutron optics technology, with a primary focus on designing and fabricating supermirror
coatings for a neutron microscope operating at a wavelength of λ = 3 Å.
This work
presents first a simulation and optimization study of a thin film coating design, followed
by its experimental fabrication and characterization.

2.1
Fundamental Properties of X-rays and Neutrons
X-rays were discovered in 1895 by William Röntgen, while neutrons were discovered in
1932 by James Chadwick. The particle nature of X-rays is described by photons, which
are elementary, massless particles that propagate at the speed of light. Neutrons, on the
other hand, have a mass of mn = 1.67 × 10−27 kg, close to that of the proton, and travel
at non-relativistic speeds. The neutron does not exist naturally in free form but decays
into a proton, an electron, and an anti-neutrino. However, the neutron lifetime, τ = 886 s,
is much longer than the time a neutron spends within a scattering experiment, which is
merely a fraction of a second [5]. Therefore, neutron decay can typically be neglected in
experiments.
Both neutrons and photons are electrically neutral, classifying them as uncharged
radiation.
The neutron interacts with the atomic nuclei via the strong nuclear force,
while X-rays interact with the electron cloud via electromagnetic force.
Since X-rays
interact with the electron cloud, their scattering power goes monotonously across the
periodic table with the number of electrons. This also means that adjacent elements in
the periodic system have very similar scattering factors. The interaction between neutrons
and the nucleus is determined by the nuclear energy levels and the specific structure of
the nucleus. Consequently, this interaction occurs irregularly across the periodic table
and can vary significantly between isotopes [6]. This means that the contrast between two
elements probed with X-rays and neutrons will be very different, and the two techniques
can be used complimentary. Neutron absorption is generally negligible except for a few
isotopes, eg. B-10. This allows neutrons to study buried surfaces, that would otherwise
not be transparent using X-rays.

The neutron is a 1/2 spin particle, meaning that neutrons have a magnetic moment
which allows magnetic scattering [6]. This gives neutrons unique capabilities for research
in magnetic materials.
This can be done by using polarized neutron beams in which
the neutron spins are oriented in the same direction, which allows measurement of the
strength and characteristics of magnetism within a material. However, in this work, the
focus is exclusively on nuclear scattering and unpolarized neutron beams, following the
requirements of the ODIN instrument.
2.1.1
Particle-wave duality
A fundamental consequence of quantum mechanics is that matter exhibits both particle
and wave-like behavior. This duality applies to both X-rays and neutrons: X-rays are
governed by the electromagnetic wave equation, and neutrons by the Schrödinger wave
equation.
Both X-rays and neutrons can be described as complex plane waves with a spatial

Reflection and Refraction from Interfaces
When an electromagnetic wave or a matter wave travels between two media with different
refractive indices, its direction of propagation changes. Because of the discontinuity at
the boundary between the two media, the wave is partially reflected back into the first
medium and partially transmitted into the second.

Typical values in the X-ray band for δ are on the order of 10−5 to 10−6, and conse-

angle. However, for materials with a negative neutron scattering length, e.g. Ti and Mn,
δ will be negative, leading to n > 1, and the condition of total external reflection does not
exist.

2.2.4
Rough interfaces
In the equations above, it was assumed that all interfaces are perfectly flat and sharp. In
the ideal case of a perfectly sharp interface between the materials, the interface profile,
p(z), is a unit step function, illustrated in Figure 2.5.
In reality, surfaces always exhibit some irregularities, reducing the overall specular
reflectance.
These imperfections are generally referred to as roughness, which can be
described by two basic types.
The first type is diffuse roughness, caused by diffusion
of atoms from one material into another.
The second type is pure roughness, where
atoms in the structure have been deposited unevenly to create ’hills’ and ’valleys’ in the
surface, causing rays to scatter into non-specular directions [16]. Real interfaces are often
a combination of the two types. The two roughness types are indistinguishable at the

measurements are required to tell them apart. This is because one measures a mean of
the SLD averaged over a large surface compared to the size of the roughness for a given

Thin Film Coatings
The X-ray and neutron performance of single- and multilayer coatings is determined by

manufactured, and the structural design. The principles for designing X-ray and neutron

Throughout this work, neutron reflectivity simulations are performed using a Python

2.3.2
Multilayer
A single-layer coating will only be effective up to its critical angle, beyond which the
reflectivity rapidly drops.
A multilayer coating can be designed to significantly boost
reflectivity beyond the critical angle by utilizing constructive interference from diffraction.
This is done through the careful construction of layers of alternating materials, that are
turned into a crystal-like structure periodic lattice. It follows from the Fresnel equations
in Eq. 2.11 that the contrast in the refractive index of the alternating layers needs to be
as large as possible to maximize the portion of the wave being reflected at an interface.
Thus, one material should have a high scattering power, referred to as the reflector, while
the second material should provide a high contrast and low absorption, referred to as
the spacer. Such a layer pair is referred to as a bilayer. The thickness of a bilayer is
called the d-spacing, where d = dR + dS. Here, dR and dS are the thicknesses of the
reflector and the spacer, respectively. The relative thickness of the bilayer pair is defined
as Γ = dR/d. A multilayer stack generally consists of a repetition of N bilayers. If the
bilayer thickness is constant through the stack, the multilayer is said to be periodic. The
d-spacings must be optimized for the application to achieve maximum reflectivity in the
desired bandpass. This is done using the Bragg condition, which gives the condition for

2.3.2.1
Material selection
Due to the simple relationship between the X-ray scattering length and the atomic number,
combining a high-Z and low-Z material is the typical choice to achieve high contrast in
the X-ray domain. For example, the X-ray telescope NuSTAR included two multilayer
designs consisting of Pt/C and W/Si [15].
In the neutron domain, Ni/Ti is the most commonly used multilayer combination.
Table 2.1 shows that both Ti and Mn exhibit large negative real components of the SLD,
making them suitable for providing good contrast in multilayers. While both Ni/Ti and
Ni/Mn multilayers have been produced, Ebisawa et al. favor Ni/Ti mirrors due to their
superior durability compared to Ni/Mn mirrors [24]. Additionally, when the number of
layers exceeds 100, Ni/Mn multilayers are prone to peeling off from the glass substrate.
As a result, Ni/Ti is typically the preferred material combination for neutron multilayers.

A periodic multilayer will produce a spectrum with a good response at a narrow set of
energies corresponding to the Bragg peaks, and a poor response in between the peaks. The
more bilayer repetitions N in the multilayer, the more narrow the response and the higher
the reflectivity. However, there may come a point where adding more bilayers becomes
ineﬀicient. In X-rays, the limiting factor is typically absorption, rendering the lower layers
in the stack ineffective. In neutrons, the primary limitation is typically the precision of
interface fabrication.

The following subsections describe the principle of a neutron microscope, which is the

3.2.1
The Wolter-I geometry
The Wolter-I geometry and its conical approximation have been the primary designs used
by most X-ray telescopes flown on satellite missions [27]. Studies have shown that the
Wolter mirror configuration is also capable of focusing neutron beams in a manner anal-
ogous to its application with X-rays [1]. The Wolter-I optic geometry is composed of a
parabolic surface and a hyperbolic surface that share a single focal point. The ray first hits
the parabolic surface, then hits the hyperbolic surface, and then focuses to a point. The
grazing angle of an on-axis ray to the mirror surface is denoted θ. Since there are two re-
flections, the ray is bent by 4θ to reach its focal point. Most Wolter-I devices nest multiple
shells to increase their collecting area. An illustration of a Wolter-I mirror configuration
is shown in Figure 3.2.

3.3.2
Hayter-Mook design
The Hayter-Mook algorithm is described in its original paper from 1989 [31].
The al-
gorithm constructs the supermirror stack on the principle of Bragg’s law by choosing
the thickness of successive bilayers such that their reflectivity profiles intersect at the half-
intensity points. The algorithm begins with the thickest layer, corresponding to the lowest
incident angles, and iteratively determines the thickness of each subsequent bilayer. The
optimal Bragg angles for each bilayer are calculated to maintain continuous reflectivity.
At each angle, the Ni and Ti layer thicknesses are chosen so that each layer acts as a
quarter-wave plate. This ensures that the total bilayer is a half-wave plate, meaning that
the neutron wave accumulates a phase shift of π when passing through a bilayer. This
is the condition for constructive interference as governed by Bragg’s law. The algorithm
terminates once the desired m-value of the supermirror is achieved.
The algorithm has a parameter ν that represents the number of bilayers contributing
to reflection at a given Bragg angle. At least one bilayer must contribute to reflection at
any given angle, hence ν ≥1.
The Hayter-Mook algorithm is implemented in the open-source GenX software as part
of its ”models” library [20]. To validate the implementation, the code is cross-checked
with the original equations from the Hayter and Mook paper. The algorithm is then used
to design a supermirror that achieves m = 3 at a neutron wavelength of λ = 3 Å. The
minimum number of bilayers required is 256, as fewer layers would result in ν < 1, causing
the algorithm to fail. The design is generated with this number of bilayers since there is
no performance gain from adding more. Finally, an encapsulating Ni layer is added to
ensure a smooth transition across the critical angle, such that the top Ni layer is effectively
50 nm, similar to the power-law design.

Figure 3.12 shows three scenarios involving missing layers at bilayer numbers 20, 70,

The power-law graded stack achieves broad angular reflectance by distributing the
Bragg condition across a range of angles. When a single layer is missing, the local gradient
is disrupted, preventing constructive interference at the intended angle and causing a
sharp drop in reflectivity.
In contrast, when an entire bilayer is missing, the overall
gradient remains largely intact, as neighboring bilayers still contribute reflectivity over
overlapping angular ranges. This minimizes the impact of the missing bilayer, resulting in
an insignificant drop in reflectivity.

This chapter introduces the coating facility at DTU Space for the fabrication of thin films.
Moreover, an overview of the coating experiments performed in this work is given.
A milestone for the DTU Space coating facility was the production of the two flight
optics for the Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array (NuSTAR), completed between
July 2010 and February 2011. This effort required a total of 239 coating runs [32]. Over
the past 15 years, the facility has also played a key role in the development of advanced
thin film coatings for ESA’s Advanced Telescope for High Energy Astrophysics (Athena).
However, the facility has not yet been used for developing coatings for neutron optics.
Since X-ray and neutron coatings operate on similar principles, the goal is to leverage
existing expertise in the fabrication of coatings for X-ray optics to develop neutron super-
mirrors.
4.1
DTU Space Coating Chamber
The coating facility at DTU Space is equipped with a bell-jar vacuum chamber measuring
1 meter in diameter and 1.2 meter in height [33]. A photo of the open chamber is shown
in Figure 4.1a. The chamber utilizes DC magnetron sputtering to deposit thin films. In
essence, this technique involves transferring atoms from a source material (the target) onto
a different surface (the substrate) [34]. Four magnetrons are installed inside the vacuum
chamber, illustrated in Figure 4.1b. This project specifically employs the magnetrons lo-
cated in positions 3 and 4, which are fitted with a Ti and Ni target, respectively. The
magnetrons are oriented vertically and powered by DC voltage. The substrates are at-
tached to metal plates mounted on a rotating outer ring that moves the substrates past
the target during the deposition.
The vacuum chamber is evacuated to a pressure below 10−6 Torr to eliminate air, water
vapor, and other contaminants before being filled with inert argon gas. Free electrons
are introduced into the system by applying a negative voltage between the magnetron
cathode and anode. These electrons are confined by the magnetic field lines generated by
three permanent magnets with alternating polarities inside the cathode. When a trapped
electron collides with an argon atom, the atom loses an electron and becomes ionized.
The resulting positively charged argon ions are then accelerated toward the target on the
negatively charged cathode. The kinetic energy of the argon ions exceeds the binding
energy of the target material, knocking off target atoms that are sputtered to settle on

4.1.1
Collimation
During the sputtering process, the sputtered material is ejected in all directions from the
target. It can be advantageous to place a honeycomb mesh in front of the target material
to limit the solid angle of sputtered particles. Vickery et al. showed that honeycomb
collimation gives a significant reduction in the roughness of W/Si multilayers [33]. One
disadvantage is that the honeycomb grid results in a slower deposition rate because of the
blocked flux of incoming atoms at high angles.
Two different honeycomb mesh grids are available at DTU Space: a small grid with a
mesh thickness of 5 mm and cell diameter of 6.4 mm, and a large grid with a mesh thickness
of 5 nm and cell diameter of 9.6 mm. The small mesh spans a narrower solid angle than
the large grid. Photos of a target inside the open coating chamber, with a honeycomb
mesh installed in front, are shown in Figure 4.3. Based on previous experiments performed
at DTU Space, it is assumed that the deposition rate increases by a factor of 2.7 when
transitioning from the small mesh honeycomb to no collimation. Assuming a direct linear
relationship with cell diameter, the deposition rate is calculated to increase by a factor of
1.5 when switching from the small to the large mesh honeycomb.

4.1.2
Reactive sputtering
A reactive gas, such as N2, can be added to the argon during the coating process. The
coating process is then called reactive sputtering. Elsenhans et al. reported that when
coating Ni, the addition of N2 dilates the Ni lattice, reorients the Ni grains from the
preferred crystal {111} orientation in pure argon to the {200} orientation with increasing
nitrogen content, and reduces the Ni grain size [25]. As a resulting effect, the roughness
of Ni samples have been observed to be significantly lowered. In particular, Girou et al.
found that reactive sputtering with ≈76% of Ar and ≈24% of N2 reduced the mean
interface roughness by a factor of ≈1.7 in Ni/C multilayers [35]. The coating rate of Ni

4.2

The experiments carried out in this project can be divided into three categories:
1. Fabrication of single-layer Ni and Ti coatings.
2. Fabrication of Ni/Ti and Ti/Ni periodic multilayer coatings.
3. Fabrication of a m = 3 supermirror for neutron applications, following the design

4.2.1
Coating conditions
Depositing hundreds of bilayers is a time-consuming process. To minimize the coating
duration for a supermirror, it is preferable to apply high power to the cathodes to enable
a high deposition rate. Meanwhile, the power output must remain stable, as any dropouts
will disrupt the plasma, preventing material from being sputtered. The power applied to
the Ni and Ti cathodes were 1000 W and 1800 W, respectively, during all experiments.
For the first 11 coating runs, the deposition was conducted in an inert argon atmosphere
at a total working gas pressure of 4×10−3 mbar. This is the standard working gas pressure
used at DTU Space for thin film deposition. It is low enough to obtain a low surface
roughness, yet high enough to maintain a stable plasma [34]. Coating Run 12 was done
using reactive sputtering with a N2 gas fraction of 20% in argon. The gas fraction was
chosen based on the results achieved by Zhang et al. [36]. The total working gas pressure
remained 4 × 10−3 mbar during the reactive sputtering.
At the start of each coating run, a pre-sputtering lasting approximately 5 minutes was
carried out to remove impurities from the target surface before depositing the material
onto the substrates. The target-to-substrate distance was 105 mm during all experiments.
The collimation type was changed between experiments to study its impact on thin film
roughness.

This chapter outlines the characterization techniques used in this project to analyze the
fabricated thin film samples. It begins with an introduction to the selection of techniques,

5.1
Selection of Characterization Techniques
A neutron reflectometry experiment would be ideal for characterizing a neutron super-
mirror to assess its performance directly in the intended application. However, neutron
scattering is a time-consuming and expensive technique, where experiments last hours to
days and are performed at large international facilities where beam time is highly compet-
itive. Securing beam time requires extensive preparation, including submitting a detailed
proposal well in advance, which must compete with numerous other applications.
In contrast, X-ray sources are far more abundant and intense, making X-ray experi-
ments the preferred choice whenever feasible [5]. For many types of multilayer thin films,
X-ray reflectometry (XRR) is an effective technique for characterizing thin film properties
such as density, layer thickness, and roughness. However, in the case of a neutron super-
mirror, XRR alone may provide limited insights because of the thick, complex structure
of the coating, which is specifically designed to work for neutrons.
In this project, neutron beam time was granted to measure the supermirror, but unfor-
tunately, the experiment was canceled. Instead, a multi-technique approach was adopted,
combining X-ray reflectometry (XRR) at DTU Space and BESSY II in Berlin, atomic
force microscopy (AFM), and scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) at DTU
Nanolab to piece together a characterization of the neutron supermirror.
An overview of all samples and the characterization methods used is provided in Table 5.1.
The X-marks indicate which characterization techniques were applied to each sample.

5.2
X-Ray Reflectometry
X-ray reflectometry (XRR) is a non-destructive characterization method for investigating
the thickness, roughness, and density of thin film structures [37]. The basic concept of
XRR is X-ray reflection at grazing incidence, with either the angle or energy held fixed,
depending on the type of facility.
5.2.1
The X-ray radiation facility at DTU Space
A sketch of the XRR setup at DTU Space is shown in Figure 5.1.
The X-ray source
is a copper rotating anode that generates a continuous Bremsstrahlung spectrum. The
intensity and shape of the beam are controlled by slits, while evacuated tubes minimize air
scattering. A two-bounce monochromator selectively transmits Kα1 radiation at 8.048 keV.
During measurements, the filter is adjusted automatically to control photon counts and
prevent detector dead time. The beam is collimated to 1 mm × 0.12 mm before reaching
the sample. The system has an angular resolution of 0.007◦.
The sample mount is aligned using three Cartesian coordinates (thx, thy, thz) and one
angular coordinate (th) such that it blocks half the beam width. The measurements are
then performed automatically by the equipment control software using the ”Autoscan”-
34

function. The specular reflectance of a sample is measured by simultaneously rotating the
sample mount and the detector during the scan (θ - 2θ scan).
All the 45 samples fabricated in this project were measured using the 8.048 keV XRR
setup. The specular reflectance of each sample was measured for grazing incidence angles
ranging from 0◦to 3◦. The sample mount position was adjusted in steps of 10 mdeg, with
an integration time of 10 seconds per step. A picture of a witness sample fixed on the
sample mount is shown in Figure 5.2.
The resulting experimental data is in the form of reflectivity curves as a function of
grazing angles. For illustration, a simulated example of a Ni single-layer reflectivity curve
was shown in Figure 2.6a, and a simulated example of a Ni/Ti periodic multilayer was

The X-ray radiation facility at Physikalisch-Technische

BESSY II at PTB in Berlin is a synchrotron facility that generates intense X-ray radiation
in an energy range from 1 −150 keV. The synchrotron, with a 240 meter circumference,

accelerates electrons to nearly the speed of light before injecting them into the storage
ring [38]. As these electrons pass through magnetic structures known as undulators, they
are forced into a slalom course, emitting electromagnetic radiation. This radiation is then
directed through beamlines into various experimental stations for scientific applications.
XRR measurements were conducted at BESSY II within an energy range of 3.4 −

5.2.3
Data fitting of XRR measurements
Samples in Category 1 and 2 experiments measured with XRR at 8.048 keV were fitted
using a Python-based software described in Gellert et al.
[39].
The software models
the optical properties of the mirror within the user-defined model space and optimizes
the fit using a differential evolution algorithm. The reflectance for each grazing angle is
computed using the modified Fresnel equations taking roughness into account, described
in Subsection 2.2.3. The optical constants are calculated using data from the Lawrence
Livermore National Laboratory [21].
The fitting area was restricted to a lower limit of 0.15◦to include only data where
the entire sample is illuminated by the beam. The upper limit was set depending on the
sample type to ensure that the measured reflectance stays above the noise floor, typically
around R = 10−6, at high grazing angles. An angular offset was added to some of the
data sets to align with the critical angle of the material. For samples ds0504 to ds0536,
an angular offset of −20 mdeg was applied, suggesting a systematic miscalibration of the
system.
5.2.3.1
Single layers
Table 5.2 shows the model space that was input to the program to fit single-layer data.
The densities of all materials were kept constant to their nominal values. The roughness
of the layers and the thin film thickness were left as variables and limited to the intervals
indicated by the model space. The substrate is modeled as a semi-infinite slab of Si and
thus no substrate thickness is specified in the model.
The residual between experimental and simulated reflectivity was minimized on a log-
arithmic scale when fitting single layers, giving a higher weight to lower-intensity regions
and enhancing sensitivity to higher-order Kiessig fringes.
This approach provides bal-
anced weighting across multiple orders of magnitude to accurately fit the film thickness,
provided that noise remains low in the low-intensity regions. Each data point was addi-
tionally weighted according to the measurement error recorded by the instrument, which
follows a Poisson distribution [40]. Determining whether there is a good agreement be-
tween the measured and the calculated reflectivity was done by inspecting the goodness
of the fit visually.

electron beam scans the sample pixel by pixel, while detectors record the scattered and
transmitted electrons at each point to generate an image.

determines the angular range of scattered electrons collected and influences the contrast
generation [45]. In these experiments, the camera length was kept constant at 110 mm.
With this setup, HAADF images show heavy elements as bright and light elements as
dark, while DF-I images exhibit the opposite contrast pattern.

5.4.1
Sample preparation
Before entering the STEM, the sample must be ultrathin, typically less than 100 nm,
to allow the electrons to pass through it. Focused Ion Beam (FIB) is a technique used
for the precise preparation of lamellae suitable for S/TEM. At DTU Nanolab, sample
ds0526 was prepared using the Thermo Fisher Helios Hydra 5 UX, shown in Figure 5.5.
The instrument features a FIB column for milling and imaging and a scanning electron
microscopy (SEM) column for complimentary imaging. Typical milling plasmas use xenon
or argon. Argon enables finer milling due to its smaller atomic size, while xenon allows for
faster material removal. The milling current also affects the precision, where a low current
provides finer milling, but at the expense of a slower material removal rate.
Pictures from the FIB procedure for preparing the TEM lamella of sample ds0526 are
shown in Figure 5.6.
The preparation of the lamella began with the deposition of a
protective platinum layer to define the region of interest. Then, a high-current xenon
plasma was used to mill trenches on both sides of the region of interest.
This left a
thin central lamella, still attached to the bulk material. The next step was to attach a
microneedle to the lamella via platinum deposition. The lamella was then cut free from
the bulk sample and transferred to a TEM support grid. The lamella was attached to the
support grid via platinum deposition and cut free from the needle. Finally, the lamella
was thinned to less than 100 nm using a low-current xenon plasma. Three pillars remained
to support the two thinned regions and provide stability. The final lamella was imaged
face-on using the SEM in Figure 5.7a, and from above using the FIB in Figure 5.7b. Once
the lamella was attached to the support grid, the grid could be inserted into the STEM
instrument.

6.4
Discussion
For Ni coatings, there are indications that the small honeycomb reduces roughness as layer
thickness increases compared to no collimation, with a reduction of 0.2 nm observed for
≈16 nm thick layers. However, the average Ni layer thickness in the supermirror design is
6.85 nm, and here no improvement is observed regardless of the collimation method. For
Ti single layers, honeycomb collimation did not result in a reduction in roughness.
It is known that Ni and Ti tend to develop rough interfaces due to large grains [25].
Furthermore, pure Ni forms crystalline structures with a preferred orientation that can lead
to columnar growth of Ni, where elongated grains extend perpendicular to the substrate,
leading to rough interfaces. Although honeycomb collimation reduces the solid angle of
sputtered atoms, it does not appear to significantly impact roughness for either Ni or Ti.
A possible explanation is that the intrinsic roughness of the Ni and Ti materials dominates
such that collimation has little or no effect on their surface morphology.
In this study, only one sample was fabricated per condition. To obtain better statistical
reliability, a more extensive roughness study could be conducted by fabricating three
samples of each type with identical parameters.
Additionally, the influence of coating
conditions, including target power, voltage, and working gas pressure, on roughness could
be explored for a more in-depth study. However, in the initial stages of neutron supermirror
fabrication, the results obtained provided a valuable foundation for how to proceed with
the experiments.
Based on these results, it was decided to proceed with the multilayer experiments with-
out collimation. Furthermore, the substantial increase in deposition rate, 1.4 to 2.6 times
for Ni and 1.5 to 2.9 times for Ti, achieved without collimation compared to large and
small honeycomb collimation, presents a major advantage. This allows the supermirror to
be coated much faster when not using collimation.

Three coating runs (Coating Run 7, 8, and 12) were conducted to investigate the perfor-
mance of Ni and Ti in periodic stacks of 10 bilayers. Coating Run 7 and 8 assessed the
impact of the deposition order of Ti and Ni on performance. Coating Run 12 investigated

Ni/Ti when Ti is deposited first followed by Ni, and Ti/Ni for the opposite case.
This chapter provides the details of the experimental procedures, expanding on the
general coating conditions described in Chapter 4. The results for the periodic multilayer
coatings are then presented. Finally, the findings are discussed.
7.1
Experimental Fabrication of Periodic Multilayers
Four samples were coated per run to test four different d-spacings. The bilayer thicknesses
in the power-law design range from 9.63 nm to 37.97 nm with Γ = 0.5171, and the average
bilayer thickness is 13.14 nm.
To cover most of this range, the target thicknesses for
the periodic bilayer experiments were chosen to be 8 nm, 11 nm, 14 nm, and 17 nm
with Γ = 0.5171. The principle of depositing Ti/Ni multilayers is depicted in Figure 7.1.
The process is similar when coating Ni/Ti multilayers, except Ti is coated first with Ni
following. Based on the results in Chapter 6, it was chosen to sputter without collimation.
The actual coating conditions, recorded in a logbook, were plotted after each run to verify
that the coating process proceeded as intended. All coatings were carried out as expected.
As an example, the recorded target power and ring speed for Coating Run 7 is plotted in
Figure 7.2.

7.2.1

first three Bragg peaks across all samples, which are crucial for obtaining reliable best-fit

parameters. However, at higher angles, features become increasingly smeared due to the
cumulative effect of imperfections throughout the film, which the model does not capture.
Additionally, the reflectance at high angles tends to be higher than the model predicts.
This could be explained by introducing a roughness gradient through the film where the

This is expected since the only difference in the designs is the deposition order of the two
materials.
The linear scaled XRR plots in Figure 7.3 highlight the effect of deposition order on
roughness around the first Bragg peak. The reflectance of the Ni/Ti design is consistently
higher than that of the Ti/Ni design, indicating a lower roughness for the Ni/Ti design.
This observation is consistent with the fitted values in Table 7.1 and 7.2. Based on these
results, the Ni/Ti deposition order is preferred.
For both coating runs, the measured thin film thicknesses are significantly lower than
the intended target values.
Additionally, the fitted Γ-values are larger than expected,
indicating that the Ni layers are relatively thicker than the Ti layers compared to the
design.
This deviation is further examined for Coating Run 8. To quantify the deviations, the
individual Ni and Ti layer thicknesses (dNi,fit and dTi,fit) are calculated using the fitted
values of dfit and Γfit from Table 7.2. These are plotted in Figure 7.5 alongside the target
layer thicknesses (dNi,aim and dTi,aim), calculated using daim and Γaim.
The residual plots show that the Ti layers deviate between −26% to −37% from their
targeted thicknesses, increasingly with greater layer thickness. One possible explanation
is that Ti undergoes enhanced re-sputtering in multilayers compared to single-layer depo-
sitions. Since Ni atoms are heavier than Ti atoms, they are more likely to re-sputter Ti,
while Ti has a lesser effect on Ni. For Ni, two different calibration samples were used: (1)
Sample ds0520 and ds0523 were calibrated using the thinnest single-layer Ni sample as
reference. (2) Sample ds0522 and ds0521 were calibrated using an intermediate-thickness
single-layer Ni sample as reference.
The results show that ds0522 and ds0521 closely
match their target thicknesses, with deviations of only −1.7% and −3.7% respectively.
In contrast, ds0520 and ds0523 exhibit larger deviations of −16% and −15% from their
target thicknesses. In Chapter 6, Figure 6.5b and 6.8b showed that the deposition rate as
a function of thin film thickness varies steeply for thin single layers, while it appears to
flatten for thicker layers. This suggests that additional data for thin coatings should be
collected to map a more accurate calibration function.

As a summary, Figure 7.7 presents the fitted roughness for all samples in Coating Run 7,

Based on these findings, it was decided to
proceed with supermirror experiments, depositing Ti first. Although the experiments on
reactive sputtering were conducted after the supermirror experiments, the results show
that sputtering Ni in pure argon yields the lowest roughness, making it the preferred
method.

a performance close to the design specified in Chapter 3 with an average reflectance of
98%. However, the results in Figure 7.7 indicate that the roughness increases with larger
d-spacing. Again, this trend is expected as successive layers build upon the existing thin-
film morphology, thereby amplifying small imperfections as the film grows. The impact of
this behavior on a thick supermirror stack will be investigated in the following chapter.

Three coatings runs (Coating Run 9, 10, and 11) were conducted to investigate the super-
mirror design presented in Chapter 3. In Coating Run 9, the 256-bilayer design was fabri-
cated without collimation, based on the results from the collimation study in Chapter 6.
Despite the single-layer results suggesting honeycomb collimation offered no significant
improvement in roughness, it was tested in Coating Run 10, where the 256-bilayer design
was coated with the large-mesh honeycomb installed. To study the bottom layers of the
supermirror, Coating Run 11 fabricated only the first 50 bilayers without collimation. In
all coatings, Ti was deposited first, followed by Ni, following the results in Chapter 7,
using a pure argon atmosphere.
This chapter provides the details of the experimental procedures, expanding on the
general coating conditions described in Chapter 4. The results of the different character-
ization methods are presented, including the data analysis from AFM, STEM, and XRR.
The primary focus is an extensive characterization of the supermirror coatings in Coating
Run 9. The characterization results are combined to create models that are then used to
predict the reflectivity of the supermirror in neutrons by simulation. Finally, the findings
are discussed.

8.2.2.1
Analysis of the low-magnification HAADF image
The low-magnification HAADF image in Figure 8.5 is used to estimate the bilayer thick-
nesses throughout the thin film stack. First, the image is rotated by −45◦and expanded
to avoid edge cropping, using the ImageJ software [46]. The rotated image is shown in
Figure 8.7a. Next, the rotated image is input into a Python script. The pixels are aver-
aged along the Y-axis to generate a 1D profile along the X-axis, passing through the stack
from the Si substrate to the Pt top layer. The resulting profile is plotted in Figure 8.7b.
Each pixel is converted into a physical distance in nanometers, given that each pixel is
1.465 × 1.465 nm2. The averaged grey value is plotted with values ranging from 0 (black)
to 255 (white), as a function of distance on the sample in nanometers. This means that
in the HAADF image, Ni has high grey values and Ti has low grey values.
The signal is interpolated with a spline function, with a distance between consecutive
points of 1 Å. The dataset is cropped to remove the Si substrate and the Pt layer, ensuring
that only the multilayer structure remains for analysis.
All the extrema of the interpolated signal are identified. Here, the identified peaks
mark the centers of the Ni layers and the identified valleys mark the centers of the Ti
layers. The layer transitions are then modeled using the error function (erf), as expressed
in Eq. 2.16, commonly used to model rough interfaces as explained in Subsection 2.2.4.
The fitting function takes the following form:

8.2.3.1
Analysis of the 50-bilayer design
The three samples with the design consisting of the 50 bottom bilayers of the supermirror
design were measured using the 8.048 XRR setup at DTU Space for angles between θ = 0◦
and θ = 2.5◦. Additionally, one of the samples was measured at BESSY II at fixed angles
of θ = 0.6◦, θ = 1.0◦, and θ = 1.3◦, covering an energy range of 3.4 - 10 keV. The
measured data is presented in Figure 8.12, with the DTU Space XRR data on the left and
the BESSY II XRR data on the right.
The DTU Space XRR measurements show that the data for the three samples (ds0530,
ds0531, ds0532), coated on the same plate, exhibit closely aligned reflectivity curves, with
the exception that ds0531 shows a slight leftward shift around the third peak. The 50-
bilayer stack has designed thicknesses ranging from 4.986 nm to 5.287 nm for Ni and
4.656 nm to 4.937 nm for Ti.
Since these thicknesses fall within a narrow range, the
multilayer is nearly periodic, leading to the observation of three Bragg peaks in the DTU
Space XRR data. The first peak is around θ = 0.55◦, the second at θ = 1.0◦, and the
third at θ = 1.45◦. The first and the third peaks appear the most prominent.
The BESSY II measurements were performed only for sample ds0530. For each fixed
angle, the reflectivity was measured at three spots on the sample: at the center of the
sample, and ±1.5 mm vertically from the center. The spots are denoted as top, center,
and bottom. The data from all spots perfectly overlap. A Bragg peak is observed at
an energy around 7.5 keV for the data at a fixed angle of θ = 0.6◦, around 4.5 keV for
θ = 1.0◦, and around 3.5 keV for θ = 1.3◦. The dip at 4.966 keV is the Ti absorption edge,
and the dip at 8.333 keV is the Ni absorption edge [47].
For further analysis, the center measurements of ds0530 will be used as the represen-
tative dataset.

Due to the complexity of the multilayer structure and the high-dimensional parameter
space, data fitting is performed using a brute-force approach rather than a dedicated
fitting algorithm. The experimental data is compared with simulated models based on a
best-guess approach, incorporating prior knowledge obtained from the deposition process
and previous characterizations.
Table 8.1 provides an overview of the applied models. Model 1 (Design) refers to the
power-law model with its intended thicknesses, nominal material densities, and uniform
interface roughness of 2.5 nm throughout the stack. Model 2 is used for the DTU Space
XRR data, while Model 3 is used for the BESSY II XRR data. The models are detailed
in the following text.
To correct for a systematic miscalibration, an angular offset of -20 mdeg is applied
to the DTU Space XRR data, similar to how it was done for samples ds0504 to ds0536.
Additionally, as shown in Figure 8.3, an error occurred during the deposition of bilayer 35,
where the Ni target failed to activate. This layer is therefore removed in all models to
reflect the actual structure.
For the DTU Space XRR model, a density multiplier of 0.93 is applied to the nominal
density of both Ni and Ti to align with the critical angle of the bulk material. For the
BESSY II model, a density multiplier of 0.90 provides the best alignment with the data.
This suggests that the materials were deposited with lower density than their nominal
values.
To align with the position of the Bragg peaks, the data modeling suggests that the
coating is deposited slightly thinner than designed. For the DTU Space XRR model, a
thickness multiplier of 0.96 allows the model to match the position of all three Bragg peaks.
For the BESSY II model, a thickness multiplier of 0.98 provides the best alignment with
the position of the observed Bragg peak.
Ideally, a single thickness and density multiplier should describe both datasets. However,
the discrepancy could arise from: (1) Additional thin-film effects beyond a simple sys-
tematic thickness and density deviation, and (2) discrepancies in the measurement setup
between DTU Space and BESSY II.
Given the multi-dimensional parameter space, the model is not unique, meaning that
different parameter combinations could produce very similar results.
For example, re-
ducing the Γ-value, such that the stack is composed of relatively more Ti than designed,
would have a similar effect to lowering the density near the critical angle. However, no
model was found that accurately captures both the critical angle and the peak positions
by variations in the Γ-value. Consequently, the simple model using the designed Γ-value
was used.
A roughness of 2.5 nm results in a good fit for the Bragg peak in the BESSY II data and the
first-order Bragg peak in the DTU Space XRR data. However, to capture the second- and
third-order Bragg peaks in the DTU Space XRR data, a roughness gradient is introduced.
Based on results from the Ni/Ti N = 10 periodic multilayers sputtered in pure argon, the
average fitted roughness of both materials ranged from 0.99 nm to 1.35 nm for d-spacings
between 6.33 nm and 12.66 nm. These values are used as a priori information to estimate
the roughness of the bottom layers in the 50-bilayer stack. The exact gradient profile of
the roughness cannot be well constrained. However, setting the bottom 7 bilayers to a
78
8.2 Results of Supermirror Characterization
==PAGE:91==
1.0 nm roughness while keeping the rest at 2.5 nm roughness provides a good fit for the
peak amplitudes. This indicates that the roughness space for the 50 bottom bilayers of
the supermirror stack is in the range of 1.0 nm to 2.5 nm, which is a key result of this
analysis. Figure 8.13 shows Model 1 (Design) and Model 2 overlaid the DTU Space XRR
data, while Figure 8.14 shows Model 1 (Design) and Model 3 overlaid the BESSY II XRR
data.
Introducing a roughness gradient in the DTU Space XRR model provides a good fit of the
Bragg peaks, however, it has no impact on the BESSY II data. For the energies beyond
the Bragg peak in the BESSY II data, the experimental reflectance is significantly higher
than what can be reproduced by any reasonable model. Several possible explanations have
been considered, though the exact cause remains unclear. These considerations are now
discussed.
As energy and angle increase, the beam penetrates deeper into the multilayer stack.
If non-specular scattering dominates, the specular beam could be buried within diffuse
scatter. However, this alone would not explain the orders-of-magnitude difference between
the experimental and modeled reflectance beyond the observed Bragg peak in the BESSY II
data.
Beam divergence was also considered as a potential explanation. For example, if the
beam exhibits a divergence of ±20 mdeg, it would smear out features and slightly increase
the average reflectance. However, this alone cannot account for the observed discrepancy,
particularly when considering realistic roughness models. The exact beam divergence of
the BESSY II system is not currently known, and since they are using a new setup, a more
detailed understanding is required to identify potential sources of such an effect.
Random thickness variations were also tested. While they alter the oscillatory pattern
in the model, they do not increase the overall reflectance. Similarly, no reasonable rough-
ness model was able to elevate the modeled reflectance to match the measured data. In
conclusion, the cause of the high experimental reflectance for energies beyond the Bragg
peak in the BESSY II remains unresolved.
Overall, the DTU Space XRR data is considered the most reliable for constraining the
roughness of the bottom 50 bilayers in the supermirror design.

8.3
Simulations of the Supermirror in Neutrons
Based on the findings from the experimental characterization, the performance of the
supermirror in neutrons is simulated in the following sections.
8.3.1
Summary of experimental characterization
Characterization of the supermirror design using AFM, STEM, and XRR allowed certain
design parameters to be constrained. STEM measurements of the full structure, coated
without collimation in Coating Run 9, showed that all bilayers are systematically approxi-
mately 10% thicker than intended. AFM analysis revealed a mean RMS surface roughness
of 3.77 nm for the top Ni layer, and XRR revealed that the roughness of the 50 bottom
bilayers falls between 1 nm to 2.5 nm. These findings are used to create models to predict
the performance of the supermirror at a neutron wavelength of λ = 0.3 nm.
8.3.2
Roughness modeling
To simulate the supermirror, three different roughness modeling approaches were employed.
The Γ-value was fixed at its designed value, and the material densities were assumed to
be nominal. The three roughness approaches are described as following:
1. Flat roughness profiles (Profile 1, 2, and 3):
• A constant roughness of 1 nm, 2 nm, or 3 nm is applied across all layers.
2. Power-law roughness profiles (Profile 4, 5, and 6):
• The roughness scales with bilayer thickness, constrained within a 1 to 4 nm
range.
• The minimum roughness varies for each of the profiles, with values of 1 nm,
2 nm, or 2.5 nm, respectively.
• The individual Ni and Ti layers have similar roughness within each bilayer.
3. Step-function roughness profiles (Profile 7 and 8):
• The roughness varies throughout the stack, from 4 nm at the top to 1 nm at
the bottom.
• The bottom 7 bilayers have a roughness set to 1 nm, based on the roughness
model that closely matched the XRR data for the 50 bottom bilayers.
• In Profile 7, the roughness decreases in four discrete steps (4 →3 →2 →1 nm).
• In Profile 8, the roughness decreases in five discrete steps (4 →3.5 →3 →2.5
→1 nm).
• The individual Ni and Ti layers have similar roughness within each bilayer.
For all models, the substrate roughness is set to 0.45 nm.

8.3.3.2
Power-law roughness profile
Figure 8.18b shows that a high roughness of 4 nm in the top layers of the thin film stack has
minimal impact on the overall reflectivity, provided the roughness decreases rapidly with
decreasing layer thickness. Since the top layers are significantly thicker than the average
layers in the stack, they can tolerate a large roughness without significantly degrading
performance. The primary influence on performance arises from the bulk region, where
layer thicknesses reach the stack average (6.85 nm for Ni, 6.29 nm for Ti) and become
progressively thinner at deeper depths. To maintain a reflectance above 90%, the roughness
in these thinner layers should be kept close to 1 nm.
8.3.3.3
Step-function roughness profile
Figure 8.18c shows that the step-function profiles, both with high overall roughness, sig-
nificantly degrade the supermirror reflectance.
In the simulated Design with Roughness Profile 7, the 3 nm roughness region severely
reduces reflectance up to m = 2.5, after which the 2.5 nm and 1 nm roughness regions
make the reflectance rise and plateau up to m = 3. Similar to Figure 8.18a and 8.18b, the
Model exhibits a higher reflectance up to m = 2.7 than the Design, due to the increased
layer thickness.
The Design and Model simulated with Roughness Profile 8 show a stronger degradation
in performance compared to Roughness Profile 7 because of the overall larger roughness.
8.3.3.4
Effect of adding more bilayers
The effect of adding more bilayers to the design using Roughness Profile 7 is investigated
in Figure 8.19. Here, the supermirror power-law stack is simulated using 256 bilayers,
512 bilayers, and 1024 bilayers. Increasing the number of bilayers significantly improves
reflectance. However, this relies on maintaining similar interface quality during fabrication.
As the bilayer count increases, the coating process becomes more susceptible to errors, and
imperfections and stress may accumulate through the thick coating. Therefore, instead
of compensating by adding more bilayers, reducing the overall roughness is the preferable
approach.

8.4
Discussion
The simulated results for the supermirror performance in neutrons demonstrated that
missing layers significantly degrade performance and that systematically thicker layers
prevent the mirror from achieving its specified requirement of m = 3. Additionally, the
impact of different roughness profiles on reflectance was analyzed.
The experimental characterization indicated that the roughness of the layers is within
a range of 1 and 4 nm. If the roughness follows a power-law scaling with layer thickness
through the stack, the simulated performance remains highly promising, closely matching
the design with a reflectance above 90%. However, if roughness evolves throughout the film
such that the majority of the stack exhibits roughness between 2.5 and 3.5 nm, performance
is severely degraded.
This scenario appears more likely, as analysis of the 50 bottom
bilayers showed roughness values ranging between 1 nm and 2.5 nm.
8.4.1
Design Considerations and Process Optimization
The initial supermirror design was optimized under the assumption of an optimistic con-
stant 1 nm roughness at all interfaces.
If future fabrication processes can reduce the
roughness to levels similar to Roughness Profile 4, where the majority of the stack has a
roughness below 1.5 nm, the current design remains viable. Achieving this will require
future investigations into methods for reducing roughness. However, if such reductions
prove impractical, an alternative design with the number of bilayers optimized for a more
conservative roughness profile could be considered, provided the higher bilayer count does
not further increase the roughness.
The effect of a potential Γ-value deviation has not been simulated in this section. However,
Chapter 3 showed that the design is robust towards a ±0.1 deviation in Γ, provided that
the roughness profile remains low with σ ≈1 nm.
The impact of density variation must also be considered since the density directly affects
the scattering length density (SLD) of the materials, and consequently, overall reflectance.
88
8.4 Discussion
==PAGE:101==
If the density is lower than the nominal material density, this would reduce the overall re-
flectance. The study of the 50 bilayers suggested that the density is lower than the nominal
value, however, the single layer and periodic multilayer studies showed no such indication,
as their models fit the XRR data well using nominal material densities. If density de-
viations prove significant, they could be incorporated into future design optimization to
ensure better performance.
8.4.1.1
Thickness calibration
Precise control of layer thicknesses is critical to achieving m = 3 with high reflectance. If
the coating is too thick, the mirror will not meet the requirement specification, while a
coating that is too thin leads to a loss in average reflectance.
A more comprehensive multilayer calibration study should be conducted, exploring a
wider range of thicknesses. Since the deposition rate varies with layer thickness, it should
ideally be mapped across all thicknesses present in the design. Rather than relying on
a fixed deposition rate, this mapping can serve as a calibration scheme based on layer
thickness. Moreover, a thick Ni single-layer coating experiment should be performed to
accurately calibrate the deposition rate for the 50 nm top Ni layer.
8.4.1.2
Mitigating coating errors
It is important to minimize process errors, as missing layers introduce significant defects
in the reflectivity curve, causing large dips at angles corresponding to the Bragg condition
of the missing layer. According to the logbook, the supermirror fabricated in Coating
Run 10 was successfully coated, whereas Run 9 and Run 11 experienced dropouts. It is
necessary to investigate why the targets failed to activate. Another way to mitigate such
coating errors could be to implement a script that checks whether the power supply is
normal in the live logbook. If a target has failed to activate, the deposition of the next
layer should be halted. The sample should complete one rotation in the chamber, and
then an automatic retry should be attempted to ensure proper layer deposition.
8.4.1.3
Strategies for roughness reduction
The greatest challenge in supermirror fabrication appears to be suppressing roughness.
Based on the single-layer studies, honeycomb collimation did not provide any overall im-
provement. Moreover, reactive sputtering of multilayers with nitrogen as a reactive gas
proved ineffective, as it increased rather than reduced roughness. Whether this is caused
by increased diffusion, increased pure roughness, or a combination of both, should be in-
vestigated further. Elsenhans et al., 1994 [25], reported that reactive sputtering of Ni/Ti
multilayers may be disadvantageous, as nitrogen readily diffuses from Ni into Ti, reducing
neutron scattering contrast and leading to unstable multilayers. Since diffusion and pure
roughness are indistinguishable at the specular condition, non-specular XRR measure-
ments should be performed on the multilayer samples to differentiate their contributions.
Additionally, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) could provide insight into diffusion
and oxidation levels across the film depth.
Alternative roughness suppression methods should be explored. Elsenhans et al., 1994
[25], suggest that introducing dry air or carbon into Ni can reduce the roughness while
suppressing the diffusion of Ni into Ti in multilayers, thereby leading to sharper interfaces
and better long-term stability. Moreover, Jiao et al., 2018 [48], have shown that NiC/Ti
multilayers exhibit smoother interfaces compared to Ni/Ti multilayers. This improvement

is attributed to a significant reduction in the crystallization of both the NiC and Ti layers.
In addition, co-sputtering Ni/Ti layers with low neutron-absorbing 11-B4C, combined
with the application of substrate bias voltage during sputtering, has demonstrated promis-
ing results in minimizing roughness (Broekhuijsen, 2021)[11].
Finally, the role of vanadium in titanium should be explored. Elsenhans et al., 1994 [25],
reported that vanadium added to Ti reduces the surface roughness of single layers, likely
due to the reorientation of the Ti crystals to an exclusive orientation, which allows constant
speed of grain growth along the layer surface. The performance of TiV in multilayers
should be explored.
8.4.2
Additional Characterization Techniques
The multi-technique approach combining AFM, STEM, and XRR demonstrated to be a
solid framework for gaining valuable insights into the thin film structure. However, XRR
measurements of the full supermirror stack were found to be highly limited in resolving its
structure. Therefore, neutron reflectometry experiments are essential for evaluating the
performance in the intended application. STEM proved to be a powerful tool for assessing
the structure and evolution of the film stack from top to bottom, while AFM provided
direct surface roughness measurements. To obtain more accurate measurements of the
interface widths from STEM, the lamella should be prepared using a low-current argon
plasma for milling instead of xenon.
This would help eliminate FIB-induced artifacts,
which contaminate the data with noise.
Additionally, non-specular measurements should be conducted to decouple pure rough-
ness from diffusion, while XPS should be used to analyze diffusion and oxidation levels
through the film stack. For a complete assessment, stress measurements are also neces-
sary, as maintaining near-zero net stress is crucial for preserving the precise figure of the
substrate.
A comprehensive characterization of the supermirror should therefore integrate neutron
reflectometry, STEM, AFM, non-specular XRR measurements, XPS, and stress measure-
ments.

Conclusion
This thesis presented the design, fabrication, and characterization of a supermirror coat-
ing for neutron optics, aimed at the application of a neutron microscope operating at a
wavelength of λ = 3 Å for the ODIN instrument at ESS.
By leveraging multilayer thin-film physics, a power-law depth-graded Ni/Ti stack was
optimized to extend the high-reflectivity response of the neutron mirror beyond the natural
critical angle of Ni at θ = 0.3◦(m = 1), reaching up to θ = 0.9◦(m = 3). The simulated
256-bilayer design achieved high and continuous broadband angular reflectivity, with a
mean reflectance of 98%. This is essential for enabling the neutron microscope to operate
at the required angles, preserving neutron flux through the optical system.
Before fabricating the full supermirror structure, preliminary experiments were conducted
on Ni and Ti single layers and Ti/Ni and Ni/Ti periodic multilayers. All samples were
fabricated by DC magnetron sputtering at the DTU Space coating facility.
Three single-layer Ni and three single-layer Ti experiments were conducted under three
different conditions: without collimation, with a small-mesh honeycomb grid, and with a
large-mesh honeycomb grid. The characterization of samples was done by X-ray reflectom-
etry (XRR) at the DTU Space X-ray lab. For both Ni and Ti, collimation did not result
in a significant reduction in roughness; therefore, subsequent experiments were conducted
without collimation. For Ni layers deposited without collimation, the fitted roughness
ranged from 0.89 nm to 1.10 nm for thicknesses between 4.85 nm and 16.42 nm. Simi-
larly, for Ti layers, the fitted roughness ranged from 0.64 nm to 0.68 nm for thicknesses
between 6.53 nm and 21.90 nm. Additionally, the deposition rate was seen to decrease
with increasing layer thickness, likely due to re-sputtering effects.
Three coating experiments evaluated the impact of deposition order and reactive sput-
tering on Ni- and Ti-based 10-bilayer coatings. X-ray reflectometry at DTU Space showed
that the Ni/Ti configuration sputtered in pure argon, with Ti deposited first, had an av-
erage roughness 21% lower than the Ti/Ni configuration. Additionally, Ni/Ti coatings
sputtered in pure argon exhibited an average roughness 24% lower than those fabricated
under reactive sputtering.
Therefore, subsequent experiments were conducted with Ti
deposited first, followed by Ni, in a pure argon atmosphere.
Three coating experiments investigated the supermirror design. The 256-bilayer design was
fabricated without collimation and with the large honeycomb, and a coating run fabricated
only the bottom 50 bilayers of the design without collimation to study the bottom layers
of the supermirror design. The supermirror samples without collimation were extensively
characterized using a combination of atomic force microscopy (AFM), scanning electron
transmission microscopy (STEM), and X-ray reflectometry (XRR) at DTU Space and
at BESSY II in Berlin. By combining experimental characterization with performance
simulation in neutrons, this work provided insights into the critical parameters governing
neutron reflectivity in supermirror coatings.
Interface roughness plays a crucial role in the simulated supermirror performance. The
experimental characterization indicated that the layer roughness falls within a range of

1 nm to 4 nm. If roughness scales within this range as a power-law with layer thickness
throughout the stack, simulations predict highly promising performance, with a reflectance
exceeding 90%. However, if the majority of the stack exhibits roughness between 2.5 nm
and 3.5 nm, performance is severely degraded. The latter scenario appears more likely,
as characterization of the bottom 50 bilayers showed roughness values ranging between
1 nm and 2.5 nm. While increasing the number of bilayers can help compensate for some
roughness effects in simulations, practically achieving high reflectance at m = 3 ultimately
depends on minimizing roughness through process improvements.
The fabricated supermirror samples without collimation were systematically ≈10%
thicker than designed. Simulations showed that if the coating is too thick, the mirror fails

Additionally, coating errors, such as missing layers, were found to have a significant impact
on performance and must be mitigated.
Future work should focus on refining ring speed calibration to obtain precise thickness
control, exploring roughness suppression strategies to improve surface quality, and mea-
suring the samples with neutron reflectometry to directly assess their performance in the
intended application.
This work represents a foundational step in the development of neutron optics at DTU
Space and CHEXS, contributing to the advancement of next-generation neutron instru-
mentation, including the neutron microscope for the ODIN instrument at ESS.
9.1
Future Work
For future studies of thin film coatings for neutron optics, the following work is proposed:
9.1.1
Roughness suppression studies
Future studies should focus on minimizing interface roughness in Ni/Ti multilayers by
exploring alternative material compositions and deposition techniques. This includes in-
vestigating:
• Material studies of NiC and TiV.
• Alternative deposition methods such as substrate biasing.
• Optimizing deposition parameters, such as gas pressure and target power, with sys-
tematic mapping of surface and interface roughness as a function of coating condi-
tions.
9.1.2
Calibration studies
Ring speed calibration of multilayers should be refined to better account for thickness-
dependent deposition rates.
9.1.3
Investigating the top Ni layer
In this study, Ni single layers were only produced with thicknesses up to ≈17 nm, while
the top Ni layer in the supermirror stack is 50 nm. A dedicated single-layer experiment
should be conducted to fine-tune the deposition rate for the thick Ni layer. Additionally,
to assess the influence of the top layer on the overall stack performance, the supermirror

9.1 Future Work

should be fabricated both with and without the top Ni layer, and roughness and stress
should be characterized for both cases.
9.1.4
Additional characterization techniques
To gain a more comprehensive picture of the supermirror coatings, additional characteri-
zation methods should be employed:
• Non-specular measurements are essential for decoupling pure roughness from diffu-
sion.
• X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy should be performed to investigate elemental com-
position, interdiffusion, and oxidation levels at the interfaces.
• Stress studies should be performed since it must be near zero for structural stability.
• Neutron reflectometry is essential to directly measure the supermirror reflectance,
which can be compared to simulations.
9.1.5
Characterization of Supermirror Samples with the Large
Honeycomb
The supermirror samples fabricated with the large honeycomb were only characterized
using XRR, which was insuﬀicient for resolving its structure. Further investigations should
include methods such as STEM, AFM, and neutron reflectometry.
