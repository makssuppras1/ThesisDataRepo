ABSTRACT
This study examines the indoor environmental quality (IEQ) in 19 auditoriums
across seven Danish universities by combining objective measurements with subjec-
tive evaluations from 2,776 students. Data were collected during regular teaching
sessions in spring 2025 using ATMO Cube sensor and a structured survey.
Key IEQ parameters, including Carbon Dioxide (CO2) concentration, temperature,
humidity, lighting, and sound pressure level, were measured and compared to stu-
dents’ reported perceptions of air quality, thermal comfort, lighting, and acoustics.
While most auditoriums complied with current standards for CO2 and thermal mea-
surements, approximately 19% of students rated the overall indoor environment as
unacceptable. Perceived air quality was a particular concern, with over 30% report-
ing dissatisfaction, despite measured data complying with standard recommenda-
tions.
Sensor placement was found to signiﬁcantly aﬀect the assessment of IEQ. Tempera-
ture measurements taken in the seating area were often higher than those recorded
at wall-mounted positions, while CO2 concentrations were generally higher at wall-
mounted sensors. These discrepancies suggest that typical building monitoring sys-
tems may not accurately reﬂect conditions experienced by occupants.
In addition, several building-related factors were associated with variations in both
measured and perceived IEQ. Auditoriums located in suburban areas, with larger
ﬂoor areas, older construction years, and high occupancy rates, generally exhibited
poorer air quality and increased thermal discomfort. Higher outdoor temperatures
further contributed to these eﬀects. Conversely, auditoriums with under-seat air
supply and more recent construction showed improved conditions.
The ﬁndings highlight a consistent gap between measured conditions and occupants
perceived comfort. Ensuring acceptable indoor environments in university auditori-
ums requires not only adherence to technical standards but also careful consideration
of sensor placement and user perception.

1. Introduction
1 INTRODUCTION
The indoor environment in schools has a signiﬁcant impact on students’ learning and
cognitive performance. This is clear from research and is well-documented literature.
However, this is not the case for university auditoriums. In this section, we describe
our literature review of the most important indoor environmental parameters and
their impact on learning and cognitive performance. Based on existing standards,
the values used to evaluate acceptable indoor conditions are presented.
Finally,
the rationale for this study is outlined, emphasising the signiﬁcant gap in research
regarding auditoriums. Furthermore, the relevance of sensor placement, building
characteristics and users’ perception of the indoor environment is highlighted as
crucial for understanding and improving the indoor environment in auditoriums.
1.1
INDOOR ENVIRONMENT
The indoor environment is the conditions present within an enclosed, non-industrial
building designed for human occupancy [2]. The IEQ can be measured in four pri-
mary factors, namely IAQ, thermal conditions, visual quality, and acoustical condi-
tions [3] [4]. The four primary factors are inﬂuenced by a variety of both outdoor
and indoor environmental parameters and are expressed as to which degree they ful-
ﬁl human requirements [2]. Furthermore, it is fundamentally impossible to achieve
complete satisfaction among all occupants due to individual diﬀerences in percep-
tion. People have diﬀerent levels of perception of all four parameters, some are more
aﬀected by odours, others by noise, and so on. Several international standards and
guidelines are introducing indoor environment categories and recommend thresholds
for each factor of the indoor environment to aim for a satisfying indoor environment
of the majority of occupants [4], [5], [6], [7],[8]. The four primary factors will be
described in the next sections.
1.1.1
IAQ
IAQ refers to the chemical, physical, and biological characteristics of the air within
an enclosed room, and is primarily determined by the presence of and/or the con-
centration of indoor pollutants. Indoor pollutants include gaseous compounds such
as CO2, Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs), Formaldehyde (CH2O), ozone, and
radon, as well as airborne Particulate Matter (PM) and biological agents such as
bacteria, fungi, and allergens. The origin of these pollutants is typically from either
indoor or outdoor sources. Outdoor sources such as radon from the soil, ozone from
photochemical reactions, and PM from traﬃc and industrial activities. To minimise
the inﬂuence of outdoor pollutants, the building envelope should be airtight, and
the ventilation air should be properly ﬁltered to preserve IAQ [4].

1. Introduction
Indoor sources include VOCs and CH2O from building materials and furniture, CO2
and bioeﬄuents from human respiration. In rooms with large occupant density the
main source is humans. Therefore, CO2 and bioeﬄuents will be high whenever the
room is occupied. From Persily et al. it is estimated that humans from 21 to 30 year
of age, that are sitting and reading/writing/typing are emitting 18.7 and 14.8 l·CO2
h
respectively for men a women [9]. CO2 is considered to be a good indicator for IAQ.
Ramalho et al. found a good correlation between CO2 and CH2O, acetaldehyde,
acrolein, benzene and PM, but also state that CO2 can not be considered a unique
indicator for IAQ [10] [11].
A good IAQ is essential for health and overall well-being. More and more studies
shows IAQ also links directly to cognitive function and learning performance. Espe-
cially classrooms have been studied, mainly on students in primary and secondary
schools. Studies have shown that poor IAQ, especially elevated CO2 levels and the
accumulation of bioeﬄuents, can signiﬁcantly impact cognitive functioning relevant
to learning outcomes. Zhang et al. demonstrated that exposure to CO2 concentra-
tions up to 3000 ppm, in combination with typical bioeﬄuents, led to measurable
increases in fatigue, sleepiness, attention, and headaches, along with slower and less
accurate performance in cognitive tasks [12] [13]. Even though CO2 is a good indi-
cator for IAQ Zhang et al. showed in controlled experiments, the presence of human
bioeﬄuents alone, even at moderate CO2 levels (800−1000 ppm), was suﬃcient to
induce subjective discomfort and lower performance in cognitive tasks, especially
those involving speed and accuracy [13].
Field studies support that these eﬀects are also evident in real classrooms. A large-
scale Danish ﬁeld study found that 56% of classrooms is exceeded the recommended
CO2 threshold of 1000 ppm. It is estimated that improving ventilation to match
Swedish standards could lead to a 6% improvement in student performance and
a 2.5−5% reduction in absenteeism [14]. Similarly, Wargocki et al. showed that
reducing CO2 levels from 2100 ppm to 900 ppm the cognitive task performance
speed is increased by 12% and a reduction in errors by 2%. These lower CO2 levels
were associated with a higher daily attendance and a better score in national tests
[15].
Tran et al. covered 88 studies and concluded that classrooms with better IAQ con-
sistently report higher academic performance and reduced absenteeism. Especially
for lower CO2 concentrations and reduced PM exposure [16]. The review highlights
that CO2 levels above 1000 ppm are frequently leading to decreased test scores and
reduced task engagement, while optimized ventilation improves focus and learning
eﬃciency. Finally, Bogdanovica et al. found that students performed signiﬁcantly
better in classrooms with CO2 levels near 1000 ppm, compared to classrooms with
higher concentrations. Supporting the strong correlation between IAQ and academic
performance [17].

1.1.2
THERMAL CONDITIONS
Thermal conditions play a fundamental role in supporting cognitive performance,
comfort, and overall well-being. These conditions are deﬁned by four physical pa-
rameters: air temperature, radiant temperature, air velocity, and relative humidity,
all of which can be regulated through Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning
(HVAC) systems. In addition, two individual factors signiﬁcantly aﬀect thermal
comfort, namely activity level and clothing. These occupant-speciﬁc variables can
not be controlled mechanically, but must be considered during system design. To
accommodate for the individual diﬀerences, standards allow for a predeﬁned level of
dissatisfaction [4] typically 10%. Acknowledging that achieving 0% dissatisfaction
is practically impossible when multiple occupants share the same space.
While subjective thermal comfort is an important aspect of IEQ, a large number
of research demonstrates that deviations from optimal temperature ranges can sig-
niﬁcantly reduce learning and work-related performance. Even small increases in
temperature can aﬀect the students attention, memory, and task eﬃciency in a neg-
ative way. Barbic et al. found that when the classroom temperatures increased
from 20−22 °C to 24−26 °C, the cognitive performance among university students
declined by 14%. Despite 90% of the occupants was reporting thermal comfort,
measurements showed a more active nervous system and reduced ability to relax,
reﬂecting a stress response from the occupants [18]. Li et al. observed similarly
that at both cooler (22 °C) and warmer (30 °C) reduced performance compared to
26 °C. They measured both EEG and subjective concentration that both have the
best results at 26 °C. The correlation between thermal condition and concentration
was strong (R2 = 0.95), underlining that actual performance levels may not result
in the best perceived comfort [19].
A Danish large scale ﬁeld study are supporting these experimental ﬁndings.
A
Danish mass experiment was conducted in 2009, 2014, and 2021, conﬁrming that
indoor air temperatures above 24 °C are correlated with an increase in reports of
fatigue, discomfort, and reduced concentration. From the 2021 study it is demon-
strated that in classrooms with better ventilation (mechanical) and thereby thermal
conditions showed improved performance in concentration tasks and the students
reported fewer symptoms. These ﬁndings align with the conclusion made already
in 2009, which links high temperatures and poor ventilation to decreased learning
eﬃciency [20] [21] [22]. Additionally, Tran et al. underline that thermal conditions
are one of the most consistently measured factors in studies linking IEQ to academic
performance, especially in naturally ventilated classrooms [16].
Clements-Croome et al. also note that occupant density is very high in classrooms,
typically ranging from 1.8−2.4

person, which can lead to overheating and may reduce
both thermal comfort and academic performance [23].
A meta analyse performed by Porras-Salazar et al. also conﬁrms the detrimental
eﬀects of thermal deviations on mental performance. Porras-Salazar et al. have

1.1.3
VISUAL QUALITY/CONDITIONS
Visual conditions is referring to the adequacy of lighting in the room. The lighting
sources can be natural or artiﬁcial. The visual quality can be determined from pa-
rameters such as illuminance, luminance distribution, glare and daylight availability.
These parameters not only inﬂuence visual comfort but also have demonstrable ef-
fects on learning outcomes, task performance, and cognitive performance in indoor
environments such as classrooms and oﬃces.
The quality of the lighting is acknowledged as one of the environmental factors
which impact human performance. Studies have shown that lighting environments
inﬂuence task eﬃciency, attention span, and error rate [27] [28]. These eﬀects can
be linked to the illuminance level, light distribution, and the presence of natural
daylight. Kanstantzos et al. found that both under- and over-illumination lead to
a degradation in performance. And with moderated and well-distributed lighting
without glare enhance the cognitive eﬃciency [27].
In 1999, a large-scale study conducted by the Heschong Mahone Group, where more
than 21000 students was involved, showed a statistically signiﬁcant correlation be-
tween daylight availability and academic performance. The study showed that in
classrooms with good daylight conditions, especially from skylight, the students was
performing 20−26% better compared to classrooms with poor daylight conditions
[29]. These ﬁndings are associated with the knowledge about the sense of well-being
in rooms with good visibility and natural lighting. The visual indoor environmental
quality are also mentioning the visual comfort being an important factor in designing
spaces for learning [30].
Further, it is supported by Laurentin et al., who found that daylight or mixed light
conditions were consistently preferred over only artiﬁcial lighting. The consistency
continued even when illuminance levels remained constant at 300 lux [28]. High-
lighting that lighting quality can not only be measured by intensity, but also has

1.1.4
ACOUSTICAL CONDITIONS
The acoustical conditions refer to the occupants experience of the indoor sound
environment. To create a good acoustic environment, there are two main factors to
control: unwanted noise and desired sound transmission. The unwanted noise can
come from internal or external sources. The external sources are road traﬃc, air
traﬃc and construction activities. The internal noise sources are HVAC systems,
noise from neighbouring rooms, occupants and echoes from hard surfaces or poorly
designed spaces. To ensure the desired sound transmission, it is important to have a
good acoustic design with well-placed sound diﬀusion and absorbent materials. To
support the sound transmission it is common to add a microphone and speakers in
auditoriums or large rooms with many occupants.
In learning settings, both chronic and acute unwanted noise exposure have been
shown to negatively impact reading, memory, and listening [32] [33]. These eﬀects
have a larger impact on children, especially children with learning diﬃculties, atten-
tion deﬁcits, or those learning a second language [32]. Field studies have shown that
typical classroom noise can reduce the performance of both verbal and non-verbal
tasks [33].
In open oﬃces, background speech is one of the most reported and disturbing un-
wanted noise sources [34] [35].
This kind of acoustic interference is particularly
vulnerable to those performing quiet, focused work. Research shows that irrelevant
speech leads to signiﬁcant reductions in working memory performance and reduces
accuracy in mental tasks [35] [36]. Experimental studies shows that be reducing
background speech, e.g. with use of sound absorbing materials, can improve ob-
jective performance and increase subjective comfort [34] [35]. Conversely, simply
reducing the sound level without reducing speech production has limited eﬀect on

cognitive disturbance [35]. Furthermore, subjective reports conﬁrm that background
speech are among the most disliked noise sources, and its presence is correlated with
reduced job satisfaction and increased perceived stress [34] [36].
In auditoriums, acoustic quality is essential to ensure the desired sound transmis-
sion. According to the Danish standard DS 490:2018, auditoriums are as a teaching
and presentation space, and normally it is recommended to meet the class B re-

• Background Sound Pressure Level (SPL) level below 30−35 dB(A) (unoccu-

STANDARDS AND REQUIREMENTS FOR AU-
DITORIUMS
International standards such as EN 16798-1:2019, DS 490:2018, ASHRAE 55, and
WHO guidelines deﬁne threshold values for key indoor environmental parameters.
However, these standards are often formulated for general spaces like oﬃces or class-
rooms and have very little focus on auditoriums. Probably due to the very few
buildings containing rooms with properties like auditoriums. In this project, the
values in Table 1.1 is used for evaluating the IEQ in the auditoriums.
Although the standards generally recommend maintaining Category II conditions for
typical indoor environments, several studies have shown that more stringent condi-
tions may be needed to achieve optimal learning outcomes. For example, Tran et al.
concluded that optimal learning outcomes are associated with CO2 concentrations
below 800–900 ppm, well within Category I limits [16].
The three Danish mass experiments all show that a majority of classrooms do not
comply with the requirements [20] [21] [22]. This indicates that even when class-
rooms and their ventilation systems are designed according to standard values, it
can be challenging to maintain these conditions in practice, especially in rooms with
high occupant density.

1.3
CONTROL OF THE INDOOR ENVIRONMENT
The indoor environment in auditoriums is typically controlled by mechanical ven-
tilation and centrally controlled heating. A centrally controlled system depends on
capacity, settings and where sensors are placed in the room. Similarly, the per-
ceived indoor environment depends on, e.g. where you are in the room and the
control strategy in relation to the current use of the room.
1.3.1
IMPORTANCE OF SENSOR PLACEMENT AND AU-
DITORIUM CHARACTERISTICS
Accurate assessment of indoor environmental quality in auditoriums depends sig-
niﬁcantly on both sensor placement and the physical characteristics of the room.
Persily highlights that buildings rarely behave as single zones, and substantial spa-
tial variations in parameters like CO2 can occur, making sensor location crucial for
obtaining representative data [37]. Several studies have demonstrated that sensor
positions signiﬁcantly aﬀect CO2 readings, as measurements taken at system sensors,
near walls, ceilings, or air inlets and outlets, often fail to reﬂect conditions within
the occupied zone and can either underestimate or overestimate concentrations, es-
pecially under displacement ventilation or in spaces with high occupant density and
uneven air distribution, leading to discrepancies between measured and perceived
7/ 128
==PAGE:30==
1. Introduction
comfort [15] [38] [39].
Rackes et al.
emphasize that sensor placement, accuracy, and quantity can sig-
niﬁcantly inﬂuence the detection of local air quality issues, especially in large or
stratiﬁed spaces where concentrations may vary spatially [40]. Recent studies have
developed models to optimize sensor placement for indoor monitoring, such as using
Gaussian process regression for temperature mapping [41] and network optimization
for CO2 sensors [42]. However, it should be noted that these studies primarily focus
on open-plan oﬃce environments, which diﬀer from auditoriums in terms of geome-
try, occupant density, and ventilation dynamics, suggesting that further research is
needed to adapt such methods for large volume spaces like auditoriums.
Beyond sensor placement, the speciﬁc architectural and operational features of au-
ditoriums also play a crucial role in shaping indoor environmental conditions. In
rooms with high occupant density, the thermal loads increase and can challenge
ventilation systems, potentially resulting in elevated temperatures and reduced IAQ
[23]. Additionally, outdoor temperatures can further inﬂuence indoor conditions in
auditoriums. Tran et al. report that large-volume spaces, such as auditoriums, often
exhibit signiﬁcant spatial variations in environmental parameters, requiring careful
consideration of sensor locations to capture representative data [16].
1.3.2
THE RELEVANCE OF MEASUREMENTS DURING AC-
TUAL TEACHING ACTIVITIES
Measurements conducted during actual teaching activities are crucial for accurately
reﬂecting the conditions experienced by occupants in auditoriums. Laboratory stud-
ies often fail to replicate the complex dynamics of real-life environments, where
factors such as occupant density, behaviour, and social interactions inﬂuence both
comfort and performance [38] [43]. Stokols highlights that perceptions of discom-
fort frequently stem from social and contextual factors that are absent in controlled
test conditions [43]. Bluyssen emphasizes that the indoor environment is shaped by
dynamic interactions between occupants, requiring ﬁeld measurements to capture
true user experiences [38]. While surveys are a valuable tool for capturing occu-
pants subjective experiences, research shows that respondents sometimes construct
a standpoint on the spot, particularly when they lack ﬁrmly established opinions.
Thus, survey responses may reﬂect not only actual experiences, but also the cog-
nitive processes involved in making judgments and providing socially acceptable
answers [44].

2. Objectives
2 OBJECTIVES
The primary objective of this study is to investigate and evaluate the indoor environ-
mental quality (IEQ) in auditoriums at Danish universities. The aim is to assess the
current conditions, identify potential shortcomings, and explore whether improve-
ments should be considered to enhance the students well-being and performance.
The study is guided by the following key questions:
• What is the general state of the indoor environment in Danish auditoriums,
both from an objective perspective (sensor measurements) and a subjective
perspective (user-reported experience)?
• To what extent do indoor climate conditions diﬀer between measurement
points located near the occupants (within the seating areas) and those located
at typical system sensor positions, such as on walls or in exhaust ducts?
• How do students perceive the indoor environment, and to what degree do they
consider the experienced conditions acceptable or unacceptable?
• To what extent do building-related parameters such as location, building year,
seating capacity, ﬂoor area, placement of air supply, occupancy rate, and out-
door temperature inﬂuence the indoor environmental quality in Danish audi-
toriums?
By addressing these questions, the study provides a comprehensive overview of IEQ
in auditoriums and supports data-informed decisions on future improvements to
indoor environments at Danish universities.
2.1
SCOPE
This study measures and analyses the IEQ in 19 auditoriums across seven Danish
universities. The data collection was conducted from March to May of 2025. The
focus is on indoor environmental parameters, which were measured during real-life
teaching.
IEQ data were collected using multi-parameter ATMO Cube sensors.
These measurements are supplemented by student feedback obtained through struc-
tured surveys.
The analysis includes the following parameters: temperature, carbon dioxide (CO2)
concentration, sound pressure level (SPL) , illuminance, relative humidity (RH), air
pressure, particulate matter (PM)(PM1, PM2.5, PM4, and PM10), volatile organic
compounds (VOCs), NOx and formaldehyde CH2O. All parameters are interpreted
with reference to relevant indoor environment standards, in particular EN 16798-1
[4].

3. Method
3 METHOD
This chapter outlines the methodological approach used to investigate the indoor
environment in auditoriums at Danish universities. The chapter provides a detailed
description of the processes that have been undertaken, from the selection of audito-
riums to the placement of sensors and how the data has been analysed. The study is
designed as an empirical study that combines data collected by objective measure-
ments of IAQ, thermal comfort, SPL and lighting with data collected by subjective
assessments from students through a survey. This method makes it possible to both
investigate the actual indoor environment and to analyse the correlation between

3.2
OBJECTIVE DATA COLLECTIONS METHOD
The objective data on temperature, CO2, RH, SPL, light, barometric pressure, PM,
VOCs, nitrogen oxides (NOx), and CH2O were collected in two ways: through specif-
ically placed sensors and supplemented with data from the university BMS sensor
systems.
This approach ensured both comparison with institutional monitoring
systems and customisation to survey-based data. To collect data directly in the au-
ditoriums, ATMO Cube sensors were used. The data from the ATMO sensors was
validated through calibration under both real and controlled conditions. The place-
ment of the sensors in the auditoriums was determined through a preliminary study
to ensure accuracy and representativeness of measurements. Data from universities
and Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) weather stations was processed, cleaned
and harmonised for reliable indoor environment analysis.

SENSOR PLACEMENT
To ensure accurate and comparable IEQ measurements across diﬀerent auditoriums,
a preliminary study was conducted to identify optimal sensor placement. Various
sensors were positioned strategically and at diﬀerent heights to examine how location
inﬂuences measured parameters. The insights gained from this study guided the
placement of sensors in all other auditoriums.
3.2.3.1
PRELIMINARY STUDY
Auditorium 34 was selected for the preliminary study of sensor placement. Audi-
torium 34 was selected due to its architectural characteristics, including windows
along one side creating potential glare, a height diﬀerence of four metres from front
to back and a high occupancy level throughout the week.
The study utilized ATMO Cubes and AirThings Space Pro sensors to monitor the
indoor climate within the auditorium. As shown in Figure 3.22, seven ATMO Cubes
were deployed at various positions throughout the auditorium. Some sensors were
placed adjacent to pre-existing installations to enable direct data comparison, while
one sensor was mounted on a wall opposite the windows to investigate potential
gradients between the seating area and the perimeter wall. Additional sensors were
positioned at diﬀerent heights in the front row, near the center, and at the back of
the auditorium, to capture IEQ conditions across multiple levels within the occupied
zone.
Furthermore, two AirThings Space Pro sensors were used to assess the IEQ condi-
tions within the HVAC exhaust system. This arrangement was intended to evaluate
possible measurement delays or oﬀsets compared to sensors placed near occupants.
One AirThings sensor was located in the exhaust duct, while the other was posi-
tioned beside an ATMO Cube in the center of the auditorium. AirThings devices
were chosen for these locations because they operate on built-in batteries and thus
do not require a direct power connection, unlike the ATMO Cubes.

3.3
SUBJECTIVE DATA COLLECTIONS METHOD
The subjective data on occupants’ perception was collected using a survey, devel-
oped in accordance with ISO 10551:2019 and reﬁned through pilot testing and ﬁeld
trials to ensure clarity and suitability for completion during lecture breaks. The
survey was distributed in both Danish and English using SurveyXact and designed
to be anonymous, with explicit consent obtained to comply with GDPR. While this
method allowed for context speciﬁc responses, several potential biases were identiﬁed
and critically reﬂected upon. Data was cleaned and validated, yielding 2776 com-
plete responses representing 2% of the student population and allowing for precise
statistical analysis with a margin of error of ±1.9%. Groupings based on building
and contextual factors further support detailed comparative analyses of subjective
IEQ perceptions.
3.3.1
DISIGN OF THE SURVEY
The initial draft of the survey was developed with reference to ISO 10551:2019
[64], which outlines standardised methods for assessing subjective perception of the
indoor environment. A pilot survey was conducted among colleagues and friends to
evaluate the clarity and estimated completion time, originally around ten minutes.
Based on the insights from this pilot, several questions were removed or rephrased
to reduce the surveys length and improve comprehension.
Subsequently, ﬁeld testing was carried out in an actual auditorium setting, conﬁrm-
ing that the revised survey could be completed during lecture breaks. Following the
ﬁrst day of ﬁeld data collection, feedback revealed noticeable temperature diﬀer-
ences between the front and rear of the auditoriums, leading to the inclusion of an
additional question: Where in the room are you sitting? to capture spatial variation
in occupant experience.
The survey was prepared in both English and Danish to ensure accessibility, as the
study was conducted in Denmark. While this dual-language approach was intended
to accommodate a wider range of participants, it also introduced potential chal-
lenges related to interpretation, as certain questions or response options might be
understood diﬀerently due to language nuances. Moreover, since respondents were
not necessarily familiar with technical IEQ terminology, there was a risk that some
academic terms might not be fully understood.
The survey was administered via SurveyXact and designed to be fully anonymous,
ensuring that individual responses could not be traced back to speciﬁc participants.
This approach served two purposes: ﬁrst, to encourage honest and uninﬂuenced
responses; and second, to avoid the need for the authors to handle personally iden-
tiﬁable or sensitive information, thereby ensuring compliance with data protection
regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).
To meet ethical and legal requirements, the ﬁrst item in the survey consisted of an

explicit consent form titled Consent to Participate in the Survey. Participants were
informed of the purpose of the study and asked to conﬁrm their agreement to the

3.3.2
CRITICAL REFLECTIONS ON THE SURVEY
The responses collected through the survey should be interpreted with caution due
to potential biases introduced by the data collection setup. The authors were phys-
ically present during the distribution of the survey to give a brief introduction and
encourage participation.
As an incentive, students were oﬀered a small piece of
candy upon completion of the survey.
The survey was accessed via a QR code that students were asked to scan using
their smartphones. While this approach enabled quick access and increased partic-
ipation, the physical presence of the authors may have introduced several forms of
response bias including, constructive judgment, priming/Bias, and authority eﬀect,
potentially inﬂuencing how students perceived and answered the questions.
CONSTRUCTIVE JUDGMENT
By asking the participants to complete the survey while physically present in the
auditorium, the response gain increased contextual validity.
The students were
prompted to reﬂect on their immediate experience, rather than relying on retro-
spective or generalised impressions. This method aligns with core principles of qual-
itative inquiry, in which contextuality is fundamental for understanding subjective
meaning [65]. When the data are collected in a natural setting, the responses reﬂect
more accurately on the lived reality and situated perceptions, thus improving the
ecological validity of the data.
PURPOSE BIAS
When introducing the study and the purpose, investigating and possible improving
the IEQ, may activate a cognitive bias.
Paul Slovic shows that preferences and
evaluations often are constructed in the moment of solicitation, especially when the
respondents do not have pre-formed judgments [66]. These constructed preferences
may lead the respondents to emphasise aspects of the indoor environment that they
would otherwise not have considered. Furthermore, when the respondents are aware
of the purpose of the study, they may try to give the right answer to support the
prescribed purpose.
AUTHORITY EFFECT
Even though responses were recorded anonymously on respondents’ phones, the prior
in person introduction by the researchers may still trigger an interviewer presence
eﬀect. On top of that, the researchers were also present and observing when the
respondents were answering. Tourangeau et al. demonstrate that even minimal

social cues can aﬀect the accuracy and content of self-reports, especially on topics
with social desirability dimensions [67]. In academic contexts, students may also
perceive presenters as authority ﬁgures, which can elicit compliance or agreement
and subtly inﬂuence response tendencies.
INCENTIVES (CANDY)
Providing a small reward, like a piece of candy, to participants may introduce a mild
form of reciprocity bias. Alvin W. Gouldner theorised that individuals tend to feel
obligated to reciprocate even small favours, which can inﬂuence behaviour in ways
that support the initiator’s expectations or goals [68]. As observed, such incentives
are typically eﬀective in increasing participation rates. But some respondents feel
compelled to answer quickly or superﬁcially just to get the reward. And thus, the
incentive can also reduce the cognitive depth of responses. In a group setting, this
eﬀect can be ampliﬁed by social signalling, where students observe peers partici-
pating and follow suit without deep engagement. It was noted that not that many
participants were interested in the candy, in some auditoriums, not a single piece of
candy was given.

3.5.2
DATA ANALYSIS METHODS
Thermal comfort was assessed and analysed using three widely recognised indices:
Predicted Mean Vote (PMV), Predicted Percentage of Dissatisﬁed (PPD), and Stan-
dard Eﬀective Temperature (SET) [4] [73] [74]. These indices provide a quantitative
assessment of perceived comfort based on a combination of environmental and per-
sonal parameters and were calculated for each respondent based on synchronised
sensor data and survey responses.
The PMV index estimates the average thermal sensation of a large group of people,
using the seven-point ISO 10551:2019 thermal sensation scale ranging from −3 (cold)
to +3 (hot) [64]. It is based on the heat balance between the human body and its
environment and incorporates six primary input variables:
• Air temperature (°C)
• Mean radiant temperature (°C)
• Relative humidity (%)
• Air velocity (m/s)
• Metabolic rate (met)
• Clothing insulation (clo)
In this study, air temperature and relative humidity were obtained from sensor
measurements. Mean radiant temperature was assumed to be equal to the air tem-
perature. A constant air velocity of 0.1 m/s was assumed across all locations. The
metabolic rate was set to 1.2 met, representing typical sedentary activities such
as studying or attending a lecture.
Clothing insulation values were individually
estimated from survey responses, based on self-reported clothing ensembles, and
assigned typical clo values as deﬁned in ASHRAE 55:2013 [74]:
• 0.36 clo walking shorts and T-shirt
• 0.57 clo trousers and T-shirt

• 1.15 clo trousers, T-shirt, long-sleeved shirt, sweater, and suit jacket

In addition to PMV and PPD, the SET was also calculated. SET is deﬁned as the
temperature of an imaginary isothermal environment at 50% relative humidity, in
which a person with a speciﬁed metabolic rate and clothing level would experience
the same skin temperature and thermoregulatory strain as in the actual environ-

wettedness, making it particularly suitable for assessing comfort in naturally venti-
lated or dynamic environments.
The same environmental (temperature, humidity, air velocity) and personal (clo,
met) parameters used for PMV were applied for SET calculations. SET was com-
puted using the pythermalcomfort package in Python, which implements the Pierce
two-node model, simulating the thermal responses of the human core and skin layers.
For each survey entry, the corresponding timestamp was extracted, and the nearest
time-matched sensor measurements were retrieved to obtain temperature and hu-
midity values at the moment of response. These paired datasets were then used to
compute individual PMV, PPD, and SET values per respondent. Additionally, ten-
dency plots was create, to see the direct link between measurements and subjective
responses.

4.3.8
SUMMARY OF BUILDING-RELATED PARAMETERS
The results indicate that both physical building characteristics and contextual fac-
tors inﬂuence perceived and measured IEQ, with distinct patterns emerging for IAQ
and thermal conditions.
The IAQ was primarily inﬂuenced by location, ﬂoor area, seating capacity, occu-
pancy level, and outdoor temperature. Auditoriums located in the suburb, with
large ﬂoor areas or high seating capacities, were associated with higher CO2 concen-
trations, both in terms of maximum and mean values, and students more frequently
described the air as stuﬀy. High occupancy levels and high outdoor temperatures
further contributed to this perception, although the diﬀerences in measured CO2
concentrations were more modest. No detectable diﬀerences in perceived IAQ were
found between building years and supply air, but CO2 concentrations were lower in
the newer auditoriums and in auditoriums with under-seat air supply systems.
The thermal conditions were inﬂuenced by location, building year, ﬂoor area, occu-
pancy, and outdoor temperature. Occupancy rate strongly inﬂuenced thermal per-
ception, students reported feeling warmer in more crowded auditoriums, although
this was only partially reﬂected in the measured data. Students in auditoriums lo-
cated in the city and in newer buildings generally felt warmer, which corresponded
with higher measured maximum temperatures. Smaller auditoriums also exhibited
higher temperatures and a broader thermal range. Outdoor temperature did not
directly determine indoor thermal conditions, but colder days were associated with
greater indoor temperature variability.
Overall, building-related parameters such as location, building year, ﬂoor area, seat-
ing capacity, occupancy rate, and outdoor temperature were found to inﬂuence both
measured and perceived indoor environmental quality in Danish auditoriums. While
many subjective perceptions aligned with objective measurements, certain experi-
ential aspects, especially under high occupancy rate, were not fully captured by
objective measurements.
4.4
SUMMARY OF RESULTS
The analyses of indoor environmental quality (IEQ) in 19 Danish auditoriums, based
on both objective measurements and subjective responses, revealed several consistent
trends. While most auditoriums operated within recommendation and limitations
of standards and health−based guidelines for parameters, a number of localized or
conditional deviations were identiﬁed.
IAQ was generally acceptable, with CO2, TVOC, CH2O, and NOx concentrations
mostly within recommended limits. However, short−term peaks occurred during
high occupancy, especially in auditoriums with large ﬂoor areas or high seating ca-
pacity. Perceived IAQ was moderately aligned with measured pollutants, although
additional factors, such as temperature, air movement, and psychological context,

appeared to inﬂuence responses. Sensor placement signiﬁcantly aﬀected CO2 read-
ings, reinforcing the need for seating−level monitoring. Lower CO2 concentrations
were observed in newer auditoriums and in those with under−seat air supply sys-
tems, although this was not reﬂected in reported perceptions.
Thermal comfort varied across auditoriums. Most temperatures complied with Cat-

5 DISCUSSION
This chapter discuss the results and method used in this study to investigate the IEQ
in in auditoriums at Danish university. The discussion follows the studys main focus
areas: thermal comfort, IAQ, acoustics, lighting, and building-related parameters.
First the strengths and limitations of using combined sensor data and occupant sur-
veys, with attention to issues such as sensor placement, sampling duration, transla-
tion, and response consistency is discussed generally.
Secondly the results obtained for the IEQ parameters one by one. The thermal com-
fort is examined through temperature data, comfort indices (PMV, PPD, SET), and
self- reported thermal sensation and this section discussed the detected discrepancies
between measured and perceived conditions and discusses contributing factors such
as solar gains, occupancy variation, and system performance. The IAQ focuses on
CO2, humidity, and VOCs, with particular attention to mismatches between mea-
sured concentrations and perceived freshness. Acoustic and lighting conditions are
evaluated through sensor data and user feedback, revealing how background SPL,
glare, and daylight access inﬂuence comfort.
Finally, the results of the building-related characteristics, including size, occupancy,
ventilation design, and sensor strategy, are discussed in relation to IEQ variation.
Key ﬁndings are interpreted in light of relevant standards and prior research, with
reﬂections on methodological limitations and implications for future studies.
5.1
METHOD AND LIMITATIONS
For this study, both objective measurements and subjective surveys were employed
to assess the IEQ in university auditoriums.
The objective measurements were conducted to provide high resolution quantita-
tive data, enabling precise documentation of the physical conditions in the indoor
environment over time. These measurements oﬀer a factual, quantitative basis for
evaluating compliance with regulatory standards or guidelines.
In parallel, subjective data were collected to capture students immediate perceptions
of thermal comfort, IAQ, lighting conditions, SPL levels, and overall environmental
acceptability. These reﬂections on the human experience of comfort or discomfort
may not always align strictly with measured values. Respondents also reported on
personal variables (e.g. clothing, age, gender), which were used to estimate values
for calculations of PMV, PPD, and SET.
The combination of these two methods to collect data enables a multidimensional
evaluation of IEQ and allows the two data sources to validate each other. Cross vali-

dation can increase conﬁdence in the ﬁndings; for example, high measured CO2 levels
correlating with reports of stuﬀy air or reduced acceptability support the validity
of both the objective and subjective results. Conversely, discrepancies between ob-
jective and subjective data can reveal important nuances. Objective measurements
may remain within standard limits while occupants still perceive conditions as un-

The combined approach oﬀers a holistic perspective on the IEQ and its potential

The data collection for this study was carried out over a relatively short period of
1–3 days per auditorium in the spring of 2025. While this was suﬃcient to capture
representative occupied conditions, it represents only a limited temporal snapshot
compared to seasonal or yearly variation in IEQ. As such, external conditions, such
as outdoor temperature, solar radiation, and wind speed, may have had a dispro-
portionate inﬂuence on indoor conditions during the data collection period.
In addition, the occupant load varied considerably across auditoriums and days.
Some auditoriums were sparsely occupied, resulting in lower internal heat gains
and reduced CO2 loads, while others were fully occupied, potentially increasing
thermal loads and ventilation demand. These variations aﬀect both the objective
measurements and the subjective experience of the indoor environment.
All environmental sensors recorded data at 1 second intervals, which were subse-
quently averaged to 1 minute resolution.
While this approach reduces SPL and
simpliﬁes data processing, it may also smooth out short term peaks, such as brief el-
evated sound levels during applause or sudden disturbances. However, this smooth-
ing may also be seen as a strength, as it eliminated the need for extensive post
processing or outlier removal.
The survey used in this study was developed using academically validated terminol-
ogy to ensure consistency with existing IEQ literature and to minimise leading ques-
tions or suggestive wording. While this approach enhances methodological rigour,
it may also have reduced accessibility or clarity for some participants, particularly
those unfamiliar with technical terms.
To accommodate the diverse student population, the survey was initially developed
in English and subsequently translated into Danish, allowing participants to respond
in their preferred language. A total of 85.7% of responses were submitted in Danish,
indicating a clear preference for the version in the respondents mother tongue.
Despite best eﬀorts to preserve meaning during translation, certain nuances or tech-
nical terms may not have direct equivalents in Danish, potentially leading to ambigu-

The calculated Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) values for the 19 auditoriums indicate
that most auditoriums fall close to neutral (PMV 0) or slightly below, towards –1,

as measured median temperatures were generally within or slightly above Category

• A ﬁxed air velocity of 0.1 m/s was applied uniformly across all rooms. Which
may not reﬂect actual airﬂow near occupants.
• Clothing insulation (clo) was estimated based on user input, which may have
involved recall bias or reporting uncertainty.
• The model does not account for localised heat sources, stratiﬁcation, or inter-
mittent occupancy patterns.
When comparing calculated PMV with self reported thermal sensation, a similar
general pattern is observed: most respondents reported feeling neutral. However,
the distribution of responses was skewed towards warmer sensations. This diverges
slightly from the PMV results, which leaned more towards slightly cool.
A possible explanation is the contextual mismatch between the PMV model and real
world conditions. The original PMV framework was developed under laboratory
conditions with small groups (typically 20 participants) [75], whereas auditoriums
involve large number of occupancy, variable activity levels, and cognitive demands.
Additionally, non environmental factors such as cognitive load, emotional stress, or

Summing up, these ﬁndings highlight the limitations of using PMV alone to assess
thermal comfort in complex, dynamic environments such as university auditoriums.
And the ﬁndings emphasise the value of integrating subjective occupant feedback

As expected, PPD values largely reﬂect the calculated PMV, since PPD is derived
directly from PMV. However, when analysing the full distribution of PPD values
across the 19 auditoriums, notable inconsistencies appear in a subset of rooms. In
particular, six auditoriums showed 95th percentile PPD values exceeding 30–40%,
suggesting that in some instances, nearly half of occupants might experience dis-
comfort.
This is surprising given that the measured temperatures in these auditoriums were
not extreme; most values remained within Category I or II, and in some cases,
the thermal distribution was even more favourable than in auditoriums with lower
predicted dissatisfaction. For example, DTU 81, DTU 49, DTU 83, AU 105, AU E,
and CBS 202 all exhibited moderate and stable temperature conditions, yet showed
elevated PPD values. Conversely, AU 113, which demonstrated clear overheating,
showed PPD values predominantly within Category II, with only limited data points
falling in Category III [4].
Several factors may explain these inconsistencies:
• Data timing mismatch: PPD was calculated based on snapshot measurements
taken at the time of questionnaire submission, whereas temperature distribu-
tions are based on continuous data across the full occupancy period of several
days.
• Spatial averaging: Temperature values were from multiple sensors distributed
within each room. In contrast, occupants responded based on their individual
thermal environment, which may diﬀer signiﬁcantly from the room mean. This
suggests that zone speciﬁc pairing between participant responses and nearby
sensors would improve accuracy.
• Input estimation uncertainties: The ﬁxed assumption of 1.2 met for metabolic
rate may not accurately reﬂect real conditions. University students are typi-
cally younger and may have elevated physiological activity due to recent phys-

Likewise, clothing levels were simpliﬁed into four categories, which may not

• Behavioural and physiological variation: Modern students often use laptops
during lectures, which can emit localized heat. Combined with cognitive load
and emotional factors, this may increase perceived warmth independently of
air temperature.
These observations underscore the limitations of using standardised PMV/PPD cal-
culations in complex environments such as auditoriums, where spatial variability,
behavioral context, and occupant physiology deviate from the assumptions embed-

To complement the PMV and PPD models, Standard Eﬀective Temperature (SET)
was also calculated as an alternative index of thermal comfort. Unlike PMV, which
estimates thermal sensation at a population level, SET approximates the individual
physiological response by accounting for skin temperature, humidity eﬀects, and
latent heat exchange.
Across the dataset, SET values generally remained within the recommended comfort
range of 22–26 °C [74], with a tendency towards the lower part of the range, and in
some cases falling below 22 °C. This is consistent with the PMV and PPD results,
which similarly indicated a general tendency towards slightly cool conditions. Given
that SET relies on the same environmental and personal parameters as PMV, this
convergence is expected.
However, SET provides a more physiologically grounded estimate, especially under
conditions where PMV may oversimplify the experience. For instance, auditoriums
such as AU 113 and CBS 033, which showed sustained high temperatures and dense
occupancy, also exhibited elevated SET values approaching or exceeding 26 °C. This
indicates a greater heat load on the body than what PMV alone suggests.
Interestingly, the elevated thermal conditions observed in KEA and RUC A01 are
clearly reﬂected in the SET distributions, but less apparent in the PMV and PPD
ﬁgures.
By incorporating eﬀects of humidity, air velocity, and thermoregulation, SET oﬀers
additional insight into occupant level comfortparticularly in large, intermittently
ventilated auditoriums.
These ﬁndings demonstrate the value of including SET
alongside PMV and PPD to assess thermal conditions more comprehensively, par-
ticularly in complex, high load indoor environments where model assumptions may
diverge from real world dynamics.

that several auditoriums had median values within Category II, and in some cases

the calculated thermal comfort indices (PMV, PPD, and SET).
Interestingly, in auditoriums such as KU HCØ, DTU 81, and AU E, which had some

Overall, RH does not appear to be a primary driver of the calculated thermal sen-

Thermal conditions were selected as the second most disturbing indoor environmen-
tal factor by the respondents. This indicates that thermal comfort is a salient issue
for many occupants.
However, only 14% rated the thermal environment as unacceptable, suggesting that
for most respondents, thermal discomfort was noticeable but not severe enough to
render the room conditions intolerable. In other words, thermal conditions were
often perceived as mildly irritating rather than outright unacceptable, highlighting
the importance of distinguishing between perceived disturbance and acceptability
thresholds. It should, however, be noted that some level of dissatisfaction is expected
even under optimal conditions. According to Fangers comfort model, a minimum of
5% of occupants will typically be dissatisﬁed [75].
Interestingly, no clear correlation was found between clothing insulation levels and
reported acceptability of thermal conditions. This may imply that most occupants
were appropriately dressed for the prevailing indoor conditions, or that they had
prior knowledge of the auditoriums thermal environment and adjusted their cloth-
ing accordingly. These observations support the adaptive comfort hypothesis, which
posits that occupants adjust to thermal environments through behavioral and psy-
chological means, such as clothing or cultural expectations.
Thermal conditions were inﬂuenced by location, building year, ﬂoor area, and occu-
pancy rate. Auditoriums in urban settings were generally warmer, both in measured
peak temperatures and in subjective thermal feedback. Newer buildings (constructed
after 1988) and smaller auditoriums were also perceived as warmer and exhibited
higher maximum temperatures. In smaller spaces, this can be attributed to reduced
thermal mass and a faster response to internal heat gains, while in newer buildings,
increased airtightness and insulation from energy-eﬃcient design practices likely
contributed [38]. Although such measures improve energy performance, they may
also increase the risk of overheating if ventilation is not adequately managed.
Occupancy rate emerged as a strong predictor of thermal discomfort. Students in

crowded auditoriums were over three times more likely to report feeling warm or
hot (20.3% vs. 6.5%), despite relatively small diﬀerences in measured temperature

The indicated a clear correlation between the measured indoor air temperature and

However, it is diﬃcult to draw conclusions about the extreme ends of the thermal

these categories. This suggests that the majority of occupants were within their
adaptive comfort range, or that extreme discomfort was rare during the monitoring
periods.
Interestingly, most respondents associated neutral thermal sensation with measured
temperatures of around 23 °C, which is slightly above the Category I median of
22 °C deﬁned in DS/EN 16798-1:2019 [4]. This may reﬂect individual preferences,
clothing adaptation, or expectations speciﬁc to lecture environments and since the
majority of respondents preferred conditions close to the measured temperatures, it
indicates that most auditoriums operated within the acceptable thermal range.
5.3
IAQ
Apart from CO2 and temperature, perceived IAQ may be inﬂuenced by other pa-
rameters, such as PM, VOCs, CH2O, and NOx. However, the results of this study
showed that the measured levels of all these parameters remained well within health
guidelines and are unlikely to explain perceptions of poor IAQ.
Generally, the CO2 measurements across all auditoriums fell within Category I, with
only a few instances reaching Category II. This ﬁnding is particularly noteworthy
given the high proportion of students who rated the IAQ as unacceptable (more
than 30%). The discrepancy suggests that CO2 concentration alone may not be a
suﬃcient indicator of perceived IAQ in auditorium environments.
This study shows that perceived IAQ was inﬂuenced by several building-related
parameters, including location, ﬂoor area, seating capacity, occupancy rate, and
outdoor temperature. Auditoriums in city locations consistently showed higher max-
imum CO2 concentrations and a greater share of students reporting unacceptable
113/ 128
==PAGE:136==
5. Discussion
IAQ (37.3% vs. 22.3%). This could indicate a poorer outdoor air quality in the city,
leading to higher levels of particles entering the auditoriums. Aligning with ﬁndings
by Bluyssen, who emphasizes the role of external air quality and local environmental
factors in shaping indoor perceptions [38]. However, the measurements did not indi-
cate any signiﬁcant diﬀerences in PM or VOCs, suggesting that perceived diﬀerences
may also stem from contextual or psychosocial factors.
Auditoriums with larger ﬂoor areas and higher seating capacities were also associated
with elevated CO2 levels (up to 1200 ppm), and more than half of the students
rated the air as slightly stuﬀy or stuﬀy (54.7–55.4%). These ﬁndings indicate that
large spaces are not inherently better ventilated, and may suﬀer from ineﬀective air
distribution, supporting earlier observations on the importance of ventilation design
in rooms with a large volume [37] [40].
Occupancy rate further inﬂuenced perceived IAQ, even though measured mean CO2
concentrations only diﬀered modestly (∼670 ppm vs. ∼620 ppm). In auditoriums
with occupancy above 50%, 35.9% of students found the air unacceptable. This
supports existing research showing that high occupant density can heighten percep-
tions of stuﬀy or poor air, even when objective indicators remain within acceptable
ranges [38, 78, Chapter 8]. According to Stokols framework and Evans et al., such
perceptions are shaped not only by spatial density but by psychological responses
to crowding and diminished personal control [43] [78].
Outdoor temperature also played a role. On days exceeding 14°C, students were
more likely to report poor IAQ, and the perception of fresh or slightly fresh air
dropped from 24.8% on colder days to 16.5% on warmer days. This may result
from reduced ventilation on warmer days to maintain thermal comfort, leading to
increased CO2 accumulation. Although participants were given time to acclimate
before completing the survey, expectations shaped by pleasant outdoor conditions
may still have inﬂuenced responses.
No signiﬁcant diﬀerences in perceived IAQ were observed between buildings of diﬀer-
ent construction years or between ventilation conﬁgurations. However, auditoriums
with under-seat air supply or newer buildings did show objectively lower CO2 concen-
trations (by 100–150 ppm on average). This may indicate a threshold eﬀect, where
perceived IAQ remains constant beyond a certain level of ventilation performance.

SPL CONDITIONS
Across the 19 monitored auditoriums, the measured SPL level, measured by the
sound pressure levels (SPL), ranged from 40 to 70 dB(A), with occasional peaks
exceeding 80 dB(A), and only minimal diﬀerences between wall and seating sensors
were detected.
This indicates relatively uniform acoustic conditions across most
rooms.

==PAGE:137==

Looking at the quality of the SPL, several auditoriums exhibited slightly elevated
median SPL, which may be attributed to poor room acoustics, use of microphones,
or increased background activity during teaching. In particular, auditoriums with
voice ampliﬁcation systems did not show signiﬁcantly higher SPLs at the seating
positions, despite proximity to the lecturer and nearby students.
To evaluate background SPL levels, BR18 recommends that unoccupied auditoriums
remain below 30 dB(A) [79]. As direct unoccupied measurements were unavailable,
the 10th percentile of sound levels during occupancy was used as a proxy. These
values ranged between 44 and 52 dB(A), well above the recommended threshold.
However, these measurements may have been aﬀected by transient occupant SPL,
external disturbances, or adjacent room activity, and thus may not represent the
true mechanical background SPL.
During lectures, sound levels commonly exceeded 55 dB(A), the threshold recom-
mended for teaching activities. This included both periods of the lecture and short
breaks, during which students often moved or talked. Interestingly, these peaks were
not conﬁned to seating locations, further supporting the uniformity of the acoustic
environment.
While most median SPL readings remained below 60 dB(A) a level at which con-
centration may begin to decline, subjective data show that 9.7 % of respondents
rated the SPL level as unacceptable, and 18.1 % identiﬁed SPL as the most dis-
turbing environmental factor. These ﬁndings suggest that a signiﬁcant proportion
of occupants perceived the sound pressure level to be high.
Participants located at urban campuses were more likely to report SPL as disturbing.
This may reﬂect either higher actual SPL levels or a lower tolerance due to ambient
SPL expectations.
When comparing perceived SPL intensity to measured SPL, a general trend emerged:
higher sound levels correlated with more reports of disturbance. Nonetheless, sub-
jective responses varied considerably.
For instance, some respondents rated the
environment as noisy at SPLs around 38 dB(A), while others found 70 dB(A) very
quiet. These discrepancies may result from mismatches between response time and
measurement time, or individual diﬀerences in SPL sensitivity and expectation.
In terms of duration of disturbance, only around 2 % of respondents reported being
disturbed most or all of the time, suggesting that while some peak values may be
problematic, long term acoustic discomfort was relatively rare.
These ﬁndings suggest that acoustic conditions were broadly acceptable across the
monitored auditoriums when looking at subjective evaluation.

5.4.2
LIGHTING CONDITIONS
Measured illuminance levels across the 19 auditoriums were generally below recom-
mended values for educational settings.
A notable limitation in the interpretation of lighting data stems from the diﬀerence in
sensor orientation and placement. At wall mounted locations, sensors were typically
installed vertically, facing into the room, and placed high up at the rear, often
away from windows to avoid solar heating interference.
In contrast, sensors at
seating positions were mounted horizontally, better representing the actual lighting
conditions experienced by occupants, especially when performing visual tasks such
as note taking. Due to this mismatch, direct comparison between wall and seating
illuminance should be interpreted with caution.
Several auditoriums were equipped with limited or no daylight access, often due
to solar shading, windowless designs, or strategic placement to avoid glare and
overheating. In auditoriums with high ceilings, strong artiﬁcial lighting was required
to maintain acceptable task lighting. However, in some cases, intense spotlights
could cause localised glare or temporary discomfort, especially if occupants looked
directly at the source or if the lights were misaligned.
From the occupant survey, only 11% rated the lighting conditions as unacceptable,
yet 14% identiﬁed lighting as the most disturbing IEQ parameter. This discrepancy
suggests that while most respondents did not ﬁnd the lighting intolerable, minor an-
noyances such as glare, low task illuminance, or diﬃculty reading from the projector
screen contributed to a perception of reduced comfort.
Interestingly, lighting was more frequently reported as disturbing in auditoriums
located in suburbs. One possible explanation is the greater prevalence of windows in
suburban buildings due to less spatial constraint, which may increase both daylight
access and the risk of glare from external reﬂections.
Additionally, auditoriums with smaller seating capacity were 6% more likely to be
rated as disturbing in terms of lighting.
This may reﬂect the higher prevalence
of windows in small to medium sized spaces, or the greater perceptual impact of
localised lighting issues in auditoriums with fewer occupants.

6 CONCLUSION
THE INDOOR ENVIRONMENT IN DANISH AUDITORIUMS
This Mater Thesis has investigated the IEQ in auditoriums at Danish universities.
The study is based on data obtained from objective measurements and subjective
data through a survey among the students in the auditoriums. In total 19 audito-
riums at seven out of nine Danish universities have been investigated. The survey
was responded to by 2776 students, which was a response rate of 86%. Both the
measured data and the survey were collected in two consecutive days in each of the
auditorium during the spring of 2025.
Based on ﬁndings from multiple studies, CO2 concentration, temperature, RH, il-
luminance, and sound pressure level have been identiﬁed as key indicators of IEQ
[16] [23] [27] [34]. For this reason, the present study employed a multi-sensor device
(ATMO Cube) to collect objective measurements of these parameters, supplemented
by a survey designed to capture the subjective experience of the IEQ.
According to several guidelines and standards, it is generally expected that approxi-
mately 5% of occupants may ﬁnd the indoor environment unacceptable, even under
optimal conditions. However, it is recommended that the proportion of dissatisﬁed
individuals should be kept below 10% to ensure adequate comfort and acceptability
[4].
Across the 19 auditoriums, the measured indoor environmental conditions were gen-
erally acceptable. The two primary parameters, thermal environment and IAQ were
mostly within the thresholds deﬁned in DS/EN 16798-1:2019. The majority of CO2
levels were within Category I, indicating a high IAQ. Thermal conditions were typi-
cally within Category I or II. However, isolated exceedances were observed, including
one auditorium with persistent overheating, with average temperature around 26°C.
The thermal comfort indicators PMV, PPD, and SET show that students should
be comfortable in most auditoriums (Category II), while several auditoriums fall
into Category III, where thermal conditions do not meet comfort standards. Using
the PMV and SET values it can be concluded that the auditoriums have a general
tendency toward slightly cool IEQ, but the students tend to feel slightly warm. PPD
indicates that discomfort levels exceed acceptable thresholds in multiple spaces
Despite these favourable measurements, occupants subjective experience diverged
in several key areas, aligning with the calculated PPD. Approximately 19% of re-
spondents rated the overall IEQ as unacceptable. The greatest dissatisfaction was
reported for IAQ, with over 30% of occupants rating the IAQ as unacceptable, de-
spite objective compliance. Thermal dissatisfaction was more moderate, with around
14% of occupants rating it unacceptable.

Other important IEQ parameters, like acoustic and lighting conditions, were also
investigated as part of the study. Acoustic conditions revealed further inconsisten-
cies between the measured levels and experienced comfort. While average measured
sound levels ranged from 48–56 dB(A), several auditoriums exceeded the recom-
mended limit of 55 dB(A), and estimated background levels were consistently above
the 30 dB(A) guideline.
A relationship was observed between higher measured
sound levels and increased disturbance from occupants. Despite this, only 10% of
respondents found the acoustic environment unacceptable.
Lighting conditions were IEQ parameter that was measured to be the least compliant
in terms of objective criteria. Most auditoriums failed to meet the recommended
300–500 lux. However, only 12% of occupants rated the lighting as unacceptable,
suggesting a lower perceptual sensitivity or adaptation to suboptimal lighting.
In general, the study concludes that the measured data is in compliance with the IEQ
standards but does not always align with the perceived comfort of the occupants.
While the physical environment generally met the recommendations, subjective dis-
satisfaction, particularly related to IAQ, highlights the importance of combining
objective monitoring with user feedback to ensure environments are experientially
acceptable.
SENSOR PLACEMENT
It was an important part of the study to investigate the inﬂuence of the placement
of sensors. Generally, the sensors used for data supporting central control systems
or BMS are mounted on the walls, whereas the occupants are seated across the
auditorium or even at the center of the room. The result of the study concludes
that indoor environmental conditions vary depending on sensor placement. Seating-
level sensors consistently recorded higher temperatures than wall-mounted sensors,
suggesting that wall-based measurements may underestimate occupants thermal ex-
posure.
CO2 concentrations were generally higher at wall-mounded sensors, likely due to
vertical stratiﬁcation, although some auditoriums exhibited the opposite pattern.
Comparisons between ATMO sensors and BMS system data did not reveal a clear
picture: in some auditoriums, BMS sensors measured higher temperatures and CO2
than ATMO sensors, while in others, the BMS system reported lower values. The
observed variability highlights inconsistent sensor placement and calibration. This
points to a broader lack of standardisation about were to measure and how often
calibration or exchange of sensors should happen in auditoriums.
For other parameters such as TVOC, CH2O, humidity and sound pressure, diﬀer-
ences between wall-mounded and seating-level placement were minimal, and these
were not measured by the BMS system.
Overall, the ﬁndings highlight that BMS data and wall-mounted sensors may not
reliably reﬂect seating-level conditions, particularly for thermal comfort and IAQ.

This underscores the importance of seating-level monitoring when evaluating IEQ
in occupied auditoriums or to place the BMS sensors close to the seating area for
better reﬂection and thus be able to regulate for a better experienced IEQ.

Across all auditoriums, the study has investigated general building-related condi-

examined by location (Suburb vs City), construction year (< 1988 vs > 1988), size
(< 196 m2 vs > 196 m2) capacity (< 194 occupants vs > 194 occupants), Air supply

The result of this study shows that some building-related parameters have an im-
pact on the indoor environmental quality. Especially in auditoriums located in the
suburb, with large ﬂoor areas and high seating capacities, the measured CO2 con-
centrations was high, and students in these auditoriums more frequently described
the air as stuﬀy. A high occupancy levels and high outdoor temperatures further
contributed to this perception, although the diﬀerences in measured CO2 concen-

the city, the measured temperature was the highest, which correspond with the stu-
dents responses of feeling warmer. Finally the study revealed that the occupancy
rate strongly inﬂuence the thermal perception. Students reported feeling warmer in
more crowded auditoriums, although this was only partially reﬂected in the mea-
sured data.

FURTHER STUDIES AND INNOVATION
This study provides a foundation for future research and practical strategies aimed at
improving IEQ in university auditoriums. It contributes to a deeper understanding
of how IEQ is measured, controlled, and experienced by occupants, and how these
factors interact to inﬂuence comfort, concentration, and learning outcomes.
By highlighting the discrepancies between objective conditions and subjective ex-
periences, the ﬁndings can inform more occupant centred approaches to IEQ man-
agement. In the broader context, improving the indoor environment in educational
settings may support enhanced academic performance and, ultimately, contribute
to societal development through a better educated population and a more resilient
knowledge economy.

This study highlights signiﬁcant variability in sensor readings depending on place-
ment in a large and complex room like audituriums, particularly for CO2 and tem-
perature, demonstrating that current practices could gain from standardisation. By

PATTERNS AND AI
To improve both energy eﬃciency and occupant comfort it could be interesting to
explore whether patterns of the use of the auditorium exist throughout the day
or week, and to analyse these using AI methods. Such an approach would make it
possible to develop more strategic control of the HVAC system. Importantly, student
perceptions should be included as a key input to ensure that control strategies reﬂect
actual user experience.
3D MODELLING OF AIRFLOW
Future work could include spatial mapping of sensor data combined with 3D mod-
elling of airﬂow patterns, temperature distribution, and daylight ingress. This would
allow for a more detailed understanding of how architectural features such as room
geometry, ceiling height, window placement, and the location of ventilation inlets
and outlets aﬀect local variations in IEQ. Such an approach could identify zones
of persistent discomfort or insuﬃcient ventilation, which are not always detectable
through average measurements. It would also support data-informed optimization
of building design and retroﬁtting strategies aimed at improving IEQ.
MATCHING SENSORS AND COMFORT MODELS TO OCCUPANTS
Future studies could aim to match environmental measurements more precisely with
the actual position and activity of occupants, rather than relying solely on nearby
or average sensor values. By capturing conditions at the exact point of occupancy,
analyses can better reﬂect true exposure. Moreover, integrating dynamic comfort
models which account for factors such as activity level, clothing, and acclimatisation
may oﬀer an even better and more realistic framework for evaluating comfort under
real-world variation.
ADJUSTING TEMPERATURE DEPENDING ON USERS
Another promising direction for future research involves using questionnaire data to
identify the optimal temperature setpoints for each auditorium. By analysing the
relationship between measured temperatures and reported thermal satisfaction, it
may be possible to determine temperature adjustments that would maximise occu-
pant satisfaction. Such a study could develop data-driven comfort proﬁles for each
space and suggest tailored temperature control strategies that better reﬂect actual
occupant preferences rather than relying solely on standardised comfort categories.
This approach could support adaptive indoor climate regulation and contribute to

FUTURE STUDIES
IDA ICE MODELLING OF FUTURE CLIMATE
Given the ﬁndings of this study, where indoor temperatures in several auditoriums
were measured to be at the higher end of Category II and Category III future re-
search should explore how these conditions may evolve under a changing climate.
One promising direction is to develop dynamic building simulations, for example
using IDA ICE, to model the thermal performance of university auditoriums un-
der future weather scenarios. By incorporating projected climate data, e.g., based
on IPCC emission pathways or regional weather ﬁles for Denmark, such simulations
could evaluate whether current ventilation and thermal control strategies remain ad-
equate. This would provide valuable insights into the risk of future overheating and
support long-term adaptation planning for educational buildings. In particular, it
could inform decisions about ventilation capacity, shading systems, and the need for
passive or active cooling solutions. Considering that elevated indoor temperatures
already emerged as a concern in this study, modelling future conditions may reveal
that thermal discomfort will become increasingly prevalent, making it essential to
proactively assess and improve the resilience of these indoor environments.
KEEPING CATEGORY I
It could also be of interest to conduct a targeted case study in which one audi-
torium is maintained strictly within Category I limits across all IEQ parameters,
thermal comfort, IAQ, acoustics, and lighting as deﬁned by relevant standards such
as DS/EN 16798-1. Such an experiment would allow for a controlled investigation
of how occupants perceive and evaluate optimal indoor conditions in practice. It
would also oﬀer insight into whether achieving full compliance with Category I leads
to a signiﬁcantly higher acceptance rate, or whether other factors, such as individual
variability or contextual inﬂuences, continue to aﬀect subjective comfort.
Future studies may beneﬁt from pilot-testing translated questionnaires or including
brief deﬁnitions alongside technical terms to improve clarity and consistency across
linguistic and cultural contexts.
This would help minimise misinterpretation of
key conceptssuch as thermal comfort, IAQ, or SPL disturbanceand enhance the
reliability of subjective responses, particularly in multilingual study settings.

indoor environment rather than outdoor conditions. A relevant future addition to
the questionnaire could be a prompt such as: Would you have worn diﬀerent clothing
if you didnt have to be in this room? This would help identify whether students dress
for the speciﬁc thermal conditions of the auditorium, rather than for the outdoor
climate. Such insights could reveal adaptive behavioral strategies and contribute to

Appendix A. Appendix
A.1
AIRTHINGS SENSOR PROPERTIES AND UN-
CERTAINTIES
The AirThings sensor weighs 360g and has physical dimensions of 17×9×3.3cm.
Measurement intervals are as follows: temperature, humidity, pressure, VOC, and
CO2 are recorded every 5 minutes; PM is measured every 10 or 60 minutes, depend-
ing on the conﬁguration; and radon is measured at 60-minute intervals [61].
• Temperature, Humidity, and Pressure are measured using solid-state sensors
with an accuracy of ±0.5°C, ±3% RH, and ±0.6hPa, respectively. [61]
• Radon is measured using a passive diﬀusion chamber combined with alpha
spectrometry. The measurement range is 0–500pCi/L. The reported accuracy
is ±10% for a 7-day average and ±5% for a 2-month average [61].
• PM is measured using a laser-based scattering method, capable of detecting
particles ranging from 300 nm to 10 µm in diameter. The sensor is calibrated
against a Grimm reference instrument using cigarette smoke as a particle
source. The PM2.5 measurement range is 0–1000µg/m³, with an accuracy
of ±5µg/m³ ± 15% below 150 µg/m³ and ±5µg/m³ ±20% above 150 µg/m³
[61].
• VOCs levels are measured using a metal oxide gas sensor with a measurement
range of 0–10, 000 ppb. The sensor features a self-calibrating algorithm that
establishes its baseline from the cleanest air encountered. A typical settling
time of approximately 7 days is required after activation [61].
• CO2 concentration is measured using a non-dispersive infrared (NDIR) sensor
with a range of 400–5000 ppm. The speciﬁed accuracy is ±50ppm ±5% within
the range of 500–2000 ppm, under environmental conditions of 10–35°C and
0–85% RH. The sensor applies an automatic baseline calibration algorithm
that updates weekly [61].
