Abstract
Directional room impulse responses (RIRs) are able to provide important information in terms of the direction
of their components (i.e. direct sound and reﬂections), when those having been captured by a microphone
array. In that way, a room impulse response measurement software (i.e DIRMER) has been constructed for
capturing the room impulse responses (i.e A-Format), using a tetrahedral microphone array (i.e. CoreSound
TetraMic) and transforming them in the Ambisonics domain, and more speciﬁcally, in the lower order format
called B-Format, in order to extract the directional room impulse response. Hence, by using energetic method,
such as energetic analysis method (EAM), the spatial components of the directional room impulse responses can
be estimated based on intensity vectors of the B-Format signals. In addition, statistical-probabilistic estimation
methods, such as the Direction-of-Arrival (DOA) estimation methods, have been introduced to investigate the
localization of “multiple sources” (i.e room reﬂections), setting strong assumptions and limitations which are
related to the microphone array geometry and the way of estimation approaches (i.e. classical beamforming,
classical sub-space and Bayesian methods).
Finally, for reducing the complexity only the ﬁrst-order room
reﬂections have been taken into consideration, investigating the directions of their arrivals using simulation and
real measurement approaches.
Key words: Room Impulse Response, Ambisonics, B-Format, Energetic-Analysis-Method, Direction-of-Arrivals

1
Introduction
In the classic Room Acoustics, the characterization of an enclosed space is based on the one-dimensional room
impulse response (RIR) measurements. For many years, the room acoustics impulse response measurements
have been mainly focused on the development of the one-dimensional measurements and as a consequence to
one-dimensional acoustical indexes. In that way, many queries have been responded, regarding the criteria and
properties that enclosed spaces need to fulﬁll, with respect to the purpose (i.e. speech or music), setting a strong
basis of knowledge. However, an important information was not able to be taken into account. This information
is associated with the direction of RIR components. In terms of the Room Acoustics, this information is related
to the direction of the direct sound and the directions from which the early and late reﬂections come to the
receiver. This parameter has been tried to be investigated by using the binaural impulse response measurements
and acoustical indexes, such as lateral fraction (LF) index, but due to the very low number of microphones
(i.e. two), directional information could not be investigated in proper and eﬃcient way. Hence, the directional
information can be characterized as crucial due to the fact that the early reﬂections amplify the direct sound
and the late reﬂections indicate the "timbre" of the enclosed space. As a result, the knowledge of the direction
of the reﬂections could expand the concept of the room acoustics, proposing more accurate solutions.
For increasing the spatial information, a microphone array can be implemented, providing more accurate res-
olution analysis in terms of the locations of the sound sources or the directions of the reﬂections. The term
microphone array is related to a microphone, in which a number of capsules is attached in a linear, circular or
other form. In Room Acoustics, the capsules of the microphone arrays are attached on the edges of a rectangular
or on the surface of the sphere. These types of arrays are referred as Format microphones, where their types
are associated with the Ambisonics (i.e. Spherical Harmonics) orders. In short, the term Ambisonics is referred
to the recording of natural sounds using Format microphone arrays and their reproduction via a number of
loudspeaker, which should be equal to the number of capsules in the microphone array [21].
The usage of Ambisonics concept provides the opportunity of capturing directional impulse response mea-
surements. By having captured a three-dimensional impulse response, using a tetrahedral microphone array,
diﬀerent methods can be used in order to estimate the direction of the early and late reﬂections in a proper way.
More speciﬁcally, the concept of the estimation of the direction of the reﬂections or the localization of the sound
sources can be investigated using the energetic analysis methods. This kind of method is based on the intensity
vectors, calculated based on the B-Format signals, considering that the ﬁrst B-Format signal as the pressure
signal and the other three signals as the particle velocity components in three directions. Another approach for
the estimation of the directions of the reﬂections is related the Direction-of-Arrival (DOA) concept. This kind
of concept explores the direction of under investigation sources or reﬂections in terms of statistical-probabilistic
techniques, with respect to a linear data model. This kind of methods could be implemented either to the
Cartesian domain or to the Spherical Harmonics (i.e. Ambisonics) domain. This decision is based on the type
of the microphone array. For example, the tetrahedral microphone array could be characterized either on the
Cartesian domain or in the Spherical Harmonics domain, considering it as spherical array.
In general, the estimation of the directions of the reﬂections from a room impulse response can be characterized
as a "diﬃcult" and "tricky" task. The term "diﬃcult" is referred to the fact that theoretical methods should be
implemented in a proper and realistic way, whereas the term "tricky" sets the fact that the extracted reﬂections
should be a reﬂection product and not a scattering product, by an object, a transmitted product through the
microphone array structure or just noise.

==PAGE:18==
In modern Room Acoustics, the concept of the spatial analysis, using directional impulse response measure-
ments, could be characterized as an invention, giving the opportunity of visualizing and estimating the directions
of RIR components, which can be used as input to further estimation tasks. In that way, it can be concluded
that the direction of the RIR components should be estimated in an eﬃcient and eﬀective way, using either
statistical-probabilistic methods or energetic method, corresponding to the main objective.
As the task of the estimation of the directions of the reﬂections is characterized as the "cornerstone" task, fur-
ther tasks1 can be implemented and developed. Firstly, the properties of the reﬂected and absorbed surfaces in
a enclosed space could be revealed with respect to the direction of the reﬂection. In other words, the properties
of the boundaries could be estimated as the percentages of the energies, which are reduced, when the sound
is transmitted into the space, reﬂected or absorbed by the boundaries. In addition to this, the problematic
surfaces are able to be investigated, posing better and more eﬃcient way on controlling those surfaces in com-
parison with conventional methods. Finally, a geometrical estimation of the under investigation space could be
achieved, by using the early reﬂections for the estimation of the main dimensions and the late reﬂections for
the estimation of the volume of the space. Similar to the classic Room Acoustics, the modern Room Acoustics
is based on the amount of the measurements in the enclosed space, which means that the higher the amount of
the measurements is, the higher the accuracy will be.
In term of this master thesis, this work mainly focuses on the spatial analysis using directional impulse response
measurements. In other words, a software, called DIRMER, has been constructed in order to capture the di-
rectional RIR, using a B-Format microphone array. The spatial analysis, which is related to the estimation of
the direction of the reﬂections, has been mainly occurred on the ﬁrst-order reﬂections of a room in order to
simplify the complexity and focus only on the estimation performance of the spatial analysis estimation meth-
ods. For that reason, the simulation of a simple rectangular room has been occurred, taking into consideration
only the direct and the ﬁrst-order reﬂection signals, using the image source method. In that simple case, the
energetic analysis method has been implemented, transforming earlier the RIR in a directional B-Format RIR.
In addition, by assuming that the reﬂections follow a Gaussian distribution in the frequency domain, diﬀerent
direction-of-arrival (DOA) estimation methods have been implemented and compared with. To be more speciﬁc,
a classical beamforming method (i.e. minimum variance distortionless response (MVDR)), two classical high-
resolution sub-space based methods (i.e. maximum likelihood (ML) and multiple signal classiﬁcation (MUSIC)
method) and a state-of-art method (i.e Bayesian maximum a posterior (MAP) method) have been used.
Although the beamforming and subspace spectral based methods are used in the room acoustics for the estima-
tion of the direction of the reﬂections from room impulse response, the acoustic reﬂections can be characterized
as highly correlated or coherent, imposing limitations on the usage of these spectral based methods [7]. In other
words, the characterization of these signals as coherent, corresponding to not full rank covariance matrices and
as a consequence to the fail of the subspace methods [73]. In order to mitigate these limitations, smoothing
methods can be used, such as the backward/forward smoothing techniques [70] [71], which can be employed
either on frequency domain or in time domain [7]. These smoothing algorithms allow the identiﬁcation of the
coherent signals, which use eigendecomposition approach. Although that the smoothing algorithms ensure the
identiﬁcation of the coherent signals, the holding of a high number of sensors and a regular structure of array
geometry is still required [72], indicating the importance of the number of the microphone capsules. The higher
the number of the capsules is, the higher the analysis is.

Since the classical methods impose some important limitations, the state-of-art methods, such as Bayesian
methods exhibit many advantages.
More speciﬁcally, these methods are based on the improvement of the
robustness to noise, the low number of snapshots as well as the correlation of the signals [54]. The sparse
Bayesian learning method could be characterized as the most popular and improved method for sparse signal
reconstruction. In this kind of methods family, none smoothing technique is required to be implemented due
to the fact the smoothing algorithms eliminate the sparse behaviour. The sparseness is expressed based on the
sparse priori information for the signal of interest [54]. Since, a sparse behaviour can be seen in the direct and
early part of the room impulse response, the implementation of the SBL approaches could be characterized as
vital. In addition to this, the direct and the early reﬂections are based on the fact that their distributions follow
a Gaussian distribution, which is not absolutely correct [7]. The latter assumption sets the fact that the Fourier
transform of Gaussian reﬂection distribution, in the time domain, is a Gaussian distribution, in the frequency
domain [74]. The main advantage of the Gaussian distribution assumption corresponds to the fact that the
product of two Gaussian distributions is a Gaussian distribution, setting an important parameter in terms of
the form of the posterior distribution, when Bayesian methods are used [51].
In contrast to the strong assumptions, which have been posed by the statistical-probabilistic DOA methods,
the energetic analysis method sets only one limitation. This limitation focuses on the amount of the reﬂections,

Fourier transformed is used, only one strong reﬂection is included into each ﬁlter of the constructed ﬁlter-bank
[33].

Theoretical Background
In this section the main theory is presented in order to become as smooth as possible the connection among the
diﬀerent topics as well as the strategy followed for carrying out this project.
2.1
Room Impulse Response (RIR)
In general, the acoustic properties of an enclosed space could be revealed using the acoustic response. This
response is captured by a microphone or a microphone array when an acoustic signal, such as an exponential
sweep tone signal, excites the under investigation space. This acoustic response is usually referred as room
impulse response (RIR). In other words, the RIR indicates the propagation of diﬀerent reﬂections as a function
of time. In the ﬁgures below the propagated paths of the excitation signal, in the form of rays, arriving at the
microphone is presented as well as the components of the captured room impulse response.
Figure 1:
The left sub-ﬁgure indicates the propagated paths of the emitted sound signal,
impinging on the measured microphone. The red arrow corresponds to the direct sound, the
blue arrow the early reﬂections and the green arrow indicates the late reﬂections or the higher
order reﬂections. The Right sub-ﬁgure shows the anatomy of an RIR. Taken from [1].
As it can be observed from the right sub-ﬁgure of the Figure 1 the amplitude and the phase of traveling
sound waves change over time. Diﬀerent factors are responsible for those changes such as the properties of the
boundaries (i.e reﬂection, absorption and diﬀraction), the medium (i.e air-absorption) and the distance among
the sound source and the receivers. All these factors aﬀect diﬀerently each frequency component of the sound
waves [7]. By assuming point sound source, the spherical spreading of the sound waves aﬀects the amplitude,
contributing on its reduction as a function of the distance [7].
The characterization of an enclosed space is related strongly with its usage. In terms of acoustics, the amount
of early reﬂections with respect to the time range is the main factor. More speciﬁcally, when an enclosed space
is going to be used for speech, the early reﬂections, which should be taken into account, are between the 5ms
and 50ms [2]. The reﬂections over this period are responsible for the ampliﬁcation of the direct signal, which
is located in between the 0ms and 5ms [2]. Respectively, when a enclosed space is used for music the early
reﬂection time intervals are between 5ms and 80ms [2]. In addition, the time intervals of the early reﬂections
are independent of the size of the enclosed space and depend strongly on the purpose of the under investigation
enclosed space. In both cases, the reverberation ﬁeld is located in between the values (50ms,+∞) in the case of
speech usage and (80ms,+∞) in the case of music usage. The late reﬂections are associated with the "tymber"
of the room, indicating that the larger the room is, the longer the "tail" will be. In general, the larger the
enclosed space is, the higher the amount of the early and late reﬂections in the respective time intervals will be.

2.4
Direction-of-Arrival Estimation (DOA)
In general, the Direction-of-Arrival (DOA) estimation is considered as one of the most important topics in the
digital signal processing, making it widely usable in more and more ﬁelds such as antennas, sonars, radars,
acoustics and many others [30]. The estimation of the direction of the reﬂections could be achieved either in
terms of the recorded sound ﬁelds and their transformation in B-Format, corresponding to an analysis in terms
of the intensity vectors (i.e. energetic analysis), or by using statistical-probabilistic estimation methods. The
most classical approaches are related to the weighting factor, such as the conventional [41] and MVDR [41]
method. By using subspace methods such as the multiple signal classiﬁcation (MUSIC) approach, invented by
R.O Schmidt in 1986 [31], and estimating signal parameter, using rotational in-variance techniques (ESPRIT)
approach, invented by R.H. Roy in 1987 [32] the estimation performance can be increased. However, the classical
methods have started giving their positions to modern machine learning techniques, using a Bayesian inference.
In the acoustic ﬁeld, the spatial acoustic analysis and synthesis are based on the direction-of-arrival, when
a acoustic wave impinging on a microphone array. More speciﬁcally, in room acoustics, the detection of the
reﬂections is based on the knowledge of the number of the reﬂections Q as a priori information [7]. In that
way, by estimating the number and the direction of the reﬂections, a various number of following tasks can be
explored such as the geometry estimation of the room could be refereed as an important example. As it has
already been mentioned in the previous section, the type of the microphone array as well as the number of the
capsules on the array play a vital role on the estimation task. In this section the energetic analysis method and
a number of DOA methods are presented and analyzed.
2.4.1
Energetic Analysis Method (EAM)
The energetic analysis method could be characterized as the most "direct" method in comparison with the
statistical-probabilistic DOA methods, presented later in this subsection. This method is based on the main
assumption that only one strong peak, corresponding to a strong reﬂection, is contained into each ﬁlter of
the used ﬁlter-bank. For increasing the possibilities of detecting the strong reﬂections from a B-Format room
impulse response an overlap ﬁlter-bank approach is used in the short-time Fourier transform.
In terms of
its implementation, this method is based on the instantaneous intensity vectors, due to the fact that the W
B-Format signal corresponds to the pressure and the other three B-Format signals are related to the three
components of the particle velocity vectors, from which the azimuth and the elevation angles can be estimated.
In the case of the real measurements or simulation approaches, using real excitation signals, the "statistical
calculation for angles for each time frame" step can be neglected. In the ﬁgure below the block diagram of the

Subspace-Based Methods
The subspace-based methods are based on the measured covariance matrix Rpp, which is characterized by the
expression (34). As it can be seen from the expression (34), the space can be partitioned into two orthogonal
spaces [41]; the signal subspace and the noise subspace.
In addition, each steering vector corresponds to
signal subspace. In terms of the noise subspace, it is spanned by the eigenvectors [41]. More speciﬁcally, the
eigenvectors are related to the smaller eigenvalues of the correlation matrix, sorting of their values in a descend
order [41]. However, the larger eigenvalues correspond to the signal subspace spanned by the eigenvectors [41].
2.4.3.1

The multiple signal classiﬁcation method is one of the most popular method of super-resolution direction ﬁnding
[41]. The MUSIC method provides asymptotically unbiased estimations of a number of tasks such as, the number
of signals (i.e sources or reﬂections), the direction of arrival (DOA), the strength and cross-correlation among
the directional wave-forms, the polarizations and the strength of noise or interference [31]. In other words, the
MUSIC method is characterized by a high performance in terms of the direction ﬁnding, having a resolution
less than ﬁve degree [35].

Maximum Likelihood (ML) Methods
The maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) is an asymptotically eﬃcient estimator, which is used for large
data records [37]. In terms of its deﬁnition, a MLE attempts to ﬁnd the values of the desired parameter that
maximizes the likelihood function [37]. In other words, the purpose of the maximum likelihood estimation is
the choice of the probability distribution, under which the observed values have estimated with the highest
probability [40].
The MLE methods are considered as the most computational intensive methods, making them less popular
[41]. However, the main advantage of this family of estimation methods is the low percentages of signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR) [41]. Here, the maximum likelihood method is based on the subspace approach, in which the power
spectrum is calculated based on the logarithmic likelihood function, under the assumption that the impingement
signal, in the data model, is a zero-mean multivariate Gaussian distribution.

2.4.4
Sparse Bayesian Maximum-a-Posterior (MAP) Learning Method
The Bayesian or Bayes estimation methods (BEMs) can be characterized as the most state-of-art methods11.
The MAP estimation is based on Bayesian criterion for achieving a high resolution of DOA estimation [49].
Two of the most famous MAP methods are the joint a posteriori (JAP) and the marginal a posteriori (MaAP)
estimation [48]. The former describes the posteriori likelihood as the multiplication of the likelihood and the
prior distributions whereas the latter one is described as the integration of JAP over "nuisance" parameters [48].
Here, a MAP method is presented which adapts the generalized Gaussian distribution to the prior information
for its adjustability [49]. In addition, in this MAP method it is mainly considered a sparse signal recovery
problem.
As it is known, the amount of applied data poses a limitation on the machine learning algorithms.
More
speciﬁcally, usually large amounts of domain-speciﬁc knowledge are needed to be projected into a smaller
meaningful space in order to be processed in a proper and eﬃcient way [52]. Sparse learning algorithms are
able to provide the way of working in high-dimensional spaces, selecting smaller sub-spaces [52].
The SBL method exploits the sparsity information, with a sparse prior assumption for the under of interest
signal, making one of the most popular approaches due to its excellent performance [53]. The success of this
performance is based on the main assumption that the true DOAs are practically very close to the sampling
grid points [53]. As the estimated DOAs are very close to true ones, and not equal to true ones, the gaps among
the true DOAs and their nearest grid point are usually referred as the oﬀ-grid gaps [53].

3.1
Directional Impulse Response MEasuRement (DIRMER) Software
The DIRMER software captures the room impulse responses, using a tetrahedral microphone array, and trans-
form them in the lower order Ambisonics (i.e. B-Format) signals. As it is known, the measurements of the
room impulse responses are based on the excitation signal. In the DIRMER software, an exponential sweep
signal17 has been constructed for the excitation of the measured enclosed space. This choice is based on the
fact that Maximum Length Sequence, MLS18, excitation signal is very sensitive during the measurements and
cannot deal with non-linear systems according to Farina [62]. In contrast to the MLS, the exponential signal
has the main advantage of dealing with and refuse non-linear harmonic distortion products. This kind of dis-
tortion is mainly produced from the measurement chain and more speciﬁcally from the loudspeakers [62]. In
this way, the linear part of the room impulse response could be possible to be deconvolved and remained linear.
Another advantage of the exponential sweep signal is its energy distribution per octave, which remains stable,
corresponding to a pink-noise shape. This means that the long term power spectrum of the exponential sweep
tone decreases by 3dB per octave. In other words, this reduction shows that the energy distribution is higher
in the low frequencies bands than in the high frequencies band (i.e. Figure 15b). The frequency range of the
excitation signal can be chosen by the user. In the default case the frequency range is from 20Hz to 20000Hz.
In the simulations and the real measurements, the default range has been been used.
During the signal construction, a transient behaviour has been presented. This behaviour has been presented in
beginning and at the end of the exponential sweep signal, being responsible for the presence of some artifacts.
These artifacts are related to the leakage in the frequency domain. In the time domain, the leakage is expressed
in terms of the discontinuities (i.e. sudden start and end) in the excitation signal. For eliminating phenomena
such this, the excitation signal should be smoothed in a proper and eﬃcient way, without biasing the initial sig-
nal. For that reason, the implementation of a half cosine window could be characterized as an excellent choice.
In terms of its implementation, the window is divided into two parts; the front side, corresponding to [−π

The exponential sweep signal is included into the non-white excitation signals family. For that reason, the room
impulse response is extracted in the time domain, by convolving the excitation signal with its inverse ﬁlter.
Since, the deconvolution is occurred in the time domain, the deconvolution ﬁlter should be constructed into the
frequency domain, for having been yielded exact the same results. The same procedure can be conducted in
the frequency domain, by multiplying the frequency response of inverse deconvolution ﬁlter with the transfer
function of the excitation signal. The usage of the inverse Fourier transform indicates the impulse response in
the time domain. The eﬃciency of this method is based on the following three criteria [60].
1. The signals should be band limited to the Nyquist frequency.
2. The signal parts with a length of 2N samples can only be processed.
3. The spectrum of the signal becomes discrete and includes the frequencies given by n · fs/2N, where N
represents the order of the FFT and n = 0, 1, 2, 3, ..., 2(N/2), when the signal parts are repeated periodically.
In terms of the deconvolution procedure, presented in the Figure 16, the used excitation signal is transformed
from the time domain to the frequency domain using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), extracting the transfer
function of the excitation signal, which is needed to be inversed. For the extraction of the impulse response,
the sizes of the excitation and recorded signal are needed to be extracted so that the matrix of the inverted
excitation signal to be repeated. This step can be characterized as crucial due to the fact that it ensures the
holding of the multiplication process in the frequency domain. In that way, the room impulse response can be
extracted by multiplying the FFT of the repeated matrix of the excitation signal with its FFT. The inverse
Fourier transform of the latter product indicates the room impulse response (i.e.
A-Format IRs), which is
purely19 real.

3.2
First-Order Room Reﬂections Simulation
As it has already been mentioned, this master thesis focuses only on the eﬃcient estimation of the direction of
the ﬁrst-order reﬂections in a rectangular enclosed space. This scenario has been simulated in the MATLAB
programming software, using a speciﬁc location for the receiver and the source. By simulating the supposed
rectangular space and the source and receiver positions, the image source method can be directly implemented
in order to calculate the direct signal and ﬁrst-order reﬂections in terms of their amplitudes and their direction
of arrivals.
The image source method (ISM) is based on the geometrical approach, in which each reﬂection is simulated as a
ray with respect to the boundary. For better understanding of the ISM, the following example has been chosen
to be presented, describing the way that the ﬁrst-order reﬂections have been simulated. By assuming that the
ﬁrst-order reﬂection, coming from the front wall, needs to be calculated, the distance between the source and
the front wall should be doubled, out of the under interest boundary. In that doubled distance, an image or
projected, source is constructed. Then, the closer distance between the image source and the receiver is related
to the total distance that this reﬂection needs to travel. The point, which the "image" ray impinges in the front
wall, corresponds to the point of the surface of which the ray from the true source is reﬂected as well, before
reaching to the receiver. As it is very logical, this point is related to the top of an isosceles triangle, indicating
the length of the closer distance between the image source and the receiver, which is equal to the length of the
full reﬂection (i.e. from the true source to the front wall and from the front wall to the receiver). In addition
to this, the angle of the reﬂection can be calculated based on the arc-tangent ratio of the opposite and adjacent
side of the respective constructed rectangular triangle. This procedure has been used in all the surfaces in order
to extract the ﬁrst reﬂections and their angles of arrival.
By having been calculated the distances that the ﬁrst-reﬂections need to cover, their traveling times can be
calculated based on the distances, such,

3.3
Direction-of-Arrival Estimation Methods - Simulations
Diﬀerent DOA estimation methods have been widely used in the digital signal and array processing ﬁeld,
involving them into a very general family of estimation methods. The separation of these methods is based
on their estimation performance. More speciﬁcally, in the Energetic Analysis Method, the estimation of the
direction of the reﬂections is based on the B-Format signals, from which their intensity vectors can be calculated.
In contrast to the EA method, the DOA methods are probabilistic-statistical approaches, which their estimations
are based on classical (i.e. beamfoarming and sub-spaces methods) or state-of-art techniques (i.e. Bayesian
methods). Due to the diﬀerent approaches between EA and DOA methods, a set of simulation procedures has
been conducted in order to investigate diﬀerent parameters, which are associated with their performance and
their accuracy, with respect to a widespread analysis.

in terms of its implementation. Although the implementation has been occurred in a logical way diﬀerent kinds
of “problems” have been presented, indicating the “tricky” and the “diﬃcult” parts of this estimation method.
More speciﬁcally, the diﬀerent stages indicate the importance and the need of optimization controls in each
quadrants. In this way, it is ensured that all the reﬂections, coming from a speciﬁc quadrant, can be estimated
in a correct and precise way. In addition, the presence of the new components should be neglected in the
beginning of the estimation performance in order to reduce and avoid their inﬂuences into the data, which
are needed to be estimated. All these reasons are responsible for the main assumption which is used in the
estimation method, relating to the presence of only strong signal in each ﬁlter of the ﬁlter-bank.
As it is
very logical, the shorter the length of the windows is, the better and more precise the estimation will be. By
increasing the length of the window, an under-estimation of the direction of the reﬂection can be occurred,
indicating higher in-percentage deviations as the the length of the windows become wider and wider.

on the estimation of the direction of the reﬂection.
This is related to the fact that the estimation of the
reﬂections in complex sound-ﬁelds may cannot be achieved in a proper and eﬃcient way, due to the fact that a
very high number of reﬂections impinge simultaneously on the low number of censors in the microphone array.
Furthermore, the low order Ambisonics sound ﬁeld could be possible to extract diﬀraction or scatter products
as reﬂection products especially in the case of the higher order of reﬂection and of the diﬀuse sound ﬁeld. By
focusing on the main assumption, corresponding to the existence of only one strong component in each ﬁlter
of the ﬁlter bank, this means that a non-reﬂection product can be considered as reﬂection even though the
diﬀerence with a real signal is negligible. The weakness of this method in the estimation of more than one
reﬂections in a single window indicates the usage of very narrow windows in the ﬁlter-bank. However, this
approach should be used in a precise way due to the fact the distribution of the IR components varies. In the
case of a non-correct windowing, it is possible to reveal more than one direct signals as well as components
which are not related to reﬂective products. In contrast to the EAM, the direction-of-arrival methods are able
to estimate more than one reﬂections in a single window but in a more complex way.

It is common known that the more detailed the analysis is, the higher the accuracy of the estimation method
will be. Nevertheless, a high analysis is related to the increase of the time, which is needed for the performance
of the estimation methods. As a consequence, the parameters which are related to the eﬃcient estimation of the
direction of the reﬂection are the the number of snapshots (i.e. the amount of the analysis) and the estimation
time. However, no one can deny the fact that the number of sensors are related to the number of reﬂections
that would be estimated. In other words, the higher the number of sensors is, the higher and more detailed
the amount of the estimated reﬂections could be, especially in the case that the SNR reaches high values. The
used estimation methods play a vital role on the number of the estimated reﬂections and the eﬃciency on the
estimations as a function of the SNR as well as the number of snapshots. The estimation of the reﬂections, using
DOA methods in combination with a four capsules microphone array sets a strong limitation on the amount of
the direction of the reﬂections could be estimated.

4.1.4
Further Discussion
The further discussion section analyzes more the results and their discussion. In other words, the expansion of
the analysis corresponds to the DOA simulation results and how these results could be expanded in order to
approach the task which has been set.
To begin with, as it has already been investigated, at least all the used DOA methods are capable to estimate
the direction of two reﬂections at most, when a tetrahedral microphone array is used, having diﬀerent estimation
performance. This sets a strong limitation on the analysis in order to estimate a speciﬁc number of directions
of reﬂections, without distorting the estimation results. Hence, when the direct and the ﬁrst-order reﬂections of
the simulated rectangular room are needed to be estimated, the reﬂections should be separated using a window
function. This function could be a ﬁlter-bank, an adaptive window or an isolated window approach.
By using a ﬁlter-bank approach, for estimating the direction of the simulated signals, the estimation times of
diﬀerent DOA methods, presented in the Table 10, are quadrupled. In terms of the signals into the windows,
the implementation of the ﬁlter-bank cannot guarantee a speciﬁc number of signals into each ﬁlter, making
diﬃcult the usage of the used methods, when the number of signals in each ﬁlter is higher than two. The
adaptive window approach is also capable to reduce the estimation time in comparison to the former technique.
In addition, this approach could ensure the presence of two strong reﬂections at most into the window functions.
Another way for reducing more the estimation time and minimizing the estimation error could be the windowing
of each reﬂection separately. In the latter two approaches, the noise presence could be reduced as well as the
unwanted components, focusing only on the reﬂections area. In contrast to the ﬁlter-bank approach, in which
unwanted components can be estimated as reﬂection products.
In general, when more than one reﬂections and less than three reﬂections are included into the analysis window,
the direction-of-arrival methods can be used for their estimation. This performance can be achieved even in the
case that the SNR is equal to -20dB SNR. This advantage is coming into collision with the strong assumption
of the energetic analysis method, indicating only one reﬂection on the analysis window. In addition, although
EAM is able to estimate the direction of the reﬂection, it is not possible to investigate if the estimated direction
of the wanted signal belongs to reﬂection product or another kind of product. In that case, the directional-of-
arrival methods could be characterized as the prevail choice in comparison with the energetic analysis method.

The graphical illustration of the diﬀerent approaches gives the opportunity to analyze the pros and cons of each
approach,separately. Firstly, by using a ﬁlter bank approach, at least one or two reﬂections should be included
into each ﬁlter. This technique does not ensure that at most two reﬂections are included, as this can be seen
in the ﬁlter where the reﬂection from rear and left walls in the Figure 48a. In this ﬁlter a percentage of the
reﬂection from the front wall is included, indicating clearly that the MAP method can be used instead of the
MUSIC, ML and MVDR methods, in which the results will be distorted and deviated. In general, the less the
presence of a third reﬂection is, the lower the distortion on the DOA estimations will be. For that approach a
Hanning ﬁlter-bank has been used, in which the length of each ﬁlter is 780 samples and the beginning of each
next ﬁlter starts from the mean value of the previous ﬁlter.
Instead of using a ﬁlter bank approach, which is able to bias the DOA estimation task, an adaptive approach
can be used. This adaptive method searching each time two reﬂections, which are close to each other, and
then a Hanning ﬁlter is implemented. When the number of the reﬂections corresponds to an odd number, the
reﬂection which is not closer in comparison to the others, will be presented into an Hanning ﬁlter by itself.
In the case, which is presented into the Figure 48b, the overlapping among the ﬁlters is prevented due to the
location of the reﬂection. However, an overlapping could be occurred, biasing the results, as in the case of the
ﬁlter-bank approach. In terms of the estimated time, this approach is faster due to the fact that the number
of the ﬁlters is speciﬁc. For the purposes of this approach, it has been extracted that the length of the ﬁrst
window is 780 samples, the length of the second one 100 samples and the length of the third and forth windows
correspond to 280 samples,respectively.
The best approach for reducing the estimation time and increase the eﬃciency on the DOA estimation methods
is related to the isolation of each reﬂection, as this presented into the Figure 48c. More speciﬁcally, by searching
for local maxima, the time of arrival of each reﬂection could be estimated. When the reﬂections are estimated
in a specif time, an Hanning window of a length of 40 samples can be implemented and then the DOA methods
could be used. The length of the window has been chosen in order to be as close as possible to the reﬂections
in order to improve the tasks under consideration.
All the sub-ﬁgures which are included into the Figure 48 illustrate the ideal case, where the reﬂections are
included without the presence of the noise. As it is very logical, the presence of noise is responsible for increasing
the diﬃculty of the estimation task, since some reﬂections can be partly hidden or disappeared. In the case
of the adaptive windowing, the distances between the closer reﬂections cannot be investigated in a precise way
because of the turbulence of the noise, indicating some peaks of the noise as reﬂections. In the case of the
ﬁlter-bank approach, empty ﬁlters are able to be investigated as areas with local maxima, corresponding to
noise peaks. As a conclusion, the isolation of each reﬂection in a proper and careful way could be characterized
as the best out of three discussed approaches, when the DOA methods are used.
Finally, it can be extracted that the energetic analysis method is able to provide a direct, eﬃcient and less
time consuming approach, in which the estimated directions, which approach the “true” values in most of the
occasions as this explored by Figure 34. The EAM method is more sensitive in the estimation of the direction
of the arrivals, than DOA methods, which provide eﬃcient estimation even in low SNR values. However, its
usage could be characterized as complex and for that reason the EAM method is preferred to be used. In both
situations, the reﬂections should be observable or to be known, in which time intervals are located. In the case
that the reﬂections are partly hidden, the DOA methods could provide an eﬃcient estimation, than EAM, due
to their optimization control, which is not included into the EAM approach.

4.2
Real Measurement Results
As the energetic analysis method is based on the capturing of the room impulse response and its transformation
into the spherical harmonics domain and more speciﬁcally into ﬁrst order Ambisonics encoding, a captured
impulse response measuring software should be constructed. The usage of other measuring software such as the
DIRAC software has been preferred to be avoided. This decision is based on the fact that the the DIRAC software
presents discontinue behaviours into the beginning and at the end of the measured signal. These discontinuities
are responsible from the presence of leakage in the frequency domain, distorting the energetic analysis method.
In addition, DIRAC is not able to capture four impulse responses simultaneously, making impossible its usage.
By understanding the sensitivity of EAM on the estimation of the direction of the reﬂections, the construction
of a measuring software, avoiding phenomena, which could bias the measurements, can be deemed necessary.

5
Conclusion
The task of estimation of the directions of the ﬁrst-order reﬂections, from a directional room impulse response,
was under investigation, hiding a number of tricky and uncertainty tasks, before reaching to "reliable" results.
In this work, a wide-band analysis has been taken into consideration. However, diﬀerent kinds of estimation
methods such as, energetic analysis methods and direction-of-arrivals estimation methods have been started to
come up with this open quote in room acoustics, throughout the last years.
To begin with, the energetic analysis method, using the intensity vectors approach, is able to estimate accurately
the direction of reﬂections, when these are isolated, reaching results which their deviations from "true" ones
can be characterized as negligible. However, when the smallest pair of reﬂections is considered, the estimation
of the directions of the reﬂections, using the EAM, deviate signiﬁcantly, even in the ideal noiseless conditions
(i.e. anechoic chamber). Although this ﬁnding does not aﬀect the main directions of the reﬂections, in the
four angle ranges, the accuracy of the results in real condition can be set in doubt, especially when those are
wanted to be used for further estimation tasks. In terms of its implementation, the energetic analysis method
is the most ”direct” direction of arrivals estimation method, which is based only on the capturing of the sound
ﬁelds, from a tetrahedral microphone array, and their transformation in the spherical harmonics domain, using
the Ambisonics approach. Moreover, the assumption of the existence of only one strong signal, in each ﬁlter of
the ﬁlter-bank, indicates that some components are related to non-reﬂecting products. In that case, non-strong
signals can be neglected, ensuring that the strong signals will be visible in the plots.
On the other hand, the statistical-probabilistic direction-of-arrivals (DOA) estimation methods could be char-
acterized as the most state-of-art methods in the ﬁeld of the estimation of the reﬂections in room acoustics.
Their estimation performances depend on their methodologies. As it is expected, the Bayesian methodology
approaches better the "true" direction of the reﬂections in comparison to the subspace (i.e. MUSIC and ML)
and classical (i.e. MVDR) methods. By taking into consideration that the room reﬂections are coherent and
highly correlated, the spectral based methods impose limitations. In addition, by focusing on the performance
of the DOA methods, in terms of the estimation eﬃciency, the calculation time, the maximum number of es-
timations and the robustness, with respect to the three diﬀerent snapshot cases and the signal-to-noise ratio,
it is extracted that the Bayes method is able to provide high and robust estimation performance in most SNR
values, having been computationally heavy. In contrast to the energetic analysis method, the DOA methods are
able to estimate more than one reﬂection in their analysis window. In addition to this, most of the used DOA
methods are able to estimate more than one signals, when the signal-to-noise ratio is very low. The extracted
results have been based on the main assumptions that the direction of estimated signals (i.e. reﬂections) follow
a complex Gaussian distribution and they are located into the far-ﬁeld.
In terms of the measuring system, it has been found out that the DIRMER software works in a proper way,
comparing it with the IRIS software. Most of the DIRMER deviations could be characterized as negligible,
whereas the highest deviations are related to the diﬀerent excitation level of the used room for the validation
of the measurements. By validating the results, the DIRMER and IRIS software have been used for the esti-
mation of the direction of some ﬁrst-order reﬂections, showing similarities in terms of their performance. In
some cases, the IRIS works better than the DIRMER, whereas, in the other cases, the DIRMER software shows
better estimation performance. Although these observations, it cannot be taken a ﬁnal decision about the most
eﬃcient estimator due to the fact that their deviations could be characterized as negligible.
89
==PAGE:106==
Last but not least, by understanding and ﬁguring out the limitations of each estimation method, their estima-
tion performance could be improved. In order this to be achieved, the main assumptions should be reduced,
making the models more realistic. In addition, by introducing optimization controls, which are related to the
distribution of the reﬂections, and the number of the reﬂections, which should be included in the analysis win-
dow, the eﬀectiveness of method could be increased. However, the biggest queries related to existence of the
real reﬂections, and not scattering or diﬀracting products, in speciﬁc (i.e. pre-calculated) time instants, relating
to the most vitals parameters, should be investigated due to the fact that these parameters are regarded as
known a lot of times. This task could be said, it is accomplished by the DOA methods, rather than EAM.
Finally, it is important to be noticed that the current energetic analysis methodology has imported an interesting
and important parameter, which is related to the estimation and visualization of the direction of the reﬂections
or the signals in an enclosure space or free-ﬁeld, when an low order microphone array is used. This could be
characterized as an "innovation", by using directly the B-Format signals. However, this sets limitations which
can be minimized by using statistical-probabilistic methods. In this way, more accurate and reliable results
could be achieved. The energetic analysis performance could be increased, when they are used in combination
with optimization controls.

Future Work
By knowing the boundaries of this work, it is important to consider and set some future tasks. In that way, the
task of the estimation of the direction of the reﬂections could be developed in an eﬃcient and a rational way of
thinking. To be more speciﬁc, ﬁve main tasks are required to be investigated, regardless the number of capsules
in a microphone array, since this number is related to the highest number of direction of the reﬂections that
could be estimated.
In the First Task, a transformation of the data model and the diﬀerent DOA methods in the Spherical Harmonics
domain is considered as one of the most important tasks. In that way, the pros and cons of the transformation
from the Cartesian to the Spherical Harmonics domain can be revealed and investigated. This task could be
characterized as crucial especially in the case that the capsules of a tetrahedral microphone array are not located
on the edges of a rectangular but on the surface of a sphere. As it is very logical, this kind of transform is
important when spherical microphone arrays are used. In that way the DOA methods in the Spherical Harmonics
domain can be expressed. In addition, an experimental approach should be conducted in order to observe how
these methods could be implemented and extract meaningful results. For that reason, the references [7], [48],
[42] and [79] can be used for the investigation of the ﬁrst task.
The Second Task is related to the neglect of the ﬁlter-bank approach in the EAM, introducing diﬀerent types of
optimization controls such, these which have been discussed in the paragraph 4.1.4. In that way, it is possible to
achieve a better estimation of the direction of the reﬂective arrivals, trying to avoid artifacts, which are related
to the diﬀraction and scattering products. Since EAM can be considered as a "tricky" method, estimating in a
good approach the direction of the isolated reﬂections, the introduction of diﬀerent ﬁltering methods, than the
ﬁlter-bank method, could increase its estimation performance in complex scenarios.
In the Third Task, the detailed and analytical investigation of the statistical-probabilistic methods in real
measurements should be carried out. In that way, the eﬃciency of the diﬀerent methods could be revealed, pre-
processing and localizing the reﬂection products using the ideas of the paragraph 4.1.4.. Then, the distribution
of each reﬂection should be optimized so that the Bayesian methods to be implemented.
In addition, the
measurement of the under search steering vector in the median and vertical plane should be achieved. An
illustration of the measured steering vector is presented in the Figure below.

In that way, the estimation of the direction of the reﬂection could be investigated in terms of the ways the
DOA and EAM can be applied in the early and late part of the measured room impulse responses, respectively.
In terms of the early reﬂections a sparse approach could be used for the estimation of the direction of the
reﬂections as a sparse behaviour, on that part of reﬂections, is presented. On the other hand, at the late part
of RIR, an EAM can be used due to the fact that a ”stable” approach, without having a detailed estimation
performance, is required. This estimation methodology could be characterized as a hybrid model, where the
direction of the arrivals of a number of early reﬂections could be estimated in a high eﬃcient way in contrast
to the late part, in which a not so eﬃcient method should be used. This topic includes all the previous tasks,
indicating the need of the construction of a software, which will be possible to predict the components from

Bayesian Theory
In classical statistics the parameters have been characterized by ﬁxed quantities. These parameters are unknown
and as a consequence they can be estimated using a sampling distribution (SD). The sampling distribution is
usually constructed by a number of samples, by which each set of selected samples is responsible for diﬀerent
estimation. As a result, although the SD demonstrates and quantiﬁes the uncertainties of the estimates, the
parameters are considered as ﬁxed.
In the Bayesian statistics the parameters are treated as random variable, which are able to be described by a
probability density distribution. In contrast to classical statistics, in the Bayesian statistics the distribution of
the probability of the parameters is not mandatory to follow the data, which means that the distribution of
the parameter could be a general belief. In other words, the parameter distribution can follow a subjective or
objective distribution.
In a given data set y = (y1, ..., yn), the Bayesian Theorem is deﬁned as,
p(θ|y) = p(y|θ) · p(θ)
p(y)
(150)
where,

The likelihood function of y given θ.
p(θ) :
The prior distribution.
p(y) :
The marginal distribution.
p(θ|y) :
The posterior distribution.
More speciﬁcally, the prior distribution characterizes subjectively or objectively the distribution of the parameter
θ.
A prior distribution can be expressed as subjective when it is factored as a guess, an assumption or a
distribution, which is based in terms of the experience. As it is very expected, the prior distribution plays a
vital role in the posterior distribution due to the fact that the broader the prior distribution is, the broader the
poster distribution will be.
The likelihood function quantiﬁes the likelihood of a parameter value in a given set of data [45]. However, the
probability distribution quantiﬁes the probability of the data for a given value of the parameter. Thus, the
notation is given as,
p(y|θ) = L(θ|y).

[46]. The marginal distribution could be used for checking the assumptions due to the fact that sometimes it
could "estimate" the y [46].
Finally, the posterior distribution quantiﬁes the probability of the given hypothesis which is may be capable
to explain the observed data of the unknown parameter or parameters [47]. In some literature the posterior
probability is referred as inverse probability.

Estimation of the Reverberation Time
When the room impulse responses have been captured by a microphone or a number of microphones, useful
acoustic quantities can be extracted. The most important acoustic quantity is the reverberation time. The
reverberation time is mainly considered as the time which is needed for the excitation signal to decay by 60dB
from the time that the excitation signal stops suddenly [65]. As a decay of a 60dB cannot be achieved, due to
the not existence of that dynamic range, a extrapolation approach is needed to be implemented [65]. In this
approach the energy decay curve (EDC) is needed to be calculated in order to estimate the reverberation time.
The EDC is obtained by a backward integration of the impulse response [67]. By using the EDC, the decay of
the sound energy in an enclosed space can be extracted more precisely [65].
