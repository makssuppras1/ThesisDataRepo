Abstract 
The increasing population raises concerns about how to mitigate climate change and prevent 
resource depletion, which has caused increased interest in the optimal use of resources. Recycling 
of nutrients is a way to use the resources of the Earth more efficiently. Wastewater needs to be 
treated due to the high concentration of phosphor, nitrogen, and organic compounds. The focus of 
biological treatment of wastewater has shifted by including value-added products as a factor in 
wastewater treatment. 
In this study, co-cultivation of hydrogen oxidizing bacteria and algae in the cultivation of Clean-
in-Place brewery wastewater was investigated. The hypothesis was that algae could produce 
oxygen through photosynthesis, which the hydrogen oxidizing bacteria could use for metabolism. 
Three growth conditions were tested; anaerobic, aerobic, and mixotrophic in a batch reactor. 
Chlorella vulgaris and Scenedesmus obliquus were used for algae cultivation and Cupriavidus 
necator as hydrogen oxidizing bacteria. Cultivation of Chlorella vulgaris, Scenedesmus obliquus, 
Cupriavidus necator, and co-cultivation were tested for all growth conditions. 
Algae was able to achieve heterotrophic growth, achieving specific growth rates of 0.551 d-1 and 
0.684 d-1 for Chlorella vulgaris and Scenedesmus obliquus, respectively. Both of the algae growth 
rates were achieved under aerobic conditions. Growth was limited by the low organic substrate in 
the Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater. The algae were unable to achieve photoautotrophic 
growth due to the failure to adapt to the conditions in the Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater. 
Cupriavidus necator was also able to achieve heterotrophic growth but was also limited by the low 
organic substrate. However, the Cupriavidus necator was able to do chemolithotrophic growth 
under mixotrophic conditions. Under mixotrophic conditions Cupriavidus necator produced an 
AFDW of 1990 mg/L, a 69.6% nitrate removal, 23% phosphate removal, and an increase in COD. 
The increase in COD was a result of the decay of Cupriavidus necator. The highest growth in co-
cultivation with Cupriavidus necator and Chlorella vulgaris also under mixotrophic conditions, 
achieving growth of 985 mg/L AFDW, 24.8% nitrate removal, and 29.5% phosphate removal. Co-
cultivation results showed that algae had an inhibitory effect on Cupriavidus necator.  
The results obtained in this report suggest that wastewater treatment with HOB is viable 
wastewater treatment. However, since algae were unable to grow photoautotrophic, they had an 
inhibitory effect on the growth.

1. Introduction 
By 2050 there will be 9.7 billion people on planet Earth, which is an increase of 26% from the 7.7 
billion people there are in 2019 (United Nations 2019). As the human race, we need to have 
resources and capacity for 2 billion more people within the next 30 years. However, the annual 
anthropogenic resource consumption is higher than the planet can provide annually. Global 
ecological overshoot day is the date each year the resources have been consumed that the Earth 
can regenerate in a year. Humanity has been in ecological overshoot since the 1970s, and today 
humanity uses the resources equivalent to the production of 1.7 Earths. The consumption means 
humanity uses resources that take the Earth one year and eight months to generate annually (Global 
Footprint Network 2019). The high resource consumption is not only caused by the increase in 
population; the consumption per person increased from 8.1 tons in 1990 to 12 tons in 2015 (United 
Nations Economic and Social Council 2019). 
 
The continuous stress on the Earth’s resources has sparked investigations of the resource limits. 
The most known is the nine planetary boundaries, which define nine different boundaries of the 
Earth and their risk level (see figure 1-1). Two of the most stressed boundaries are nitrogen and 
phosphorus. Nitrogen and phosphorus are essential elements for an organism to grow and are 
therefore used as nutrients for organisms. However, only a fraction of the nitrogen and phosphorus 
used in fertilizers and nutrient-rich water are taken up by the organisms; the majority goes into 
waterways or evaporates. The inefficient use of nutrients is one of the reasons for the depletion of 
the Earth's terrestrial storage of these elements and the contamination of waterways. (Steffen et 
al., 2015). Contamination of waterways with nitrogen and phosphorus leads to eutrophication. 
Eutrophication is the over-enrichment of mineral and nutrients, causing excessive algae growth 
called an algae bloom. The algae blooms can be toxic and lead to oxygen depletion, resulting in 
the death of fish and other aquatic organisms (Rittmann and McCarty 2001; Henze 2008).

1.1 Initiatives to Change 
 
The United Nations member states adopted The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development in 
2015.  This agenda stated 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) that should be the blueprint 
for more sustainable use of the Earth. The SDGs contain economic, environmental, and social 
goals (see figure 1-2). (United Nations 2015)

In order to achieve these goals, individual companies followed suit and made initiatives to work 
on the goals. The SDGs and sustainability issues have caused Carlsberg, a Danish beer brewery

company, to start the program Together Towards Zero. The purpose of Together Towards Zero is, 
according to Carlsberg, “We do not settle for immediate gain when we can create a better 
tomorrow for all of us. We are brewing for a better today and tomorrow” (Carlsberg A/S 2019). 
It contains four initiatives Zero Carbon Footprint, Zero Wastewater, Zero Irresponsible, Zero 
Accidents Culture. This study is to help tackle two of the initiatives, Zero Waste Water and Zero 
Carbon Footprint, which are both are sustainability goals. Zero Carbon Footprint is the ambition 
for Carlsberg to reduce its emission according to a 1.5°C level of the Paris Agreement. Zero Waste 
Water is Carlsberg's target to halve water usage at their breweries and to partner to safeguard 
shared water resources in high-risk areas (Carlsberg A/S 2019). 
 
1.2 Solution  
 
In the theme of these initiatives, this project is to make clean water and high-value products from 
brewery wastewater using microalgae and bacteria. A large portion of nutrients during 
fermentation is not being utilized, resulting in wastewater with high phosphorus and nitrogen. 
Carlsberg is spending time and money on removing nutrients from the wastewater to be able to 
dispose of it. Several species of algae can convert CO2, nitrogen, and phosphate into biomass and 
O2 such as Chlorella vulgaris (CV) and Scenedesmus obliquus (SO). Reducing CO2 emissions and 
creating a valuable product in biomass can be used for a variety of purposes. However, studies 
have shown that nitrogen and phosphate removal is not optimal (Raposo et al. 2010). For these 
reasons, this project is looking into using a HOB (Hydrogen Oxidizing Bacteria), Cupriavidus 
necator (CN), which utilizes CO2, H2, organic compounds nitrogen and phosphate to create 
biomass (Khosravi-Darani et al. 2013). The idea is to co-cultivate CV or SO with CN on Clean-
in-Place (CIP) brewery wastewater (BWW), increase nutrient removal, and create biomass for 
potential value-added products. The implementation of co-cultivation is to increase the 
sustainability of wastewater treatment of BWW. 
 
1.3 Objective 
The objective of this study is to test the feasibility of co-cultivating HOB and algae in CIP brewery 
wastewater. Three environments are tested with the following parameters: 
1. Investigate co-cultivation under mixotrophic conditions, supplying CO2 and H2 in the gas 
phase. So algae produce O2 for substrate to the HOB.

2. Investigate co-cultivation under heterotrophic conditions, supplying natural air in the gas 
phase.  
3. Investigate co-cultivation under mixotrophic conditions, supplying CO2, O2, and H2 in the 
gas phase.  
 
Parameters monitored: temperature, shaking speed, light intensity, optical density (OD), chemical 
oxygen demand (COD), dissolved oxygen (DO), pH, dry weight (DW), and gas composition.

2. Background 
2.1 Beer Brewing Industry 
 
Brewing is one of humankind’s oldest types biotechnology, with over 125 years devoted to the 
research of production, malting, and brewing. Beer, in the simplest term, is a beverage produced 
from the fermentation of sugar, mostly or entirely from malted cereals, and flavored with hops. 
Beer brewing can include other processes like the flavoring of cherries, citrus, coriander, juniper, 
and spruce but varies from beer style to beer style (Bamforth 2009). Today beer production 
accounts for a 216 billion dollar industry in 2019 (IBISWorld 2019). The global beer production 
was 1.94 billion hectolitres in 2018 with China, the United States, and Brazil being the leading 
producers of beer (Statista, 2018). Denmark alone produced 6.04 million hectolitres and had a 
consumption of 62 liters per year per capita (The Brewers of Europe 2018).  
 
2.1.1 Beer Brewing Process 
Despite the simple nature of beer brewing, industrial beer brewing is a complex process. In order 
for beer companies to produce a consistent beer over simultaneous sites while upholding food and 
beverage regulations, companies must apply good fermentation practices with precise control of 
the variables influential on the progress and outcome of the process (Boulton, 2006). Illustrated in 
figure 2-1 are the basic stages of brewing. 
==PAGE:17==

Figure 2-1. Schematic representation of the brewing process (Bamforth 2009). 
For this whole process, four raw materials are essential: barley, hops, water, and yeast. Barley is 
the grain, supplying the carbohydrates and other nutrients for the fermentation process. The 
average barley grain contains 78–83% total carbohydrates (63–65% starch, 3–8% -glucans, 4–7% 
pentosans, and 4–5% cellulose), 10–12% proteins and amino acids, 2–3% lipids, and 2% minerals. 
Hops are the flowers from the plant Humulus lupulus and are the flavoring of the beer. They give 
a bitter taste, mostly due to the soft resins with α-acids and to a lesser extent with their β-acids. 
Water is essential in large quantities, as to make one hectoliter of beer between 3 and 10 hectoliters 
of water are needed. Processing uses the most substantial portion of water. Yeast is a eukaryotic, 
single-celled microorganism classified as a fungus. Yeast converts fermentable sugars to ethanol 
by glycolysis, followed by decarboxylation of the pyruvate to acetaldehyde and reduced to ethanol. 
The classification of yeast is mostly the various Saccharomyces species. (Bamforth 2009) 
 
Fermentation starts with the addition of around 20 million yeasts added per milliliter of wort. Yeast 
is usually used from the preceding fermentation, causing the yeast to be active. The yeast can

2.2 Brewery Wastewater  
Due to the extensive process of beer brewing, there is a large variety of wastewaters that have 
different characteristics depending on where in the process the wastewater is from. Brewery 
wastewater includes both primary water used in the production of beer and secondary water that 
does not come in contact with the beer, e.g., utility cooling, water used in the packaging process, 
and general cleaning water. (Amenorfenyo et al. 2019). This study focuses on CIP BWW. 
 
2.2.1 Clean-in-Place 
A CIP process is necessary at larger breweries to ensure hygienic conditions for beer brewing. The 
practice of CIP is a method to effectively clean fermentation tanks and pipes without having to 
take the process apart (Meyers 1959). Contamination in the tanks can easily happen at the 
introduction of malt, hops, or airborne transmission. The brewing process promotes microbial 
growth; failing to keep the tanks sterile could result in foreign microorganisms. Foreign microbial 
growth and other contaminants can damage the quality and flavor of the final product. (Davies et 
al. 2015)  
The basic concept of CIP is the cleaning of a tank or vessel without taking it apart. Larger breweries 
have automated CIP systems, relying on monitoring temperature, connectivity, flow meters, and 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
other factors to ensure hygienic conditions. Cleaning machines are typically high-pressure and use 
spray heads to distribute the detergents. Various detergents are an important tool for proper 
cleaning, using both chemical interaction and temperature to remove contaminants (Simate et al. 
2011). A typical CIP process is illustrated in figure 2-2. 
 
Figure 2-2. Flow diagram of five-step CIP cleaning process (Zeng, Duan, and Ma 2014). 
CIP process often include using an alkaline and acidic cleaner. The brewery wastewater 
investigated in this study uses an alkaline detergent of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and acidic 
detergent of nitric acid (HNO3) and phosphoric acid (H3PO4). There are two types of CIP systems: 
single-use where the detergents in the CIP process are used once, and reuse, where the detergents 
are recycled and reused in the next CIP process. The advantage of recycling is less water use, but 
it accumulates contaminates. Too much recycling could lead to contaminated conditions. (Davies 
et al. 2015) 
 
2.2.2 Characterization of Wastewater 
BWW often contain high concentrations of nitrogen, phosphorus, and high organic loads by having 
a high COD (chemical oxygen demand) (Dvořák et al. 2014).  The presence of organic components 
(sugars, soluble starch, ethanol, volatile fatty acids) results in a high COD and BOD (biological 
oxygen demand). The pH depends on the composition of chemicals, like added cleaning and 
sanitizing chemicals. Examples of sanitizing chemicals are sodium hydroxide, nitric acid, 
phosphoric acid. The concentration of nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) depends on the stage of 
the brewing process and the handling of raw materials (Simate et al. 2011). Table 2-2 shows the 
general characteristics of brewery wastewater.

2.3 Legislation and Environmental Management of Wastewater 
There are several ways to discharge BWW; directly into waterways (the ocean, rivers, and lakes), 
into the municipal sewer system, or the brewers' wastewater treatment. The concentration of 
organic load, phosphorus, nitrogen, and microbial growth substrate could lead to algae blooms, 
contamination of waterways, and other negative environmental impacts (Attiogbe, Glover-
Amengor, and Nyadziehe 2009). Wastewater management is, therefore, under a lot of 
governmental regulations. In order to run a legal beer brewery, breweries need to follow numerous 
laws and regulations on wastewater management. (Simate et al. 2011) 
The Danish Environmental Protection Agency (DEPA) states the following: “In order to discharge 
wastewater (treated or not treated) it is required to obtain a discharge permit from the proper 
environmental authority. Based on the Environmental Protection Act, the regulation of discharge 
permits is stated in the statutory orders no. 501 of 21 June 1999 and no. 921 of 8 October 1996. 
In general, the county gives permission to discharge waste-water to streams, lakes or the sea and 
the municipality gives permission to connect wastewater to public sewerage systems” (DEPA 
2000).  
 To acquire a permit, the effluent wastewater must be below the limits on a wide variety of 
chemical concentrations and parameters. The most important to BWW is a BOD below 556 mg/L, 
Total-N below 112 mg/L, and Total-P below 25 mg/L. (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2016) 
 
2.4 Microorganisms 
Microorganisms include bacteria, yeast, fungi, and viruses and range in size from less than 100 
nm up to a millimeter (Batt 2016). Over the years, microorganisms have seen increased 
applications due to their small size and ability to perform specific tasks chemically. A variety of 
fields has included them as solutions, such as treatment of wastewaters, enhancement of drinking 
water quality, restoration of contaminated sites, and production of environmentally benign 
chemicals. The use of microorganisms in environmental engineering is quite different from other 
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
applications since the organisms often have limited resources to their disposal and have to compete 
against other organisms.  In order to build a robust microbial system that can carry out specific 
tasks, all parameters need to be accounted for. (Rittmann and McCarty 2001) 
Cells are what make up organisms and are the fundamental building block of life. A cell is a living 
entity; the definition of living means it differentiates itself in four critical ways from nonliving 
entities.  
1. Growth and reproduction, being able to create or replicate new cells. 
2. Highly organized and secluded from the outside environment, resulting in being at low 
entropy compared to their environment. 
3. Four significant elements compose a cell; carbon (C), nitrogen (N), oxygen (O), and S 
(Sulfur), all of which are chemically reduced by having gained extra electrons. 
4. Self-feeding, being able to consume the necessary elements, electrons, and energy to 
reproduce, maintain chemical processes, and chemically reduce the significant elements. 
These resources are gathered from the external environment through metabolism.    
Microorganisms` different metabolic pathways make them ideal for the treatment of wastewater, 
as different metabolic pathways can remove particular unwanted chemicals in the water. The large 
variety of microorganisms provides the ability to remove different chemicals under different 
conditions. The utilization of specific species to clean the chemical composition of a certain kind 
of wastewater could pave the way for future wastewater treatment. (Rittmann and McCarty 2001) 
 
2.5 Factors for growth 
In order to use microorganisms optimally, it is essential to understand the different factors that 
influence growth. The growth of microorganisms refers to the reproduction, which is usually the 
binary fission, where a cell divides into two daughter cells, which are exact replicates. (Rittmann 
and McCarty 2001) 
 
2.5.1 Metabolism 
Metabolism is the combination of catabolism and anabolism, as illustrated in figure 2-3. 
Metabolism is the oxidation of the electron donor, reduction of the electron acceptor, and the 
formation of new biomass. Catabolism is the energy-yielding part of metabolism based on redox 
reactions. Energy is created by transfer of electrons from an electron donor to electron carriers, the 
==PAGE:23==
12 
 
reduction of one compound, and oxidation of another compound. Electron transfer is used for the 
regeneration of energy carriers. The electron donor and acceptor are the critical factors for 
catabolism. The source of energy a species uses is divided into two categories: The first is 
phototrophs, which use light as an energy source. Phototrophs are subdivided into oxygenic 
phototrophs that produce O2 as a waste product, compared to anoxygenic phototrophs that produce 
other chemicals as waste. The second is chemotrophs, which use chemical energy as an energy 
source. Chemotrophs are further divided into chemoorganotrophs utilizing organic chemicals, 
differentiating from chemolithotrophs that use inorganic chemicals. (Rittmann and McCarty 2001; 
Henze 2008) 
 Anabolism is biosynthesis, cell synthesis from carbon sources, and other nutrients. One of the 
crucial factors is the source of carbon and nutrients. Microorganisms are also often described by 
their carbon source. Autotrophs use inorganic carbon, whereas heterotrophs use organic carbon. 
Microbes are also able to mixotrophic, meaning they have multiple metabolic pathways. (Rittmann 
and McCarty 2001; Henze 2008)

2.6.2 Carbon Management 
Water contains different carbon sources that can be characterized by COD. COD is the chemical 
oxygen demand, which measures the amount of O2 equivalents needed to oxidize the compounds 
of a solution fully. COD can be equivalent to the electrons transferred by oxidation. COD gives an 
indicator of the capacity of a solution to donate electrons and is often used to quantify the organic 
compound concentration. However, there are soluble, biodegradable organics, like sugars such as 
glucose, but also soluble non-biodegradable organics, for example, pharmaceuticals and other 
various synthetic compounds. Particulate biodegradable organics, like protein and carbohydrates. 
Particulate non-biodegradable organics, cellulose, and plastics. Lastly, particulate organic carbon 
from biomass. BOD is the oxygen demand by microbes for chemical oxidation, giving an indicator 
of biodegradable organic matter.  
 
2.6.3 Nitrogen Management 
Ammonium (NH4+), nitrite (NO2-), and nitrate (NO3-) are the most common nitrogen fractions 
soluble in water. They are toxic to aquatic animals and promote algae blooms. Nitrogen is an 
essential nutrient for microbial growth; nitrogen is required for anabolism. Nitrogen can also be 
fixed from air, which is available in large quantities due to the composition of air.  
 
2.6.4 Phosphorus Management 
Phosphorus is a key nutrient for algae and other microbial growth. Phosphorus released into an 
aquatic environment can lead to eutrophication. Eutrophication is the over-enrichment of mineral 
and nutrients, causing excessive algae growth called an algae bloom. The algae blooms can be 
toxic and lead to oxygen depletion, resulting in the death of fish and other aquatic organisms. 
Phosphorus can only be released from upstreams, compared to nitrogen, which can be fixed from 
the air. Phosphorus is mostly present in environments as phosphate (PO43-). P is required for 
anabolism, the building of new cell biomass.

2.8 Microalgae 
Algae are photosynthetic organisms that predominantly live in aquatic environments. Algae can 
be photoautotrophic, heterotrophic, and mixotrophic. Phototrophic means they utilize inorganic 
carbon like CO2 as a carbon source and light as an energy source. Synthesizing O2 and organic 
metabolites like carbohydrates.  However, some algae can use organic carbon or both inorganic 
and organic carbon. Algae are defined in two big groups of algae, macroalgae, and microalgae. 
Macroalgae are multicellular and range in size from a few centimeters to several meters. Seaweed 
is an example of a group of macroalgae. Microalgae are single-celled organisms and have a size 
of less than one centimeter. (Singh and Saxena 2015) 
There are more than 25.000 species of algae, making them suitable for a lot of different 
environments and industrial uses (Balat 2011). Microorganisms have a short growth cycle, being 
able to accumulate biomass in a short time (Miranda et al. 2017). It is a heterogeneous group 
containing both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. Microalgae are often more robust than bacteria due 
to having a cell wall (Sydney et al. 2019).  
Microalgae is mainly used for its ability to use photosynthesis. Photosynthesis is divided into two 
parts, light reactions that need light, and dark reactions where light is not necessary. Light reactions 
are the direct light striking the chlorophyll, exciting the electrons to higher state. Through various 
reactions are converted to ATP, NADPH, and oxygen is released due to the splitting of water 
molecules. The dark reactions fixate CO2 from the air, and which is reduced with H2 to 
carbohydrate (Sydney et al. 2019).  The two species used in this study are Chlorella vulgaris and 
Scenedesmus obliquus. 
 
2.8.1 Chlorella vulgaris 
CV is a unicellular green alga and is a freshwater alga. One of the key characteristics of CV is a 
thick cell wall (100-200 nm), making it very robust. The thick cell wall makes CV one of the most 
utilized microalgae for wastewater treatment (Sydney et al. 2019). CV can utilize autotrophic, 
heterotrophic, and mixotrophic growth. The nitrogen source can also vary by being able to utilize 
nitrate, ammonium, and urea. CV is more efficient in alkaline environments, 9 to 10 pH, but can 
grow from pH 4 to 10. The optimal lighting is around 5 kLUX, but can grow from 2800 LUX to 
7400 LUX. The best concentration of CO2 is around 6% (de Morais and Costa 2007). The highest 
biomass production is found around 30 °C and denaturation at 38 °C (Chinnasamy et al. 2009).  
==PAGE:35==
24 
 
 
2.8.2 Scenedesmus obliquus 
SO, is like CV, a freshwater alga and is also able to use autotrophic, heterotrophic, and mixotrophic 
growth. SO is also a highly studied microalga. Being highly versatile and able to grow under a 
large variety of conditions. SO is often seen in nature playing a primary production, contributing 
to the purification of eutrophic waters (Kessler 1991).  The optimal growth conditions for SO is 
around 7 pH and 25-30 °C, and light intensity varies a lot from study to study (Hodaifa, Martínez, 
and Sánchez 2010; 2009). However, SO can grow under low light intensity. 
 
2.8.3 Added-value Product 
Microalgae's unique composition makes them eligible to create a large number of added-value 
products. Microalgae can contain as high as 70% lipids, 60% carbohydrates, and 65% of proteins 
and essential amino-acids (Afzal et al. 2017). The advantage of products from wastewater 
treatment is that they do not compete with food crops and clean wastewater but is a necessary 
process. 
 
Biodiesel 
The release of lipids creates biodiesel. Biodiesel is long-chain alkyl esters. The special about 
biodiesel is it is a drop-in biofuel, meaning a standard diesel engine can utilize it. The high lipid 
content does so microalgae can be used to create biodiesel. Algal biodiesel is a carbon-neutral fuel 
because the carbon released is from carbon fixation, providing a more sustainable fuel (Najafi, 
Ghobadian, and Yusaf 2011). Algal biodiesel has been found to meet The International Biodiesel 
Standard for Vehicles (EN14214) (Islam et al. 2013). Given the value-added product viability of 
being used now, unlike other products that have not yet been certified.  
 
Other Fuels 
Algae has also been found to be able to be used to produce other fuels. The carbohydrate content 
can be converted to bioethanol by microorganisms fermenting the simple sugars of the algae 
(Harun, Danquah, and Forde 2010). Bio-ethanol is simply ethanol made from biomass and can 
substitute gasoline. Another fuel is renewable jet fuel. Green bio-jet fuel is made by mixing 
==PAGE:36==
25 
 
microalgae bio-fuels with petroleum-derived jet fuel to get the right properties (Farag and 
Elmoraghy 2012).  
 
Single-cell Protein 
The high protein content and essential amino acids make microalgae a possible substitution for 
animal feedstock. Proteins are crucial nutrients for humans and animals, algae high protein content 
and essential amino acids making them a substitute for human and animal nutrition (Lin et al. 
2019). Protein can be utilized for more purposes, such as fertilizers, industrial enzymes, 
bioplastics, and surfactants. The well-known algal spirulina is already being utilized as a dietary 
supplement. (Trivedi et al. 2015) 
The extensive utilization of algae is endless, and other added-value products include pigment, 
carbohydrates, carrageenan, vitamins (A, B1, B2, B6, B12, C, E) and many more uses. (Trivedi et 
al. 2015) 
 
2.9 Brewery Wastewater Treatment 
Breweries need various wastewater treatment methods to achieve a concentration below the legal 
limits. BWW treatment methods can be categorized into three different groups of treatment; 
Physical, chemical, and biological. In table 2-4. the different wastewater treatment methods can 
be seen in the three categories. Chemical and physical treatment methods are often used to pre-
treat BWW before biological treatment. (Simate et al. 2011) 
==PAGE:37==
26 
 
Table 2-4. Wastewater treatment unit operations and processes (Simate et al. 2011). 
 
Physical operations use physical forces to remove contaminants. Physical forces include 
sedimentation and flocculation to let contaminants fall to the bottom or float to the top.  Physical 
wastewater treatment targets suspended particles, ignoring dissolved contaminants. It often leads 
to incomplete contaminant removal since a significant fraction of contaminants are dissolved 
(Sheehan and Greenfield 1980).  
Chemical BWW treatment utilizes the interaction of chemicals to alter the water chemistry 
(Huang, Schwab, and Jacangelo 2009). If the BWW does not have a pH between 6-9, the pH can 
be changed with acids or bases. Coagulation and flocculation are physiochemical processes and 
can be used to remove colloidal materials. Organic compounds can also be oxidized by the addition 
of chlorine, ozone-oxygen, or permanganate to generate CO2, H2O, and other neutral compounds 
(Benitez et al. 2003).  
Biological wastewater treatment plays a crucial role in the treatment of wastewater. Using 
organisms to biodegrade and metabolize pollutants is natural since they are often already present 
(Degrémont 1989). There is a large variety of biological wastewater treatment methods. The BWW 
composition makes it ideal for biological treatment. Being high in nitrogen, phosphor, and organic 
compounds creates the right conditions for biological growth. There are three advantages of 
biological methods; the treatment technology is mature, high efficiency in COD and BOD removal 
(80% to 90%), and low investment cost. (Haiping et al. 2010)

2.9.1 Brewery Wastewater Biological Treatment Methods 
Biological treatment of BWW, is widely used since BWW contains the nutrients and substrate to 
support microbial activity. Biological treatment is divided into whether the organisms are supplied 
with oxygen or without, aerobic and anaerobic treatment. The aerobic and anaerobic treatment 
processes can also be used in a series to decrease the discharge of COD, nitrogen, and phosphorus 
(Amenorfenyo et al. 2019). The principle behind the treatment is given by the following: 
Anaerobic:   
 
𝐶𝑂𝐷= 𝐶𝐻4 + 𝐶𝑂2 + 𝑎𝑛𝑎𝑒𝑟𝑜𝑏𝑖𝑐 𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠 
Aerobic:  
 
 
𝐶𝑂𝐷+ 𝑂2 = 𝐶𝑂2 + 𝐻2𝑂+ 𝑎𝑒𝑟𝑜𝑏𝑖𝑐 𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠 
Oxygen is a highly reactive chemical, being a strong oxidizing agent. Aerobic treatment has a 
higher biomass production, but anaerobic treatment is capable of production of biomass plus 
energy, usually in the form of biogas (70-85% CH4 and 15-30% CO2). Purely anaerobic treatment 
of BWW is not applicable, but a combination of purely aerobic is. However, a combined 
anaerobic/aerobic treatment of brewery is advantageous over purely aerobic treatment: giving a 
positive energy balance, reduced (bio)sludge production, and significant lower space requirements 
(Driessen 2014). A comparison of general aerobic and anaerobic systems can be summed up by 
table 2-5. 
Table 2-5. Anaerobic treatment as compared to aerobic treatment (Driessen 2014). 
 
Standard aerobic treatment methods are activated sludge process, biofilm process, trickling filter 
process, and lagoons. In the activated sludge process: Wastewater is treated in an aeration tank by 
a mixed microbial community. The aeration mixes the microorganisms and supplies O2. The 
biofilm process has microorganisms fixed to a solid surface, which can both be fixed in place or 
freely moving in the tank. The wastewater is forced in contact with the biofilm that removes 
contaminates and creates biomass. The trickling filter process distributes the wastewater over a 
surface of rough solids. The wastewater goes through the surface covered with microbes. Lagoons 
are one of the oldest treatment methods, sending the wastewater into a volume with a large surface 
==PAGE:39==
28 
 
area having access to natural air. Relying on microorganisms and algae interaction with air and 
sunlight. (Simate et al. 2011) 
Conventional anaerobic treatment methods are up-flow anaerobic sludge blanket (UASB) and 
fluidized bed reactor (FBR). UASB is one of the most used anaerobic treatment methods. 
Wastewater is pumped from the bottom of a tank through a dense bed of anaerobic sludge, where 
the microbes degrade contaminants. FBR is similar to UASB, except the wastewater flows through 
a media colonized by microorganisms, like sand or activated carbon. (Simate et al. 2011) 
 
2.9.2 Brewery Wastewater Treatment by Microalgae 
Microalgae is a useful treatment method of wastewater, being able to utilize light as an energy 
source, and synthesize nutrients into biomass (Chinnasamy et al. 2010). Microalgae assimilate 
HCO3 and CO2 and also release oxygen, so they reduce the amount of GHGs in the air. The 
application of microalgae in wastewater treatment has mostly been confined to theatrical and 
experimental wastewater treatment but has recently started to be applied for industrial use. Two 
microalgae reactors have been applied to conventional use, raceway ponds and photobioreactor 
(PBR) (Amenorfenyo et al. 2019). A raceway pond is a type of PBR. PBRs take advantage of 
phototrophic metabolism, emphasizing light source. They, therefore, often have a large surface 
area and turbulent flow to ensure access to light. Often also the availability of CO2 as a carbon 
source, usually as a CO2 supply system. See figure 2-10, for standard PBR systems.

3.1  Microalgae 
In this study, two species of microalgae were used: Chlorella vulgaris (CV) and Scenedesmus 
obliquus (SO). CV (strain CCAP 211/11B) was obtained from The Culture Collection of Algae 
and Protozoa (CCAP), where the original strain came from freshwater in Delft, Netherlands. CV 
was further cultivated on EG:JM medium described in section 3.1.1 Medium for Microalgae 
(Culture collection of algae and Protozoa 2014). SO (strain UTEX 2016) was obtained from the 
Culture Collection of Algae at the University of Texas at Austin. SO was further cultivated on 
Polytomella medium, described in section 3.1.1 Medium for Microalgae (UTEX Culture 
Collection of Algae 2019) 
 
3.1.1 Medium for Microalgae 
Due to the difference in algae species, two different media were made for the algae. The CV was 
grown in an EG:JM medium, which is a 1:1 mixture of Euglena gracilis Medium (EG) and 
Jaworski's Medium (JM). EG is a medium for freshwater algae and protozoa. The EG medium has 
concentrations of 1 g/L sodium acetate trihydrate, “Lab-Lemco” powder 1 g/L, tryptone 2 g/L, 
yeast extract 2 g/L, and CaCl2 10 mg. The JM has concentrations of Ca(NO3)2.4H2O 20 mg/L, 
KH2PO4 12.4 mg/L, MgSO4.7H2O 50 mg/L, NaHCO3 15.9 mg/L, EDTAFeNa 2.25 mg/L, 
EDTANa2 2.25 mg/L, H3BO3 2.48 mg/L, MnCl2.4H2O 1.39 mg/L, (NH4)6Mo7O24.4H2O 1 mg/L, 
Cyanocobalamin 0.04 mg/L, Thiamine HCl 0.04 mg/L, Biotin 0.04 mg/L, NaNO3 80 mg/L and 
Na2HPO4.12H2O 36 mg/L. Both mediums were separately autoclaved at 15 psi for 20 min before 
being mixed in a 1:1 ratio, then autoclaved at 15 psi for 20 min before being used as a medium 
(Culture collection of algae and Protozoa 2019). 
For SO Polytomella medium was used, which is suitable for all axenic cultures of Polytoma, 
Polytomella, and Astasia, the colorless strains of Euglena and Scenedesmus obliquus. The 
Polytomella had concentrations of yeast extract 1 g/L, triptone 1 g/L, and Sodium acetate 2 g/L. 
Before using the medium was autoclaved at 15 psi for 20 min (Pringsheim and Koch 2008).

3.1.2 Cultivation of Algae 
Both original algae cultures were collected from Carlsberg laboratory. To ensure active algae 
cultures, the algae were subcultured every seven days. For sub-culturing 20 ml of medium was put

used; checking for green coloration, homogenous algae growth, and that the cultures had not been 
contaminated. 1 ml of former culture was added to the medium. Each species was subcultured 
twice, and all operation was under a clean bench to ensure no contamination from other bacteria. 
The subcultures were kept at room temperature (22 °C) with 12 hours of LED illumination a day.

The materials and methods used to analyze the experiments' different parameters are described 
below. 
3.5.1 Optical Density 
The optical density was measured with Varian Cary® 50 UV-Vis Spectrophotometer. 1 ml of 
sample was injected into a 10 mm cuvette and measured at wavelength 640 nm and 440 nm. The 
optical density (OD) is the measurement of the speed of light through a substance. The more the 
light is delayed or reflected can be used as an indicator of the concentration of bacteria or other 
microorganisms. OD is the logarithmic difference in light intensity at the start (I0) and end (I), 
given by the equation below. (Myers, Curtis, and Curtis 2013)

3.5.2 pH 
The pH was measured with PHM210 Standard pH Meter. pH is the measurement of hydrogen ions 
(H+) on a logarithmic scale, which determines the acidity and alkalinity of a solution. The samples 
were measured by calibrating the pH-meter with a buffer of pH 7 and pH 4. Then the sensor was 
put in the sample until the value remained a staple. (MacInnes 1951) 
 
==PAGE:54==
43 
 
3.5.3 Gas Measurement 
The gas composition was measured with TRACE™ 1310 Gas Chromatograph from Thermo 
Scientific™ (ThermoFisher Scientific 2019c). 2 ml of gas sample was injected with a needle into 
the gas chromatograph and analyzed with Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC/MS). 
GC/MS first separates the various components of the sample with a capillary column packed with 
a stationary (solid) phase. The compounds are carried through the column by an inert gas; in this 
experiment, nitrogen was used. The different properties cause the compounds to exit at different 
times, referred to as the retention time. Compounds that have left the column are ionized through 
a mass spectrometer by electron ionization or chemical ionization, then accelerated through a mass 
analyzer, which separates the compounds on mass-to-charge (m/z) ratios.  In m/z m refers to the 
molecular mass and z to charge number of the ion. Lastly, the peak heights of the sample are 
analyzed, with peaks referring to the m/z of the compounds (ThermoFisher Scientific 2019b). For 
analysis, the software Chromeleon™ Chromatography Data System (CDS) Software was used, 
being able to calculate the composition of a sample by providing a calibration curve (ThermoFisher 
Scientific 2019a). 
In this study, two GC/MS settings were used. The first was to measure CO2 and CH4 at temperature 
70 °C and a retention time of 2.7 min. The second measured CH4, O2, H2, and N2, at temperature 
130 °C and retention time of sample 2.5 min.  
 
3.5.4 Dissolved Oxygen 
For the measurement of dissolved oxygen (DO), the level of free oxygen present in water, the 
HQ40D Portable Multi Meter from Hach, was used (Hach 2019). The oxygen sensor was put into 
the sample and was measured until the measurement was stable. The sensor works by having a 
clear oxygen impermeable hard substrate with an overlay of dark pigment to prevent stray light 
from affecting the measurement. The luminescent dye in the measurement cell gives a red light 
when exposed to blue light. The duration of the luminescence is proportional to the concentration 
of dissolved oxygen; an internal red light is used as reference (Hach 2015). 
 
3.5.5 Nitrate and Phosphate Measurement 
Nitrate and phosphate concentration were determined with photometric kits. Before tests, 1 ml of 
the sample was centrifuged for 2 min at 12000 rpm to prevent solids from intervening with the

measurement. For nitrate, the 1.09713.01001 Spectroquant® Nitrate Test was used. The kit works 
by sulfuric and phosphoric solution nitrate ions reacting with 2,6-dimethylphenol to form 4-nitro-
2,6-dimethylphenol that is determined photometrically.  It has a range of 1.0 to 25.0 mg/L NO3-N 
in 10 mm cell, and the samples were therefore diluted 80 times with distilled water (Merck KGaA

For Phosphate measurement, the 1.14842.0001 Spectroquant® Phosphate Test was used. The

distilled water (Merck KGaA 2018b).

4. Results and Discussion 
4.1 Wastewater Composition 
The wastewater had an OD above zero at both wavelengths, which indicates suspended particles 
in the wastewater. The wastewater also has an AFDW of 357 mg/L and ASW of 67.5, meaning 
that it is a mix of organic and inorganic particles. The AFDW and ash weight also shows that there 
are suspended particles in the CIP BWW. The COD confirms the presence of oxidizable pollutants; 
it is important to note that BOD is was not measured. The pollutants are, therefore, not necessarily 
biodegradable. These compounds are most likely residual compounds from the brewing process.  
The BWW had a pH of 13.07, making it very alkaline, which could be due to a higher concentration 
of alkaline CIP water compared to acidic CIP water. The initial DO was 10.31 mg/L, which is 
around 100% air saturation; the wastewater is around the equilibrium. The phosphorus 
concentration is quite high in comparison to other BWW, but the nitrate is even higher, with ten 
times the average BWW. A contributing factor is the added nitric acid. 
 
4.2 Photoautotrophic Growth of Algae Coupled with Chemolithotrophic Growth 
of HOB

able to be co-cultivated in CIP BWW, where algae produce O2 by photosynthesis, and HOB uses 
the O2 for chemolithotroph growth? Anaerobic conditions were created in the flasks by only 
supplying H2 and CO2. The optimal gas composition for HOB is 6:2:1 H2:O2:CO2, since algae 
should produce the O2, the O2 fraction was exchanged with CO2. The other premise tested was the 
ratio of algae to HOB. HOB has a faster metabolism than algae; hence a lower ratio of HOB

4.9 Comparison with Legislation and Comparative Studies 
When comparing with the Danish legislation legal limits, BOD below 556 mg/L, Total-N below 
112 mg/L, and Total-P below 25 mg/L (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 2016). The lowest 
concentration of COD was 169 O2 mg/L by the cultivation of CV at mixotrophic conditions, which 
is within the legal limit, but the CIP BWW was already below that limit. The lowest concentration 
of nitrate was 344 mg/L from CN cultivation in mixotrophic conditions, and the lowest phosphate 
concentration was 57.5 mg/L from co-cultivation of CN and CV under mixotrophic conditions. 
Nevertheless, both of these were above the legal limit. None of the cultivations were able to treat 
the CIP BWW for legal discharge. 
As for the comparison to other co-cultivations of microalgae and bacteria wastewater treatments, 
most of them were superior in the removal of pollutants.  However, the CN under mixotrophic 
conditions had a competitive nitrate removal of 70% and grew for only three days, meaning it 
could remove it in a short time. The prospect of optimizing it in CSTR could make it competitive 
to other wastewater treatments. The study, Rasouli et al. 2018 showed that algae was able to make 
produce oxygen for methanotrophic bacteria. If the same premise could be done for HOB and algae 
cultivation, it could create a wastewater treatment method that is competitive with conventional 
wastewater treatments. HOBs ability of creating a plethora of added-value products, makes them 
relevant for wastewater treatment.

5. Conclusion  
The Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater was high in nitrate and phosphate but low in COD 
compared to other brewery wastewaters. The harsh cleaning process had destroyed all active 
biomass. The harsh treatment of Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater, low COD but high phosphate 
and nitrate, provide sanitary wastewater that is ideal for carbon fixation by axenic or microbiome 
cultures. 
Both Chlorella vulgaris and Scenedesmus obliquus had little growth in the Clean-in-Place brewery 
wastewater. Several of the environmental conditions could cause the algae stress, high salt 
concentration, pH, and heavy metals. The six days cultivation was not enough time for the algae 
species to adapt. The highest nitrate removal was 11% under aerobic conditions and 39% COD 
removal under mixotrophic conditions both by the cultivation of Chlorella vulgaris. 
The HOB, Cupriavidus necator showed an impressive growth under mixotrophic growth with a 
removal of 70% and 23% of respectively nitrate and phosphate. Cupriavidus necator also had an 
impressive biomass production around 2 g/L. Cupriavidus necator showed a fast decay rate after 
maximum biomass had been achieved; this resulted in an increase in COD. Most of the biomass 
production was the result of autotrophic growth; the growth was limited by the gas available, not 
the Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater. A higher removal and growth could be possible with more 
gas available to Cupriavidus necator. The most efficient gas composition was found to be between 
6:2:1 or 5:1.1.2 of H2:O2:CO2 gas ratio.  
The co-cultivation of algae and Cupriavidus necator under mixotrophic conditions showed no 
decay rate of biomass. The algae species can grow on hydrolyzed Cupriavidus necator. For co-
cultivation of algae and hydrogen oxidizing bacteria, the results were inconclusive. Algae was 
unable to grow autotrophic, and therefore did not produce oxygen. Algae inhibited the growth of 
Cupriavidus necator instead of promoting it because of the biocidal activity of the algae.

6. Future Work 
 Several future studies should be investigated from the results of this study. Focusing on algae, a 
longer cultivation time, should be tested to see if the algae could adapt to the Clean-in-Place 
brewery wastewater. Dilutions of the wastewater could prove interesting, to dilute concentrations 
might prove more favorable for algae. Adjusting the pH and gas composition to favor the 
autotrophic growth of algae should also be investigated. 
 For hydrogen-oxidizing bacteria, the Clean-in-Place brewery wastewater proved to be ideal for 
the axenic cultivation of Cupriavidus necator. However, the Cupriavidus necator was grown with 
20% oxygen, and oxygen concentration as low as 4% has been shown to inhibit growth (Matassa 
et al. 2015). The Cupriavidus necator could be ideal to use at Carlsberg in a similar scheme as 
figure 2-11. Denmark provides a lot of wind energy, and the excess energy could be used for water 
electrolysis. Carlsberg has different wastewater streams, meaning sludge treatment of other 
brewery wastewaters could create biogas. The hydrogen oxidizing bacteria could upgrade the 
biogas by using a combination of knallgas (CO2 and H2) and biogas (CO2 and CH4). Whether 
Cupriavidus necator can upgrade biogas with added knallgas in Clean-in-Place brewery 
wastewater should be investigated as it could show to be a sustainable wastewater treatment 
scheme and provides several added-value products. 
As for co-cultivation, the ability of algae to use hydrolyzed Cupriavidus necator as a substrate 
should be investigated, as this could provide more robust biomass and higher phosphate removal. 
Co-cultivation of algae and hydrogen-oxidizing bacteria should be investigated, where algae can 
grow photoautotrophic as the original hypothesis seems plausible.
