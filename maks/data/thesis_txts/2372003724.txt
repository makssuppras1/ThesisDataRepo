Abstract
This thesis considers computation of a speciﬁc normal form for polynomial matrices
called Popov form. We give a Las Vegas algorithm which can compute shifted Popov
form of an arbitrary matrix A P Krxsmˆn, where K is a ﬁeld, using O˜pnω degpAqq
operations in K. In order to put this result into context we also present some of the
highlights from literature which considers this problem.

Introduction
In this thesis we consider the problem of eﬃciently computing canonical bases for an impor-
tant class of ﬁnitely generated free modules.
The modules that we will focus on are deﬁned over the ring Krxs of univariate polynomials
over an arbitrary ﬁeld K. For any positive integer n we are interested in Krxs-submodules of
Krxsn, which denotes the n-ary Cartesian product of the underlying ring. It follows from the
fact that Krxs is a principal ideal domain (PID) that these modules are free, which means
that each of them is generated by some ﬁnite linearly independent subset of Krxsn called a
basis. Our main problem assumes that modules are already given by a basis, thus it can be
viewed as “Given any basis, compute one which satisﬁes speciﬁc properties”.
The bases that we are interested in are called Popov bases. For any Krxs-module M Ď Krxsn,
there is a unique basis of M, call it P, which is in Popov form (precise deﬁnition given in
Section 2). If B is any basis of M, then P is said to be the Popov form of B. Because of
uniqueness of Popov form, if we are given two linearly independent subsets of Krxsn, we can
determine whether they generate the same module, by comparing their Popov forms. An-
other important feature of Popov bases is that their elements have smallest possible degrees,
where the degree of a tuple in Krxsn is deﬁned to be the maximal degree of its entries. This
feature implies that Popov bases have speciﬁc degree properties which are especially useful in
computations with such modules, by giving more control on the degrees of the manipulated
polynomials. In particular, a Popov basis is generally cheaper to store digitally than other
bases because it requires fewer polynomial coeﬃcients to be represented. Popov bases are
also known as the univariate case of Gr¨obner bases.
We are interested in eﬃcient algorithms for computing Popov form. Our notion of “eﬃ-
cient” is deﬁned by the number of ﬁeld operations in K used during the computation. This
measure of eﬃciency gives a good estimate of running times of implementations when K is a
ﬁnite ﬁeld. This follows from the fact that every operation in a ﬁnite ﬁeld can be computed
in constant time. The situation can be very diﬀerent for inﬁnite ﬁelds, as complexity for a
single ﬁeld operation can be arbitrary large. For instance, if K is the ﬁeld of rational num-
bers, then the cost of ﬁeld operations depends heavily on the absolute values of the numbers
involved.
We will follow common practice and restrict our attention to asymptotic complexity given
by the big O notation. We will also be using the soft O notation, which ignores any loga-
rithmic factors. For example, instead of writing Opn logpnq logplogpnqqq we will simply write
O˜pnq. This has also become common practice in the ﬁeld, as it makes it much easier to
deal with complexity analysis. The product of two polynomials with degree bounded by
d can be computed with the cost bound Opd logpdq logplogpdqqq Ă O˜pdq using the famous
Sch¨onhage-Strassen algorithm [Sch77, Nus80, GG13]. In cases like this, cost bounds which
are linear in some variable (in this case d) when ignoring the logarithmic factors are often
5
==PAGE:7==
called quasi-linear.
When considering algorithms related to bases of Krxs-submodules of Krxsn it is conve-
nient to identify basis elements as rows of polynomial matrices in Krxsmˆn, where m is the
cardinality of the given basis, and is therefore such that m ď n. Since bases are linearly
independent sets, the matrices that represent these bases must have full row rank, meaning
that any nonzero Krxs-linear combination of rows of such matrix is nonzero.
Notice that permutation of rows of a polynomial matrix which represents a basis does not
change the underlying basis. Because of this we will sometimes pay little attention to the
order of rows. However, if we want to preserve uniqueness of Popov form when extending
the deﬁnition to polynomial matrices, we must choose some total order on Krxsn. Some
algorithms might also require the rows to be ordered in certain ways, for example they are
often sorted by degree.
It is often useful to consider Popov bases with respect to more general measures of the
degree of its elements, which involve degree weights on the columns of the matrix. These
weights are speciﬁed by a tuple of integers. Any such tuple is called a shift; if a matrix is in
Popov form with respect to some shift, then it is said to be a shifted Popov form. If all of
the entries of the considered shift are equal, then the notion of shifted Popov form coincides
with Popov form. Such shifts are said to be uniform. As one might expect, computations
which consider arbitrary shift are much more involved and require stronger tools compared
to the uniform case.
Popov form belongs to a larger class called row reduced forms.
These are generally not
unique, which means that the same basis generally has many row reduced forms. However,
similarly to Popov form, row reduced forms have as small degree of rows as possible. Compu-
tation of a row reduced form, also called row reduction, is sometimes viewed as an analogue
to basis reduction for Euclidean lattices when working over the ring Krxs. Row reduction is
an interesting problem in itself, for instance it can be applied to the shortest vector problem,
which asks to ﬁnd an element of smallest degree in a module.
We will mention some of the highlights in literature dedicated to eﬃcient Popov form com-
putation. Additionally, we will present new, fast algorithms in the rectangular case (m ă n).

row reduced matrices we can detect whether the computation was successful. Even though
we direct most of our attention to the case when the input matrix A P Krxsmˆn has full rank
and m ă n, we also show how to deal with the case of arbitrary m and n, and also without
assuming full rank.

2.2
Modules
Throughout the rest of this thesis K is a ﬁeld. We focus on Krxs-submodules of Krxsn,
where n is a positive integer. Recall that a basis of a module, when it exists, is a linearly
independent generating set, while the rank of a module is the cardinality of any of its bases.
Cardinality is the same for all bases of submodules of Krxsn, thus rank is well-deﬁned. We
proceed to the following fundamental result [DF04, Chapter 12], but ﬁrst recall that a module
is said to be free if it has a basis.
Lemma 2.2.1. Let M be any Krxs-submodule of Krxsn. Then,
(i) M is free and has rank at most n;
(ii) any Krxs-submodule of M is free and has rank at most the rank of M.
8
==PAGE:10==
Let A P Krxsmˆn. The row space of A, which is the set of all Krxs-linear combinations of
rows of A, is a Krxs-submodule of Krxsn. Thus it makes sense to deﬁne row rank of A as the
rank of the row space of A. Similarly, column rank of A is deﬁned as the rank of the column
space of A. Even though row rank and column rank are always equal, it can be useful to
distinguish between them. For example, if a matrix has full row rank, meaning that rank is
equal to the number of rows, it does not need to have full column rank. However, we will
not be needing column rank in this thesis, and thus will simply say rank whenever we are
talking about row rank.
Any basis B of M Ď Krxsn, consisting of m ď n elements, can be represented by a full
row rank matrix B P Krxsmˆn by letting the rows of B be precisely the elements of B
(meaning that M is the row space of B). The choice of identifying M as the row space of
some polynomial matrix (instead of the column space) is arbitrary, but will be used as a
convention throughout this thesis.
Given a Krxs-module, it can have more than one basis. Diﬀerent bases of the same Krxs-
module can be related using unimodular matrices.

it satisﬁes any of these equivalent conditions:

Now we are ready to deﬁne the shifted Popov form for a polynomial matrix. The deﬁnition
for non-shifted Popov form is credited to [Pop72]. We will present a deﬁnition which involves
shifts, as in [BLV99, BLV06].

rank. Then P is in s-Popov form if the following conditions are satisﬁed.
(i) The s-pivot indices of P are pairwise distinct.
(ii) The s-pivot entries of P are monic.
(iii) The rows of P are sorted by their s-pivot indices in increasing order.
(iv) Any s-pivot entry of P has strictly greater degree than any other entry in its column.

4
Results
So far we have mentioned some of the highlights from literature that considers computation
of Popov form. We will now present our contribution to this topic. Our main result is a Las
Vegas algorithm which computes the shifted Popov form of a matrix A P Krxsmˆn with de-
gree d using an expected number of O˜pnωdq ﬁeld operations in K. This is an improvement as
we are no longer limited to the square full rank case. However, this comes with the expence
of introducing randomization. In addition, we will mention some cases when randomization
can be removed, if we have extra information about the input matrix.
All of these ideas assume existence of a fast algorithm which solves the square case (like
[NV17] does), which we then use as a black box.
4.1
Reduction to non-square full rank case
Our aim is to compute shifted Popov form of an arbitrary input matrix A P Krxsmˆn, without
any assumptions on m and n. This forces us to also consider the case where A does not
have full rank. Even though we have deﬁned Popov form exclusicvely for full rank matrices,
we can still look for (shifted) Popov basis of the rowspace of A. We split our problem into
multiple cases, and then show that they can all be reduced to just one case.
case 1: A has full rank and m “ n,
case 2: A has full rank and m ă n,
case 3: A does not have full rank.
We will use Theorem 5.6 from [ZL13] to reduce the general case problem to case 2.
Theorem 4.1.1. Let A P Krxsmˆn. There exists a deterministic algorithm which computes
a row basis of A using O˜pmωrssq ﬁeld operations in K, where s is the average row degree of
A.

5
Conclusion
In this thesis we have directed our attention to computation of Popov form, which is an
important normal form for polynomial matrices over ﬁelds. Since Popov form is canonical
it can be used to determine if two matrices describe the same row space. We have also
seen that matrices in Popov form are also row reduced, which means that they have a lot of
structure on their degrees.
In order to provide context for our main result we have mentioned some highlights in the
literature that considers computation of Popov form. We started by looking at the iterative
approach in [MS03], which has the cost bound Opm2ndq for any matrix A P Krxsmˆn with
degree d. Then we gave an overview of some of the ideas in [GSSV12, SS11], which when
combined give an algorithm which can compute Popov form of a square full rank matrix,
with the cost bound O˜pnωdq. Lastly, we mentioned the approach of [NV17], which achieves
the same cost bound for arbitrary shift.
We ended our investigation of Popov form computation by extending the results of [NV17]
such that the input matrix is no longer required to be square, or have full rank. However,
this is achieved at the expence of randomization. We have also seen how randomization can
be removed if additional information about the input matrix is available.
Future work could attempt to remove randomization entirely.
This might be achievable
by somehow computing the pivot indices of the desired Popov form without computing the
Popov form itself. As we have shown, if these pivot indices are available, then we can reduce
the general problem to the square full rank case which is already solved by [NV17]. This
approach would have the cost bound O˜pnωdq. If the goal is to achieve an even better cost
bound, such as O˜pmω´1ndq, a completely diﬀerent strategy must be used.
