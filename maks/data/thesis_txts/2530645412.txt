Abstract
Background
This thesis investigates a Thermoplastic Elastomer (TPE) integrated in an in-development Novo Nordisk
A/S (NN) pen device named the ICON. In its application, the TPE, i.e. the Insulator, is intended to
provide suﬃcient reaction moment when deformed inside the glass tube and rotated, to prevent excessive
rotation of the ICON. TPEs share overall same physical properties of rubber, including ﬂexibility and re-
silience. Their mechanical properties can be characterized by diﬀerent experimental tests. The numerical
data obtained can be used to model a constitutive material model able to predict the material behavior in
a Finite Element Model (FEM). Rubber-like materials are often modeled with hyperelastic strain energy
functions combined with viscoelasticy. Rubber-like materials that exhibit non-linear viscoelastic proper-
ties can be modelled with more complex material models represented by rheological parallel networks. A
Finite Element Analysis (FEA) can be used to predict the behavior of the Insulator in its application, if
a suitable material model and a friction coeﬃcient is provided.
Methods
In this study, diﬀerent TPE samples were manufactured by injection molding. These were tested under
diﬀerent loading and deformation conditions utilizing a universal tensile tester and Digital Image Correla-
tion. Representative test data from the uniaxial static tensile tests were used to calibrate two constitutive
material models with the Abaqus/CAE evaluation method and in MCalibration. The Material models
were evaluated by simulating diﬀerent experimental tests with a one-element 2D FEM.
The ﬁnal material model was assigned to a FEM representing the Insulator in its application. This was
used to deﬁne a friction coeﬃcient by reverse engineering.
Results
The tests revealed that the geometry and the injection molding process have an impact on the mechanical
properties of the TPE. The TPE proved diﬃcult to test in compression due to instability in the manually
carved cuboid samples. Uniaxial tensile tests revealed that the TPE exhibits a non-linear viscoelastic
behavior with pronounced stress relaxation and Mullin’s eﬀect, when strained to levels observed in the
application FEM. It was revealed that a hyperelastic strain energy function was inadequate to model
the reversible part of the non-linear elastic response, when combined with a viscoelastic Prony series to
account for stress relaxation. The more complex PRF-3YPM constitutive material model proved suit-
able to predict to mechanical characteristics of the studied TPE. A FEM representing the application
provided trustworthy predictions with the embedded material model and a simpliﬁed Coulomb’s friction
coeﬃcient of 0.064 found by reverse engineering. Dynamical Mechanical Analyses and FEA simulating
the application demonstrated that the reaction moment, when the Insulator is rotated in a glass tube,
decreases over time as a result of stress relaxation in the Insulator.
Conclusion
This study provided NN Device R&D with a workﬂow on how to test rubber-like materials when a
material model is desired for a FEM. The characterization of the TPE and the ﬁnal FEA with the
embedded calibrated material model revealed that the viscoplastic properties of the TPE raised concerns
in regards to its intended function, as the TPE underwent pronounced stress relaxation when strained.
These ﬁndings lead to the conclusion that the TPE used in the Insulator might be less suitable for the
intended function than previously anticipated. Another TPE with a less pronounced hard thermoplastic
domain could prove more suitable, but should be veriﬁed with future studies.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1
Introduction
Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic, metabolic disease, causing elevated blood glucose levels. The disease af-
fects more than 400 million people worldwide, the prevalence of which is expected to increase drastically
over the next years. The disease is associated with high mortality and every year millions die from the
disease or from its complications.[1] To circumvent complications, it is important that the patients control
their blood glucose levels, which is commonly carried out by performing daily self injections of insulin.
In addition, patients beneﬁt from keeping track of their daily blood glucose level and administered in-
sulin dosage, which can provide valuable insight towards their treatment plan. To accomplish the latter
purpose Novo Nordisk A/S (NN) Device R&D is developing an integrated insulin pen device; the "ICON".
The NN Device R&D department rely highly on Finite Element Analyses (FEA) in device development.
In the ICON, a thermoplastic elastomer (TPE) component is included; the "Insulator". The Insulator is
the main focus in this thesis. During intended use, the ICON rely on the Insulator’s ability to withstand
excessive rotation when it is deformed against a solid glass followed by a force trying to rotate it. The
driving force for NN’s FEA of the ICON is to understand the impact of time variation on the Insulator’s
ability to withstand such rotation. To gain an optimal simulation-based prediction of a such solid it is
essential to understand the underlying mechanical material property dependency of geometry, manufac-
turing process, temperature, and time.
The main goal of this thesis has been to characterize the mechanical properties of the TPE in question
and use representative stress and strain data to calibrate a constitutive material model. This material
model can be used to predict the material behavior of the Insulator geometry in a FEA. In addition,
this industrial thesis provides Novo Nordisk A/S with a workﬂow on how to test similar materials in the
future, when a material model is needed in FEA. Furthermore, NN can use the results presented in this
project to reconsider whether the TPE is the right material for the desired function of the Insulator.
The mechanical properties of the TPE were quantiﬁed by diﬀerent experimental tests performed on in-
jection molded TPE samples. TPEs are relatively soft rubber-like materials with viscoelastic properties.
The commonly used clip-on extensometers would damage such materials and could therefore not be ap-
plied. 3D Digital Image Correlation was used to measure the surface strain as the samples were deformed
in a universal tensile tester; a combinatory technique which was a novel course of action in NN.
Rubber-like materials are commonly modeled with a hyperelastic strain energy function combined with a
linear viscoelastic material model. However, more complex viscoelastic materials often need to be deﬁned
by a more advanced constitutive model to predict the non-linear time dependence.
When modelling the contact between the TPE and a glass, a friction coeﬃcient needs to be deﬁned. This
was found by reverse engineering.

Chapter 1. Introduction
Thesis Aims and Success Criteria
The main aims of the thesis are to:
1. Provide a common improved way to measure the mechanical properties of a TPE with the main
goal of calibrating a material model.
2. Calibrate a material model that can predict the TPE behavior in a simulation.
3. Estimate a friction coeﬃcient that can simulate the Insulator-glass interaction in the ICON appli-
cation.
The main success criteria were:
1. A combination of a universal tensile tester and Digital Image Correlation can be used to characterize
the mechanical properties of the TPE.
2. The material model calibrated can predict the actual material properties of the Insulator in a FEA.
3. The material model and the friction coeﬃcent embedded in a FEM can be used to examine the
reaction moment.
The ﬁndings obtained in this thesis can be used to discuss the main questions which NN Device R&D
strives to answer:
1. Will the TPE undergo excessive stress relaxation over a prolonged shelf life with a similar decrease
in the reaction moment and as a result not be able to withstand an excessive rotation of the ICON
during initial gab? This leads to the ultimate inquiry:
2. Is this material suitable for the desired function in the application?
These questions will be discussed based on the ﬁndings of this thesis.

Background
These sections are aimed to provide the reader with background knowledge relevant to this project. The
chapter will therefore introduce the basics of Thermoplatic Elastomers (TPEs), mechanical properties of
polymers and how these are characterized by diﬀerent experimental testing methods. Furthermore, an
introduction to Finite Element Analysis (FEA) is described, including an outlining of relevant material
models. An introduction to Digital Image Correlation (DIC), used during experimental characterization,
and the associated software Aramis will be elaborated, in addition to a brief outlook on the Injection
Molding method used to manufacture test samples.
Why this speciﬁc material characterization is relevant to Novo Nordisk A/S (NN) is addressed in a
conﬁdential Appendix A describing where the speciﬁc TPE is used and why this project was important
to carry out. The TPE application geometry will be referred to as the Insulator throughout the project.
2.1
Thermoplastic Elastomers
Thermoplastic Elastomers (TPEs) are a class of copolymers consisting of an elastomer physically com-
bined with a thermal plastic.[2] Most commercially used TPEs contain two or more polymeric phases
making the characteristic of each TPE depend on how these phases are mixed. This implies that each in-
dividual polymer that constitute a phase has its own glass transition temperature (Tg) or crystal melting
point (if the polymer is a crystalline). These determine when the TPEs physical properties transition,
typically separated into three regions; 1) At low temperatures, the elastomeric phase and the hard phase
are solid, making the material brittle and stiﬀ. This state depends on the Tg of the elastomer. 2) At
increased temperatures, above the Tg of the elastomeric phase, the elastomer becomes soft and the TPE
acts like a vulcanizate. This region is relatively constant also at further increased temperatures and is
referred to as the rubbery plateau. TPEs at room temperature will have mechanical properties deﬁned
in this region. 3) This changes when the temperature is increased to the Tg or melting temperature
(Tm) of the hard plastic phase where the hard phase softens and transforms the TPE to a ﬂuid. The
mechanically properties of TPE changes drastically at varying temperatures making it very important
to consider the application area of the material. The thermal performance, the chemical and physical
resistance properties, and the weatherability of the TPE depends strongly on the chemical composition
of the phases and their Tg, Tm and chain inconsistency if present.[2]
The advantage of TPEs are that they share physical properties of rubber, including ﬂexibility and re-

2.3
Material Characterization
One of the fundamentals of polymer mechanics is experimental material characterization. Often experi-
mental tests are carried out to characterize speciﬁc aspects of the material structure or response but can
also be used to gain information about material failure. Another main reason for experimental testing is
to obtain mechanical information used to calibrate one or more material models. This thesis focuses on
experimental testing aiming at material model calibration for later use in numerical models.
Numerous diﬀerent testing techniques can be used for this purpose. However, this project focuses on
some of the most common and useful methods used to experimentally characterize the mechanical re-
sponse of polymers. This includes applying a uniaxial load or displacement to a polymer test specimen
to obtain the force-displacement data which can be used to derive the stress-strain relationship of the
given polymer.
When designing such mechanical test it is important to consider that the stress response during the
applied deformation strongly depends on the applied rate of deformation, the load history, the loading
mode, and the temperature.[9] These factors were therefore considered in relation to the application of
the TPE characterized in this project.
Two of the most commonly used experimental test methods to determine the stress-strain response of a
material are uniaxial tension and compression tests. During these tests the samples are loaded (either
pulled or compressed) at a static deformation rate (mm/s) in a universal tensile testing machine, see
Figure 2.3. The two types of tests have diﬀerent strengths and limitations which will be elaborated in
the two following sections.

2.3.1
Uniaxial Compression Test
In a uniaxial compression test, the sample is deformed between two compression plates.
The most
common problem associated with these tests are related to the interface friction occurring between the
test specimen and the compression plates. For a non-zero friction coeﬃcient, the stress-strain in the
test sample becomes inhomogenous caused by an inhomogenous deformation often indicated by a barrel
shape, see Figure 2.4. If there is perfect slippage (zero friction) every element of the test specimen will
be subjected to the same stress and strain and the test sample will keep its shape. The error of the
friction inﬂuence can be found by comparing results from tests with friction to tests without friction.
One common way to reduce friction at the interface is to lubricate the interface with a non-aggressive oil.
Another way is creating a sample with a larger height/diameter ratio. However, if the specimen is too
tall instability can occur during compressive loading, which can cause out-of-center bending. In order to
use experimental compressive data for calibrating a material model the friction coeﬃcient needs to be
known or estimated.[6][9]
Figure 2.4: A cubic sample compressed between two plates with and without friction at the interface.
Inspired by Bergström J. S. et al [9].
2.3.2
Uniaxial Tensile Test
In a uniaxial tensile test, the specimen is ﬁxated between two grips in the tensile tester applying a load
to the specimen by moving the grips apart with a static deformation rate. When testing rubber-like
materials, it is essential that these grips tighten automatically when the tension is increased to avoid
slippage as the sample cross section decreases when strained. One mechanical design solving this is using
a grip design consisting of a roller against a ﬂat plate where the sample is inserted in between. During
the tensile tests, the grips will always introduce a concentrated stress in the test specimen close to the
grips. To reduce this inﬂuence, the test specimens are typically given a dogbone shape (or dumbbell
shape). This design will create a uniform stress and strain state in the known central gauge region of the
dogbone geometry.[6][9]

Rate Dependent Tests
The time-dependent material behavior of viscoelastic materials also means that the stress-strain response
is rate-dependent.[5] This property can be determined by applying a static tensile or compressive load
at varying deformation rates. Such tests will illustrate that when applying increasing strain rates, the
stress increases with time, resulting in an increased stiﬀness behavior.

Cyclic Tensile Test
When viscoelastic materials are loaded and unloaded repeatedly, they will respond in a hysteresis loop,
see Figure 2.6. The Figure illustrates how the stress-strain curve during loading and unloading vary from

energy. The diﬀerence between these areas, ∆AUC, is the heat energy lost during the cyclic deformation.
This loss of energy arises form the frictional sliding of the long polymer chains against one another and
is referred to as damping or hysteresis. The increased straining can cause some damage (permanent
deformation) of the molecule chains in the polymer called Mullin’s eﬀect. If damage takes place in the

2.4
Finite Element Analysis
The sections above introduce diﬀerent experimental tests which can be used to quantify the mechanical
response of a polymer. The numerical data obtained from the tests can in addition be used to select and
calibrate a material model to be used in Finite Element Analysis (FEA).[9]
Finite Element (FE) methods make it possible to analyze complex geometries that can be diﬃcult to
solve using traditional closed-formed analytical techniques. FEA’s ability to solve advanced problems
can reduce cost and time consuming experimental tests.[9] FE models basically need three types of in-
puts: 1) the geometry, 2) the loading and boundary conditions, and 3) the speciﬁc material behavior, see
Figure 2.7. The latter is determined by a material model deﬁned by a constitutive material model and
the respective material parameters. The geometry and the applied loading are often well deﬁned inputs,
however the material behavior can be diﬃcult to capture accurately when considering complex, advanced
materials like elastomers and TPEs.

Material Models
Most FE software contain diﬀerent constitutive models. However, only a limited amount of these are
suitable when predicting the exact deformation behavior of polymers and especially more complex poly-
mers like TPEs.[9] It is often necessary to consider existing tools created to calibrate advanced material
models from diﬀerent real experimental data. These include tools already existing in FE software, Isight
(Dassault Systems Simulia corp., USA Rhode Island) and MCalibration (Veryst Engineering, USA, Need-
ham).[12] This project focuses on Abaqus/CAE (Complete Abaqus Environment) (Dassault Systemes
Simulia Corp.) material evaluation method and MCalibration as these two material model calibration
methods are widely used in the NN department from where the project was carried out. Abaqus/CAE
was in addition used to simulate the material behavior of the TPE as Abaqus is an universal FE software
application used to model and analyze mechanical components or assembles.
When using the simulations in the industry it is important to verify and validate the material mod-
els to increase the accuracy of the results. Validating the material model is the process of ensuring that
the chosen model match the response of the real material suﬃciently. However, this is often limited to a
certain range of strain-rates and temperatures.[9]
When characterizing the material, aiming to calibrate a material model, it is important to perform
the tests within realistic strain levels. It is in addition important to match the load history and the
pre-conditions of the real component to the test specimen. Furthermore, it is essential to test at de-
formation rates consistent with the real component in the used situation and at consistent deformation
rates for each deformation mode (could be tension and compression). TPE’s material properties are
temperature-dependent, making it crucial to test the material at the temperature required in the simula-
tion. In addition, the tested material should have the same process history as the actual part, since the
process method can inﬂuence the mechanical behavior of the material.[5] Viscoelastic materials are time-
dependent why it is essential to perform tests within a realistic time span or gather enough numerical
data to extrapolate over time. In general, one can obtain a more precise material deﬁnition by gathering
several types of deformation test data. This will provide more information about the material which can
be used to verify the chosen material model.
2.5
Material Models
The mechanical reversible behavior of solid polymers can often be predicted by two classes of constitutive
models; linear elasticity and non-linear elasticity (also referred to as hyperelasticity). These are used
in analyses where the eﬀect of time can be neglected. That is for an elastic material the constitutive
equation is independent of time, meaning there is no dependence on the rate of loading or deformation
and that the unloading stress-strain path is the same as the loading path.
To ﬁnd a model that can predict viscoelastic behavior, linear and non-linear elastic models can be com-
bined with a model considering the eﬀect of time during a loading period.[13] These models include linear
and non-linear time-dependent (viscoelastic) models.
TPEs exhibit both elastic and time-dependent behavior during deformation, why hyperelasticity and
viscoelasticity will be described in the following sections.

2.5.2
Linear Viscoelasticity
Viscoelastic materials are inhomogenous materials with characteristics from elastic materials and new-
tonian viscous ﬂuids. In general, elastic materials have the capacity to store mechanical energy with
no energy dissipation where newtonian viscous ﬂuids can dissipate energy but can not store it. Hence
viscoelastic materials have the capacity to store and dissipate mechanical energy. In addition, such ma-
terials are said to have "memory"; meaning they can return to their original state when deformed to a
certain range.[18] In general viscoelastic materials exhibit deformation rate dependency; the faster they
are stretched the larger a stress is required to reach the same deformation. Linear and non-linaer vis-
coelasticity is therefore deﬁned as the study of materials that are time dependent which is widely applied
when modelling polymers.

2.5.3
Non-linear Viscoelasticity
The Maxwell and the Kelvin models, with linear expressions for the dash-pot and the spring, represent
the most simple viscoelastic models, and therefore do not represent viscoelastic material responses in
the most realistic way. The TPE has a more complicated viscoelastic behavior that exhibit a non-linear
relaxation rate which is depending on the strain magnitude. More realistic responses of TPEs could
therefore be modelled by replacing the linear spring and dash-pot with non-linear stress-strain relations
and further by combing more elements.
The Parallel Rheological Framework (PRF) model is a deﬁnition of a non-linear viscoelastic-elastoplastic
material model that consists of multiple networks connected in parallel. An illustration of the model can
be seen in Figure 2.12. The PRF model can be used to predict the behavior of complex materials exposed
to a ﬁnite strain which can be diﬃcult to model using the other models available in Abaqus.
Figure 2.12: A nonlinear viscoelastic-elastoplastic rheological model with multiple parallel networks.[20]
The spring and the dash-pot represent a viscoelastic network, N. The number of these networks in the
material model can be arbitrary. Network 0 represents a equilibrium network which can be purely elas-
tic (only a spring) or elastoplastic (a spring and a plastic as shown here). The elastic response of the
networks are described by any of the hyperelastic material models available in Abaqus. The same hy-
perelastic material deﬁnition is used for all networks, which is scaled by a stiﬀness ratio, that is speciﬁc
for each network. The elastic response is speciﬁed by deﬁning an instantaneous- or a long-term response.
Mullins eﬀect can in addition be included to predict material softening.[20]

networks with Power-law ﬂow and Mullins softening (PRF-3YPM). This material model is available in
Abaqus and is a special case of the PRF model. It has a relative simple parallel network structure that
can be suitable for some polymers. The model consists of 3 networks with 21 material parameters in total.
These include the hyperelastic Yeoh parameters, C10, C20, and C30, mullins parameters, and non-linear
viscoelastic parameters with stiﬀness scale factors that specify how much stiﬀer the viscoelastic networks
(Network 1 and 2) are compared to the hyperelastic equilibrium response (Network 3).[21]
2.6
Material Model Calibration in Abaqus
Abaqus/CAE has an ’Evaluate’ option that displays the behavior predicted by a hyperelastic or vis-
coelastic material and from where a suitable material model can be selected. The ’Evaluation’ option is
particularly useful when comparing test data with the behavior predicted by strain energy potentials. If
a hyperelastic material is deﬁned by experimental data and no strain energy potential is yet speciﬁed,
the evaluation option allows one to perform standard tests with the experimental data using multiple
strain energy potentials. Abaqus then calculates the material responses based on the experimental data
using the selected strain energy potentials. When the test is completed, Abaqus/CAE displays a plot
with the experimental data and a curve for each evaluated strain energy potential. In addition, a dialog
box displays the stability limits and coeﬃcients for each strain energy potential.
If any of the built-in strain energy potentials seem inadequate, the quality (accuracy and stability)
of the hyperelastic ﬁt can be tried optimized by using either compressive or tension data, as it can be
diﬃcult to ﬁt a single material model to both accurately. Another approach can be to delete some data
points at very low strains if large strains are anticipated or delete data points at high strains if small
strains are expected in the application. When large strains are expected, low strain points can bias the
accuracy of the ﬁt toward low strains, what can cause errors on the large strain range. When anticipat-
ing a small strain range, high strain points can cause the ﬁt to lose accuracy and/or stability at low strains.
The quality of selected material model behavior must be assessed.
This can be done by predicting
the material behavior under diﬀerent deformation modes and compare this with experimental data. This
can be done by creating a single-element test case, which can be used to derive the stress-strain response
of the material model. From this the correlation between the Abaqus prediction and the experimental
data can be determined to evaluate whether it is acceptable.[13]
When establishing a strain energy potential it is also important to consider the stability of the model
responses, called the Drucker stability. Abaqus checks the Drucker stability of the material for diﬀerent
deformation modes (uniaxial tension and compression, equibiaxial tension and compression, and planar
tension and compression) for nominal strain ranges of -0.9 ≤ε1 ≤0.9 at intervals Σλ1 = 0.01.[13] If an
instability is found, Abaqus prints the lowest absolute value of ε1 for which the instability is observed.
Ideally no instability should take place. If an instability is found within the strain levels that are likely
to occur in the actual analysis of the component, it would be wise to select another material model.
Abaqus assumes that viscoelastic materials are deﬁned by a Prony series expansion of the dimensionless
relaxation modulus. The relaxation parameters can be deﬁned by directly specifying the Prony series
parameters or by including creep test data, relaxation test data, or frequency-dependent data.

2.8
Digital Image Correlation
Digital Image Correlation (DIC) is a non-contact optical method used to measure and evaluate surface
motion and deformation in a 3D plane. DIC enables an analytical analysis of the behavior of any material
specimen by creating stochastic patterns on the area of interest, which then can be determined by subpixel
accuracy.[23] The specimen area to be analyzed is manually speciﬁed and will be divided into a virtual
grid from where the displacement of the center point is measured. This is done by a software that divides
the desired area at the initial state of the specimen into squares (facets) with diﬀerent speciﬁc greyscale
patterns. When the specimen is deformed, the greyscale pattern will be displaced likewise, see Figure
2.13. The DIC software will then track the initial greyscale pattern when it starts to deform. The facets
deform by rotation and translations in regards to a global coordinate system P(x,y). The local coordinate
system of the facet P(x,y) vary from the coordinate system in the deformed state P’(x’,y’), see Figure
2.14. To ﬁnd the center point P’ when the specimen deforms the following equation is used[24]:
P ′ = u + P · F
(32)
where P is the initial state center point coordinate, u is the translation of the rigid body, F is the
deformation gradient tensor deﬁned by:
F = U · R∗
(33)
where R* is the rotation matrix and U is the stretch tensor. The deformation of the facet is described
by U and the rotation and the direction of the point is described by R*.[25][24]

When creating the surface pattern on the specimen, it is important to achieve a high pixel contrast so
the pixels in the initial state can be to distinguish from pixels in the deformed state. In addition, the
patterns should be randomly distributed and not contain large areas with dark or light spots what would
make it impossible to tell neighboring facets apart.[25][24]
DIC can be used as an alternative to widely used displacement measurement methods, including clip-on
extensometers and laser extensometers. Both latter methods have advantages of being low cost methods
with high accuracy. The two methods can also provide a very high sampling rate compared to DIC
(controlled by the frame rate). In addition, the data analysis process is more simple compared to DIC.
The DIC method however has several advantages in multiple applications.[9] Most importantly, the clip-on
extensometers contain the risk of damaging rubber-like materials, which is avoided by using non-contact
methods. Furthermore, non-contact methods have the ability to measure very high strains often seen
when testing rubber-like materials, where clip-on extensometers are limited to small deformations (1 mm
- 10 mm). DIC is in addition able to measure transverse strains during uniaxial compression or tension
tests.
This makes it possible to measure Poisson’s ratio of a rubber-like material where the lateral
contraction is close to half of the tensile strain. This would not be possible with laser extensometers and
if clip-on extensometers were to be used, this could again damage a soft material.[6] Both alternative
methods are in addition only able to measure the average strain in the tensile direction where the DIC
method can measure strain in points and in full ﬁelds. This means that DIC is able to measure any
localized deformation state which can occur during tensile testing of polymers.[9]

torsion, tension, bending, or compression. Such measurements combined in real time can enable exact

2.10
Moment & Coeﬃcient of Friction
Torque, τ, (sometimes referred to as moment) is the measure of the force that causes an object to rotate
about an axis. Torque is a vector quantity where the direction of the torque vector depends on the
direction of the force on the axis. Torque can be static or dynamic; static torque does not produce an
angular acceleration (the object is not rotating when a force is applied), whereas dynamic torque produce
an angular acceleration. The radius at which the force acts is referred to as the moment arm. The torque
vector for a torque created by a given force, F, is deﬁned as:
τ = F · rsin(θ)
(34)
where r is the length of the moment arm and θ is the angle between the force vector and the moment
arm. When the force angle is 90◦to the moment arm the term becomes[29]:
τ = F · r
(35)
The reaction moment is the equal moment that the object exerts back when a force is applied to the
object trying to rotate it:
τr = Ft · r
(36)
Where τr is the reaction moment, Ft is the tangential force.
Torque can be aﬀected by an opposing force that results from a resistant surface. This opposing force is
referred to as friction.[30] In the context of continuum mechanics, Coulomb’s law of friction (also termed
dry friction) is widely used for contact problems.
This law states that when two bodies are pressed
together under action of a normal force, FN, the shear force, V, which causes the relative sliding of one
object on the other, is directly proportional to FN. This implies that the direction of the relative sliding
of the object on the other and the direction of V on the object are the same. The shear force is therefore
also referred to as the frictional force, Ff. The proportionality constant is called the friction coeﬃcient,
µ. The friction coeﬃcient at the initiation of slipping from a clamped condition is often diﬀerent from
the friction coeﬃcient during established sliding. These are referred to as the static friction coeﬃcient,
µs, and the kinetic friction coeﬃcient, µs, respectively.[31]

Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
As a part of the material characterization, the TPE was analyzed with Fourier-transform infrared spec-

Phase 2: Material Model Calibration
3.5
Material Model Calibration in Abaqus
Choosing a suitable material model in Abaqus involved selecting a strain energy potential to model the
reversible part of the non-linear characteristic and establishing Prony series parameters to model the
non-reversible part in a time domain. This was done in Abaqus/CAE’s material evaluation capability.
This method ﬁts the experimental data with the response curves from diﬀerent strain energy potentials
and Prony series parameters, as described in Section 2.6. The evaluation was performed by ﬁrst creating
a material in Abaqus/CAE with an isotropic hyperelastic material behavior. In this, a representative
uniaxial tensile test data set (engineering stress and strain) was embedded. The dogbone sample ge-
ometry was the closest to the Insulator geometry and the middle deformation speed tested (0.3 mm/s)
came closest to the expected applicable deformation speed. Therefore, a representative test data set from
this test was used to calibrate a hyperelastic strain energy potential. The material was then combined
with a viscoelastic material behavior deﬁned in a time domain, where the measured time and normalized
engineering stress from an experimental uniaxial relaxation test was imported as "shear test data".
The hyperelastic/viscoelastic material behavior was evaluated ("Evaluate Material") by specifying a stan-
dard uniaxial test setup creating a stress-strain response plot from 0.0 to 0.6 strain and choosing diﬀerent
strain energy potentials. In addition, a viscoelastic evaluation was performed creating a normalized re-
sponse plot in a Stress Relaxation Response Mode over at time period of 177000 seconds (48 hours). The
normalized stress was speciﬁed as the normalized shear relaxation modulus, gR(t) over the measured
time (which needs to satisfy that t > 0).[36] The stress-strain response curves were plotted along with
a table outlining the material parameters and the stability limit information. From visual judgement
of the stress-strain curves and from the criteria; the hyperelastic material model needed to be stable in
uniaxial tension at nominal strains up to at least 0.3 and in uniaxial compression at a nominal strain
down to at least -0.3 (due to application), a strain energy potential was selected. This selected strain
energy potential and its parameters alongside the Prony parameters were assigned to a 2D model, which
will be described in Section 3.7.

3.7
Finite Element Validation Model
A 2D FE model of a quadratic plate measuring 1 mm x 1 mm x 1 mm was created in Abaqus/CAE.
This one-element model was created to represent any segment of the tested samples making it easy to
compare the experimental data with simulation output.
The initial step in creating the model was deﬁning the geometrical structure. This was done by creating
a 2D deformable solid part measuring 1 mm x 1 mm. Afterwards, a material was created deﬁning the
material behavior. The properties of interest were found in Mechanical →Elasticity where hyperelastic
(isotropic) and viscoelastic (with instantaneous response) were selected. It is in this step the material
model parameters are implemented. A solid homogeneous section was created for the established material
with a plane stress/strain thickness of 1 mm. This section was assigned to the model and ﬁve sets were
created choosing each edge and one point (x0, y0, x1, y1, and xy0) as shown in Figure 3.11. A load
step was created of the type visco where the large-deformation theory was used by setting NLGEOM to
"on" in the step command. This will include the non-linear geometric eﬀect of deformation.11 The edges
were then seeded to create a one-element mesh. A Plane stress element type was used since the stress
distribution throughout the thickness of the 2D model is assumed to be zero and as there is a strain in
the thickness direction (perpendicular to the force) due to the Poisson eﬀect. The model was deformed
in the x direction by deﬁning the boundary conditions, u1 = 0 for x=0 and u2 = 0 in the point (x,y) =
(0,0). The load during uniaxial tension was controlled by deﬁning the deﬂection, δ, along x = 1 dictating
the boundary condition u1 = δ. The type of the selected steps when deﬁning the boundary conditions
were set to Displacement/Rotation. A simple schematic of the model can be seen in Figure 3.11.
Figure 3.11: A schematic of the 2D FEM model created in Abaqus/CAE.
This FE geometry having a cross section area of 1 mm2, makes it easy to convert deformation and force
to engineering stress and strain. Engineering stress will be equivalent to the force applied and engineering
strain to the deformation.

Phase 3: Friction Coeﬃcient
3.8
Dynamic Mechanical Analysis
DMA tests were carried out by Janus Helbo (Advanced R&D Engineer in Device R&D). The DMA tests
were carried out to measure the resulting torque as the ICON with the integrated Insulator module, is
forced through a 45 mm long glass tube in a translational and rotational movement, resembling the actual
application. When the device is inserted in the glass tube the Insulator will be deformed against the
glass, implying deﬂecting and compression of the Insulator. When it is moved through the glass it will
therefore encounter a resistance caused by the friction developed at the interface. The resulting torque
measurements were used in reverse engineering to establish a friction coeﬃcient in the ﬁnal FEA of the
Insulator with the assigned material model.
Two diﬀerent test procedures were carried out as follows;
1) One test was performed by inserting the device in the glass tube, causing the Insulator to be deformed
against the glass surface. It was then moved translational and rotational (with 5 rpm) down through

samples to have an decreasing stiﬀness furthest from the inlet as the cross section area remains constant
over the entire gauge length.

1) The ﬁrst involves the injection molding process settings. The two geometries were packed with two
diﬀerent packing pressures, which as described earlier will decrease over the length of the mold. The
beams were molded with a packing pressure of 450 barr where the dogbones were packed with 500 barr.
However, this does not imply that the ﬁnal geometry was packed with this exact pressure, as the pressure
already decreases over the coldrunner (inlet). The coldrunner was diﬀerent from the two samples, the
beams having a much shorter and thicker coldrunner. This could imply that the beams were packed
with a higher pressure what would make sense as a high pressure is needed to compensate for shrinkage
especially when dealing with a large specimen. When injection molding the smaller dogbones with a
somehow similar packing pressure, a higher ﬁlling pressure in the mold can be achieved than that of the
beams. This means that the material gets more compressed in the smaller mold cavity and the sample
gain a higher density. Another diﬀerence during the injection molding process of the two geometries
was the size of the screw used. These varying process parameters make it diﬃcult to conclude what the
largest impact value is on the material density.
2) Another important impacting eﬀect is a varying molecular orientation in the diﬀerent sample ge-
ometries. For long slim samples, like the dogbones, the long polymer chains tend to align in the ﬂow
direction during injection molding. For more bulk samples, like the beams, the chains are orientated in
a more tangled mess, twisted and wrapped around each other. This is mainly a result of the cooling
process during the molding, causing the long polymer chains to solidify close to the mold cavity wall.
The polymer chains will as a result be stretched and aligned in the ﬂow direction as they are pulled along
with the still ﬂuid polymer. This would have a much higher impact on the slim dogbone samples.
When a tensile force is applied to the samples, the polymer chains are stretched out. If they are aligned
in advance, they are unable to unfold to the same extend compared to polymer chains that are initially
tangled up in a complete mess. This will as a result cause the material to behave more stiﬀ.
In summary, the geometry of the samples has an impact on the mechanical properties. This is thought
to be caused by 1) the injection molding process, which was signiﬁcantly diﬀerent during the production
of the diﬀerent test samples, and 2) by the molecular orientation in the diﬀerent bulk geometries.

Phase 2: Material Model
4.11
Experimental Tests Used for Material Model Calibration
The diﬀerent compression tests revealed obvious complications, what let to the conclusion that the tensile
tests were more trustworthy and were therefore used to ﬁt a constitutive material model. This implies an
assumption that the TPE is isotropic and ’compression independent’, what means that the stress-strain
behavior is similar in tension and in compression, within the strain levels used in this project. This
will be discussed further in Section 5. In addition, it has been demonstrated in literature that tensile
experimental data can be used to predict approximate rubbery behavior in compression.[37][38]
The experimental tests used to calibrate a material model was initially the following: 1) A representative
sample from the static tensile tests of dogbones deformed with 0.3 mm/s. This was used to model the
reversible part of the non-linear characteristics. 2) The test data from the tensile stress relaxation test
over 48 hours, which was used to establish the non-reversible part by a Prony series.
4.12
Material Model Calibration - Abaqus Evaluation
A material model was ﬁrst tried established using the Abaqus/CAE evaluation method. This was carried
out in two steps. In the ﬁrst step, a hyperelastic model without any time eﬀects was considered. In the
second step, a viscoelastic material model was included in the model.
In the application local strains varies between -30% to 30% (read from the Abaqus model of the Insulator
when deformed). Finding a data-ﬁt that is stable at larger strain areas would make this model applicable
for other model geometries. However, establishing a strain energy function that was stable within the
measured strain ranges was of highest priority in this method.

5
Discussion
This chapter includes an interpretation of the experimental results obtained from the material charac-
terization. It is discussed whether the ﬁndings can be vindicated to represent the actual geometry and
therefore used to calibrate a material model capable of predicting the behavior of the TPE in the ﬁnal
FEA. In addition, the diﬀerent methods used during each phase of the project will be discussed, focusing
on their potentials, limitations, and future optimization. Conclusively, the results from the DMA tests
and ﬁnal FEA will be discussed, focusing on the intended requirement of the Insulator.
Phase 1: Material Characterization
Aim of Phase 1: Characterize the TPE under varying loading and deformation conditions.
Success Criteria: Collect representative numerical data that can be used to calibrate a constitutive
material model.
Contributions to NN: In NN, structural complicated polymers are often included in devices. Improv-
ing the current method of characterising similar materials, with the aim of calibrating a material model,
can prove beneﬁcial and proﬁtable. Presenting a thorough method applicable for future studies of similar
materials would enable optimization of future product development processes.
In general, when characterising a material, aiming towards calibrating a material model, it is important
to match the real component’s deformation history, temperature, time span, the manufacturing process
to the test sample. A discussion on how this was interpreted and the consequences of the necessary
assumptions will be reviewed in the following sections.
5.1
Temperature Fluctuations
TPE’s mechanical properties depend strongly on temperature. All strength properties decrease as the
temperature increases above ambient temperature, while the rate of creep and relaxation increases. The
degree to which temperature aﬀects the properties varies between diﬀerent types of TPEs. However, in
general the degree of change with temperature is considerably higher than with conventional elastomeric
compounds.[40] For this reason, it was crucial to establish that the temperature did not deviate signiﬁ-
cantly during the testing.
A minor diﬀerence in the average temperature was found on samples before and after being exposed to
the LED light from the DIC setup. This was deemed insigniﬁcant to inﬂuence the mechanical properties.
However, this should be taken into consideration if tests including DIC were to be carried out over a
longer time period in the future. In such tests, the LED lights could heat up the TPE to a greater extend
and inﬂuence the mechanical properties.
5.2
Injection Molding Process Impact
The calibrated material models were based on numerical stress-strain data from the dogbone samples. It
was assumed that the Insulator geometry share similar mechanical properties as the dogbones. However,
it was demonstrated that the injection molding process and the geometry of the samples had an impact
on the mechanical properties.
To recap; the packing pressure during injection molding inﬂuences the density of a polymer sample. This
pressure decreases over the mold cavity length with a larger eﬀect when the bulk size is increased. In
75
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 5. Discussion
Test Evaluation
addition, it decreases more over a longer coldrunner (inlet). From this knowledge, it was hypothesized
that the varying strain, seen over the gauge length of the test samples, were caused by an increasing TPE
density towards the injection molding inlet. This was supported by the varying stiﬀness measured in
diﬀerent regions of the dogbone samples. In addition, Poisson’s ratio in the diﬀerent areas of the gauge
length revealed no signiﬁcant variance. This proved that no necking contributed to increased localized
strain measurements. The observed diﬀerence in the stress-strain behavior of the diﬀerent geometries
(dogbones and beams) was also attributed to the injection molding process and their varying polymer
chain orientations as a result of their bulk geometry, as described in Section 4.4.
These factors can also have an impact on the Insulator geometry, which is signiﬁcantly smaller than the
dogbone sample. The decreasing packing pressure over the length of the mold cavity, causing a varying
strain, is thought to have a smaller impact on the small Insulator. However, the observed geometry de-
pendency could have an impact on the stiﬀness response of the insulator. It could be hypothesized that
an even smaller geometry will exhibit a more stiﬀresponse under deformation. However, it was diﬃcult
to predict to what extend this will have an impact without knowing the injection molding process of
the Insulator, including the packing pressure, the coldrunner length, and the screw used. Furthermore,
the Insulator is two-component injection molded, which makes the entire molding method diﬀerent from
the samples tested in this project. As TPEs are anisotropic materials, the mechanical properties will
depend on the orientation of the molecules. The orientation of the molecules depend on the angle of the
injection of the molten polymer. This means that the injection molding angle also aﬀect the mechanical
properties of the TPE.[41] All these contributing settings make it diﬃcult to predict the process and
geometry inﬂuence on the mechanical properties of the Insulator.
The ﬁnal goal of this thesis and the ultimate inquiry from NN was to examine the long term response of
a deformation, to evaluate the desired function of the Insulator. For this, the mechanical properties of
the dogbones were a proper representation of the properties of the Insulator.
5.3
Test Evaluation
Tensile Strain Rate Assumptions
The Insulator is deformed at diﬀerent deformation rates during the application, depending on whether
it is during the insertion into the glass tube or when pushed through the glass tube. The velocity of the
insertion is not established at this point of the device development. However, it is known to be much
higher than the deformations rates tested in this project. The velocity at which it is forced through the
glass tube is close to constant, where the interest lies in the relaxation over time and not the deformation
rate. Hence, the goal was not to perform tests within the exact deformation rates seen in the application.
However, tensile and compression tests were performed at three diﬀerent deformation rates to explore
the deformation rate dependencies in the TPE. The tests can be used to verify if a material model is
able to predict the varying stiﬀness response. These deformation rates were limited to the DIC method’s
maximum frame rate and the quality of the speckle pattern. It was observed that even with the fastest
frame rate used in this project the DIC was not able to capture the upload in the stress relaxation tests,
which was signiﬁcantly faster than the static deformation tests.
Tensile Test Grip Evaluation
A minor obstacle during the tensile tests was slippage of the beams in the grips. The large bulk volume
proved to be more diﬃcult to ﬁxate in the grips. This was improved by moving the grips closer together
initially (still within the grip length) and tightening the grips thoroughly. However, tightening the grips

Compression Test Limits
The compression tests proved to be overall problematic. First of all, cutting the injection molded beam
samples into cuboids created some variance among the cuboids, as the edges are not completely even.
This is due to the known diﬃculties of cutting nearly incompressible rubber, as the material will be
compressed and change shape when a force from a knife is applied. This could have been avoided by
creating injection molded samples of the ﬁnal cuboid geometry.
The main problem occurred when the cuboids were deformed in compression. This caused instability in
the samples, which resulted in unreliable test data. The shape of the cuboid samples, having half the
width of the height, could be a contributing factor to the observed bending. It is possible that another ge-
ometry could have improved the test method. This could be a cubic shape or a cylinder as recommended
in ISO-7743 or ASTM-575. However, compression tests of rubber-like materials have been stated overall
problematic, even when using above mentioned geometries.
Suggested method improvement involves
lubricating the compression plates to obtain close to zero friction at the interface.[9] Another suggested
approach is inserting thin Teﬂon foil sheets between the specimen surfaces and the compression plates.[42]
This was predicted to cause other problems, including sliding during deformation, especially when testing
samples with uneven edges, like the cuboids. These actions were therefore not carried out.
The tensile tests showed reliable results, consistent with the mechanical response of rubbers found in
literature.[12][10][43] As a result, the tensile tests of the dogbones were used to calibrate the material
model in this thesis. This was done under the assumption that the TPE is isotropic and independent of
compression within the examined strain range (here focusing on strains around 30%). This means, it was
presumed that the stress-strain behavior was the same in compression and tension within these strain
ranges. The TPE had a Poisson’s ratio close to 0.5, which veriﬁes that the TPE exhibits a mechanical
behavior similar to rubber materials, which behave similar in compression and in tension until a certain
strain level.
Stress Relaxation Evaluation
The deformation modes of the Insulator in its application can be consistent for up to two years of stor-
age. Therefore, the stress relaxation phenomenon was crucial to examine. This could provide relevant
information in regards to the Insulator’s intended function. In addition, a stress relaxation test was used
to calibrate the non-reversible part of the TPE.
The stress relaxation tests carried out revealed that the TPE was less rubber-like than expected. This was
compared to earlier used rubbers in other devices from NN, like butyl rubber. However, the experimental
ﬁndings are consistent with literature, which states that the stress relaxation rate in general is greater for
TPEs than for conventional thermoset rubbers (like butyl rubber), even at ambient temperatures.[40][44]
The properties of TPEs are highly inﬂuenced by their thermoplastic nature. This implies that the me-
chanical properties of TPEs have a considerable dependence on time and temperature in contrast to
vulcanized rubber. A study carried out by LE H.H. et. al [45] examined the stress relaxation behavior
of diﬀerent TPEs. They found that the structural diﬀerences in TPEs lead to diﬀerences in their stress

relaxation behavior. This diﬀerence dependeds on their hard theromplastic domain. The Styrene-based
TPE (SEBS) tested in their study underwent increasing stress relaxation over time compared to the four
other TPEs examined.[45] Styrene-based TPEs, like the TPE examined in this study, are likely to have
a pronounced hard domain, leading to a pronounced time dependence. If the TPE undergoes excessive
stress relaxation over time, there is a risk of the Insulator not working as intended. Therefore, these

The TPE examined in the present study consists of other compounds, such as chalk and silicone in un-

The numerical stress data were normalized after the upload and overshoot was sorted out of the data.

by force per second. By increasing the maximum limit of the load cell to 98 N, a greater deformation rate
could be accomplished by deforming the samples with 98 N/s. This was carried out in the compressive

be uploaded with the maximum load achieved by the load cell per second.
The tensile relaxation test, used to ﬁt the material model, was measured without DIC. The strain was
therefore correlated from extension. Although capable of estimating the strain, this could lead to some
uncertainty. In addition, only one test was carried out due to the lock down situation at the time of testing.
It was therefore not established if this one test was representative, remaining for future studies. However,
the test seemed to be in acceptable correlation with the remaining stress relaxation tests performed.
5.4
Digital Image Correlation Method Evaluation
The DIC method was a novel way to characterize a material in NN Device R&D. This method was used
due to the risk of damaging the TPE with conventional clip-on extensometers. The non-contact laser
extensometer method was also deemed inadequate, as the samples were tested in compression with very
small deformations and a known risk of instability. In addition, Poisson’s ratio could be measured with
ease using the DIC method.
The drawbacks of the DIC method were that it is a time consuming data acquisition technique and it
required extended practice. The DIC was very sensitive to the calibration and to the speckle pattern
on the sample surface. Research from the pilot study revealed that a black painted background with a

same day of the experiment to prevent cracking, which was prone to occur when dried over night. After
implementing this speckle pattern, the DIC accurately captured sample straining during the tests.
The thickness of the spray paint layer cannot be controlled completely. With practice this should not
cause signiﬁcant measurement deviations. However, when covering the sample completely, there is a risk
of the paint having an inﬂuence on the mechanical properties. As the paint has diﬀerent strength prop-
erties, a covering layer could be load-bearing to some extend. In contrast to not providing a suﬃcient

Phase 2: Material Model Calibration
Aim of Phase 2: Calibrate a material model that can predict the mechanical behavior of the TPE.
Success Criteria: The material model is adequate in predicting the behavior of the actual geometry in
a FEA with desirable approximation.
Contribution to NN: The current simpliﬁed material model can only provide rough estimates on the
expected response of the Insulator. An improved material model will enable more accurate predictions,
which can be used in a FEA to evaluate the Insulator.
A constitutive material model was initially calibrated in Abaqus/CAE, as this is a common procedure in
NN to model elasto-plastic materials. In addition, it is a prevalent method used in literature.[10][16] The
results found in Phase 2 revealed that a hyperelastic strain energy function combined with a viscoelastic
Prony model was inadequate to model the TPE. The third order Ogden hyperelastic material model
alone proved suﬃcient to predict the reversible behavior of the one static tensile test used for the ﬁt.
However, the hyperelastic material models would be incapable of predicting the compressive dependency.
In addition, when combined with the linear viscoelastic Prony model it proved inadequate to predict the
tensile behaviour of the TPE.
It was discussed in Section 4.12.2 how this could be inﬂuenced by the non-instantaneous data used to
calibrate the material model. Using the instantaneous material response in Abaqus/CAE require a model
calibrated from test data representing the highest stiﬀness response the material is able to exhibit. How-
ever, this cannot be carried out in practice. In addition, it is unspeciﬁc how fast such a test should be
carried out to avoid any relaxation occurring during the upload.
Even if approximate instantaneous tests could be performed, it was found that the TPE material response
should be modelled with a more complex material model. This was based on the observed non-linear
time dependent response. In addition, linear viscoelastic models cannot predict plastic or viscoplastic
deformations. Hence, linear viscoelasticity is not always capable of predicting the viscoelastic response of
all elastomers accurately.[9] The TPE proved to exhibit time-dependent irreversible strains (viscoplastic
deformation) and the behavior will therefore not be well predicted by a linear viscoelastic model. This
was also stated and demonstrated in a study by Jörgen Bergström.[9]. In his studies, he demonstrated
that linear viscoelasticity was inadequate to predict the response of a chloroprene rubber accurately. The
reason being that the material is non-linear viscoelastic. This support that linear viscoelasticity should

mainly be used to predict the response of elastomer-like materials and to a lesser degree the response of
thermoplastic materials.
A more suitable material model for predicting the data obtained for the TPE was therefore a more com-
plex constitutive model, which could predict the non-linear time dependent response and estimate the
eﬀect of Mullin’s damage. The downside of choosing a complex material model is that it requires more
experimental data for proper calibration and it required the additional MCalibration software.
The PRF-3YPM material model calibrated in MCalibration provided proper quantitative predictions of
the TPEs mechanical behavior. The model was able to predict the non-linear behavior in uniaxial tension
at three diﬀerent strain rates and cyclic upload and unload reasonably well. The stress relaxation rate
predicted deviated slightly within the ﬁrst minutes after being strained. However, the long term response
is of most importance in this study where the model was able to provide accurate predictions.
An advantage when using the MCalibration tool is the ability to assign the diﬀerent tests varying weight
value during the calibration. In this project, the relaxation should be weighted high compared to the
cyclic test used to calibrate the model. In addition, virtual experiments can be performed, making it
easy to verify a material model.
In this project, the material models were veriﬁed by simulating experimental tests in a 2D FEM. This
allowed deformation simulations more similar to the experimental data compared to virtual experiments
carried out in MCalibration. The 2D one-element FEM proved very advantageous and provided overall
valuable simulations, which could be used to examine the quality of material models.
Limitations of Material Model
The data used in these models were tested under room temperature (∼23◦C). The material model will
therefore not be valid for other temperatures. However, as the prominent deformation and resulting
relaxation takes place in 23 ◦C when the ICON is inserted in the glass tube, the tests were conducted at
room temperature as a simpliﬁcation.
The stress relaxation measured in this study was performed over a signiﬁcantly shorter time than what
can be expected from the actual application. The stress relaxation over a longer time period should be
tested or extrapolated from the numerical data. A proper FEM would be able to extrapolate the time
and give an adequate estimation of the stress relaxation tendency over a longer time period. This was
not carried out in this study, since the focus was on the initial relaxation of the Insulator, from where
the tendency in this time span can be used to evaluate the function.

Simpliﬁcations and Limitations
In practice, no polymers obey Coulomb’s law of friction. The friction coeﬃcient will among others de-
pend on the contact area, the normal load, the temperature, and the velocity. When a polymer deforms
against a solid surface, the contact area will increase with increasing applied load and hence the coeﬃ-
cient of friction increases with increasing normal force.[6] The rotation velocity of the ICON is close to
constant in the application. A Coulomb’s friction coeﬃcient can therefore be assumed to simplify the
FEM. Ideally, the actual rotation velocity should be simulated in the FEA. However, this would require
a rate dependent friction coeﬃcient which would be more complicated to establish.
During the ICON application, the Insulator is deformed against the glass over a unspeciﬁed time before
rotation initializes. The static friction coeﬃcient is known to be sensitive to dwell (also referred to as
squeeze out) over time.[46][47][48] If this is prevalent, a higher force is required to create an initial rotation.
Longer dwell time increases the contact parameters like the static friction, static friction coeﬃcient, and
contact area.[48] Coulomb’s friction coeﬃcient does not account for this phenomenon. However, it was
assumed that the surface pressure on the Insulator was not signiﬁcant. Hence, this would not have a sig-
niﬁcant impact on the friction coeﬃcient. The extent of this eﬀect is currently studied in a project in NN.
The friction coeﬃcient also depends on the glass being wet or dry.
It will always be higher on dry
glass.[49] In the physical application, the glass is siliconized with a cross-linked silicone. This give rise to
a very complicated frictional system that depends on the applied force and the rotation velocity; increased
rotation velocity would lead to increased forces. Therefore, this was not accounted for in the model.
The simulated deformation is simpliﬁed in regards to the initial deformation of the Insulator in the actual
application. This implies that the Insulator is deformed against the glass in an arbitrary time span in an
axial movement in the FEA, where the Insulator in the real application will be forced translational into
the glass tube. This simpliﬁcation was carried out as the actual movement would be very complicated
to simulate in a FEM. The reaction moment was as a result examined in the time after the deformation
and stabilization, which captures the most important features of the application; the reaction moment
after stress relaxation.
More general simpliﬁcations and limitations involve:
1) The TPE is assumed isotropic. This implies that the Insulator has identical stress and strain behavior
in all directions. For an injection molded TPE, this is a simpliﬁcation as the polymer chain orientation
will inﬂuence the mechanical properties.
2) Residual stresses from the moulding process are ignored even though this might inﬂuence the results
slightly.
3) The physical properties of the TPE is established from tension tests, even though the predominating
loading mode of the material is deﬂection and compression.
4) The FEM is limited to represent the TPE in room temperature only.

Chapter 5. Discussion
Digital Image Correlation Method Evaluation
Final Application Evaluation
The ﬁndings in Phase 1, and 3 can be used in a ﬁnal discussion and the ultimate inquiry for NN; is this
respective TPE a suitable material for the desired function of the Insulator.
The results found in the material characterization (Phase 1), can prior to a FEA, provide an estimation
of this, as the stress relaxation rate observed provides an estimation of the decrease in reaction moment
over time when rotated in the glass tube. Found was that the rate of relaxation measured in the TPE
seems overall concerning. The tests revealed that when strained 20 %, the stress in the TPE decreases
58 %. This was measured over 48 hours and can therefore be expected to decrease further to a lower
non-zero steady state over an extended time period. In addition, the cyclic tensile tests revealed that
TPE undergoes permanent deformation, which could prove to have a negative eﬀect on the arising friction
in the application when the Insulator is deformed over an extended time period. Both property changes
have an eﬀect on the contact pressure and therefore on the arising friction and reaction moment. When
the stiﬀness decreases and the stress relaxation increases the contact pressure will decrease. This would
result in a decreased friction coeﬃcient and decline in the reaction moment.
This excessive stress relaxation matches the decreasing reaction moment seen in the DMA measurements.
The reaction moment measured after 20 minutes provides an estimation of the tendency over time in the
actual application. This decrease can also be expected to drop even further when the time is extended.
Furthermore, knowing that the degree of stress relaxation is greater for TPEs than for conventional rub-
ber and that all strength properties decrease at temperatures above ambient while the rate of relaxation
increases, rise a concern.[40] In the application the function of the Insulator depends, to great extent,
on the stress relaxation, which could be accelerated further if the device was stored over extended time
periods at varying temperatures.
The friction coeﬃcient depends on the geometry of the Insulator, i.e. the surface contact area of the
Insulator and the glass wall. NN could reconsider the geometry of the Insulator aiming towards a higher
surface contact area providing an increased friction and as a result an increased reaction moment.
More drastic intervention might be necessary to gain the desired function of the Insulator. From the
ﬁndings in this thesis, NN should consider if they could provide a conventional rubber with similar ﬂex-
ibility and resilience.
As the manufacturing process of the Insulator rely on the TPEs ability to be
two-component injection molded onto a plastic, another subclass of TPE with a less pronounced hard
thermoplastic domain could also be examined. Such materials would be less aﬀected by temperature and
stress relaxation, which could improve the results in regard of the intended function.
Examining other geometries and diﬀerent rubber-like materials will remain for further studies.

Chapter 5. Discussion
Future Studies
5.5
Future Studies
Static tensile tests with known injection molding inlet
It was hypothesized that the varying strain, measured over the gauge length of the dogbones, was caused
by a varying material density. This hypothesis could be conﬁrmed in a future study where the coldrunner
inlet is noted prior to uniaxial static tensile tests.
Tests at varying temperatures
The actual device can be subjected to varying temperatures in the application; from cold storage temper-
ature of 3 ◦C to hot climate conditions up to 40 ◦C. However, the prominent deformation and resulting
relaxation takes place when the device is inserted in the glass tube. This occurs at 23 ◦C and the tests
were therefore conducted at room temperature as a simpliﬁcation. Therefore, the material model cali-
brated will only be valid for this temperature.
Future studies should include testing at temperatures of storage (3 ◦C) and hot climate conditions (40
◦C). Such data could be included in a material model calibration, creating a material model with tem-
perature dependent parameters that would be valid for varying temperatures. From this, the ﬁnal FEA
could be used to establish if the Insulator would undergo excessive stress relaxation in the application at
higher temperatures, which could lead to an excessive decrease in the reaction moment. This would be
important to examine, since the TPE needs to exhibit a minimum reaction moment to work as intended,
which also applies to other temperatures.
Extrapolate Relaxation
A future study where the stress relaxation is extrapolated over 2 years chould be performed. This would
provide an estimation of the decreasing reaction moment after 2 years of storage and could contribute
to the discussion on whether the TPE is suitable for the desired function over an extended time period.
This could be performed in a FEM with the assigned material model.
Examine Other Insulator Geometries
Diﬀerent designs of the Insulator could be tested in similar DMA measurements to examine if such course
of action could improve the observed reaction moment over time. The geometries should aim at a higher
contact surface area. However, squeeze out should be considered to make sure that the ICON can move
when initialized.
Test diﬀerent type of TPEs or other rubbers
Diﬀerent TPE compounds or similar rubber-like materials, known to provide good resilience and capable
of two-component injection molding, could be tested in a tensile stress relaxation test. This could be
compared with the ﬁndings in this thesis from where a more suitable material could be established.
Note to Future Work
Worth noting is the batch to batch variation that can exist, especially when the material is bought from
an external company. This can also contribute to variation in the mechanical properties. However, this
is only important to consider in the future production of the Insulator, as all samples in this project was
manufactured from the same batch.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
6
Conclusion
This industrial thesis characterized the mechanical properties of a Thermoplastic Elastomer (TPE) inte-
grated in a novel in-development device. The experimental tests was performed using a universal tensile
tester and 3D Digital Image Correlation, which was a novel course of action in NN device R&D. This
combination of techniques proved to be a suitable test method to measure the strain of a rubber-like ma-
terial in tension. The presented method can be used by NN when stress-strain data of similar materials
are needed to calibrate a material model to be embedded in a Finite Element Model (FEM).
The material characterization revealed that the TPE exhibits a non-linear viscoelastic behavior with
pronounced stress relaxation and Mullin’s eﬀect when strained to values observed in the FEM of the
application situation. These two properties will have a negative eﬀect on the intended function of the
Insulator, as they both inﬂuence the contact pressure and therefore the arising friction and resulting
reaction moment.
The TPE characteristics proved too complex to be predicted by a hyperelastic strain energy function
combined with a linear viscoelastic Prony model. The more complex Parallel Rheological Framework
3-Yeoh-Power-Mullins material model calibrated in MCalibration was able to predict the TPE behavior
observed in a cyclic tensile test, static tensile tests performed with diﬀerent deformation rates, and tensile
stress relaxation tests.
Compression tests proved to be overall problematic due to instability in cuboid samples when a compres-
sive deformation was applied. As a result hereof, tensile test data were used to calibrate the material
model assuming an identical stress-strain behavior within the strain range tested. Tensile tests performed
on two diﬀerent geometries revealed that the mechanical behavior is inﬂuenced by the injection molding
process and the bulk size, introducing an increased stiﬀness response with a decreased bulk size. Smaller
dogbone samples were found adequate to represent the Insulator having a slim gauge length.
The main inquiry from NN was to examine if the Insulator provides a suﬃcient reaction moment when
rotated in the glass tube, making it able to withstand excessive rotation of the ICON. This leads to
a discussion on whether the TPE is suitable for this desired function. Dynamical Mechanical Analysis
(DMA) tests and a Finite Element Analysis (FEA) with the assigned material model and a simpliﬁed
friction coeﬃcient of 0.064 demonstrated that the reaction moment decreases drastically over 20 minutes,
as the Insulator is rotated. These ﬁndings together with the observed excessive stress relaxation and
permanent deformation in uniaxial tensile tests lead to the conclusion that NN should investigate if they
can purchase a diﬀerent TPE compound. Suggested is a TPE with the same resilience however with a
less pronounces hard thermoplastic domain, since this increases the stress relaxation and as a result have
a negatively aﬀect on the arising reaction moment. Providing a qualiﬁed suggestion of another material
was out of scope in this thesis and will remain for future studies.

Chapter A. Background of The Insulator
Appendix
A
Background of The Insulator
A.1
ICON
The material characterized in this thesis is a part of a component, the ICON, under development intended
to be included in pen-injection devices. The ICON should be able to track the dose distributed from
the pen very precisely. The dosage count will be send to a "device" which can track the daily insulin
distributed to the user. Implementing the ICON into insulin pens means that Novo Nordisk A/S will be
able to sample real life data. With this feature the patient and "stakeholders" can gain a better overview
of the daily insulin use which can lead to a more controlled and therefore better overview of the the
disease. In addition, it will be easier to gain knowledge about the disease in regards to research and
development of better treatments.
A.1.1
Diabetes and Measurements
Diabetes patient’s major goal is being able to control their blood glucose level in order to prevent com-
plications. Diabetics therefore need to monitor their blood glucose level in order to distribute the right
amount of insulin when their blood glucose level is too high. In addition, the patient beneﬁt from keeping
their blood glucose level in a certain range, as too high or too low levels can cause diﬀerent complication.
Tracking or keeping a log of the daily blood glucose level and insulin dosage will reveal if their diabetes
care plan is working optimal in order to prevent diabetes related complications.[50][51]
Often doctors experience that diabetic patients don’t keep a desired track on the blood glucose level
and the injected insulin dosage. This can cause problems regarding the patient’s treatment and health.
In addition, tracking the insulin dosages distributed could be used for researchers to gain more knowledge
about the disease aiming at the best treatment for the patients.
Novo Nordisk A/S strives to make this tracking easier for all patients and caregivers by implementing
the ICON device into all injection devices. The ICON will keep track on all the dosages given by the
injection pen and therefore gives a picture of the individual patient’s disease. How the ICON works will
be elaborated in the following section.
A.2
Insulator
The entire ICON device is intended to be placed inside the insulin pens in the glass tube just behind the
piston forcing the insulin forward when a dosage is distributed. Inside the ICON device is a piston that
will rotate on the chip counting the dosages. The entire ICON however needs to move forward without
rotating in the glass tube. This has been made possible by implementing polymer brakes in the ICON
casing intended to create a moment force against the glass tube wall, making it possible for the ICON
device to slide in the tube without rotating.
The ICON brakes are made of a Thermoplastic Elastomer (TPE) and are called the Insulator. This
material was chosen at it is known to provide excellent features in accordance to deforming and returning
to its original shape. This is important when the brakes initially are packed closer and folded and needs
to be able to unfold in the tube.

Chapter B. Injection Molding
B
Injection Molding
The widely used Injection Molding method is a very cost eﬀective process for large productions of polymer
parts as the mold can contain multiple cavities and as the process is fully automatic once the process
is stabilized. An injection molding machine with its most essential elements can be seen in Figure B.1.
The process includes a injection unit where the desired material pellets is poured into a barrel from the
feed hopper. Here the material is heated by friction produced by a rotating screw until a certain melting
point. In the ﬁrst stage of the production the mold cavity closes where after the polymer melt is injected
into the speciﬁed mold cavity under a high pressure.[52] This is done in three stages; 1) the ﬁlling phase
where the ram moves forward at a steady speed ﬁlling up the mold. 2) The pressurization phase where
the ram moves further forward but at a decreased speed forcing extra material into the mold. 3) The
compensation phase where the ram speed decreases further but continue to creep forward holding the
pressure in order to compensate for any volumetric change when the material goes from melt to solid.[53]
The mold, which is preconstructed in order to produce the speciﬁc part geometry, is instantly cooled
down to make the polymer melt solidify and conform to the cavity shape. The mold is automatically
opened and the part is injected by ejector pins. All these steps in the process are predeﬁned manually
after multiple test steps in order to ﬁnd the optimal process parameters including mold temperature, ﬁll
time, and ﬁlling time. [52] The complete cycle time of the process highly depends on the cooling of the
specimen in the mold cavity and are typically within the range of 15 seconds to one minute.[54]
Figure B.1: Injection Molding Machine with its diﬀerent components. The TPE is poured in the feed
hopper where the screw heats it up by friction. It will be moved to the nozzle and injected into the cavity.
When polymer parts with a long ﬂow length is injection molded it is not uncommon to see ﬂow defects on
the surface often called ﬂow marks or "tiger stripes". These have a negative eﬀect on the part appearance
which can be important when components need to have a certain aesthetic design but will not be of high
importance if the appearance of the part is irrelevant, like for an example "dogbone" test specimens.
The occurrence is very often seen on thermoplastic blends. The reason for these defects have not been
clariﬁed yet but is though to be related to a slip-stick phenomenon and/or a unstable ﬂow during the
ﬁlling.[55][56]
Another defect, referred to as "ﬂash", occurs when the polymer melt leaks out through the mold cavity
line. This can occur if too much material is injected into the cavity or if the packing is done at a too high
pressure, both making the cavity pressure force exceeds the clamping force. More often polymer parts
can have so called "sink marks" which can occur on the ﬁnal specimen during the cooling as a result of
material shrinkage. This is seen as an immersion on a surface.[57]

ﬁlling time, packing pressure, and packing time were determined for this speciﬁc mold and material.
The established conditions were an injection speed of 7cm3 as the material data sheet suggest a medium
injection speed. In addition a too fast injection can cause the material to shear which can cause stress
in the material and a too slow injection speed can cause the material to harden too fast. The packing
pressure chosen were 450 bar as this gave the best results with less sink marks compared to lower pressures
tried. For this chosen packing pressure the opening time of the gate had to be decreased, the cooling time
were increased, and the rejection pin length were increased before the samples were able to loosen form
the mold. The packing time were set to 11 seconds. This was determined from 5 diﬀerent tests where the
sample were weighted (average of three diﬀerent samples) after changing the packing time. Decreasing
the packing time to less than 11 seconds resulted in larger sink marks.

def FuncRange(x,xRange):
    ia=0
    ib=len(x)            
    for i in range(len(x)):
        if x[i] > xRange[1]:
            ib=i-1
            break           
    for i in range(ib):
        if x[i] < xRange[0]:
            ia=i   
    return ia,ib
def FuncBalanceStrain(x1,x2,x1Range):  
    x2Range=(np.max(x2)*0.05,np.max(x2)*0.1)       
    (ia2,ib2) = FuncRange(x2,x2Range)
    p=np.polyfit(x1[ia2:ib2] , x2[ia2:ib2] , 1)
    x1 = x1 + p[1]/p[0]       
    for i in range(30):
        (ia1,ib1) = FuncRange(x1,x1Range)
        p=np.polyfit(x1[ia1:ib1] , x2[ia1:ib1], 1)
        x1 = x1 + p[1]/p[0]        
        # Consider new break condition
        if np.abs(p[1]/p[0]) < np.max(x1)*0.0000001:
            break              
    return x1

Chapter R. Compressive Stress Relaxation 48 hours
R
Compressive Stress Relaxation 48 hours
Multiple 48 hour compressive stress relaxation tests were performed with a Lloyd tensile tester (Lloyd
Instruments, LR5K plus) without the DIC setup. The cuboids were compressed 2.3 mm with 100 mm/s
and were held at this position for 48 hours. This was programmed in the the associated software Nexygen
Plus. This study was initially carried out to examine the friction at the interface between the sample and
the compression plates. The tests were performed in room temperature and with no ﬁxation between
the compression plates and the sample. Over the 48 hours a digital camera (Nikon D5300) on a tripod
was programmed to take a picture of the specimen every 10th minute. These pictures were put together
in Matlab12 to create a movie in order to visualize any creep at the interface over the 48 hours. Four
pictures, the two ﬁrst and the two last, were in addition uploaded to Fiji (ImageJ-win64) where the width
of the cuboids closest to the interface were measured, in order to establish if the friction between the
compression plate and the specimen was suﬃcient enough to assume complete constriction, which else
could cause false low force measurements.
Three TPE cuboids were examined in an uniaxial compressive stress relaxation test where the cuboid
was compressed 0.23 mm held for 48 hours. This test was performed as a pilot study before involving
DIC. The cuboids were placed like group 2 (standing on the cutted edge). The test revealed that the
cuboid deforms in a barrel shape as expected due to friction in the interface between the sample and the
plates. Images were taken every 10th min during the entire test to examine if the samples slide at the
interface. This was used to conclude whether the friction is large enough to assume complete friction in
the interface. This assumption make it possible to exclude that the force reduction over time is inﬂuenced
by the sliding at the interface, which makes it possible to assume that the observed stress relaxation is
caused by relaxation in the material only.
The top width was measured to 10.09 mm (an average of three measurements) on the ﬁrst picture captured
after the deformation and the bottom width was measured to 9.91 mm. The last picture taken after 48
hours measured 10.09 mm at the top width and 9.93 mm at the bottom width. The slight diﬀerence is
most likely caused by the measuring method which is done manually in ImageJ. During the 48 hours the
friction force between the compression plates and the TPE specimen seems to be suﬃcient enough for
the specimen not to slide on the compression plates.
Figure R.1: a) The ﬁrst picture of cuboid 5 in the compression test taken after 10 min. b) The last
picture of cuboid 5 in the compression test taken after 48 hours. The red lines indicate where the length
where measured in ImageJ.
