Abstract 
Oral delivery of peptides and proteins is met with several obstacles due to their sensitivity due to the acidity 
and enzymatic degradation in the gastrointestinal environment and also their lack of permeability properties 
through the epithelial wall.  This project is based on the concept of utilizing biodegradable drug-loaded 
microneedles that protect the drug from the gastric environment and release it in close proximity to the 
intestinal epithelium after penetration of the intestinal mucus layer. The focus is solely revolving on the 
construction and composition of the microneedles. We have used Paracetamol as a model drug and 
visualized the drug distribution in microneedles. Furthermore, we have investigated several materials to 
obtain an optimal drug release profile and the drug distribution in the microneedles. We have discovered 
that Eudragit FL30 D55 had a promising release profile for Paracetamol. A mucoadhesion study was also 
made to observe the microneedles’ effect on the sheer force with pig intestine.

Introduction 
 
The development of drugs is an ongoing process and constant battle against pathogens’ natural processes. 
Drug discovery through natural sources and synthetic production has provided us with useful tools making 
us go e.g., from a death sentence when getting an infection, to a manageable treatment plan.  
Oral Administration 
Oral administration is the most preferred administration method for several reasons, such as better patient 
compliance and safety3. Approximately 60% of small compound therapeutics are administered orally. 
Furthermore, does oral administration occupy 90% of the pharmaceutical market for humans 4. The oral 
route also provides sustained and controllable delivery5 
Challenges regarding small compounds include, among others, their limited specificity and off-target binding 
resulting in unwanted interactions 3. Comparing parenteral routes with oral administration, these routes 
avoid the gastrointestinal environment5. However, needle injection is required which can cause severe 
issues5,6. Injection-associated problems consist of the need for trained personnel, pain, and damage to 
surrounding tissue among other risks5. 
Therapeutic Peptides 
Several new opportunities have been presented with the use of therapeutic peptides in the last decade; One 
of them is antimicrobial peptides (AMPs). Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has been classified as one of the 
most alarming threats worldwide due to the excessive use of antibiotics7, resulting in pathogens (bacteria, 
viruses, fungi, and parasites) developing resistance7. Pathogens have evolved different properties to 
overcome the effect of antibiotics such as efflux pumps, inhibitors, etc. These crucial developments have 
resulted in multi-drug resistance. A 2016 report projected a worst-case scenario estimate that around 10 
million deaths caused by AMR can occur by the year 20508. 
The rate of discovery and approval of new small compound antibiotics is slowly overtaken by the rate of 
evolving multi-drug resistance pathogens. Therefore, the discovery of new methods and new types of 
antibiotics is highly relevant and needed. 
AMPs are naturally found in several organisms and can also be produced synthetically. Additionally, they 
even play a key role even in the human body’s first line of defense7.  
Several AMPs have been in development since their discovery in 19209, however, only a few have been 
approved and are primarily administered through parenteral- or topical routes10. 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
Many types of AMPs have been discovered recently because of, not only their antimicrobial activities but 
also their effects on the modulation of the immune responses of patients11. Types of AMPs vary in many 
properties, such as hydrophobicity, where hydrophobic, amphipathic, and hydrophilic AMPs are to be found. 
In terms of bacteria, the composition of some positively charged residues in AMPs makes it possible to 
interact with the cell membrane due to its negative charge. This interaction causes pore formation and cell 
lysis and an overall cell destabilization7.  
Oral AMPs are also superior to conventional antibiotics showing advantages with regard to biofilm activity 
and development of resistance. Due to the AMPs composition of amino acids, they are generally in 
compliance with patient safety.  Furthermore, another key advantage is the surface area of AMPs allowing 
the ability to make or disrupt protein-protein interactions7. 
 
Small Intestine and Drug Absorption 
Several elements in the small intestine contribute to the absorptive properties, especially the extensive 
surface area (app. 300m2) containing particularly enterocytes specialized in the absorption of peptides, 
sugars, and fatty acids 5,12 and M cells, that are less protected by the mucus, transporting antigens13 and many 
other types of cells. Oral drugs are required to solubilize gastric fluid to be absorbed in the stomach, small 
intestine, or colon.5 
Location and physiological environment in the gastrointestinal tract are important factors for retention time 
and transport processes for oral drug delivery.13  
Two types of absorption pathways occur in the absorption of drugs; the transcytotic pathway and the 
paracellular pathway, whereas the transcytotic pathway, is the primary pathway for small compound drugs 
to be absorbed14. 
 
The small intestine is divided into three parts: the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. The duodenum is 
connected to the gut as the first part of the small intestine to which drugs are introduced. The pH of the 
duodenum is affected by the gastric juices and thereby is lower than the rest of the small intestine12. Along 
with gastric enzymes, the pancreas also secretes enzymes into the intestinal lumen5. The pancreas produces 
zymogens and secretes them into the duodenal lumen where they are activated by Enterokinases secreted 
by the epithelial cells15. The main task of these activated enzymes is to degrade fats, starch, proteins, and 
peptides5,15. The concentration of pancreatic secreted compounds is thereby highest in the duodenum and 
decreases further along the small intestine. A study showed that a decrease of 90% in enzymatic activity was 
observed in the middle of the jejunum, compared to the duodenum16. Additionally, another study showed 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
that the residence time in the duodenum only accounts for 10% of the overall residence time in the small 
intestine17.  
The absorptive epithelial cells in the small intestine are covered by a submucosal- and a mucus layer13. The 
mucus is made of negatively charged proteoglycan-coated mucin molecules and water, capturing and 
discarding foreign compunds13. The mucins form fibers and prevent molecules larger than the space in 
between them to pass, furthermore, mucins might also form weak bonding with compounds trapping them 
or leading to structural changes to the drug of interest5. A study performed by Marie Boegh et al. discovered 
the mucus as the main obstacle to bioavailability for therapeutic peptides18. Mucus is constantly secreted by 
goblet cells, and in connection to the rapid turnover of cells, the residence time for any attached compounds 
to reach the absorptive surface is limited to app 50-270 minutes19. If the drug of interest is able to bypass the 
mucus layer, it is met with the submucosal layer, this narrower submucosa is contrastingly known for 
enhancing the uptake of drugs. 
 
Until now, over 80 peptide drugs have been approved for therapeutic use3. Drawbacks concerning 
therapeutic peptides involve mainly their instability and membrane impermeability20. Peptide length and 
composition of amino acids are some of the main factors affecting the membrane permeability, thus making 
crossing the cell membrane for intracellular targeting generally impossible3. Secondary or tertiary structures 
are very sensitive to environmental changes, and as amide bonds are easily disrupted by the intestinal 
environment, protection or bypassing is crucial3.  Overall, the environment of the GI tract and its proteolytic 
compounds together with obstacles regarding permeability results in oral peptide bioavailability at less than 
1-2%, we want to change that.

pancreatic juice and bile5. This liquid is composed of compounds specialized in breaking down any larger 
component, especially proteins15.

Materials and methods 
 
Silicon-Wafer (Si-Wafer) 
The microneedles on the SI-wafer were made by a triple etching scheme with an initial thickness of ～1µm. 
Patterning of the hard mask is done by using UV lithography and Advanced Oxide etching. The SiO2 layer had 
been grown by wet thermal oxidation of the Silicon substrates. Isotropic dry etch is used to construct the 
needle tips, and the cylindrical shafts were made by anisotropic deep etching. The plate consisted of a 
20x20mm2 array of microneedles where 2500 microneedles were made measuring 75µm in diameter and 
130 µm in height for each microneedle. The production of the SI-wafer was done by Senior Consultant Lasse 
Højlund Eklund Thamdrup, IDUN Research Group.  
Polydimethylsiloxane(PDMS) Negative mold 
PDMS was mixed with a solidifier with a ratio of 10:1 and stirred. The solution was then vacuumed until 
almost no bubbles were to be found and loaded onto the SI wafer. The plate was then heated at 70°C for 30 
minutes and dismantled from the SI wafer. The hardened PDMS plate was cut into 1X1 cm squares.

Drug Loading of Microneedles 
The drug was loaded in three different ways; Diluted in the microneedle material, powdered onto the PDMS 
plate before loading material and powdered onto PDMS, and centrifuged before loading material onto the 
PDMS mold. 
- 
Diluted in Material 
The material of interest and the drug were mixed in order to obtain a concentration of the drug at 5 mg/ml.  
- 
Powdered Drug 
The drug was powdered onto the PDMS molds with a brush and 150-200 µl of the polymer material was then 
loaded onto the powdered PDMS mold. 
- 
Powder and Centrifugation 
The drug was powdered onto the PDMS molds and centrifuged at 3200 RPM for 10 minutes. This procedure 
was repeated 2 times before 150-200 µL of the polymer material was loaded onto the PDMS mold. 
Drying 
The mixture was poured into the negative mold and the samples were placed in vacuum to remove air 
bubbles, excess air bubbles were removed with a pipetting tip. The samples were then placed in an oven at 
37°C for one hour and then left overnight at room temperature until solid stage was achieved. The procedure 
is visualized in Figure 2.

For the in vitro study, a MicroDISS profiler was utilized as an in-situ fiber optic UV monitoring system.29 A 
calibration curve was created for the model drugs and the polymers before initiating the experiment, and

MicroDISS Profiler together with 10ml PBS at pH 7.5, and pH 2.5 under stirring, while the optical sensors were 
lowered into the solution. All in vitro release studies were done with a constant temperature of 37°C. The 
shutter was declared dysfunctional at one point, and a new one was ordered and installed

Microneedle dissolution test 
Microneedles of the two materials; Eudragit FL30 D-55 and Eudragit L30 D-55 were constructed and followed 
through a needle endurance test. Three microneedle patches of each type of material were immersed in 
Phosphate Buffered Saline pH 7.5 separately and placed onto a platform shaker at 260 Rounds per minute 
(RPM). Pictures were captured at different times and the microneedles were observed. 
Ex vivo Mucoadhesion studies 
The ex-vivo studies were conducted with the buildForce Analyzer in collaboration and produced by Tien-Jen 
Chang from the IDUN research group. The microneedle patches were cut into pieces of 0.5x0.5 cm2 and 
attached to a sensor facing a piece of pig small intestine (2cm X 0.8 cm) as seen in Figure 7. The buildForce 
Analyzer moves the intestine sample and reads the force of which the microneedle is being affected by the 
distance of the intestine sample traveled. Ten patches were produced, five with microneedles and five 
without microneedles. Each sample was attached to the sensor and the intestine sample was adjusted to be 
just underneath the patch. The moving distance was determined by conducting three trials ensuring the 
whole patch was covered during the tests. The optimal mode was found to move the intestine upwards and 
let the patch travel along the intestinal surface until the patch was fully engulfed in the intestine. Afterward, 
the intestine sample moved downwards until the microneedle exited the intestinal mucus. Intestine samples 
were switched after every run and were used after less than 10 minutes after cutting.

Coherent Anti-stokes Raman Scattering Microscopy, CARS 
CARS is a four-wave microscopy tool utilizing the properties of molecular vibrations and energy absorption 
to generate a new blue-shifted field.  This enables us to distinguish between different molecules in the same 
sample. The method takes advantage of the Raman scattering effect and provides images without labeling 
and destroying the samples.31 The imaging was conducted in collaboration with the CARS specialist of IDUN 
Research Group, Zhongyang Yang.  PVP and Paracetamol were individually dissolved in Milli-Q water in order 
to determine the optimal wavelength for both compounds. It was reassured no interference was present 
between the compounds in their respective wavelength to ensure a clear and reliable observation. Laser 
imaging of PVP absorbed at an excitation wavelength of 3075 cm-1 correlating to the aromatic CH stretch in 
benzene. PVP is absorbed in an excitation wavelength at 2930 cm-1 correlating to the vibrational n-Alkanes. 
Paracetamol-loaded PVP microneedles were produced where microneedle patches were placed into the 
microscope and images were captured.

Discussion 
In this project we have aimed to develop biodegradable mucus-penetrating microneedles that allow release 
of active pharmaceutical compounds near the intestinal epithelium. We have investigated several materials 
that would fit our concept by constructing acceptable microneedles and loading and drying them onto a 
PDMS negative mold. Furthermore, it should also be safe and dissolve at intestinal pH at a time frame where 
100% of the drug of interest is released approx. after one hour.   
Earlier studies have been conducted in this research group with PVP presenting an immediate release of the 
drug. We wanted to prolong the release and began investigating the properties of PVP with a high molecular 
weight as it increases toughness and several other properties32. 
 We visualized the distribution of Paracetamol in the PVP microneedles with CARS. We observed a 
homogeneous distribution throughout the whole patch, thereby, that Paracetamol was not focused on the 
microneedle tips, also the Images present some darkness in the middle of each microneedle which could be 
due to scattering from the microneedle tips having curvature as the patches could be disproportionate. The 
omnidirectional release could result in loss of drug when introduced to the intestinal environment as the 
patch itself is significantly bigger and would contain most of the diluted drug than the microneedle tips close 
to the absorption area. 
 PVP microneedles did not meet our expectations due to the fast release profile of paracetamol and 
therefore, we attempted spray-coating PVP microneedles to further prolong the release. The spray-coating 
resulted in deformed microneedle structures which could weaken the penetration force, and the release did 
not differ significantly. We did not investigate other types of coating materials as it is preferable to investigate 
one kind of material fulfilling all requirements instead of layers, which would also keep the production 
process simple. 
We investigated different materials, which complied with our criteria, and attempted to construct 
microneedle patches with the same protocol as with the PVP microneedle patches. Some of the rejected 
materials could be debatable. Kollicoat SR 30D microneedles presented an acceptable microneedle structure, 
however, consistently occurring crust and discoloration features resulted in deciding that it was not a 
compatible material. Kollicoat SR 30D is often used as a coating agent where curing of the coated object is 
optimally done at 60°C33. Other temperatures were not investigated in this case as peptides could be 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
significantly affected by temperatures too far from physiological temperature. Additives, such as plasticizers, 
have not been introduced to any of the materials, and could possibly have influenced the number of useful 
materials in this project. Two of the seven tested materials presented acceptable microneedle construction 
The microneedle dissolution test is done with Eudragit FL30 D55 and Eudragit L30 D55 showed the state of 
the microneedles at different fixed times. The test did not reveal how the microneedles were dissolving, 
which could be done by observing and utilizing in situ microscopy for more continuous visualization of the 
process.  
Any pore formation and specific timing for behavioral changes could be assessed. Eudragit FL30 D55 
displayed microneedle endurance for app. one hour. After one hour the patches became flexible and lost 
their ability to penetrate.  
 Eudragit FL30 D-55 presented a promising release profile for Paracetamol with 100% release after app. 2 
hours, furthermore, it released less to no drug in the acidic environment strengthening the concept to be 
resistant in the acidic environment of the stomach making the microneedle patches less dependent on the 
capsules’ ability to sustain the gastric environment. 
We attempted to move on to a more comparable model drug as Paracetamol differs in several properties 
compared to general therapeutic peptides, such as molecular weight, stability, etc. (para ref + Nisin ref). One 
of the main elements in which they also differ, is absorbance, as we observed a clear range in wavelength in 
which we could detect Paracetamol without the interference of our material candidate. In order to 
circumvent these issues, we attempted by using bovine serum albumin as a model drug, however, clear 
results without any interference from Eudragit FL30 D-55 were not possible.  
To obtain an even better comparison to therapeutic peptides, we exchanged bovine serum albumin with 
Nisin. Mo microneedles were to find, which we assumed was due to the blockage of the undissolved drug on 
the tips. To be able to create a drug delivery method that is not limited by the solubility of the drug of interest, 
we would need to investigate a procedure in which we can introduce a non-dissolvable drug into the 
microneedle patches together with correct needle construction. Furthermore, to be able to visualize a proper 
release profile of peptides we should consider labeling the drug of interest which would discard the obstacle 
of interference from other compounds. 
As we have identified materials with an optimistic release profile, we have yet to visualize the consistency of 
this release with other drugs. Also overcoming the need to dissolve the drug of interest in the material as not 
all drugs carry this function.  
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
Ex vivo studies were also conducted, as we modified our force analyzer to attempt to investigate the sheer 
force of the microneedles in the pig intestine. We attempted to incorporate the microneedles onto the pig 
intestine and then commence the reading, but as the intestinal piece and the microneedle patch were closed 
in manually, the initial read often differed and did not provide a am optimal visualization of the sheer force. 
We were able to provide reproducible data presenting a difference in sheer force between patches with and 
without microneedles. Initiating the experiment with a free Microneedle patch which is then slowly 
introduced to the intestine and freed afterward proved to be the most acceptable approach for now.  
To further realize our concept of an oral administration of peptides we also need to reflect on the localization 
of the SPED-microneedle patch complex. As we identify the different processes occurring in the intestine we 
see a higher concentration of pancreatic secretion in the duodenum in which, the main property is to break 
down, among others, peptide compounds. The concentration of peptide-breaking elements decreases 
significantly when entering the jejunum. The jejunum is observed to have the highest absorption of peptides 
and thereby an optimal area for therapeutic peptide release34.  
Besides the proteolytic enzymes present in the intestines, the mucus also acts as an obstacle as its main 
function is to remove foreign objects. The submucosal layer is found underneath the mucus. Thereby 
penetrating the mucus and releasing the drug of interest near the submucosa would theoretically overcome 
the mucus as a barrier.  
Even if we would be able to transport the drug of interest through the GI tract and let the SPED unfold at the 
most absorptive area of the intestine penetrating the mucus and releasing the peptide drug of interest, we 
would still not experience optimal bioavailability and high drug concentration in the blood. The intestine is 
not great in the uptake of peptides longer than 3 amino acids34. Molecules are absorbed either via 
paracellular- or transcellular (incl. endocytosis) transport5, so further permeation/absorption enhancers are 
needed for a significant change in bioavailability. Several permeation enhancers have been suggested 
throughout many papers. For example, PIP peptides have been shown to likely open tight junctions and 
promote paracellular transition35. It has been claimed that these peptides can dynamically adjust tight 
junctions allowing passage. Other permeation enhancers have also been seen to improve absorption by 
chemical modification of the peptide of interest3. 
 
Future Perspectives 
Future studies would involve ensuring a universal drug-loading method that is not affected by the properties 
of the drug of interest. As we have produced microneedle patches with one material, the drug release is 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
expected to be Omni-directional, therefore, it could create similar results if the loading of the material was 
split into two phases where the microneedles were produced first, and afterward introducing the powdered 
drug and another layer of the material on top enclosing the drug. This would increase the distance of the 
drug and the absorption area and possibly not result in a bioavailability that theoretically could occur if the 
drug was located solely on the needle tips. Another approach is to create hollow microneedles which would 
ensure the drug is located as close as possible to the area of absorption and avoid the dependency on drug 
solubility. The release profile of the hollow microneedles should be investigated since this thin-layered 
polymer could result in earlier and faster release of drugs in comparison to a solid microneedle. 
To overcome the obstacles of measuring release profiles on peptides if the material interferes with the 
absorbance, it would be convenient to introduce a form of labeling the drug of interest with a fluorescent 
tag. As tags are excited at a specific wavelength and emit a specific wavelength, we ensure the credibility of 
the measurement. The size of the tag should be taken into consideration as therapeutic peptides are in the 
lower size range, and bigger covalently attached elements like Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) might affect 
the release. An appropriate size could be Fluorescein (FITC) which is an organic compound with a molecular 
weight of 332.3 g/mol having an excitation peak at 494 nm and an emission peak at  518 nm36.  
To ensure a more comparable dissolution profile one must also consider another dissolution solvent. As we 
have been using only PBS to focus on the pH dependency lacking several other important compounds in the 
intestinal, as well as gastric fluids. Simulated Intestinal Fluid(SIF) provides the advantage to forecast a more 
correct effect. Furthermore, to distinguish between fasted and fed state consequences37.  
 
 
 
Conclusion 
In this project, we have investigated different materials in their ability to construct microneedles and further 
release the drug of interest. We have used Paracetamol as a model drug in the first phase of discovering a 
suitable material. We visualized the release profile of Paracetamol with a high molecular PVP material and 
Eudragit FL30 D-55. We observed an immediate release where 100% of the drug was released after app. 20 
minutes with PVP and a delayed release for Eudragit FL30 D-55 at app. 2 hours. We also investigated several 
other polymers and their ability to construct microneedles, where most of them failed to produce proper 
microneedle structures. Eudragit L30 D-55 and Eudragit FL30 D-55 proved to be able to construct proper 
microneedles like PVP. Eudragit L30 D-55 failed to sustain its needles for longer than 15 minutes in the pH 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
endurance test proving it unfit as a candidate as a material. The limited period would not ensure proper 
mucus penetration and thereby not result in optimal bioavailability as this concept could result in. Eudragit 
FL30 D-55 presented endurance of the microneedles for at least 1 hour and fulfilled all our criteria, except its 
biodegradable properties, as it is only partially enteric. Even though Eudragit FL30 D-55 does not fully 
degrade, the pH endurance test showed a crucial change in behavior as the microneedle patches became 
soft and flexible and thereby loss of ability to penetrate after 1 hour. As no other types of drugs have been 
inspected, we can not conclude a consistent release profile, and it is also important to take into account how 
to ensure absorption via co-administering of permeation enhancers.
