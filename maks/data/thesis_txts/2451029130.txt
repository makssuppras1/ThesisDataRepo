Abstract
The time until absorption in a continuous Markov jump process under certain conditions, is called
phase-type distributed. Using a single such Markov jump process, a multivariate phase-type distri-
bution can be created by applying a linear reward scheme to the amount of time spent in each state
prior to absorption. This class of distributions is denoted MPH⋆.
This project examines 4 major models:
1. The Raftery model,
2. The Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern model,
3. The Marshall Olkin model,
4. The Geometric Mixture model,
and explain how each ﬁt into the framework of MPH⋆. Furthermore, the project then introduces the
theory of copulas and derives the copula for each model in great detail, such that anyone with prior
training in basic probability can follow the derivations.
Having explored the copulas, theory from stochastic simulation is applied to test the ﬁt of each model
on data simulated using the diﬀerent models. This allows for an analysis of each models sensitivity to
the underlying dependence structure, from which it is seen that the Geometric Mixture model is the
most versatile as it can be used, to some degree, to describe data simulated using the other models.
The main contributions of this project are
1. Thorough derivation of copulas for the above models
2. Simulation tests showing the correlation structure of the models is signiﬁcant when simulating
data with N = 104 observations
3. Thorough testing of stock price distribution to be used in further work

phase-type fordelt variabel, ved at knytte en "belønning" til tiden brugt i hvert state. Denne fordelings
klasse kaldes MPH⋆.
Dette projekt undersøger 4 modeller kaldet:
1. The Raftery model,
2. The Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern model,
3. The Marshall Olkin model,
4. The Geometric Mixture model,
og forklarer hvordan hver af dem passer ind i MPH⋆klassen. Derudover introducerer projektet teorien
bag copulas og udleder copulaet for hver af de ovenstående modeller i en detaljegrad således at alle
med en basal uddannelse indenfor sandsynlighedsregning bør kunne følge udledningerne.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project aims to explore phase-type distributions as a tool for modelling data where the marginals
are exponential while the joint distribution is not known. In particular dependent bivariate models
with speciﬁc marginals will be examined in great detail. The framework used is that of multivariate
phase-type distributions with a linear reward scheme denoted MPH⋆.
The project is inﬂuenced by the book Matrix-Eponential Distributions in Applied Probability by Mo-
gens Bladt and Bo Friis Nielsen [1], with part of the projects goal being to provide clarity as well as
new observations regarding some of the results presented in the book.
In particular this project aims to provide additional information and clarity regarding the dependence
structure of the following MPH⋆models:
1. The Raftery model,
2. The Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern model,
3. The Marshall Olkin model,
4. The Geometric Mixture model.
This is done by thoroughly examining each model using the theory of copulas and by use of stochastic
simulation, in order to test the ﬁt of the diﬀerent models on simulated data.
Throughout the project the theory and derivations are written to be as transparent and easy to follow
as possible. This means that certain concepts are derived in great detail in order to showcase the
strength of thorough analysis founded in basic probability tools such as First-Step analysis, law of
total probability etc.
In addition to the above, the project examines the feasibility of modelling the development of stock
prices using the MPH⋆framework, laying the foundation for further work to be done in this area.
Speciﬁcally the data set explored is the bivariate data set available by looking at the relative increase
from the opening price until the max price as well as from the closing price to the max price for a
company stock over a long period.

Chapter 2
Phase-Type Distributions
When studying Markov jump processes with an absorbing state, the time until absorption is often of
interest. The properties of this, the time until absorption, is what is examined within the theory of
phase-type distributions. This means that one way to think of a continuous phase-type distributed
variable, is as the time until absorption in a continuous Markov jump process under certain conditions.
Using this framework, the phase-type distributed variable is deﬁned by the initial distribution and
intensity matrix of the underlying Markov jump process.
2.1
Deﬁnition of a Phase-Type Distribution
Let {Xt}t≥0 be a Markov jump process with the state space E = {1, 2, . . . , p, p + 1}. Where the
ﬁrst p states are transient, and state p + 1 is absorbing. Then the formal deﬁnition of a phase-type
distributed variable can be written: Let τ be the time until absorption of the Markov jump process
{Xt}t≥0, i.e:
τ = inf{t ≥0 | Xt = p + 1},
(2.1)
then τ is said to be phase-type distributed.
Before describing τ within the Markov jump process framework, the terminology must be introduced.

Chapter 3
Bivariate modelling using MPH⋆
This chapter focuses on showing that the class of MPH⋆is a versatile modelling tool allowing for
the creation of a wide range of joint distributions with speciﬁc marginal distributions. Speciﬁcally
this chapter will also introduce the models that will be used for creating exponential marginals, as
well as highlight the versatility by also showing the ease with which other marginals can be created.
As shown in [1, p. 448], a number of the constructions possible within the class of MPH⋆can be
summarized in three types each with a distinct structure of their (α, S, R) representation. Type I
constructs joint distributions with Gamma distributed marginals, while Type II and III constructs
exponential marginal distributions each with a diﬀerent approach and methodology behind it in the
basic form.
As this project focuses on modelling bivariate data with exponential marginal distributions, the models
suitable for this is introduced ﬁrst, followed by a model allowing for gamma distributed marginals and
a more general Phase-Type mixture model.
3.1
Exponential Marginals
3.1.1
Type II
The Type II models are deﬁned by the subintensity matrix in their MPH⋆representation having
an upper triangular structure, in which there is a positive probability of a state not being visited.
Therefore the maximum amount of jumps is at most the dimension of the representation.
3.1.1.1
Distributions from Decomposition
Let Wi and Zi for i ∈N be independently exponentially distributed with rate 1. Independently of these
exponentially distributed random variables, let Ji be a bernoulli random variable with P(Ji = 1) = ρi,
and let λi > 0.

3.3
Introduction to Copulas
Copulas are a class of multivariate cumulative distribution functions of which the marginals are uniform
allowing for an examination and visualization of the dependence between random variables.
Let X = (X1, . . . , Xp) be a vector of random variables with continuous marginal cumulative dis-
tribution functions Fi(x), then the copula, denoted C(·), of the random vector X is deﬁned as the
joint cumulative distribution function of the random vector U = (U1, . . . , Up), which has uniformly
distributed marginals as a result of Ui = Fi(Xi):
C(u1, . . . , up) = P[U1 ≤u1, . . . , Up ≤up] = P[F1(X1) ≤u1, . . . , Fp(Xp) ≤up]
(3.25)
Sklar’s theorem states that for any continuous p-dimensional cumulative distribution function F with
marginals Fi, i = 1, . . . , p there exists a unique copula C [7].
For the bivariate case given 2 random variables X1 and X2 deﬁne the new random vector
(U1, U2) = (F1(X1), F2(X2))
(3.26)
where Fi(x) = P[Xi ≤x] is the marginal cumulative distribution, then the copula of (X1, X2) is
deﬁned by
C(u1, u2) = P[U1 ≤u1, U2 ≤u2].
(3.27)
It can be shown that the marginals of the random vector (U1, U2) are uniformly distributed by applying
the inverse of the cumulative distribution function, which is ensured to exist as Fi(x) is continuous.
P[Ui ≤ui] = P[Fi(Xi) ≤ui]

C(u1, u2) = min{u1, u2} [7].

Model Sensitivity Analysis
Having examined the diﬀerent dependence structures for each of the models, it is of interest to examine
the sensitivity of each model. This is done by simulating a data set from one of the models, and then
ﬁtting the simulated data with each of the 4 models. Then it can be examined whether all models ﬁnds
the correct parameters, which in this case are the marginal rates as well as the correlation. Further
a goodness of ﬁt test can be done, in order to see if the models are sensitive to the model used when
simulating the data.

data, a χ2 goodness of ﬁt test is done for each of the models. The χ2-test works by binning the
observed data and examining if the results correspond to the expected distributions. This means that
the method is susceptible to the binning process. As the marginal rates of the exponential distribution
as well as ρ is estimated from the data for all 4 models, the critical area is given by χ2(nclasses −1−3)
where nclasses is the number of bins [2].
The bivariate binning is done using the joint survival functions available in [1] for each of the models
except the Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern model, where the joint CDF is available. A detailed description
of this process is available in Appendix B.

Chapter 7
Modelling of stock price behavior
The ﬁnancial markets, in particular the stock markets, have been the focus of attention from a lot of
angles due to the monetary gain available from ﬁnding a suitable model and predicting its behavior.
The diﬃculty in this task lies in the many factors at play in determining the stock prices for diﬀerent
companies. Therefore it is very interesting to see if any information can be found using stochastic
modelling. As made evident by the models examined and shown so far, the goal is to ﬁnd data from
the stock markets which follows a bivariate distribution with exponential marginals. Therefore it is
necessary to require that all observations are positive and unbounded, and, ideally, that there is some
link between the observations creating the need for a bivariate model such as those presented in this
project.
With the above in mind, the data chosen is the development of stock prices for a given company over
the course of a day. In particular the relative diﬀerence between the opening price to the maximum for
the day, and the relative diﬀerence between the closing and max price of the day. The reason that the
relative diﬀerence is looked at rather than the absolute diﬀerence, is because of the upwards drift of
the market, i.e. inﬂation. If this is not corrected for, the newer data points would show larger values,
despite the relative diﬀerence being lower, thus skewing the data.
Let Xti be the price of a stock at time t ∈[0, T] over the course of day i. Then the question becomes
whether the following holds:

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The goal of this paper was to introduce the reader to the MPH⋆class and framework as a powerful tool
for modelling bivariate dependent data. In doing this copulas as a tool for working with dependent
bivariate data were also introduced to the reader.
This was done by a short introduction to the
necessary theory followed by rigorous explanation of the derivations needed to understand the concept
of copulas.
In addition, 4 MPH⋆models:
1. The Raftery model,
2. The Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern model,
3. The Marshall Olkin model,
4. The Geometric Mixture model,
were introduced and worked with in great detail in order to help the reader achieve a comfortable level
of understanding.
As part of this, it was shown that the copulas of the Raftery, Farlie-Gumbel-Morgenstern, Marshall
Olkin and Geometrix Mixture models can be derived through rigorous use of basic principles of prob-
ability such as the law of total probability. Further each of the copulas were derived and presented
in full detail for the bivariate case. These were also visualized and commented upon in order for
the reader to gain an insight into copulas as a tool for working with dependence structures between
random variables.
It was also shown that the model choice is important when using the MPH⋆framework, as the main
models outlined were shown to be sensitive to the correlation structure of the data. In particular it was
shown that the correlation structure of simulated data using either of the models typically becomes
signiﬁcant when comparing models on data of N = 104 observations.
When comparing the sensitivity of the models, it was also seen that the Geometric Model was the
most versatile and adaptable, as it was the best for describing the data simulated by the other models.
==PAGE:80==
68
Conclusion
Finally, by analyzing the development of stock prices from January 1st 2014 to June 10th 2019, it was
shown that some stock prices exhibit exponential marginals when looking at the relative diﬀerence
between the opening price and the maximum price of the day, as well as the closing price and the
maximum price of the day. Moreover, it was shown that the occurrence of exponential marginals was
rare, as most of the analyzed stocks failed both the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and the χ2-test. All the
examined data also failed both run tests introduced. This is therefore a result open for further work,
as alternate models are necessary to properly model the data. This frees the reader from applying the
distribution and mutual independence tests, as this was done thoroughly.
