Abstract 
Li-S batteries represent a promising technology thanks to the high theoretical capacity of S 
(1672 mAh⋅g−1). The main obstacle to overcome for this technology is the polysulphide (PS) shuttling 
mechanism. The work presented in this thesis aims to investigate the possible application of solid-state 
electrolytes and the use of a bio-binder for lithium-sulphur batteries with solid or with liquid electrolytes 
respectively. The first task was to develop a three-electrode setup for impedance measurements in solid-
state Li-S batteries, and to analyse the evolution of the impedance during the cycling of the batteries. The 
main evolution of the impedance was found to be connected with the changes at the positive electrode, the 
sulphur electrode. The second part of the thesis focuses on the analysis of two new possible solid 
electrolytes: LiBH4-LiBF4 and LiBH4-AlO(OH) mixtures, connecting them with XRD patterns of the 
sample in order to identify the conductive phase. The first mixture presents lower conductivity values than 
expected (around 0.03 mS⋅cm−1 at room temperature) and the composition suggests a reaction between the 
two chemicals. The second mixture presents high Li conductivity 0.15 mS⋅cm−1) at room temperature 
thanks to the interaction between the -OH groups and the BH4- anions. Finally, in a third part, the 
experimental work shows the possibility to use carrageenan (an organic molecule produced from algae) as 
a binder for the sulphur electrode for the suppression of the PS shuttling.

1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Green House Gasses and Climate Change 
One of the most concerning challenges that humanity is forced to face in the post-globalised society 
is Climate Change and its effects on our planet. This phenomenon has been subject to an increasing 
concern among the scientific community, as well as among the population. This new interest can be 
quantified by the number of publications per year that contained the words “climate change” or 
“global warming” in their title, abstract, or key words: in the last four decades, publications went 
from few hundreds to over 13 thousands per year [1]. 
There is incontrovertible evidence from different studies that the change in the average temperature 
of our planet is strictly connected with the increase in concentration of Greenhouse Gasses (GHGs) 
[2], [3]. Figure 1 (left) presents the abrupt increase of the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere during 
the last 100 years in comparison with the fluctuations in the past thousands of years. On the right, the 
image shows the historical development of temperature, since the data was recorded [4], [5]. It is 
clear that the rise of the recorded temperature shows an anomalous behaviour, most likely to be related 
with the variation of GHG concentration in the atmosphere.  
 
 
Figure 1-(Left) Development of the temperature change over the past 1000 years. The different lines show different 
references taken into consideration, and their relative uncertainty. From 1850, instrumental records are considered [6]. 
(Right) CO2 concentration in ppm over the last 1000 years recovered from the analysis of Artic ice cores [2], [3]. 
As it was reported by the Intergovernmental Panel for Climate Change in its Fifth assessment Report 
(2013), there are many other evidences that can be presented to prove the strict relationship between 
the increase in temperature and the change in CO2 levels. Furthermore, it has been proved that the 
GHG concentration increase during the last 200 years originates from anthropic sources [1]. 
 
1.2 A new pathway to sustainability 
In the last decade, great awareness has led the governmental institutions around the world to take 
action against climate change. The Paris agreement on December 12, 2016 was one of the milestones 
for the fight against climate change. The document was ratified by 176 government representatives, 
including USA, China and India. The second article of that document states that the aim of the

agreement is “to strengthen the response to the threat of climate change in the context of sustainable 
development and efforts to eradicate poverty, including by: 
 
a) “Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-
industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-

These words can represent the foundation for a new way of thinking our impact on the environment. 
The Paris agreement also stresses on an important aspect: the transition to a sustainable development 
not only includes the absence of harm to the environment, but it also requires being free of any 
consequence of social nature, as well as economically viable. This approach is fundamental to 
undertake any challenge that the globalized world is about to face.  
 
1.3 Fossil Fuels and Renewable Energy Sources 
Nowadays, the need for energy is strictly bound to the functioning and development of our economic 
system. Fossil fuels as energy source represents more than 80% of the world primary energy supply 
[7]. Electricity, heat production and transport are responsible together for over 48% of the total GHG 
emissions, as reported below Figure 2.

The urgent need for new means of producing energy in a cleaner and more efficient ways is driving 
the share increase of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) in the production mix. The installed capacity 
for hydroelectric, solar and wind power generation is rising thanks to the increase in incentive policies 
coming from the market regulators all over the world. From the World Energy Outlook published in 
2017, RES have achieved an average annual increase of 128 GW in the period 2010-2016, and they 
are expected to increase (on average) by 160 GW each year.  
The potential of these sources is represented by the low level of emission of pollutants and greenhouse 
gasses during operation, and the inexpensiveness of the almost endless primary sources, leading to 
marginal costs that are close to zero. From an economical point of view, the main obstacle to the 
development of these sources is the high initial investment cost. Their intermittent nature also 
represents a problem in terms of compliance between energy demand and production, which makes 
it still essential to include stable sources in the energy mix. Energy storage is thought to be part of the 
solution to accelerate the penetration of RES in the energy mix, allowing to be exploited more 
efficiently. With fast and reliable energy storage systems during peak production, a larger share of 
the demand could be covered by RES, even when the power plants are not collecting energy.  
 
1.4 Energy storage  
Electrical energy storage can be achieved in several ways depending on the energy carrier and the 
conversion process involved [9]: 
• 
Thermal energy storage (Latent heat, phase change materials, heat pumps) 
• 
Chemical energy storage: hydrogen, organic molecules (biomass, fuels) 
• 
Mechanical energy storage: kinetic, compressed air, pumped-hydro (potential energy 
storage using gravity)  
• 
Electromagnetic storage (capacitors, magnetic fields and superconductive materials) 
• 
Electrochemical storage (primary and secondary batteries, flow batteries) 
All these technologies present different characteristics that make them suitable for different 
applications. The main characteristics to determine the possible application of an energy storage 
technology are scalability, cost per installed capacity, efficiency, energy and power densities, and 
discharge duration. Table A-1 in the appendix reports the main values for the main technologies now 
commercialised. 
The focus of the present work is on electrochemical energy storage, more precisely in the field of 
lithium-ion rechargeable batteries. This technology, firstly brought up to the world by John 
Goodenough [10] in 1991, is suitable for portable applications since its gravimetric energy density is 
higher (up to 250 Wh/kg [11]) than other technologies and energy can be stored for a long period of 
time. However, the cost per kWh is particularly high, making it unsuitable for scaling up in large 
applications [11]. Lithium based storage arouses great interest for the up-to-date economy: they offer 
the best solution in terms of performance to power any portable rechargeable device, and lately they 
have penetrated and ruled the market of Electric Vehicles (EVs). Despite their relatively good 
performance, power delivery and efficiency, they present some drawbacks that are object of research 
and do need improvement:  
• 
cost of materials, cost per KWh 
• 
availability of materials  
• 
environmental impact: disposal/recycling

• 
Life time  
• 
Safety 
These five characteristics are crucial for the development of this technology in a sustainable way. It 
is straightforward how they are interconnected with the technical performances (namely efficiency, 
cyclability, capacity, power and energy density), and push the research towards new boundaries.  
Other than lithium, also other light elements have been studied with different chemistry and 
challenges. Cations with similar properties have also been investigated as charge carriers (Mg, Na, 
and Zn) with different configurations and operating conditions. Li-air and Zn-air batteries are worth 
to be mentioned for their high energy density values [11]. 
 
1.5 Aim of this project 
This project focuses on Lithium-Sulphur batteries (Li-S). Lithium-Sulphur batteries are attractive due 
to their high theoretical energy densities. (2600 Wh/Kg of sulphur compared to 400 Wh/Kg for 
commercial LiCoO2 cells [13]) First, after a brief introduction on the electrochemistry and the state-
of-the-art technologies related to Li-S batteries, a general description of the experimental and 
characterization techniques used is the present study is given. In a second part, the experimental 
results are presented and discussed. We give some conclusions and outlooks in the third part.  
The experimental work was performed mainly around three different topics: 
Implementation of a Three-Electrode setup for Impedance measurements on solid-state 
lithium-sulphur batteries. This work is a follow-up of the work done during a special course named 
“Three-Electrodes Setup for Impedance Measurements in Solid-State Lithium-Sulphur Batteries” 
held during the fall semester 2017. Impedance Spectroscopy performed on the three-electrode setup 
provides separate information of the contribution of the positive and negative electrodes. First we 
validated the method and then performed galvanostatic charge / discharge cycles on the batteries in 
order to understand the origin of the fading of the capacity upon cycling.  
New solid electrolytes. The ionic conductivity of LiBH4-LiBF4, LiBH4-AlO(OH) mixtures have been 
measured and compared to the LiBH4/nano-porous SiO2 composite electrolyte. X-Ray diffraction 
patterns have been collected and analysed with Rietveld refinement in order to connect the conductive 
properties with the crystalline phase composition. 
The use of a bio-binder for the realisation of carbon-sulphur based electrode. This work was 
inspired by the work from Ling et al. [12] who showed that organic molecules can trap the 
polysulphides through nucleophilic substitution and therefore prevent the detrimental shuttle 
mechanism. A new and simple crafting process has been implemented and the electrodes successfully 
tested in Li-S batteries. The successful application of this binder, coming from sea weed, to the Li-S 
technology, might represent a breakthrough towards the implementation of a cleaner and cheaper 
process in the construction of efficient Li-ion batteries.

2 Lithium-sulphur batteries  
 
Different battery chemistries have been applied over the years with more or less valuable results. One 
of the possible directions that was taken to improve Li-ion batteries energy density is the use of 
sulphur. Its intrinsic qualities make it a suitable material that might overcome problems related with 
the environmental impact, the high cost of materials and low energy density. Sulphur is one of the 
most abundant and therefore inexpensive elements on earth. [13] It has a considerable theoretical 
energy capacity if compared with other cathodic materials. The value of 1672 mAh∙g-1of S, is based 
on the conversion reaction of sulphur to lithium sulphide (Li2S) and is more than 5 times higher the 
one of LiCoO2 [14], material typically found in current Li-ion batteries. However, this reaction 
presents some limitations due to the increment in volume (around 80% volume expansion from S to 
Li2S). In addition, sulphur has a very low electronic conductivity, in the order of magnitude 
of 10−30S ∙cm−1. This feature obliges the addition of an electron conductive phase thus lowering the 
final energy density of the cell. Furthermore, the intermediate stages of the reaction between sulphur 
and lithium produce lithium polysulphides (PS), soluble in liquid electrolytes [15]. PSs migrate to the 
negative electrode during discharge passivating the lithium surface and resulting in loss of capacity. 
These challenges represent the main obstacles to overcome for the creation of long-lasting, reversible 
and efficient lithium-sulphur batteries. 
 
2.1 Mechanism in liquid electrolyte cells 
Sulphur, reacting with lithium to form Li2S, presents one of the highest values of theoretical capacities 
when compared to transition metal-oxide materials. The open circuit cell voltage is of 2.15 𝑉𝑉, which 
multiplied to the theoretical value of capacity gives up to 2.6 Wh∙g-1[16].  
Sulphur tends to form long homoatomic chains or homocyclic rings of different sizes. Octasulphur 
(S8) crystallizes in an orthorhombic phase at room temperature. Considering an ideal discharge 
reaction, the rings open, forming high-order polysulphides, Li2Sx (6 < x ≤ 8). Going further in the 
discharge, lower order compounds are formed (Li2Sx with 2 < x ≤ 6). In ether-based liquid electrolytes 
the discharge presents two plateaus at 2.3 and 2.1 V, which correspond to the reactions of S8 to Li2S4 
and Li2S4 to Li2S [15].  
Zhang et al. [16] proposed a four phase interpretation of the discharge process as shown in Figure 3.

Region II: the high order polysulphides are reduced, forming lower order compounds with a steep 
decline of the voltage curve. The sulphur chains shorten considerably, and the viscosity of the 
electrolyte solution reaches a maximum at the end of this region (Eq. 2). Up to this point the reaction

At the beginning of the charge, a small peak can be noticed (Arrow 2 in Figure 3). This shape is due 
to the decrease in polarisation because of the phase transition to the dissolved polysulphides. 
Being soluble, PSs tend to migrate at the Li electrode surface following the concentration gradient. 
This phenomenon is called redox shuttle and brings several problems like the decrease in coulombic 
efficiency and fast self-discharge rate. During discharge, lithium polysulphides tend to form on the 
anodic surface and polarise it. 
Discharge products present lower volumetric mass densities compared to sulphur (1.66 g ∙cm−3 for 
Li2S against 2.07 g ∙cm−3 for S). The change in volume of almost 80% can bring several issues and 
compromise the stability of the positive electrode material. 
There are many ways to address these two limitations. The following sections will present four 
approaches: 
==PAGE:25==

• 
Confinement of sulphur and PSs. This technique aims to immobilise sulphur and the discharge 
products in order to avoid the shuttling mechanism. 
 
• 
Saturation of the electrolyte. By saturating the liquid electrolyte with a lithium salt, the shuttling 
of PSs can be limited by acting on the thickness of the electrolyte. This approach makes the 
transport of PSs slower and can enhance the cell performance. 
 
• 
Physical membranes and Li coatings. The formation of a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) 
during the first discharge at the negative electrode surface has been reported to increase 
cyclability by preventing the passivation effect of polysulfides. A physical conductive membrane 
can also be able to stop the migration of PSs towards the negative electrode, without influencing 
the transport of ions through the electrolyte. 
 
• 
Solid electrolytes. By using a solid-state conductive material as electrolyte, it is possible to stop 
the polysulfides to move towards the negative electrode. The main problem is the lower 
conductivity of solid-state electrolytes if compared with the commonly used liquid electrolytes. 
 
In this direction, the present work is addressing the problem of PSs shuttle mechanism by applying 
two possible solutions: a solid electrolyte can mechanically immobilise PSs and prevent them to reach 
the negative electrode, the use of a bio-binder is investigated in order to prove the possibility to 
chemically immobilise PSs. 
 
2.2 Confining the sulphur and polysulphides at the cathode  
2.1.1 
Cathode materials  
A first approach is to try to confine the discharge reaction products at the positive electrode, so that 
they do not shuttle towards the negative electrode and passivate it. The material used should be able 
to immobilise the sulphur without preventing the PSs formation. Since sulphur has low electronic 
conductivity, the positive electrode substrate must be conductive. The volume of the conductive 
additive must be minimised in order not to reduce too much the final capacity of the batteries. A 
reasonable sulphur loading into the conductive phase would correspond to a final capacity of the 
cathode of 2 −3 mAh ∙cm−2 [16]. The volume change introduced by the redox reaction of sulphur 
must also be taken into account and porous materials will be a first choice to accommodate it.  
The most common choice is porous carbon. It provides high electronic conductivities and active sites 
for the PSs nucleation. The structure and porosity of the carbon can be tuned to adapt its 
morphological and conductive properties. 
Many different types of carbon have been tested: from amorphous carbon black of different porosity, 
to nanostructured materials (nanofibers, nanotubes, carbon spheres, graphene, etc.). 
The best performances have been obtained with porous structures that are able to retain sulphur in 
their structure. Hollow carbon spheres present relevant performances and are considered a less costly 
way to obtain sulphur and PSs immobilisation [15],[17]. By trapping sulphur in spherical carbon 
scaffolds, the reaction is confined inside the porous structures. Furthermore, in order to prevent or 
minimize the detrimental effect of the polysulphides shuttle, the addition of functional groups on the 
==PAGE:26==
8 
 
carbon surfaces may help for the adsorption of PS anions [18]. Other solutions have been also 
investigated to create ordered nano-structures presenting promising performance (Metal/Covalent 
Organic Frameworks, MOF, COF [4], [5]). 
The addition of metal oxides as substrate has also been tested showing a slight improvement in 
retaining the PSs at the positive electrode. The performance is influenced by the affinity between PSs 
and the metal atoms in the structure, as well as by the porosity and surface area of the substrates [21], 
[22].  
Other than carbon and metal oxides, conducting polymers have been tested for this application. 
During the first discharge, these polymers are able to chemically bond with sulphur and create a 
hydrogen type bond with PS [15]. Surprisingly, some composite materials present high cyclability, 
opening up to a different approach. Wang et al. [23] (and previously Yin et al. [24]) successfully 
tested a positive electrode material synthesised from pyrolyzed polyacrylonitrile (pPAN@S) and 
graphene nano-sheets. This material showed better cyclability and higher degree of sulphur utilization 
but with a lower S loading, due to the limited number of functional groups. This positive electrode 
has safely been used in carbonate-based electrolytes, presenting a different cycling development: 
instead of the two plateaus around 2.4 V and 2.1 V, a single plateau was observed, opening the 
hypothesis of a quasi-solid reaction at the interface as can be observed from Figure 4.

2.1.2 
Binders 
A way to trap polysulphides during the discharge can be to utilise a binder that is able to keep the 
positive electrode material in place, without dissolving into the electrolyte or creating undesired side 
products during cell operation. An important feature that binders should have in order to enhance the 
cell cyclability is the presence of functional groups that allow chemical bonding, electrostatic 
interaction, or even just weak bonding (Van Der Waals, hydrogen bonding) with the LixS species 
[25]. In addition, binders should be electrically conductive not to increase the internal losses in the 
electrodes.

The push for the research of new binders is also driven by the need for cheaper and more sustainable 
process for cathode production. The most commonly used solvents (e.g. N- methyl-2-pyrrolidone 
(NMP) used to dissolve PVDF) are very expensive and particularly toxic.  
In this work, the use of a bio-binder is investigated. Carrageenan is an organic molecule coming from 
algae processing that might possess the characteristics to make it a suitable binder: it has an important 
number of sulphate leaving groups that leave room for nucleophilic substitution with PSs, and it is 
also non-toxic and water soluble [27]. Besides the most common approach of using an active binder 
to immobilise the polysulphides, binder-free solutions are being investigated. For example, nickel 
foam template can be created and coated with graphene. After removing the nickel structure through 
an acidic solution, sulphur is deposited [28]. The devices built with this electrode material show good 
cyclability.  
 
2.3 Saturation of the electrolyte  
Polysulfides are very reactive species, especially with esters, carbonates, and phosphates [16], so they 
bring instability to a possible setup. Suitable solvents appear to be linear and cyclic ethers, such as 
dimethyl ether (DME) and 1,3-dioxolane (DOL). Dimethyl ether presents higher solubility to PSs and 
lower viscosity, but is more reactive with Li. Cyclic DOL forms a more stable solid electrolyte 
interface at the negative electrode, inducing loss of performance in the reaction kinetics and higher 
solubility of PSs. The joint use of these two solvents enhances the performance even if side reactions 
still occur between them and the active materials. Carbonate solvents may be used with particular 
positive electrode configurations that do not allow PSs dissolution.  
The balance in solubility of PSs is crucial for the optimal utilisation: too low solubility would make 
it impossible to exploit the bulk sulphur and it would reduce significantly the available capacity. 
Conversely, too high solubility would provoke the immediate PSs shuttle, resulting in fast and 
irreversible discharge. Overall, the characteristics of a good electrolyte would be:  
• 
Chemical stability towards PS species and towards lithium 
• 
Ability to solvate polysulphides 
• 
Low viscosity when PSs are dissolved in it.

2.3.1 
Additives 
Different salts can be added to the electrolyte in order to perform different tasks:  
• 
Protection of lithium electrode by forming a protective layer 
• 
Control of the PSs solubility by saturating the electrolyte 
• 
Reduction of the viscosity of the electrolyte 
The additive is to choose carefully: commonly used salts (LiPF4, LiBF4, lithium bisoxalatoborate 
LiBOB, LiBF2C2O4) react with PSs, forming unreactive LiF. These salts also start DOL ring-opening 
polymerisation due to their Lewis acid property. It is important to balance the ratio between the effect 
on viscosity and the protection of the lithium electrode. Li[CF3SO2)2N] (LiTFSI) has been proved to 
significantly improve the surface deposition of Li at the negative electrode, reducing the shuttling 
effect and maintaining relatively low viscosity at room temperature [29].  
Mikhaylik et al. [30] discovered that adding LiNO3 led to a considerable reduction of the detrimental 
effect of the shuttling phenomenon This effect is given by the formation of a thin protective layer on 
top of Li. In [16], Zhang warns about the possible reduction of this salt below 1.5 V with the 
consequent formation of compounds that irreversibly affect the performance of the cell. Still, at 
controlled potentials the thin layer tends to be consumed with cycling. In addition, the strong 
oxidative potential could cause a safety issue at high concentration in case of high temperature [16]. 
 
2.4 Physical membranes and Li coatings 
As explained in the previous section, the purpose of some additive salts is to act on the lithium 
electrode to create a protective layer. Similar Solutions have been implemented for the creation of an 
ionic conductive layer that allowed better lithium deposition during the charge and prevented the 
negative electrode to be passivated by PSs. A successful application of this technique was obtained 
by Lin et al. [31], by adding P2S5. Phosphorus pentasulfide formed a Li3PS4 layer with high lithium 
ion conductivity and the cyclability of the battery was increased. Besides, the electrolyte solvent 
strongly influences the formation of the passivation layer. For example, DOL has the tendency to be 
reduced by Li, enhancing the morphology of the lithium surface upon charge [15]. 
The possibility of adding a membrane to stop the PSs diffusion was also investigated. By 
mechanically preventing the shuttling mechanism, the viscosity of the electrolyte can be kept at 
reasonable values. Nafion membranes and other perfluorinated membranes (commonly used as 
proton exchange membranes in fuel cells) have been tested by Jin et al. [32]. Nevertheless, the overall 
internal resistance of the cell is increased; the membranes should have a high cationic conductivity. 
The total energy density is also affected by adding more “dead weight” to the system [33], [34]. 
 
2.5 Solid electrolytes  
In the last decades, the substitution of the liquid electrolyte with solid lithium conductive phases has 
been investigated to overcome a series of problems resulting from the usage of liquid electrolytes in 
the batteries. Most of the liquid or gel electrolytes are flammable and present low boiling point, with 
the risk of pressure increase and violent reaction in case of exposure to air. Furthermore, the possible 
dendrite formation at the negative electrode requires having a significant distance between the 
electrodes in order to avoid contact between electrodes. With a solid electrolyte, possible short circuit 
due to the formation of Li dendrites is decreased, possibly allowing for much thinner electrolyte 
==PAGE:29==
11 
 
layers, hence reducing the internal resistance and increasing the volumetric energy densities of the 
cells. The application of solid electrolytes to Li-S batteries is thought to remove some of the most 
common problems derived from the PSs presence. The solid-solid interface should prevent 
completely the PSs shuttling, protecting the lithium electrode from passivation.  
Despite of the positive sides of solid electrolytes, their main drawback is the conductivity at room 
temperature that is lower compared with the most common liquid electrolytes [15]. The reason for 
this phenomenon is that the conductivity mechanism in a solid phase is slightly different from the one 
in the liquid phase. In a solution, the diffusion of solvated molecules follows a different energy 
pathway: the energy barrier is theoretically constant along the entire pathway thanks to the relatively 
fast interaction between solvating molecules and ions. Conversely, the motion of an ion through a 
solid phase presents different obstacles: diffusing into a perfect crystal, species face the patterned 
interaction with the structure. Thus, the high-energy barriers to overcome are found between local 
minimum values (usually the crystallographic sites for Li). Furthermore, the energy barrier between 
grain boundaries can be even higher, resulting in higher conductivity in the bulk than through the 
grain boundaries. In addition, ion valences and sizes can influence the ionic conductivity of solid 
electrolytes: ions with higher valence tend to have lower diffusivities because of the stronger 
electrostatic interaction with the surrounding atoms. Ionic size behaves differently: the diffusion 
coefficient in beta-alumina, presents a maximum value with Na+ ions because smaller atoms would 
be trapped by electrostatic “wells” created by the counterions, increasing the activation energy for 
diffusion dramatically [35].  
A commonly used mixture of 1M LiPF6 with EC and DMC as solvents presents a conductivity of

Nevertheless, the performance of many compounds is considered to be promising. Among many 
inorganic solid electrolytes studied, the most promising are perovskite lanthanum titanates (LLTO), 
LISICON-type, LiPON-type, garnet lithium oxides and Li-P-S composites [35] (Figure 5). Some of 
these materials present remarkable conductivities (10−3~10−4S ∙cm−1), but there are some 
limitations due their electrochemical stability under high potentials and because of the high cost of

Complex metal hydrides are solids composed by an ionic lattice of metal cations and complexes

As it can be seen from Figure 6, lithium cations in the high temperature phase are arranged on a single

experimental determination of the activation energy confirmed that the calculated migration energy

hexagonal LiBH4 (0.56 eV) [43].  
Great effort has been done to stabilise the high temperature phase at room temperature: solid solutions 
with lithium halides have been successful, obtaining values of conductivity close to the ones of the 
hexagonal phase at room temperature. It was demonstrated that the substitution of the BH4- anion by 
halide ions (I-, Br- or Cl-) stabilises the hexagonal structure at room temperature and below. An 
important study to be mentioned has been developed by Orimo [44]: his group studied the 
development of conductivity and the electrochemical stability of solid solutions of lithium 
borohydride with LiI and LiNH3, reaching stable conductivities around 10−4S ∙cm−1 at room 
temperature.

3 Experimental and characterisation techniques  
 
3.1 Electrochemical measurements 
3.1.1 
The three electrodes setup 
In order to perform electrochemical measurements, it is necessary to have a stable potential against 
which it is possible to measure the potential of the electrodes. The reason for this need is given by the 
fact that any polarizing interface connected to an electrochemical system will definitely add a 
perturbation of voltage and current to the final measurement.  
In order to avoid any interference with the electrochemical system, this Reference Electrode (RE) 
must be non-polarizing, meaning that the exchange of charges at the interface should have virtually 
infinite velocity. For liquid state electrochemistry, there are several different electrodes used as 
reference, thanks to their non-polarizing ability and to their versatility towards a number of 
electrochemical environments. Among others, the most used are the Standard Hydrogen Electrode 
(SHE, it exploits the redox couple H+/H2) which is conventionally set to the value of 0 V in standard 
conditions; and the silver chloride electrode (Ag+/Ag couple in a saturated Cl- solution), with a 
standard potential of 0.230 V against SHE. 
When electrochemical measurements are performed on a cell composed by two electrodes, the 
potential difference between the RE and each electrode is monitored and can be controlled. As shown 
in Figure 7, the reference is placed in between the Counter Electrode (CE) and the Working Electrode 
(WE). The CE serves the purpose of current collector in order not to influence the interface of the 
RE. The WE is usually the element of study of the analysis, hence the interface at which the studied 
reaction occurs. Current flows only between CE and WE in order not to influence the reference 
potential. For this reason, the CE is usually a metallic mesh (generally Pt) with a large surface area 
in order not to add any potential loss due to charge deposition limitations.  
 
 
 
Figure 7-Scheme of a three electrodes setup. RE: reference electrode; CE: counter electrode; S/WE: sense/working 
electrode. Adapted from [46]. 
Concerning lithium ion batteries, many practical issues related with the positioning of the RE have 
been encountered. In order to reduce the internal resistance, the distance between electrodes should 
be small. In addition, the RE should be placed in the centre of the cell in order to provide a meaningful 
reading of the internal impedance. The wrong placement could not only lead to a misleading 
==PAGE:34==

attribution of the internal losses, but also distort the measurement by shifting the impedance spectra 
[47]. 
One of the tasks of this work was to implement a three-electrode setup for impedance measurements 
on solid-state Li-S batteries. The solid electrolyte brings some inevitable limitations to this technique: 
the contact between the solid electrolyte and the reference electrode might add new resistance to the 
system, as well as the possible presence of inductive currents. As mentioned before, it is crucial to 
ensure that the RE is not polarizing during cycling to avoid shifts in the measurements. A copper wire 
with pure lithium on the tip has been chosen as a first try, as it is believed to have an equal potential 
to the negative electrode, and should be stable in the battery environment. In chapter 4 a more detailed 
explanation of the process and results is given.

3.1.4 
Kramers-Kronig test for impedance spectra 
The Kramers-Kronig test permits to assess the reliability of impedance spectroscopy data. By 
applying a model composed by a certain number of RC circuits connected in series and in parallel, 
the test assess whether the data can be related with an equivalent circuit or not. By a least squares 
method, the model is fitted to the data. However, there is no direct evidence that if the deviation 
between fit and data is minimal, the data set can be automatically recognised as reliable. Nonetheless, 
==PAGE:39==
21 
 
the model is commonly accepted as a term of comparison [48]. When pure capacitive or inductive 
elements exist in the system, these circuit elements have to be added “manually” in the model.   
In this work, the Lin-KK Test software developed by Karlsruhe Institute of Technology was used. 
The model is based on the equations reported in [49]. 
 
3.2 X-Ray Diffraction and Rietveld refinement 
X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) is a technique utilised to analyse crystal structure and composition 
of solid-state samples. The x-rays used for this technique have a wavelength between 0.5 and 2.5Å 
because it is the same order of magnitude of the shortest interatomic distances observed. X-rays are 
commonly generated either by the interaction of electrons with a metal or by their centripetal 
acceleration (synchrotron). In the first case the interaction between electrons and the metal produces 
a large amount of heat that needs to be removed in order to maintain the signal. In the second case, 
there is no interaction of the electrons with atoms, thus there is no heat formation, but a large magnetic 
field has to be maintained in order to obtain the electron acceleration.  
The sample is exposed to a monochromatic beam. The scattered beam is recorded by a detector. A 
powder sample is generally composed of a large number of crystals arranged into random directions, 
giving a random distribution of crystal planes. When the interaction between waves scattered by 
parallel planes is constructive, the signal is “amplified”. The larger the amount of scattering planes 
facing the same direction, the larger the scattered signal at a certain angle will be. Braggs’ law 
describes the relation between the wavelength  𝜆𝜆 of the X-ray beam, the inter-plan distance 𝑑𝑑ℎ𝑘𝑘𝑘𝑘 and 
the incident angle 𝜃𝜃ℎ𝑘𝑘𝑘𝑘 (schematic representation in Figure 14 and formula in Eq. 18):  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 14-Geometrical representation of the Braggs' law [50].

therefore analyse the crystallographic structure of the sample. The scattering response at different 
angles gives diffraction peaks of which the intensity is proportional to the number of parallel crystal 
planes illuminated and the atoms, via their electron density contain in the plans.

The interaction between the beam and the atoms is different depending on the energy state of the 
electrons. Atoms of high atomic number will provide a stronger signal due to their larger electron 
density. Conversely, lighter atoms (like H or Li) give weaker signal. For this reason, each 
crystallographic compound can be identified form its unique XRD pattern. Apart from the atoms and 
molecules that form the crystal lattice, the intensity of the peaks can be influenced by an anomalous 
statistical distribution of diffraction planes. If a certain set of parallel planes is predominant, the 
intensity will be larger than expected. Nevertheless, when the conditions for a good statistical 
distribution are met (i.e. by simply repeating the measurement for a sufficient number of samples, or 
by carefully preparing the sample to avoid preferred orientation) the difference in intensity of the 
peaks will be mainly related with the possible substitution or presence of defects inside the structure 
[50]. 
The diffraction pattern is not always a perfect distribution of sharp peaks at stable positions; it strongly 
depends on several different conditions. First, instrumental precision will certainly influence the XRD 
pattern in several ways. The peak position can be shifted due to unavoidable geometric tolerances, 
but they can be detected by measuring a known sample. In addition, the wavelength of the beam is 
not always perfectly monochromatic which makes the response of the wavelength a slightly larger 
distribution of signals. Background noise can also be part of the causes for peak broadening.  
The sample itself can also be source of peak broadening in certain conditions. When the crystallite 
dimensions are too small, the amount of crystal planes that are only slightly off the Braggs’ angles 
increases, increasing the noise signals in proximity to the Braggs’ angles. With larger crystallites, the 
larger number of parallel planes allows a cleaner signal and the random distribution of off planes 
nullifies itself. 
 The measurements presented in section 5 were performed on a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer. 
For such a diffractometer, the x-ray source and the detector move simultaneously to maintain the 
same incidence angle to the sample. The sample is mounted on a rotating sample holder to guarantee 
better representability of the sample. The x-ray source is a Cu-Kα radiation (λ=0.15418 nm). 
All the parameters that influence the shape, position and intensity of the peaks can be gathered into a 
mathematical model to obtain a valuable interpretation of the XRD pattern. If the model fits with 
sufficient accuracy, quantitative results can be obtained, asserting the phase parameters of each 
present phases, as well as their quantity. In this work, a least square method implemented by Rietveld 
[51] was used. The software used is a free access program called Rietica. 
 
3.3 Scanning Electron Microscopy 
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a high resolution imaging technique involving the use of a 
high-energy electron beam interacting with the sample. By analysing the radiation and electrons 
scattered, it is possible to analyse the sample composition as well as its structure and surface 
morphology. Figure 15 shows the different possible interactions of the electrons with the sample. 
Electrons can collide with the sample’s atoms at different depth, depending on the sample 
characteristics and on the energy of the beam. Some electrons are transmitted through sufficiently 
thin samples. In such a case we are in presence of Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) [52]. 
==PAGE:41==
23 
 
 
Figure 15-Schematic of the possible interactions between the electron beam and the sample. 
 
Electrons not transmitted can have elastic or inelastic collision with the sample. If the interaction is 
elastic, the electrons have a negligible energy loss and the angle of incidence does not vary 
significantly. Whereas, for inelastic interaction, electrons from the sample can be ejected or produce 
x-ray radiation when returning their lower energy state. These secondary electrons have generally 
low energies (3-5 eV) and are collected for SEM imaging. 
Most of the incoming electrons penetrate the sample. When the sample contains heavy atoms, more 
energy is needed to penetrate the sample. In principle, more energy means more penetration, but lower 
resolution because of the larger number of secondary electrons emitted.  
 
Figure 16-Schematic of a basic SEM setup.

The interactions that occur deeper in the sample produce Back Scattered Electrons (BSEs) that can 
give information on the deeper structure of the sample.  
There are other signals (Auger electrons, x-rays, cathodoluminescence, transmitted electrons) that 
can be detected and give more information about the sample.  
The most common setup for SEM is composed by the electron source or cathode, the anode, a series 
of coils or condenser lenses that create an electromagnetic field to trap and direct the electron beam, 
and by the sensors (Figure 16). The electron source is commonly a heated tungsten filament, and a 
really high potential difference (0.1-30kV) is applied between the cathode and the anode. All the 
setup is under vacuum in order to avoid any interference with the beam. Vacuum is also fundamental 
to prevent the system contamination. Detectors may be included for other techniques such as TEM, 
x-ray detectors for Energy Dispersive x-ray Spectroscopy, EDS.  
 
3.4 Ball milling  
Ball milling is a widely spread technique utilized to alloy or react different components under 
determined conditions. The different components are mixed thoroughly, providing mechanical energy 
by means of rotational force. Hardened steel spheres are generally used as grinding medium in order 
to better distribute the mechanical energy throughout the mixed materials. The vessels that host the 
chemicals can also be filled with different gasses that can either take part in the reaction or be inert. 
In most cases, this process is used to significantly reduce the particle size and to enforce the 
interaction of two or more chemicals involved.  
In this particular work (section 4 and 5), a planetary ball mill was used (Fritsch Pulverisette P7 milling 
machine). This particular mill combines the rotation of the vessel with its counter-revolution against 
the main rotation. This mechanism provides the maximum mechanical perturbation to the grinding 
balls. Tungsten carbide balls were used in stainless steel vessels.

4 Three-Electrode setup for Impedance measurements on 
solid-state lithium-sulphur batteries 
 
4.1 Introduction 
To our knowledge, there has not been any proposal on a viable way to perform impedance 
measurement on each electrode of a solid-state battery. However, accurate study of the evolution of 
the internal impedance of a battery should help understanding the degradation processes during 
operation. It is clear from the examination of the impedance spectra collected across the full cell, that 
degradation occur but it is impossible to tell whether it is at the positive or negative electrodes or the 
electrolyte. A considerable step forward can be made by understanding the mechanism occurring at 
each electrode, allowing to face directly and to solve the problem of the degradation observed during 
cycling of solid-state Li-S batteries.  
The choice of materials and chemistries for the reference electrode is the first obstacle for the 
implementation of a three-electrode setup for solid-state batteries. It was mentioned in paragraph 3.1 
that a good RE should have a non-polarising reaction occurring in order to not interfere with the 
voltage measurement. A second obstacle is the building process: it is fundamental to assemble the 
cell so that the electrodes and the solid electrolyte are in close contact, meaning that some mechanical 
pressure should be applied. The application of pressure might influence the position and the 
morphology of the RE. It is fundamental to be able to place the RE in the centre of the device in order 
to collect meaningful data.  
A third issue that has to be taken into account is the interface between a hypothetical metal reference 
electrode and the solid electrolyte: the excessive accumulation of charge at this interface might cause 
the formation of a parasite capacitance that might distort the impedance measurement. Overall, the 
electrode should not add any further impedance to the system that cannot be isolated from the measure 
of the actual electrode impedances. As long as the contribution is constant, it can be isolated and 
subtracted from the data. Nevertheless, this procedure may require the implementation of a precise 
and accurate way of analysing the data from the impedance measurements.  
Herein, the final choice for the reference electrode was a 0.5 mm copper wire with some lithium 
mechanically deposited on the top. The top of the wire was positioned in the centre of the cell. Copper 
wire was chosen because it has the ability to alloy with Li[53]. Furthermore, the lithium interface 
should be stable towards the electrolyte material, reducing the chance for interference in the 
measurement and garantying the same electrochemical potential to both the anode and the RE. The 
diameter of the Cu wire was chosen as a compromise between mechanical strength and minimal 
impact in the overall functioning of the cell: the larger diameter is, the more the electrolyte thickness 
must be to avoid contact between the RE and the other electrodes. Reduced dimensions would also 
have less impact on the internal resistance of the cell. 
 
 
==PAGE:44==

4.2 Experimental  
Figure 17 presents a schematic of the three-electrode cell. It is composed of a PEEK cylindrical holder 
and two steel electrodes to collect the current. The diameter of the current collectors is 8mm and the 
diameter of the inner cylinder is 9 mm. This setup has a lateral hole where a wire can be held in place 
by a plastic piece. The plastic piece has a hole of 0.6 mm in the centre to host the wire of 0.5 mm in 
diameter.  
The final configuration was chosen after trying different solutions that involved the use of different 
materials and thicknesses for the wire (0.5 or 1 mm for steel and copper wires). After a series of 
attempts, it has been noticed that mechanically depositing some lithium on the tip of the metal wire 
would provide a stable potential to compare the development of the two electrode potentials.  
 
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 17-Configuration of the three-electrode setup. 3D cut and lateral view. 
 
The solid-electrolyte chosen for this study is a ball-milled mixture of SiO2 Aerogel and LiBH4 in the 
proportion of 70 to 30 wt. % and a ready-made carbon-sulphur cathode, prepared following the 
procedure explained in section 3.5. Aerogel is a porous amorphous SiO2 with pores in the order of 4 
to 5 nm. The mixture was ball-milled for five hours at 300 rpm with a balls to powder weight ratio of 
118:1. Such a solid-electrolyte was used after the work performed by J. Lefevr showing the possibility 
to cycle Li-S solid-state batteries [13]. 
A study varying the pressure to press the powder into pellet was conducted to understand the impact 
of the pressure when a Cu wire was included in between two layers of powder. The purpose was to 
avoid deformation of the wire while obtaining a compact pellet with the wire in the middle. The best 
result was given when the pellet was subject to a pressure of around 1.25 105 kPa (1 ton over 0.7854 
cm2). Conductivity tests were first performed on the composite SiO2/LiBH4 electrolyte. Results are 
shown in Figure 44 in section 5.3.5.  
All the cells were prepared under inert atmosphere, i.e. in an argon glove-box (O2 and H2O < 1ppm), 
to prevent the contact of LiBH4, Lithium and other components with air and humidity. First, a lithium 
disc of 8 mm and thickness of 40 µm was cut and scraped to remove possible surface oxide layer. 
==PAGE:45==
27 
 
After being placed on the stainless steel current collector and inserted inside the PEEK cylinder, a 
known quantity of electrolyte (80mg) was put on top of the lithium and pressed mechanically with at 
1 ton for 30 to 60 seconds. Next, the extremity of the Cu wire, flattened with sand paper, was 
mechanically covered with Li metal. The positioning of the extremity the Cu wire within the cell is 
important because the potential will be measured between the electrodes and the lithium coated on 
the wire. As explained before, the tip should be placed in the centre of the battery.  
After the positioning of the reference electrode, more electrolyte powder is added and pressed. A 
larger quantity (120 −150 mg) was added in order to account for the added thickness of the wire 
and avoid the contact between the positive electrode and the wire. At this point, the mechanical 
pressure applied is lowered (0.75 −1 ton) to prevent the deformation of the wire and its penetration 
in the electrolyte layer underneath it.  
Finally, the ready-made C-S cathode is placed on top of the electrolyte and the setup is closed with 
the current collector and a sealing gasket. Everything is clamped together to ensure contact and 
prevent external air to penetrate and affect the cell and the battery is placed in a climate chamber with 
a set temperature of 55 °C. 
The procedure followed to build the C-S electrode was inspired by Das et al. [39], with some 
modification due to the available appliances and materials. The conductive phase is composed by  
Ketjen Black EC-600JD (surface area 1400 m2g−1, pore volume 4.80 −5.10 m3g−1, Shanghai 
Tengmin Industry Co., Ltd.) and activated carbon Maxsorb® MCS-30 (surface area 3000 m2g−1, 
1.7  m3g−1,, Maxsorb®, Japan) in a 1:1 weight ratio. The two types of carbon were used in order to 
give a balance between large pore size and sufficiently large surface area. Sulphur from Sigma 
Aldrich (99.9% purity) was added in a 45:55 sulphur to carbon weight ratio and the mixture was ball-
milled for 30 minutes at 400 rpm in a Fritsch Pulverisette P7 milling machine. Next, the powder was 
heated to 155 °C for 6 hours in a sealed vessel under argon atmosphere. The heating rate was 
0.2°C/min in order to ensure the melt infiltration of S into the carbon pores. After cooling down, the 
sulphur content was checked with thermo-gravimetric (TG) analysis.   
Finally, the electrode was prepared previously by mixing overnight the C-S in a suspension with 
PVDF (10-15% wt.) and NMP. The slurry was casted on aluminium foil (0.2mm) and dried at 50-
60°C for 24 hours.  
All measurements have been collected digitally, utilising the Bio-logic software for electrochemical 
measurements. After a first OCV of 1 hour to allow the stabilisation of temperature, a first 
Potentiostatic Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (PEIS) is recorded. Then discharge- charge 
cycles are operated. After each charge and discharge, PEIS is recorded between the three electrode 
combinations: negative electrode-reference, positive electrode-reference, and negative-positive 
electrodes.  
The frequency range imposed for the impedance spectroscopy is set from 1 Hz to 10 kHz with a 
voltage amplitude of 10 mV, and sampling 10 points per decade. 
For the cycling of the battery, a constant current of 10 𝜇𝜇𝐴𝐴 is set, and the cut-off voltages are 0.9 V for 
discharge and 5 V for charge in order not to stress the cell by reaching extreme potentials.

interphase. The larger amount of Li contains in the negative electrode than the amount required to 
reduce the sulphur contained in the positive electrode, would allow to form this interphase without 
reducing the capacity of the battery during the following cycles. The nature of this interphase, is 
formed is not known, but could be formed by a reaction with the carbon constituting the electrode. 
The fact that during the following cycles, the theoretical amount of sulphur entering in the 
composition of the electrode is used proves that the interphase formation did not involve sulphur. The 
“extra” lithium cations involved in this parasitic reaction are furthermore bound irreversibly (at least 
with the cut-off voltage used herein). Attempts to clarify the nature of this parasitic reaction and the 
possible composition of the interphase have not been concluding.  
Interestingly, both the total and positive electrode impedance face an initial drop, so it could be that 
the created interphase has a beneficial effect and increases the charge transport capability between 
the electrolyte and the positive electrode. This enhancement of the SE/Sulphur electrode interface 
conductivity could also be explained by the first expansion the positive electrode brings a better 
contact. With further cycling, the increase in impedance might originate from the mehcanical stresses 
impose by the expansion / contraction of S and reaction products. The oscillation of the value of 
==PAGE:52==
34 
 
positive electrode resistance could be explained by this “breathing” mechanism. This mechanism 
could also involve the difference in conductivity between S and Li2S. The overall increase in internal 
impedance cannot be related with the electrolyte because its high stability has been demonstrated in 
several cases [13],[40], [54], [55]. 
As explained before, the change in the negative electrode impedance is most likely to be related with 
the morphological modification of the RE/solid electrolyte interface, and should not involve the 
negative electrode. From this experience there is no evidence that the presence of the RE is 
influencing the cycling performance, but further investigation is strongly suggested. 
 
4.3 
Conclusions 
A three-electrode setup for impedance spectroscopy measurement was implemented for all-solid state 
batteries. The Kramers-Kronig test showed that the values of impedance for high frequencies did not 
match with a combination of RC circuits. For this reason, data were further analysed without 
considering the points recorded at high frequency. The reason for this disturbance can be mainly 
related with the sensitivity of the instrument at high frequencies and the difficulty for the instrument 
to measure with a good accuracy and low noise extremely low current (in the order of pA). 
The contribution to the total resistance related with charge transport limitations was successfully 
assigned to the positive half-cell. After the initial discharge, from a parasitic electrochemical reaction 
a new interphase might form at the SE/S electrode interface to combine with the expansion of the S-
electrode which might provide better contact at the interface, with the positive effect of decreasing 
the overall impedance of the cell. With cycling, the impedance at this side of the cell increases 
probably due to the expansion and contraction of the electrode that can cause bad contact.  
The negative electrode half-cell impedance showed an inductive behaviour that is most likely to be 
related with the RE. The increase of L and RL could be explained with the progressive change of the 
Cu wire with deposition or maybe even alloying with Li. Since symmetrical cells showed high 
reversibility, the major contribution to the increase in resistance at the lithium electrode is believed 
to come from the interaction between lithium and copper at the RE. 
To conclude, the following improvements should clarify the cell behaviour: 
 
• 
Higher precision: the instrument used to record the EIS should have a higher precision in the 
measurement, especially at lower current (high frequencies). In this way, the influence from the RE 
could be better identified and isolated with a precise model. 
• 
Changing RE material: changing the material from Cu to another material that is inert in contact 
with Li and the electrolyte could improve the measurement. Without any interaction, the inductive 
contribution and the related resistance can be stabilized and taken out from the final EIS.  
• 
Changing the RE configuration: the presence of the Cu wire inside the electrolyte might be the 
source of the inductive behaviour. This is why it could be preferable to have a point RE instead of a 
wire. Since the active section of the RE was the tip of the wire, a dot placed in contact with the 
external part of the electrolyte should be used. This solution might still bring some problems related 
to the conduction and close contact of the RE with the electrolyte, but it should supress the inductance 
component.  
==PAGE:53==
35 
 
• 
Check for changes in the electrolyte: it could be interesting to see if the electrolyte has some kind 
of alteration after cycling. In this way, the hypothesis of phase change could be proved or eliminated. 
• 
Analysis of the interaction between S and LiBH4: the nature of the positive electrode / SE 
interface might play a crucial role in the decrease of the cell capacity upon cycling, so it is important 
to understand the processes occurring there. 
• 
 EIS at different discharge states: impedance spectroscopy was only recorded in fully charged or 
discharged state. It could be important to know the dynamic of the process during charge and 
discharge.

5.1 
Introduction 
The expectation for the (LiBH4-LiBF4) mixture was to obtain an enhanced conductivity via the 
substitution of H by F in the BH4- anion. In the orthorhombic phase, the BH4- anions have smaller size 
than the fluorinated anions (1,14 Å for B-H and 1.39 Å for B-F bond length [56], [57] ), therefore it 
was expected that such a substitution would modify the crystal structure with a possible positive effect 
on the lithium conductivity. Furthermore, F is more electronegative than hydrogen, which could be 
able to modify the position of the surrounding Li ions, bringing a structure destabilisation and causing 
the formation of defects (Frenkel pairs) or the stabilization of a more conductive phase. The 
possibility for such substitution has been documented by Richter et al. [58]. 
They proposed an in situ study of the same system. They showed that partial substitution of hydrogen 
by fluorine atoms in LiBH4 occur at low temperature, lowering the decomposition of the borohydride 
down to 80 °C, an interesting temperature for the application of the material as solid-state hydrogen 
storage. Unfortunately, this substitution also triggered unwanted release of diborane (B2H6), and the 
activation of a side-reaction with LiBH4 (forming Li2B12H12) in competition with the hydrogen release 
and with the fluoride substitution itself. In addition, with the substitution of H by F atoms, the 
formation of LiF and BF3 was also detected.  
Herein, very different synthesis conditions have been applied aiming to obtain a compound of high 
Li conductivity.  
The study of the LiBH4-AlO(OH) composite follow the very recently published findings from Lefevr 
et al. [13] and Choi et al. [45]. In these publications, is it proposed that the hydroxide groups (-OH) 
present at the surface of the insulating oxides (SiO2 [13], Al2O3[38], [45]) alter the structure of LiBH4 
by the formation of a bound between the oxygen and boride groups (BH4-). It results an amorphous 
layer of modified LiBH4 at the interface between the oxides and bulk LiBH4 giving the high Li+ 
conductivity of the composites. The reaction proposed by Lefevr is the following:

This interaction is supposed to increase the concentration in anion vacancies in the interface lithium

AlO(OH) offers a higher density of surface -OH groups if compared with SiO2. This experiment can 
give a confirmation of the good benefit of the interaction between the hydroxide group and the 
borohydride anion. 
These two newly synthesised electrolytes, LiBH4-LiBF4 and LiBH4-AlO(OH) were characterised by 
EIS in order to obtain the value of their conductivity. The study of conductivity cannot explain by 
itself the connection between this parameter and the changes in the conductive phase. Thus, XRD 
was used to identify the different phases in the samples. By detecting the trend in concentration and 
the modification of the phase parameters, it might be possible to isolate the conductive phases and 
give a first suggestion for the origin of the ionic conductivity.

5.2 
Experimental  
5.2.1 Solid electrolyte preparation 
The LiBH4-LiBF4 mixtures were prepared by Peter N’Gene from Utrecht University, in different 
ratios (from 5 to 35 wt. %. of LiBF4, in steps of 5%). The required amounts of LiBH4 and LiBF4 were 
thoroughly mixed in a mortar and the mixture placed inside a stainless steel reactor which was then 
inserted into an autoclave. This was done in an argon filled gloved box (typically < 1 ppm of O2 and 
H2O). The autoclave was properly tightened and pressurized with an initial hydrogen pressure of 50 
bar and heated to 200-250 °C at a rate of 5 °C/min and with a dwell time of 30 minutes at the 
maximum temperature. After this, the autoclave was allowed to cool down to ambient temperature, 
after which the hydrogen gas was evacuated and the autoclave brought back to the glove box for the 
removal of the samples. 
To prepare the composite solid-electrolyte (SE), AlO(OH) was first dried for 12 hours at 393 K in a 
vacuum furnace and thereafter hand mixed with LiBH4. To prove the success of the drying process, 
AlO(OH) was analysed with XRD before and after the process. The difference can be clearly spotted 
for a peak at around xxx that could be related with the presence of Al2O3 (pictures A 2 and A 3 in the 
appendix). All the sample handling was performed in a glove box under argon atmosphere. The 
sample was further mixed by ball-milling using a Fritsch Pulverisette P7 planetary ball mill, at 500 
rpm for 5 hours with intervals of 5 minutes of milling interrupted by 5 minutes of dwelling to prevent 
a too large temperature increase in the milling vials. 
On the top of achieving a homogeneous mixture of the two compounds, ball milling brings in close 
contact the reactant and should promote the reaction between the hydroxide groups and LiBH4. 
Composite electrolytes of three different compositions were prepared (20/80, 30/70, 40/60 wt. % 
LiBH4/AlO(OH)) 
As a benchmark, a 30/70 wt % LiBH4/SiO2 Aerogel SE was also prepared following the same 
procedure. As reminder, this stoichiometry was found to bring the higher conductivity [13].

5.3.2 XRD and Rietveld refinement of LiBH4-LiBF4 
X-ray diffraction has been performed for all the samples and followed by identification of the 
composing crystallographic phases and Rietveld refinement. According to [58], possible chemical 
reaction between LiBH4 and LiBF4 may occur, releasing di-borane in the gaseous phase (B2H6) and 
thus producing new different phases such as lithium dodeca-borane (Li2B12H12) and lithium fluoride 
(LiF). The further phase identification suggested the presence of lithium deca-borane (Li2B10H10) 
which was then included in the analysis. 
For this reasons, Rietveld refinement was performed considering the presence of the following 
phases: LiF (cubic, Fm-3m), LiBH4 (orthorhombic, Pnma), LiBF4 (orthorhombic, Pnma), Li2B12H12 
(cubic, Pa-3) and Li2B10H10 (hexagonal, P64 22). During the refinement, peak positions were satisfied 
but some of the peaks intensities were difficult to model with full stoichiometric compositions. This 
phenomenon is usually observed when there is substitution of some atoms in the crystal structures or 
presence of defects such as vacancies. An optimization of the occupancy parameters of certain species 
(Li, H and F) did not end up in meaningful results. Nevertheless, letting these parameters free to 
adjust, we observed a tendency to decrease the occupancy of F and Li especially in the LiBH4 and in 
LiF. To further improve the model (and as suggested from [58]), F atoms were set at the hydrogen 
position in LiBH4. The optimization with this model resulted in a minor improvement of the fit, 
reducing the presence of H atoms in the same occupational site. The following pictures (Figure 33 
and 34) show two examples of XRD patterns for the 80/20 wt. %. and for the 90/10 wt. %. sample 
with the corresponding Rietveld refinement fit (red line). The green line shows the deviation between 
data and the model.

6 Use of a bio-binder for S-C based cathodes 
 
6.1 Introduction 
As explained in chapter 2, the main problems in using sulphur as active material for lithium secondary 
cells is related with polysulfide dissolution and shuttling towards the negative electrode side. In order 
to overcome this issue, many solutions have been suggested so far: changing the solubility of PS in 
the electrolyte by saturating it with a lithium salt, encapsulating sulphur into a matrix in order to 
prevent the reaction products to leave the positive electrode, or mechanically stop the shuttling with 
an ionic conductive membrane or with a solid electrolyte. All these techniques represent a viable 
solution, but they often imply the addition of non-active materials, as well as the use of complicated 
and costly techniques to build the cell itself. Important advancement has been also carried out in the 
research for novel binders able to maintain the cathode structure. A good binder should be able to 
provide good stability towards the reducing and oxidising environment, while being chemically stable 
and providing PS immobilisation. Another challenge regarding binders is represented by the 
simplification of the crafting process and the environmental impact. Common binders like PVDF and 
PTFE are commonly solvated in N-Methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) [60], and they require inert 
atmosphere with complicated ventilation system for the recovery of the volatile fraction. In this 
direction, the use of aqueous solutions to produce the sulphur electrode would certainly decrease costs 
and the impact on the environment. The main obstacle of this approach is represented by the 
wettability of carbon that is low in water-based solutions.  
An interesting work has been carried out by Ling at al. [12], presenting a viable approach to fulfil the 
requirements explained before. In their paper, they present the possibility of exploiting nucleophilic 
substitution to strongly bind PS to an active binder. Poly(vinyl-sulphate) potassium salt (PVS, a 
commonly used synthetic polymer) and carrageenan (a material derived from seaweeds and used 
primarily in food industry as thickening agent). Those binders incorporated in the electrode via  water-
based solutions, resulted in  consistent nucleophilic substitution of the sulphate groups with PS. The 
cathode performance was then rated. The cycling performances, of test cells build with those binders, 
are shown in the following graph (Figure 46): 
 
 
Figure 46-Performance of cells with PVDF, PVS, and carrageenan binders, from [12]. 
==PAGE:72==
54 
 
Nucleophilic substitution on the polymeric chain is believed to produce a strong covalent bond 
between the carbon and the sulphur contained in polysulfides. Many factors can influence the 
performance of this solution: the ability to polarise for both the leaving and the nucleophilic group, 
the chance for the leaving group to react with the environment, and the possible interaction between 
the two functional groups. The publication from Ling et al. [12] gave the inspiration for the 
experimental work presented herein. 
The aim of the experimental work presented here below was to prove the functionality of carrageenan, 
by implementing a new procedure to produce the sulphur electrode, using a simple procedure under 
ambient conditions. Carrageenan is soluble in water and completely non-toxic, it has high 
adhesiveness, a remarkable concentration of sulphate leaving groups. Furthermore, it is 
biodegradable. Its functional groups as shown in Figure 47 are supposed to bind with PS by 
nucleophilic substitution and prevent their shuttling. Carrageenan is also supposed to stick to the 
carbon substrate while maintaining a relatively high elasticity to accommodate any volume changes 
in the electrode.  
 
 
Figure 47-Carrageenan structure and interaction with lithium PS [12]. 
 
Ling et al. [12] proved the bonding of PS with carrageenan through nucleophilic substitution by 
ultraviolet-visible analysis. The sulphate group leaves the molecule, being substituted by the PS that 
bonds with the active carbon site by the nucleophilic sulphur. In the same article Li-S battery with a 
C-S cathode built with this bio-binder gave capacity of 700 mAh/g (of sulphur) after 140 cycles.  
The purpose of the work presented herein was to be able to reproduce the results from [12],  
implementing a method to prepare the positive electrode . The positive electrodes were tested to prove 
the feasibility of the process and assess the possibility of using this bio-binder for Li-S batteries.  
 
6.2 Experimental  
6.2.1 
Powder preparation and tape casting of the positive electrode 
The same procedure as explained in section 4.2, to produce the positive electrodes with PVDF, was 
used to prepare the C-S powder that was then mixed with carrageenan. Carrageenan was kindly 
provided by CP-Kelco, and the one containing 36 wt % of sulphate groups was used. The weight ratio 
adopted was initially of 1:8.3 sulphur to active binder, then it was raised to 1:2.35 to ensure the 
bonding between sulphur and the active binder. Distilled water was used as a solvent. The 5:1 solvent 
to powder weight ratio suggested in [12] could not initially be respected due to the poor wettability 
of the powders. More solvent was therefore used. The solution was stirred while being heated up until 
obtaining sufficiently thick slurry to be casted on aluminium foil, as shown in Figure 48. Some of the 
==PAGE:73==
55 
 
samples were subject to sonification before being casted in order to test the effect on dispersion of 
this technique and to help mixing the components, given the low wettability of carbon. 
 
 
Figure 48-Tape casting of the C-S slurry on Al foil. 
The different parameters were tested for the implementation of a viable procedure.   
• 
Stirring of the solution for different duration (1 hour, 1 day, 8 days) 
• 
Temperature of the solution during stirring (50-60°C and 80-85°C) 
• 
Stirring and bath sonification duration:  1, 2 or  3 hours (always below 90°C) 
• 
 Different drying process (RT, oven at 45°C overnight, 30 mbar vacuum for 5-10 minutes, 30 
mbar vacuum overnight) 
• 
 Different blades for casting (4MIL and 6MIL corresponding to 101.6µm and 152.4µm 
thicknesses) 
These parameters have been varied in order to obtain satisfactory results in the homogeneity of the 
tape casting, consistency and dispersion of the slurry. The speed of the blade was kept constant at 
around 0.1 m/s for all the samples. Ultimately, batteries were assembled in CR2320 type coin cells, 
built following the procedure in section 6.2.2. and tested to assess the effectiveness of the crafting 
process. 
 
6.2.2 
Electrolyte and cell assembly 
Positive electrodes have been punched into 18mm discs to be assembled into CR2032 type coin cells

1M concentration of bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiTFSI).

also likely that the thick electrode did not allow to reach all the active sulphur. A better pore size 
distribution may increase the performance.

shape of discharge does resemble the ones found in literature: there is a first plateau around 2.39V, 
followed by a second plateau around 2.1V.  
These two plateaus are connected with the reactions 1, 3 and 4 presented in section 2. An unexpected 
development is noticed at the end of the discharge for the battery with PVDF. Around 1.93 V, the 
discharge takes a smoother trend, increasing the total capacity. In previous cycles the capacity of this 
battery was over the theoretical value of 1672 mAg-1. This trend suggests that some side reaction is 
occurring after all the available sulphur has reacted.  
The two carrageenan cells present a much smoother end of discharge, even if a small change of trend 
can be noticed around the same voltage. Overall, the capacity of the fifth cycle is higher in the case 
of the PVDF battery (1347 mAg-1, against 1028 mAg-1 for the vacuum-dried and 1049 mAg-1 for the 
air dried), but the difference is mostly related with the behaviour of the PVDF battery at the end of 
discharge. This phenomenon can be explained by the slow transportation of PSs towards the negative 
electrode that is activated because all sulphur has reacted into PSs.  
The air-dried cathode presents a slightly improved capacity if compared with the vacuum-dried. The 
reason for this phenomenon can be explained with the faster drying of the latter that did not allow 
cracking of the surface, thus reducing significantly the contact between the electrolyte and the active 
material (S) in the positive electrode. 
 Another important feature of the carrageenan batteries is that the drop in voltage after the first plateau 
ends more smoothly than for the PVDF battery. This drop is related with the phase change of Li2S8 
into lower order polysulfides (Eq. 2), and a smoother drop remarks a smaller drop in energy 
dispersed for the phase transition. 
In Figure 55.a the evolutions of capacity, for the three batteries throughout cycling are reported. The 
smaller number of data points for the carrageenan was due to the lack of time and the long battery 
cycling. After the first 6-10 cycles, the trend for the all three cells seems to be constant around a 2% 
capacity loss. This constant loss could suggest that the reaction that provokes this capacity fading is 
always the same throughout the cycling for the three different types of electrode. 
For the same current, different periods of time were required to discharge completely the cells. Indeed 
the sulphur amount in the batteries was different giving different capacities: 0.41 mAh/cm2 for the 
air-dried, 0.64 mAh/cm2 for the vacuum-dried and 0.23 mAh/cm2 for the PVDF. The higher capacity 
were obtained for the blade giving the thicker electrode when performing the casting on the 
aluminium foil, and the larger amount sulphur present in the slurry. However, higher sulphur loading 
does not necessarily means higher capacity since not all the sulphur might be available for the 
electrochemistry to perform. .

The Coulombic efficiencies (reported in Figure 55.b), for the cells built with carrageenan is lower if 
compared with the cell built with PVDF. After a first fluctuation, the efficiency for the PVDF battery 
is constant around 97% throughout cycling. The air dried cathode with carrageenan showed a 
stabilisation of the efficiency around 95% after a peak of 102% at the forth cycle, while the vacuum-
dried carrageenan battery shows oscillating values between 85 and 96%. The variation   of the 
coulombic efficiencies can have different origins. Most likely, the instability is related with changes 
in the morphology of the interface between charge and discharge, the possible presence of side 
reactions during charge, or more simply the slow depletion of S due to the shuttling mechanism. With 
further cycling, a difference in the capacity-fading trend could clarify this phenomenon. 
 
6.4 Conclusions 
The experiment successfully demonstrated the possibility of applying carrageenan as a bio-binder to 
avoid PS shuttle mechanism during battery cycling. Even though the performance did not surpass the 
one from the battery built with PVDF, the sharper end of discharge suggests that PSs were better 
immobilised by the binder. The most important reason for the lower performance of these batteries 
must be connected with the difficult tuning of the electrode material. Even if the loading was higher, 
the amount of S available for the reaction was smaller due to the thickness of the electrode. Comparing 
the two different carrageenan cells, the lower capacity of the vacuum dried can be related to  the 
surface morphology. With a faster drying, the surface is expected to be less cracked. Consequently, 
the electrolyte won’t wet as easily the interior of the electrode and therefore  less S will be available.  
For the air dried cathode, the surface shows much more cracks, as much as entry point for the 
electrolyte to diffuse in the electrode.  
The constant capacity fading of 2 % suggests that the mechanism of degradation inside the cell is 
always the same after a reduced amount of cycles (6 to 10). The lower efficiency is yet to be explained 
but it is most likely related with changes in the morphology of the sulphur electrode, the possible 
presence of side reactions or the presence of PSs shuttling. The behaviour of carrageenan as a binder 
has not yet been studied in terms of volume change, binding with the conductive carbon and voltage 
stability.

In conclusion, carrageenan can be seen as a viable binder for Li-S batteries. Further analysis on its

Porosity and thickness should also be tuned in order to allow full contact between the liquid 
electrolyte and the sulphur.

7. Summary and Outlook 
 
7.1 Summary 
The aim of this project was to study two different solutions to resolved the problem of the 
polysulphides shuttle in Lithium-Sulphur batteries. By using a solid electrolyte, PSs are expected to 
stay at the sulphur electrode during cycling. This simple solution brings other consistent drawbacks 
to the system:  the low electrolyte conductivity and  unknown interactions at the solid-solid  
electrolyte -sulphur electrode interface. 
For this reason, a new three-electrode setup for impedance measurements in solid-state Li-S batteries 
was created to analyse the development of impedance at the positive and negative electrodes.  
New electrolytes were synthesised and studied.  The study on the conductivity of different mixtures 
of LiBH4 (LiBH4-LiBF4 and LiBH4-AlO(OH)) was carried out. XRD analysis on the LiBH4-LiBF4 
mixture was also carried out to identify the change in phase parameters and suggest the conductive 
phase. 
Finally, the possible use of a bio-binder, to exploit nucleophilic substitution to bind PSs at the positive 
electrode was also investigated during this work. Finding a new and clean binder for these batteries 
represents a source of great interest since it might greatly improve the cell performance without 
increasing or even reducing costs and environmental impact.  
Carrageenan (an organic molecule produced from algae) was used in this work because its 
nucleophilic substitution capabilities of sulphates was believed to provide good anchoring of PS  [12].  
The main findings of this work are reported as follows:  
• 
The three-electrode setup created for the impedance analysis on solid-state batteries gave 
promising results, showing that it is indeed possible to assess the two half-cell impedances 
and to separate the contribution of the two from the total impedance of the cell. 
 
• 
The increase in impedance during cycling can be mainly assigned to the positive electrode. The 
creation of a conductive interface at the positive electrode could be  the main reason for the 
change in available capacity from the first to the second discharge. Mechanical stresses due 
to the breathing of the positive electrode could also have a positive effect during the first 
discharge but negative effect afterwards  
 
• 
The measurement obtained still requires some improvement in order to obtain data with less noise 
. The use of a non-polarizing interface for the reference electrode is crucial for the reliability 
of data. 
 
• 
Conductivity analysis of LiBH4-LiBF4 did not result into high enough values that could make the 
material a possible candidate for the use as a solid-electrolyte. Nevertheless, some 
substitution inside the conductive phase was spotted. The possible interaction between the 
dominant phase (LiF) and the others could have been the origin for the conductivity obtained 
and the temperature shift in phase transition. 
 
==PAGE:82==
64 
 
• 
The LiBH4-AlO(OH) mixture presents comparable conductivity (0.15 mScm-1 at 30 °C for the 
30/70 wt. % mixture) with the LiBH4-SiO2 powder (0.1 mScm-1 at 30 °C for the 30/70 wt. % 
mixture), opening to a new composition for composite electrolyte. The 30/70% wt. 
composition showed high temperature stability and high conductivity, making it a good 
candidate as solid-electrolyte in Li-S batteries. 
 
• 
Carrageenan was successfully applied as a binder for C-S positive electrolytes. The cycling shape 
and performance showed promising results, with room for improvement and tuning of the 
different parameters that influence the cyclability as well as the efficiency. 
A simple method to prepare the electrode has been implemented.  
 
7.2 Outlook 
The number of possibilities to extend this work is virtually endless due to the variety of parameters 
that influenced the results of this experimental work. Some of the possible suggestions for further 
research are collected in the following lines. 
Relatively to the three-electrode setup, there is room for improvement especially thanks to the variety 
of possible configurations that can be created to implement a three-electrode setup. A possibility 
would be to try a dot configuration instead of a wire configuration. In this way the noise due to 
inductive behaviour could be avoided, and the positioning of the reference would be easier from a 
mechanical point of view. The choice of material for the reference electrode can also influence the 
final result: a more stable interface translates into a more stable measurement and the absence of 
misleading inductance in the final impedance measurement.  
For what concerns the conductivity measurement on the LiBH4 mixtures, the substitution that was 
noticed in the LiBH4-LiBF4 could be further improved by changing the approach to the creation of 
the mixture. A deeper structural analysis could also provide more information about the conduction 
mechanism inside the phases. It was in fact clear from this work that the conductivity of this material 
has to be connected with a much complex mechanism that involves more than two phases. 
The LiBH4-AlO(OH) sample requires a more detailed tuning of the ratio between materials. Once the 
optimal ratio is found, a structural analysis is suggested in order to prove the hypothesis according to 
which conductivity is given by the amorphous LiBH4 situated at the interface with alumina oxide 
hydroxide. Once this interaction is well studied, the possibility of using even more OH reach materials 
could be a further step to enhance conductivity even more.  
The use of carrageenan as a binder represents a viable solution to many issues related with Li-S 
batteries. This experiment only confirmed that it is possible to use it for this particular application. 
The following step would be to tune the characteristics of the electrode in order to maximize its 
performance. The amount of binder in proportion with the other materials should be optimized. 
Moreover, thickness and porosity play a crucial role to the cyclability and sulphur utilization. And 
finally, a more detailed imaging study could be done in order to analyse the actual interaction between 
the materials involved.
