ABSTRACT
Nowadays, the proportion of renewables is growing fast thanks to the increase number of
prosumers and distributed energy resources (DERs). In this context, the electricity markets
need to evolve in order to be adapted to the evolution of the generation mix, player types as
well as a more decentralized structure. In this regards, the concept of energy communities
based on peer-to-peer energy network can potentially boost the transition by providing
optimized integration of prosumers into the system. In parallel, the Distribution System
Operator requires a steady operation of the grid to avoid congestion and voltage problems.
With continual grow of residential consumption and distributed resources, congestion
becomes one of the most challenging operation issues in distribution network. Therefore,
a certain amount of ﬂexibility services are needed to face the stability problems at the
distribution level.
This thesis investigates the provision of ﬂexibility services through energy communities.
Peaks shaving, backup power and smoothing power are tested in this study. The context
is an energy community composed totally by prosumers supported by controllable loads,
with additional integration of local wind turbines and an electric energy storage. The
community can share their resources by means of P2P markets. New business models can
be developed in relation to this technological, regulatory and economic framework.
The results of this analysis show an eﬃcient provision of the treated services by means
of energy communities. Peak shaving for small case studies can be optimally performed
reducing up to 30% the grid demand. As regards backup power, it is found out that
an energy community can operate in islanding mode for 70% yearly for at least 6 hours
daily supported by the DERs. Moreover, energy communitie can eﬃciently smooth the
power injection fed into the grid, avoiding voltage and frequency issues due to steepness
of prolonged duration of ramps. These services are eﬃciently performed thanks to the
synergy between battery and community ﬂexible consumption.
Moreover, this thesis proves the attractiveness and the feasibility of the proposed exchanges
for energy communities. P2P trades, increased prosuming power self-consumption and the
battery used as a virtual buﬀer lead to a considerable enhancement of community welfare
and local commitment.
Besides, a business model for peak shaving contract has been discussed. In general, the
business is not particularly proﬁtable with the proposed contracted price per rated power
shaved. This is due mainly to the high costs of batteries. However, in future, they may
have a considerable potential with favorable contracted prices.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
The European Commission has settled the goal of reducing the greenhouse gas (GHG)
emission by more than 80% within 2050 compared to the nineties [8]. Similarly, many
countries worldwide are setting energy targets to limit the GHG and increase the share of
renewable energy generation. For instance, Australia has set the target of 25% increment of
renewable power production in respect to 2017 [9] and reducing the GHG to 26-28 per cent
on 2005 levels by 2030 [10]. Therefore, new strategies to achieve this goal are developing
worldwide.
Nowadays, the proportion of renewables is growing fast. Due to the integration of ﬂuctuating
generation, the grid needs a new design that enhance the power system. Hence, with
increased electriﬁcation, the way we use energy is changing too. Energy ﬂexibility and
demand-side response are essential to meet sustainable energy goals. Moreover, with
traditional dispatchable generators going oﬄine for environmental reasons, more ﬂexibility
in new forms will be required in the power system [11].
At the same time, distributed energy resources (DERs), like photovoltaics, are spread
and dislocated in several areas at the distribution level. Individuals can install their own
renewable resources, becoming energy prosumers, drawing power from or feeding it to
the grid. Prosumers have the potential to deliver that new form of ﬂexibility the power
network needs. With the increasing role of prosumers, possibly with some forms of storage
in power system operation, there is a need for the electricity markets to evolve in order to
adapt to the evolution of the generation mix, player types as well as a more decentralized
setup. New capabilities related to Information- and Communication- Technology (ICT)
and internet-of-things (e.g. cloud-based and blockchain technologies) technology are the
backbone for the possibility to have new types of very ﬂexible and decentralized markets
where all actors of the system, down to the appliance level, may then exchange energy
(and possibly data and services) with each other.
In that context, it may be diﬃcult for traditional actors of the power system to appraise
the potential impact of that evolution and the potential new revenue streams. Business
models are to be rethought, also in relation with the regulatory framework, economic and
cultural environment. One may naturally see for instance the Northern European countries
where such peer-to-peer environment may be used to operate based on communities. An
example is the Energy Collective project that has been developed in Svalin. It is about
a community with 20 households in Trekroner, Roskilde (Denmark). Its aim is to create
a new approaches for all members to directly exchange energy in a truly ﬂexible and
consumer-centric manner [12]. Svalin is the ﬁrst demonstration side in Denmark of a
community where it is possible to share electric energy by supporting peer-to-peer (P2P)

allowing for micro-payments, may be attractive also for the emerging countries where the

In this regards, the concept of energy communities is becoming popular. These are local

on peer-to-peer (P2P) energy network have started already to emerge [13]. Decentralized
P2P market networks can eﬃciently help to provide high quality energy for power grid by
optimizing the integration of prosumers into the system [4]. However this architectures are
eﬃcient, scalable and fast in routing, they might be vulnerable from security outlook due
to deterministic and structural architecture [14]. For this reason, blockchain technologies
could help to ensure traceable and fast transactions, preventing malicious attacks [15].
In parallel, the Distribution System Operator (DSO) requires a steady operation of the grid
to avoid congestion and voltage problems. In fact, the intermittent nature of renewables
could cause fast ﬂuctuations and, hence, undermine the stability of the power system [16].
Moreover, in the future a higher number of loads could increase the peak consumption,
resulting in high voltage instability and high power losses. Hence, with high penetration of
distributed resources, congestion becomes one of the most challenging operation issues in
distribution network [16]. Improved congestion management will be required to maximize
the distribution hosting capacity, while keeping a high quality of supply [17]. Today,
stability is normally a responsibility of the T&D operators. However, some researches even
claim that in the future responsibility and cost might be shared with the distributed RES
generators [18]. Therefore, a certain amount of ﬂexible services for the grid, such as peak
shaving, are needed to face the stability problems at the distribution level.
Organized-community assets with integrated storages could support the grid stability,
while improving the use of existing assets as well as increasing resilience. For example,
community energy storage is emerging as a modular concept to be implemented close to
energy demand side in connection with distributed RES generation owned by communities.
Community energy storages bring signiﬁcant opportunities for citizen participation within
communities and helps to increase the awareness of energy consumption and environmental
inputs [19]. The consideration of electric storages can be possible since they are becoming
more and more aﬀordable and their quality is improving. According to the cost projection
of Bloomberg, the price for lithium-ion batteries will considerably drop by 2026 supported
by the evolution of electric vehicles [20].

1. Introduction
1.2
Formulation of the problem
From these above-mentioned scenario, a new trend in moving towards smart grids and
smart houses can be envisaged. Along this work, the considered context is a community
of people that is conceived as a group of geographically co-located residences mainly or
totally prosumers. The community members may have installed solar panels already on
their roofs. This energy assets can be named energy communities.
This residential organization is moreover characterized by the implementation of controllable
loads inside of each house.
Current residential load activities are mainly controlled
manually that makes the optimal operation scheduling challenging [21]. However, recently,
controllable load management is becoming an active research area. With the development
of smart grid technologies, such as smart meters, the customers can actively participate
in demand-side management [22]. In parallel, P2P community-based markets may allow
community members to share and exchanges part of their resources with their neighbours
by actively controlling their electricity.

1. Introduction
1.3
Objective of the master thesis
This thesis aims to analyze disruptive models in the context of future electricity markets
where organized peer-to-peer exchange can coexist with the traditional wholesale/retail
market as we have today. Therefore, innovative business models must be discussed. First,
a new actor will be proposed playing in the power systems: the Smart Energy Service
Provider (SESP). Secondly, the project will evaluate the business from the direct perspective
of the community.
The treated study will analyze the provision of ﬂexibility services to mitigate the congestion
problems in a low voltage distribution grid. A distributed optimization approach to provide
ﬂexibility services, such as peak shaving, to the distribution grid operators will be supported
by the community energy asset and an electric energy storage (EES) system, installed
behind-the-meter, in the vicinity of the community. Hence, the EES and the energy
community ﬂexibility will be considered as main tool to optimize the electricity exchanges
and perform network services.
To sum up, the problem statement of the treated project is
"Can energy communities assets be used to provide ﬂexibility services for power networks?".
In order to assess if the proposed approach can be feasible diﬀerent investigations will be
carried out. The following questions will be addressed along this project:
• What are the advantages and disadvantages in performing ﬂexibility services by
means of energy communities?
• What are the technical, economic and environmental advantages and disadvantages
of P2P community markets?
• What are the technical, economic and environmental advantages and disadvantages
in using an EES?
• What are the beneﬁts in undertaking the proposed business models?
The outcomes can be compared with a set-up that can describe the current power system
setup. P2P interaction levels, CO2 emission avoided, level of self-suﬃciency, cost and proﬁt
of each actor can be some of the targeted results that this thesis will yield.
This proposed work will prove the feasibility and the attractiveness of this kind of innovative
energy communities, by evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of the project according
to diﬀerent contexts.
It is important to specify how this investigation is carried out. This project will be
formulated as a mathematical model to be optimized. It will consider the behaviour of the
energy system, representing the essential aspects of the exchanges and their related costs.
In the model, the goal will be to minimize the total costs faced by each actor involved in
the community asset by limiting the battery degradation and providing ﬂexible services.
Therefore, the model pursuits to ﬁnd the optimality for the energy community assets.
Hence, the solution of the model represents the optimal community economic dispatch
found per each day of optimization over a year of simulation.

Energy communities
This thesis presumes that, in the future, social, technology and economical changes will
aﬀect the traditional structural character of society. Community-led initiatives are growing
fast worldwide [25]. Speciﬁcally, the number of communities that generate their own energy
has notably increased. Energy communities is a term that refers to initiatives where groups
of consumers and prosumers in a neighbourhood exhibit a high degree of ownership and
control of energy projects beneﬁting collectively from the outcomes [26]. In fact, the social
impact of renewable energy systems is potentially diﬀerent from the conventional fossil fuel
power plants. Renewable energy gives the opportunity for a local governance of energy
production [25].
Energy communities can signiﬁcantly boost the energy transition by assisting a penetration
of distributed energy resources at the household level. Moreover, energy community can
open a market for new business in the energy ﬁeld. In [25] it has been investigated the
growing trend of local community energy. They consists in volunteering initiatives within
villages, cities and regions. Source [25] analyzes the energy community according to two
levels of dimensions: the relations with outside networks and the degree of commitment of
local actors to community projects.
As regards the ﬁrst investigation, it is found out that all the initiatives show multiple
interactions of energy community projects with other organizations, such as local
governments and entities. Overall, just in few initiatives it appears a direct relationship
with the business and community.

2. Electricity market structure
2.2
Peer-to-peer local markets
A local market is considered as an electricity market where the electricity exchange among
neighbours is made possible. Trading ﬂoors for local exchange of information and electricity
must be accommodated. This will facilitate the launch of local peer-to-peer markets and
create distributed local markets. Energy communities seem to be the appropriate context
where this kinds of market can thrive.
First, a deﬁnition of these distributed markets is needed. Peer-to-peer (P2P) is a network
whose members, called peers, share and exchange part of their resources and information
in order to promote certain applications, such as projects collaborations [28]. Each person
participating in this exchange is a provider and a receiver of resources. The main principles
for P2P are three. The principle of resource sharing that makes possible the realization of
the application. The principle of decentralization to make the network work not anymore
centrally. The principle of self-organization that is a direct eﬀect of the previous principles.
In fact, since there is not an external central node of coordination, the information are
made available and interacting locally [14].
Diﬀerent architectures of P2P depending on the degree of these mentioned principles can
be distinguished. Three main P2P structure designs can be deﬁned:
1. Full P2P market
The ﬁrst market design sees market participants directly bidding their electricity
resources among themselves. This P2P market structure do not need a central control
between buyers and sellers. Overall, this P2P typology is the most unstructured and
autonomous within the ones discussed.
2. Community-based P2P markets
The community-based P2P market envisages a local market where community
members are the peers, geographically close located. In this case, a community
manager ﬁgure is required to coordinate the tradings in the community. Hence, this
P2P approach relies on distributed negotiation between community members and
manager. This community manager, that can correspond to a ICT platform, should
regulate interaction between the internal market and the rest of the power system.
This second typology results to be more organized in comparison with the ﬁrst
prototype, and, diﬀerently, it can be seen as automatic trading among participants.
3. Hybrid P2P market
The hybrid structure is a combination of the two previous P2P market formulation.
Source [14] states that that this market structure is the most suitable in terms of
scalability for P2P applications in the power system. In this market architecture,
each micro-grid will manage their own tradings. In general, this P2P approach makes
the two ﬁrst formulations coexist together.

2.3
Flexibility service markets
The number of DERs are expected to grow signiﬁcantly in the next decades. However, small
generators are diﬃcult to be envisaged in the current wholesale markets if their generation
is not enough to participate. The European Union deﬁnes as Virtual Power plants (VPP)
the emerging concept of aggregations of DERs able to compete in the wholesale markets
[16]. Nevertheless, the usual size limitations, cited up to 50 MW of capacity in [16], are
considered big barriers to access the wholesale market. In this paper small scale distributed
energy resources are discussed as a diﬀerent concept in respect to the VPPs.
In a scenario with high penetration of intermittent renewable generation, the power
quality could be aﬀected in terms of voltage limits and overloaded lines [24]. Such grid
congestion issues would force the distribution system operator (DSO) to expensively
reinforce and maintain the network [29]. One possible solution is to promote distribution
grid management to handle local resources and their technical limitations actively by
performing services for the grid applications and avoid congestion. With the hypothetical
establishment of local energy markets, it would be clear that the current electricity market
set-up, as centralized as now, would not be able to support this decentralized control
approach. Reference [24] proposes the integration of wholesale markets with geographical
distributed local markets. These local markets can be understood as electricity trading
platforms to exchange electricity and ﬂexibility in the area.
Flexibility has been deﬁned in [29, 30] as the capability of altering the DERs
consumption/generation patterns with limited impact on their primary energy services
in response to an external signal. Flexibility is the captured value of these distributed
markets used within the energy system to contribute to the security and the stability of
grid operations.
Therefore, new markets to deliver the ﬂexibility to the grid operators should be established.
These markets, deﬁned as ﬂexibility service markets in this paper, are supposed to run
in parallel to the existing market and be specialized on distribution markets1. Flexibility
markets are useful to assist, for instance, the DSO to mitigate the congestion that aﬀect
the distribution networks. In general, congestion management can be classiﬁed in:
• Feeder overload management: when high power ﬂow comes over feeder capacity-limit,
possibly caused by imbalances of consumption/generation, mobilization of reserve
capacity or low prices of electricity.
• Feeder voltage: voltage oscillations can be due to reactive power issues, caused by
variations of local generation or demand.
Hence, these alternative markets would conceive new market players, such as aggregators,
carry the interactions with the electricity markets. DSOs should be enabled to request
ﬂexibility services from providers.
1This solution has been proposed in Denmark with the name of FLECH market. In this paper the
hereby proposal has been derived from that, since it is diﬃcult to ﬁnd reliable information on these new
markets worldwide.

2.3.1
Flexibility services
Flexibility services are, therefore, the products delivered to these markets and they should
include detailed speciﬁcation of the service procurement, activation, delivery, validation
and settlement. The main tool used to perform this kind of services are undoubtedly
storage systems. In the treated analysis, only electric energy storages (EES) have been
considered. Electric storage systems coupled with renewable assets are considered the most
eﬀective manner to provide speciﬁc beneﬁts to help the operation of power systems [31].
The major beneﬁts in employing EES may include peak load reduction, renewable energy
intermittent problem mitigation, as an emergency/stand by power supply, and provide
auxiliary services to networks. These mentioned beneﬁts are the ﬂexibility that the power
network requires. In parallel, energy communities ﬂexible consumption can be certainly a
mechanism that can help to provide these services. Flexible consumption refers to certain
loads that can be shift, switch oﬀand consumed at the desired time.
Overall, two diﬀerent typologies of ﬂexibility services can be distinguished, as referred by
[29]:
(A) Fully scheduled services: characterized by a contract between aggregator and DSO,
without real-time DSO intervention during the activation period.
(B) Reserve services: characterized by the needed reserve or availability which necessitates
activation by DSO over a short-time.
In this thesis, only some of the ﬂexibility services have been investigated and proposed
in the analyzed models. It has chosen the ones that could be the most suitable for the
deﬁned context. Moreover, an essential factor to be examined is the economic value of
these ﬂexibility services. Appendix B presents the results of the applications research.
According to these outcomes, the treated ﬂexibility services are described hereafter.
2.3.1.1
Peak shaving
Peak shaving can be deﬁned as the potential of minimizing the electricity consumption
peaks during period of maximum demand from the power utilities (grid imports) [19]2.
This service is used to compensate for the power shortage and avoid over-voltages and
equipment over-loading [32]. During peak periods of power demand, utility companies in
some countries cannot always generate suﬃcient power. Utility companies follow a strict
schedule in which they shut down power to certain parts of the country for certain periods
to avoid the power demand reaching such a high level that it causes a black-out. Even
though these times are scheduled, having no access to power is costly and inconvenient for
companies and consumers [33].
This application is becoming increasingly more relevant for DSOs in charge of distributing
electricity to end users. This ﬂexibility service is used to shave the demand peaks and not
exceeding the capacity of the system. This oﬀers the opportunity to grid utilities to defer
the cost of network expansion, such as upgrading line ratings and transformers capacity,
and increasing the utilization of the grid asset.
2Peak shaving is similar to load leveling, but may be for the purpose of reducing peak demand rather
than for economy of operation.

2.3.1.2
Backup power
In the event of grid failure, energy storage paired with a local generators can provide
backup power at multiple scales, ranging from second-to-second power quality maintenance
for industrial operations to daily backup for residential customers [5]. Therefore, backup
energy, in case of grid failure or disconnection from the grid, is used to maintain the
energy community in "islanding" operation. This means that the distributed generators
and storages are used to power the local circuit until the grid power is restored [34].
As [5] refers, this ﬂexibility service is not dedicated, so far, for applications useful for DSOs.
Hence, it is often assigned zero economic value from the perspective of a ﬂexible service
market as result of extreme conservatism [5]. Nonetheless, customer-sited storage can be
optimally located to provide backup power that, in reality, many customers would assign
great value to this service.
In this thesis, however, backup power is implemented for some speciﬁc cases. Since the
model is optimized by each day, it is diﬃcult to model backup power service in response of
real-time signal. It has been assumed that the energy community would be informed about
the planned outages. For instance, Ausgrid in [35] lists all the current and future planned
outages in each area, deﬁning the number of customers aﬀected and the duration of the
disconnection. In the simulated model, it has been taken advantage of this information in
order to evaluate for how many hours and in which conditions the energy community asset
would be able to perform this ﬂexible service under scheduled delivery.
2.3.1.3
Power ramp rate reduction
The wind and solar power penetration is signiﬁcantly increasing in the last decades, but the
their intermittent generation can potentially cause grid stability problems. These power
ﬂuctuations could lead to voltage variations, frequency oﬀset and, hence, aﬀecting the
grid steadiness and reliability [36]. In fact, renewable power output can vary frequently,
because of many environmental factors, such as shadows due to clouds for solar or wind
speed variability for wind energy. The most obvious choice to apply this service is to use
electric storages. During ramping events, for instance, the EES can eﬀectively mitigate the
extreme steepness or duration of ramps by controlling the charge/discharge activity and
reducing the the variability of power produced in a given moment. Moreover, by modelling
the ﬂexibility consumption of the customers, these ramps can be further decreased.
In this thesis it has been modelled this ﬂexibility services for the renewable generation
injected into the grid. In fact, wind and solar generation self-consumed and stored into the
EES do not aﬀect the status of the grid. Therefore, the ﬂexibility service is considered as
a control strategy to smooth the renewable electricity injection into the power network3.

From the energy community perspective, the SESP as a local market facilitator works as
an agent which aims to create new market relationships and invest in resilient interventions.
Market facilitators act strategically by connecting community members by means of
platform to enable P2P markets.
By obtaining a change in members consumption
behaviours, the consumers start to become more aware about their energy patterns.
Hence, they can drive the penetration of local prosuming generation. This decentralized
approach can lead to many member beneﬁts, from a reliable electricity supply to a more fair
price of electricity. Therefore, at the costumer side, the SESP will provide demand response
services. The expected results are an increase in self consumption of local generation, local
arbitrage because of the P2P exchange and, indirectly, a improvement of power reliability.
On the other hand, the SESP, working as aggregator for grid operators perspective, has
access to the electricity ﬂexibility from the customer side to deliver certain services to
the grid. Aggregators can be deﬁned as service providers that sell their services to other
market actors [37]. In other words, the aggregator pools ﬂexibility from the customer
side and converts it into electricity market services [38]. This services can assist the grid
operators to mitigate the network congestion and, therefore, help to stabilize the power
system. Since in the future the number of dispatchable generators, mainly fossil fuel power
plants, will decrease in number due to the energy transition, a certain level of ﬂexibility
will be requested to balance the power system, characterized by growing demand and
intermittent renewable generation.
This SESP ﬁgure has been described for instance in EMPOWER project4 aiming to
encourage and enable the active participation of citizens who consume and produce energy
in the electrical system [39]. The SESP is an entity that should accommodate three main
things, as referred by [40]:
• trading ﬂoor for energy and related services
• organization and facilitation of neighbouring energy community
• consolidated and integrated ICT platforms for both local markets and ﬂexibility
markets
Therefore, to ensure the operations in these markets, a SESP needs platforms for sharing
information, signals, scheduling actions, energy trading and ﬂexibility. In short, ICT will
manage the control of energy ﬂows and optimize activities that lead to a better energy
experience for the community, its members and the society beyond.
The SESP can be confused with a conventional VPP concept. Firstly, as mentioned before,
there is not the opportunity to propose VPP as actor in this work. Then, whereas the VPP
is considered as intermediate between prosumers and the ancillary markets, the SESP is a
entity that aims to mitigate the risk and the lack of technical awareness of other market
players [40]. Moreover, the SESP role could be conceived as a fusion between a VPP and
a typical ESCo (Energy Service Company) roles, by working as a provider of tools for end
users beyond local electricity markets at the low voltage grid level.

assumed that the DER is owned collectively by the energy community. Also in this

Flexibility service implementation
In this section, the implementation in the mathematical model of each ﬂexibility service
investigated has been described.
3.4.1
Peak shaving
Peak shaving service has been described in Section 2.3.1.1. In order to perform peak
shaving for the DSO, two diﬀerent ways are viable.
The ﬁrst one is to implement an additional soft constraint in the objective function. This
penalizes the high consumption during certain hours to shave the peaks. However, according
to what found in the attempts, this approach is diﬃcult to pursue. This method signiﬁcantly
aﬀects the results of the model. In fact, in the objective function a soft constraint related to
the battery degradation has been implemented. By using the peak shaving soft constraints,
they would overlapped and the battery penalization would be altered. In spite of the
fact that the model, in this way, can be calibrated, would be diﬃcult to ﬁnd an optimal
service procurement. In case of high demand peaks penalization, the battery would not
perform eﬃciently. Otherwise, with high degradation penalization, the intended peak
shaving threshold would not be reached. Moreover, even by not save-guarding the health of
the battery and by increasing enormously the grid demand penalization, an optimal peak
shaving service would not be achieved. For this reason, this approach was disregarded.
The alternative is to exploit an hard constraint. This approach, more easily applicable,
simply sets a certain consumption threshold that the energy community cannot surpass.
This demand restriction can be believed as a portion of demand that a community would
be willing to respect according to the DSO request. This approach can still contemplate a
realistic way of service provision, since it can reﬂect the limited portion of the line capacity
dedicated to the energy community. A similar approach has been treated in the project
"Smart grid, smart city" by Ausgrid in [43]1. Also in the referred trial, the battery operation
for peak shaving has been assumed in regards to a pre-deﬁned feeder load. Therefore, the
battery and the community ﬂexibility consumption would attempt to maintain the grid
demand under this threshold.
Therefore, this model is going to investigate the demand threshold that the energy
community would be able to respect.
Assumptions were made regarding this value.
In fact, the maximum demand was considered hypothetically the same of the previous
year. Similarly to [43]2, diﬀerent battery operation scenarios have been demonstrated:
• Conservative peak shaving - 80% of the pre-deﬁned feeder load
• Balanced peak shaving - 75% of the pre-deﬁned feeder load
• Aggressive peak shaving - 70% of the pre-deﬁned feeder load

4.4.2
P2P prices
The prices of the internal electricity trades have been found by means of a utility cost
curve, as explained in Section 3.2.1. The utility curve gives the price per unit that each
customer is willing to pay to cover their electricity demand. Expressed in other words,
each community member will pay the electricity at the retailer price, the highest, if he/she
is willing to consume just their general consumption, considered immutable. On the other
hands, if he/she would be willing to consume a portion of electricity bounded in the
range of the general consumption plus the available quota of controllable consumption,
the electricity would be cheaper. In case that the customer will consume their maximum
possible consumption, he will pay a price really close to the feed-in-tariﬀ, equal to the
selling price for feeding the PV surplus to the grid. Hence, this setting of the marginal
curve guarantees that the internal exchanges price assumes a value for each time step
between the retailer price and the FiT price. To note that the internal exchanges are
characterized by extremely low transaction costs.
As mentioned along this thesis, it has been used Gurobi Optimizer to carry out the
simulations. It has been used a binary for avoiding charging/discharging battery power at
the same time. This makes the model a MIP problem. However, the P2P price is a dual
price that cannot be found with a binary since Gurobi does not support this approach.
To solve this inconvenience, it has been run a second ﬁle to ﬁnd the dual prices. It has
been decided to export the power charged, discharged and the SoC of each time step
from the MIP code. Then, these values have been used as parameter to remove the
binary variable in the second model in order to ﬁnd the dual prices. Equation (3.11)
corrisponds to the constraint used to ﬁnd these dual prices. They represent the electricity
price that each costumer will pay for P2P exchange. However, it has been found out
that the optimization outcomes result to be slightly diﬀerent from the ones found in the
MIP model. Nevertheless, the optimal objective solution found in each optimized day is
identical. Therefore, it can be established that multiple optimal solutions can be found. It
has been suspected that a mathematical issue may be due to the solver. However, it is
clear that this is a convex optimization, hence, it should lead to the same optimal solution.
As result of this inconvenience, the P2P prices can slightly diﬀer from the ones that could
be found with the same electricity dispatch solutions.

BUSINESS MODEL
This chapter aims to set down the business model structure for the proposed work.
In order to formulate this business, existing models and ongoing projects have been
investigated to understand the limits and the opportunities in undertaking diﬀerent
approaches. This business model identiﬁes the Smart Energy Service Provider as main
actor in the architecture, player deﬁned before in Section 2.4. However, at the end of this
chapter, it has been also brieﬂy discussed the case in which the energy community would
not entrust a third party but would perform a direct business. Overall, hereafter, the
information have been deﬁned mainly from the perspective of the SESP.
The intention of the model description is to describe the beneﬁts that a business will deliver
to customers and how the value is captured by its design [60]. Hence, it is relevant to
deﬁne the key partners, key resources, customer segmentation, customer value proposition,
relationships and revenue streams, that reﬂect the value capture.
This procedure is
commonly used to assess the eﬀectiveness of a business. The recent advances in technology
can realistically help to overcome the problems related to the capture of complex values.
Complex value has been deﬁned by the authors of [60] as "the production of ﬁnancial,
developmental, social and environmental beneﬁts which accrue to diﬀerent parties across
the several systems". Business model with complex value proposition, like the one proposed,
must capture more than one value streams.
In this Chapter ﬁrst of all the business model will be outlined. Secondly, model 1,regarding
the provision of peak shaving, will be investigated from the business perspective. In fact,
model 2 does not reveal any substantial revenue, since, as mentioned before, it cannot be
deliver as a ﬂexibility service to a DSO. As regards the provision of smoothing power for
renewable generation (model 3) it has not been collected any consistent contract evaluation.
However, as found in Appendix B, McKinsey&Company predicts that within 2020 this
service will be considerably proﬁtable. In this thesis, it has been proved that a small energy
community of 10 people can potentially perform eﬃciently this service on the wind power
injection for a turbine of 20 kW. Moreover, it has been found that by increasing the ramp
penalization factor, it would be possible to smooth the power injection of greater wind
turbines. In this cases, for instance, the DSO or even the DER owner should remunerate
the SESP and even the energy communities.

Customer segmentation
Customer segmentation is the practice of specifying the targeted clients according to their

not rely just on one single customer. As explained before in Section 2.4, the model involves
two main clients: energy communities and DSOs. First, within the local market innovation,

catalyze local energy programs. In general, energy communities can be interpreted as
diﬀerent kind of clients. Energy communities, in fact, can be autonomous group of people,
as deﬁned until now: they can be existent communities or communities under development.

projects or even countryside mini grids. For this reason, this targeted segmentation can be
considered as an attractive market segment.
Nevertheless, according to some researches, such as [60], a business based just on the
household market segmentation cannot be competitive. Additionally, in [61] it has been
stated that business models that involves residential customers, like energy communities,
implicate challenges in comparison with the large-scale consumers models. For this reason,
it has been decided to couple the business model into customer-centric, based on the
demand-response services provided to the energy community group, and network-centric,
focused on the provision of grid services to market agents, such as DSO. It is important to
underline that the business conﬁdes on the synergy between the the two customer groups.
Moreover, the energy communities are seen as partners in business and not as sources of
revenues. However, they can be still considered customers, since a service will be provided

Business model outline - community perspective

fact, it is not certain that private groups of people would be allowed to make agreements

the DSO would not have a central node, as the SESP is, to communicate with for the

each community member.

panels and the wind turbine are already installed), the only necessity is the promotion of
the platform and controllable loads.

CONCLUSION
This chapter proposes a conclusion elaborated out of this thesis as well as an opening of
future possible investigations. The objective of this thesis was to investigate the provision
of ﬂexibility services through energy communities. Besides, disruptive business models
were introduced from the perspective of a Smart Energy Service Provider (SESP) and
from the direct community point of view.
The energy communities were characterized by a local prosuming production from PV
panels, but also by the integration of micro wind turbines as local DERs. Moreover, the
possibility of community-based peer-to-peer trades were made possible by taking advantage
of controllable loads in the customers’ dwellings. Beside these resources, a community
electric energy storage was installed to support the asset.
Overall, diﬀerent ﬂexibility services were investigated. First, peak shaving service can be
deﬁned as the capability to limit the electricity demand peaks during certain periods of
the year. Secondly, backup power provision was analyzed. This service is used to provide
adequate power to support the energy community in stand-alone mode in case of grid
failure. The last service studied was the so named power ramp rate reduction. This service
is performed in order to mitigate the extreme steepness or duration of RES power injection
ramps. In fact, intermittent generation can potentially cause grid stability problems.
The approach was, ﬁrstly, to develop a main model to describe the corresponding energy
community setup. Then, according to the ﬂexibility service provided, diverse methods
were implemented.
This thesis proved the attractiveness and the feasibility of the proposed exchanges for
energy communities. Moreover, the results showed an eﬃcient provision of the treated
services by means of energy communities. The power network can have considerable
beneﬁts by exploiting these assets. It can be recommended to undertake this approach.
Special focus was placed on the outcomes for peak shaving service, since also economic
and business perspectives were analyzed.
Peak shaving model
The impacts of deploying energy communities as aggregated systems to perform peak
shaving are relevant. First of all, the energy communities are capable to lower by 30% their
electricity demands, oﬀering the opportunity to defer the cost of network expansion and
limiting the grid congestion. The synergy between community ﬂexibility consumption and
the electric energy storage have been highlighted to be a proﬁcient mechanism. According
to the presented results, the opportunity to shift the controllable loads consumption has
been proved to be the main tool to decrease the grid imports during the service activation.
However, without a battery, this service would not be delivered all the year, as also

P2P markets have signiﬁcant beneﬁts since one fourth of the total PV generation is traded
in the energy community, by increasing the local commitment and maximizing the usage
of the local resources. Overall, adequate P2P volumes and the PV power self-consumption
make possible to have 111 hours of the year without imports from grid, wind turbine
and storage power ﬂows. Additionally, the eﬀect in integrating a wind power turbine is
relevant. In fact, a great portion of wind electricity is directly absorbed by the energy
community in order to take advantage of the favorable PPA tariﬀs. The usage of the
battery as virtual buﬀer permits to use the wind power in beneﬁcial times. In general, the
use of the EES is not excessively relevant for the community optimal dispatch. However,
it helps to shift the RES over-production to be used in more opportune moments. and
avoid grid imports. Moreover, a limited exploitation of battery can potentially safeguard
the battery lifespan. As matter of the fact, the battery used as a virtual buﬀer assist
eﬃciently the energy community. A notable eﬀect is that almost 100% of the PV electricity
is consumed internally of the community, limiting the exports and, so, creating a value for
the community. The only observed disadvantage is that the battery prices are still not
convenient, even by using the most economical in the market. Moreover, the treated model
is a lithium-ion battery, diﬃcult to be stoked and recycled. Therefore, environmental
consequences can be pointed out.
From an economic perspective, by undertaking the proposed exchanges, the energy
community can have signiﬁcant beneﬁts.
In fact, the results yield to the conclusion
that the community considered collectively can overall save more than 15% of their costs
compared to the current setup. Moreover, each customer can reduce their annual bills
by almost 300 $ on average. This is made possible mainly by the increased PV power
self-consumption but also thanks to the advantageous P2P prices and the favorable tariﬀs
from the battery power exchanges. In addition, the energy community can avoid more
than 32% of potential emissions. Therefore, the customers can be encouraged to stipulate
agreements with a SESP (or directly with the DSO) since advantages are observed, even
without being remunerated for their pooled ﬂexibility.
From the business perspective, this thesis demonstrated that the model seems to not be yet
considerably proﬁtable, especially because of the high costs of EESs. In fact, the return of
the battery investment would be only during the end of the second year. Nevertheless, by
envisaging a drop in battery prices, the business can become gradually more remunerative.

7. Conclusion
Backup power model
The results of this study yielded to the conclusion that the proposed energy community
setup can permit to work in islanding operation for more than 6 consecutive hours for
almost 70% of the days. However, in only 37 days it is possible to work as a stand-alone
energy system for more than 12 hours. Overall, this service can be eﬃciently performed by
taking advantage of an optimal dispatch of wind, PV and stored powers. Nevertheless,
without the wind turbine connected to the feeder, the duration of this service would be
certainly lowered. In general, backup power service could be considerably advantageous
from the community perspective, even if it does not show proﬁtability from the business
perspective. In fact, instead of planning the outages,the DSO could notify the planned
power interruptions and reach an agreement for the disconnection duration. The DSO may
further beneﬁt for a longer-lasting power interruption. Moreover, the energy community
can potentially react to a sudden blackout but not for long interval of time. It can be
thought that this service can be performed in parallel to peak shaving, creating synergism
between the two applications. This can be proposed as additional incentive for energy
communities to stipulate agreements with a theoretical SESP for using its battery.
Ramp power rate reduction
The energy community can eﬃciently smooth the power injection fed into the grid. As
presented in the results, the combined eﬀorts of battery usage and ﬂexibility consumption
can compensate the swift ﬂuctuation in the DER generation. In parallel, the battery
degradation can be possibly safeguarded. In general, it is a noteworthy outcome that energy
communities provide an eﬀective mechanism to avoid voltage and frequency issues due to
steepness of prolonged duration of ramps. The presented case illustrates a small scale test
case with a wind turbine od 20 kW. However, a small energy community would be able to
perform this service also for bigger wind farms. In general, energy communities scattered
in diﬀerent areas in future can potentially mitigate the DERs intermittent production
helping the DSO to improve the grid stability.
Final considerations
To conclude, this thesis proved that ﬂexibility services can be eﬃciently performed by using
the resources of energy communities. The results of this study can yield to the following
statements:
• Flexibility markets for delivering network services must be promoted to improve grid
reliability and stability
• Energy communities are considerable resources of pooled ﬂexibility
• The synergy of community ﬂexible consumption and electric energy storage is a
powerful tool to deliver ﬂexibility services
• Energy communities can highly beneﬁt from the proposed exchange framework,
relying on community-base P2P markets
• Business models deﬁned for service provision can become potentially proﬁtable in
the future, by envisaging lower battery prices and favorable contracts with DSOs

7. Conclusion
7.1
Future works
Distributed optimization In this thesis the optimization has been performed centrally.
However, several distributed and decentralized methods can be applied. For instance,
the Alternative Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) makes possible to solve this
problem in a decomposed manner. The main beneﬁt is that this approach permits to solve
the Economic Dispatch where all communications for negotiation are taking place solely
between peers. This oﬀers the opportunity to realistically assess the treated dynamics
by taking care of the privacy of each peer. In this manner, the optimization would be
performed without sharing information. This approach could also permit to simulate
new types of ﬂexible and decentralized markets where all actors may exchange energy
(and possibly data and services) with each other trough internet-of-things technologies
(e.g. cloud-based and blockchain technologies). Since community-based P2P markets
might be vulnerable from security outlook due to structural architecture, internet-of-thing
technologies, such as blockchain, could help to ensure traceability and fast transactions,
preventing malicious attacks.
Scalability It has been proposed small scale models. It would be interesting to assess the
interactions among diﬀerent energy communities. An augmented communication could
potentially yield to results where the the energy communities can even beneﬁt each other.
Diﬀerent battery typologies Overall, it has been found out that the electro-chemical
batteries are eﬀective tools to perform ﬂexibility services. However, other typologies are
available in the markets, for example fuel cells. Moreover, electric vehicles can potentially
be used for the same purpose since their growing trend. Further investigations can be
carried out in order to evaluate this analysis by means of diﬀerent types of storage systems.
Diﬀerent applications Energy communities can eﬃciently boost the energy transition
by assisting the grid stability. A second investigation could be carried out on industrial
and commercial customers instead or residential customers. In fact, potentially, the pooled
ﬂexibility and resources of industrial and commercial assets can provide even a bigger scale
of aggregated systems.

The proposed vision envisages an organized community-based and community-organized
power system. Future smart cities are expected to be complex. Interactions between the
diﬀerent entities are necessary. Neighbourhoods will be more autonomous and able to

DSOs will be able to understand the behaviours to improve the network utilization. Better
utilization will imply better strategic investments on the infrastructure within a smart city.

targeted to supply the local needs.

research has been conducted for diﬀerent reasons.

setup of model 1 in Section 4.5.2 was possible. It has been decided to focus on small
communities with single EESs instead of a bigger one with a bigger-scale battery. This
because, from the perspective of the author of this thesis, this can bring more advantages
to each community. In fact, by using a bigger scale storage, the optimal electricity dispatch
for each community would be altered from the single-community perspective. Moreover,
it would more diﬃcult to create agreements with larger scale communities. On the other
hand, a bigger EES would be cheaper than multiple community-scale storages. This study
has been used to investigate a diverse customer behaviours and having more sample results.
Nevertheless, there is another disadvantage in undertaking this investigation instead of a
bigger scale case. In fact, with more resources pooled in the P2P market, the trades would
be probably higher.

D SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
A sensitivity analysis is a procedure used to quantify how the assumptions made on input
variables in a model can aﬀect the ﬁnal results. In this section a sensitivity analysis has
been performed by varying diﬀerent parameters. In general, this chapter will focus on the
peak shaving model.
D.1
Variation of prosumer portion
First of all, the main models consider the total portion of prosumers in the energy
community. However, this assumption can be unrealistic and aﬀect the results. To note
that this sensitivity analysis is aﬀected by the removal of certain prosumers.
Figure D.1. Sensitivity analysis - change of parameter results by varying portion of prosumers.
In Figure D.1 has been decided to show the variation in percentage of the diﬀerent results
in respect to the presented model in Section 5.1. As can be observed from the ﬁgure, the
P2P volumes and the quantity of electricity stored are obviously very sensitive on the value
of the prosumers. In general, the value of electricity stored is not considerably inﬂuential on
the community exchanges. P2P trades can decrease by 60% when the share of prosumers
are only 2 out of 10. Consequently, the PV electricity available to be self-consumed is
increased by 40%. Therefore, the prosumers can increase their quota of self-consumption.
Overall, the wind power self-consumed and the retailer imports grow because of lack of
shared PV electricity. As result, also the costs for the entire community grow. However,
with the exception of P2P volumes and stored electricity, the other parameters are less
sensitive. In general, the results yield to the conclusion that with less PV power generation
the peak shaving can be anyway optimally performed with a similar level of synergy
between storage operation and ﬂexible consumption.
