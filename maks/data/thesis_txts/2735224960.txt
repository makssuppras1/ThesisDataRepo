Abstract
SARS-CoV-2 is an RNA virus that causes the infectious disease COVID-19. After the outbreak
of SARS-CoV-2 in 2020 in Denmark, the level of viral infection has been monitored by the
Danish government. One way to monitor the spread of the disease is wastewater surveillance.
The advantages of wastewater surveillance include that it is not dependent on the testing of
individuals, and it can give an early indication of a change in the growth rate trend. In Denmark,
wastewater surveillance is monitored by Statens Serum Institut.
This thesis investigates the uncertainty of the wastewater sample estimates from qPCR stan-
dard curve regression models to simulate data with associated uncertainties that account for the
variability that occurs when performing qPCR. Furthermore, the simulated data is transformed
to create a uniÔ¨Āed measure of SARS-CoV-2 RNA in wastewater while considering the contri-
bution of each of the N1 and N2 targets. Feces normalization utilizing PMMoV is performed
to make the data robust to wastewater dilution. Afterwards, the possibility of geographical
aggregation through diÔ¨Äerent types of weighting of the feces normalized data is explored. This
aggregation method is ultimately decided against, as aggregation is implemented in the state
space model used for Kalman Ô¨Āltering.
A state space model is constructed to look deeper into the dynamics of the RNA levels. A
common mean linear trend state space model is created to model aggregation on national and
regional levels, including a common mean and growth rate for each model. A Kalman Ô¨Ālter
is then applied to estimate and predict the data while considering the simulated uncertainties.
The Ô¨Ālter is modiÔ¨Āed to handle missing data in a multidimensional model setting to retain as
much information as possible. Noise parameters are optimized using the Huber loss function
to minimize the prediction error.
The Ô¨Āndings of this study oÔ¨Äer a method for monitoring SARS-CoV-2 RNA in wastewater by
providing a more complex understanding of the viral RNA dynamics in wastewater to aid in
the intervention of disease spread. The versatility of this method allows for the monitoring of
other infectious diseases eligible for wastewater surveillance by applying minor modiÔ¨Ācations.

1| Introduction
Since the outbreak of SARS-CoV-2 in Denmark at the start of 2020, the national infection level
has been closely monitored [1]. As of the 28th of January 2024, the total cumulative reported
COVID-19 cases is 3.4 million, with 9.4 thousand deaths in Denmark and 774 million cases
and 7 million deaths worldwide, according to the World Health Organisation (WHO) [2, 3].
SARS-CoV-2 is a single-stranded RNA virus from the family Coronaviridae and is the cause
of the disease COVID-19 [4]. From the 27th of February 2020, the date of the Ô¨Ārst positive
polymerase chain reaction (PCR) test in Denmark, to the 16th of December 2020, 63.1% of
the Danish population was tested at least once. By the 10th of March 2022, this number had
increased to 78% [1]. During some periods of the pandemic, the per capita testing in Denmark
was among the highest in the world. In December 2021, 27% of the Danish population were
tested weekly. It is estimated that 70% of the active SARS-CoV-2 cases were captured at the
start of 2022. However, as restrictions changed, testing activity was scaled down to <1% of
the population per week by June 2022 [5]. In order to monitor the SARS-CoV-2 level in the
population at low-frequency testing, an alternative to human surveillance is required.
When relying on human surveillance for testing of individuals, the sensitivity of SARS-CoV-2
surveillance is inÔ¨āuenced by the frequency of testing of individuals. This changes with the
epidemiological state of the country, the testing capacity, and the illness assessment ability
and compliance of the population, among others [6]. This emphasizes the need for alternative
testing methods that demand less of the individuals.
Wastewater surveillance was utilized, among other methods, in 1988 when the Global Polio
Eradication Initiative (GPEI) was initiated to eliminate Polio, leading to a ‚Č•99% decrease
in annual polio cases [7].
After the outbreak of SARS-CoV-2, wastewater surveillance was
established in many countries, such as Denmark, as SARS-CoV-2 RNA copies are found in
feces in about 50% of infected cases [8]. Wastewater surveillance is a type of environmental
surveillance. Some advantages to environmental surveillance of infectious diseases deÔ¨Āned by
WHO are [9]
‚ÄĘ Financial aspect: Environmental surveillance is cost-eÔ¨Äective.
‚ÄĘ Indication of incidence: Can give an early indication if the incidence changes, additionally,
it can indicate if a peak has been reached.
‚ÄĘ Guidance: Can help guide if there is a need for clinical testing and potential vaccination
in areas with a high infection level.
Among the advantages of wastewater surveillance is its ability to circumvent the under-reporting
caused by pre- or asymptomatic infections and the incompliance of individuals. A plethora of
factors aÔ¨Äecting the measured viral RNA concentrations exist, including wastewater dilution,
wastewater composition, and variability of viral shedding [10]. The quantiÔ¨Ācation of viral RNA
in wastewater can be limited by the wastewater containing inhibitors of the qPCR process or the
presence of external factors capable of destroying the viral RNA. Despite these limitations, the

hypothesis is that detecting SARS-CoV-2 RNA in wastewater can estimate the SARS-CoV-2
infection level in a population without depending on the testing of individuals.
DiÔ¨Äerent methods have been attempted to model the SARS-CoV-2 RNA copies found in fecal
shedding. Time series models have been used by Bertels et al. (2023) [10] and McManus et al.
(2023) [5], among others, in an attempt to deÔ¨Āne the relationship between wastewater surveil-
lance and the COVID-19 incidence. The study conducted by Bertels et al. (2023) [10] found
that in 28 out of 40 wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs), the optimal model for COVID-
19 incidence cases included wastewater-based surveillance data. This study was conducted in
Belgium during the period with the highest frequency of diagnostic COVID-19 tests with twice-
weekly wastewater sampling [10]. The useful application of wastewater-based surveillance data
is supported by the study conducted by McManus et al. (2023) [5], which demonstrated the
prediction of incidence based on wastewater surveillance to be possible [5]. During the study
period, the testing rate was scaled down. The highest testing rate was used when modeling,
where it was estimated that 70% of cases were captured. After the change in the testing rate, it
was estimated that approximately 15 % of the actual cases were captured by the national PCR
testing, resulting in a diÔ¨Äerence in the model prediction and observed incidence, which could
be used as a measure of underreporting. Using the model of this study, wastewater surveillance
can be useful as an indicator of incidence. During low-frequency testing, one should be mindful
that the incidence cannot be directly compared with the observed incidence due to the variation
in the testing rate [5]. These studies emphasize the relevance of the monitoring of SARS-CoV-2
using wastewater.
An extensive SARS-CoV-2 wastewater surveillance system has already been set up in Denmark
as a response to the pandemic [5].
The infection level in Denmark is currently monitored
through wastewater by Statens Serum Institut (SSI). Figure 1.1 shows a map of Denmark, with
the 29 wastewater WWTP inlets used for data collection, covering approximately 47% of the
Danish population. The Ô¨Āve regions translated from Danish to English are Region Nordjylland;
Region Northern Jutland, Region Midtjylland; Region Central Jutland, Region Syddanmark;
Region Southern Denmark, Region Hovedstaden; Region Capital, and Region Sj√¶lland; Region
Zealand. This thesis refers to the Ô¨Āve regions and the WWTP inlets by their Danish names.

Wastewater-based epidemiology (WBE) can be an important tool to aid in early warning of
changes in infection levels by estimating and predicting the SARS-CoV-2 RNA levels and
their growth rate. The uncertainty, coupled to the quantiÔ¨Ācation of the SARS-CoV-2 RNA
level in wastewater, contributes to the noisiness of the wastewater surveillance data. Utilizing
techniques such as Kalman Ô¨Āltering allows for modeling with noisy data. The Kalman Ô¨Ālter is
a recursive algorithm used on time series data to estimate and predict system dynamics while
accounting for uncertainties, allowing for the construction of smooth models [11].
This thesis proposes a robust method for monitoring SARS-CoV-2 using wastewater.
The
developed method applies the Kalman Ô¨Ālter to a linear trend state space model for smooth
estimation and prediction of the observed SARS-CoV-2 RNA level in wastewater as well as the
underlying system dynamics, leading to a more complex understanding of the dynamics of viral
RNA in wastewater.
The thesis aims to use qPCR (quantitative PCR) data of the Danish wastewater provided by SSI
to accurately reconstruct the SARS-COV-2 RNA levels at diÔ¨Äerent geographical aggregation
levels. To achieve this, uncertainties of the qPCR standard curve regression model are used for
data simulation to incorporate the variation of sample triplicates when Ô¨Āltering. The simulated
data is transformed by weighting the average of the SARS-CoV-2 targets N1 and N2, followed by
feces-normalization with Pepper Mild Mottle Virus (PMMoV) as the normalization factor. Data
weighting and state space model structures are explored as means of geographical aggregation to
represent the SARS-CoV-2 RNA levels on a regional or national level rather than for a speciÔ¨Āc

WWTP. Minimization of the Huber loss function optimizes noise parameters and accounts for
outliers.
The method of monitoring SARS-CoV-2 using wastewater developed in this thesis aims to
become a valuable tool for early warning of increasing infection levels and thereby aid in the
timely intervention of further disease spread.

2| Data Description
SSI provided the data used in this thesis. Table 2.1 shows an overview of the qPCR data
used in this thesis from the Ô¨Āle qpcr_results.RData. It is comprised of data from the
4th of April 2023 to the 9th of January 2024. The data set consists of the cycle threshold
(Cq), the estimated starting quantity (Sq) of RNA copies per well from the 29 wastewater
sample sites, the controls, and the standard samples. SSI utilized qPCR to detect viral RNA
to construct the data set. AmpliÔ¨Ācation is performed on three targets: the SARS-CoV-2 RNA
nucleocapsid gene fragments 1 and 2 (N1 and N2) and the fecal indicator pepper mild mottle
virus (PMMoV). The nucleocapsid protein which N1 and N2 are a part of primarily has the
function of packaging viral genome DNA into a ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complex [12]. RNP
is important in viral self-assembly. It may also have a regulatory role in the viral life cycle
[13]. The 29 sample sites are taken from 28 diÔ¨Äerent WWTPs in Denmark, as samples from
the WWTP Lynetten are taken from two inlets. The samples are taken twice a week, Tuesday
and Thursday. From the 1st of April 2023 and onwards, the laboratory analysis was performed
by TestCenter Danmark, SSI [14]. For the full names of WWTP inlets and their corresponding
sample preÔ¨Āx, see appendix A.1.

qPCR standard curves are created from a batch of assumed RNA concentrations called a
standard lot.
A challenge when collecting qPCR data for an extended period is that the
standard lots become exhausted and, therefore, are exchanged. This can introduce variability,
resulting in a shift in the concentration between diÔ¨Äerent lots. To account for this, a total
adjustment factor was provided for each observation to scale the number of RNA copies per
liter. The total adjustment factor is calculated based on digital PCR and is outside of the scope
of this thesis. Figure 2.2 shows the total adjustment factor for all three targets over time. The
factor is stable in periods. Each change of the total adjustment factor indicates a change in
either method or standard lot, impacting the qPCR results.

4| State Space Model
This chapter dives into the construction of a state space model that explores the dynamics of
the RNA levels in the wastewater. The state space model can be extended to aggregate on a
regional and national level instead of describing just one WWTP inlet at a time. Furthermore,
this chapter describes the implementation of the Kalman Ô¨Ālter to reconstruct and predict the
RNA levels of the wastewater in the presence of missing data, outliers, and the uncertainties
estimated in the previous chapter.
4.1 Model Construction
The data resulting from section 3.3.2 Feces Normalization and the steps before is used for state
space modeling, but to be applicable for a time series model, the structure of the data set is
changed. The time points of the data need to be equidistant, which it currently is not, as
samples are taken on Tuesday and Thursday. Therefore, the data is extended with NAs to
contain all days between the Ô¨Ārst and last observation. This results in many instances where
all observations are missing at time t, rather than one to a few WWTP inlets missing an
observation. How the missing data is handled depends on whether just some or all observations
are missing at time t and is elaborated further in section 4.1.3.3 Missing Data.

5| Discussion
5.1 Data Simulation
The method in which SSI estimates the Sq value per well after performing qPCR has room
for improvement.
When analyzing the qPCR data, the uncertainty of the standard curves
and the variation of sample triplicates are not considered.
In this thesis, to mitigate this,
each Sq variable is estimated anew by sampling 10,000 times from normal and multivariate
normal distributions in equation (3.12) that uses the variances ő£ő≤ and ňÜŌÉ2 generated from the
standard curve regression models and the variance of the Cq sample triplicates ő£Cq.
The
simulations use the standard curve estimated sample values as the means of the distributions,
which essentially recreates each Sq observation of the qPCR reaction. The diÔ¨Äerence in the
Sq estimation methods is reÔ¨āected in the simulation variances, as the same observations are
achieved but with more plausible variances. By including a data simulation step like the one
presented in this thesis, SSI can include more real-world representative uncertainties with their
estimation of Sq without changing their existing laboratory method.
5.2 Feces Normalization of SARS-CoV-2 Genes
The weighted average of the SARS-CoV-2 targets N1 and N2 is performed as they are both
present in one virus particle, thereby obtaining a single appropriate measure of SARS-CoV-2
RNA copies. To account for the changes in the dilution of the wastewater, the weighted average
of the SARS-CoV-2 targets N1 and N2 is feces-normalized using the fecal indicator PMMoV.
The normalization stabilizes the estimated RNA level per liter of wastewater so external factors
such as rainfall do not distort the data.
5.3 Data Weighting
Weighting the data is a reasonable consideration. There are multiple possibilities of weight
type and when the weighting should be performed. This thesis considered weighting after the
feces normalization of the simulated qPCR data. Three weight types were considered: inverse-
variance, population, and the combination of population and inverse-variance. The combination
weight seems the most appropriate as it considers both the population and the variance. When
in disagreement, the two weights complement each other to prevent over-adjusting the obser-
vation value. The weighting is a method of aggregation. No matter which of the weights is
used, the data still Ô¨āuctuates considerably, emphasizing the need for a smoothing Ô¨Ālter. When
the state space model was constructed to be Ô¨Āltered, it became apparent that the aggregation
ability could be implemented in the state space representation itself, negating the need for
data transformation by weighting before Ô¨Āltering. The weights still need to be considered in
the Ô¨Āltering process. Coincidently, the Kalman gain considers the observation noise during the
reconstruction step, thereby already possessing an inverse-variance weight. The selected model
implements the population weight in the Ô¨Ālter by inversely scaling őł2. Statistically, this was

mation provided by the population weighting is a desired attribute as it reÔ¨āects the trust in

modeling.
5.4 State Space Model Considerations
Many possible conformations of state space models could appropriately represent the observa-
tions. As the time spacing of the data was expanded to be daily, a linear trend model with
a time-variant growth rate was deemed Ô¨Ātting as this allows for a daily change in slope. Two
linear trend state space representations were considered: the general model, which does not
include a common mean in the system equation, and the common mean model, which does.
If the purpose of the model solely considers the modeled observation results, Y t, the general
model is simpler to implement, as both models can reach the same observation results. The
system equation of the general model directly utilizes the RNA levels of the WWTP inlets
with the addition of a common slope when updating. This means that the growth rate of the
aggregation level can be monitored, but a common RNA level cannot. Therefore, implementing
the common mean to the general linear trend state space model creating the common mean
linear trend state space model is worth the elevation of complexity if monitoring this also is of
interest. Implementing the common mean clearly indicates the mean RNA level of the entire
aggregation level, making it possible to consider what actions should be taken to control the
disease spread on a greater scale than only for the population linked to a speciÔ¨Āc WWTP inlet.
In the constructing of the common mean model, the transformation matrix Q is crucial to
create the interconnecting dynamics of each WWTP inlet to depict the common mean of the
RNA levels. Refraining from including the Q matrix when constructing the system noise can
somewhat also be remedied in the Kalman Ô¨Ālter with the addition of őł2 to the observation
noise, as seen Ô¨Āgures 4.4 and 4.5. The addition of noise to the observation noise during the
optimization can substantially reduce the error caused by the exclusion of Q by proportionately
increasing the value of őł2. Despite the redeeming abilities of the optimization, the inclusion
of Q in the construction of ő£1 is still selected as it is the most accurate method. It explicitly
expresses the system dynamics through the equations.

average based on population size. Hence the concentration of a site with a small population
contributes less to the common mean than a site with a large population size. The weights
should then also be included in the calculations of the deviation from the common mean of

5.6 Kalman Filtering
The initial system variance V 0 is chosen when initiating the Kalman Ô¨Ālter. The value of V 0
aÔ¨Äects not only the magnitude of the variance of the initial system but also the length of the
transient. This thesis uses V 0 = ňúő£1 ¬∑ 100. The relatively high scaling in V 0 is chosen as ňúő£1 is
time-invariant and optimized for, making a greater initial uncertainty a reasonable assumption.
A large initial system noise usually results in a shorter transient. This can be deducted from the
Kalman gain in equations (4.21a) and (4.22). The large system noise increases the Kalman gain,
resulting in greater changes to the estimated values towards the observations. The transient is
most noticeable in Ô¨Āgure 4.11, where it seems to span approximately half a month. Having a
short transient is generally desirable, as this determines how many observations are necessary

before the model is reliable. Half a month is a relatively long time before the model estimates
are trustworthy.
The Kalman Ô¨Ālter can reconstruct and predict the observations appropriately. Allowing for
a smoother progression through time since the reliability of the observations is considered
when Ô¨Āltering. This consideration is valuable for data extracted from highly variable processes.
The Kalman gain is adjusted based on noise, such that noisy data has less inÔ¨āuence on the
reconstruction and the associated prediction. Some assumptions have been made in the Ô¨Ālter
to accommodate missing data in the matrix structure of the state space model necessary to
depict the aggregation levels. At time t, Y t is a vector with observations from the regionally
appropriate WWTP inlets. If observations from all sites are missing, reconstruction does not
occur, resulting in the prediction being constructed using the prediction of the previous time
step rather than an updated version of this value. If not all sites are missing, data reconstruction
occurs with the assumption that the observed value is the same as the last observed value, with
a high variance of 107 ascribed to this assumption to emphasize the uncertainty arising from
this decision. Thereby prioritizing the inÔ¨āuence of the more reliable observations. This results
in high uncertainty and, thereby, a large prediction interval of the predictions made using this
assumption. Reconstruction does not take place without this assumption, eÔ¨Äectively throwing
away information. If this continues for many iterations, prediction error accumulates, as the
predictions in this period solely rely on the previous prediction with the continued addition of
noise, in which the dynamics of the state space model determine the direction of growth. As
stated in the name, the linear trend model will progress linearly for an extended continuum,
increasing the prediction variance until the reconstruction step adjusts the state vector. As
previously stated, multiple rows of missing data were added to the data set provided by SSI
to make the data equidistant in time, these are not reconstructed in the Ô¨Āltering process.
Implementing the missing data assumption allows for appropriate reconstruction on greater
aggregation levels, so temporary limitations of individual WWTP inlets have a smaller impact
on the estimated regional or national mean or growth rate than otherwise.
The Kalman Ô¨Ālter itself does not diÔ¨Äerentiate outliers from other data points. If the outliers
have a high variance, the Kalman gain, like for any other observation, decreases the reliability
of that point. However, it will also increase the reliability if the variance is small. To consider
outliers, the Huber loss function is implemented to approximate the negative log-likelihood
when optimizing for the noise parameters őł. Implementing the Huber loss function considers
an observation an outlier if its standardized residual deviates beyond the outlier threshold.
The loss of data points classiÔ¨Āed as outliers behave linearly rather than quadratically. This
approximation makes the model more robust to outliers.
5.7 Potential Study Extension
WBE can be used in times where there is low testing frequency to measure the dynamics of
infections over time without relying on testing of individuals. As mentioned previously, WBE
can indicate if the level of infection increases or decreases. This can help guide if there is a need
to intervene in the spread of disease or hospitalizations. Through the timely implementation

The RNA levels in wastewater are used to monitor the presence of SARS-CoV-2 in a population.

6| Conclusion
This thesis aimed to aid SSI by contributing to the utilization of WBE to monitor SARS-
CoV-2 in wastewater. This thesis has developed a robust data-driven method to estimate the
SARS-CoV-2 RNA level at diÔ¨Äerent geographical aggregation levels through data simulation
and transformation, model construction, noise parameter optimization, and Kalman Ô¨Āltering.
The implementation of the extensive wastewater surveillance system in Denmark is a valuable
asset in the utilization of WBE to monitor infectious diseases such as COVID-19. To accommo-
date the variability that arise when performing qPCR the uncertainties of the standard curve
regression models was considered for simulating data and the associated uncertainties.
Data transformation was performed to construct more easily interpretable measures of the
observation. Utilizing the weighted mean of the SARS-CoV-2 targets N1 and N2 allowed for the
construction of a common observation appropriately considering the contribution of both targets
to the level of SARS-CoV-2 in the wastewater. Feces normalization of the transformed data
accounts for both the population size and the dilution of wastewater of the individual WWTP
inlets. This resulted in the level of SARS-CoV-2 RNA in the wastewater to be represented as
the number of SARS-CoV-2 copies per 1 million PMMoV RNA copies. Further transformation
through data weighting was considered but ultimately decided against as the Kalman Ô¨Ālter
already considers the observation variance in the reconstruction step. Population weighting
was implemented in the additive parameter of the observation variance to make added noise
dependent on the population size of the speciÔ¨Āc WWTP inlet. This was not the statistically
favorable model, but the added information from the population size was a desirable trait to
include in the model.
By utilizing a common mean linear trend state space model the state vector Xt was constructed
to explicitly model diÔ¨Äerent aggregation levels and their common means and growth rates,
essentially quantifying unmeasurable entities.
The study found that the combination of noise parameter optimization and Kalman Ô¨Āltering
allowed for estimations of the SARS-CoV-2 level at diÔ¨Äerent aggregation levels that are robust
to outliers with Ô¨Ātting adaptability to changes in the trend. The Ô¨Ālter‚Äôs ability to reconstruct
the state vector and the associated covariance while considering the prediction error and the
observation noise was crucial for managing the uncertainty. When aggregating, the handling
of missing data for the individual WWTP inlets ensures that information is not lost.
Future extensions to this study include the investigation and implementation of seasonal
properties of the SARS-CoV-2 levels, develop a model to use the modeled SARS-CoV-2 RNA in
wastewater to predict hospitalizations due to COVID-19, and utilizing the versatility of the de-
veloped method to monitor other infectious diseases in wastewater through minor modiÔ¨Ācations.
The Ô¨Āndings of this study oÔ¨Äer a method of monitoring SARS-CoV-2 using wastewater by
providing a more complex understanding of the dynamics of the viral RNA in wastewater to
aid in the intervention of disease spread.
