Abstract 
 
Six coatings containing an aliphatic polyaspartic binder and different particles as fillers were formulated, 
produced, characterized, and tested. Barite, quartz, alumina, hematite, and marble particles with diameters 
between 2.1 вҖ“ 6.8 microns (volume mean) were used in addition to a filler-free formulation (hereafter 
referred to as the вҖҳbinderвҖҷ formulation). The selection of filler greatly influences coating durability in the 
Rain Erosion Test (RET). The RET was run at 1280 RPM (~160 m s-1 was the maximum panel speed). 
Hyperbolic epoxy-fiberglass composite panels were used as the substrate with a ~200-micron thick coating 
applied. The barite formulation displayed the longest incubation time of 1300 minutes while the marble 
formulation displayed the shortest incubation time of 200 minutes. The hematite formulation displayed an 
inconsistent failure mode which was concluded to be the result of particle fracture instead of polymer 
rupture. Excellent resistance combined with severe creep over time prevented regular failure in one barite 
panel and two binder panels. The binder formulation outperformed all of the particle-containing 
formulations except for barite; this comparison was inconclusive. Acoustic wave speeds of each coating 
were measured on films measuring ~600 microns using a scanning electron microscope equipped with a 25 
MHz transducer using a conventional pulse-echo technique. Acoustic wave speeds varied between 1720 
(barite) and 1900 m s-1 (quartz, marble). Acoustic impedances varied from 2.87 (hematite) вҖ“ 2.02 (binder) 
MPa s m-1. Longitudinal wave attenuation was calculated by measuring pressure wave magnitude 
reductions on films of varying thicknesses between 130 вҖ“ 430 microns which resulted in attenuation 
coefficients ranging from 9090 (quartz) вҖ“ 11300 (barite) m-1 between the particle-containing formulations 
and 4270 m-1 for the binder formulation. An oscillatory rheometer was used to measure PoissonвҖҷs ratio of 
1-mm thick coating strips; values between 0.21 (binder) вҖ“ 0.37 (quartz, hematite) were found. The pressure 
dependence of the shear modulus was measured for each coating using a 25-mm parallel plate fixture, 
resulting dependences ranging from 1.45 (hematite) вҖ“ 0.31 (binder) Pa Pa-1. The shear modulus pressure 
dependence was found to be the most effective RET lifetime predictor for the particle-containing 
formulations indicating coating shear strain under impact to be a critical variable lending credence to a 
polymer fatigue failure mechanism. A simple uniaxial stress model predicted peak stresses in the coatings 
to be between 176 (hematite) вҖ“ 158 (binder) MPa. Corresponding predicted shear strains likely fall outside 
of the elastic regime, and better methods are needed to quantify the shear stress вҖ“ strain curves at higher 
pressures.

1. Introduction 
 
 
The phenomenon of Global Warming has, in recent decades, given impetus to the transition of the 
fossil fuel energy economy to a renewable вҖҳgreenвҖҷ one in order to mitigate the increase of global temperature 
and its potential negative side effects. This transition is made even more necessary by the fact that the global 
consumption of energy is rising both with global population and global economic development. Along with 
hydroelectric and solar power, wind energy is one of the leading sources of renewable power sources being 
used to replace fossil fuels. This energy comes in the form of large wind turbines installed both on land and 
offshore in many different areas in the world.1 
 
The basic principle of wind turbines is that the convert energy from wind into mechanical energy 
by accelerating blades which are bound to a rotor which is linked to a generator which converts this energy 
to electrical energy. Wind turbine blades are the largest rotating structures on earth, with lengths exceeding 
80 m. It is thus imperative to carefully design their structure to optimize their aerodynamics which enables 
the most efficient extraction of power from a given wind stream. A generic airfoil has a general structure 
as shown in Figure 1.1. If the leading edge is not shaped perfectly, air will not flow well around it and there 
will be an unnecessary amount of turbulence. This is problematic in the case of surface defects or damage 
which may accumulate over time due to environmental stressors.2  
 
 
Wind turbines have gotten steadily bigger over the years as shown in Figure 1.2. The tip speed of 
these blades increases proportionally to their length which means anything that may hit them will hit them 
with a higher relative velocity. This implies more energy will be transferred to the blades in the form of 
stress as they become larger.3 Despite this, wind energy companies typically limit the blade speed 
presumably in an effort to limit blade erosion (shown in Figure 1.3).4 Blade erosion due to repeated raindrop 
impacts is a well-established phenomenon in the wind energy industry. This erosion implies some degree 
of surface roughness on the leading edge which then results in suboptimal airfoil aerodynamics. It is 
estimated that this rain erosion causes somewhere between 1 вҖ“ 25% loss in the annual energy production 
of a wind turbine depending on the severity of the damage. In 2021, it was estimated that across all European

2. Theory 
 
2.1 
Functional Coatings 
 
 
 
While there are many different classes of coatings (often colloquially called paint), for the 
remainder of this paper вҖҳcoatingsвҖҷ will refer to the organic functional type. That is to say, polymer-based 
thin films containing pigment and other components which are typically applied to fairly rigid materials 
(called the вҖҳsubstrateвҖҷ) which they are intended to functionalize. This functionality can be a number of 
things not limited to anti-corrosion, anti-fouling, anti-septic, mechanical protection, and many other 
functionalities. In the context of this study, the primary functionality examined will be mechanical 
protection. Commonly, organic functional coatings will contain some volatile component (i.e. the solvent), 
but the coatings in this study are solventless. In the following subsections, the main components, 
production, and application of coatings will be elaborated as they apply to this study.15 
 
2.1.1 Basic Components 
 
 
The main constituent in protective coatings, as with other organic functional coatings, is the 
вҖҳbinderвҖҷ or polymeric matrix. These binders typically fall within one of two classes: either thermoplastic or 
thermosetting. The former refers to coatings which solidify as the volatile components vaporize while the 
latter refers to coatings which chemically вҖҳcure.вҖҷ Both of these coatings become вҖҳsolidвҖҷ as their polymer 
chains begin to lose degrees of freedom to move. Practically speaking, there is a temperature associated 
with this phase change called the glass transition temperature. Technically speaking, the glass transition 
temperature is the temperature range at which the derivative of the polymerвҖҷs specific volume with respect 
to temperature changes (this derivative is higher in the liquid phase, lower in the solid phase). The diagram 
shown in Figure 2.1 illustrates this concept. 
 
For thermoplastic polymers, this transition to the solid phase occurs as solvent evaporates. For 
thermosetting polymers, the class of polymers with which this study is concerned, the transition to a dry 
solid film occurs largely due to the polymer chains in the solution chemically reacting, polymerizing and 
possibly also forming cross-linking bonds thereby reducing the movement degrees of freedom. This may 
or may not be accompanied by solvent evaporation. The time it takes for this solidification depends entirely 
on the kinetics of polymerization and cross-linking; incorporating molecular groups on monomer units that 
facilitate or inhibit cross-linking can alter the curing time (called the вҖҳpot lifeвҖҷ) by orders of magnitude. 
Thermosetting polymers are often called 2k (вҖҳ2-komponentвҖҷ) because this chemical curing necessitates

Perhaps the second most consequential part of an organic functional coating is the pigment. While 
pigment typically implies the solid particles contained within the coating with some specific functionality 
(such as color or anti-corrosion), in this study the terms вҖҳfillerвҖҷ and вҖҳpigmentвҖҷ will be used interchangeably 
since the primary function of protective coatings is mechanical durability which is imparted by filler 
particles (which, incidentally, also give some color to the coating). However, it is worth noting that вҖҳfillerвҖҷ 
conventionally refers to solid particles which, at most, offer some mechanical and rheological benefit. In 
some cases, the filler is just that: вҖҳinertвҖҷ particles to increase the volume of a coating such that more of it 
can be produced and sold while using the same amount of binder etc. But, as mentioned, the filler can 
impart mechanical durability and can make a large difference in many cases including the present study. 
 
Inorganic pigment and filler are often crystalline materials. While there are many amorphous filler 
materials, the scope of this study only considers crystalline ones. One of the main tenets of filler materials 
is that they are very cheap (and often abundant naturally occurring materials). One common example of a 
crystalline filler is calcium carbonate from ground limestone or more porous вҖҳchalkвҖҷ; CaCO3 deposits are 
found in many places in the world and are often highly accessible. Other common fillers include silicas 
(both amorphous and crystalline in the form of crushed quartz), barite, and aluminum silicates.17

be ignored as it is irrelevant to this study) are the additives. This is a broad range of compounds, but their

common denominator is that they are all added in small amounts relative to the other components of a 
coating. The first additive considered is the wetting agent which is critically related to the pigment and, in 
some cases, the filler. вҖҳWettingвҖҷ is a critical process where the binder contacts particle surfaces in its matrix. 
If there is poor wetting, residual air, moisture, or other contaminants on the solid surface will prevent contact 
and result in вҖҳlooseвҖҷ particles within the coating. This is particularly problematic for protective coatings 
whose main function to remain mechanically durable.18 
 
A surface will be wet well by a liquid if the critical surface tension of the liquid is less than that of 
the surface. Crystalline solids are inherently high-energy surfaces and will be wet by most compounds. 
However, pigment and filler come into contact with many environmental contaminants before being mixed 
into coatings. In some cases, a solidвҖҷs surface energy can be reduced by an order of magnitude through 
contamination. Therefore, wetting agents are often used in coating formulations to ensure good wetting 
(thus good adhesion) between particles and binder. These compounds are either surfactants or, more 
commonly, block copolymers with one moiety which has high affinity to inorganic solid surfaces (thereby 
displacing any contaminants) and another moiety which has a high affinity to organic polymers. These 
wetting agents surface as a sort of chemical вҖҳbridgeвҖҷ between the binder and pigment or filler and ensure 
good adhesion. Furthermore, the wetting agents will often impart some stability (called steric stabilization) 
to the particles in the binder to prevent them from gradually agglomerating which would otherwise occur 
spontaneously due to attractive intermolecular forces such as van der Waal forces among others. This is 
shown in Figure 2.2.18 
 
 
The next additives which are critical to coatings (particularly their application) are rheological 
agents. As mentioned, filler will often modify the rheology of the wet coating. However, it is typically 
necessary to add some small amount of a compound which will facilitate application and ensure the final 
dry film has the right thickness among other qualities. In the case of the coatings used in this study, the 
rheological agents used are shear thinning. Shear thinning agents are often compounds which can form 
temporary 3-D networks when at rest. Once shear is applied, the networks are gradually and temporarily 
broken until the flow stops again. This specific time-dependent shear thinning behavior is called thixotropy. 
This is in contrast to, for example, a filler whose particle geometry is lamellar (planar) and will almost 
immediately align with flow and thin under applied shear. One of the functions of shear thinning agents is

agents. In the case where a coating is highly shear thinning, there may be some cases where the application

elasticity effect. While this may seem counterintuitive, the compounds (which form a monolayer on the

possible. Since the tightest packing will be achieved in a plane, any non-planar geometries on the wet 
coating surface will be eliminated. This is illustrated in Figure 2.3.18

The last major additives to be mentioned are defoamers and antifoamers. While these two are not 
the same, they utilize similar mechansims to be effective. Defoamers, as the name suggests, are compounds 
which are added to destroy foams. Antifoamers are compounds which generally inhibit or prevent the 
formation of a foam. While both may be used in coatings (sometimes at the same time), antifoamers are 
much more common; the elimination of foam is especially desirable during application at which time the 
only foam prevention would be any antifoamer present in the formulation. The general mechanism by which 
these compounds decrease foam stability is not well understood, but it is known that some combination of 
hydrophobic oils and particles will be effective at both foam prevention and destruction. The 
hydrophobicity of the oils destabilizes thin liquid films because any contact between the two will cause the 
liquid in the film (i.e. around a bubble) to be repelled thus causing a localized thinning of the film which 
inevitably leads to rupture. The hydrophobic particle action is similar but takes this mechanism a step 
further; the particles, designed to have a very specific size and hydrophobicity, will partially enter a bubble 
thereby destabilizing it and rupturing it. The particles and oil tend to work together; the combination will 
be more effective than either on their own.18 
 
2.1.2 Production & Application 
 
 
While there is a large amount of precise engineering which goes into large scale production and 
application of coatings formulations, it is perhaps easier to manage these two aspects of coatings technology 
at the research and development laboratory-scale. The single most important part of lab-scale production is 
ensuring good particle dispersion. This relies on creating a binder (+ dispersing agent) вҖ“ particle system 
(called the вҖҳmill baseвҖҷ) which has the right rheology to achieve adequate shear forces on the particle 
agglomerates. Maximum shear will be applied to a mixture if its flow pattern follows the вҖҳdoughnutвҖҷ shown 
in Figure 2.4. This flow pattern can only be achieved if the mill base is viscous enough; if the entire binder 
is mixed with the solids, it will likely be too thin to apply adequate shear. While it is possible to guess the 
binder : solids ratio in the mill base (based on prior experience with such compounds), it is also relatively 
straightforward to run some quick rheology experiments to generate a curve of the viscosity behavior of the 
mill base as a function of solids content. An example of this curve is shown in Figure 2.4. This dispersion 
must continue until a fineness of grind is taken of the mixture which ensures there are no agglomerates 
which exceed the coating specification. The most common вҖҳfineness of grindвҖҷ tool is called a Hegman gauge 
which is a metal rectangle that gives a wet coating thickness gradient when a coating is scraped along it 
lengthwise; the thickest portion which shows a clear high concentration of agglomerates is deemed the 
вҖҳfineness of grind.вҖҷ While there are many other ways of dispersing pigment, a standard dispersing disk was 
used in this study therefore the others will not be elaborated.20

As with production, there are many different techniques to effectively apply coatings, and each 
coatings system typically has one method which is optimal. However, in this study, only a simple brush 
application was used. There is perhaps one main consideration when using a brush at a small scale. When 
paint is applied using a brush, вҖҳbrush-strokesвҖҷ are left behind due to the constant discontinuity of the wet 
coating between the leading and trailing side of the brush. These form into ridges parallel to the direction 
of the brush, and it is critical that they are able to level out before the coating dries.21 Whether these ridges 
level out is dependent on three factors: the viscosity under shear, the thixotropy (specifically the time scale 
of the thickening), and the leveling action imparted by any leveling agents in the formulation. Generally, 
the compatibility of the first two can be roughly quantified by running a sag test, although this is mostly 
helpful when prior experience exists which can give an indication of the maximum sag resistance which 
may be permissible for a given coating and application (e.g. the maximum sag would be less for a horizontal 
application compared to a vertical one). Since the viscosity of a wet coating is roughly inversely 
proportional to the speed at which the brush is passed, a light quick brush stroke will decrease viscosity to 
the effect of assisting leveling in the case of a coating with rapid thixotropy and high sag resistance without 
removing too much of the coating from the substrate.22 
 
2.2

develops into a shockwave as it propagates through the coating. The reason for this is the wave speed 
through the compressed part of the medium (i.e. any вҖҳtrailingвҖҷ stress) will be faster than the leading portion

2.2.1 Acoustic Impedance 
 
 
When a longitudinal wave encounters a material, the material will resist compression and 
deformation to some extent. This resistance can be quantified by the materialвҖҷs impedance. When 
considering acoustic waves (i.e. soundwaves), this is called the materialвҖҷs acoustic impedance. When 
dealing with shockwaves, this impedance is referred to as its shock impedance. The expressions for acoustic 
and shock impedance are shown in equations 2.1 and 2.2. In these equations Z0 is the acoustic impedance 
(sometimes called the reference impedance), CB is the material bulk sound speed, ПҒ0 is the material density 
at rest, Us is the wave speed, and Z is the shock impedance. When shockwaves are weak, they are often 
referred to as acoustic waves since the material properties in these weak limits are reasonably close to the 
properties when subjected to sound waves. It should be noted that, even in the weak limit, treating a 
materialвҖҷs shock properties the same as its acoustic properties will introduce some error. For example, 
longitudinal wave speed is linear with respect to material velocity when subject to a shockwave; the 
intercept is the materialвҖҷs bulk speed of sound. Therefore, assuming the acoustic sound speed through a 
material is the wave speed is an underestimation.

Understanding how shockwaves propagate and interact with material boundaries or interfaces is 
essential to understanding the stresses they impart on a material. When a shockwave encounters an 
interface, some fraction of the incident shockwave will be transmitted to the other material in the interface. 
However, depending on the impedance match between the materials, either a reflected compression wave 
or decompression wave will propagate through the original material. If a shockwave propagating in a low 
impedance material encounters an interface with a high impedance material, the reflected wave will further 
compress the low impedance material. This can be rationalized by considering the вҖҳexchangeвҖҷ at the 
interface. As the low-impedance material is compressing and moving towards the interface, the high-
impedance material will resist this motion and cause a secondary reflected compressive shock in the low 
impedance material. And, because all stresses must be equal and opposite, this high-compression shock 
will propagate forward in the high-impedance material. If the second materialвҖҷs impedance is very low (e.g. 
air), then the first materialsвҖҷ mass in motion will immediately have no resistance at the interface which will 
cause a temporary expansion and subsequent reflected decompression shock which travels back in the 
direction the original shock came. This is illustrated in Figure 2.7.23,24

2.2.2 Elastic вҖ“ Plastic Waves 
 
 
Because this study focuses on the destruction of wind turbine coatings, some consideration must 
be given to the nature of plastic shockwaves and material failure limits. The yield criterion which is 
generally accepted as the most critical in shock physics is shear failure.23,26вҖ“28 Specifically, shear failure 
should occur at a plane which has an angle of 45В° with respect to the shock front. It should be noted that 
вҖҳshear failureвҖҷ at a molecular level could be due to compressive, tensile, or shear forces on the macroscopic 
level. The yield compressive stress which causes plastic deformation, called the Hugoniot Elastic Limit 
(tHEL), is often estimated using simply measured values such as compressive yield. This is because it is 
generally more complicated to find the exact stress in the shock regime which causes failure.23 While there 
exists no comprehensive equation of state or deformation theory for viscoelastic polymers, some attempts 
have been made at estimating their yield criterion. Rosenberg and Partom postulated in 1993 that, due to 
the pressure-dependent nature of the shear strength of polymers, the elastic limit could be estimated 
knowing a few parameters such as this pressure dependence as well as PoissonвҖҷs ratio for a given polymer. 
This model had good success with glassy polymers such as Plexiglas.27 Few attempts have been made at 
compiling an equation of state for polymers; any attempts generally focus on polymer composites with 
military or other extreme protective scenarios such as rocket propulsion.29,30

When the stress imparted by a shockwave exceeds a materialвҖҷs elastic limit, plastic deformation 
will occur in the material and the shockwave will split into plastic and elastic waves. While the plastic wave 
portion of the shockwave will propagate at the bulk sound speed (which is somewhere in between the 
longitudinal and transverse wave speeds) thus more slowly than the elastic wave, it is also common to 
assume the wave front remains continuous when solving problems over short time scales with respect to 
the shock attenuation.23,28 As shockwaves propagate, various mechanisms will act on the wave which will 
gradually reduce its intensity. Some examples of such mechanisms include plastic deformation (i.e. 
cracking), scattering (sometimes called spreading), viscous effects, and the subsequent material relaxation 
вҖҳrelease wave.вҖҷ Even though one may think rubbery polymers will relax on time scales much greater than 
rigid crystalline materials, the corresponding release waves will still come quite rapidly after the shockwave 
stress is unloaded. For example, Amirkhizi et al. found this release wave to begin roughly 2 microseconds 
after unloading in polyureas.31 Practically speaking, the release wave is often assumed to begin 
simultaneously with stress unloading. Shock attenuation (and longitudinal wave attenuation in general) is 
generally accepted to follow an exponential decay with respect to distance travelled.32,33 
 
2.3 
Polymer Fatigue, Crazing, & Failure 
 
 
It is a well-established phenomenon that polymers which are subject to repeated localized stresses 
below their ultimate strength limits will gradually experience вҖҳfatigue.вҖҷ Polymer fatigue is perhaps most 
relevant to tribology; polymer fatigue is often defined in the context of permanent deformation arising from 
repeated friction-induced stresses. However, these accumulating micro-deformations are not necessarily 
unique to friction-induced stresses; any rapid, highly localized stresses will cause local deformation and 
temperature gradients. The slight temperature increase as a result of local stress facilitates more deformation 
than the strain already temporarily being imparted. The degree to which temperature rises in a local stressed 
area is dependent on multiple factors and cannot be estimated without proper experimental or EoS data. 
The deformation from the local stresses then tends to break intermolecular bonds (i.e. hydrogen bonds), 
although it is entirely possible these bonds may reform following load removal. There is, however, a 
significant chance that a small fraction of these intermolecular bonds will not reform after deformation as 
well as subsequent deformations due to the random orientation of polymer chains in amorphous polymers.  
Once one or more intermolecular bonds has broken in a local area, the remaining intermolecular bonds 
which are preventing further deformation are more highly stressed thus more likely to break (and possibly 
remain broken).34

Eventually, as enough intermolecular bonds are broken in an area, a вҖҳmicrodefectвҖҷ will form. It 
should be noted that polymers which experience some degree of вҖҳplastic deformationвҖҷ (i.e. a microdefect in 
this case) always have a chance to revert their structures back to the initial state. This can only occur if they 
are concurrently or subsequently subject to temperatures significantly above their glass transition 
temperature. This is especially observed in polymers which have undergone mild heat treatment following 
some plastic deformation. This is in contrast to crystalline materials which would require melting and 
reforming to re-establish their unbroken crystal structure. The reason for this вҖҳhealingвҖҷ is due to the mobility 
of polymer chains at these relatively elevated temperatures which enables them to find the most optimal 
chain configuration once again. This configuration, in many cases, would be one which enables a maximum 
number of intermolecular bonds (i.e. the lowest energy state). As such, any polymer which may rest during 
the fatigue process has some chance to heal and reverse the fatigue which has accumulated. If, however, 
there is no chance for a polymer to rest at temperatures reasonably above its glass transition temperature, 
then fatigue will continue to accumulate in the form of additional microdefects.35 An accumulation of 
adjacent microdefects will lead to a вҖҳmicrovoid,вҖҷ and an accumulation of adjacent microvoids will lead to 
a microcrack (rupture).34 The implication of polymer fatigue is that polymer chains with fewer degrees of 
freedom will be more fatigue resistant. For example, it has been shown that highly-entangled polymers 
(whose entanglements preserve the matrix structure through deformation) display less plastic deformation 
hysteresis and will suffer fatigue over timescales much larger than un-entangled polymers.36 
 
 
Once a microcrack has formed, it will gradually get larger with each new stress wave. This 
formation of isolated microcracks which lead to failure is also referred to as вҖҳcrazing,вҖҷ and it is a well-
established polymer failure mechanism which arises from repeated, highly concentrated local stresses. 
Crazing will only occur if a polymer is subject to strains above a critical crazing strain which can be 
estimated through empirical models which are fitted to multiaxial mechanical data. The time to failure will 
mostly depend on the crack propagation rate as well as the orientations of the various crack propagations.35 
The propagation of this crack depends on many factors; in a polymer matrix reinforced with solid particles, 
both the polymer material properties as well as the reinforcing solidвҖҷs properties will play large roles in 
determining the crack propagation rate. Generally speaking, inhomogeneities will increase the local stress 
around it. For example, a large agglomerate of solid particles could act as a crack initiator. However, solids 
will often improve the crack resistance by causing complex cracking paths (i.e. cracks spreading out in a 
вҖҳwebвҖҷ or convoluted path from particle to particle) as opposed to a single linear crack which would be more 
likely to cause some larger fracture or material erosion. Typically, smaller particles will impart better crack 
resistance. In rubbery materials, the length of a crack will grow as a function of the вҖҳtear energyвҖҷ of the 
material which is something that must be found experimentally. A typical plot showing the crack growth

2.5.2 LEP Filler 
 
There is a dearth of literature regarding any comparison of the effects of different particle fillers in 
LEP coatings. Publications often do not mention the types of fillers used, but some common reinforcing 
particles include barite, silicas (both glass and quartz), alumina, marble, rubbers, various carbon compounds 
(i.e. carbon black or carbon nanotubes), and others. And, while their purpose is debated, Mishnaevsky Jr. 
asserts particle-reinforced coatings have two key property differences compared to homogenous coatings. 
The first relates to their ability to scatter shockwaves. As mentioned previously, an impedance mismatch at 
a non-planar surface will cause the transmitted portion of the shockwave to follow an analog to SnellвҖҷs law 
of refraction (see equation 2.3). This effect can be applied to small, dispersed scatterers in a matrix. Multiple 
models exist which attempt to quantify this effect. These models incorporate quantities such as scattering 
cross section, concentration, size, and other particle characteristics. Biwa et al. had significant success when 
examining the attenuating capacity of carbon fiber and glass reinforced epoxies (shown in Figure 2.16). It 
is consistently seen that the particlesвҖҷ attenuating effect is linear with respect to the wave frequency, and 
the particle concentration will affect this linear trend.42,48

The second principal effect particle fillers impart on polymeric coatings is a modification of the

others are all argued to be influential. It is perhaps unclear which models would give better results; none 
are proposed for specific material systems, although some indicate they are better in dilute or concentrated 
systems. The same report indicates that particle size can play a significant role in determining these 
relations, especially at the nanometer scale. What is certain is that increasing particle concentrations will 
increase the stiffness of a polymer. Smaller particles are found to impart a higher yield strength, and larger 
particles will result in a negative trend of loading versus yield strength. In many cases, particle loading 
exhibited a peak in yield strength at fairly low (< 10%) weight fractions. The strength imparted to polymers 
is due to a trade-off between stress concentrations at вҖҳinhomogeneitiesвҖҷ versus cracking and crazing 
resistance due to particle вҖҳbarriers.вҖҷ This idea is further solidified by observed trends which show cracking 
resistance decreasing with smaller particles; larger particles imply larger stress concentrations, but they are 
also larger barriers to propagating cracks.49

Rain Erosion Testing 
 
As mentioned, Rain Erosion Testing (RET) is the principal form of accelerated testing done on wind 
turbine LEP coatings. In this form of testing, 3 test specimens are fixed into a whirling arm which rotates 
at high speed under a simulated rain field which mimics the field distribution of raindrops in real weather 
events. The test specimen, in the case of this study, is a hyperbolic panel made of the same epoxy-fiberglass 
composite material used to create the shell of a wind turbine blade. The panel is coated in whichever LEP 
coating is desired to be tested. Most of the panel is exposed to rainfall (the length designating this exposure 
zone is called the вҖҳgauge zoneвҖҷ), and it is relatively easy to calculate the relative impact velocity along the 
blade as the rig dimensions are known. In these calculations, the velocity of the raindrops is generally 
negligible compared to the rotational velocity of the arm. The panel is then subject to the rain field and high 
rotating speeds over a period of time, during which the arm is periodically stopped to enable live image 
updates of the panelsвҖҷ surfaces (e.g. every 20 minutes). Eventually, as erosion begins on the panels, too 
much weight is lost from one panel or another, and the arms become off-balance. At this point, the rig must 
be shut down. It is often at this point all three panels which have been tested have shown some degree of 
erosion, and the time at which this erosion began is determined to be the вҖҳincubation time.вҖҷ

LEP Lifetime Modeling 
 
It is problematic that RET is the sole industry-accepted LEP testing method. RET does simulate 
expected environmental stresses well enough, but these rigs are expensive and resource consuming. It does 
not take extensive calculations to estimate the massive quantity of water which is used from a continuously-
operatied RET rig when there is a flow of, for example, 65 liters per hour. Another issue with RET is that 
there is limited capacity to run samples. If a sample is вҖҳgood,вҖҷ it may run for more than 100 hours depending 
on the speed. When this is the case, the rig is occupied for several working days to obtain 3 data points. Or, 
if samples were prepared poorly and must be re-painted, a scientist will have wasted upwards of one week 
waiting for their coatings to cure and will have to spend another week before getting the desired data. The 
last major negative aspect of RET is a situation where one panel breaks prematurely due to, for example, a 
local defect in the coating. This often requires the rig to be shut down due to inevitable weight differences 
(recall that once erosion initiates, mass loss proceeds relatively rapidly) which may complicate obtaining 
data from such a set. It is not surprising, then, that there have been extensive efforts to predict RET 
performances to potentially reduce the need for gathering data through this method thereby accelerating 
LEP coating development.

through each other. It should also be re-noted that, in the case of compressive shocks, the stress which is 
causing deformation is in fact shear stress, and one would not expect two colliding compressive shocks to 
produce additional shear by virtue of their non-planar collision; it would be additive stress in the direction 
perpendicular to the plane of impact (which would, as previously shown, increase the shear in the material).

the cases where this occurs).23

2.7.3 Other Work 
 
 
While there have been many other attempts at creating models which predict stresses, failures, and 
lifetimes of LEP coatings, none are as comprehensive as the Springer or modified Springer models. These 
are largely computational models. Doagou-Rad et al. used finite element to estimate the stresses in a coating 
which were then incorporated into a complex mechanical theory called multiaxial fatigue theory. This 
model showed reasonable agreement with previously established models, but no comparison was made 
with experimental RET data.8 One potential problematic aspect of this approach was using the von Mises 
criterion to calculate stresses in the material when it is known this approach generally does not apply to 
polymers or elastomers.27 In one study, Castorrini et al. applied finite element to an entire wind turbine

lifetimes, it did not compare any of its results to real LEP erosion data.6,10 In summary, computational 
models may have an pronounced effect in the future, but they have been fairly un-impactful in terms of

The production trial-and-error procedure was as follows. The coating was produced with the first 
guess (along with the associated quality control). This was done by adding the components in the order 
shown in Table 3.2. First, the wetting / dispersing agent was mixed into roughly 2/5 of the binder. Then, all 
of the filler was gradually added using a makeshift hopper with the dispersion running at ~500 RPM. This 
mill base was then dispersed at 2500 RPM for about 30 minutes. Then, the speed was reduced to 500 RPM 
again and the fumed silica was added extremely slowly. This was done because if local concentrations of 
fumed silica built up, the mill base would become too shear-thinning and only the mixture in close contact 
to the dispersing disc would flow while the rest would be completely stagnant. Once the fumed silica was 
added, the dispersing arm would occasionally be stopped and raised so some manual scraping of the rotating 
scraper and can-sides would be done (this was done roughly 2 вҖ“ 3 times per production). The fumed silica 
was typically dispersed for ~30 minutes at 2500 RPM. Following this, the remainder of the additives were 
added and mixed at ~1500 RPM. The dispersing speed was kept reasonably high because the mixture was 
so shear-thinning a slower speed would not have agitated the entire volume even with the rotating scraper 
arm. The dispersion was kept at this speed while the vacuum was applied and maintained for ~1 hour. 
Following this, the sag resistance of the curing agent (E529 вҖ“ primarily 1,6-hexamethylene diisocyanate вҖ“ 
Covestro) mixed with the base was measured. Based on previous experience, the sag resistance was required 
to be between 16 вҖ“ 40 mils. If the sag resistance was out of specification, the formulation was altered (too 
low implied a need for more fumed silica, too high meant a need for everything else). Once new numbers 
were obtained, the production process repeated itself until the sag resistance was within specification.

3.4.1 Speed of Sound Determination 
 
To determine the speed of sound (more technically, the longitudinal acoustic wave speed) through 
the thin films, 1000-micron films were prepared. The resulting films were roughly 600 вҖ“ 700 microns. The 
films had to be this thickness. Films which were any thinner would give too much error in measuring the 
thickness, and films which were any thicker would not have given an echo signal due to the highly 
attenuating nature of these coatings. Initially, attempts were made to measure the thickness of thinner films 
(~100 вҖ“ 300 microns) with a standard thickness gauge. However, these thickness gauges require applying 
some pressure to a film to get its thickness. The films examined were highly compressible which led to a 
lack of confidence in these measurements in addition to a high amount of variation in measurements. 
Therefore, thicker films were prepared and measured with a caliper instead. This was done because, even 
with thicker films (~1 mm), the thickness gauge measured the thickness to be roughly 20 вҖ“ 30% lower than 
the caliper indicating unacceptable compression. The thickness was measured with the caliper by gradually 
adjusting it to the point where no light could be observed between the caliper and the coating. In this way, 
no excess force was applied to the coatings thus a more accurate thickness measurement was obtained. It 
was also for this reason thicker films were used; an error of 50 microns represents a much smaller relative 
error if the film is 600 microns thick as opposed to 100 or 200 microns. 
 
 
 
An example of the films used to measure the speed of sound is shown in Figure 3.8. 10 points were 
selected on the film. These were on the interior of the film because the film edges tended to be slightly 
thicker due to edge effects from the drawdown bar. These points were marked with a cross, then the time 
of flight (ToF) through them was measured by pointing the SAM transducer at them. This was done 
somewhat roughly; a 1-mm circle around the cross was deemed acceptable. The transducer occasionally

To speed up ToF processing, a quick Matlab script was written. First, the data was extracted from 
the raw data file which was a .txt file with one column of data which corresponded to the y-axis data with 
a 0.2 ns step in between each point. When gathering data, the primary echo was always set to a time value 
of 12,000 ns in order to simplify processing. The script first trimmed off the first 55,000 data points (i.e. 
every data point before 11,000 ns). Then, the remaining data points had a 20-point moving average applied 
(a 5 ns moving average). The script then located the minimum value after 11,000 ns (which corresponds to 
the maximum-magnitude peak of the primary echo). Since an estimate of the speed of sound was known 
from previous experiments, the script was then written to identify the maximum value at least 500 ns past 
the primary echo. This maximum value would identify the phase-inverted peak of the maximum-magnitude 
peak of the primary echo. Then, the time between the two peaks was determined. Once each point had its 
ToF determined, a caliper measured the thickness at each cross. Because the edges of the films were slightly 
thicker, attention was paid to properly measuring the pointвҖҷs thickness. Once all 10 ToFвҖҷs and 
corresponding thicknesses were known, an estimate of the filmвҖҷs speed of sound was easily calculated by 
dividing the thickness by the ToF.

3.5 
Oscillatory Rheometer 
 
To properly estimate how the coatings will behave under raindrop impact, some mechanical 
characterization is required. Recalling section 2.2.2, yield is a shear phenomenon, and shear stress cannot 
be estimated with a known pressure without PoissonвҖҷs ratio.23 Furthermore, knowing the compressibility, 
shear strength, and shear modulus as a function of pressure could give insight to the deformation which 
occurs during an impact event. A Discovery HR-2 (TA Instruments) oscillatory rheometer was used for 
characterization. 
 
3.5.1 PoissonвҖҷs Ratio 
 
PoissonвҖҷs ratio was determined by fixing a strip of coating in the tension holder of the oscillatory 
rheometer and gradually stretching the sample which measured approximately 5 mm by 25 mm by 1 mm. 
At each interval, a caliper was used to measure the change in width, and the oscillatory rheometer software 
tracked the change in length. The measurements started at 1 N of tensile force, and measurements were 
taken at each 0.5 N interval up to 4 N of tension. PoissonвҖҷs ratio is calculated from equation 3.16. In this 
expression, Оө is the material strain, the subscript x and y indicate the transverse and longitudinal directions, 
respectively. Strain is calculated with equation 3.17. It is important to note that at 1 N, there was a small 
amount of deformation. As such, the starting length in the rheometer clamp was extrapolated knowing the 
starting width (the deformation slope is also linear with respect to stress). Figure 3.11 shows the set-up 
which was used to measure PoissonвҖҷs ratio. At each force interval, the values were not recorded until they 
stabilized (due to viscoelasticity). While the more conventional way to estimate PoissonвҖҷs ratio of polymers 
is to measure the elastic moduli then apply common relations for isotropic, homogenous materials, the 
method used in this study is the other accepted alternative in the absence of the possibility of the former 
method.56

3.5.2 Elastic Moduli & Tensile Strength 
 
 
If typical polymer strengths under pressure and typical raindrop impact pressures are to be assumed, 
then it is highly likely that the stresses imparted on the coatings fall within the conventional вҖҳelasticвҖҷ regime 
of rubbery polymers.10,27 A highly-entangled particle-reinforced polyaspartic-based coating is inherently 
non-isotropic; it was decided to measure the shear modulus directly instead of attempting to estimate it 
based off of tensile experiments. Furthermore, the shear modulus at ambient conditions does not necessarily 
give any valuable information since the strain under consideration takes place at high pressures. Therefore,

With PoissonвҖҷs ratio, the pressure-dependence of the shear modulus, the acoustic impedance, and 
the attenuation coefficient,48 the peak stress and strain in a coating under impact can be estimated using a 
simplified uniaxial model. First, it is assumed that the pressure loading from the impact will persist until 
the shockwave from the impact is attenuated. This is to say, the decompressive release wave will not begin 
until the shockwave has echoed many times. As with Springer and the modified Springer model, the impact 
pressure is calculated via the well-known Water Hammer Pressure equation. It should be noted that this is

assumption may be a gross one, but it simplifies the analysis tremendously. This hydrostatic pressure

4.1 
Thixotropy 
 
The wet coating thixotropy for each formulation was not the same; the alumina and binder 
formulations displayed much higher shear-thinning properties. Shear-thinning bases were expected due to 
the use of fumed silica, and it was expected that the binder formulation would have a significantly different 
thixotropy since fumed silica was the only particle used. However, it was not expected that the alumina 
formulation would be as shear-thinning as it was. This was slightly problematic as it indicated some 
additional particle interaction which was not present in the other formulations (aside from the binder 
formulation). The number of formulations it took to get within the desired range of 16 вҖ“ 40 mils of sag 
resistance is indicated by the sag resistance as a function of fumed silica content shown in Figure 4.1. The 
hematite and barite formulations had to be re-formulated once (likely due to the fact that the bases were the 
densest and sagged easily), and the alumina and binder formulations had to be re-formulated 3 times each 
resulting in a total of 14 production attempts for the 6 formulations. A lack of knowledge regarding the sag 
resistance was due to the fact that there did not exist a prior formulation on which to model the thixotropy; 
using only fumed silica as the thixotropic agent was a new concept during this study.

SEM 
 
The SEM was used to look at the coatings as a means of quality control and particle geometry 
analysis. A Hegman gauge will give a general idea of dispersion, but a high-resolution image may give a 
better qualitative indication of whether agglomerates below the fineness of grind threshold (set as 40

using the SEM; this is done by assessing whether there are rough boundaries between the polymer and 
particle domains. This can be explained by incomplete wetting causing contact angles which are greater 
than 0 degrees; this will be visible under the microscope (although not necessarily quantifiable. In this 
study, 1-mm thick films (prepared with a 2000-micron drawdown bar) were cut such that their cross-
sections could be viewed in the SEM. An example of the SEM images taken of the barite coating is shown 
in Figure 4.2. This example of a small agglomerate was fairly common, although it can be seen that the 
bulk of the particles are much smaller than the ~3 micron d50 designation. Wetting appears to be good as 
there are some particles which are protruding from the polymer with no apparent contact angles. Particle 
geometry appears to be quasi-spherical. The barite, quartz, hematite, and marble formulations all appeared 
similar.  
 
The alumina formulation appeared to have a much narrower particle size distribution; whereas the 
other coatings have an abundance of tiny particles in addition to larger ones, the alumina coating had 
particles which were mostly the same size. This is shown in Figure 4.3. Furthermore, these particles seem 
to be fairly spherical in many cases. While the vendor does not give full details on its particle processing it 
is possible these particles have undergone some degree of heat treatment in order to control particle size 
and geometry. The vendor does mention that these particles are surface treated which might explain the 
shear-thinning behavior observed. This shear-thinning behavior would not be explained by the observed 
geometry in the SEM images; there is no clear indication of shear-thinning geometry such as a lamellar 
shape. Unfortunately, the precise nature of this surface treatment is not indicated.59 In these formulations, 
a wetting / dispersing agent was used as previously mentioned. This co-polymer (whose chemistry is not 
specified by the vendor) is designed to assist the wetting of inorganic particle surfaces. It is likely that the 
binder вҖ“ particle surface interactions were significantly different in the alumina coating due to the presence 
of a surface treatment therefore comparisons in performance must be done cautiously.

4.3.1 Speed of Sound

in Table 4.2. The uncertainty of the coating density was assumed to be the standard tolerance for density 
variation at Hempel. The speed of sound did not vary proportionally to the inverse square root of density

be analyzed as such. The acoustic wave speed through a solid medium is generally dependent on how well 
the medium is able to transfer mechanical energy through itself.60 As such, one might guess the longitudinal 
wave speed through a reinforced polymer would be a function of many things aside from the wave speed 
through the polymer such as particle size distribution, wave speed through the particles, particle geometry, 
etc. It would then be difficult to predict this value or draw some sort of trend based on the aforementioned 
characteristics. Practically speaking, the quickest way to assess the acoustic wave speed through a particle-
reinforced polymer is to indirectly measure it using ultrasound as was done in this study and others.7

Oscillatory Rheometer 
 
The experimentally determined values for PoissonвҖҷs ratio of the different coatings are shown in 
Table 4.4, and the raw data is shown with its regression in Figure 4.5; PoissonвҖҷs ratio is simply the slope of 
these regression lines. Polymers typically have a value somewhere between 0.3 вҖ“ 0.4 with rubbery 
polyurethanes being closer to 0.5,27,56 so one might initially think these values are slightly low (with the 
exception of the quartz and hematite formulation. However, polyureas have been shown to have lower 
Poisson ratios.65 These results make sense if one assumes that the only part of the coating which is 
experiencing stress and strain is the вҖҳfree polymer,вҖҷ or the polymer which lies outside of particle pores and 
surfaces in the coating. This idea is reinforced by the linear trend observed in Figure 4.6 which shows the 
dependence of PoissonвҖҷs ratio on the fraction of free polymer in the coating. The reason this trend is 
observed can probably be attributed to the different degrees of polymer entanglement in the different 
coatings. When a polymer has a high degree of entanglement, it becomes much stiffer in tension. Generally 
speaking, materials which are stiffer in tension have lower values of PoissonвҖҷs ratio. Based on the monomer 
structure indicated in Figure 3.1 in addition to the fact that the polymer is cured in a solventless (i.e. 
crowded) environment, one would expect a high degree of entanglements in the binder used in this study.36  
 
However, the concentration of entanglements would surely be lower in the formulations which 
have less free polymer; polymer which is trapped within a particleвҖҷs pores would probably not be able to 
become entangled with any free polymer experiencing stress or strain. It is also quite possible there is

It should be noted that in each coating, there is a different fraction of polymer which is able to 
undergo strain as estimated from the oil absorption. This is important because larger fractions of free 
polymer will have a higher concentration of entanglements thus higher fatigue resistance and strain 
hysteresis. These two ideas are likely linked; it would make sense that polymers which are better at returning 
to their pre-stressed morphology would have a lower chance of forming point defects through cyclic 
stresses. In addition to reduced hysteresis, entanglements have been shown to increase fracture toughness. 
It has been postulated that an entangled polymer chain which breaks will transfer this energy to many 
entangled chains instead of a small local area.36,75 However, it is difficult to determine the difference in 
entanglement concentration between two different polymer volumes. This вҖҳdegree of entanglementвҖҷ is 
traditionally quantified by empirical expressions which typically employ dynamic material 
characterization. It can affect many properties including the Tg of a polymer. Something which lends

5. Conclusion 
 
 
Six coatings containing the same binder but different particle systems were formulated, produced, 
characterized, and tested. This was the first time a вҖҳpure binderвҖҷ formulation was created and run in the 
RET using this specific polyaspartic binder system. It was found that the selection of filler particles can 
change the lifetime of a coating in the RET at high speeds by a factor of at least 7. Irregular panel failure 
was observed in the hematite coatings, and this was attributed to low particle strengths which presumably 
resulted in crack initiation due to particle fracture. It was found that the inclusion of filler particles does not 
necessarily improve the coating performance in the RET; the binder coating which only contained a small 
volume of thixotropic particles outperformed all particle-containing formulations except for, perhaps, the 
barite formulation. Panel creep in RET prevented obtaining results on one of the barite panels and two of 
the binder panels. A lack of failure in these panels prior to creep becoming too significant was attributed to 
partial recovery due to resting at temperatures much higher than their respective glass transition 
temperatures. This conclusion was mostly drawn due to the fact that failure would have resulted in highly 
anomalous results after multiple successive runs followed by long resting periods. This inability to achieve 
failure in the binder panels means the performance comparison of the barite and binder formulations is 
inconclusive. 
 
 
Even when the PVC is kept constant, coating properties such as acoustic attenuation, acoustic wave 
speed, PoissonвҖҷs ratio, elastic modulus pressure dependence, and others varied. It is asserted that the 
inclusion of particles alters the bulk polymer chemistry either through differences in entanglement 
concentrations, H-bond concentrations, or chain lengths. Denser, high-impedance particles were found to 
attenuate acoustic waves more effectively than the binder or lighter particles. Barite outperformed the other 
particles in this metric. It is suggested that the amount of binder which is not trapped within particle pores 
is relevant to the coating attenuation and that low oil-absorbing particles of similar size, geometry, and 
impedance will result in a coating with higher attenuation. The stress-reductive impact of wave attenuation 
due to the inclusion of particles in the coating was found to be outweighed by the increased stress in particle-
containing coatings. Furthermore, peak stresses were found to be limited to about 20% greater than the 
initial impact stress at the lower impact frequency limit regardless of the coating system. 
 
 
PoissonвҖҷs ratio was found to vary between 0.21 вҖ“ 0.37 for the coatings, and a trend was shown that 
PoissonвҖҷs ratio will decrease with an increasing fraction of free polymer in the coatings. The reason for this 
is unclear, but it is suggested that this is influenced by either the degree of entanglement, concentration of

hydrogen bonds, differences in average chain lengths or all three. Tensile strengths were observed to be 
roughly the same for each formulation except for alumina coating. The main difference with this coating 
was the surface treatment of its particles, so it is assumed this is the principal reason for this discrepancy. 
The corresponding shear strengths of each coating (calculated from PoissonвҖҷs ratio) at ambient pressure 
showed no trend with RET incubation times. In future studies, it may be useful to know the pressure 
dependence of the shear strength of a coating to determine how close a coating is to failure during each 
raindrop impact. 
 
 
The hematite formulation displayed the highest shear-modulus pressure dependence which was 
twice that of the marble formulation and nearly 5x that of the binder formulation. The reason for this 
pressure dependence is uncertain, and more attention should be paid to this. If one excludes the hematite 
formulation due to a different failure mode, the shear modulus pressure dependence was found to be the 
best predictor of particle-containing RET performance which indicates the coating strain under impact is 
highly relevant. The particle packing calculated from particle size distribution data did not give any 
indication of why the different systems displayed different pressure dependences; the cause of these 
differences should be investigated further. Generally speaking, if one is to believe a general fatigue or 
crazing failure mechanism, quantifying the strain under high impact stresses and at high hydrostatic 
pressures is necessary. This likely necessitates gathering sufficient high-pressure data such that both the 
elastic and plastic regimes of elastomer strain can be estimated at the estimated hydrostatic pressures 
implied from raindrop impact. With better estimates of peak strains, cyclic loading experiments could then 
be precisely carried out such that hysteresis could be measured to give an indication of fatigue resistance. 
Even without knowing the peak strain, quantifying coating hysteresis at high strains could still be indicative 
of a coatingвҖҷs ability to resist rain erosion. 
 
 
As mentioned, the best predictor of RET performance in this study was the pressure dependence of 
the coating shear modulus. However, this does not necessarily provide a means to confidently recommend 
filler particle characteristics which should result in superior coatings. That being said, in this study and 
prior studies, formulations containing barite have regularly outperformed other formulations substantially. 
As such, the only possible recommendation is to use particles which would be as similar to barite as 
possible. That is, particles which impart a high shear modulus pressure dependence low oil absorption 
would be preferable. The latter characteristic is suspected to be advantageous because it is possible the 
amount of free polymer in a coating is advantageous from a fatigue resistance perspective. Of course, 
particles which can withstand several hundred MPa of compression are also necessary. As far as tests which 
might be the most practical moving forward, running repeated nanoindentation tests to quantify a coatingвҖҷs
