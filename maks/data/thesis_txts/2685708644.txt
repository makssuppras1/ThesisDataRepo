Abstract
The development of materials in automobile, aircraft and wind energy industries are
highly motivated by achieving a high strength to weight ratio. Where densities are
low and strength superior. In addition, reducing manufacturing and maintenance cost
are desirable. The majority of the industry involving composites manufactured today
consist of thermoset polymer, such as epoxy. This makes the materials very difﬁcult to
recycle and manufacturing takes a long time.
This study analyses the material properties of a thermoplastic composite/aluminium
hybrid material. Thermoplastic makes the hybrid recyclable and reduces manufactur-
ing time. On the other hand, some challenges may arise, as heat is required to produce
the material.
Through experimental work, a preferred temperature of manufacturing the material
was found to be at 240°C. This was concluded through visual inspection, tensile tests
and four-point bending tests, where the Young’s modulus, maximum stress, strain and
ﬂexural stress was found. Other factors like surface treatments of aluminium and dry-
ing the composite was tested to see if it would increase the material properties and
adhesion between the layers. In general, it was found that dried composite combined
with surface treated plates provided by RadiSurf ApS gained the highest performing
material.

Introduction
The development of composite materials has lead to many new approaches and so-
lutions. Among those are ﬁber metal laminates (FML), which consists is layers of
composite combined with metal sheets. One type of FML is called GLARE - GLass
Aluminium Reinforced Epoxy. This hybrid material was developed for use in aircraft,
where GLARE replaced aluminium to reduce weight, but it also showed superior light-
ning strike performance, impact resistance and resistance to corrosion [1]. Because of
the epoxy, which is a thermoset polymer, this material is very difﬁcult to recycle, and
while being a great material to create complex components, the manufacturing takes a
long time because the polymer has to be cured.
Thermoset composites comprise of about two thirds of the market, but as the interest in
recyclables increases, more research and new solutions are appearing [2]. For example,
wind turbine blades often ends up in landﬁlls when they reach end-of-life and this
has often been criticized. Due to this, recyclable blades are being developed, where
thermoplastic is used instead of thermosets. [3]
Thermoplastics can be re-melted and creates a possibility of a more sustainable ma-
terial. In addition, the manufacturing with thermoplastics can be done much more
rapidly, as it does not need curing time. These factors create a possibility of saving cost
and efﬁciently mass produce components.
1.1
Problem Deﬁnition
Consolidating a hybrid material with thermoplastic requires heat. When combining
different materials, it is important to ﬁnd the proper manufacturing method which
will achieve the greatest performing material. Temperature could affect the resulting
material properties, as heat can lead to residual stresses in the material, but can also
affect the individual material properties on a microscopic level. Too high temperature
1
==PAGE:13==
and degradation of polymer could occur, too low temperature and the layers might not
be completely consolidated. Testing both the strength of the material and the adhesion
between the layers are important as they might draw different conclusions.
1.2
Objective
The objective of the study is to investigate the manufacturing of a thermoplastic com-
posite/aluminium hybrid and to state its material properties. From experimental work
the obtained results will determine which manufacturing process yields the greatest
performing hybrid material.
1.3
Scope and Limitations
The material properties that will be investigated are the Young’s modulus, maximum
stress, maximum strain and ﬂexural stress. Tensile tests will be done for all hybrids, as
well as composite and aluminium separately. The data from composite and aluminium
testing will be used to model the stress-strain curve for the hybrid and compared with
experimental results. Bending tests will be conducted to inspect the adhesion between
the layers of the manufactured hybrids.
One lay-up will be used for all manufactured plates, where the same composite and
aluminium alloy will be used for all plates. Three different surface treatments will
be applied on the aluminium to investigate how it affects the adhesion between the
layers. Temperatures in the range of 230°C to 300°C will be used to investigate how
the material properties changes with the temperature.
1.4
Methodology
For this study, the adapted methodology consists of three main parts; microstructural
analysis, manufacturing and experimental work. The ﬁrst part includes compositional
analysis, microscopy and estimating material properties using rule of mixtures and
classical laminate theory. The second part covers testing of the manufacturing process
where different temperatures are tested. The last part involves tensile test, four-point

bending test and estimations of the hybrid material properties using results from ten-
sile test.

Background

2.1
Hybrid materials / GLARE
This section will brieﬂy mention important events in the development of hybrid mate-
rials, in order to gain an understanding of the motivation behind the project. There are
endless variants of hybrid materials, but only the most relevant for this study will be
mentioned.
Hybrid materials is a general term for describing a material that consists of a com-
bination of several materials. There are many different categories of materials, and
normally an overall name for the hybrid materials presented here are Fiber Metal Lam-
inates (FML). FML are materials built up by layers of metal sheets and ﬁber-reinforced
adhesives (composites). Back in 1945, a company called De Havilland was the ﬁrst
company to bond metal parts together. This lead to the development of Arall, which is
a type of FML. Arall stands for Aramid Reinforced ALuminium Laminates. This ma-
terial was used in aircraft and replaced metal parts. Most of the research was focused
on the wings of military aircraft. In the late 1980s, another material entered the mar-
ket - GLARE (GLass Aluminium Reinforced Epoxy). At this time, another area of the
aircraft became the main focus - the fuselage. In 1990, a fuselage study was made by
Airbus Industrie where the two materials were compared. It turned out that GLARE
would give a 25.9% weight reduction while Arall gave 8%. In addition Arall showed
to be much more expensive. [4]
4
==PAGE:16==
In 2005, Airbus presented the biggest passenger aircraft yet. GLARE was again present
in the fuselage and provided a weight saving of 794 kg [5]. Airbus mentions that
the material were used for its signiﬁcant reduction in weight and advanced fatigue
and damage resistance characteristics [6]. This is compared with using aluminium
alloys. Compared with ﬁber reinforced composites, FML has higher bearing strength
and impact resistance. [7]
The last decades, reducing weight has become increasingly important in the auto-
mobile and aircraft industries. Reducing weight while also increasing the material
strength and safety performance have been - and continues to be the main focus. [8]
As mentioned in Chapter 1, the scope of the project is not GLARE, but a glass ﬁber-
reinforced thermoplastic material. In the next sections, the composite and aluminium
will be further presented, and differences between thermosets and thermoplastic will
be further explained, as that is the difference between this projects material and GLARE.
2.2
Composite
The last century, composite materials has become one of the most important materials
for developing new technology where high strength and low weight is desired. The
ﬁrst synthetic ﬁbres and organic resins were manufactured in the 1930s. This started
the development of composites that progressively has become more common and can
be noticed every day in bikes, boats, cars and wind turbines [9]. In 2009 Boeing com-
mercialised the 787 Dreamliner. This aircraft gained a lot of interest, as 50% of the
primary structure was made by composites [7]. Initially it was believed that compos-
ites could be ﬁre hazardous, as it consists commonly of resin and ﬁbers. But research
showed that as long as the thickness was sufﬁcient, it was more safe than steel in a ma-
jor ﬁre, as metal is a better conductor of heat and will buckle due to high temperatures.
[9]
The combination of low weight and high strength in composites is possible because the
ﬁbres behave stronger than bulk material, such as pure metal. This leads to a stronger,
but also lighter material, as high strength is obtained by ﬁbers that can be combined
with a low density matrix. The reason why the ﬁbers are stronger is because there are
5
==PAGE:17==
far fewer defects in ﬁbers than in the same bulk material. The dimensions of ﬁbers are
so small that there is less chance for a defect to appear in the material. The diameters
of ﬁbers are commonly between 5 - 15 µm. In a composite, ﬁbers are embedded in a
matrix material that holds the ﬁbers together. Most commonly the matrix is a polymer,
but it can also be for example metal or ceramics. As the matrix is isotropic, the angle
and direction of the ﬁbers is the deciding factor for the mechanical properties in the
different directions of the composite [9]
2.2.1
Glass ﬁber
There are several types of glass ﬁbers with varying properties. Table 2.1 presents some
properties of some glass ﬁbers. The most common glass ﬁber used for ﬁber reinforced
composites is E-glass. Usually, S-glass or R-glass are used where even greater me-
chanical strength is needed. C-glass has superior resistance to corrosion in an acidic
environment, and D-glass has great dielectric properties [9]. E-glass is commonly re-
ferred to as general-purpose ﬁbers, and the other types are special-purpose ﬁbers. The
main compound of glass ﬁbers is silica (SiO2). For E-glass, the main compounds are
SiO2 (∼56 wt%), Al2O3 (∼14 wt%), CaO (∼25 wt%), MgO (∼3 wt%) and B2O3 (∼1
wt%). In industrial production; mat products or chopped strands are most commonly
used. Other major categories of glass ﬁber products include direct draw rovings and
assembled rovings. For composites chopped or continuous strand mats are widely
used. [10]
Which glass ﬁber product used, depends on the application. In for example the hull of
small boats, short ﬁbers are distributed in a matrix in a random order. The assumption
is that the properties are the same in all directions, which is necessary in the hull, as
forces can be applied from all directions. In wind turbine blades the distribution of
forces from the wind causes high stresses in some sections and high shear forces in
other sections. The ﬁbers are therefore used in a speciﬁc way to counter these different
forces created of the wind. [9]

2.3
Aluminium
Aluminium is a material that has relatively low density compared to other metals like
steel. Where aluminium has a density of around 2.7g/cm3, steel has around 7.9g/cm3.
In general, aluminium have very high speciﬁc strength, which is the relation between
tensile strength and speciﬁc gravity ratio. This means that even though the strength
is lower than for steel, it can carry more load per weight. Another special property of
aluminium is that it has a very high ductility that is present even at very low temper-
atures. The ductility gives the material high formability, and can therefore be found in
several different applications. By adding alloying elements, or cold working the alu-
minium, the mechanical strength can be greatly improved. Both of these processes can
however also decrease resistance to corrosion. [11]
8
==PAGE:20==
The mentioned properties are some of the characteristics of aluminium in general, but
the most prominent is its versatility. There are hundreds of different alloy compositions
where the range of the mechanical properties have huge variations. [16]
Common alloy elements for aluminium are copper, manganese, magnesium, zinc and
silicon. Cold working is one way of fabricating aluminium sheets, but it can also be
used as a tempering method to improve the strength, as mentioned previously. Dif-
ferent tempers are used for changing the mechanical properties of aluminium. Every
alloy has its respective aluminium association number and must also be assigned with
its temper designation. Some examples of tempers are F, O, H and T, which represent
respectively as-fabricated, annealed, strain-hardened and solution heat-treated. [11]
Annealed tempers are softened alloys, where O without a digit afterwards refers to
a fully annealed alloy. Annealing is heat treatment where the microstructure of alu-
minium is changed. This happens generally over three stages - recovery, recrystalliza-
tion and grain coarsening. Recovery is release of stored energy caused by dislocations
in the material. For an alloy with high aluminium purity, this starts typically at around
0.3 times the melting temperature. Recrystallization is when a new microstructure
forms, where new material grains starts to grow. Grain coarsening is a continuation of
the recrystallization where the grains grows. Annealing causes the material strength to
decrease and the ductility to increase [17]. Solution heat-treated are alloys where heat
treatment is done to control the distribution of the alloy elements. T4 is solution heat-
treated and naturally aged afterwards [18]. Strain-hardened tempers are commonly
work hardened, where H18 is called the full-hard temper, where cold working of the
aluminium is done to approximately decrease the thickness by 75%. H32 is also hard-
ened, but heated afterwards to increase the ductility [19]. Some examples of alloys,
their temper designation and mechanical properties are listed in Table 2.3.

Tensile testing are frequently done to ﬁnd the tensile properties of a material. Those
properties are commonly used in material selection where the results can directly be
compared. Tensile tests gives information about the strength of the material, its ductil-

uously. On the sides of the samples, extensometers are placed that registers the strain

be plotted. Typical stress-strain curves for metal and polymer are shown in Figure 2.4.

sile strength, and maximum strains of the material. This is when the specimen goes
to failure. For ductile materials the cross-section will start decreasing before the spec-
imen fails, and for more brittle materials this happens more suddenly and less or no
deformation can be observed. [23]

Data processing
After all the tests were conducted, the stress-strain curves were made for every sample
using their individual width and thickness measurements. Further, an average curve
for each plate was created by calculating an average stress-value and strain-value from
all the individual specimens.
The strains were found by calculating the average values from the two extensometers.
To plot the stresses, some conditions were set: If any negative values were present
in the beginning, these were removed, as this is from the initial test conditions of the
machine. When the maximum stress or strain was obtained, the rest of the data points
was removed from the data set. This is because when failure occurs in the samples, the
thickness and width of the sample changes, therefore the true stress cannot be found
beyond this point.
Modelling of hybrid
With all the tensile test results from aluminium and composite gathered, the results
could be used to estimate the curve of the hybrid. The modelling of stress-strain curve
for the hybrids were done using the principles from rule of mixtures.
To be able to get one dataset, the test results had to be interpolated for a given x-vector,
and thereafter the rule of mixtures was applied for every datapoint in that x-vector. The
stresses and strains from the aluminium and composite was mixed, and an estimation
of a hybrid consisting of the selected materials could be plotted.

Initial tests of hybrid plates
The initial tests were conducted using specimens with tabs glued on the end of the
specimen. When the specimens are fastened to the machine, the clamps will dig into
the specimen to hold it tight. This can lead to failures in the surface that can cause
fractures on the ends of the specimen. This is not desirable, as good strain readings are
dependent on the fracture residing between the extensometers.
The visual results of the HW03 specimens after testing can be seen in Figure 4.18a -

did not extend much. Also, one can see that the composite becomes more visible after
testing, which either means that the ﬁbers are breaking and are pulled out of the matrix,
or that the composite layer is loosing adhesion to the aluminium. From the other ﬁgure,
the side of the specimen can be seen. The fractures occurred mainly near the ends of
the tabs. The entire sample seems to have been broken off and is only held together by
the tabs.
A similar behaviour was seen on the next sample which was made at 240°C, HW04. In
Figure 4.18c - 4.18d, the initial fractures also happened close to the tabs. On the other
samples all the layers broke off in the same spot, but here one can see that the outer
layers have delaminated after the initial fracture occurred close to the tabs. One could
assume that these two samples would behave similarly as there is not much difference
in the manufacturing, but from these pictures it is clear that the temperature is a very

Conclusion
Throughout this study, 11 different plates has been manufactured and tested. Different
combinations of temperature, surface treatment and drying of composite was tested
to ﬁnd their effect on the material properties. From microscopic analysis no apparent
issues regarding consolidation of the material was found. Using the hot press proved
to be a successful operation. From tensile testing it became clear that the temperature
of the hot press was very important as it made great changes in the strength of the
material. The stress-test made at 300°C (HW05) showed clear signs of degradation in
the polymer. From comparison of 230°C and 240°C it became apparent that an increase
of 10°C greatly improved the material strength.
When using dried composite plates, it was seen that the strength improved in tensile
testing, but in bending test it indicated to lower the adhesion. A clear conclusion could
not be determined, but the phenomenon was observed. From testing aluminium it was
seen that the temperature greatly affected the material properties.
Three different methods for surface treatment was tested. A fourth was made, but
could not be tested because the layers delaminated when the plates were cut. Meaning
that there was little adhesion between the composite and the aluminium. For the other
methods, small variations were found for plates manufactured at 240°C and below.
For plates manufactured at 260°C and above, there was large deviations in the results
caused by interlaminar failures. Delamination was found in all those specimens, but
not for the samples made at lower temperatures. This means that the greatest impact
of adhesion is the state of the polymer.
Using tensile test data from aluminium and composite and combining them with rule
of mixtures showed to give great estimations of the resulting hybrid. This was partic-
ularly seen for estimating the Young’s Modulus, but more limited for the other prop-
erties as it was dependent on the strains of the composite.

Future Work
There are many different topics that would be interesting to further investigate. A list
of suggestions are gathered below:
• Surface treatments: Other surface treatments, like chemical or electrochemical
treatments on the aluminium sheets, but also different grits could be tested to
ﬁnd the optimal solution. HW03 showed great strength in the bending test, and
it could also be interesting to try other surface treatments on that sample to see if
the strength could be further improved.
• SEM: Scanning electron microscope could be used to achieve a better microscop-
ical analysis of both aluminium and composite. One could also thereby look into
the heat-treatment of aluminium and see whether the aluminium recrystallizes
over such short time span.
• Aluminium alloys: Other alloys could be tested that potentially could react dif-
ferent to the heat from the hot press, and keep its strength.
• Mechanical testing: To get more information on the bonding between the layers,
other tests like double cantilever beam test (DCB) or end-node-fracture (ENF)
could be conducted to ﬁnd values for bonding strength and compare it with other
materials.
• Other polymers: The same study could be conducted with other polymers for
comparison.
• Recycling: What would happen if recycling of the hybrid was tried, that would
be an exciting study to read.
