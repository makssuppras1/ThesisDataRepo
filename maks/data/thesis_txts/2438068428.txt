Abstract
The current energetic situation is not stable in the long term: the energy consumption
is increasing and more than 80% of this consumption comes from fossil fuels. Therefore,
the energy sector needs an evolution to solve this problem and wind energy is in a good
situation to start this evolution.
In the last years, wind energy has experienced a lot of improvements such as bigger
structures with the capacity of obtaining higher power from the wind and either new
improvements that make the wind energy to obtain a better proﬁt. For these reasons,
control of wind turbines has been identiﬁed as an area where improved understanding
can yield higher eﬃciency, maximize the power production and minimize fatigue.
In this Thesis, diﬀerent types of generator torque and blade pitch controllers are investi-
gated, comparing them to some of the traditional approaches prevalent over the industry
and academics. The switching mechanism between the partial and full-load operational
conditions is also studied. The control structures are found to vary substantially between
the wind speed regions. Therefore, diﬀerent operational regions are presented showing
their diﬀerent goals in each region.
Furthermore, the performance of diﬀerent control strategies is evaluated based on the
power output and the fatigue damage of the key turbine components. Based on these
evaluations, this project deﬁnes the best possible combination of the controllers studied
and shows how this controllers work for diﬀerent input wind speeds.

Preface
This project has been carried out at Wind Energy Department at the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark with the collaboration of the company Vestas Wind Systems A/S.
This project was proposed by Vestas Wind Systems A/S and have been supervised by
Vestas engineer Søren Dalsgaard, by Postdoc Alan Wai Hou Lio from DTU Wind Energy,
and by Associate Professor Taesong Kim from DTU Wind Energy.
The thesis deals with the study of diﬀerent control methods for a wind turbine. The
goal of the controller is to maximise the produced electrical power while fulﬁlling some
operational conditions, where some variables need to be inside diﬀerent intervals, and
as well minimise the fatigue. With this principal goal diﬀerent regions are deﬁned with
diﬀerent objectives and diﬀerent needs of controlling.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Control
Nowadays wind turbines are controlled by a set of controllers as the ones designed by
the National Renewable Energies Laboratory in [5] or the by DTU in [8]. The tuning
process is done by an engineer and the performance is manually optimized.
In this Thesis diﬀerent controllers are analysed and compared with some of the tra-
ditional approaches prevailed over the industry and academics. The control methods
studied in this report are just for HAWTs with variable generator speed and variable
pitch.
As a control background, the book [15] is used as the reference literature, for the deﬁni-
tion of the simpliﬁed systems and for getting a deeper knowledge about PI(D) controllers.
Moreover, the tests made in this Thesis are performed using a simulation tool developed
by NREL called FAST.
1.2
Objectives
This Thesis studies the control of a wind turbine analysing diﬀerent generator torque
and blade pitch controllers and diﬀerent switching policies. The project is, therefore, one
approximation for deﬁning the best option for a crucial part in wind energy technology
as controlling is. The principal objectives of this project is pursuing are:
• Exposition of the actual wind energy technology and the wind energy conversion.
• Description of how a wind turbine is controlled, identifying the strategy to follow
and the main goals for the diﬀerent operation regions.
• Tuning and deﬁnition of diﬀerent types of generator torque controllers and blade
pitch controllers.
• Implementation of a Simulink model using FAST code and simulations of the
diﬀerent controllers for diﬀerent wind speeds in Simulink.
• Calculations of the fatigue stresses in the diﬀerent simulated cases.
• Analysis of the diﬀerent controllers based on the power output and the fatigue
damage of the key turbine components.
• Deﬁnition of the optimum controller based on the previous analysis and discussion
of the results and the decision made.

2.1
Wind Turbines
Wind turbines are mechanical devices speciﬁcally designed to convert the kinetic energy
of the wind into useful mechanical energy, that will be converted to electrical energy
in a generator afterwards (Information in this chapter from [12],[13],[14]). There have
been diﬀerent designs throughout the times. Actual wind turbines are classiﬁed into
vertical-axis or horizontal-axis turbines, depending on the position of the rotor axis.
• Vertical-axis Wind Turbines (VAWT): The most attractive characteristic of
this type of turbines is that the generator and transmission devices are located at
ground level. As well, as they are able to capture wind from any direction, they
do not require any yaw mechanism to turn the rotor into the wind. Furthermore,
they require lower wind speeds for start up than HAWTs. However, the eﬃciency
of the VAWTs is lower due to a reduced energy capture, due to the blades are
not receiving energy during the entire rotation. In addition, although they have
the generator and transmission at ground level, the maintenance of VAWTs is not
a simple thing since it usually requires to remove the rotor. The most common
vertical-axis wind turbine is the Darrieus rotor, shown in ﬁgure 2.1.
• Horizontal-axis Wind Turbines (HAWT): The rotor of this kind of wind tur-
bines is located at the top of the tower, which also holds the nacelle. The eﬃciency
is higher than for VAWTs, since the blades receive energy from the wind during
the entire rotation. Moreover, this wind turbines need a yaw mechanism to keep
the rotor plane perpendicular to wind direction. Almost all current commercial

Chapter 3
Wind Turbine Control
In this section it is deﬁned how a wind turbine is controlled, the objectives of this
controlling and the diﬀerent regions of control inside the operational range.
A speciﬁc wind speed range is deﬁned for current wind turbines as an operational range.
The limits of this range are the cut-in speed and the cut-out speed. The cut-in speed
(Vmax = 3 m/s) is the wind speed at which the turbine starts to move and to generate
power. Moreover, the cut-out speed (Vmax = 25 m/s) is the wind speed at which the
turbine shuts down in order to prevent extreme loads in its structure due to very high
wind speeds. The control methods deﬁned in this chapter will be deﬁned always inside
this range.
The main objective of the wind turbines is to maximise the generated electrical power.
Therefore, the power should be maximised for each wind speed in the speciﬁc range
already deﬁned. This is subject to some restrictions of the wind turbine:
0 ≤Pe ≤Prated
(3.0.1)
ωmin ≤ω ≤ωrated
(3.0.2)
Therefore, the control methods are deﬁned to maximise the power inside these ranges of
wind speed, rotor speed and electric power.
3.1
Aerodynamic methods for limiting power
There are two methods to limit the power to the nominal electric power at high wind
speeds [11]. Pitch control which turn the blades out of the wind, and stall regulation
13
==PAGE:36==
Chapter 3. Wind Turbine Control
where the blades loose their aerodynamic eﬃciency at high wind speeds. In this project
only the pitch control is considered.
3.1.1
Pitch control
In this method, the blades turn around their longitudinal axes due to a speciﬁc mecha-
nism. At low wind speeds an optimum constant blade pitch is set to maximise the power
extracted, normally around β = 0◦. At higher wind speeds the power is limited to its
rated value by adjusting the pitch angle β. In addition, while pitching out, the thrust
force is reduced as well. It is basically used in variable-speed wind turbines.
Figure 3.1: Sketch of a mechanism to change the pitch angle [14]
3.1.2
Stall regulation
There are two diﬀerent options of this method: Passive stall regulation and active stall
regulation.
For passive stall regulation, the pitch angle β is always constant, so no mechanism to
turn the blades around their axes is needed. The blades are aerodynamically designed
to stall at higher wind speeds, so the power is limited close to the rated value. The
angle of attack increases with the wind speed to higher values. Above certain value of
the angle of attack the stall eﬀect occur, causing the lift to decrease and the drag to
increase, yielding a lower tangential load.
14

The advantage of the stall regulation system is that moving parts of the blades are
avoided and no complex control is needed. On the other hand, as the stall control has
a drop in the electrical power output for high wind speeds, it produces less power than
pitch control.
Active stall regulation uses pitch-controlled blades achieving a a higher power output
than passive stall regulation. The diﬀerence with the pitch controlled wind turbines is
that when the machine reaches its rated power, the blades will pitch in the opposite
direction, increasing their angle to the wind and going into a deeper stall.
This method is used specially in constant speed wind turbines.

3.3
Control Strategy
In this section the control strategy for wind turbines are mentioned, the diﬀerent control
objectives and the operational regions are described, as well as a small introduction to
the baseline controllers.
3.3.1
Regular Control Objectives
Wind turbines have some steady state objectives and some dynamic objectives [4] [8]:
• Steady state control objectives:
Maximise the power until the rated power is reached (Prated).
Keep the operation within the generator limits of rotational speeds (between
ωmin and the rated rotational speed ωrated).
• Dynamic control objectives:
Minimise the mechanical loads in the structure, taking in consideration ex-
treme conditions and fatigue. There are two types of mechanical loads: Static
16
DTU
==PAGE:39==
Control of wind turbines
loads due to the interaction of the turbine with the mean wind speeds, and dy-
namic loads induced by spatial and temporal distribution of the wind speed ﬁeld.
The dynamic loads are much more important from the control point of view.
Minimise the variation of the generator speed and the generator power to
guarantee a better power output. This is achieve by conditioning the generated
power to fulﬁll with interconnection standards.
Do not exceed the maximum limits, designing the wind turbine in a way that
it can not get much higher than the nominal values (e.g. 10% above ωrated).
3.4
Operation regions
To comply these objectives four diﬀerent operation regions are deﬁned depending on the
incoming wind speed [4] [8]. Each operation region has diﬀerent objectives.
Regions 1 to 3 correspond to partial load operation and region 4 corresponds to a full
load operation where the nominal power is achieved.
In partial load control the Cp
is maximised in order to obtain the maximum power and the pitch angle is constant,
corresponding to the maximum Cp. And in full load operation the power is maintained
at the rated and the Cp starts decreasing.
Region 1:
In Region 1 almost no power is extracted and the wind is used to accelerate
the rotor for the start up. In this region, the rotor speed is constant corresponding to
the minimum value ωmin, and the pitch angle is ﬁxed.
The upper limit of this region will be the wind speed at which for the minimum rotational
speed an optimal tip-speed ratio is obtained:

will increase with the square of wind speed. The regulation is performed maintaining the
pitch angle at a constant angle in its optimal value and adjusting the generator torque
to obtain the optimal value of the tip-speed ratio.

Chapter 4
Generator torque controller
The generator torque controller is used during the partial load operation to control the
rotational speed [4]. The pitch angle is kept constant at is optimal value, in this case 0
degrees. This partial load region covers three of the sub-regions deﬁned in the previous
chapter:
• Regions 1 and 3 are constant speed regions. The rotor speed for these regions are
the minimum and the nominal rotational speed, respectively. The rotational speed
can be controlled by a Proportional Integral (PI) controller.
• Region 2 is a variable speed region where the desired rotational speed can be
achieved by either a K-Omega controller or by tip-speed-ratio-tracking methods.
In this chapter the objective is to tune the generator torque controller for both constant
speed region and variable speed region. For the variable speed region diﬀerent controllers
are studied.
At the end of the chapter, this diﬀerent controllers are compared and
evaluated based on the power output and the fatigue stresses.
4.1
Constant speed regions
In these regions the control strategy is to keep the pitch angle at its optimum value and
use the generator torque to regulate the rotational speed to either the minimum rotor
speed (Region 1) or the rated rotor speed (Region 3). The optimum value of the pitch
is the value that maximises the power, in this case β = 0◦.

Chapter 4. Generator torque controller
The aerodynamic torque on the rotor-side of the drivetrain due to aerodynamic forces is
determined by the wind speed, the rotational speed and the pitch angle. In the generator-
side, the torque is applied in the opposite direction by electro-mechanical forces. Thus,
the resulting torque determines the change in the rotation speed.
The PI controller
designed must be able to adjust the generator torque to achieve the desired constant
rotational speed.
To set the value of the generator torque, the PI controller takes the measurement of
the rotational speed. And for that, the proportional and the integral gains need to be
identiﬁed. The PI gains can be obtained by pole placement of the closed-loop poles of
a simpliﬁed model of the drive train and controller. In this simpliﬁed model, actuators,
ﬁlters or higher-order aeroelastic modes are disregarded.
The nonlinear equation of the drivetrain motion is:
J ˙ω(t) = Tr(β, λ) −NgTg(t)

4.2.1
K-Omega controller
K-Omega controller’s strategy is to keep the pitch angle at optimum and use generator
torque to obtain the optimal tip-speed ratio λ in a torque equilibrium.
In region 2 the objective is to capture as much power as possible, since the wind speed
is not high enough to reach the rated power value. The pitch angle and the tip-speed
ratio are then set to its optimal values to maximise the power coeﬃcient Cp. Following
the NREL speciﬁcations a pitch angle of β = 0◦and a tip-speed ratio of λ = 7.55 are
set. The K-Omega control will be a non-linear control that achieves these values.
However, sometimes a higher operational tip-speed-ratio than the optimal is chosen to
minimise the time in stall conditions. This is due to the optimal operation point is very
close to stall conditions, which imply higher loads.
The resultant of the aerodynamic and generator torque must be zero to reach an equi-
librium. The objective is to deﬁne a law which ensures that the equilibrium is reached
while the maximum amount of power is captured.

TSR-tracking using wind speed
Another alternative is to obtain the desired rotational speed from a ﬁltered wind speed.
In this case the rotational speed reference is obtained from the ﬁltered wind speed, using
the optimal tip-speed-ratio λ. The rotor speed can be tracked by the PID controller
also used in regions 1 and 3 to achieve the reference value. The D component of the
controller seems to be irrelevant for this model as the D term ampliﬁes the noise.
The ﬁlter used for the wind speed is a low pass ﬁlter with a cutoﬀfrequency of 0.3 Hz.
This ﬁlter just passes the signals with a frequency lower than the cutoﬀfrequency and
attenuates other signals with higher frequencies. The natural frequency of the tower for
the NREL 5MW wind turbine is 0.33 Hz. Therefore, the cutoﬀfrequency selected for
the low pass ﬁlter should be below this value in order to avoid coupling between the
rotor and the tower dynamics.

Chapter 6
Switching Policy
The switching policy has to be determined in order to fulﬁll the diﬀerent objectives of the
diﬀerent control regions. In this direction two diﬀerent possibilities are studied, the ﬁrst
one changes the controllers used as a function of the wind speed, the second one changes
the controllers depending directly on the rotational speed and the generator torque.
However, both possibilities imply the same switching policy. The diﬀerent controllers
used because of the switching policy are:
• For the ﬁrst region the pitch controller is not actuating and the torque controller
used is the controller described in Chapter 4 for constant speed regions for a
rotational speed of 6.9 rpm. This conﬁguration remains till a wind speed of 6.03
m/s. This speed is the speed at which the optimum tip-speed ratio λ is reached
for a rotational speed of 6.9 rpm, deﬁned in equation 3.4.1.
• This second conﬁguration begins when the 6.03 m/s wind speed is reached, switch-
ing the generator torque controller to one of the three controllers described in
Chapter 4 for variable speed region. Therefore, the K-Omega controller, the K-
Omega-Omegadot controller and the TSR-tracking controller are simulated in this
region. The pitch controller is not used neither in this region. This conﬁguration
will remain till a wind speed of 10.57 m/s is reached, in the case the switching
policy is changing with the wind speed. If the second possibility is studied, this
conﬁguration continues till a rotational speed of 12.1 rpm is overcome. This rota-
tional speed is the rated rotational speed and the 10.57 m/s wind speed corresponds
to the wind speed needed to obtain the optimum tip-speed ratio λ at the rated
rotational speed, deﬁned in equation 3.4.2.

Conclusions and Perspectives
In this chapter the conclusions of the project and the future perspectives the work
developed in the Thesis are shown.
7.1
Conclusions
After presenting the problem studied, running simulations and obtaining various results,
some conclusions should be extracted:
1. Firstly, it is important to realise the main objectives of the control of wind turbines
and this Thesis particularly. These objectives are to maximise the power during the
operational interval of wind speed while minimising the fatigue. These objectives
have to be fulﬁlled inside certain limitations of the wind power which has some
maximum and minimum values for the rotor speed and a rated power.
2. It has been shown in this Thesis and in the literature, the importance of controlling
for obtain the better and more stable power output and for minimising the eﬀorts
suﬀer by the wind turbine. Controlling appears as an indispensable part in wind
turbine technology for both power procurement and maintenance of the turbine.
3. In this Thesis a real wind turbine is simulated, the NREL 5MW turbine with all
its magnitudes and properties. As well a simulation tool used in the industry is
used for running the simulation called FAST.
4. During the operational interval it is possible to deﬁne 4 diﬀerent regions, all of
them with diﬀerent properties and objectives, therefore, they should be tuned

diﬀerently. It is crucial to deﬁne a proper switching policy for changing from one
controller to another depending on the region the turbine is working in.
5. In region 2, the chosen controller has been the TSR-tracking controller due to
its better response to variable speed simulated with a Kaimal distribution. With
constant incoming wind speeds K-Omega and the K-Omega-Omegadot are working
good but their response is not fast enough for turbulent wind speeds.
6. In region 4, the PI gains obtained from the DTU strategy are the ones ﬁnally se-

In this project, the ﬁnal controller selected based on the

swithcing as a function of rotor speed and generator torque, using a TSR-tracking

9. Moreover, it is important to understand how to interpret the results. The results

Furthermore, the conditions deﬁned by NREL in the FAST code are considered as

diﬀerent to the ones deﬁned in FAST code.

The inverted pendulum wind turbine is a new type of wind turbine that is created to
reduce the material costs of the tower by reducing the eﬀorts caused by the diﬀerent
loads in the tower. The tower of this kind of wind turbine has a hinge-lie structure which
allows the tower to lean forward and balance the eﬀorts caused by the thrust force with
the gravitational loads. This gravitational loads create a moment when the tower leans
forward in the opposite direction to the moment created by the thrust force, and by
doing this it reduces the eﬀorts inside the tower. As the eﬀorts are reduced, it is possible
to use a lighter and cheaper structure for the tower.
For the controlling of this kind of wind turbine a linear model has to be deﬁned and a
MIMO (Multiple Input-Multiple Output) controller should be used. The objectives of
the controllers are not only to maximise the power and minimise the fatigue, but as well
stabilise the tower for a system that is inherently unstable.

Rainﬂow counting
The Rainﬂow counting is a method to obtain the amplitude of a signal and the number
of cycles of it from obtaining the extreme values of the signal.
In [10] a new equivalent deﬁnition of the rainﬂow cycle counting method is presented,
expressing the rainﬂow cycle amplitudes in an explicit analytical formulae. The method
attaches the amplitude of a corresponding cycle to each maximum of the strain function,
evaluating them independently from each other.
This deﬁnition is considered more
convenient for the statistical analysis of rainﬂow cycle amplitudes.
In [10], there are imposed several rules on rain dripping down the roofs so half cycles are
deﬁned: ”Rainﬂow begins successively at the inside of each strain peak. The rainﬂow
initiating at each peak is allowed to drip down and continue except that, if it initiates
at a minimum, it must stop when it comes opposite a minimum more negative than the
