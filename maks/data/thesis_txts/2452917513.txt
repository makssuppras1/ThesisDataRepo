ABSTRACT 
 
Therapeutic glycoproteins including lysosomal replacement enzymes are predominantly expressed 
in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell lines due to their ability to produce human-like glycans on 
recombinantly expressed proteins. With the great advances in nuclease-based precise genome 
editing tools it is now becoming possible to engineer the glycosylation capacities of CHO cells and 
generate glycoproteins with custom-designed and rather homogenous glycans. Lysosomal storage 
diseases (LSDs) are a related group of more than 50-60 inherited metabolic disorders usually 
resulting from mutations in genes encoding lysosomal acid hydrolase enzymes. Currently, the only 
effective treatment available for select LSDs is enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) with the 
missing recombinantly produced lysosomal enzyme. Lysosomal enzymes are N-glycoproteins and 
the N-glycans contain the characteristic mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) feature directing targeting to 
the lysosome, which is utilized in most current ERTs for uptake into cells as well. The efficacies of 
current replacement enzymes in clinic use are in general compromised by poor biodistribution and 
short circulation times, which are largely due to rapid liver clearance through receptor-glycan 
interactions by three main receptors, including the M6P receptors (MPRs), mannose receptor (MR), 
and in some cases the asialoglycoprotein receptor (ASGPR). Recent studies from my host group has 
challenged the paradigm of using ERTs with M6P for cellular uptake. Thus, analysis of 
biodistribution and circulatory half-life of distinct glycoforms of the lysosomal -galactosidase 
(GLA) enzyme involved in Fabry disease, revealed that GLA without M6P and with 2-3linked, 
and not 2-6linked, sialic acid capping improved circulation time and targeting to hard-to-reach 
organs such as heart in Fabry disease mouse model. This provides proof-of concept for 
glycoengineering and custom-design of N-glycans on lysosomal enzymes for improving their 
therapeutic efficacy. My Master project aimed to explore the degree to which the 2-3linked sialic 
acid glycodesign was universally applicable to lysosomal enzymes both from an engineering 
perspective as well as from a structural outcome perspective. I implemented the 2-3linked sialic 
acid gene engineering design in CHO cells and expressed three additional lysosomal enzymes, 
including glycosylasparaginase (AGA), -glucuronidase (GUSB) and cathepsin D (CTSD). The 
recombinantly produced proteins were purified to near homogeneity and analyzed the glycosylation 
outcome by site-specific N-glycan analysis using mass spectrometry. The results demonstrate that 
the 2-3linked sialic acid glycodesign is applicable to N-glycans processed with M6P and complex 
N-glycans, but not to glycosites naturally processes with high-mannose N-glycans as found for one 
==PAGE:5==
glycosite in GUSB. This is presumably because such N-glycans are “hidden” in the protein 
structure and escape the normal glycan processing enzymes, and future studies are needed to 
explore to what extend such high-mannose glycans are recognized by lectin receptors and affect 
circulation and biodistribution. In summary, my thesis work demonstrated that the N-glycan 
engineering design for improved ERTs can be applied to some but not all lysosomal enzymes. 
 
Techniques I used in the thesis work include: 1) stable protein expression in CHO cells; 2) precise 
gene editing (KO and KI); 3) protein purification and characterization by SDS-PAGE; 4) site 
specific glycopeptide analysis using mass spectrometry.

CHAPTER 1 - INTRODUCTION 
 
Lysosome and lysosomal storage diseases 
 
A lysosome acts as a material disposal system and recycling center of the cell by breaking and 
recycling many kinds of biomolecules including carbohydrates, sphingolipids, peptides, nucleic 
acids, glycosaminoglycans through different mechanisms. The lysosomes contain more than 60 
different acidic hydrolytic enzymes which are responsible for catabolic function. These acidic 
hydrolytic enzymes can be classified into different protein families such as glycosidases, sulfatases, 
phosphatases, nucleases, peptidases and lipases, according to the direct catabolic substrates. Recent 
studies have also shown that the lysosomes have other functions relating to nutrient sensing, vesicle 
trafficking, signaling and other basic cellular functions. It is clear and evident that the lysosome 
plays an important role in cell metabolism and growth1. 
 
The LSDs are a collective group of more than 50-60 inherited metabolic disorders usually resulting 
from mutations in the genes encoding lysosomal acid hydrolytic enzymes. These inactive enzymes 
are unable to degrade one or more of the complex substrates resulting in progressive accumulation 
of undegraded substrates and leading to cell death. Depending on deficient enzymes and the 
accumulated substrates, LSDs can be classified to a group of diseases such as -galactosidase 
(GLA) in Fabry disease, glucocerebroside in Gaucher disease and glycogen in Pompe disease. The 
accurate epidemiological data of incidence and prevalence of LSDs are not available because of 
possible under-diagnosis or mis-diagnosis. However, the cumulative prevalence is thought to be 1 
in 2,000-5,000 live births which is higher than many other rare diseases. LSDs exhibit a broad-
spectrum of variable clinical manifestations according to deficient enzymes and particular cell 
types. In general, the diseases are multi-system involvement which progress and evolve over time 
from the newborn period to late adulthood and most of them have severe central nervous system 
(CNS) disorders such as vision loss, movement disorders, seizures, ataxia, dementia and 
developmental delay, which can lead to death2.

Treatments for lysosomal storage diseases (LSDs) 
 
As mentioned above, LSDs are caused by functional deficiencies of lysosomal acidic hydrolytic 
enzymes which can generate a pathogenetic cascade resulting in accumulation of undigested 
substrates in many tissues and organs. The past two decades have been characterized by novel, 
intensive, continual and variable strategies and efforts towards treatments for LSDs according to 
different events and principles in the pathogenetic cascade1. The pathogenetic cascade of LSDs and 
representative strategy of treatment are presented in Figure 1. There are five main approaches that 
are currently clinical approved or under investigation which exhibit promising data for developing 
advanced treatments for LSDs in the future. However, all of them have advantages and limitations 
which are summarized in Table 1. On the whole, there are no known cures for LSDs. However, the 
mainstay of treatment among these approaches mentioned above for some LSDs is enzyme 
replacement therapy (ERT) aiming at preventing permanent damage to the body by intravenously 
infusing exogenous enzymes either recombinantly expressed in selected and appropriate cell lines 
or extracted from biological materials3.

Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) 
 
Normal exogenous enzymes can be used to replace deficient enzymes as originally demonstrated by 
Duve in 19649 and Brady in 196610. Subsequent studies later also demonstrated that exogenous 
enzymes can be taken up by fibroblasts, thus degrading the accumulating substrates in the 
lysosomes11–13. It was possible and rational to apply ERT for LSDs based on these encouraging 
discoveries, while still confronting huge challenges including protein production, specific targeting, 
CNS crossing and lack of animal models4. However, these obstacles did not impede many 
researches exploring lysosomal biology and diseases. Brady and his group applied purified -
glucocerebrosidase derived from human placentae to treat patients with type 1 Gaucher disease 
which was the first clinical proof of concept for ERTs in LSDs14. However, requirement for high 
doses of enzyme isolated from placentae was a big challenge. Hence, researches turned to 
alternative sources and methods that could produce and purify appropriate lysosomal enzymes 
including specific glycoforms sufficiently24.  
 
With the progress of some biotechnologies such as recombinant DNA technology15–18, eukaryotic 
expression systems, disease-related animal models, it becomes more realistic and easier to 
investigate and produce recombinant lysosomal replacement enzymes. Although most of lysosomal 
replacement enzymes are under investigation or in clinical trials12, some of them such as 
replacement enzymes for Fabry disease, Gaucher disease type I, Pompe disease, Wolman disease 
and Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS I, II, IV A and VI) are on the market now12. There are several 
physiological factors which can influence cellular uptake, transportation, biodistribution of 
lysosomal replacement enzymes such as endogenous receptors interaction, biological barriers, 
blood flow rate etc. In general, it is still difficult to improve the efficacy of ERTs for LSDs to 
delivery infused replacement enzymes to hard-to-reach organs such as brain, heart, bone, muscle 
and kidney4. An overview of current status of ERTs for LSDs together with unsolved problems are 
summarized in Table 2.

N-linked glycosylation and endogenous receptors for lysosomal 
enzymes 
 
With more and more lysosomal acidic hydrolytic enzymes have been identified over recent decades, 
researchers found that targeting of enzymes to the lysosome required appropriately post-
translationally processed N-glycosylation on the nascent polypeptides24. Early studies found that 
when folding properly with a (Glucose)3-(Mannose)9-(GlcNAc)2 oligosaccharide on asparagine 
(Asn) residues in the rough endoplasmic reticulum (ER), lysosomal enzymes are directed to the cis 
Golgi network after trimming by glucosidases and mannosidases, followed by being phosphorylated 
at position 6 on mannose residues. In the trans Golgi, mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) residues which 
are exposed by a phosphodiesterase enzyme, then can bind to M6P receptors (MPRs), following by 
==PAGE:18==
directing to endosome and fission-fusion to lysosome25,26. More details are explained in the 
following section. 
 
Importantly, M6P independent pathways have also been reported to participate in the trafficking of 
enzymes to the lysosome. For instance, enzyme glucocerebrosidase in the ER will bind to the 
lysosomal membrane protein LIMP-227. In general, there are three main receptors responsible for 
participating in uptake and delivering of enzymes to lysosome which will be introduced more in the 
following section: mannose 6-phosphate (M6P) receptors (MPRs)28, asialoglycoprotein receptor 
(ASGPR)29, and mannose receptor (MR)30. These receptors can be found on different cell types 
recognizing distinct N-glycans.  
  
N-linked glycosylation 
 
N-linked glycosylation refers to a series of processing steps which start with the attachment of the 
oligosaccharide to asparagine (Asn) residues in a protein by a N-glycosidic bond31. The consensus 
sequence motif of N-linked glycosylation is Asn-X-Ser/Thr, where X can be any amino acid except 
Proline. Meanwhile, non-canonical N-glycosites such as Asn-X-Cys are also observed and 
reported32. Although N-linked glycosylation is highly conserved in eukaryotic cells, the final 
processed and mature glycan structures are different in different species. There are three general N-
glycans in eukaryotic cells: oligomannose, complex and hybrid. These three N-glycans types are 
shown in Figure 2 and common N-glycan structures in different species are shown in Figure 3.

N-glycan biosynthesis contains two major phases proceeding in two compartments including ER 
and Golgi. The first phase which occurs in ER membrane is attachment of an oligosaccharide from 
the Dolichol-linked precursor to Asn residues in Asn-X-Ser/Thr of secretory and membrane 
proteins when they translocate into the ER. The second phase which occurs in the lumen of ER and 
Golgi is processing N-glycans by a series of glycosidases and glycosyltransferases which are 
differential expressed according to species, cell type, functions and glycosites of glycoproteins34.  
 
==PAGE:20==
To synthesize the Dolichol-linked precursor, several asparagine-linked glycosylation proteins 
(ALGs) and dolichol-phosphate mannosyltransferases subunits (DPMs) are involved in initial steps 
depicted in Figure 4. Synthesis starts with transferring GlcNAc-1-P from UPD-GlcNAc to form 
Dol-P-P-GlcNAc by a GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase named ALG7. The next step is catalyzed by 
ALG13/14 that add another GlcNAc to GlcNAc-P-P-Dol. Subsequently, Mannose residues are 
added from GDP-Mannose by ALG1, ALG2 and ALG11 to form Man5GlcNAc2-P-P-Dol precursor. 
The Man5GlcNAc2-P-P-Dol precursor translocates from the cytoplasm side of ER to ER lumen by a 
flippase which relates the RFT1 in yeast. Whereafter, four Mannose and three Glucose are added 
from Dol-P-Man and Dol-P-Glc to the Man5GlcNAc2-P-P-Dol precursor by ALG3, ALG9, ALG12, 
ALG6, ALG8 and ALG10 respectively to form mature 14-sugar N-glycan precursor, 
Glc3Man9GlcNAc2-P-P-Dol. The following step is catalyzed by oligosaccharyltransferase complex 
(OST) to transfer this mature 14-sugar precursor to Asn residues in selected Asn-X-Ser/Thr sequons 
in regions of protein when translocating into the ER34.

After the transfer of the 14-suger glycan to the nascent polypeptide chain, processing of 
Glc3Man9GlcNAc2Asn is catalyzed by -glucosidase I (MOGS) and II (GANAB) that remove Glc 
residues. MOGS and GANAB remove the terminal 1-2 Glc residues and two internal 1-3 Glc 
respectively. Moreover, it is also reported that endo -mannosidase (MANEA) can remove all three 
Glc35. To exit the ER and enter to the cis-Golgi, ER -mannosidase I (MAN1B1) works first to 
remove the terminal 1-2 Mannose from the central arm of Man9GlcNAc2 to form Man8GlcNAc2. 
Next steps which occur in cis-Golgi are removal of 1-2 Mannose by 1-2 mannosidases IA and IB 
(MAN1A1, MAN1A2) to form Man5GlcNAc2. This Man5GlcNAc2 structure is an important 
intermediate in the biosynthesis pathway to generate hybrid and complex N-glycans which occur in 
the late processing steps of N-glycosylation34. 
 
In the medial-Golgi, the first continual step is catalyzed by a N-acetylglucosaminyltransferase 
named GlcNAc-TI (MGAT1) that adds a GlcNAc residue on the 1-3Man in the Man5GlcNAc2 
structure. The majority of N-glycans will be trimmed of the terminal 1-3Man and 1-6Man by 
Golgi -mannosidase II (MAN2A1 or MAN2A2). Then, a second GlcNAc is added to the 1-6 arm 
to form precursor of all biantennary and complex N-glycans by GlcNAc-TII (MGAT2) after 
removal both of Man residues, while hybrid N-glycans will be formed if -mannosidase II does not 
work. More branches can be generated either at C-4 on the 1-3 Man by MGAT4A/MGAT4B or at 
C-6 on the 1-6 Man by MGAT5 to form tri- and tetra-antennary N-glycans structures34.  
 
The maturation of N-glycans comprises more steps including sugar additions, elongation of 
branching and capping of elongated branches to form diverse complex structures. The main core 
modification is catalyzed by 1-6 fucosyltransferase (FUT8) which adds 1-6 Fuc to the Asn-
linked GlcNAc in the N-glycan core in vertebrates. To extend branches, Gal can be added by 
members of the  1-4-galactosyltranferase family (B4GALT1-6) with the formation of Gal1-
4GlcNAc (LacNAc) structure. The major modification of capping or decoration of elongated 
branches is sialic acids (SA) capping with 2-3 linkage or 2-6 linkage catalyzed by 2-3 or 2-6-
sialyltransferases (ST3GALs or ST6GALs). It is also possible to further elongate with 2-8 linkage 
by 2-8-sialyltransferase family (ST8SIA). Other capping reactions include addition of Fuc, Gal, 
GlcNAc and sulfate which can lead to complex N-glucan branches34.

The series and complex steps mentioned above yield multiple and variable N-glycan structures 
which are different from composition, length, branch number, core structure and capping patterns.

Endogenous receptors for lysosomal enzymes 
 
As mentioned above, membrane specific receptors play a critical role for therapeutic effects of 
ERTs where receptors bind a high affinity ligand and enter the cell through receptor-mediated 
endocytosis. Although it is reported that there are a number of receptors participating in uptake and 
lysosomal delivery, mannose 6-phosphate (M6P) receptors (MPRs), mannose receptor (MR) and 
asialoglycoprotein receptor (ASGPR) are the three most important receptors for lysosomal 
replacement enzymes. Distinct glycoforms can be predicted and identified through binding 
specificities with these different receptors37. 
 
In the canonical sorting process, it is critical to generate phospho-mannosyl residues on the 
lysosomal enzymes when passaging through the Golgi apparatus, which act as high affinity ligands 
for binding to MPRs in the Golgi. The targeted lysosomal enzymes will be released into a lysosome 
while the receptor either returns to Golgi or functions to internalize exogenous lysosomal enzymes 
on the plasma membrane. There are two kinds of MPRs, cation-dependent mannose 6-phosphate 
receptor (CD-MPR) and cation-independent mannose 6-phosphate receptor (CI-MPR). CI-MPR is 
bigger and more important for binding and internalizing lysosomal enzymes than CD-MPR because 
that CI-MPR can not only transport hydrolases from trans Golgi network, but also mediates 
endocytosis of secreted acid hydrolases from cell surface37. UDP-GlcNAc: lysosomal enzyme N-
acetylglucosamine-1-phosphotransferase (GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase) and N-
acetylglucosamine-1-phosphodiester -N-acetylglucosaminidase are needed in the Man-6-P 
synthesis. Impaired GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase due to mutations will result in two autosomal 
disorders which are mucolipidosis II (Inclusion-cell disease or I-cell disease) and mucolipidosis III 
(pseudo-Hurler polydystrophy)38. These diseases are defined by a defective mannose-6-
phosphorylation which give rise to hypersecretion of acid hydrolase and accumulation of 
undegraded substrate in the lysosomes. Enlarged lysosomes and autolysosomes, therefore, 
accumulate within the cell. However, other studies also found that morphological alterations were 
==PAGE:25==
only found in some cell types and tissues in GlcNaC-1-phosphotransferase knock-out and knock-in 
mice, while some morphology and their acid hydrolases retained or partly at least39–47. These 
observations were also found in mice deficiency of the two MPRs48. All these studies suggested that 
targeting of acid hydrolases to the lysosomes did not exclusively rely on M6P dependent pathway.  
 
The functions of MR expressed mainly on macrophages and hepatic sinusoidal endothelium are 
numerous including clearance of endogenous molecules and lysosomal targeting.  MR binds 
glycoconjugates with exposed mannose, fucose or N-acetyl-glucosamine (GlcNAc) in a calcium-
dependent manner. In Gaucher’s disease which is one of the most prevalent lysosomal storage 
disorders with accumulation of glucocerebroside within the lysosomes of macrophages. So, it is 
necessary to deliver replacement enzyme to macrophages. Barton et al. reported the clinical 
effectiveness of treatment for Gaucher’s disease using macrophage-targeted strategy relying on 
MR49. 
 
The discovery that a specific receptor on hepatocytes mediated clearance of desialylated 
glycoproteins using desialylated ceruloplasmin by recognition of galactose residues was critical for 
understanding ASGPR50. ASGPR binds to -Gal and -GalNAc-terminating glycans usually because 
of insufficient sialylation or desialylation. The function of ASGPR is to mediate rapid clearance of 
glycoproteins from circulation37,51. Based on these discoveries, improving SA capping can be applied 
to reduce binding affinity of N-glycans to ASGPR thus increasing circulation time of lysosomal 
replacement enzymes. As mentioned above, there are different linkages of SA capping including 
2,3, 2,6 and 2,8, which can also influence binding between glycans and ASGPR. However, many 
studies demonstrated that glycoproteins with 2,3 SA had longer circulation time than glycoproteins 
with other linkages of SA capping. 
 
During the last ten years, great efforts have been made to identify and characterize variable M6P 
independent trafficking receptors for lysosomal acid hydrolases. Some of the receptors or proteins 
for endosomal and lysosomal sorting and trafficking and examples of their binding lysosomal 
enzymes are simply structurally visualized in Figure 7. Moreover, other glycan-binding proteins 
such as Galectins and Siglecs can also bind to N-glycans thus playing roles in bioavailability of 
therapeutic glycoproteins, but these effects are not well elucidated29,52.

Mannose 6-phosphate tagging and efficacy of ERTs 
 
As mentioned above, previous studies demonstrated that efficient uptake of lysosomal enzymes 
requires M6P tag by using either chemical inhibitors or alkaline phosphatase55. Tissue uptake and 
therapeutic effect may vary between lysosomal replacement enzymes due to different M6P contents 
resulting from M6P mediated endocytosis. The difference caused by M6P contents can be observed 
by comparing two replacement enzymes which are used to treat Fabry disease, Fabrazyme 
(Genzyme) and Replagal (Shire). To compare glycosylation pattern, Fabrazyme have more M6P 
and higher SA capping than Replagal. Although Fabrazyme have better kidney and heart delivery in 
==PAGE:27==
murine disease models according to some studies56,57, there is no significant clinical therapeutic 
effect among these two drugs which can be explained by too little M6P difference.  
Many strategies have been made to increase the M6P content on lysosomal replacement enzymes 
thus improving the MPRs-mediated endocytosis. The first strategy was chemical conjugation. 
Studies of recombinant acid-glucosidase (GAA) conjugating chemically with either M6P-
containing purified or synthetic oligosaccharides58,59, or M6P analogues AMFAs showed improved 
MPR binding and uptake60,61.  Following the progress in chemical conjugation, increasing of M6P 
was carried out in gene modified yeast. Tiels et al. generated GAA produced in gene-modified yeast 
which showed improved cellular uptake in vitro and reducing glycogen storage in a murine disease 
model62. 
 
However, the circulation, biodistribution and stability of lysosomal replacement enzymes are 
determined by multiple factors and mechanisms. Generally, current ERTs have comparatively short 
half-lives because of uptaking by ASGPRs, MPRs and MRs in the liver2. Shen et al. reported that a 
non-phosphorylated glycan form of -galactosidase A produced from moss was effective to clear 
accumulated substrate in the heart and kidney in a mouse model of Fabry disease63. Grubb et al. 
investigated that chemically modified -glucuronidase had an extended half-life from less than 10 
min to 18h without lectin interactions between M6P or Man on glycans and M6PR or MR. These 
studies led to a hypothesis that M6P residues on glycans may not always be essential for ERTs and 
glycan-mediated may influence circulation and biodistribution adversely64. 
 
Studies made in the host group presented a comprehensive glycoengineering screen in Chinese 
hamster ovary (CHO) cells and enabled production of lysosomal enzymes with different N-
glycoforms. This study provides further rationale to explore effect of key glycan on cellular uptake 
and circulation of lysosomal enzymes through custom designed N-glycans by glycoengineering.

Double strand break (DSB) repair mechanisms 
 
Homology-Directed Repair (HDR) and Non-Homologous End-Joining (NHEJ) are the two main 
and distinct DNA DSB repair mechanisms. When DSB occurs, a template with homologous 
sequences to both sites of the generated DSB will be introduced to the specific site. This is the HDR 
mechanism which has a highly precise repairment66. On the other hand, NHEJ will cause insertions 
or deletions due to join the break ends directly67. 
 
Zinc Fingers Nucleases (ZFNs) 
 
ZFNs which are chimeric proteins are composed of a zinc finger (ZF) DNA-binding domain and a 
non-specific cleavage domain of the FokI restriction enzyme. The DNA-binding domain of a ZFN 
consists of 3-6 ZFs which can recognize a DNA triplet per ZF. Two FokI domain are positioned 
oppositely and work actively when dimerized to induce DSB65. 
 
Santiago et al. reported that ZFNs can be designed to KO gene in a CHO cell line in 2008. In their 
study, frequencies of gene disruption were more than 1%68. Meanwhile, ZFNs can not only be used 
to disrupt gene, but also be used to introduce genes by using the HDR repair mechanism69,70. 
 
CRISPR/Cas9 
 
The CRISPR-Cas modules which are adaptive immunity systems found in many archaea and 
bacteria functioning against invading genetic elements are the latest advent precise genome editing 
tools. There are many CRISPR/Cas systems found in nature. CRISPR/Cas9 belongs to type II 
CRISPR/Cas system consisting of short CRISPR RNA (crRNA), trans-activating crRNA 
(tracrRNA) and the Cas9 nuclease hybridize71. In general, the mechanism is that when foreign DNA 
is present in the cell, tracrRNA and crRNA start to express and bind together leading the Cas9 to 
site-specific cleave the foreign DNA72.  
 
The first study that Cas9 enzyme, along with a dual-tracrRNA : crRNA RNA chimera, called 
single-guided RNA (sgRNA) can be used to generate site-specific DSBs in vitro was reported in 
2012 by Jinek et al.73.  Subsequently, CRISPR/Cas-based genome editing was applied in 
==PAGE:30==
mammalian cells with similar or even higher efficiency compared to ZFNs and TALENs. There are 
two main advantages of CRISPR/Cas system. First, there is no protein engineering needed. Second, 
it can be used for multiplex genome engineering by introducing multiple sgRNA. The only target-
site requirement is the presence of a proto-spacer-adjacent motif (PAM) with the consensus 
sequence 5’-NGG-3’ from Streptococcus pyogenes, where N is any nucleotide. The PAM sequence 
locates downstream of the 20-bp target immediately74.  
 
On the whole, the activity and specificity of CRISPR/Cas-based gene targeting are influenced by 
many factors such as the selected Cas9, the PAM, the sgRNA, the targeted cell, pH, temperature 
and others factors75–77. 
 
In summary, precise nuclease-based genome editing techniques like ZFNs and CRISPR/Cas9 are 
easier and more efficient to engineer and manipulate DNA compared to techniques in the past 
which heavily relied on selected pressure78,79. However, there is still a challenge to generate 
controlled homogenous glycan structures of relevant therapeutic glycoproteins. 
 
Genetic engineering N-glycosylation capacities in CHO cells 
 
With the progress of nuclease-based precise genome techniques stated above, it is possible to apply 
N-glycosylation engineering to produce recombinant therapeutic glycoproteins with desirable 
glycan structures80. Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell line has been used broadly to produce 
recombinant therapeutic glycoproteins due to its human-like endogenous glycosylation capacity, 
thus avoiding immune response to non-human glycans. However, capacity of glycosylation in 
human cell lines is often more complex than CHO cell lines. For example, CHO cells produce 
complex N-glycans with 2-3 linked SA only because of the St6gal1 sialyltransferase gene is not 
expressed.

promising strategy to improve bioactivity and bioavailability is to change the glycan structures of 
therapeutic glycoproteins81,82. Some studies demonstrated that increased size of glycans may

Mass spectrometry and site-specific analysis of glycoproteins 
 
It is difficult and challenging to characterize particular glycan structures in specific glycosylation 
sites in a given glycoproteins, because of the huge complexity and heterogeneity of the glycan 
structures at individual site. However, this dilemma has been solved to some extent by progress in 
mass spectrometry technology. Mass spectrometry has increasingly become the most powerful tool 
to analysis glycans and glycoproteins with high accuracy and comprehensive structural 
information87. 
 
To identify the glycan structures custom-designed by N-glycan engineering mentioned above, mass 
spectrometry-based analysis at site-specific level was applied. An essential part is to analyze 
glycosites using de-N-glycosylated peptides. N-glycans can be removed completely or partially by 
enzymes. For example, PNGase F can remove the entire N-glycans which causes a +0.98Da change 
due to Asn residues converting to Asp. Therefore, N-glycosites can be identified by detection of 
Asn deamidation. However, Asn can also convert to Asp in the process of sample preparation 
without PNGase F treatment. Additionally, glycan information can be obtained with advances of 
fragmentation methods such as Higher-energy collisional dissociation (HCD) and (Electron-transfer 
dissociation) ETD88. 
 
To characterize the different glycoforms in GLA variants in the study of the host group, they also 
developed a more accurate strategy to analysis site-specific N-glycoproteins deeply. The methods 
will be explained in detail in the following section.

CHAPTER 2 - MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
Introduction 
 
Glycosylasparaginase (AGA) encoding by gene Aga is responsible for hydrolyzing the protein-
oligosaccharide linkage in Asn-glycoprotein substrates89. The mutation in the Aga gene results in 
deficient activity of glycosylasparaginase which leads to a recessively inherited lysosomal storage 
disease, aspartylglucosaminuria (AGU). Because of the deficiency of glycosylasparaginase activity 
in the lysosome, aspartylglucosamine and other glycoasparagines accumulate in the body fluids and 
tissue90. Decreased cognitive abilities, skeletal abnormalities and facial grotesqueness are the most 
common clinical manifestation in AGU patients. Currently, there is no curative treatment for AGU. 
Some studies demonstrated that enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) can degrade the accumulating 
substrates in many somatic tissues in adult AGU mice model, however, the efficacy in the brain 
tissue was limited91. 
 
-glucuronidase (GUSB) encoding by gene Gusb is responsible for degrading glucuronate-
containing glycosaminoglycan. Deficiencies in the activities of GUSB resulting from gene mutation 
lead to a type of mucopolysaccharidosis (MPS), MPS VII. Due to the deficient activity of GUSB, 
non- or partially degraded mucopolysaccharides accumulate in the lysosomes of many tissues, 
leading to cellular damage and organ dysfunction. It is reported that ERT prevents the accumulating 
substrates in the lysosome in MPS VII patients. However, enzymes aiming to treat 
mucopolysaccharidosis with CNS involvement are still under investigation92. 
 
Cathepsin D (CTSD) encoding by gene Cln10 is responsible for degrading proteins in the pre-
lysosomal compartments93. The mutations in the CTSD gene results in deficient activity of aspartyl 
protease leading to a type of the most frequent lysosomal diseases, the neuronal ceroid 
lipofuscinoses (NCL). The common clinical manifestation of NCL disease are poor intrauterine 
foetal growth rate, microcephaly and seizures. Currently, there is no effective treatment for 
congenial NCL disease94.

Workflow 
 
To implement 2-3linked sialic acid gene engineering design, I used CHO cells as expression 
system of recombinant enzymes. The experimental workflow is illustrated in Figure 10. The 
modified pCGS3 containing expression construct of AGA, GUSB or CTSD was transfected in CHO 
GS-/- cells and plated in a 96 well plate with appropriate and same amount of cells in each well. 
SDS-PAGE or enzyme activity assay were used to select wells of cells with relative high expression 
of recombinant enzymes. Then single cell sorting and a new round of SDS-PAGE or enzyme 
activity assay were conducted to select the stable clone with high expression of recombinant 
enzymes respectively (Figure 10A). Precise genome editing including KO Gnptab by 
CRISPR/Cas9 and KI ST3GAL4 by ZFNs were carried out in stable clone high expression of WT 
enzymes. The following bulk sorting and single cell sorting were performed to isolate potential 
successfully genome edited cells. Then the effect of genome editing was further confirmed by 
IDAA or Junction-PCR. A new round of SDS-PAGE or enzyme activity assay was conducted 
subsequently. The clone used to produce glycoengineered enzymes was selected based on above 
results and condition of cells (Figure 10B). The WT and glycoengineered enzymes were produced 
to appropriate amount, followed by purification. AGA and GUSB were purified by ion exchange 
chromatography. CTSD was purified by immobilized metal affinity chromatography (His-tag). 
After purification, SDS-PAGE was used to detect WT and glycoengineered enzymes in purified 
fraction. LC-MS/MS was carried out to perform site specific N-glycans analysis to conform if our 
glycoengineering strategy was applicable (Figure 10C).

Site specific N-glycopeptide analysis by LC-MS 
 
Samples preparation 
 
Approximately 25µg of purified AGA, GUSB or CTSD were dissolved in 50mM ammonium 
bicarbonate (AmBic) buffer (pH 7.4) and reduced with 10mM dithiothreitol (DTT) at 60°C on 
shaker for 45 min, followed by alkylation with 20mM iodoacetamide (IAA) at room temperature for 
30 min in darkness. Chymotrypsin was used to perform digestion (1:40 enzyme-substrate ratio). 
Then the proteolytic digested samples were acidified with 1µl TFA and desalted by custom-made 
modified StagTip columns which contained three layers of C18 and two layers of C8 membrane 
(3M Empore disks, Sigma-Aldrich). Samples were eluted with two times of 50µl 50% methanol in 
0.1% formic acid, followed by dividing each sample into two panels including 50µl in a glass insert 
and 50µl in an Eppendorf and drying both in SpeedVac (Eppendorf, Germany) for approximately 2 
hours. When dried completely, samples in glass inserts were re-solubilized in 50µl 0.1% formic 
acid. The other samples were re-solubilized in 50µl 50mM AmBic buffer (pH 7.4) and added in 1U 
PNGase F per sample at 37°C on shaker for overnight. Samples which are treated with PNGase F 
were desalted and dried by the same methods mentioned above.  
 
Liquid chromatography MS/MS analysis 
 
An EASY-nLC 1000 LC system (Thermo Fisher Scientific) interfaced via nanoSpray Flex ion 
source to an Orbitrap Fusion MS (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to perform liquid 
chromatography MS/MS analysis. A single analytical column setup using PicoFrit Emitters (New 
Objectives, 75µm inner diameter) custom packed with Reprosil-Pure-AQ C18 phase (Dr. Maisch, 
1.9-µm particle size, 19-21 cm column length) was applied in nLC. 2µl of each sample was injected 
onto the column, followed by eluting with a gradient of Solvent B from 2 to 25% at 200nL/min for 
45 min (Solvent A: 100% H2O + 0.1% (v/v) formic acid, Solvent B: 100% acetonitrile + 0.1% (v/v) 
formic acid). With the nominal resolution setting of 120,000, precursors of MS1 scan (m/z 350-
2000) were obtained. Then Orbitrap HCD-MS2 of the five most abundant multiply charged 
precursors in the MS1 spectrum were acquired at the nominal resolution setting of 60,000. To 
trigger data-dependent fragmentation events, the minimum MS1 signal threshold was 50,000. 
==PAGE:43==
Targeted MS/MS analysis was performed by setting up a targeted MSn (tMSn) Scan Properties 
panel 30 targeted entries were included in the Mass List Table. 
In-house written R scripts were used to perform glycopeptide compositional analysis through m/z 
features which were recognized from full MS scans LFQ Profiler Node in the Proteome discover 
2.3 (Thermo Fisher Scientific). There were five main building blocks which were NeuAc, Hex, 
HexNAc, dHex and phosphate used for the glycopeptide compositional analysis. The most 
prominent peptides corresponding to each potential glycosite were determined experimentally by 
comparing the yield of deamidated peptides before and after PNGase F treatment and also added as 
a building block for the compositional assignment. The peptide sequence was determined by HCD 
MS/MS and the abundance level was calculated from PD 2.3. A list of potential glycopeptides and 
glycoforms for each glycosite was generated and the top 10 or top 15 of the most abundant 
candidates for each glycosite were selected for targeted MS/MS analysis to conﬁrm the proposed 
structure. Each targeted MS/MS spectrum was manually interpretated. Note, the same N-glycan 
composition may represent isobaric structures, so the listed glycan structures were assisted by and 
in agreement with the predicted enzyme functions of the targeted genes together with information in 
MS/MS fragments.

Glycoengineering strategy 
 
As mentioned in the N-glycan biosynthesis section, most N-glycans of lysosomal enzymes have 
M6P. The phosphorylation step is generated by UDP-GlcNAc:lysosomal enzyme GlcNAc-1-
phosphotransferase (GlcNAc-P-T) encoding by gene Gnptab and Gnptg. Because the catalytic 
function of the GlcNAc-P-T is controlled by Gnptab. It is assumed that the GlcNAc-P-T would not 
work to transfer GlcNAc-P to mannose residues if Gnptab is KO. Moreover, it is clear to increase 
SA capping by KI ST3GAL4 based on production of complex N-glycans. The glycoengineering 
strategy is simply depicted in Figure 11. 
 
 
Figure 11. Glycoengineering strategy for generating 2-3linked sialic acid gene engineering design 
lysosomal enzymes.  
Following the biosynthesis pathway of lysosomal enzymes, N-glycans of some enzymes would have M6P. There are two steps in the

Site-specific N-glycoforms in N-glycosylation engineering lysosomal 
enzymes 
 
In this project, we included three lysosomal enzymes (AGA, GUSB and CTSD) in the site specific 
N-glycan analysis to explore if the  2,3 SA capping glycoengineering design can be universally 
applied. A wildtype form and a Gnptab KO with ST3GAL4 KI form of AGA were selected for the 
analysis. For both GUSB and CTSD, a wildtype form and a Gnptab KO form were chosen. 
 
All these enzymes contain multiple N-glycosylation sites and to cover them with appropriate 
peptide lengths, proteases for the proteolytic digestion of these enzymes need to be carefully 
picked. We firstly conducted an in silico digestion of these enzymes and found chymotrypsin was 
the most suitable protease for all these three enzymes. The chymotrypsin digests were then 
aliquoted into two and one aliquot was furthered treated with PNGase F to remove N-glycans. Both 
PNGase F treated sample and non-treated sample were analyzed in MS. The rational that we treat 
the chymotrypsin digest with PNGase F is to determine the most prominent peptide sequence of 
each N-glycosite as protease efficiency and specificity can be affected.  
 
With the information of the prominent peptide sequence, compositional assignment was performed 
by extract MS1 features to generate a list of glycopeptides. The main building blocks used for the 
compositional analysis contain NeuAc (S), Hex (H), HexNAc (N), dHex (F), and phosphate (P). 
The proposed glycopeptides were also confirmed by MS2 analysis by detection of characteristic 
glycopeptide fragment ions and oxonium ions formed after the HCD fragmentation of glycan 
structures: 204.09+, 366.14+, etc. Glycan compositions are expressed as: Nx, number (x) of 
HexNAcs; Hx, number (x) of Hexoses; Sx, number (x) of NeuAcs; Fx, number (x) of Fucoses; Px, 
number (x) of Phosphates. The steps of site-specific N-glycans analysis are generally depicted in 
Figure 14. 
 
==PAGE:50==

Purified AGA, GUSB or CTSD are reduced, alkylated and subjected to chymotrypsin digestion (1:40 enzyme-substrate ratio). 
Samples are divided into two panels and one aliquot is further treated with PNGase F. Both samples are then desalted and subjected

glycopeptides are also confirmed by targeted MS2 analysis with manual annotation.

of alpha/beta heterodimers. Both alpha and beta chains carry one N-glycosite (N38 on alpha chain

the prominent peptide sequences for N38 and N308 were found to be VVNTWPFKNATEAAW 
and GAVICANVTGSY, respectively. The annotated MS2 spectra of these two peptides with Asn 
deamidation in the PNGase F treated samples are shown in Figure 15 and Figure 16. These 
sequences were then subjected to the compositional analysis as a main building block.

CHAPTER 4 - DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 
 
The unmet clinical needs for ERTs are mainly due to low efficiency in delivering the infused 
enzymes to difficult-to-target sites. The glycoengineering of GBA carrying terminal mannoses is a 
wonderful example that optimization of N-glycans can improve the desired targeting of ERTs. 
However, targeting macrophages which reside part of their life in circulation is relatively easy, 
which seems likely an exception instead of the rule107. The fact that no other replacement enzyme 
has been able to match the efficacy of ERTs for Gaucher disease asks new strategies for 
optimization of glycan structures. 
 
For ages, the manufacture of glycoproteins was mostly determined by the intrinsic property of the 
production hosts. The intractable glycosylation lead to diverse and heterogeneous glycan structures 
on therapeutic glycoproteins which cause inconstant efficacy. CHO cells are the most widely used 
mammalian expression hosts in biopharmaceutical industry due to easy growth, products high 
expression, human-like post-translational modification pathways and the enzyme extracellular 
secretion2. With the advances in precision genome editing especially the application of 
CRISPR/Cas9, it is now possible to recombinantly produce glycoproteins with distinct glycan 
structures. Previous study from the host group has explored the glycoengineering capacities in CHO 
cells using GLA and GBA as reporter proteins86. 
 
To further explore the applicability of  2-3 linked sialic acid glycodesign to other lysosomal 
enzymes, I established stable CHO clones with high expression of three other lysosomal enzymes 
including AGA, GUSB and CTSD. The removal M6P through KO Gnptab were conducted 
following the glycoengineering strategies above. The M6P tag blocks the mannosidases trimming 
and performs as a barrier for becoming complex N-glycans. For AGA and CTSD, it was obvious 
that remove M6P tag enables, production of enzymes with rather homogenous complex N-glycans 
on all N-glycosites. Subsequently, KI ST3GAL4 can increase  2-3 linked sialic acid capping of N-
glycans on AGA as expected. However, there is an obstacle to generate homogenous complex N-
glycans on all sites of GUSB because one glycosite of GUSB naturally processes high-mannose N-
glycans which is irrelevant to the M6P blockage. 
 
==PAGE:66==
In summary, in this Master thesis, I demonstrated that N-glycan site possesses high-mannose 
structure is not applicable to  2-3 linked sialic acid glycodesign using our current 
glycoengineering matrix. How to convert this type of high-mannose N-glycans into complex 
structure is an interesting topic for later studies.
