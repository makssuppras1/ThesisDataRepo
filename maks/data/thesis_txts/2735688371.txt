Abstract
Biofilms represent the predominant form of microbial life in natural environments. Their dualistic naôÙ
ture poses significant challenges and opportunities within healthcare, the food industry, and in bioproôÙ
cesses for production of biofuels and valuable chemicals.
This study explores the intricate dynamics of biofilm formation, emphasizing the pivotal role of Quorum
sensing (QS) molecules, specifically NôÙAcyl homoserine lactones (AHLs), in modulating microbial
community behavior. Through a series of experimental setups, including microtiter plate assays and
flowôÙcell systems, the impact of different AHLs on the growth and development of biofilms derived
from anaerobic digestion (AD) cultures and syngas cultures were investigated. The initial longôÙterm
experiments in microtiter plates with anaerobic cultures provided foundational insights, leading to more
focused shortôÙterm anaerobic biofilm growth studies. The flowôÙcell experiments were performed in
order to investigate the effect of AHLs on biofilm growth in realôÙtime, and confocal laster scanning
microscopy was used to quantify the biofilm formation and visualise its architecture.
The findings highlight significant differences in biofilm formation and structure in response to specific
AHLs, with C10ôÙHSL showing a marked increase in biofilm thickness and complexity compared to
control setups. Additionally, it presents an exploration of hydrophobic and hydrophilic biofilm support
materials in microplate experiments.
This work provides valuable insights into different AHLs and their role in governing biofilm formation,
offering knowledge which can be used to design further experiments investigating the life of biofilms.

Introduction
In the microbial world, biofilms reign as the most prevalent and organized form of collective life, far
surpassing the solitary existence of planktonic microorganisms [1]. Biofilms exert their dominance
across Earthãs diverse natural environments accounting for a substantial portion, ranging from 40% to
80% of all bacterial and archaeal populations [2].
Biofilms have farôÙreaching implications in both healthcare and the food industry [3]. These implications
stem from their dual nature: a potential threat due to their role in infections and their ability to bolster the
resilience of pathogenic bacteria, while simultaneously offering opportunities for bioprocesses based
on a wholeôÙcell fermentation [4]. In the latter context, microorganisms serve as biocatalysts, fueling
the production of biofuels and valuable chemicals [4].
The allure associated with bioreactor systems centered around biofilms as biocatalysts, lies in their
capacity to outperform suspended ôÙgrowth systems in terms of volumetric productivity [5]. This adôÙ
vantage derives from two key factors: first, attachedôÙgrowth systems enable higher microbial biomass
concentrations within the reactor, and second, they allow operation at dilution rates exceeding the
microorganisms maximum growth rates, since biofilms retain the cells in the reactor preventing their
washout [5].
Biofilms, acting as cellular anchors prevent washout of the cells from the system [6]. Furthermore,
biofilms often induce significant physiological changes in the microorganisms, making synergistic imôÙ
provements in process outcomes. These changes include enhanced resistance to toxicity, shared
nutrient utilisation, and metabolic shifts leading to higher product yields [6].
Microbial communities, whether composed of multiôÙor singleôÙspecies populations, emerge from comôÙ
plex interaction networks driven by cellôÙtoôÙcell communication, primarily facilitated by QS signaling [7].
QS has been found to orchestrate a wide array of phenotypical changes across the microbial specôÙ
trum, spanning biofilm formation and cell density regulation, among numerous others. QS molecules,
which mediate this intricate language of microbial communication, can be categorized broadly into
NôÙacyl homoserine lactones (AHLs), autoinducer peptides (AIPs) and autoinducerôÙ2 (AIôÙ2 facilitating
interspecies communication) [7].
Anaerobic microbiological culturing techniques will be used, as well as, highôÙthroughput culturing
methods, biofilm quantification using confocal microscopy, FISH staining, DNA/RNA extraction proôÙ
cedures, chromatography, and LCôÙMS [8].
1.1
Problem Statement and Objectives
The primary objective of this research is to investigate the mechanisms of cellôÙtoôÙcell communication
that trigger biofilm formation within microbial communities. The investigation will revolve around the
impact of various potential Quorum sensing (QS) molecules, with a primary emphasis on AHLs, on
biofilm formation within microbial communities. Additionally, the study will explore the influence of
different biofilm support materials. To gain insight into the evolving biofilm dynamics over time, the
experiments will employ flow cells in tandem with confocal microscopy.

2.1
Biofilms
For the last four decades microbiologists have described bacteria as exhibiting two distinct life forms in
their natural habitat. In one form bacteria manifest as solitary, autonomous cells floating freely, while
in the other form they exist as organized microbial aggregates (biofilms) [9].
Microbial biofilms are complex structured communities of microorganisms, predominately bacteria
that adhere to surfaces[10]. They have been defined as aggregates of microorganisms in which cells
are frequently embedded in a selfôÙproduced matrix of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) [11].
A biofilm community is capable of living and reproducing as a colony. Biofilm formation is a part
of bacteriaãs survival mechanism. By working together bacteria can withstand extreme environments,
such as extreme temperature and pH, high salinity, high pressure, poor nutrients, antibiotics, ultraviolet
(UV) radiation, etc., with the biofilm acting as a protective shield [11]. The formation of biofilms involves
several stages, including initial attachment, microcolony formation, EPS production, and maturation.
Originally the term biofilm referred to biomaterial on surfaces, however recently aggregated bacteria
not attached to surfaces have also been proposed to be classified as biofilms [9]. The traditional model
emphasizes the role of surfaces in biofilm formation and development, from the initial attachment to
eventual dispersion [9]. An illustration of the traditional model for biofilm formation can be seen in 2.1
[9].
Figure 2.1: Traditional Biofilm Formation Model [9]
The new conceptual model of biofilm formation was suggested in an article from 2022, where the
model consists of three major steps, aggregation, growth and disaggregation evolving independently
of surfaces and lastly an initiation from singleôÙcell planktonic bacteria [9]. The model is supposed to
represent a broader range of biofilm systems [9].
This initial step involves the coming together of individual bacterial cells to form clusters. Unlike tradiôÙ
tional models where biofilm formation begins with the attachment of bacteria to a surface, this model
suggests aggregation can start in a freeôÙfloating (planktonic) state, independent of any surfaces [9].
After aggregation, the clusters of bacteria enter a growth phase. During this phase, the bacteria
multiply within the aggregate, leading to an increase in the size and complexity of the biofilm structure.
This growth is not dependent on a surface for the biofilm to expand [9].

The final stage of the model is the breaking apart or disaggregation of the biofilm. In this phase,
parts of the biofilm separate from the main structure, potentially to spread and initiate the formation
of new biofilms elsewhere. This step underscores the dynamic nature of biofilms, emphasizing that
they are not permanently fixed entities but can disperse and reform. The model posits that biofilm
formation is a fluid process that can initiate from singleôÙcell planktonic bacteria, evolving independently
of surfaces. This approach allows for a broader understanding of biofilm systems, accommodating
various environments and conditions where biofilms might form[9].

The key to the extraordinary survival mechanisms of biofilms lies in the communication properties they
exhibit, where the planktonic bacteria can ãtalkã through specific signaling molecules.
2.2
Quorum Sensing (QS) ôÙ The Bacterial Language
Quorum sensing (QS) is a regulatory mechanism in bacteria and some other microorganisms that
enables them to communicate and sense information from other cells in their respective population
when they reach a critical concentration and communicate with them [12]. In this responseôÙregulation
system genes are expressed in accordance with the cell population density [13]. The ãlanguageã in
which the bacterial cells communicate intercellularly is based on small chemical signaling molecules
called autoinducers and can induce group behaviors, consequently the cell can act actively against
fluctuations in external environmental conditions [13].
Overall, quorum sensing plays a pivotal role in biofilm development by regulating processes such as
adhesion, EPS production, and the expression of genes responsible for biofilm structure and resisôÙ
tance [12].
Quorum sensing inhibition can be achieved through various strategies that target the reduction of
quorum sensing signaling molecule concentrations. These approaches include the degradation of the
signaling molecules, disruption of their functional mechanisms, or inhibition of their synthesis within biôÙ
ological treatment systems [14]. Quorum sensing inhibition can be accomplished through four primary
methods: (i) adding quorum sensing inhibitors, (ii) cultivating quorum quenching bacteria, (iii) degradôÙ
ing quorum sensing signaling molecules with reactive oxygen species, and (iv) enzymatic degradation
of quorum sensing signaling molecules [14].

Adding enzymes to break down quorum sensing (QS) signal molecules represents a novel and develôÙ
oping strategy for inhibiting QS. A wide range of enzymes capable of degrading these molecules has
been explored, with those targeting NôÙacyl homoserine lactones (AHLs) receiving the most attention
[14]. Enzymes such as acylase, deaminase, lactonase, and decarboxylase have been identified as
highly effective for AHL degradation in wastewater treatment systems [14]. In membrane bioreactors
(MBR), the application of acylase, which is notably the enzyme most commonly utilized for dismantling
AHLs, resulted in a significant reduction in the levels of QS signaling molecules [14].
2.2.1
NôÙacyl homoserine lactones (AHLs)
AHLs were first discovered and described by Eberhard, they consist of a homoserine lactone ring
attached to a fatty acid side chain which is again linked by an amide bond [15]. The sum of carbons
on the side chains will vary due to the functional groups on the third carbon. Here, the carbon can be
substituted by ketone or hydroxyl groups [15].
QS signalling molecules can be categorized into various types. Three different categories is for examôÙ
ple NôÙacyl homoserine lactones (AHLs) for gramôÙnegative bacteria, autoinducer peptides (AIPs) for
gramôÙpositive bacteria, and autoinducerôÙ1(AIôÙ2) for interspecies communication [7]. However there
are more different types and new QSôÙmolecules are still being dicovered. Quorum sensing allows
individual bacteria within colonies to coordinate and carry out colonyôÙwide functions such as biofilm
formation [7].
Figure 2.3: Examples of chemical structure of AHLs: A general structure of a NôÙacyl homoserine lactone with
an R group is shown at the top. The remaining structures are examples of AHLs produced in Vibrio fischeri and
Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Adapted from [16] to highlight the general AHL structure.
Overall, quorum sensing can be divided into two types: speciesôÙspecific communication and interôÙ
species communication [7]. Within speciesôÙspecific quorum sensing in gramôÙnegative bacteria it is
the AHLs mediating the communication. However in gramôÙpositive bacteria the speciesôÙspecific comôÙ
munication is predominately promoted through small peptides [7]. For a compound to qualify as a
signalling molecule, it must effect a reaction in a group of cells that differ from how the cells would
behave individually. Bacteria generating AHLs can react to nearby concentrations of the signalling
molecules, and at higher cell densities further promote the buildup of levels inducing AHLs [7].

The Lux indicator (I) proteins are the synthesizers of AHLs. The AHL molecules can diffuse through
the cell membrane and enter the surrounding environment of the bacteria as a messenger molecule
[15]. This is followed by the the detection of local AHL concentration by the Lux Regulator (R) homolôÙ
ogous proteins [15]. The recognition of an elevated AHL concentration can then trigger a coordinated
response, for instance biofilm formation, extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) production, surôÙ
face motility, and interspecies competition [15]. More than 200 species of gramôÙnegative bacteria are
known to use AHLs for signaling purposes. In addition, specific bacterial species that use AHLs with
various sideôÙchains and functional groups have been described [15].
2.2.2
LuxRôÙLuxI Circuit
For almost 30 years it has been known that there exists a genetic regulatory circuit, which is conserved
among many different species of Proteobacteria. The system is now known as the LuxRôÙLuxI type
quorum sensing circuit.
It was first described to control the light production in the gramôÙnegative marine bacterium Vibrio fisôÙ
cheri in the early 1970s and lately became the first thoroughly described signal and response QS
system [16]. The experiments in question had revealed an induction of bioluminescence in V. fisôÙ
cheriwhen it was freshly inoculated. When cultured in a liquid medium the cells did not emit light until
the late logarithmic growth phase was reached [16]. However, experiments showed that the delay
in luminescence during the early and midôÙlogarithmic growth phases could be shortened by adding a
small amount of a supernatant fluid from a culture in the stationary phase [16]. The culture from the
stationary phase was believed to contain a chemical autoinducer. Close to a decade later the autoinôÙ
ducer was identified as NôÙ3ôÙoxoôÙhexanoyl homoserine lactone (3OC6ôÙHSL), which has the ability to
passively diffuse over the cell membrane [16]. The genes responsible for V.fischeriãs luminescence
were then isolated and cloned Escherichia coli (E.coli), this showed light production which depended
on the cell density. The two genes LuxI and LuxR were found to be crucial for the autoinduction of
luminescence. LuxI encodes an enzyme that is responsible for the production of 3OC6ôÙHSL, while
LuxR in turn encodes for a 3OC6ôÙHSLôÙresponsive transcriptional activator, inducing the expression of
genes necessary for the cells producing light [16].
It was not until the early 1990s that this type of genetic control received attention among microbiolôÙ
ogists. During this period there was also notable advancements in DNA sequencing and analysis

2.2.3
Extracellular Polymeric Substances (EPS)
The biofilm matrix serves as a communal environment for the bacterial cells. It is produced and
regulated by the biofilms resident microbes. The EPS consists of among other substances, polysacôÙ
charides, proteins, lipids, extracellular DNA (eDNA) and membrane vesicles [1]. The EPS serves as
the foundation for a very adaptable and flexible microbial existence [1].
The EPS matrix works as a structured and shared space and is both generated and controlled by the
organisms within the biofilm [17]. Much of its functions have yet to be discovered, but the current
knowledge has been well described in a recent article by HansôÙCurt Flemming, among others [17].
The composition and dynamics of the matrix have constant influence on the biofilms mode of life. The
EPS matrix provides the architecture for a highly flexible, versatile and adaptable form of multicellular
microbial life. In this way it demonstrates many properties which has not shown to be shared by plankôÙ
tonic cells [17]. The dynamics within the biofilm is very complex, many different EPS components are
produced in the matrix at various points in time during the growth process. In addition, other microbial
species in the biofilm can influence the production of different EPS components. This in particular
evolution of the matrix is dependent on the amount and type of nutrients available, as well as the
environmental conditions the matrix encounters. This can be for example hydrodynamic stress, presôÙ
sure, salt concentration, temperature, light availability. Since millions of bacterial species coexists in
biofilms they are difficult to characterise. All of them contribute in producing their own EPS compoôÙ
nents[17]. It is for examples believed that millions of different exopolysaccharides exist in the matrix.
The large variety in species are in themselves also often undescribed in literature [17]. However, reôÙ
cently many advances in characterising the physioôÙchemical properties of many EPS components in
biofilms with mixed species have been made. Yet, it remains challenging to pinpoint which member
in the biofilm community is responsible for producing certain components, both for which function and
at what time. Most of the current methods for studying EPS composition have given snapshots of the
matrix, but not the dynamics and interactions between different community members and their unique
matrix components, and how it evolves over time [17].
Studying EPS is pivotal for understanding the microbial life of biofilms. It has direct implications for
developing new medical treatments, addressing environmental changes and improving industrial proôÙ
cesses. Considering industrial processes, it has been proven to enhance the anaerobic fermentation

Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a centuriesôÙold technology that represents a valuable method of generatôÙ
ing renewable energy. It is currently attracting considerable research interest for its future enhanceôÙ
ment. Despite being a wellôÙestablished theory the current studies primarily focus on optimizing the
process across various conditions [20].
Anaerobic digestion involves a series of biochemical steps where different microorganisms break
down organic matter like sewage sludge, manure, and food waste without oxygen [20]. This proôÙ
cess mainly produce methane and carbon dioxide gases along with a wet organic residue called a
digestate. The anaerobic digestion process is today used to manage and treat food and other organic
wastes, minimize greenhouse gas emissions from waste which would otherwise be landfilled, and
generate renewable energy by producing biogas [21]. The AD process is used across a variety of
sectors, such as in agriculture for the treatment of manure, energy crops and agroôÙindustrial waste. In
the food industry it is used for handling for example food processing leftovers, pulp, paper byôÙproducts
and biochemical waste [21]. Additionally, municipal waste management and water treatment facilities
use anaerobic digeston to process organic waste and sewage sludge, aiding in waste minimization
and management [21]. It is worth mentioning that anaerobic digestion is among several strategies for
waste treatment, which include methods like alkaline stabilization, aerobic digestion, composting and
thermophilic aerobic digestion. However, AD distinguishes itself as a particularly sustainable method
for its dual benefits in the sense that it produces renewable energy and at the same time reduces
waste volumes [20].
The process of anaerobic digestion includes four main stages, these are in order hydrolysis, acidogeôÙ
nesis, acetogenesis, and methanogenesis. An overview of the steps can be seen in fig 2.5. In each
step the anaerobic process is highly dependent on the interactions between different microorganisms.
The process of hydrolysis can break down the complex polymers which is typically found in organic
biomass. The organic macromolecules which are broken down into its smaller components can then
be utilized by acidogenic bacteria. Specifically in this process hydrolytic bacteria are able to secrete
extracellular enzymes which can can convert carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins into sugars, long
chain fatty acids, and amino acids, respectively [20]. Other substrates such as cellulose and lignin
may be to difficult to degrade and enzymes may need to be added to enhance the hydrolysis in these
cases.
After the complex polymers have been broken down into smaller molecules they can pass through the
cell membrane of acidogenic or fermentative microorganisms, this enables them to syntesize volatile
fatty acids (VFAs) and other products [20]. VFAs represents a class of organic acids such as acetates,
propionate and butyrate. The specific intermediate compounds produced in the acidogenesis step
may vary depending on the conditions (e.g. pH) present in the digester in question. It is believed that
the acidogenesis stage progresses at a quicker rate than any other stages in anaerobic digestion.
Even though this step is considered to be faster the VFA acidification has also been reported to be
one cause for a digester to fail. ProteinôÙrich feedstock, such as sewage sludge, lead to a significant
production ammonia through the decomposition of amino acids, complicating the anaerobic process.
In addition to ammonia, carbon dioxide and gases like hydrogen sulfide may also be produced.
The third step called acetogenesis is where the acetate which is formed from shortôÙchain/volatile fatty
in acidogenesis is produced together with hydrogen and carbon dioxide. More specifically acetogenôÙ

isms[20].

with and without biofilm support material. The first washing step in the CV protocol was eventually
also removed, in this way the sonication and CV protocol had the same amount of washing steps and

The Biofilm Flow Cell System
A flow cell system was set up to investigate biofilm growth. Flow cells are today an important tool
in studying biofilms [31]. In nature biofilms are often difficult to investigate partly due to the vague
conditions in which they exist [32]. However, by using transparent flow cells it is possible to observe
biofilms in realôÙtime, nonôÙdestructively with defined conditions [32]. The system allows for biofilms to
be studied in vitro with a hydrodynamic flow [31]. Following the cultivation, the biofilm can be examined
using Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (CLSM) and detailed 3D images of the microbial biofilm
can be created [32]. An overview of the flow cell setôÙup can be seen in figure 3.2.
The flow cell consists of 3 parallel channels which serve as replicates. Silicone glue was applied
between each channel and connected at each end of the flow cell to avoid leakage. A glass coverslip
was then placed on the silicone and carefully pressed down to be attached to the flow cell. Following,
the flow cell with the coverslip and silicone was left to dry overnight. The finished assembled flow cell
serves as a growth chamber for the biofilm. Tubes were connected to the inlets and outlets of the
channels in each flow cell, and clamps placed on each tube, in this way the flow cell can be clamped
off from the rest of the system, and removed to perform microscopy readings.
The biofilm is provided with nutrients from the medium flasks, in this case 3 different ones for each
run for the different AHLs investigated, including a flask with no AHL acting as the control. In the first
flow cell experiment the AHLs C6 and C10 were tested.
Bubble traps were constructed and placed upstream of the flow cells. The traps are designed to
remove bubbles before they can enter and potentially disrupt the system. Before the system was
inoculated bubbles which had made it passed the traps and into the flow cells were removed by
shaking the flow cells. Together these measures are intended to ensure a more stable and controlled
environment for the biofilm to grow in. It is important to remove air bubbles from the system as they
can disrupt the biofilm architecture. Often the air bubbles come as a consequence of temperature
changes, pressure changes due to the different sized tubing and from the pump itself [31].
The pump used in this experiment was a multichannel peristaltic flow rate pump from Shengen. The
effluent flask was a 1000 mL erlendmeyer flask, where all the tubes were fixed and the used medium
could be collected.
Before the inoculation of the flow cells, the system was tested at different flow rates ranging from
1ôÙ100 rpm by running medium through. The flow cells and tubing was checked for possible leakage.
If the flow cells showed leakage then silicone was used to cover the spot. If the leakage was too
substantial, then the flow cells were required to be reôÙassembled with a new cover slip and silicone.
The flow was stopped and the tubing between the flow cell and inlet was clamped off. The flow cells
were inoculated by filling a 1mL syringe with 0.15 mL of the active 10% AD bacterial culture. The
syringe needle was inserted into the tubing close to the flow cell inlet and the bacterial culture was
injected into the channel. After inoculation the injection hole in the tubing was covered with silicone.
Following the flow was reapplied for 10 seconds to make sure the bacterial cells reached the flow
cells. Then to let the bacterial cells establish on the glass coverslip they were inverted with the glass
coverslip facing down and incubated for 24h at 37CãÎ. After 24 h the flow was put on to 2rpm for 1h.

EPS Characterization in Mixed Cultures
To gain more insight into the components in the extracellular polymeric substances both the loosely
bound and tightly bound EPS were investigated and the protein, polysaccharide, and DNA content was
quantified. The EPS samples were taken on day 8 for the first growth profile experiment. The quantities
of proteins, polysaccharides and DNA are showed separately in figure 4.6, where the distribution
across LBôÙEPS and TBôÙEPS is also displayed. The amount of proteins, polysaccharides and DNA are
also showed together for LBôÙEPS ans TBôÙEPS in 4.5.
The distribution of LBôÙEPS and TBôÙEPS can vary, both for different bacterial communities and it can
depend on the different extraction treatments[36]. For this experiment the overall amount of LBôÙEPS
is higher compared to the TBôÙEPS (fig 4.5, this might be due to the method used or that the quantaties
of TBôÙEPS were in fact smaller. It is possible that more TBôÙEPS could have been extracted with a
different method.
The amount of protein in LBôÙEPS and TBôÙEPS are in this case very similar, and the same can be said
for the polysaccharides. For the DNA, the LBôÙEPS contained more when compared to TBôÙEPS.
The quantities of polysaccharides in LBôÙEPS and TBôÙEPS are substantially lower than both the protein
and DNA content. It was expected that the protein content would be higher than the polysaccharide
content, as this is what is usually found in literature as well [36]. However, the high DNA content
was a surprising finding. The DNA content is higher for LBôÙEPS than TBôÙEPS, this can be seen in
figure 4.6 C. In figure 4.5 A and B, it is clear that the DNA content is higher than that of proteins and
polysaccharides. The high DNA content could be due to cell death, but then the protein content would
be expected to match the high DNA content a bit closer. To investigate this further the EPS content
would need to be quantified again in a new experiment.
Given the absence of a oneôÙsizeôÙfitsôÙall approach to EPS isolation, tailoring the extraction method
to the specific biofilm being studied is essential. Techniques such as centrifugation, filtration, heatôÙ
ing, blending, sonication, and the use of complexing agents and ion exchanger resins have all been
documented as part of the EPS extraction process [37].
In general, the process of identifying extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) components is closely
tied to the method of isolation employed [37]. Isolating EPS efficiently poses a significant challenge,
especially when dealing with biofilms that harbour many species, where each contributes to a unique
EPS composition. The EPS mixture remains in the matrix even after the original EPSôÙproducing
species have perished or exited the biofilm [37].
It is nearly impossible to achieve a quantitative isolation of EPS from any biofilm. This is because a
portion of the EPS fraction remains attached to the bacterial cells, and the isolation process itself can
harm the cells, leading to the release of intracellular substances into the matrix [37].
To get a better understanding of the EPS composition in relation to biofilm growth over time it is relevant
to in the future take EPS samples on each day of an experiment of this character.

Flow Cells ôÙ Confocal Microscopy
After finishing investigating the results from the two anaerobic digestion biofilm growth experiments
which ran over 8 days a flow cell system was set up. Running an anaerobic flowôÙcell system is chalôÙ
lenging in terms of keeping oxygen out of the system
This setôÙup was run with the same AD culture as in the biofilm growth experiments. Here, the AHLs
were supplied continuously, in this way it was possible to control the concentration of the AHLs better
in the medium. This is controlling the actual exposure of the cells to AHL concentration over the
threshold. For this reason, it is much more likely to get sustained effects on biofilm growth over time.
This was a key difference in comparison to the previous experiments on batch microplates, where one
of the main limitations was the control the AHL concentration.
Two rounds were run with the setôÙup, however in the first setôÙup there was several issues with leaks
from the flow cells, making it difficult to keep the system anaerobic. For this reason it was also chalôÙ
lenging to stain and sample the flow cells, which resulted in the decision to reôÙstart the experiment.
With the knowledge from the first run in terms of flow rate, risk of leakage, and the sampling method,
a second experiment was set up.
C6 and C10 were chosen to be investigated in the flow cell setôÙup because these showed the highest
biofilm growth in the first experiment with support materials and the first biofilm growth experiment
over 8 days. The concentration of C6 and C10 was kept at 10 ôçg/L, since throughout the previous
experiments it was also this concentration which was the most promising. The setôÙup was run with
three flow cells, consisting of the control, C6, and C10, each having three channels that were set as
replicates.
In literature it has been reported that adding high concentrations of exogenous AHLs does not necôÙ
essarily enhance the bacterial adhesion [38], this backed up the choice of keeping the concentration
midôÙrange.
The samples of the biofilms in the flow cells were taken from the middle of the channels. This was to
avoid sampling too close to the inlet where the medium flows in or too close to the outlet. The reason
for this is that the closer to the outlet the lower the concentration of nutrients and carbon sources
that the cells experience is. On the other hand, the cells close to the inlet have better access to high
concentration of nutrients and carbon source, so the medium flowing closer to the outlet is depleted
of the nutrients and substrates resulting in fewer cells.

control test and C10. On day 1 the control shows a bit higher amount of biofilm formed compared to
C10. Following, on day 3, C10 shows approximately double the amount of biofilm formed. On day
7 the amount of biofilm for C10 is substantially higher than that of the control. The C10 AHL clearly
affected the biofilm formation rate over the seven days of the experiment.

In addition to investigating the optimal AHL concentration in terms of enhancing biofilm growth, the
response time of bacteria to AHL should also be taken into account [38]. For future experiments with
the flow cell setôÙup the response time can for example be defined as the time required for the biofilm
to be substantially higher for the experimental groups compared to the control group. In this case, the
experimental groups would be the different AHL molecules. From the literature it has been described
that the AHLs showing the best effect on biomass adhesion are not necessarily the same ones showing
the most rapid response time in terms of promoting adhesion [38]. Since quorum sensing is a cellôÙ
dependent mechanism, it is activated when the extracellular concentration of AHLs reach the threshold
[38]. Adding AHLs can in this way reduce the time required to reach the specific concentration value
for the autoôÙinducing process and EPS production, hence, speeding up the whole adhesion process
[39].
In the microplates and the flow cells, this theory can also be applied and possibly show the same effect.
However, it is first maybe more important to investigate additional AHL molecules in the flow cell setôÙup
to get a better sense on which ones help increase biomass the most. The monitoring and sampling
of the flow cells are timeôÙconsuming in itself even with three flow cells running simultaneously. If one
wants to test a higher number of AHLs in the same experiment it will require a lot of time, thus, it is
possibly better to rather run the identical experiment again with different AHLs.
To better grasp the differences in QS response time and the different AHLs effect on biomass producôÙ
tion it is essential to investigate the bacterial community in the particular experiment. Some bacteria
might have a negative response to a specific AHL, while others show a positive response in terms
of response time and promoting biofilm formation. If some bacteria are found to have a very specific
preference to particular AHL molecules it may be beneficial to add these to the community rather than
a mix of different ones.
Quorum sensing is generally used to describe cooperative behaviours in biofilms. Even though the
bacteria in the biofilm community can respond together in response to signalling molecules, they
also act as individuals [40]. In young biofilms of Pseudomonas putida it has been shown that the
production of AHLs by single bacteria does not follow a predictable pattern, however, it varies on
random from one bacterium to the next at any given time [40]. This stochastic nature of AHL production
contributes to phenotypic heterogeneity within the microbial community, particularly in the early stages
of biofilm development. Such randomness in signal production can lead to a variety of behaviors
among the cells, with some producing AHLs and others not, at different times [40]. This variability plays
a crucial role in how these communities regulate behaviors that are not coordinated or cooperative but
are instead selfôÙregulatory or even asocial, as the induced behaviors are directed towards individual
benefits rather than the collective [40]. In the future, it could be interesting to study this more in young
biofilm, although in a mixed culture, it would be more difficult compared to in a pure culture.

here. The same syngas inoculum can be used, and the effect of AHLs on the mixed syngas culture
can then also be observed.

Conclusions
The findings of this investigation can contribute to a broader understanding of quorum sensing and
biofilm dynamics, offering potential implications for controlling and leveraging biofilms in various apôÙ
plications. Insights from these discoveries can be used to help design different methods for analysing
biofilms, and in that way, in the future, provide a clearer picture of the intricate world of biofilms.
The exploration of biofilm growth in microtiter plates, despite yielding small trends in the first longôÙterm
experiments, set a foundation for the shortôÙterm growth experiments and the flowôÙcell experiments.
This emphasizes the importance of the cultivation and quantification protocols, which are key for obôÙ
taining reliable results.
In the end there was clear differences between the thickness and complexity of the biofilm formation
for the control and the C10 AHL in the flow cell experiment. This encourages a broader investigation
into the nuanced aspects of microbial life of biofilms and how AHLs as quorum sensing molecules can
enhance biofilm formation.

Future perspectives
For future experiments it will be relevant to investigate the bacterial community in more depth, both beôÙ
fore inoculation and after sampling. This could create a better understanding of the appropriate AHLs
required for various biofilms. Different DNA/RNA analysing methods could be applied for this purpose.
The findings in the biofilm growth experiments and flow cell experiment performed here emphasize the
importance of refining experimental designs and analytical methods to capture the subtle dynamics
of biofilm communities. Future investigations may benefit from including more detection techniques
in addition to microtiter plate assay and confocal laser scanning microscopy. For instance, DNA/RNA
sequencing, fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH)ôÙstaining and liquid chromatographyôÙmass specôÙ
trometry (LCãMS).
An interesting aspect to explore further is also the response time of different AHLs on bacteria, for
when the concentration threshhold is reached. In terms of the flowôÙcell experiments a next step could
be to experiment with different support materials, to further investigate the relevance of hydrophobic
and hydrophilic materials. In addition, the culture using syngas as a substrate would also be interesting
to perform experiments with in the flowôÙcell setôÙup.

A.1.1
Protocol for Quantitative Analysis of Biomolecules in EPS Samples
The objective of this protocol is to quantify proteins, polysaccharides, and DNA in EPS (Extracellular
Polymeric Substances) samples using colorimetric assays.
Proteins: Add 100ö¥L of EPS sample and 100ö¥L of Bradford reagent (from a Modified Bradford Protein
Assay Kit)into each well of a 96ôÙwell plate. Mix and shake the solution well at room temperature. Allow
to stand in room temperature for 10 minutes. Measure the absorbance at 595 nm using a microplate
reader. Generate a standard curve using BSA to calculate sample concentrations. Keep the ratio of
the sample in question and the BSA at 1:1.
Polysaccharide Quantification: Combine 50ö¥L of EPS sample, 25ö¥L of 6% phenol, and 125ö¥L of
98% sulfuric acid in each well of a 96ôÙwell plate. Mix and shake the well contents. Incubate in an oven
at 90CãÎfor 15 minutes. Allow the microplate to cool to room temperature. Measure the absorbance at
490 nm using a microplate reader. Use glucose to create a standard curve for sample concentration
calculations. Keep the volume of the sample, phenol and sulfuric acid at the ratio 2:1:5.
DNA Quantification To each well of a 96ôÙwell microplate, add 100ö¥L of EPS sample, 200ö¥L of
diphenylamine reagent, and 1ö¥L of acetaldehyde solution. Mix and shake the well contents. Incubate
at 60CãÎfor 1 hour. Allow the solution to cool to room temperature. Measure the absorbance at 595
nm using a microplate reader. Employ calf thymus DNA to establish a standard curve for determining
sample concentrations. Keep the volume of the sample, diphenylalanine reagent and acetaldehyde
solution at the ratio 100:200:1.
