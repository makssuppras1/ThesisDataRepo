Abstract 
 
 
Interest in behavioural design research has significantly increased over the last years 
because of its potential in positively changing society throughout design.  At the same 
time, analogical reasoning is considered a relevant research area within design because 
thanks to analogies, designers transfer knowledge across domains to solve problems, 
deal with uncertainty and generate new ideas. Although academic research has shown 
that analogical reasoning is important in traditional design, there is a lack of 
understanding about the roles of analogical reasoning in behavioural design.     
 
This thesis explores how analogical reasoning is being used in behavioural design 
projects. To conduct the research, 14 behavioural design practitioners located in  
multiple countries were interviewed.  Results reveal that analogical reasoning plays an 
important role in behavioural design. The study research suggests that analogies have 
multiple purposes and they are used in synthesis, analysis and simulation activities. 
Furthermore, they are relevant  to reduce behavioural uncertainty in projects, as well as 
relational uncertainty when communicating with clients. Also, when using analogies, 
behavioural design practitioners have experienced different effects related to the design 
outcome, people’s cognition and people’s affect.   
 
Key words:  Analogy, analogical reasoning, behavioural design, uncertainty, thematic 
analysis.

1 Introduction 
 
Behavioural design is an approach of significant interest in research and industry. Its 
importance is increasing thanks to its potential of making a positive impact in society by 
understanding human behaviour. This approach is more complex than traditional design 
and still very emergent (Nielsen et al., 2018). Thus, there are still challenges when 
behavioural design comes to practice and many possible research areas within this topic.    
 
Analogical reasoning is a cognitive process that has demonstrated to be very relevant 
for design and product development. Analogies are used often by designers throughout 
the design process, and they help with problem-solving, understanding and managing 
complexity related to the process (Chan et al., 2012). Furthermore, analogical reasoning 
is important in creative tasks. In product design, originality of ideas is influenced by 
analogical thinking (Dahl and Moreau, 2002). 
 
Little is known about analogical reasoning in behavioural design. Therefore, this could 
be an excellent opportunity for new research within the field.  
 
 
1.1   Aim of the project 
 
The project aims to investigate how analogical reasoning is being used in behavioural 
design and its relation with activities and challenges in this field. Exploratory qualitative 
research will be conducted to gain insights into the use of analogies within the context 
of behavioural design processes.

2 Literature Study 
 
2.1 Behavioural Design 
 
Behavioural design is an emergent approach that focuses on changing human behaviour 
through design. Behavioural designers develop interventions to replace current 
undesired behaviour with desired behaviour (Cash, Hartlev and Durazo, 2017). 
Interventions can be presented as a product, process, system or combination of them. 
They are designed based on understanding people's behaviour; by doing so, it allows us 
to change a specific behaviour pattern.  
 
Behavioural design is considered a multidisciplinary approach, in which different 
backgrounds are combined to develop interventions successfully.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 1. Branches of knowledge in Behavioural Design (Wendel, 2017) 
 
 
Wendel (2017) explains that in order to design for behaviour change, it is required 
expertise from 3 main branches:  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
First, behavioural economics and psychology play an important role to understand the 
mind; therefore, how does human behaviour work. Secondly, design knowledge within 
interaction design and user experience is needed to implement the intervention. Design 
expertise in behavioural design is rooted in human centred-design: " An approach that 
puts human needs, capabilities, and behaviour first, then designs to accommodate those 
needs, capabilities, and ways of behaving ” (Norman, 1976). Norman argues that good 
design starts with a good comprehension of both technology and psychology. The last 
branch of knowledge is data analysis, which is essential to support behavioural science 
research.   
 
Behavioural design has an emphasis in understanding behaviour and how to change it.    
There are many theories and frameworks concerning human behaviour that enables its 
understanding. They are used in behavioural design as considerations during the process 
and theoretical foundation for the approach. Some of the most common behaviour 
concepts used in behavioural design are explained in this chapter's following sessions.  
 
 
 
Dual-process theory  
 
Dual-process theory elaborates on how human thinking process functions. In this 
theory, the mind is represented by 2 different neurological systems, and both determine 
our reasoning and behaviour. They are popularly known as System 1 and System 2. The 
following table  (Evans and Stanovich, 2013) shows and compare the main 
characteristics that are often correlated to each system:

The system 1 represents our intuitive mind, reasoning, and decision-making are 
instinctive and based on previous experiences, senses and basic emotions. System 1 
works by generating associations, and it is very good at recognising complex patterns. 
The thinking process is automatic and unconscious; consequently, humans are not self-
aware of the steps that involve reasoning in System 1, either the underlying reasons for 
behaviours driven by it.   
 
A clear example of System 1 process is when we recognise face expressions (Kahneman, 
2011), we automatically can identify if a person looks angry without effort. This instant 
and intuitive recognition is being done by System 1. Another example is when we do 
brainstorming and suddenly ideas pop-ups in our mind, the system 1 is generating 
associations between related concepts with the topic to ideate.  
 
System 1 also can be seen when we have learned a skill such as playing guitar. It gets to 
a point where it becomes so familiar to play the instrument that it is not required for us 
to think about where to place the fingers, the thinking is done unconsciously, thanks to 
our previous experience (System 1).   
 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
The system 2 represents our reflective mind. It corresponds to our conscious thinking; 
therefore, we identify ourselves with this system. System 2 is associated with deliberate 
actions, decisions and choices. Its decision-making process is more rational, systematic 
and rule-following, requiring mental effort and use of working memory (Kahneman, 
2011). Solving a math problem, making predictions, evaluating circumstances, thinking 
retrospectively and willpower are examples of activities attributed to System 2.  
 
Systems 1 and 2 are both active whenever we are awake. System 1 runs automatically, 
and System 2 is normally in a comfortable low-effort mode, in which only a fraction of 
its capacity is engaged. System 1 continuously generates suggestions for System 2: 
impressions, intuitions, intentions, and feelings. If endorsed by System 2, impressions 
and intuitions turn into beliefs, and impulses turn into voluntary actions. 
 
D. Kahneman describes how System 1 and System 2 interact. System 1 operates 
automatically following and generating complex patterns, whereas System 2 constantly 
gives attention to mental activities that are demanded at the time. System 1 can suggest 
impressions and feelings to System 2, which it can consider those suggestions to form 
beliefs or conscious actions (Kahneman, 2011). It is generally accepted that most of our 
behaviour is driven by System 1. This is because the working capacity of System 2 is 
limited; hence many of the choices we make are guided by the operations of System 1 
(Kahneman, 2011; Lehner, Mont and Heiskanen, 2016; Wendel, 2017).  
 
The two systems are always active, and they can operate independently of each other 
or work in parallel (Kahneman, 2011; Wendel, 2017). Also, there are moments of conflict 
between System 1  and System 2  (Kahneman, 2011). For example, when we drive from 
our working office planning to visit an unusual destination, we take the route home by 
mistake.  
  
An important consideration in Dual-process theory, clarified by Evans and Stanovich 
(2013), is that characteristics often associated with System 1 and System 2  (Table 1) are 
correlated attributes, but not defining attributes. Indeed, System 2 is not always in 
control of behaviour, and either conscious thoughts are only abstract, they can be 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
contextualised too. System 1 is not always responsible for bias reasoning. Furthermore, 
fast processing does not always correspond to System 1.  Due to overstatement of the 
theory over the last years, dual-process theory has received some critiques in 
psychological science arguing that System 1 and System 2 constitute a vague and 
misaligned categorisation (Keren and Schul, 2009).  
 
Based on Evans and Stanovich (2013), the only proven characteristic that defines System 
1 is autonomous processing. At the same time, what defines System 2 is using working 
memory and maintaining mental representations, allowing hypothetical thinking.   
 
 
Nudge theory 
 
Nudge is a concept that comes from behavioural economics proposed by Sunstein and 
Thaler (2008) and has become very popular in behavioural design practices. The theory 
explains that people's decisions are influenced by how their context is organised. 
Nudging consist of changing the user's environment so that it implicitly suggests making 
a certain choice. A nudge is defined as:  
 
"Any aspect of the choice architecture that predictably alters people's behaviour without 
forbidding any options or significantly changing their economic incentives" (Sunstein and 
Thaler, 2008).   
 
Choice architecture refers to how the context in which people have choices is organised. 
For example, if we place a chocolate bar in front of customers' eyes while waiting at the 
cashier, we design the architecture choice not to force, but influencing their behaviour 
(in this case, to decide buying chocolate). A particular characteristic of Nudge is that it 
does not constrain freedom of choice; for instance, the person at the cashier queue can 
decide whether buying the chocolate or not.  
The way people choose is influenced by using Nudge by subtly suggesting a starting point 
in the thought process. Depending on the decision-making process's starting point, the 
choice might be different (Sunstein and Thaler, 2008).  
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
 
Nudge is grounded on dual-process theory, and its application focuses on targeting 
System 1 to change people's decisions driven by unconscious thinking. Humans often 
have systematic errors when making decisions, which could be due to lack of attention, 
simplification of decision-making by using the rule of thumb and other cognitive 
limitations (Sunstein and Thaler, 2008). Also in many cases, people know the outcome 
we want with our behaviour, but our cognitive limitations hinder our direction. A clear 
example is when we want to avoid irresponsible consumption, but we end up not 
behaving to reach the goal due to the circumstances. Nudging is often done to help 
people make good choices and behave in the desired way by giving guidance throughout 
the decision-making process. It is a helpful way to help people with complexity in 
decision-making (Sunstein and Thaler, 2008).

Fogg’s Behaviour Model 
 
Fogg's Behaviour Model is one of the most popular behavioural science models, 
published by B.J Fogg in 2009. As opposed to nudging, Fogg's behaviour model mostly 
focus on behaviour change persuasive strategies instead of seductive. The model 
represents human behaviour based on 3 factors: Motivation, ability and triggers.   
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Fogg’s Behaviour Model (B. Fogg, 2009) 
 
 
As can be seen in the Figure 2, Fogg's Behaviour Model is represented with 2 axes. The 
vertical axis stands for the motivation factor, whereas the horizontal axis is for ability. 
Motivation refers to the number of incentives and reasons to perform the desired 
behaviour, whereas ability means how easy it is for the person to perform the 
behaviour. Both motivation and ability are necessary to make the target behaviour 
happen.  The last factor in the graph is triggers, which are essential because there is no 
call to action without them. A trigger needs to be effective in order to make the 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
behaviour happen. At the same time, a trigger only becomes successful when the person 
has enough motivation and ability.  
 
Sometimes there may be a trade-off between motivation and ability (B. Fogg, 2009). For 
example, if the ability to pass a videogame is reduced, motivation increases because the 
challenge becomes exciting for the gamer. The Fogg Behaviour Model helps behavioural 
design practitioners to analyse and understand human behaviour in a simple way. It is 
being used to work in teams during behaviour change projects of many domains since it 
can give insights related to the target behaviour (Wendel, 2017).

2.2 Behavioural Design Process  
 
 
Behavioural design involves a complex process. Practitioners need to understand human 
behaviour within a specific context, where contextual and inherent factors may have 
effects in the results (Khadilkar, Ratnakar and Cash, 2019). The Organisation for 
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) has presented the BASIC framework, 
which simplifies the process into 5 generic steps and serves as guidance for practitioners 
(Hansen, 2019). The BASIC framework is a simple way to illustrate a behavioural design 
process. It presents the following steps:  
. 
• Behaviour: Explore the problem and identify the target behaviour 
• Analysis: Investigate the behaviour by using behavioural science approaches 
• Strategy: Conceptualisation of the solution 
• Intervention: Develop and testing interventions to evaluate the solution 
• Change: Implementation and further evaluation and continuity of the solution.   
 
 
Figure 3. BASIC framework (Hansen, 2019) 
 
In addition, other authors have also proposed behavioural design process models, such 
as The Eight-steps design process (B. J. Fogg, 2009), The Behavioural Design Process 
(Cash, Hartlev and Durazo, 2017) and Behaviour Change Process (Wendel, 2017).  
 
Behavioural design can be understood from a design process perspective (Khadilkar et 
al,. 2019). Previous research has related behavioural design processes with the 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
traditional design process. As shown in the literature, behavioural design follows a 
design process that shares the same generic flow as traditional design processes. It 
starts with a customer need and a problem to be investigated (in this case, a behaviour 
problem). The investigation leads to problem definition and design specifications. Then, 
there is a concept development stage, concept solutions are tested and develop 
iteratively, and finally, results are implemented (Cash, Hartlev and Durazo, 2017).  
 
In this thesis, the behavioural design process is viewed as a design process, which main 
differential characteristic from traditional design, is that activities in the process involve 
behavioural aspects. This is because besides the overall design problem, a target 
behaviour comes with the problem need (Cash et al., 2017). The activities done by 
behavioural design practitioners can be better understood based on a representation of 
the behavioural design process used by Khadilkar et al. (2019), in which activities are 
structured so that they align with prior design process models. The generic activities in 
behavioural design are presented as the following (Khadilkar et al., 2019):  
 
• Analysis: This activity involves defining the behavioural aspects for the 
intervention, such as technical, psychological, economic and cultural. This 
activity's tasks include determining the overall design problem, behaviour, 
person and defining the overall solution specifications and target behaviour.  
 
• Synthesis: Ideation of possible solutions to the behaviour problem defined in the 
analysis. This includes ideating the target behaviour, context and intervention.  
 
• Simulation: Knowledge, experiences and theories are used to predict how 
possible solutions may work while developing prototypes and models of the 
possible solutions. The individual's response to the target behaviour and the 
new context after the possible solutions are simulated.  
 
• Evaluation: This activity consists of testing and evaluating prototypes and 
models created in the simulation, comparing them with the expected outcome.  
 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
Dealing with human behaviour implies more challenges in design activities compared to 
traditional design. Elaborated and rigorous research relied on behaviour theories is 
required to analyse and understand the complexity of the behavioural problem (Cash et 
al., 2017). Furthermore, the complexity is extended to synthesis activities, making the 
design solution space more challenging to explore (Nielsen et al., 2018). Human 
behaviour has high uncertainty due to the constant changing factors that may affect it 
(Cziko, 1989). In addition, behaviour is innate to each individual; therefore the ability to 
simulate, evaluate and measure behaviour factors and outcome is hindered (Khadilkar 
et al., 2019). In conclusion, the uncertainty that involves behaviour supposes challenges 
in all process activities.

2.3 Analogical reasoning 
 
Generally speaking, an analogy is a comparison between things, in which recognised 
similarities provide explanation or new meaning (Cambridge Dictionary, 2020).  
 
Based on Vosniadou (2009), analogies “involve the identification and transfer of an 
explanatory structure from a known system (the source) to a new and relatively unknown 
system (the target) “. The cognitive process that has this ability to access knowledge by 
mapping similarities between source and target, therefore forming analogies, is called 
analogical reasoning or thinking.  
 
It has been recognised that analogical reasoning is important in human cognition, 
especially to acquire new knowledge. This is because our brain learns by association; 
humans come to new discoveries and understanding by building from what they know 
already (Oppenheimer, 1956). People use analogies to associate new situations and 
concepts with what they have learned before.  
 
In the same vein, analogical reasoning is relevant when designing. It enables designers 
to transfer structures and elements from a base of knowledge (source) to solve a design 
problem (target) (Vosniadou, 2009; Christensen and Ball, 2016). Analogies frequently 
appear along the design process; they can occur when identifying problems, generating 
solutions and explaining concepts (Christensen and Schunn, 2007). Studies have shown 
that analogies contribute to problem-solving; for instance, they are used to understand 
problems, as well as interpret data and deal with process complexity (Chan, Paletz and 
Schunn, 2012). Furthermore, evidence shows a high presence of analogies during co-
evolution, suggesting that they might have influence in problem and solution 
exploration (Wiltschnig et al., 2013).   
 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
Analogical reasoning is associated with uncertainty reduction in design problems. 
Different studies have revealed that while designers were working on a design task, 
uncertainty went to baseline levels immediately after analogical reasoning (Ball and 
Christensen, 2009; Chan, Paletz and Schunn, 2012).  
 
In addition, analogies have also been strongly associated with creative problem-solving. 
Analogical reasoning has been considered by researchers as the mechanism used in 
creative tasks, that generates associations and transfers knowledge between different 
systems to generate new ideas (Goel, 1997; Dahl, 2002; Daugherty et al., 2008). Previous 
research in creativity shows that analogies play an important role in the creative 
cognitive process. In product design, originality of ideas is influenced by analogical 
thinking (Dahl and Moreau, 2002). Furthermore, the usage analogies as external stimuli 
are widely used as a method to change the perception and expand the solution space 
(De Bono, 1970).   
 
 
2.3.1 Analogy characteristics 
 
In this session, we review how analogies and their properties have commonly been 
categorised in prior research. 
 
Distance 
 
As mentioned before, analogies involve transferring information structure from a source 
to a target system and mapping similarities (Vosniadou, 2009). Analogies can be 
classified based on the analogical distance between the source and target domain (Ball 
and Christensen, 2009). The most common classification based on analogical distance 
divides analogies into two types:  
 
- 
Within-domain analogy: The target and the source are in the same 
domain. This could be, for example, comparing a pencil with a pen, both 
objects share the same conceptual domain. In design for instance, if 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
designers search for examples of chairs to design their own chair, that 
would be a within-domain analogy. They are transferring knowledge 
from the same context to develop a solution.  
 
- 
Between-domain analogy: The target and the source are in different 
domains. A clear example of this type is the comparison between the 
solar system and an atom; although they are from different domains, 
they share similar structure because a nucleus and elements are moving 
around it (Chan, Paletz and Schunn, 2012).  
 
In the same vein, Dunbar (1997) has categorised analogies used by biologists based on 
analogical distance, referring it as the range of analogy: "Range is an index of how far 
apart the base and target were for each analogy” (Dunbar, 1997). In this case study, 
analogies classification was elaborated specifically for biology observations, coded as 
“Within organism”, “Other Organism” and “Non-Biological”.  
 
The analogical distance is a characteristic that has been investigated concerning 
creativity.  Studies reveal that analogies with more analogical distance, for instance, 
between-domain analogies, are strongly related to originality and innovation of the 
concepts ideated (Dahl and Moreau, 2002).  On the other hand, analogising within the 
same domains is easier to retrieve and find the usefulness in analogies.  However, it is 
also more likely to appear fixation when using within-domain analogies, therefore being 
low in originality (Christensen and Ball, 2016).  Furthermore, Ahmed et al. (2009) show 
that analogies within-domain hinders originality in expert and novice designers. Also, 
within-domain analogies increase usefulness in experts but not in novice. This suggests 
that novice are less capable of recognising structure explanation compared to experts 
(Ahmed and Christensen, 2009); instead, they focus on surface similarities that analogies 
within-domain have.  
 
Both types of analogies, between-domain and within-domain appear for solving 
problems, for instance, certain studies indicate that their frequency of use is relatively 
equal for that purpose (Christensen and Schunn, 2007). Nevertheless, other studies 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
have shown that analogies between-domain appear more, suggesting that the 
frequency of analogies between-domain and within-domain may variate depending on 
the design sub-discipline and industry (Ball and Christensen, 2009). 
 
 
Similarity 
 
In an analogy, different types of similarity between the source and the target can be 
identified:  
 
- 
Structure similarity: The mapped feature is relevant to achieve the 
purpose of the analogy. Structure similarities in analogical properties are 
relational, abstract and conceptual (Vosniadou, 2009). Having structure 
similarity between source and target means that there are similitudes in 
terms of how their elements are arranged and related. Indeed, if there is 
a structural difference between source and target, it changes the causal 
relations of them (Koh and Holyoak, 1987), therefore the analogy can 
become weaker or less useful.  
 
- 
Surface similarity: The mapped feature is not relevant to achieve the 
analogy's purpose; thus, an alteration in the feature does not influence 
the analogy goal (Koh and Holyoak, 1987). Examples of surface 
similarities can be, as Vosniadou (2009) relates,  “…any non-analogy-
related similarity in simple, descriptive properties of objects, like shape, 
colour, size, names, profession, workplace of story characters, kinds of 
animals, etc.”. Surface similarity is referred to either similitude in 
properties that can be perceived through senses or can be described as 
object attributes (Vosniadou, 2009).  
 
 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
In addition to these two types of similarities,  Vosniadou (2009) proposes using the term 
“salient similarity" when a similarity either structure or surface, is easily recognised by 
the person. Based on the salient similarities,  the person can map more properties and 
build an analogy that can be used to generate new knowledge, ideas, and understanding 
of the target system. How easy it is to recognise similarities depends on the person's 
underlying representations (Vosniadou, 2009); in other words, mental models (See 2.3.2 
Models). However, it has also been argued that generally, surface similarities are more 
likely to be recognised (Gentner, 1989). This is because surface properties are perpetual, 
therefore they may be more familiar to people than abstract properties.  
 
 
Purpose  
 
Analogies have also been categorised based on their purpose when they are being used. 
Previous research in design has classified the use of analogies based on cognitive 
functions during design ideation sessions (Ball et al, 2004; Christensen et al., 2007). The 
different type of functions are:   
 
- 
Explanation: Knowledge from the source is used to explain some aspect 
of the target, providing understanding to other team members. 
Analogies as an explanation or illustration appear in design activities.  
 
- 
Problem identification: The analogy is used to identify a possible issue in 
the current design proposals and concepts, assessing whether the 
solution might work or not based on sources mostly from previous 
experiences.    
 
- 
Problem-solving:  The analogy is used to solve problems. Knowledge from 
the source is transferred to bring a solution to the target problem.   
 
- 
Function-finding: This last type is suggested by Ball and Christensen 
(2009) after realising that some analogies are not aimed to solve the 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
design problem like the problem-solving type directly. Although instead, 
they are used for creative exploration. Designers also generate analogies 
with the goal of exploring novel ways to function and change perspective 
during the ideation.   
 
 
 
 
2.3.2 Models 
 
A model is generally defined in science education as "a representation of an idea, object, 
event, process or system"(Hug, 2003; Koll, France and Taylor, 2005). Mental models are 
models in our mind, they are cognitive representations that simplify and shape our 
understanding of the real world (Farnam Stree, 2020; Norman, 1976). They are also 
viewed as the way we structure knowledge in our head about the world. The relational 
properties of a mental model are analogous because they correspond to the real world 
elements; for this reason, mental models have also been accepted as analogies or 
analogous structures (Landriscina, 2015, p. 4).  
 
In the same vein, models are widely recognised to be strongly related to analogical 
reasoning. The relationship between them has been explained with the diagram 
presented by Duit and Glynn (1996):   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 4. Diagram of analogical model (Duit and Glynn, 1996) 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
 
As can be seen in the Figure 4, the source (analogue) is compared with the target system 
forming an analogy. Based on Duit (1996), the model (Rm) is the conceptual 
representation of the mapped properties between source and target, and it has been 
built after forming the analogy. This explanation matches the point of view of Collins 
and Gentner (2012), who emphasise that people use analogies to build mental models. 
The mental model, generated by analogies, allows keeping an abstract understanding of 
the target in our mind. At the same time, Duit (1996) remarks that the source (analogue) 
is often defined as a model. This happens because the person might already have a 
model that represents the source domain. Hence, that model is being used for making 
a comparison with the target. In this case, the model could be seen as the channel to 
reach knowledge from the source domain.  
 
Summing up, models are created thanks to analogical reasoning, but even more, they 
are also used for analogical reasoning as representations of source domains. For 
instance, managers tend to face new situations based on knowledge obtained from 
mental models that have been already preconstructed based on experiences and 
learnings at work (Gary, Wood and Pillinge, 2012). This transferring knowledge example 
points out that the mental model represents the source, from where knowledge is 
transferred to the target system to bring meaning.  
 
An important characteristic of mental models is that they are dynamic (Gentner, 1989). 
This means that mental models are in constant change and they can evolve, they get 
updated when new analogies are drawn and information is added. This dynamism also 
allows using mental models to make predictions in the target system and imagine how 
it will work depending on diverse situations (Gentner, 1989; Collins and Gentner, 2012). 
This cognitive process is called mental simulation, and it is used in design to estimate 
the cause-effects of new ideas generated, concepts or events (Ball and Christensen, 
2009).    
 
Mental models are shared among people to teach knowledge and also reach a common 
understanding (Davison and Blackman, 2005).  In order to do so, a mental model can be 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
visually illustrated so that they become a conceptual model, in other words, an external 
representation that can be shown to people (Gentner, 1989). These models can be 
presented in the form of graphic diagrams, frameworks, tables, sketches, physical 
models etc. There are already visual techniques to develop conceptual models; for 
example, designers use mind mapping (Malycha, 2017) to illustrate their mental models 
and organise the different elements that comprise it. In addition, conceptual models are 
popular in science to teach others complex theories, systems and phenomenon. 
Conceptual models in science are backed up by theories that are accepted among the 
scientific community (Gentner, 1989). Previously in the 2.1 Behavioural Design chapter, 
some behavioural science models have been shown, such as System 1 and System 2 or 
Fogg’s behaviour model. Other popular models in science are the conceptual 
representation of the atom, electric circuit diagram or structural formula of chemical 
compounds.  
 
According to Norman (1976), models can be incomplete or not very accurate, but this is 
not relevant as long as the model is useful for the context in which is being applied.  
However, it has been argued that sometimes models, if built based on weak analogies, 
can lead to a misinterpretation of reality. Based on Gary et al. (2012), the formation of 
a productive analogy depends on how evolved mental models of source domain are; 
better the understanding of the relational properties related to the source, better the 
analogy. Lastly, it is also argued that the more mental models that we learn, the more 
good associations we reach to understand reality from different points of view; thus 
increasing our capacity for problem-solving (Farnam Stree, 2020).

2.4 Aspects related to the role of analogical reasoning in 
design teams 
 
Previously in the introduction to analogical reasoning, it has been mentioned different 
ways in which analogical reasoning play an important role in design teams.  The aim of 
this session is to understand better certain aspects associated with analogical reasoning 
and that might be linked with the contribution that analogies give to behavioural design 
teams.  
 
First, analogical reasoning has been related many times in the literature with creativity 
(e.g. De Bono, 1970; Dahl and Moreau, 2002; Ball and Christensen, 2009), therefore 
creativity is an aspect to be looked at. In addition, uncertainty is another aspect of design 
that analogical reasoning has been proven to play an important role dealing with it (Ball 
and Christensen, 2009; Chan, Paletz and Schunn, 2012). Lastly, analogical reasoning as 
a cognitive process, it might be related to team’s cognition and other team emergent 
states that have influence on teamwork effectiveness.  
 
2.4.1 Creativity 
 
Creativity is defined as the "ability to reorganise knowledge, information, cues, facts to 
generate new and useful solutions" (Ma, 2009). Novelty plus usefulness are attributes 
that determine whether an idea is creative or not. Based on Bono (2015), creativity is 
about changing perception. When we solve problems, we tend to follow the most 
obvious pattern. Creativity appears when we move sideways across patterns instead of 
following linear thinking. The creativity model proposed by Bono (Figure 5) shows that 
creativity appears when suddenly we jump to a new pattern, which may not be directly 
related to our current problem-solution space. Then, we achieve to link it to the problem 
we were solving and make it useful. This means that creative ideas are logical in 
hindsight (Bono, 2015).   
 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5. Creativity model (Bono, 2015) 
 
The connections formed between the new pattern and the starting point is achieved 
thank to analogical reasoning, considered the core mechanism of creativity (Vasconcelos 
et al., 2017). A relevant factor in creativity is the external and internal stimuli that are 
present during the creative task. The stimuli are the sources of information available, 
they can provoke both inspiration and fixation:  
 
- 
Inspiration: Inspiration is considered the moment in which the designer 
feel mentally stimulated and creativity level is high. In this case, the 
stimulus generates a creative leap, jumping to an unusual pattern.  
 
- 
Fixation: Fixation occurs when designers only focus on certain ideas, 
concepts or aspects,  therefore limiting the problem-solution space and 
blocking creativity (Vasconcelos and Crilly, 2016). It has been proven that 
stimuli can also cause this effect in designers (Crilly, 2015). This can 
generate excessive adherence to previous knowledge and ideas, as well 
as a tendency to ideate based on familiar models

2.4.2 Uncertainty  
 
In uncertainty theory literature,  uncertainty is defined as “ having a number of possible 
alternative predictions or explanations” (Solomon, 2015). Uncertainty becomes high 
when there are many possibilities that explain or predict a situation. Uncertainty is 
reduced when we make sense of the situation and understand it better. Its reduction 
depends on the ability we have to predict or explain accurately (Solomon, 2015).  
 
There are different types of uncertainty that have been recognised by diverse authors. 
For instance, in innovation research, it can be observed technical, market, organisation 
and resource uncertainty (O’Connor and Rice, 2013). Also, there is research focused on 
environmental uncertainty, which generally covers the difficulties in knowing the 
outcome of the decisions due to external factors perceived in our surroundings 
(Milliken, 1987).  
 
The types of uncertainty that were found to be relevant in behavioural design are 
behavioural and relational uncertainty. Behavioural uncertainty is described as the 
“uncertainty associated with being able to predict or explain a person’s behaviour, or in 
knowing what behaviours are expected of us or predicting our own actions” (Solomon, 
2015). In behavioural design, practitioners need to understand other people's behaviour 
in every behaviour change intervention. Thus, dealing with behavioural uncertainty is 
part of the process in behavioural design projects. Relational uncertainty is about not 
knowing how would be future of a relationship with someone.  It is described as "the 
lack of confidence a person feels in his or her ability to predict or explain issues associated 
with a given relationship" (Solomon, 2015). Doubts that cause uncertainty in a 
relationship can be related to norms, cost and rewards, commitment, plans, objectives 
and defining the relationship (Solomon, 1999). Relational uncertainty may be seen, for 
example, when companies are building new relationships and collaborating with new 
clients and partners.

2.4.3 Teamwork effectiveness 
 
Previous research in team working processes explain that cognition is an important 
emergent state for team's effectiveness. It is associated with many benefits such as 
team's performance, communication and viability (Mohammed, S. and Hamilton, 2010).  
 
Cognition in teamwork research mainly refers to shared understanding among team 
members (Grossman, Sarit B. Friedman and Kalra, 2017). This allows people to have 
common knowledge and interpretation of the problem context, tasks and objectives. 
Shared understanding is achieved thanks to the interaction between people. During this 
interaction, common knowledge is obtained because people share their mental models.   
Besides sharing mental models, transactive memory systems are another component in 
shared understanding. They constitute the internal and external memory within the 
team, which allows to store and retrieve the shared knowledge (Grossman, Sarit B 
Friedman and Kalra, 2017). In short, cognition is responsible for sharing, retaining and 
making knowledge accessible in team members, allowing shared understanding and 
therefore contributing to team’s effectiveness.   
 
Another dimension in team emergent states, which is also of central importance for 
team's effectiveness, is affect. Affective emergent states refer to team's feelings and 
emotions, and they indicate relationships among team members. In teamwork research, 
the affective emergent states that have received more attention are cohesion, 
confidence and trust (Grossman, et al., 2017).  Team cohesion is about feeling part of 
the group, share the same interest, goals, and commitment. Casey-Campbell et al. 
(2009) explain that team cohesion involves mutual attraction and bonding among 
members, that is caused thanks to social and goal-oriented aspects of team's 
relationship. This allows teams to act as a united group. Having good cohesion means 
that the team is aligned and their members work well together. Confidence consists of 
team's feeling that goals and task can be achieved successfully; having high confidence 
is a team characteristic associated with high efficacy-performance (Grossman, et al., 
==PAGE:35==
 
35 
2017). Lastly, trust is recognised as critical to work effectively.  According to Mayer et al.  
(1995),  trust is defined as “willingness to be vulnerable to the actions of another party 
based on the expectation that the other will perform a particular action important to the 
trustor, irrespective of the ability to monitor or control that other party” (Mayer, Davis 
and Schoorman, 1995). Thus, team trust involves shared expectations among people, 
believing that it is possible to rely on each team member to solve crucial tasks and 
accepting vulnerability due to the responsibility of each individual.

3 Conceptual Framework 
 
According to the literature, behavioural design is an emergent approach that involves 
multiple disciplines, combining behavioural science and design. Its process is 
characterised by being complex because the design problem involves a target 
behaviour. Therefore, it required to manage and understand human behaviour in order 
to achieve developing a successful intervention. For this thesis, "behavioural design" is 
defined as a design approach that involves developing an intervention to change human 
behaviour. 
 
Literature related to analogical reasoning indicates that analogies play an important role 
in design. Analogical reasoning in design is associated with uncertainty reduction,  
creativity and problem-solving. It allows designers to transfer information from known 
systems and use it to deal with the design problem and challenges in the design process.   
 
Although analogical reasoning has been previously studied in traditional design, little is 
understood about the usage of analogies in behavioural design. No literature on 
analogical reasoning within the field of behavioural design has been observed 
throughout the literature study. This results in an interesting research gap to explore.  
The research direction of this thesis focuses on this gap and aims to better understand 
how analogical reasoning is being used in behavioural design projects.  
 
Based on the literature study in analogical reasoning and behavioural design, a 
conceptual framework has been developed, representing a relationship between 
analogical reasoning and behavioural design. An analogy is defined as a comparison that 
involves the identification and transfer of an explanatory structure from a known system 
to a new and relatively unknown system. The known system is called "the source", 
whereas the relatively unknown system "the target". Analogical reasoning is defined as 
==PAGE:37==
 
37 
the process of accessing knowledge and mapping similarities between source and 
target. The mapped similarities can be abstract and relational (structural) and 
perceptual object attributes (surface).   
 
In addition, models are added to the investigation of analogies as they involve analogical 
reasoning. In this thesis, models are defined as abstract analogous structures; they are 
formed by analogies of the real world. Also, models are seen as representative source 
domains, which behavioural design practitioners can use to draw analogies and transfer 
knowledge to the target problem.  
 
The behavioural design process is treated as a design process that involves a target 
behaviour as part of the design problem. The behavioural design activities within the 
process include analysis, synthesis, simulation and evaluation; their definitions are 
presented in 2.2 Behavioural Design Process chapter. It is assumed that analogical 
reasoning, is used in behavioural design activities like in traditional. However, it is not 
well understood what kind of analogies appear, their particular roles in behavioural 
design and how they relate to the design process.   
 
 
 
 
Figure 6. Conceptual framework 
 
The framework proposes 3 research question to guide the investigation within the topic.  
First question aims to categorise and define analogies used in behavioural design. 
Second and third questions are proposed to better understand the usage of analogies 
and their relationship with challenges in behavioural design.  
 
 
==PAGE:38==
 
38 
 
Research questions:  
 
RQ1: What analogies are being used in behavioural design?  
 
RQ2: Why analogies are used in behavioural design?   
 
RQ3: What do practitioners experience when analogies are used in behavioural design?

4 Methodology 
 
This chapter reports how the investigation of the research questions was done and the 
methods used. The research study is qualitative exploratory. Due to the limitation of 
theoretical knowledge about analogical reasoning within behavioural design, the 
investigation is supported by following a theory building approach, meaning that 
observations from the data collected were converted into empirical generalisations 
which contribute to new understanding of the area of interest (Handfield and Melnyk, 
1998). Following the recommendations of Handfield et al. (1998), the selected research 
methods were aligned with the research purpose, which is discovery. Interviewing was 
the method used for data collection and categorisation methods for data analysis 
(Handfield and Melnyk, 1998).    
 
 
4.1 Participants selection 
 
The sampling for the study case was done based on the following criteria.  
 
In order to investigate analogical reasoning and use of analogies in behavioural design 
activities, the sample should represent practitioners within the field of behavioural 
design, who have experience at working in behavioural design projects.  
 
The chosen candidates are behavioural design practitioners. They are considered as 
practitioners in the industry due to their reputation, experience and knowledge within 
the field. Potential candidates can be founders or employees of successful established 
behavioural design companies and consultancies. They can also be independent 
consultants socially recognised as experts in behavioural design.  
 
Some considerations to choose the candidates were their working experience, project 
portfolio, education, publications, public recognition and recommendations by other 
behavioural design practitioners. In addition, their behavioural design process also was 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
another consideration to evaluate the validity of the sample. Chosen candidates follow 
behavioural design processes, which in general cover the stages defined in the BASIC 
behaviour process framework and behavioural design activities (Hansen, 2019; 
Khadilkar et al., 2019).  
 
Since Behavioural Design is an emerging approach, the potential candidates for this case 
study are relatively few. Candidates were found not only in Denmark but also in other 
countries where behavioural design practises also are globally recognised.  
 
The process of searching for potential candidates started with online research through 
Google and LinkedIn search engines. In addition, behavioural design practitioners known 
by the researcher’s network were added. Potential candidates were contacted by email, 
phone call or LinkedIn Premium InMail messages. More potential candidates were 
contacted by snowball sampling (Goodman, 1961), following the recommendations of 
already interviewed behavioural design practitioners.  
 
41 potential candidates were contacted to participate in the research study. As a result, 
a total of 14 practitioners became participants in the study case. After reaching 14

4.2 Data collection  
 
The research method used to collect the data was a semi-structured interview. An 
interview was individually conducted to each participant, making a total of 14 interviews 
that constitute the data collected. Participants were treated anonymously; therefore a 
number was assigned to each interview. A semi-structured guide was prepared with 
open questions and prompts in accordance with the research questions and criteria 
(Appendix 1) The headline questions of the interview guide are listed below.  
 
Initial headline questions were demographic, made to classify the participants based on 
the sampling criteria.  
 
1. What is your nationality? 
2. What is your educational background? 
3. Could you tell me briefly what your experience with behavioural design is? 
4. Could you briefly sketch out a typical behavioural design process at your work?

research questions formulated for the case study. Participants were asked to come up 
with some analogy examples that have been used in behavioural design projects and 
reflect on their use.

behavioural design projects? 
6. Why do you use the analogies mentioned? 
7. What do you experience when these analogies are used in behavioural design?

4.3 Data analysis 
 
The collected data were analysed by doing thematic analysis (Joffe, 2011). Interviews 
were transcribed and coded inductively, forming themes and topics related to the 
conceptual framework.   
 
As an initial step, 4 transcribed interviews were randomly chosen to start the analysis. 
The interviews were first analysed by open coding (Khandkar, 1998). Data was carefully 
read and interpreted, highlighting relevant quotes and defining possible themes. During 
the process, quotes were gradually grouped and classified into first-order categories 
that summarise findings from the interviews. Then, the next step was axial coding 
analysis (Joffe, 2011). The codes were evaluated, compared with existing literature and 
then forming groups of more abstract and theoretical categories. Thus, codes were 
related to each other, second-order categories and aggregate dimensions gradually 
emerged throughout the analysis. At this step, literature theories such as Uncertainty 
Reduction (Solomon, 2015) and Teamwork Processes (Grossman et al., 2017) were 
reviewed, and they helped to understand data results better. Due to the research area's 
novelty, there were cases in which the existing theory had limitations to categorise new 
findings from the study case. Therefore, new theoretical categories and dimensions 
were built in the process to better explain the findings in relationship with the area of 
interest.   
 
The analysis followed an iterative process. Codes were iterated several times as new 
groups of transcribed interviews were included to the analysis, constantly comparing 
with prior analysis and theory, adding and refining the categories definitions, 
consequently following an organic growth of the themes. Every time new information 
was found, it was evaluated to see if it is related to current categories. If not, a new 
category was defined.  
 
Lastly, relationships between different dimensions and categories were observed and 
interpreted. This led to new knowledge and understanding of the usage of analogies in 
behavioural design. A total of 42 analogies were observed in the data and were 
==PAGE:45==

to the research topic. They characterise the analogies and summarise their relationship 
with their use in the behavioural design process and their correlated outcome in 
behavioural design projects. The Figure 8 represents the final data structure originated

5 Results 
 
This chapter describes the results of the research case. Aggregate dimensions 
discovered during the qualitative data analysis have been divided into groups according 
to the research questions. The first group corresponds to the dimensions that describe 
the analogies and their properties. The second group is related to analogies' usage, and 
the last group are possible outcomes that analogies cause in behavioural design. 
Besides, some relationship across categories and dimensions have been defined.    
 
 
5.1 Descriptive properties dimensions 
 
Different characteristics were identified in the analogical findings. As a result, three 
dimensions related to their properties were defined: Type of analogous element, 
Analogical distance and Analogy mapping.  
  
5.1.1 Type of analogous element  
 
This dimension divides the analogical findings into categories that define the type of 
analogical example mentioned by behavioural design practitioners. Findings from the 
interviews reveal different types of analogies and models (see Table 3 for selected 
quotes). As expected, behavioural design practitioners work with models of behavioural 
science during their design process. More than one participant mentioned popular 
models within behavioural design, confirming that diverse behavioural design 
companies are using the same models. The models most mentioned were "Fogg’s 
behaviour model” (Fogg, 2019), “System 1 and System 2 ” (Kahneman, 2011),  
“Behaviour forces field analysis” (Lewin, 1951), “Com-B” (West and Michie, 2020) and 
“Mindspace” (Dolan et al., 2012). Also, other behavioural science models are “ISM 
==PAGE:50==
 
50 
model” (Schwartz et al., 2017) “Cognitive bias codex” (Valdez et al., 2017), “Zmet 
zaltman model” (Zaltman, 2008) and “EAST” (Service et al., 2014). The behavioural 
science models that appeared are mainly conceptual; they were illustrated with 
diagrams or frameworks. However, evidence shows that behavioural design 
practitioners also use conceptual models as mental models. Indeed, the large 
experience of behavioural design experts makes behavioural science theories and 
models being integrated on their mind, and they are retrieved according to the context 
that they are facing:  
 
“ It's in the back of my head, you know, a lot of these behavioural studies, like in the

Practitioners also use models that are not coming from behavioural science. For 
instance, each practitioner or company has on their own process model that is followed 
during the project. Also, some practitioners also use other models that can come from 
diverse sources such as mathematics, business or biology. These type of models are seen 
as mental models used as a resource during intervention development.  
 
Besides models, findings reveal that practitioners use analogies in which they refer to 
prior cases and experiences. The cases used for comparison are often examples of 
behavioural biases, behavioural science experiments, and interventions that have been 
proven to work in certain contexts. In addition, they also refer to cases that are not 
rooted in behavioural science. Instead, these cases are exciting solutions from other 
industries, which are related to a design aspect of the product/system intervention or a 
working method.

5.1.3 Analogy Mapping 
 
Similarities in the mapped properties between source and target of analogies found 
have been observed. First, analogies mentioned by behavioural design practitioners 
show some relational properties of the source and the target that are similar. A very 
interesting example of these similarities can be seen in the Monkey and Suit metaphor, 
that symbolises System 1 and System 2. The relationships between monkey and suit are 
the same as between System 1 and System 2 (see Table 5). Those similitudes are 
presented as part of the analogy explanation and are considered structure similarity. In 
addition, few analogies also have surface similarity, meaning that some objects and 
perpetual properties of source and target are similar. As an example, the case 
mentioned in Table 5 shows a case where a previous solution is used in another project: 
Sticks on the floor used as indicators to guide customers. Here, the evidence of surface 
similarity is the fact that the objects "floor" and "stick" appear in both source and target 
domains. The same case also shows structure similarity, which evidence is the fact that 
both are used to guide customers.   
 
Findings also reveal that sometimes an analogy has some structure dissimilarity 
between the source and target:

5.2 Usage Dimensions  
 
3 dimensions have been discovered regarding the use of analogical thinking in 
behavioural design. The first dimension, called "Behavioural design activities", describes 
the usage in relation to the design process. In the other 2 dimensions, "Reduce 
behavioural uncertainty” and “Reduce relational uncertainty”, usage is related to 
pushing understanding in people working on the behavioural design projects.  
 
5.2.1 Behavioural design activities 
 
Findings manifest that analogical reasoning is being used in different activities of the 
behavioural design process. During these activities, analogical reasoning plays an 
important role to push design forward.  
 
One of the activities that show a high presence of analogical reasoning is analysis. Here, 
analogies are used to find patterns that define the problem behaviour and their factors. 
The use of these analogies during analysis also are used with clients. For instance, 
evidence in Table 6 shows that a metaphor is used in co-creation with the client so that 
he or she can analyse better the case. Consequently, the client can give relevant data 
about the target customer behaviour to the consultancy. Furthermore, analogies also 
appear very often in synthesis activities, such as ideation sessions, with the aim to bring 
inspiration and explore the solution space. Different types of analogies are retrieved by 
practitioners to generate ideas (Table 6).  Another observation about their usage is that 
a single analogy or model can have multiple purposes and be used in more than one 
activity: 
 
“…So explain to the client. And this is also a brainstorming technique or an ideation 
technique and also analysis tool.”  Interview 6 
 
Lastly, it has been observed that models are also used in simulation activities to predict 
how interventions that are being developed will work, and determine the estimated of 
the outcome of target behaviour in different situations (Table 6).

Findings also show that the use of analogies is associated with certain outcomes related 
to design and teamwork. 3 dimension have been identified:  First dimension 
corresponds to the design outcome. The other 2 dimensions constitute the outcome in 
team and client, revealing that analogies have influence in their affect and cognition.    
 
 
5.3.1 Design related outcome 
 
Based on results, behavioural design practitioners have observed both positive and 
negative effects in design solutions outcome when analogies have been used in 
behavioural design projects.   
 
On the one hand, practitioners have related the use of analogies with creativity and 
lateral thinking.  
“ [Speaker 1] And have you ever compared with similar solutions and get inspired by it? 
[Speaker 2] Yeah, that's a good and we yes, the answer is yes. We call it lateral 
thinking…We tend to use lateral thinking the most and where it allows us to be a bit 
more creative…I think especially like the gaming analogy that has been really 
interesting when doing conference talks, because that captures their imagination. So 
it's really important. And when you expanding ideas to have a combination of very 
robust, small academic principles that are a bit boring that no one cares about and 
making sure that you include them in the videos, but then also these fun, exciting 
things that feel more creative.”  
 
They also have commented that new ideas often appear when doing analogical 
reasoning. For instance, participant 14 have stated that once models are being used to 
==PAGE:64==
 
64 
relate the target problem with certain factors and insights, suddenly people come up 
with unexpected ideas  (See Table 9).   
 
On the other hand, practitioners have also experienced that the use of some analogies 
can lead to familiar ideas or thinking of the same concepts. Evidence that proves this is 
the case of using a "Ferry" as a metaphor of "Timely" (Table 9), which is a behaviour 
principle for applying behavioural insights (Service et al., 2014).  People who have used 
this analogy to generate ideas tend to rely on the same aspect of time, which is finding 
the right moment to trigger the user. However, timely involves more aspects such as 
considering immediate cost and benefits 
 
Results also show that concepts originated from analogies have become feasible and 
successful solutions. For example, participant 7 narrates a project in which an analogy 
was used as part of lateral thinking in ideation, comparing pension videos with gaming 
videos in 1st person view. The analogy contributed to developing a solution that was 
awarded in the industry (See Table 9). Note that this does not mean that the analogies 
are the cause of good results in solutions; nevertheless, there is a correlation.   
 
At the same time, practitioners have observed cases in which analogies have generated 
a bad result in an intervention. A clear example mentioned by practitioners is social 
proof, which has been used in many contexts and some of them did not work, such as 
using social proof to try getting people not to cancel doctor's appointment (See Table

As can be seen, like in traditional design, analogies in behavioural design influence the 
originality and effectiveness of final solutions. Furthermore, they can have dual effect in 
the design outcome.

6 Discussion 
 
Insights obtained in the study are aligned with previous research about analogical 
reasoning in design. The following discussion assess and relate the results with the 
existing literature. The discussion also summarize and highlight relevant findings from 
the study research, reflecting on its contributions and implications. Lastly, limitations, 
further work and future research directions are also discussed.    
 
 
6.1 Type of analogies in behavioural design  
 
In the literature, analogies are commonly classified into within-domain and between-
domain (e.g. Ball et al., 2009; Vosniadou, 2009; Chan et al., 2012). The study research 
shows that behavioural design practitioners use both within-domain and between 
domain analogies, which also occurs in traditional design based on existing research (e.g. 
Ahmed and Christensen, 2009; Ball and Christensen, 2009; Christensen and Ball, 2016). 
This makes sense because behavioural design is considered a design approach (Cash et 
al., 2017). From a design perspective, behavioural design has a design problem within a 
specific context. Then while problem-solving, behavioural designers apply knowledge 
from that context (within-domain), but also others (between-domain). Results also 
show that analogies in behavioural design can have both surface and structure 
analogical mapping similarities, properties that have been described in previous 
research (Koh and Holyoak, 1987; Vosniadou, 2009).  
 
Analogical mapping and domain origin are dimensions that already exists in the 
literature. They have been observed in the results; however, they were not enough to 
explain the research findings. Thus, the new dimension "Type of analogous elements" 
have been proposed in this research study. This dimension covers some characteristics 
of the analogical findings that have not been categorised in the design literature. In 
particular, with this dimension, it is explained that in behavioural design there are 
==PAGE:79==
 
79 
certain analogies that address the behavioural aspects of design problem. They are 
categorised as behavioural models and prior behavioural cases. These types of 
analogous elements are related to behavioural science and focus on dealing with the 
target behaviour. In addition, second-order categories in the type of analogous 
elements also differentiate between simple analogies and models. Models are abstract 
and complex analogous structures (Landriscina, 2015), whereas simple analogies, such 
as metaphors and prior cases, are more concrete. This differentiation has also somehow 
seen in Gentner's (2013) structure mapping theory, which classifies types of domain 
comparisons and separates analogies from abstractions. Gentner argues that analogies 
and abstractions have relations (structural properties) and attributes (surface 
properties), but abstractions have few surface properties in both source and target 
domains (Gentner, 2013). Within the type of analogous elements, categories also 
explain that on the one hand, some analogies are originated based on past realities, 
these are prior behavioural cases and other prior cases. On the other hand, metaphors 
are particular analogies that are made up, and normally the comparison is not that 
obvious like in prior cases. This last type of analogy is recognised in the literature related 
to analogies theory. For instance, Gentner (2013) refers to metaphor as a type of 
analogy that is expressed poetically and often involves symbolic objects and attributes.  
 
Characteristics in categories of this new dimension are in sync with previous theories in 
analogical reasoning. At the same time, grouping and classifying analogies observed in 
design activities based on the characteristics mentioned is something considered to be 
novel. The categorisation was defined according to the examples of analogies 
mentioned by behavioural design practitioners and revealed new insights about 
analogical reasoning within behavioural design. Furthermore, it has been observed that 
some types of analogous elements have particular purposes in behavioural design and 
interesting relationships between other categories. For example, metaphors and prior 
cases in behavioural design are used to explain behaviour models. This makes sense 
because models are abstract, but metaphors and prior cases are more concrete; thus 
their source domain has more objects and attributes that form an intuitive and visual 
image in people's minds,  helping to explain the behaviour model's relational properties.   
 
==PAGE:80==
 
80 
6.2 Importance of using analogies in behavioural design  
 
The research study points out that analogical reasoning plays an important role in 
behavioural design since they are used for different purposes, contributing in many 
ways throughout the behavioural design process.  
 
Findings show that analogies are used in 3 behavioural design activities: Synthesis, 
analysis and simulation. According to the literature, evaluation is another behavioural 
design activity, consisting of testing and comparing prototypical solutions with the 
expected outcome (Khadilkar et al., 2019). Results from this research do not 
demonstrate that analogies are used for this particular activity, although this does not 
mean that analogies are not present in evaluation activities. It is assumed that analogical 
reasoning also exists for evaluation, and some models might be used during this activity, 
for example, logic models (The Scottish Government, 2015; NHS, 2016).   
 
Analogies in behavioural design have also the purpose of increasing understanding 
throughout the process. A key challenge in behavioural design is understanding the 
complexity of behaviour problems and deal with the high uncertainty that implies 
human behaviour (Cash et al., 2017). The research study reveals that analogies play an 
important role in handling this challenge; they are used to reduce uncertainty during the 
design process. This aligns with prior research in traditional design, that has 
demonstrated that analogies are linked to uncertainty reduction (Ball and Christensen, 
2009). Taking a step further, findings in this research study specify 2 types of 
uncertainties in behavioural design that are solved thanks to analogical reasoning. The 
first type is the behavioural uncertainty common in behavioural design projects. 
Analogies are used to explain and understand people’s behaviour and their expected 
actions, thus reducing behavioural uncertainty. The second type is relational uncertainty 
(Solomon, 2015). It has been found that analogies in behavioural design are also crucial 
to reduce clients' relational uncertainty. Literature in uncertainty reduction theory 
remarks that reducing uncertainty involves increasing both explanation and prediction 
capabilities (Solomon, 2015). Analogies are used when communicating with clients, 
contributing to relational uncertainty reduction in both ways, explaining and predicting. 
==PAGE:81==
 
81 
Behavioural design is explained to clients, increasing their understanding of the subject 
and what is required to achieve the behaviour change. In addition, analogies also reduce 
behavioural uncertainty because they simplify behaviour, helping to understand how to 
use behaviour knowledge from theories in behavioural design activities. Furthermore, 
analogical reasoning is directly used to increase client's prediction of the expected 
rewards, for instance, by drawing analogies from prior behavioural cases that have 
worked before. Analogies are often used to communicate with clients; their reasons for 
usage and effects indicate that they are excellent resources to build relationships.  
 
Findings have presented 6 types of relationships between analogies and their usage in 
behavioural design. These relationships indicate that the knowledge transferred by 
analogical reasoning from domain to target helps practitioners to: explain, make 
predictions, simplify working with behaviour and navigate within the solution-space. 
The literature shows that analogies' purpose in traditional design has been previously 
classified based on cognitive function categories: explanation, problem identification, 
problem-solving and function-finding (Christensen et al., 2007).  These functions are 
seen in the nature of the relationships between analogies and their usage discovered in 
the research study. For instance, analogies' explanation function can be seen in analysis 
activities. Also, it can be seen that analogies reduce behavioural and relational 
uncertainty thanks to giving explanation.  Function-finding and problem-solving can be 
seen synthesis activities, in analogies used to explore the problem solution-space.  
Furthermore,  problem identification can also be seen in simulation activities, where 
behavioural designers use models to predict how intervention ideas will work and detect 
possible issues. In this thesis, it is also considered that in synthesis activities, 
practitioners make predictions too, although this has not been expressed in the results. 
During synthesis, they might make predictions like in simulation activities because when 
ideating, designers use mental simulation together with analogical reasoning to assess 
ideas (Wiltschnig, Christensen and Ball, 2013).

6.3 Effects and considerations when using analogies  
 
This research has also shown some relationships between the usage of analogies and 
possible outcome in behavioural design projects. It confirms that the analogical 
reasoning during synthesis activities, like in traditional design, has influence in creativity. 
Previous research in design creativity has shown that analogies can affect positively, by 
bringing inspiration, or negatively, by generating fixation (Vasconcelos and Crilly, 2016). 
This can also be seen in this research results, which shows that analogies are sources of 
inspiration for practitioners and help generate new ideas, but some ideas are also 
fixated on previous solutions. It can be observed that there might be a trade-off 
between the feasibility of prior behaviour cases and creativity. While prior behaviour 
cases are used because there is evidence that has worked before, there is an over-
reliance on them, thus generating fixation. This trade-off is also mentioned by authors 
within the field of creativity, such as De Bono, who argues that people tend to repeat 
ideas that they know they work due to high failure costs (Bono, 2015).   
 
This study also suggests that analogical reasoning used to reduce uncertainty have 
effects on people's cognition and affect. In the literature, metaphors, as well as mental 
models, are associated with organisation development with building shared 
understanding (Davison and Blackman, 2005; Jacobs and Heracleous, 2006). The study 
confirms this in behavioural design, showing that practitioners use analogies and share 
models with team members and clients to reach a common understanding. People's 
cognition has been previously related to people's affect. According to a 15-year review 
of mental models' construction, team's cognition is strongly associated with emergent 
affective states (Mohammed, S. and Hamilton, 2010; Grossman et al., 2017). 
Considering that, it makes sense that findings indicate that analogical reasoning 
influences in team and client's affect since it is a cognitive process.  Regarding type of 
affective emergent states, the research shows that analogies used to reduce relational 
uncertainty in clients produce outcomes on the client's trust. This is in sync with previous 
research that has stated that relational uncertainty reduction allows building trust 
(Kreye, 2017). In addition, this study also suggests that reducing uncertainty by using 
analogies has positive effects on team and client's cohesion.   
==PAGE:83==
 
83 
 
Findings show that analogies used in behavioural design generate very positive, but also 
sometimes negative effects in design outcome, as well as people's affective and 
cognitive outcome. This study research does not conclude that some types of analogies 
are bad or good, either that certain usages are correct or wrong. Rather, it proposes that 
effective analogies mostly depends on context factors and people's capabilities to 
interpret source and target domain. This leads to reflect on considerations and 
implications when using analogies in behavioural design, based on the research results.   
 
The first consideration is regarding wrong interpretation and misunderstanding when 
people are given an analogy as an explanation. According to the literature, the capability 
of mapping and identifying similarities between source and target depends on 
preconstructed person's mental models (Vosniadou, 2009). In other words, a person will 
understand the analogy only if it is attached to something familiar to the person. The 
interpretation of the analogy and its meaning will depend on people's mental models, 
which are grounded on their prior experiences, acquired knowledge and background. 
This is relevant in behavioural design because it is a multidisciplinary approach (Wendel, 
2017) and people within teams come from many different backgrounds.  Furthermore, 
clients come with varied backgrounds because behaviour change provides solutions in 
many industries, such as finances, retail sector, digital marketing, littering, etc. Based on 
this study, contextual differences between people are why practitioners have 
experienced cases where clients did not understand well an analogy or even did not 
believe in its explanation. Analogies need to be adapted to people's background and 
culture so that the comparison becomes meaningful. Therefore, a good practice is to 
consider who are we talking when using analogies to explain behavioural science.  
 
In the same vein, wrong interpretations of behavioural science models and concepts can 
lead to drawing weak analogies, leading to less effective behavioural design solutions. 
This study suggests that the cause of possible misinterpretation is the person's mental 
model about human behaviour and behavioural science. This aligns with prior research 
in analogical reasoning, which explains that the capability of making productive 
analogies depends on how good the person's mental model of the source domain is 
==PAGE:84==
 
84 
(Gary et al., 2012). Findings reveal that people entering the market are developing 
ineffective solutions because they are misinterpreting nudging and seeing it as the 
solution to all kinds of behaviour problems. Consequently, they use nudges in wrong 
contexts. There is an emergent risk of a wrong usage of behavioural science theory by 
people who lack knowledge about this field. This is because, like nudge, behavioural 
science concepts have become popular and have been overstated. Another example can 
be seen in the literature; it is argued that dual-process theory has been overstated due 
to its popularisation (Evans and Stanovich, 2013). In order to avoid misinterpretation, it 
is required knowledge and experience in the field. This could be considered an entry 
barrier to the market for people who do not have psychology or behavioural science 
background, such as traditional designers.    
 
Lastly, an important consideration is that behaviour is very context-sensitive.  This 
means an analogy may have positive or negative effects in problem-solving outcome 
depending on context details. Sometimes a solution or a behaviour principle works 
perfectly in a certain context, but it may not be effective in a slightly different context. 
Findings in this study show that practitioners have experienced that small changes in an 
intervention can influence the results. To prevent this limitation when drawing analogies 
in behavioural design, it suggested investigating the target domain's context. For this 
reason, adequate research of the behaviour problem is relevant to draw analogies in 
behavioural design. The better the data obtained, the better the understanding of the 
context and therefore, the greater the analogies during the process. In addition, tests 
are also of central importance; they are necessary to correct errors caused by analogies 
used during the process that are weak due to the specific context.  
 
Due to behaviour's contextual sensitiveness, models cannot fulfil all aspects that 
determine a successful intervention. Also, some models may not be suitable for certain 
contexts. Behavioural design practitioners are aware of it, and they argue that those 
limitations are not relevant. Behavioural design is not just about using a model on, it is 
about redefining an entire process, developing an intervention suitable for a specific 
context. Practitioners have the task to find a solution to a particular behaviour problem. 
Then during the process, analogies are a resource that helps explain,  predict, simplify 
==PAGE:85==
 
85 
behavioural design activities and explore the solution space. According to practitioners' 
opinion, analogical reasoning help to move forward the process, although some models 
used to draw analogies are incomplete, they still work in practice. This opinion aligns 
with Don Norman’s argument, which says that “A model doesn’t need to be accurate, 
what it is important is that it is useful” (Norman, 1976). In the case of behavioural design, 
it has been proven that they are very useful.   
 
 
6.4 Research study Implications  
 
This study research has been done to explore the use of analogical reasoning in 
behavioural design projects. The results have brought new insights with respect to 
analogies in behavioural design. This involves a new understanding of what kind 
analogies are used, as well as relationships between analogies and the behavioural 
design process. Also, the study points out the importance of analogical reasoning for 
practitioners and reveals some considerations when using analogies.  
 
 
6.5 Limitations and further work  
 
This research has some limitations to be considered and areas of further work.  
 
The method used to collect information about analogies in behavioural design was 
interviewing. Because of this, the findings from interviews only cover conscious 
analogies mentioned by practitioners. However, analogical mapping is a process that 
works automatically and unconscious, analogies can or not be remembered afterwards 
by our consciousness (Reber et al., 2014). This means that practitioners may use more 
types of analogies that could be identified throughout this research. Therefore, it is 
suggested to further investigate analogies in behavioural design by doing observational 
research. In addition, the interview results are affected by the observer's paradox 
(Shanmuganathan, 2005). Participants were not in a natural context, and they were 
==PAGE:86==
 
86 
aware that their speech would be used for academic research, so this could influence 
the speech.   
   
Another consideration is that in qualitative research, the researcher is constrained by 
his own interpretation of the data when coding schemes in the thematic analysis 
(Burnard et al., 2008). Consequently, it causes some limitations to the reliability of this 
study. In order to achieve more reliability, a future researcher or evaluator can repeat 
the coding with the same data and methods. Then, it would be possible to calculate 
inter-rater reliability (Hoyt, 2000). Also, further reliability and validation of findings 
could be obtained by more triangulation, using more data collection methods besides 
interviewing to research the same topic, for example, by doing observational studies 
methods (B. Miles, Huberman and Saldaña, 2014).  
 
This study has identified different themes about analogies in behavioural design. Also, 
it has recognised some relationships between aggregate dimensions and their 
categories. Further research is needed to improve and better understand the 
relationship between analogies, usage and effects discovered in this research. This could 
be done by applying a “relationship building” research strategy (Handfield and Melnyk, 
1998). At the same time, it is suggested further investigation of the existing themes 
categorised in this study. For instance, relevant research directions from this research 
study are investigating uncertainty reduction, creativity and teamwork emergent states 
in relation to behavioural design and analogical reasoning. Lastly, further work within 
this thesis research topic could also lead to a better understanding of how to benefit 
from using analogies in behavioural design, with the possibility of developing a 
framework or a tool to work with analogies and models.

7 Conclusion 
 
 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the usage of analogical reasoning in behavioural 
design. First, an extensive literature study related to analogical reasoning and 
behavioural design was carried out. Then, the following research questions were 
formulated to scope and guide the research study:   
 
RQ1: What analogies are being used in behavioural design?  
RQ2: Why analogies are used in behavioural design?  
RQ3: What do practitioners experience when analogies are used in behavioural design?  
 
To answer these questions, a semi-structured interview has been conducted to 14 
behavioural design practitioners from multiple countries. The data collected has been 
analysed by doing thematic analysis, using open and axial coding methods. Throughout 
the analysis, the themes found reveal new insights related to the type of analogies used 
in behavioural design, their reasons for usage and effects that practitioners have 
experienced.  
 
RQ1 has been answered by first, identifying a total of 42 analogies mentioned by 
behavioural design practitioners (Appendix 2). Secondly, multiple properties of these 
analogies have been recognised and categorised into themes.  Results show that 
practitioners use analogies within-domain and between domain. Also, the analogies can 
have structural and surface similarity, and in some cases, it has been observed 
dissimilarity in the analogy within the context that was used. In addition, the analogies 
found have been classified into the type of analogous elements, concluding that the 
analogies used in behavioural design can be metaphors, prior behaviour cases, other 
prior cases, behaviour models and other models.    
 
Regarding RQ2, findings reveal different important roles of analogies in behavioural 
design.  Analogical reasoning is used during analysis, simulation and synthesis 
behavioural design activities. They are used in these activities because the analogies 
==PAGE:88==
 
88 
help make explanations and predictions and act as stimuli to generate ideas.  
Furthermore, in behavioural design, analogical reasoning is relevant to reduce 
uncertainty. Analogies are used with clients to explain behavioural design and also give 
predictions about the project's outcome, thus reducing clients' relational uncertainty.  
Also, analogical reasoning plays an important role in reducing behavioural uncertainty. 
This is because analogies are used to explain behaviour and simplify behavioural design, 
allowing to understand better human behaviour and how to apply behaviour theory to 
change behaviour.   
  
Results also respond to RQ3, showing that practitioners have experienced effects in the 
design outcome, team and client's affect and cognition when using analogies in 
behavioural design for the different purposes mentioned. Analogical reasoning used in 
synthesis activities can new ideas or fixated ideas, having an influence on creativity. Also, 
analogies used to reduce uncertainty causes positive effects on the team and the client's 
shared understanding, cohesion and trust. However, sometimes an analogy used with 
clients could provoke misunderstanding or trust reduction.  
 
Results have shown positive and negative experiences with analogies concerning 
people's affect, cognition and problem-solving. The study research concludes that the 
negative outcome relies on context factors and people's capabilities to interpret source 
and target domain, not in the type of analogy and either its reason for usage. Therefore, 
when using analogies in behavioural design, people's background and experience within 
behavioural design may be considered. Also, since behaviour is context-sensitive, 
rigorous research and testing are important in behavioural design to avoid wrong 
assumptions made by analogical reasoning. Regardless of these considerations, 
analogical reasoning has demonstrated to be an important resource in behavioural 
design. Its usage is recommended to deal with behavioural and relational uncertainty, 
facilitate behavioural design activities and communicate among team members and 
clients.    
 
This study research has contributed to a better understanding of the role of analogies in 
behavioural design. This paves the way for further research within the topic that could 
==PAGE:89==
 
89 
lead to more considerations or even methods to optimise analogies usage. Possible 
research directions could be exploring the dimensions found concerning behavioural 
design, as well as further investigating and refining the identified relationships between 
them.

9.1 Preliminary assignment  
 
 
Initial scope and aim of the project  
 
Behavioural design is an approach of significant interest in research and industry. Its 
importance is increasing thanks to its potential of making positive impact in society by 
understanding human behaviour. However, there are still many challenges when 
behavioural design comes to practice. Specifically, during the ideation stage, creativity 
and synthesis are key challenges (Cash, Hartlev and Durazo, 2017).  
 
Previous research in creativity shows that analogies play an important role in the 
creative cognitive process. In product design, originality of ideas are influenced by  
analogical thinking (Dahl and Moreau, 2002).  
 
Little is understood about the role of analogies in the behavioural design ideation. The 
aim of this thesis is to investigate analogical reasoning in behavioural design, and its 
relation with the creative process at the ideation stage. Qualitative research will be 
conducted in order to gain insights into creativity, within the context of behavioural 
design processes.  
 
 
Based on the aim of the project, a research question has been formulated: 
 
- 
How does analogical thinking influence the ideation stage of behavioural design?

9.2 Actual plan and reflection 
 
 
Changes in scope and aim  
 
The direction of the project was adapted according to circumstances and opportunities 
presented during the process. The project's preliminary scope focused on studying analogies in 
behavioural design ideation and understanding how they influence creativity. As an initial plan, 
it was decided to arrange a collaboration with a behavioural design company and perform an 
observational study in ideation sessions, so that it would be possible to trace the role of 
analogies used in ideation. However, it was not possible to reach an agreement with the initial 
company because there was no suitable case for the investigation. Consequently, it was decided 
to allocate time to contact and find cases in other local and also foreign behavioural design 
companies. After a deadline established, there was still no agreement with the companies 
contacted, mainly due to NDAs and other policies constraints. Therefore, it was decided to 
change the plan for the research study. This concluded in changing the strategy, and conducting 
interviews to behavioural design practitioners, taking advantage of the international companies 
already contacted to find participants. Also, the scope was slightly changed since interviewing 
had limitations in researching creativity, being more difficult to obtain promising results 
regarding the initial scope. Unlike observations, interviews do not enable tracing the analogies 
and analysing how they affect the creative outcome.  Furthermore, many analogies in ideation 
are be generated unconsciously, and people might not remember them. If the interview focuses 
only on ideation and creativity, there was a high risk of finding very few insights. For this reason, 
the scope was expanded, and the aim of the project focuses on understanding the usage of 
analogies in any activity within behavioural design. This also brought new research questions. 
The decision ended up to be very positive since many insights were discovered in the research 
study. After this, the project title was changed,  from "Behavioural Design ideation" to 
"Analogical Reasoning in Behavioural design".

The scheduling of the project had few differences compared to what was expected in 
the preliminary plan. There was a delay in the data collection due to changes in the 
initial plan, as well as the long time that takes to contact potential participants for the 
interviews, time that was underestimated. After a set deadline, it was decided to stop 
contacting more potential participants and instead allocate time to analyse and report.  
Also, the literature study was expected to finish at the end of September. However, 
once the thematic analysis started, more literature was required to understand better 
the results obtained.  The review of the literature also continued to support the report 
discussion.  Furthermore, a part of the analysis and evaluation of the results was done 
simultaneously with writing the report results.
