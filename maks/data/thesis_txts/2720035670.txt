Abstract
Early detection of structural damages is crucial for maintaining a good
health level in buildings before further irreversible damage is caused. Most
of the structural inspections for damages are currently done by professionôÙ
als in the industry with the help of drones that allow faster inspection and
reach areas that would be otherwise inaccessible.
In this work, we will create a neural networkôÙbased algorithm that classifies
damages in buildings belonging to known damage types and is capable of
detecting other damages that do not belong to the initial damage classes.
This approach will open the window to creating a pipeline potentially imôÙ
plemented in drones that can be used for automatic damage detection and
classification of damages in buildings, an area known for its scarcity in reôÙ
gard to public data availability.

1
Introduction
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have presented great success in
image recognition [1] which has been possible thanks to the public image
repositories, such as ImageNet [2] and highôÙperformance computing sysôÙ
tems. However, when dealing with neural networks, there is always preôÙ
dictive uncertainty present that is indispensable to measure for machine
learning applications and deploying CNNs in realôÙworld systems such as
damage detection for buildings.
The predictive uncertainty of CNNs is related to detecting abnormal samôÙ
ples that are drawn far from the InôÙDistribution, i.e, the distribution of trainôÙ
ing samples. For detecting OutôÙOfôÙDistribution (OOD) samples (also called
OutôÙOfôÙOrder), previous works have utilized the posterior distribution from
the classifier using the Softmax activation function as a baseline method
[3].
However, the accuracy of both the InôÙDistribution classification network and
the OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection is highly constrained by the amount of
available data to train the neural networks, often requiring large datasets
to achieve high accuracy and recall. While some of the damage categories
have less amount of data, some other damage types might not have any of
the data. On the other hand, we might not be aware of all types of damages
which might arise. Hence the classifiers developed based on the available
data may represent only a subset of damages that are possible to arise.
Therefore, detecting OOD samples is important not only for preventing the
misclassification of data but also opens the window for detecting samples
that can potentially belong to the neural network, i.e. new damages that
are detected as OOD.
1.1
State of Art
Previous literature has explored the utility of artificial neural networks for
damage classification in buildings [4]. These approaches involve the clasôÙ
sification of damage types of datasets that contain a very limited range of
damage types such as cracks and spalling. The lack of variety in damage
types along with the scarcity of publicly available datasets leads to naively
trained neural networks and often overlooks the presence of new varieties
or damage types, which leads to misclassification and hinders understandôÙ
ing over the range of damages that could be encountered in realôÙworld civil
structure damage detection.
Thus, current approaches fall short when it comes to the detection of new
damage types since the neural networks are trained with a small subset
of preôÙlabeled damaged types. We provide a method not only for classiôÙ
fying already commonly known structural damage types that serve as the
base classes of a neural network classifier, but also opens the window to

belonging to damages, preventing misclassification.
1.2
Our Contribution
While our approach is also based on convolutional neural networks suôÙ
pervised learning, we explore the possibility of classifying the known damôÙ
age types the network has been trained with (InôÙDistribution damages),
while detecting potential new damages whose label is unknown (OutôÙOfôÙ
Distribution) damages.
1.3
Objectives
As mentioned before: our goal is to classify known damage types while
detecting unknown damage types that do not belong to the original clasôÙ
sification classes.
Nevertheless, from a technical perspective, OutôÙOfôÙ
Distribution detection has a limitation: intractability. This is caused by the
fact that, to train a neural network to detect images that are outside of their
original decision boundary, it needs to be trained with OutôÙOfôÙDistribution
images and such region is infinite and unbounded. Figure 1.1 depicts the
different regions that will be present in our use case.

1.5
Sustainability
Improving the performance of a neural network in detecting damages in
civil structures would have a significant positive impact on global sustainôÙ
ability. This is because early detection of damages in these structures can
prevent major disasters from occurring, such as building collapses, which
can result in loss of life and significant damage to the environment. By usôÙ
ing neural networks, engineers and architects can quickly and accurately
identify potential issues in structures, allowing for prompt repairs and mainôÙ
tenance to be carried out before any major problems occur.
Additionally, the use of neural networks in detecting damages in civil strucôÙ
tures can also lead to cost savings for both governments and private orgaôÙ
nizations. By identifying and addressing issues early on, the need for costly
repairs and replacements can be reduced, which can help to conserve
resources and reduce waste. Furthermore, the use of neural networks
can also help to reduce the need for manual inspections, which can be
timeôÙconsuming and laborôÙintensive. Overall, the use of neural networks
in detecting damages in civil structures can lead to a more sustainable and
efficient way of maintaining and preserving these structures.

Theoretical Background
This section is aimed to give a quick walkthrough of the concepts related to
the artificial neural networks we will use, that are key to understanding the
following chapters. We will focus on two types of neural networks: ConôÙ
volutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Generative Adversarial Networks
(GAN).
2.1
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
Convolutional Neural Networks, also known as CNNs, are designed for
image recognition and processing, and specialize in handling pixel data.
These networks are composed of two main types of layers: Convolutional
layers and Pooling layers.
2.1.1
Convolutional Layer
Convolutional layers are the fundamental building blocks of CNNs. They
take input data, a filter, and a feature map as inputs. To understand the
concept of convolution, letãs consider a RGB (3 channels) color image as
input. This image has three dimensions, namely height, width, and depth
(channels). The Convolutional layer applies a feature detector or kernel,
which is a 2ôÙdimensional array of weights, typically a 3x3 matrix, over the
receptive fields of the image by performing dot product operation. This
process is known as convolution. After the dot product is calculated, the
filter shifts by a stride and repeats the process until it has swept over the
entire image. The final output is known as a feature map, which is an
array of all the convolutions performed on the input image. There are some
parameters that we need to understand about Convolutional layers that will
be mentioned in future Chapters. These are:
1. Number of kernels: affects the depth of the output. For example,
100 distinct kernels would yield 100 different feature maps, creating
a depth of 100.
2. Kernel size: This is the size of the kernel matrix. Typically it is a 3x3
matrix like in Fig. 2.1 or a 5x5.
3. Kernel weights: These are the values the kernels are composed of.
Note how in Fig. 2.1 the weights of the kernel remain constant for
all the steps in the convolution. In a CNN the weights of the kernels
conform the weights of the neural network. Thus, they will be upôÙ
dated on every step of the training process, being adjusted through
the process of backpropagation and gradient descent.
4. Stride: distance (number of pixels) that the kernel moves over the
input matrix on each step of the convolution.
5. Padding: it is a technique used when the kernels do not fit the input
image. It involves setting all elements that fall outside the input matrix

2.1.2
Pooling Layer
Pooling layers are employed to decrease the number of parameters in the
input data by downsampling. Like convolutional layers, pooling layers slide
a kernel over the input. However, unlike convolutional layers, pooling layôÙ
ers do not have any weights. Instead, it applies an aggregation function to
the values in the receptive field. There are two types of pooling:
1. Max pooling: As the kernel moves over the input, it identifies the
pixel with the highest value and assigns it to the corresponding posiôÙ
tion in the output array.
2. Average pooling: As the kernel slides over the input, it calculates
the mean value of the pixels in the receptive field, and assigns it to
the corresponding position in the output array.

5.1
VGGôÙ13 Classifier
For the classification task, we will import the VGGôÙ13 architecture used in
the previous section and train it using the CrossôÙEntropy Loss ??. On top
of the CE loss term, we can add a KullbackôÙLeibler (KL) divergence term:
Lvgg = LCE(ùyin) + öýKL [S(ùyout), 1/N]
(5.1)
where S(ùyout) is the SoftmaxôÙactivated output of the VGG13 for OOD samôÙ

penalty parameter. In this thesis, we will set öý = 1. The intuition behind
this added KL loss is that it forces the predictive distribution on OutôÙOfôÙ

into low confidence scores, while that for InôÙDistribution samples still folôÙ

In other words, the KL loss is designed for assigning higher maximum
prediction values (confidence scores) to InôÙDistribution samples than OOD
ones.

remain constant. Thus stopping the learning process.

6.1.1

First, it is worth noting why WGAN is the architecture of choice for this task

found out that the one that better synchronized with the VGG13 Classifier

For our current use case (data augmentation), the WGAN training process

Discussion
In the following chapter, we will discuss the results obtained in Chapters
4, 5, and 6. In Chapter 3 we introduced how OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data, i.e.,
data that does not belong to the original training set, can be detected by
looking at the softmax prediction probabilities (confidence scores) of the
output of a neural network. In Chapter 4 we began by using a standard
VGG13 architecture. We found out that for such a case, there was very
little difference between the confidence scores of InôÙDistribution and OutôÙ
OfôÙDistribution data, making OutôÙOfôÙDistribution samples of both Dout,1 and
Dout,2 almost indistinguishable from Din. This suggested the need for an
improved training routine that would increase the gap between the conôÙ
fidence scores of In and OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data, i.e., assign low confiôÙ
dence scores to OutôÙOfôÙDistribution samples and high confidence sores to
InôÙDistribution samples.
With that purpose in mind, we introduced the architecture of Chapter 5,
which included an assistant GAN network to the VGG13 classifier. With
the help of a KLôÙdivergence loss term the GAN network was capable of
generating data close to the classification boundaries of the VGG13 clasôÙ
sifier and in return, the classifier would output lower confidence scores for
OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data. In Chapter 6 we explored the possibility of furôÙ
ther improving the architecture by using a second (external) GAN generôÙ
ating InôÙDistribution data, as a form of data augmentation technique. As a
result, we obtained both an increment in InôÙDistribution classification and
OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection performance as shown in tables 8.1, 8.2 in
the Appendix.
Out of these results, we can conclude the following:
1. CNN classifiers such as VGG13 can benefit from OutôÙOfôÙDistribution
training using assistant GANs, improving both InôÙDistribution classiôÙ
fication and OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection performance.
2. OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection performance in CNN classifiers is enôÙ
hanced by both boundary OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data and InôÙDistribution
data. As seen in Tab. 8.2, the biggest improvement in OOD detecôÙ
tion was caused by training the classifier with an assistant GAN that
generated data in the borders of the classification boundaries of the
classifier classes (healthy, cracks, spalling). Nonetheless, data augôÙ
mentation techniques and thus more real data availability can also
further improve OOD detection accuracy.
3. ODIN OOD detection method improves over Baseline on more data
availability. For the VGG13 and GAN+VGG13 cases we obtained
similar OOD detection results for both methods. When more data
(real or synthetic) is used for training the gap between both methods
grows further, seeing a clear dominance of ODIN over Baseline.

7.1
Conclusion
The current research aimed to use OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data detection techôÙ
niques as a way of identifying new damages in buildings. This was moôÙ
tivated by the scarce data availability in related public datasets and the
intractability of covering all possible damage types.
For such purpose, we analyzed the performance of CNNs networks usôÙ
ing VGG13 as the core of the architecture and compared results attendôÙ
ing at the InôÙDistribution classification accuracy and a range of OutôÙOfôÙ
Distribution detection stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart metrics. The results showed us that
using an assistant Generative Adversarial Network trained along the VGG13
classifier that generates data on the borders of the classification boundôÙ
aries of a classifier greatly improves OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection, along
with some minor increase in InôÙDistribution classification as well. Thus not
requiring strictly providing OutôÙOfôÙDistribution to the classifier (i.e., data
that is far from the decision boundaries of the classifier), since it would
raise intractability issues.
However, data scarcity always remains a problem and that is reflected in
the OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection accuracies. We believe that they can be
further improved and one way to do so is by training the architectures with
more InôÙDistribution data. Artificial data augmentation is a possible way to
go, and that was our goal by using a secondary GAN assistant that was
preôÙtrained to generate InôÙDistribution data that could be batched along the
original training sets. As a result of this, we also saw an increase in OutôÙ
OfôÙDistribution data detection, but further improvement can be achieved by
providing more real data in future work.
As a final remark, this research aimed to detect OutôÙOfôÙDistribution damôÙ
ages from structural images. In Chapter 7, not only we exposed how InôÙ
Distribution structural damages can be distinguished from OutôÙOfôÙDistribution
structural damages but also further OutôÙOfôÙDistribution data, completely
unrelated to structures can be distinguished from these two as well using
the same technique.
This research has shown that OutôÙOfôÙDistribution detection can be used as
a way of potentially discovering new classes within an area of interest, conôÙ
sequently opening the gate to creating a classôÙincremental learning routine
that continuously adds new classes into an existing field until all (or most)
of the classes are covered. Therefore, we see great potential in the use of
this technique and we expect further improvements in future research.

7.2
Future work
This framework admits many straightforward extensions:
ãÂ ClassôÙincremental learning: The current implementation is trained
with a subset of classes and in case new classes are discovered
and have to be added to the network, the entire network needs to be
retrained to cope with the additional class. Following [18] work, it is
possible to implement a class incremental learning architecture that
allows adding new classes on training time to expand the network
without having to completely retrain the network.
ãÂ Other classifier architectures: The chosen classifier architecture
for this thesis has been the VGG13. Nevertheless, there is a wide
range of other CNN architectures that could also be tested such as
ResNet or the Inception Network. It is also important to note that
the architecture of choice has a high impact on the training routine
since it is trained alongside the auxiliary GAN. This would require
a personalized tuning of hyperparameters for every other classifier
architecture.
ãÂ Implementation on dronesã cameras: The work done in this theôÙ
sis is researchôÙoriented and has not been designed or optimized for
its final implementation in realôÙtime drone camera processing. More
testing and optimization should be done for the such end goal.
ãÂ Efficiency improvements: training could be improved by choosing
a better selection of hyperparameters to coordinate the Generator,
Discriminator, and Classifier during training time.
This thesis has demonstrated the viability of the VGG13+GAN framework
for OutôÙOfôÙDistribution damage detection in civil structures, suggesting these
research directions could prove useful.
