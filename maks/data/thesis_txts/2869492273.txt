Abstract
Recent advancements in transportation services, particularly First- and Last-
Mile (FLM) solutions, offer convenience by providing door-to-door connectiv-
ity. However, these services often overlook broader societal impacts, such as
reductions in physical activity and associated health benefits. This project in-
vestigates the societal welfare implications of introducing FLM public trans-
port services, focusing on the trade-offs between accessibility gains and health-
related externalities from active travel modes like walking. Building on sim-
ulation based methodologies previously applied to synthetic data, this study
evaluates their applicability to real-world data from the Danish National Travel
Survey (TU data). A key contribution is the identification of a 1 km distance
threshold, below which FLM services are unlikely to be welfare-efficient; how-
ever, if FLM services must be introduced for such short distances, making
them free appears to minimize the negative welfare impacts.
By integrat-
ing simulation models and scenario discovery methods, the analysis refines
these thresholds, offering actionable insights for transport planning and pol-
icy, while emphasizing the importance of prioritizing active modes and robust
main transit networks over short-distance FLM services.
Keywords: Transportation · Logit models · First- and last-mile transport · Demand-
responsive services · Cost-benefit analysis · External costs · Transport economics ·
Scenario discovery.

Introduction
Inspired by the simulation study presented in ”Let’s walk! Examining urban first-
and last-mile public transport” ([1]), which generates synthetic data for its analysis,
we became interested in exploring its applicability to real-world data. Consider-
ing the comprehensive analysis conducted in ”Assessing welfare-optimal public
transport fares: Insights from Denmark including environmental costs and health
benefits” ([2]), we decided to utilize the dataset from that study to apply and test
the methodology proposed by Rich [1]. This dataset is derived from real-world
data collected in Denmark through the Danish National Travel Survey (TU data).
The dataset includes a wide range of variables, such as individual travel behav-
ior, accessibility, and costs, which align closely with the synthetic data generated
by Rich [1]. The richness of this dataset makes it particularly valuable for test-
ing the applicability of simulation-based methodologies in a real-world context.
However, working with real-world data introduces several challenges, including
variability, noise, and the need for careful preprocessing to ensure consistency and
comparability with the synthetic data. Despite these challenges, the TU dataset
provides a unique opportunity to validate the findings of ”Let’s walk!” by offering
an empirical basis for assessing the societal welfare contributions of FLM services
in real-world conditions.
More specifically, door-to-door services ([3]), transit feeder systems ([4]), and
first- and last-mile public transport options ([5]; [6]), the primary mobility solu-
tions discussed in this study, have received growing attention in recent years. Col-
lectively referred to as first- and last-mile (FLM) services, these solutions are cat-
egorized into two main types: those that operate on a demand-responsive basis,
providing direct door-to-door connectivity, and those that adhere to predefined
routes with designated stops. While FLM services are often celebrated for their
convenience, especially for specific user groups, they also come with societal costs
that are frequently overlooked. Among these costs are welfare losses due to a de-
cline in physical activity levels among users. Despite the increasing recognition of
the positive health impacts of walking and cycling ([7]; [8]), the broader societal
consequences of reduced physical activity remain insufficiently studied. This gap
underscores the necessity for further research to explore these effects comprehen-
sively.
The objective of this study is to evaluate the societal welfare implications of in-
troducing an FLM service, with particular attention to the positive external health
effects associated with active travel modes. This approach integrates health-related
considerations—specifically, the health benefits of walking—into a broader welfare-
6
==PAGE:10==
economic framework. Using realistic and conservative assumptions about the ac-
cessibility and cost of such services, we analyze the conditions under which FLM
services are likely to fail from a welfare-efficiency perspective. Employing sce-
nario discovery methods ([9]) and simulation approaches, the analysis identifies
a critical distance threshold. In the simulation study by Rich [1], FLM services
fail to provide societal benefits for trips shorter than 1 km, something that is con-
firmed when applying the methodology to real-world data. Additionally, the find-
ings highlight that societal resources would be better utilized in developing public
transport networks that operate beyond the minimum threshold, rather than pri-
oritizing FLM solutions or door-to-door transport services for shorter distances.
Finally, the results emphasize the need for investments in improving access to ac-
tive modes around key transit hubs, as suggested by Ha et al. [10] and Jiang et al.
[11]. These insights contribute to the broader discussion of Mobility-as-a-Service
(MaaS) within Smart City initiatives, particularly how FLM services interact and
compete with active modes and primary transit networks, as also discussed by
Currie [12].
This research is organized into several sections, each addressing a specific as-
pect of the study. Following this introduction, Sect. Literature Review presents
an overview of general literature on transportation models, narrowing the focus
to first- and last-mile (FLM) public transport services and providing the theoreti-
cal and empirical foundation for the study. Sect. Data and Notation describes the
dataset used in the analysis, detailing its sources, variables, and key features, along
with a comparative assessment between synthetic and real-world data. Sect. Method-
ology outlines the modeling framework and methods employed, including the
simulation approach and welfare evaluation techniques. Sect. Simulation Results
presents the findings of the analysis, comparing baseline and alternative scenarios,
and discussing their implications for transportation policy and planning. Sect. Ge-
ographical Distribution of Benefits investigates the geographical distribution of
benefits, focusing on identifying zones in Denmark where FLM services are most
viable and effective. Sect. Summary and Conclusions provides a summary and
conclusions, synthesizing the main findings and their relevance to the study’s ob-
jectives. Finally, Sect. Discussion offers a broader discussion, highlighting the
study’s contributions, limitations, and directions for future research.

2.1
Transportation and Logit Models
This paper draws upon foundational concepts in transportation modeling and
logit models. As a primary reference, the book Transport Models - From Theory to
Practice by Rich [13] serves as a cornerstone for understanding the mathematical
theory and practical application of transportation models. This comprehensive re-
source provides insights into key methodologies that underpin the field.
In addition, significant contributions by Michel Bierlaire have been considered
to complement the theoretical framework. Notable works include A Short Intro-
duction to Biogeme [14], a technical report that introduces Biogeme, an essential
software tool for estimating discrete choice models, and the chapter Discrete Choice
Models from Operations Research and Decision Aid Methodologies in Traffic and Trans-
portation Management [15], which provides a detailed examination of logit mod-
els and their extensions. These resources collectively provide a robust theoretical
foundation for the analysis and modeling conducted in this paper.
2.2
The Scenario Literature
The concept of scenario discovery has become increasingly significant in method-
ological research, with a focus on exploring policies and scenarios that may lead
to adverse outcomes or system failures. Lempert et al. [9] introduced the founda-
tion for ”scenario discovery analysis,” a framework that enables researchers and
policymakers to identify conditions under which policies may underperform or
produce unintended consequences. This approach has since been applied across
8
==PAGE:12==
diverse domains, including climate assessment studies [16], biodiversity research
[17], logistics and transportation systems [18], and land use policies [19].
The methodology facilitates understanding by simulating and analyzing poten-
tial failure regions, offering insights into system vulnerabilities. While large-scale
applications often demand computationally intensive and sophisticated statistical
methods to interpolate solution spaces [20, 21], the study at hand adopts a more
straightforward approach. Drawing on the simplicity of its simulation framework,
it enumerates the solution space efficiently, adhering to predefined assumptions.
The paper “Let’s Walk!” by Rich [1] closely informs this work by emphasiz-
ing the welfare implications of first- and last-mile (FLM) transport services. Rich
evaluates failure regions where such services lead to net societal welfare losses,
particularly due to diminished health benefits associated with reduced walking.
Similarly, the work of Argyros et al. [2] extends this discourse by assessing the
welfare-optimality of public transport fares in Denmark, incorporating environ-
mental costs and health benefits. By adapting the methodological framework of
Rich [1] to real-world datasets, this study evaluates the robustness of theoretical
insights when applied to actual transport scenarios.
By leveraging these methodologies, the current research not only extends the
findings of ”Let’s Walk!” but also assesses their applicability using real-world data,
further validating or challenging the theoretical conclusions presented in these
prior works.
2.3
Health Impacts of Active Travel
Active travel modes, such as walking and cycling, provide substantial health ben-
efits, as consistently highlighted in various studies. For instance, Kelly et al. [22]
conducted a systematic review revealing that cycling and walking significantly re-
duce all-cause mortality, although results vary between studies. Complementing
this, the HEAT (Health Economic Assessment Tool) model, detailed by Breda et al.
[7] and Martin et al. [23], quantifies health benefits for active travelers. According
to this model, an individual cycling 1 km daily at an average speed of 14 km/h
generates an annual health benefit valued at approximately dkk337. This calcula-
tion considers a reduction in premature fatalities by 0.0000800 per kilometer, with
a value of statistical life (VSL) set at dkk4,540,000 per death.
In Denmark, studies have sought to refine these health-related estimates fur-
ther. The application of such findings in infrastructure projects, such as supercycle
highways, has been demonstrated in Rich et al. [8], emphasizing the importance of
health in transport policy evaluation.
9
==PAGE:13==
However, the benefits of active travel are not without associated risks.
In-
creased accident rates, particularly for cyclists, introduce a trade-off that must
be considered in health assessments. The costs of accidents—such as production
losses and treatment expenses—are often underreported for nonfatal incidents and
differ between bicycles and e-bikes. In response to these challenges, updated eval-
uations by Foley et al. [24, 25] link physical activity to reduced risks of chronic
diseases, including cancer, diabetes, and cardiovascular conditions. These find-
ings provide a basis for Danish cost-per-kilometer values, as standardized by the
Ministry of Transport Ministry of Transport [26] for use in infrastructure project
evaluations.
In addition to cycling, the health benefits of walking have also been extensively
analyzed. Walking 1 km daily at a speed of 6 km/h generates benefits similar
to cycling, with an estimated value of dkk337 annually, as noted in Breda et al.
[7]. Such parity between walking and cycling is reflected in Danish transportation
policy practices Ministry of Transport [26]. Despite uncertainties surrounding dif-
ferences in accident risks and external benefits, the collective evidence underscores
the critical role of active travel in promoting public health.
2.4
FLM Literature
The first and last mile (FLM) literature addresses the critical challenge of connect-
ing public transport systems with users’ origins and destinations. A significant
body of research, as summarized in Rich [1], highlights the growing interest in
using FLM solutions as feeder transportation modes. Rich critically examines the
welfare implications of reduced walking, emphasizing the substitution effect that
FLM services often create. Using a simulation model based on Copenhagen, the
study demonstrates that FLM services are frequently welfare-inefficient for short
distances, particularly under 1 km, due to high operational costs and lost health
benefits.

Furthermore, Rich et al. [27] expand this analysis by employing an agent-based
simulation of Copenhagen’s transport network. The study evaluates fixed-route
and demand-responsive FLM services, demonstrating that accounting for health
effects significantly influences the economic feasibility of these systems. Fixed-
route options emerge as more scalable and economically viable compared to demand-
responsive alternatives due to their reduced complexity and operational costs.
Critics of FLM services, such as Currie [12] and Enoch et al. [28], argue that
these systems often fail to deliver scalable and efficient solutions in dense urban
areas. Instead, they advocate for enhanced investment in main transit networks
and active transport options like walking and cycling. Similarly, Ha et al. [10] and
Jiang et al. [11] emphasize the need to integrate active modes with public transport
planning to achieve broader societal benefits.
Other studies, such as Perera et al. [29], note that FLM services can play a critical
role in suburban or low-density areas where active modes and traditional transit
systems are less practical. These studies highlight the importance of considering
the economic viability and regulatory frameworks when implementing FLM so-
lutions. Furthermore, Rich et al. [8] demonstrate that accounting for the external
health benefits of walking and cycling is essential for avoiding welfare losses and
ensuring sustainable urban transport planning.
In summary, the literature underscores the importance of balancing accessibil-

Contribution of the Thesis
The motivation behind this thesis lies in addressing the gap between theoretical
studies and real-world applications of first- and last-mile (FLM) transport solu-
tions. By leveraging real-world data, the study seeks to provide insights into the
societal impacts of FLM services, particularly their trade-offs between accessibility,
health benefits, and operational costs.
This paper contributes to the growing body of research on first- and last-mile
(FLM) transport solutions by addressing critical gaps identified in the literature.
While existing studies, such as Rich [1], focus on synthetic data and theoretical
frameworks, this paper extends these efforts by applying simulation-based method-
ologies to real-world data from Denmark’s National Travel Survey (TU data). The
primary and secondary contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
3.1
Main Contributions
1. Bridging the gap between theoretical models and real-world data: By inte-
grating real-world travel data into the methodological framework proposed
by Rich [1], this study demonstrates the applicability and robustness of simulation-
based methodologies in real-world contexts. This approach highlights the
critical differences between synthetic and real-world data, such as variabil-
ity, noise, and practical constraints, which are often overlooked in theoretical
studies.
2. Evaluation of welfare impacts using real-world data: The study develops
and tests a welfare function that explicitly incorporates the health benefits of
active travel modes, such as walking and cycling, alongside accessibility con-
siderations. By applying this framework to real-world data, it confirms a dis-
tance threshold for FLM service viability at approximately 1 km, as suggested
by Rich [1]. This threshold provides actionable insights for policymakers and
urban planners, emphasizing the importance of prioritizing active modes for
short trips.
3. Comprehensive analysis of FLM services in Denmark: Leveraging the rich

==PAGE:16==
datasets derived from the TU survey and external sources, this paper offers
a detailed evaluation of FLM services, emphasizing their societal costs and
benefits. The analysis underscores the trade-offs between health impacts and
accessibility improvements, providing evidence-based recommendations for
urban transport planning.
3.2
Other Contributions
• Scenario discovery in a real-world context: The application of scenario dis-
covery methodologies [9] to real-world data highlights potential failure re-
gions for FLM services, emphasizing the importance of tailored strategies for
different urban and suburban contexts.
• Insights into active travel benefits: This study reinforces the importance of
walking and cycling in urban mobility, demonstrating their significant health
and societal benefits. By quantifying these benefits using real-world data, it
provides further evidence for prioritizing active modes in transport policy.
• Validation of synthetic data methodologies: By comparing real-world and
synthetic datasets, this paper evaluates the strengths and limitations of syn-
thetic data in representing real-world travel behavior.
This analysis con-
tributes to the ongoing discourse on the reliability and applicability of syn-
thetic data in transport research.

The analysis conducted in this study is based on the scenario presented in Fig-
ure 1, which illustrates a mode-choice comparison scenario inspired by the work
of Rich [1]. This scenario evaluates first- and last-mile (FLM) services in relation to
their impact on accessibility and societal welfare.
The scope of this study is focused on short-distance trips (less than 1 km) within
an urban context, where FLM services are introduced as an alternative to walking
for accessing public transport. Long-distance trips and other access modes (e.g.,
cycling or private shuttles) are beyond the scope of this analysis.
Figure 1 depicts the hierarchical structure of mode-choice decisions, segmented
into two levels. The upper level represents the primary mode of transportation
(Car or Public Transport), while the lower level corresponds to the access mode
(Walking or FLM). This structure reflects the nested logit model used to capture
the interdependencies between main and access modes.
On the left side of the figure, the baseline scenario considers Walking as the
sole access mode for both Car and Public Transport. This configuration reflects the
traditional system where walking is the primary means of accessing main transit
modes. On the right side, the scenario introduces FLM services as an additional ac-
cess option alongside Walking. This represents an alternative system where FLM
services (such as feeder shuttles) are available to users, potentially improving ac-
cessibility but at the cost of reduced physical activity.
While [1] utilized synthetic data to evaluate the welfare implications of such
scenarios, this study extends his framework by applying it to real-world data col-
lected through the Danish National Travel Survey (TU data). This approach allows
for the evaluation of variability and external factors inherent in real-world condi-
tions, offering a more robust validation of the methodology.
The hierarchical structure allows for the evaluation of trade-offs between these
options. The upper-level choice is influenced by generalized travel time, costs,
and preferences for the main mode of transportation, while the lower-level choice
accounts for factors like walking time, accessibility, and the convenience of FLM
services. This approach facilitates a detailed comparison of the baseline and FLM-
enhanced scenarios, enabling the analysis of their respective impacts on accessibil-
ity, travel behavior, and societal welfare.

5.1
Notation and Explanation
The analysis uses several indices and variables to model travel behavior and wel-
fare effects. Let n represent individuals or users making travel decisions [1], i
denote access modes such as walking (i = 1) or feeder modes (i = 2) [9], and j
refer to main transport modes, specifically car (j = 1) or public transport (j = 2).
This notation aligns with standard practices in discrete choice modeling [8].
In the mode choice structure illustrated in Figure 1, j represents the main mode
decision at the upper level, while i represents the access mode decision at the lower
level. In the baseline scenario (left panel), individuals select between car (j = 1)
and public transport (j = 2) as their main mode, with walking (i = 1) as the
sole access mode for public transport. In the alternative scenario (right panel), an
additional feeder mode (i = 2) is introduced, giving individuals a choice between
walking and the feeder mode when accessing public transport (j = 2).
Travel-related variables include TTn,j, the travel time for individual n using
main mode j, Cn,j, the travel cost for individual n using main mode j, and V oTn,
the value of time for individual n (monetized in DKK/min), reflecting the trade-off
between time and cost [30]. These variables capture the fundamental characteris-
tics of main mode trips.
Access modes represent the first leg of a trip, connecting users to the main
transport mode.

5.2
Utility Functions
The utility function for a given alternative describes the overall utility related to
that alternative, which can be a mixture of attributes yielding utility and others

6.1
Data Sources
This study employs a combination of real-world data derived from various offi-
cial and standardized sources to ensure robust and reliable analysis. The primary
data source is the Danish National Travel Survey (TU data), which provides com-
prehensive insights into individual travel behavior, including mode choice, trip
characteristics, and socio-demographic variables. The dataset is further enriched
with additional data on travel costs, travel times, and external costs related to emis-
sions, noise, accidents, health effects, and climate change. These external costs, as
presented in Argyros et al. [2], are based on guidelines from the (Ministry of Trans-
port and Building ) Ministry of Transport and Building [34]. For walking trips, in
the absence of specific Danish recommendations, the external costs are assumed to
align with those for bicycles and e-bicycles, consistent with findings from G¨ossling
et al. [35].
Additional data components include:
• Zonal Data: Provided by the Danish Road Directorate, including travel times,
costs, population figures, and the distribution of workplaces and educational
institutions per zone.
• Employment Data: Derived from the Danish CVR register, which compiles
company-level data submitted monthly to the Danish Tax Agency.
• Vacation Houses: Estimated using data from The Central Register of Build-
ings and Dwellings (BBR).
• Level-of-Service (LOS) Data: Developed for various transport modes (walk-
ing, biking, public transport, car, and air travel) using networks consistent

==PAGE:25==
with the updated GMM model for 2023. LOS data includes travel times and
costs calculated for intra- and inter-zonal trips across 3,677 zones within Den-
mark.
6.2
Descriptive Statistics and Features
The TU dataset is highly detailed, incorporating a wide range of variables such as
trip purpose, age group, accessibility, and trip length. For instance:
• Walking and biking travel times are calculated using dedicated speed models
that account for trip purpose and demographic variability . Walking speeds
are fixed at 5 km/h, and biking speeds vary based on regression analysis.
• External costs are categorized by transport mode and include factors such as
air pollution, climate change, noise, accidents, and health effects [2].

Challenges and Limitations
Using real-world data introduces challenges such as variability, noise, and the need

Application and Case Study
The selected case study focuses on first- and last-mile (FLM) public transport ser-
vices for short trips in urban areas. This case was chosen because it highlights a
critical challenge in urban mobility: balancing accessibility improvements with so-
cietal costs such as reduced physical activity and high operational expenses. As
FLM services are increasingly promoted as a solution to enhance urban accessi-
bility, this case provides a meaningful opportunity to evaluate their true societal
impact using a welfare-economic framework. By addressing this case, the study
establishes a proof of concept that demonstrates how transportation policies can
be assessed holistically, integrating both health and economic metrics.
10
Impact
Investing in first- and last-mile (FLM) public transport services for short distances
presents significant societal, economic, and environmental challenges. While these
services aim to improve accessibility and convenience, they often come at a cost to
public health. By replacing active modes such as walking or cycling, FLM services
reduce physical activity, which can lead to negative health impacts and welfare
37
==PAGE:41==
losses [22, 7]. Furthermore, these services for short trips frequently incur high
operational costs and fail to scale effectively, diverting resources away from more
impactful solutions, such as enhancing mass transit systems or improving access to
transit stations [10, 12]. This misallocation of resources not only creates costly and
inefficient systems but also neglects the broader needs of urban mobility [1, 27].
To address these challenges, this case and the corresponding model play a crit-
ical role by integrating health impacts into the economic assessment of FLM ser-
vices—an aspect that traditional transport planning often overlooks. Through a
welfare-economic framework, the model identifies key thresholds where FLM ser-
vices become inefficient and fail to provide societal benefits [1]. By examining the
trade-offs between accessibility improvements and health losses, this approach of-
fers a more holistic perspective on urban mobility solutions, making it especially
relevant to policymakers, urban planners, and researchers who aim to optimize
public investments in transportation infrastructure.
The study further tells a compelling story of unintended consequences. While
FLM services are often designed to enhance accessibility and convenience, they can
inadvertently do more harm than good for short trips. By replacing walking and
cycling with vehicle-based modes, FLM services undermine the physical health
benefits associated with active transportation. Additionally, their operational in-
efficiencies make them economically unsustainable and socially inequitable. This
research challenges the assumption that FLM solutions are universally beneficial,
advocating instead for a shift in focus toward investments in mass transit and ac-
tive transportation infrastructure, which are more scalable, sustainable, and equi-
table.
In examining the inefficiencies of FLM services, the study addresses a funda-
mental issue in urban mobility: the problem of inefficient resource allocation. By
identifying distance thresholds and conditions under which FLM services fail to
deliver value, it helps policymakers avoid investing in services that lack societal
benefits [1]. This is especially important given the limitations of public funding.
Redirecting resources toward efficient, scalable, and sustainable solutions is crucial
for addressing the mobility needs of growing urban populations while simultane-
ously reducing environmental and health-related costs.
Beyond these findings, the study highlights a broader lesson for transporta-
tion planning. It demonstrates the need to move beyond purely operational and
accessibility metrics by incorporating health impacts into the evaluation process.
By showing the value of promoting active modes like walking and cycling, the
research emphasizes their importance as essential components of urban mobility
[22, 7]. Moreover, it argues that public subsidies should be allocated to solutions
38
==PAGE:42==
that provide broad societal benefits, such as mass transit systems, rather than niche
services that cater to smaller user groups [12, 10].
Ultimately, this research shifts the focus of urban transport studies toward a
more integrated assessment of health, economics, and accessibility. It underscores
the need to evaluate mobility solutions not only in terms of operational perfor-
mance but also through their broader societal impacts, particularly health-related
outcomes.
This expanded perspective has the potential to influence future re-
search, encouraging the development of more comprehensive models and frame-
works for assessing transportation systems in a way that ensures sustainability,
equity, and long-term societal benefits.
11
Summary and Conclusions
This study evaluates the societal and welfare implications of First- and Last-Mile
(FLM) transport services, which aim to improve accessibility in urban areas by
connecting travelers to main transit systems. While these services are often pro-
moted as convenient solutions, the analysis highlights their significant trade-offs,
including reduced physical activity and high operational costs. Using real-world
data from the Danish National Travel Survey and advanced simulation models, the
study identifies key conditions under which FLM services provide societal benefits
and where they fail to deliver meaningful improvements.
The findings reveal that FLM services are unlikely to be welfare-efficient for
very short trips, particularly those under 1 km, where walking remains the most
beneficial mode. In scenarios with higher health benefits (Θ), fewer areas experi-
ence positive consumer surplus (CS), with winning zones primarily concentrated
in urban or highly accessible regions. As Θ increases, rural and poorly connected
areas remain in the losing category, illustrating persistent inequalities in the dis-
tribution of benefits. These results suggest that FLM services often fail to deliver
scalable and sustainable benefits, especially when health impacts and operational
inefficiencies are considered.
Interestingly, the results indicate that introducing feeders is generally detri-
mental to welfare, particularly for shorter trips. However, if feeders must be in-
troduced, offering First- and Last-Mile mobility services for free appears to mit-
igate some of the harm. This approach, though counterintuitive, minimizes the
negative welfare impacts by encouraging usage without adding significant finan-
cial burdens to users. It underscores the inherent inefficiencies of such services,
especially when contrasted with active modes like walking.
An important observation from the results is that FLM services tend to perform
39
==PAGE:43==
better in urban areas with high population density and strong transit accessibility.
These regions consistently show a higher concentration of winning zones. This in-
dicates that FLM services are more suited to environments where the infrastructure
and demand align with their operational efficiencies, emphasizing their potential
as a complementary solution for urban transit systems.
In conclusion, while FLM services may address accessibility for specific groups,
such as the elderly or disabled, their broader societal value is limited. Policymak-
ers are encouraged to prioritize investments in active transport modes like walking
and cycling and in enhancing main transit networks, which offer more sustain-
able, equitable, and scalable solutions. This approach ensures that urban mobility
systems not only meet accessibility needs but also maximize societal welfare and
health outcomes.

and egress distances on travel behavior and welfare outcomes.
The results show notable shifts in welfare outcomes when egress distances are
included.
For shorter distance segments, such as [0, 0.5] km and [0.5, 1] km,
the inclusion of egress distances emphasizes the diminishing welfare benefits as
feeder costs increase. For intermediate and longer distances, the combined dis-

with significant welfare losses observed under high feeder cost scenarios. These
findings underscore the importance of considering the entire transport chain in the
analysis, as neglecting egress distances may underestimate the operational ineffi-
ciencies and broader welfare implications of FLM services. This holistic evaluation
provides valuable insights for policymakers and urban planners aiming to design
equitable and efficient mobility solutions.

12.2
Environmental Cost
The environmental cost of implementing First- and Last-Mile (FLM) services is an
important aspect that requires further attention. While this study focuses on soci-
etal welfare, including the health and accessibility benefits of FLM services, the en-
vironmental impact, particularly in terms of emissions and resource use, remains
unexplored. Vehicle-based FLM services, if not carefully integrated with existing
public transport networks, could lead to higher carbon emissions and increased
pollution, which may counteract the intended benefits of these services.
To ensure a balanced approach, it is essential to include environmental con-
siderations when evaluating FLM services. By assessing the environmental costs,
such as emissions and resource consumption, policymakers can better prioritize
sustainable solutions, such as active transport modes or low-emission options.
This would help align FLM services with broader climate and sustainability goals.
Future research should aim to incorporate these environmental factors, providing
a more comprehensive understanding of the trade-offs and ensuring that FLM ser-
vices contribute positively to urban mobility without harming the environment.
12.3
Future Research
Future research could focus on addressing some of the current limitations by ex-
ploring alternative assumptions and scenarios to better understand the conditions
under which FLM services succeed or fail. Incorporating the entire transport chain
(from home to the final destination), by adding both access and egress distances
would provide a more holistic view of how FLM services integrate within broader
mobility systems, especially regarding their flexibility and timeliness. Addition-
ally, future studies could expand the evaluation framework to include environ-
mental costs, complementing the societal welfare analysis by briefly assessing sus-
tainability factors, such as emissions and resource consumption. This balanced ap-
proach would provide a comprehensive understanding of FLM services’ impacts,
aiding in the design of more effective and sustainable urban mobility policies.
