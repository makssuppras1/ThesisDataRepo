Abstract
The growing transition to renewable energy and electric mobility has increased demand
for essential metals like lithium, nickel, and steel. This thesis compares conventional
and emerging green extraction strategies for these vital metals, focusing on sustainability
parameters such as energy consumption, greenhouse gas emissions, water usage, and
byproduct creation. The study employs a literature­based analytical methodology that
integrates mass and energy flow calculations with life­cycle environmental effect assess­
ments. For each metal, traditional extraction routes—such as the BF­BOF steel process,
sulfide and laterite nickel pathways, and spodumene/brine­based lithium production—are
compared to greener alternatives such as hydrogen­based steelmaking (HYBRIT), low­
emission nickel refining, and electrified kiln systems for lithium are explored. Green solu­
tions minimize CO2 emissions and water usage, but face challenges in energy intensity,
economic feasibility, and process scalability. A case study on sustainable battery de­
sign demonstrates how obtaining materials directly influences the environmental impact
of electric vehicles. The thesis concludes with proposals for improving cleaner metallurgi­
cal methods, emphasizing the role of policy, innovation, and lifecycle thinking in designing
a sustainable metal extraction future.

Introduction
1.1
Global Energy Landscape and Environmental Challenges
Throughout history, economic and technical growth has been closely linked to rising en­
ergy consumption. As societies industrialize, energy consumption rises to meet the in­
creasing demands of transportation, manufacturing, digital infrastructure, and modern liv­
ing conditions. While energy­driven progress has greatly aided human development, it
has also resulted in enormous environmental devastation. Currently, traditional fossil fu­
els like coal, oil, and natural gas account for around 80% of global energy supply. The
use of carbon­based fuels has resulted in significant pollution, including CO2, SOx, NOx,
and particulate matter [1].
According to the International Energy Agency, worldwide energy­related CO2 emissions
exceeded 3.31 billion tons in 2018, the highest level ever recorded [1]. Unchecked emis­
sions cause global warming, sea level rise, extreme weather events, biodiversity loss,
and public health issues, all of which endanger ecosystems and socioeconomic stability.
Recognizing the gravity of the climate catastrophe, the international community has adopted
a number of cooperative frameworks, most notably the Paris Agreement, which com­
mits signatory countries to keeping global temperature rise to well below 2°C above pre­
industrial levels, with efforts to restrict it to 1.5°C. To achieve these objectives, consider­
able reductions in greenhouse gas emissions are required across all sectors, particularly
in energy and industry, the two greatest sources [2].
1.2
The Role and Rise of Renewable Energy
Renewable energy has emerged as an important part of the global energy shift. By 2020,
global installed renewable energy capacity reached 2,799 GW, with hydropower account­
ing for 1,211 GW (43%), followed by wind (733 GW, 26%) and solar PV (714 GW, 26%).
Notably, solar PV and wind saw record capacity additions in 2020, recovering strongly
from the COVID­19 delays. Despite this improvement, fossil fuels continue to dominate,
accounting for more than 65% of worldwide energy generation and fueling both climate
change and socioeconomic injustice [3].
Renewable energy provides numerous benefits, including reduced GHG emissions, im­
proved energy security, and major contributions to achieving the Sustainable Develop­
ment Goals (SDGs). Research indicates that renewable energy can support up to 50% of
economic SDG targets, 45% of environmental goals, and 41% of social indicators. How­
ever, the rise of RE creates economic and technical obstacles. These include concerns
about energy affordability, infrastructure integration, voltage and frequency stability, and
overall power quality. Addressing these constraints is critical for guaranteeing environ­
mental sustainability and system resilience [3].
RE not only reduces CO2 emissions, but also offers societal and economic benefits.
These include improved energy security, lower health risks from air pollution, and con­
tributions to job development and economic resilience. In 2019, the renewable sector
employed almost 11.5 million people globally, with solar PV accounting for 33% of total
RE employment [3].
However, the transition is not without its problems. Technical constraints such as voltage
and frequency stability, grid integration, and power quality must be addressed to ensure

infrastructure, and affordability continue to impede equal deployment, especially in de­
veloping nations. Furthermore, while RE currently accounts for 29% of worldwide power

1.3
Green Hydrogen Integration
Green hydrogen, a versatile energy carrier created through electrolysis from renewable
sources, is a promising supplement to renewable electricity. Green hydrogen is a zero­
emission alternative to grey or blue hydrogen, which are created from fossil fuels and
emit significant CO2 [4]. The decarbonization of the global economy is one of the most
pressing issues of our day, with many governments setting lofty targets for 2050 to tackle
climate change. Green hydrogen is a viable option at the forefront of this energy shift,
produced by the electrolysis process using renewable­source electricity. Unlike traditional
hydrogen manufacturing processes, which emit enormous amounts of carbon dioxide,
green hydrogen is a clean energy source that emits no greenhouse gasses. Adopting
green hydrogen at scale may reduce CO2 emissions by up to 830 million tons per year,
making it a key technology for a sustainable future, according to the International Energy
Agency [5].
Replacing coal with hydrogen generated from renewable energy provides a major chance
to decarbonize the steel industry, which is one of the most carbon­intensive sectors in the
world. Although the technology to replace coal with hydrogen in steel production is widely
understood in concept, current pilot projects are paving the way for refinement and scale
of these processes. Initially, this change will raise the cost of steel production by about
one­third per ton, but the cost disparity is predicted to shrink dramatically over the next
few years. By 2030, rising carbon pricing and emissions laws, together with dropping
renewable electricity costs, breakthroughs in hydrogen production efficiency, and process
optimisations, might eliminate this gap entirely [6].
Achieving full decarbonisation of the steel sector with green hydrogen will necessitate
a significant increase in electricity generation—estimated at 20% over existing levels—
highlighting the necessity for a rapid and ambitious expansion of renewable energy in­
frastructure. Furthermore, large­scale hydrogen production plants provide a dual benefit:
they provide clean fuel for industrial use while also improving power system reliability.
These facilities can operate flexibly, creating hydrogen during periods of excess electric­
ity, thereby balancing supply and demand on the grid and maximizing the use of renewable
energy [6].
1.4
Material Demand and the Hidden Footprint of Clean
Energy
While renewable technologies minimize operational emissions, widespread adoption cre­
ates new environmental challenges, particularly upstream in the raw material supply chain.
Wind turbines, solar panels, electric vehicle batteries, and electrolyzers all require enor­
mous amounts of key minerals, including nickel, lithium, copper, cobalt, aluminum, and
graphite. A recent analysis published in Nature Communications found that the mining
industry accounts for around 10% of worldwide energy­related CO2 emissions [7].
The World Bank estimates that to achieve the low­carbon infrastructure required to limit
global warming to 2°C, mining of minerals like graphite and lithium will need to expand by
approximately 500% by 2050—even after accounting for aggressive recycling scenarios

[7]. This paradox—in which efforts to minimize emissions through clean technologies may
unintentionally worsen environmental deterioration through raw material sourcing—is one
of the most pressing concerns of the energy transition. As a result, understanding and
minimizing the environmental impact of material manufacturing is critical for sustainable
growth.
Steel, the most frequently used engineering material globally, is mainly manufactured
using the carbon­intensive blast furnace­basic oxygen furnace (BF­BOF) process, which
accounts for roughly 7% of direct industrial CO2 emissions worldwide [8]. Nickel, which
is essential for stainless steel and electric vehicle batteries, is mined from sulfide and
laterite ores using energy­ and emissions­intensive methods. Lithium, which is essential
for modern battery technologies, is obtained by hard rock mining and brine evaporation,
both of which require a lot of water and are limited in some regions. Traditional extraction
of these resources is naturally associated with substantial carbon emissions, complex
waste streams, and significant energy requirements.
Green extraction technologies represent a promising but challenging alternative to tra­
ditional mining and refining methods. Hydrogen­based steelmaking, like HYBRIT, has
the potential to reduce CO2 emissions by over 90% compared to BF­BOF. However, it
faces scalability and cost issues. Lower­emission options to nickel processing, such as
bioleaching, is being tested to reduce both carbon output and chemical use. Electrify­
ing spodumene manufacturing leads to less CO2 emissions and Direct lithium extraction
(DLE), which is emerging as a novel approach for lithium brines, promises lower water
use and higher recovery rates, although its economic feasibility remains limited.
Assessing the sustainability of extraction technologies requires a lifecycle approach, in­
corporating metrics such as energy consumption (MWh per ton), water use (tons per ton),
and CO2­equivalent emissions (t CO2­eq per ton). This thesis applies such a multi­criteria
analytical framework to compare conventional and green pathways for extracting lithium,
nickel, and steel, integrating mass and energy flow analysis with environmental impact as­
sessment. Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions—as defined by the GHG Protocol—are included
to account for both direct and indirect environmental burdens.
Furthermore, material sourcing for electric vehicles (EVs) has a significant impact on the
end product’s lifetime emissions, making sustainable extraction a necessary condition for
true decarbonization in transportation. While green pathways provide major environmen­
tal benefits, they also bring problems in terms of energy intensity, capital cost, and process
maturity, necessitating a careful consideration of trade­offs. Policy incentives, regulatory
frameworks, and technology innovation will all play an important role in allowing the in­
dustrial transition to green metallurgy.
1.5
Aim of the Thesis
This thesis addresses this developing problem by comparing the environmental impact
of traditional and green extraction paths for three important elements utilized in EV bat­
tery systems: steel, nickel, and lithium. The objective is to examine key environmental
indicators, including CO2 emissions, energy consumption, and resource efficiency, us­
ing mass balance­based calculations informed by the most recent available data. Where
there were gaps in the literature, estimates were supplied with generative techniques such
as ChatGPT.
A unique aspect of this study is the calculation of the carbon cost per electric vehicle (EV)
under both traditional and green sourcing scenarios. By converting process emissions
into economic terms using a standardized carbon price, the study provides a concrete

The thesis’s goal with this analysis is to inform about the potential of green extraction

2
Methodology
This thesis uses a literature­based, technical analytical method to evaluate current and
emerging extraction strategies for major transition metals, including steel, nickel, and
lithium. The methodology assesses the environmental impact of both conventional and
green metallurgical processes by combining mass and energy flow calculations, emission
estimation, and comparative assessment methods.
2.1
Research Design and Approach
The study is based on secondary data from peer­reviewed literature, technical studies
(e.g., IEA, Eurostat), industry publications (e.g., Steel.org), and environmental databases.
A combined qualitative­quantitative framework is employed, which includes:
• Descriptive analysis of each extraction method.
• Quantitative modeling for energy consumption, mass balance, and CO2 emissions.
• A comparison of traditional and emerging green technology.
This comprehensive approach enables cross­process comparison and sustainability as­
sessment under various operational settings (e.g., energy sources, ore grade, process
design).
2.2
Tools for Data Visualization
Diagrams and flow charts are important visualization tools that show mass and energy
inputs/outputs, as well as essential processing phases. These methods improve the un­
derstanding of system boundaries, emission points, and process interactions.
2.3
System Boundaries
The study specifies system limits based on either cradle­to­gate or cradle­to­grave views,
depending on the metal
• Steel: It covers the entire Blast Furnace­Basic Oxygen Furnace (BF­BOF) produc­
tion chain, including iron ore preparation, sintering, coke production, blast furnace
operation, basic oxygen steelmaking, and casting. Finishing activities (e.g., rolling,
coating) are also included when it comes to environmental impact.
• Nickel processing is modeled using pyrometallurgy and HPAL for both sulfide and
laterite ore deposits: Sulfide ores are beneficiated, roasted, smelted, converted,
and refined by electrorefining or carbonyl processes. Laterite ores include High­
Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL), as well as neutralization, precipitation, and inter­
mediate product recovery processes. The system boundary includes provisions for
treating process water, tailings, and sulfur emissions.
• Lithium: The analysis comprises both hard rock (spodumene) and brine­based ex­
tractions. The hard rock (spodumene) process includes mining, crushing, bene­
ficiation, calcination (α to β conversion), acid roasting, leaching, purification, and
precipitation of lithium compounds.
Brine evaporation: This process includes
brine extraction, pond evaporation, impurity elimination, chemical precipitation, and
lithium carbonate or hydroxide purification. The energy usage, water consumption,
and environmental impact of both routes are all mentioned.

2.6.1
Carbon Accounting Boundaries: Scope 1, 2, and 3 Emissions
To assess the environmental impact of steel, nickel and lithium processing routes, this
study follows the Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Protocol’s classification of emissions into three
categories: Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 [9].
Scope 1 emissions are direct greenhouse gas outputs from sources that are owned or
controlled by the reporting entity. This includes emissions from on­site combustion of
fossil fuels in calcination kilns, smelters, or diesel generators used in mining operations

[9]. For example, CO2 released during the conversion of α­spodumene to β­spodumene
or from fuel­fired roasting of nickel sulfide ores falls under Scope 1.
Scope 2 emissions represent indirect emissions from the generation of purchased elec­
tricity, heat, or steam consumed by the company. These are relevant for all electrically
powered processes, such as grinding, flotation, leaching, or electrorefining [9]. The car­
bon intensity of Scope 2 depends heavily on the grid mix or power purchase agreements
used to supply the electricity [10].
Scope 3 emissions include all other indirect emissions that occur along the company’s
value chain. This includes upstream activities such as the production of chemical reagents
(e.g., sulfuric acid, sodium carbonate), mining and transport of raw materials, and down­
stream activities like product distribution and end­of­life treatment [11]. In most metal
supply chains, Scope 3 emissions typically account for the majority of the total carbon
footprint—sometimes exceeding 70% of the total [12].
By categorizing emissions into these scopes, this methodology enables a more com­
plete and transparent assessment of the environmental burden across different lithium
and nickel production pathways.
2.7
Data Sources and Calculation Approach
A literature­based analytical approach was used to assess mass balances, energy con­

pathways. Data were gathered mostly from peer­reviewed scientific journals, industrial re­
ports, and respected databases such as the International Energy Agency (IEA), Eurostat,
and environmental lifecycle evaluations. When precise numerical figures (for example,
material inputs per ton of output, emission factors, or energy intensities) were not imme­

methodological integrity, all final values were validated for plausibility and compared to

2.8

While this study provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of traditional and green
extraction pathways for steel, nickel, and lithium, several limitations must be acknowl­
edged:
• Limited Economic Modeling: A full economic evaluation, including capital expen­

technologies mature and more cost data becomes publicly available.
• Carbon Price–Only Economic Metric: The only economic analysis included re­

ton). This serves as a proxy to quantify the potential carbon­related cost savings of
green extraction routes, but does not account for broader financial or market­based
factors.

but not deeply analyzed. These co­products can influence both the environmental

3
Current Extraction Technologies
The extraction of crucial raw materials is the foundation of modern industrial systems,
especially given the global energy shift and rising demand for electric vehicles, renew­
able energy technologies, and high­performance electronics. Metals such as steel, nickel
and lithium are particularly important because of their functional qualities and uses in bat­
teries, structural materials, and other critical components. As worldwide manufacturing
grows to meet demand, understanding the present status of extraction technologies is
critical for assessing not only technical efficiencies but also environmental and economic
implications.
This chapter explores the primary industrial techniques used to extract and purify impor­
tant metals from their ores. The analysis contains a breakdown of the process steps,
input­output mass balances, energy requirements, and emissions for each major route.
Traditional procedures such as pyrometallurgy and hydrometallurgy receive special atten­
tion, as do process­specific operations such as flotation, roasting, smelting, converting,
and electrorefining.
It also provides a comprehensive overview of how raw materials are transformed into
usable metal products by examining extraction technologies for sulfide and laterite nickel
ores, lithium from spodumene and brines, and steel production from iron ores. These
insights set the basis for understanding the environmental trade­offs of current systems
and help understand the materials industry’s push toward more sustainable, low­carbon
alternatives.
3.1
Current Extraction Technologies of Steel
Steel is an alloy made mostly of iron and carbon, with the carbon percentage often reach­
ing 2%. Above this level, the material is classified as cast iron. Steel is the most often
used engineering material due to its remarkable strength­to­weight ratio, adaptability, and
low production costs. It serves as the foundation for infrastructure, transportation, indus­
try, and energy systems. In 2013, worldwide steel production reached over 1.6 billion
tons, underlining its supremacy over other engineering metals such as aluminum, which
only reached around 47 million tons in the same year [13].
Steel’s characteristics are greatly influenced by its internal structure and carbon con­
tent. Pure iron is soft and flexible, but adding carbon and other alloying components
changes the mechanical properties of steel dramatically. Steel structures can include fer­
rite, austenite, pearlite, bainite, and martensite, depending on heat treatment and com­
position. Steel is a uniquely tunable material due to the capacity of iron to exist in dif­
ferent crystalline forms (allotropy) — body­centered cubic (BCC) at lower temperatures
and face­centered cubic (FCC) at higher temperatures. Carbon content and cooling rate
during production have a significant impact on the hardness and strength of the finished
steel product [13].
Steel is widely classified into three varieties based on its chemical composition: carbon
steel, low­alloy steel, and high­alloy (typically stainless steel). Carbon steel is the most
common, and it is further classified as high, medium, and low­carbon steels based on car­
bon content. Alloy steels, which contain chromium, nickel, molybdenum, and vanadium,
are designed for specific purposes such as high­strength structures, corrosion resistance,
10
Sustainable Extraction of Critical Metals
==PAGE:20==
and high­temperature performance. Stainless steels, for example, include at least 10.5%
chromium to generate a protective oxide layer that offers corrosion resistance [13].
Modern steel grades are designed for specific industrial purposes, ranging from wear­
resistant steels in mining equipment to high­strength low­alloy (HSLA) steels in pipelines
and automobile frames. Electrical steels are formulated with a high silicon content for
usage in transformers and motors, whereas tool steels are developed for exceptional
hardness and wear resistance. Steel’s versatility extends to high­temperature conditions,
where nickel or cobalt superalloys (with little iron) are chosen for applications such as
jet engines. These specific steels highlight how metallurgy is always evolving to meet
changing performance and environmental demands [13].
3.1.1
Steel Extraction Process
The traditional steel production route is based on the blast furnace­basic oxygen furnace
(BF­BOF) process, which uses coal (in the form of coke) as both an energy source and
a reducing agent. The steelmaking process begins with the extraction of raw materials,
which are mainly iron ore, coal, and limestone. Iron ore, generally known as hematite
or magnetite, is the major source of iron. Coking coal is required to generate the high
temperatures required to reduce iron ore, whilst limestone functions as a flux, aiding in
the removal of impurities such as silica and sulfur during smelting. These minerals are
mined and transported to integrated steel factories, where they are transformed into steel
[14].
This section describes the current industrial steel production process, with a focus on
mass and energy balances at each major stage—iron ore preparation, sintering, blast
furnace operation, basic oxygen steelmaking, and casting. It examines the significance
of essential additives like fluxes and lime in encouraging chemical reactions and slag
production, as well as the accompanying emissions, specifically CO2. Understanding this
process is critical for assessing the environmental baseline against which developing low­
carbon alternatives like hydrogen­based direct reduced iron (H­DRI) can be evaluated.

3.1.2
Process description
Coke Production
The first step in conventional steelmaking is to transform coal into coke, which is a criti­
cal input for the blast furnace. Bituminous coal is crushed and burned in coke ovens at
980°C without oxygen for 18 to 24 hours [16]. During this process, volatile components
like tar, ammonia, hydrogen, sulfur, and hydrocarbons are removed, leaving behind coke,
a hard, porous, carbon­rich substance. Coke, which typically contains 90% to 93% car­
bon, serves as both a fuel and a reducing agent as well as structural support within the
blast furnace. Its high carbon content and hardness make it perfect for withstanding the
enormous temperatures required to convert iron ore to molten iron [15].
Sintering
Due to its low permeability, iron ore, particularly in small particle form, is unsuitable for
direct use in the blast furnace. To solve this, sintering is used to collect fine iron ores
into bigger, porous clumps known as sinter. In a sintering facility, iron­bearing fines are
combined with fluxes such as limestone and recovered resources like iron­rich dust from
air pollution control systems. This combination is heated just below the melting point,
causing the particles to partially fuse into porous agglomerates (assemblages of particles
that are found in dry powders and powders in liquid suspensions[17]). The resulting sinter
enhances air flow and lowers pressure drop in the blast furnace, increasing fuel efficiency
and output. Sintering also promotes recycling inside the steel factory, which reduces
waste and raw material consumption [16].
Figure 3.2: Sintering Process
Blast Furnace Operation
The blast furnace is the core of conventional steel production. It is a huge vertical shaft
lined with refractory bricks into which raw materials—primarily sinter, solid iron ore, coke,
and limestone—are charged from above.
Hot air heated to 1500–2200°C is injected
through the furnace’s tuyeres [14]. The air combines with coke to produce CO, which
lowers iron oxides (Fe2O3 or Fe3O4) to molten metallic iron. Simultaneously, impurities in
the ore, like silica (SiO2), react with lime (CaO) to generate a separate molten slag layer,
which floats atop the denser iron and is periodically removed. The molten iron—known
as pig iron—contains 3–4.5% carbon and is soft, requiring further refinement [16].

Basic Oxygen Steelmaking (BOF)
In the following step, molten pig iron is transformed into steel in a Basic Oxygen Furnace
(BOF). The pig iron is placed into a lined converter, together with up to 30% scrap steel, to
absorb excess heat and reduce carbon content[15]. Pure oxygen is then supersonically
delivered through a lance into the molten metal. This oxidizes contaminants like carbon,
silicon, and phosphorus, which are then eliminated by slag formation. Lime and dolomite
are added to help create slag[14]. The end result is low­carbon steel, which typically
contains less than 0.3% carbon. Depending on the desired qualities, alloying elements
like as nickel, manganese, or chromium may be added at this step[19].

Casting & Solidification
After refining, molten steel must be formed into useful forms. Most current facilities use
continuous casting, in which molten steel runs into water­cooled copper molds and so­
lidifies as it advances downhill in a continuous strand [14]. This strand is cut into typical
shapes such slabs for flat items, billets for long products, and blooms for structural pur­
poses. When compared to traditional ingot casting, continuous casting minimizes waste,
boosts yield, and productivity [15].
Figure 3.5: Casting Process [21]
Forming and Finishing
The semi­finished steel shapes from the caster are mechanically processed in rolling
mills. Hot rolling involves heating steel over its recrystallization temperature and passing
it through rollers to reduce thickness and increase mechanical qualities. This is commonly
applied to structural beams, railroads, and sheet metal. Cold rolling at or near room tem­
perature produces a higher surface polish, tighter tolerances, and enhanced toughness.
After rolling, steel can be finished with pickling (to remove oxides), galvanizing (zinc coat­
ing for corrosion resistance), heat treating (to modify strength and toughness), or paint
and polymer coatings [14].

Nickel is a silvery­white metal that is robust, ductile, and oxidation­resistant [7]. Nickel
is a critical industrial metal utilized in the production of over 300,000 items in over 3,000
alloy formulas, proving its broad adaptability and strategic relevance in the global econ­

The stainless steel sector consumes the most nickel, accounting for over 65% of total
primary nickel use in 2023, including nearly 972,000 tonnes obtained from stainless scrap
recycling. Batteries, particularly lithium­ion batteries for electric cars, were the second­

High­temperature nickel­based superalloys are essential in the aerospace, petrochemical,

3.2.1
Nickel Extraction Process from Sulfide Ores
Beneficiation
Nickel extraction from sulfide ores begins with beneficiation, which is a vital step in upgrad­
ing the ore and concentrating nickel­bearing minerals while eliminating gangue elements
like quartz and silicates. Typically, the ore is crushed and processed to get adequate
mineral release. The ground slurry is next treated to froth flotation, in which chemical
reagents (collectors and frothers) and air bubbles preferentially attach to nickel and cop­
per sulfides, causing them to float away from the less valuable minerals. Depending
on the ore and process parameters, the resultant concentrate can contain 5% to 25%
nickel [32]. Magnetic separation is commonly used before or after flotation to minimize
pyrrhotite, a nickel­containing iron sulfide that contributes significantly to SO2 emissions
during smelting [33].

Roasting
The roasting stage, while optional, can be used to pre­treat the concentrate by partially
oxidizing sulfide minerals and eliminating some of the sulfur. This decreases sulfur diox­
ide emissions during the following stage—smelting—and also alters mineral structure to
improve process efficiency [33].
Smelting
Following roasting (if applicable), the concentrate is transferred to smelting, which is the
main thermal phase in nickel sulfide processing. The purpose is to create a nickel matte,
which is a molten mixture of nickel, iron, and sulfur. There are two basic smelting methods:
flash smelting, which injects finely dried concentrate into a heated reaction chamber and
oxidizes it in seconds, and bath smelting, which feeds the concentrate into a furnace
and reacts more slowly. At 1,200­1,550°C, both processes produce a nickel­iron matte
containing 25­45% nickel and slag made of silicates and SO2 gas [35]. The slag, which
forms when silica combines with oxidized iron, is less dense and can be skimmed off the
molten matte because to its buoyancy [36].
Converting
After smelting, the matte is converted by blowing it through converters with oxygen and
silica flux to oxidize residual sulfur and iron. Sulfur is emitted as SO2, and iron produces
new slag with silica. This stage elevates the nickel concentration of the matte to 70­75%,
and the result is known as the Bessemer matte or white matte, which is appropriate for
the final refining [35]. The smelting and conversion steps are energy­intensive and emit
high levels of CO2 and SO2, causing environmental and regulatory concerns [36].

Refining
The last process is refining, which turns the high­grade matte into high­purity nickel. This
can be accomplished through a variety of methods. Electrorefining is a popular process in
which the matte is dissolved in an acid solution (usually sulfate­based) and pure nickel is

temperatures to give 99.99% nickel. Although successful, this procedure includes the
handling of dangerous gasses, requiring extreme safety precautions [35]. In addition,
some operations use ammonia leaching, in which nickel is dissolved under pressure and
recovered using hydrogen reduction, with byproducts such as ammonium sulfate used as
fertilizer [36].

Nickel Extraction from Laterite Ores
Nickel laterite ores are formed by prolonged tropical weathering of ultramafic rocks such
as peridotite and serpentine, which results in the leaching of mobile elements such as
24

silicon and magnesium while enriching immobile elements such as iron, aluminum, and
nickel in the soil profile [41]. This geological transition produces stratified deposits divided
into three types: limonite (iron oxide­rich), nontronite (clay­rich), and saprolite (silicate­
rich) ores [42]. Mineralogy and chemistry differ between subtypes; for example, limonite
ores contain primarily goethite and hematite, whereas saprolites have more serpentine
and chlorite minerals [36]. These compositional differences determine the optimal extrac­
tion technology.
Figure 3.11: Profiles of silicate (saprolite), clay and limonite (oxide) nickel laterite de­

Laterite ores, unlike sulfide ores, are not suited for traditional flotation because to the
fine dispersion of nickel in oxide matrix [41]. As a result, they require metallurgical ex­
traction using either hydrometallurgical procedures such as High­Pressure Acid Leaching
(HPAL) or pyrometallurgical techniques such as ferronickel smelting [42]. Limonite ores
are usually processed with HPAL, which dissolves nickel and cobalt by reacting crushed
ore with concentrated sulfuric acid at high pressure (30­50 atm) at temperatures between
250 and 270°C. When the solution pH is carefully regulated, impurities such as iron and
aluminum precipitate out as hydroxides, allowing nickel and cobalt to be recovered as
mixed hydroxide or sulphide intermediates [36].
High­Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL)
HPAL is highly effective, with recovery rates of 90­95% for nickel and cobalt, but it is
capital­intensive and operationally demanding due to the requirement for corrosion­resistant
autoclaves composed of titanium­palladium alloys [42]. Furthermore, the process pro­
duces a large amount of acidic tailings—up to 150­200 tons per ton of nickel produced—
which must be properly managed to minimize environmental damage [44]. Despite these
obstacles, HPAL is still the preferred process for treating low­magnesium (<5%) limonite
ores.

Solid Liquid Separation
Following leaching, the resulting slurry comprises both dissolved metal ions and insoluble
residue. To extract the valuable nickel­bearing solution (pregnant leach solution or PLS),
solid­liquid separation is required. Filtration or thickening procedures are used, with effec­
tiveness greatly impacted by particle size distribution and suspension chemistry. Effective
separation is critical for ensuring clarity and throughput, while poor separation could hin­
der downstream processing [46].
Netralization and Prcipation
Neutralization is used to eliminate any leftover acidic species and precipitate contami­
nants. Alkaline reagents such as sodium hydroxide or lime are used to change the pH,
allowing dissolved iron and aluminum to be removed as solid hydroxides [47]. Recent ad­
vances, such as the application of hematite­based additions during neutralization, have
enhanced settling properties and solution clarity, facilitating downstream processing [46].
Nickel Recovery
Nickel is often recovered through precipitation from purified leachate. This is frequently
accomplished by altering pH and adding precipitating agents to produce compounds such
as nickel carbonate or mixed hydroxides. Fluidized­bed reactors are preferred in modern
operations because they have better precipitation kinetics and product quality than previ­
ous hydroxide sludge systems [48].

Current Extraction Technologies of Lithium (Spodumene

Lithium (Li) is a soft, silvery­white alkali metal and the lightest solid element, falling within
Group 1 of the periodic table. Johan August Arfwedson discovered lithium in 1817, and
it can be found in pegmatite ores such as spodumene and lepidolite, as well as brine
deposits and mineral springs. It accounts for around 0.002% of Earth’s crust. Major
global producers include Australia, Chile, China, and Argentina, with Bolivia having the
most reserves. Lithium is often recovered as lithium carbonate (Li2CO3 ), which can be
transformed to lithium chloride (LiCl) for metal manufacture through electrolysis. The
metal is appreciated for its low density, strong electrochemical potential, and distinctive
features that set it apart from other alkali metals [49].

3.3.1
Lithium from Hard Rock (Spodumene)
Lithium extraction from spodumene, a lithium­aluminum inosilicate mineral with the for­
mula LiAl(SiO3)2, is the basis of hard rock lithium production, contributing significantly to
world supply, notably for high­purity, battery­grade lithium compounds [51]. The process
begins with mining spodumene­bearing ore, followed by beneficiation procedures like as
crushing, grinding, and froth flotation to separate lithium­rich minerals like α­spodumene
[52]. Magnetic separation is sometimes used to improve mineral purity before chemi­
cal treatment begins [53]. To convert α­spodumene to its more reactive polymorph, β­
spodumene, a high­temperature calcination process at around 1050°C is required [54].

Calcination
Calcination, a high­temperature treatment that causes chemical or physical changes in
solid materials, is critical in spodumene processing because it facilitates two important
steps: decrepitation and acid roasting [54]. The term ”calcination” refers to thermal disso­
ciation procedures, such as carbonate breakdown or mineral phase conversion, that are
carried out under regulated atmospheres to maximize chemical reactivity. Calcination
causes decrepitation in spodumene treatment, causing the ore’s monoclinic α­form struc­
ture to fracture and expand when heated, resulting in the more leachable β­form. This
shift is crucial as the β­form structure makes lithium ions more accessible for chemical
extraction through acid roasting [51].

Decrepitation
Decrepitation occurs at elevated temperatures ranging from 1075°C to 1100°C, a care­
fully controlled range to avoid eutectic reactions, which can occur if the temperature ap­
proaches 1400°C and result in undesirable bonding between spodumene and other sil­
icates, lowering lithium recovery efficiency [54]. A direct­fired rotary kiln is commonly
employed for this step because it can maintain these high temperatures while letting the
ore to move continually through the system via gravity and rotational motion, providing
complete thermal exposure [53]. The heat treatment extends the spodumene crystal lat­
tice by about 30%, enhancing its reactivity to sulfuric acid in future phases [52].
Acid Roasting
After successful decrepitation, the β­spodumene undergoes a subsequent calcination
stage known as acid roasting. The lithium­enriched material is intimately mixed with con­
centrated sulfuric acid (about 93%) and subjected to temperatures around 250°C [51].
Sulfuric acid digestion produces Li2SO4, which is extremely soluble in water and ideal
for leaching [53]. To prevent contamination from gaseous byproducts, β­spodumene is
treated using an indirect­fired kiln or calciner. The material is heated indirectly via the
revolving kiln’s external shell, which ensures uniform thermal exposure and reaction com­
pletion[54].
Acid roasting generates lithium sulfate Li2SO4, which is subsequently leached with water
at ambient temperature using a solid­to­liquid ratio of 1:1.85, yielding a crude Li2SO4 solu­
tion [52]. The solution is then neutralized with lime Ca(OH)2 or calcium carbonate CaCO3
to eliminate residual acids and metal impurities such as iron, aluminum, magnesium, and
calcium [51].
After purification, sodium carbonate Na2CO3 is added to precipitate lithium carbonate
Li2CO3, while sodium sulfate Na2SO4 remains dissolved in the aqueous phase and is
typically collected as waste or subjected to additional treatment [53].

Water Usage
Water is a critical resource in the lithium extraction process, and con­
sumption patterns change dramatically between phases. The leaching stage requires
roughly 5 tons of water per ton of lithium sulfate (Li2SO4) to dissolve it from the roasted
spodumene matrix[60]. Beneficiation consumes around 2.0 tons/ton of water, largely for
flotation, slurry conveyance, and particle separation. Tailings retention causes significant
losses. Minor water inputs are documented during mining, acid roasting, and calcination,
primarily for dust control and cooling [62]. In purification, an additional 1.0 tons/ton is
required to hydrate lime and maintain process pH. Water is lost by evaporation, reten­
tion in solid waste streams (e.g., leached spodumene residue, sludge), and assimilation
into process solutions. Managing and recycling process water is critical for sustainability,
especially in dry regions where spodumene facilities are commonly found.
In comparison to earlier stages, water is used sparingly during precipitation. However, it
is still required for dissolving sodium carbonate, preserving fluidity in the lithium sulfate
solution, and aiding the reaction kinetics at around 60°C. Additional water may be used
to wash and filter the lithium carbonate product in order to achieve battery­grade purity.
As with previous stages, effective water recycling and treatment are critical, especially in
dry places where spodumene operations are increasingly concentrated [62].

3.3.3
Lithium from Brine Evaporation
Lithium is extracted using two methods: hard rock mining and brine extraction. To sepa­
rate lithium, hard rock mining often involves spodumene­bearing ores and uses energy­
intensive operations such as drilling, blasting, crushing, calcination, and chemical treat­
ment. Although effective, this technology has significant environmental consequences,
such as land disturbance, greenhouse gas emissions, and excessive energy and water
usage. In contrast, brine mining is a more sustainable and more popular alternative, par­
ticularly in areas with abundant subsurface lithium waters. This technology allows access
to naturally occurring saline groundwater in areas such as salt flats, geothermal reservoirs,
and oil fields. These waters include lithium in its ionic form, which is dissolved in the brine
and therefore more accessible than lithium bound in solid mineral formations.Brine ex­
traction is a more environmentally responsible option in the global lithium supply chain
since it is minimally invasive, has less surface damage, and requires less energy [56].

Brine, sometimes known as generated water, is a byproduct of oil and gas production. It
contains geologic formation water, injection water, oil, and salts [66]. Brine is defined as
a concentrated saline solution containing a variety of dissolved minerals and salts, such
as lithium, magnesium, boron, and potassium. Naturally occurring lithium­rich brines can
be found beneath arid places such as the salt flats of South America’s ”Lithium Triangle,”
which includes Argentina, Bolivia, and Chile. These salars are ancient lakebeds that have
seen substantial natural evaporation, concentrating lithium in underlying reservoirs. Other
notable sources are continental brines found in closed­basin saline lakes with no outflow,
which allow lithium concentrations to accumulate over thousands of years. Geothermal
brines, which are accessed during geothermal energy generation, provide a potential to
produce sustainable energy as well as important minerals. Furthermore, oilfield brines,
which were formerly considered waste from fossil fuel production, are now being inves­
tigated as an unexplored resource for lithium recovery. Repurposing these brines helps
to promote circular economy principles by converting a waste stream into a source of
strategic mineral supply [56].

Water Usage and Waste Disposal
Beyond economic incentives, two major sustain­
ability concerns dominate current discussions around lithium extraction from brine: water
use and waste generation. These issues are closely tied to the preservation of fragile
ecosystems, particularly in the salt flats. Two types of water are used: brine water, with
high total dissolved solids (TDS ∼300 g/L), and fresh water, typically under 10 g/L TDS,
sourced from nearby aquifers and river systems [60].
Brine is evaporated in large quantities to concentrate lithium, with up to 95% of the volume
lost to the atmosphere. It takes around 2 million liters of brine to produce 50 tons of
lithium, or 40,000 liters(40 tons) per ton—a striking figure, despite brine’s high salinity
and limited ecological value. Though often seen as static, salt flats are actually in slow
dynamic equilibrium, with limited natural replenishment [60].

4
Emerging Green Extraction
Technologies
As the global economy move to more sustainable and low­carbon operations, green ex­
traction solutions for vital metals like steel, nickel, and lithium are becoming increasingly
important. Traditional techniques of extracting and refining these metals are frequently
energy­intensive, environmentally destructive, and contribute significantly to greenhouse
gas emissions. In response to growing environmental concerns and increasingly demand­
ing laws, new approaches are being developed to reduce ecological footprints, increase
energy efficiency, and improve resource recovery.
Green technologies for steel mainly aim to reduce carbon emissions related with iron
and steelmaking processes. The traditional blast furnace technique, which mainly relies
on coal­derived coke, is gradually being replaced by low­carbon alternatives including
hydrogen­based direct reduction and electric arc furnace (EAF) steel manufacturing uti­
lizing renewable electricity. Technologies like hydrogen direct reduced iron (H­DRI) and
carbon capture and storage (CCS) coupled with traditional plants are altering the path to
fossil­free steel production.
In the case of nickel, green extraction technologies seek to overcome the significant en­
vironmental costs of typical pyrometallurgical and hydrometallurgical processes. Sus­
tainable nickel extraction is especially important considering the metal’s growing use in
battery technology for electric vehicles and renewable energy storage.
For lithium, green extraction solutions focus on increasing efficiency and lowering the en­
vironmental effect of brine and hard rock extraction. Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) tech­
nologies, which selectively isolate lithium from brines without the use of large evaporation
ponds, are a significant advancement. Similarly, low­impact hard rock processing meth­
ods, lithium­ion battery recycling, and advances in membrane filtration, ion exchange, and
adsorption processes all help to provide a more sustainable lithium supply chain.
Overall, these green technologies not only reduce the environmental impacts of metal ex­
traction, but they also provide economic benefits by improving material efficiency, lower­
ing operational costs, and opening up new avenues for resource recovery from secondary
sources.
4.1
Green Extraction of Steel
The production of steel contributes 7­9% of global CO2 emissions, making it one of the
most carbon­intensive industries globally [1]. Traditional steelmaking via the blast furnace­
basic oxygen furnace (BF­BOF) technique is primarily reliant on fossil fuels, particularly
coke, as both an energy source and reducing agent.
With rising environmental con­
cerns and a global push for decarbonization, major research and industry projects have
emerged to create green steel technology. Among these, hydrogen­based steel produc­
tion stands out as a transformative strategy, with the potential to significantly reduce emis­
sions while preserving steel quality and economic viability [2].
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is the most relevant greenhouse gas for the global steel sector.
Steel production generates an average of 1.9 tons of CO2 emissions [25]. The iron and
steel industry contributes 6.7% of global CO2 emissions, as reported by the International
Energy Agency [23].

Hydrogen Injection into Blast Furnaces
One immediate application of hydrogen in
traditional steelmaking is the infusion of hydrogen­containing medium into blast furnaces.
Here, hydrogen­rich gases such as Coke Oven Gas (COG), Natural Gas (NG), and even
reformed plastics are supplied through the tuyeres to partially replace coke as a reducing
agent [1].
COG’s high hydrogen concentration ( 57%) enhances furnace performance, reduces coke
usage, and lowers CO2 emissions. Historical deployments, such as as the Solmer plant
in France and the MON VALLEY plant in the United States, have shown early success
with COG injection, resulting in significant reductions in fossil fuel usage and emissions.
In 2008, Japan’s COURSE50 project advanced COG injection with CO2 capture and stor­
age, resulting in a 10% reduction in emissions during operating trials [1].
The benefits of hydrogen injection include [1]:
• Lowering the blast furnace’s heat consumption.
• Accelerating the reduction kinetics due to hydrogen’s superior diffusion rate com­
pared to carbon monoxide.
• Reducing CO2 emissions, as hydrogen reduction produces water vapor instead of
carbon dioxide.

Hydrogen­Based Direct Reduction of Iron (H2­DR)
Aside from improvements to ex­
isting blast furnaces, totally new processes have been created based on Direct Reduced
Iron (DRI) production with hydrogen. The HYBRIT program, pioneered this strategy, aim­
ing for completely fossil­free steelmaking by replacing natural gas with green hydrogen in
DRI production [28][69].
In the H2­DR process, iron ore pellets are reduced using pure hydrogen gas at tempera­
tures ranging from 800­1000°C:
Fe2O3 + 3H2 →2Fe + 3H2O
This technique produces only sponge iron (Fe) and water vapor as byproducts [71]. The
sponge iron is subsequently melted in an Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) using renewable
electricity, resulting in a closed­loop, low­emission steelmaking system [1].

The HYBRIT project is a significant step forward fossil­free steel production, combin­

Electric Arc Furnaces (EAF), primarily used in secondary steel production, offer consid­

thermal energy and use it to preheat scrap metal, combustion air, or even produce steam
for auxiliary plant activities. Scrap preheating systems, including shaft­type furnaces or
continuous charging systems, can cut energy usage by 0.075­0.100 MWh per ton of steel,
while also lowering CO2 emissions by 0.05­0.1 tons per ton. Furthermore, incorporating
regenerative burners and off­gas heat exchangers can considerably increase furnace ef­
ficiency. Although technical hurdles remain—especially with dust handling, off­gas un­
predictability, and equipment fouling—heat recovery in EAFs provides a vital transitional
method for decarbonizing electric steelmaking plants [77].
4.2
Green Extraction of Nickel
Traditional nickel mining, has long been linked to serious environmental and social conse­
quences, including deforestation, water contamination, air pollution, and the exploitation
of vulnerable groups [78].
In response, the term ”green nickel” has evolved, stressing more ethical mining processes.
These include using renewable energy to power operations, installing waste and water
remediation systems, and ensuring that local people benefit from investments in employ­
ment, education, and infrastructure. Green nickel helps to achieve both climate and social
goals by reducing environmental damage and promoting ethical labor practices. Nonethe­
less, the economic sustainability of green nickel is uncertain due to greater expenses as­
sociated with modern technology and infrastructure such as hydrogen­powered vehicles
and solar systems. Furthermore, not all nickel­producing countries meet the environmen­
tal requirements required for sustainable production, which limits green nickel scalability
in the absence of global policy and market alignment [78].
From an ecological point of view, green nickel, also sometimes described as sustainable
nickel, is produced by implementing more sustainable and environmentally friendly mining
practices, such as the use of renewable energy (to replace coal or oil­fired power plants for
example), waste reduction and treatment (particularly in hydrometallurgical processes),
and water remediation [78].
4.2.1
Green Extraction of Nickel Sulfide Ores
Nickel sulfide ores, have long been the principal source of nickel production via pyromet­
allurgical and hydrometallurgical processes. However, typical procedures are energy­
intensive and have major environmental consequences, such as greenhouse gas emis­
sions and sulfur dioxide pollution. In response to these issues, bioleaching has arisen as
an alternative extraction process that uses microorganisms to dissolve metals from ores
in mild environmental conditions. This biotechnological technology not only minimizes
the carbon footprint of nickel production, but it also allows for the processing of low­grade
ores that would otherwise be uneconomical under traditional methods [79].
The bioleaching of nickel from sulfide ores involves microbial oxidation of the ore’s sulfur
and iron components, which enables the release of nickel ions into the solution. Au­
totrophic acidophiles, including Acidithiobacillus ferrooxidans, Acidithiobacillus thiooxi­
dans, and Leptospirillum ferrooxidans, produce energy by oxidizing ferrous iron (Fe2+)
and reduced sulfur compounds. The mechanism involves microbial oxidation of Fe2+ to
Fe3+, which chemically oxidizes nickel sulfide [80].

Despite its advantages, bioleaching of nickel sulfide ores presents various obstacles. Re­

factors like temperature and pH, which might reduce process efficiency in colder climates
[82]. Acid production must be carefully managed to avoid acid mine drainage, even if
the danger is often lower than in traditional operations [81]. Furthermore, the mineralogy
of some ores can reduce leaching efficiency, especially if nickel is bound in refractory
phases as pyrrhotite or silicates [87].

4.2.2
Alternative Sustainable Methods for Laterite Ores
The extraction of laterite ores is more complex and energy­intensive than sulfide ores
due to the mineralogical variability and low­grade nature of lateritic deposits [90]. As
environmental and social sustainability become increasingly important in mining practices,
major efforts are being made to reduce the environmental impact of laterite processing
while enhancing economic viability.
One of the most promising ways is bioleaching, which employs microorganisms like As­
pergillus niger and Penicillium species to generate organic acids capable of removing
nickel from limonitic laterites [91]. Unlike high­pressure acid leaching (HPAL), bioleach­
ing functions under ambient circumstances and does not require high temperatures or
pressures, resulting in significant energy savings and carbon reductions [83]. While bi­
oleaching kinetics are sluggish, and industrial­scale applications are limited, pilot studies

have demonstrated that fungal strains and molasses­based nutrition sources can recover
up to 60% nickel from low­grade ores [92].
Another technique for increasing sustainability is to optimize energy use in traditional pro­
cessing processes like HPAL. This can be accomplished by incorporating renewable en­
ergy sources, such as solar thermal or hydropower, to counteract autoclaves’ high energy
demand [93]. Furthermore, modern plants are increasingly using waste heat recovery
systems and cogeneration technologies to boost total process efficiency. These solu­
tions can lower nickel production’s carbon footprint by more than 30%, especially when
combined with decarbonized electrical grids [94].
Water conservation is another important aspect in the long­term viability of laterite pro­
cessing, particularly in arid or tropical locations. HPAL uses a lot of freshwater, although
this can be reduced by using closed­loop water circuits, rainwater collection, and high­
rate thickeners, which allow process water to be recovered and reused [95]. Furthermore,
employing brackish or recycled industrial water in non­critical circuit sections can greatly
lower the demand for freshwater resources [96].
Tailings and residue handling pose additional sustainability issues because to the huge
amount and geochemical complexity of HPAL wastes. To solve this, mining companies are
increasingly looking at tailings valorisation, which includes recovering cobalt, scandium,
and rare earth metals from leach residues [95]. Furthermore, neutralized tailings can be
reused in civil engineering applications including road construction and mine backfilling,
lowering environmental risk and boosting circularity [93].
Low­acid or other leaching processes show potential for sustainable laterite processing.
For example, atmospheric acid leaching (AL) and chloride­based leaching are being de­
veloped to replace HPAL in certain ore types while using less acid and energy [96]. Fur­
thermore, heap leaching of mixed limonite­saprolite ores under ambient conditions has
been investigated as a lower­capital alternative with lower emissions and environmental
disruption [97].
4.3
Green Extraction of Lithium
As global demand for lithium rises, mostly because of the need for electric vehicles and
renewable energy storage, so does the need to reduce the environmental impact of its
mining. Conventional lithium production, whether from hard rock ores such as spodumene
or natural brine sources, frequently involves energy­intensive operations, significant water
consumption, and chemical waste. These effects have prompted concerns about the
sustainability of lithium supply chains, especially as governments and companies work to
align battery raw materials with climate targets and environmental, social, and governance
(ESG) norms. In response, a new generation of ”green lithium” technologies is emerging,
with the goal of minimizing emissions, lowering chemical inputs, and conserving water
across both resource types. Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) for brines is one example of
an innovation, as is electric spodumene processing.
As of 2023, global lithium production capacity is dominated by hard rock sources, which
account for the majority share due to the large­scale spodumene mining operations in
countries like Australia. Conventional brine extraction methods—primarily used in South
American salt flats—represent a significant but smaller portion of global capacity [98].
4.3.1
Lithium Spodumene Ore Green Solutions
Spodumene (LiAl(SiO3)2), a lithium­rich pyroxene mineral, is the principal hard rock source
for lithium production, particularly in Australia, where large­scale operations dominate

the global supply of battery­grade lithium carbonate and hydroxide [37]. Traditional spo­

more reactive β­phase, followed by acid roasting with strong sulfuric acid and water leach­

requiring up to 1.2­1.5 MWh per ton, and it generates significant CO2 emissions when

To reduce the environmental impact of this process, several electrified kiln systems are
being researched and marketed. One promising option is the indirectly heated rotary kiln
developed by FLSmidth, which employs electric heating to attain working temperatures of
up to 800°C ­ suitable for many thermal applications, though spodumene calcination typ­

the process produces nearly no emissions in terms of thermal energy input. Furthermore,

and low maintenance needs, enhancing overall process sustainability [99].
Kanthal has developed electrically heated acid roasting systems employing Fibrothal®
heating modules, which are vacuum­formed ceramic fiber with integrated components
capable of reaching 1150°C. These technologies remove the requirement for burners and
flue gas systems, simplifying plant design and reducing NOx emissions. Kanthal’s heating
systems integrate into spodumene refineries, providing precise temperature control during
sulfuric acid roasting and facilitating zero­combustion lithium extraction techniques [100].
Metso has created the RotarEkiln, the world’s only commercially available electric kiln sys­
tem optimized for large­scale rotational applications with diameters up to 5 meters. The
RotarEkiln applies indirect radiant heat to the shell through resistive emitters, resulting
in consistent calcination conditions for α­β spodumene conversion. Its modular heating
zones allow for precise temperature control and simple maintenance. The full electrifica­
tion of spodumene conversion through this kiln can save up to 100% of CO2 emissions
associated with conventional fossil­fueled calcination when driven by renewable sources
[101].
Collectively, these developments show that electrifying spodumene manufacturing is both
technically viable and environmentally friendly. These methods minimize the carbon in­
tensity of lithium production by removing fossil fuel combustion and boosting thermal ef­
ficiency, while also aligning with broader decarbonization targets for essential minerals
supply chains.

4.3.2
Brine Evaporation Green Solutions
One of the most significant issues in lithium extraction is excessive water usage, particu­
larly in brine­based processes. Closed­loop water recycling systems can greatly reduce
water use by treating and reusing water during the extraction process [102]. Furthermore,
using desalinated seawater or wastewater instead of freshwater can alleviate the burden
on local water supplies [103].
Long processing periods are another obstacle in classic evaporation processes, which
might take months. Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) is emerging as a more efficient op­
tion, utilizing technologies such as adsorption, ion exchange, and electrochemical tech­
niques to extract lithium in hours or days [104]. Additional developments, such as vacuum
evaporation and mechanical vapor recompression (MVR), can accelerate water removal,
while hybrid processing techniques that combine membranes with classic evaporation
can improve efficiency even more [105].
Traditional methods likewise had low lithium recovery rates, extracting about 30­50% of
lithium and incurring significant losses. DLE technologies are a game changer in this
area, enabling up to 90% lithium recovery using mechanisms such as adsorption (using
lithium­selective sorbents), electrodialysis, and sophisticated membrane and nanofiltra­
tion systems [102]. Optimizing brine composition through better brine management can
also aid in maximising lithium precipitation efficiency [106].
Large evaporation ponds have a negative impact on the environment since they demand
a lot of area and disrupt ecosystems. However, DLE technology enables modular and
compact facilities that require significantly less room. Converting existing ponds into high­
efficiency extraction facilities and using underground brine pumping with closed­loop sys­
tems can help to decrease surface disturbance and habitat loss [104].
High chemical usage and waste creation also put a strain on existing extraction proce­
dures, which rely heavily on lime and soda ash, resulting in toxic waste. Green chemistry
techniques that use less hazardous chemicals are being developed to offset these conse­
quences. Reagent recycling minimizes the need for new chemicals, and biotechnological
alternatives, such as bioleaching with microbes, offer exciting opportunities for low­impact
lithium recovery [103].
Finally, lithium mining has a substantial carbon impact and energy consumption, particu­
larly when using fossil fuels. The utilization of renewable energy (solar, wind, geothermal)
to power extraction devices is a cleaner option [107]. MVR and electrochemical DLE are
energy­efficient recovery systems that reduce energy utilization. Carbon capture and stor­
age (CCS) technologies can further reduce CO2 emissions from processing plants [103].
Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technologies
Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technologies have emerged as a more efficient and eco­
logical option. DLE is a set of improved methods for selectively extracting lithium ions
from brine without the requirement for large­scale evaporation. Techniques include ad­
sorption, where lithium ions adhere to selective materials; ion exchange, where lithium is
exchanged for other cations on resin surfaces; solvent extraction, which uses organic sol­
vents to separate lithium from other salts; and membrane filtration, which uses advanced
membranes to isolate lithium ions in a highly energy­efficient manner [56].

The advantages of DLE over traditional approaches are diverse. First, DLE considerably

due to established infrastructure and suitable meteorological conditions, DLE’s scalability,
speed, and environmental benefits will position it at the forefront of the next generation
of lithium production. Furthermore, as technical developments enhance membrane se­

lithium extraction from brines will decrease, contributing to larger efforts to decarbonize
industrial supply chains. As a result, sustainable brine extraction methods are likely to
play an important role in satisfying future lithium demand while protecting environmental
integrity and reducing greenhouse gas emissions [56].

5.2
Comparative Analysis of Nickel Production Pathways
Nickel is a critical enabler of the global energy shift due to its use in batteries, stainless
steel, and a variety of high­performance alloys. However, the environmental effect of
nickel production, particularly through traditional processes, poses considerable issues in
terms of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, energy use, water usage, and waste genera­
tion. This section compares conventional and green nickel extraction procedures, focus­
ing on the two principal ore types: sulfide and laterite. The comparison focuses on key
sustainability indicators such CO2 emissions, energy intensity, byproduct generation, and
technological feasibility.
5.2.1
Sulfide Ore Route: Smelting & Refining
Nickel sulfide ores have long been the favored raw material due to their high metal con­
centration and low chemical complexity. The typical process involves several stages:
beneficiation (froth flotation), roasting, smelting to nickel matte, converting, and final re­
fining.
• CO2 Emissions: Traditional sulfide nickel processing releases approximately 3.93
tons of CO2 per ton of nickel. Emissions stem from fuel combustion during roasting
and smelting, as well as carbonate decomposition (e.g., limestone).
• Energy Use: The total energy consumption is around 11.66 MWh per ton of nickel,
with 3.86 MWh from electricity and 7.80 MWh from fossil­based thermal energy.
• Air Pollution: The process emits up to 14 tons of SO2 per ton of nickel, posing se­
rious environmental and health risks unless SO2 capture systems are implemented.
• Water Usage: Approximately 330–340 tons of water per 100 tons of ore is used,
largely in flotation and cooling systems, with 90% being recycled.
• Waste Generation: Solid wastes include large quantities of tailings (approximately
95 tons per ton of nickel) and slag (several tons), both of which require careful
handling to prevent acid drainage or metal leaching.
5.2.2
Laterite Ore Route: High Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL)
Laterite ores, which are commonly found in tropical areas, are more numerous than sulfide
ores but require more aggressive processing due to their complicated mineralogy. HPAL
is the most often used method for extracting nickel from low­Mg limonite laterites.
• CO2 Emissions: HPAL emissions vary widely depending on the energy mix, for this
case 2.09 tons of CO2 emissions were calculated.
• Energy Use: The process consumes around 7.28 MWh per ton of nickel, with 1.18
MWh from electricity and 6.1 MWh from heat (usually fossil­based).
• Water and Reagents: Extremely high water use (up to 150 tons per ton of nickel)
and heavy reliance on sulfuric acid (0.1 tons/t Ni) and neutralizing agents (lime or
limestone).
• Waste Generation: Generates around 200 tons of acidic tailings and sludge per
ton of nickel, which contain sulfates, metals, and acidity, posing significant disposal
challenges.
• Environmental Risks: HPAL is associated with deforestation, biodiversity loss, and
potential leachate spills that threaten surrounding ecosystems.

5.3
Comparative Analysis of Lithium Production Pathways
Lithium is a key component of the global transition to sustainable energy technologies,
with rechargeable batteries found in electric vehicles (EVs), energy storage systems, and
a wide range of portable electronics. As demand for lithium grows, it becomes increasingly
vital to consider the environmental consequences of how this metal is harvested. This sec­
tion compares the two primary traditional lithium extraction methods—hard rock mining
(spodumene) and brine evaporation—to emerging green alternatives, such as renewable­
powered spodumene processing and direct lithium extraction (DLE) from brines. The re­
search takes into account key sustainability parameters, including CO2 emissions, energy
usage, water use, processing efficiency, and environmental effect.
5.3.1
Traditional Lithium Extraction ­ Spodumene­Based Processing
Conventional lithium extraction from spodumene ore necessitates energy and resource
expensive procedures. The process starts with open­pit mining of spodumene­rich peg­
matite rock, followed by crushing, grinding, and beneficiation to boost lithium concentra­
tion. Concentrated spodumene is calcined at 1050°C to convert α­spodumene to its more
reactive β­form. This is followed by acid roasting with strong sulfuric acid at roughly 250°C,
which allows the lithium to be leached out as lithium sulfate. The leachate is subsequently
treated with soda ash (Na2CO3), precipitating battery­grade lithium carbonate.
• Energy Consumption: The process consumes around 2.87 MWh per ton of lithium
carbonate (0.73 MWh electricity and 2.14 MWh heat), primarily from fossil fuels used
in calcination and roasting stages.
• CO2 Emissions: Estimated at 6.242 tons of CO2 per ton of Li2CO3, mostly from
fuel combustion and chemical reactions such as limestone decomposition.
• Water Usage: Roughly 9.8 tons per ton of product, used mainly in ore processing
and chemical precipitation stages. Much of it is recycled, but water management
remains a concern.
• Environmental Issues: Land degradation, dust emissions, and high chemical in­
put. Waste byproducts like sodium sulfate and leached residues require proper
disposal.
5.3.2
Traditional Lithium Extraction ­ Brine Evaporation Extraction
In South American countries like Chile, Argentina, and Bolivia, lithium is extracted from
lithium­rich salt flats (salars). Brine from underground aquifers is pumped to the surface
into large evaporation ponds. Over 12–24 months, water evaporates under solar radi­
ation, gradually concentrating lithium salts. After several intermediate chemical stages,
lithium carbonate or lithium hydroxide is recovered.
• Energy Consumption: Relatively low direct energy requirement at 0.116 MWh per

• CO2 Emissions: 2.838 tons CO2 per ton, largely from indirect sources (chemical

• Water Usage: More than 40 tons of water lost per ton of lithium produced, often

6
Environmental Impacts & Byproducts
Steel, nickel, and lithium production have significant environmental consequences, es­
pecially as global demand for these essential resources rises in line with the energy shift
and industrial electrification. These operations are resource­intensive, generating multiple
emissions, solid wastes, and chemical byproducts that must be dealt within a sustainable
framework.
6.1
Environmental Impact of Steel
The steel industry is still one of the most energy­ and carbon­intensive sectors, accounting
for roughly 5% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from fossil fuels. Steel manu­
facturing, mostly through the blast furnace­basic oxygen furnace (BF­BOF) process, gen­
erates around 1.8­2.0 tons of CO2 every ton produced. While Electric Arc Furnace (EAF)
technology reduces emissions, the carbon intensity of electrical sources should also be
considered [112].
Figure 6.1: Worldwide steel production with energy consumption and CO2 generation in
the steel industry[112]
In addition to GHG emissions, the steel sector uses a lot of water, mostly for cooling and
steam generation. The average water intake in integrated steel facilities is 28.6 m³ per ton
of steel, with a discharge of 25.3 m³. In China, water usage is considerably greater, aver­
aging 7­8 m³ per ton of steel, due to antiquated technology and an emphasis on equipment
rather than water network optimization. The resulting effluent frequently contains ammo­
nia, cyanide, benzene, and chlorides, along with altered pH values, all of which represent
environmental risks if not appropriately treated [112].
According to lifecycle assessments, the most significant environmental impacts of steel
manufacturing are caused by fossil fuel consumption, which affects human health and
contributes to global warming. Wastewater discharge, while accounting for a lesser pro­
portion of total impact, adds to aquatic toxicity and eutrophication. Pinch technology and
water network restructuring have been shown to effectively reduce freshwater intake and
improve water reuse rates in steel mills [112].
6.1.1
Byproducts
Steelmaking generates less waste than other metal sectors, especially when high­grade
iron ores are used. However, the amount of waste increases dramatically when lower­
66
Sustainable Extraction of Critical Metals
==PAGE:76==
grade ores are treated or integrated pathways are used. Mini­mills produce less waste
due to their more efficient production methods [112].
The most common byproducts of steelmaking are dust, sludge, and slag. Dust collected
from off­gas systems contains iron, carbon, and traces of metals such as zinc, which
can be harmful if not handled appropriately. Slag, the most abundant byproduct, differs in
composition depending on its source. Reducing slags, which have low iron oxide content,
are produced in blast furnaces and can be recycled in cement manufacturing. Oxidizing
slags produced during BOF and EAF operations are more difficult to recycle, however
they are frequently used as aggregates in road construction [112].
Slag’s mineral composition is becoming recognized as important, and sophisticated pro­
cessing technologies aim to treat it similarly to iron ore, removing precious components
by crushing, separation, and high­temperature treatment. This method converts slag, a
waste product, into a usable raw material [112].
Byproducts like blast furnace and steel slags are often reused in construction materials,
but they must be handled carefully to avoid heavy metal leaching and other environmental
hazards. The combination of water value measures and reverse osmosis treatment has
increased water efficiency in modern steel mills [112].
The steel sector is also taking part in broader recycling initiatives, such as the reuse of
plastics and tires. These materials, when pumped into furnaces, can act as alternate
carbon sources, lowering the demand for coke or anthracite. Such approaches not only
minimize industrial waste, but also support circular economy principles by incorporating
social trash into industrial processes [112].
Overall, sustainable steelmaking relies on optimizing energy and water use, lowering
emissions, and maximizing the value of byproducts. With continued innovation and col­
laboration, the steel sector can play a critical role in transitioning to a more sustainable
industrial future [112].
Steel and the Circular Economy: Recyclability and Reuse
Steel is the world’s most recyclable material, and its environmental credentials are unpar­
alleled in the building sector. Steel items are 100% recyclable when they reach the end
of their useful life, making them an essential component of the circular economy. Steel,
once created, becomes a permanent resource that can be recycled indefinitely without
losing quality. A steel beam from a structure can now be converted into another beam
or recycled into completely different goods such as food cans, freezers, or roof panels
[113].
In the United States alone, 60 to 80 million tons of steel scrap are recycled each year
into new steel products. Over the last 30 years, the American steel industry has reused
more than one billion tons of steel scrap. To put this in context, the United States recycles
enough steel scrap each year to build more than 650 Golden Gate Bridges, or 25 Eiffel
Towers per day [113].
Modern sorting and separating technology have substantially increased the steel recy­
cling process, diverting millions of tons of iron and steel from landfills and into productive
use. Nearly all of the steel industry’s co­products find new uses. Slag, a byproduct of
steelmaking, is used in cement manufacturing, road construction, fertilizers, and hydraulic
engineering, among other applications. Additionally, process gasses are used to gener­
ate heat or power, and important metal oxides like iron, nickel, and zinc are extracted from
steelmaking dust [113].

6.2
Environmental Impact of Nickel
As global demand for sustainable energy technology grows, nickel—a essential compo­
nent in electric vehicle batteries, wind turbines, and solar panels—becomes increasingly
important. However, the environmental and socioeconomic consequences of nickel ex­
traction are deeply concerning. According to researchers at the Massachusetts Institute of
Technology, the nickel supply chain faces numerous challenges, including deforestation
and community displacement during mining, as well as high greenhouse gas emissions
during energy­intensive processing stages [114].
6.2.1
Influence of Ore Type and Processing Technologies
Nickel production’s energy and emissions profile are heavily influenced by the type of ore
used and the extraction process selected. Sulphidic ores are treated using pyrometal­
lurgical techniques that take use of exothermic processes, decreasing the demand for
external energy. Lateritic ores require substantial thermal or chemical treatment. This dif­
ferentiation occurs in the main extraction and refining stages accounting for 57% to 70%
of overall energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions. Lateritic ore processing
consumes over 93,920 kWh of energy and emits over 21 metric tons of CO2 per ton more
than sulphidic ore­based production [37].
6.2.2
Environmental Profile of Sulfide Nickel Ores
Nickel sulfide deposits are generally thought to have a smaller environmental impact than
laterite deposits, owing to the lower energy and chemical requirements for processing.
Sulfide ores are normally treated using standard flotation, smelting, and refining, which,
while still energy and emission intensive, do not involve the extreme conditions of High­
Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL). The International Energy Agency reports that sulfide­
based nickel production emits 8.8 to 18.8 kg of CO2­equivalents per kg of nickel, or 8.8­
18.8 metric tons CO2 per ton. This is much lower than the 24­40 tons associated with
laterite HPAL processing. Furthermore, sulfide ores require less chemical reagents, such
as sulfuric acid, which reduces the risk of harmful liquid waste streams [38].

Air Pollution and Smelting Emissions
Sulfide nickel processing involves smelting,
which emits air pollutants, especially sulfur dioxide (SO2), which is linked to acid rain.
Modern smelters use sulfur capture systems to convert SO2 into sulfuric acid for reuse or
sale. However, emissions remain a significant environmental risk if not handled effectively.
Elevated sulfur emissions from outdated or poorly controlled plants can degrade local air
quality, acidify neighboring soils and water bodies, and have a severe impact on nearby
residents’ health [40].
Water Use and Acid Mine Drainage (AMD)
While sulfide ore processing requires less
water than HPAL, it has a unique set of environmental hazards, including acid mine
drainage (AMD). When sulfide minerals are exposed to air and water, particularly dur­
ing open­pit or underground mining, they can oxidize and produce sulfuric acid, which
then leaches heavy metals from the surrounding rock. If not effectively managed, acid
and metal­rich runoff can reach surface and groundwater systems, producing long­term
contamination lasting decades [40][39].
Land disturbance and habitat fragmentation
Sulfide mining is most common in tem­
perate or boreal locations (such as Canada, Russia, and Australia), and it is often carried
out underground or in smaller­scale open­pit operations. While sulfide mining often has
a smaller land footprint than laterite mining in tropical rainforests, it nonetheless causes
significant landscape alteration, plant clearance, and habitat fragmentation. Waste rock
and tailings, while less abundant than laterite operations, can nonetheless pose geotech­
nical and pollution hazards if storage facilities fail or leach contaminants. Furthermore,
infrastructural development, such as roads, electricity lines, and smelters, leads to overall
ecological disruption in these vulnerable areas [115].
6.2.3
Environmental Profile of Laterite Nickel Ores
A big worry is the move toward limonitic laterite ores, which require significantly more
energy to process than typical sulfide ores. High­pressure acid leach (HPAL) processes,
which extract battery­grade nickel from laterites, release 24 to 27 kg of CO2­equivalent
per kilogram of nickel. This is significantly greater than sulfide operations, which release
8.8 to 18.8 kg CO2e/kg Ni, depending on processing processes and energy sources [116].
Beyond carbon emissions, laterite nickel activities frequently pose significant environmen­
tal and social problems. Laterite ores are frequently extracted and processed in tropical,
biodiverse regions such as Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, and the Philippines, resulting
in vast volumes of chemically reactive tailings [116].
High­Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL), a critical step in recovering battery­grade nickel
from laterite ores, is extremely energy­intensive. The technology necessitates high tem­
peratures and pressures, often at 270°C and 725 psi, which consume significant quanti­
ties of energy, which is frequently derived from coal, diesel, and natural gas—particularly
in countries with fossil­fuel­heavy energy systems such as Indonesia and Papua New
Guinea. Laterite­derived nickel emits between 25 and 40 tons of CO2 per ton of nickel
[38].
Sulfuric Acid Use and Wastewater Risks
A major environmental risk connected with
HPAL operations is the huge use of sulfuric acid, which can range between 300 and
600 kg per ton of ore processed. This chemically intensive process produces significant
amounts of acidic wastewater, which must be properly controlled to avoid environmental
contamination. Without appropriate treatment, these effluents can cause soil acidification,
Sustainable Extraction of Critical Metals
69
==PAGE:79==
aquatic ecosystem degradation, and long­term groundwater pollution. In tropical climates
where heavy rainfall can worsen leachate and runoff, the danger of inadvertent discharge
or chronic seepage rises, making water quality management one among the most critical
ESG concerns for laterite­based nickel producers [115].
Tailings Generation and Long­Term Storage Hazards
HPAL also generates huge
amounts of solid tailings, estimated at 20 to 30 tons per ton of nickel produced. These
tailings frequently contain residual acids, toxic metals like arsenic, chromium, and iron ox­
ides, as well as other hazardous substances. These waste materials, usually deposited
in enormous tailings dams, represent long­term dangers of leakage, seepage, or catas­
trophic dam failure, especially in geologically unstable or high­rainfall areas. If contain­
ment measures fail, the environmental repercussions can be severe, including river and
soil contamination, ecological damage, and harm to local residents. Thus, proper tailings
management and strong environmental safeguards are essential for lowering the ecolog­
ical imprint of nickel extraction using HPAL [115].
Water Use, Wastewater, and Groundwater Risks
High­Pressure Acid Leaching (HPAL)
is both energy and water intensive, especially during the leaching, washing, and neutral­
ization processes. Throughout the process, large amounts of water are required to pres­
sure wash ore and dilute acids and chemicals. This increasing water demand poses risks
in water­scarce areas and puts a strain on local freshwater supplies. Furthermore, the
process produces enormous amounts of effluent containing acid residues, sulfates, and
heavy metals, which, if not properly handled or stored, can contaminate the surrounding
environment. Leachates and seepage from waste ponds represent a concern of ground­
water contamination, which could damage surrounding residents and ecosystems. These
dangers are amplified in regions with heavy rainfall or permeable soils, making contain­
ment more challenging [39].
Laterite nickel is primarily mined using open­pit methods, which are inherently harmful to
the environment. Such mining is common in biodiversity­rich tropical rainforest locations
like as Indonesia, the Philippines, and New Caledonia. The land removal required for
laterite extraction causes deforestation, biodiversity loss, and long­term changes to ter­
rain and ecosystems. Native flora and animals are frequently displaced or exterminated,
and crucial habitats for endangered species are damaged or destroyed. Furthermore,
removing surface vegetation increases the risk of soil erosion, sedimentation in streams,
and long­term ecological imbalance. These consequences call into question the notion
of nickel as a ”green” metal, given that its extraction directly leads to forest degradation
and biodiversity loss [40].

Environmental impact of Lithium
6.3.1
Spodumene Ore Impacts
Spodumene mining and processing currently account for the vast majority of global lithium
carbonate and lithium hydroxide output. However, the environmental consequences of
these procedures are largely unknown, especially as the majority of spodumene is exported—
primarily to China—for refining. This geographical separation between mine locations and
processing facilities adds to a lack of openness and public debate about the environmental
impact of spodumene­based lithium extraction [117].
Spodumene, a lithium aluminosilicate mineral (LiAl(SiO3)2), is commonly found in granitic
pegmatites. Major deposits can be found in Australia, Africa, and North America. The
Greenbushes mine in Australia has some of the highest­grade ore at around 4% Li2O.
70
Sustainable Extraction of Critical Metals
==PAGE:80==
In comparison, African and North American deposits typically vary from 0.6% to 1.6%
Li2O. The ore grade has a substantial impact on the environmental footprint of the mining
and refining processes—higher­quality ores need less raw material extraction, produce
fewer greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, and generate less chemical and mine waste
than lower­grade alternatives [117].
Spodumene mining is often done open­pit, which involves removing overburden, blast­
ing, and extracting ore using heavy machinery. This operation generates vast amounts
of displaced rock and overburden, which can damage the landscape for years and poten­
tially contaminate it. Once recovered, the ore goes through beneficiation, which involves
crushing, grinding, and flotation to concentrate the lithium content. This stage produces
tailings, which are normally stored in lined ponds. Despite regulatory restrictions for pond
linings to prevent leaching, chemical pollutants routinely penetrate nearby groundwater
due to liner breakdown over time [117].
Environmental Pollutants from Spodumene Mining and Processing
The environmental impact of spodumene mining begins with the extraction stage, when
open­pit mining and ore crushing and grading produce significant mine waste. These
activities also add to greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions due to the usage of large diesel­
powered machines. Although these problems are common in large­scale mining, their
cumulative effects, particularly in biologically sensitive areas, present serious environ­
mental issues [117].
The beneficiation stage, which includes wet milling and froth flotation, is intended to
improve lithium concentration in the ore. However, this technique produces enormous
amounts of tailings contaminated with flotation chemicals. Furthermore, it requires a sig­
nificant amount of energy, which contributes to additional GHG emissions, mainly from
equipment operation and steam generation. Improper treatment or leakage of tailings can
introduce harmful compounds into nearby water systems, damaging local ecosystems and
populations.
Roasting is one of the most energy­intensive steps in spodumene manufacturing. The
mineral is dried and treated at high temperatures of roughly 1,000°C. These operations
release considerable amounts of CO2, NOx, and SOx. The fuel source, calciner design,
and the effectiveness of emissions control devices all have an impact on the amount of
emissions produced. These pollutants have been linked to acid rain and atmospheric
degradation, underlining the importance of strong emissions reduction efforts [117].
Following roasting, the spodumene is leached with sulfuric acid at around 250°C. This
stage maintains the pattern of high CO2, NOx, and SOx emissions. Furthermore, it raises
the possibility of acid contamination if leachates are not effectively stored or neutralized
[117].
In the subsequent lithium sulphate production stage, neutralization with soda ash or lime
produces highly contaminated gypsum sludge and waste water. These byproducts are
usually held in lined ponds, which, while intended to retain contaminants, are prone to
leaking over time. This endangers soil and groundwater quality in the nearby areas of the
operation [117].
The last phases of processing include turning lithium sulfate to lithium carbonate or lithium
hydroxide. Lithium hydroxide is often manufactured using slaked lime, whereas lithium
carbonate is created using soda ash. Both approaches produce more CO2, NOx, and
SOx, primarily from thermal breakdown of limestone or energy­intensive purifying oper­
ations. Furthermore, contaminated water from wash steps may be dumped into nearby

==PAGE:81==
watersheds, depending on regulatory standards and infrastructure, potentially impacting
aquatic life and water quality [117].
By­products such as sodium sulfate and gypsum are produced during the latter stages of
lithium product production, when lithium carbonate or hydroxide is converted. Additional
purification is required to achieve battery­grade standards, which increases energy con­
sumption and trash generation. Overall, the environmental impact of spodumene manu­
facturing is extensive and diverse. Each operational stage adds to total energy demand,
GHG emissions, and chemical waste, confirming the recurring theme that more process­
ing equals higher environmental impact [117].
6.3.2
Brine Evaporation Impacts
Brine evaporation is a popular method of extracting lithium, especially in South America’s
”Lithium Triangle,” which includes Chile, Argentina, and Bolivia. This method includes
pumping lithium­rich brine to the surface and letting it evaporate in vast ponds, leaving
behind lithium salts for further processing. While cost­effective, this technology has major
environmental implications [118].
Water Consumption Impacts
One of the key challenges is the significant water con­
sumption caused by brine evaporation. Water usage in lithium extraction from brines is
divided into two categories: direct and indirect use. Direct water consumption occurs
within the plant for activities such as brine pumping, chemical preparation, evaporation,
and leaching, while indirect water consumption includes water embedded in the manu­
facturing of reagents, diesel fuel, and electricity for operations [119].
In typical lithium evaporation technologies, direct water loss is primarily by brine evapora­
tion in vast surface ponds, where millions of cubic meters of water are lost each year to so­
lar radiation. Evaporation rates in dry places like the Salar de Atacama and Cauchari can
reach 101 m³/ton LCE and 289 m³/ton LCE, respectively, resulting in significant losses.
Water loss is irreversible, and only a small fraction of brine can be re­injected into aquifers.
This leads to decreased water levels and limited availability for ecosystems and indige­
nous groups. In contrast, Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) use closed­loop water recycling
technologies to practically reduce evaporative losses and achieve little direct water use
[119].
The indirect water footprint is caused by upstream operations such as reagent production
(e.g., sodium carbonate, sulfuric acid) and power generation. The Atacama system has
the largest indirect water use, reaching 154 m³/ton LCE, mostly due to the heavy use of
sodium carbonate in solvent extraction stages. DLE methodologies reveal varying indi­
rect consumption depending on energy sources: 113 m³/ton LCE when driven by diesel
generators, 288 m³/ton with grid electricity, and 126 m³/ton when powered by solar en­
ergy. DLE’s large chemical input accounts for 52­82% of its total indirect water use, with
electricity contributing up to 48% depending on the source [119].
In response to these environmental concerns, alternate lithium extraction technologies
are being investigated. Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technologies, for example, seek
to reduce water consumption while minimizing environmental impact by extracting lithium
straight from brine without substantial evaporation. While promising, these technologies
are still in development and not extensively used [118].
Brine effects on Soil
Additionally, the evaporation process can contaminate soil and
water. Chemicals used in lithium manufacturing, such as hydrochloric acid, can escape
into the environment, hurting wildlife and damaging water sources. In certain cases, this

has resulted in the extinction of aquatic life and the deterioration of areas vital to local
biodiversity [118].
The physical footprint of evaporation ponds adds to environmental damage. These large
ponds can disturb land use, causing habitat loss for many species and changing the natu­
ral landscape. Furthermore, the infrastructure required for brine extraction and processing
could impact protected regions and culturally significant locations, causing stress among
local residents [118].
Brine Spill Remedation
Brine spills, whether from lithium brine extraction, oil field ac­
tivities, or industrial processes, pose a substantial environmental danger because they
introduce high levels of chloride and salt into soils and surrounding water systems. If
not addressed quickly and efficiently, these contaminants can impair soil structure, re­
duce vegetation viability, and contaminate surface and groundwater resources. The fun­
damental goal of brine remediation is to reduce salt concentrations in soil to levels that
allow for vegetation regrowth and long­term soil productivity. There are two types of Brine
spill remedation:Ex Situ Remediation and In Situ Remediation. Ex situ remediation is the
physical removal of brine­contaminated soil. In situ remediation, which fixes polluted soil
without excavation, is frequently preferred due to its reduced disruption and cost [66].
6.4
Heat Recovery Potential in Traditional Metallurgical
Processes
Significant amounts of thermal energy are lost as waste heat in typical metallurgical pro­
cesses, particularly those involving high­temperature reactions such as blast furnace­
basic oxygen furnace (BF­BOF) steelmaking and nickel smelting. Integrating heat recov­
ery systems provides a realistic way to increase energy efficiency and minimize green­
house gas emissions.
In the BF­BOF steelmaking process, devices such as Top­Pressure Recovery Turbines
(TRT) use the pressure and temperature of blast furnace gas to generate electricity,
whereas slag heat recovery machines absorb latent heat from molten slag for steam pro­
duction or district heating. These methods can recover 20­30% of the thermal energy
input, depending on plant layout and slag handling systems. This can lower net energy
demand by up to 1 MWh per ton of crude steel, resulting in an estimated emissions reduc­
tion of 0.2­0.3 tons of CO2 per ton of steel produced (assuming fossil fuel­based energy
inputs) [120].
Equivalent prospective exist in nickel production, notably in the smelting of sulphide ore.
Heat recovery from off­gas systems and converting furnaces can be utilized to preheat
incoming feed or generate electricity, reducing fuel consumption and indirect emissions.
While less prevalent than in steelmaking, integrated waste heat boilers in modern nickel
smelters have been demonstrated to lower energy consumption by 10­15% when oper­
ating optimally [121].
Despite its benefits, heat recovery methods confront obstacles such as high capital ex­
penditures, the necessity for steady working conditions, and material degradation caused
by corrosive gasses or slags. However, as carbon pricing and regulatory pressures rise,
waste heat utilization is projected to play an increasingly important part in the near­term
decarbonization of heavy industry [121].

Sustainable EV Battery Design Using
Green­Mined Materials
The availability of important minerals such as lithium, nickel, and cobalt, which are re­
quired for the production of batteries, electric vehicles (EVs), and large­scale energy stor­
age systems, is critical to the transition to a low­carbon economy. These metals form the
foundation of technologies needed to decarbonize the power and transportation sectors
[50].
The sustainable use of essential metals, particularly steel, nickel, and lithium, is critical
to the worldwide transition to decarbonized infrastructure, clean energy, and low­carbon
mobility. Steel is an important component of green construction due to its strength, re­
cyclability, and flexibility to prefabrication, modular design, and renewable energy infras­
tructure. Its use in wind turbines, solar panel frameworks, and energy­efficient building
systems renders it critical to climate­resilient development activities [122].
High­strength steel (HSS) and advanced high­strength steel (AHSS) continue to be crit­
ical components in EV construction. These materials lower vehicle weight by 25­39%,
leading to enhanced energy efficiency and saving up to 4.5 tons of greenhouse gas emis­
sions per vehicle throughout its lifecycle [123]. Despite the rising shift toward lightweight
alternatives, prominent manufacturers like BMW and Tesla have reverted to steel for new
EV models, favoring affordability, recyclability, and crash toughness. Steel has higher
end­of­life recyclability and lower embodied emissions than aluminum or carbon fiber,
especially when green steel derived from hydrogen­based DRI is employed [124].
Battery casing design, which is crucial for safety, thermal stability, and structural support,
benefits from steel’s excellent impact resistance and modular adaptability. Ultra­high­
strength steels (UHSS) surpass aluminum and composites in fire protection and structural
durability, aligning with circular economy concepts by easing disassembly and recycling
at vehicle end­of­life [124]. EV motors use specialized electrical steels for their magnetic
efficiency, highlighting steel’s multifunctional role in lowering material complexity and en­
vironmental effect [125].
Nickel is a key component in high­energy lithium­ion battery chemistries such as NMC and
NCA, accounting for up to 80­90% of the cathode mass. Higher energy density allows for
greater driving ranges, a key factor in consumer adoption [126]. Global demand for nickel
in EVs is expected to increase approximately 20­fold by 2040. Vehicles like the Tesla
Model Y and Ford F­150 Lightning demonstrate how nickel­rich batteries help to achieve
excellent performance while remaining small [127].
Nickel’s recyclability improves its long­term viability. Nickel­based batteries align with both
performance and circularity aims, since 68% of consumer nickel is already recycled and
ethical sourcing norms arise [126]. However, obstacles persist because to the restricted
supply of Class 1 nickel, which is important for battery applications, requiring energy­
intensive processing and extended development timetables [127].
Lithium is crucial to electrification initiatives, particularly in the transportation and energy
industries, where it is used in lithium­ion batteries to achieve high energy density, fast
charging, and long cycle life. However, lithium mining and processing raise serious en­
vironmental and ethical challenges, including as water scarcity, habitat destruction, and

labor issues in cobalt co­mining. These problems highlight the need for more sustain­
able extraction methods and innovative recycling systems to close the loop on battery
materials [50] [128].
Lithium’s high energy density and rechargeability make it an essential component in EV
batteries. The environmental and geopolitical consequences of lithium extraction, includ­
ing water­intensive brine operations in South America and hard rock mining in Australia,
represent considerable sustainability threats [129]. Brine extraction can absorb up to 65%
of a region’s water, damaging local ecosystems and agriculture. Despite increased local
battery assembly in the US, over 60% of lithium refining remains concentrated in China,

need for improved recycling infrastructure and second­life uses. Direct lithium extrac­

efficiency and lessen environmental consequences [129].
Material Composition and Life Cycle Management
A 60 kWh EV battery typically
contains 185 kilograms of components, including 52 kg of graphite, 29 kg of nickel, 8
kg of cobalt, and 6 kg of lithium, coupled with 20 kg of steel for casing and crash safety
[131]. As battery chemistries change to lower cobalt concentration in favor of greater
nickel ratios, lifespan analysis and ethical sourcing become more critical. Second­life
applications for batteries in stationary energy storage, such as buildings or grid backup,
can extend their useful life by 10­20 years before recycling becomes essential [132].
Under normal driving circumstances, lithium­ion batteries in electric vehicles normally last
about 10 years, with many lasting up to 15­20 years before needing to be replaced. As
battery technologies and management systems improve, battery longevity increases, low­
ering the frequency of replacement and the environmental impact of new battery produc­
tion. Importantly, end­of­life EV batteries are rarely wasted; instead, they are rapidly being
recycled for stationary energy storage applications like grid balancing or backup power
in buildings. When a battery’s performance no longer matches reuse requirements, it is
usually recycled to recover valuable materials such as lithium, nickel, and cobalt. This
expanding reuse and recycling ecosystem is critical to promoting a circular economy and
reducing the environmental impact of broad electric vehicle adoption [133].

Conclusions & Future Outlook
The transition to electrification and renewable energy has increased demand for crucial
raw materials, particularly steel, nickel, and lithium, which are required for the production
of electric vehicles (EVs) and battery technology. However, when these elements are
extracted and refined using traditional processes, they impose significant environmental
costs. This thesis compares the environmental performance of traditional and emerging
green manufacturing paths for metals, including CO2 emissions, energy consumption,
and resource use, with a focus on the implications per electric vehicle unit
The findings clearly show that existing extraction methods are quite carbon intensive.
Conventional BF­BOF steelmaking emits 2.56 tons of CO2 per ton of steel, while nickel
production from sulfide ores results in 3.93 tons of CO2 per ton of nickel, and lithium
from brine evaporation contributes up to 6.2 tons of CO2 per ton of lithium carbonate.
These procedures need high energy inputs and entail substantial environmental hazards,
including water depletion, SO2 emissions, and chemical waste.
In contrast, green extraction technologies significantly reduce emissions and environmen­
tal damage. Using hydrogen­based DRI steelmaking with electric arc furnaces (EAF) can
cut CO2 emissions to as low as 0.3 tons per ton of steel. When powered by renewable
electricity, bioleaching of nickel reduces emissions to just 0.022 tons of CO2 per ton and
the decarbonization of the calcination and acid roasting stage of spodumene ores reduces
emissions to just 0.165 tons per ton.
A carbon cost study at the vehicle level was undertaken using a standardized carbon price
of €73.18 per ton of CO2 [111]. The entire carbon cost of traditional material production
per EV was found to be around €14.83, while the equivalent cost utilizing green production
methods was only €0.56. EV manufacturers can reduce their CO2­related cost burden by
97%, providing a strong incentive to shift to sustainable raw material sourcing.
This study’s economic and environmental evaluations are based on a carbon price of
73.18 €/ton CO2, which aligns with the EU ETS. As a result, the findings are based on
the assumption that key metal extraction and processing take place inside the legisla­
tive and economic framework of Europe. Carbon pricing policies varies greatly among
regions, and adopting the same methodology to non­European markets may result in dif­
ferent outcomes in terms of cost competitiveness and emission reduction. As a result, the
findings given here are mainly relevant to European industrial contexts.
These findings demonstrate the revolutionary environmental and economic potential of
green solutions in the battery supply chain. However, the study did encounter numerous
significant drawbacks. A complete economic evaluation of production costs, and market
prices, was not possible for green processes of nickel and lithium due to a lack of publicly
available data. These technologies are still in the pilot or early commercialization stages,
and reliable cost structures are being developed. As a result, the economic analysis in
this study concentrated primarily on carbon­related externalities rather than overall cost
competitiveness.
Another important constraint is the absence of co­products like cobalt recovered during
nickel processing or boron from lithium brines, which can have a considerable impact on
both environmental implications and economic viability. Furthermore, the environmental

text, ore grade, energy supply, and regulatory environment—factors that were generalized
in this analysis but require more extensive investigation in future work.
Despite these restrictions, the overall result is clear: decarbonizing the manufacture of
battery materials is both technically feasible and environmentally imperative. As global
governmental frameworks, such as the EU Critical Raw Materials Act and corporate ESG
standards, place greater emphasis on low­emission supply chains, green extraction meth­
ods will become critical to building a sustainable and circular electric car sector.
This thesis adds to the growing body of research supporting the transition toward greener
metallurgical practices, while also emphasizing the urgent need for data transparency,
industrial collaboration, and public investment to accelerate the development and deploy­
ment of low­carbon technologies in the raw materials sector.
Future Outlook
Looking ahead, the materials sector’s continued decarbonization will be critical to the
green transition’s overall climate impact. As emerging technologies like hydrogen­based
reduction, bioleaching, and decarbonizing the calcination and acid roasting stage of lithium
spodumene ore pace, their integration into mainstream supply chains will necessitate col­
laborative efforts from business, policymakers, and academics. Future research should
concentrate on producing clear and consistent life­cycle data for green technology, as
well as advancing economic modeling that incorporates CAPEX/OPEX, payback periods,
and market volatility. Attention must also be directed to supply chain localization, key raw
material recovery from end­of­life products, and cross­sectoral cooperation that support
closed­loop systems.
Governments and investors may speed up this transformation by funding trial projects, fi­
nancing green infrastructure, and including sustainability factors into procurement proce­
dures. With escalating regulatory pressure and rising consumer awareness, green mate­
rial sourcing is becoming a competitive necessity and distinguishing feature of responsible
industrial growth.
To summarize, achieving net­zero targets and constructing a robust, low­carbon economy
would need not only clean energy generation, but also the transformation of the materi­
als that power it. The findings of this thesis demonstrate that this transition is not only
achievable, but also underway—and that it must be scaled with urgency.
