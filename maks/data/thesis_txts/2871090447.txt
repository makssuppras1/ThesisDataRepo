Abstract
Chronic respiratory conditions require continuous monitoring to detect and catch early
signs of deterioration, but the current standard is spirometry tests at doctors appointments
which can be weeks to months apart. This thesis takes some of the initial steps to explore
the possibility of using diaphragmatic Electromyography (dEMG) as a non­intrusive and
continuos monitoring method. A critical barrier is the presence of Electrocardiographic
(ECG) artifacts in the dEMG recordings, that have to me removed before any further
downstream analysis such as breath event classifications of any form of pattern recogni­
tion.
To do just that, a deep learning model based on the U­net structure have been developed,
for dEMG decomposition separating the ECG component form the dEMG component.
The U­net model has been trained and validated using real ECG data from the PTB­XL
database and synthetically simulated EMG signals using the BioMime model. Contrary
to other methods such as gating or wavelet­based approaches this model does not rely
on QRS peak detection. The model has achieved on average an Signal to Noise ratio
improvement (SNRi) of 14.04dB across a range from ­10dB to 10dB of SNR input signals,
outperforming existing baseline and performs on par with some of the ECG denoising
models.
These results have demonstrated that with some further work, a model like this could
possibly enable accurate and useful continuous respiratory monitoring for future systems
capable of detecting deterioration faster than current standard and/or detecting specific
behavioural events like coughing, apnoea or any other irregular breathing patterns, both
clinically or in­home.

Introduction
Chronic respiratory diseases are among the most common non­transmissible diseases
worldwide. Asthma and Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) are the most
prevalent obstructive respiratory diseases with COPD of 3.9% and Asthma of 3.6% global
prevalence [1]. In 2015 3.2 million people died from COPD and 0.4 million died from
Asthma, where Asthma is more prevalent in children and adolescents, COPD is most
prevalent in old­adults (age 70­79). With COPD mostly being caused by smoking and
prolonged exposure to chemical fumes, dust and vapours typically from the workplace,
the patients are predominately adult or elderly but can be seen in younger people. Asthma
is the most common chronic disease among children. [2]
Globally, asthma ranks 24th among the leading causes of years lived with disability and
34th among the leading causes of burden of disease, as measured by disability­adjusted
life years (DALYs) [3]. It is regarded as a significant social and health issue, contributing
to a substantial economic burden, estimated to account for approximately 1% to 2% of
total healthcare costs in industrialized nations. [1].
With most obstructive pulmonary diseases being chronic and not curable but can be main­
tained with the proper treatment and lifestyle changes, a proper way of measuring con­
dition is of outmost importance resulting in better and faster treating. Individuals with
poorly managed asthma experience disrupted sleep, daytime fatigue, and difficulty con­
centrating. This condition can lead to missed school and work days for both sufferers and
their families, resulting in financial strain on the household and the broader community.
Furthermore, young children can have a hard time cooperating and/or successfully con­
veying their symptoms, therefore increasing probability of misdiagnosis, which makes it
even more important to have an automatic measure and not just an increase in the fre­
quency of doctors appointments. [3, 4]
The current standard for evaluating the condition for a patient with asthma is spirome­
try tests performed during a doctors appointment. These appointments are performed at
specific intervals where the patient might not have a worsening of asthma on that spe­
cific day.[5] Studies also suggest that there are a significant amount, from 20% to 70%
of asthma cases being undiagnosed and therefore not being treated until the symptoms
have worsened. At the same time it is also reported that 30­35 % of children and adults
not suffering from asthma is being diagnosed suggesting that asthma is also being over­
diagnosed. [6]
This highlights the gaps in the current diagnostic methods and encourages the develop­
ment of improved approaches, including a more precise way of monitoring the patient’s
respiratory condition. Continuous monitoring of respiratory features can potentially enable
earlier intervention against deterioration in hospital settings, addressing the limitations of
intermittent measurements currently used in hospital wards and for patients not admitted
to the hospital. Where examples of these limitations can be one or several of the follow­
ing: a miss of fluctuation of symptoms leading to incomplete understanding of the patients
condition, since the inflammation caused by asthma can vary significantly over time. A
delayed detection of the deterioration could happen since there are a given time between

more is a single (or few) measurements spread out over a long time may not accurately

In 2015 the CDC’s recommendations for a patient with asthma that is under control is to

This project is part of the Eurostar project, the Diaphragmic Monitoring of Diseases us­

Prior Analysis of dEMG have shown significant associations to the Forced Expiratory Vol­

lenge tests may serve as indicators of respiratory deterioration. [15, 16] It is hypothesized
that dEMG can provide continuous, non­invasive measurements of the effort needed for
breathing, thereby offering a continuous respiratory condition metric.
As dEMG is measuring an electrophysiological process, it is inherently susceptible to
artefacts from other physiological processes. For analysis it is therefore crucial to re­
move signal artefacts from the dEMG signal. Due to the electrode location during dEMG
recordings, a prominent artefact and a common problem in dEMG analysis is the cross talk
from the electrical activity of the heart beating. [17, 18]. Prior suggestions to remove the
the ECG component have either been using gating to remove the QRS­complex, which
heavily depends on knowing where the QRS are located or wavelet decomposition which
require expert knowledge in the feature engineering and doesn’t perform well in a well
defined frequency structure or adaptive filtering which doesn’t perform well under non­
stationary noise or non­linearity in data. All these limitations might better be handled with
machine learning given enough data. [17, 19, 20]
Since there are not a lot of recorded publicly available datasets of dEMG and ECG, it is
possible to change perspective of what is noise and what is needed data. Since there
are a lot of available ECG data, it is possible to add a dEMG­look­alike signal to an ECG
recording to train a model for noise removal. Furthermore is the removal of noise of ECG
very researched and there are well functioning methods already produced. [21, 22]

1.1
Thesis statement
This thesis aims to explore the potential of diaphragmatic electromyography (dEMG)
as a less intrusive, more continuous alternative to spirometry for monitoring respiratory
condition—particularly in patients with chronic respiratory diseases, both in clinical set­
tings and at home. A central focus of this work is addressing the foundational challenge
of accurately decomposing dEMG signals into their constituent EMG and ECG compo­
nents, which is essential before any meaningful classification of respiratory events can be
performed. By improving signal separation through deep learning techniques, this project
lays the groundwork for downstream applications such as detecting irregular breathing
patterns, coughing, or other behavioural events—ultimately contributing to the develop­
ment of early warning systems and intelligent respiratory monitoring tools.
Based on this project the paper ”U­Net Based Decomposition of Respiratory Surface Elec­
tromyography for Removal of Cardiac Artifacts” has been prepared and can be seen in
Appendix A.

1.3.1
The anatomy of breathing
Breathing is the process of transferring the oxygen in the air into the lungs, to the blood
stream and CO2 out.
Inhalation occurs as the effect of contracting the external intercostal muscles, lifting the
ribcage and contracting the diaphragm flattening it out to increase the volume in the tho­
racic cavity and thereby the lungs. According to Boyle’s law P = k/V , where P is pres­
sure, k is the pressure gradient, and V is volume, an increase in Volume where temper­
ature and molecules are kept constant (expressed through the pressure gradient, k) the
pressure decreases. The pressure difference between the surrounding air outside of the
body and the air inside the lungs makes the lungs fill up with air due to the natural ten­
dency of molecules to move towards lower pressure. [23]
During Exhalation the diaphragm and the external intercostal muscles relaxes, decreas­
ing volume in the thoracic cavity forcing air out of the lungs. During inhalation the lungs
and chest wall has also build up elastic recoil, that helps reducing volume. Exhalation
can be split up into two parts. Passive exhalation and active exhalation. In a rested state

Respiratory diseases
Respiratory diseases are pathological conditions making it more difficult to breathe. It
consists of many different conditions targeting the trachea, bronchii, alveoli, plaurae or
nerves and muscles connected to respiration. Respiratory diseases are split into two
main groups, Obstructive and Restrictive lung diseases.
Obstructive lung Disease
Obstructive lung disease is defined by obstruction of the airflow from the lungs, making it
difficult to exhale, which traps air in the lungs. The most common examples of obstruc­
tive lung diseases are Asthma and COPD. Asthma is a heterogeneous inflammatory dis­
ease of the airways periodically causing wheezing, shortness of breath, coughing and in­
creased production of mucus. The inflammation is associated with hyper­responsiveness
of the airways causing the obstruction, seen in figure 1.1. The common hyper­responses
are due to an allergic reaction to dust, pets, pests or fungi. [24]

1.3.3
Surface Electromyography
When any form of movement is initiated by a person, a neuronal signal in the motor cor­
tex of the brain starts to generate. This signal travels along the spinal cord via neurons
towards the muscle. In the spinal cord there are alpha motor neurons that integrates
the signals from the brain with sensory signals like stretch or tension from the muscles
to then generate an action potential (also called spike trains later), where each of these
action potential is a representation of a neural command from the brain to the muscle.
A motor units consist of a single alpha motor units connected to the rest of the fibres in
that particular unit. The action potential generated by the alpha neuron begin a release
of neurotransmitters that result in a change in electrical potential of the membranes of the
muscle cells. As a muscle consist of many motor units generating their own signal, the
final composite electrical signal is a summation of all the motor units. [26]
EMG is a method of measuring the motor unit electrical signals produced by the skeletal
muscles via contraction. Where the classic way of recording the motor unit EMG is done
invasively via needle electrodes, the preferred way is non­invasive as it offers less com­
plication puncturing the skin. Surface EMG a non­invasive method of measuring these
muscle action potentials. Surface EMG is measured by placing electrodes on the skin
above the target muscle. The recorded signal is therefore a composition of all the motor
units action potentials it can measure. That recorded combination of action potentials is
the Motor Unit Action Potential (MUAP). Seen in figure 1.2 is the representation of putting
two electrodes on the skin to measure MUAP. [26]

1.3.4
Electrocardiography (ECG)
ECG is the recording of the electrical activity of the heart usually over many cycles. The
representation of the ECG is formed form voltage over time through electrodes placed on
the skin. These electrodes can record the changes in electrical potential when the heart
goes through depolarization and repolarization which is a heart beat. Seen in figure 1.3 is
a basic representation of a heart and a heart beat. A heart beat starts from the SA node in
the upper right atrium that functions as a pacemaker for the heart, it generates electrical
pulse spontaneously at a regular interval. The pulse from the SA node travels along the
atrium muscle fibre walls resulting in contraction and pushes blood into the ventricles.
This is seen as the P­wave. The electrical pulse continues into the AV­node between the
atria and ventricles where it is slowed down to make sure atrium is fully contracted and
blood is finished flowing into the ventricles, depicted as the distance between the P­wave
and the QRS (known as the PR­interval). The pulse then travels through the heart via
the ventricular conduction system starting with the bundle of His, through purkinje fibres.
This makes the electrical pulse travel from the apex of the heart and up the ventricular
walls ensuring ventricular depolarization (contraction) upward, also known as the QRS­
complex. The QRS complex is the biggest signal spike the ECG have. Lastly the T­
wave in the heart beat is the repolarization of the fibres in the ventricles restoring resting
membrane potential making it ready to contract again. [27]
ECG, and especially the QRS­complex is a relative large and measurable electrical spike,
making it subject to become an artifact in other physiological measuring techniques. It is
seen in EEG [28], EMG [19] and every other type of electrode on skin measurement
conducted within some range of the heart.

1.4
Prior work
Removing noise from an ECG signal is very common and have been done in many dif­
ferent ways. In 2016 some of the modern methods was tested: FIR and IIR filtering, low
and high frequency noise removal techniques, Quadrature filtering, Adaptive noise can­
cellation techniques, Non­Local Means denoising techniques (NLM), Empirical Mode De­
composition(EMD), Variational Mode Decomposition (VMD), Wavelet transform denoising
methods. They also state that there are many more ways to do it and it is not possible
to pick a ”best one”. To do that, one would need to try every single method for that spe­
cific scenario, since length of signal, amount of noise, type of sensor e.g. all affect the
outcome of the model. [22]
The removal of ECG from dEMG has been done by Gronska et. al. [18] using Singular
Value Decomposition in combination with a frequency ratio index criterion put on the power
spectral density operating in the SNR range between ­20 and 0.
The dEMG is contaminated with a clean ECG and some motion artifacts. 2 performance
metrics are used: Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), for time­domain error cal­
culation, and Mean Frequency Difference (MFD) for error performance in the frequency
domain. The SNR difference of the reconstructed signal ranges from 6 to 26dB, where
the highest SNR gains are closer to the operating range of ­20 dB and the SNR gain of
6 for the reconstructed signal in operating SNR of 0. The MAPE decreases from 50% to
about 35%. The MFD is almost constant around 0.3 across the whole operating range.
It should be noted that the measured EMG data used in this study is measured on the
biceps and triceps of the participants.

2.1.1
ECG data
The ECG data is from the database PTB­XL dataset [29, 30], which is a large and publicly
available ECG database. It comprises 21,799 12­lead ECG recordings, each lasting 10
seconds, collected from 18,869 patients. The signals are sampled at 500 Hz and include
diagnostic labels, noise annotations, and detailed metadata. PTB­XL encompasses a
broad spectrum of cardiac pathologies, making it a valuable resource for supervised learn­
ing and clinical research. The dataset is accessible via Physionet.org, and the recordings
were retrieved using the WFDB package (version 4.1.2) [31, 32].

2.1.2
dEMG data
The EMG data used in this project is simulated using the BioMime model developed by Ma
et al. [33]. BioMime is a biophysically inspired, conditional generative model that synthe­
sizes realistic surface EMG signals based on underlying motor unit activity. The model
simulates motor unit spike trains and generates motor unit action potentials (MUAPs),
which are linearly summed and combined with environmental noise to produce EMG sig­
nals representative of those measured on the skin surface during naturalistic movements.

2.2.1
Spike train generation
It is hypothesized that the recruitment and firing rate distributions of muscle fibres dur­
ing force generation in muscles differs. Muscles needed to make fine and precise force
adjustments like finger movement seems to have a full recruitment of motor units even
at relatively small force levels. This does not seems to be the case regarding muscles
not needing to have the same fine­control of force, where the recruitment changes with
the needed force generation. Likewise the intensity of EMG signals are influenced by the
amount of recruited muscle fibres and their firing rates. This tells us that the relation be­
tween EMG intensity and isometric muscle force is linear in muscles with fine­control and
10

parabolic in others. [34] Which is why the generated spike trains will be of parabolic/trape­
zoidal form, as the muscles in the diaphragm do not require small fine­tuning and likely
have a parabolic relation between force and EMG intensity. Further more does Fogarty
et al. [35] state that changes in motor unit firing frequency within 10­30Hz have the most
significant effect on force generation, so modelling the dEMG under normal to elevated
breathing effort seems justifiable. An example of the trapezoidal form can be seen in fig­

2.2.2
Data generation
To simulate diaphragm muscle activity during respiration, parameters were randomly sam­
pled from predefined intervals. The spike rate were set within a range of 10 to 30 Hz to
reflect previously mentioned values. The ramping times for the trapezoidal profile form
was randomly selected to a value between 1­5 to model normal inhalation and exhalation
durations.
To match the segment length of 10 seconds, so merging with the ECG signal would be
smoother, i randomly simulated 1 to 5 non­overlapping segments in random places in a
20 second window and then cut a random 10 second segment. This makes sure that the
simulated data comprises of segments without an EMG component, some with a few fully
represented breaths and some segments where a breath could be ”cut in half”, to make
sure when training on the data the model learns, that is also a possibility.
Simulating a segment took a bit over 1 second, which is too long if it needs to be done
for every single input of the model, so i generated 500.000 segments and stored them
for later use. These were subsequently divided into training, validation, and test sets.
During model training, EMG samples were randomly drawn from this pre­generated pool,
to further avoid any form of data starvation or overfitting.
To introduce variability and improve the robustness of the model, two types of synthetic
noise were added to the simulated EMG. The first is a constant white noise across the
whole 10 second segment with a varying amplitude across segments. The second is

2.2.3
Noise factors
It is important to recognise that any signal and especially non­invasive measurements
comes with noise and is corrupted by several factors. Sources of noise regarding surface
EMG stems from both extrinsic factors, like environmental and other equipment related,
and intrinsic factors from other physiological events.
In general every non­invasive surface sensor measurements are highly impacted by mo­
tion artifacts, which are movements between the skin and the electrode connected to the
skin. It typically shows as low­frequency events, under 20Hz. These movements can oc­
cur due to external movement or voluntary movement of any joint or muscle contraction.
Another common factor is ambient electromagnet noise source which is the 50/60Hz in­
terference from the power line. Since the dEMG is measured so close to the heart and the
heart being such a powerful muscle, the ECG will be an ever problem hence the motivation
for this project. [26]
2.3
EMG/ECG separation
2.3.1
U­net
U­net architecture has proven very effective in biomedical signal and image analysis due
to its encoder­decoder structure in combination with its skip connections [36]. This allows
for preservation of fine grained local features while also capturing the bigger and broader
features. In comparison to other convolutional network, the U­net model tends to have
a lower GPU requirement and shorter training time, allowing for easier application when
there is a lot of data to be processed [37].
In this project, the U­net has been programmed to take in a single channel signal con­
taining both an EMG and an ECG component going through a series of encoder blocks,
followed by a series of decoder blocks. In figure 2.23 an overview of the model can be
seen.
The encoder introduces a series of reductions in spatial and temporal resolution by convo­
lution and pooling, where convolution functions as the feature detection for current layer
and pooling is a reduction in size of the signal by downsampling. This enables a hierarchi­
cal structure of feature extraction for different levels of abstraction the further down in the
12
Exploring Breathing Patterns Using Surface Electromyography of the Diaphragm
==PAGE:21==
encoder block series you go ­ meaning the further down in the encoder you go the more
compression is applied to the signal while keeping overall shape and essential patterns,
like peaks and general trend rather than specific detail.
The decoder reverses this process by reintroducing temporal resolution by upsampling
and decreasing the number of feature channels. For every level in the structure of the
model the skip connections depicted by the dotted lines reintroduces the high­resolution
feature map learned in the corresponding encoder. This enables the model to reconstruct
the signal based on the current abstraction in the decoder with the high­resolution from the
encoder through skip connections. The final output of the model is then a reconstruction
of the signal based on these different level of abstract representation of the signal.

Encoder/decoder blocks
Each encoder block consists of two consecutive one­dimensional convolutional layers,
each followed by a batch normalization and a ReLU activation function, and ends with
max pooling. An illustration of the encoder and decoder blocks can be seen in figure 2.24
The first convolutional layer in each of the encoder blocks detects local features such as
sudden amplitude spike changes where the next captures more complex patterns. The
batch normalization normalizes the output of the convolution across a batch making each
feature have a mean of 0 and 1 variance which improves training stability and speed. The
rectified linear unit (ReLu) is an activation function that introduces non­linearity making
learning higher complexity possible. Lastly the max pooling layer reduces the resolution
by downsampling the signal which increases computation time while introducing previ­
ously stated hierarchical structure.

Each decoder is modelled to progressively reconstruct the signal from the compressed
representation of the encoder output.
The first layer is a transpose convolution which upsamples the feature map, decreasing
the feature map and increasing the spatial and temporal resolution again. This is followed
by the same process of the convolutional layer that again refines the features, making the
model learn at the current complexity level followed by batch normalization as earlier. The
skipped connections makes the corresponding level encodes feature map before max
pooling be added to the feature map of the current decoder after upsampling but before
the convolutional layer, making sure the high­resolution information from the decoder is
used and not lost during the downsampling.

Training
In training, the U­net model was given a composite signal as input sample containing
the simulated EMG segments added to the ECG data, both from their respective train­
ing sets. The clean ECG without any noise or EMG is added as a reference making the
model optimize for ECG reconstruction. The training is done with the mean squared error
(MSE) as the loss function, making it possible for the model to calculate ”how far” away
the reconstruction is to the reference ECG signal. MSE has been chosen due to its sen­
sitivity to both small and large changes in signal, making it possible to both quantify rapid
deviations like QRS complex but also small like the P or T­wave. The used optimizer is
ADAM, set to an initial learning rate of 10−4, which is a middle of the pack choice base don
models broadly works with learning rates from 10−1 to 10−8 promoting adaptive gradient
handling and robust convergence properties. [38] A learning rate scheduler is used to
reduce learning rate by a factor of 10 when the validation loss fails to improve over five
epochs. Full termination of training is done when the learning rate is reduced twice (so
when no validation loss is achieved over 10 epochs) serving as a practical stop since the
model is unlikely to optimize any further.

4
Discussion
To further evaluate the performance of the model in denoising dEMG signals, a compar­
rison to previous work and state of the art methodologies should be carried out. It seems
that the U­net model outperforms other current approaches:
Grónska et al. used Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) and reported a median im­
provement of SNR of 12dB when the model is denoising signals with input SNR in the
range of ­20dB to ­8dB [18]. Although the SNR input range is not identical to this project,
th ecomparrison can still be made. This model has a SNRi of 21.97 on SNR input of ­10,
which is substantially better than 12.
Wang et al. proposed a fully convolutional auto­encoder structure to remove ECG artifacts
form dEMG signals with similar results to this model. Their model obtained a SNRi of 21dB
for SNR input of ­10dB, SNRi of 18dB for SNR input of ­6dB and SNRi 14dB for SNR input
of 0dB, which are very close to this projects results of SNRi values of 21.9,dB, 18.6,dB,
and 14.6,dB for SNR input ranges of <­10dB, ­10dB to ­5dB, and ­5dB to 5dB, respectively.
Even through other approaches of prior work regarding EMG/ECG decomposition en­
gages in removal of EMG from the ECG signal, this model can still be compared to their
results due to the structure of the U­net and it reconstructing the ECG signal. Which is
the reason for calculating table 3.2, since it can be used for benchmark comparison for
previous work regarding ECG denoising removing EMG.
Chiang et al. developed a denoising auto­encoder that achieved SNRi values ranging
from 10.5,dB (for SNR input = 7dB) to 15.5dB (for SNR input = ­1dB) [40]. Under similar
noise conditions, this model got comparable SNRi values of 11.49,dB and 14.89dB. This is
no surprise as their models structure is very comparable but without the skip­connections.
So with some fine­tuning one could hope to outperform their model.
Boudnik et at. proposed a temporal convolutional neural network and evaluate on com­
posite signal containing ECG signals and EMG signals measured on the lower right leg.
Their signals contained an average of 7dB SNR input and their model had a SNRi of
17.4dB making it outperform this projects model. [42]
Wang et al. proposed a denoising generative adverserial network [43] and reported a
SNRi of 25dB to 30dB, but the exact same model evaluated by Boudnik et al. was reported
to have SNRi of 10dB to 12dB, making it hard to make any comparison. This could be due
to the very different signal composition, form simulating EMG signals to recording EMG
on the arm or leg, to the many different approaches to incorporate noise.
4.0.1
Advantages and limitations of the proposed model
A key strength of the proposed U­net model is the ability to adapt to the diversity range
of ECG waveforms across different leads. Unlike the relative consistent pattern a regular
ECG recording might have, heartbeat­related noise in respiratory dEMG can be highly
irregular due to the natural changes of thorax and the tissue between the heart and the
sensor and thereby the ”transfer function”. As the thorax expands and contracts the rep­
resentation of the signal will change. This makes the modelling more complex and the
importance of recreating the morphological change the noise can bring even more valu­
able. [19]. Traditional wavelet­based approaches typically depend on mother wavelets
thus might not adequately mirror the possible changes in QRS complex shapes, which
Exploring Breathing Patterns Using Surface Electromyography of the Diaphragm
23
==PAGE:32==
could limit this this type of model. This projects model have been trained on all 12 leads
form the ECG database thus engaging in the potential shape variability of the ECG wave­
forms.
Another notable advantage of the U­net model is that it doesn’t need the prior detection
of QRS complex annotation. As found earlier the need for prior detection of the QRS
complex locations can serve problems. Seen in the examples in figure 3.6, the annotation
of QRS location can be quite the obstacle.
The model also preserve the amplitude of the EMG signal post decomposition, compared
to SWT which seemed to reduce the amplitude in all examples even though it contained
the overall fluctuation of the signal. This can be especially seen in figure 3.5b and fig­
ure 3.6a. For any future work of classifying normal vs deep breathing or coughing etc.,
retaining amplitude and general signal ratio is of high importance.
A clear limitation of the current model is how the simulated EMG data is generated. Look­
ing at figures 3.5 compared to figures 3.6, the residual ECG component contains sub­
stantially more EMG in the test done of the real dataset from Biagetti. This tells us that
the synthetically generated EMG signal does not emulate normal EMG signal as well as i
would have like to begin with. Any future work should focus on improvement of the realism
in the simulated EMG dataset, so the model can train on more correct data. Or this could
also be addressed by completing a large data collection study so the model could train
on real recording instead of simulations.
4.0.2
Thoughts and future work
Redundancy in MUAP Simulation
Simulating all the MUAPS around the arm as it is done in the study of Ma et al. could
possibly introduce redundancy. Current approach has been to cover complete MUAP
representation, which seemed beneficial at the time but might include a risk of over repre­
senting similar overlapping MUAP waveforms. This could lead to some overfitting of the
model or just in general inaccurate EMG representation. Future work could comprise of
variability in MUAP usage when simulating EMG signals.
Model Comparability Challenges
As mentioned earlier comparability of performance of models with other studies can quickly
be difficult due to changes in source of data, different length of input signals and different
noise distributions.
Frequency Bandwidth of Simulated dEMG
The frequency content of the simulated dEMG signals is narrower than what is typically
observed in real­world recordings. This was initially chosen due to the nature of muscle
type and their primary frequency range near the diaphragm. After some thought i realize
the frequency spectrum of a single output node from a muscle compared to the destination
at the sensor of many signals is not the same. Expanding the ranges for the simulation
could prove beneficial and better simulate real world data.
Expanding Noise Types During Training
The current training of the model only includes very limited noise profiles that should be
improved upon. Adding a more diverse selection of noise could make the model more
robust and accurate.
Behavioural Classification
A future goal could be to extend the model to include classification of various events such
as coughing, deep breathing, snoring, talking, or muscle spasms. Such classifications
could prove useful for general health monitoring and especially for patients with respiratory
diseases. Then detection of personalized irregular breathing could be possible. This

patient with a chronic disease.
Personalized Model Fine­Tuning
As a long term goal, with the DIAMOND project in mind, a fully personalized tuned model
for each person, could be a in­home solution and a great early detection of any worsening
of the patients respiratory condition.

Conclusion
This project has proposed a U­net based model for separating the ECG and EMG compo­
nent of a signal based on real ECG data and simulated EMG data based on the BioMime
model. And contrary to traditional approaches the model does not rely on pre annotation
of the QRS complexes, which proved to be difficult for ECG contaminated dEMG. The
model retains signal amplitude and shape while achieving a substantial SNR improve­
ment of 14.04dB across a range SNR input from ­10 to 10dB.
While there is room for improvements, the current results has potential to advance non­
invasive respiratory monitoring and be a part of a bigger system with the goal of enabling
downstream tasks such as continuous respiratory assessment combating the currently
problematic far­in­between doctoral visits for people with chronic respiratory diseases.

U-Net Based Decomposition of Respiratory Surface Electromyography
for Removal of Cardiac Artifacts
Morten G. Vorborg1, Jacopo de Araujo1, Sadasivan Puthusserypady1, Søren S. Rasmussen1†
Abstract— Diaphragmatic surface electromyography (dEMG)
has emerged as a promising tool for continuous respiratory
monitoring, with broad clinical applications. However, real-
time analysis of dEMG is hindered by strong electrocar-
diogram (ECG) contamination, limiting its utility in clinical
settings. To address this, we propose a U-Net-based model
for dEMG decomposition, enabling separation of ECG and
EMG components. Unlike traditional methods, our approach
does not rely on R-peak detection and effectively handles
non-linear signal decomposition, overcoming key limitations of
gating and wavelet-based techniques. The model was trained on
synthetic mixed signals, combining PTB-XL ECG recordings
with simulated EMG signals generated using the BioMime
model, and random noise. We demonstrate that our method
significantly improves dEMG separation, achieving a superior
SNR improvement (SNRi) of 14.03 dB, compared to 0.18 dB and
4.46 dB for conventional gating and wavelet-based approaches,
respectively.
Clinical Relevance—This work represents a key component
of a broader analysis pipeline required to improve non-invasive
respiratory monitoring. Enhancing dEMG signal quality, our
approach could significantly advance wearable respiratory
monitoring devices, facilitating continuous assessment of lung
function for asthma and other respiratory diseases in both
hospital and home settings.
I. INTRODUCTION
Asthma is a chronic condition causing inflammation and
narrowing of airway, leading to wheezing, shortness of
breath, chest tightness, and coughing. Symptoms vary in
severity and are often triggered by allergens, exercise, cold
air, or infections. Management includes avoiding triggers,
using inhalers, and monitoring lung function. Respiratory
surface electromyography of the diaphragm (dEMG) is a
potentially valuable tool for quantifying respiratory improve-
ments or deterioration in asthma patients, thereby supporting
treatment strategies.
Previous studies have shown that increases in dEMG
values correlate with declines in forced expiratory volume
(FEV1) during induced airflow limitation [1, 2] and that
dEMG is associated with the clinical asthma score in dysp-
neic infants and toddlers [3]. Additionally, research suggests
that real-time dEMG recording and analysis could be a
valuable tool for continuous respiratory assessment, with
applications spanning critical care, pulmonary rehabilitation,
and neuromuscular disease monitoring in both infants, chil-
dren, and adults. [1, 4–6]. Unlike conventional monitoring

methods that rely on intermittent spirometry or basic respira-
tory rate metrics, dEMG provides a continuous, non-invasive
measure of breathing effort, offering real-time insights into
respiratory function and potentially enabling earlier detection
of deterioration [7, 8].
However, due to the placement of dEMG electrodes bi-
laterally on the lower thorax, the electrocardiogram (ECG)
contaminate the dEMG signal, making the separation of these
signals crucial [7–9]. Current approaches in related works
include gating to remove the QRS complex, wavelet decom-
position strategies and adaptive filtering i.a. [8–10]. Several
methods depend on robust identification of the R-peak in
the ECG component, which can be challenging in dEMG
signals due to the presence of EMG. Recently, Gr´onska et
al. suggested using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
combined with a frequency ratio index criterion based on
the power spectral density to more accurately target ECG
and motion artifacts in dEMG signals [11] and Wang et
al. proposed an autoencoder structure for removing cardiac
artifacts using neural networks [12].
To address the limitations of the commonly used methods,
we propose an end-to-end approach using a U-Net based
model to decompose the dEMG into its ECG and EMG
components. The U-Net-based approach mitigates several
limitations of traditional methods, as it does not require infor-
mation about R-peak location, functions well across different
expressions of the ECG component, and enables non-linear
decomposition of the signal components. Furthermore, the
abundance of ECG datasets and methods for simulating EMG
signals facilitated a straightforward optimization strategy for
the model to learn the distinction between the two signal
components.
Our research improves the reliability of EMG-based res-
piratory monitoring, potentially aiding the development of
wearable devices for continuous respiratory health assess-
ment.
II. DATA
To optimize the model, we utilized a simulated combina-
tion of ECG and EMG components. This approach allows
for easy estimation of reconstruction errors for each signal,
which would not have been feasible with real dEMG record-
ings. An example of a constructed signal is shown in Fig. 1.
For the ECG dataset, we used the PTB-XL database
[13, 14]. This dataset includes 21,799 12-lead recordings
of ECG, each 10 seconds long from 18,869 patients. The
recordings are sampled at 500 Hz, and contain diagnostic
annotations, noise descriptions, and metadata. This data set

III. DECOMPOSITION MODEL
The proposed model for decomposing a dEMG signal
recording into its ECG and EMG components, consists
of three parts: Pre-Processing, Segmentation, and Post-
Processing. The proposed pipeline is visualized in Fig. 2
A. Pre-Processing
During pre-processing, the signals were resampled to a
frequency of 500 Hz, and baseline wander was removed
using a second-order Butterworth high-pass filter with a 1 Hz
cut-off frequency. Subsequently, all samples were z-score
normalized.
Recordings longer than the 10-second duration accepted
by the model were split into 10-second windows with a 5-
second overlap. Each window was standardized individually
and the standardization coefficients for each window were
saved to allow rescaling the output back to the original scale.
B. Segmentation - The U-Net Model Architecture
The decomposition leverages a neural network of the
U-Net architecture [19]. The U-Net architecture excels in
biomedical signal analysis, as its encoder-decoder structure
with skip connections preserves fine details while capturing
hierarchical features [20]. This design is computationally
efficient and performs well even with limited annotated data.
Additionally, a beneficial aspect of the U-Net architecture
for this project is that the input and output share the same
temporal-spatial dimensions. In this case, the dimensions
were [1 × l · fs], and the model was optimized to take a
single channel of a signal with both an EMG and an ECG
component as input to predict the ECG component as output.
The EMG component can thus be obtained as the difference
between the model input and the output.
We designed the U-Net using convolutional blocks, as il-
lustrated in Fig. 3. The convolutional layers had a kernel size
of 3, with a stride and padding of 1, ensuring the temporal
dimension of the signal remained unchanged through the
block. The first convolutional layer adjusted the number of
input channels. In the encoder, each block was followed by
a factor-two max pooling, while in the decoder, the blocks
were preceded by a factor-two up-sampling via a transpose
convolution. The bottleneck connections were established
using a fully connected layer.
C. Post-Processing
During the training and primary evaluation on simulated
signals, no post-processing is required, as the predicted
output is directly compared to the clean ECG. However,
for recordings longer than 10-seconds, the signals need to
be stitched and rescaled to obtain a complete signal in the
original magnitude.
The model outputs are stitched together using a weighted
mean approach. To ensure smooth transitions, weights in-
crease from the borders of each segment toward the center,
giving higher importance to the central region and minimiz-
ing any boundary artifacts. The weights are normalized to
ensure proportional contributions from overlapping regions,
resulting in a seamless reconstruction of the full signal.
IV. METHODOLOGY
A. Data splitting
The ECG recordings of the PTB-XL database were split
into individual leads, considering these as independent sam-
ples. All samples were divided into training (60%), validation
(10%), or test set (30%) while ensuring that samples origi-
nating from the same patient could only exist within one of
the sets. The samples were split at random with a fixed seed
for reproducibility. The comparison methods were given the
same test set as the model.

B. EMG Simulation
Electromyography (EMG) is a technique used to measure
the electrical activity of muscles. The waveform generated
by the combined action potentials of all fibers within a single
motor unit (MU) is known as the motor unit action potential
(MUAP). After propagating through the surrounding tissues,
the MUAP reaches the sensing electrodes. The surface EMG
signal reflects the summation of MUAPs from all active
motor unit sources, along with additional environmental
noise. We use the BioMime model [17], which is a generative
model which simulate the activation and summation of
MUAPs along with the effect of the surrounding tissue by
setting the firing rate of the spike train of motor neuron
activation and applying a trapezoidal mask to model the
muscle recruitment at the start and end of the muscle
activation. The above calculation is performed for each of
320 simulated MUAPs in the BioMime model and then
aggregated to estimate the surface EMG.
We set the parameters to be randomly chosen within a
predefined interval to represent the muscle activity of the
diaphragm during respiration. We set the firing rate in the
range of 10 to 30 Hz for the spike train, along with a ramp
up/down time of 1 to 5 seconds for the trapezoidal mask
simulating difference in the inhalation and exhalation phases.

In addition to the simulated EMG signals, we introduced
two types of randomly generated noise to enhance variance in
the EMG source. The first was a constant level of white noise
applied throughout the 10-second segment, with amplitude
varying between segments.The second set comprised bursts
of white noise, filtered to the EMG frequency range (10–250
Hz), with five bursts per segment, each having its own
independent noise level.
C. Training
During training, each sample was constructed by adding
the simulated EMG signal and noise to the ECG signal
drawn from the training set. This synthetic mixed signal was
given as the input to the model, while the original ECG,
i.e., without any added EMG component, was used as a
reference. Hence, the model was optimized to reconstruct the
ECG component of a signal with mixed components. The
mean square error (MSE) between the predicted ECG and
the original ECG was used as the loss function. We used an
ADAM optimizer with lr = 10−4 along with a learning rate
scheduler, which sets lr = 0.1lr if the validation loss does
not improve in 5 epochs. Training was terminated when the
learning rate had been dropped twice, as this would arguable
mean convergence of optimization.

Stationary Wavelet Transform (SWT). Both methods were
implemented using the ReSurfEMG python package [21].
Methods were implemented using the default parameters in
the package.

B. Clinical Impact
The proposed U-Net model for EMG/ECG decomposition
in respiratory dEMG recordings has potential clinical rele-

the separation of ECG artifacts from respiratory EMG, the
model may enhance the reliability of extracted respiratory
features, which could be particularly useful for tracking dis-
ease progression in both hospital and at-home settings. How-

only one component of a larger signal analysis pipeline, and
additional processing steps are required to derive meaningful
indicators of respiratory decline or improvement to support
more informed clinical decision-making.

C. Strengths and Limitations
A notable advantage of the model for ECG artifact removal
in dEMG is its capacity to adapt to the diverse morphologies
of ECG signals across different leads. Heartbeat artifacts
in respiratory EMG may exhibit greater irregularity than
the ECG features commonly observed in leads I and II
due to electrode position variations, with diaphragmatic
electrodes being more affected by anatomical changes during
breathing [10]. Traditional wavelet-based methods often rely
on predefined mother wavelets, which may not effectively
capture the variability in QRS complex shapes, potentially
limiting its accuracy in EMG recordings on the diaphragm.
In contrast, we trained the U-Net using all 12 leads available,
thus allowing for a larger adaptation to the variation in the
morphology of cardiac artifacts.
Additionally, the proposed model has the strength that it
is independent of prior detection of R-peaks in the signal,
which the implementation of both gating and SWT denoising
uses. From Fig. 4 and Fig. 5 it is clear, that detecting R-peaks
is difficult in ECG signals contaminated with artifacts, which
in this case the EMG component can be considered. The R-
peaks are detected using the same method in Fig. 4 and
Fig. 5, but it is evident, that the accuracy is substantially
decreased on the real dataset. Utilizing the proposed model,
the components can be split prior to detecting R-peaks,
thus potentially increasing the accuracy of this for further
analysis.
Another strength lies in the retainment of the amplitude of
the denoised EMG component. Comparing the amplitudes of
Fig. 5 it can be seen that while SWT denoising retains the
fluctuations of the EMG signal, the amplitude is much lower
than in the original signal, and the difference between normal
breathing (first 60 seconds) and heavy breathing (last 60
seconds) is low. Opposite, in the U-Net denoised signal, the
amplitude is much closer to the original and the difference
between normal and heavy breathing more clear. Gating also
has a high retainment of the amplitude, however, as only
the QRS complex is removed, other waveforms from the
ECG are still influencing the denoised signal (evident in both
Fig. 4 and Fig. 5).
A limitation to the proposed model is inherited from the
signals used for training the model. As the model learns
the representation of the ECG from a corrupted source, the
method for corrupting the signals become highly important.
Comparing the residual ECG components from the proposed
model in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5, it is clear that a larger proportion
of the EMG component is remaining in the real signals.
This implies that there is a difference in the simulated and
real contamination applied during training, which in return
influences the performance on real signals. Thus, future
improvements should focus on constructing more life like
signal components during training. This could be achieved
by refining the process of simulating dEMG making it more
realistic or by conducting large-scale collection studies to
gather sufficient amounts of data that deep learning models
require. However, the latter is extremely resource-intensive.
Additionally, our model currently does not account for mo-
tion artifacts in the dEMG segments, which is a common
form of noise. Including that in future simulations could
improve the model.
D. Future Work
An important aspect not explored in this study is the
impact of the denoising method on improving accuracy in
downstream tasks. Kahl et al. demonstrated that removing
cardiac artifacts from respiratory EMG significantly en-
hances the detection of respiratory fatigue [10]. While the
present study focuses solely on dEMG denoising, future
work should investigate its potential benefits for applications
such as breath segmentation, breathing effort estimation,
along with other respiratory feature extraction. In this con-
text, the ability to accurately decompose dEMG into both
ECG and EMG components is likely advantageous, as both
signals contain respiratory features that could enhance task
performance.
VII. CONCLUSION
In this study, we introduced a U-Net-based model for the
decomposition of dEMG into its ECG and EMG components,
addressing challenge of ECG artifact contamination in res-
piratory EMG recordings. Unlike conventional approaches
such as gating and wavelet-based techniques, our method
does not rely on R-peak detection, effectively handles non-
linear signal decomposition, and adapts to variations in ECG
morphology, leading to superior signal separation.
Through evaluation using synthetic mixed signals com-
posed of ECG recordings from PTB-XL and simulated
EMG signals from the BioMime model, we demonstrated a
substantial improvement in SNR, with our model achieving
an SNRi of 14.03dB, significantly outperforming gating
(0.18dB) and wavelet-based methods (4.46dB).
These findings highlight the potential clinical impact of
our approach in advancing non-invasive respiratory moni-
toring technologies. However, while our results are promis-
ing, their true significance lies in their ability to enhance
subsequent analysis. Future research should thus focus on
assessing the broader benefits of this model within the
context of continuous respiratory assessment. A key advan-
tage of our approach is its ability to accurately separate
dEMG components, enabling downstream analysis to lever-
age respiratory influences from both ECG and EMG signals,
thereby providing a more comprehensive and robust basis for
evaluating respiratory function. By improving dEMG signal
quality, this work contributes to the development of wearable
respiratory monitoring devices, facilitating continuous lung
function assessment in both hospital and home settings.
