ABSTRACT 
To support outgrowth of the changes in the production and delivery of clean electricity, 
it is important to have a test platform which would allow verification of standard 
technical requirements set for grid connected power electronics systems 
 
This thesis proposes a complete modelling and control of a grid connected converter. 
The main task of grid connected converter system is to provide a controlled injection 
of active power flow from the input side (DC source) to the utility grid. In addition, on 
demand it can provide independent control of reactive power. Behind active and 
reactive power control concept, lies the converter current control based on decoupled 
dq synchronous reference frame. In order to achieve active and reactive power flow 
control, the synchronization with the grid has to be done, this lead to the implementation 
of the three phase PLL based on dq synchronous reference frame. The power flowing 
to the utility grid, should meet the IEEE standard for grid connected application. This 
constraint is fulfilled by the implementation of the three phase LCL filter. 
 
For testing the Grid connected converter system, the Hardware in the loop is used, 
where the current control method is tested on the provided Typhoon HIL602 system, 
which gives an environment for evaluating the performance, the operation, and the 
testing of the grid connected converter.

CHAPTER 1     
1 Introduction 
 
 
The penetration of wind and photovoltaic (PV)-generated electrical energy into 
the grid system worldwide is increasing exponentially [1]. A limiting factor is the 
increasingly stringent grid requirements imposed by different grid operators aiming to 
maintain grid stability [1]. Both wind power (WP) plants and PV power plants are 
connected to the grid through grid converters which, besides transferring the generated 
DC power to the AC grid, should now be able to exhibit advanced functions like: 
dynamic control of active and reactive power; stationary operation within a range of 
voltage and frequency; voltage ride-through; reactive current injection during faults; 
participation in a grid balancing act like primary frequency control [1]. 
 
 
A photovoltaic system (PV system) uses one or more solar panels to convert 
sunlight into electricity. However, the solar energy produces the DC power, and hence 
power electronics and control equipment are required to convert DC to AC power [2]. 
In the case of residential or rooftop grid connected PV systems, the electricity demand 
of the building is met by the PV system and only the excess is fed into the grid. Its 
control function must follow the voltage and frequency of the utility-generated power 
presented on the distribution line [2].  
1.1 Project motivation 
 
The feeding of electricity into the grid, requires the transformation of DC into 
AC by a grid controlled solar converter. The power system is influenced by the variation 
of the load, causing the power system frequency to change accordingly. Adding a load 
will lead to a change of the system frequency, forcing the operator to adjust the system 
power in order to stabilize it. Moreover, having a load consuming reactive power will 
make the voltage to drop and the need to supply reactive power. That is the reason why 
active and reactive power have to be controlled in a grid connected system. Solar grid 
connected converters should be designed to operate within allowable power quality 
limits set by IEEE standards. The main purpose of the grid connected solar system is to 
transfer maximum solar array energy into grid with a unity power factor [3]. The PV 
==PAGE:12==
                                                                                                                    Page 12 of 88 
 
 
power is influenced by environmental factors, such as temperature and irradiance. Since 
the characteristic curve of a solar cell exhibits a nonlinear voltage current characteristic, 
a maximum power point tracker (MPPT) controller is required to match the solar cell 
power to the environmental changes [2].  
 
 
The major constraints in the fast development of rooftop photovoltaic system 
are the cost of photovoltaic module and the challenge of interfacing PV converter and 
grid. Because of these challenges it is necessary to use the energy of PV module 
optimally and also to design the necessary controller for the efficient interfacing with 
the grid [2]. In order to maximize the utilization of the solar array for a given insolation 
and temperature, it is necessary to extract maximum energy from the PV module [2].

2.1 Use of Active and reactive power 
 
The main task of the grid connected converter (GCC) is to provide a controlled 
injection of active power from the DC source to the utility grid in order to stabilize the 
power system frequency. On demand the grid-connected PV system should provide 
independent control of reactive power in order to support the utility grid voltage.   
 
 
While active power is the energy supplied to run a motor, the heat a home, or 
illuminate an electric light bulb, reactive power provides the important function of 
regulating voltage [4]. If the voltage on the system is not high enough, active power 
cannot be supplied. Reactive power is used to provide the voltage levels necessary for 
active power to do useful work. Reactive power is essential to move active power 
through the transmission and distribution system to the customer [4]. 
 
 
Active power accomplishes useful work while reactive power supports the 
voltage that must be controlled for system reliability. Reactive power has a profound 
effect on the security of power systems because it affects voltages throughout the 
system. Alternating current (AC) is supplied in a 50Hz waveform. Reactive power is 
produced when the current waveform is out of phase with the voltage waveform due to 
inductive or capacitive loads. Current lags voltage with an inductive load, and leads 
voltage with a capacitive load. Only the component of current in phase with voltage 
produces real or active power that does real work. Current is in phase with voltage for 
a resistive load, like an incandescent light bulb. Reactive Power is necessary for 
producing the electric and magnetic fields in capacitors and inductors [4]. 
 
 
The additional current flow associated with reactive power can cause increased 
losses and excessive voltage sags [4]. Transmission system operators have to ensure 
that reactive reserves are available to handle system contingencies such as the loss of a 
==PAGE:16==
                                                                                                                    Page 16 of 88 
 
 
generator or transmission line, because increased current flows after the occurrence of 
contingencies can produce greatly increased of reactive power absorption in 
transmission lines [4]. With the right control scheme and algorithms, grid connected 
PV system could be controlled to supply local reactive power and to regulate local 
voltage. The grid connected PV system must be interfaced to the local power system 
through a converter because they produce DC that has to be converted to 50Hz AC.  
The converter can also be designed and controlled to “inject” reactive power locally 
and regulate voltage. Thus, grid connected PV system with the converter could supply 
reactive reserves [4]. Figure 2-1 show the power triangle where the power factor less 
than one indicate the presence of reactive power and a unity power factor will show that 
only active power is being supplied.

2.2 DC-AC converter 
2.2.1 Introduction  
 
The DC-AC converter is the central element in the GCC system. The primary 
function of a voltage source converter (VSC) is to convert a fixed DC voltage to a three 
phase AC voltage with variable magnitude and frequency [5]. A simplified circuit 
diagram for a two level voltage source converter for high-power medium-voltage 
applications is shown on figure 2-2. 
 
 
 
 
The VSC is composed of six group of active switches, Sa_top, Sa_bot, Sb_top, 
Sb_bot, Sc_top, Sc_bot, with a free-wheeling diode in parallel with each switch. Each 
switch consists of one IGBT switching device. The converter is connected to two 
identical voltage sources, each with voltage of 𝑉𝐷𝐶 /2 . The common point of the 
sources labeled as node 0 is the DC-side midpoint and is the voltage reference node. 
The multilevel converter topologies like the three phase two level VSC, are suited for 
high-power medium-voltage industrial applications due to their ability to meet the 
demand of power ratings and power quality associated with reduced current and voltage 
harmonic distortion, lower electromagnetic interface, and higher efficiencies [6].  
2.2.2 Sinusoidal Pulse width modulation (SPWM) 
 
Various modulation methods for a three phase two level VSC have been 
developed. They could be generally classified into three groups, which are carrier-based 
pulse width modulation (CB-PWM) or sinusoidal PWM (SPWM), the space vector 
Figure 2-2: Two-level voltage source inverter [8] 
==PAGE:18==
                                                                                                                    Page 18 of 88 
 
 
modulation (SVM), and the selective harmonic elimination (SHE). Among these PWM 
strategies, the SPWM has probably been the most popular due to its simplicity of 
Implementation. The main result of PWM modulation strategy is to generate a PWM 
signal with fast average value over switching PWM period equal to modulating signal. 
The output PWM signal waveform depends on carrier waveform type. Figure 2-3 gives 
overview of PWM output obtained using saw-tooth and triangle carrier signal. 
 
 
 
In a saw-tooth Carrier, the leading (rising) edge of PWM output occurs at fixed 
instants in time while the position of the trailing (falling) edge is modulated as the 
reference signal level varies [7].  
 
 
In the triangle Carrier Both the leading edge and the trailing edge of the PWM 
output are modulated. The rising and falling edge of the triangle are usually symmetric 
so that the pulse is centered within a carrier cycle when the reference is a constant. The 
method is also called double-edge modulation [7]. 
 
 
The triangle carrier is optimal selection for three phase converter, because it 
gives minimum switching state transitions during control. In every commutation, there 
is change in the converter output line voltage which is not the case with saw-tooth 
carrier (which have additional unnecessary switching state between two successive 
changes of output voltages) [8]. Current ripple harmonics are at two times higher 
frequency which at final gives harmonics of lower amplitude. It becomes standard 
option in modern microcontrollers to select between saw-tooth carrier or triangle carrier 
Figure 2-3: Inverter phase output in case of saw-tooth and triangle carrier [8] 
==PAGE:19==
                                                                                                                    Page 19 of 88 
 
 
signal (timer/counter) within PWM peripheral module, so there is no need to worry on 
more complex implementation of triangle-counter [8]. 
 
 
If the modulating signal is sinusoidal waveform, regardless of carrier signal 
waveform, we get sine PWM modulation. If we consider the three phase converter, we 
need three phase sine PWM modulator [8]. In that case, a unique PWM carrier signal 
is used which modulates a set of three-phase modulating signals. One modulating signal 
defines two PWM signals for the same converter phase leg. One PWM signal is for 
upper switch, and other is for lower switch in the same phase leg.  
 
 
In a three phase SPWM, a triangular carrier is compared to three modulating 
signals (𝑉𝑎, 𝑉𝑏, 𝑉𝑐)  which are 1200  from each other. The results of the comparison are 
the control signals to the switches. The switches are controlled in a complementary way 
so that in one converter leg, one switch is turned on while the other is turned off.

CHAPTER 3     
3 LCL filter 
3.1 Introduction 
 
The converter previously described is the most popular power electronic 
converter used to connect renewable energy sources to the utility grid. However, the 
high frequency PWM modulation generated in converters can cause harmonic injection 
to the grid. Grid filter reduces harmonic injection to the utility grid [10]. 
 
 
Grid filter also known as line filters are connected between the power converter 
and the utility grid. [10] gives the two main functions of the grid filter which are: 
 To Reduce high frequency harmonics caused by the PWM modulation to improve 
the power quality supplied to the grid. 
 To couple the impedance between the two voltage sources, the power converter and 
utility grid, preventing occurrence of short circuit by connecting directly the power 
converter to the grid. 
 
 
Some of the techniques used to control harmonics injection to the grid are 
magnetic flux compensation, injection controlled of harmonics in the current, 
multilevel converters and passive filters [10]. The passive filter topology is simple and 
inexpensive compared to other filtering techniques. 
 
 
L and LCL filters are the most used topologies for passive grid filters. L filter 
consists of an inductor connected in series between the converter and the grid. 
Attenuation of the L filter is -20dB/decade over the full frequency range. When the L 
filter is used, switching frequency in the converter must be high, allowing the L filter 
to reduce the total harmonic distortion (THD) with reasonable size, weight, and cost 
[10]. 
 
  
The LCL filter is composed of two inductors and one capacitor. Attenuation of 
the LCL filter is -60dB/decade for the higher order harmonics to the resonant 
==PAGE:23==
                                                                                                                    Page 23 of 88 
 
 
frequency. The LCL filter reduces current and voltage harmonics in the converter 
output more effectively compared to the L filter [10]. LCL filter had an inherently high 
cut-off frequency and strong penetrating ability in low frequency [10]. So LCL filter 
has come into wide use in the converter. The challenge arises in the selection of the 
parameter and control resonance [10]. 
3.2 Advantage of LCL filter 
 
[11] presents some advantages of the LCL filter which includes: 
 The capability of attenuating harmonics at lower frequencies, which is a significant 
feature for high power applications. 
 A better decoupling between the filter and grid impedance (as it reduces the 
dependence of the filter on the grid parameters)  
 A lower ripple of the current stress across the grid inductor. 
Furthermore, LCL filter provides better attenuation than other filters with the same size 
and by having an inductive output, it is capable of limiting current inrush [12]. 
On the other side, an unstable LCL filter may cause both dynamic and steady state input 
current distortion due to resonance. In order to reduce oscillations and unstable states 
of the LCL filter, a passive damping is can be used where the damping resistor is added 
in series with the capacitor [13].

The GCC system output should have low current distortion levels to insure that 
no adverse effects are caused to other equipment connected to the utility system. 
Therefore, current injection must meet the standard established by [14] for 
Interconnecting Distributed Resources with the utility grid.  The harmonics current 
requirements are shown on table 3-1.

4 Phase locked loop (PLL) 
4.1 Introduction  
 
Renewable power generation systems utilizing power electronics converter rely 
on accurate grid phase angle determination in order to successfully close grid voltage 
vector oriented control loop usual for this kind of application [17]. Phase locked loop 
(PLL) is the most modern and most common method for determination of the phase 
angle and frequency of the grid voltage [17].  
 
 
Within the grid connected converter control algorithm it is necessary to 
accurately and precisely determine the grid voltage phase angle, in order to achieve 
independent control of active and reactive power flow between the converter input side 
and the grid, or transform the feedback variables to a reference frame suitable for 
control purposes [17]. Error in the phase angle estimation can lead to the significant 
errors in the imposed converter output voltage, and thus the error between the reference 
and injected power (current) into the grid [17]. So, it is important to carefully consider 
synchronization unit design and test it in various voltage conditions relevant to the real 
utility grid. In the literature related to the grid synchronization, different methods can 
be found which are applied in practice for grid connected converter. The most common 
method used today is PLL implemented in the dq synchronous rotating reference frame, 
shown in figure 4-1.

5 Active and reactive power control 
through Currents control 
5.1 Introduction  
 
One of the main tasks of GCC system is to provide controlled injection of active 
power flow from the input side source to the utility grid. In addition, on demand it can 
provide independent control of reactive power. 
 
 
[20]  presents two methods for active and reactive power control. The first 
method is the voltage mode control where the active and reactive power are controlled 
respectively by the phase angle and the amplitude of the voltage source converter 
(VSC). The main disadvantage is that the VSC is not protected against overcurrent’s 
because of the lack of current control loop. The second method is the current mode 
control where the VSC line current is regulated by a dedicated current control scheme, 
through the VSC AC side terminal voltage. Due to this current regulation the VSC is 
protected against overcurrent conditions [20]. The current mode control will be the base 
of the active and reactive power control in the remaining of this report. 
 
 
Behind active and reactive power control concept lies converter current control 
algorithm. This control algorithm is based on reference frame theory, where grid 
currents and voltages are transformed into a reference frame rotating synchronously 
with the grid voltage. The PLL described in the previous chapter provides the phase 
angle of the grid voltages required for the synchronization. As a consequence, the 
control variables (grid currents and voltages) appears as a DC values, also called dq 
components. In such system the grid current dq components Id and Iq, independently 
determines the active and reactive power flow between the converter and the grid. 
 
5.2 Modelling of the Grid connected converter (GCC) 
 
As previous mentioned, the voltage source converter is connected to the grid 
through the LCL filter in order to fulfil the requirement of limiting the current distortion 
==PAGE:47==

5.3 Choice of Current controller scheme 
 
The current controller of the three phase VSC plays a major role in controlling 
the GCC system. The performance of the converter system is highly affected by the 
quality of the applied current controller [11]. Among many control mechanisms which 
have been developed to regulate the converter output current that is injected into the 
utility grid, three major types of current controller have evolved: Hysteresis Controller, 
Predictive Controller and Linear Proportional-Integral (PI) controller [11]. 
 
 
Predictive controller has a very good steady-state performance and provides a 
good dynamic performance. But, its performance is sensitive to system parameters. 
The Hysteresis Controller has a fast transient response, non-complex implementation 
and an inherent current protection. But it has some drawbacks such as variable 
switching frequency and high current ripples. These cause a poor current quality 
and introduce difficulties in the output filter design [11]. 
==PAGE:51==
                                                                                                                    Page 51 of 88 
 
 
 
The PI controller is the most common control algorithm used for current error 
compensation. PI controller calculates an error value as the difference between a 
measured converter output current and a desired injected current to the grid, then the 
controller attempts to minimize the error between them.   
 
 
The PI controller calculation algorithm involves two separate constant 
parameters, the proportional constant Kp and the integral constant Ki. The proportional 
term of the controller is formed by multiplying the error signal by a gain Kp. This tends 
to reduce the overall error with time. However, the effect of the proportional term will 
not reduce the error to zero, and there is some steady state error. The Integral term of 
the controller is used to fix small steady state errors. The Integral term integrates the 
error then multiplies it by a Ki constant and becomes the integral output term of the PI 
controller. This removes the steady state error and accelerates the movement of the 
process into the reference point [11]. 
 
 
The advantages of PI current control scheme are that it offers an excellent 
steady-state response, low current ripple, constant switching frequency, in addition to 
well defined harmonic content. Moreover, the controller is insensitive to system 
parameters since the algorithm does not need system models. PI controllers can be 
applied either in the stationary (αβ) or in synchronous (dq) reference frame. When the 
synchronous PI controller is used, the control variables become DC and the PI 
compensators are able to reduce the stationary error of the fundamental component to 
zero [11], whereas with PI controllers working in the stationary system, there is an 
inherent tracking error of phase and amplitude. Therefore, current control in a 
synchronous (rotating) reference frame, using PI controllers is the typical solution in 
the three-phase grid connected converter. This synchronous PI controller will be the 
scheme used to control the active and reactive power and will be designed in the next 
section.

6.1 Introduction  
 
In previous chapters, Simulink was used to model the grid connected converter. 
The discrete simulation results in Simulink have been explained and it showed that the 
reactive and active power is controlled independently given a current reference. 
However, the interaction with the real world has to be considered now. The actual 
system should be embedded in a simulation loop to get the results. This kind of 
simulation is referred as hardware in the loop simulation. Since it is also performed in 
real time, it is also called real time simulation. Real time hardware in the loop replaces 
the emulated hardware under test with the real hardware that interacts with the computer 
Simulink models [25]. 
 
 
The main purpose with the HIL Simulation is to test the hardware device on a 
simulator before we implement it on the real process. According to [25], the real time HIL 
testing eliminates the costly hardware and greatly reduce the design cost because the 
process can be developed and tested with and emulator before hardware implementation. 
Moreover, the HIL testing is more safe because the testing can be done without damaging 
equipment or endangering lives [26]. This means that we do not need to build the converter 
and the LCL filter. In the next section the Typhoon HIL602 emulator is described and will 
be used to simulate the GCC system.

The ADC block 
 
The ADC is a 12 bits’ analogue to digital converter. The grid voltages and 
currents from the Typhoon HIL emulator are converter from analogue to digital through 
the ADC. Normally there are six signals we want to sample from the ADC, which are 
the three grid voltages and the three grid currents. But for testing purposes and for 
convenience, the reference currents for reactive and active power control were also 
supply through the Typhoon HIL emulator and therefore sampled from the ADC block. 
That’s why we are sampling eight signals instead of six.  
 
 
The PWM frequency is 10kHz, therefore the sampling frequency of the ADC 
should be two time the PWM frequency in order to allow a full conversion within one 
PWM period. The ADC convert any analogue input between 0 V and 3 V to digital 
values from 0 to 4095. Voltages above 3V produce the full scale conversion result. The 
digital outputs of the ADC have to be scaled to value representing the sinusoid from the 
HIL emulator and that is done by the current and voltage scaling blocks. More 
information about the ADC block can be found in [29] 
 
The currents and voltages scaling blocks 
 
The grid voltages and currents are both sinusoidal, this means we have positive 
and negative value of both voltages and currents. The current and voltage sensor 
measures positives and negatives values. The calibration is done so that the 0 A or the 
0 V correspond to the half range of the ADC (2048). The digital value 4095 represents 
the maximum current or voltage that can be measured and the digital value 0 represents 
the maximum negative current or voltage that can be measured.   
 
 
In order to scale these inputs, the 12-bit ADC value are shifted by 6 bits to the 
left. The result is a normalized representation of the current and voltage, where +1 is 
the maximum positive current or voltage and -1 is the maximum negative current or 
voltage. These value is then converted into double and multiplied by the maximum grid 
phase voltage of 400 V ∗√2
√3 = 326.6 V for the voltages and multiplied by the rated 
current of 10 A for the currents. The scaling block is shown on figure 6-8.

and follow the step change on the current reference moreover each current has a 
frequency of 50 Hz. As the current reference Id=0, only the active power is supplied. 
 
Case 2:  Step of Iq from 2A to 4A and then from 4A to 2A at 50 Hz, Id=0; 
 
We make step of the current reference Iq from 2A to 4A and then from 4A to 
2A respectively as seen on figure 6-11. We can see that the phase A grid voltage and 
current are in phase, and according to the theory previously developed, only the active 
power is supplied. Moreover, the phase A grid current in green follows the step change 
of the current reference Iq. This implies a good precision of the control algorithm. 
 
Case 3:  Step of Id from 2A to 4A and then from 4A to 2A at 50 Hz, Iq=0; 
 
We make step of the current reference Id from 2A to 4A and then from 4A to 
2A respectively as seen on figure 6-12. We can see that the phase A grid voltage and 
current are out of phase. In this case, only the reactive power will be supplied and this 
is in accordance with the theory explained in the previous chapter. It is also noticed on 
figure 6-12 that the phase A grid current in green follows the step change of the current 
reference Iq, implying the robustness of the control algorithm. 
 
Case 4:  Step of Iq and Id from 0A to 5A at 50 Hz; 
 
We make step of the current references Id from 0A to 5A we measure the voltage 
and the current then we also make the step of Iq and do the same measurement as seen 
on figure 6-13. On the top on figure 6-13, the step of the current Iq is performed. We 
can see that the phase A grid voltage and current are in phase and the phase difference 
between them are approximately 2 degrees. Ideally, the phase difference should be zero, 
but because of the noise and the timing of the measurement, we can only get a value 
close to zero.

On the bottom on figure 6-13, the step of the current Iq is performed. We can 
see that the phase A grid voltage and current are out of phase. The measure of the phase 
difference between them gives a value of 91 degrees as seen on the bottom on figure 
 6-13. This phase difference is very close to 90 degrees. This confirms that only the 
reactive power will be supplied and this is in accordance with the theory explained in 
the previous chapter.  
 
Case 5:  Step of Iq from 0A to 5A at 48 Hz then at 53 Hz; 
 
For this test, we change the grid frequency respectively to 48 Hz and to 53 Hz.  
On the top on figure 6-14, the step of the current Iq from 0A to 5a is performed with 
the grid frequency of 48 Hz. We can see that the phase A grid voltage and current are 
in phase. The step change of the current reference Iq is well followed by the phase A 
grid current in green.  
 
 
On the bottom on figure 6-14, the step of the current Iq from 0A to 5a is 
performed with the grid frequency now being 53 Hz. The same results are observed as 
when the grid frequency is 48 Hz. In both scenario, on active power is generated.  
 
 
For the results obtained in case 5, two conclusions can be drawn:  
 The Grid is well synchronized with the connected converter due to the robustness 
of the PLL, and this allows power exchange between the converter and the grid. 
 The current controller is also very reliable, and any change in the reference signals 
is accurately followed by the phase current. 
 
HIL testing results summary 
From all the cases tested using the HIL emulator, the behavior of the grid currents is in 
accordance with the theory and the Simulink results. Every change in any of the 
reference currents is followed by a change in the grid current amplitude, and depending 
on the reference current applied, reactive power and active power are controlled. Even 
the change of the frequency (case 5) does not affect the results, the currents are still 
controlled.

CHAPTER 8     
7 Conclusion and Future work 
7.1 Conclusion  
 
The main focus of this project was to design and implement the grid connect 
converter for the control of the flow of active and reactive power. This project is 
organized in 6 main chapters from which the conclusion is drawn. 
 
 
In the first chapter, an introduction to the project has been made, including the 
project objective which is to control the active and reactive power flow between the 
converter and the utility grid. 
 
 
In chapter 2, the use of reactive power and active power have been explained, 
showing their importance in the stability of the utility grid. The two level voltage source 
converter has been presented with the sine PWM modulation strategy, where the 
triangle carrier help minimizing the switching states during control. The VSC is the 
main component converting the DC voltage to AC with an appropriate modulation 
index. 
 
 
In chapter 3 the LCL filter has been designed. The advantage that the LCL filter 
has over the L make the LCL filter the optimal choice for the design. The transfer 
function of the LCL filter was derived and the values of the filter components were 
calculated, the damping resistor was added to attenuate the gain at the resonance 
frequency value. The filtering of converter output showed a THD fulfilling the 
requirement imposed by the IEEE standard for grid connected applications. 
 
 
In chapter 4, the PLL was designed for synchronization with the grid in order to 
achieve independent control of active and reactive power flow between the converter 
input side and the grid. The PLL showed also good synchronization performance in 
case of balanced three phase voltage where the grid angle was tracked accurately. Also, 
the PLL was still able to track the grid angle in case of a utility grid frequency of 48 Hz 
==PAGE:83==
                                                                                                                    Page 83 of 88 
 
 
or 53Hz, or in case of imbalanced three phase grid voltages. This has shown the 
robustness of the PLL. 
 
In chapter 5, the focus has been on the design of the controller for active and 
reactive power control through currents control. The current mode control has been 
designed, where the active and reactive power are regulated by controlling the d and q 
components of the currents. There were some requirements imposed on the current 
control, regarding the phase and gain margin, and also the bandwidth of the system. 
The Root Locus, the bode plots and step response graphs, plotted with the Matlab 
toolbox, Sisotool, showed that the current controller loop fulfilled the imposed 
requirements. Moreover, the reference values given to the current and their 
corresponding measured values showed that we can independently control the active 
and reactive power. 
 
 
In chapter 6 of this project, the focus has been on the implementation of the grid 
connected converter system in the Hardware in the loop and the testing results. The 
control system was implemented on the TI F28335 control card. The current controller 
was tested and results displayed on the Typhoon HIL GUI showed that, for a given 
current reference on either the d or q component representing respectively the reactive 
and active power, the grid currents were controlled and were either in phase or out of 
phase with the grid voltage depending on the reference current component applied. 
 
 
In general, the development of GCC system for the active and reactive power 
control showed good results in Simulink where we were able to control the active and 
reactive power and display both the grid current shape and the power flow between the 
converter and the grid. The implementation on the HIL emulator was a bit troublesome 
as we encountered some problems with the stability of the measured grid currents which 
we solved by placing a 2 Ω resistor between the LCL filter and the grid and by 
modifying the current controller loop bandwidth.  
 
 
Our solution proposed for the control of active and reactive power is robust and 
reliable. Even in case of grid frequency change as we have seen in the HIL testing 
results in case 5, the grid currents can still track accurately the reference current applied. 
==PAGE:84==
                                                                                                                    Page 84 of 88 
 
 
It will have been nice to be able to set as reference in the control algorithm, the desired 
active and reactive power directly in the HIL emulator and measure the output power.  
 
7.2 Future work 
 
Good results were obtained in this project however, due to the time constraint 
and some debugging issues encountered in the project design process the following part 
need to be investigated or designed in future work. 
 
 It has been observed during the course of this project that the HIL emulator gave 
some results with noise for the same program used on it. in the future, the Typhoon 
HIL Emulator output has to be studied in order to quantify the noise and filter the 
output of the results. 
 
 In the project only the current controller has been tested on the Typhoon HIL 
emulator. It would be a good feature to implement the GUI in order to directly give 
the active and reactive power reference and then observe the results of the control 
controller output and the measured active and reactive power on the same GUI. 
 
 The two level VSC and the LCL filter were implement in the HIL emulator, it would 
have been nice to physically design the VSC and the LCL filter and test the 
application using real live values of the utility gird. By having the converter, and 
therefore the prototype of the GCC system, it will be possible to test our control 
method and make an objective comparison between the HIL design process and 
results, and the complete Hardware design.   
 
 Renewable energy like solar energy are unpredictable, as it depends on environment 
factors. It will be nice to explore the energy storage domain in case the production 
is high and we need to store the excess of energy. I do believe that in the future there 
will be robust solution concerning energy storage.
