Abstract
The transition to fully autonomous train operations presents new challenges in ensuring
passenger safety at rail stations. This thesis investigates the development of a vision-
based obstacle detection system designed to enhance safety for Grade of Automation 4
rail networks by identifying potential hazards on and around train tracks in real time.
The proposed system integrates deep learning-based object detection with classical
computer vision techniques and LiDAR validation to achieve high accuracy in detecting
and classifying objects. YOLOv10 was selected as the primary detection model due
to its efficiency in real-time applications, while background subtraction was used as a
supplementary method to identify objects not recognized by the deep learning model.
LiDAR was incorporated to mitigate false detections caused by environmental factors such
as shadows and lighting variations.
The system was tested in controlled environments and real-world conditions, demonstrat-
ing reliable detection capabilities across varying lighting scenarios, including nighttime
operations. Results show that the combination of machine learning and sensor fusion
significantly improves detection accuracy while maintaining computational efficiency. A
final output video can be viewed here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7ZnDuvE0xGU
This thesis contributes to the development of autonomous train safety systems, providing
a scalable and adaptable solution that can be integrated into existing rail infrastructure.
Future work will focus on expanding the training dataset with real-world rail station data and
optimizing detection algorithms to further enhance reliability and operational readiness.

Introduction
In 2023, the S-Train network in the Copenhagen metropolitan area experienced a 7%[1] in-
crease in passenger kilometers traveled, with projections to surpass pre-covid-19 pandemic
numbers in 2024. This highlights the growing demand for more frequent and punctual train
services. As outlined in the ’Infrastrukturplan 2035’ (Infrastructure Plan 2035)[2] political
agreement from 2021, the Danish Parliament responded to the demand by deciding to
implement automated metro operations on the S-Train network, aiming to enhance overall
efficiency and service quality.
Advancements in technologies such as machine learning (ML), computer vision (CV),
artificial intelligence (AI) aided planning, and digital communication have facilitated the
increasing demand for advanced rail solutions. This is particularly evident in the growing
interest in fully automated public train transit systems, as reflected in the significant market
value of autonomous train technology, valued at $5.88 billion in 2018, with projections
to reach $15.57 billion by 2026[3]. Even with the development of new deep learning
(DL) algorithms and advancements in CV over the last decade[4], there has been little
exploration of object detection in direct relation to passenger safety[5]. This makes real-
time video detection virtually an unexplored field for obstacle detection systems (ODS).
Openly available detection algorithms are becoming faster and more accurate, and are
already in commercial use by some industries.
This project, conducted as independent students, will, in collaboration with Siemens Mobility
A/S, investigate the expansion of the safety system for the existing 87 stations in the
Copenhagen S-Train network, as outlined in the system requirements (see section 1.1.1)
from the Danish State Railways (DSB), to achieve compliance with grade of automation 4
(GoA4) standards. These standards represent the highest level of train automation and
enable unattended train operation (UTO) without on-board staff[6]. As the final contractor
for the public tender proposed by DSB has not yet been chosen, Siemens is maintaining
a cautious and reserved stance regarding the disclosure of information, which limits
direct access to their insights or solutions in regard to this project. This independence
necessitates a broader exploratory approach to the investigation. Specifically, this project
will focus on the integration and development of a fully automated safety system that is
required to operate a GoA4 rail network safely.

1.1
Limitations
The opportunity to work independently on this project comes from Siemens Mobility A/S,
which has granted full autonomy in how the problem is approached. However, the system
requirements provided by DSB impose significant constraints on the overall system design.
In section 1.1.1, these requirements are explored in detail, as they serve as the foundation
for many key decisions made in this thesis.
Note on the Primary use of the Terms Obstacle and Object in this Thesis
The term obstacle is used in accordance with the wording in DSB’s system requirements
and in the context of the final product of this thesis (e.g., developing an obstacle detection
system). The term object, on the other hand, is used within the domain of computer vision
and image processing, where object detection is a fundamental component (e.g., utilizing
object detection to develop an obstacle detection system).
1.1.1
DSB Public Tender
The following public requirements specified by DSB in the Future S-network Supporting
Systems report 1 outlines the implementation of an Obstacle Detection System for Platforms
(ODS-P) which will facilitate the expansion of the S-Train network to a GoA4 compliant
system. This project will strive towards fulfilling most of these objectives; however, in
certain cases, they will be used primarily as guidelines to supplement the stated objectives
of the project from section 1.2. The approach ensures alignment with DSB’s vision while
allowing flexibility to adapt the system to the specific needs of this project.
Many automated rail networks worldwide use platform screen doors (PSDs) as physical
barriers to separate passengers from moving trains and tracks, significantly reducing the
risk of people or objects falling onto the tracks. However, DSB has opted not to implement
PSDs due to the high financial cost of installing them across 87 stations, many of which
have platforms that were not designed to support the weight of a PSD system.
The ODS-P should be designed to enhance railway safety and operational efficiency by
detecting obstacles that may impede train movement within the designated detection areas.
A primary requirement for the ODS-P is that no sensors are placed on the trains, instead,
all detection hardware related to safety must be placed at the stations themselves. The
system’s purpose is to provide real-time detection and communication of obstacles to both
the SCADA (supervisory control and data acquisition) and CBTC (communication-based
train control) systems, enabling immediate action. By intelligently distinguishing between
genuine threats and non-threatening objects such as debris, the ODS-P should ensure safe
train operations without compromising punctuality. It should thereby operate continuously
with a high level of accuracy and should retain all detection data for analysis and review,
ensuring that no critical information is lost.
The ODS-P system must reliably identify obstacles within a specified detection area,
which is defined as the platform length plus an additional 2 meters at both ends, a width
extending 2100mm from the track center on the non-platform side, and a height ranging
from 200mm below platform level to the top of the rail. The system must detect obstacles
that could interfere with train operations and transmit real-time alarms to the SCADA
system. Simultaneously, the system must inform the CBTC system about changes in
detection status. Even when multiple trains are present, the system is required to continue
detecting obstacles in unoccupied track sections. To ensure safety, the ODS-P should

incorporate a fail-safe design, where any failure in detection leads to an automatic obstacle
indication being sent to the CBTC system.
In addition to obstacle detection, the ODS-P should provide comprehensive surveillance
of the detection area. The system should be designed to display surveillance data along
with all essential information, such as the time, location, and date.
The ODS-P is also required to continuously record all detection and surveillance data
streams around the clock. It must retain these records for a configurable duration, automat-
ically overwriting the oldest recordings when storage capacity is full. All alarms, metadata,
system modifications, and activities should be meticulously logged to maintain system
integrity. Measures should be in place to ensure that 100% of the data is recorded without
tampering, packet loss, or system limitations, with potential issues triggering alarms for
immediate attention.
From a technical standpoint, the ODS-P must detect at least 99.8% of obstacles and
provide operators with accurate information on the time and location of detected obstacles.
It is critical that no more than 0.2% of detections result in incorrect obstacle releases due
to system faults. The system is required to process obstacle detections and update the
CBTC system within a maximum response time of two seconds, ensuring swift and reliable
operation.
The key takeaways from these requirements will be pursued with a focus on overall au-
tonomous train safety for a GoA4 rail network, particularly with respect to sensor placement,
full coverage within the detection area, optimizing operator outputs, and enabling real-time
obstacle detection and response. These objectives will guide the development and evalu-
ation of the system, ensuring alignment with both the project’s goals and DSB’s broader
requirements.
1.2
Objectives
This project aims to develop a vision-based deep learning system for autonomous train
safety in collaboration with Siemens Mobility A/S. The following outlines the required
objectives that need to be achieved to complete this project successfully:
• Develop a solution that is capable of detecting obstacles that enter an area around
the tracks that would pose a threat to safe train operations.
• Develop a solution that is capable of classifying the most commonly occurring objects
around train tracks to assess their potential hazard level.
• Develop a solution that includes detection and proper handling of unpredictable
objects.
• Develop a solution capable of operating under varying lighting conditions, such as
night and day.
• Develop a solution that can be scaled to accommodate varying train station lengths
and layouts.
• The solution should effectively present relevant data in a clear and concise manner,
enabling an operator to make informed assessments.

Analysis
The following section aims to provide a detailed analysis of critical components within the
project framework. It will evaluate key obstacles, assess the necessary decisions regarding
detection systems and their implementation, and explore general solution approaches.
The analysis will also address challenges inherent to object detection technologies, such as
false positives, environmental resilience, and the trade-offs between human and machine
detection systems. By examining these factors, the section seeks to contextualize the
project’s design choices, highlight areas requiring optimization, and provide insight into
the broader implications of the chosen solutions for passenger safety in fully automated
rail systems.
2.1
Exploration of Existing Safety Systems for GoA4 Rail
Networks
When discussing passenger safety for GoA4 commuter or suburban rail systems, such as
the Copenhagen S-Train network, valuable insights can be gained from the experiences of
similar implemented systems, particularly metro systems. By the end of 2020, more than
25% of metro systems globally had implemented at least one fully automated GoA4 line[7].
Such advancements highlight the industry’s commitment to leveraging technology for
enhanced operational efficiency. A critical aspect of maintaining safety in GoA3 and GoA4
metro and rail networks is the implementation of track protection systems. These can take
many forms, but one of the simplest yet most expensive forms is platform screen doors. By
2018, 87% of these systems featured versions of these PSD systems [6]. Beyond PSDs,
ODS play vital roles in accident prevention. These include laser and infrared light barriers,
which create an invisible safety net from the station’s ceiling to the platform edge to detect
any intrusions into the area around the tracks. Moreover, Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV)
surveillance systems are widely used to manually monitor passenger behaviors and allow
operators to detect unusual activities. While these require human operators, automated
pedestrian tracking on train platforms, though not primarily a safety measure, leverages
CCTV and computer vision to enhance passenger flow. These systems monitor real-time
crowd density and movement patterns to manage platform congestion and optimize the
transit experience. Lastly, human personnel are used to patrol the train platforms and take
action/alert train operations if accidents occur.

Despite these advancements, a significant challenge with ODS technologies is their
propensity to generate false positives. Such incidents can lead to unnecessary system
shutdowns, disrupting the transportation network and affecting passenger experience.
This issue was particularly evident with the GOA4 Copenhagen Metro back in 2014. The
frequent occurrence of false positives from their detection systems led to unwarranted
halts in operations, prompting the later installation of PSDs at its 12 above-ground stations
to mitigate these disruptions[8].
While PSDs are highly effective, they are not feasible for the S-Train network due to
structural limitations of the current train platforms and financial constraints. Therefore,
this project will rely solely on monitoring technologies for passenger safety and real-time
hazard detection.
2.2
Sensor Placement in Relation to DSB Tender
The public tender by DSB mandates that the sensor array system be placed on train
platforms rather than on the trains. Although this parameter is pre-determined, it establishes
several defining characteristics for the frame of the project.
Enhanced Processing Power and Algorithm Complexity
With the sensor array located on the platform, data can be processed on-site or offloaded
to external servers via high-speed connections. This removes the need to account for the
power and size limitations imposed by train-mounted systems. This allows the integration
of computationally demanding detection algorithms and improves flexibility in the design
of data handling systems.
Improved Sensor Reliability and Algorithm Calibration
Stationary sensors benefit from reduced exposure to vibrations and movement, enabling
the use of highly sensitive equipment. This stability reduces noise in the data, making it
easier to calibrate and fine-tune detection algorithms. With fewer variables introduced by
motion, algorithms can be optimized for precision and consistency, improving the system’s
overall performance and reducing potential false positives or negatives.
Targeted Detection Algorithms for High-Risk Areas
By focusing the sensor system on station platforms, development efforts can prioritize
designing algorithms that are optimized for the unique challenges of high-risk areas. For
instance, detection algorithms can be tailored to identify objects or individuals crossing the
tracks, stationary debris, or foreign objects left behind. The removed need to monitor the
long stretches of track between stations allows the system to focus on improving detection
precision and response times for the localized, high-traffic zones where accidents are
more likely to occur.
Reduced Range Requirements
Since platform-mounted sensors monitor the immediate vicinity of the station, the detection
range requirements are significantly reduced compared to train-mounted systems that
would need to detect obstacles far in advance to accommodate braking distances. This
reduction in range allows development to focus on optimizing sensors and algorithms for
shorter distances, which can be cost-effective and increase accuracy.
Flexibility in Sensor Placement
The ability to place sensors in strategic locations, such as overhead or at elevated angles,
can improve the efficiency of the detection system. Overhead placement provides an
unobstructed view of the track. This flexibility also allows the system to incorporate multi-
angle views, enhancing the detection algorithms by providing additional context for object
recognition and reducing blind spots.

Accommodating Varying Platform Lengths
The layout and length of train stations within the S-train network vary widely, with the
shortest at 90 meters all the way up to the longest at 270 meters [9]. This suggests that a
single setup of sensors will be insufficient to provide complete coverage of the detection
area for all stations. This mandates that sensor arrays must be designed with modularity in
mind, enabling multiple of the same sensors to be deployed along the platforms as needed.
This modular approach ensures full coverage and adaptability to platforms of varying
lengths, maintaining the system’s effectiveness across different station configurations.
Modularity in System Design
Individual stations might be prone to unique disturbances or conditions, such as having
multiple tracks or varying levels of vegetation. A platform-based system allows for a modular
approach to both hardware and algorithm development. Sensor arrays and detection
algorithms can be customized for individual station layouts and upgraded incrementally.
For instance, stations with higher traffic might incorporate more advanced sensors and
multi-layered detection algorithms, while smaller stations might utilize simpler setups.
2.3
The Complexity of Identifying Hazardous Track Obstacles
Defining which track obstacles necessitate stopping a train is a critical yet complex chal-
lenge in automated rail systems. Based on information obtained from (Kent Aanæs, train
driver for DSB, November 2024), no specific protocols outline which obstacles on or around
the track area require a train to halt. Instead, these decisions rely on the intuition and
judgment of individual train drivers. While some obstacles, such as humans, large animals,
or debris of a size and weight capable of damaging the train upon impact, are clearly cause
for concern, other situations are less straightforward.
Kent Aanæs shared an example of a windy day when a bouncy castle was blown onto
the tracks and got wrapped around the front of the train cabin. Although this situation was
resolved as a minor incident (the train was stopped, and the bouncy castle was removed
by the driver), it illustrates the complexity of determining whether an obstacle poses a
genuine safety hazard or a minor inconvenience. In this case, the fabric could have tangled
in the wheels, potentially causing a derailment, or it might have reached the catenary lines
and caused an electrical hazard, leading to substantial safety and operational issues.
Assessing such risks currently happens in real time and relies on the driver’s ability to
evaluate the situation, a process that often surpasses the capabilities of conventional
machine detection algorithms. This highlights the need for a more systematic approach,
such as developing a comprehensive, predefined list of objects that could appear on the
tracks and establishing methods for their identification.
2.4
Human vs. Machine Detection
The ability to detect and assess track obstacles differs significantly between human opera-
tors and machine-based systems. Human train drivers rely on visual perception, intuition,
and experience to detect and evaluate potential hazards. While this allows them to
interpret ambiguous situations effectively, human operators are susceptible to fatigue,
distractions, and limited perceptual range, particularly in poor weather or low visibility con-
ditions. Machine-based detection systems, in contrast, offer consistency and scalability.
Sensors can operate across a broader range of perception methods in order to detect
objects in conditions where human perception would fail. Machines also do not experience
fatigue or distraction, allowing for continuous operation without any decrease in perfor-
mance. However, machine systems can struggle with edge cases and novel scenarios,
where their pre-programmed algorithms may lack the flexibility of human intuition.

2.5
General Detection in Automated Train Safety
The primary purpose of any safety system is to prevent harm to humans. This is particularly
significant in train systems where pedestrians, passengers, or even maintenance workers
might inadvertently enter an operational track area. Unlike simple objects, humans present
unique challenges for detection systems due to their variability in behavior and appearance.
For example, individuals may be standing, crouching, or lying down, potentially obscured by
obstacles or even partially visible in cluttered environments. The system must be designed
to consistently detect humans across a wide range of scenarios and environments.
Managing Unknown Obstacles
The unpredictability of obstacles that may end up on train tracks adds another layer of
complexity to the detection system. While some objects, such as humans, dogs, bags, and
bikes, will be common occurrences, the possibility of encountering unusual or unknown
objects is inevitable. To address this, the detection system must incorporate algorithms
that detect indiscriminately based on shape, color, size, and general appearance.
Accuracy and False Detections
A critical design requirement of the detection system is achieving high accuracy while
maintaining a low false detection rate. False detections disrupt train operations, causing
delays, reducing efficiency, and resulting in economic losses. Conversely, false negatives,
where actual hazards are overlooked, pose a direct threat to safety.
Environmental Resilience
Train operations are exposed to diverse environmental conditions, making resilience to
weather and lighting critical. Rain, snow, fog, and extreme temperatures can obscure
sensors or degrade their performance, while nighttime operations introduce additional
challenges related to visibility.
Low Real-Time Requirements
Unlike autonomous vehicles, which require split-second decision-making to avoid collisions,
the real-time demands of a train detection system are less stringent. This is because
platform accidents often occur long before the train arrives at the station, allowing sufficient
time for detection and mitigation. Moreover, trains operate on predefined tracks with fixed
stopping locations, and their long braking distances make abrupt stops virtually impossible.
While attempting to stay within the 2 seconds of response time mandated by DSB, the
system can allocate resources to maximizing accuracy and reliability rather than focusing
on ultra-fast processing.
Given the challenges discussed, computer vision emerges as a clear choice for train safety
systems. The prospect of processing complex visual data, recognizing diverse objects,
and adapting to varying scenarios makes it highly suitable for this application, offering a
wide range of implementable solutions.
2.6
Visual Detection Methods
Computer vision can be implemented in various ways, ranging from simple rule-based
techniques to complex deep learning-driven models. The choice of approach depends
on factors such as computational constraints, environmental conditions, and detection
requirements. Some methods, like feature-based detection, background subtraction, and
optical flow analysis rely on predefined patterns and static scene modeling, making them
efficient but sometimes limited in dynamic settings. Others, such as DL, offer greater
adaptability and robustness but often require more computational power and training data.

Feature-Based Object Detection
Feature-based methods rely on predefined patterns, such as edges, corners, or textures, to
detect objects in an image or video stream. These algorithms use classical computer vision
techniques to identify objects, such as histogram-based detection, template matching, or
edge detection. Feature-based methods are computationally efficient and can perform
well in controlled environments with predictable features. However, their performance can
degrade in dynamic or cluttered scenes, where object variations or occlusions may occur.
Background Subtraction
Background subtraction is a foundational approach to motion and anomaly detection
in video streams. This method involves modeling the static background of a scene and
identifying foreground objects by detecting deviations from the background. It is particularly
effective in scenarios with fixed cameras and relatively stable environments. Background
subtraction is computationally lightweight and capable of real-time processing, making
it suitable for resource-constrained systems. However, it can struggle with changes in
lighting, shadows, or background dynamics, which may lead to false detections if not
carefully managed through adaptive techniques.
Optical Flow and Motion-Based Detection
Optical flow methods analyze the motion of objects in consecutive frames of a video to
detect moving entities. By estimating the apparent motion of pixels, these algorithms can
identify and track objects, making them suitable for applications like video surveillance or
autonomous navigation. While optical flow is effective in dynamic environments, it can
be computationally intensive and may struggle with high-speed motion or scenes with
significant camera movement.
Deep Learning-Based Detection
DL has revolutionized detection algorithms by enabling systems to learn patterns and
features directly from data. Algorithms trained on large datasets can generalize across
diverse scenarios, providing diverse detection capabilities even in complex environments.
DL-based methods also support multi-modal data integration, enabling robust, real-time
detection across a wide range of applications. However, they require substantial compu-
tational resources and large datasets, and their performance can be influenced by the
quality and diversity of the training data.
Given the diverse and dynamic nature of detection tasks in the contexts of a ODS-P system,
DL-based solutions present a compelling approach due to their adaptability and robustness.
However, DL is inherently constrained by the scope of its training data and predefined
parameters, which can limit its generalization to novel scenarios. Additionally, its complex,
often opaque decision-making processes can lead to unpredictable or undefined behavior,
making interpretability and reliability key challenges in certain applications. To mitigate
these challenges, classical detection methods such as background subtraction will play
a complementary role. These techniques can provide efficient pre-processing steps,
enhance model reliability in specific conditions, and serve as fallback mechanisms in cases
where DL models struggle. By combining DL with well-established classical techniques, the
detection system can achieve a balance between accuracy, efficiency, and computational
feasibility.

Overview of Sensors for Safety Systems
With a basis in computer vision, the system will be highly versatile and have the potential to
cover a wide range of scenarios. However, relying solely on cameras presents challenges,
particularly in adverse lighting and environmental conditions. To enhance reliability and
performance, additional sensors can be integrated to complement camera-based detection.
Choosing the appropriate sensor for any given system requires careful evaluation of several
Object Detection on Rail Station for Autonomous Train Safety
11
==PAGE:20==
factors, including accuracy, versatility, environmental robustness, and cost-effectiveness.
A key consideration for this application is the characteristics of the sensor’s detection field,
here referring to the physical shape and varying sensitivity of the area that a sensor is
able to cover. The following segment will examine some of the most commonly applied
sensors for this type of application.
LiDAR
LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) sensors are widely recognized for their precision
in mapping and distance measurement. By emitting laser pulses and measuring their
reflection times, LiDAR can generate accurate 2D or 3D representations of an environ-
ment. They are particularly valued for their reliability in detecting objects and obstacles,
even in low-light conditions. LiDAR is commonly used in dynamic systems where spatial
accuracy and fast response times are critical. However, it is worth noting that LiDAR can
face challenges in environments with reflective or absorptive surfaces. LiDAR sensors
commonly have 2D or 3D detection fields, depending on their configuration. 2D LiDARs
scan in a single-plane fan shape, typically 360° horizontally if rotating, or in a fixed arc
for stationary models. 3D LiDARs use multiple laser layers or rotating mirrors to cover
a spherical or hemispherical field, often 360° horizontally and 30°–90° vertically. They
generally have a notably high resolution, which diminishes with range.
Radar
Radar sensors are known for their ability to detect objects and measure their velocity over
long distances, making them indispensable in applications such as air traffic control and
automotive collision avoidance systems. Radar is highly resistant to environmental factors
such as rain, fog, and dust, which can impair other sensors. However, the resolution
of radar systems may be lower than that of optical sensors, which can limit their ability
to distinguish small or closely spaced objects. RADAR sensors emit radio waves in a
cone-shaped detection field, with coverage depending on antenna design and frequency.
Common configurations include narrow-beam RADARs for long-range precision and wide-
beam or phased-array RADARs for broader coverage. Many automotive and surveillance
RADARs use fan-shaped or hemispherical fields, often with 360° horizontal scanning when
rotating. Sensitivity varies with object size, material, and motion, with metallic surfaces
providing strong reflections, while absorptive materials or angled surfaces can affect
accuracy. They are generally more effective at longer ranges rather than short distances
while exhibiting lower resolution characteristics.
Ultrasonic Sensors
Ultrasonic sensors operate by emitting sound waves and measuring their reflection times
to detect objects and distances. These sensors are highly reliable in short-range detection
scenarios and commonly used in proximity sensing and obstacle avoidance applications.
They are relatively unaffected by lighting conditions, making them well-suited for environ-
ments where visual sensors might struggle. However, their performance can be influenced
by the material and texture of detected objects, as well as environmental noise, while
having resolution limitations similar to those of radar. Ultrasonic sensors exhibit a cone-
shaped detection field. Sensitivity is highest for hard, dense surfaces (like metal, glass,
and walls), which reflect sound well, while soft or irregular surfaces (like fabric, foam, or
human skin) absorb or scatter sound, reducing detection reliability. Ultrasonics work best
at short ranges (a few centimeters to a few meters) but struggle with small, thin, or angled
objects, as sound waves may deflect away instead of returning to the sensor.

Passive Infrared Sensors (PIR)
PIR sensors detect objects by measuring emitted infrared radiation, primarily from heat
sources like humans or animals. They operate within a wide fan-shaped detection field
(typically 90°–180°) and are commonly used in motion sensing for security and surveillance.
PIR sensors typically have a short-to-medium range (up to 10 meters) and low resolution,
detecting only general motion rather than precise object shapes or distances. They work
well in various lighting conditions but struggle in extreme heat or when detecting objects
with low thermal contrast against the background.
Magnetic Sensors
Magnetic sensors detect changes in magnetic fields within a localized or directional de-
tection area, depending on the sensor type. Their detection field can be omnidirectional
(detecting magnetic fields from all directions) or directional (sensitive to specific axes).
They are highly sensitive to ferromagnetic materials (like iron, nickel, and cobalt) and
can detect static or dynamic magnetic fields. The range varies from millimeters to sev-
eral meters, depending on sensor type and field strength. Resolution is typically high,
allowing precise detection of magnetic field changes, but they can be affected by external
electromagnetic interference and are only applicable for detecting ferromagnetic materials.
Detecting objects on train tracks requires a precise and reliable sensing solution capable of
identifying a variety of materials and shapes within the targeted area. LiDAR stands out as
a strong candidate due to its high resolution, which enables differentiation between objects
and reduces the risk of false positives or missed detections. Additionally, LiDAR’s favorable
detection field provides broad coverage across the tracks, ensuring that obstacles are
detected with minimal blind spots. Unlike some alternative sensors, LiDAR can detect
objects regardless of material, making it effective for identifying both solid and low-contrast
obstacles. However, highly reflective surfaces, such as metallic objects, can cause signal
distortions or false returns, potentially affecting detection accuracy. While strong sunlight
can introduce interference, modern filtering techniques and strategic sensor placement
help mitigate these effects, ensuring consistent and reliable performance.

2.9
Overview of Vision-Based Deep Learning
Over the past few years, significant advancements have been made in the field of vision-
based DL, particularly in detection, classification, and tracking. Among the most noteworthy
developments is the evolution of Region-Based Convolutional Neural Networks (R-CNNs),
which have fundamentally transformed how systems interpret and track visual data.
R-CNNs and their subsequent iterations, such as Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN, and Mask
R-CNN, have significantly enhanced the ability of machines to classify and localize objects
within images. The core concept of R-CNNs involves using selective search methods to
extract proposed regions from an image which are then passed through a convolutional
neural network. This process allows the network to focus specifically on the regions of
interest, producing more accurate classifications and bounding box predictions. Training an
R-CNN involves multiple stages. A base CNN is initially pre-trained on a large-scale dataset
of labeled images to learn generic image features. This pre-trained model is then fine-tuned
on a task-specific dataset, where region proposals generated by algorithms like Selective
Search or Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) are used to train the classifier to distinguish
between objects within proposed regions. The multi-step process includes classification,
bounding box regression, and in the case of Mask R-CNN, instance segmentation.
The versatility and power of R-CNN architectures have enabled diverse real-world applica-
tions, with pedestrian tracking being a prominent use case. In pedestrian tracking, these
networks can be employed to accurately detect and follow individuals across video frames
in real time. Beyond basic detection, the integration of R-CNN with tracking algorithms
can optimize the real-time tracking performance. This combination merges region-based
detection with tracking-by-detection approaches. Enhancing the ability to maintain ac-
curate tracking identities across video sequences is crucial for autonomous navigation
applications and pedestrian flow analysis. This capability is particularly well-suited for
DL-based safety systems designed to prevent accidents in rail networks, where precise
and reliable detection of people and objects is essential for ensuring operational safety.

Visual Object Detection
Obstacle detection is a critical technology for applications such as autonomous driving,
robotics, and industrial automation, where real-time identification of objects in the environ-
ment is essential for safety and efficiency. Systems designed to detect obstacles must
recognize a wide variety of objects in dynamic and often unpredictable settings.
Traditionally, object detection was achieved using classical computer vision techniques,
such as edge detection and optical flow analysis. However, these methods often struggle
to handle the complexity and variability of real-world environments. This is where machine
learning, particularly deep learning, has made significant advancements by enabling
systems to learn from data and adapt to different scenarios more effectively.
DL models, such as CNNs, have become the standard for visual object detection due
to their ability to learn complex features from large datasets, outperforming classical
methods in terms of accuracy and robustness. However, developing such models from
scratch requires significant expertise, time, and computational resources. To address
this challenge, leveraging open-source pre-trained models like the commonly used YOLO
(You Only Look Once) and RT-DETR (Real-Time DEtection Transformer) has proven to

in commercial applications, employ different underlying techniques, with YOLOv10 as of

data, meanwhile having 2.8 times smaller number of parameters FLOPs(G)[11]. Given
its superior performance, YOLOv10 was selected for this project, as it offers optimized
object detection capabilities that can be adapted to object detection within a railway safety
system.

3.2
Classical Detection
Based on the previously identified shortcomings of YOLO and DL in general, supplementary
detection methods must be implemented. To determine an appropriate supplement to the
implemented YOLO algorithm, it is essential to analyze the characteristics of the most
commonly missed detections. The nature of the problem dictates that all objects requiring
detection initially exist in a ”neutral” position or a state where they pose no threat. These
objects subsequently move into a critical position, such as onto train tracks, where they
become hazardous and require detection.
Additionally, any objects missed by the YOLO will likely exhibit unpredictable sizes, shapes,
and dimensions, as common objects would be recognized.
Given these considerations, an effective supplementary detection method should focus
on identifying movement while remaining indifferent to the appearance of the object itself.
In this project, a background subtraction algorithm is implemented, which analyzes new
frames compared to previous frames or a constructed ”background” to detect changes in
pixel values. This approach aligns well with the need to identify objects based on motion
and environmental changes rather than their visual characteristics.
3.2.1
Implementation of Background Subtraction
For a visual representation of the background subtraction process, refer to Figure 3.5. This
illustration provides a step-by-step depiction of the methodology described in this section,
offering a useful reference while reading through the following implementation details.
Initially, a simple frame differencing method was implemented to detect objects on the
tracks. While this approach showed some success, it highlighted some flaws. Objects
were successfully detected in motion, such as when moving onto or along the tracks.
However, once the objects ceased moving, such as a suitcase that fell onto the tracks and
remained stationary, they were no longer detected.
To address this limitation, a custom function was developed to create a background.
Importantly, all operations are applied only within the specified detection area as outlined
in section 3.1.1, to minimizes the impact of irrelevant activity outside the area. For the
function to produce a reliable and accurate background, the scene must be static and free
from disturbances. To ensure these conditions are met, the process begins with a simple
comparison between frames. Frames are converted to grayscale, and pixel values are
averaged. The absolute difference between consecutive frames is computed, and if this
difference exceeds a predefined threshold, background creation is delayed until the scene
stabilizes and the difference falls below the threshold.
Once the scene meets the stability criteria, the next 20 consecutive frames are captured,
with ongoing checks to confirm the absence of significant changes. These frames are
then converted to grayscale and averaged, resulting in a clean and accurate background
image.

With the background established, the function proceeds to perform frame differencing
between incoming frames and the generated background. This method proved effec-
tive as both moving and stationary objects introduced into the scene became clearly
distinguishable.
Thresholding is then applied to the resulting difference image to create a binary mask. This
mask isolates regions of significant change by highlighting pixels that exceed the threshold
value. Morphological operations, such as dilation and erosion, are then applied to refine
the mask by removing noise and closing small gaps within detected regions. Contours
are extracted from the mask, and bounding boxes are created. Finally, non-maximum
suppression (NMS) is applied to eliminate redundant detections (overlapping bounding
boxes).
However, some challenges were observed during implementation. While new objects
were detected reliably, scene-wide changes, such as shifts in lighting, caused the entire
frame to be interpreted as a difference. This led to false positives and compromised the
accuracy of the detection process.
To address this issue, an additional thresholding mechanism was introduced. If the detected
changes in a frame are significant enough to affect the entire scene, the function interprets
this as a global disturbance (e.g., lighting changes). In such cases, the background
creation process is re-triggered. The system repeats the earlier steps to ensure the new
background reflects the updated conditions, thereby maintaining robustness and accuracy
in dynamic environments.
Notably, in nighttime scenarios, the increased camera gain introduced significant noise,
often leading to premature threshold triggers. To address this, a Gaussian blur was applied
to the frames being compared, reducing noise by averaging pixel values and ensuring
accurate comparisons (See Figure 3.4).

Bounding Box Sorting
While YOLO proved highly reliable in testing, producing very few false detections, it
occasionally failed to detect certain objects, particularly those not included in the COCO
dataset or those in difficult poses. To compensate for these missed detections, background
subtraction was introduced as an additional detection method.
However, because background subtraction is more sensitive to noise, such as lighting
changes and moving shadows, it is inherently less reliable than YOLO. Furthermore,
background subtraction can sometimes generate multiple distinct bounding boxes for the
same object (see Figure 3.7(a)). This occurs when certain regions of the object closely
resemble the background, creating gaps in the mask, this is then exacerbated by the
morphological operations. As a result, the algorithm interprets the object as consisting
of separate regions. To ensure accuracy and to retain classifications for as long as
possible, YOLO detections take precedence over background subtraction. When YOLO
detects an object, any background subtraction bounding boxes within a YOLO bounding
box are discarded. This approach minimizes false detections while allowing background
subtraction to contribute when YOLO fails to detect an object (See Figure 3.7).

By combining both methods, the system balances YOLO’s precision with the broader
detection capability of background subtraction, improving overall reliability in identifying

YOLOv10x was chosen for this project. Leveraging pre-trained models from Ultralytics,
the system detects and classifies objects based on the COCO dataset. A selection

Sensor fusion
At its core, sensor fusion is the process of combining data from multiple sensors to provide
a more comprehensive, accurate, and reliable understanding of a system or environment.
It leverages the strengths of individual sensors while compensating for their limitations. For
this project, sensor fusion will involve integrating data gathered from the camera, specifically
the outputs of the background subtraction process, with data from the lidar. This integration
aims to eliminate the false detections mainly produced by shadows described in section
3.2.1 by cross-checking bounding boxes from the camera against the physical data points
generated by the LiDAR. To achieve this, the lidar points must be aligned and fused with
the camera frame in 3D. The process of achieving this fusion will be detailed in the following
section.
5.1
Fusing LiDAR Data onto Camera Frame
To fuse LiDAR data onto the camera frame requires:
1. Conversion of Polar LiDAR Coordinates to Cartesian Coordinates
2. Transformation of LiDAR Coordinates to the Camera’s Coordinate System
3. Projecting of the 3D Camera Coordinates onto the 2D Image Plane
To convert the 2D polar coordinates, represented by the radial distance (range) from the
LiDAR, r, and the angular position (bearing), θ, to cartesian coordinates, the following
equations are used[22]. These equations map the polar measurements produced by the
LiDAR to Cartesian coordinates (x, y) in the LiDAR’s 2D plane:
x = r · cos(θ),
y = r · sin(θ)
(5.1)

calibration parameters. These parameters consist of:
• A rotation matrix R describing the orientation of the LiDAR relative to the camera.
• A translation vector T describing the position of the LiDAR relative to the camera.

Once the LiDAR points have been successfully transformed into the camera frame, the
next step involves integrating these points with the bounding boxes generated from the
background subtraction process. While a common implementation might involve clustering
the LiDAR points into blobs and generating bounding boxes for these blobs, this approach
was deemed unnecessary for the purposes of this project.
Instead, a simpler and more computationally efficient implementation was developed.
The bounding boxes generated by the background subtraction process are directly cross-
referenced with the LiDAR points. Specifically, the implementation checks whether a given
bounding box contains any LiDAR points.
This approach leverages the perspective of the camera, which is positioned primarily from
above. In this configuration, the LiDAR points tend to concentrate near the base of objects,
providing a reliable means of validation. By ensuring that at least some LiDAR points
are contained within a bounding box, false positives, such as shadows or reflections, are
effectively filtered out (See Figure 5.5).

6.4
Detection Range
The following test, recorded at Ishøj Light Rail Station, demonstrates the approximate
detection range of the visual system. In this scenario, a person walks along the tracks from
one end of the station to the other. The station is approximately 43 meters in length, and the
camera is placed 3 meters from the platform edge to capture the entire scene. The video
demonstration can be viewed here (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=juwdS2J5fxo).
The purpose of this video is to illustrate at what point YOLO begins to lose consistency
in object detection. As the person walks along the tracks, the video shows how YOLO’s
ability to maintain accurate detection decreases as the distance between the camera and
the object increases. In contrast, the Background Subtraction method is able to detect
objects as long as there are changes in pixels within the frame.
Assessing the performance of a deep learning algorithm in terms of its detection range
presents a significant challenge, primarily because the effectiveness of object detection
depends on numerous variables. In this particular scenario, the person in the test clip
assumes different stances and positions during the test, which inevitably impacts detection
accuracy. Variations in body posture, lighting, and even slight movements can affect
YOLO’s ability to consistently track and identify objects on the tracks. This introduces a
layer of unpredictability to the assessment, making it difficult to isolate the influence of range
alone. The first part of the range test demonstrates the performance of YOLO in isolation.
During this segment, the system consistently detects objects up to approximately 30 meters
from the camera. However, the algorithm begins to lose track of the detected object beyond
this distance. This behavior has been consistent throughout the development process. In
contrast, the second part of the test utilizes background subtraction as the sole method for
detecting objects. This technique remains highly effective over a broader range, with no
discernible loss of tracking even at distances greater than 30 meters. As the background
subtraction works by evaluating changes in pixel values, the system can continuously
track the object as long as it remains sufficiently large and distinct from the background. In
practical terms, this means that objects only become undetectable when they shrink to a
size too small for the camera to capture. To ensure comprehensive coverage of extended
train platforms, cameras would be required at intervals of no more than 30 meters, with
shorter intervals preferred to allow for slight overlap in detection areas. As outlined in
Section 4.3, the LiDAR system used in this project has a range of 3 meters, necessitating
multiple units along each section or an upgrade to hardware with greater range capabilities.

Future Work
This section explores a selection of necessary and potential future developments required
to transition the proposed train safety system from a prototype to a fully scalable solution
compliant with DSB’s requirements.
Custom Deep Learning Model
One of the most crucial steps in enhancing the system is the creation of a custom dataset
with an expanded class list. While the current implementation relies on a pre-trained DL
model that provides a solid foundation, a large-scale deployment would require a dataset
specifically tailored to train station environments. This dataset should include a wide variety
of objects commonly found in and around train stations, such as baby strollers, cargo bikes,
clothing items, maintenance equipment, and many more. By training a custom model on
such data, the system can achieve higher accuracy in object detection and classification,
reducing the likelihood of false positives by enabling the identification of more objects that
do not necessarily pose a threat.
Edge Case Handling
Handling edge cases remains a significant challenge, particularly under adverse weather
conditions such as heavy rain, fog, and snow, which can impair both camera and LiDAR
performance. Additional testing and model refinement are required to ensure that the
system maintains a high level of reliability even in poor visibility conditions. This could
involve incorporating additional sensor modalities or developing advanced filtering and
preprocessing techniques to compensate for weather-related distortions.
Sensor Array Expansion
Another key improvement lies in the integration of consecutive sensor arrays along a
station platform. The system is designed so that each sensor pair can be replicated along
the station length, ensuring comprehensive coverage. However, this requires extensive
software integration to manage overlapping detection areas efficiently. While redundancy
improves reliability, it also introduces challenges in consolidating detections across multiple
zones. Strategies must be developed to avoid redundant alerts while ensuring that any
detection, whether it appears in one or multiple sensor zones, is appropriately processed
and communicated.

==PAGE:62==
Integration with Existing Railway Protocols
For real-world deployment, seamless integration with existing train communication proto-
cols is essential. This includes defining how the system’s alerts and detections should be
interpreted by railway operators and automated systems. Establishing clear communica-
tion standards will ensure that the system’s outputs are actionable and compatible with
existing safety infrastructure, minimizing disruptions to train operations.
Logging
Extensive logging and data retention will be a core requirement for compliance with railway
safety standards. DSB mandates thorough event logging for post-incident analysis and
system audits. Implementing robust logging mechanisms will not only meet these regulatory
requirements but also facilitate continuous improvement by providing data that can be
used to refine the DL model over time.
Automated Calibration
Another crucial development is automating the system calibration process. Currently, sen-
sor calibration is performed manually, requiring precise measurements of relative sensor
positions for effective sensor fusion. To scale the system across a complex rail network,
an automated calibration method should be introduced. One potential approach could be
to calibrate using an object with well-defined features that can be simultaneously detected
by both the camera and LiDAR, allowing the system to determine sensor alignments
autonomously and reducing setup complexity.
3D Perception
An obvious yet ambitious improvement would be the incorporation of 3D perception ca-
pabilities. Moving from 2D to 3D vision could significantly enhance object localization,
improving both position estimation and size measurement. This advancement would in-
crease detection precision and help differentiate between objects of similar appearance but
varying heights or depths. However, integrating 3D vision would introduce additional com-
putational requirements and complexity, necessitating careful consideration of hardware
requirements and processing efficiency.
These improvements outline the key areas that must be addressed to transform the system
into a fully operational and scalable train safety solution. By refining object detection,
improving environmental robustness, enhancing integration with railway systems, and
automating calibration, the proposed solution can move closer to real-world implementation,
ultimately increasing the safety and efficiency of train operations.

Conclusion
This thesis explored the development and implementation of an object detection system
for rail station safety aimed at supporting autonomous train operations. By leveraging
deep learning, computer vision, and sensor fusion techniques, the system was designed
to enhance the safety and efficiency of GoA4 rail networks by reliably detecting obstacles
on and around train tracks.
The thesis demonstrated that a combination of YOLO-based object detection and back-
ground subtraction, supported by LiDAR validation, significantly improves the system’s
ability to detect and classify objects with high accuracy. The integration of LiDAR served
as an essential mechanism for reducing false positives caused by environmental factors
such as shadows and lighting variations. Furthermore, the modular sensor placement
strategy ensures scalability across varying platform layouts, addressing the challenge of
station-specific infrastructure constraints.
Test-driven results highlighted the effectiveness of the proposed system in a range of sce-
narios, showing strong detection capabilities under different lighting conditions, including
nighttime operations. However, challenges remain, particularly in classifying ambiguous
or novel obstacles.
Future work should focus on improving model robustness by expanding training datasets
with real-world rail station data and incorporating tailored deep learning models with en-
hanced case-specific capabilities. Further testing in operational environments is also
necessary to refine system performance and integration with existing railway safety infras-
tructure.
Overall, this thesis contributes to the ongoing development of autonomous rail safety
technologies, demonstrating the potential for automated solutions to enhance the reliability
and security of future train operations.
