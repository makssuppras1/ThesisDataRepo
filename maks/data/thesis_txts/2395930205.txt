1.0.1
Machine learning
Machine learning is broadly speaking a set of methods and algorithms that is based
on observed data. The goal is often to make software that can utilize this data and
thereby being able to predict future event, label unseen data points, find patterns
in data or generally gain insights from these observations. The amount of data be-
ing generated is at an all time high and is ever increasing some_stats The field of
machine learning provides tools to utilize and process this data in a meaningful man-
ner naturally following an increase in awareness towards machine learning. Machine
learning has recently gained a lot of attention due to very impressive result in various
assignments, such as in computer vision. Beginning around 2015 computer vision
systems started surpassing human level performance in multiple problems, most fa-
mously in the ImageNet competition (Russakovsky et al., 2015), where human level
performance is estimated at 5.5 % error. Computer vision systems as of 2017 is now
at ??% for the same task. Another remarkable aspect of the results are the fact that
the systems are not particularly engineered, so more or less everything was learned
from the data alone. The results comes from the use of deep learning, in which large
networks are meant to learn features of raw data. These remarkable results within
computer vision are now being utilized as an ingredient in groundbreaking applica-
tions such as self-driving cars. Deep learning models have been beating state of the
art systems is other domains than computer vision. Another example of this is in
the game of Go. An artificial Go player constructed with method from reinforcement
learning, deep learning and others were able to beat the best human players Silver
et al., 2016. The newest version of the system, called AlphaGo Zero, did not have
any historical game data, but learned everything by playing against a version of it
self. This is a problem which was considered intractable only few years ago, due to
its very large state space of more than 10170.
Much of the success can be coined to the increase in computer power foreseen by Gor-
don Moore, known as Moore’s lawMoore, 1998. This increase allows to solve more
computationally expensive tasks such as training bigger machine learning models.

1.0.2
NLP
Much of the data generated revolves around language whether it being text or spoken
language. Methods for dealing with this kind of data is categorized as Natural Lan-
guage Processing (NLP). Most problems in NLP are currently solved using machine
learning but was historically also solved in a more rule-based fashion.
The list of topics covered in NLP is very long, covering every aspect of language such
as semantics, syntax or speech. Within these areas a vast amount of sub-fields exists
as well. In machine translation deep learning models are also surpassing all other
methods. The widely used Google Translate service is powered by deep learning and
is probably considered one of the best machine translation systems. The same goes for
almost all problems related to NLP where deep learning methods are superior both
on performance but also on generality in the sense that do not use feature engineering.
1.0.3
Summarization
In online internet culture a common and increasingly popular expression is tl;dr which
is short for ”too long, didn’t read”.
It seems like a clear indicator of decreasing
attention-span among people or at least lowered allocated time for pieces of infor-
mation. It is another parameter for which we as humans are trying to increase our
efficiency such we can take in more information whether it being news, emails, social
media content, educational material or something entirely else. This is probably due
to the fact that access to information and the sheer amount have increased immensely.
One of the sub-fields within NLP revolves around automatic summarization. This
task is the ability to generate a shortened edition of some text. This should capture
the important aspect and essence of the original text in a short and precise manner.
The task is essentially a lossy compression of the information presented in the text.
With the ever increasing amount of information available through our phones and
computers the need for ways to do information compression seems inevitable, enabling
people to digest information more efficiently. Whether it being news articles, medical
reports or an annual business reports the consumer of these text formats would be
able to increase their efficiently significantly if presented with a proper condensed ver-
sion of the overall content. This is therefore interesting for any company presenting
textual data to its customers, users or employees.
The interest is evident by the recent research efforts by most of the tech-giants such
as Google (See et al., 2017), Facebook (Chopra et al., 2016; Fan et al., 2017), IBM
(Nallapati et al., 2016) and Microsoft (Cao et al., 2017). All of these publication were
presented within the last two years.
In automatic summarization, two prominent types of algorithms exists. They are
called extractive- and abstractive summarization. In the extractive method the sum-
mary is created by copying parts from the input and stitching these together to form
an output, most typically sentence by sentence.
This is can be done by using a
sentence classifier to classify whether to use a sentence in the summary or not. In ab-

stractive summarization, an entirely new output is created word by word potentially
creating phrases not present in the input. The extractive approach is perceived as an
easier task since copying from the input ensure a certain level of grammatical correct-
ness and general sentence structure. Despite the fact that the abstractive approach
is a harder problem it is still very desirable because of its more sophisticated and
flexible nature. It is able to paraphrase the text, perhaps ensuring a more digestible
output.
Abstract summarization is a bit like machine translation (MT) in the sense that
in both cases we take a sequence input and produces a new sequence as output. In
MT there is a mapping from one language to another, whereas in summarization we
map from a language to the same language, but in a compressed version. Many of
the advances in the field of MT is therefore transferable to abstract summarization
which have been done with success for many concepts.
In abstractive document summarization we construct a fixed-sized vocabulary as
the most frequent words appearing in the available data. Our model reads the input
document word-by-word constructing a hidden representation for this document. The
hidden representation of the input document is then decoded into a summary.
During decoding all SOTA models are experiencing problem with repetitive be-
haviour such that the model outputs the same sentences multiple times.
Various
suggestions for resolving this problem have been proposed in the literature with two
dominant approaches called temporal attention and coverage In this work we will
investigate these methods capabilities to resolve this problem.
We will look into
their respective empirical performances both in terms of repetitiveness but also for
their ROUGE score, which is the defacto standard for measuring performance on
summarization models.
A problem that occurs very often in natural language processing tasks is the need
to handle the rigid fixed-sized vocabulary. Computationally efficiency motivates small
(restricted) vocabularies requiring methods for handling words out of the vocabulary.
We will explore and analyze a popular method for dealing with this called point-
ers(Vinyals et al., 2015).

2.1
Machine learning
This section contains common concepts from the field of machine learning.
The
concepts covered is by no means exhaustive but merely an introduction to common
notions used later throughout the project.
Problems in machine learning are often categorized into two distinct types, namely
supervised- and unsupervised methods.
In supervised learning the objective is often to construct a function approximation,
mapping some input data to an output label. To obtain this mapping, both the the
input data and the labels are required to approximate the function, in a procedure
often referred to as training. The name supervised learning arise from the fact that
the labels are needed to construct the model, i.e. the function. This model can then
be used to infer the labels for unseen data points.
For unsupervised techniques, as the name indicated the label is not given. So the
general idea is to utilize the data some other manner than prediction. This could
be clustering data, investigating latent variables in the data or some other concept.
There exist a vast amount of unsupervised models and they are in a sense more
desirable than supervised methods since they do not required labeled data.
2.1.1
The model
In machine learning, the concept of a model can be seen as an entity containing the
information learned from data. It is a function which maps some input to another
output. Take linear regression as an example, given two variables describing some
phenomenon, like house prices, where the variables could be the house size and the
number of bedrooms. Given the input data and the labels, it is possible to learn
parameters describing the phenomenon and for some new houses, the model can pre-
dict, or at least estimate, the price of the house. The way to obtain these parameters
vary but they can be obtained through solving linear systems or through optimiza-
tion. The main ingredient in the model is thus the learned parameters for each of the
variables, which is learned from past experience(data).

2.1.2
Overfitting
A very important concept in machine learning is the concept of overfitting. Overfitting
happens when a model is trained and finds a set of parameters that perform very well
on a specific dataset. Consequently the model looses its ability to generalize to new
and unseen data, and the model is said to have been overfitted to the dataset. An
overfitted model are thus not able to predict unseen data points well. There exists
several situations in which overfitting can occur, one being the model having more
parameters than observations to train from.
2.1.3
Regularization
A way to deal with overfitting is through regularization. There exist many ways to
perform regularization and it is often very dependent on the model type. A way to
do it, is to penalize the size of the parameters when performing optimization, such as
L2-regularizationNg, 2004. Another technique to combat this is to observe how well
the model performs on the validation set during optimization. If the model starts
performing worse on the data not used for optimization, it is possible to stop the
optimization when the model is at an optimal solution w.r.t the validation set. This
procedure is called early stopping.
2.1.4
Hyperparameters
As stated in the previous sections, the objective is to learn from the data thus obtain-
ing a model that performs as good as possible on unseen data points. This is called
generalization. Very often, models contains so called hyperparameters. These are
parameters of the model which are not optimized through the optimization process.
This could be the strength of regularization, the size of a neural network or the learn-
ing rate used in the optimization. These all have impact on the generalization of the
model thus selecting optimal values can be crucial. For smaller model, good hyperpa-
rameters are often found by a method call cross validation. The training dataset is
split into k equally sized subsets and the model is trained on k −1 of these partitions.
The process is repeated for holding each k-fold out for a good estimate of model per-
formance. Cross validation is often computationally intractable with large models,
where it is a more common practice to split the dataset into three subsets called the
training-, validation- and test set. The validation set is used to tune hyperparameters.
The test set can never be used until measuring the final model performance to gain
an unbiased estimate.
2.1.5
Model performance
To measure how well a model performs on unseen data is important to see how well
the model generalizes.

2.2
Artificial Neural Network
Artificial neural network(ANN) is a class of machine learning models. An ANN can
be seen as a directed graph which can take in some input signal and process this. The
vertices in the graph performs some computation on the signal and the edges weigh
the importance of this signal throughout the network. Another popular analogy is
that the general architecture of ANNs is in part inspired by the structure of the brain
where neurons are connected in a network serving as input to each other.
ANNs are used in both supervised- and unsupervised learning and come in a wide
range of varieties most generally depending on their use case. The ANN is simply
Figure 2.1: Feedforward neural network illustration.
a statistical model in which the objective is to learn a set of parameters, θ, that
maximize the probability of some desired output y given a certain input X, P(y|X, θ).
The edges in the graph make up the parameters where θ is a general notation for all
learnable parameters in the network, i.e. the weights and biases. The initialization
of the parameters are most often initialized by a random function, which have been
shown to be an important area of research for neural networks Bengio, 2012. The
parameters are then optimized using back propagation (Section 2.2.2) whenever the
network is exposed to data points, X.
This family of models are very flexible for approximating functions and even a
simple network with one hidden layer is said to be able to approximate almost any
function. This is often referred to as the Universal Approximation Theorem Cybenko,
1989.
A simple case of an ANN, is the feedforward neural network, which does not have
any cycles in the graph. It is mostly fully-connected such that all nodes in one layer

2.2.3
Optimizing
When training an ANN with gradient descent from Equation (2.16) it is assumed
that all training data is used when updating the parameters. Since ANNs are often
trained using a very large amount of data, it is not possible to hold the entire dataset
in memory.
To cope with this limitation, the stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
Bottou, 2010 optimization algorithm allows for making parameter updates using only
one data point. Instead of computing the gradient of the loss exactly, SGD estimates
this gradient on the basis of a single datapoint. This also has the benefit of getting
out of local minima in the loss function. However updating the parameters for each
datapoint gives a lot of noise in the gradient descent. This is addressed by using
mini-batches for each parameter update effectively smoothing out some of the noise
in SGD, which is known as mini-batch gradient descent. Since the size of the mini-
batch is small, the performance of SGD is still kept as well as the ability to get out
off local minima. Using mini-batch gradient descent requires several more iterations
than the standard gradient descent.
There exists other optimizers also using mini-batches such as the AdaGrad opti-
mizer Duchi et al., 2011.

2.2.4
Deep Neural Networks
A specific type of ANN is the deep neural network (DNN) which is an ANN with sev-
eral hidden layers between the input and output layers. DNNs can similar to ANNs
learn very complex underlying functions in some data. There exists several archi-
tectures for DNNs where especially Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) (LeCun
et al., 1990) for use on spatial data such as images, and Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNN) such as LSTMs (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997) or GRUs (Cho et al.,
2014) for use on sequential data such as text.
The performance in the image classification task called ImageNet (Russakovsky et
al., 2015) has improved significantly in the recent years due to the use of deep learning.
The performance gains comes from using CNNs with and increasingly number of
hidden layers.
In AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) they use 8 hidden layers, in
VGGNet (Simonyan and Zisserman, 2014) 19 and in GoogLeNet (Szegedy et al.,
2015) 22 hidden layers. However He et al. (2016) showed that it also possible to go
too deep when naively stacking more and more layers in the network. To address
this problem they introduced a CNN architecture called ResNet that allowed them to
have a network with 152 layers that acheived state of the art performance in ImageNet
competition among other competitions.
With these DNNs having a huge capacity to learn very complex function, they are
prone to overfitting (Section 2.1.2). Therefore they are often trained with regulariza-
tion methods such as L2, Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) or Batch Normalization
(Ioffe and Szegedy, 2015).
2.3
Recurrent Neural Network
Recurrent neural networks (RNN) is another variant in the family of ANNs. A huge
short-coming for traditional neural networks is that the temporal information is lost
between each iteration of training which can be essential information for some prob-
lems. Recurrent neural networks seeks to capture these dependencies within the data
by forwarding its output or state into the network in the next time step. Another
limitation of other neural network architectures are their rigidity regarding the size
of the input and output, they can mostly only handle fixed sized vectors or matrices.
RNN allows for variable length input like sequential data such as different sized text
strings. A way to perceive RNNs is by unrolling them over time, which is shown in
Figure 2.3.

Vanishing and exploding gradients
The problem of vanishing and exploding
gradients arise due to the nature of RNNs and the way these are optimized, as de-
scribed in the previous sections. Due to all of the products involved with applying
the chain rule to compute the gradients these become very sensitive. Values > 1 can
quickly explode and become too big to compute. In the same fashion, if the numbers
are < 1 the information carried in the gradient can rapidly be non existing because
the numbers become too small.
Solving the problem of exploding gradient have a very easy solution which have been
shown to work very well in practice. It is solved by simply setting a threshold and
if the gradient is above this threshold it is clipped to the threshold value. This tech-
nique is called gradient-clipping Pascanu et al., 2013.
The authors suggest a heuristic for setting this hyperparameters by observing the
average norm over a sufficient set of training steps. The threshold is set to something
slightly above this average.
In the domain of RNN architectures the most common way to combat the vanishing
gradient is by using a more complex architecture to model the temporal dependency,
using a method called Long Short-Term Memory.

2.4
Word Representation
Text or words do not have an obvious mathematical representation as images which
can be described as pixel intensities on each channel of red-green-blue color scheme.
The number of words in a language is massive, so in a machine learning setting this is
limited to the words appearing in the dataset that is used. This set of words is known
as the vocabulary V and can be seen as the words available to the model. Historically,
the naive approach to represent tokens, e.g. words, in natural language processing
were one-hot encoded vectors where each token were represented by a vector of the
size of the vocabulary, M = |V|. In this vector, only the words own unique entry is
set to one, and the rest are zeroes. This is a problem because in many NLP problems
vocabulary sizes can be in the millions, resulting in input document to be in RMxN,
N denoting the number of words in the document. Furthermore, the encoding does
not provide the model with any notion of relationship between words, their semantics
or any other statistics about the words.
2.4.1

A popular way to introduce more information in the representation is by encoding
documents as a bag-of-words (BOW). This results in a document to be encoded to RM

sure is to represent words as their Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency(TF-
IDF)Salton and McGill, 1986, with a term meaning a word in this context.

2.5
Sequence to sequence
RNNs are able to deal with sequential data which the FFNN struggles with. However
RNNs still have issues when the machine learning goal is to get a variable length
output sequence given an input with a different length. This is because a RNN will
output for each timestep resulting in an output sequence with the same length as the
input sequence, or the RNN will only output when the full input seuence has been
read which results in a static sized ouput. This problem has been addressed with the
sequence to sequence architecture Sutskever et al., 2014 also known as an encoder
decoder architecture Cho et al., 2014. The sequence to sequence architecture is the
foundation for lots of state of the art NMT models.
The general idea of a sequence to sequence model is that an encoder will read an
input sequence word by word (or character by character) and encode this sequence
into a fixed sized vector representation. This vector representation is then passed into
the decoder component that decodes it into another sequence. In NMT a sequence of
words would be encoded into a hidden representation and then decoded into another
language. This architecture is illustrated on Figure 2.5. The encoder and decoder
component is most often a RNN such as an LSTM Sutskever et al., 2014 or a GRU
Cho et al., 2014. Since works has also considered using a convolutional neural net-
work (CNN) for the encoder component Kalchbrenner and Blunsom, 2013. In NLP
problems the natural choice for the encoder and decoder is often LSTMs due to their
ability to learn long range dependencies.
Figure 2.5: Example of a sequence to sequence architecture.
2.5.1
Encoder
Considering a sequence to sequence using a LSTMs for the encoder, each encoding
timestep i takes an input word represented by its word embedding together with the
state from the previous timestep i−1 as input. When the input sequence ends, which
is marked by an end of sequence symbol, the hidden state of the LSTM is a fixed
sized vector representation of the input sequence.
In the traditional sequence to sequence setup, the hidden representation for a
given time step only depends on its previous inputs and their hidden states.
To
better capture a word’s meaning in its context, information about future time steps
is needed as well.
This shortcoming is addressed with the bidirectional RNN Schuster and Paliwal, 1997

Evaluation metrics
In the domains of NMT and summarization the goal is for a model to produce a
good translation or summary, but how do you measure the quality? As mentioned
in Section 2.1.5 it is specific to the ML problem how to measure the perfomance of
a model and in this section we will look into how to do this in the domain of NMT
and summarization.
The outputs from a model can be evaluated manually by a human, however this
is very time consuming. Since machine learning models needs to be evaluated often,
an automatic evaluation system is very desirable. Often NMT and summarization
datasets provide a set of translations or summaries written by humans which are
considered to be the ground truth.
But it is not obvious how to mathematically
compare the output from a model with a set human written texts to capture how
good the output was. We will call the output of a model the candidate and a set of
human written texts the references.

3.2
Repetition in the decoder
Despite that the temporal attention and coverage mechanism focuses on what parts
of the encoder that already been attended to during decoding the model can still
repeat itself.
If we assume that the temporal attention and the coverage mechanism ensures
that the decoder does not attend to the same encoding states, then the decoder
does not have any explicit information about what it has been outputting in the
previous decoding steps. This again lets the decoder rely on the LSTMs long term
capabilities, which obviously falls short in preventing the repetitions. We hypothesize
two possible scenarios where the decoder will output repeated sentences on the basis
of the information from either the attention mechanism or the LSTMs hidden state.
1. Say the decoder has outputted a sentence and the attention mechanism has
attended to some encoder states. The attention mechanism in the next decoding
step will then attend to some other encoder state. However the information that
the encoder state contains might be similar to that of the encoder states that
has already been attended to. This is a possible scenario given that an article
can repeat some of the same information but paraphrased.
2. Say the softmax output layer does not weight the input from the attention
mechanism as high as the hidden state of the LSTM. Then we rely entirely on
the decoder LSTM to be able to forget information when it has been outputted.
However if the LSTM does not forget the information after having outputted a
sentence that describes this information then the LSTM wil still preserves some
of this information in its cell state.
In both of the above scenarios the softmax output layer is given some information that
has already been outputted in a previous sentence. The beginning of a new sentence
often starts with one or more common words, and the decoder might output the same
first words as the first words of the previous sentence. The softmax layer takes both
the previous output, the context vector and the hidden state of the LSTM as input
as in Equation (2.33). It is clear that since either the context vector or the hidden
state contains already outputted information and the sentence has started with the
same pattern as the previous sentence then the decoder is very likely to produce an
identical sentence.

To avoid repetition at test time Paulus et al. (2017) forced the decoder to never
output the same n-gram more than once. In specific they use n = 3, ie. trigrams.
The justification for constraining the decoder in this way comes from the observation
in the datasets (NYT and CNN/DM) that none of the reference summaries contains
the same trigram more than once as well as that this is a valid constraint in general
for abstract summarization. This is done by setting the probability for the decoder
to output a word p(yt) = 0 if the word would have created a duplicate trigram.

3.3
Pointer
A common problem in NLP is when a model is presented with words it has never
seen before, which is known as Out Of Vocabulary words and denoted OOV. The
model operates on a fixed sized vocabulary constructed as a subset of all words in the
training data. The subset consists of the most frequent words, often capped at some
threshold.
Whenever the model encounters an OOV word it is assigned a specific word ’UNK’,
which is the universal representation for all unknown tokens. This obviously deprives
the model of information about these words.
Often, OOVs are named entities such as persons and organizations. This is a huge
problem for a model to produce useful results, since the relevant entity often is im-
portant information.
At each decoding time step a softmax operation over the vocabulary is performed.
This is often the computational bottleneck when training sequence to sequence models,
which motivates the use of smaller vocabulary sizes thus reducing the denominator
sum in the softmax function. The smaller the vocabulary the bigger the chance of
having to deal with OOV words.
To address the problem of OOV words in the model a mechanism is constructed,
allowing the decoder not only to output words from the vocabulary but also from the
input sequence. This is done with the pointer introduced in Vinyals et al., 2015. We
want our model to be able to point to an input word only when it makes sense as with
the case of OOV. To do this we use a switch function that decides at each decoding
step whether to point to a word or to generate a new word from the vocabulary, as
described in Section 2.5.2. We denote ut ∈[0, 1] the probability of pointing to a word
instead of generating a word. This probability is calculated as

3.4 Related works

3.4
Related works
Most of the past work in summarization has been in extractive summarization. Early
works in abstractive summarization where inspired by the statistical machine trans-
lation and based on techniques such as phrase-tables (Banko et al., 2000). Rush et al.
(2015) was the first to use neural networks for abstract summarization. This let the
domain of abstract summarization to follow the same shift as in machine translation
that went from SMT to NMT with the introduction of the sequence to sequence
models (Sutskever et al., 2014). The DUC-2003 and DUC-2004 datasets (Over et al.,
2007) together with the Gigaword dataset (Graff et al., 2003) has traditionally been
the standard datasets for the task of sentence-level abstract summarization where the
model summarizes sentences into a single sentence (also known as headline genera-
tion) (Rush et al., 2015; Nallapati et al., 2016; Chopra et al., 2016; Takase et al., n.d.;
Suzuki and Nagata, 2017; Li et al., 2017). The CNN/DM dataset (Hermann et al.,
2015; Nallapati et al., 2016) has been used for document level summarization (Nalla-
pati et al., 2016; See et al., 2017; Paulus et al., 2017; Fan et al., 2017; L. Liu et al.,
2017) where the input text is often larger and the models produces a multi sentence
output. The NYT dataset (Sandhaus, 2008) was recently proposed by Paulus et al.
(2017) as another document level summarization dataset.
Sequence to sequence architectures
All modern neural methods for abstract
summarization uses the sequence to sequence architecture (Sutskever et al., 2014)
often in combination with an attention mechanism (Bahdanau et al., 2015). Most
works has been using gated RNNs for both the encoder and the decoder. In particular
there has been works using GRUs (Cho et al., 2014) with BiGRUs for the encoder
and a single GRU for the decoder (Nallapati et al., 2016; Gu et al., 2016; Shen et al.,
2016; Chen et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2017). Other works has used a BiLSTM together
with a LSTM for the encoder and decoder (Suzuki and Nagata, 2017; See et al., 2017;
Paulus et al., 2017; L. Liu et al., 2017).
Rush et al. (2015) introduced their model called Attention-Based Summarization
(ABS) that uses an attentive encoder that utilizes the attention mechanism to produce
a context vector that is used to condition a FFNN for decoding. ABS was improved
by Ranzato et al. (2015) and Chopra et al. (2016) with convolutions in the encoder
to capture spatial information, and a RNN replacing the FFNN decoder. To enrich
the encoder representation Takase et al. (n.d.) improved the ABS model by using
additional features such as abstract meaning representation parser. Nallapati et al.
(2016) also tried to enrich the encoder by providing more information as input to
the model, i.e. Part-Of-Speech tagging(POS), Named Entity Recognition(NER) and
TF-IDF statistics to be able to better capture syntax in the input. To better capture
semantics, pretrained wordembeddings has been used to get a better representation
of words (Gulcehre et al., 2016; Paulus et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2017). The sequence
to sequence model has also been extended with a variational autoencoder component
for inference (Miao and Blunsom, 2016; Li et al., 2017) in abstract summarization.
==PAGE:42==
32
3 Abstract summarization theory
The softmax layer in the decoder becomes a computational bottleneck when the
vocabulary is large.
To overcome this Nallapati et al. (2016) and Gulcehre et al.
(2016) use the large vocabulary trick on abstract summarization proposed by Jean et
al. (2014) for NMT. The target vocabulary is divided into subsets according to a split
of the target sentences and their containing words. This reduces the dimensions of the
final softmax layer to the size of the subsets without having to limit the vocabulary.
RNNs are often used in sequence to sequence models but they are slow to train
due to the difficulty of parallelizing them to take full advantage of modern GPUs.
Therefore computational efficiency has received attention for sequence to sequence
models recently. Kalchbrenner et al. (2016) introduced ByteNet that replaces RNNs
with a parallel tree structure based on CNNs and Vaswani et al. (2017) introduced
the Transformer that relies solely on intra attention mechanisms. Using CNNs only
for both the encoder and decoder Gehring et al. (2017) achieved an improvement in
speed and accuracy in NMT and in abstract summarization.
Pointer mechanisms
As discussed in Section 3.3 a main problem in abstractive
text summarization is handling OOV words. This has been addressed by several in
the literature inspired by the pointer mechanism originated in NMT with the pointer
networks (Vinyals et al., 2015). The pointer mechanism as described in Section 3.3
consists of a switch function which is trained to choose whether to generate a word
or to point to a word. When pointing, the attention weights are used for deciding
what word should be copied with the distribution described in Equation (2.31). This
approach is used by Miao and Blunsom (2016), Zeng et al. (2016), and See et al. (2017).
Paulus et al. (2017) explicitly trains the pointer switch function to only turn on for
OOV words and named entities. Gu et al. (2016) explicitly trains a distribution over
the input instead of using the attention weights. Nallapati et al. (2016) and Gulcehre
et al. (2016) both train the pointer switch for OOV words and named entities as well
as training a distribution over the input.
In contrast to learning a probability for using a pointer Chen et al. (2016) and
Shen et al. (2016) uses a a technique where they let the model generate a summary
first and then postprocess it. If it contains a ’UNK’ token it is then replaced with the
most likely input word at that decoding timestep based on the attention weights.
Repetition avoidance
As the problem of repetitive outputs is very common for
sequence models this is a problem that has been dealt with in several ways. The
temporal attention mechanism (Nallapati et al., 2016; Paulus et al., 2017) as described
in Section 3.1.1 has been one solution to the problem. Another solution is the coverage
mechanism (See et al., 2017) which is also referred to as distraction (Chen et al., 2016).
The intra decoder attention introduced by Paulus et al. (2017) has also recently
been adopted in other summarization work (Fan et al., 2017). The harder n-gram
constraint (Paulus et al., 2017) has also recently been adopted by L. Liu et al. (2017)
and Fan et al. (2017). Another approach is to use a word-frequency estimator in the
encoder (Suzuki and Nagata, 2017) to estimate the upper-bound frequency of the
==PAGE:43==
3.4 Related works
33
words in the target vocabulary. This information is then used during decoding to
avoid repetition.
Training the model
To train the model on a sentence level Shen et al. (2016) uses
minimum risk training (MRT) which uses the distance between a candidate summary
and a reference summary. The MRT then defines a loss function that approximates
an expectation over all possible candidate summaries.
Using the MRT allows for
optimizing directly against the ROUGE metric.
Other works has also optimized directly on an evaluation metric.
Ranzato et
al. (2015) use reinforcement learning to optimize directly on the discrete ROUGE
metric. Similarly Paulus et al. (2017) optimizes directly on the ROUGE score using
reinforcement learning. However they find that using only the reinforcement learning
produces higher ROUGE-score, but reduces the readability. Therefore Paulus et al.
(2017) introduces a novel hybrid learning objective that combines the reinforcement
learning with the teachers force learning algorithm.
Recently L. Liu et al. (2017) use General Adverserial Networks (GAN) to train
an abstractive summarization model.
They jointly train a generative sequence to
sequence model similar to the model in See et al. (2017) and a discriminative text
classifier that can classify the candidate summaries as machine or human generated.
Alternative summarization work
All the previous mentioned works focuses on
getting a model to perform as good as possible in terms of the ROUGE score. Recently
there has also been some works considering other aspects of summarization. Fan et al.
(2017) argues that a summaries are generated at a reader’s request and the should
therefore be able to get summaries that adhere to the users preferences. They consider
three different control variables: length of the summary for readers with limited time,
entities that a reader might be interested in (such as a sports fan that might be
interested in a specific athlete) and writing style of the summary. For the reference
summaries that complies with a given preference they they prepend markers to the
input articles when training the model, and show that the model has learned to
produce outputs that adhere to the preferences at test time.
Cao et al. (2017) argues that a very important aspect for a summary is that it
is faithful to the input. While this is not a problem in extractive summarization
the problem occurs in most of the state of the art neural abstractive summarization
models. They propose to extract facts from the input article by extracting entity
relations and using dependency parsing. The facts are then used as input together
with the original article and a dual attention mechanism in the decoder attends to
both the facts and the original article. They show that this approach can greatly
reduce the number of unfaithful summaries.
