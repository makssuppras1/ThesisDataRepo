Abstract 
 
iii 
 
Abstract 
There is a societal shift towards the use of natural ingredients in cosmeceutical products, 
including sunscreens. The aim of this study was to ascertain whether antioxidants and 
photoprotective components could be obtained from the abundant seaweed Bladderwrack 
(Fucus vesiculosus), and whether growth depths had any influence on the occurrence of such 
components. 
Phlorotannin-rich extracts of F. vesiculosus grown at four different depths were prepared using 
solid-liquid extraction (SLE) and water [FV-WE] and 67 % ethanol [FV-EE] as solvents. The 
antioxidant- and photoprotective properties of the extracts were screened by measuring total 
phenolic content (TPC), DPPH radical scavenging capacity and ferrous iron chelating FIC) 
ability, as well as HPLC analysis for pigment and phenolic composition. No significant 
(p<0.05) difference between the four growth depths were observed. FV-EE performed slightly 
better in extracting phenolic compounds and DPPH radical scavenging capacity, whilst FV-WE 
were superior in FIC ability. The pigment fucoxanthin was only detected in FV-EE, whilst 
results for phenolic composition was inconclusive, highlighting the challenges in characterizing 
these complex compounds. Furthermore, the sun protection factor (SPF) of lotion enriched with 
freeze-dried seaweed extract was estimated using the Mansur method. The seaweed-enriched 
lotions presented negligible photoprotective capacity in vitro (up to SPF 1.06), indicating that 
SLE alone is insufficient in providing photoprotective components in significant amounts.  
Further investigation is required in detecting, characterizing, and isolating purified antioxidant 
and photoprotective components from F. vesiculosus, as well as closing the gap between in 
vitro assays and complex in vivo systems.

Introduction 
 
1 
 
1. Introduction 
New consumer trends, modern technology and the current challenges in resource scarcity are 
providing new and exciting opportunities for the cosmeceutical industry. Seaweed is a widely 
distributed and underutilized resource, with enormous potential in providing bioactive 
components for food, nutraceuticals, and cosmeceuticals, and has therefore gained the interest 
of several researchers (Holdt & Kraan, 2011). 
Recently, there has been a massive societal shift towards clean labels and the use of natural 
ingredients in various products, as well as sunscreens. Along with intriguing several 
researchers, it has also led to a proliferation of websites promoting the use of natural oils and 
extracts as home-made remedies to replace synthetic UV-filters (Merten et al., 2020). Exposure 
to solar irradiation can lead to several harmful effects, and solar-induced skin cancer rates are 
increasing every year. Therefore, it is important that statements presented in the field of 
photoprotection is sufficiently substantiated, to avoid promoting harmful advice (de la Coba et 
al., 2019; Merten et al., 2020; World Cancer Research Fund, 2019).   
The promising in vitro antioxidant potential of the brown seaweed F. vesiculosus compared to 
other species of seaweed has been established by several authors. This seaweed contains high 
levels of phlorotannins, carotenoids, and sulphated polysaccharides, which has been shown to 
exert excellent in vitro antioxidant activity (Farvin & Jacobsen, 2013; Hermund, 2016; Honold 
et al., 2016). Subtidal seaweed species, such as F. vesiculosus, are exposed to high levels of 
solar radiation, and therefore, synthesis and accumulation of ultraviolet (UV) absorbing 
components is a common photoprotective strategy in this group of organisms (Pallela et al., 
2010). This has spiked the interest of several researchers to investigate the potential in utilizing 
seaweed-components as a supplement to UVB and UVA-absorbing filters in sunscreen (Ácsová 
et al., 2021; Pallela et al., 2010). Phlorotannins, for example, has an important role in the 
seaweeds natural defence against UV-induced cell damage, indicating that phlorotannin-rich 
extracts of F. vesiculosus can be utilized as an antioxidant-rich photoprotective ingredient in 
sunscreen formulations (Pangestuti et al., 2018; Pavia et al., 1997; Wang et al., 2012).

Aims, hypothesis, and objectives 
 
2 
 
1.1 Aim, hypothesis, and objectives  
The aim of this study is to develop a phlorotannin rich F. vesiculosus extract with high 
antioxidant and photoprotective properties for application in cosmeceutical products.  
This study investigates the TPC, radical scavenging capacity, FIC ability, and SPF of 
phlorotannin rich extracts of F. vesiculosus harvested at four different growth depths.  
This thesis investigates the following hypotheses: 
• [H1] Intertidal species such as F. vesiculosus are frequently exposed to harmful levels 
of UV-radiation. As a result, individuals positioned higher up in the depth gradient 
possess more bioactive components with roles in photoprotection and antioxidant 
activity.  
• [H2] F. vesiculosus exhibits in vitro antioxidant activity, and the polarity and chemical 
nature of extraction solvent can affect the composition and activity of antioxidants in F. 
vesiculosus extracts. 
• [H3] F. vesiculosus contains photoprotective components that can increase the SPF in 
sunscreen formulations. 
The hypotheses are investigated by performing the following objectives: 
• Create phlorotannin-rich extracts from brown seaweed F. vesiculosus using different 
extraction solvents (water and 67 % ethanol). 
• Determine the in vitro antioxidant capacity of the extracts using DPPH radical 
scavenging capacity and FIC ability. 
• Identify the antioxidant and UV absorbing compounds in the extracts including some 
co-extracted compounds using spectrophotometric assays (Folin-Ciolcalteu assay) and 
HPLC. 
• Implement a method for SPF-determination of seaweed-enriched lotions (Mansur 
method), and validate the method by analysing the SPF of commercial sunscreens with 
known SPF.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Seaweed – current application and future potential 
Seaweeds, or macroalgae, are commonly classified into three groups – brown (Phaeophycae), 
red (Rhodophyceae) and green (Chlorophyceae) seaweeds, each characterized by the dominant 
pigments and preferred wavelength absorption. As primary producers, they are key elements in 
sustaining complex food webs in aquatic ecosystems (Pereira, 2021). Seaweeds has for 
generations been utilized as a food source, primarily in coastal communities. It has a high 
moisture content (up to 94 % in fresh seaweed), but also contains several nutritional elements 
such as proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids, as well as vitamins and minerals. The nutritional 
compositions varies depending on species, season, and area of production (Holdt & Kraan, 
2011). Currently, the use of seaweed comprises food, fuel, fertilizer, phycocolloids (emulsifiers, 
stabilizers, gelling agents, thickening agents) and cosmetic ingredients, but is still considered 
to be an under-utilized resource globally (Holdt & Kraan, 2011; Milledge et al., 2016). 
Around 221 seaweed species are of commercial value, and the global seaweed industry is worth 
more than USD 6 billion annually. The total production has grown from 14.7 million tonnes in 
2005 to 30.4 million tonnes in 2015, of which the culture and capture sectors are responsible 
for 29.4 million tonnes and 1.1 million tonnes, respectively. Seaweed-culturing occurs 
primarily in China, Indonesia, the Republic of Korea, and the Philippines, whilst capture-rates 
are highest in Chile, China, and Norway. About 85 % of the total production comprise food and 
foodstuffs for human consumption (FAO, 2018). 
Brown seaweed and its components has become of increasing interest to many researchers 
worldwide. The diverse biochemical composition of seaweed provides an appealing reservoir 
for exploring functional components, in which many are rare or absent in other taxonomic 
groups. These components may serve as ingredients in functional foods, or act as ingredients 
or topical antioxidants in various cosmeceutical products (Holdt & Kraan, 2011).  
Another interesting potential is seaweeds content of photoprotective components. Seaweeds are 
highly exposed to ultra violet radiation (UVR) and photosynthetic active radiation (PAR) and 
must therefore counteract and minimize photodamage by producing photoprotective substances 
such as polyphenols, carotenoids, sulphated polysaccharides and mycosporine-like amino acids 
(MAAs). Research is being performed to explore the potential of extracting these components 
and utilizing them as photoprotective ingredients in sunscreens (Pallela et al., 2010).

2.1.1 Biology of Fucus vesiculosus  
F. vesiculosus, more commonly known as bladderwrack, is one of the 
most common brown seaweeds, and is found in both the littoral and 
sub-littoral zone of temperate waters. It can reach around 90 cm in 
length, and a blade width of around 2.5 cm. As its name suggest, F. 
vesiculosus is covered in tiny bladders, which are often found in pairs 
on its thallus (Rueness, 2005).  
As illustrated in Figure 1, F. vesiculosus consists of the holdfast 
(anchoring mechanism to hard substrates), stripe, a blade with smooth 
edges, dichotomously branched fronds, and air bladders to maintain 
a vertical position in the water column. Conceptacles, which appears 
as “dots” on the receptacles, contains the reproductive material 
(Hermund, 2016).  
  
2.1.2 Variation of biochemical composition as a response to biotic and 
abiotic factors 
In nature, seaweeds are not only exposed to single, independent environmental stresses, but 
experience several stresses that may exert synergistic physiological effects. The effect of an 
single environmental stress (such as UVR) may therefore be contingent on one or several other 
factors (Sampath-Wiley et al., 2008). The content and diversity of seaweed metabolites is 
subject to both abiotic and biotic factors, such as age/life stage, salinity, nutrient availability, 
intensity of herbivory, pollution, location and time of collection (Cotas et al., 2020).  
According to Koivikko et al., (2008), the phlorotannin content can vary even amongst adjacent 
populations of F. vesiculosus, either as a result of genetic divergence among a population, or as 
plastic responses to environmental conditions. The study utilized neutral molecular markers and 
found proof of genetic differences amongst adjacent populations of F. vesiculosus in the Baltic 
Sea.  
Sampath-Wiley et al., (2008) evaluated the effect of solar exposure and emersion on the red 
seaweed Porphyra umbilicalis and revealed that populations located in the upper intertidal zone 
during the summer months contained elevated levels of antioxidants compared to submerged 
seaweed. The effect of solar exposure can be indirectly measured by comparing the biochemical 
composition across the months. Mendes (2017) evaluated the composition of pigments,

phenolics, total phenolic content, and antioxidant activity of Danish F. vesiculosus throughout 
one year and found that the highest level of pigments was found in March and the lowest in 
July, suggesting a compensation towards decreasing light availability in the winter months. 
Phenolic compounds were highest and most diverse in September, whilst the least diverse 
month was march. The cause of increased phenolic compounds during summer could be a 
photoprotective mechanism to tolerate UV-induced stress (Heffernan et al., 2015; Mendes, 
2017). Moreover, for intertidal species like F. vesiculosus, there has been observed a positive 
correlation between air temperature and variation in phenolic compounds for the brown 
seaweed species Pelvetia canaliculate, Ascophyllum nodosum and Bifurcaria bifurcate 
(Connan et al., 2004). Increase in polyphenols has also been correlated with intensified grazing 
during the summer months. Antioxidant activity has also been shown to vary throughout the 
year, with the highest radical scavenging capacity and FIC ability found in January, and the 
lowest in September and May, respectively (Mendes, 2017). 
The age/life stage of seaweed also influence the phlorotannin content in several species. 
Juveniles of Fucus gardneri, Lessoniopsis littoralis, Egregia menziesii and Hedophylum sessile 
presented higher contents of phlorotannins compared to adult species (Van Alstyne et al., 2001). 
Honold et al., (2016) performed a study comparing the composition and antioxidant activity of 
old (non-growing) and young (growing tip) parts of F. vesiculosus and found that old parts were 
lower in TPC and FIC ability, but higher in radical scavenging capacity.  
It is evident that multiple abiotic and biotic factors are exerting both individual and possibly 
synergistic effects that could influence the biochemical composition of several seaweed species. 
This complex web of interactions presents a huge challenge in relation to selecting optimal 
seaweed individuals for extraction of biochemical components.   
 
2.2 Antioxidants and photoprotective components in brown 
seaweed 
Due to its widespread nature and chemical composition, seaweed has enormous potential of 
becoming a valuable source of natural antioxidants and photoprotective substances. Natural 
antioxidants derived from seaweeds has been shown to improve the oxidative stability of food 
and cosmetics (Hermund, 2016). Hermund et al., (2018) demonstrated that ethanolic extracts

of F. vesiculosus reduced peroxide oxidation rates from 84.8 % to 41.3 % in facial creams 
stored over 42 days.  
The most common method used for reduction of lipid oxidation is the addition of 
multifunctional antioxidants with high radical scavenging capacity and ability to chelate 
prooxidative transition metals. Amongst 16 different seaweed species along the Danish coast, 
Farvin & Jacobsen (2013) found that F. vesiculosus and Fucus serratus displayed higher radical 
scavenging activity and polyphenol content compared to other brown seaweeds. 
Amongst seaweeds, the most significant antioxidants are phlorotannins, sulphated 
polysaccharides, carotenoids and MAAs. The following paragraphs outline each of these groups 
individually. Finally, a paragraph will be included to address how the photoprotective capacity 
and FIC ability of seaweed antioxidants could play a role in the oxidative stress in the skin.  
 
2.2.1 Phenolic compounds 
Phenolic compounds are present in both land- and marine plants. The structure of phenolic 
compounds can vary from simple moieties to high molecular polymers, but they are all 
characterized by the presence of an aromatic ring with one or more hydroxyl groups (-OH) 
(Cotas et al., 2020).  The hydroxyl group functions as an electron donor towards free radicals 
or reactive species (Pangestuti et al., 2018).  
2.2.1.1 Phenolic acids 
Phenolic acids (PA’s) (Figure 2) are bioactive compounds involved in several functions, 
including nutrient absorption, protein synthesis, enzymatic activity, photosynthesis, and 
allelopathy. PA’s are regularly bound to other molecules, such as simple and/or complex 
carbohydrates, organic acids, and other bioactive molecules such as flavonoids or terpenoids. 
PA’s are formed by a single phenol ring and at least one functional carboxylic acid group (R-
COOH), and they are usually classified based on the number of carbons in the chain bound to 
the phenolic ring (Cotas et al., 2020).  
Studies revolving PA’s in seaweeds are scarce, and are mainly focused on characterization 
rather than determining the bioactivity (Cotas et al., 2020). Farvin & Jacobsen (2013) identified 
several PA’s in water- and ethanolic extracts of F. vesiculosus. In water extracts, they located 
gallic acid (13.5 mg/g extract) protocatechuic acid (1.7 mg/g extract), gentisic acid (19.4 mg/g 
extract), chlorogenic acid (0.9 mg/g extract) and caffeic acid (0.9 mg/g extract), whilst in

2.2.1.2 Phlorotannins 
Out of all the seaweed phenolic compounds, the main attention has been focused on 
phlorotannins. Phlorotannins is a group of extremely heterogenous water-soluble molecules, 
which provides a wide range of potential biological activity (Farvin & Jacobsen, 2013; Holdt 
& Kraan, 2011).  
Phlorotannins are polyphenolic compounds, and oligomers phloroglucinol (Cotas et al., 2020). 
The structure of phlorotannin (Figure 3) consists of up to 8 of the basic phloroglucinol units 
(PGU). The linkage between the PGU-groups determines whether the phlorotannin is a fucol 
(phenyl linkage) phloroethol (ether linkage) or fucophloroethol (ether and phenyl linkage) 
(Isaza Martínez & Torres Castañeda, 2013). The size ranges from 0.126-650 kDa, but are most 
commonly observed between 10-100 kDa, and the complexity and structural diversity increases 
with increasing numbers of PGU’s (Steevensz et al., 2012).  
 
Figure 3: Chemical structure of (A) the basic unit phloroglucinol (126.11 Da) and the main classes of phlorotannins derived 
from brown seaweed: (B) fucol, (C) phloroethol, (D) fucuphloroethol and the subgroup (E) eckol. The red squares marks the 
different linkages (Hermund et al., 2016). 
==PAGE:18==

Phlorotannins are restricted to brown seaweeds, where they exert functions as primary and 
secondary metabolites. The quantities of phlorotannins can range from 5-30 % of the seaweed 
dry weight (Heffernan et al., 2015). 
In seaweed, phlorotannins are found within the cell in vesicles called physodes, which are 
located both in the periphery of the cell and in perinuclear regions, where they are formed (Cotas 
et al., 2020). Phlorotannins will also form complexes with macromolecules. When physodes 
come in contact with the plasmalemma, phlorotannins are released and polymerized in the cell 
wall. Here, they form various complexes with polysaccharides, for example alginic-acid-bound 
phlorotannins (Gómez & Huovinen, 2020). 
Phlorotannins have been found to play a role in herbivore-deterrence, photoprotection and  
antioxidant activity of brown seaweeds (Gómez & Huovinen, 2020; Wang et al., 2009, 2012). 
These components also act as chelating agents of metal ions. When seaweed is faced with 
surrounding copper contamination, increased levels of phlorotannins is formed in the cell-wall, 
along with increased exudation of phlorotannins to their surroundings. Hence, copper is 
chelated and thereby prevented from entering and damaging the photosynthetic apparatus 
(Mannino et al., 2016). Additionally, phlorotannins have been found to possess several 
bioactive properties which could be of interest for skin care products, including anti-
inflammatory, anti-bacterial, anti-ageing and photoprotection (Dutot et al., 2012; Swanson & 
Druehl, 2002). 
Increased tolerance to UVR has been correlated with the presence of phlorotannins. 
Phlorotannins can absorb light in the UVC (100-280 nm) and short UVB (280-300 nm) range. 
A study performed by Pavia et al., (1997) revealed that the absorption of Ascophyllum nodosum 
extracts in the short UVB region decreased by 70 % when phlorotannins were removed using 
polyvinylpolypyrrolidone (PVPP). Due to presence of various interconnected rings and 
hydroxyl groups acting as reducing agents, phlorotannins also act as highly potent antioxidants, 
scavenging various types of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (superoxide anions, singlet oxygen, 
hydroxyl radicals and peroxides) (Gómez & Huovinen, 2020).  
 
 2.2.2 Carotenoids 
Carotenoids are natural pigments found in photosynthetic organisms, as well as some non-
photosynthetic archaea, fungi, bacteria, and animals, and are divided into the two classes 
carotenes and xanthophylls. Carotenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons, whilst xanthophylls 
==PAGE:19==
Theoretical background 
 
9 
 
includes one or more functional groups containing oxygen (Pangestuti et al., 2018; Pangestuti 
& Kim, 2011). Carotenoids acts as crucial components in the photosynthetic light-harvesting 
complex, as well as being effective antioxidants by scavenging singlet oxygen and peroxyl 
radicals (Young & Lowe, 2001). Additionally, a strong correlation has been found between 
carotenoid accumulation and UVB-radiation in both terrestrial and marine plants, suggesting 
that carotenoids plays a role as photoprotective substances in seaweed (Hupel et al., 2011; 
Pangestuti et al., 2018).  
Fucoxanthin (Figure 4), a xanthophyll, is the most abundant carotenoid in brown seaweed 
species, and is an accessory pigment giving brown seaweed its distinct colour (Pangestuti & 
Kim, 2011). Nygård & Ekelund (2006) found that F. vesiculosus contains 1 µg fucoxanthin / 
mg dw. Fucoxanthin and other xanthophylls are efficient singlet oxygen quenchers. These 
pigments also act as photoprotective substances by dissipating excess energy as heat (Pangestuti 
et al., 2018). Fucoxanthin has also been demonstrated to stimulate filaggrin promoter activity 
in UV-induced sunburn. Filaggrin is a UV-sensitive gene that reflect the state of skin damage 
(Matsui et al., 2016). A study performed by Urikura et al., (2011) found that fucoxanthin also 
exerts a protective effect against UVB-induced photoaging, possibly through antioxidant and 
angiogenic activity.  
 
Figure 4: The chemical structure of Fucoxanthin. Created in ChemDraw 
 
Tocopherol, a member of the carotenoid family, is another potent antioxidant, and exert 
efficient radical scavenging activity and direct inactivation of free radicals. They exist as four 
different isomers (α-, β-, γ-, and δ-tocopherol) with antioxidant activity in increasing order 
(Decker, 2002). α-tocopherol (vitamin E) is the main tocopherol found in F. vesiculosus, with 
levels ranging between 38-73 mg/kg dw, depending on season (highest in autumn and winter) 
(Jensen, 1969). α-tocopherol is only produced by photosynthetic organisms, and its main 
cellular function is to protect cellular membranes against UVR or oxidative damage (Kim et 
al., 2008).

2.2.3 Sulphated polysaccharides 
Marine seaweeds are the most important source of non-animal sulphated polysaccharides. In 
brown seaweed, fucoidan (Figure 5) is the most important group and exert excellent in vitro 
antioxidant activity. Generally, these linear polysaccharides have a α13-linked 1-fucose 
backbone with residues containing various substitutions of sulphated groups (Rupérez et al., 
2002). The structure and bioactivity of fucoidans differ between brown seaweed species 
(Pangestuti et al., 2018).  
The photoprotective capacity of fucoidan isolated from brown seaweeds has been evaluated by 
several authors (Hyun et al., 2013; Kim et al., 2018; Ku et al., 2010; Maruyama et al., 2015; 
Moon et al., 2009), with the majority reporting that the photoprotective activity is mediated 
though suppression of matrix metalloproteinase-1 (MMP-1) activity. MMP-1 is a major enzyme 
implicated in collagen damage and photoaging of UV-irradiated human skin. Kim et al., (2018) 
reports that low-molecular-weight fucoidan exhibits higher photoprotective capacity compared 
to high-molecular weight fucoidan, primarily through photoprotective effects rather than UV-
filtering effects.

2.2.4 Mycosporine-like amino acids 
Mycosporine-like amino acids (MAAs) are found in a variety of organisms, such as 
cyanobacteria, fungi, corals and micro- and macroalgae. Over 30 different MAAs are 
discovered to date, and the type and accumulation of MAAs differs between species, season, 
climate, depth, and other environmental variables (Pangestuti et al., 2018). Whilst the presence 
of MAAs in red seaweed is well established, Cotas et al., (2020), reports that there is still some 
debate revolving the occurrence of MAAs in green and brown seaweeds. Carreto & Carignan, 
(2011) reports that levels of MAAs are highest in red>brown>green seaweed.  
==PAGE:21==
Theoretical background 
 
11 
 
MAAs are low molecular weight (<400 Da), water soluble molecules, and are distributed in the 
cell cytoplasm. The chemical structure is constructed around a ciclehexenone or cyclohexenine 
ring, with substituents of amino acids (Figure 6). MAAs are often referred to as “nature’s 
sunscreen”, with the conjugated bonds within the molecules absorbing UVR between 310-365 
nm wavelength, according to the substituents in the chemical structure. It functions by 
dispersing UVR into heat to the surroundings, instead of forming reactive photoproducts (Cotas 
et al., 2020; Yoshiki et al., 2009). MAAs are of high interest due to their notable ability to 
absorb UVA rays. Sunscreen containing 0.005 % of the MAA Porphyra-334 (346.33 Da) was 
found to neutralize UVA rays just as efficient as a sunscreen containing 1 % synthetic UVA 
filter and 4 % UVB filter (Daniel et al., 2004). Porphyra-334 also exhibits antioxidant properties 
through suppressing the formation of ROS (Oren & Gunde-Cimerman, 2007). Additionally, 
purified fractions of the MAA palythine from the red seaweed Chrondus yendoi  was found to 
exhibit an SPF of 17.9 and a UVA-protection factor of 1.6 at 10% concentration in cream base 
(Lawrence et al., 2018).

2.2.5 Topical application of seaweed-antioxidants: an example with iron-
induced oxidative stress 
As established in the sections above, seaweed contains components with actions related to FIC 
and photoprotection. Combined, these two properties could aid  in the issue of iron oxidization 
in the skin, which is a common challenge especially for post-menopausal women (Pelle et al., 
2011).  
The consequences of iron deficiency are well established, as iron is a vital element and a 
constituent in several important macromolecules, including those involved in energy 
production, respiration, DNA synthesis and metabolism. However, excess of labile iron can 
potentially be detrimental to the cell, because of its tendency to participate in oxidation-
reduction reactions, and consequently generating harmful free radicals through heterolytic 
==PAGE:22==
Theoretical background 
 
12 
 
scission of hydrogen peroxide. This dual nature makes maintaining iron homeostasis highly 
important (Mackenzie et al., 2008; Pelle et al., 2011). 
When women reach menopause, the first observable physiological change is the cessation of 
menstrual period, and consequently, iron will accumulate in the body (Jian et al., 2009). In post-
menopausal women, the body has limited capacity to remove excess iron, with skin 
desquamation becoming the main route (Pelle et al., 2011). When body iron storage increases, 
the skin is exposed to higher levels of iron, which may cause oxidative damage and skin aging 
during this process. Moreover, the increased stress may make skin more susceptible to UV 
damage (Jian et al., 2009). Coupled with lifestyle changes leading to increased sun exposure as 
well as increased longevity, the combination of UVA and increased skin iron levels may pose 
a serious health risk to post-menopausal women (Pelle et al., 2011). Exposure of human skin 
cells to solar UVA radiation leads to an immediate dose-dependent increase of labile iron that 
subsequently promotes oxidative damage and necrotic cell death (Yiakouvaki et al., 2006).  
Strong iron chelators have been shown to suppress cell damage and necrotic cell death by 
moderating the amount of labile iron (Yiakouvaki et al., 2006). Iron chelators are chemically 
diverse, but are typically characterized by the presence of oxygen, nitrogen or sulphur-donor 
atoms which forms coordinate bonds with iron (Hatcher et al., 2009). There is evidence of 
compounds responsible for in vitro FIC activity in F. vesiculosus extracts. Hermund et al., 
(2018) reported FIC EC50 (µL mL-1) of 87.7±12.2 and 121.5 ±29.0 in F. vesiculosus ethanolic 
and water extracts, respectively. Farvin & Jacobsen, (2013) also reported FIC ability, but to a 
lesser extent, with FIC EC50 (µg mL-1) of 1000.0±75.0 and 128.6±23.0 in F. vesiculosus 
ethanolic and water extracts, respectively.  
Although in vitro metal chelation has been identified, several challenges arise when transferring 
from in vitro laboratory assays to complex in vivo systems. The effectiveness of topical 
antioxidants is hard to determine, due to their different bioavailability and the difficulties in 
unravelling the intricate mechanisms of absorption and metabolism in the skin. As a rule of 
thumb, molecules larger than 500 Daltons are unable to penetrate the skin barrier (Bos & 
Meinardi, 2000). The human skin is a dynamic, living tissue, and the ability to penetrate skin is 
influenced by several factors such as physicochemical properties of the substance, the vehicle, 
possible occlusion, substance concentration, exposure pattern, and skin particularities across 
the body (Addor, 2017). As mentioned, the majority of phlorotannins are 10-100 kDa 
(Steevensz et al., 2012), and it is therefore unlikely that these substances will aid in chelating 
iron in the skin. Focus should be aimed at characterizing low-weight bioactive compounds with 
==PAGE:23==
Theoretical background 
 
13 
 
high FIC ability and photoprotective properties to (1) decrease the discharge of labile iron as a 
response to UVA, and (2) chelate iron in the skin.  
The flavonoid quercetin (302 Da) has been found to bind Fe2+ more strongly than ferrozine, 
which is a well-known iron chelator (Hatcher et al., 2009). Amongst 11 species of brown 
seaweeds, Ummat et al., (2020) found that ultrasound-assisted extracts of F. vesiculosus had 
the highest flavonoid content out of all the species analysed, with a total flavonoid content of 
281.0±1.7 mg quercetin equivalents/g. Additionally, other components in seaweed has found 
to exert FIC ability, such as sulphated polysaccharides. Presa et al., (2018) found evidence of 
iron chelation activity from sulphated polysaccharide-rich extracts of the green seaweed Udotea 
flabellum.  
The present study was unsuccessful in finding any published articles revealing the effectivity 
of topical chelating agents in seaweed extracts. However, several clinical studies has evaluated 
the in vivo effects of seaweed-enriched lotions as an anti-aging ingredient. Fitton et al., (2015) 
demonstrated that fucoidan-rich extracts from F. vesiculosus presented a significant effect in 
age-spot reduction, skin brightness, soothing and reduced wrinkle-depth, relative to placebo. 
Improved skin elasticity and decreased thickness of human cheek skin was demonstrated by 
Fujimura et al., (2001).  
 
2.3 Ultraviolet radiation and sunscreen 
2.3.1 Ultraviolet radiation  
The sun is for many people synonymous with comfort. When the sun peeks out after long and 
dark winter months, people rush out of their homes to sunbathe, relax, or to perform various 
recreational activities. Simultaneously, many people avoid wearing sun-protective gear, such 
as sunscreen, shade cloth and sunglasses – either deliberately to develop a tan, out of comfort, 
or due to unawareness of possible consequences. (Ichihashi et al., 2003; Lautenschlager et al., 
2007). When human skin is exposed to solar radiation, the skin is also exposed to acute and 
chronic effects (de la Coba et al., 2019).  
The sun emits UVR, which is commonly categorized as either UVC (100-280 nm), UVB (280-
315 nm) or UVA (315-400 nm) radiation. UVC is absorbed by stratospheric ozone, and does 
not reach the earth’s surface (Young et al., 2017). All effects that occur as a result of UVR-
exposure is underpinned by molecular or cellular effects, such as the generation of ROS, DNA 
==PAGE:24==
Theoretical background 
 
14 
 
damage, melanogenesis, apoptosis (cell death), depletion of Lagerhans cells, and expression of 
several genes and their related proteins (de la Coba et al., 2019).  
UVB accounts for approximately 5 % of terrestrial UVR, and the intensity varies by season, 
geographical location, and time of day. Although the short wavelengths only reaches the outer 
layer of the skin (epidermis), it typically exerts much greater effects compared to UVA (Young 
et al., 2017).  UVB radiation causes direct photochemical damage to the DNA and is considered 
one of the main causes of UVR-induced skin cancer. UVB radiation is also the main contributor 
to acute effects such as erythema (sunburn), photosensitivity and photoimmunosuppression (de 
la Coba et al., 2019; Ichihashi et al., 2003; Lautenschlager et al., 2007). UVB induced 
conversion of epidermal 7-dehydrocholesterol into pre-vitamin D3 is the only recognized 
benefit of UVR (de la Coba et al., 2019). 
UVA radiation is present with relatively equal intensity throughout the year. It displays more 
chronic effects, as it penetrates deeper into the epidermis and dermis layer of the skin, where it 
may induce the production of ROS, which in turn may damage the DNA. It is also the radiation 
responsible for premature photoaging of the skin (Ichihashi et al., 2003; Lautenschlager et al., 
2007). 
Action spectrum contains information of the relationship between specific wavelengths and the 
corresponding biological responses. Action spectra has been produced for several responses in 
the skin, such as erythema, photoaging, vitamin D3 synthesis and persistent pigment darkening 
(PPD) (Figure 7).

2.3.2 Sunscreen 
Photoprotection is crucial to prevent the negative effects of UVR and is commonly performed 
through the application of sunscreen. Sunscreen is generally divided into inorganic and organic 
sunscreens. Inorganic sunscreens, or sunblock, contain physical agents (e.g., titanium oxide and 
zinc oxide) that reflects and scatters UVR, whilst organic sunscreens contain chemically active 
ingredients that absorbs UVR. The agents can be classified as UVB, UVA or broadband filters, 
depending on the nature of the chemicals (Fonseca & Rafaela, 2013). 
Although it is now known that inorganic filters are typically more efficient compared to organic 
filters, inorganic sunscreens are typically less cosmetically acceptable. Many consumers prefer 
translucent lotions that is absorbed into the skin, as opposed to inorganic lotions that appear as 
a thick, white layer. Several sunscreens therefore contain both inorganic and organic filters in 
combination  (Diffey & Grice, 1997; Kaye et al., 1991; Lautenschlager et al., 2007). 
Several studies have posed concern regarding how synthetic organic filters are associated with 
allergic reactions or photo-toxicity, (Gaspar et al., 2008; Gaspar & Maia Campos, 2006; 
Kawakami & Gaspar, 2015), endocrine disruption (Krause et al., 2012), skin penetration  
(Sarveiya et al., 2004), low photo-stability and biodegradability, as well as lack of efficient skin 
protection (Kockler et al., 2012; Morabito et al., 2011). Pan et al., (2009) found that inorganic 
ingredients, such as TiO2, unless covered by a functional polymer coating, may cause adverse 
effects by decreasing cell area, cell proliferation, mobility, and ability to contract collagen. 
The concerns also cover how sunscreen ingredients are affecting the environment. Inorganic 
and organic particulate filters may accumulate in coastal and continental waters either directly 
when the user is swimming, or indirectly when the sunscreen is washed off in the shower (Fent 
et al., 2010; Zenker et al., 2008). UV filters have been identified in fish (Balmer et al., 2005) 
and invertebrates (Kaiser et al., 2012). Danovaro et al. (2008) claims that certain ingredients in 
sunscreen may be particularly harmful for coral reefs, by inducing a rapid and complete 
bleaching of hard corals even at very low concentrations. Several areas (e.g., Hawaii, Key West 
in Florida, Caribbean Island of Bonaire) has banned the use of sunscreens containing 
oxybenzone and octinoxate, ingredients estimated to be the main active ingredients in 70-80 % 
==PAGE:27==
Theoretical background 
 
17 
 
of all sunscreens (McMahon, 2019; Raffa et al., 2019). The  described effects of oxybenzone 
include bleaching of coral fragments and cells, as well as damage and deformation of coral 
larvae (planulae) (International Coral Reef Initiative, 2018). Additionally, inorganic filters such 
as TiO2 produce H2O2, a major oxidizing agent entering coastal waters with direct consequences 
to ecosystems, especially towards phytoplankton (Sánchez-Quiles & Tovar-Sánchez, 2014).  
Choice of sunscreen ingredients is therefore vital to ensure both human and environmental 
health. Globally, there is a trend towards the use of natural ingredients and clean labels, and 
consequently, the cosmetic industry is exploring the potential of utilizing natural ingredients in 
various products (Jacobsen et al., 2019). “Coral safe sunscreens” is already an established term, 
with almost 16 million hits on google search as of June 1st 2021 (Google, 2021), indicating a 
large market potential for sunscreens containing natural ingredients. Several studies have 
proposed the use of natural compounds in combination with synthetic agents as an effective 
UVR-protective strategy. Amongst the proposed components are flavonoids such as quercetin 
and rutin (polyphenolic compounds) found in various fruits, vegetables, tea and red wine, 
offering protection by suppressing UV-induced damage in keratinocytes (the primary cell found 
in the epidermis) (de la Coba et al., 2019; Park et al., 2012). Additionally, certain isoflavones 
like genistein isolated from soy beans have been found to suppress UVB induced skin erythema, 
as well as exhibiting anti-photocarcinogenic and anti-photoaging effects (Wei et al., 2003). 
Finally, marine seaweeds has shown to contain several bioactive compounds such as 
polyphenols, pigments and MAAs with high antioxidant activity and photoprotective 
properties, and has therefore been suggested as possible bioactive components in sunscreen (de 
la Coba et al., 2019).  
 
2.3.3 Determination of sun protection factor  
2.3.3.1 Traditional in vivo testing 
The term “sun protection factor” (SPF) is widely accepted as a measurement of sunscreen 
performance. It is defined as the solar radiation dose that is required to produce the minimum 
erythemal dose (MED). Erythema is a term that describes UV-induced reddening of the skin 
(Lautenschlager et al., 2007).  
Traditionally, the SPF of sunscreens are determined in vivo on human skin. In 1994, the 
European Cosmetic and Perfumery Association (COLIPA) developed and published an in vivo 
SPF test method, which became the blueprint of the currently used ISO 24444:2019 standard 
==PAGE:28==
Theoretical background 
 
18 
 
(International Organization for Standardization, 2019; Pissavini et al., 2018). This method 
utilizes the erythemal reaction of the human skin as a response to UVR. Sunburn occurs as an 
inflammatory response resulting in redness, as superficial blood vessels direct blood to the 
exposure site, and this colour alteration can be quantified (Bentham, 2021). 
The in vivo procedure subjects 10-20 volunteers with Fitzpatrick skin phototypes I, II and III to 
artificial solar irradiation through a solar simulator. The skin area exposed is typically the area 
between the waist and shoulders, and consist of unprotected skin, areas protected by the 
analysed sunscreen, and areas protected by a reference SPF-formulation. After 16-24 hours the 
erythemal effect is generated, and trained personnel will conduct a visual colour discrimination 
test (International Organization for Standardization, 2019). The SPF can then be determined 
using the following equation:

method does not quantify the protection towards UVA-radiation. The method is also expensive, 
time-consuming, normally subject to some variability, and poses some ethical concerns. As a 
result, the European Commission has encouraged the sunscreen industry to develop in vitro test 
methods to quantify sunscreens protection towards both UVA and UVB (European 
Commission, 2006).  
 
2.3.3.2 Transition towards in vitro testing 
In 2007, COLIPA developed a method that formed the basis of the ISO 24443:2012 in vitro 
UVA test method (International Organization for Standardization, 2012). The method 
introduced the term UVA protection factor (UVA-pf), which is calculated based on the action 
spectrum for PPD. For a product to be denoted as a broadband protector, the sunscreen must 
protect against a critical wavelength of 370 nm or higher, as well as a UVA-pf of at least one 
third of the labelled SPF (de la Coba et al., 2019; International Organization for Standardization, 
==PAGE:29==
Theoretical background 
 
19 
 
2012). The principle of the in vitro UVA-pf method is based on the assessment of UV-
transmittance through a thin film of sunscreen spread upon a skin analogue before and after 
exposure to a controlled dose of radiation from a defined source of UV-exposure.  
Considerable efforts have been made towards developing an in vitro spectroscopic method for 
SPF-determination, based upon the COLIPA UVA-pf method. The efforts have resulted in the 
development of various UVR-transparent skin analogues, irradiation protocols and automated 
spreading procedures. As of June 4th, 2021, no methods has yet been approved, but one (ISO/CD 
23657) is under ISO committee draft and is showing promising results (International 
Organization for Standardization, 2021). This protocol utilizes a skin analogue, 
polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) plates. The sunscreen is applied and spread across the 
PMMA plate using a robot, to ensure reproducible, standardized, and homogenous spreading. 
A spectrophotometer measures the initial absorbance (290-400nm), and the initial SPF-value is 
calculated based on the absorbance results. The initial SPF-value is used to calculate an 
appropriate irradiation dose, which is followingly applied using a solar simulator. The final 
SPF-value can then be calculated when the post-irradiation absorbance (290-400 nm) is 
measured with a spectrophotometer (Pissavini et al., 2020). 
 
2.3.3.3 Evaluation of photoprotective properties of natural extracts 
To evaluate the photoprotective capacity of natural extracts, as opposed to sunscreen 
formulations, Schneider et al., (2020) developed a method similar to the COLIPA UVA-pf and 
ISO/CD 23657 method described above. It utilizes PMMA plates and a solar simulator, and 
transmittance is recorded using a spectroradiometer.  
The Schneider method differs from the two in vitro methods in several aspects. As opposed to 
only utilizing a single action spectrum (i.e., erythemal or PPD) the method utilizes several 
different action spectra (vitamin D3 synthesis, erythema, immunosuppression, lipid 
peroxidation, elastosis, persistent pigment darkening and photoaging). This led to the 
introduction of the term “Effective solar absorption radiation” (%ESAR), indicating the extracts 
potential for absorbing different ranges of UV-wavelengths. Another new term introduced by 
the Schneider study is the extract photoprotective index (EPI). This is calculated analogous to 
SPF by detecting the radiation transmitted through the natural extracts, but differs from SPF as 
it uses several biological action spectra.

20 
 
Essentially, this allows for establishing not only the SPF and UVA-pf of natural extracts, but 
also quantifying the extracts’ ability to protect towards several biological responses associated 
with certain wavelengths. The method was applied to evaluate 4 different seaweed species 
(Carpodesmia tamariscifolia, Porphyra umbilicalis, Sargassum vulgare, and Ulva lactuca).  
The brown seaweed Sargassum vulgare the red seaweed Porphyra umbilicalis exhibited the 
most promising results. For instance, the %ESAR of Sargassum vulgare (at 16 mg dw extract

means that extracts containing S. vulgare at 16 mg dw cm-1 absorbs 90.25±3 % of the

photoaging-protection factor) is 15.

simplicity and slight improvement in accuracy should be evaluated. 
 
2.3.4 Natural sunscreens – huge potential or a recipe for sunburn? 
Many consumers are concerned about the role of sunscreen in disruption of human and 
environmental health. Such consumer concerns are harmonious with the societal shift towards 
natural and organic food, cosmetics, and various other products. As a result, there has been a 
proliferation of websites promoting natural oils/juices as a topical alternative to synthetic 
sunscreens. The recipes are typically built around shea butter as a moisturizing base, beeswax 
for increased water resistance, and some sort of natural oil/juice (typically oils of carrot, 
coconut, raspberry seed and rosehip seed) with a proclaimed photoprotective capacity (Merten 
et al., 2020). Some of these recipes claims to result in SPF up to 50 (Oomah et al., 2000). 
For example, online retailers of raspberry seed oil as a photoprotective oil (e.g., Escentials of 
Australia; Pacific Scents; Praan Naturals.)  refers to SPF-values of 28-50 reported in a study 
performed by Oomah et al., (2000). This study does not provide any details as to how these 
SPF-values were determined. Other commonly cited reports are Kaur & Saraf, (2010) and 
==PAGE:32==
Theoretical background 
 
22 
 
Suryawanshi, (2016). These two studies applied the Mansur method to determine the SPF value 
of various vegetable oils. Suryawanshi (2016) reported the SPF of wheat germ oil (SPF 22.40), 
carrot seed oil (SPF 18.80) and olive oil (SPF 9.3) whilst Kaur & Saraf, (2010) reported SPF 
values of coconut oil (SPF 8), castor oil (SPF 6) and almond oil (SPF 5). Contrastingly, other 
authors argue that most vegetable oils provide no or negligible SPF (Ácsová et al., 2021; 
Ranjithkumar et al., 2016).  
Uncritical publication and interpretation of such results can be very misleading and potentially 
harmful to consumers. Annex VI of the EU Cosmetic regulation (European Commision, 2020) 
lists 45 different UV-filters approved for cosmetic use, with none originating from vegetable 
oils. Although some vegetable oils do possess some UVB-blocking activity in vitro and in vivo, 
they do not possess adequate protection alone (Ácsová et al., 2021; Gause & Chauhan, 2016; 
Merten et al., 2020). 
A study performed by Ácsová et al., (2021) aimed to investigate the UVB-protective capacity 
of vegetable oils commonly promoted online, by comparing in vitro Mansur SPF results with 
in vivo SPF results of 14 different vegetable oils. The Mansur results revealed that all but 1 of 
the vegetable oils had a SPF value < 1 (results ranging from SPF 0.004 for coconut oil to SPF 
0.580 for shea butter). Contrastingly, the in vivo results were higher for all vegetable oils tested, 
ranging from SPF 1.2 for coconut oil to 4.5 for shea butter.  
Another aim for the Ácsová study was to investigate how studies such as the ones performed 
by Kaur & Saraf (2010) and Suryawanshi, (2016) found such high SPF of vegetable oils. The 
Ácsová study reported several methodological inconsistencies in the Kaur & Saraf, (2010) 
study. Instead of using petroleum ether as solvent, they used an aqueous-alcoholic solution 
(60:40). As oils does not dissolve in water, the final solution was unclear, which affects the 
spectrometric results. The study also used a dilution factor of 0.1 % instead of 0.02 %, 
overestimating the SPF-results by a five-fold. In the study performed by Suryawanshi, (2016) 
the concentration of oil was not listed, but due to the extremely high absorbance values (>3) for 
wheat germ oil and carrot oil in particular, it is likely that the oil was not diluted correctly.  
Kasitowati et al., (2021) applied the Mansur method to determine the SPF of the seaweed 
Euchea sp., and reported SPF of 19.65 and 26.45 for methanol and ethyl acetate extracts, 
respectively. There is no mention of diluting the extracts. Hence, assuming that the study 
analysed the extract (100 %) instead of diluting to the reference concentration of 0.02 %, this 
could mean a 5.000 fold increase and a huge overestimation of the determined SPF value.

5.1.1 Effect of growth depth 
Due to the penetration of UVR and PAR into the water column, marine organisms are faced 
with several challenges that may impact depth distribution along the vertical zonation pattern 
(Steinhoff, 2010). The effect of growth depth is therefore investigated.  
All extracts displayed presence of phenolic compounds and exert in vitro DPPH radical 
scavenging capacity and FIC ability. The results display no clear pattern in TPC and antioxidant 
activities along the depth gradient. For TPC and DPPH radical scavenging capacity, only group 
3 performed significantly lower (p<0.05) compared to the other groups for both FV-WE and 
FV-EE. The FIC ability was the lowest in group 2 for both FV-WE and FV-EE. These results 
therefore suggest that growth depth/solar exposure alone cannot explain the variation in 
phenolic content and in vitro antioxidant activity. 
The lack of significant differences  could possibly explained by the low variability in growth 
depths. Lann et al., (2012) evaluated the correlation between depth and TPC, and found that 
TPC content decreases with depth for Sargassum and Turbinaria. However, the depth zones in 
this study varied much more than the present study, with growth depths of samples from 0-1m; 
1-3m; and 3-6m, whilst the growth depths in the present study ranged from 15-70 cm. A study 
performed by  Torn et al., (2006) found that in the Danish belts, the average depth limit of F. 
vesiculosus were 150 cm (and in extreme cases in the Baltic sea, the depth limit exceeded 10 
meters). In the present study, there is also no data provided regarding the length of the 
specimens collected. F. vesiculosus can typically grow up to 90 cm long and is held vertically 
in the water column by air bladders, possibly decreasing the variation of solar exposure. Data 
on tidal fluxes also show that group 1 was emersed twice a day in the days leading up to 
harvesting (tidal fluxed from -0.23 m to +0.20 m on august 19th 2021), whist group 2-4 remained 
submerged at all times (tidal fluxed from -0.16 to +0.19m on September 6th 2021) (Danmarks 
Meteorologiske Institut, 2021). Acute physiological stresses that occurs as a result from high 
irradiance and dehydration during tidal emersion could influence the TPC and antioxidant levels 
(Connan et al., 2004). Although a negative correlation between temperature and phlorotannins 
has been established (Mannino et al., 2016), the temperature difference between 15-70 cm is 
not likely to be large enough to have an impact.   
The lack of significant differences could also be explained by the quality differences between 
the groups analysed. Group 1 was denoted as a low-quality seaweed (higher levels of biofouling 
and epiphytes). This sample was included in this study to investigate whether a low-grade 
sample, not suitable for human consumption, could be utilized in cosmeceutical purposes. 
==PAGE:47==
Results and discussion

However, this quality-grade did not significantly decrease the TPC or antioxidant activity, 
indicating that low-quality seaweed unsuitable for consumption could still be a valuable source 
of bioactive components.   
Group 3 was the only group that presented significantly different from the other groups, with 
significantly (p<0.05) lower TPC and radical scavenging capacity for both FV-WE and FV-EE. 
This could have been influenced by e.g., the age of the seaweed, or possibly release of 
phlorotannins as a response to stress conditions in the surroundings. As mentioned in section 
2.1.2, there are several abiotic and biotic factors influencing the biochemical composition of 
seaweed, even in adjacent populations. This makes quantification and determination of 
responses to single environmental stresses not only difficult, but also less useful.  To fully 
understand he biochemical and biological variability, all the biotic and abiotic factors must be 
taken into account. 
 
5.1.2 Total phenolic content 
The total phenolic content (TPC) ranged from 6.91±1.07 to 13.73±1.67 g GAE/100 g dw for 
FV-WE, and 7.82±0.37 to 13.95±0.58 g GAE/100 g dw for FV-EE (Table 10). For both FV-
EE and FV-WE, the highest TPC was found in group 2, whilst group 3 had significantly lower 
TPC.  
The TPC found in the present study is similar to levels reported in previous studies. Hermund 
et al., (2018) reported 13.4±0.4 and 16.5±0.4 g GAE/100 g dw for WE and EE of Danish F. 
vesiculosus, respectively. Honold et al., (2016), investigated the TPC of WE of old and young 
segments of F. vesiculosus, and found 12.6±2.1 and 6.9±0.3 g GAE/100 g dw, respectively. 
Honold et al., (2016) found considerably higher phenolic content (20.4±2.4 g GAE/100 g dw) 
in EE compared to our findings. The seaweeds analysed in this study were Icelandic seaweeds, 
and environmental differences could therefore have influenced the results. 
There was no significant difference between the TPC extracted in FV-WE and FV-EE. This is 
dissimilar to other findings, as Farvin & Jacobsen, (2013); Hermund et al., (2018); Honold et 
al., (2016); and Wang et al., (2012) found significantly higher levels of TPC in EE compared 
to WE. Phenolic compounds are increasingly soluble in less polar solvents, and ethanol and 
acetone is hence the recommended solvents for extraction of polyphenolic compounds (Farvin 
& Jacobsen, 2013; Honold et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2009, 2012)

5.1.3 Antioxidant properties 
The DPPH radical scavenging capacity ranged from EC50 µg dw/mL of 31.05±3.59 to 
76.31±2.46 in FV-WE and 15.53±2.42 to 30.92±0.76 in FV-EE. The FIC ability ranged from 
EC50 µg dw/mL 311.21±71.67 to 365.13±31.28 in FV-WE, and 625.28±37.97 to 
1052.42±43.50 in FV-EE (Table 11Table 11). The term EC50 describes the concentration of a 
substance that provides half of the maximal response of a biological or chemical pathway, i.e., 
a low EC50 signifies that a low concentration of extract is required to reach the maximal radical 
scavenging capacity or FIC ability, in this case.  
The Icrude (the extracts ability to inactivate the DPPH radical) ranged from 90.18±1.60 - 
95.14±2.14 % for both FV-WE and FV-EE. This shows that the extracts displays higher ability 
to scavenge and inactivate DPPH radicals compared to the positive control (Icrude 0.06 mM 
EDTA ≈ 50 %). Simultaneously, the Acrude (the extracts ability chelate and bind ferrous iron) 
ranged from 88.11±3.89 – 96.41±2.81 % for both FV-WE and FV-EE, making the extracts 
more efficient in chelating ferrous iron compared to the positive control (Acrude 0.2 mg/mL BHT  
≈ 70 %). 
FV-WE exhibited significantly lower DPPH radical scavenging capacity and significantly 
higher FIC ability compared to FV-EE. Between groups, only group 3 displayed a statistically 
significant difference from the other groups, except for the FIC ability of FV-EE, where none 
of the groups were statistically different (p>0.05).   
Farvin & Jacobsen (2013) reported levels of DPPH radical scavenging capacity of 8.3±0.8 and 
9.9±1.6 EC50 µg dw/mL for WE and EE of F. vesiculosus, respectively. This is higher 
compared to the results found in the present study. The FIC ability reported in the study by 
Farvin & Jacobsen, (2013) however, were similar to the findings in the present study, with 
EC50 µg dw/mL of 128.6±23.0 and 1000.0±75.0 for WE and EE, respectively.   
Similarly to Honold et al., (2016) and Wang et al., (2012), the present study also displays a 
strong correlation between the phenolic content and DPPH radical scavenging capacity, as

5.2.2 Phenolic composition and level 
The UHPLC-MS results for determination of polyphenolic compounds in the extracts were 
inconclusive. As can be seen below in Figure 14, the chromatogram revealed only one, wide 
split peak, which formed in its entirety within the first 1 minute.  
 
Figure 14: UHPLC-MS chromatogram for phenolic identification in FV-EE G1.  
The method was verified by analysing chocolate/cocoa bean extracts from another study (data 
not shown). Contrary to the results presented by seaweed extracts, the chocolate samples 
displayed several distinct peaks, and allowed for determination of phenolic compounds.  
Another attempt of determining phenolic compounds was performed by using HPLC, this time 
focusing on PA’s. Using HPLC, peak identification of phlorotannins is generally challenging 
due to the lack of phlorotannin standards (Melanson & Mackinnon, 2015). Several studies 
claims that PA’s are not present in brown seaweed, and that the content of polyphenolics are 
limited to phlorotannins (Jormalainen & Honkanen, 2004; Koivikko et al., 2007; Mahmoud et 
al., 2021). Contrastingly, Farvin & Jacobsen, (2013) did successfully detect and quantify 
several PA’s (gallic acid, protocatechuic, gentisic, chlorogenic, vanillic, caffeic) in both WE 
and EE of F. vesiculosus, with the two latter PA’s only present in WE. The HPLC results in the 
present study are inconclusive. Several peaks were formed (Figure 15) for both FV-EE and FV-
WE, demonstrating that polyphenolic compounds are present, with a larger variety present in 
FV-WE. As none of the peaks aligned with the external PA standards, along with the 
inconclusive UHPLC-MS result, characterization could not be performed. For future reference, 
the retention time of the various PA’s analysed are included in Appendix A3.

Several studies have pointed to the challenges of characterizing phlorotannins and polyphenolic

water-solubility, structure, and complex-formation (Koivikko et al., 2007). Over 1.200 
phlorotannins have been reported from brown seaweed (Melanson & Mackinnon, 2015). Many 
phlorotannins also displays very similar weight and structure, which further challenges 
separation of such complex isomeric mixtures (Heffernan et al., 2015). 
The Melanson & Mackinnon, (2015) study provides a detailed protocol for characterization of 
phlorotannins from brown seaweed using UHPLC operating in hydrophilic interaction liquid 
chromatography (HILIC) mode combined with high-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS). 
The extensive work performed in the Melanson & Mackinnon study could be advantageous for 
future assessments of phlorotannin characterization.

A possible explanation could be because they all contain a UVA-filter. As the Mansur method 
is based on erythemal effect and absorption is only measured in the range of 290-320 nm, it is 
likely that UVA-absorption is not included in this analysis. The Mansur-method was also 
developed well before the introduction of the COLIPA UVA-pf determination method. As 
mentioned, for a product to be denoted as a broadband protector, the UVA-pf/SPF ratio must 
be ≥1/3rd of the labelled SPF, suggesting that at least 1/3rd of the claimed SPF would not be 
incorporated by the Mansur-equation.  
To test this theory, the in vitro test report for the two broadband-protective sunscreens (B and 
C) were requested from the manufacturer. A Eurofins report was provided, which presented a 
detailed report of the SPF, UVA-pf, and ratio of UVA-pf/SPF. The report revealed that the 
UVA-pf of sunscreen B and C were 11 and 22, respectively. When the UVA-pf is added to the

In fact, sunscreen B was measured by Eurofins to have a combined SPF + UVA-pf of 27, which

sunscreen C displayed a combined SPF + UVA-pf of 43.25, which is in very close correlation

A similar test was performed by Ácsová et al., (2021). This study tested 6 commercial sun oils 
with a SPF of 6-30. Their results were in close correlation compared to the claimed SPF for all 
6 lotions. All but two only contained UVB-filters, but two also contained an UVA-filter (Bis-
ethylhexyloxyphenol) in low amounts (1-5 %). There was no mentioning whether these two 
sun oils were marked as broadband absorbers.  
Dutra et al. (2004) utilized the Mansur method to test 10 different commercially available 
sunscreens with claimed SPF-values ranging from 8-30. Out of the 10 sunscreens, 3 of them 
presented discrepancies that could not be explained by the presence of UVA-filters. The study 
states that the discrepancies could be caused by several factors, for example interaction of 
vehicle components (e.g. emulsifiers, esters), interaction between the human skin and other 
active ingredients (anti-inflammatory, antioxidants) and emulsion rheological properties, which 
in combination could decrease or increase the UV absorption of each sunscreen in vivo.  
When quantifying the SPF-value spectrophotometrically, it is also important to consider the 
chemical nature of the UV-filters present. UV-spectrophotometers uses a single UV-beam to 
measure the spectral transmittance of sunscreens. Thus, sunscreens that contains inorganic UV-
filters that mainly scatters/reflects light, as opposed to organic filters that absorbs light, are more 
difficult to measure due to the heterogenous spreading of light (Qu et al., 2016). Accordingly, 
the Mansur method is less suitable for analysing sunscreens with inorganic UV-filters present.   
The absorption spectra of sunscreen A, B and C were obtained spectrophotometrically (Figure 
19). The spectrum reveals two prominent peaks for all three sunscreens at around 315 nm and 
355 nm. The absorption spectra were compared to the absorption spectra of the organic UV-
filters present in the sunscreens. The spectra for the UV-filters present in the sunscreens was 
sourced in literature (Osterwalder & Herzog, 2010).

the same as the Osterwalder analysis, where pure chemicals are tested.  
The benefits of the Mansur method are that it utilizes low-cost solvents, is fast, and only requires 
conventional laboratory spectrophotometers. The Mansur method can therefore be useful as a 
“rough prediction” during product development, particularly for products containing only 
UVB-filters. If UVA-filters are present, the application of the COLIPA UVA-pf method is 
necessary. For accurate SPF-determination, the photoprotective properties must be confirmed 
in an accredited laboratory through the ISO 24444:2019 in vivo method (Ácsová et al., 2021). 
5.3.3 SPF screening of seaweed-enriched lotion  
Seaweed-enriched lotions (SWE-L) were prepared by combining freeze-dried seaweed extract 
at different concentrations (10-28.2 %) in cream base with two different concentrations of 
freeze-dried FV-WE (10 and 20 %) and FV-EE (14.1 and 28.2 %). Typically, UV-filters are 
also added in this range (4-25%) (Osterwalder & Herzog, 2010). Both FV-WE and FV-EE-
enriched lotions were visibly coloured – FV-WE was distinctly brown, whilst FV-EE had a 
brown-green tint (Figure 20).

It is assumed that the in vivo results could be slightly higher. This was also highlighted in the

against erythema in other means than just absorbing UV-rays. The Ácsová study explains the 
in vitro/in vivo discrepancy due to the presence of accompanying substances in the oil (e.g. 
tocopherols, carotenoids, phytosterols) that exerts anti-inflammatory, antioxidant and anti-
erythemal properties, essentially protecting towards reddening of the skin in other ways than 
solely absorbing UV-rays.   
Although this suggest that the in vivo results could be slightly higher, the gap between the found 
and desired results are substantial. After much consideration, the most likely explanation to the 
low SPF is the fact that the inorganic and organic filters used in commercial sunscreens are pure 
chemicals, i.e., each molecule is specifically designed or chosen to absorb/reflect light at a 
specific wavelength. Contrastingly, in seaweed extracts, a ballpark value of 1:10.000 chemicals 
absorb UV-light, whilst the remainder is other chemicals/compounds/solvent. Therefore, the 
presence of UV-absorbing chemicals is simply too low to have any significant effect (Figure

Despite the poor results, several lessons can be learnt. The SPF value of the FV-WE in particular 
increased when the filtration step was omitted. This indicates that the photoprotective 
components extracted with water as solvents are either large in size, or attached in complexes 
(e.g., phlorotannin-alginate acid complexes).  
Additionally, the traditional SLE-extraction method co-extracts certain components that makes 
the rheological properties of the cream with FV-WE relatively sticky and unpleasant. Steps 
must therefore be taken to identify and remove these components (most likely polysaccharides) 
before incorporation in cosmeceutical products. The brown coloration of the lotion is also far 
from the traditional white/faint yellow colour of sunscreens that consumers are familiar with.  
Finally, absorption spectra of the SWE-L (not shown) also displayed some absorption in the 
UVA-range, indicating the presence of UVA-absorbing compounds. As the UVA-pf is not 
measured through the Mansur-method, the photoprotective capacity is expected to be somewhat 
higher than what is presented in Table 14. Additionally, if the theory presented by Ácsová et 
al., (2021) regarding the in vitro/in vivo discrepancy is correct, it is also possible that the in vivo 
results could be somewhat higher, due to other properties than simply UV-absorbance.

6. Conclusion and future prospects 
6.1 Conclusion 
The use of seaweed as a potential source of bioactive components is appealing to many 
researchers. This main objective of this study was to evaluate the capabilities of phlorotannin-
rich F. vesiculosus extracts of exerting antioxidant- and photoprotective properties in 
cosmeceutical formulations.  
It was hypothesized that the growth depth of F. vesiculosus would influence the antioxidant and 
especially the photoprotective properties due to increased levels of solar exposure. The growth 
depth of the seaweeds did not significantly influence the TPC or antioxidant activity, nor the 
photoprotective capacity of FV-WE or FV-EE. The most likely explanations to this are the low 
variation in harvesting depth, as well as the multitude of biotic and abiotic factors influencing 
the biochemical properties of seaweed.  
It was confirmed that SLE successfully extracted polyphenolic components, with both water 
and 67 % ethanol as solvents. The type of solvent did not significantly impact the TPC levels 
of the F. vesiculosus extracts, but FV-EE was more efficient in extracting components exerting 
radical scavenging capacity, whilst the FIC ability was higher in the FV-WE. The TPC levels 
correlated with DPPH and not FIC, indicating that the degree of FIC ability is influenced by 
other co-extracted components, and not only phenolics. FV-EE also co-extracted a larger 
variety of pigments, whilst a higher variety of phenolic compounds was found in FV-WE. 
Fucoxanthin was quantified in low levels in FV-EE, whilst the characterization of phenolic 
compounds was inconclusive. It can be concluded that F. vesiculosus is a rich source of natural 
antioxidants in vitro, and should therefore be further evaluated as in vivo topical antioxidants 
for cosmeceutical purposes.  
Finally, it was hypothesized that F. vesiculosus extracts provides photoprotective capacity and 
can be utilized as an ingredient in combination with synthetic filters to increase the SPF of 
sunscreens. The results show that the photoprotective capacities presented by FV-WE and FV-
EE in cream base are negligible. The most likely explanation is that the photoprotective 
components are too few, and that the distance between them in the cream will therefore be too 
large. It can therefore be concluded that employing SLE of F. vesiculosus is insufficient in 
yielding an extract with any substantial SPF, without additional fractioning or purification.

6.2 Future prospects 
The combined societal shift towards clean labels and natural products, along with evidence of 
in vitro antioxidant activity of F. vesiculosus extracts, presents several exciting opportunities. 
Several authors have suggested that components from brown macroalgae possess anti-aging, 
anti-inflammatory, anti-bacterial and photoprotective properties. Further research should be 
aimed at closing the gap between in vitro and in vivo systems, ensuring the safety (i.e., potential 
allergens, toxicity) and unveiling the true potential of topical application of F. vesiculosus 
extracts.  
The present study found that although SWE-L possess photoprotective capacity in vitro, it is 
unlikely that the extra effort of harvesting, pre-treatment, extraction, and post-treatment is 
worthwhile, only to increase the SPF of sunscreens with + SPF 1. Research efforts should be 
directed at characterizing the specific photoprotective components present, as well as methods 
for fractionation and purification of these compounds, which could increase both the 
photoprotective and antioxidant capacity. This can be highlighted by the findings of Lawrence 
et al., (2018), who reported that cream base enriched with 10 % of the MAA palythine from red 
seaweed exhibited a combined SPF and UVA-pf of almost 19. 
Another prospect that needs to be addressed, is storage conditions of SWE-L. Bacterial and 
mould-formation presents a challenge for the durability and quality of finished formulations. 
Therefore, uncovering efficient sterilization methods is also addressed by the overall SEATAN 
project, of which this master thesis is a part. Proposed methods are UV-filtration and 
autoclaving, as heat-treatment (100°C for 30 minutes) has proven insufficient.
