Summary
This Master thesis examines the impact of developing an Advanced Planning and Schedul-
ing (APS) software for improving the demand planning and customer service and satis-
faction in a company that manufactures and supplies medical devices for the ear and
hearing care field. The study motivation sprang from the supply chain uncertainties, that
can stem from various factors, including geopolitical tensions, economic fluctuations, and
now, trade tensions and with the ongoing pandemic, disruptions caused by COVID-19,
e.g., raw material and labour shortages.
Addressing these uncertainties requires proactive risk management strategies, in-
cluding diversification of suppliers, enhancing visibility across the supply chain, investing
in digital technologies for planning, and fostering collaboration and agility within supply
chain networks. Compliance with such strategies can entail the improvement of lead time,
rescheduling, or optimisation processes. Due to this wide range of strategies, this thesis
assesses digital solutions, a planning software, based on the three levels of supply chain
management for developing a plan for a company based on the time horizon: strategic
level (long-term), tactical level (mid-term) and operational level (short-term). In order
to encapsulate the lead time and tardiness effectiveness in relation to the purchase orders
planning.
From a demand planning perspective, the most prominent methods of forecasting
are the moving average (MA), a no trend or seasonality approach, and the exponential
smoothing (single, double and triple), an approach that assigns larger weights to more
recent observations while assigning exponentially decreasing weights as the observations
get increasingly distant. At first, the study evaluates the prospects of two products of
the company to forecast the demand by analysing the sales performance of each product.
The results show that market and demand conditions influence the overall effectiveness
of a forecast method and that the attained adaptations, although significant, are insuf-
ficient to reach similar levels of accuracy for both products. Moreover, considering the
adaptation of the demand type for both products into trend and seasonality demand,
the thesis examines the adaptation for triple exponential smoothing (TES). The outputs
reveal that replacing the demand type with trend and seasonality behaviour, the TES
method becomes the best error-effectiveness method, therefore the best forecast method.
This method would result in better demand and planning understanding, i.e., lead time
and customer service improvement.

technological changes, this thesis evaluates the improvement and resilience of the soft-

Preface
This thesis was carried out in the Department of Technology, Management of the Eco-
nomics at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) as a partial fulfilment of the
requirements for acquiring the Master of Science (MSc) in Transport and Logistics.
The project has been conducted between September 2023 and February 2024 under
the supervision of Associate Professor Emeritus Peter Jacobsen.
This thesis consists of three parts:
The first part introduces the domain of supply chain management and planning, and
integrates their scope within the advanced planning and scheduling (APS) software’s.
Different types of APS software’s are identified and presented in particular in relation
their inclusion in demand planning and scheduling solutions.
The second part investigates the ability of forecasting methods to induce demand
impacts by innovating and adapting the product market trends. Due to supply chain
complex nature, company’s supply chain characteristics are described in terms of supply,
manufacturer and distributor views.
The third part focuses on the impacts of an Advanced Planning and Scheduling
(APS) software on company’s operational, tactical and strategical levels and explores its
implementation with scenarios on usual supply chain practices such as the increase of the
demand of an specific order and a global disruption of demand. These short- and mid-
term measures deployed as a response capability (resilience) of the APS software have the
potential to achieve the minimum maximum lead time and tardiness promptly, that are
currently considered as main issues for an optimum customer service satisfaction for the
company.

Abstract
Purpose: Supply chain environment has undergone tremendous change in re-
cent decades due to technology, and this shift is intensifying.
One of the main
concerns of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) is how to cost-effectively in-
tegrate, implement, and manage technologies across the supply chain of a company.
The purpose of this paper is to explore how the implementation of an Advanced
Planning and Scheduling (APS) software can be used for solving order planning
problems at tactical, strategic and operational levels, and to identify the perceived
effects of using APS in a ear and hearing care company. The APS software addresses
the scheduling problems with unrelated parallel manufacturers, batching, and or-
der sequence with the objective to minimise the lead time and minimise tardiness
criteria.
Design/methodology/approach: Developing and implementing APS soft-
ware for a strategic network planning and master production scheduling are con-
ducted. Comparative analysis explores how the planning situation, the model design
and use of the APS software impact the perceived planning effects.
Findings: Four Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) models are formu-
lated in order to obtain optimal solutions based on different modelling ideas. The
first objective function tries to minimise the lead time, the second one tries to min-
imise the tardiness, third and four models represents different scenarios. Numerical
experiments show that MILP models dependent on diverse modelling ideas perform
very differently.
Research limitation/implications: Tailored case for an ear and hearing care
company is studied.
Practical implications: The findings present experiences of APS software
usage and identify issues to consider when using APS software and potential benefits
to gain from usage. APS software becomes a tool for decision-makers and company
to enhance company’s performance and customer service level.
Originality/value: The practical use of APS software is low and the knowledge
about how it impacts supply chain planning and performance is unexplored. The
findings of this paper fill some of these gaps.
Keywords: Pharmaceutical industry, Supply Chain Planning, Industry 4.0,
Pharmaceutical Supply Chain, Systems software.
Paper type: Case study

Introduction
The global medical devices industry is expansive and continuously growing, driven by
various factors such as the aging population, increasing prevalence of chronic diseases,
technological advancements, and rising healthcare expenditure. Market research reports
indicate significant growth projections for the industry in the coming years [60]. Regula-
tory requirements for medical devices vary by country and region, with stringent standards
in place to ensure safety, efficacy, and quality. Regulatory bodies such as the U.S. Food
and Drug Administration (FDA), and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) oversee
the approval and marketing of medical devices. The medical devices industry in Europe
is subject to stringent regulatory requirements governed by the European Union Medical
Device Regulation (MDR). Compliance with regulatory standards is essential for market
access, driving companies to invest in quality management systems, clinical trials, and
regulatory affairs expertise [1].
Medical devices designed for earwax management play a crucial role in maintaining
ear health and addressing common issues such as cerumen impaction. In Europe, where
approximately 2-6% of the general population experience impacted earwax at any given
time [27], these devices are integral components of ear care practices, offering solutions
for the prevention, removal, and treatment of earwax buildup. Ear irrigation systems,
ear drops, ear sprays, and vacuum devices are among the medical devices commonly used
for earwax management. These devices are readily available in Europe through various
distribution channels, including medical supply companies, pharmacies, and online retail-
ers. They are regulated according to European Union (EU) directives and standards for
medical devices to ensure their safety and efficacy.
The increasing demand for medical devices related to earwax management in Europe
reflects several factors shaping the healthcare landscape in the region. One significant
factor is the ageing population, as older adults are more prone to earwax buildup due to
changes in ear canal physiology and increased use of hearing aids. With the proportion of
elderly individuals in Europe projected to rise steadily in the coming years, the demand
for earwax management devices is expected to increase accordingly [3].
Furthermore, growing awareness of the importance of ear health and preventive care
contributes to heightened demand for earwax management devices. Public health cam-
paigns and educational initiatives emphasising the significance of regular ear care and
the potential consequences of untreated earwax buildup encourage individuals to seek
appropriate solutions for managing their ear health. Moreover, the COVID-19 pandemic
has influenced healthcare-seeking behaviours, leading to a greater emphasis on remote

1.1
Problem statement
In this project, a company with a multi-product and multi-plant supply chain with mani-
fold supply and distribution channels is considered for analysis. As illustrated in figure 1,
the supply chain consists of two major subsystems: a production subsystem and a distri-
bution subsystem. The production subsystem includes raw material suppliers, component
fabricators and manufacturers (CMO). As discussed in the previous section, raw material
suppliers and component fabricators are out of scope, the problem starts when the fin-
ished products arrive at the manufacturer. The distribution subsystem consists of three
levels of product channel points: distribution companies (DC), retailers and end-user cus-
tomers. The distributor flow is not considered since the company is not the responsible
of the distribution of its products into the market. Therefore, the key stage and the one
that the planning software is focused on is the manufacturer process. For this reason, the
structure of the manufacturer plants is analysed in order to obtain a better understand-
ing to develop and implement the planning software based on their manufacturing process.
The manufacturer plants, which operate in a make-to-order (MTO) environment,
classify the specific order requisitions made by downstream distributors into families of
product modules and commit resources to satisfy the demand within the specified due-
dates. Taking the volume of orders in the queue and the capacity limitations into account.
Each order requires different components and/or sub-assembled units, which have to be
purchased from external sources (suppliers).
In general, make-to-order supply chains
operate as a pull system driven by customer orders at the downstream end of the network.
Customer pick their preferred product styles from retailers’ catalogues and sign order
requisitions. Retail dealers are the market outlets from which the final products are sold
to customers and new orders are passed to distributors. Distributors pass orders from
retail dealers to the manufacturer plants ahead of the projected production and delivery
lead time. An efficient communication channel between distributors and manufacturers is
therefore a critical factor to ensure product availability and raise customer service level.
The distribution companies are middle agents that collect the final products from the
manufacturer plants and distribute them to the respective retailers.
Each distributor
may accumulate some inventories of final products in order to decouple the unequal flow
rate of incoming and outgoing finished products.

Level I identifies the important supply chain
processes: plan, source, make, deliver and return. The Plan process consists of processes
that balance aggregated demand and supply to develop a course of action which best meets
the business goals. Plan processes deal with demand/supply planning, which include the
activities to assess supply resources, aggregate and prioritise demand requirements, plan
inventory, distribution requirements, production, material, and rough-cut capacity for all
products and all channels. The Source process contains processes that procure goods and
services to meet planned or actual demand. Sourcing/material acquisition includes the
jobs of obtaining, receiving, inspecting, holding, and issuing material. The Make process
includes functions that transform goods to a finished state to meet planned or actual
demand. Make is the core process of the system in which actual production execution
takes place. It includes the orders of requesting and receiving material, manufacturing,
packaging, holding and/or releasing the product eventually. The Deliver process consists
of processes that provide finished goods and services to meet planned or actual demand.
This includes the functions of order management, and distribution management. Return
is the latest addition to SCOR model. It deals with managing reverse flow of material and
information related to defective or surplus products. Level II explains the main process
categories that exist in real and created supply chain.
For instance, the source part
has “source make-to-order products”, make part has “make-to-order”, and deliver part
has “deliver make-to-order products”. Level III is the process element level and is the
lowest level in the scope of the SCOR model, the elements model contains performance
attributes, metrics, best practices and software features required for that element.

2.1
Supply Chain Management (SCM)
The connections and nodes in a Supply Chain achieve functions that contribute to the
value of the goods transporting through the chain and thus its achievement. Any con-
nection that does not carry out well reduces the overall effectiveness of the whole Supply
Chain. [20]
Customers are demanding products consistently delivered faster, exactly on time,
and with no damage. Each of these necessitates closer coordination with suppliers and
distributors. This global orientation and increased performance-based competition, com-
bined with rapidly changing technology and economic conditions, all contribute to market-
place uncertainty. This uncertainty requires greater flexibility on the part of individual
companies and supply chains, which in turn demands more flexibility in supply chain
relationships. [21]
To get benefits, all partners should sign all necessary arrangements for collaborative
practices; do according to agreements and work to achieve the required supply chain
benchmarks. It consists different areas like purchasing, production, inventory, logistic
and organisational relationships followed by performance measures. [22]
Supply chains consist of all the steps involved in getting a product from a raw mate-
rial into the hands of the customer. Typically, the supply chain begins with the vendors or
suppliers. These are the businesses that provide raw materials. Next in the supply chain
is manufacturing. This is the process of converting the raw materials into products that
are ready to sell. The final step is distribution which can involve multiple different in-
termediaries. Some of these middle-men could be wholesalers, retailers, distributors, and
even the internet [26]. Some organisations simplify their management by dividing the
supply chain in two parts: upstream and downstream supply chain [25]. The upstream
supply chain refers to the flow of inventory in the pre-production stage, mostly in relation
to the organisation’s suppliers. It involves all the activities related to the sourcing and
transportation of raw materials needed in the manufacturing process. Meanwhile, the
downstream supply chain involves activities post the manufacturing process, which would
mean getting the finished goods to the end consumer. [24]
Key terms in Supply Chain are defined following the Council of Supply Chain Man-
agement Professionals definitions [23]:

Supply Chain Planning (SCP)
Supply chain planning has in recent years developed to be supported by optimisation
and simulation tools, especially concerning “higher” planning levels. The general trade-
off in planning is between service, costs, capital expenditure and working capital. Cost
minimisation and profit maximisation are the two most common ways to control the
solution. [6]
Page 13
==PAGE:23==

Since supply chain management is driven by demand, the starting point of planning
are available and planned customer orders. The longer the planning horizon the greater
the portion of forecasted demand. The step from pure demand forecasting to demand
planning is made by adding to the formal demand forecast those exceptional influences
expected to happen in the future and their impact on sales. So far sophisticated models
are very rare in demand planning. According to the length of the planning horizon and
the importance of the decisions to be made, planning tasks are usually classified into three
different planning levels:
1. Long-term planning: Decisions of this level are called strategic decisions and should
create the prerequisites for the development of an enterprise/supply chain in the
future. They typically concern the design and structure of a supply chain and have
long-term effects, noticeable over several years.
2. Mid-term planning: Within the scope of the strategic decisions, mid-term planning
determines an outline of the regular operations, in particular rough quantities and
times for the flows and resources in the given supply chain. The planning horizon
ranges from 6 to 24 months, enabling the consideration of seasonal developments,
e.g. of demand.
3. Short-term planning: The lowest planning level has to specify all activities as de-
tailed instructions for immediate execution and control. Therefore, short-term plan-
ning models require the highest degree of detail and accuracy. The planning horizon
is between a few days and 3 months. Short-term planning is restricted by the deci-
sions on structure and quantitative scope from the upper levels. Nevertheless, it is
an important factor for the actual performance of the supply chain, e.g. concerning
lead-times, delays, customer service and other strategic issues.
Nearly always, reality will deviate from the plan. The deviation has to be controlled and
the plan has to be revised if the discrepancy is too large. Planning on a rolling horizon
basis is an implementation of this plan-control-revision interaction. The planning horizon
(e.g. 1 year) is divided into periods (e.g. months). At the beginning of January a plan
is made that covers January to December. But only the first period, the so-called frozen
period, is actually put into practice. At the beginning of the second period (February) a
new plan is made considering the actual developments during the first period and updated
forecasts for the future periods. The new planning horizon overlaps with the previous one,
but reaches one period further (until the end of January of the next year; see Fig. 6) and
so on. [10]

2.3
Planning System
The Planning System function is responsible for the coordination of activities along the
supply chain, by making decisions on the quantities and timing of material and resource
releases. In reality a planning concept does not only generate immediate release decisions,
but also provides information on future release decisions. These future release decisions
are provisional because they will be affected by future unknown events. In particular the
future demand is unknown. To cope with this the forecast future demand is a must. [48]
Some key features of supply chains that naturally lead to hierarchical structures:
• Products: Supply chains contain thousands of SKUs (stock keeping unit) and deci-
sions may be required, for example, at the individual SKU level in inventory control,
at a product family level in Master Production Scheduling, or across all SKUs in
aggregate capacity planning. [50]
• Suppliers: Supply chains involve the supply of goods from a range of suppliers that
may be as near as next door to their clients or as far away as the other side of the
world. Similar geographical dispersion applies to distribution centres. Emphasis
on specific suppliers may relate to the development of collaboration strategies and
demand information sharing; or companies may be interested in a group of suppliers,
that are geographically located close to each other, as part of a lead time reduction
strategy from a specific part of the world. [50]
• Customers: Supply chains involve servicing a large number of industrial customers
or final consumers that, further, will also be typically geographically dispersed.
Companies will often be interested in the needs of a specific (big) customer in terms
of prioritisation of activities, or in a group of customers that are geographically

clustered together for transportation purposes, or in all customers for aggregate
sales planning. [50]
• Locations: Suppliers and customers have in common that they may be geograph-
ically dispersed. In addition, a company that is forecasting for its supply chain
will itself often have multiple locations to plan for, e.g., different factories and/or
distribution centres. [50]
In addition to the hierarchical effects discussed above, time may also lead to hierarchical
structures in supply chains for the following reasons:
• Forecast horizons: The forecast horizons involved in supply chain problems are very
different. Inventory control, for example, necessitates forecasts over a lead time (or
a lead time plus the review period, for periodic inventory control applications) (or in
the presence of logistical constraints or complex ordering cost structures, which may
offer opportunities for cost reductions by batching orders, forecasts may be needed
for horizons longer than the lead time) whereas annual sales planning necessitates
forecasts over a financial year. [50]
• History of the data: Despite the recent advancements in IT, companies do not nec-
essarily store long demand histories (and, depending on the circumstances, long
histories may become obsolete quickly). Aggregation of demand data across (simi-
lar) SKUs may help to identify seasonal patterns, or other components that cannot
be “seen” at the individual SKU level due to the shortness of data. [50]
Arguably, judgement and forecasting are fundamentally inseparable.

Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS)
During the last decade, the use of APS software for design, integration and control of
supply chain processes has increased. Especially the interest among industrial companies
has increased, some have invested in the software, but only few uses it in practice on
strategic and tactical planning levels. There are also few documented cases showing how
standardised APS software are used, especially concerning the tactical planning level
which is the focus of this project.
Many companies that act on both domestic and global markets experience a growing
international competition and recognise the need for supply chain efficiency. This need
stems from increasing customer demands for high-quality products at a low price plush
higher expectations of accurate deliveries and customer service. APS software can be used
as a tool to meet the ever-increasing demands for effectiveness that put new pressures on
swift and efficient planning and control of the supply chain. APS software as a decision
support system for production and distribution planning is still a new and unexplored
tool. [6]
It is well-known that the strength of transactional enterprise resource planning (ERP)
systems is not in the area of planning. Hence, APS software have been developed to fill
this gap. [8]
APS software aims at taking into account the finite nature of resource capacity and
other constraints in order to provide reliable planning. [6] APS software tries to automate
and computerise the planning through simulation and optimisation. Still the decision-
making is done by planners, who have insight into the particular supply chain, know
about the software constraints and also have a feeling about feasibility in the plans that
are created. Planners also do the modelling and decisions regarding use of input to the
model. APS software is, thus, based on a system and process approach and try to bridge
the gap between the supply chain complexity and the day-to-day operative decisions.
Advanced Planning and Scheduling software can be defined and explained through
different perspectives but commonly APS software is viewed as an extension of ERP.
Many of the cases reported in the literature are in-house developed software based on
optimisation solver-engines, and not standard APS software.
While being aware of the broad area covered by supply chain management (SCM),
the research in this paper focuses on Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS) software,
the recent software development that supports planning of the supply chain. Unlike tra-
ditional enterprise resource planning systems (ERP), APS software tries to find feasible,

4.1.1
Bullwhip effect
The supply chain bullwhip effect is “the phenomenon where orders to the supplier tend to
have larger variance than sales to the buyer (i.e., demand distortion), and the distortion
propagates upstream in an amplified form (i.e., variance amplification).”[45] Distorted
information from one end of a supply chain to the other can lead to tremendous ineffi-
ciencies: excessive inventory investment, poor customer service, lost revenues, misguided
capacity plans, ineffective transportation, and missed production schedules. The ordering
patterns share a common, recurring theme: the variabilities of an upstream site are always
greater than those of the downstream site. Three major causes of the bullwhip effect have
been identified:
• Demand forecast updating: Forecasting is often based on the order history from the
company’s immediate customers. Moreover, customers are recommended to send
and update the forecast monthly.
• Order batching: Often batches or accumulates demands before issuing an order to
join as many orders as possible to manufacture bigger batches or number of units
and obtain a better financial situation.
• Rationing and shortage gaming: When product demand exceeds supply, the man-
ufacturer often rations its products to customers. The effect of “gaming” is that
customers’ orders give the supplier little information on the product’s real demand,
e.g., seasonal products.

Functional attributes
Sales Type. It is quite often that the standard products are packaged under several

site. Orders of the manufacturer’s customers, wholesalers, are delivered directly from the
factory to the respective wholesalers’ unloading point (warehouse).
Production Type. Production of medical devices comprises two production stages,
formulation and packaging. On each production stage one production line is organised
in a flow shop. A line execute various operations. The capacity of the production line is
limited , therefore, it represents potential bottleneck. A short-term expansion of working

4.3
Advanced Planning
In make-to-order manufacturing the performance of production planning and scheduling
is evaluated by customer satisfaction and production costs. A typical measure of the
customer satisfaction is customer service level, i.e., the fraction of customer orders filled
on or before their due dates. On the other hand, to achieve low unit production cost,
Page 45
==PAGE:55==
Implementation of an APS software at an ear and hearing care company
both the input inventory of purchased materials waiting for processing in the system and
the output inventory of finished products waiting for delivery to the customers should be
minimised.
A make-to-order manufacturing environment is dynamic in nature and the customers
may modify, cancel or add orders during the planning horizon. As a result, a predeter-
mined production schedule may become inefficient and may need to be revised in reaction
to unexpected changes of customer orders. In practice, reactive scheduling algorithms are
applied for a dynamic rescheduling.
The purpose of this project is to present new algorithms, based on integer program-
ming formulations, for reactive scheduling in a dynamic, make-to-order manufacturing,
where customers may modify or cancel their orders or place new orders during the plan-
ning horizon. In the algorithms, different rescheduling policies are proposed, from a total
reschedule of all remaining and unmodified customer orders to a non-reschedule of all such
orders. The scheduling objective is to dynamically assign/reassign customer orders with
various due dates to planning periods with limited processing capacities, to minimise the
number of tardy orders and the total input and output inventory over a planning horizon.
4.3.1
Computational optimisation
An optimisation problem is determining the minimum or maximum of a function f(x),
in which the domain of x forms a set of alternative values. The function f is called the
objective function of this problem. The problem becomes more difficult when f is a func-
tion of more variables, like f(x1, . . . , xn). Now the goal is to find permitted values for the
variables x1 to xn such that f is maximised or minimised. The variables x1, . . . , xn are
called decision variables.
The values of decision variables can be restricted by constraints. An example of a
constraint is that the sum of the decision variables x1 and x2 has to be equal to one.
The set of feasible solutions of an optimisation problem is the set that contains all com-
binations of the values in the domains of the decision variables that obey all constraints.
Solving an optimisation problem is finding a feasible solution that provides the maximum
or minimum objective function value. Such a solution is called an optimal solution.
A linear programming (LP) is a problem that has a linear objective function, subject
to linear equality and/or linear inequality constraints. The decision variant is defined as
follows:
Instance: A ∈Rmn, b ∈Rm, c ∈Rn and a constant o.

Any linear program can be solved in polynomial time. Hence, the class of LP problems
is an element of the complexity class P. If the decision variables are all required to be
integers, then the problem is called an integer program (IP). The special case that the
objective function and all constraints are linear is called an integer linear program (ILP).
Often the word linear is omitted. A mixed integer linear program (MILP) is a linear
program with a set of linear constraints and a linear objective function, but at least one
of its decision variables is required to be integer. [53]
A Mixed Integer Linear Program (MILP) is a mathematical optimisation problem
that involves linear functions with some or all variables restricted to be integers [54]. In
MILP, the objective is to minimise or maximise a linear objective function subject to
a set of linear equality and inequality constraints, where some of the decision variables
are required to take integer values [55].
The combination of integer and continuous
variables makes MILP a powerful tool for modelling and solving a wide range of real-
world optimisation problems.
• Objective Function: MILP aims to optimise a linear objective function, which can
either be to maximise or minimise a certain quantity [56]. The objective function
is typically expressed as a linear combination of decision variables.
• Decision Variables: These are the variables that the optimisation problem seeks to
determine. In a MILP, some or all of these variables are constrained to take integer
values, while others may be continuous [57]. Decision variables represent quantities
that the optimisation process can control or determine.
• Constraints: MILP involves constraints that restrict the values that decision vari-
ables can take. These constraints are expressed as linear equations or inequalities
involving the decision variables [58]. Constraints can represent limitations on re-
sources, capacity, or other relevant factors in the problem being modelled.
MILP enables the systematic exploration of feasible solutions to planning and scheduling
problems [55]. By leveraging advanced optimisation algorithms and solvers, MILP effi-
ciently searches the solution space to identify the most optimal schedule that meets project
objectives. This exploration process considers various scheduling scenarios, resource al-
locations, and task sequences to identify the schedule that minimises costs, maximises
resource utilisation, or meets project deadlines.
One of the primary roles of MILP in planning and scheduling is its ability to system-
atically formulate complex scheduling problems into mathematical optimisation models
[54]. By representing tasks, resources, and constraints as decision variables and linear
equations, MILP provides a structured framework for defining the problem’s objectives

and constraints. This systematic approach ensures that all relevant factors, including task
dependencies, resource constraints, and project objectives, are appropriately considered

4.3.2
Formulation of the model and assumptions
A complete APS software includes strategic planning, demand planning, master planning
and factory planning. [52] Splitting the planning horizon into two as before and after the
delivery lead time, demand planning takes two levels: (1) short-term demand: equal to
the accumulated customers’ orders, and (2) long-term demand: equal to the forecasted
value as determined by Triple Exponential Smoothing method (described in section 4.1.6).
Therefore, for the case of MTO supply chains, it is reasonable to consider the demand
level for final products to be deterministic.
Since the demand in the far future is more uncertain than the near future, a series of
short-term plans are extracted from the long-term production plan sequentially by taking
the accumulated orders into account. A plan is drawn up for all periods in the horizon,
but only the first few periods that fall within the current delivery lead time will actually
be implemented. In general, the entire production–distribution plan operates in a rolling
horizon to allow changes in later periods as new plans are constructed.
In order to simplify the complexity of the environment, the investigation will be
restricted with the following assumptions to provide a comprehensive overview of the key
assumptions made during the development of the program model. These assumptions
encompass various aspects of the modelling process, including the behaviour of tasks,
system dynamics, external influences, and data quality. By identifying and documenting
these assumptions, this section aims to enhance transparency, rigour, and reproducibility
in the modelling process.
• All manufacturers are available at the beginning.
• Each manufacturer can produce one order at the same time.
• The plant is make-to-order (MTO), the demand rate for the material is different in
different periods.
• Full availability of employees to carry out each operation
• Transportation times between operations and machine setup times are negligible.
• There are no emergency stops or maintenance needs throughout the process.
• All orders are available at the start of the model.

In addition to the lead time, the objective Cmax aims at completing the production order
as fast as possible and completing them fast in average, respectively. The total completion
time represents average flow time and is also used as an implicit measure of work in process.
Furthermore, where due dates Do for completing of some orders o ∈O are given, then min-

minimise the maximum and the average delay, respectively, of part completion times with
respect to given due dates.

completion time constraints (eq. 21) should be replaced with the maximum tardiness
constraints to ensure that for each tardy order o, its tardiness (cmo −Do) cannot exceed
the maximum tardiness Tmax to be minimised:

Recommendations
Advanced Planning and Scheduling (APS) software plays an important role in real-time
order-promising and fulfilment. The MILP-based APS model presented in this project
considers multiple orders, which allows the company to configure their orders, e.g. the
model can provide a delivery date for each order. Hence, the company can improve its
customer service with more reliable order promising and fulfilment.
To face this challenge, two variants of objective functions were defined and analysed,
one regarding the lead time and another about the tardiness. These variants provide the
decision-maker two approaches to solve the planning schedule depending on the objective.
The first approach, lead time analysis production, refers to the optimisation of the
total time taken to complete a set of orders. This allows the decision-maker to analyse
the cost reduction, efficiency improvement, resource utilisation and improved planning
and scheduling. These aspects can lead to cost saving by minimising inventory holding
costs, reducing overtime expenses; also, production processes can be optimised to achieve
faster turnaround times, ensuring timely delivery of orders to distributors by identifying
bottlenecks and scheduling orders effectively; and provide valuable insights into the se-
quencing and scheduling of the production activities. Overall, lead time analysis plays a
crucial role in enhancing the competitiveness and performance of production systems by
reducing lead times, and improving overall operational efficiency.
The second approach, tardiness analysis in production, refers to the examination and
management of delays or lateness in completing production orders. Then, decision-maker
is allowed to identify bottlenecks, optimise of production schedules, capacity planning, and
improve customer service and satisfaction. These points help in identifying bottlenecks
and critical paths in the production process. By analysing the causes of delays and iden-
tifying orders that consistently contribute to tardiness, production manager can prioritise
resource allocation and process improvements to address these bottlenecks effectively.
By understanding the factors contributing to tardiness, production planner can optimise
production schedules to minimise delays and improve on-time delivery performance. It
also aids in optimising capacity planning by identifying underutilised resources or over-
burdened manufacturers. Lastly, it leads to improved on-time delivery performance and
enhanced customer satisfaction. By fulfilling orders promptly and reliably, company can
strengthen customer relationships, build trust, and differentiate itself from competitors.
In conclusion, tardiness analysis is a valuable tool for optimising production processes,
improving on-time delivery performance, and driving continuous improvement in man-
ufacturing operations. By identifying bottlenecks, optimising schedules, and enhancing
resource utilisation, company can minimise delays, reduce costs, and deliver greater value
to customers, thereby gaining a competitive edge in the marketplace.

it allows to anticipate potential challenges or obstacles that may arise during the exe-

ing unexpected challenges. Additionally, considering various scenarios increases resilience
