Abstract 
The salmon louse (Lepeophtheirus salmonis) is known to limit the growth of the salmon 
farming industry as well as compromise its sustainability. As the louse has developed 
resistance to almost all available drug treatments, there is a need for alternative treatments. 
One alternative treatment receiving a lot of attention is the use of cleaner fish, including the 
lumpfish (Cyclopterus lumpus L.). Cleaner fish welfare in sea cages is, however, likely 
compromised, as the mortality of the lumpfish is often high. As a suggestion to increase 
lumpfish welfare is the use of seaweed shelters as opposed to plastic- or no shelters. The 
seaweed shelters might have a positive effect on lumpfish welfare and health as well as have 
an effect on lumpfish behavior. In this study, the effects of seaweed- (Saccharina latissima), 
plastic- and no shelter on lumpfish health, welfare and behavior was evaluated. In order to 
determine the effects of the different shelters, three experiments were set up. One for 
studying growth and survival rate in juvenile lumpfish (0.32 ± 0.032 g body mass), one for 
studying effects on behavior and health of adult lumpfish (563.2 ± 226 g body mass) and an 
acute stress test, measuring cortisol, glucose and lactate, on adult lumpfish. The results 
showed a significant effect of shelter type on the feeding behavior and on length and height 
increase of adult male lumpfish. No effect was found on growth or survival of juvenile 
lumpfish or on skin, spine or fin condition, liver color or acute stress response of adult 
lumpfish, indicating that the type of shelter used had no effect on lumpfish welfare. As the 
effect on length and height was on adult male fish only, where males with seaweed shelters 
increased more than in other shelters, the shelter type seems to be more important for male 
fish than female. More studies are needed to evaluate the gender related effects of the 
shelters. Even though this study did not show welfare benefits of seaweed shelters, the 
situation in full-scale salmon cages can be very different and it would still be very relevant to 
study the effects of seaweed shelters on lumpfish welfare in a full-scale commercial cage.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background: The sea lice problem in salmon aquaculture 
Since the 1970s, the salmon louse (Lepeophtheirus salmonis) has been a major problem for 
the Atlantic salmon farming industry, and the louse has a greater economic impact on the 
industry than any other parasite (Torrissen et al., 2013). The louse is now limiting the growth 
of the industry as well as compromising its sustainability (Powell et al., 2017). As the lice 
feed on the mucus, skin and blood of the host, infections can lead to skin lesions, 
osmoregulatory stress, altered feeding behavior, reduced growth and increased risk of 
secondary infections (Mennerat et al., 2012; Torrissen et al., 2013). Traditionally, salmon lice 
infections have been treated with pharmaceutical treatments. There has, however, been 
developed resistance to almost all available drug treatments (Hamre et al., 2013) and thus, 
there is a need for non-pharmaceutical treatments. Several non-pharmaceutical treatments for 
salmon lice have been tested or are under development (Torrissen et al., 2013). These include 
cleaner fish, immunological interference through feed, removal by water jets or laser, 
selecting for louse resistant salmon, warm water or freshwater treatments and shielding skirts 
(Frank et al., 2015; Gulla, 2015; Steinsvik, 2017). 
1.1.1. Lumpfish as cleaner fish 
As a non-pharmaceutical treatment, the use of cleaner fish is particularly attractive, as they 
can reduce the use of pharmaceutical treatments, may be more cost efficient than medicating 
(Liu and Vanhauwaer Bjelland, 2014) and less stressful to the salmon (Powell et al., 2017). In 
contrast, pharmaceutical treatments usually involve starving the salmon prior to treatment, 
and appetite is often suppressed after the treatment for a period of time. Both these factors 
contribute to lost growth of the salmon (Imsland et al., 2018a). There is also reduced public 
acceptance of the use of pharmaceuticals in food production (Imsland et al., 2014b). 
The ability to use European wrasse (Labridae) as cleaner fish was first described 
already in the late 1980s (Bjordal, 1988). Wild-caught wrasse has been used as cleaner fish in 
Norway, but in recent years, farmed lumpfish (Cyclopterus lumpus L.) has been introduced as 
a cleaner fish species. The main advantage of using lumpfish, compared to wrasse, is that 
they maintain lice-eating activity at lower water temperatures (Imsland et al., 2014a). Also, 
there is a perception of lumpfish being more robust to handling etc. than wrasse. The 
lumpfish also grows faster and has no swim bladder, so they will spend a lot of time attached 
to structures in their surroundings by the use of a ventral sucker (Imsland et al., 2014a).

The number of cleaner fish used by the salmon farming industry has increased 
exponentially since 2008, and in 2015, there was used close to 26 million cleaner fish in 
Norway alone. It is estimated that by 2020, 50 million cleaner fish will be required, most of 
which will be lumpfish (Powell et al., 2017). 
1.1.2. Challenges to lumpfish in aquaculture 
To supply the salmon farming industry with the increasing numbers of lumpfish required to 
control the sea lice problem, the species needs to be reared entirely in captivity (Powell et al., 
2017). However, knowledge on lumpfish biology in captivity and its potential in aquaculture 
is still very limited. Some examples of critical gaps in knowledge are (Powell et al., 2017):  
• Broodstocks are often almost entirely wild caught 
• Males are culled to collect sperm  
• Limited knowledge on the risk of infection to salmon  
• No selective breeding programs 
• Variable sea lice grazing, which is also difficult to assess in sea cages  
• Limited knowledge on seasonal variation in the grazing efficacy 
There are also major health problems to the lumpfish. Very few lumpfish survive through a 
full salmon production cycle, as lumpfish mortalities in salmon farms are often high. 
Increased mortalities are most often caused by bacterial agents (European Union Reference 
Laboratory for Fish Diseases, 2016). Currently, the bacterial infections causing the largest 
problems for lumpfish aquaculture are: atypical Aeromonas salmonicida, an unnamed 
Pasteurella sp., Vibrio anguillarum serotype O1, Pseudomonas anguilliseptica, Vibrio 
ordali, Moritella viscosa and Tenacibaculum sp. (Bornø et al., 2016; European Union 
Reference Laboratory for Fish Diseases, 2016). The high mortalities in sea cages have led to 
ethical concerns about their utilization as cleaner fish (Hvas et al., 2018). 
1.1.3. Some notions on lumpfish biology 
The lumpfish is naturally found in high latitudes and is most abundant in the Artic and sub-
Arctic waters of the North Atlantic (Kennedy et al., 2016). The lumpfish can be considered a 
semi-pelagic (or semi-demersal) fish, as it has characteristics of demersal species, i.e. the 
pelvic fins have evolved into a suction disc and it lacks a swim bladder, and of pelagic 
species, i.e. an almost uncalcified cartilaginous skeleton, which gives them almost neutral 
buoyancy (Kennedy et al., 2016). However, the lumpfish have shown not to be strictly 
demersal nor pelagic, but spend a significant amount of time associated with both the seabed

and the sea surface (Kennedy et al., 2016). Lumpfish migrate from offshore areas to 
spawning areas along the coast, where they spawn in shallow water (Eriksen et al., 2014). 
The mating takes place on rocky shores, particularly in areas that are overgrown with kelp. 
The males guard and care for the eggs until they hatch, and they do not seem to feed during 
the breeding period (Davenport, 1985). During the first year after hatching, the juveniles can 
often be found among algae, attached or free living. After about a year, the lumpfish 
juveniles are found in pelagic habitats, not among algae (Ingólfsson and Kristjánsson, 2002).  
1.1.4. Lumpfish efficacy and shelters 
Lumpfish in salmon cages have shown to approach docile salmon and pick lice off the 
salmon (Johannesen et al., 2018). In the Faroe Islands, at any time point, 10% of lumpfish 
can be expected to have recently consumed lice. This is however, highly affected by seasonal 
variability of plankton, which can cause a shift from 0-5% of lumpfish that consume lice 
when planktonic prey is available, to 15-20% when planktonic prey is unavailable (Eliasen et 
al., 2018). It is possible that lumpfish prefer planktonic prey because they can stay attached to 
their shelters and sit and wait for the prey to float by (Johannesen et al., 2018).  
Since the natural requirement of lumpfish is to have some surface to attach to, it is 
reasonable to assume that lumpfish in sea cages with Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) will need 
some kind of shelters or substrates to rest on, which can also provide shelter from the 
environment (Imsland et al., 2014b). It is generally considered that lumpfish use shelters for 
two main purposes: They hide in or under them and attach to them to rest (Imsland et al., 
2014b). Efforts are therefore made to provide lumpfish with shelters that accommodate both 
of these needs. Artificial seaweed shelters have commonly been used, which provide 
excellent hiding spots, but might not be sturdy enough as a suitable resting spot (Johannesen 
et al., 2018). Flat vertical shelters from hard plastic can provide a good rigid resting space, 
but are not ideal for hiding (Johannesen et al., 2018). Seaweed shelters might serve as an 
alternative shelter for lumpfish. Seaweed shelters might also be beneficial for lumpfish 
health, as seaweeds have shown to exhibit pharmacological properties, such as anti-oxidant, 
anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial and even anticancer properties (Vatsos and Rebours, 2015). 
Green, brown and red marine algae have metabolites that may be useful for the inhibition of 
bacteria, viruses, fungi and other epibionts (Pérez et al., 2016).  
Feeding practices for lumpfish in sea cages usually involve automatic feeders in a 
limited area, or manual feeding along the net and near the shelters (Johannesen et al., 2018). 
If the shelters are located so that there will be a trade-off between the ideal resting/hiding

1.1.5. Lumpfish welfare considerations 
The use of cleaner fish plays an important role in the battle against sea lice in modern salmon 
aquaculture. It is therefore important that the breeding, transportation and use of cleaner fish 
happens under conditions of good welfare and sustainable development (Bornø and Linaker, 
2014). It is, however, unlikely that this is true, due to the high mortality of lumpfish in 
aquaculture. Over the recent years, attention and knowledge on this issue has increased. 
Better transport, using good shelters and the right feed for the fish has already contributed to 
better welfare, higher survival and thereby better effects from using lumpfish. But the fish 
still have a limited “working time” in the sea cages, which is still a welfare issue (Bornø and 
Linaker, 2014). The locations for the sea cages are also chosen primarily based on 
environmental conditions that allow Atlantic salmon to thrive, and it is currently 
underappreciated that the lumpfish might not thrive in similar conditions as the salmon due to 
fundamental physiological differences between the species (Hvas et al., 2018). As the basal 
physiology of lumpfish is poorly studied, there is a need for more knowledge on their 
physiological response in relation to environmental change and stress. An understanding of 
the environmental limits of lumpfish is also crucial for the development of legislation and for 
the ethical use of lumpfish in aquaculture (Hvas et al., 2018).

1.2. 
Fish welfare 
Animal welfare is a complex concept, and it is used by many different people with very 
different backgrounds. There are different definitions of welfare, which can generally be 
grouped in one of the following categories: 
i. 
Function-based definition: An animal in poor health, with ex. an impaired immune 
system, is suffering, while an animal in good health, is growing/breeding well, and is 
in good welfare. It can be discussed whether this is sufficient, as many would argue 
that a social animal that is denied companionship, although in perfect health, is 
experiencing poor welfare (Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). 
ii. 
Nature-based definition: An animal is experiencing good welfare if it is able to 
express the same kind of natural behavior as it would in the wild. However, the 
concept of behavior is complex, and much has to be known about the animal’s 
motivation to perform a certain behavior. Salmon might migrate long distances in the 
wild to track food and stop when they find a place to feed. If the salmon are provided 
with plenty of food, there might not be any reason to suppose they are in bad welfare 
if they cannot migrate. However, if the salmon migrate in the wild regardless of food 
supply, confinement might cause suffering (Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). 
iii. 
Feelings-based definition: The animal is in good welfare if it is free of negative 
experiences such as pain, fear and hunger and it has positive experiences, such as 
social companionship. By this definition, it does not matter if an animal is injured if it 
does not generate negative feelings (e.g. fin damage or hooking might not cause 
suffering) (Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). 
Some important aspects of animal welfare are included in each of these definitions, arguably 
however, neither of them captures everything. Due to this, a different approach was 
developed in the early 1980s by the UK Farm Animal Welfare Council to distinguish 
between good and bad welfare, and it is represented in the five freedoms. The five freedoms 
can however, be unrealistically interpreted as “rights” which have to be met before our 
treatment of animals is acceptable, so Mellor and Reid (1994) transformed the five freedoms 
into “domains of potential compromise” and redefined them to better emphasize the extent of 
welfare compromise rather than the ideal of absence of compromise (Mellor and Reid, 1994). 
The domains and how they can be prevented or corrected are shown in Table 1.

This approach has been much used and forms a broad theoretical framework that underpins 
much regulation and legislation about the welfare of mammals, birds and, increasingly, fish 
(Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). An animal would be in excellent welfare if its needs in all 
five domains are met completely. This is, however, unlikely to be the case for an individual 
animal throughout its lifecycle. It is therefore usual that there will be some level of animal 
welfare compromise throughout an animal’s life, although during some or most of its life, it 
might experience good welfare (Mellor and Stafford, 2001).  
 
Fish have had an independent evolutionary history from other vertebrate groups such as 
mammals and birds for more than 350 million years. This is important to consider when 
trying to understand fish welfare. For example, one obvious difference is that fish are fully 
adapted to an aquatic lifestyle, and therefore are in intimate contact with their environment 
through the large surface of their gills. Water quality is therefore a critical aspect of their 
environment and domain 2 is therefore particularly important in fish. Domain 1 might be less 
important in fish, as fish are ectothermic and do not need to maintain a constant body 
temperature. Therefore, periods of food deprivation do not necessarily compromise fish 
welfare (Huntingford and Kadri, 2008).

Systems related to stress and welfare in fish have been much less studied than in 
mammals, and this lack of information is particularly important when understanding their 
cognitive processes and the link between physical welfare and mental suffering in fish 
(Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). Compared to the brains of birds and mammals, the fish brain 
is relatively small and simple and is also organized differently. One significant difference is 
that the fish do not have a neocortex, which in humans is important in mediating an 
emotional state of suffering (Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). The question of fish being able to 
feel pain or not, is still an open debate in the scientific community. Rose (2002) argues that 
fish lack the most important neural structures for awareness of pain and suffering, and the 
fish’s reactions to noxious stimuli thus are nociceptive without conscious awareness of pain. 
Sneddon (2009) does not agree and argues that fish are capable of nociception and appear to 
experience a negative affective state. Therefore, researchers should consider fish to be 
capable of some sort of pain and aim to minimize it. 
1.3.1. Welfare indicators 
To evaluate the welfare of an animal, specific welfare indicators are used (Huntingford and 
Kadri, 2008). Since the physiological and behavioral needs of fish are not only different from 
those of terrestrial animals, but also between species, it is important to know which welfare 
indicators one should use to evaluate the welfare of a particular species (Hjeltnes et al., 
2016). It can be difficult to measure welfare in fish, as most cues that are used to identify fear 
and distress in other vertebrates (e.g. facial and vocal cues) are not available for fish 
(Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). It is therefore important to have other welfare indicators to 
rely on, such as the limit values for what the species can tolerate of environmental conditions 
(temperature, oxygen saturation, density etc.). It is also important to know that survival is not 
a guaranty for good welfare, and thus has to be supplemented by other indicators. An 
important indicator of welfare is physical condition. This includes injuries (fins, eyes, skin, 
gills etc.), disease (presence or absence), nutritional status, metabolic status, condition factor, 
hormonal status, brain biochemistry, growth, immune- and reproductive systems and up-
regulation of particular genes (Hjeltnes et al., 2016; Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). However, 
good welfare includes more that the absence of bad welfare. There is thus a need for 
indicators of good welfare as well. To know these indicators, it is important to know the 
fishes preferences for environmental condition, not just their limit values (Hjeltnes et al., 
2016).

1.3.2. Stress and its connection to welfare 
Minimizing an animals exposure to stress relates to domain 5, and as objective indices of the 
welfare status of fish, stress indicators can be used (Sopinka et al., 2016). It is a widely held 
view that there is an inverse association between stress and well-being and therefore, the 
detection of stress has been employed as a tool in assessing the welfare of animals (Pottinger, 
2008). Stress markers could therefore be a good proxy to determine which things can induce 
poor or good welfare.  
The stress response consists of three responses: the primary, secondary and tertiary 
stress response, where the tertiary stress response reflects changes to the whole animal 
(Iwama, 2007). Some of the markers of the tertiary stress response are the same as the 
welfare indicators mentioned above; this can be inhibited growth, increased mortality, 
increased disease prevalence etc. which are all also clear indicators of poor welfare 
(Huntingford and Kadri, 2008). Also, measuring the acute stress response of the fish can 
suggest which condition the fish are in. Chronic, prolonged or repeated stress has been 
reported to diminish the cortisol response to an acute stressor (Gesto et al., 2018; Jeffrey et 
al., 2014). Therefore, after exposure to an acute stress episode, a clear activation and fast 
recovery of the stress markers can be considered signs of good welfare in farmed fish, while a 
blunted activation and longer recovery period of the stress markers suggest a poorer condition 
of the fish. 
1.4. 
The fish stress response 
In response to a wide range of biological and physiological stressors, fish, like other 
vertebrates, undergo a series of biochemical and physiological changes. This response is 
known as the generalized stress response, and, as commented above, it can be categorized 
into three categories: the primary, secondary and tertiary stress response. The primary 
response consists of a neuroendocrine response, which includes the rapid release of the stress 
hormones catecholamines (CAs) and cortisol, into circulation (Iwama, 2007). The secondary 
stress response consists of the biochemical and physiological effects that are associated with 
stress and mediated by stress hormones. These hormones activate a number of metabolic 
pathways, which results in alterations in blood chemistry and hematology. Some commonly 
used indicators of the secondary stress response in fish are changes to plasma glucose and 
lactate concentrations (Iwama, 2007; Sopinka et al., 2016). The secondary responses happen 
over a slower timescale than the primary responses (Sopinka et al., 2016). Lastly, the tertiary 
response can reflect changes to the whole animal or population. These usually appear only 
==PAGE:14==

DTU Aqua

9
after prolonged or repeated stressors. These responses can be: decreased reproductive 
capacity, impaired immune function, impaired growth etc. (Iwama, 2007). 
1.4.1. Assessing primary and secondary indicators 
When assessing responses to specific aquaculture or handling practices, it is useful to assess 
changes in certain primary and secondary stress indicators. The fastest primary response to 
stress is CAs. They can, however, be very difficult to measure since they are highly 
responsive to capture and handling. The most used primary stress indicator is cortisol, which 
responds more slowly than CAs to specific stressors. If the animal is sampled within a few 
minutes of capture, one can obtain both baseline and posterior stress levels (Sopinka et al., 
2016).  
When measuring secondary stress indicators, it is common to measure glucose 
concentration, which is a result of an increase in glycogen catabolism and release of glucose 
into to blood stream, and lactate concentration, which is an indicator of anaerobic metabolism 
following hypoxia or exercise stressors (Sopinka et al., 2016). Most of these physiological 
indicators are measured in the plasma but not all of them, e.g. liver glycogen content 
(Sopinka et al., 2016). 
1.4.2. Assessing tertiary indicators 
Tertiary responses, also called whole-organism responses, include a number of aspects of fish 
performance. This includes changes in growth, condition factor, disease resistance, 
metabolism, cardiac activity, behavior, survival etc. (Pottinger, 2008; Sopinka et al., 2016). 
As tertiary indicators represent an integration of various mechanisms, unlike primary and 
secondary indicators that focus on direct indicators of homeostasis, the ability to infer a 
stressed state becomes more challenging. For example, reduced feeding behavior does not 
have to be an indicator of a stressed state, it may simply be that the fish is not hungry, e.g. 
because of metabolic demands, food availability, seasonality or genetics (Sopinka et al., 
2016). Therefore, context is very important, and when evaluating tertiary stress indicators, 
control groups must be used (Sopinka et al., 2016).  
1.4.2.1. 
Behavioral responses during stress 
When fish are exposed to chronic stress their behavior may be affected. This can be seen as 
significant changes in swimming patterns, change in anti-predator behavior, disrupted 
feeding behavior, increased shelter seeking, change in territorial behavior and interference 
with learning processes (Galhardo and Oliveira, 2009). When quantifying modifications in

1.4.2.2. 
Condition-based indicators 
Compared to measurements of blood chemistry or other physiological indicators, condition-
based indicators appear to be relatively insensitive to acute environmental stressors. They are 
however, easy to collect, and can contribute to an understanding of long-term trends in a fish 
population exposed to chronic environmental stressors (Barton et al., 2002). One of these 
measures that is widely used is the condition factor. Fulton’s condition factor is a measure of 
robustness and is a function of the fish weight divided by the cube of the length and 
approaches unity by a scaling constant. Fulton’s condition factor K is expressed as 
! = !/!!! ∙10! (Barton et al., 2002). Changes in this factor can reflect the nutritional or 
energy status of the fish, as the fish weight relative to the length will change with storage or 
mobilization of energy reserves such as proteins, carbohydrates, or lipids (Barton et al., 
2002). This condition factor in the form of ! = 100 ∙!/!!, where W is the weight (g) of the 
fish and L the length (cm) has previously been used for lumpfish (Imsland et al., 2014a; 
Imsland et al., 2018b, 2018c). 
1.4.2.3. 
Necropsy-based indicators 
When detecting changes in health and condition of fish populations, the necropsy-based 
assessment method is a rapid, easy and inexpensive procedure to do this. This method is 
based on a systematic examination of external and internal tissues and organs of the fish. 
Working with this method requires the following assumptions: (1) in fish under stress, tissue 
and organ function will change in order to maintain homeostasis; (2) if a change in function 
persists in response to continuous stress, there will be a gross change in structure of organs 
or tissues; (3) if the appearance of all organs and tissues is normal according to the necropsy 
criteria, there is a good likelihood that the fish is normal; and (4) if the appearance of an 
organ or tissue system departs from the normal or control condition, the fish is responding to 
changes brought about by the environmental stressor (Barton et al., 2002). The necropsy data 
can be recorded in a standardized sheet, and provide information on e.g. (1) percentage of 
fish with ex. normal and abnormal eyes, gills, pseudobranchs, thymus, spleens, hindguts, 
kidneys, and livers; (2) mean index values of damage to fins, thymus hemorrhage, mesenteric

1.4.3. Considerations when measuring and interpreting stress 
Different species of fish respond to the same stressors differently (Sopinka et al., 2016). This 
therefore largely limits the comparison of stress across species. Even when working with 
only one species, measures of stress can vary within a species. For example, factors like sex, 
size, social status and domestication can cause variation in stress indicators within a species 
(Sopinka et al., 2016). There is also a rapidly developing appreciation of heritable differences 
in stress-responsiveness (Sopinka et al., 2016). The stress response can vary in magnitude 
depending on the environmental and ecological context of the stressor that the animal is 
exposed to. E.g. predator-induced cortisol response in winter flounder (Pseudopleuronectes 
americanus) differed depending on predator species. How the stressor is applied 
(acute/single, repeated or chronic/prolonged) is also important for the stress indicator levels 
(Sopinka et al., 2016). It is also important to notice that the stress responses may have an 
effect on themselves: the life history of the fish can modify their response to further stressor, 
due to exhaustion (after severe exposure to stressors) or to learning/habituation processes. In 
this regard, fish have also shown to be able to habituate to mild, repeated and predictable 
stressors (Gesto et al., 2018).

2. Objectives 
Lumpfish require some kind of shelter in the sea cages to rest on, and the hypothesis is that 
the lumpfish welfare, behavior and health might differ depending on the type of shelter 
provided for them. Experience at Fiskaaling (Við Àir, Hvalvík) and Hiddenfjord (Við Ánna 
1, Norðagøta) suggest that seaweed in sea cages could be beneficial for lumpfish survival. To 
test this hypothesis, a series of experiments were carried out to answer the questions: 
• What effects do seaweed shelters have on lumpfish behavior, welfare and 
health compared to plastic shelters or no shelters? 
• Do seaweed shelters have an effect on the lumpfish stress response? 
• Altogether, do the shelters promote better welfare for lumpfish in the tested 
lab-scale set-up?

3. Materials and methods 
3.1. 
Fish and research facilities 
The experiments were carried out at the Aquaculture Research Facility in Nesvík (Fiskaaling 
- við Áir, Hvalvík) Faroe Islands in the period of 13/02/18 to 09/03/18. The fish were bred at 
the station. The fish were used in three experiments, which were approved by a local Ethics 
committee (Fiskaaling). Prior to the experiment, 360 adult lumpfish (563.2 ± 226 g body 
mass) and 360 juvenile lumpfish (0.32 ± 0.032 g body mass) were selected and randomly 
distributed between the appropriate tanks. This resulted in 9 tanks with 40 adult fish in each, 
and 9 aquariums with 40 juvenile fish in each. The adult fish were kept in tanks with no type 
of shelters for an acclimation period of two weeks. After the acclimation period, the tanks 
and aquariums were randomly assigned one of the three treatments: seaweed shelter (SS), 
plastic shelter (PS) or no shelter (NS). The tanks and aquariums were kept under a controlled 
photoperiod (12 h:12 h day:night) and temperature (5.6-7 °C) in tanks and (8-10 °C) in 
aquariums. Oxygen saturation in the tanks and aquariums was kept over 70%. The water in 
both the tanks and aquariums had been cleaned through a mechanical filter, biological filter 
and UV-light. 
3.2. 
Shelters 
The seaweed used in the experiments was sugar kelp (Saccharina latissima). This species 
was chosen since a local salmon production company had started using this species of 
seaweed in their sea cages as lumpfish shelters. The plastic shelter treatment was included as 
commercial companies (e.g. OK Marine AS, Kristiansand, Norway) offer synthetic seaweed 
shelters as lumpfish shelters. The use of plastic shelters was also used to differentiate the 
effects of just the physical cover (the same for seaweed and plastic shelters) from any 
potential effect due to the chemical nature of the material introduced in the fish-rearing units. 
On the 12/02/2018, seaweed was collected for the experiments just outside the research 
station in Nesvík. Figure 1 shows the author and Ása Johannesen (Fiskaaling) collecting 
seaweed in Nesvík.

The experiment with the juvenile fish was set up with 9 aquariums each containing 40 fish. 
The aquariums had a capacity of 20 L and were filled up with cleaned seawater and seeded

were randomly distributed among the aquariums, three replicates for each treatment. The 
amount of seaweed and plastic had an area of around 25x20 cm. The plastic was in stripes of 
around 25x5 cm. Prior to the experiment, the fish biomass in each aquarium was weighed. 
Stable temperatures at 8-10°C and oxygen saturation above 70% were maintained in the 
aquariums. A sketch of the setup of the aquariums is shown in Figure 2 and a picture of the

3.3.3. Experiment 3: Stress testing adult fish 
At the end of the 4 week period, a stress test was performed to evaluate the acute stress 
response of the fish in the different tanks. Prior to the stress test, the fish were fasted for 24 
hours. Each tank was individually exposed to an acute stressor, being the water level was 
lowered and kept low for 30 minutes, crowding the fish. Prior to lowering the water level, 
four fish from each tank were randomly sampled as a pre-stress group, and anaesthetized with 
Finquel (MS-222) 200 mg L-1. The fish were blood sampled, weighed, measured and the 
condition of the skin, spines and fins was evaluated. The fish were then euthanized in a lethal 
dose of Finquel, opened and the color of the liver was recorded according to a printed copy of 
a six-step color scale (Figure 10). The blood was sampled from the caudal vein using 4 ml 
vacuum blood collection tubes with lithium heparin (BD Vacutainer Heparin tubes, Cat. No. 
368884). The blood was then transferred to 1,5 mL Eppendorf tubes and centrifuged to 
separate blood plasma from blood cells. 0,5 mL blood plasma was transferred to a new 
Eppendorf tube and frozen for later analysis. Four fish were then again randomly sampled at 
45 min, 1.5 hours, 2 h 45 min, 6 hours and 24 hours after stress exposure. The blood plasma 
was later analyzed for cortisol, glucose and lactate content.

3.3. Statistics 
Data from juvenile fish and behavioral observations of large fish was analyzed by a one-way 
ANOVA (one factor: treatment). Data from evaluations of condition were analyzed by a two-
way ANOVA (two factors: treatment and gender) and data from the stress test was analyzed 
by a two-way ANOVA (two factors: treatment and time). All ANOVA tests that showed a 
significant result were followed by Tukey’s HSD post hoc test. Differences were considered 
statistically significant at p≤0.05. Statistics were done using Matlab.

5. 
Discussion 
5.1. 
Juvenile fish 
In this study, no significant effect of treatment was found on growth or survival rate of 
juvenile lumpfish. One reason for this could be the low sample size (n=3) for each treatment. 
Although not significant, the fish had the highest survival rate and relative growth in the 
aquariums with the NS treatment, which might suggest a trend. The reason behind this is not 
known. There is a perception of lumpfish requiring particularly well oxygenated water of 
high quality (European Union Reference Laboratory for Fish Diseases, 2016). Jørgensen et 
al. (2017) found that decreasing water exchange rate and oxygen saturation increasingly 
compromised the growth performance of juvenile lumpfish. They also found that oxygen 
saturation above 80% is necessary to achieve maximal growth. The optimal temperature for 
growth of juvenile lumpfish was found by Nytrø et al. (2014) to be at 13-16 °C. In this study, 
the oxygen saturation was mostly over 80%, which should be optimal for growth according to 
the study by Jørgensen et al. (2017). The temperature in the aquariums was at 8-10 °C, so the 
fish were likely not kept at optimum temperature for growth.  
If the water quality in the start feeding tanks is not very high, feed particles can cause 
bad tank hygiene and lowered water quality in regards to bacterial load and bacterial 
composition (Dahle et al., 2017). The bacteria are likely to create a biofilm on the tank walls, 
and since the lumpfish spend a lot of time attached to surfaces in the tank, the contact 
between the fish and the biofilm is a potential source of infection (Dahle et al., 2017). Since 
the aquariums in this study were seeded with untreated water, it is possible that bacteria 
caused bad water quality. This was done on purpose to see if the seaweed possibly had a 
positive effect on water quality and lumpfish survival. It can be hypothesized, that since 
seaweed have metabolites that may be useful for the inhibition of bacteria (Pérez et al., 
2016), the lumpfish might be protected from some bacteria as they attach to the seaweed. 
However, by the end of the experiment, there was no difference in survival rate between 
treatments. This might be due to the seaweed compounds having no effect on the lumpfish 
and/or water quality or the presence of the seaweed might have decreased the water quality. 
The NS treatment tanks had a tendency to have the clearest water over the course of the 
experiment, indicating the best water quality. To be sure of this, water quality parameters 
should ideally have been measured over the course of the experiment as well as bacterial 
composition.  
 
==PAGE:39==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
34
The setup of this experiment probably cannot be compared to typical practices in the 
industry, as the fish are most likely kept in tanks with flow-through water and not separate 
aquariums with still water as in this experiment. Having flow-through would possibly result 
in better water quality, as uneaten feed particles would ideally be flushed out of the tanks. 
Having flow-through in the aquariums was, however, discarded to see if the seaweed had an 
effect on the lumpfish in untreated water. 
5.2. Adult fish 
5.2.1. Behavioral observations 
One of the main behavioral responses to stress in fish is a decrease of appetite, and therefore, 
fish that are in good welfare conditions are expected to have more motivation for feeding 
than fish that are not. This study showed a significant effect of treatment on feeding behavior, 
with fish in the NS treatment showing most interest in feeding and the fish in the PS 
treatments showing the least interest. This pattern was, however, not reflected in the growth 
of the lumpfish, with the only significant result being in relative height and length change in 
male fish, where the males in SS increased significantly more in height than in both PS and 
NS, and increased significantly more in length in SS than NS. This can be due to the fish in 
the NS treatment being more active, as the observations clearly showed the most activity in 
the NS tanks. The fish might therefore eat more, but also burn more energy. Another 
explanation could be that the intentions of the fish were misinterpreted. Feeding behavior is 
possibly accompanied by problems of observation and interpretation. Fish swimming close to 
the feed could be misinterpreted as showing interest in feeding, while simply just swimming 
past it. Also, fish not responding immediately to the food does not necessarily mean the they 
are not eating: they might prefer to be “hidden” while the food was falling in the water and 
go for the food a little bit later. In view of these results, a deeper exploration of the feeding 
behavior in the presence or not of shelters would be interesting to assess the potential 
capacity of the shelters to affect food intake of the lumpfish. 
In case the fish are eating more and not growing more with no shelters, compared to 
having shelters, is most likely an unwanted situation in aquaculture, as this would require 
more money for feed, which is not reflected in the growth of the fish. This suggests that the 
fish in the NS treatment had poorer feed conversion ratio (FCR), which is the ratio at which 
eaten food contributes to increase in fish mass, than the fish in the other treatments. As the 
fish were fed by appetite, the food given was not weighed, so it was not possible to calculate 
==PAGE:40==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
35
the FCR. However, when using lumpfish as cleaner fish, the main goal is not growth, but 
maximal lice eating. It can thus be ideal that the fish spend more time searching for food if 
the same pattern would be reflected in lice eating. There is also a perception that lumpfish 
stop eating sea lice when they reach a certain size, where some authors claim that lumpfish 
stop eating sea lice when they reach 300-400 g (Powell et al., 2017). Both Eliasen et al. 
(2018) and Imsland et al. (2016) found smaller size classes of lumpfish being more efficient 
at sea lice grazing than larger size classes. However, Eliasen et al. (2018) found lumpfish as 
large as 545 g to have ingested sea lice. Having lumpfish that actively search for food but do 
not grow fast could thus be a desired situation. It can however, not be recommended to have 
lumpfish without shelters for these purposes, as having no shelters would likely greatly 
compromise their welfare. 
5.2.2. Evaluations of condition 
This study has showed a significant difference between male and female fish in multiple 
condition parameters: weight, length, height and liver color.  
Weight, height, length 
Female fish increased significantly more in weight, length and height than male fish, but 
there was no significant difference between treatments in female fish. In male fish however, 
there was found a significant effect of treatment on relative height change, where the height 
increased significantly more in SS than both PS and NS. Males in SS also increased 
significantly more in length in SS than NS. There was also found a significant interaction 
between gender and treatment on the relative height and length change, meaning the effect of 
treatment depends on the gender, or conversely, the effect of gender depends on the 
treatment. As the interaction effect between gender and treatment (p=0.054) and the effect of 
treatment (p=0.059) on the relative weight change of the fish were close to significant, a 
higher sample size might have resulted in a significant result. Johannesen et al. (2018) found 
the availability of shelters to negatively affect growth in lumpfish, but this could be 
outweighed by manual feeding. As the fish in this study were fed manually, it might be the 
reason why there was not any difference in growth between treatments. Johannesen et al. 
(2018) suggests that the lower growth in fish provided with shelters and fed by automatic 
feeders to be caused by competition for the spot in the shelter closest to the feeder, which 
would cause higher energy consumption and lower growth.  
 
==PAGE:41==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
36
Lumpfish is a relatively new species in aquaculture, and it can be assumed that the 
species is not fully domesticated yet, so the reason that the males increased more in height 
and length in SS than other treatments might be found in the natural life of wild lumpfish. As 
the mating of wild lumpfish takes place in shallow waters, often among kelp, and the males 
stay and guard the eggs (Davenport, 1985; Eriksen et al., 2014), it can be assumed that wild 
lumpfish males spend a lot of time among seaweed. Therefore, the males in the present study 
might have increased more in height and length in SS because they thrive better in seaweed. 
It could also be that they were preparing for breeding season and a period of not feeding 
while protecting the eggs. The males might therefore be eating more in advance, as wild 
lumpfish males do not seem to feed while guarding the eggs (Davenport, 1985). An increase 
in height caused by changes in the environment has been shown in other fish species. 
Brönmark and Miner (1992) showed that crucian carp increased in height in the presence of 
pike, which they suggest is a mechanism to avoid predation by the pike. This is not the case 
for the lumpfish in this study, but the increase in height and length of the male lumpfish in SS 
might have some other social relevance. 
There was found no significant difference between treatments in any parameter for 
females. This indicates that the type of shelter used does not seem to have an influence on the 
welfare or health of female lumpfish. The females, however, increased significantly more in 
weight, height and length than male fish in all treatments. If the perception that lumpfish stop 
eating sea lice when they reach a certain size is true, then male lumpfish might have a longer 
“working time” in sea cages. It could thus be beneficial to have a male only population of 
lumpfish in sea cages. More studies would be needed to assess the gender-related growth 
effects in this species. 
Condition factor 
The relative change in the condition factor (both K and B) of the fish was found to be 
negative in both males and females, indicating that the weight did not increase proportionally 
to the length/height. The fish might thus have had ticker bodies by the start of the experiment. 
The changes in condition factor might just be an adaption to the new conditions. However, a 
change in nutritional or energy status or external stressors (e.g. high rearing densities, 
acidification and other environmental factors) have also shown to cause a change in the 
condition factor of fish. Such stressors can directly influence feeding behavior and food 
intake, alter metabolic rates or divert energy away from reserves. The decrease in condition 
factor might also be a normal evolution of the growth pattern of this species: It is possible, 
==PAGE:42==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
37
for example, that the decline in condition factor is due to sexual maturation and gonad 
maturation. Condition factor is an easy measurement of health, but should be interpreted with 
caution due to the reasons above (Barton et al., 2002).  
Skin, spines, fins 
There was found no effect of treatment or gender or any interaction between gender and 
treatment on either skin, spines or fins, which supports the view of the different shelters 
having no effect on fish welfare in the tested conditions. Overall, the condition of the skin, 
spines and fins decreased in all treatments. As aggression can be expected at lower stocking 
densities (Johannesen et al., 2018), the decrease in skin, spine and fin health could be caused 
by aggression, since the fish were moved from higher stocking densities in the stocking tank 
to a lower stocking density in the trial tanks before the study start. Even though not 
significant, the average spine condition of male fish in the NS treatment improved slightly, 
which might indicate less aggression in the NS treatment, supporting the hypothesis by 
Johannesen et al. (2018) that the availability of shelters can cause competition for optimal 
resting spaces. 
Liver color 
The results showed no effect of treatment or interaction between gender and treatment on the 
liver color of the lumpfish. However, there was an effect of gender, with females having on 
average brighter liver color (higher on scale) than males. The effect of treatment was close to 
significant (p=0.06), so it is possible that with a larger sample size, the results would have 
been significant. Fiskaaling uses liver color as a part of lumpfish monitoring in salmon cages, 
where they consider a score of 4 to be optimal, then score 3 and then 2. As the liver color was 
on average 3.4 ± SEM 0.10 for males and 3.7 ± SEM 0.05 for females, the female lumpfish 
can be considered to be in better condition than males, based on liver color alone. The 
lumpfish were fed salmon feed that contains astaxanthin for coloring, which also works as an 
antioxidant. A bright orange liver contains more astaxanthin than a paler liver, and a liver 
with score 1 lacks astaxanthin (K. Eliasen, Fiskaaling, pers.com.). On the other hand, starved 
lumpfish, where the hepatosomatic index (ratio of liver weight to body weight) is low, have 
shown to have a dark liver (score 5 and 6) (K. Eliasen, Fiskaaling, pers.com.). As sexually 
mature male lumpfish turn pink/red (Davenport, 1985), it is possible that the paler liver color 
of the males was caused by the astaxanthin coloring the skin red. Assuming that the liver 
color can be used as an indicator of welfare or metabolic status of lumpfish, the results from 
==PAGE:43==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua

38
this study indicate that the fish in the different tanks have not experienced different welfare 
conditions. 
5.2.3. Stress test 
Cortisol 
The results from the acute stress test showed no significant difference in cortisol 
concentration between treatments. As fish are able to habituate to mild, repeated and 
predictable stressors (Gesto et al., 2018; Pottinger, 2008) it might be an explanation why the 
stress response of the fish with no shelter was not at a higher magnitude than the treatments 
with shelters, as might be expected. This is because a fish’s prior experience in the 
environment, which they have been residing, can greatly affect how the fish respond to and 
during stress (Schreck and Tort, 2016). The fish were all kept in tanks with no shelters for an 
acclimation period of two weeks, and might thus have habituated to that environment. 
Fish exposed to chronic, prolonged or repeated stressors often have a limited ability to 
respond to further acute stressors stressor (Gesto et al., 2018; Jeffrey et al., 2014). In this 
study, all groups showed a similar response to acute stress, which suggests that their recent 
exposure to stressors was not different, and therefore, the different groups do not seem to 
have experienced different welfare conditions. 
Glucose and lactate 
There are different non-stress factors that can influence the magnitude and duration of the 
stress response, these include genetic, developmental and environmental factors. Not 
knowing which extent these factors have on the stress response makes it difficult to interpret 
the biological significance of the stress response in a relative context (Barton et al., 2002). 
There is also often a variation and inconsistency among different fishes in regards to different 
blood chemistry characteristics. A species that shows a great endocrine response increase in 
e.g. plasma cortisol might not be the same species that shows a great increase in secondary 
responses, such as glucose and lactate (Barton et al., 2002). In this study, the lumpfish 
showed a clear rise in plasma cortisol concentration, but this increase was not seen in plasma 
glucose or lactate. The absence of change to the plasma glucose in lumpfish following an 
acute stress episode has been observed before by Hvas et al. (2018). There was found a 
statistically significant effect of time on both glucose and lactate concentration, but no 
significant difference between any two time points. As the sample size was only n=3 for each 
treatment in each time point, a larger sample size would maybe have resulted in there being 
==PAGE:44==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
39
some significant differences. The low sampling size is due to the four fish from each time 
point being pooled into one. Pooling four fish into one and only having four time points for 
glucose and lactate was done to adjust the budget of the study to the resources available. The 
trend of the lines, even though not significant, showed a decrease in both plasma metabolites 
after stress, which then returned to normal values. This is just the opposite of what was 
expected. This could be due to measurement errors, or the glucose and lactate stress response 
of lumpfish is very special in this regard. Hvas et al. (2018) did an exercise stress test on 
lumpfish and found lactate to increase significantly after the stressor and then return to 
normal values after 1 hour of recovery. They also found plasma glucose to be statistically 
unaffected. As the first sampling time was after 45 minutes, it is possible that the peak in 
lactate concentration occurred before the sampling. Lactate concentration usually increases 
after stress when the stressor forces the fish to swim/escape/exercise as in the study by Hvas 
et al. (2018). Crowding the fish, as done in this study, would likely not cause the fish to move 
a lot, which could be the reason for the absence of increase in lactate concentration. 
5.3  Final considerations 
If the welfare of lumpfish is compromised due to a stressful environment, the most obvious 
approach to limit this is to reduce the frequency, duration and severity of stressors that the 
fish are exposed to. Since lumpfish is a relatively new species in aquaculture, it has to be 
borne in mind that they might not be fully domesticated yet, and thus not adapted to all the 
stressors related to aquaculture. Domestication is a result of a long term and continuing 
selective breeding program. When selectively breeding lumpfish, a factor in the breeding 
program could be enhanced tolerance of stressful situations. By reducing the magnitude of 
response to stressors, the long term cumulative effects of stress on the performance and well-
being of the fish population can be reduced (Pottinger, 2008). 
In order to establish the full effects of different shelter types on lumpfish health, 
welfare and behavior, additional parameters should be included. As the hypothesis was that 
seaweed compounds might have a positive effect on health by protecting the lumpfish against 
harmful bacteria, the bacterial composition in the different treatments should be examined. 
An animal’s welfare also includes the animal’s preference for environmental conditions, so a 
preference test could ideally be made to see if the fish have a preference for seaweed shelters 
as alternative to plastic shelters. 
Even though this study did not show benefits of seaweed shelters on lumpfish 
welfare, it may still be relevant to study the effect of seaweed shelters on a full-scale cage 
==PAGE:45==
Sandra Ljósá Østerø 
DTU Aqua 
2018 
 
 
40
facility. This study did not expose the fish to the type of stressors that the lumpfish would 
encounter in a salmon cage: presence of salmon, currents, pathogens etc. The shelters might 
thus have an effect on lumpfish welfare in a more challenging environment than this 
laboratory set-up.

6. Conclusion 
This thesis has shown that both growth and survival rate of juvenile lumpfish was not 
affected by the type of shelter used in this laboratory set-up. The shelter type did have a 
significant effect on the feeding behavior, length and height of adult lumpfish. However, it 
had no effect on the skin, spine or fin condition, on the liver color or the acute stress response 
of the adult fish. The results therefore indicate, that the type of shelter might not have an 
effect on the general welfare of the lumpfish. However, as the effect on length and height was 
on adult male fish only, where males with seaweed shelters increased more than in other 
shelters, the shelter type seems to be more important for male fish than female. More studies 
are needed to evaluate the gender related effects of the shelters. Even though this study did 
not show benefits of seaweed shelters on lumpfish welfare, the situation in a full-scale 
salmon cage can be very different compared to a laboratory set-up, and therefore, full-scale 
tests in commercial cages would still be very relevant to assess the potential effects of the 
shelters on the welfare of lumpfish.

Glucose
