Abstract 
Obesity is a global and increasing problem in the societies. Obesity has been linked to many diseases 
and disorders, such as insulin resistance, cardiovascular diseases, and risk of the Metabolic Syndrome, 
building up to almost 3 million yearly deaths associated to obesity. Obesity is known to induce a chronic 
inflammatory state in the body, inducing changes in the cellular metabolism. Obesity has also been 
observed to alter the phenotype of innate immune cells. However, the precise role of trained immunity 
in the cellular adaptation to the inflammatory state of obesity is still unknown. It is shown here that 
obesity indeed induces alters the state of innate immune cells and shifts Innate Immune cells towards 
different state. Decreased secretion of cytokines IL-1β and TNF-α after TLR2/1 activating ligand 
Pam3cys was observed in the PBMCs from obese animals. Furthermore, the activation of transcription 
factor Nf-κB showed trend towards decreased activation after TRL stimulation in obesity. Our results 
demonstrate that the inflammatory state in obesity indeed results to cellular adaptation and tolerance 
towards certain PRR activating molecules. Furthermore, the results support the earlier findings of 
alterations in immune cells phenotypes by found indications of altered Nf-κB activation dynamics and 
increased total ubiquitinated protein levels in cells from obese animals. These findings are important 
in terms showing involvement of Trained Immunity in adaptation to the state obesity. Further 
investigation in the PAMP or DAMP tolerance and its mechanisms could offer possibilities on 
understanding the full effects of obesity to the Innate Immune System as well as provide therapeutic 
targets to obesity linked diseases and further prevent obesity related morbidities. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: Obesity, Inflammation, Innate Immunity, Trained Immunity, Immune Tolerance

Chapter 1 
Introduction  
1.1 Motivation 
Obesity is a constantly increasing global problem. On 2016 more than 1.8 billion adults were 
overweight from which 650 million were obese and the prevalence of childhood obesity has increased 
from 4% to 18% in the past 45 years (WHO  2021) .Obesity has been shown to have clear negative 
impact on physical and mental health and is linked with increased prevalence of several types of cancer 
and increased risk of wide variety of disorders including cardiovascular diseases, type 2 diabetes. 
Obesity is also considered as one of the dominant risk factors of the metabolic syndrome (MetS) (Dixon 
2010; Fontaine and Barofsky 2001) and  a risk factor for the development of nonalcoholic fatty liver 
disease (NAFLD) and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) (Sarwar, Pierce, and Koppe 2018). While 
obesity lowers life quality and life expectancy on an individual level, it is also becoming larger issue 
and cost in communities by increasing the need of healthcare (Dixon 2010). Even though obesity can 
often be treated by weight loss by change of diet and increase of physical exercise, not all the effects 
and consequences of obesity are fully reversible upon weight loss (Schmitz et al. 2016). 
One of the hiding dangers of obesity has is the chronic inflammatory state it causes in the host (Patel, 
Buras, and Balasubramanyam 2013). One driving factor to the chronic inflammation is the 
malfunctioning adipose tissue in obesity (Reilly and Saltiel 2017). Weisberg & al. (2003) observed an 
increased accumulation of macrophages in visceral adipose tissue of obese individuals in a study 
conducted with mice. The macrophage phenotype has also been observed to shift towards pro-
inflammatory M1 phenotype instead of normal M2 phenotype (Lumeng, Bodzin, and Saltiel 2007; 
Odegaard et al. 2007).  Furthermore, accumulation of other immune cells, such as NK cells, neutrophils, 
and Treg cells, in the adipose tissue in obesity has been shown in several studies (Winer et al. 2016). 
Obesity is also linked with higher circulating levels of, C-reactive protein (CRP), IL-6 and TNF-alpha, well 
known biomarkers of inflammation, indicating a low-level systemic inflammation state (Pérez De 
Heredia, Gómez-Martínez, and Marcos 2012).  
Due to the inflammatory state, obesity is also linked with innate immune training. In trained immunity 
immune cells maintain a lower threshold for activation and response upon secondary stimulation 
(Bekkering et al. 2020; Netea et al. 2020). While in traditional exposures, such as pathogen infections 
trained immunity can offer an advantage, in chronic inflammation, such as in obesity it might lead to

a disease promoting cellular adaptation towards the inflammatory state. However, innate immune 
training being fairly new concept (Netea 2013), it has not been studied in great depth in contact to 
obesity and all of its mechanisms and effects are not know in great extent.  Furthermore, earlier work 
in the group suggested some level of adaptation to the chronic inflammation in obesity, in experiments 
with PBMCs from lean and obese animals, stimulated in vitro by different TLR activating ligands 
(Ventura 2021) .  
1.2 Project aim and overview 
The aim of this project aims  is to increase understanding of the role of innate immune mechanism in 
the development of obesity- associated disease and investigate the cellular adaptations of innate 
immunity to the chronic inflammation in obesity. The main hypothesis is that due to the chronic 
inflammation and innate immune training in obesity, innate immune system adapts to a different state 
and therefore the immune responses of cells from lean individuals differ from the obese individuals. 
Specifically, it is hypothesized that obesity leads to a state of immune tolerance against Innate Immune 
cell activation characterized by a decreased innate immune responsiveness against certain pathogen 
associated molecular patterns (PAMPs).  
The experiments consist of stimulations of porcine peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) with 
different PAMPs and different timepoints, to induce activation of cells through different TLRs. PBMCs 
consist of blood cells with round nucleus including many types of immune cells, mainly lymphocytes 
and monocytes (Murphy and Weaver 2016), and therefore provide an overview of innate immune cells 
in the blood circulation. Furthermore, PBMC are not tissue specific as they reside in the circulation and 
therefore, they can be used to study the systemic inflammation of obesity. Analysis of the stimulation 
assays consisted of measurements of inflammatory cytokines IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α levels by ELISA (enzyme 
linked immunosorbent assay) from harvested supernatants as well as investigation of early activation 
of inflammatory pathways, through inspecting the activation of transcription factor NF-kB by Western 
Blotting. Furthermore, the state of inflammation was investigated by the amount of ubiquitin 
conjugates in the cells. The cytokines for ELISA were chosen as they are known regulated by NF-κB so 
in combination of Nf-κB activation analysis by Western Blotting it is possible to obtain information 
from different parts of the pathway. Furthermore, higher circulation levels of all these cytokines (IL-6, 
IL-1β, TNF-α) have been reported in obesity (Ballak et al. 2015; Pérez De Heredia et al. 2012).

Chapter 2 
Background 
2.1. Obesity and inflammation 
Obesity, a chronic relapsing progressive disease process (World Obesity Federation 2017), is often 
linked to inflammation. Recent studies have shown differences in the immune system of obese 
individuals compared to lean ones (Bekkering et al. 2020).  Several studies have found evidence 
indicating an increased inflammatory state in different organs, including metabolic organs such as liver, 
pancreas, intestines, and the adipose tissue (Bekkering et al. 2020; Winer et al. 2016). In addition, 
studies have also observed elevated levels of inflammatory proteins in circulation such as IL-6, TNF-α, 
IL-1β and CRP, indicating a systemic chronic inflammation state (Ballak et al. 2015; Pérez De Heredia 
et al. 2012). This systemic inflammation is believed to derive from the alterations in adipose tissue 
composition during development of obesity (Park, Park, and Yu 2005; Pérez De Heredia et al. 2012). 
Aggregation and different phenotype of macrophages have been observed in the adipose tissue in 
obesity. The phenotype of adipose tissue resident macrophages has been observed to be shift towards 
the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype instead of the usual anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype (Lumeng 
et al. 2007; Odegaard et al. 2007). M1 phenotype macrophages have a higher secretion of pro-
inflammatory cytokines and can partly induce the systemic inflammation by secreting pro-
inflammatory cytokines and therefore activate other immune cell types (Pérez De Heredia et al. 2012). 
Furthermore, adipocytes are also believed to have immune cell like behaviour as they can also 
recognize pathogen- and damage-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs and DAMPs, respectively) 
through pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) and facilitate immune responses by producing 
inflammatory cytokines and interacting with immune cells (Chan et al. 2019). PRRs are receptors that 
are expressed by innate immune cells, playing crucial part in the activation of innate immune 
responses. Different PRRs are developed to specifically recognize different PAMPs, such as LPS and 
bacterial peptides or nucleic acids, initiating immune responses against pathogens. Some PRRs can 
recognize DAMPs, such as uric acid or extracellular ATP, and initiate responses towards damaged host 
cells (Jounai et al. 2013). 
Western diet is considered as one of the main external inducers of inflammation in obesity. Western 
diet is a diet rich with saturated fats and sugar with low amounts of fibre(Schmitz et al. 2016; Statovci 
et al. 2017). This diet is associated to a shift of gut microbiota towards Bacteroides, enrichment in 
lipopolysaccharide (LPS) biosynthesis pathways and increased systemic inflammation. This metabolic 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
endotoxemia can induce gut permeability leading to leakage of  lipopolysaccharides (LPS), triglycerides 
and fatty acids from the gut to the adipose tissue and blood circulation (Bekkering et al. 2020). 
Presence of these compounds can lead to immune response as they are considered as PAMPs or 
DAMPs that immune cells recognize through PRRs (Jounai et al. 2013). The constant stimulation of cells 
through the presence of these compounds induces inflammation in different tissues  and contributes 
to chronic inflammation (Figure 2.1) (Bekkering et al. 2020; Pérez De Heredia et al. 2012).Chronic 
inflammation can trigger innate immune training in immune cells causing different responses in the 
immune systems of lean and obese individuals under stimulation and induce disorders such as insulin 
resistance and risk of atherosclerosis (Bekkering et al. 2020; Choudhury et al. 2021). 
Sorensen & al. (2011) analysed the dynamics of cytokine production in porcine PBMCs showing the 
protein level being on its highest at 24h (previous timepoint 8h) for IL-6 and IL-1β. However, TNF-α 
protein level already decreases on 24h (Sorensen, Skovgaard, and Heegaard 2011), which alongside 
logistical reasons supported 20h stimulation length for this experiment. Cells were analysed 
individually from each pig as well as in pooled samples. The stimulators used (LPS, PGN and Pam3cys) 
were chosen to investigate the immune response through different PRR activations. LPS, a common 
surface protein of Gram-negative bacteria is recognized by TLR4. Pam3cys (Pam3CSK4, Invivogen) is a 
synthetic compound mimicking the acylated amino terminus of bacterial LPs and is recognized through 
TLR2/1 dimer (Corn and Vucic 2014; Invivogen n.d.). The mechanism of PGN, common bacterial cell 
wall protein especially in Gram-positive bacteria, recognition has not been studied as extensively but 
PGN has been associated to TLR2 and NOD1 recognition (Wolf and Underhill 2018).

2.1.2 Pig models of Obesity 
Several minipig lines such as Yucatan, Göttingen and Ossabaw minipigs are used to model obesity and 
diabetes mellitus, as they can be grown with relatively low costs, due to their small size (Kleinert et al. 
2018).  Ossabaw pig is known for its ability to quickly form fat and gain weight when fed with a high 
energy and high fat diet, ultimately leading to complete metabolic syndrome  (Sturek, Alloosh, and 
Sellke 2020). It has been stated that Ossabaw pig has evolved an extremely “thrifty” phenotype during

its 500 year isolation on Ossabaw Island, having the highest adipose reserve of terrestrial animals, 
making it a promising and practical model for obesity (Brisbin and Mayer 2001). 
2.2. Trained immunity in obesity 
Innate immune system has traditionally been considered as the non-adaptive, natural part of the 
immune system alongside more specific adaptive immune system. However, recent studies have 
suggested that innate immune cells are also able to obtain some level of memory (Gourbal et al. 2018; 
Netea 2013). This includes different innate immune cells such as natural killer (NK) cells, macrophages, 
and monocytes being able to recall pathogens after first encounter and therefore protect host from 
re-infection (Figure 2.2) (Hamon and Quintin 2016; Netea et al. 2020). This ability to maintain immune 
responses by innate immune memory is also called trained immunity. The increased ability to respond 
acquired by trained immunity is considered to last longer than increase in responsiveness acquired by 
innate immune cell priming after exposure to stimulus, while primed innate immunity typically results 
in a stronger response upon secondary stimulation (Figure 2.2 Dynamics of innate immune response 
in priming, trained immunity, and immune tolerance (Netea et al. 2020).). Trained immunity can also 
last longer than the lifespan of most innate immune cells, which indicates that the trained immunity is 
in the memory by cells with longer lifespan such as stem cells or non-immune cells (Hamada et al. 
2019; Netea et al. 2020). 
Trained immunity has been observed in several studies recently (Bekkering et al. 2020; Netea et al. 
2020). Dorneles et al. (2017) showed that PBMCs of obese male humans show higher secretion of IL-6 
and TNF-α, under LPS stimulation compared to the PBMCs from lean ones. They also observed an 
increase in the acetylation of histone H4, a central factor in regulation of immune responses, in the 
obese individuals. These findings have also been confirmed in childhood obesity studies (Carolan et al. 
2014). For instance, the activation of transcription factor Nf-κB (See section 2.4.1. Nf-κB) is highly 
dependent on the H4 acetylation (Zhang et al. 2011). Nf-κB is a very central transcription factor, 
activated through PRR signaling, regulating production of many inflammatory responses, such as the 
production of cytokines IL-6, IL-1 and TNF-α.  Increase of Nf-κB in the nucleus has been linked in the 
innate immune training and the inflammation of state of mononuclear cells in the obesity (Ghanim et 
al. 2004; H-H Sheu et al. 2008; Zhang et al. 2011)

Recent studies have shown, also adipose tissue to have immune cell-like behaviour (Bekkering et al. 
2020). The main task of adipose cells, adipocytes, is to store energy from nutrition in form of lipids and 
secretion of adipokines to regulate metabolic homeostasis. Furthermore, they can also recognize 
PAMPs through PRRs and facilitate immune responses by producing inflammatory cytokines and 
interacting with immune cells (Chan et al. 2019). Adipocytes have been found to express different TLRs, 
NLRs and RLRs, and being to produce and recognize wide range of cytokines and chemokines (Andrea 
et al. 2009; Frasca et al. 2018). Furthermore adipocytes can take part into antigen presentation of 
adaptive immune system (Chan et al. 2019). Fischer et al. (2018) showed that obesity induces 
inflammatory phenotype in adipose tissue, which is not completely restored after weight loss. In a 
study conducted with mice, they found that expression level of inflammatory cytokine IL1beta was 
higher and macrophage phenotype was shifted towards M1 in adipose tissue of formerly obese mice 
than lean mice, indicating that adipose tissue memory could be facilitated by tissue macrophages 
(Fischer et al. 2018). This immune memory facilitated by adipocytes is also called obesogenic memory 
(Chan et al. 2019). Obesogenic memory has also been linked to rapid regaining of weight and 
maintained inflammatory state of formerly obese people after weight loss (Blaszczak et al. 2020; 
Schmitz et al. 2016).

2.3 Relationship of Trained Immunity and Immune Tolerance 
Constant exposure to a PAMP such as LPS has been linked to immune tolerance. Whereas low levels 
of endotoxin exposure have been linked to trained immunity, high levels have been linked to immune 
tolerance, leaving a thin line between development of immune training and tolerance (Draisma et al. 
2009; Ifrim et al. 2014). In immune tolerance, the immune cells have a decreased level of immune 
response, which is considered to be caused by downregulation and reprogramming of metabolic 
pathways, such as glycolysis and the TCA cycle that are important at facilitating immune responses 
such as activation of immune cells and production of inflammatory cytokines (Grondman et al. 2019). 
Maitra & al (2012) showed that development of immune tolerance or trained immunity was depending 
on the dosage of LPS. With low dosage of LPS they observed low grade inflammation without sufficient 
activation of inflammatory regulators such as Nf-κB or PI3K, presenting more supressed basal line 
phenotype in macrophages. High dosage of LPS on the other hand strongly activated Nf-κB and PI3K 
through Myd88 with prolonged activation of anti-inflammatory genes shifting macrophages towards 
immunotolerance (Maitra et al. 2021). Another study by Ifrim & al (2014) supported this finding by 
arguing that low concentrations of TLR ligands train monocytes towards increased pro-inflammatory 
state and confirming that high dosages induced tolerance (Figure 2.3). Interestingly this study also 
suggested that especially engagement of NLRs (NOD2 or NOD1) induces trained immunity (Ifrim et al. 
2014).

2.4 Central signalling pathways in inflammation 
2.4.1. Nf-κB 
Nf-κB signaling pathway is one of the most central and conserved signalling pathways in regulating 
immune and inflammatory responses. Nf-κB family involves five different subunits, p65, c-Rel, RelB, 
p50 and p52 in the signaling (Christian, Smith, and Carmody 2016). Nf-κB includes two different 
signaling cascades, canonical (classic) and non-canonical (alternative) (Karin and Greten 2005) , from 
which the canonical will be discussed in more depth here. The classical pathway (Figure 2.4) is triggered 
by TLR or RRL (RIG-I like receptor) stimulation by PAMP or DAMP, leading to phosphorylation of 
inhibitory subunits of IKK (Nf-κB, inhibitory (IκB) kinase complex), IKKα and IKKβ allowing the 
phosphorylation of IκBα. Phosphorylation of IκBα unbinds it from Nf-κB and directs IκBα to proteasome 
degradation through UPS (Liu et al. 2017). This allows the phosphorylation of the p65 subunit of free 
Nf-κB and translocation to the nucleus, where the transcription factor recognizes and binds to its 
specific DNA sites, called κB sites, to initiate transcription (Christian et al. 2016). Nf-κB is associated 
with many central inflammatory signals, such as production of cytokines IL-1, IL-6 and TNF-α as well as 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
production of chemokine CCL2 (Taniguchi and Karin 2018), and can therefore be used as a marker of 
innate immune response activation. Phosphorylation of p65 subunit is an indication of freed Nf-κB that 
is being translocated to nucleus. This signal is activated during the first minutes to hour from the TLR 
signal and can serve as a marker of the magnitude of the response (Dorrington and Fraser 2019).

2.4.2 Ubiquitin signalling 
The ubiquitin system plays an important role in regulation of inflammatory signals in both innate and 
adaptive immune cells. Ubiquitination is a post-translational modification at the N-terminus of protein. 
Ubiquitination can be either mono-, multi- (multiple monoubiquitinations), or polyubiquitination. The 
ubiquitin system consists of a large family of E3 ubiquitin ligases with over 700  proteins (Park et al. 
2014), which attach ubiquitin to lysine (K) residues in substrate proteins. Ubiquitination of proteins is 
reversible process as over 100 deubiquitinating enzymes, specialised proteases that cleave ubiquitin 
==PAGE:19==
11 
 
linkages and remove ubiquitin modifications from substrates, are known (Park et al. 2014)(Won et al. 
2016) . Ubiquitin, a 76 amino-acid polypeptide, contains seven lysine residues (K6, K11, K27, K29, K33, 
K48 and K63) and N-terminus methionine (M1) residue, to which additional ubiquitin moieties can be 
conjugated leading to formation of polyubiquitin chains (Park et al. 2014). Different residue linkages 
in ubiquitin target different cellular functions. Proteins conjugated with ubiquitin linked through K48 
and K11- residues of ubiquitin are targets for proteasomal degradation. K29 linkage is also believed to 
interact with proteasome (Pickart and Fushman 2004).  K63 linked ubiquitin chains target cellular 
functions such as signal transduction and DNA repair (Park et al. 2014), while K27 and K29 linked 
ubiquitination has been linked with cellular protein aggregation (Nucifora et al. 2016). M1, K11 as well 
as K63 has been associated with IKK activation  (Won et al. 2016), while K11 is also interacting with cell 
cycle regulation (Michel et al. 2017). K6 and 33 alongside K63 are associated with DNA repair (Elia et 
al. 2015).  
The ubiquitin system has been linked to adaptive and innate immune responses, and it is associated 
with multiple processes such as regulation of immune tolerance, immune cell development and 
cytokine induced intracellular pathways such as Nf-κB (Christian et al. 2016; Park et al. 2014). In Nf-κB 
signaling ubiquitin system plays important part in ubiquitination of IκBα, directing it to proteasomal 
degradation, allowing the phosphorylation and translocation of released Nf-κB (Dorrington and Fraser 
2019). Ubiquitination is also associated in Nf-κB activation through K63 linked ubiquitination of RIP1 
and cIAP. Ubiquitinated cIAP then becomes a recruitment platform for the linear ubiquitin chain 
assembly complex (LUBAC) and IKKγ, which will be ubiquitinated through M1 linked ubiquitination. 
Recruitment of IKK complex allows the phosphorylation of IκBα, releasing Nf-κB (Figure 2.5).

Dysfunction of Ubiquitin proteasome system (UPS) has been linked with many neurodegenerative 
diseases(Gong et al. 2016). Dysfunction of 26S proteasome (Figure 2.6) can lead to aggregation of 
ubiquitinated proteins as they are not degraded properly. This aggregation can lead to malfunctioning 
and death of cells (Gentier and Leeuwen 2015; Gong et al. 2016). Decreased activity of 26S proteasome 
has also been linked to obesity. In a study conducted with volunteer human subjects, decrease of 26S 
proteasome activity in red blood cells of obese individuals was observed (T.-L. Chang et al. 2009). 
Furthermore, E3 ligases that catalyse the ubiquitination of proteins for degradation by 26S 
proteasome, has been also linked in development of inflammation, diabetes mellitus and insulin 
resistance (Corn and Vucic 2014; Walczak 2011; Yang, Xiang, and Yang 2016).

Materials and methods 
This project included two main analytical methods ELISA and Western Blotting. All the samples were 
derived from stimulation experiments that were conducted with PBMCs from lean and obese 
Ossabaw pigs. Prior to any stimulation the PBMCs were thawn and recovered for stimulations. 
Different length stimulations were set-up to inspect different aspects of immune responses, 
intracellular (short-lived, 2h PBMC stimulation experiment) and extracellular (secreted cytokines, 20h 
PBMC stimulation experiment). Intracellular reactions were analysed from lysed cells by Western 
Blotting and extracellular secreted cytokines were analysed by ELISA from supernatants. See Figure 
3.1Figure 3.1 to see the whole flow of the experimental setup.

3.1 Dieting Experiment 
Dieting experiment was conducted with Ossabaw minipigs. Experiment population consisted of 6 
normal weight pigs and 9 obese pigs. The obese group was fed with restricted feeding up to 
1kg/pig/day on AU diet (25% Fat, 23% fructose, 0.5% cholesterol, 13% protein), and the normal weight 
control was feeding on restricted standard feed.  Except for an episode of diarrhea around week 1 in 
the 11-week experiment all animals appeared normal with no sign of disease at any time. Attainment 
of the metabolic syndrome was confirmed by measuring weight and serum biomarkers of 
inflammation (Heegaard et al. 2011) (Figure 4.1). 
3.2 Isolation and storage of PBMCs 
PBMCs were isolated from blood using StemCell SepMate (Stemcell, 85460). First blood was diluted 
1:2 in sterile PBS (equal amount of blood and PBS (Gibco 70013-016)).  Diluted blood was carefully 
added on top of 15 ml lymphoprep (Stemcell, 07861) in a 50ml StemCell SepMate tube. Tubes were 
centrifuged 20min in 1200g in room temperature. Supernatant was moved to fresh 50ml tubes and 
washed in sterile PBS + 2% FBS (Gibco 10500-064) twice (by filling the tube up to 50ml and centrifuging 
in 300g for 8min in room temperature). In case of too many red blood cells (red pellet when viewed 
from above), 3ml of RBC lysis buffer (x 20 (41.45g NH4Cl, 5g KHCO3, 185 mg EDTA in 500 ml MQ water)) 
was added for max 2-3 minutes and washed in sterile PBS + 2 % FBS twice (300g, 8min). After wash 
steps cells were resuspended in sterile PBS + 2% FBS and counted with NucleoCounter NC-200 
(Chemometec) using Via1 cassettes (Chemometec). 
Isolated and counted PBMCs were and pelleted in centrifuge. The pellet was resuspended to room 
temperature sterile foetal bovine serum (FBS) to a concentration of 2 * 107 cells / ml. Suspension was 
aliquoted to 1.8ml cryovials, 500 µl / vial. 500 µl of freezing medium (RPMI-1640 with 20 % DMSO) was 
added to each vial. Suspension was mixed by flicking the closed vial and the vial was immediately 
placed on ice. Vials were transferred and stored at -80 oC. One to three days (maximum 7 days) later 
the vials were moved to nitrogen tank (-196 oC) for storage.  
3.3 PBMC thawing 
The PBMCs were stored in cryovials in liquid nitrogen in -196 oC. The storage medium consists of RPMI-
1640 (Gibco) + 10 %FCS + 10% DMSO. Upon thawing, one vial of cells (107 cells) was quickly 
resuspended to 10ml of 37 oC medium (RPMI-1640 + 10% FCS + 1% Penicillin Streptomycin) by pipetting 
medium up and down in the freezing tube with sterile Pasteur pipette. The cells were pelleted by 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
centrifuging in 1700 rpm for 5 minutes and medium was removed. After DMSO removal, the cells were 
washed twice by resuspending to 10ml of warm medium and centrifuging in 1700 rpm for 5 minutes. 
After the last wash cells were resuspended in 5ml of medium for cell counting. Cell count and viability 
was determined with NucleoCounter NC-200 (Chemometec) using Via1 cassettes (Chemometec). 
3.4 20h PBMC stimulation experiment 
After thawing and cell counting the cells were resuspended to appropriate volume of medium to 
achieve a concentration of 1.11 * 106 cells/ml. 900 µl (106 cells) of cell suspension was transferred to 
their respective well on a 6 well plate. To recover cells after thawing they were pre-cultured on the 
plate at 37 oC with 5% CO2 for 3 hours. After pre-culturing, the baseline sample was harvested 
according to 3.6 Harvesting cells for analysis by Western Blotting. 100 µl of respective stimulator or 
medium (control) was added to the other wells keeping in mind that the stimulator is diluted 10 times 
more upon addition to well. Final concentrations of stimulators in wells were LPS (5 µg/ml), PGN (20 
µg/ml), Pam3cys (2 µg/ml). Cells were incubated at 37 oC with 5% CO2 for 20h.  
The cells were harvested according to 3.6 Harvesting cells for analysis by Western Blotting, to be 
blotted for total Ubiquitinated proteins. Supernatants were stored in Eppendorf tubes in -80 oC until 
cytokine analysis by ELISA. 
3.5 2h PBMC stimulation experiment 
After thawing and cell counting the cells were resuspended to appropriate volume of medium to 
achieve a concentration of 1.11 * 106 cells/ml. 900 µl (106 cells) of cell suspension was transferred to 5 
wells on a 6 well plate. To recover cells after thawing they were pre-cultured on the plate at 37 oC with 
5% CO2 for 3 hours. 100 µl of stimulator was added to the well on different times starting the 60- and 
120-min stimulations at the same time and incrementing down in a way that all the timepoints (except 
120 min) could be harvested at the same time. The plate was kept in the incubator 37 oC with 5% CO2 
through the stimulations and only removed momentarily upon addition of stimulator to the next 
timepoint. After adding the stimulator to the 15 min timepoint the plate was placed back to incubator 
for 3 minutes, after which all the samples except the 120 min timepoint were moved to Eppendorf 
tubes and the plate was placed back to incubator. Cells were harvested according to 3.6 Harvesting 
cells for analysis by Western Blotting. When the last stimulation reached the 120 min timepoint, the 
cells were harvested according to 3.7 Harvesting cells for future qPCR analysis.

3.6 Harvesting cells for analysis by Western Blotting 
Cell suspension was moved to Eppendorf tubes and centrifuged in 2000 rpm for 5 min to pellet cells. 
Supernatant was discarded or collected (cytokine analysis), and cells were washed in 0.5 ml PBS () and 
centrifuged in 700g for 2 min to pellet cells. PBS was discarded and cell pellets were stored in -80 oC

3.7 Harvesting cells for future qPCR analysis 
Cell suspension was moved to an Eppendorf tube and centrifuged in 2000 rpm for 5 min. Supernatant 
was discarded and the cell pellet was resuspended in RLT (Qiagen) with 1% β-mercaptoethanol and

3.8 Western Blotting 
After harvesting and storing the cells were thawn on ice and lysed by adding 45 µl of EBC lysis buffer 
(50mM Tris-HCL, 120mM NaCl, 0.5 % NP-40 in dH2O) supplemented with appropriate amounts of 
protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche) (diluted to 1X working concentration), PhosStop (Roche) (diluted to 
1X working concentration), Dithiothreitol (DTT) (1mM), and N-Ethylmaleimide (NEM) (5mM) and 
mixing thoroughly by pipetting up and down. Cells were lysed on ice for 20min, after which the lysate 
was cleared by centrifuging in 17000 g in 4 oC for 30 min. The cleared lysate (37.5 µl) was moved to a 
new Eppendorf with 12.5 µl of 4x SDS loading buffer (50mM Tris HCL, 100mM DTT, 10% glycerol, 2% 
SDS, 0,1 % bromphenol blue in dH2O). and mixed thoroughly. These samples were boiled in 70 oC for 5 
min and either continued to SDS-Page or stored in -20 oC.   
Samples were thawn and heated in 70 oC for 5 min before loading the gel. The gel cassette was placed 
in an electrophoresis chamber and the wells were washed and the chamber was filled with SDS running 
buffer (glycin 75 g/mol, Tris trizmabase 12 g/mol and SDS 288 g/mol in milliQ water). The samples (15 
µl) and the ladder (Precision Plus Protein Dual Color Standard (Bio-Rad)) (5 µl) were loaded into their 
wells. Empty wells were loaded with an appropriate volume (4 µl) of 4x SDS loading buffer. 
Electrophoresis was run in 150V for needed time (45-60 min). After electrophoresis, the proteins were 
transferred from the gel to 0.2 µm nitrocellulose membranes using Trans-Blot Turbo (Bio-Rad). The 
gels used for electrophoresis were 9% polyacrylamide gels casted according to the Bio-Rad protocol 
(Bio-Rad n.d.). However, when blotting for ubiquitin conjugated proteins the Invitrogen NuPAGE 4-
12%, Bis-Tris gel (1.0 mm, 15 wells) were used, for optimal blotting for wide range of molecular weights 
(Invitrogen n.d.).

Chapter 4  
Results and Discussion 
Cellular pathways being very complex, with high number of possible mediators inside the cascade from 
stimulation to protein secretion, this project aimed to investigate different parts of innate immune 
pathways from activation to protein secretion on different timepoints. For inspecting the PAMP 
recognition by TLRs and initiation of immune response the activation of transcription factor Nf-κB 
during the first minutes of stimulation was inspected.   
For further inspecting the immune response, another experiment with 20-hour stimulation was 
conducted. From these samples the cytokine levels of chosen cytokines were determined as secretion 
of inflammatory cytokines serves as indication of the type/strength of the immune response. 
Furthermore, the initial plan included harvesting cells at 2 hours of stimulation for mRNA extraction 
and RT-qPCR analysis. On 2 hours of stimulation porcine PBMCs show already sufficient mRNA 
expressions as a response to stimulation (Sorensen et al. 2011). RT-qPCR analysis would provide 
extensive data about gene expressions initiated after the stimulation. Furthermore, it is relatively easy 
to screen for high number of genes in a RT-qPCR analysis giving possibility to inspect the pathways on 
a wider scale. However, RT-qPCR analysis does not provide as reliable information about the end-
product as for example cytokine detection after 20h, as post-transcriptional regulation may take place 
later.  
 
4.1 Dieting experiment  
The experiments were carried out with PBMCs harvested and purified from Ossabaw minipigs from a 
11-week dieting experiment (see section 3.1). Attainment of the metabolic syndrome was confirmed 
by measuring weight and serum biomarkers (CRP) of inflammation (Heegaard et al. 2011) (Figure 4.1).  
The relative weight as well as the serum CRP were higher in the obese group confirming state of obesity 
and inflammation.

4.2 20h stimulation experiment and cytokine analysis 
To analyse the cytokine production of the PBMCs, the frozen cells were thawn, recovered for 3 hours 
and subsequently stimulated for 20 hours. After the stimulation, the supernatants were analysed by 
ELISA to determine the levels of IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α. The cytokines were chosen based on their central 
role in the innate immune response as well as their link in Trained Immunity and Obesity (Bekkering et 
al. 2020). Sorensen & al. (2011) analysed the dynamics of cytokine production in porcine PBMCs 
showing the protein level being on its highest at 24h (previous timepoint 8h) for IL-6 and IL-1β. 
However, TNF-α protein level already decreases on 24h (Sorensen et al. 2011), which alongside 
logistical reasons supported 20h stimulation length for this experiment. Cells were analysed 
individually from each pig as well as in pooled samples. The stimulators used (LPS, PGN and Pam3cys) 
were chosen to investigate the immune response through different PRR activations. LPS, a common 
surface protein of Gram-negative bacteria is recognized by TLR4. Pam3cys (Pam3CSK4, Invivogen) is a 
synthetic compound mimicking the acylated amino terminus of bacterial LPs and is recognized through 
TLR2/1 dimer (Corn and Vucic 2014; Invivogen n.d.). The mechanism of PGN, common bacterial cell 
wall protein especially in Gram-positive bacteria, recognition has not been studied as extensively but 
PGN has been associated to TLR2 and NOD1 recognition (Wolf and Underhill 2018).With the 
combination of these three stimulators, it was expected to have a broad view of effects of obesity in 
the signalling of different PRRs.  
==PAGE:31==
23 
 
The cytokine analysis from 20h stimulated samples showed significant difference in the cytokine 
responses of lean obese cells under pam3cys (2 µg/ml) stimulation. For Il-1β and TNF-α the cytokine 
concentrations in the supernatant are collectively lower in the obese group, with small variation inside 
the group. For the pooled cells in pam3cys stimulation concentration of these two cytokines is much 
higher than the highest individual in the group (Figure 4.2). As the pool is expected to represent the 
average of the group, it is interesting to see this difference between the pool and the individuals. Yet, 
the comparison between lean pool and obese pool is also showing significantly lower concentration of 
Il-1β and TNF-α in the supernatant supporting the values obtained from the individuals. The difference 
between the pool value and individual mean could originate from cell-to-cell interactions in the pools. 
Immune cells and immune responses are not activated only by PRR signalling, but also through other 
signalling pathways, such as cytokine receptors. Therefore, it is possible that for example the higher 
responsive cells can further activate other cells in the pool and thereby amplify the signal. 
In LPS and PGN stimulations there is not much difference between the two groups. Only in the PGN 
stimulation, the level of Il-1β is found slightly higher in the obese group. However, the finding with 
pam3cys stimulated cells is highly interesting and supporting the original hypothesis of cellular 
adaptation to chronic inflammation in obesity that results to altered immune responses. In this case 
the lowered response by cells from obese individuals indicates tolerance towards TLR2/1 ligands.  
Furthermore, the results correlated with earlier experiments in the group carried out with pooled 
samples from the same animals. In the earlier work similar decrease in responsiveness to Pam3cys was 
observed in the obese pools for both Il-1β and TNF-α (Ventura 2021). The results from the individual 
samples presented in Figure 4.3 confirm that the decrease in the pool reactivity is not caused by few 
outliers with extremely low responsiveness but a collective decrease in the cytokine response towards 
pam3cys (TLR2/1 ligand). In the earlier work a decrease in TNF-α response to LPS stimulation was also 
observed in the obese pool. A decrease of same magnitude in LPS pool was also observed here but in 
the stimulations with cells from individual animals no statistically significant difference was found.

4.3 Nf-κB activation  
Activation of Nf-κB was investigated in a separate experiment, due to the short-lived nature of 
phosphorylation of the p65 subunit. Nf-κB activation through TLR stimulation is a fast cellular reaction, 
normally taking place on the first minutes after stimulation and fading out during the first hour.  
After several optimization experiments (see Appendix 1.2 and 1.3) the final setup for the stimulation 
experiment to investigate Nf-κB activation was decided with timepoint on 0 (control), 15, 30 and 60 
minutes. Due to limited time, only two of the three stimulators used in the 20h stimulations, pam3cys 
and LPS, were chosen for this experiment, both used in the final well concentration of 10 µg/ml. PGN 
was left out due to LPS and pam3cys having been studied in more depth and their stimulation reaction 
being well established. LPS stimulation would offer data about TLR4 activation, while pam3cys is 
known to activate TLR2/1 responses (Corn and Vucic 2014; Invivogen n.d.). Furthermore, the results 
from the 20h stimulation experiments showed significant differences between cytokine responses of 
obese and lean cells when stimulated with pam3cys (see Error! Reference source not found.), which 
made pam3cys stimulation very interesting for further experiments. 
From two animal groups 6 obese – lean pairs were chosen for the short stimulations. The obese group 
being larger, the three least obese (in terms of relative weight gain) were left out from the setup. The 
6 animals from each group were randomly paired. Many possible factors from stimulation to imaging 
the blot might affect the results, which was taken into consideration while planning the experiment 
setup.  All the pairwise stimulations were conducted side to side (e.g., LPS stimulation of both animals 
from the pair was carried out simultaneously and so on) and the cells were lysed from each pair at the 
same time. Lysates from each stimulation were loaded in the same gel and blotted on the same 
membrane (e.g., Lysates from LPS stimulation of one pair were loaded side to side on same gel and 
therefore transferred to same membrane). This way variation in the results, due to small timing 
variations in stimulations, or technical variations in the multiple step process of western blotting, could 
be minimized. Therefore, all the pairs should be comparable within the pair (blots on the same 
membrane) but comparing results of different pairs should be done with caution and keeping in mind 
the non-quantitative nature of Western Blotting. 
4.3.1 Pooled experiment 
First, the short stimulation experiment and western blotting was carried out with pooled cells. Working 
with cell pools can be very practical as it can reduce the workload drastically. Experiment with pools 
can give indication of differences between different groups but on the other hand, a pooled sample 
==PAGE:35==
27 
 
only gives the average of the population and no information about the differences inside the 
population. Therefore, the stimulations were also carried out with cells from individual animals to have 
pairwise comparison data on individual animal level. Experiments with individual animals also gives 
indication of differences inside the group on the expense of much higher workload. Also, Western 
Blotting being qualitative/semi-quantitative method, different pairs are not directly comparable. 
Nonetheless, by individual animal experiments it is possible to investigate if differences in pool 
experiments are caused by differences collectively in the whole group or few outliers with hugely 
altered responses.  
The pool experiment gave interesting indication about the Nf-κB activation. In LPS stimulation a clear 
difference between the Nf-κB p65 phosphorylation of the two groups can be observed (Figure 4.4, a). 
On 15 minutes from the start of stimulation the band on the P-Nf-κB p65 is clearly stronger on the lean 
samples than in the obese, indicating a stronger activation in the Lean group. This could be due to 
development of LPS tolerance in obesity, which has been described in earlier studies (Draisma et al. 
2009; Foster, Hargreaves, and Medzhitov 2007) 
The result of the pam3cys stimulation is not as clear as the LPS stimulation (Figure 4.4, b). The P-Nf-κB 
p65 band on the blot on the 15-minute sample is slightly higher in the obese sample than in the lean 
sample. However, the actin and total Nf-κB p65 levels are lower on the lean sample. Actin, a 
constituently expressed protein in the cells was used as a loading control and its lower level indicates 
lower level of total protein in the sample, meaning a lower number of cells in the lysate. Nf-κB p65, on 
the other hand, represent the total amount of Nf-κB in the cells. As it is not expected to have changes 
in the amount of Nf-κB during this short stimulation, the lower level of Nf-κB p65 can be used to 
confirm the lower amount of total protein suggested by the actin blot. Therefore, it could be argued 
that phosphorylation of Nf-κB p65 in the lean is at least equal if not higher than the obese. This result 
would also support the findings in the 20h stimulation (Figure 4.3) where the cytokine production of 
obese cells was significantly lower for IL-1β and TNF-α in pam3cys stimulation, indicating a tampered 
immune response from TLR2/1 activation. Furthermore, a low pre-stimulation phosphorylation of Nf-
κB p65 may be seen on the obese pool at the 0-minute (unstimulated control) timepoint. This 
indication of low activation state of Nf-κB p65 could be a sign of shift towards pro-inflammatory state 
in the cells because of obesity. The low constant activation of Nf-κB could be one explanatory factor 
of elevated levels of Nf-κB regulated cytokines in obese individuals reported in literature (Pérez De 
Heredia et al. 2012).

4.4 Total ubiquitinated proteins  
As UPS is associated with inflammation, obesity and general cell stress (T.-L. Chang et al. 2009; Yang et 
al. 2016), Western Blotting for total ubiquitinated proteins was carried out on the individual baseline 
cells (lysed after 3h recovery culturing) to inspect the total amount of ubiquitinated proteins in obese 
and lean groups which could indicate a stressed state in the cells with increased or dysregulated 
ubiquitin signalling. In addition, after the observed changes in the cytokine responses in 20h 
stimulation with pam3cys, it was decided to lyse the cells harvested from the experiment and blot for 
total ubiquitin, even though much difference was not expected as ubiquitin is highly expressed in cells 
making changes in the ubiquitin balance during 20h stimulation unlikely to occur. Due to unknown 
reasons the Western Blot for ubiquitin from 20h pam3cys stimulated cells failed completely and the 
Blot from baseline samples resulted in a very poor quality (Appendix 2). This could have resulted from 
a mistake in the handling of samples, or simply from too low number of cells used for the lysates.  
However, a blot in the last of optimizing the ubiquitin blot was done with cells harvested from the 20h 
stimulation experiment with pooled cells. Even though 20h stimulated cells do not perfectly describe 
the baseline, the differences between the groups most likely derives from the baseline homeostasis 
due to the highly expressed nature of ubiquitin. The possible changes in the total ubiquitin amount 
during the stimulation would only be a small fraction. From this blot, it can be observed that the total 
ubiquitin is higher in the obese cells (Figure 4.7).  Almost all the bands in the obese samples show 
higher total ubiquitin than the lean ones, especially on the high molecular weight. This could indicate 
about accumulation of large ubiquitinated molecules in the cells. This accumulation could be due to 
generally higher number of damaged molecules that have been ubiquitinated, due to the chronic 
inflammation state and stress in obesity. The accumulation could also indicate dysfunction in UPS and 
the 26S proteasome being unable to degrade ubiquitinated proteins, which has been also been 
observed in other studies related to neurodegenerative diseases and obesity (T.-L. Chang et al. 2009; 
Gong et al. 2016; Ignacio-Souza et al. 2014).

Chapter 5 
Conclusions 
This project aimed to increase knowledge of the role of innate immune mechanism in the development 
of obesity- associated disease and investigate the cellular adaptations of innate immunity to the 
chronic inflammation in obesity The innate immune responses were evaluated in porcine PBMCs from 
lean and obese animals by investigating cytokine responses (IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α) upon stimulation 
and by investigating the PRR activation through Nf-κB activation. The leading observation was the 
significant difference in PBMC cytokine responses with TLR2/1 activating ligand, pam3cys (Figure 4.3). 
Furthermore, this finding of decreased cytokine response in pam3cys stimulation, supports the 
hypothesis of obesity causing cellular adaptations to inflammatory state, leading to decreased 
responsiveness of innate immune cells to certain PAMPs.  
The results of the NF-κB, not being extremely conclusive, still give indication on altered responses in 
the state of obesity (Figures 4.4-6). However, these results should be confirmed with a more 
quantitative method such as Mass Spectrometry. Alternatively, even repeated Western Blotting could 
offer more quantitative data and a better overview of the differences in the two groups if lysates were 
prepared from samples with higher cell number. Furthermore, the increased amount total 
ubiquitinated proteins in obese animals is highly interesting, indicating increased stress or 
malfunctioning of the UPS in the state of obesity. It has already been shown that obesity is associated 
with decrease in the 26S proteasome activity in red blood cells (T. L. Chang et al. 2009) and 
investigating this in PBMCs from obese individuals could provide  interesting information for further 
understanding about the effects of obesity as well as therapeutic targets.  
These results give interesting glance to the innate immune cell adaptation to the chronic inflammation 
in obesity. Yet, the diet experiment on the Ossabaw pigs was only 11 weeks long. Even though, the 
state of obesity and inflammation could be determined by weight difference and difference in the CRP 
levels in the circulation (Figure 4.1) the state of obesity is still very freshly acquired in these animals. 
Therefore, further experiments with animals with obesity that has been established and maintained 
for longer time (for example 9-12 months) and with sampling throughout the development of obesity, 
could provide interesting information on long term effects of obesity.  
Furthermore, the very next step would be to carry out the RT-qPCR analysis that was originally planned 
to be included in this project. This analysis would provide information about the differences in gene-
==PAGE:41==

expressions during the stimulations between the lean and obese groups. It his highly likely that 
differences in the gene-expressions would be found between the obese in lean groups especially upon 
stimulations like pam3cys that showed significant differences in the cytokine secretion. To conclude, 
the results provide fascinating indication about cellular adaptations of Innate Immune cells to the state 
of chronic inflammation in obesity. Further studies chould increase the understanding of Trained 
Immunity and tolerance in obesity and provide indication about therapeutical targets in treatment of 
obesity and obesity associated diseases.

1. Pilot experiments 
Several pilot experiments were carried out to optimize the experimental design to a reproducible 
protocol. The effect of pre-culturing after cell thawing as well as stimulator dose dependency in 
cytokine production were investigated. For the short time stimulation stimulator dose and effect of 
container of the suspension on stimulation were investigated. 
1.1 Stimulator dose dependency on 20h stimulation and effect of cell pre-culturing after thawing 
The stimulator dose dependency and effect of pre-culturing on the cytokine response was investigated 
on control cells. The dose dependence experiments aimed on finding stimulator concentrations that 
provide sufficient cytokine responses without over-dosing the stimulator. The pre-culturing step was 
established to recover the cells before stimulation, as freezing and thawing can be a very stressful 
process for the cells.  
In the first experiment two concentrations of each stimulator was used. As can be seen in Figure A1 
the increase in dose for both LPS and PGN resulted in elevated cytokine responses for both IL-1β and 
TNF-α (Figure A1), so the higher doses were chosen (5 and 20 µg/ml, respectively). For IL-6 the result 
was not as conclusive. However, at this point, some problems were experienced with IL-6 ELISA and 
the optimization was still in process. For pam3cys the first dose dependency experiment showed very 
low signals on cytokine levels. After receiving a new batch of pam3cys (Pam3CSK4, Invivogen) another 
experiment investigating pam3cys dose dependency on cytokine production was planned. From this 
experiment (Figure A2) it was observed that concentrations above 2µg/ml did not increase the 
cytokine production in any of the three cytokines. However, from doses 2 ng/ml until 2 µg/ml increase 
in cytokine levels was observed, 2 µg/ml giving sufficient signal. Therefore 2 µg/ml was chosen as the 
final concentration. 
The preculturing experiment (Figure A1, g-i) showed significantly increased production of TNF-α for all 
the stimulators increased production of IL-6 for LPS and PGN and IL-6 production was increased in PGN 
and pam3cys stimulations, indicating the 3h preculturing having a recovering effect on the cells to 
restore cellular functions and metabolism. Furthermore, the recovery of cells could minimize the effect 
of cellular stress from freezing and thawing to the stimulation, allowing better comparison of obese 
==PAGE:48==
40 
 
and lean cells. Therefore, the 3h preculturing was included in the final set-up for both 20h and the 
short 1h stimulations.

1.2 Stimulator dose dependency on short stimulations  
Due to suspicion of the stored pam3cys (Pam3CSK4, Invivogen) having been degraded and the 
extremely high concentration that it had been used before (100 µg/ml) the possibility of using lower 
concentration was evaluated upon the arrival of new batch of pam3cys (Pam3CSK4, Invivogen). 
Stimulating the cells with 100 µg/ml of pam3cys compared to 10 µg/ml did not show difference in the 
activation of Nf-κB through p65 phosphorylation (Figure A3) and therefore 10 µg/ml was chosen as 
the concentration used for final stimulations. Even though the optimal working range suggested by the 
producer was 0.1 – 10 ng/ml, lower concentrations were not tried due to time restrictions and as the 
10 µg/ml was still in the range of concentrations used with PBMC stimulations found from the 
literature.

1.3 Comparison of tubes and wells on short stimulations  
The 1h stimulations were earlier carried out in Eppendorf tubes with a small hole in the lid for gas 
exchange. However, while setting up the workflow with control PBMCs the difference between 
stimulating in Eppendorf tubes and wells on 6 well culturing plate was investigated. Interestingly the 
phosphorylation of Nf-κB p65 subunit seemed weaker and more maintained in the stimulations carried 
out in Eppendorf tubes, while the dynamics of stimulations in the wells were striking out more 
conventional and expected as the signal is quickly activated and subsequently shut down quickly 
(Figure A4). However, stimulating in wells added one more step (moving suspension from wells to 
Eppendorf tubes) at the start of the harvesting process, making it difficult to reach the 10-minute 
timepoint (from start of stimulation to adding lysis buffer to washed cells on ice). Therefore, the 
timepoints were changed to 0, 15, 30, 45 and 60 minutes. Later, due to logistical reasons and to save 
cells, the 45-minute timepoint was removed from Nf-κB analysis, and changed to 2h stimulation, from

For ubiquitin analysis the original plan included Western Blotting for total ubiquitin conjugated 
proteins from baseline samples. Baseline samples were harvested after 3h preculturing and used to 
evaluate the total ubiquitin on non-stimulated cells so they would represent the cellular homeostasis 
in the two (lean and obese) populations without any stimulation. However, the Western Blot from 
these baseline samples was not successful as the bands are not strongly visible from the background.
