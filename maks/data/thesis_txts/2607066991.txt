Executive summary 
 
European policies are progressively promoting renewable energy sources, particularly with the 
European Union’s (EU) Green Deal outlining several initiatives aiming at making the union 
climate neutral by 2050. These initiatives include a new strategy to boost offshore wind power 
(OWP) development. Investments in offshore renewable energy should be front loaded in 
response to the recovery period of covid-19 in boosting the European economy – and contribute 
to a sustainable long-term economy.  
The emerging OWP industry introduces great industrial opportunities for countries such as 
Norway and Denmark. This thesis reviews the applicability of using patent data and social 
network analysis as part of a possible OWP developers sourcing strategy. The method addresses 
how local suppliers can be identified, both suppliers with direct and indirect offshore 
competences. 
OWP supplier patent networks are developed based on their shared patent classification – 
indicating their relevance according to shared technologies based on common subject matter. 
The networks provide a holistic overview over the innovation landscape, where different 
technology clusters emerge based on their respective classifications. Their position within the 
network seems to be useful in finding suppliers with both indirect and direct competencies in 
relation to the emerging OWP industry. The methodology does however have weaknesses, 
which are elaborated in the study. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: Strategic sourcing; Supply chain management; Patent analysis; Social network 
analysis; Technology management; Innovation management; Offshore wind; Renewable energy

Introduction 
 
In the global transition to low carbon and renewable energy, offshore wind power (OWP) 
stands out as a promising technology together with floating OWP (FOWP). According to the 
major Norwegian energy company Equinor, close to 80% of potential OWP resources 
worldwide are only reachable in waters deeper than 60 meters, underscoring FOWP’s 
relevance (Equinor, 2020). Eliminating water depth constraints provides tremendous 
opportunities, as winds are both stronger and more reliable offshore. Due to lower surface 
resistance, offshore wind speeds are 20% higher compared to nearby lands – providing a 45-
60% increase in power generation (Dinh & McKeogh, 2018). 
The green shift towards offshore wind provides tremendous industrial supply chain 
opportunities for the two North Sea region nations, Norway and Denmark, with their relevant 
industrial capacities. In Norway’s case OWP can both draw upon and diversity against its 
complementary industrial base in offshore petroleum. Denmark can be categorized as 
transitional since its OWP sector is developing based on the country’s core and 
complementary industrial capacities - partly because the world’s largest wind turbine suppliers 
are located in Denmark (Alkærsig, et al., 2017). 
Focusing on OWP developers in Norway and Denmark (e.g. Equinor and Ørsted respectively), 
this thesis aims to examine whether patent analysis can be used as a strategic sourcing support 
tool and aid in finding relevant local suppliers with the appropriate technology. It also 
investigates whether patent analysis can help identifying the most innovative suppliers by 
category. The vast supply chain (figure 1) illustrates the diverse supply categories involved in a 
OWP project.

Problem identification 
 
The global OWP supply chain will need to ramp up drastically to support the European Green 
Deal, making Europe the world’s first climate neutral continent. The European Commission 
estimates a need for offshore wind capacities to be between 400-450GW by 2050, with 20GW 
installed today (European Commission, 2019). Ensuring a cost-efficient growth and 
maintaining high quality under such demand pose challenges. Significant investment into the 
industrial supply chain is required for the OWP industry to grow and meet Europe’s goal to 
deliver a quarter of Europe’s power. 
To date the European OWP supply chain has been able to scale consistently and currently has 
3-4GW installed capacity annually. However, Europe must average 20GW installed capacity 
annually between 2030-2050 (Ørsted, 2020). This can lead to bottlenecking challenges, cost 
issues, and other risks shown in figure 2 (Dinh & McKeogh, 2018). 
 
Figure 2: OWP project life cycle, costs and risks breakdown (Dinh & McKeogh, 2018) 
 
Research question 
 
A key question that must be addressed to facilitate the European Green Deal’s 450GW OWP 
target is how to enable the industry to scale (Ørsted, 2020). New industrial opportunities will 
come with the European OWP industrial development, providing unique opportunities for 
relevant Norwegian and Danish suppliers - with their fundamentally different industrial 
capacities. For OWP developers in these two countries to be successful, their ability to scan the 
environment for breakthrough innovations and develop technology together with suppliers 
through knowledge exchange is central to improved new product development and financial 
performance (Cousins, et al., 2011). Identifying the right partners is therefore a critical factor.  
Moreover, literature suggests that enabling knowledge sharing and learning between 
multinational companies and local suppliers help to create and sustain international 
competitive advantage for all parties involved. Improving local sourcing and supplier 
development should be an important part of an OWP developer’s strategic sourcing strategy 
(Roper & Cronew, 2003). 
Using patent data can provide insights in technological trends within desired supply categories 
and when conducting competitive analysis to gain insight into market landscapes (Shang-Jyh 
==PAGE:8==

Liu, 1997) (Biju Paul Abraham, 2001). However, patent data use in future procurement and 
sourcing decision support has not been widely discussed in recent literature. On this basis, the 
research question becomes: 
Can patent data be utilized as an information source, in an OWP developer context, to support 
strategic sourcing processes in finding local suppliers and potential R&D partners?

Literature research methodology  
 
To identify literature on strategic sourcing, a subject search was conducted in two main 
research databases: DTU Findit and Google Scholar. The searches were continuously

sourcing, supply chain management and patent analysis. In some cases, two keywords are

Strategic Sourcing  
 
In its traditional form, sourcing can be defined as locating and employing suppliers. Whereas 
strategic sourcing is "an organizational procurement and supply management process used to 
locate, develop, qualify, and employ suppliers that add maximum value to the buyer’s products 
or services” (Semanik, 2011). The strategy term underlines that many sourcing projects should 
be part of an integrated supply chain plan that seeks to meet strategic business and operational 
goals. 
The sourcing process can be summarized in an integrated general overview, as seen in figure 3, 
with both operational and strategic elements. The process map can be divided into four major 
subprocesses: 
1. Strategic planning. Development and alignment of strategic sourcing strategy together 
with organizational strategy. 
2. Research. Includes analysis of internal requirements, market analysis and supplier 
prequalification. Suppliers are investigated in the prequalification phase to develop a 
competitive supply bidders’ group that are financially adequate.  
3. Solicitation. Includes preparation of RFx or Request for: 
a. Quotation (RFQ) – Formal request document with detailed specifications from 
customer to supplier asking for a price quotation (and other details including 
lead time).  
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
b. Proposal (RFP) – Formal request document to a potential supplier outlining a 
set of available specifications for a particular requirement asking for a solution 
proposal and possible cost estimation.  
c. Information (RFI) – Formal request document from buyer to potential supplier 
questioning information about its organization, products and capabilities. 
Does also include evaluation of responses, supplier selection, negotiations, and contract 
management. 
4. Contract administration. Lastly, a continuous supplier monitoring is important to 
ensure that contractual obligations are met (compliance), as well as conducting 
business reviews frequently and performing continuous improvements by generating 
metrics. 
 
 
Figure 3: The strategic sourcing model (Semanik, 2011) 
 
With a holistic view on strategic sourcing in mind, two related concepts in the research 
subprocess are essential to any sourcing project: supplier research and market analysis. 
Supplier research is defined as the process of acquiring information for sources specific to 
either a product or service that is acquired so that a sufficient practices and supplier selection 
is being achieved. Market analysis is the process of collecting information from economic 
indicators and evolving trends within a particular industry, and the commercial environment 
in which the sourced product or service operates in. This will contribute to a comprehensive 
overview of a specific marketplace, so that different suppliers can be assessed in terms of both 
organizational requirements but also in relation to what is commercially available. Combining 
supplier research and market analysis provides a fundamental understanding of available 
products and services, qualified potential suppliers, and how the market for a product or 
service operates (Semanik, 2011). 
There are a few established methods and techniques for locating potential suppliers: 
• 
Experts. Such as internal expertise, but also commodity centers and external 
consultants.

• 
Current suppliers. They can have in-house expertise and solutions that may meet 
sourcing requirements. 
• 
Other recent market research. Possible that previous suppliers have the right

World wide web. Search engines can be powerful information sources - but also 
overwhelming when it comes to the amount of unstructured information. 
• 
Online databases. Industry databases on the internet can provide catalogs of useful

• 
Source lists from other departments of an organization.

Trade shows.

Literature does not here highlight the use of patent data as an established method that can be 
used for locating potential suppliers.  
 
Patent analysis and supplier identification 
 
Introduction of new products and services or entry into new markets are likely to achieve more 
success if they are accompanied by innovative supply chain designs, innovative supply chain 
management practices and enabling technology (Jan Stentoft Arlbjørn, 2011). Although the 
onshore wind industry is recognized as a developed renewable technology, OWP, and FOWP 
in particular, are still at an early development stage and faced with many technological 
challenges (Emanuel Soares-Ramos, 2020). 
Patent analysis has been extensively utilized in technology and innovation research (Assad 
Abbas, 2014) and it is indicated that patent analysis can be useful to identify partners for 
procurement or collaboration at matching supply chain level (Bernd Fabry, 2006). Using 
patent data can provide insights in technological trends in a desired field and when conducting 
competitive analysis to gain an understanding of the market landscape (Shang-Jyh Liu, 1997) 
(Biju Paul Abraham, 2001).  However, its usage in future procurement and sourcing decision 
support has not been widely discussed in recent literature. 
Since patent analysis is not established as a method to provide information to support strategic 
sourcing in the procurement profession nor used as a managerial tool for supplier selection 
decisions (Alexander Trautrims, 2017), the purposed methodology examine how patent data 
and network analysis can potentially support local sourcing and procurement strategies, 
especially when innovation is a key factor in supplier selection. 
Suppliers that have an active patenting strategy can be considered innovative suppliers with 
interesting R&D competencies. Furthermore, if a supplier is having a market monopoly within 
its specific technology it can represent a supply chain risk which should be mitigated. Network 
analysis with patent data can here be valuable as relevant classifications to a desired 
technology or service can be identified to find similar market players which may have the 
capacity and adaptability to develop new technologies and challenge a current monopoly.  
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
Monitoring patent landscapes can furthermore be an important information source for 
technology management, as empirical studies indicate that patent documents contain 80% 
information which cannot be elsewhere found. Valuable technical knowledge is likely to be 
published in a patent, whereas patents focus on how to make the invention work in 
comparison to i.e. research papers which tend to focus on research findings in a scientific 
context. Although the time factor plays a role for patents - generally patent applications are 
published 18 months after the earliest priority date of the application (EPO, 2019) - research 
proves that patents are generally published earlier than other information sources such as 
research papers (Bregonje, 2005). This demonstrates the strength of patents in relation to 
breakthrough scanning (Cousins, et al., 2011). 
 
Patents 
 
A patent is an exclusive right granted for an invention, which usually is a product or a process, 
for a limited time (generally 20 years from the filing date of the application). To gain patent 
protection the invention is required to satisfy five requirements (EPO, 2015): 
1. There must be a de facto invention belonging to a technology field. 
2. The invention must be new. 
3. It must possess a degree of inventiveness over what has gone before. 
4. Susceptible of industrial application. 
5. It must not fall within the list of excluded subject matter (e.g. scientific theory or 
mathematical method). 
The innovative, public accessible and informative nature of patents makes them a unique 
source of information (Bregonje, 2005).

Patent classification systems 
 
Classifications systems are being used by patent offices, including 
both the European Patent Office (EPO) and the United States 
Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), to organize patent 
documents based on common technical subject matter. It is 
consequently a very powerful and efficient search tool for 
retrieving relevant patent data containing novel technical and legal 
information. The most prominent classification systems are the 
International Patent Classification (IPC) and the Cooperative 
Patent Classification (CPC), where the last system is jointly 
developed between the EPO and the USPTO to harmonize their 
existing classification systems. The CPC system was built on the 
existing European Classification System (ECLA) and modified to 
ensure accordance with the IPC system. The CPC is consequently IPC-compliant where most 
subdivisions are harmonizing with IPC entries. Figure 5 illustrates the minor structural 
differences between the IPC and the CPC. The CPC has more subdivisions and subgroups than 
the IPC. The IPC also has groups that are not used in the CPC. The CPC was introduced 1st of 
January 2013 (EPO, 2013). 
The CPC system’s hierarchical levels are divided into sections, 
classes, subclasses, groups and subgroups. The CPC is divided 
into nine sections, where A-H correspond to the eight sections 
of the IPC: 
• 
A: Human necessities 
• 
B: Performing operations; Transport 
• 
C: Chemistry; Metallurgy 
• 
D: Textiles; Paper 
• 
E: Fixed Constructions 
• 
F: Mechanical engineering; Lighting; Heating; Weapons; 
Blasting 
• 
G: Physics 
• 
H: Electricity 
• 
Y: General tagging of new technological developments; 
general tagging of cross-sectional technologies spanning over several sections of the 
IPC; technical subjects covered by former USPC cross-reference art collections [XRACs] 
and digests (ninth section is exclusively represented in the CPC) 
An important difference between the CPC and the IPC classifications is how subclasses is 
broken down into subdivisions referred to as groups, which are either main or subgroups. Main 
groups represent the fourth hierarchical level, where subgroups form subdivisions under the 
main groups. In the CPC, the subclass symbol is followed by a 1-4-digit number followed by an 
oblique stroke, whereas it in the IPC is followed by a 1-3-three digit number (WIPO, 2020)

Social network analysis – a brief background 
 
The analysis explores patent relationships through visualized social networks, where 
classifications connects to applicants. The methodology has much of its history from sociology 
(Scott, 1987) but originates from the field of graph theory which makes it universally applicable 
in modelling a vast number of different relationships, such as patent relationships (Christian, 
et al., 2008). The use of social network analysis (SNA) facilitates visualization, analysis and 
explaining the relationship between technologies (IPC/CPC classifications) and patent 
applicants. However, there is a lack of literature which specifically employs SNA to investigate 
patent information in a strategic sourcing context. It is initially believed that SNA can identify 
local technology patterns and locate influential suppliers which in return can result in a better 
understanding of supplier dynamics (Liu, et al., 2019). Patent data and SNA will be used to 
explore and investigate local innovation patterns for supply categories related to OWP in 
Denmark and Norway. SNA will for the record also be referred to as network solely in this 
study. 
 
Network analysis and bipartite network projection 
 
The patent networks in the method are originally bipartite. Bipartite networks have nodes 
that can be split into two groups, group X and group Y, where no edges connect two group X 
nodes together nor does two group Y nodes connect. The network in the presented 
methodology are consisting of either applicant nodes or classification nodes. Applicants are 
connected to the classifications they are registered with. To avoid having a differentiation 
between the two groups, the bipartite network can be one-mode projected - compressing the 
bipartite network to a monopartite network. The bipartite patent network is accordingly 
projected based on edge weighting to a network of applicants only, so that only applicants are 
connected (Zhou, et al., 2007).  
As the one-mode projection, meaning a network only containing one node type, is less 
informative than a bipartite representation, a proper weighting method is essential in order to 
make sure that the monopartite network is mirroring the original bipartite information. 
Although projecting the network contributes to information loss it can be beneficial for 
highlighting specific features of a network and gain readability.

Offshore wind power in Denmark and Norway 
 
OWP is a promising renewable energy source. To date, wind power has mainly been developed 
onshore – being considered both a sustainable and economically sound energy source. As 
stated in the European Green Deal, increasing OWP production will be essential in 
transforming the European Union’s economy to having no net emissions of greenhouse gases 
in 2050 (European Commission, 2019). The European Commission estimates that OWP needs 
to be up to 450GW by 2050 in order to meet the unions objectives (European Commission, 
2020). With European energy policies supporting the development of the OWP industry 
heavily, there is a tremendous potential for regional cooperation and trade between member 
states.  
It is particularly interesting to differentiate Denmark and Norway when researching its supply 
chains in relation to OWP. Norway has a major offshore oil and gas industry (Norsk 
Petroleum, 2020), where the Norwegian service and supply industry had a total turnover of 
NOK 340 billion in 2017, with 29% coming from international markets (Rystad Energy, 2018). 
Denmark has similarly a significant and highly developed wind energy industry – representing 
different industrial capabilities (Energistyrelsen, 2020). 
 
Offshore wind power in Denmark 
 
Denmark has been a pioneer in developing onshore wind power projects since the 1970s, after 
the government took measures in promoting increased diversification of energy supplies. The 
oil crisis in 1973 can be described as the game changer which made it clear that Danish energy 
supplies could not continue on its fragile path and called for improved energy security 
(Rüdiger, 2014). Danish companies have since become world leaders in both wind turbine

contributes heavily to the Danish economy. In fact, Denmark was the first country in the

commissioned in 2019, represents 49 turbines with a maximum effect of approximately 
400MW (Energistyrelsen, 2020).

Floating wind turbines are increasingly gaining ground in Denmark, with research projects 
such as the FloatStep project led by DTU. The project is testing a floating TetraSpar 
foundation, which is developed by Siesdal Offshore Technologies and will be tested in 2020 
(Bredmose, 2019) (Henrik Stiesdal, 2019). Denmark has the potential to become market leader 
in floating wind as industrialization of the technology takes place. 
 
Offshore power wind in Norway 
 
Norway, with its substantial maritime and petroleum-oriented competence base, has great 
potential in developing OWP, with a special focus on the FOWP segment. In the updated 
ocean strategy, launched June 2019, the Norwegian Government explicitly highlights “offshore 
wind power” as a future-oriented ocean industry together with “technology and knowledge 
transfer between industries” where emerging industries are building on the skills and 
technology developed by established industries, such as the Norwegian petroleum supply 
industry (Norwegian Ministry of Trade, 2019).  
The Norwegian energy company Equinor finalized the first-ever full-scale FOWP farm outside 
the coast of Scotland in 2017, based on its 2009 Hywind pilot floating wind turbine stationed 
outside the coast of Karmøy, Norway (Windeurope, 2017). The newly initiated Hywind Tampen

world’s largest FOWP farm and a vital step in the industrializing process as well as contribute

Hywind – A floating offshore wind supply chain 
 
A case study of Equinor’s Hywind Scotland production network provides relevant insights of 
the first-ever FOWP park supply chain. It points out the important role national governments 
have in developing renewable energy markets by promoting institutional frameworks and 
efforts to attract foreign direct investments (FDI) projects with availability of generous 
financial support for floating offshore wind. The study also stresses the importance of 
developing local content policies (LCP) in maximizing the national and regional economic 
benefits (Karlsen, et al., 2019). 
The Scottish Government highlights their commitment to tackling climate change through 
support for renewable energy whilst also emphasizing the potential of floating wind power for 
sustainable economic growth and industrial benefits. A main driver has been to develop supply 
chain capabilities in renewables, resulting in a first-mover advantage. Scottish development 
agencies were in continuous contact with Equinor to help them identify relevant suppliers that 
could meet desired requirements. The agencies were also organizing supplier events in 
different regions of Scotland to facilitate “meet the buyer” events (Offshorewind, 2013) 
(Karlsen, et al., 2019). 
Scottish authorities had expectations of many local suppliers, but there were no stipulations in 
the contracts requiring local sourcing. Equinor, on the other hand, had a risk minimization 
strategy that prioritized suppliers they already had experience with. Many of the suppliers

Equinor were able to assemble the turbines at Stord in Norway and tow them in upright

assembled anywhere and conveniently transported to a desired site. The Hywind Scotland

supply chains in Denmark and Norway.

Method 
 
Benefits of using patent data includes its consistent structure regardless of national affiliation 
in addition to unrestricted access. These are important qualities when understanding the 
method’s usage of patent data. In addition, focused and directed technology search processes 
can be conducted as patents are categorized by a standardized classification system. 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
Search strategy 
 
Starting to define which technology areas that are most relevant is the first step of the 
proposed methodology. Searching for relevant technologies can be done by: 
- 
Cooperative Patent Classification (CPC), International Patent Classification (IPC) or US 
Classifications to different areas of technology. Providing a hierarchical system for 
classification of patents according to different areas of technology to which they 
pertain (WIPO, 2020). It enables patent searches of different languages, and similar 
technical context. E.g. CPC search: F03D 13/00 (Assembly, mounting or commissioning 
of wind motors; Arrangements specially adapted for transporting wind motor 
components). 
- 
Using keywords that describes essential parts of a desired technology search – 
searching for keywords in the title, abstract and claims of a patent. Several keywords 
can be included in the search, by using a Boolean query, allowing combinations with 
operators such as AND, NOT and OR to produce more relevant results. For examples, a 
Boolean query could be “offshore” AND “wind”, limiting results to only patents 
containing the two respective keywords in either title, abstract or claims.  
- 
Searching for specific applicants can be a meaningful approach when investigating the 
technology profile of a specific company, research institutes or universities (applicant), 
and moreover identify potential collaborative partnerships. 
Obtaining data 
 
When finding the right patent data provider for the method, some minimum criteria were 
defined. These includes:  
1. Open source and fast database with continuously updated patent data. International 
data coverage so that the methodology could easily be adapted to other industries 
and/or jurisdictions. 
2. Advanced search support for minimum CPC/IPC classifications, keyword search (title, 
abstract and claims), applicants. Filter for filling date and jurisdiction (Norwegian and 
Danish). 
3. Possible to download patent data. CSV file export with min 10 000 patents; providing 
patent data that includes applicants and CPC/IPC classifications information.  
There are numerous patent databases available, both open source and fee based. Common 
open source databases that were considered according to the method criteria: 
- 
Google Patents (www.patents.google.com). It is a very fast search engine with over 120+ 
million patents. Possible to download CSV file. However, the CSV file does not include 
classifications, which is essential information for the network analysis. 
- 
Patentscope (www.wipo.int/patentscope). With 91 million patent documents with an 
advanced search function. Possible to download up to 10,000 records with registered 
account. Information from CSV file is limited as it does not contain applicant 
information and CPC classifications (only IPC classifications). 
- 
Espacenet (www.worldwide.espacenet.com). One of the largest available patent 
databases: “free access to over 120 million patent documents”. Export includes 
numbers, titles, applicants, IPC classifications and CPC classifications. Possible to 
export both CSV and XLSX file from search but restricted to maximum 500 patents.

Preparation and normalization of patent data 
 
Data sets, large and small, are messy. Data sets with errors result in incomplete data, leading 
to incorrect results. Cleaning data is therefore a crucial step in the pipeline of data 
processing (Jones, 2017). Ambiguity is a common error in patent data, with e.g. different ways 
of typing the same name, whether it be a company or a person. This can be the lack of 
consistency for a specific company when filing a patent, use of abbreviations, capitalization 
differences, usage of special characters (such as the Scandinavian letters æ, ø and å in for 
instance “Mærsk” and “Maersk”). If not managed, these minor errors will result in a network 
with node duplications. Since an analysis based on an inaccurate network is not desirable, 
refining and standardization is key. 
To prepare and normalize the patent data, Openrefine is used. By using a tool called facet, 
the user can make use of its integrated cluster feature. It helps when cell values that might 
be alternative representations of the same thing, such as ambiguity in names, are identified 
and merged. After processing the patent data, it is converted from CSV to XLSX format. 
 
Classification precision in the networks 
 
The purposed method focuses on the relation between classification codes (either CPC or 
IPC) and applicants. Further analysis will be based on these relationships, and how different 
clusters emerge in the patent networks. There are several alternatives regarding the chosen 
precision of classification codes, where the chosen hierarchy level counts for the level of 
precision in the networks. The networks presented in this case study are either on subclass 
or main group level. Chosen precision should aim on a classification level restriction that are 
broad if a wide range of different suppliers are desirable in the search – to e.g. find suppliers 
in other industries with a slight OWP relevance. Also, the more patent data obtained and 
imported to a network, the more classification level precision can be justified. The case study 
incorporates data sets with no more than 357 patents, which does not match with using the 
subgroup hierarchy level as the nodes will not connect properly – consequently leading to a 
poor network analyzes as clusters will be minimal. 
 
Data preparation in Openrefine and Excel 
 
The process of creating patent network diagrams starts with some essential data preparation 
steps. It is important to pay attention to details when conducting this phase, as every patent 
data set is unique and requires manual time-consuming work. The process: 
1. Initially start Openrefine and important obtained data from The Lens and select 
character encoding UTF-8. 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
2. Remove incomplete patent data, such as lacking applicant information. These can be 
typically removed by using facet and remove all blank cells.  
3. Split multi-valued cells. This is important for both applicants and CPC/IPC 
classification columns as there can be patents with two or more applicants and 
classifications – separated by “;;”. 
4. The CPC/IPC classification column require to be transformed based on desired number 
of digits in the classification. Using custom text transform feature in Openrefine, the 
following expression can be used in General Refine Expression Language (GREL), to 
split the value to 5 digits;  
 
 
splitByLengths(value,5)[0] 
 
5. Focus on ambiguities in applicant data, by using the mentioned merge feature in 
facet. A manual screening is required, as the merger merely detects a minority of the 
actual ambiguity. 
6. Reversely, join multi-valued applicatns and CPC/IPC classification cells. The 
separator of choice should be “;”. 
7. As the method focuses on local applicants, predominantly in Norway and Denmark, 
foreign based applicants must be identified and removed from the data set. These are 
identified by foreign corporate suffixes such as “inc.”, “limited”, GmbH”, “AG” and 
more. Some applicants represent private persons, whom the method is not focused 
on - these are therefore manually identified and removed from the data set. There are 
also some undefinable applicants with e.g. no corporate suffix, requiring a manual 
investigation whether they origin from either a Norwegian or Danish based applicant. 
 
Figure 12: Screenshot of the filtering process using the facet feature in Openrefine 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
8. Lastly, export the file to a XLSX format. Step 7 can also be done after exporting – as 
Excel is a powerful tool when it comes to data processing. 
 
Creating network diagram in Gephi 
 
The next part in the process is the actual making of the network diagram based on a 
normalized and processed patent data set. The method make use of Gephi, a leading open-
source visualization and exploration software (Anon., 2017). Once Gephi is open and fully 
functioning: 
1. The patent data sheet is imported by using a plug-in called “Convert Excel and csv 
files to networks” in Gephi. The plug-in enables the user to connect links (edges) 
between Applicants agents (nodes) and either CPC or IPC classification codes agents 
(nodes).    
2. A network is now created in the “overview section”, where applicants are connected 
to CPC or IPC classification codes. Each network node (agent) will have an attribute 
(called frequency), which represents how many times this agent was present in the 
patent data. The network edge weight represents the number of connections between 
every two agents. 
3. At this stage, the network needs to be converted to a monopartite network as the 
current is a bipartite network. This is done by projection and based on the principle 
of considering a type of nodes (in this case CPC/IPC classifications) as edges. 
Although the graph is losing information the user gain readability and synthesis – 
contributing to improved insights in how patent applicants relates to each other and 
potentially form technology clusters. The Gephi plug-in “Multimode Networks 
Transformation” makes the process user-friendly, whereas the attribute type is 
chosen continued by setting the left matrix as “Applicants-CPC/IPC classification” 
and the right matrix inversely “CPC/IPC classification-Applicants”. Before running the 
plug-in it is important to check “remove nodes” and “remove edges”.  
4. The network is now ready for graphical adjustments, such as applying different 
layouts algorithms. If the network emphasis complementarities, Gephi suggests 
either use of: 
a. ForceAtlas layout (focused on quality – slow, use edge weight, max 10 000 
nodes) 
b. Yifan Hu layout (does not use edge weight, max 1000 nodes) 
c. Frushterman-Reingold layout (fast algorithm, does not use edge weight, max 
100 000 nodes) 
If the network emphasis divisions, Gephi suggest use of: 
d. OpenOrd (aims to distinguish clusters, use edge weight, long edges are cut to 
allows clusters to separate, max 1 000 000 nodes) 
5. Graphical adjustment should be applied regardless of layout use: 
a. Label adjust – adjust labels so they do not overlap 
b. Expansion – expands overall network to make room  
c. NoOverlap – prevent node overlap without while keeping the shape of the 
graph 
6. Further improvement measures should be implemented - such as coloring the nodes 
according to offshore relevance and edges according to frequency and weight.

Results and discussion 
 
The Danish and Norwegian case analyses are discussed in a common section to draw cross-
case conclusions. The cross-case analysis aggregates findings across the cases, where results are 
discussed in an OWP developer’s sourcing and technology management context (Yin, 2003). 
==PAGE:39==
 
39 
 
Cross-case synthesis 
 
The following section compares the analyze and results from the two cases, with an 
elaboration on the method’s strengths and constraints in relation to the research question: 
Can patent data be utilized as an information source, in an OWP developer context, to support 
strategic sourcing processes in finding local suppliers and potential R&D partners? 
 
Strengths 
 
The main patent networks, in both the Danish and Norwegian cases, show how technology 
clusters emerge and group. Since the purposed supplier screening method is based on how 
applicants are related to each other based on patent classifications and common technologies, 
the method can be particularly practical in identifying joint R&D partnerships. If applicants are 
aligned close to each other in the network, it might signal combined effort opportunities in 
specific technological areas. It is estimated that €60 000 million annually are wasted on re-
inventing existing technology according to the Austrian Patent Office (EPO, 2012). Proper 
technology management potentially represents vast effort and cost savings. 
Using OWP oriented keywords, such as “offshore”, and patent classifications with a direct 
industry relevance (e.g. the IPC/CPC classification for wind motors, F03D, used in the case) 
can facilitate improved insights about the supplier landscape’s relation to OWP in the 
networks, as it separates applicants with direct and indirect competencies. The method can be 
used to gain inference on innovation landscapes for technology categories and support 
strategic sourcing.  
From an OWP supplier’s perspective, results from the patent network analysis could be used to 
reflect on current suppliers and R&D partnerships. If e.g. dependency on a tier 1 supplier is 
very high and critical, the method could possibly also support supply chain risk management. 
Investigating suppliers that have a strong correlation, clustering or aligning together, in the 
patent network, based on patent classification, with the critical supplier could indicate similar 
competencies. This can provide a list of alternative suppliers and ease the dependency on tier 1 
suppliers. The patent data and network analysis could also potentially provide insights in 
unknown competencies to a familiar supplier. 
 
Constraints  
 
Obtaining, refining, and analyzing patent data is a time-consuming task whereas identifying 
local suppliers and potential R&D partners can be done using several more established 
methods and techniques such as online supplier databases. Analyzing patent data for local 
suppliers makes it evident that internationally based companies dominate OWP-related 
Danish patent profiles making the local content minimal and the method more suitable for 
finding international suppliers. 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
Using patent data can be more convenient in some supply categories over others. Certain 
categories, such as electronics, prove to be more specific in its nature and therefore more 
manageable with the method. Suppliers shown in the jack-up networks are highly diverse, 
mainly due to the lack of a more specific jack-up classification and that the industry is heavily 
specialized. Due to smart specialization factors (Alkærsig, et al., 2017), it is, especially for small 
economies such as Denmark and Norway, a fact that some competencies cannot be found 
locally, such as the vessel category for Denmark. The method is consequently, and logically, 
most suitable with technology categories where smart specialization is high. 
 
Conclusion 
 
The overall objective, when establishing the purposed method, has been to find out whether 
patent data can be utilized as a strategic resource in local supplier scanning, in support of 
sourcing and improved technology management. The findings indicate that patent data is, by 
using network analysis, compatible for local supplier scanning purposes – especially in areas of 
high smart specialization.  
By identifying and investigating emerging technology clusters in the patent networks, 
potential local R&D partners can be found, in support of technology management. Some 
technology areas are more suitable with the method than others, explained by smart 
specialization theory. The study contributes to supporting the relevance of patent data in 
strategic sourcing and technology management.
