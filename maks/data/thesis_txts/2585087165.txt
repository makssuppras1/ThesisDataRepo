Abstract 
Biochar is increasingly gaining momentum in the context of climate change mitigation and its production in 
Sweden could potentially become a large-scale system. Carbon stability in biochar is a crucial factor to assess 
its the carbon sequestration potential. Currently specific methodologies to assess biochar carbon site-specific 
stability are missing. This work aims at filling in part this knowledge gap assessing stability for Sweden 
specific soil conditions. Moreover, this work aims at assessing biomass feedstock availability for biochar 
production from a system perspective and aims at estimating biochar production and carbon dioxide removal 
potentials in Sweden. Preliminary carbon stability specific thresholds are provided for soils at 10°C 
temperature and, thus, representative for Sweden conditions. Carbon dioxide removal functions are obtained 
for different feedstock categories (woody, herbaceous, biosolids and animal waste) dependent on pyrolysis 
conditions (Highest Treatment Temperature), and conditions for maximum carbon removal are assessed. 
The need for future analysis in order to validate the presented results is highlighted. Future work should 
focus on collecting new experimental results of biochar mineralisation based on the requirements presented 
in this work. An opportunity mapping for biochar production system is provided, focusing on some aspects of 
the interaction of the former with existing systems (agricultural, energy production and waste management). 
From the results of the opportunity mapping, an inventory of the available feedstock for biochar production 
is presented including woody residues, sewage sludge, manure, garden waste and straw. From the available 
feedstocks, biochar production and carbon dioxide removal potentials are estimated to range respectively 
between 0.9 and 1.7 million tbiochar/year and between 2 and 4.2 million tons CO2 sequestered per year (in a 
100 years perspective). In terms of carbon dioxide removal potential, biochar production can significantly 
contribute to the goals set by Sweden in terms of climate change mitigation and emission offsetting for 2030 
and 2045, potentially covering all the measures needed from carbon sinks from forest and land. It was found 
that the most significant contribution derives from the availability of woody residues in Sweden, whose 
analysis should be prioritised for future assessment of feasibility of biochar large scale production. 
Keywords 
Climate change mitigation, biomass, pyrolysis, soil

Introduction 
Biochar has proven to be a highly versatile material with several potential applications in different sectors, 
including environment, agriculture and energy (Leng et al., 2019). In addition, biochar is increasingly gaining 
momentum in the context of climate change mitigation. As a matter of facts, it was recently included in the 
“Special Report: Global Warming of 1.5°C” (SR1.5) by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change 
(IPCC) as a promising carbon dioxide removal solution to be implemented in the Agriculture, Forest and 
other Land Use (AFOLU) sector (IPCC, 2018).  
There are many examples showing the interest for biochar production from both the private and the public 
sectors, especially in Scandinavia (Nordic Biochar Network, 2018), and Sweden is a clear example of this. For 
instance, the Stockholm Biochar project was launched in 2017 and consists in turning garden and park waste 
from urban centres into biochar, producing heat for the city’s district heating system; the produced biochar 
is, then, collected by residents or sold to local authorities as a growing media for plants and trees in the green 
urban areas. The success of this project led other cities and municipalities in Sweden to explore the 
possibility to replicate similar systems (Gustafsson, 2017). Another example involves Stockholm Exergi (SE), 
an energy utility jointly owned by Fortum and Stockholms Stad (Stockholm municipality) which provides 
services of district heating and cooling to the city of Stockholm and to other businesses. SE, in fact, is 
currently investigating the possibility of integrating biochar production among its technologies as part of the 
greater vision to make Stockholm’s district heating become carbon negative (Gustafsson, 2020).  
Considering that in Sweden biochar production could potentially evolve to become a large scale system 
(Gustafsson, 2020), and considering Sweden’s commitment to climate change mitigation measures (Statens 
offentliga utredningar, 2020), there is the need of investigating the potential of this solution at a national 
scale, in order to include the former among viable carbon dioxide removal solutions. 
What is biochar?  
Biochar is a carbon-rich product obtained from the carbonisation of biomass heated at high temperatures 
under low oxygen concentration through a process known as pyrolysis. The feedstock biomass can include 
lignocellulosic materials (e.g. wood and wood residues), agricultural waste and organic waste in general (e.g. 
animal manure and sewage sludge) (Li et al., 2019) and the pyrolysis process can vary according to the 
specific process conditions such as heat transfer, maximum temperature and residence time (Li et al., 2019).  
Biochar’s potential for climate change mitigation mainly derives from the fact that it contains polycyclic 
aromatic carbon structures (Bird et al., 2015), which are formed during the pyrolysis process. In fact, these 
structures make the biochar more recalcitrant to mineralisation (i.e. the conversion of carbonaceous material 
to carbon dioxide) than the original non-pyrolysed biomass (Lehmann, 2015). Thus, converting biomass into 
biochar and using it as soil amendment would ensure sequestering and storing carbon for a period ranging 
from decades up to centuries (IPCC, 2018). In this way the sequestered and stored carbon is prevented from 
being released back into the atmosphere. In addition, biochar has been proven to have other positive effects 
in terms of climate change mitigation when used as a soil amendment. In fact, it can lead to decreased N2O 
and CH4 emissions from soils (Lehmann, 2015) and it can induce a reduction in native soil organic carbon 
mineralisation (negative priming) (Liu et al., 2018). Moreover, biochar can improve soil fertility by 
increasing water retention and nutrient uptake, by regulating soil pH and by enhancing cation exchange 
capacity (Li et al., 2019), reducing the need for mineral fertilisers.  
Carbon stability 
Biochar equivalents deriving from natural fires or anthropogenically produced biochar have been found in 
soils dating back to centuries and millennia (Lehmann, 2015), which supports the evidence that part of 
biochar carbon is recalcitrant to mineralisation for a time ranging from centuries to thousands of years (Fang 
et al., 2014). Quantifying and predicting biochar carbon stability (or recalcitrance) is crucial in order to 
estimate its carbon sequestration and, thus, its climate change mitigation potentials to compare the latter to 
other solutions, and to ensure an optimal use of the biomass resources. However, a mechanistic model which 
allows to predict mineralisation and persistence based on biochar material properties, environmental 
conditions and decomposer dynamics, is currently missing (Lehmann, 2015). A significant part of the current 
knowledge and research on biochar carbon stability focuses on mineralisation experiments. These are field or 
laboratory trials in which biochar is incubated and either CO2 or carbon content are measured to test the 
persistence over time in different conditions. However, these experiments are methodologically constrained, 
as they constitute a short-term assessment from which long-term mineralisation parameters are to be 
quantified (Lehmann, 2015).

Currently in literature there are different examples of methodologies which provide stability estimates based 
on biochar material properties which are proxies of carbon stability. A widely accepted methodology 
retrieved from Budai et al. (2013) provides values of the unmineralised fraction of carbon in biochar at 100 
years (BC+100) based on the H/Corg (i.e. organic carbon) molar ratio of the biochar. H/Corg is an indicator for 
the aromatic structures in the biochar and, thus, in this case, it is chosen as the proxy indicator for stability. 
Only organic C is considered because inorganic C includes carbonates (calcite and dolomite) which are not 
part of the condensed aromatic structure and are not expected to persist over long-time scales. This method 
provides stability values (BC+100) based on a statistical analysis on experimental mineralisation results, 
which are used as conservative estimates of carbon stability. The H/Corg  methodology has been assessed as 
the most suitable method to assess stability for its connection to measured degradation, accessibility and 
acceptance (Söderqvist, 2019) and it has been adopted by the International Biochar Initiative (IBI) as their 
reference Stable C Protocol (Lehmann, 2015). 
Another methodology is presented by the IPCC who provides a method for estimating the change in mineral 
soil organic carbon stocks from biochar amendments (IPCC, 2019), in which the factor Fperm represents the 
fraction of biochar carbon remaining (unmineralised) after 100 years, equivalent to the BC+100 presented by 
Budai et al. (2013). The IPCC provides estimated stability values (Fperm) based on different temperature of 
pyrolysis or Highest Treatment Temperature (HTT) ranges, obtained through a statistical analysis on 
experimental mineralisation results. In this case, the proxy indicator for stability is the HTT of the pyrolysis 
process, as HTT and the degree of aromatisation of carbon structures in biochar are positively correlated 
(Bird et al., 2015). 
There are similarities between these two approaches: both, for instance, use a selection of specific 
mineralisation experiments to perform a statistical analysis and obtain stability estimates. Moreover, it is 
highlighted that both methodologies are only based on biochar material properties and thus, can be said to 
only provide generic results. A drawback of both methods is that they are lacking a differentiation for biochar 
stability dependent on location or site-specific conditions (e.g. environmental conditions). Since the latter 
have a significant influence on carbon stability (Lehmann, 2015), they necessarily need to be considered 
when developing methods to estimate carbon stability. Future research should, therefore,  focus on 
developing location-specific methodologies for biochar carbon stability (Budai, 2020), to be representative 
especially for places or countries such as Sweden which are likely to produce biochar and use it in soils. 
Aim 
The aim of this work is dual: the first goal is to assess biochar carbon stability and to develop preliminary 
estimates for biochar carbon stability in location-specific (Sweden) soil conditions. Moreover, this work aims 
at assessing the potentials for biochar production and carbon sequestration in Sweden. 
Objectives 
The research objectives of this work are: 
- 
To obtain preliminary confidence thresholds of carbon stability for biochar for Sweden specific soil 
conditions 
- 
To assess appropriate conditions for maximum carbon sequestration, considering pyrolysis 
parameters, carbon stability and biomass feedstock  
- 
To assess potential feedstock availability for biochar production in Sweden in the present conditions  
- 
To quantify biochar production and carbon sequestration potentials for selected available feedstocks 
in the present conditions 
Limitations 
The focus of this work is the use of biochar in soil and thus, literature review and data collection were focused 
around this specific use. Results and conclusions are to be related to this specific use of biochar as all other 
uses were disregarded in the stability analysis. More specifically, the use of biochar in soil is to be intended as 
limited to agricultural sector. Additionally, the concept of location-specificity used in this work is limited to 
Sweden.

Methods 
The methods used in this study are comprised of two parts: the first part (see section Part 1) describes the 
procedure used to obtain biochar carbon stability thresholds and closely follows the methodology provided 
by the IPCC (2019) described in the introduction. The followed steps in this part consisted of: 
- 
Preliminary operations on the dataset (processing, cleaning, and selection) 
- 
Calculation of necessary data (BC+100) from mineralisation experimental results retrieved from the 
dataset 
- 
Data preparation (division in classes) and statistical analysis to obtain a 95% confidence interval (i.e. 
the chosen stability thresholds) 
- 
Uncertainty and sensitivity analyses 
The second part (see section Part 2) focused on feedstock availability, biochar production and carbon 
sequestration potentials. The followed steps in this part consisted of: 
- 
Development of Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) functions 
- 
Inventory of specific feedstocks and opportunity mapping for biochar production  
- 
Estimation of biochar production and carbon dioxide removal potentials (BP and CDRP) 
Part 1 
Dataset 
A comprehensive set of biochar mineralisation experiments in soil was retrieved by Lehmann (2015) which 
includes results from various biochar samples (feedstock, production process), different experimental 
settings (type of study, temperature of mineralisation, period of assessment, soil type) and different methods 
of calculation of mineralisation. This dataset was chosen for the analysis mainly for the following reasons:  
- 
It is the most comprehensive collection of biochar mineralisation experiments including experiments 
performed until 2015. 
- 
It includes additional information about pyrolysis process and experimental conditions (e.g. Highest 
Treatment Temperature, residence time, feedstock, soil types). 
- 
It presents mineralisation data, namely the Mean Residence Time (MRT), adjusted to 10°C soil 
temperature. 
The last point, in particular, is utterly significant for the purpose of this work. In fact, using data adjusted to 
that specific soil temperature allows to consider location specificity for Sweden in the stability analysis, which 
is part of the aim of this work. More on the concept and methods behind temperature adjustment can be 
found in later sections of this work (see Temperature adjustment). The choice of a reference soil temperature 
of 10°C was justified by an assessment on environmental conditions of soils in Sweden. In fact, most of 
Swedish soils are classified as Cryic (USDA-NRCS, 1997) according to the soil temperature regime 
classification, with a mean annual temperature lower than 8°C (Rossiter, n.d.) and only a smaller fraction of 
soils is classified as either Mesic or Pergelic (USDA-NRCS, 1997), with temperatures ranges 8-15°C and < 
0°C, respectively (USDA, 2015). Thus, it was assumed that such reference temperature (10°C) could relatively 
well represent most of Swedish soils. This assumption seems to be valid even considering results from 
models predicting soil temperature increase due to climate change provided by Jungqvist et al. (2014). 
Data processing, cleaning and selection 
As the initial dataset was retrieved in a format that could not be directly analysed, it was necessary to process 
it in order for it to be suitable for data and statistical analyses. Then, a process of data cleaning was 
performed comparing each processed data with the original source, in order to avoid potential errors. 
The criteria for selection of suitable data for the purpose of the study were based on the criteria presented in 
the analyses performed by Budai et al. (2013), Lehmann (2015) and the IPCC (2019). In fact, these three 
analyses select their data to perform an analysis on biochar carbon stability based on duration of the 
experiment and on the method used to obtain mineralisation parameters. The criteria used in this work were 
the following: 
- 
Experimental duration greater than 1 year 
Experimental duration significantly affects the estimated biochar mineralisation parameters, as  
Wang et al. (2016) concluded from their meta-analysis on 128 experiments, which showed a 
decreasing pattern of experimental biochar decomposition rates by increasing experimental

duration. This result is consistent with basic theory and experimental evidence, as the decomposition 
during the initial period (first year) of incubation is more likely to affect the pyrolysis co-products of 
the char (Kuzyakov, 2019). Thus, experiments shorter than one year generally underestimate carbon 
stability in biochar, as the measured mineralisation does not reflect the behaviour of the more stable 
carbon structures in the biochar. This justifies the criterion of experimental duration greater than 
one year used for the selection of data. 
- 
Double or triple exponential model fitting 
During biochar incubation experiments, either CO2 fluxes or the remaining carbon in soil are 
measured through isotope labelling which allow to estimate the evolution of biochar carbon over 
time. Then, exponential decay models are generally used to fit a mineralisation curve from the 
measurements to obtain mineralisation parameters such as the Mean Residence Time (MRT) or the 
decay rate (k) (Leng et al., 2019). Single exponential models assume a single pool of carbon in 
biochar with a single decay rate (Leng et al., 2019), whereas multiple exponential decay models 
(double, triple or infinite) assume that biochar is composed of different pools of carbon, with 
different decay rates (Leng et al., 2019). Experiments which used double or triple exponential models 
to fit the mineralisation curve were selected for this work, and mineralisation data obtained with 
single exponential model were disregarded. This is because it is proven that double or triple 
exponential curves provide a better fit than single exponential ones (Kuzyakov, 2019), and the latter 
ones do not represent well the real mineralisation behaviour especially on the long term. Infinite 
pool models provide adequate results but they are rare due to the high requirements of experimental 
measurements (Kuzyakov, 2019, Leng et al., 2019) and were not available in the original dataset. 
Selected data are visible in the Appendix (Table 13).

anhydrosugars and methoxylated phenols; the semilabile one is composed of polycyclic aromatic compounds 
with ring number lower than 7; stable pool is composed of polycyclic aromatic compounds with ring number 
higher than 7. This presented model was simplified for the purpose of this work, assuming no distinction 
between semilabile and stable pools. The reason behind this choice is that the distinction between semilabile 
and stable pool suggested by Bird et al. (2015) is only based on the number of the polycyclic rings and the 
related expected difference in minerasability by microbes in soils. In the dataset not all experiments were 
performed with microbial inoculant and thus, considering a third semilabile pool, highly degradable by 
microbes for all the experiments would have led to unrealistic assumptions about the experimental 
mineralisation of the original sample. In addition, the majority of experiments used a double exponential 
fitting model, thus, assuming three pools would have meant assuming additional mineralisation parameters 
adding potential uncertainty to the results. Assuming only labile and stable pool in this work, was considered 
also, more consistent with the approach and results of pool sizes obtained by  Wang et al. (2016). In this way, 
c2 could be estimated using the empirical curve showed in the Appendix (Figure 12), whereas c1 was obtained 
as the remaining fraction of carbon (c1 = 1-c2). The value of MRT1 was assumed to be 108 days (roughly 0.3 
years) consistently with the findings in  the meta-analysis performed by Wang et al. (2016).

95% confidence interval calculation 
The 95% confidence interval was calculated as it allows to have more information about the precision of an 
estimate, which in this case is the mean of the BC+100 for each of the temperature classes. The choice of 
using HTT as the proxy indicator in this work influenced the statistical analysis on the data: in fact, the non-
linear relationship between HTT and BC+100 (IPCC, 2019) did not allow to do use a linear regression to 
obtain the confidence interval. It was, therefore, decided to perform a bootstrap analysis on different classes 
of samples to obtain the confidence intervals, instead of a linear or polynomial regression. The bootstrap is a 
widely applicable statistical tool belonging to the simulation methods (Navidi, 2011) which was also used by 
the IPCC for its stability analysis (IPCC, 2019). When a sample is drawn from a non-normal distribution, 
==PAGE:11==
6 
 
confidence intervals can be obtained through bootstrap methods. As the probability distribution of the 
sample of BC+100 is not known, a non-parametric bootstrap was used. The underlying assumption was that 
each class represents a statistical population of BC+100 with an unknown probability distribution function 
and the experiments in each class are a sample of the population. 
These steps were followed to obtain the 95% confidence interval of the mean BC+100 for each of the 
temperature categories mentioned previously (Navidi, 2011): 
- 
Drawing bootstrap samples (10000) from the data, with the same size of original sample and drawn 
with replacement. 
- 
Computing the sample mean for each bootstrap sample 
- 
Computing the 2.5% and 97.5% percentiles (x.025, x.975) which denote the lower and upper values of 
the 95% confidence interval. 
Similarly to what shown by Budai et al. (2013) the x.025 was be taken as the stability threshold for the specific 
HTT class. The choice of the lower value of the 95% confidence interval as the value for carbon stability is 
justified by its statistical meaning: in fact, being X.025 the lower value of the interval, there is 2.5% confidence 
that the true mean of the BC+100 will be lower than X.025 and a 97.5% confidence that it will be higher than 
X.025. This same approach can be considered as a conservative approach to estimate carbon stability, based 
on the available experimental data. 
Uncertainty analysis 
A qualitative uncertainty analysis was performed focusing on the temperature adjustment and on the 
methodological assumption related to the carbon pools (size, number and mineralisation parameters) and 
BC+100 calculation. The analysis consisted in classifying uncertainty according to three dimensions as 
defined by Warmink et al. (2010):  
“(1) the location, which is where the uncertainty manifests itself [...], (2) the level, which is where the 
uncertainty manifests itself along the (continuous) spectrum between deterministic knowledge and total 
ignorance, and (3) the nature of the uncertainty.[…] five possible locations of uncertainty (are): (a) context 
uncertainty, (b) input uncertainty, (c) model uncertainty, which consists of model structure uncertainty and 
model technical uncertainty, (d) parameter uncertainty, and (e) uncertainty in the model outcomes. The 
levels of uncertainty range from statistical uncertainty and scenario uncertainty through recognized 
ignorance to total ignorance. For the last dimension, they distinguish between epistemic uncertainty (due to 
a lack of knowledge) and variability uncertainty (due to the variability in the behaviour of the natural, social, 
economic or technical system)”. 
Sensitivity analysis on methodological approach 
A sensitivity analysis was performed in order to assess the sensitivity of the results to the methodological 
approach, focusing on temperature adjustment and the choice of the model used to estimate BC+100 (single, 
double or triple exponential pool and mineralisation rates of pools). Additional bootstrap analyses were 
performed on the dataset using different approaches and were compared to the reference method (REF) used 
in this work. 
In the first approach (A1), the same HTT classification as the reference was used, and BC+100 values were 
estimated through a double exponential decay model. The sizes of the labile and stable pools were obtained 
according to the HTT, in the same way as described for the reference approach. The MRT of the labile pool 
was set to 108 days, as for the reference, whereas the ‘Persistence as reported’ value was taken as the MRT of 
the stable pool. As presented previously, this factor represents the MRT obtained in the original experiment, 
related to the original temperature of mineralisation and not adjusted to 10°C. Therefore, this approach 
allowed to assess the sensitivity of the results to the adjustment of the mineralisation temperature. 
In the second approach (A2), the HTT classes were kept as the reference. However, to estimate the BC+100 
values, a single exponential decay was used, assuming that 100% of the biochar carbon belonged to the stable 
pool. Thus, only a single MRT was needed and it was chosen to use the values adjusted to 10°C. In A3 and A4, 
a triple exponential decay was used including the semilabile pool which was disregarded and merged with the 
stabile pool for the reference analysis. The MRT of the labile pool was set to 108 days, and for the stable pool 
the adjusted MRT was used, as for the reference. The semilabile MRT was set equal to 10 years and 90 years, 
respectively for A3 and A4, to assess the sensitivity to different mineralisation rates of the semilable pool.

Part 2 
CDR functions 
The purpose of biochar production can be different as presented by Lehmann (2015): in this work, the focus 
was to assess potential production in the context of carbon sequestration for climate change mitigation and 
therefore, carbon dioxide removal (CDR) functions were developed. These functions represent the equivalent 
amount of CO2 (mass) that is originally sequestered by the feedstock biomass and stored in the form of 
biochar organic carbon in a 100 years’ timeframe per unit mass of feedstock biomass. The functions include a 
yield factor to account for the mass of biochar produced from the feedstock undergoing the pyrolysis process, 
a factor that represents the amount of initial organic carbon content of biochar (FC), a factor to account for 
the estimated mineralisation of organic biochar carbon (BC100) and a factor to convert mass of organic 
carbon to the equivalent mass of CO2 (CF).

The BC100 is the stability stepwise function obtained in the first part of this work (lower limits of the 95% 
confidence intervals for each HTT class), and it provides the unmineralised carbon in biochar after 100 years 
as a function of pyrolysis temperature. The yield functions were obtained from the meta-analysis performed 
by Li et al. (2019) on 154 different independent studies. In their analysis Li et al. (2019) show that pyrolysis 
temperature (HTT) affects biochar yield and the carbon content biochar (FC) and empirical fitting functions 
for these factors based on the HTT were provided. These functions were retrieved from the source using a 
web plot digitiser tool (Rohatgi, 2019), obtaining stepwise values of yield and FC. These values were obtained 
for HTT ranging from 250 to 650°C, consistently with the range of HTT available from the dataset and used 
to assess the stability function BC+100. In this step the following assumptions were made: firstly, the FC 
factor only included organic carbon, disregarding inorganic carbon fraction (e.g. carbonates). Secondly, the 
yield was assumed to be expressed in dry matter (kg biochar dry/kg feedstock dry).

(5) 
 
One function was obtained for each of the biomass category defined by Li et al. (2019) (Animal Waste, 
Woody, Herbaceous and Biosolids), since the yield and the FC functions were specific for each biomass type. 
It is pointed out that, on the other hand, BC100 function was not feedstock specific. It can be seen that the 
final CDR functions are HTT dependent, which allows to assess how CDR is related to the pyrolysis process.

Preliminary opportunity mapping  
In order to assess the realistic availability of each feedstock for biochar production it was necessary to 
perform a preliminary opportunity mapping. As described by Lehmann (2015) opportunity mapping for 
biochar production system requires taking a system perspective in order to assess biochar biophysical and 
socioeconomic system fit. Lehmann (2015) categorises the overall integrated system in three phases 
(biomass, conversion and use phases) and for each of those specific environmental and socio-economic 
factors are identified. Moreover, the interaction with the other systems is highlighted in the different phases. 
In the specific case of this work the analysis was limited to certain aspects taken from Lehmann (2015) which 
are summarised as follows: 
- 
Biomass phase: 
o 
Systems: waste, farming (agriculture) and forestry. 
o 
Environmental and socio-economic factors: feedstock production, price or economic value of 
feedstock, transport & storage of feedstock, current uses of feedstock. 
- 
Conversion phase (i.e. pyrolysis process): 
o 
Environmental and socio-economic factors: suitability of feedstock. 
- 
Use phase (of produced biochar):  
o 
Systems: climate (C sequestration), farming (agriculture) 
o 
Environmental and socio-economic factors: regulations and standards. 
The needed information to assess each aspect of the considered system were retrieved through an extensive 
literature review. Missing information was retrieved through experts’ advice and experience. In addition, 
each of the mentioned factors together with other feedstock specific factors were categorised as being either a 
constraint or an opportunity to the implementation of a biochar production system.  
For this part of the work all retrieved information were assumed to be representative for present and near 
future conditions of the system: for instance, present feedstock production was considered and future 
changes or trends were not addressed; the same can be said for all the assessed components of the system. 
The final assessment on the availability of feedstock was the result of a qualitative assessment on the 
integrated systems.

Carbon Dioxide Removal functions 
The CDR stepwise functions for each feedstock are represented graphically in Figure 2. It is clearly visible how

Uncertainty analysis 
Temperature adjustment  
It is necessary to highlight that the 10°C adjusted MRT values were not calculated in this work, but were 
retrieved directly from Lehmann (2015). From the selected dataset (Appendix - Table 13), it can be noted that 
none of the included experiments are performed at an incubation temperature of 10°C, as all show am 
original mineralisation temperature above this value. It is stated from Lehmann that MRTs were adjusted, 
using Q10 values which are factors generally related to soil respiration and which represent the increase in 
soil respiration resulting from an increase in soil temperature of 10°C (Meyer et al., 2018). This same factor 
(Q10 ) is generally used in literature to describe the difference in biochar mineralisation with a 10°C increase 
in temperature (Fang et al., 2014, Lehmann, 2015, Budai et al., 2013). Lehmann (2015) states that the Q10 
which were used were retrieved from an empirical function represented in Figure 3, however, it is not clear 
which values were used, how they were related to original incubation temperature and how the Q10 factors are 
related to the MRT.

mechanisms (fitting method and the results of the fitting of the mineralisation curve). The nature is lack of 
knowledge (or epistemic) which could be filled with more transparency and completeness in input data.

Feedstock inventory and potential mapping 
In the following section the results related to the feedstock inventory and potential mapping are presented 
going through each assessed feedstock. An overview of feedstock availability is represented in Figure 6 and a 
summary of the results of the opportunity mapping is provided in Table 14 (Appendix). 
Woody residues 
Feedstock production 
Most residues from Swedish forest are currently exploited for district heating and biofuel production 
(International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019). However, a fraction of the harvested biomass which 
includes stumps, roots and slash (branches and tops) remains unexploited and these residues are currently 
left to decompose at the thinning and final felling sites (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019). 
According to the findings of the report by International Renewable Energy Agency (2019), it was estimated 
that this unused fraction in 2015 corresponded to approximately 55.4 TWh of branches and tops (slash) and 
83 TWh of stumps and roots (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019). Considering a Lower Heating 
Value (LHV) of 17.6 MJ/kg (de Jong et al., 2017) it was estimated that in terms of mass the mentioned figures 
correspond to 11300 kt of slash and 16900 kt of stumps and roots for a total of 28200 kt of forestry residues, 
currently left to decompose. 
Constraints  
Price 
Forestry residues are purchased from the supplier per megawatt hour of energy content of the fuel: for 
energy slash the price is 65 SEK/MWh (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019) which, considering a 
LHV of 17.6 MJ/kg (de Jong et al., 2017), corresponds to approximately 317 SEK/ton. This factor needs to be 
taken into account when assessing cost-benefits of using this feedstock for other purposes than simply 
conversion to energy, since the price of the resource is directly dependent on the energy content.  
Current use  
Part of the slash is currently used for district heating purposes (International Renewable Energy Agency, 
2019), and therefore it is possible that there could be competition for this resource which could hinder the 
availability for biochar production. However, it is likely that pyrolysis process will be included in the context 
of district heating production, as showed by the Stockholm Biochar project and the project of Stockholm 
Exergi, presented in the introduction. Thus, it is possible that biochar production and district heating would 
work in a unique system, partly reducing the possible competition for the biomass resource. Other woody 
residues such as stumps may show a lower competition as they are not currently exploited (International 
Renewable Energy Agency, 2019). 
Environmental quality objectives 
As shown by de Jong et al. (2017) an increased exploitation of these resources is constrained by the 
environmental quality objectives (EQO) adopted by Sweden, namely ‘Sustainable Forests’ and ‘Natural 
Acidification Only’ for slash and ‘Sustainable Forests’ for stumps. In terms of ‘Sustainable Forests’ it is in fact 
required that biological diversity is preserved, which means that additional harvesting of slash and stumps 
must be planned in order not to affect the (de Jong et al., 2017) “composition of forest habitats, the 
abundance of crucial resources, the continuity of resources and connectivity at the landscape level”. 
Additional harvesting of both stumps and slash may alter the ecosystem and may be detrimental towards 
reaching this EQO. As for ‘Natural Acidification Only’ it was proven that harvest of slash affects soil and soil 
water acidification in the form of reduced base cations (de Jong et al., 2017). 
Opportunities 
Transport & Storage 
As mentioned previously, part of the slash is already exploited for district heating: its management includes 
the storage at the logging or thinning site, the natural drying and, when the biomass is needed as fuel, the 
chipping and transport to the heat plant (International Renewable Energy Agency, 2019). Having these 
management processes already in place could significantly facilitate the use of this resource in a biochar 
production system in Sweden. 
Availability 
In his work de Jong et al. (2017) show that “ash can be harvested on 50% of the annually thinned and clear-
cut areas in Sweden” and “stumps can be harvested on up to 10–20% of the clear-cut area” without reducing 
the possibility of achieving the environmental objectives mentioned previously. This would correspond to 70 
and 100 PJ of additional harvestable biomass from the forestry sector (de Jong et al., 2017), considering

For sludge to be pyrolysed, its humidity ideally needs to be lower than 10% (Kosov et al., 2015). Therefore,

production which would depend on how sludge is treated at the wastewater treatment plant. Moreover, the 
highly variable composition of wastewater sludge may as well represent a constraint for its suitability for 
pyrolysis.

Opportunities 
Economic value 
Generally, sludge can be considered as a waste and in this sense, its price is likely to be very low or even 
negative, meaning that sludge producers may be willing to pay potential sludge users to avoid disposal costs 
(Badgett et al., 2019). This can significantly benefit sludge use for biochar production, as it would mean 
producing a valuable product from a low value and cost input.  
Sludge P trilemma 
The Ministry of the Environment (2018) presented an inquiry which is expected to propose a ban on the 
spreading of sewage sludge on farmland, due to the risk related to the reintroduction in the environment of 
substances contained in the. Moreover, the inquiry should produce proposals for a requirement to recycle 
phosphorus from sewage sludge (Ministry of the Environment, 2018). This issue clearly highlights the sludge 
trilemma (Thomsen, 2019), according to which a sludge treatment process is needed which should ensure 
phosphorus recovery, risk mitigation due to harmful substances and which should be economically and 
technically feasible. As highlighted by Thomsen (2019), pyrolysis represents a possible solution which could 
be successfully applied in this context. 
REVAQ certification system 
Nearly half of the wastewater treatment plants in Sweden are part of the REVAQ system which allows the 
plants to obtain a certification for their sludge ensuring that the wastewater treatment process are designed 
to bind nutrients to the sludge so to reintroduce the latter in the environment through spreading of sludge on 
agricultural land. Moreover, through this system, xenobiotics and unwanted pollutants contained in the 
sludge are regularly monitored (Swedish Environmental Protection Agency, n.d.). In this way, the sludge 
could be selected in order to avoid the risk of introducing unwanted substances in agricultural soils. In this 
context, it is likely to assume that the usable sludge for biochar production to avoid the mentioned risk is the 
fraction of the total already used on farmland. 
Transport & Storage 
Since sludge is used for different purposes it is expected that there is already an existing system of transport 
and storage of the former, connecting the wastewater facility to the final use location. Therefore, the issues 
related to these processes should be already dealt with in the existing system and should not limit the 
integration of sludge in a biochar production system.  
Availability 
Considering that all the sludge is already used but it is possible that the spreading on farmland could be 
banned in the future, the available sludge for biochar production was limited to the fraction which is spread 
on farmland. The other fractions were disregarded mainly for the competition of the resource, the 
composition of the sludge (pollutants) and the missing information on the current use.  
Thus, it was estimated that the availability could be of 54.98 kton/year calculated as the average of the sludge 
spread on farmland between 2008-2016 (Statistics Sweden, 2018). 
Manure 
Feedstock production 
It is estimated that manure generation from cattle, pig, horse, poultry and sheep in Sweden amounts to 2716 
kt dry matter/year. This estimation is presented by Lonnqvist (2012) who assessed the results of 13 different 
studies about biomass potential for biogas production. 
Constraints  
Suitability 
The composition of the different manure feedstocks significantly affects the pyrolysis process and the 
conversion of biomass to gas, liquid or char products (Shannon Hollis, n.d.). Horse manure is considered to 
be the most suitable for biochar production, mainly because equine manure generally contains a greater 
amount of carbon compared to manure from other sources (swine, cattle, poultry) (Shannon Hollis, n.d., 
Wen-Tien Tsai, 2015, Gustafsson, 2020). As for sludge, manure may need to undergo additional processes 
such as dewatering and drying before pyrolysis according to handling at farm, which may affect the 
possibility to use the source as pyrolysis feedstock. 
==PAGE:24==
19 
 
Current use 
Part of the manure is currently used as organic fertiliser on agricultural fields (Statistics Sweden, 2014, 
Jordbruks verket, 2013), as a source of nitrogen and it must be pointed out that the pyrolysis of manure 
eliminates its fertilising properties (Sundberg, 2020), since nitrogen at high pyrolysis temperatures is 
present at low percentages (Li et al., 2019). This aspect may hinder the use of this resource for biochar 
production. Other uses are expected to be primarily combustion or biogas production, as in the case of horse 
manure (Jordbruks verket, 2013). It is not possible for the case of manure to allocate manure fractions to 
each of the mentioned uses.  
Transport & Storage 
Wet agricultural residues can be generally found in rural and urban areas in some cases (Lonnqvist, 2012), 
and it is, therefore, expectable to assume  that this resource will be generally produced in relatively low 
amounts per location of production and widely distributed over the territory. This may be an issue in the 
context of a large-scale biochar production system with few industrial scale production facilities, as the 
collection and transport of this resource could be not technically-economically viable. On the other hand, this 
aspect may promote the development of a smaller scale and more distributed biochar production system, 
ideally closer to each manure production site. 
Opportunities 
Economic value 
The same can be said for manure as what stated for sludge: the former can be considered as a waste with a 
very low or even negative value. Similarly to the case of sludge, this factor can enhance manure use for 
biochar production, as it would mean producing a valuable product from a low value input. 
Availability 
Since horse manure was assessed to be the most suitable among other manure types, it was assumed for the 
purpose of this work to consider only this source as technically viable and available for biochar production. It 
was found out that 2700 kton of horse manure are produced in Sweden yearly (Jordbruks verket, 2013). 
Comparing it with the quantification provided by Lonnqvist (2012), it seems reasonable to assume that the 
former figure is related to the wet matter. The solid content of manure depends on the management system 
in place at the farm level: in this case it was assumed that the figure presented by Jordbruks verket (2013) is 
related to the raw manure with a solid content of 8% (Bhatia, 2014). Thus, the final available horse manure 
would be 216 kt/year dry matter.  
Garden waste 
Feedstock production 
Total data about garden waste generation in Sweden could not be retrieved, and it was necessary to 
investigate waste management and treatment inventories. Data from Avfall Sverige (2018) show that the 
amount of generated garden going to central composting was 251.45 kt in 2017. Additionally, garden waste 
can be used in order to produce digestate, biogas or used for energy recovery Avfall Sverige (2018), however, 
the amount of garden waste for each of these uses could not be retrieved. 
Constraints 
Current use 
As mentioned previously, garden waste is currently treated in central composting plants: compost can 
generally be used as a soil conditioner and plant growth media, and as a source of nutrients as organic 
fertiliser (Smith, 2001). 
Suitability 
Garden waste is generally composed of two fractions: a fraction which includes fallen fruits from trees and 
another one which comprises leaves and branches. Only the latter one was considered as suitable for 
pyrolysis and biochar production which was assumed to account for 50% of the total dry matter weight 
(Gustafsson, 2020). 
Transport & Storage 
It is expected that most of the garden waste will be distributed over the territory in the proximity of urban 
areas. This, similarly to what stated for animal manure, can either be a constraint or an opportunity 
according to the biochar production system (large scale or small scale). 
==PAGE:25==
20 
 
Opportunities 
Alternative to compost 
According to results from field studies it is possible to assume that compost may be at a certain extent 
replaceable by or integrated with biochar: Miguel et al. (2019) showed that biochar-blended compost (10:90 
w:w) was effective in increasing soil organic carbon content, soil fertility and enhancing for quality 
parameters for certain crops. This could be an opportunity for part of the garden waste to be redirected from 
compost production to biochar production: the cascading use of biochar could ensure additional benefits and 
still provide the ones resulting from compost use. 
Economic value 
Similarly to sludge and animal manure, being this feedstock a waste it is likely to have a very low or even 
negative price, enhancing its use for biochar production, as it would mean producing a valuable product from 
a low value input. 
Availability 
The available garden waste for biochar production was estimated as the amount going to central composting 
adjusted to the suitable fraction (50%) for a total of 125.72 kt/year.  The year 2017 was taken as 
representative for current and near future garden waste production and availability. 
Straw 
Feedstock production 
The residual straw available for bioenergy purposes, which is the surplus of the straw not used for fodder and 
bedding, amounts to approximately 1000 kt/year (Nilsson, 2009). 
Constraints 
Current use 
As stated previously, part of the straw produced is used for fodder and bedding in animal husbandry 
(Nilsson, 2009). Additionally straw is currently used as a fuel; it was estimated that the current use of straw 
as fuel adds up to a total of 112 kt/year in Sweden divided as follows (IEA Bioenergy, 2016):  
- 
100 small scale local heat production (50 kt/year) 
- 
40 medium scale local heat provision with excess for sale (50 kt/year) 
- 
4 medium scale conversion and district heating (12 kt/year) 
Price 
Similarly to forestry residues, the cost of straw is likely to be determined by its energy content as fuel (IEA 
Bioenergy, 2016),  which may be a constraint when considering producing biochar for other purposes than 
simply conversion to energy. 
Availability 
Since it was not possible to assess whether the surplus straw from animal husbandry (1000 kt/year) included 
the fraction sold for energy purposes, in order not to overestimate the availability of straw, the latter was 
removed from the surplus straw. Thus, it was estimated that the available straw amounts to 888 kt/year. 
Biochar use phase 
An analysis on the land use in Sweden showed that the total arable agricultural land in Sweden amounts to 
2590052 ha (Statistics Sweden, 2019): this figure was taken as representative in order to assess the amount 
of existing land where biochar could potentially be applied. Other land categories were not included for 
biochar application as it was not part of the aim of this work.

Discussion 
Methodological approach 
It is highlighted that the presented approach used to obtain stability thresholds is comparable with the two 
methodologies presented in the introduction, as they all rely on biochar or process properties used as proxies 
for the final stability of biochar carbon. As presented by Lehmann (2015): 
“The material property most likely responsible for the relative persistence of charred organic matter 
compared to uncharred organic matter are the fused aromatic C […]. Plant residues do not contain fused 
aromatic C forms, which are created by charring and increase in proportion e.g. with higher pyrolysis 
temperature and duration […]”. 
Therefore, a valid proxy should indicate the degree of aromatisation of carbon structures in biochar in order 
to represent the overall expectable persistence of carbon. In this work, similarly to the IPCC (2019) 
methodology, HTT was chosen as a proxy for aromatisation and, therefore, for stability: this is because the 
proportion of aromatic carbon is positively correlated with pyrolysis temperature. However, there are other 
factors that affects the final proportion of aromatic carbon structures in biochar such as the ash content of 
the feedstock, the feedstock itself as well as other process conditions such as residence time of the pyrolysis 
process, which are not represented by HTT. Another proxy has been used and accepted, namely the H/Corg 
ratio presented in the introduction (Budai et al., 2013) which directly gives an indication of the aromatic 
structures: the highest the ratio, the more H there are in respect to carbon which means that the less 
saturated carbon bonds there are in the biochar. 
==PAGE:30==
25 
 
Given this, H/Corg may be a more appropriate indicator for aromatisation than HTT since it entails directly 
measuring a biochar property. However, the ratio is not always reported in the description of the 
mineralisation experiments or in some cases it is mentioned as H/C (not organic C) without indications on 
the content of ash which could allow somehow to adjust the H/C to the H/Corg: this could be explained by the 
fact that obtaining the H/Corg entails additional measurements which do not contribute to the success of the 
experiments and that are not needed. On the other hand, it was assessed that the HTT is always reported as it 
is one of the most significant parameters to characterise the pyrolysis process and, thus, the biochar. 
Therefore, in this work it was chosen to use HTT as the proxy which still provides an indication on the degree 
of aromatic carbon in the biochar and allows a greater availability of data based on current research. 
Considering the bootstrap approach, this has been proven to be effective with relatively small samples, as in 
this case. However, this method implicitly assumes that the samples are representative of the entire 
population, which in this work corresponds to the HTT classes. In this case the representativity of the sample 
can be questioned, especially in terms of types of feedstock and process conditions. Therefore, in order to 
improve future analyses, the representativity of the collected data should be taken into account. Some 
suggestions of how the representativity of the data could be improved are provided in the section Additional 
experimental data. 
Location specificity 
A major part of this work is motivated by the need to develop location specific estimates of carbon stability in 
biochar. Therefore, it may be argued if soil temperature, used in this work to define location specificity, is an 
adequate parameter for this purpose. Soil temperature is a key environmental parameter which significantly 
affects physical, biological and chemical processes occurring in the soil system: in soils located in colder 
climates, for instance, rates of biological decomposition are slower compared to soils in warmer climates 
(Brady, 1999). Since biological decomposition is one of the main processes determining biochar carbon 
mineralisation, soil temperature can be considered as a significant factor, with increasing temperatures 
leading to higher mineralisation of biochar carbon (Lehmann, 2015). This assumption is also supported by 
experimental results in which samples of the same biochar were incubated at different temperatures and 
which confirm that increasing incubation temperature increases carbon mineralisation (Fang et al., 2014, 
Nguyen et al., 2010). Therefore, a first step towards providing location specific biochar stability estimates 
should include the temperature of the soils where biochar will be used in the analysis. 
However, there are other aspects which are to be assessed when developing such location specific 
approaches. For instance, soil moisture is another major factor controlling mineralisation of organic carbon 
in soils and it can potentially affect biochar carbon mineralisation (Kuzyakov, 2019, Lehmann, 2015). A well-
established conceptual model shows how soil microbial activity is limited both by lack of substrate and lack 
of oxygen diffusion which occur at very low and very high levels of soil moisture, respectively (Li, 2016). 
Therefore, location specific soil moisture conditions should be assessed when estimating stability of biochar 
carbon in soils. In addition, to fully characterise location specificity, also microbial activity and its dynamics 
should be assessed as it would directly represent the level of potential biological mineralisation that the 
biochar may undergo. The three mentioned factors soil temperature, moisture and microbial activity 
(decomposer dynamics) are needed in the assessment in order for any mineralisation model to be aligned 
with basic soil science theory (Lehmann, 2015). 
Considerations about uncertainty and sensitivity analysis 
The uncertainty of temperature adjustment and the assumptions on carbon pools parameters were 
qualitatively categorised and were found to be comparable in terms of level, nature and location: overall it 
can be seen that for both, uncertainty is related somehow to missing information in the input data. Moreover, 
results have proven to be sensitive to both temperature adjustment and methodological approach to estimate 
BC+100 (which include assumptions of carbon pools). However, it can be stated that overall, the 
methodological approach to calculate BC+100 accounts for a higher change in the results. Both temperature 
adjustment and methodological approach are to be considered as key parameters with comparable 
uncertainty and sensitivity and it will be necessary to attempt to close this knowledge gap in future studies 
focusing on the transparency of experimental results and methodological approach. Considering the 
generally higher sensitivity of the results to the methodological approach to obtain BC+100, it may be 
advisable to prioritise the latter. 
==PAGE:31==
26 
 
Additional experimental data 
A key aspect that should be considered in order to improve stability assessment in the future is certainly, 
together with more transparency in the existing data, the need for additional biochar mineralisation 
experiments. Through the presented analysis it was possible to highlight which aspects should be prioritised 
in experimental data collection in order to improve future location specific stability analyses, which are listed 
and explained as follows: 
- 
Missing data – HTT coverage 
A first aspect that should be considered is the lack of data in terms of HTT coverage: namely it can be 
seen  that there are no experimental data in the HTT range between 250 °C and 350°C; moreover, 
additional experiments should be performed with biochar obtained at HTT greater than 650°C to be 
representative for pyrolysis processes which generally exceed that temperature. 
- 
Feedstock categories 
The dominant feedstock categories in terms of collected mineralisation results overall HTT are 
woody (WD) and herbaceous (HB), whereas less experiments were performed using animal waste 
(AW) and biosolids (BS) derived biochars (see Appendix - Figure 20). Since future biochar 
production systems are expected to exploit also the two latter feedstocks, future studies should 
gather additional experimental evidence of stability of biochar derived from these sources and 
produced at HTT which cover the presented range. This would also ideally allow to perform stability 
analysis with feedstock specific results. 
- 
Residence time 
Most of biochar samples were produced with a pyrolysis residence time of 40 minutes or 3 hours. As 
for the rest, most samples were produced with 24 hours residence times with very few exceeding this 
figure (48 and 72 hours) (see Appendix - Figure 21). Considering that the residence time of pyrolysis 
processes generally do not exceed 30 minutes (Forte, 2012), future experiments should take into 
account this factor and should be tailored to represent the most common production processes. In 
this case, biochar produced with a residence time of more than 30 minutes would not be 
representative for anthropogenic processes, but more for other processes such as natural wildfire 
derived chars. Additionally, the homogenisation of experiments on shorter residence times could 
partially reduce the variability in the results due to difference in pyrolysis conditions.  
- 
Temperature of mineralisation 
As far as the temperature of mineralisation is concerned, it is necessary for future experiments to 
proceed with a double scope: first, experiments should be performed at lower temperatures to cover 
a wider range of environmental conditions and include more soil temperature regimes. In fact, the 
selected experiments at original incubation temperature ( > 13°C - Figure 22) can be said to represent 
solely Thermic or Hyperthermic temperature regimes which can only be found at Saharan latitudes 
(Kettler Tim, n.d.). Considering continental Europe, most of the soils belong to the Mesic regime 
with mean soil temperature comprised between 8 and 15°C (Kettler Tim, n.d.) and soils in 
Scandinavian countries, similarly to what showed previously for Sweden, mostly belong to the Cryic 
regime with mean soil temperatures below 8°C (Kettler Tim, n.d.). Thus, future studies should collect 
experimental evidence for biochar incubation at temperatures lower than 13°C to represent directly 
mineralisation for soil regimes which were disregarded so far, reducing the uncertainty deriving from 
temperature adjustment. Secondly, more experiments to assess temperature sensitivity of biochar 
should be performed which would entail incubating same biochar samples (same feedstock and 
pyrolysis process) at different temperatures, similarly to what showed by Fang et al. (2014) and 
Nguyen et al. (2010). Experiments like this are needed in order to further validate the empiric 
methods presented by Lehmann (2015) to adjust samples to the same incubation temperature and 
to, subsequently, improve future location specific stability analyses. 
However, ideally, enough experiments should be available in order to avoid any temperature 
adjustment and related uncertainty, to carry out stability analyses representative for every soil 
temperature regime. For instance, considering the aim of the presented work, a set of experiments all 
performed at a mineralisation temperature of 10°C would be more appropriate. This could 
potentially validate the assumption based on empirical evidence that biochar used in soils at colder 
climates such as Sweden’s, would have significantly lower mineralisation rates.  
- 
Experimental setting 
The original dataset included 111 experiments (Lehmann, 2015) which were reduced to 58 after the 
process of data selection. That means that almost half of the total experiments was not suitable for 
the following analysis according to the selected criteria. It is, therefore, advisable for future 
==PAGE:32==
27 
 
experiments to be tailored in order to fulfil these criteria, which are accepted in literature (Budai et 
al., 2013, Lehmann, 2015, IPCC, 2019) and presented in the Data processing, cleaning and selection 
section of this work, to be used for future stability analysis. Namely, experiments should exceed 1-
year duration and provide results obtained using double or triple exponential fitting for the 
mineralisation curve. 
- 
H/Corg 
As presented in previous sections, a valid proxy indicator for carbon aromatisation and stability is 
the H/Corg ratio of the biochar sample (Budai et al., 2013). Since it was assessed that this factor is 
often missing in experimental results, the former should be included in order to allow further 
stability analysis. 
- 
Location specific factors  
Existing experimental data lack information about soil moisture which was highlighted as a major 
factor influencing carbon mineralisation and it is, thus, difficult to assess the representativeness of 
the results to site specific soil moisture levels. Future studies should report information about soil 
moisture for the purpose of developing site-specific stability results. Moreover, as presented 
previously, in order to fully be aligned with basic theory, the decomposer dynamics should be taken 
into account. Therefore, future studies should focus on including this factor in the experimental 
setting and report it.  
 
Carbon Dioxide Removal functions 
Considering the results obtained showing the conditions for expected maximum sequestration, it is necessary 
to say that they are to be intended as preliminary. One reason for that is that the CDR results do not take into 
account the uncertainty and sensitivity shown in previous sections. Considering that for the majority of 
feedstocks the maximum sequestration occurs for classes which show a relatively high sensitivity to 
methodological choices, it is concluded that further studies are needed in order to support those results. 
Following a more conservative approach based on the current knowledge and available information, it might 
be more appropriate to state that stability of biochar produced at the highest temperatures and thus the 
corresponding CDR values are the least sensitive to different methodological approaches; High pyrolysis 
temperatures should be taken as reference for CDR of biochar until further studies will close the highlighted 
knowledge gap to support different conclusions. An additional aspect for future studies to assess CDR of 
different feedstocks would be to obtain feedstock specific BC+100 values, which were not possible to obtain 
in this study. 
Feedstock inventory and opportunity mapping 
In terms of the quantification of the available feedstocks some of the major uncertainties were: the current 
use of straw as it was not possible to retrieve how much of the remaining straw from bedding and animal 
husbandry is not used for energy production; the retrieved figure representing the amount of produced horse 
manure, which was assumed to be on a wet basis as not consistent with the quantification of total manure 
production in Sweden. Future work aiming at mapping feedstock potential should focus on validating the 
assumptions which led to the listed uncertainties.  
It was highlighted that for some feedstocks, price and economic value represent a potential constraint 
whereas for others they represent a potential opportunity: in fact, the price of woody residues and straw is 
based on their energy content as fuels and therefore their use for other purposes such as biochar may not be 
economically viable. On the other hand, for the remaining feedstocks the possibility of producing a valuable 
product such as biochar (due to its different potential applications) from a low value feedstock may be an 
opportunity to divert those feedstocks for this purpose. Moreover, it is highlighted that current use was 
included as a constraint for every feedstock category:  in fact, although some feedstocks are not currently 
fully exploited (slash and stumps), there might be competition with other sectors which may affect the 
availability the former. These sectors include the agricultural/farming sector which uses sludge, manure and 
compost from garden waste which otherwise could be directed to biochar production and the energy sector 
which may compete for feedstocks with higher calorific content (woody residues and straw) to recover 
energy. The considerations about economic value and current use support the assumption that to a certain 
degree different sectors may compete for the availability of biomass. 
Thus, it was concluded that there are some questions that would need to be answered to improve the 
opportunity mapping analysis in this regards (Lehmann, 2015): which are the merits of pyrolysis over the 
alternatives? Which are the merits of deploying biochar over alternatives? Lehmann (2015) highlights some 
drivers for biochar use and system integration which could guide the research to answer these questions.

feedstock could reasonably be redirected to biochar production. In this way it would also be possible to 
identify specific systems synergies in terms of biomass and biochar cascading use to reach a win-win setting 
for all identified subsystem. An example of these synergies is showed by Azzi et al. (2019), who suggest that 
biochar produced for district heating should be used first in animal agriculture and then added to soil with 
manure, generating benefits for all the presented subsystems. 
Biochar and carbon dioxide removal potentials 
The total BP for Sweden considering that all available feedstocks are used for biochar production could range 
between approximately 0.9 and 1.7 million t/year which would correspond to a CDRP of 2 to 4.2 million tons 
CO2 sequestered per year (in a 100 years perspective). These ranges consider in the minimum value the 
lower potentials and woody residues harvested only after thinning whereas the maximum includes the upper 
potentials and woody residues deriving form thinning and felling.  
To put the total BP potential into context, the obtained results were compared to the production capacity of 
standard pyrolysis plants of three different sizes: small, medium and large plant with capacities of 2, 16 and 
185 kt of biochar per year (Shackley, 2011). Woody residues derived biochar potential is equivalent to the 
annual capacity of 4 to 6 large scale biochar plants. The yearly production of sewage sludge and manure 
derived biochar is the equivalent of the annual capacity of 1 and 6 medium scale plants, respectively. For 
garden waste derived biochar, the amount is the equivalent of the annual capacity of 1 medium scale plant 
whereas the biochar derived from straw is the equivalent to the annual capacity of 1 large scale plant. 
In order to put CDRP figures into context, the strategy for negative greenhouse gas emission for Sweden was 
assessed. The strategy (Statens offentliga utredningar, 2020) sets the principles and guidelines in order to 
achieve the overall goal of net 0 emission by 2045 with net negative emissions thereafter and includes 
additional goals for 2030. In the document it is stated that supplementary measures will be needed to 
compensate for emissions that cannot be eliminated for some specific sectors (e.g. agricultural) and to attain 
net negative emissions. The highlighted targets for supplementary measures are:  
- 
3.7 million t CO2/year at least in 2030 (with up to 1.2 million tons increased carbon sinks in forests 
and land which includes biochar). 
- 
In 2045 10.7 million t CO2/year (with at least 2.7 million tons increased carbon sinks in forests and 
land which includes biochar) 
- 
Between 2021 and 2045 the volume of supplementary measures will constantly increase  
It is visible that the calculated CDRP ranging from 2 to 4.2 million tons of CO2 sequestered per year would in 
the worst case almost double the required measures coming from carbon sinks in forest and land needed by 
2030 (1.2 millions) and cover most of those needed from 2045 (2.7 million).  
The BP and CDRP results are to be considered as prospective and they will need to be specified with further 
investigation. The actual BP potential will greatly depend on the available biomass for biochar and on the 
pyrolysis processes. Future analyses should focus on specific process conditions and the related feedstock 
==PAGE:34==
29 
 
yields: in fact, in this analysis the biochar production process conditions (slow vs. fast pyrolysis) were not 
specified. In terms of CDRP it is clear from the comparison between the results and Swedish climate targets 
that biochar has a significant potential for carbon sequestration. In fact, it can potentially account for a large 
part of the supplementary measures categorised as increased carbon sink and can even cover all of them. In 
addition, in this work, only the carbon sequestration effects of biochar were considered. However, as 
mentioned in the introduction, biochar has been proven to have additional benefits in terms of climate 
change mitigation and other (e.g. fertilising properties). Therefore, biochar production at such scales could 
significantly contribute to climate change mitigation while providing additional benefits to the whole 
integrated system and subsystems (energy production, agriculture, waste management).  
It is pointed out that woody residues, considering both thinning and thinning and felling harvest scenarios, 
show the highest potentials in terms of biochar production and carbon dioxide removal (between 69% and 
76% of the total BP and between 73% and 79% of the total CDRP considering the average values). Thus, it is 
concluded that woody residues could play a major role in the implementation of a biochar production system 
at national scale in Sweden. This resource should be prioritised for future assessment of biochar large scale 
production in a climate mitigation perspective. This would mainly entail furtherly assessing the availability of 
biomass, determining the harvesting conditions (thinning, felling) and determining the location specific 
stability analysis that was described previously, as well as pyrolysis specific process conditions.  
Conclusion 
In conclusion, preliminary thresholds for biochar carbon stability for Sweden were obtained, using a 
methodology which included temperature of the soil as a location specific parameter and HTT as a proxy 
indicator. It is highlighted that this is a first step in order to obtain stability analyses which would reflect site 
specific conditions. Uncertainty and sensitivity of the methodological choices show that future studies are 
needed in order to validate the presented stability analysis and to improve general knowledge around biochar 
carbon stability. Future studies should particularly focus on the collection of new mineralisation 
experimental results, following the principles and requirements highlighted in this work. The main aim of 
this experimental collection should be to increase the representativeness of the samples in terms of 
feedstock, process and incubation conditions. Considering in particular incubation conditions, consistently 
with the need of developing site-specific analyses and stability results, the experiments should be tailored in 
order to represent the environmental conditions (temperature, moisture, decomposer dynamics) of the 
specific site were biochar will be used as soil amendment . This need is crucial primarily for colder climates 
as they are not represented directly in terms of temperature by existing experiments. This conclusion is 
motivated also by the fact that colder soils are the ones which could ensure significantly higher stability. 
In terms of carbon sequestration potential, intermediate pyrolysis temperatures could lead to the highest 
carbon dioxide removal values per mass of feedstock for most feedstocks (Woody, Herbaceous and 
Biosolids). However, these conclusions would need to be validated with further analyses including, for 
instance, feedstock specific stability thresholds. Taking into account the uncertainty and sensitivity analyses, 
it is concluded that the reference carbon dioxide removal of each feedstock should be referred to the biochar 
produced at high temperatures (>600°C), until further work will support other conclusions.  
The opportunity mapping primarily shows that there are significant existing flows of residual biomass but 
different sectors and subsystems such as agriculture and energy may compete for the available biomass, 
affecting the availability for biochar production. Future research should prioritise the assessment of the 
benefits of biochar production in a life-cycle perspective and comparing different systems which include 
biochar production to current system. It was found that biochar carbon dioxide removal potential in Sweden, 
based on presented analysis and results could significantly contribute to the goals set by the country for 2030 
and 2045 in terms of mitigation measures to offset carbon emissions. 
It is concluded that the highest contribution to the production and carbon dioxide removal potentials is due 
to a very high availability of woody residues. Woody residues exceed by far the availability of the other 
assessed biomass feedstocks which have a marginal contribution to the total potentials. Therefore, future 
work should prioritise the assessment for woody residues to assess the feasibility of large-scale biochar 
production in Sweden.
