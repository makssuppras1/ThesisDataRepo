ABSTRACT
Electric vehicles emit less noise at low speeds compared to internal combustion engine vehicles.
Thus, auditory cues necessary for other road users to detect an approaching electric vehicle is less than
for internal combustion engine vehicles. Consequently, warning sounds are produced by electric vehicles
to maintain the same level of auditory cues. It is of great interest to maximize the detectability of these
warning sounds while keeping the perceived annoyance as low as possible. The current procedure for de-
termining detectability and perceived annoyance of sounds is to conduct experiments and listening tests
which are very time consuming procedures. In this project it is investigated if a computational partial
loudness model can be used as an objective measure for predicting detectability or perceived annoyance
as it would improve the warning sound design- and test process. A partial loudness model has been
implemented in MATLAB and diﬀerent listening tests have been carried out to obtain subjective de-
tectability and perceived annoyance data for diﬀerent warning sounds in diﬀerent noise conditions. Using
the implemented model on the experimental data it was shown that the model performed well for station-
ary warning sounds. However, the partial loudness model could not appropriately predict detectability
or perceived annoyance across all tested warning sounds and noise stimuli when the time varying sounds
were included.
Improvements speciﬁcally concerning modelling the temporal eﬀects should be made
before the partial loudness model can be used as a universal model for predicting detectability for all
sound/noise stimuli. The partial loudness model could not predict perceived annoyance of the tested
warning sounds but performed well in predicting the change in perceived annoyance when the noise
condition was changed. A partial psychoacoustic annoyance model, PPA, was developed speciﬁcally
for EV warning sounds based on the partial loudness model combined with a modiﬁcation of Zwicker’s
psychoacoustic annoyance model. The model was developed based on the experimental data obtained in
this project and thus should be validated with further experimental data. The PPA-model predictions
yielded a correlation with the experimental obtained data of 0.77 (R2).

Chapter 1.
Introduction
In recent years, electric vehicles (EVs) have seen a remarkable increase in popularity/sales and the trend
is forecasted to continue [7]. Internal combustion engine vehicles (ICEVs) however, are still the dominant
type of passenger vehicles in all markets. Despite the many advantages EVs have compared to ICEVs,
several challenges exist in order to reach the same level as ICEVs across all performance parameters.
One performance parameter is safety.
A study conducted by the National Highway Traﬃc Safety Administration of the U.S. government [8]
(NHTSA) concluded that Hybrid Electric Vehicles (HEVs) are involved in more accidents with pedes-
trians / cyclists in manoeuvres at low speeds such as turning, backing up, entering or leaving a parking
space. At speeds below 30 km/h the dominant diﬀerence between HEVs and ICEVs is engine noise
emission. The NHTSA report indicates that this diﬀerence is the cause of the accident increment. Noise
emission of EVs and HEVs is similar at low speeds as HEVs are typically driven by only the electric
motor at low speeds.
Due to the concerns spawned by the NHTSA report, a collaborative project “Electric Vehicle Alert for
Detection and Emergency Response” (eVADER) was created by the European Union (EU). A study con-
ducted by eVADER [9] showed that noise emission of EVs at 10 km/h is approximately 11.3 dB(A) lower
than a gasoline-ICEV and approximately 15.2 dB(A) lower than a diesel-ICEV. From an environmental
point of view this is good, as it causes soundscapes as well as cabin sound to be less annoying. However,
it also reduces the auditory cues for other road users, which are of signiﬁcant importance in order to
detect an approaching car. In later research, it was shown that the sound of a slightly accelerating ICEV
can be detected at a distance of approximately 36 meters whereas this decreases to approximately 14
meters for an EV, which often has a better acceleration performance [10].
The ﬁndings have caused both the US and Japanese governments as well as the EU to propose regulations
for minimum sound emission [11].
The challenge for car makers is to make the EVs as detectable as ICEVs while keeping annoyance at a
low level. This is possible by simply copying the noise of ICEVs and emit it artiﬁcially. However, this
would cause the EV to be as noisy as ICEVs which would eliminate a great marketing factor for EVs
and eliminate the potential to reduce ambient noise in residential areas. Thus, EV manufactures try to
develop their own optimal warning sounds in terms of high detectability, low annoyance and a product
sound quality that ﬁts the brand. In a paper by Zeitler (BMW Group) the psychoacoustic assessment
and corresponding evaluation of diﬀerent warning sound alternatives is presented [1]. The evaluation is
presented in table 1.1.

==PAGE:17==
Table 1.1: Psychoacoustic assessments and corresponding evaluation (positive/negative) of diﬀerent
warning sound alternatives [1].
A new term, recognisability, is introduced in table 1.1. Recognisability is the ability to recognise the
warning sound psychologically to the meaning of an approaching car such that an action to avoid the
car is made by other road users. As this parameter is merely psychological and depends on previous
experience with traﬃc sounds it is diﬃcult to quantify and hence this project does not deal with this
parameter. The assessments in table 1.1 are merely qualitative. With an objective measure it would be
possible to compare the sound alternatives quantitatively as well and based on this decide amongst the
sound alternatives.
So far the evaluation of warning sounds in terms of detectability and annoyance has been based on various
experiments and listening tests. Experiments and listening tests are very time consuming procedures
and can be easily inﬂuenced by bias. A model for predicting detectability and annoyance would improve
the warning sound design process as the model would serve as an early indicator if a warning sound
would be good or not. Furthermore, if a model is suﬃciently trustworthy, it would be possible to test
warning sounds for detectability/annoyance or even to certify sounds for being suﬃciently detectable /
not annoying.
1.1
Previous works
Various studies concluded that it is possible to obtain a good detectability at a lower sound pressure
level than existing auditory cues from ICEVs. This can be done by creating synthesized sounds that
exploit the characteristics of human sound perception [10,12–15]. Thus, it would make sense to develop
a metric based on human sound perception that predicts the detectability of warning sounds such that
better warning sounds, in terms of detectability, can be designed.
By investigating the literature it was found that detectability is inﬂuenced by diﬀerent factors. It has
been shown that temporal eﬀects such as amplitude modulation aﬀects the detectability [16]. Thus, a
metric to predict detectability should include temporal eﬀects.
It would make sense if a correlation between the sound pressure level of the emitted warning sound and
detectability exists. However, it seems there is no consensus in literature concerning this topic. In one
paper by Altinsoy [10] the following is claimed:
”There is no correlation between the reaction time of the pedestrians and the sound pressure level of the
– 6 –
==PAGE:18==
vehicle sound which is essential for the detection”
In the experiment by Altinsoy, the reaction time represents the detection distance.
Thus, Altinsoy
claims that no correlation between warning sound SPL and detectability exists. On the other hand
Sakamoto [17] claims the following:
”The audible distance of the approaching sounds can be proportional to the overall sound pressure level”
It should be noted that in the experiment made by Altinsoy [10] the sound stimuli have very diﬀerent
characteristics, whereas in the experiment made by Sakamoto [17] the sound stimuli have similar charac-
teristics. This might have an eﬀect. Finally in a paper by Yamauichi [18], it is shown that the warning
sound stimuli with equivalent SPLs in each 1/3 octave bands have similar detectability even though
the impression was changed due to changed frequency characteristics. This might explain the diﬀerent
ﬁndings in Altinsoy and Sakamoto’s experiments [10,17]. If the SPL in each 1/3 octave band is not held
constant across the warning sounds it can cause diﬀerent detectability as with the case in E. Altinsoy’s
experiment [10] and the opposite could explain the results of Sakamoto [17]. Since one of the current
loudness model implementations is based on 1/3 octave band SPLs (see section 2.2.1) the ﬁndings made
by Altinsoy, Sakamoto and Yamauichi indicate that a loudness model might be suﬃcient for predicting
detectability.
1.2
Problem deﬁnition and research scope
EVs often operate in a noisy urban environment and thus background noise might mask a warning
sound and thereby inﬂuence the detectability of the vehicle. Therefore background noise should be taken
into account when predicting detectability. In terms of loudness models this is done by calculating the
so called partial loudness, that is, the loudness of a target signal in presence of a masker. In 2005 a
study by Moore and Glasberg (M&G [19]) investigated if their partial loudness model could predict the
detectability of cell phone ringtones in diﬀerent noisy environments. The model performed very well,
yielding a correlation of 0.94 between predictions and measured thresholds. The same may be possible
for EV warning sounds in diﬀerent background noises.
This project investigates if a partial loudness model can predict detectability of EV warning
sounds in noisy environments appropriately.
Literature also indicates that loudness is highly correlated with perceived annoyance [20, 21]. Thus, it
would be interesting to investigate if a partial loudness model can predict the perceived annoyance of
EV warning sounds in noisy environments.
This project investigates if a partial loudness model can predict perceived annoyance of
EV warning sounds in noisy environments appropriately.

Chapter 2.
Theory
In this chapter some of the key concepts used in this thesis are brieﬂy explained. For a more detailed
description of the individual topics please refer to the following literature [2,4,20,21].
2.1
Auditory perception of loudness
2.1.1
The human ear
As proposed in several papers [10,12–15] a metric for predicting detectability and annoyance should be
based on human sound perception. In order to do that it is of critical importance to understand how
human hearing works, thus a very short simpliﬁed explanation is presented.
The ear can be divided into three parts - The outer ear, the middle ear and the inner ear. The human
ear anatomy is shown in ﬁg. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Anatomy of the human ear. [2]
Sound enters the concha as pressure waves in air. At the eardrum a translation from pressure changes
in air into mechanical movement of the eardrum is made. The eardrum is connected to a series of bones
(ossicles) that works as impedance matchers for the vibrating eardrum to the movements of ﬂuids in the
inner ear. In the inner ear the movements of the ﬂuid causes a membrane (basilar membrane) inside
the cochlea to move at speciﬁc points depending on the frequency content of the sound. The movement
of the basilar membrane triggers hair cells that send nerve impulses down the auditory nerve which is
connected to the auditory cortex in the brain. Especially the translation from mechanical movement in
– 8 –
==PAGE:20==
the inner ear to electric nerve impulses and summation of these in the auditory cortex are diﬃcult to
simulate and hence the inner ear is considered as the most complex part of the ear.
2.1.2
Loudness
Loudness is the subjective perception of strength of a sound. In the case where a masker is present,
loudness of the target sound decreases and is described as partial loudness, PL. It is obvious that partial
loudness has an impact on detectability - the higher the partial loudness the higher detectability of the
target sound. However, the exact relationship is unclear. In this project it is investigated if partial
loudness can predict detectability appropriately. Therefore, fundamental concepts of loudness will be
presented. Loudness can only be determined exactly by conducting subjective listening tests. However,
several standards have been established such that loudness is quantiﬁable. The unit for loudness is sone
(also denoted son). By deﬁnition a 1 kHz pure tone of 40 dB SPL corresponds to a loudness of 1 sone.
Doubling the loudness eg. from 2 to 4 sone, doubles the perceived loudness and halving the loudness, eg.
from 1 to 0.5 sone halves the perceived loudness [20].
2.1.3
Equal loudness contours
By asking listeners to adjust the sound pressure level of pure tones for various frequencies such that
they are as equal in loudness as a 1 kHz tone, an equal loudness curve for 1 sone is made. In order to
make loudness comparable with sound pressure level, another unit, loudness level, is deﬁned as the sound
pressure level for a 1 kHz pure tone and its corresponding equal loudness contour. Loudness level has
the unit phon (also denoted phone). Consequently, 1 sone equals 40 phon. The SPL adjustment of pure
tones can be done at diﬀerent loudness levels and thereby several equal loudness curves can be created.
This creates the standardized equal-loudness contours as presented in ﬁg. 2.2. Each contour line in ﬁg.
2.2 corresponds to a certain loudness level, only for 1 kHz does the loudness level correspond to the SPL.
– 9 –
==PAGE:21==
Figure 2.2: Equal Loudness Contours, from ISO 226:2003 [3]. The blue line shows the original standard
from 1953.
As the equal-loudness contours are not a horizontal line they reveal that loudness of a sound is dependent
on its frequency content. Also it is seen that frequencies below 500 Hz are weighted non-linearly as the
slope of the equal loudness contours changes with SPL.
2.1.4
Stevens’ Power law
The scaling of loudness follows Stevens’ power law at levels above 40 dB SPL [4]. Stevens’ power law is
a model that describes the correlation between stimulus increase and sensory increase and is described
in eq. 2.1:
S = kIa.
(2.1)

intensity, and a is an exponent determined experimentally. For loudness of a 1 kHz tone, a is 0.3. If
the subjective magnitude of sensation (S) is described in terms of sone and the stimulus intensity (I ) is
changed with the RMS sound pressure (p), k will be 0.01 and a will be 0.6. An expression for loudness
(N ) using these units is given in eq. 2.2:
N = 0.01p0.6.
(2.2)

2.1.5
Spectral eﬀects
So far it has been described how loudness is dependent on frequency and SPL. However, it was shown
by Zwicker in 1957 that loudness is also inﬂuenced by spectral eﬀects [23]. Consider a stimulus which is
band limited and varied such that the bandwidth changes but the overall power is held constant. Below
a certain bandwidth the changes in bandwidth has little or no inﬂuence on the perceived loudness. This
bandwidth is called the critical bandwidth, CB. If the bandwidth of the noise exceeds the CB the loudness
will increase. The CB is dependent on the center frequency of the sound and can be approximated by
eq. 2.4 [20]:
– 11 –
==PAGE:23==
CB = 25 + 75[1 + 1.4(f/kHz)2]0.69.
(2.4)
The ﬁnding of the critical bandwidth led to the concept of an auditory ﬁlter bank, that is, the basilar
membrane in the cochlea can be modelled as a frequency analyser having several auditory ﬁlters.
2.1.6
Binaural Eﬀects
As humans have two ears, two input channels exist. Loudness is perceived in two separate channels but
results in a total perceived loudness. When calculating loudness it is therefore necessary to calculate
loudness for each ear. It can be approximated that the total perceived loudness can be obtained by
summing the loudness output of each ear [5].
2.1.7
Temporal Eﬀects
It is obvious that most sounds vary over time and consequently loudness changes over time as well. What
is not obvious is that loudness takes time to build up and fade out. This is the reason why a sound
of very short duration is less loud than an identical sound of longer duration. For stimulus durations
longer than 500 ms the loudness is constant but for durations below 200 ms the intensity necessary
for detection increases signiﬁcantly as duration decreases. Other temporal eﬀects include forward and
backward masking also known as non-simultaneous masking, that is, noise can mask a target signal
forward and backwards in time over short durations. It is generally said that forward masking does not
have an eﬀect after 100-200 ms and for backward masking 20 ms. [4,6,20].
2.2
Loudness Models
Loudness calculation of a sound is based on advanced models of the human ear. Primarily two models
have gained popularity - one developed by Zwicker & Fastl [20] and one developed by Moore & Glasberg
[5]. Diﬀerent standards are based on Zwicker & Fastl’s model (ISO532:1975 [24] and DIN 45631 [25]) as
well as Moore & Glasberg’s model (ANSI S3.4-2007 [22]).
In the models, some diﬀerent assumptions are made and the procedures for calculation is slightly diﬀerent
but the fundamental concept is the same. The overall steps follows ﬁg. 2.4.
– 12 –
==PAGE:24==
Figure 2.4: Flow chart of procedures in the two most popular loudness models.
Each step in ﬁg. 2.4 models a speciﬁc part in the human ear. A ﬁxed ﬁlter represents the outer ear
ﬁltering and similarly a ﬁxed ﬁlter represents the middle ear ﬁltering. Based on experimental data,
values for designing the ﬁlters are obtained. The movement of the basilar membrane in the cochlea
can be simulated by calculating an excitation pattern based on the auditory ﬁlter bank concept. The
transformation from mechanical movement into electric nerve impulses in the inner ear and summation
of these in the auditory cortex can be modelled as Stevens’ power law with some modiﬁcations such that
it ﬁts experimental data.
The two models vary in the ﬁnal three steps which all are related to the complex inner ear. The procedure
is similar but the concepts behind the calculations are slightly diﬀerent. The ﬁnal three steps for each
of the models are described below.

Previous research has indicated there is a strong correlation between loudness and detectability [18].
Hence, in order to make a model for prediction of detectability of EV warning sounds, it is assumed to
be of critical importance to include loudness and especially partial loudness, as EVs operate in noisy
environments. Since it has already been described in literature how partial loudness can be calculated
with M&G’s model [5] and since this model is used in recent loudness standards (ANSI S3.4-2007 [22]) it

consisted of implementing M&G’s partial loudness model in MATLAB. It is based on the models de-

loudness model. An overview of the model is shown in ﬁg. 2.10.

Outer- & middle ear
As described in section 2.2, values obtained experimentally are used as weightings on the input signal
in order to represent the changes the outer and middle ear causes on the stimulus. The values used are
listed in the ANSI S3.4-2007 standard [22]. In the model, FIR ﬁlters are made to represent the weightings
and applied to the sound signal as well as the noise signal. If the stimulus is recorded binaurally with
a head and torso simulator (HATS) with microphones at eardrum position, only the middle ear transfer
function has to be applied, as the HATS already do the outer ear ﬁltering.
Time-frequency analysis
In order to include the spectral and temporal eﬀects described in section 2.1.2, the signal must be
analysed in terms of frequency and time. A spectrogram can be used for this purpose. To obtain a
satisfying frequency resolution at low frequencies, a relatively long time segment is required. Whereas
for higher frequencies a long time segment would result in poor temporal resolution compared to the
auditory system. In order to compensate for this, six FFTs with six diﬀerent Hanning window sizes of
respectively 64, 32, 16, 8, 4, 2 ms are calculated. All FFTs are updated every 1 ms. The FFTs are used
for respectively 0-80 Hz, 80-500 Hz, 500-1250 Hz, 1250-2540 Hz, 2540-4050 Hz, 4050-(fs/2) Hz.

2.3
Psychoacoustic measuring methods
In order to understand the conducted experiments better, the fundamental experimental concepts of psy-
choacoustic measuring methods are explained brieﬂy. Psychoacoustics deal with human sound perception
and since humans are diﬀerent, psychoacoustic experiment results will diﬀer. Moreover, humans have
memory which leads to bias eﬀects. When conducting psychoacoustic experiments, one of the objectives
is always to minimize the diﬀerence in results across test subjects and to minimize eventual bias eﬀects.
This is done by using some of the developed psychoacoustic measuring methods.
2.3.1
Psychometric function
A psychometric function is a function that represents the probability of getting a ”positive” response
as function of a variable parameter. It is made from experimental data. For instance, say that you
want to examine the hearing threshold of a pure tone. The subject is told to respond ”yes” (positve
response) if he hears the tone and ”no” if he cannot hear the tone. This procedure is repeated several
times for diﬀerent levels of the tone. If the percentage of yes responses are plotted as function of the tone
level a function will show up - the psychometric function. The threshold can then be determined as, for
instance, the point that yields 50% yes responses. The 50% point is often called the point of subjective
equality, PSE. An example of a psychometric function is shown in ﬁg. 2.17.
– 26 –
==PAGE:38==
Figure 2.17: Example of a psychometric function.
2.3.2
Transformed up-down method
The transformed up-down method is an adaptive method for determining threshold, that is, the pre-
sentation levels depend on the course of previous presentation levels and responses. In the transformed
up-down method several of the last responses are used to decide if the presentation level should be
increased or decreased. An example is the ’one-up, two-down’ method. One wrong answer causes an
increase in stimulus making, it easier for the subject - ’one-up’. Two consecutive correct answers are
needed in order to cause a decrease in stimulus making the task more diﬃcult - ’two-down’. The ’one-up,
two-down’ method yields the 70.7% point on the psychometric function which means that subjects will
respond correctly 70.7% of the time at the given stimulus level. An example of the ’one-up, two-down’
method can be seen in one of the experiments carried out in this project (experiment 4) in section 3.5.
2.3.3
3AFC method
The three-alternative forced choice method (3AFC) is a method where the subject is presented with
three stimuli of which one diﬀers. The subject has to pick out the stimuli that diﬀers and the experiment
does not continue until the subject has picked a stimuli - thereby ’forced choice’. Since three options
exist there is a 33% chance of picking the correct response but not 33% chance for detection. Typically
the task will be more diﬃcult for a correct response and easier for a wrong response. Based on the set of
rules made for the experiment, the threshold can then be found. For instance if the ’one-up, two-down’
method is used, the threshold will yield a probability for detection of 70.7% on the psychometric function.
– 27 –
==PAGE:39==
2.3.4
Rating method
In the rating method, the subject has to rate a given stimulus in terms of a describing factor on a scale.
Thus, this method is direct as the subjects input is directly used to evaluate a certain parameter. This
method is widely used to reﬂect perceived quality of a parameter. An example is: ’How soft is this sound
on a scale from 0-10?’.
2.3.5
Bias
Bias is a description of unwanted eﬀects on the measurement of sensitivity. Many diﬀerent bias eﬀects
exist since the human brain is complex and is easily inﬂuenced by external eﬀects. Examples of bias eﬀects
include learning and order eﬀects. The learning eﬀect is simply that the brain, with more repetitions of
a task, learns how to answer correctly. The order eﬀect, on the other hand, is an induced eﬀect due to
the presentation order of stimuli - For instance if one out of ten investigated stimuli is repeated several
times instead of being randomized with other stimuli properly, then the subject ’remembers’ the stimulus
from previous and will perform better. It is of critical importance to take bias eﬀects into account when
doing experimental designs. Latin squares is an example of a procedure to present subjects with stimuli
in a systematic way to minimize bias. A latin square is an n x n array with n diﬀerent symbols. Each
symbol occurs exactly once in each row and column. A balanced latin square follows the same rules
but furthermore the last symbol in a row is diﬀerent from the ﬁrst symbol in the next row. Thus, it
minimizes ’carry-over’ eﬀects since no stimulus is presented consecutively. An example of a 4x4 balanced
latin square is shown in table 2.1.

In order to test if the M&G PL model correlates with detectability or annoyance, experimental data

3.1
Preliminary experiment 1 - Detectability in stationary noise
3.1.1
Purpose
The purpose is to investigate if M&G’s partial loudness model can predict detectability. A subject is
presented with stationary noise and a warning sound. The warning sound increases in level until the
subject indicates detection of the warning sound. Based on the detection point, the maximum PL before
detection can be calculated. If the maximum PL before detection is similar across diﬀerent warning
sounds, a general threshold is found and thus, the partial loudness model can predict if the warning
sound is detectable or not for given SPLs of warning sound (target signal) and noise (masker) as it will
be above or below the detection threshold. The simple signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) measure is investigated
similarly.
3.1.2
Procedure
The subject was presented with four diﬀerent warning sounds, one at a time, in the presence of a
stationary masker representing urban noise. The masker was held constant at a certain level whereas
the warning sounds, when played, were increasing in level. The listener was instructed to raise his/her
hand when he/she was absolutely certain that he/she detected a warning sound. The time it takes for
the listener to detect the warning sound is the detection point and based on this the SNR and PL can
be calculated. At some occasions a break between stimuli was made to see if it caused false detections.
Each warning sound was presented four times nonconsecutively. The procedure is shown in ﬁg. 3.1.

The EV warning sound stimuli consisted of three EV sounds acquired from previous research done by

as a good reference. All stimuli are diotic (same signal to left and right channel/ear). The warning

• S1 is a stationary sound with a bandwidth of approximately 1 kHz and a signiﬁcant tone around

• S3 is very complex and seems to consist of two parts.
1. A fast amplitude modulated part (approx 6 Hz modulation frequency) with a bandwidth of
approximately 900 Hz centered at 500 Hz and a tone around 500 Hz.
2. A slow strongly amplitude modulated part (approx 2 Hz modulation frequency and large
modulation depth) with a bandwidth of 700 Hz centered at 1500 Hz.
• S7 is also highly complex containing two parts.
1. A slowly amplitude modulated part (approx 2 Hz modulation frequency) centered at 300 Hz
with a bandwidth of 400 Hz.
2. A tone around 700 Hz that after 4 seconds have decreased to 500 Hz - a pitch change.
A spectrogram of each warning sound can be seen in ﬁg. 3.4.
The warning sound stimuli were modiﬁed such that the sounds increase 40 dB in level linearly over a
period of 15 seconds - approximately 2.7 dB/s. The sampling frequency was 44.1 kHz and the stimuli

3.3
Main experiments
Based on the preliminary experiments it was decided to conduct three main experiments with the same
warning sounds used previously in order to achieve suﬃcient answers concerning the problem deﬁnition.
• Experiment 3 - the detection points for the warning sounds in diﬀerent noise conditions are obtained
in a similar manner as the conducted preliminary experiments, that is, the warning sounds increase
in level until detection is indicated. The experiment simulates a real situation where the subject has
to decide for himself when he is absolutely certain that he detects the EV warning sound. Based on
the detection points, the maximum STPL level and SNR before detection can be calculated. If the
maximum STPL level for detection is similar across warning sounds and noise conditions, a general
threshold is found and thus, the partial loudness model can predict if a warning sound is detectable
or not for given SPLs of warning sound (target signal) and noise condition (noise/masker signal)
as it will be above or below the detection threshold. The simple SNR measure is investigated
similarly.
• Experiment 4 - the masked threshold for the warning sounds are obtained using the 3AFC method.
This experiment simulates a diﬀerent detection point on the psychometric function and should be
comparable with previous research done by M&G [19]. Likewise experiment 3, if similar STPL
levels for detectability across warning sounds are obtained the model can predict if the warning
sounds are detectable or not for a given SPLs.
• Experiment 5 - a subjective evaluation of the perceived annoyance for the warning sounds in
diﬀerent noise conditions is obtained. The mean STPL level and SNR for each stimuli can be
calculated and based on this, eventual correlations between annoyance and STPL level/SNR can
be found.

3.4
Experiment 3 - Detectability in various noise
An experiment similar to those conducted at KAIST was designed and conducted at DTU. This time,
time varying realistic noises were used as noise conditions as well. Several changes were made in order
to better control the experiment and make the experimental data more trustworthy. Experiment 3 was
carried out in combination with experiment 4. In order to check for order eﬀects, half of the subjects did
experiment 3 ﬁrst and the other half did experiment 4 ﬁrst. Due to a mistake made in the presentation
order design described in section 3.4.3, experiment 3 was rerun and hence two results sections exist for
this experiment.
3.4.1
Purpose
The purpose of experiment 3 was the same as with the experiments conducted at KAIST - to investigate
if the maximum STPL level for detection is similar across warning sounds and noise conditions.
If
that is the case a general threshold is found and thus, the partial loudness model can predict if the
warning sound is detectable or not for given SPLs of warning sound (target signal) and noise condition
(noise/masker signal) as it will be above or below the detection threshold.
3.4.2
Procedure
The experiment consisted of four parts:
1. In case the subject could not present a recently conducted audiogram indicating normal hearing,
a screening was made. Using an audiometer it was checked that the subject could detect sounds
below +20 dB HL at frequencies, 1 kHz, 2 kHz, 4 kHz, 6 kHz, 8 kHz, 500 Hz and 250 Hz since
that is how normal hearing is considered in general [29].
2. The subject listens to the warning sounds in quiet in order to know ’what to listen for’. When the
subjects feels familiarized with the warning sounds, the setup and the experiment procedure, the
subject proceeds.
3. The individual reaction time is measured. The subject has to press a button as soon as a noise
burst is presented. 6 trials are done and the reaction time is determined as the average of the last
4 trials. Each noise burst is presented after 3 seconds plus a random delay of 1-3 seconds in order
to minimize learning eﬀects.
4. Similarly to the preliminary experiments a warning sound, in the presence of a background noise at
constant level, is increased in level until detected. The detection point is determined as a function
of the warning sound level. Five diﬀerent background noises are used. Based on the detection point,
the STPL level and SNR for detection can be calculated. The ﬁrst ﬁve trials are used as training in
order to familiarize the subject with the procedure and the diﬀerent noise stimuli. Thereafter, two
identical sessions of 20 unique cases of sound/noise are presented. The subject had the opportunity
to take a break between the two sessions.

3.5
Experiment 4 - Masked threshold
3.5.1
Purpose
The purpose of experiment 4 was to determine the masked threshold of the warning sounds which
corresponds to a detection threshold at lower point on the psychometric function. This was done in
order to see how the results varies from the results obtained in experiment 3. In this experiment the
results are not dependent on the subjective interpretation of being ”absolutely certain” as was the case
in experiments 1, 2 and 3.
3.5.2
Procedure
In experiment 4, the 3AFC method (see section 2.3.3) is used to determine the subject’s masked threshold
for a given warning sound. The subject is presented with three consecutive sound samples. All three
samples contain the same 1 second segment of the simulated urban noise but one sample also contains a 1
second segment of a warning sound. The subject has to indicate which sound sample contains the warning
sound. The one-up-two-down method is used (see section 2.3.2). The stepsize of the increase/decrease
was 8 dB until two reversals had occured, then 4 dB for one reversal and ﬁnally a stepsize of 2 dB until
two reversals occured (8, 8, 4, 2, 2). The threshold was determined as the mean of the last 6 trials’ SNR.
Three runs were conducted for each warning sound yielding a total of 12 runs for each subject. Since
the minimum step size (’resolution’) was 2 dB the starting SNR for the second run was + 1 dB in order
to allow an uneven threshold. An example of the procedure is shown in ﬁg. 3.36.
Figure 3.36: Example of a run for obtaining threshold in experiment 4.
3.5.3
Experimental setup
Experiment 4 was conducted in combination with experiment 3, thus the experimental setup in terms of
equipment and calibration was the same. Only the user interface and stimuli diﬀered.

Stimuli
The warning sound stimuli were the same as in the other experiments: 1 kHz, S1, S3 and S7. The noise

options in each trial. The randomness of the 1 second noise segment is important to avoid frozen noise,
which subjects can adapt to and thus reach lower SNR’s [20].

Chapter 4.
Discussion
The purpose of this project was to investigate if a partial loudness model can appropriately predict
detectability or perceived annoyance for various EV warning sounds in various noise conditions. With
respect to the obtained results in experiment 3, 4 and 5 there is no clear answer. Concerning detectability,
experiment 3 and 4 have indicated signiﬁcant diﬀerent means of max STPL level for detection for all
warning sounds / noise conditions. The means of max STPL level at threshold have been in the range of
26-36 phon in experiment 3 and 16-29 phon in experiment 4. Thus, it is reasonable to say that in order
to be ’absolutely certain’ that other road users with full attention on traﬃc sounds will detect an EV
emitting one of the sounds tested in this project, the sound needs a STPL level of 36 phon which is a quite
high level and will be an ’overshoot’ for some of the sounds. Concerning the perceived annoyance, only
a correlation of 0.26 between mean STPL level and perceived annoyance rating was achieved indicating
that a partial loudness model itself cannot appropriately predict perceived annoyance of EV warning
sounds.
4.1
Detectability
In the previous mentioned study by M&G concerning detectability of cell phone ringtones in noisy
environments (see section 1.2) [19], the STPL at detection threshold was obtained in a one-up-three-down
2AFC experiment to be 0.014 sone corresponding to 6.59 phon at the 79.4% point on the psychometric
function. This was achieved in stationary noise (white and pink) with a 1 kHz tone as signal. Later,
the obtained threshold was used to predict the detectability of diﬀerent cell phone ringtones in diﬀerent
background noises. The predictions were tested by conducting 2AFC and ”yes/no”-experiments in order
to see if the experimental obtained thresholds corresponded with the predicted ones.
A correlation
between the experimental obtained thresholds and the predicted ones of 0.94 (Pearson’s r-value, not
squared) was obtained.

reference threshold (as by M&G [19]) the deviations can be found. The deviations are presented in table

good predictions for the time-varying sounds S3 and S7?
The variation in STPL level at threshold for S3 and S7 is probably due to the sound characteristics.
In the M&G experiment the target stimuli, cell phone ringtones, might have been time varying but
they were not as complex as the S3/S7 warning sounds used in this project. The cell phone ringtones
were either monophonic or polyphonic.
Thus, while containing diﬀerent frequencies, they were not
amplitude or frequency modulated. The amplitude/frequency modulation might induce eﬀects which
the partial loudness model does not suﬃciently predict. Previous studies have indicated that frequency-
and especially amplitude modulation have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on detectability when the SPL is held
constant [13, 14, 16]. In the STPL model, modulation eﬀects are included by the simple attack- and
release time which is approximately 25 ms and 200 ms, respectively. That means modulation eﬀects
are only included if they have a modulation frequency below 5 Hz (200 ms). If modulations above this
frequency induces eﬀects on perceived loudness, and hence detectability, the model do not include these.
Thus, it should be investigated if stimuli with modulations above 5 Hz have an eﬀect on loudness and
hence detectability.
The mean STPL level for detection has for all warning sounds and noise conditions been signiﬁcantly
above the threshold obtained by M&G (6.59 phon). The smallest mean of max STPL level for detection
obtained in this project was 16.12 phon in experiment 4 for S3 in stationary simulated urban noise (see
table 3.24 in section 3.5.4). That is a deviation of +145%! Experiment 4 was conducted in a similar
manner as M&G’s experiment (3AFC instead of 2AFC), with a one-up-two-down procedure yielding the
70.7% point on the psychometric function which is even lower than the 79.4% point used in M&G’s paper
and hence the threshold should be lower as well. Apart from the diﬀerence in noise stimuli used (urban
simulated noise vs white/pink noise), the noise was frozen in M&G’s experiment which has not been the
case in this project. This can lead to lower obtained thresholds due to the human ability to adapt to
frozen noise. Moreover, M&G’s results were only based on 4 subjects which is signiﬁcantly less than the
23 subjects used in this project. M&G’s subjects were probably very experienced listeners which has not
been the case for most subjects in this project, this can lead to even lower thresholds for M&G. However,
the deviation in threshold should be investigated further as it is large. This can be done by reproducing
M&G’s experiment to see if similar results are achieved.

4.2
Annoyance
In experiment 5, only a limited correlation between partial loudness and perceived annoyance was found.
In previous studies, it has been shown that annoyance can be dependent on the subjective expectation
and experience for a given purpose of a sound. Thus, annoyance metrics can typically only be used for
one product category and thus it is diﬃcult to make a universal annoyance model [20,21,31]. Based on
the ﬁndings in experiment 5, it is obvious that the investigated M&G partial loudness model alone is
not appropriate for predicting annoyance for EV warning sounds and a more speciﬁc annoyance metric
concerning this product type should be developed. It should be investigated if other computational psy-
choacoustic metrics such as ﬂuctuation strength, sharpness, roughness or tonality inﬂuences or correlates
with perceived annoyance. Some of this has been investigated and is presented below.
4.2.1
Perceived annoyance model
A brief explanation of the most relevant SQ metrics related to perceived annoyance is introduced in
this section. For a complete explanation and derivation of the SQ metrics please refer to the following
literature [20,21].
• Sharpness is related to the frequency content of the signal. The higher in frequency the centre
of gravity for the amplitude spectrum is located, the higher sharpness. Hence, a sound mainly
consisting of low-frequency components will have a lower sharpness than a sound consisting of
mainly high-frequency components. 1 acum is deﬁned as the sharpness of a narrowband noise of
width 1 Bark centered at 1 kHz with a SPL of 60 dB produces.
• Fluctuation strength is related to the amount of amplitude and frequency modulations between 0.5-
20 Hz. The higher the amount of amplitude/frequency modulation the higher ﬂuctuation strength.
1 vacil is deﬁned as the ﬂuctuation strength a 1 kHz sinusoid at 60 dB SPL and 100% amplitude
modulated at 4 Hz produces. Fluctuation strength peaks at a modulation frequency of 4 Hz.
• Roughness is related to the amount of amplitude modulation at high modulation frequencies, 15-
300 Hz. For a 100% modulated sound, the roughness is highest around a modulation frequency of
70 Hz. 1 asper is deﬁned as the roughness a 1 kHz sinusoid at 60 dB SPL and 100% amplitude
modulated at 70 Hz produces.
• Prominence ratio is related to the amount of tonal content in a signal and is calculated as a
diﬀerence in dB between the output energy of a given Bark band and the output energy of the
adjacent Bark bands. The calculated value is the maximum obtained value across the frequency
range analysed.
– 89 –
==PAGE:101==
• Tonality - is related to the pitch strength of a sound that is, the tonal components’ relative peak
amplitude to the background broadband levels. Depending on the center frequency of the pitch a
weighting function is applied. Tonality has no unit but is an index between 0 and 1. 1 corresponds
to the tonality of a 60 dB 1 kHz pure tone.

4.3
Further work
4.3.1
Detectability
In order to make a universal model for detectability of EV warning sounds the inﬂuence of temporal
eﬀects such as amplitude- and frequency modulation should be investigated further. This can be done by
conducting detection threshold- and masking threshold experiments (like experiment 3 and 4) speciﬁcally
investigating how the change of amplitude- and frequency modulation aﬀects the thresholds. Concerning
the amplitude modulation both the modulation frequency and modulation depth should be investigated.
Concerning the frequency modulation both the modulation frequency and the frequency deviation should
be investigated.
4.3.2
Perceived Annoyance
Further experimental data should be obtained in order to validate the PPA model as it in its current state
is highly biased towards the data obtained in this project. Another possibility is to investigate if a partial
version of the SQ metrics used in Zwicker’s PA-model (sharpness, ﬂuctuation strength, roughness) can
be developed. If that is the case it is possible to make a ’true’ partial version of Zwicker’s psychoacoustic
annoyance model.
4.3.3
Divided attention
If a model can successfully predict the detectability of EV warning sounds, it should be investigated
what amount of divided attention other road users can be expected to have on the traﬃc situation. The
amount of attention will have a large impact on the detectability threshold in real life situations. If
a certain level of attention can be assumed, an experiment simulating exactly this amount of divided
attention should be made to obtain a realistic detectability threshold.
4.3.4
Recognisability
A successful model should also look into the recognisability of warning sounds.
This parameter is
diﬃcult to quantify as subjective experience and expectations have a large impact. An investigation of
this parameter should include a very realistic setup and could possibly be carried out in combination
with the investigation of divided attention.
4.3.5
Binaural model
For this project it has not been necessary to include advanced binaural models as the stimuli used were
diotic and thus a simple summation is suﬃcient. However it is very uncommon that a road user is facing
the EV at normal incidence. Thus, binaural recordings should be used in order to simulate reality to a
greater extent. New binaural concepts should be included in the partial loudness model instead of the
simple summation for the ears that is used in this project. An example of a recent binaural model is one
made by M&G in 2007 [33].

Chapter 5.
Conclusions
The purpose of this project was to investigate if a partial loudness model can appropriately predict
detectability or perceived annoyance of EV warning sounds in noisy environments. The Moore & Glasberg
partial loudness model was used to investigate this problem statement.
Based on two experiments
with diﬀerent procedures for obtaining thresholds, the max STPL level at threshold was calculated for
each warning sound. The results showed that no obvious partial loudness threshold for detectability
exists across all of the tested warning sounds.
This was conﬁrmed by conducting a two-way within
subjects ANOVA that yielded p-values below 0.05 indicating the means diﬀered signiﬁcantly depending
on sound and noise stimuli on a 95% conﬁdence interval. Similar results were obtained for the simple
SNR measure.
Thus, the Moore & Glasberg partial loudness model cannot appropriately
predict detectability of all EV warning sounds in noisy environments. However, the M&G
model yielded similar partial loudness thresholds for detection concerning the tested stationary sounds,
thus it is reasonable to say that the Moore & Glasberg partial loudness model can appropriately
predict detectability of stationary warning sounds. The obtained detection threshold was
27 phon near the 100% point on the psychometric function and the detection threshold
was 17 phon at the 70.7% point on the psychometric function. Thus, if the STPL level for a
stationary warning sound is above 27 phon it can be assumed that it is detectable for most road users
with attention on the traﬃc sounds.
An experiment with subjective rating of the perceived annoyance for each warning sound in diﬀerent
noise conditions was carried out and the mean STPL level was calculated for each sound/noise com-
bination. The results showed a weak correlation(0.26) between perceived annoyance and STPL level.
A similar correlation (0.20) was found using the simple SNR measure. However, one warning sound
consistently deviated positively compared to the model while another warning sound consistently devi-
ated negatively. Thus, it can be concluded that the Moore & Glasberg partial loudness model
cannot appropriately predict perceived annoyance of all EV warning sounds in noisy en-
vironments. However, the PL model could approximately predict the change in perceived annoyance
for a given warning sound when the noise condition was changed. The PL model was combined with a
modiﬁed psychoacoustic annoyance model as a partial psychoacoustic annoyance model, PPA. Using
this model the predictions yielded an overall correlation of 0.77 with the experimental data.
Overall, the results indicate that the M&G PL model is useful for predicting detectability but temporal
variations in the warning sounds induce eﬀects on detection threshold which the model does not account
for. Thus, further work should be done in order to address this. Concerning detectability, the ﬁrst
priority in eventual further work would be to conduct experiments that should clarify how amplitude
and frequency modulation aﬀects detectability. This can be done by speciﬁcally measuring the detec-
tion threshold for diﬀerent modulation frequencies. Furthermore, modulation depth should be varied for
amplitude modulation and frequency deviation should be varied for frequency modulation. The exper-
imental data should be used for ﬁtting a better time-varying model. Concerning perceived annoyance,
more experimental data should be obtained in order to validate the suggested model, PPA, speciﬁcally
made for EV warning sounds.

Appendix A.
Recommendation concerning warning
sound design approach
At my thesis defense for KAIST, I was suggested to add a personal recommendation concerning the EV
warning sound design approach. As it is not directly related to my research it given here in the appendix.
As mentioned in the introduction a qualitative assessment of the diﬀerent kinds of warning sounds were
made by Zeitler [1]. The assessments are seen in table A.1.
Table A.1: Psychoacoustic assessments and corresponding evaluation (positive/negative) of diﬀerent
warning sound alternatives. From [1]
As it is possible to achieve greater detectability with synthesized sounds, it is my personal opinion that,
over time, emitted warning sounds should be speciﬁcally synthesized in order to enhance detectability.
The qualitative assessment of annoyance for this choice is ’very high’ and for recognisability is ’very
low’. However, annoyance and recognisability are largely dependent on experience and expectations for
a certain product. Thus, over time, people will expect warning sounds for EVs to sound synthesized and
hence be perceived less annoying and more recognizable. However, there should be a transition phase.
This transition phase could be a combination of ICE components and synthesized Non-ICE sounds, this
option only gives a ’high’ detectability compared to ’very high’ for fully synthesized non-ICE sounds,
but it will be more recognisable and less annoying due to the familiarity with the ICE components.
To sum up the recommendation: First a transition phase with a combination of ICE components and
non-ICE components should be included in the designed warning sounds and slowly a transition towards
the completely synthesized sounds should be made as humans adapt to the new sounds.
