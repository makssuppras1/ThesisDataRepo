Abstract
We propose an Adaptive Wavelet Diffusion Network (ADWN) for high-quality single im-
age super-resolution (SISR). Based on the diffusion probabilistic model framework, our
method combines frequency-aware wavelet decomposition with a dual-path U-Net to clearly
separate and process low- and high-frequency information. The low-frequency branch
uses Transformer-style attention to capture global structure, while the high-frequency
branch focuses on enhancing details through edge-aware modules and spatial attention.
We also add selective kernel fusion and anti-aliasing methods to reduce artifacts and
improve the visual quality.
To test our approach, we run experiments on four standard benchmarks: Set5, Set14,
BSDS100, and General100. Our model gets the best LPIPS scores (lower is better)
among all diffusion-based methods, such as 0.3037 on BSDS100, which is better than
SR3[1] (0.3282), DiWa[2] (0.3583), and also CNN models like EDSR[3] (0.3063).
At
the same time, it shows strong SSIM results too (e.g., 0.8465 on Set5 and 0.8127 on
General100), with only 33M parameters—much fewer than SR3[1] (349M1 ) and DiWa[2]
(36M2). These results show that our frequency-guided model can reach both good per-
ceptual quality and structural accuracy in image super-resolution.

Introduction
In recent years, generative models have made good progress in image synthesis and
restoration tasks. From GANs [4] to VAEs [5], and more recently, diffusion models, this
area is moving toward more controllable and step-by-step generation, which help to im-
prove image quality and structure. Among them, diffusion models [6] are especially useful
for high-quality image generation because they are robust, flexible, and can use condi-
tional information at different scales.
Super-resolution (SR), as a basic vision task, has benefited from recent progress. It
is useful in areas like medical imaging, satellite remote sensing, and video restoration,
where both global structure and fine details need to be recovered from low-resolution
inputs. Among different SR tasks, Single Image Super-Resolution (SISR) is especially
important and often studied, because it tries to get high-resolution results from only one
low-resolution image, without using extra views or temporal information.
Single Image Super-Resolution (SISR) has many applications in real-world situations.
In medical imaging, increasing the resolution of MRI or CT scans can help detect small
lesions and improve diagnosis accuracy. In satellite and aerial remote sensing, super-
resolution gives clearer object edges and terrain features, which is useful for land use
classification and disaster monitoring. In surveillance systems, it helps recognize faces
or license plates from low-quality video.SISR is also helpful in video streaming services,
where low-resolution frames are sent to save bandwidth and then upsampled on the client
side to keep smooth playback while keeping good quality. These domain-specific applica-
tions show that SISR is not only a technical benchmark but also a task with real practical
value. However, meeting real-world needs is still hard, since many current methods can-
not provide both good perceptual quality and structural accuracy under low-resolution
inputs.Although CNN- and Transformer-based methods have made progress, there are
still some limitations. CNNs tend to overfit to local textures and have trouble recovering
global structures [7], While Transformers are good at modeling long-range dependencies
and keeping structural semantics, they are sensitive to noisy or unstable inputs when
proper normalization is mssing [8]. This makes them less reliable when the signal-to-
noise ratio is low.
To solve these challenges, we explore using diffusion models for SR. These models can
refine image details step by step through an iterative denoising process. They have sev-
eral advantages, like strong ability in structural modeling, robustness to input changes,
and avoiding mode collapse — problems that often happen in GAN-based methods. How-
ever, diffusion models still have some drawbacks: they often hallucinate high-frequency
details and find it hard to keep structural consistency in ambiguous or low-signal areas,
such as edges or texture transitions [1].
To reduce these problems, We propose a frequency-aware U-Net backbone within the
standard diffusion framework. With Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), the model splits
structural and textural parts and processes them through separate paths, which helps
improve perceptual quality and keep structure better. The Transformer is used in the low-
frequency branch to capture long-range structural information and provide rich context for
the diffusion model [9]. At the same time, the high-frequency branch focuses on refin-
ing textures and suppressing hallucinated noise, which improves visual fidelity and detail
alignment.

By combining the advantages of DWT, Transformer-based encoders, and diffusion-based
generation, we try to build a wavelet-guided diffusion model that can capture both global
structure and fine details, while reducing artifacts and unrealistic high-frequency details.
We test our method on four benchmark datasets: Set5, Set14, BSDS100, and Gen-
eral100. Our model gets the lowest LPIPS scores (which means best perceptual quality)
among all diffusion-based models on all datasets, and even beats strong CNN-based
models like EDSR [3] and RCAN [10] in terms of perceptual quality. For example, on
BSDS100, our model reaches an LPIPS of 0.3037, which is lower than DiWa [2] (0.3583),
SR3 [1] (0.3282), RCAN [10] (0.3290), and EDSR [3] (0.3063).
In terms of SSIM, our method gets the best performance among diffusion models on
several datasets. For example, it reaches an SSIM of 0.8465 on Set5 and 0.8127 on
General100, both higher than DiWa [2] and SR3 [1]. With only 33M parameters, our model
is also more efficient compared to DiWa (36M1) and SR3 (349M2), while still keeping
strong perceptual and structural quality.
To summarize, our main contributions are:
• We introduce a combined enhancement and attention design in the high-frequency
processing path to improve fine textures while reducing unstable high-frequency
noise.
• We design a dual-path U-Net architecture that models and balances low- and
high-frequency parts together, which helps to keep the structure well and reduce
the hallucinated details.
• We show that our model reaches state-of-the-art perceptual and structural per-
formance among diffusion-based SR methods, while using fewer parameters
than earlier works like SR3 [1] and DiWa [2].

Related Work
2.1
Traditional and Deep Super-Resolution Methods
Image Super-Resolution (SR) aims to recover a high-resolution image from a low-resolution
one. In the past decades, SR methods have made great progress, going from heuristic
techniques to deep learning-based methods.
In early heuristic methods, such as bicubic or Lanczos interpolation, smoothness assump-
tions were used to fill missing pixels.These methods are fast to compute, but they often
give blurry images without textures or clear structures. To solve this problem, some prior-
based approaches were proposed, such as edge-preserving regularization [11], sparse
coding, and dictionary learning [12]. These methods try to use prior knowledge or repeat-
ing patches to guess the high-resolution details, but they need heavy optimization and
usually don’t work well on various natural images.
Deep learning methods have made big progress in the super-resolution field. After the
appearance of SRCNN [13], researchers found that it is possible to use end-to-end CNNs
to learn how to map from low-resolution images to high-resolution ones. This idea gives
better results than traditional heuristic ways. Based on SRCNN, many strong models were
proposed: VDSR [14] used residual learning, EDSR [3] removed batch normalization to
improve fidelity, and RCAN [10] and SAN [15] added channel or non-local attention to
improve details. These models got much better scores on PSNR and SSIM in standard
datasets.
However, most CNN-based SR models still suffer from some crucial issues:
• Texture bias and local overfitting: CNNs have a known texture bias in classification
tasks [16], which means they usually rely more on local patterns. In super-resolution,
this often causes repeated textures or hallucinated details in areas where the global
structure should be consistent.
• Lack of global structure awareness: CNNs rely a lot on local features and are not
good at capturing the spatial relationships between different parts of the object,
which makes them insensitive to the overall structure [17]. In super-resolution tasks,
this usually leads to outputs that are not structurally consistent.
• Poor uncertainty modeling: Deterministic models always give just one prediction
and cannot show how confident the model is — which is important when used in
real-world situations [18].
These problems become more obvious when the upscaling factor is large, because it gets
harder to recover high-frequency content that matches the global structure. Based on
this, recent works have started using generative models, especially diffusion-based ones,
which treat SR as a conditional generation task. By step-by-step denoising from random
noise with LR input as condition, diffusion models give a good alternative to deterministic
CNNs. They may help to improve frequency recovery, uncertainty handling, and variety
in the details.

2.2
Diffusion-Based Super-Resolution Models
Generative modeling gives a strong way for solving image synthesis tasks, like super-
resolution. Some popular methods are Variational Autoencoders (VAEs [5]), Generative
Adversarial Networks (GANs [4]), and more recently, diffusion models. Among them,
diffusion models catch more attention because they are stable and can generate high-
quality images.
Unlike GANs [4], which suffer from unstable training and mode collapse, or VAEs [5], which
usually make too smooth results because of variational approximation, diffusion models
are based on a principled formulation. They treat the data distribution as a Markov chain
of gradual noising and denoising steps. Starting from Gaussian noise, the model learns
how to reconstruct the image by reversing a diffusion process, which makes it possible to
control the generation in a more detailed way.
DDPM [6] (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models) showed that diffusion models can
work well for generating images without any condition. Later, DDIM [19] and other faster
samplers helped to make the process quicker and also increased the variety of results.
These works gave a good base for using diffusion models in conditional tasks like super-
resolution.
In the area of image super-resolution, SR3 [1] (Super-Resolution via Repeated Refine-
ment) is a major milestone. It proposes a conditional denoising U-Net that begins from
Gaussian noise and refines the output step by step with the help of a low-resolution in-
put. SR3 showed that diffusion models could produce realistic textures and achieve better
performance than CNN-based approaches in human preference evaluations.
After SR3 [1], a series of diffusion-based SR methods came out to improve perceptual
quality and enhance structural reconstruction. For example, SRDiff [20] enhances the
diffusion model by introducing a cosine noise schedule and optimizing the conditional
training objective. CDPMSR [21] performs SR in two stages: first extracting conditional
features from a pre-trained SR model, and then using a diffusion model for structure-aware
denoising. SAM-DiffSR [22] injects SAM-derived segmentation priors into the forward dif-
fusion process to guide region-specific noise modeling, improving structural fidelity without
requiring SAM at inference time. Although these models use different designs, they all
aim to generate perceptual results while keeping structure and meaning consistent.
As perceptual fidelity becomes more important, researchers started to look into frequency-
domain modeling inside diffusion frameworks. DiWa [2] combines DDPM [6] with Discrete
Wavelet Transform (DWT) for single image super-resolution. By working in the wavelet
domain, the model can capture more high-frequency details and generate sharper results
with better structure. It shows that breaking the image into wavelet subbands at different
scales helps to recover fine details. Later, DWA [23] proposes a Differential Wavelet Am-
plifier module to further enhance high-frequency parts and improve texture reconstruction
through wavelet-guided features.
While these works show the potential of using wavelet priors in diffusion models, there
are still several important challenges that are not fully solved:
• In diffusion-based SR, frequency mismatch and detail oversmoothing are still com-
mon problems. Some methods like HF-Diff [24] try to solve them by using high-
frequency perceptual loss and distribution matching, but most models still lack clear
supervision or deep operation in the spectral domain.
• Moreover, hallucination is still a common problem in diffusion models, especially

in uncertain areas where the signal is weak. These models often generate sharp
but not semantically correct textures, which hurts the structure consistency. Some
recent works try to fix this by using complex loss functions [24],[21],[20], external
priors [25],[22], or multi-stage design [21], but these solutions usually bring more
model complexity and reduce generalization ability.
• Despite the success of diffusion-based SR models in generating fine textures, keep-
ing the global structural consistency is still a big challenge. Most models use spa-
tially uniform operations and do not have explicit structure priors, which causes se-
mantic inconsistency in complex scenes. StructSR [25] tries to solve this by using a
retrieval-based guidance during the sampling to bring in structure-aware constraints,
but it relies on external data too much, which makes it hard to generalize well across
different domains.
• Limited utilization of wavelets is still a problem in diffusion-based SR. Although some
works try to combine wavelet features, they usually focus only on one frequency
band or use them at early shallow stages of the network. For example, FDDiff [26]
uses wavelet decomposition to guide the training by high-frequency signals, but it
does not consider the low-frequency part clearly. On the other side, Wavelet Dif-
fusion Models are Fast and Scalable Image Generators [27] mostly work on the
low-frequency subbands, and ignore the recovery of high-frequency details. This
kind of incomplete frequency modeling makes it hard for these methods to achieve
both good semantic consistency and rich details.
These limitations encourage us to design a more deeply integrated frequency-aware dif-
fusion framework. In this work, we continue this research direction by:
• Explicitly split the image into low- and high-frequency subbands by using wavelet
transform;
• Using frequency-aware attention and conditional feature injection to refine both struc-
ture and texture together.
• Designing a U-Net denoiser that controls the diffusion process inside the wavelet
domain.
Unlike the earlier methods that only use wavelet features in shallow parts or focus on
separate frequency bands, our method clearly integrates wavelet decomposition during
the whole generation process. We split the input image into low-frequency and high-
frequency parts, then process them together in a frequency-aware U-Net backbone. We
also apply attention-based refinement and conditional feature modulation to help improve
both global structure and detailed textures. This design helps reduce frequency mismatch
and semantic inconsistency, avoids hallucination to some extent, keeps the model not too
complex, and keeps a good balance between low and high frequency modeling during
the denoising steps.
2.3
Wavelet Representations in Deep SR
Wavelet transforms offer a strong mathematical tool for analyzing signals at different res-
olutions. Compared with Fourier transforms, which split a signal into infinite sinusoidal
bases and lose the spatial location, wavelets are able to keep both spatial and frequency
information by using localized basis functions. Because of this, wavelets are quite suitable
for modeling signals that are not stable, like natural images.

In the field of image processing, the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) splits an image
into four subbands, separating low-frequency (approximation) and high-frequency (detail)
parts. This kind of decomposition helps the algorithm to work on specific frequency areas,
making it easier to handle textures, edges, and structure-related features more effectively.
With the development of deep learning, researchers started to explore how to combine
wavelet transforms with convolutional neural networks. In the early approaches, wavelets
were mainly used as a pre- or post-processing step — either to improve the input be-
fore sending it into the network, or to refine the output at the end [28]. Later, more ad-
vanced methods began to use wavelet transforms inside end-to-end models. For ex-
ample, wavelet decomposition was used to replace pooling operations [29], and some
models also fused wavelet subbands with convolutional features to help with texture en-
hancement and structure recovery [30].
Motivated by these ideas, we design a diffusion model that puts wavelet decomposition
as a core part of its probabilistic denoising process. We apply it directly inside our custom
U-Net architecture that runs the reverse diffusion. This design allows the model to:
• Dynamic interaction between low frequency and high frequency information across
scales;
• Frequency-aware conditioning of noise predictions;
• Structural and spectral coherence in the generated output results.
By aligning wavelet-based representations with the diffusion model, our method combines
strong prior knowledge and flexible probabilistic reasoning.
2.4
Transformers in Frequency-Aware Modeling
Transformer architectures were first developed for sequence modeling in natural language
processing (NLP), but later got widely applied in computer vision. Models like Vision
Transformer (ViT) and Swin Transformer have shown strong performance in tasks like
classification, detection, and dense prediction. This is mainly because of the self-attention
mechanism, which lets each element get information from other positions in the input,
helping the model to capture global features.
Compared with CNNs, which work with local receptive fields and slowly gather spatial
context, Transformers can catch long-range dependencies from the start. This helps the
model keep structure and semantic consistency in super-resolution tasks, especially when
recovering things that are hard to restore using only local operations.
Some recent works also looked into Transformers from the spectral point of view. For
example, ”Exploring Adversarial Robustness of Vision Transformers in the Spectral Per-
spective” [9] shows that self-attention layers keep more low-frequency content and are
less sensitive to high-frequency noise than CNNs. This kind of spectral tendency fits well
with wavelet-based images, where the low-frequency part from Discrete Wavelet Trans-
form (DWT) carries global structure and semantic meaning.
These findings suggest that self-attention works well in the low-frequency domain of wavelet-
decomposed images, making it possible to:
• Better modeling of global semantics in the resolution space;
• Robust reconstruction of low-frequency parts, which is important to keep the struc-
ture.

Several SR models already include attention mechanisms, like RCAN [10] and SAN [15],
but they mainly use local attention (such as channel or spatial) inside CNN backbones.
In comparison, combining Transformer-like self-attention with diffusion-based SR gives a
way to capture long-range structure.
Motivated by these ideas, we combine self-attention modules with the frequency-aware
U-Net backbone especially focusing on low frequency representations. This can deal
with problems like local texture overfitting and global structure misalignment in super-
resolution models.
Overall, although wavelet-based SR, transformer modeling, and diffusion models each
have their own strengths, they are often used separately.
Our method tries to bring
these methods together into one design — using wavelet decomposition for frequency
information, attention for keeping structural features, and diffusion models for stable sam-
pling. These parts work together as a complementary and effective solution for the super-
resolution task.

Preliminaries
3.1
Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT)
Single Image Super-Resolution (SISR) tries to recover high-resolution images from low-
resolution inputs. One key challenge is to recover detailed high-frequency textures while
also keeping low-frequency structure at the same time. CNNs often cannot clearly sepa-
rate these parts in feature space, which results in over-smoothed results or some halluci-
nated textures [7].
Wavelet decomposition offers a useful way to deal with this problem by clearly splitting
the signal into low-frequency and high-frequency parts. In our work, we apply discrete
wavelet decomposition (DWT), especially the Haar wavelet, together with the diffusion
process to improve structure-aware generation and make image details more clear.

3.2
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) [6] design image generation as a step-
by-step denoising process that slowly recovers clean data. This framework includes two
stages: the forward process, which adds noise to clean images gradually, and the reverse
process, which learns how to remove the noise step by step. At each timestep, a neural
network—usually a U-Net—predicts either the noise or the clean signal based on the
current noisy input. This iterative correction allows the model to handle small changes at
each step, and has shown better stability and performance compared to generating the
whole image in a single pass [6, 33].

4
Methodology
4.1
Overall Framework
Our super-resolution model is built on a frequency-aware diffusion framework that handles
image decomposition, processing, and reconstruction across different frequency bands.
The full architecture follows a U-shaped encoder–decoder design, as shown in Figure 4.1.
The core idea is to make a clear separation and keep balance between low-frequency
and high-frequency information. This is done by using the Discrete Wavelet Transform
(DWT) as a native operation inside the denoising network, so the model can learn how to
denoise and reconstruct images in a frequency-disentangled way. The goal of the model
follows the ideas described below:
Preserving the Integrity of Frequency Signals.
An important motivation is to preserve
the semantic and structural fidelity of the input before applying DWT. So, we add a power
normalization (details in 4.2.2), layer to keep the feature stability during the diffusion
steps, making sure that DWT works on a well-behaved signal. By doing this, the model
can keep a meaningful frequency representation, especially in the early noisy stages.
Balanced Low-/High-Frequency Processing.
Compared with previous SR models that
only use frequency information partly, we use task-specific processing paths for low-
frequency and high-frequency components:
• The low-frequency pathway uses residual blocks and self-attention to get global
structure, object layout, and semantic consistency.
• The high-frequency pathway uses the High-frequency Edge Enhancer (HFEE)(details
in 4.2.2) and Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM)(details in 4.2.4) to
enhance sharp edges and reduce noisy gradients, so the model can recover details
without learning from irrelevant noise too much.
This design makes sure the network doesn’t rely on low-frequency context too much and
also avoids high-frequency hallucination. By treating structure and detail as equally im-
portant but basically different things, the model gets better fidelity and realism across
different textures.
Preventing Hallucinated Detail.
To avoid fabrication and artifacts from high-frequency
patterns, we introduce multiple control mechanisms:
• High-frequency features are passed through skip connections to the decoder,
keeping the original edge information got from the decomposition;
• Decoder features are fused with outputs from previous stages using Selective Ker-
nel Feature Fusion (SKFF) module(details in 4.2.4 ), which act like semantic gates
to make sure the structure stays reliable;
• The final AFWU stages use anti-aliasing filters and residual correction to reduce
checkerboard artifacts and fix detail inconsistency.

Together, these modules help control the model’s ability to generate reasonable details
when there is enough low-resolution signal and structural information to support it.
In general, our framework brings a more principled way to combine wavelet theory with
diffusion models, which helps the model focus on structure during denoising and generate
images that look more realistic.
4.2
Network Architecture
4.2.1
Backbone Design
The backbone of our model uses a U-shaped structure, with a multi-scale encoder, a
bottleneck for context, and a decoder that mirrors the encoder. Each part is built to work
with frequency information, using wavelet modules to handle low- and high-frequency
signals separately. The full model is shown in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: The overall architecture of our proposed frequency-aware denoising U-Net in-
cludes an encoder with AFWD blocks and self-attention for modeling low-frequency struc-
tures, and a decoder with AFWU blocks. We also apply multi-scale skip connections and
SKFF modules to keep spatial consistency and align semantic information.
Encoder
The encoder is made of three stacked Adaptive Frequency Wavelet Decomposition
(AFWD) blocks. Each AFWD splits the input into low-frequency and high-frequency parts
using 2D DWT. The low-frequency branch goes through a residual block with timestep
input and a self-attention layer, which helps learn global structure. The high-frequency
part is refined by HFEE(details in 4.2.2). Each AFWD sends its output both to the next
layer and to the decoder through skip connections.

Decoder
The decoder follows the encoder and also has three Adaptive Frequency Wavelet Up-
sampling (AFWU) blocks. Each AFWU gets the upsampled input from the last layer and
the skip connection from the encoder. These are combined by the SKFF module (details
in 4.2.4). The high-frequency part is refined by CBAM attention (details in 4.2.4), while
the low-frequency path goes through residual blocks. At the end of each AFWU, we use
an anti-aliasing filter to reduce artifacts in reconstruction.
Bottleneck
Between the encoder and decoder, there is a multi-layer ConvNeXt-style bottleneck
built with several MixerBlocks. These blocks mix channel-wise MLPs, depthwise convo-
lutions, and modulation based on timestep. This part helps to combine and refine the
latent features, while keeping global consistency over different scales and steps.
Skip Connections and Feature Fusion
To keep spatial details and allow high-frequency to be reused in deep layers, we add
skip connections between the encoder and decoder at the same level.And we also use
SKFF modules (details in 4.2.4)to adaptively combine information from different scales,
helping the model keep important structural cues and enhance only the details that match
the semantic meaning and spatial layout. This leads to more realistic and trustworthy
results.
Overall, this backbone combines frequency decomposition, spatial attention, and mul-
tiscale fusion into a single design that supports frequency-aware denoising and stable
structural reconstruction.

ing, so that the separation between high- and low-frequency parts becomes more stable
and easier to understand.

4.2.4
Attention Mechanisms
Self-Attention for Low-Frequency Structure.
We use multi-head self-attention mod-
ules on the low-frequency path of the U-Net after AFWD. This design is motivated by two
main observations:
• Low-frequency subbands contain the global shape and semantic structure of the
image, which are very important for keeping perceptual fidelity.
• Self-attention is good at modeling long-range dependencies between different spa-
tial positions, which CNNs usually find hard to capture effectively [9].
High-Frequency Modulation via CBAM
In the high-frequency subband of AFWU, we
use the Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) to enhance structural details
while reducing useless high-frequency noise.
CBAM includes both channel and spatial attention mechanisms. In the channel atten-
tion part, it gathers global information (through average and max pooling) to guess which
feature channels—related to different wavelet directions—carry useful high-frequency sig-
nals, such as vertical or diagonal edges. In this way, the model can reduce the influence
from noisy or unimportant channels that do not really help the final reconstruction.
Next, the spatial attention mechanism enhances the spatial response within each chan-
nel by focusing on regions that contain important edges or detailed textures. This helps
the model to better capture high-frequency patterns, such as clear contours or repeating
textures, while also reducing the influence of noisy or unstable areas across the image.
Together, this two-stage attention pipeline makes CBAM work as a structure-aware se-
lector that keeps high-frequency features with rich details.
Multi-Scale Contextual Bottleneck
The bottleneck module mixes spatial, channel-wise,
temporal, and global context information to refine the latent features between the encoder
and decoder. It contains several ConvNeXt-style blocks, which combine depthwise con-
volution for local spatial mixing, MLP-based transformations for channel processing, and
a timestep-dependent conditioning module. Compared with Transformer-based designs,
these blocks do not bring much computation cost or require careful position encoding.
They allow the bottleneck to adaptively enhance structured features while keeping stable
performance during different denoising steps.

Selective Kernel Feature Fusion (SKFF)
To better combine features from different
branches in the encoder and decoder, we use a selective attention method inspired by
the SKFF module from MIRNet [34]. SKFF uses a shared global context to decide how
much focus each branch should receive. Rather than simply adding or stacking the fea-
tures, it helps the model give more attention to useful channels and reduce the impact of
the ones that are less helpful.
In our design, SKFF combines deeper semantic features with shallower structural priors,
especially the low-frequency components that have more stable spatial layouts. This
fusion strategy plays three important roles. First, it helps to reduce artifacts like aliasing
or fake textures by fusing features based on more reliable low-level information. Second,
it keeps the output structure more aligned with the input—preserving boundaries, object
shapes, and overall layout. Third, it adjusts how much the semantic features contribute
in the early denoising stage, acting like a gate that prevents the model from generating
unrealistic high-frequency details.
Differences Between Skip Connections and SKFF
Our architecture uses two kinds
of feature fusion paths, and each one plays a different role in the model.
First, we add skip connections that pass high-frequency features from each encoder
stage (AFWD) to its matching decoder stage (AFWU). These connections help preserve
fine details—like edges and textures—across the network and make sure they are added
back during upsampling.
In contrast, Selective Kernel Feature Fusion (SKFF) modules are used to merge the
upsampled decoder features from the previous stage (AFWD) with the features from
the current level. Instead of passing exact structural details, SKFF calculates attention
weights across different branches and lets the coarse features from deeper layers guide
the semantic importance during upsampling. This design complements the skip connec-
tions by helping the model avoid over-amplifying unstable or noisy activations in the high-
frequency decoder path.

Datasets
We train and test on several common datasets for single image super-resolution (SISR),

Training Set.
We train our model on the DIV2K dataset [35], which includes 800 high-quality natural im-

sion. During training, we randomly crop 48×48 low-resolution patches and their 192×192
high-resolution ground truths with aligned sampling. All experiments are done with scale
factor s = 4.
We use standard data augmentation methods, including horizontal flip and 90-degree
rotation, to improve the robustness and reduce overfitting.
5.1.2

For evaluation, we select four standard SR benchmark datasets, and each one tests dif-
ferent aspects of the model performance:
• Set5: A small-scale test set with 5 images, with few textures and clean edges.
It is often used as a fast sanity check and for comparing with classical models.
Because of its simplicity, getting high scores on Set5 often means the model is good
at preserving structures
• Set14: This dataset includes 14 images with more complex textures and more di-
verse scenes. It is commonly used to test structure generalization and how well the
model can handle different visual content, like human faces, buildings, and trees.
• BSDS100: Including 100 natural images with rich textures and different levels of
complexity, BSDS100 is widely used for large-scale evaluation. It is especially help-
ful for checking how stable and consistent the model is when dealing with real-world
content.
• General100: A dataset of 100 high-resolution, uncompressed images with sharp
textures. It is used as a benchmark to evaluate how well the model can recover fine
details and keep high perceptual quality, especially under clean signal conditions
with low noise.

Evaluation Protocol.
We use the entire test image during inference. PSNR and SSIM
are calculated on the Y (luminance) channel after converting the RGB images into YCbCr

with the pretrained AlexNet as the backbone.

metrics, we aim to give a balanced and trustworthy evaluation of both pixel-wise accuracy
and visual quality.
5.4
Compared Methods
To evaluate our model, we compare it with several representative SISR methods from
both traditional CNN-based and diffusion-based models. The selected baselines cover a
wide range of architectures and reflect how SR techniques have evolved in recent years.
EDSR [3].
Enhanced Deep Super-Resolution Network (EDSR) is a strong CNN-based
baseline that removes redundant components such as batch normalization from residual
blocks to improve performance. It also uses deeper residual layers to increase model
capacity, leading to high accuracy on standard benchmarks. We use EDSR as a repre-
sentative of classical deep CNN approaches.
RCAN [10].
Residual Channel Attention Network (RCAN) builds on the residual learn-
ing framework by combining a channel attention mechanism. This allows the network to
adaptively reweight feature channels, placing more focus on informative regions. RCAN
is included as a representative of attention-enhanced SR models and is widely regarded
as a strong state-of-the-art baseline.
SR3 [1].
SR3 is a diffusion-based super-resolution model that formulates SR as a de-
noising process. It refines the output step by step using a denoising diffusion probabilistic
model (DDPM [6]), and achieves strong reconstruction fidelity, especially at large upscal-
ing factors. We include SR3 as a representative of early diffusion-based SR methods.
DiWa [2].
DiWa is a recent diffusion-based super-resolution model that combines the
Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) into the DDPM [6] framework to get better high-frequency
details. Unlike SR3, which operates directly in pixel space, DiWa performs the diffusion
Adaptive Wavelet Diffusion Model for High-Fidelity Super-Resolution

==PAGE:32==
process in the wavelet domain. This allows the model to focus on learning sparse high-
frequency residuals, resulting in more accurate reconstructions with fewer parameters
and faster inference. It also introduces an initial wavelet-domain predictor to estimate a
coarse reconstruction, which is then refined through iterative denoising. We include DiWa
as a representative of frequency-aware diffusion models.
Our Method.
Our model is a frequency-aware diffusion framework that incorporates a
new architecture combining multi-scale wavelet priors with structural attention mecha-
nisms in both the encoder and decoder. Compared to DiWa [2], our design further en-
hances high-frequency fidelity by introducing dedicated frequency refinement blocks and
semantic guidance modules. This leads to more accurate reconstruction of fine textures
while maintaining global structural consistency.
To ensure a fair comparison, all diffusion-based methods—including SR3[1] and DiWa[2]—
are trained and evaluated using the same 1000-step linear noise schedule, consistent with
the configuration of our model.

Discussion
This part explains the wider meaning behind our design, shares main findings from the
experiments, and also talks about some possible limitations.
Frequency-Aware Signal Decomposition.
One key result is that adding frequency-
domain priors into the diffusion process is very helpful. Our model clearly splits the signal
into low- and high-frequency parts and handles them through different paths. This de-
sign helps recover both semantic structure and fine textures, giving better LPIPS scores
than normal CNNs and basic diffusion models, and also performs competitive on SSIM
and PSNR. The ablation (A1) also shows that removing the wavelet path quickly lowers
performance on all metrics, which proves it is an important part.
We also noticed that SR3 [1], which works in the pixel domain and uses a pure MSE-
based noise prediction loss, often gives slightly desaturated or gray-looking results ( Fig-
ure 6.1,6.2,6.3). This is a common issue in diffusion models trained with standard recon-
struction losses, where the network tries to reduce pixel-wise error but ends up losing per-
ceptual richness. The outputs are usually correct in structure but look conservative, with
less color variation and weaker vibrancy. On the other hand, our model uses frequency-
aware signal decomposition, which splits low-frequency color and high-frequency texture.
Together with our attention-based UNet design, this helps the network not only keep struc-
tural details but also bring back more vivid and natural colors.
Balancing Attention and Frequency Enhancement.
Our ablation results give more
understanding of how attention and high-frequency enhancement work. Removing global
self-attention (A3) gives the lowest LPIPS score, but the final images still have problems,
like edges that are too sharp or details that look fake. This shows that LPIPS alone may not
fully show perceptual issues, and self-attention is useful for keeping semantic consistency.
On the other hand, when all high-frequency enhancement modules (A2) are removed—
including frequency-based attention and refinement paths—the perceptual quality drops.
The model cannot bring back fine textures or sharp edges properly, which means high-
frequency information needs to be handled in a special way.
These results tell us a general idea: attention and enhancement blocks should not be
used randomly. They need to be placed with purpose—self-attention helps model global
structure, and high-frequency enhancement helps improve texture details. Keeping a
good balance between them is important to get natural and realistic outputs.
Lightweight Design with Competitive Performance.
Our model achieves strong per-
formance with only 33M parameters, which is much less than SR3 [1] (349M1) and DiWa
[2] (36M2). Even with fewer parameters, our model gets the best LPIPS scores on all
datasets, and still keeps competitive results on PSNR and SSIM. This shows that com-
bining frequency-aware design with attention modules can make a lightweight, efficient,
and expressive SR model.

Limitations.
Although our design shows strong performance by separating low- and
high-frequency features, it may not be the only good solution. Other design options might
bring even better reconstruction quality. Also, using self-attention in the low-frequency
path adds a clear computation cost. For high-resolution images, this step makes inference
slower and uses more memory under the same hardware, which may limit the model’s use
in real-time or low-resource cases. Finally, our current method uses fixed settings like a
linear noise schedule and the Haar wavelet. Trying other types of noise schedules or
wavelet bases may help the model work better on different types of images.

Future Work
While our ADWN framework shows strong performance in frequency-aware super-resolution,
several aspects remain worth further exploration.
Architecture Efficiency & Design Flexibility.
The model uses self-attention in the low-
frequency branch. While it works well, it also brings high computation cost. In the future,
we may try more efficient attention types, like window-based attention or even attention-
free designs.
Also, our model now uses a fixed linear noise schedule and the Haar
wavelet. To make the design more flexible, we plan to test adaptive wavelet transforms
and different noise schedules (such as cosine), which might match the image character-
istics better.
Accelerated Sampling Strategies.
Our model follows a 1000-step DDPM schedule,
which makes the inference slow. To support real-time use, we plan to try faster sampling
methods like DDIM [19], DPM-Solver [36]/ DPM-Solver++ [37], SDE-based Sampling [38]
or PLMS [39] so we can reduce the number of inference steps.
Extending to Broader Image Restoration Tasks.
Although ADWN is mainly designed
for super-resolution, its frequency-aware and modular design makes it possible to adapt
to other tasks like inpainting, deblurring, and denoising. These tasks can also benefit from
separating structure and detail, which suggests that our framework could be extended to
more image restoration problems.

Conclusion
In this work, we propose ADWN, a frequency-aware U-Net backbone designed for sin-
gle image super-resolution (SISR) within a standard diffusion framework. It combines
Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), Transformer-based encoders, and diffusion-based
generation to enhance both structural consistency and fine texture recovery.
This U-Net architecture includes frequency-related modules: Adaptive Frequency Wavelet
Decomposition (AFWD) and Upsampling (AFWU) blocks, a High-Frequency Edge En-
hancer (HFEE), localized attention using CBAM, and cross-scale semantic fusion using
SKFF. The model is conditioned through a simple low-resolution/high-resolution concate-
nation and trained with multiple loss functions, including Smooth L1 and a frequency-
domain (high-frequency) loss.
Experiments on benchmark datasets show that our method gets competitive or even bet-
ter results compared to strong CNN models (like EDSR[3] and RCAN[10]) and recent
diffusion-based methods (SR3[1], DiWa[2]), especially for perceptual quality and structure
consistency. The ablation studies also confirm that each part of the model—especially the
wavelet decomposition and frequency-based attention—plays an important role in the fi-
nal result. At the same time, ADWN uses fewer parameters than all the compared models,
but still gives sharper textures, better directional structure, and fewer artifacts.
Our results highlight that frequency-aware representation is important in generative super-
resolution, and suggest possible future directions like faster sampling and adaptive trans-
forms. We hope this work can be a small step toward combining perceptual realism and
stable generation in SR models.
