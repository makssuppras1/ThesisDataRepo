ABSTRACT
This thesis investigates the degradation testing of coloured materials for use in photovoltaic
(PV) modules.
The research involves extensive materials testing, including Ethylene
Vinyl Acetate (EVA), Polyolefin Elastomer (POE) and Thermoplastic Polyolefin (TPO) as
encapsulants, coated ColorFoil and coloured Solaxess as interlayers. Their performance is
evaluated under accelerated aging conditions: UV light exposure and Damp Heat aging,
according to IEC 61215.
The experimental work includes the preparation of samples with several combinations of
encapsulant and interlayers, followed by performance characterization through IV curves
and electroluminescence imaging, mechanical peel tests, and optical assessments. The
study aims to understand the impact of coloration and of the inclusion of interlayers on
the durability of PV modules, observing eventual side reactions or incompatibilities.
Results indicate that coloured encapsulants and coloured/coated interlayers have relatively
low impact on the durability. Higher performance degradation after UV aging is observed
for samples where TiO2 is present or supposed, suggesting incompatibility. All the colours
appear acceptably stable with aging, but non uniformity on some foils could be highly
influencing. Adhesion results show no negative influence by colouration, but imperfect
lamination makes this conclusion uncertain.
The findings highlight the critical role of materials selection in the design of durable
and efficient coloured PV modules, with high potential observed from the technologies
implemented and additional investigation needed on specific aspects.

FOREWORD
4.1
Motivation
In 2024, climate change and global warming are widespread and established concepts
among the world population, due to the extreme events gradually becoming common
recurrences. Meanwhile, according to IEA [1], Russia’s invasion of Ukraine continues to
reshape the global energy system in profound ways. Trade patterns for oil and natural gas
have shifted dramatically since the war began, as governments look to strengthen their
energy security. At the same time, cleaner alternatives to fossil fuels are growing faster
than ever towards a complete replacement.
Solar Photovoltaic (PV) plays, and is expected to do even more, a decisive role in this
transition. In 2023, PV power production accounted for 5.4% of the global electricity
generation, with an installed capacity increase of almost 32% from the previous year. IEA
reports that, to achieve the tripling goal for the Net Zero Scenario by 2030 set after the
COP28 of Dubai, PV installed capacity is expected to quadruplicate (Figure 4.1). [2]
Figure 4.1. Cumulative renewable electricity capacity in the main (forecasted) and accelerated
cases and Net Zero Scenario. [2]
Most of the PV modules are attached to existing structures or in open fields. However,
especially in urban areas, the exponential growth of installation is related to an increasing
Not In My Backyard (NIMBY) syndrome. Some residents find disturbing the view of PV
panels on their or neighboring roofs, and their reluctance slows down the decarbonization
of the electricity grid [3] [4] completely ignoring the huge potential. Indeed, incentivized
local electricity production reduces the need for grid expansion, and the use of existing
structures reduces the Balance Of System (BOS). [5]
A way to overcome the NIMBY is to integrate and camouflage the PV modules with the

building components, making them almost not distinguishable in some cases. Building
Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV) add to the active role of the module also the structural
function of a building material (roof, walls, windows...). Moreover, coloured BIPV solutions
confirmed to enhance the appeal of PV products in the building market.
IEA PVPS [6] recommends the following definition for BIPV:
"A BIPV module is a PV module and a construction product together, designed to be a
component of the building. A BIPV product is the smallest (electrically and mechanically)
non-divisible photovoltaic unit in a BIPV system which retains building-related functionality.
If the BIPV product is dismounted, it would have to be replaced by an appropriate
construction product."
Since BIPV serve then both electrical and structural functions, assessing the degradation
is fundamental since the lifetime has to match the one of the building components, which
generally spans from 60-70 years up to 120 for residential buildings [7].
Previous research deeply investigated PV modules components deterioration under outdoor
weather conditions (heat, humidity, UV light) [8] [9] [10] [11], but studies on the influence on
PV modules durability of coloured/coated interlayers and coloured varieties of encapsulants
are still few [12], and as new solutions are developed more testing is needed. Hence, the
research questions of this thesis are:
• Will the added coloured/coated interlayers be compatible with the polymeric structure
of a typical PV laminate, without significantly affecting its durability?
• Will the aging mechanisms (heat, UV light) degrade the coloured materials at a pace
that makes them unsuitable for their purpose?
• Will the adhesion of the encapsulants be affected by their colouration?
4.2
Approach and thesis structure
The research questions are addressed fabricating PV samples covered by three different
encapsulants available in the PV industry (EVA, POE, TPO transparent and coloured)
from different manufacturers and two different interlayer technologies (ColorFoil, Solaxess).
Accelerated aging conditions are recreated in the lab, according to the Standard IEC 61215
[13] and the samples are analysed and intercompared.
The thesis report is structured in three main parts: Part I - Introduction, Part II -
Experimental, and Part III - Results and Discussion.
In Part I, following this section, Chapter 5 outlines the essential theoretical background
needed to fully understand the aim of this work and illustrates the state-of-the-art of the
involved technologies.
Part II, which consists of Chapters 6 and 7, describes the experimental approach. An
overview of the materials used and of the samples manufacture is presented in Chapter 6;
Chapter 7 is dedicated to the developed methodology for performance, mechanical and
optical characterization of the samples, visual inspection and accelerated aging realization.
Part III contains the last five chapters, with results analysed from different perspectives

THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
This chapter gives to the reader the necessary theoretical knowledge to fully understand
the final results. A general overview of PV and BIPV modules is shown, with focus on the
specific areas of interest of this thesis.
5.1
PV module composition
Two typical compositions of PV modules, reported in Figure 5.1, are dependent on the
material used as back side, either backsheet (Glass-Backsheet modules, G-B) or glass
(Glass-Glass modules, G-G), the latter becoming more used with the advent of bifacial PV
modules because of higher stability, and of BIPV due to improved fire safety [14].
Figure 5.1. Exploded schematic view of generic G-B (left) and G-G (right) modules, from [8]
The main components are:
• Front glass:
transparent, it gives mechanical stability to the module while
maximizing the light transmitted to the underlying PV cell, eventually with the
introduction of antireflective patterns. For G-G modules, an extra glass is located at
the back;
• Encapsulant: thin transparent layer that adheres and connects the other components
while sealing and protecting the PV cell;
• PV cells: mostly doped-silicon based, convert photons in electricity. They are serial
interconnected in parallel strings;
• Polymer foil (only G-B): also referred to as Backsheet, it electrically insulates and
minimizes water and oxygen diffusion through the back;
• Metal frame: usually made of aluminium, it seals the module edges.

5. Theoretical background
Note that the aluminium frame may not be necessary, depending on the purpose, and can
also be applied to G-G modules even if not showed in Figure 5.1.
The focus of this thesis is on the encapsulants and different technologies are tested.
5.2
Encapsulants
The encapsulant of a PV module is a transparent polymeric foil that is located between
each layer of the PV laminate. It is of particular importance for the long term durability
of the latter and fulfills several different functions simultaneously, all of which are essential
to the proper functioning of the module: adhesion to all the other components, sealing and
protection of the solar cells, optimal optical coupling between glass and cells, maintaining
electrical insulation and contrasting humidity and oxygen penetration. [15]
Key technical specifications of encapsulants include: melting temperature, volume resistivity,
moisture transmission rate, light absorption, Young’s modulus, glass transition temperature,
breathability/diffusion rates and crosslinking strategy. The latter is of great importance,
since it is a stabilization process in polymer chemistry that promotes the multidimensional
interconnection of polymer chains, turning a liquid polymer into a solid or gel.
Crosslinking is essential for polymers to become mechanically stronger and resistant to
heat, wear or solvent attack, and for commonly used encapsulants it can be either chemical
or physical. The first one occurs with formation of covalent linkages through chemical
agents, while the second one involves physical interactions between polymer chains [16]. A
general example of both crosslinking networks between two different polymers can be seen
in Figure 5.2.

5.2.1
EVA
EVA is a random ethylene/vinyl acetate copolymer, with a vinyl acetate content typically
in the range 28–33% (w/w). It is composed by the polymer resin, the curing agent (usually
organic peroxide) and other additives (i.e.
UV stabilizer, radical trapping stabilizer,
antioxidant agents).
Thermoplastic, with a melting range of 60–70 ◦C, mildly opaque, soft and easily deformable,
the native EVA material would not fulfill either the mechanical nor the optical requirements
needed for a PV module. However, by crosslinking the copolymer chains in the course of
lamination, the moldable EVA sheet is transformed into an elastomeric, highly transparent
encapsulation: a loose, three-dimensional network is formed by chemical crosslinking of
Page 7 of 111
==PAGE:19==
5. Theoretical background
the polymer chains causing an increase in thermal stability of the material and preventing
it from melting. The crosslinking process starts at temperatures between 120 and 130 ◦C
and is initiated via decomposition of peroxides to start a radical cross-linking reaction
(Figure 5.5). [23]
Figure 5.5. Visualization of EVA cross-linking adhesion process between the EVA layer and an
adjacent one ("other layer"). [24]
Peroxides are decomposed by heat (or UV+O2) and form free radicals that will in turn
lead to the formation of covalent bonds between polymer chains. The end of the polymer
chains, that has diffused into the adjacent surface, forms crosslinking bonds while still being
bound to the EVA, creating a strong adhesive link between the surfaces (called diffusive
bonding). Incomplete crosslinking of the polymer may lead to residual (unconsumed)
peroxide catalyst which can then take part in further UV-initiated photo-chemical reactions,
causing degradation during the module service lifetime. [25]
The degree of crosslinking is controlled by lamination time, lamination temperature
(affecting the amount of peroxide activated per time unit) and initial peroxide concentration.
EVA manufacturers suggest minimum lamination parameters (temperature, duration)
necessary to obtain an acceptable degree.
Degradation
The main degradations of EVA observed in past experiences include:
1. Discoloration. EVA is subject to gradual yellowing in PV modules exposed to the
outdoor environment over the years, inevitable and mainly caused by the degradation
of the UV stabilizer additives in the EVA compound [9]. In fact, EVA resin doesn’t
normally absorb light in the UV-visible spectral range passing through the front
glass.
2. Acetic acid formation. Acetic acid (HaC) is produced by hydrolysis of EVA,
mainly happening at temperatures between 120°C and 150°C before curing [26]
(temperatures reached during EVA manufacturing and lamination) and because of
humidity after curing. Despite being HaC volatile, in a PV module it is sealed
between the glass and the backsheet so it manifests its corrosive effects on the tin
coating of the cell metallization. It consumes the metal and exposes the underlying
copper core of the cell interconnection, whose direct contact with EVA causes a
brown discoloration.
Page 8 of 111
==PAGE:20==
5. Theoretical background
3. Delamination. Delamination can occur at any of the interfaces within the PV
module (glass/EVA, cell/EVA, contacts/EVA, ribbon/EVA), with the interfacial
bonding mainly being deteriorated by temperature, humidity and Ultraviolet (UV)
light. Then, stress exerted at the interface from residual thermal stresses or from
external mechanical stress trigger the delamination. New interfaces are created (e.g.
glass/air and air/EVA, in case of glass/EVA delamination) and cause reflection losses
(Fresnel’s equations estimates an interfacial reflectance of about 4% [27]).
Moreover, moisture diffusing into the laminate can condense in the delaminated area
and accelerate its propagation [28]. forming pathways that facilitate even more water
permeability and formation of HaC.
5.2.2
POE
POE encapsulant, introduced as alternative to overcome the EVA limitations, consists
of a polyethylene backbone with different side groups [10]. The main advantage of these
materials is the replacement of the vinyl acetate side groups of EVA, by acrylates, acrylic
acids or n-alkanes; and consequently, no HaC can be formed/split off [29] with reduced
risk of corrosion and delamination.
Comparative studies have also demonstrated that POE are semi-transparent in the UV
region, whereas EVA has a sharp cut-off at wavelengths of 375 nm absorbing all the UV
radiation below. Apart from a higher melting temperature measured for POE, EVA and
POE exhibit similar thermal and thermo-mechanical properties; like EVA, POE films
contain crosslinking agents, so the lamination process is similar [10].
In addition, POE encapsulants in PV modules are not pure polymers; the formulations
contain various additives, including stabilizers, curing agents and adhesion promoters,
which readily affect their performance and susceptibility to degradation. [30]
Degradation
Not many studies are available about long term degradation of POE: despite being marketed,
it is still a novel material and extensive research is needed. Generally, it was observed
that POE is subjected to UV aging, susceptible to photo-oxidation of the polymer
chain and depletion of additives and stabilizers [10] [31] [32]. The optical degradation
after exposure to UV radiation is manifested by the changes in optical transmittance and
yellowness index, at even faster rates when UV stabilizers are not included [11].
Lastly, no corrosion of adjacent metals were observed after aging [10].
5.2.3
TPO
TPO encapsulant is structurally similar to POE, consisting as well of a polyethylene
backbone with different side groups [10] and replacing the vinyl acetate side groups of
EVA, by acrylates, acrylic acids or n-alkanes [29]. Moreover, TPO is also transparent in
the UV region (like TPO), allowing more UV light to reach the PV cell.
Differently from POE, TPO doesn’t cross-link chemically but has small crystalline segments
in its morphology, that act as physical cross-links below the melting point. It is fabricated
Page 9 of 111
==PAGE:21==
5. Theoretical background
without a peroxide curing agent, has few chemical interactions and requires a short
lamination time (about 10 min) [33], simply melting and adhering to the adjacent layers.
Moreover, the elastic modulus and shear viscosity values are higher for TPO than for POE
and EVA [10].
Degradation
Not much literature is available about TPO degradation, but results similar to those for
POE (Section 5.2.2) were observed. Moreover, yellowing occurred after thermal aging,
which could be attributed to interactions of additives and was reversible upon irradiation
[10].
5.3
Adhesion
Adhesion is the ability of dissimilar surfaces to cling to one another and is a fundamental
necessity for PV modules. Adhesion strength of polymeric encapsulants to the glass
superstrates is a factor that can critically affect the performance reliability and durability
of modules [34].
There are five types of adhesion: mechanical, chemical, dispersive, electrostatic and diffusive
[35]. EVA, POE and TPO respectively use diffusive, chemical and mechanical ones in
PV laminates. For EVA, diffusive bonding occurs when its species penetrate an adjacent
surface (glass/color layer) while still being bound to the phase of their surface of origin.
Adhesion is not a property of EVA itself but of the system. For POE chemical adhesion is
expected [36] while TPO is physically bound (mechanical adhesion) [10] [30].

5.5
Material compatibility
The general PV laminate previously presented in Figure 5.1 is a complex polymeric system,
and the compatibility between the various components is crucial when it comes to durability.
[41]
Page 11 of 111
==PAGE:23==
5. Theoretical background
Previous studies have examined EVA in combination with a selection of different backsheets;
higher oxidation rates of the encapsulant strictly were observed for backsheets with high
oxygen transmission rates, discouraging their coupling [42]. Oxidative ageing is a well known
mechanism where the degradation propagates through a radical chain reaction, described
satisfactorily by a general mechanistic scheme called ’standard scheme’, estabilished in the
1940s by the RAPRA co-workers [43] and reported in Figure 5.9. It is a clear example
where reactive byproducts initiate a chain reaction within the same polymer, accelerating
the degradation. Nowadays, antioxidant additives are introduced in modern encapsulants,
inhibiting the reactive species (radicals). [44] [11]
Figure 5.9. ’Standard scheme’ of the oxidative ageing. (1) corresponds to the initiation step,
(2) and (3) to propagation steps; (4)–(6) to termination steps. P◦and PO◦
2 refer respectively to
alkyl and peroxy radicals; Per refers to peroxide groups; µ is the formation yield of radicals in the
initiation step. The initiation step (1) can be thermal, photochemical or radiochemical. [44]
When more materials (polymers in this case) are connected, the degradation products
of one might degrade the neighboring ones or incompatibilities could occur. For PV
laminates, HaC formed from EVA degradation was observed facilitating the cracking of
polyamide-based (PA-based) backsheets with the aid of light, heat, and moisture [45] or,
as previously outlined, corroding the metal of the PV cell interconnections [26]. Moreover,
different materials could need to match their intrinsic characteristics to be put together,
like UV transparent encapsulants that require backsheets with UV stabilized inner layers
in order to avoid degradation of the latter [46].
Introducing new materials in the already complex structure of a PV laminate is then
delicate, and crucial is to identify any interaction that could affect performance and
durability.

7. Methodology
The methodology adopted in this thesis it hereby presented in detail. Performance, optical
and mechanical characterizations, together with visual inspection, are performed to assess
and correlate eventual effects due to the involved aging mechanisms. Refer to the flowchart
of Figure 7.1 for the chronological order of the different phases.
7.1
Performance characterization
The aging of the PV modules can occur with different mechanisms, and the the samples’
quality characterizations chosen in this research are Current-Voltage (IV) curves and
Electroluminescence (EL) testing. Each measurement is undertaken for raw cells, for the
unaged modules and after all the aging procedures (UV exposure and Damp Heat) as
shown in Figure 7.1, in order to distinguish handling damage from induced degradation.
7.1.1
IV curves
The most reliable way to assess a cell/module’s performance is to determine its IV curve,
from which important performance parameters can be extrapolated (e.g. Open Circuit
Voltage Voc, Short Circuit Current Isc, Maximum Power Pmax) and adjusted to Standard
Conditions (STC) (GSTC = 1000W/m2, TSTC = 25◦C).
Setup
The setup used (Figure 7.2) is a commercially available Pasan cell tester, consisting of
a Xenon arc lamp (working with high voltage) that illuminates the test plane, where a
reference cell measures the irradiance and a temperature sensor monitors the sample’s
temperature. Current and voltage measurements are performed with the 4-wires method,
avoiding the influence of cables resistance, and the synchronization of light flash and
IV measurements is handled by a Pasan dedicate software (PasanSLAB) governing an
electronics box.
Figure 7.2. Views of the Pasan setup: general (left) and zoom on the test plane (right)
Page 23 of 111
==PAGE:35==
7. Methodology
Note that inside the lamp housing, a series of deflectors improve the spatial uniformity of
the light. However, uncertainty measurements are performed to verify this condition in all
the three dimensions (results can be seen later on in this Section).

7.2
Mechanical characterization
Essential aspect for a PV module lifetime is its mechanical integrity, and even more for
BIPV since it serves structural functions as well. The front glass is subject to most of the
physical stresses, but the encapsulant’s adhesion to it is also crucial and can vary with the
technology used. In this thesis, peel test is performed to assess this last aspect for all the
encapsulants used (both transparent and coloured).
7.2.1
Peel test
Peel test is a diagnostics method that allows to measure the adhesion strenght between
two materials by pulling them apart at a specific relative angle; a 180◦peeling between
encapsulant and front glass is performed, according to the Standard EN ISO 8510-2:2010
[58].
Setup
The setup used (Figure 7.10) is an Instron® force transducer model 2519-105 [59] equipped
with a force gauge of 1000 N capacity and two manual wedge action grips 2716 series [60],
available at DTU Construct. The transducer is controlled by the Bluehill 2 software, which
exports the acquired data in CSV files.
Page 30 of 111
==PAGE:42==
7. Methodology
Figure 7.10. Setup for peel test
Figure 7.11. Sample position
Four parallel strips 10 mm wide and 100 mm long are cut with a scalpel on the back of
the sample (more details in the following ’Sample preparation’). The sample is secured
to the lower grip, while the upper one holds and pulls the strip (as in Figure 7.11), on
which lubricant is applied to reduce the friction with the sample. According to the ISO
8510-2:2010, the rate of grip separation has to be 100 mm/min ± 10 mm/min, with
minimum peel length of 125 mm and excluding the first 25 mm from results analysis. Due
to experimental constraints, the last two requirements are not fully followed in this thesis,
and a pull speed of 50 mm/min is used (in some cases down to 25 mm/min, for more
fragile strips) as in other relevant literature [12] [61] [51].
Note that in a past project with similar materials and conditions (Marco Marcellan’s
master thesis [12]) a critical thickness of tc = 0.934 mm was found after performing pull
test on the strips. Since they are at least 1 mm thick, the plastic deformation component
is negligible and not supposed to influence the results.
Samples preparation
The preparation of the samples designated for peel test starts before lamination and
required some prior research. To ensure that the peeling occurs at the interface between
encapsulant and glass, the first portion of the peel strip should remain unbonded from the
latter even after lamination, functioning as ’peel primer’. Moreover, the primer has to be
long enough to pass the glass edge to reach the upper grip of the setup, when bent 180◦
over the rest of the strip.
In the Standard ISO 8510 [58] it is suggested to place a release tape in correspondence of
the desired peel primer, impeding the adhesion between rigid and flexible specimen. Some
other papers about peel test introduced it with different lengths and orientations: in [12]
the tape was more than 5 cm long, with a peel length of around the same length; in [62]
backsheet+encapsulant were 5 cm longer than the glass, on the side of the primer, but
without release tape; similarly, in [63] longer backsheet+encapsulant and release tape were
implemented together.

7.5
Accelerated aging
Over its lifetime, a PV module is subject to weather conditions that affect its durability
and degrade it with time: Ultraviolet (UV) light, heat and humidity as main ones. The
Standard EN IEC 61215:2021 [13] was issued with specific parameters for aging tests,
reproducing the more common stressors and with a pass/fail criteria depending on eventual
early failures. In this thesis, UV exposure and Damp Heat (DH) are implemented.
7.5.1
UV exposure
Starting from the IEC 61215 requirements, the UV exposure setup is presented and
characterized, with final overview of the total irradiation received by the samples.
IEC 61215 requirements
The reference is MQT 10 and it reports minimum 15 kWh/m2 total irradiation in
the wavelength interval [280,400] nm, with 3-10% of it in [280,320] nm, constant
Tmodule = (60 ± 5)◦C during the test and maximum light intensity 250 W/m2 with spatial
uniformity ± 15%.
Page 37 of 111
==PAGE:49==
7. Methodology
Considering that the out of the 1000 W/m2 of AM 1.5 spectrum [69] 46.1 W/m2 fall in the
[280,400] nm interval, the required 15 kWh/m2 correspond to 325 h (14 days) of exposure.
A typical outdoor day/night average is about 250 W/m2 [70], 4 times lower than the AM
1.5, bringing the needed exposure up to 1,302 h (54 days). Thus, the test does not provide
assurance that a PV module will withstand 20 or more years of UV radiation, but is only
designed to provide minimum standards for PV panel construction and precondition the
modules for later pass/fail criteria.

8.2
Discussion
IV values variability
The variability observed for the IV values of the raw cells is most likely due to binning
of the PV manufacturer, with the cells grouped by range of power and not necessarily
all identical [75]. The differences are not relevant when assembling PV modules, but for
performance analyses are to be taken into account.
Moreover, Voc usually changes significantly with the temperature (up to 35%/◦C), justifying
the variability observed. Its value can be considered a temperature proxy.
Cell-module losses
Cell-to-module losses of the Solaxess samples reflect the expected values from the datasheets
[52], within a range of ±5%, and are the highest registered among the total population,
suggesting that the technology might not be energy efficient for BIPV modules production.
Coloured TPOs seem more promising, with lower losses compared to the same SX colours.
CF interlayers show relatively low losses, as expected due to not showing real colour
apapearance, with small difference between SiO2 coated and uncoated ones; colour
appearance difference between TiO2 samples makes it hard to assess precise losses.
Cells cracks
The cracks here reported (consequence of the lamination process since manual handling
of the cells and high pressure are involved) are not expected to develop further with the
considered aging mechanisms (UV, DH) since thermal cycling is not performed at any
stage.

9.1.2
Discussion
IV losses
∆Isc,% and ∆Voc,% values around -1% are supposedly caused by Light Induced Degradation
(LID) due to Boron-Oxygen defects initially present in the PV cells and accompanied by
reduction of the minority-carriers lifetime. As demonstrated by Sopori et al. [76], light
soaking of PV cells under 1-sun for 72h resulted in Isc and Voc losses around 0.5%.
Samples with TiO2 coated CF interlayer (E1F TiO2 and P1F TiO2) present high Isc
losses. Considering that TiO2 is the most important white pigment used in the polymer
industry [77], it was likely used as a pigment for the White and Beige SX interlayers. TiO2
thin films, when UV light irradiated, generates electron-hole pairs and resistivity that
decreases with the intensity; without UV light, the resistivity is immeasurabily large [78].
All these samples (E1F TiO2, P1F TiO2, Y1S Beige, P1SG White, Y1SG Beige) could the
be subject to significant ∆Isc,% due to irreversible interactions between PV cells and the
photo-activated TiO2, during UV exposure. No such effect nor the photo-activation of
TiO2 when used as coating or pigment have been found in literature
Adhesion
Peel test shows an adhesion decrease after UV light exposure for EVA and POE, while
POEYP and TPO have higher adhesion after aging. However, the low number of strips on
which the peel test results are based for POE and POEYP samples is not enough to draw
conclusions on these two encapsulants.
Interesting is instead the consistent 20-30% increase of adhesion for TPO samples. TPO
seems to be less stabilized to UV light than POE and EVA and is usually subject to
significant photo-oxidative degradation [79] and also increased crystallinity is observed
after UV exposure [11]; however, in this thesis the UV irradiation is comparably low to the
one of reference literature. A reason for this effect could then lay in the common surface
activation of polymers using UV light activation, where adhesive bond strength increases
[80], but there is no scientific evidence that the benefit occurs also in already-bonded
polymers.
Colour change
∆E00>2 for E1S Red and Y1S Beige might suggest colour change due to UV aging.
However:
• if UV was responsible of colour change, then both Y1S and Y1SG Beige would
undergo the same effect, which is not the case;
• calculating ∆E00 between the 4 measurement points (2 on backsheet, 2 on cell) of
E1S Red and Y1S Beige, the values are around 0.1-0.3. Considering that only the
two 2 points over the PV cell underwent exposure to UV light, the latter doesn’t
seem to affect the colour appearance;

9.2.2
Discussion
IV losses and colour change
Overall no big differences in IV performance are observed after DH2000, as in relevant
literature [30], where comparable electrical behaviour was observed between unaged and
DH3000 modules. Any significant ∆Isc,% is then supposedly related to optical differences
in front of the PV cell.
Considering the values of ∆Isc,% of all the samples, the following can be pointed out:
• E0G and E1SG Green most likely show degradation because of the bubbles formed
in front of the solar cell, with vapour condensation that increases light reflection
[30] (more details in Section 10.1.2). The interlayer of E1SG Green appears also less
transparent, with colour change confirmed by the ∆E00 (even if not too significant),
justifying the higher loss compared to the transparent E0G;
• E1S Red and P1SG White present more opaque interlayers after DH2000 and ∆E00
values over 4, supposedly justifying the Isc loss. However, the same doesn’t happen for
the other SX interlayered samples (especially Y1S Beige and Y1SG Beige), suggesting
that the reason of apparent colour difference might be due to other reasons (like
spatial colour uniformity in the original SX interlayer);
• Not much can be said about the CF interlayers, since the relative values are within
±1% and no clear effect is spotted. The corrosion of transversal busbars could be
one of the causes, but more electrical investigation would be needed to assess it;
• TPOs (T0, T1 Yellow, T1 Green, T1G Red) show an increased Isc, following the
same principle of certain TPOs of higher transmittance after DH [30]. This effect is
in line with the observed higher transparency of T1 Yellow (and ∆E00>2).
Page 56 of 111
==PAGE:68==
9. Aging overview
Overall, interlayers and coloured TPOs don’t seem to have significantly affected the
performance of the samples and showed relatively good colour stability. CF interlayers
appear as best performers in this last sense, followed by coloured TPOs and SX
interlayers.
However, due to not perfect colour uniformity of the unaged materials,
optical characterizations of the same samples before and after DH aging would give more
consistent results.
Adhesion
The high loss of adhesion of the POE encapsulants (both POE and POEYP), passing from
very high values to the lowest among the tested samples, could be due to two main factors:
• mechanism of chain scission, to which POE are more subject than the other
encapsulants [30];
• possible presence of adhesion promoter (not specified by the manufacturers),
undergoing heavy degradation after DH. No evidence was found in literature.
The adhesion values are significantly higher than the 160 J/m2 (0.8 N/cm) threshold after
which delamination is expected [81]. However, as outlined by Wu et al. [82], "adhesion
requirements for PV modules to ensure long-term reliability has been studied but not well
defined due to the complexity of testing methods, material differences, and stress variation
etc. Therefore, there is a lack of standard value or minimum value of adhesion strength".
Relatively high degradation is also observed for T1 Green, where an unaged high value
compared to the other TPOs degrades to the same adhesion after DH. Reasoning behind this
could be an incorrect lamination procedure (not enough heating time before compression
of the laminates, more details in Section 10.4.2) rather than adhesion differences due to
the composites involved in the encapsulant colouration.
Backsheet degradation
The degradation of the backsheet in all the samples is a sign that the available encapsulants
are not the weakest component of PV modules. However, it has to be taken into account
that it could be strictly related to the materials and the aging involved in this thesis:
alternative backsheet technologies could show higher durability and the outcome from
different stresses could change.

Visual defects and IV values
Corrosion of the metal components is, as expected [83], supposedly caused by HaC formation
during DH. More severe corrosion is observed in the G-B samples compared to the G-G
ones, supposedly because of the formed bubbles that reduced the HaC concentration in the
affected areas. This is however not expected to affect the IV performance, since H. Park et
al. [84] observed that corrosion effects (with increased Rs) are followed by Isc losses only
after 6000h of DH.
The bubbles formed in the G-G samples (E0G and E1SG Green) after DH1000 and DH2000
are evidently cause of Isc loss, considering the vapour condensation with subsequent higher
reflection. In relevant literature, analogue but different behaviour was observed:
• in [85], EVA delaminates from the busbars after DH aging the same way (Figure 10.8
left and center). Moreover, field studies reported delamination as a common failure
for G-G modules [86] [87]. However, in this thesis this happens mostly along on the
perimeter of the PV cell, rather than on the transversal busbars;
• similar ’ramified’ shape is observed in [88] after UV light exposure (Figure 10.8 right).
However, in this thesis no UV light component was shined on the samples when
inside the DH chamber.
Figure 10.8. Delamination examples of EVA from literature, after DH [85] (left and center) and
after UV light exposure [88] (right).
As a general conclusion, the bubbles could be a direct consequence of imperfect lamination
next to the edges of the G-G samples. The fact that they didn’t develop further from
DH1000 to DH2000 may also suggest that physical bonds are initially broken to then reach
an equilibrium and not develop further.
Delamination after DH of the CF interlayers is expected due to poor adhesion of the PET
foil to standard encapsulants. However, the CF foils appear more white than it was before
DH, eventually due to side reactions or simple degradation of the PET itself.

As previously outlined in Section 8.2, the cracked cell of P1S Terracotta was not expected

G-B than for G-G samples due to higher impermeability of glass compared to backsheet.
However, heavier corrosion of the transversal busbars is atypical and can’t be identified in
relevant literature; further investigation is needed.

12 CONCLUSIONS
12.1
Perspectives
The goal of this research was to investigate the consequences of introducing innovative
coloured/coated materials on the durability of the PV laminates. This was done using four
encapsulants (EVA, two POEs from two manufacturers, TPO transparent and coloured)
with two interlayers (ColorFoil, Solaxess) in different combinations. Accelerated DH and
UV aging was simulated and the effects on performance (IV curves, EL), adhesion (peel
test), optical properties (colour appearance, EQE) and visual aspect were assessed.
After manufacturing the samples, the measured IV values clearly highlight that Solaxess
interlayers cause significant losses in Isc due to optical reasons, with values spanning from
15% to almost 35%. Coloured TPOs reduce less significantly the Isc value, with Green being
the best performer (<5%) followed by Red (15%) and Yellow (20%). Clear conclusions can’t
be drawn for ColorFoil interlayers: the colour perception is low (followed by neglectible
optical losses) and, even when some colour can be identified, the non uniformity makes it
hard to actually assess its impact.
Performance characterization after UV exposure shows interesting results. An overall
minimum loss of 1% is observed for both Isc and Voc, supposedly LID due to Boron-Oxygen
defects initially present in the PV cells and accompanied by reduction of the minority-
carriers lifetime [76]. Significantly higher losses are observed for samples with ColorFoil
TiO2 coated interlayer (E1F TiO2, P1F TiO2) and where TiO2 is likely present as a
pigment [77] (P1FG White, Y1S and Y1SG Beige). Photo-activation of TiO2 is supposed
[78], but no evidence have been found in literature.
Isc and Voc changes in values after DH1000 and DH2000 seem mainly due to side
effects (bubbles formation in E0G and E1SG Green in front of the PV cell, with vapour
condensation) than being direct degradation of the PV cells, as expected from literature
[30]. However, an increase in performance for all TPOs samples (T0, T1 Yellow, T1 Green,
T1G Red) seems to happen, supposedly due to typical increased transmittance of the
encapsulant after DH [30].
Comparing the ∆E00 values after both the aging mechanisms (UV and DH), reported in
Figure 12.1, similarities for the G-B Solaxess samples (E1S Red, P1S Terracotta, Y1S
Beige) could confirm the hypothesis of colour non uniformity of the original Solaxess
foil; colour difference is noted for E1S Red and Y1S Beige from visual inspection, but
slightly different light conditions and colour correction of the used camera could have high
influence.

Green, P1SG White, Y1SG Beige) is interesting and requires further investigation. Finally,
∆E00 values of the TPO samples are probably affected by imperfect lamination and angle
dependence of the colour appearance. Again, conclusions on ColorFoil interlayers can’t be
drawn due to different colour appearance between samples with same composition.
Results from adhesion show a drastic loss for POE samples (both POE and POEYP) after
DH2000, supposedly caused by chain scission (to which POE is particularly weak [30]) and
degradation of an eventual adhesion promoter; due to low number of strips, conclusions
can’t be however drawn for UV aging. Differently, TPOs show higher adhesion after UV
exposure and lower after DH2000, with coloured samples performing similarly (T1 Yellow)
or even better (T1 Green) than the transparent T0; however, possible imperfect lamination
could be heavily affecting the results.
General remark can be done for G-B and G-G modules, due to the implementation of both
structures in this thesis. The second ones are getting more used recently, both for the
advent of bifacial modules and for improved fire safety of BIPV [14]. Results from this
thesis show comparable performance and durability between the two technologies; however,
for the lamination of G-G samples the sealing of the sides can be critical and supposedly
cause easy infiltration (like the bubbles formed in two EVA G-G samples).
Overall, for most of the samples performance and durability don’t appear affected by
the addition of coloured/coated materials; only TiO2 seems to be incompatible due to
supposed photo-activation and relative damages to the PV cell. Colours don’t seem to
affect even the adhesion, if not improving it, and overall the colour degradation doesn’t
appear critical.
12.2
Future work
UV aging requires further analysis: UV irradiation should be increased to obtain comparable
conditions to the real operation of the PV modules, but also a deeper analysis to verify
the minor carrier lifetime reduction due to UV. Moreover, additional optical analyses are

==PAGE:92==
12. Conclusions
recommended on the interlayers to determine with less uncertainty if any degradation
happens, considering also a full monitoring of the colour appearance throughout the whole
aging process and not making aged/unaged comparison right after it.
The apparent incompatibility of TiO2 due to photo-activation requires additional electrical
and optical analyses, to elaborate any passivation technique if confirmed. ColorFoil samples
seem promising, but testing on foil with more colour uniformity is needed to obtain clear
results.
The very significant drop in adhesion of POE also requires additional investigation to confirm
the causes, since the values observed after DH are significantly low and there is still no
clear threshold for delamination risk. Moreover, furthers studies of the coloration influence
on the TPO adhesion would be relevant to assess their applicability compared to other
encapsulants, with particular attention to the lamination process to avoid imperfections.
Improvements to the visual inspection setup can be implemented by ensuring always the
same measured illumination on the samples (IEC 61215 [13] requires minimum 1000 lx)
and orientation of the camera, especially when the materials reproduce angle-dependent
colours. Automatic colour adjustment of the camera is also to be avoided, since it can
produce misleading results.
Lastly, due to their expected growing applicability for bifacial modules and increased fire
safety for BIPV [14], durability tests on G-G modules should be performed more accurately.
In this thesis, materials availability limited the number of G-G samples, but the different
permeability and the edges sealing are fundamental to be studied since different effects
could arise. Indeed, less moisture would permeate but also more byproducts (like HaC)
would accumulate in the module and increase possible damages, as observed in previous
studies [86] [87]. Moreover, the influence of interlayers on these mechanisms are still to be
studied.
