Executive Summary 
 
Advanced distributed communication technologies play a vital role today in the control and 
maintenance of safety critical systems and in the daily operations of activities found in a number 
of different sectors such as manufacturing, transportation, energy production, healthcare and 
banking. In the years to come, reliance on communication technologies is expected to grow at a 
breathtaking pace, as they suggest benefits for operability, maintainability and efficiency of the 
systems up to the full automation of some functions which seemed infeasible some years ago. 
 
The focus of this thesis is the identification of hazards stemming from current and future 
communication technologies implementations. Literature has shown that while a number of 
attempts have been made so far on the subject, none of them has accomplished to reach a high 
level of acceptance between risk analysts as they present a number of limitations. 
 
To enhance the predictive performance of hazard identification in advanced distributed 
communication systems, the master thesis envisions and suggests a novel approach based on 
Hazard and Operability Studies (HAZOP); a hazard identification method that stimulates creative 
thinking using a set of guidewords which are applied to parameters of the components of a system 
in order to derive parameter deviations from normal operation. Focal points of the approach are: 
 
 
identifying appropriate system representations 
 
identifying relevant system parameters and deviation guidewords for hazard identification 
 
The goal is to develop a methodology agile enough to capture both design and development faults 
as well as operational deviations from the design intent, while being adaptable to different system 
representations, respecting the designers’ choice of formalism. 
 
Regarding the system representations, unified modelling language (UML) behavior diagrams are 
heavily exploited since their deployment provides a number of advantages; the most important one 
being managing complexity. Using the diagrams’ particular feature of “behavior” as a key element, 
relevant parameters and deviation guidewords are established, structuring an umbrella framework. 
The framework can be applied to six different UML diagrams, ensuring a logically complete 
exploration of deviations from the system’s design intent. 
 
In order to evaluate the practicality of the suggested methodology, a distributed maintenance 
management system in a nuclear power plant is used as a case scenario. The parameters 
characterizing the scenario, such as the actors, equipment and implemented functionality are all 
based in developed prototypes of the OECD Halden Reactor Project. Results from the 
implementation of the methodology demonstrate the benefits and limitations of the approach 
regarding its usability, applicability and validity.

1. Introduction 
 
Advanced distributed communication technologies play a vital role today in the control and 
maintenance of safety-critical systems. As safety-critical [1], we define systems whose failure or 
malfunction may lead to: 
 
 Loss of life or serious injuries 
 Loss or significant damage to equipment or property 
 Damage to the environment 
 
There are plenty of well-known examples of safety-critical systems which can be found in a wide 
range of application areas such as infrastructure, medicine, transport and nuclear engineering.

In the years to come, reliance on communication technologies is expected to grow at a breathtaking 
pace, as they suggest benefits for operability, maintainability and efficiency of the safety-critical 
systems up to the full automation of some functions which seemed infeasible some years ago. 
However, excessively optimistic reliance on the new technology without recognizing the threats 
against its successful functioning, may result in problems and delays in achieving the full benefits. 
 
Any new technology comes with new risks; and advanced distributed communication systems, 
mostly due to their complexity, are not an exception. Hazardous behavior of such systems can 
cause impairments which may lead to catastrophic consequences, especially when considering 
implementations of communication systems into safety-critical domains. Some typical sources of 
risks are defects in the specification and design, software and hardware failures, unintentional 
human errors, intentional manipulation, malicious acts, lack of situational awareness etc. 
 
1.1 Motivation and scope 
 
This thesis was greatly inspired and encouraged by the OECD NEA and OECD Halden Reactor 
Project [2] whose research work focuses on the dependability of software systems in nuclear 
power plants (NPP), the development and application of novel technological solutions for 
condition monitoring, maintenance and operational support. Recent publications [3], [4] and [5] 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
expressed “design thinking” efforts, identifying areas where computerized tools could support 
collaborative tasks during NPP refueling outages; these are very intense and time-limited periods 
where extensive maintenance efforts are performed involving the whole organization. Through the 
publications, new communication prototypes were suggested, aiming to enhance and improve 
outage team performance such as innovative human machine interfaces (HMI) and advanced 
integrating software technologies. 
 
Examples like the one above reveal a shift paradigm towards automation and computerization of 
communication processes in safety-critical domains such as NPPs and reasonably raise concerns, 
such as the one outlined by one of the former Director Generals of the HSE, Mr. J.D. Rimmington 
[6]: 
 
“It is essential that industry should be able to reap the enormous benefits computerization has to 
offer. But the sophistication and unfamiliarity of the new technology can conceal hazards, 
sometimes at the interface between man and the process, which could inhibit the rate of advance 
unless we learn to recognize them…” 
 
The key words here are “hazards” and “recognize”.  Hazard identification techniques have been 
under continuous development for more than 50 years and have now managed to become an 
integral part of every engineering field. Although the underlying science of those techniques is far 
from being flawless, it has reached a stage where methods perform reasonably well, providing 
significant and measurable improvements in recognizing hazards. However, for new 
communication technologies, hazard identification methods have received less attention and 
require significant refinement in order to achieve the same level of results, as in traditional 
industries. At the current state of knowledge it is even unclear whether we can develop a full range 
of valid causal paths between hazards of different natures and their consequences 
 
The scope of this master project is to investigate current trends on hazard identification techniques 
which are applicable to communication systems, identify their limitations and propose potential 
improvements. More specifically, a novel approach is proposed which is based on the notions of a 
widely used hazard identification technique in the process industries, HAZOP (Hazard and 
Operability Studies) coupled with system description notations based on functional modelling. A 
distributed maintenance management system is used as a case-scenario where the authors’ 
approach is successfully applied and results of the study are presented and analyzed to demonstrate 
its benefits and limitations. 
 
1.2 Research objectives 
 
While some of the research objectives were explicit from the very beginning of the project, a few 
more were naturally developed during the process of completing this thesis. A summary of the 
research objectives is: 
 
 Establish the adequacy of state-of-the-art hazard identification techniques in advanced 
distributed communication technologies. 
 Identify potential limitations and exploit valuable “lessons learned”. 
 Investigate system representations and how they affect the hazard identification process.

 Determine the factors that influence the “level of completeness” in hazard identification. 
 Develop a new hazard identification approach and assess its practical use.

2. Hazard Identification Techniques 
 
2.1 Terminology 
 
Before we continue any further with discussing hazard identification techniques, it is very 
important to introduce some basic terms and concepts, in order to establish some common ground 
with the reader.  
 
Some of the most popular terms associated with hazard identification are those of hazard, risk and 
accident. People tend to mix sometimes those terms and use them interchangeably and for that 
reason explicit definitions need to be given.  
 
According to the Oxford dictionary [7], an accident is “an unfortunate incident that happens 
unexpectedly and unintentionally, typically resulting in damage or injury”. Based on this 
definition, a system’s hazard can be interpreted as “a set of conditions that in combination with 
certain system or environmental conditions can lead to an accident” [8]. ISO 17776:2000 [9] also 
defines hazard as a “potential source of harm”. Finally, risk is “a combination of the consequences 
of a hazardous event and the associated likelihood of occurrence” [10]. Table 2.1 demonstrates 
some examples of accidents, hazards and risks in various systems.

Hazard identification focuses on identifying potential hazards and establishing causal and 
consequence paths, in order to prevent an accident. The outcome of a hazard identification process 
is usually a list of recommendations, responsible to eliminate the identified hazards or mitigate 
their consequences. 
 
 
2.2 Methods for identifying hazards 
 
The list of methods that currently exist to identify hazards can be daunting at first glance, since 
there are dozens of different methodologies, depending on the application area. Fortunately 
though, it is actually possible to filter this list by using a set of objectives that ideally a 
methodology should fulfill in order to be considered as a candidate for hazard identification in 
distributed communication technologies.  
 
Those objectives are: 
 
1. widely used and understandable between different stakeholders 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
2. applicable from the very first stages of the development life cycle, up to decommissioning, 
Figure 2.1. 
3. stimulate creative thinking of the analyst 
4. consider human error as potential source of hazard 
5. focus on identification of operational hazards; hazards found in the internal interactions of 
the communication systems as well as in the external interactions with humans 
 
 
 
Figure 2.1: Systems Development Life Cycle – Waterfall method [11] 
 
2.2.1 Objective #1: widely used and understandable between different stakeholders 
 
While someone might argue that popularity and reputation should not be an objective, as it may 
have nothing to do with the quality and effectiveness of a methodology, one should take into 
account how the world actually works. History has shown that it can take up to decades for a 
hazard identification methodology to reach a certain credibility and acceptance level between 
analysts. This means that people are usually bound to work with methodologies that feel 
comfortable with and well understand and as an immediate result, procedures outside their comfort 
zone might be easily dismissed. 
 
2.2.2 Objective #2: applicable from the design stage of the development life cycle 
 
Probably one of the most important objectives is the fact that the methodology should be applicable 
from the design or even specification stage of the development life cycle. Communication 
processes tend to become more and more computer based. Unfortunately though, there is a price 
to be paid for the advantages that computerization has to offer and this is complexity. Extra 
functionality can be easily added to a system and this will inevitably lead to systems that are overly 
complex. The more complex a design becomes, the more difficult becomes to reason logically 
about it and thus mistakes will naturally occur. As the number of mistakes rises, the greater the 
chance that some of them will remain latent during the system development life cycle and may not 
reveal themselves until some operational conditions trigger them. Since design faults tend to 
dominate over other kinds of failures (such as random hardware and software failures), it is crucial 
to be able to identify potential hazards, as early as possible. 
 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
2.2.3 Objective #3: stimulate creative thinking 
 
The methodology should be able to push and stimulate creative thinking to its limits, as identifying 
hazards is not always a straightforward process and the risk analyst will greatly benefit from any 
tool available to assist. Some hazard identification methodologies are simply based on 
brainstorming which is the absolute minimum tool to use during any hazard identification process. 
Others provide tools such as guidewords and guiding questions that can be applied to maximize 
the number and nature of the identified hazards. 
 
2.2.4 Objective #4: consider human errors 
 
Humans interact with communication systems by either directly using them or indirectly 
supervising/controlling them. In either case, human actions affect the way communication systems 
operate and because of the human nature, those actions will always be prone to error. Therefore, 
it is important for the methodology to be able to consider human errors as they can easily propagate 
into the system. 
 
2.2.5 Objective #5: focus on identification of operational hazards 
 
Last but not least, the methodology should be able to identify both design faults and operational 
hazards. While a static design might look precise and hazard identification on the individual 
elements of the design complete, there will almost always be cases where unidentified hazards will 
come to surface during operational life. Hazards regularly tend to hide at the interactions between 
elements and thus all internal and external interactions should be taken into account. 
 
Literature [12] revealed that among hazard identification techniques, the most widely used are: 
 
1. What - if analysis 
2. Fault Tree Analysis 
3. Failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA) 
4. Preliminary hazard analysis (PHA)  
5. HAZOP  
 
What - if analysis is a systematic study based on asking “what-if” questions, in order to develop 
scenarios and explore potential hazards. While it can be applied at any stage of a project and indeed 
may be forced to identify human errors and operational hazards, creative thinking is stimulated 
with plain brainstorming, making it rather easy to oversee potential hazards. On top of that, it is 
no secret that it was developed as a simpler and faster alternative to HAZOP which in any case 
makes it inferior to HAZOP, as far as hazard identification is concerned. The performance of “what 
- if” analysis is also dependent on the quality and extent of the checklist used and the checklists 
are usually company-specific. 
 
Fault tree analysis is a technique for identifying and analyzing factors that can contribute to 
specific hazardous events. However, it is highly dependent on an initial identification of hazards 
known as top events and for this identification, another method is needed. For this reason it is more 
relevant for later steps, such as focusing on aspects of reliability. 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 
FMEA is based on identifying failure modes of a process, system or procedure and then their causes 
and consequences. However it usually requires sufficient detail for meaningful analysis and that 
makes it suitable only for the final stages of the development process. 
 
PHA uses guide word tables to stimulate creative thinking and is suitable for identifying major 
hazards very early in the designing phase. As its name states though, it is just a “preliminary” 
technique that will not be able to identify detailed hazards. 
 
HAZOP is a hazard identification method that stimulates creative thinking using a set of 
guidewords which are applied to parameters of the components of a system in order to derive 
parameter deviations from normal operation. Causes and consequences of deviations are then 
identified. It is applicable at any stage of the development life cycle and can typically identify a 
wide range of hazards, including operational ones. It is also considered a predominant method for 
analyzing human errors [13], [14], [15].

2.3 Hazard and Operability Studies (HAZOP) 
 
2.3.1 History of HAZOP 
 
HAZOP is a systematic and structured hazard identification technique that was developed in the 
1970s by the British company Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI). The objective was to analyze 
major chemical process systems and identify potential hazards that could arise from the presence 
and interaction of various hazardous chemicals in high temperatures and pressures. The history 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
behind its initial perception is an interesting one and has been described by Kletz [16] who served 
as the company’s advisor from 1968 to 1982. 
 
Since then, HAZOP has managed to develop into the most important hazard identification method 
in the process industries by becoming a standard (IEC 61882: 2001) [17] and even a requirement 
of legislation for every chemical engineering design involving hazardous chemicals or materials. 
 
2.3.2 HAZOP procedure 
 
The first step of the process is to divide the overall system under investigation into subsystems, 
usually referred to as parts. The division is undertaken in such a way to allow the specification of 
a meaningful design intent of each individual part. The choice of size of each part is of great 
importance since too small parts can easily make the study tedious and very slow where larger 
parts carry the risk of missing important details. The representation that is used to describe the 
system is piping and instrumentation diagrams (P&ID) and Figure 2.2 represents an 
oversimplified version of a P&ID of an ozonation reactor which is usually found at a butanol 
production plant. The figure shows how some of the parts might be chosen (e.g. Part No.1, Part 
No. 4). 
 
 
Figure 2.2: P&ID example of an ozonation reactor. Source [12] 
 
When the choice of parts has been made, parts are divided into elements which may or may not 
have a number of meaningful attributes or parameters. IEC 61882: 2001 [17] defines an element 
as: 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
“… a constituent of a part which serves to identify the part’s essential features. Elements can 
include features such as the material involved, the activity being carried out, the equipment 
employed etc. ...” 
 
Figure 2.3, represents an example of different elements that can be found inside a part and some 
of their attributes. 
 
Figure 2.3: Example of elements found inside a part. Source [12] 
Three unique elements are identified here: 
 
1. Material in tank A, 
2. Transfer of material from tank A to tank B, 
3. Material in tank B 
 
The material in tank A has at least three meaningful attributes which are the level of the material 
inside the tank, its composition and temperature. Material in tank B has four meaningful attributes 
which are the level, temperature, pressure and reaction. The transfer operation has attributes such 
as speed and flow. Table 2.3 shows some of the most commonly found attributes or parameters of 
elements in industrial processes. 
 
The hazard identification process is based on a standardized set of guidewords which are utilized 
to identify potential deviations from the design intent. Deviations are considered for both the 
elements and their attributes, if any. Table 2.4 shows the basic guide words and their 
interpretations. Guide words alone are meaningless and in order to generate deviations they must 
be combined with the identified elements or their attributes, as it's shown in Figure 2.4. Getting 
back to our example from Figure 2.3 we can generate potential deviations like: 
 
 MORE ⊕ Temperature (of material in tank A) → Temperature of material A too high 
 LESS ⊕ Pressure (of material in tank B) → Pressure of material B too low 
 ...

2.3.3 HAZOP study team 
 
A HAZOP study team should be multidisciplinary [18], preferably with persons with experience 
in design and operation of the system to be analyzed. For a chemical plant, a basic choice is a 
process engineer, an instrument engineer, and a process operator. The team needs a facilitator, a 
person who has experience in conducting HAZOP studies and preferably also experience of the 
kind of system under study and of safety engineering. The facilitator is generally supported by a 
scribe, who is responsible for recording the HAZOP results in the HAZOP worksheets. Often the 
HAZOP team is much larger than this, involving up to 20 individuals [16]. Very large teams are 
sometimes used, including all persons with responsibility for aspects of the design, and for decision 
making concerning new risk reduction measures.  Companies sometimes also delegate observers, 
who do not play a major role in the analysis process, but are included in the meetings for 
educational purposes.

2.3.4 Control Hazard and Operability Studies (CHAZOP) 
 
Back in the 1980s, the first programmable electronic systems (PES) started to make their 
appearance. Their design intent was to serve as an upgrade to the conventional controllers and 
hard-wired relay logic systems that were usually found in process control and emergency-
shutdown implementations of that generation. In 1987, the Health and Safety executive defined 
PES as [19]: 
 
“...a computer based system which controls, protects or monitors the operation of plant or 
machinery…” 
 
Due to the undeniable advantages that PES had to offer, their usage rapidly increased in a number 
of application sectors such as:  
 
 
Process Industries - fire and gas detection systems, emergency shutdown systems, alarm 
management systems 
 
Medical - electro-medical equipment 
 
Manufacturing - machine tools, industrial robots 
 
Transportation - railway signaling, braking systems 
 
A typical HAZOP study would identify hazards related to individual or sequence control functions, 
commonly found in a P&ID representation, with the use of standard guidewords. With the growing 
implementation of more and more PES, control functions on the P&ID would inevitably be part 
of the PES. However, potential hazards stemming from the subsystems, controller units, 
communications or power failures of a PES would be completely omitted from the identification 
process; PES clearly had a different spectrum of failure modes than conventional controllers [6]. 
Companies started treating PES like conventional controllers and even worse considering them as 
“black-boxes” during HAZOP studies as they did not know how to use HAZOP on them.  
 
A number of different authors and organizations recognized the need to exploit HAZOP to include 
PES on the hazard identification process and the control hazard and operability studies 
(CHAZOP) was born. CHAZOP was basically identical with the typical HAZOP with the only 
difference being the guidewords/attributes utilized. Since PES had a complete different spectrum 
of attributes and consequently failure modes, new appropriate guide word and attribute lists were 
required. 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:25==

3. Literature Review 
 
Identifying potential hazards stemming from the use of PES in control and safety functions was 
the initial purpose of CHAZOP development. Unlike HAZOP that managed to keep its original 
characteristics (base representation, standardized guide word list) intact for more than 40 years of 
hazard identification, CHAZOP never accomplished to maintain or even obtain a standard form. 
Since the 1980s it became the subject of study of a number of safety organizations and authors and 
as a result numerous variations have flourished in literature ever since. While CHAZOP’s original 
focus was on PES, a number of other CHAZOP-like methodologies were developed in parallel, 
focusing on aspects of computer based systems such as software, hardware and human interaction. 
They can be found in literature with a number of different labels such as “computer HAZOP”, 
“software HAZOP”, “computer-based HAZOP”, etc. Considering that all of them are directly 
relevant to communication technologies (being primarily computer-based systems), the objective 
of this chapter is to act as a literature review on CHAZOP and CHAZOP-like variations and list 
the most significant attempts so far.  
 
3.1 History of CHAZOP development and HAZOP for computer based systems 
 
In 1987, the HSE recognizing the advantages of computerization, published two documents [19] 
proposing general guidelines for the use of PES in safety related applications. The proposed 
guidelines, had a form of a “design and assessment framework”, intended to ensure safe operation 
of safety related PESs. Two basic categories of failures were considered; random hardware failures 
and systematic ones. In order to tackle both types of failures, the framework was centered around 
three system elements: 
 
 Configuration - Arrangement of PES and combination of PES and non-PES in safety 
related systems. 
 Reliability of hardware 
 Overall System Quality - Measures taken against systematic failures. 
 
Although it was clear that the HSE intended to support the industry take advantage of the 
“enormous benefits computerization has to offer”, the proposed guidelines were too generic and 
hence difficult to understand and apply in practice.  
 
In 1991, Andow [6] presented the very first structured HAZOP-like procedure for computer 
controlled plants; the control HAZOP or CHAZOP. Following the need for a structured hazard 
identification approach for PES, the author proposed a two-stage methodology; preliminary and 
full CHAZOP. In the preliminary phase, issues such as the proposed system architecture, review 
of all the safety-related functions and expected performance upon system, power and utility failure 
were to be considered. The full CHAZOP phase, involved a full evaluation of the PESs in relation 
to three different aspects;  
 
 The computer and system environment, 
 Input and output (I/O) signals, 
 Complex control schemes. 
 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
For each aspect, a set of guiding questions, considerations, headings and deviations in the form of 
guidewords were provided. An important characteristic of the methodology was that both the 
preliminary and full CHAZOP were intended to be carried out as part of the preliminary and full 
HAZOP analysis respectively and not dealt with in isolation. 
 
A short publication from Earthy [20], in 1992, considered the application of HAZOP in software 
safety assessment. The document served as a set of guidelines, rather than an explicit stand-alone 
methodology. According to the author, the key to success was stressed to be the selection of an 
appropriate hybrid system design model that would be used as a base/input to the software analysis, 
just like P&IDs are used in conventional HAZOP. The proposed base documentation included: 
 
 Process Flowcharts for user input, 
 Schematic network chart for hardware elements, 
 Data flow diagrams, 
 Transaction networks, 
 and finally the very expertise of the study team for addressing communication protocols 
and functional behavior of the system. 
 
The same year and a year after, Chudleigh M. [21], published his own methodology through a 
paper and a follow-up case study, applying HAZOP to computer based systems, both at a software 
and hardware level. Chudleigh’s work focused explicitly on two different areas: 
 
 Finding appropriate representations for the system (software and hardware), 
 Developing appropriate parameter and deviation guidewords. 
 
For electronics and hardware, the author recognized clear parallels with block and circuit diagrams 
and consequently used them as appropriate representations of the hardware components in place. 
A set of new guidewords was developed which included terms such as voltage, current, amplitude, 
power and frequency. Additional guidewords, based on the study team’s intuition were also 
welcomed. As far as the software part of the system was concerned, data flow representations 
using a CASE tool called Software through Pictures [22] were used, as according to the author 
were “readily understandable by all interested parties, even those without any computing 
background”.  New software guide words were also introduced, including data and control values, 
algorithm functions, communication protocols, timing of actions and rate of data. A case study 
[23] published in 1993, demonstrated the application of the methodology in a Medical Diagnostics 
Application and proved its feasibility. 
 
In 1993, Burns & Pitblado [24] published a 3-stage modified HAZOP methodology to address 
hazards related with PESs. The 3 stages of the methodology were: 
 
 Initial / Conventional HAZOP 
 PES HAZOP 
 Human Factors HAZOP 
 
Of particular interest are the PES and Human Factors HAZOP, since the conventional HAZOP 
was used for completion purposes of hazard identification in computer control plants. The PES 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
HAZOP enabled a review of the computer control and programmable logic controllers (PLC), 
based on two basic variables/guidewords; signal and action. The base documentation included 
ladder, logic and vendor diagrams as well as cause and effect charts, although logic diagrams were 
found to be the most suitable, as they were “easily understandable by the wide range of personnel 
involved in the study”. Recognizing that a person could also be a part of a safety-related system, 
whether is a control or protection one, the authors extended HAZOP to review human factors; 
stage 3 of the methodology. The variables/guidewords to explore human factors variations, 
information and action, were quite similar with those related to PES, although they targeted 
explicitly human actions and not computer control ones. Additional base documentation for stage 
3, included control room layout and ergonomics, access times to data, communications, job role 
and task definitions, procedures, hardcopy devices, system interfaces and information of all 
important VDU displays. 
 
A year after, McDermid and Pumfrey [25] presented a new approach of applying HAZOP to assess 
the safety of application software; a “software HAZOP”. Inspired by the flow-based analysis of 
the conventional HAZOP, they considered exploring deviations in the information flows, between 
software components. MASCOT 3 [26] was selected as the base notation to describe the system, 
since MASCOT’s communication paths could easily be interpreted as information flows. Based 
on the work of Bondavalli and Simoncini [27]  the authors developed what they considered a 
complete set of software failure classes which included: 
 
 Service provision: Omission or Commission 
 Service timing: Early or Late 
 Service value: Coarse Incorrect or Subtle Incorrect 
 
The term “service” in this context was analogous to the “information flow”. By establishing the 
data type and path protocol of every information flow (communication path) in the MASCOT 
diagram, the study team could dynamically define guide words by considering the interpretation 
of each of the three failure classes in the context of every possible combination of type and protocol 
of each flow. The final step included applying appropriate guidewords to establish possible 
deviations, as each flow was analyzed. 
 
In 1994, Fenelon & Hebbron [28] proposed general guidelines and recommendations on applying 
HAZOP to software engineering models. The focal point of their work, was looking into 
appropriate system representations which could be used as a base for the software HAZOP. 
Although they did not give a definite answer, we believe that the points made were of considerable 
research value and deserve to be cited here. The authors expressed their concern, that the software 
representation remained a key issue for controlling the effectiveness of HAZOP. More specifically, 
even the slightest omission of either the control, data or temporal aspects of the system behavior, 
could reduce dramatically the effectiveness of the review. Recommendations were made that the 
software should not be dealt with in isolation, but always through its interaction with the external 
environment. Moreover, top-down hierarchically decomposed models were deeply criticized as 
they rely on the analysts’ anticipation of the required division of functionality, which could lead 
to arbitrary decisions, impairing the hazard identification process. Their work concluded that a 
formal definition of the notation should be considered like the Ward and Mellor Essential Models 
[29]. 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
A quite different hazard identification methodology for computer-based systems was published in 
Kletzs’ [30] book “Computer control and Human Error” in 1995. Although it was not directly 
analogous with the conventional HAZOP, the methodology shared important elements with it, 
such as being a systematic approach and being based on guiding words (attributes here), 
considerations and questions. The methodology was the only one in this review to be based in 
incident analysis and to be using an innovative graphical technique, called the Event Time Diagram 
(ETD). According to the approach, system functional requirements were decomposed into tasks 
and each task was analyzed for potential deviations through the use of guiding task considerations 
and attributes, as well as through the use of appropriate questions derived from past incidents. 
Despite being undoubtedly intriguing, the approach can be expected to be very time-consuming 
and heavily dependent on the past incidents taken into account; it is unrealistic to expect that a 
complete and up-to-date incident list will always be available. 
 
A rather interesting approach came into sight in 1997, when Schubach S. [31] published his own 
methodology for developing a HAZOP procedure for PES, by attempting to integrate previous 
valuable contributions from various authors, instead of promoting a new one. Recognizing that the 
well-established documentation in the form of P&IDs was one of the conventionals’ HAZOP 
greatest contributors to efficacy, the author proposed a modified P&ID base documentation, with 
the addition of control, sequence, software and interlock details. The main hazard identification 
procedure, included consideration of input and output (I/O) signals with the use of supplementary 
prompts/questions, as suggested by Andow [6], combined with deviations suggested by Burns & 
Pitblado [24]. Considerations about control types (continuous and sequence), as suggested by Lear 
[32]  and software interactions, found in Nimmos’ [33] work, were also suggested for integration. 
The methodology could be summarized as a set of “aspect and consideration” combinations of: 
 
 Hardware: signal, action 
 Control: timing, response, limits, tuning, interlocks 
 Sequence/Software: initiation, interaction, communication 
 Operator: information, access, response. 
 
Unlike Andow and other authors, Schubach suggested that the methodology should not be 
regarded as an integral part of HAZOP, as the required information to implement it would become 
available much later than the “design” phase that conventional HAZOP usually takes place. 
 
The British Ministry of Defense published in 2000 a document with general application guidelines 
for applying HAZOP in systems containing PES [34]. The document suggested the partition of the 
components of the system under investigation into individual elements and then the identification 
of the attributes (parameters) of each element. Appropriate guide word lists were to be combined 
with the identified attributes in order to generate deviations from the design intent. As an example, 
the document defined PES as a component with the “processing unit” being one of its elements 
and “throughput” one of its characteristics. The document was found to be quite ambiguous, as 
exploration of design flaws was often mixed with deviations from the design intent. Moreover, 
most of the proposed diagrammatic representations were found to be out-of-date and hence not 
applicable in practice. 
 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
A modified HAZOP, focusing on the identification of operator errors in safety-critical interactive 
systems, was proposed by Hussey A. [35] in 2000. More specifically, the author recognized that 
user-interfaces could be a potential source of operator errors and consequently his methodology 
focused around them. In order to describe user-interfaces, task-analysis models were exploited and 
in particular Sum [36] was used to produce formal models, in terms of objects, actions, tasks and 
goals. Tasks were later to be examined using HAZOP-style guidewords and scenarios were 
developed to explore the behavior of the system under potential deviations. 
The basic steps of the methodology could be outlined as: 
 
 Use of operational scenarios → identify user goals 
 For each user goal → determine possible action sequences (tasks) 
 Model tasks using Sum 
 Examine each task, using HAZOP-style guidewords → explore deviations through 
scenarios 
 
A UML - based approach, focusing on software for safety critical systems, was proposed in 2004 
by Hansen K. [37]. Recognizing the power of UML representations for describing software 
architectures, the author extended the conventional HAZOP methodology by considering UML 
model elements as HAZOP entities and their UML attributes as HAZOP attributes, where entity-
specific guidewords were to be applied, in order to reveal deviations. Considerations of applying 
the HAZOP-like methodology in different abstraction levels were also made, such as  the four 
viewpoints of the software architectural level (module, C&C, allocation and architectural 
requirements viewpoint) down to more specific UML diagrams such as the class, package, object, 
sequence, deployment and use case diagram. 
 
Perhaps the most comprehensive approach, targeting hazards related to industrial control systems, 
was given by Love J. in his Process and Automation Handbook [38] published in 2007. One of the 
most distinctive features of his approach, was the segregation of the methodology between a 
computer hazard and operability study (CHAZOP) addressing the control systems’ hardware 
design, I/O organization and system software and a complete separate control and operability study 
(COOP) focusing on the application software and human factors. Both studies included a 
preliminary and full stage while CHAZOP was suggested to precede the implementation of COOP, 
as the design of application software usually lags behind the design of the rest of the system. The 
preliminary phase of CHAZOP, included establishing strategic decisions about the systems’ layout 
and failure handling, with the results being incorporated in the USR document for the control 
system. The full CHAZOP phase focused on the safety-related I/O signals and their respective 
channels by applying a relevant set of HAZOP-like guidewords to reveal possible deviations. 
Results were to be incorporated in the detailed functional specification (DFS) document. As far as 
the COOP study was concerned, the preliminary phase included an analogous review of the 
application software during the software design stage. The full COOP phase was based upon the 
full software designs, preceding the software development stage, where considerations about the 
functionality of every software control scheme and procedure were made. Appropriate guidewords 
were developed to ferret deviations while particular attention was given to the operator's’ role 
through a set of guiding questions. Base documentation for CHAZOP included copies of the user 
requirements specifications (URS) document and DFS, architecture and configuration of the 
control system, physical layouts and infrastructures, channel loop diagrams and system 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
malfunctions. COOP was based on configurable and procedural software designs as well as copies 
of the URS and DFS.

3.2 Review of CHAZOP and computer based HAZOP variations 
 
Thirteen different variations are listed in Table 3.1 and those are only what this study considered 
to be the most significant ones in literature; the actual list could be much longer, since in practice 
private companies develop and probably use their own variants. 
 
Although the vast majority of these methods are able to introduce some novel aspect to the hazard 
identification area, not a single one can be considered practical to address hazards found in 
distributed communication systems. The identified issues are unfortunately several and quite 
significant. The most important ones are illustrated below; 
 
 Most of the methods focus on specific aspects of the system under investigation and not 
the system as a whole. Communication technologies, being by definition interactive 
computer-based systems, incorporate both software and hardware implementations. In 
addition, their operational modes are heavily dependent on human actions. Interactions on 
the majority of the listed methods are not treated sufficiently and even worse are 
completely disregarded. 
 
 The base documentation (design representation) used during the hazard identification 
process, is probably one of the most important factors to take into consideration for 
performing a complete and meaningful analysis. Numerous base documentations have 
been suggested so far such as control schemes, logic diagrams, block and circuit diagrams, 
MASCOT designs, I/O signals, models based on formal languages, functional block 
diagrams and even hybrid P&IDs augmented with PES details. None of them though is 
appropriate or sufficient to fully describe a communication system; failure to create a 
concrete base documentation design will inevitably lead to poor analysis. 
 
 Since all of the methods are heavily based on the of use guide word and parameter lists to 
identify potential hazards, they may also suffer from four methodological and technical 
insufficiencies [39], Figure 3.1: 
 
1. Incompleteness; proposed guide word/parameter combinations are not able to identify all 
potential hazards. Although it is impossible to guarantee completeness, there are actually 
ways to deliver a sense of “logical completeness”. Some sets of terms were found to be 
logically incomplete, at least with respect to the thesis’ objective. 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
 
2. Nonsensicality - some guidewords and parameters are not straightforward and in some 
applications they cannot be logically associated with any deviation 
 
3. Redundancy - a number of guidewords can lead to the same deviation. Although this is 
quite natural and not always an issue, many of the terms used in some approaches are not 
orthogonal; some disturbance types are covered by several list items, making the hazard 
identification process tedious and consequently ineffective. Moreover, excessive 
redundancy may even lead to an incomplete analysis, due to confusion of how to address 
a particular hazard. 
 
4. Ambiguity - guide word/parameter combinations may lead to a number of different 
deviations. Although again this is something to be expected, the level of ambiguity should 
be kept to manageable levels.

4. First attempt: Multilevel - Multidimensional (MLMD) HAZOP analysis 
framework 
 
This small chapter is dedicated to demonstrate a first published attempt [40] of the author to 
develop a novel HAZOP-based hazard identification technique, addressing issues stemming from 
new communication technologies. While it could have been part of the literature review, the thesis 
was heavily influenced by the lessons learned and the results of that particular work and hence 
earned a special place in this master project. 
 
The methodology that was proposed (given the name of multilevel-multidimensional HAZOP 
analysis at the time), aimed to deal particularly with the issues of incompleteness and 
nonsensicality and create a logical framework where, while redundancy and ambiguity naturally 
existed, they could be easily handled and used to one’s advantage. 
 
The multilevel - multidimensional methodology divided the given system into levels and then used 
a multidimensional guiding philosophy for identifying potential hazards. The two levels of division 
were: 
 
 Hardware Level, including all the transmitters, receivers, channels and I/O signals. 
 Human Interaction Level between software, hardware and operators 
 
For each level of the design, an appropriate deviation checklist was developed that could be utilized 
during the analysis to identify potential hazards.  
 
Starting with the hardware level, a computer network diagram was used as base documentation. 
In order to stimulate creative thinking towards hazardous deviations, a two-dimensional guiding 
framework was developed, Table 4.1.  The first dimension of the framework separated the 
hardware components, in “continuous” and “on demand” working ones. A third row was also used 
to represent both types of components. The second dimension classified deviations according to 
three different criteria: “working state” of the component, “time” and “component interaction” 
with the rest of the system. After the classification, the table was populated with potential guiding 
deviations that could be used during the analysis. 
 
For the human interaction level, an IDEF0 functional block diagram [41] was used as base 
documentation. Creative thinking was also stimulated here through the use of a multidimensional 
guiding framework, Table 4.2, although the dimensions were selected to match the human 
interaction with the software, hardware and other humans. The first dimension separated the 
human interaction in regard to the “data” that was being transferred during the interaction, the 
“time” of the interaction and finally the “place” that the interaction occurred. The second 
dimension considered all the different possible interactions that could take place in a distributed 
communication system; “input to machine”, “output from machine” and “interaction with other 
human”. Once again, the table was populated with potential guiding deviations that could be used 
during the analysis.

4.1 Results - Lessons learned 
 
In order to test the practical use of the methodology, a distributed maintenance management system 
(DMMS) toy scenario was used. The DMMS was analyzed first at the communications and 
hardware level. This took six hours, identified twenty failure possibilities, no design errors, and 
eleven proposals for safety measures. The human interaction analysis was conducted separately, 
with the actual analysis spreading over several days. It took a total of twenty-two man-hours, 
identified three design errors, and forty-four proposals for safety measures. One of the most 
surprising results from the analysis was the early detection of a major design error in the initial 
concept, within the first hour of work. 
 
Early results, such as the ones demonstrated above, confirmed the initial expectations of a 
“logically complete” analysis. The computer network diagram in combination with the IDEF0 
functional block diagram were also found to be sufficient design representations to describe a 
distributed communication system, like a DDMS. 
 
Even though this first attempt demonstrated the feasibility of applying a HAZOP-based approach 
to distributed communication systems, two things in particular revealed that there was still a lot of 
room for improvement: 
 
1. The analyses for the selected system were carried out by a study team of three persons; one 
with risk analysis experience, one with IT experience and one with maintenance 
experience. This would be a bare minimum for a practical project but at the time being was 
more than enough. Although all three were very familiar with the methodology (being co-
authors in that study), something quite surprising happened during the hazard identification 
process. In a number of occasions, opinions diverged regarding the interpretation of 
guidewords and the way they were supposed to be used on the design representations.  
 
2. The computer network and IDEF0 diagrams proved to be feasible design representations 
to describe a communication system. While a computer network diagram is a pretty 
standard and universally accepted tool to represent hardware implementations, this is not 
also the case with the IDEF0. Reality is that designers might not even be acquainted with 
it. This alone causes a pile of issues, as in order for the methodology to work in practice, 
designs need to follow the IDEF0 notations. A workaround to this problem could be to ask 
designers translate their designs into IDEF0 models and then apply the hazard 
identification methodology. However, the process of translating designs, especially to ones 
that designers may have no prior experience with, can be extremely time consuming and 
even worse, prone to errors. 
 
Both of the identified issues demonstrated that although the multilevel-multidimensional 
methodology was feasible, it was rather impractical. Further work was required on establishing 
appropriate system representations and straightforward sets of guide words with unambiguous 
interpretations.

5. Methodology 
 
The development of the MLMD HAZOP framework proved to be an invaluable pilot study, since 
lessons learned from that first attempt clearly highlighted areas that required significant 
improvements. More specifically, the two areas of focus are: 
 
1. Appropriate design representations 
2. Appropriate guidewords / attributes lists and interpretations 
 
5.1 Design representations 
 
As stated before, the design representation that is used as input during the hazard identification 
process plays a significant role to the outcome of the analysis. HAZOP and HAZOP-based 
approaches (such as CHAZOP variations and MLDM) in particular, are heavily based on those 
designs, as creative thinking is stimulated through the use of guidewords that are directly combined 
with system elements and their attributes (parameters). If some of those elements are omitted from 
the design representation, potential deviations and consequently hazards deriving from them will 
remain unidentified.  
 
A solution to that would be to use representations covering all aspects of the system under 
investigation. However, there is no “one-design solution” to represent a highly complex system 
such as a distributed communication one. Using different representations for the software, 
hardware and human interactions would not be beneficial either, mainly for two reasons: 
 
1. There are dozens of different design techniques for representing software and hardware 
implementations. Even if the suggested methodology was based on some of them, 
designers would still have to translate their designs, into a suggested representation. The 
process though of the translation may easily introduce misinterpretations, masking 
potential hazards. 
 
2. The second reason is that software and hardware in isolation are not hazardous. Hence 
studying them in isolation would only result in identifying some potential random failures; 
e.g. failures that can arise naturally from degradation mechanisms. What is particularly 
important though, is the exploration of systematic failures stemming from errors made 
during specification, design, development and especially end-user configuration / 
operation of the system. Therefore, interactions between software, hardware and human 
actions are significantly important.  
 
Taking into consideration all the above, the answer to the design representation dilemma seems to 
be hiding in functional modelling. 
 
5.1.1 Functional Modelling Concepts 
 
As its name states, a functional or function model refers to a systematic and structured way to 
represent a given system or subject area through the use of functions [41]. The term “function” is 
a quite abstract one and may refer to activities, actions, behaviors, processes, operations etc. 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
Functional modelling concepts originate back in the 1950s and 1960s. In complex engineering 
systems the properties of a system as a whole, may greatly vary from the sum of the parts’ of the 
properties [42]. The need to identify and manipulate those properties led to the development of the 
first functional modelling concepts. British engineer William Gosling was one of the first to deal 
with functional modelling notions and in his book “The design of engineering systems” [43] he 
stated: 
 
“A functional model must thus achieve two aims in order to be of use. It must furnish a throughput 
description mechanics capable of completely defining the first and last throughput states, and 
perhaps some of the intervening states. It must also offer some means by which any input, correctly 
described in terms of this mechanics, can be used to generate an output which is an equally correct 
description of the output which the actual system would have given for the input concerned.” 
 
Functional models feature a number of advantages over other design representations with some of 
the most significant ones being: 
 
 Efficiently handling complexity, especially in highly dynamic systems such as computer-
based ones which are deeply characterized by numerous functional relations, while shifts 
in perspective are also a very common phenomenon [44]. 
 Ability for hierarchical decomposition of a given system or subject area into its constituent 
parts, in such a way to allow reconstruction of the original system/area from those parts. 
 Applicable from the very first stages of the development life cycle. 
 
A number of industries were motivated by the advantages that functional modelling had to offer 
and as a result numerous diagrammatic techniques and modelling notations rapidly 
evolved.  Among the functional modelling techniques, UML behavior diagrams were found to be 
the most straightforward and easy to use. Moreover, UML notation has a growing popularity in 
various industrial domains, with application areas including industrial software production and 
systems engineering [45]. 
 
5.1.2 Unified Modelling Language (UML) 
 
The unified modelling language (UML) is a general-purpose, developmental graphical notation 
tool, widely used in the system and software engineering domains. It was created in 1997 by the 
Object Management Group (OMG) and its development has been managed by this organization 
ever since. In 2005, UML managed to become an approved ISO standard [46] and its current 
version (released in June 2015) is UML 2.5 [47]. 
 
UML 2.5 consists of thirteen diagrams. A UML diagram is a graphical representation of a model 
of a system under design, implementation or even existence. According to its specification, the 
diagrams are divided into two major categories; 
 
 structure (or static) diagrams; they emphasize on the static objects of a system and are 
commonly used to describe software or hardware architectures 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
 behavior (or dynamic) diagrams; they illustrate the dynamic behavior of a system through 
interactions between the different system components and potential changes in their 
internal states.

5.2 Guidewords, parameters and interpretations for UML behavior diagrams 
 
Finding appropriate system representations was the first step only in developing a HAZOP-based 
hazard identification methodology. The true challenge though lies in establishing explicit 
guidelines for identifying system elements and their attributes, as well as developing appropriate 
guide word lists with straightforward interpretations. 
 
As it was already mentioned, UML behavior diagrams are designed to express the dynamic 
behavior of the different objects inside a system. Every diagram uses different notations and is 
meant to describe a system from a different perspective, so it’s only logical to assume that we 
would need a diagram-specific methodology for every different behavior diagram. This is not 
entirely true though. All the diagrams share in common the feature of behavior. Using behavior as 
a focal point, it is actually possible to create a mapping tool, which all the diagrams will eventually 
“adopt”, in order to successfully identify deviations from the design intent. Keeping an open-mind 
attitude, it is possible to claim that the very basic element of a behavior diagram is behavior itself. 
Depending on the diagram, this behavior can be interpreted as a state, signal, message, action etc.; 
it is still though a behavior of the system. 
 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
Now that we have a better idea of what a behavior might look like and considering it as the very 
basic element of every UML behavior diagram, it is possible to define all the possible attributes 
that a behavior might have. The first attribute is the occurrence or in other words the initiation of 
a behavior. While the occurrence of a behavior might be arbitrary, in most cases there is a condition 
or in other words an event that needs to be satisfied / happen in order for a behavior to occur. This 
very condition is the second attribute. A behavior does not simply exist in a system and as all 
things in life needs to be born or initiated by something (object) or someone (actor). Moreover, a 
behavior may have a target in order to fulfill its purpose which again can be something (object) or 
someone (actor). As an example, imagine a person (initiator) that sends a text message (behavior) 
to another person (target). We formally call the initiator and the target of a behavior as predecessor 
and successor and together compose the third attribute. Depending on the case, a behavior might 
have a meaningful duration. As an example, imagine a person waiting for a bus. “Waiting” in this 
case is regarded as a behavior. If the person waits long enough, he will catch the bus. Duration is 
the fourth attribute. In addition, a behavior might have a meaningful content. In the first example, 
where behavior is interpreted as the action of “sending a text message”, the alphanumeric 
characters used, compose the actual content of the behavior. Content is the last and fifth attribute. 
 
Conventional HAZOP is based on identifying all the system elements and their attributes and 
applying meaningful guidewords to those attributes in order to explore potential deviations from 
the design intent. Considering that the very basic element of a behavior diagram is behavior itself, 
it is possible to apply guide words to all the previous identified attributes and explore deviations. 
This gives a sense of “logical completeness” as all the different aspects (behavior attributes) of the 
system undergo a full failure classification.

Before going any further with exploiting the UML behavior diagrams, a few things have to be 
noted. First of all, UML is not a formal specification language and that means it is quite flexible 
when used in practice. There are many different variations of every diagram, particularly in the 
way that notations are used. This master thesis chose to exploit the most popular of those versions. 
However, it should be fairly straightforward to apply the methodology to others. Moreover, every 
UML behavior diagram is designed in such a way to naturally express some specific attributes of 
behavior. The methodology chose to exploit those attributes that were the most meaningful and 
reasonably expressed through each diagram. However, if during the analysis more attributes are 
found meaningful to explore for deviations, it is strongly advised to do so.

5.2.1 UML State Machine Diagrams 
 
The UML state machine is a deterministic automata diagram used to describe the discrete behavior 
of a system through the use of states and transitions, based on the Harel state charts [48]. As an 
example, Figure 5.1 represents a UML state machine diagram of an electronically operated door.  
 
 
Figure 5.1: UML state diagram of electronically-operated door 
 
 
During its “lifetime”, the door can be in one of the following states; opened, closed or locked. 
Every state is denoted in the diagram by a round-cornered rectangle, with the name of the state 
written in bold inside it. Every state can have up to three optional internal actions; 
 
 
entry action; this is an action that is immediately performed upon entry in the state 
 
do action; this is an ongoing action that is performed during the state 
 
exit action; this is an action that is immediately performed upon exit from the state 
 
In our example when the door opens, a green bulb lights; when it closes, an orange bulb lights; 
and when it locks, a red bulb lights, all denoted in the diagram as entry actions. Moreover, while 
in the locked state, a security camera will start recording (denoted as do action in the locked state). 
Finally, each bulb will switch off when leaving the corresponding state, denoted in the diagram as 
exit actions. 
 
The door “travels” through the different states through the use of transitions, denoted in the 
diagram by arrows connecting the different states. The basic notation of every transition is defined 
in the UML 2.5 specification as: [optional guard] event / optional effect 
 
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
When an event is triggered (e.g. close button pressed), an optional guard is evaluated (e.g. is the 
doorway empty) and if the guard is satisfied, the transition is triggered along with an optional 
action effect (e.g. sound close alarm). 
 
Now that we have a clear understanding of the basics of the UML state machine diagram, it’s time 
to explicitly specify what we will regard as “behavior” of the diagram. The behavior of a state 
machine diagram is expressed through: 
 
 
Transitions, 
 
Actions; entry, do, exit, effect 
 
When considering transitions as behaviors, the meaningful attributes (parameters) to investigate 
for potential deviations are: 
 
1. The occurrence of the transition 
2. The conditions of the transition. As a condition in this context we consider both the events 
and the (optional) guards. While both are considered conditions, the exploration of 
deviations is slightly different. As an example in the case of guards the “earlier than 
intended evaluation of guard” is meaningful, while in the case of events the guide word 
“early” is meaningless. 
3. The duration of the transition. 
 
As far as actions are concerned, the only meaningful attribute (parameter) to investigate for 
potential deviations is their occurrence. Table 5.3 summarizes the guide word list for UML state 
machine diagrams and most importantly their interpretations. 
 
Someone might argue why events and states are not considered behaviors themselves and that is 
actually a fair question to make. Interpretation is the key-word here and as long as there is a 
meaningful and complete interpretation of the terms used, ambiguities like the one above can be 
fairly easily dissolved. To answer the question, events and states are regarded here simply as 
attributes of the system behavior. More specifically they are both attributes of the system behavior 
transition. Events are conditions of the transitions while states are their predecessors/successors.

5.2.2 UML Sequence Diagrams 
 
UML sequence diagrams represent how actors or objects of a given system interact with each 
other. Their focus is on the messages that are exchanged during those interactions, in respect to 
sequence; interactions start at the top of the diagram and end at the bottom. As an example, Figure 
5.2 is an oversimplified sequence diagram, representing how a computer and mail-server may 
interact with each other through the use of messages. 
 
 
Figure 5.2: UML sequence diagram of computer and mail-server interaction 
 
The basic notation of every message is: [optional guard] message (optional parameters) 
In this particular scenario, a computer sends a message to a mail-server in the form of an email, 
with a number of content parameters such as a subject, body and a recipient. In the case of a new 
mail, the mail-server will notify the computer through the use of another message, denoted as 
“newEmail” in the diagram, containing some content. In order to delete an old email the computer 
interacts with the mail-server through a third message, denoted here as “deleteOldMail”. 
 
In the case of sequence diagrams, behavior is expressed through the use of messages. When 
considering a message as a behavior, the meaningful attributes to investigate for potential 
deviations are: 
 
1. The occurrence of the message. 
2. The predecessor/successor of the message. In this context we mean the sender and receiver 
of the message. 
3. The condition that needs to be satisfied to trigger a message. In this context all guards are 
considered as conditions. 
4. The content of the message. In this context we mean the parameters and data that a message 
includes. 
5. The duration of the message. UML 2.5 states that a message may be instantaneous 
(negligible duration) or non-instantaneous.

5.2.3 UML Activity Diagrams 
 
UML activity diagrams represent how activities/actions are undertaken inside a system. 
Essentially, they are like classic flowcharts, however they are also equipped with means for 
expressing aspects of decision and concurrency. Some of the basic notation includes: 
 
 round-cornered rectangles for representing activities / actions 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
 diamond shaped symbols for representing decision making 
 bars for representing concurrency (split and join symbols) 
 
 
Figure 5.3 is an example of a UML activity diagram, representing the process of a shopping order. 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5.3: UML activity diagram of processing a shopping order 
 
The customer in this example requests an order and if the order is accepted, a representative at the 
company’s headquarters will fill an order form. If for some reason the order is rejected from the 
system it will automatically close; decision making here is implemented through the use of a 
diamond symbol. If the order is accepted and the order form filled, two activities will be undertaken 
concurrently (split symbol); an invoice will be sent by a representative while the order will be 
shipped from the company’s warehouse. The order will not close in the system until both activities 
of “send invoice” and “ship order” are achieved (join symbol). 
 
In the case of activity diagrams, behavior is expressed through the use of activities. When 
considering an activity as a behavior, the meaningful attributes to investigate for potential 
deviations are: 
 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
 The occurrence of the activity 
 The condition, if any, that needs to be satisfied to trigger an activity. As a condition here 
we refer to all concurrency (split and join bars) and decision making (diamond) symbols. 
There are also cases where time events (not presented in this example) might also be used. 
If any time events are used, they should also be regarded as conditions.

5.2.4 UML Timing Diagrams 
 
UML timing diagrams are used to represent how objects of a system interact with each other

consider both axis reversed; time is passing on the x-axis, from left to right and objects are arranged 
on the y-axis vertically. Figure 5.4 is an example of a UML timing diagram representing an HTTP 
(web browser) request. 
 
 
Figure 5.4: UML timing diagram of a web browser request. Source: uml-diagrams.org 
The interaction objects here are the web user, web browser, DNS receiver, servlet, data access and 
JSP. Every object has a number of states. For example, the web user can be either in the idle, 
waiting or viewing state. As time passes (from left to right), objects travel through different states. 
 
Behavior here is expressed through the states of the different objects. While it is not clear how a 
state invokes another state in this type of diagram (we can only see states in respect to time), it is 
still possible to make a hazard identification analysis in respect to time. Considering states as 
behaviors here, meaningful attributes to explore for deviations are their occurrence and their 
duration.

5.3 Related work 
 
Most of the related work (CHAZOP variations) was included in the literature review chapter. 
However, there is a specific part of literature that focused explicitly on the use of UML 
representations and therefore it was decided to be presented in this small section. Hansen [37], 
Gorski [49] and Jarzebowicz [50] attempted to exploit structural UML diagrams. However, their 
work focused on identifying development faults and potential defects on the UML representations, 
excluding from the hazard identification process hazards of operational nature. Allenby [51], 
Johannessen [52], Iwu [53] and Arlow [54] on the other hand, utilized behavioral UML diagrams. 
However, the guide word lists that they developed were quite incomplete and a number of UML 
elements were not sufficiently explored.  
 
The work that was found most related to this thesis is that of Guiochet [55], [56], [57], [58], [59], 
[60]. The author focused on exploring deviations during operational life and the actual guide word 
lists that he developed, were very similar to the ones presented in this thesis. The difference 
between that work and this thesis was that Guiochet’s approach was heavily based on the UML 
metamodel, in order to identify the basic elements of the UML diagrams. This thesis on the other 
hand, managed to develop an “umbrella-framework” based on behaviors and their relevant 
attributes. On top of that, Guiochet only exploited use case, sequence and state machine diagrams, 
while this thesis managed to also exploit activity, communication, interaction overview and timing 
ones.

6. Case Scenario 
 
In order to demonstrate the applicability of the methodology, a distributed maintenance 
management system (DMMS) inside a nuclear power plant will be used as a case scenario. The 
parameters characterizing the scenario, such as the actors, equipment and implemented 
functionality are all based in developed prototypes of the OECD NEA and OECD Halden Reactor 
Project [3], [4], and [5]. 
 
The purpose of the DMMS is to enhance performance during collaborative tasks such as 
monitoring the plant’s status, identifying potential issues and planning maintenance tasks. The 
system is intended to interface well with the operations center, work planning center, main control 
center and field workers: 
 
 operations center (OC); The OC is responsible for monitoring the overall plant health 
status and ensure that all components are operating as specified. Monitoring is established 
through the use of interactive display status boards, which are utilized for displaying real-
time data collected from a number of different sensors, distributed around the plant’s 
facilities. If an issue is identified, the OC proposes an electronic work order which is 
automatically forwarded to the work planning center for further assessment and planning. 
 
 work planning center (WPC); The WPC is responsible for assessing and planning all the 
maintenance activities inside the nuclear plant. It actually works as a buffer between the 
OC, main control center and field workers. When a new work order is proposed from the 
OC, the WPC is authorized to assess the situation and directly deal with all the necessary 
planning procedures. Among other things, planning requires checking the availability of 
replaceable parts, consumables, materials as well as selecting and notifying sufficiently 
qualified field workers to execute the job in place. Moreover, the WPC collaborates closely 
with the main control center to ensure that all appropriate safeguard measures are included 
in the process. 
 
 main control center (MCC); The MCC is responsible for planning all the safeguards prior 
to any work order execution. Based on the location of the work, the MCC evaluates the 
latest radiation levels, the duration of exposure during the maintenance work and the 
expected radiation dose that will be received. The result is a number of safety measures, 
such as suggesting which field workers have the lowest accumulated radiation dose and are 
appropriate for the work, safety equipment required, preparatory steps to be taken including 
depressurization, draining, decontamination, purging, ventilating, isolation, cautions and 
warnings for the task steps, etc. Moreover, MCC is also responsible for issuing permits to 
work (PTW) and Lock out - Tag out (LOTO) clearances to the field workers. 
 
 field workers; The field workers are the “spearhead” of the DMMS implementation, as in 
the end of the day they are the ones responsible for executing the maintenance work orders. 
Field workers are spread around the plant area and they communicate with the WPC and 
MCC through the use of mobile handheld devices (e.g. iPads) that offer customized 
software implementations for receiving work orders and requesting permits to work. 
 
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
As far as the DMMS implementation is concerned, some technical specifications include: 
 
 Distributed network based on a main server for data processing and transfer with a 24/7 
uptime. Main server redundancy with a backup server and uninterruptable power supply 
(UPS). 
 Desktop workstations in the OC, WCC and MCC. 
 Handheld mobile devices with Wi-Fi connectivity capabilities for field workers. 
 Connectivity through the use of Ethernet cables, routers and 2.4 GHz wireless access points 
(WAP) covering all plant area.

7. Implementation and Results 
 
The purpose of this chapter is to demonstrate how the methodology can be applied to a real-life 
distributed communication system, such as the DMMS and act as a form of validation on the 
applicability and practicality of the approach. 
 
The previous chapter illustrated the basic specifications of the DMMS in a “natural language”; 
that means that there are no design representations at this stage, since the system is still at a very 
early designing phase. This is actually a quite desirable feature, as the merits of identifying hazards 
as soon as possible during the designing phase, have been already mentioned. The first step now 
is to choose an appropriate design representation for the DMMS that will be used as input during 
the hazard identification process. In comparison to other hazard identification techniques that 
mandate the use of a specific design representation, the methodology developed in this thesis was 
based around the “umbrella framework”, providing the flexibility of representing a given system 
through six different UML behavior diagrams. 
 
The choice of representation at this point is of paramount importance, as there are a few criteria 
that need to be satisfied in order to maximize the efficiency of the hazard identification process. It 
is important to understand that each UML behavior diagram is deeply characterized by its own 
particular features and hence there are diagrams that might be more appropriate than others to do 
a certain job. As a rule of thumb, the representation should be able to cover all of the DMMS’ 
functionality, since anything missing from the representation will naturally be excluded from the 
hazard identification process. In addition, it is essential that the representation can fit in an A4 or 
at least A3 designing area. Otherwise, the design could get excessively complicated by mixing up 
different levels of detail; as an example, mixing high-level specifications with low-level 
implementation constraints can be particularly subject to errors, compromising the whole purpose 
of the process. The “level of detail” is the solution adopted here for this problem. 
 
According to [34], if a system introduces many layers of complexity, it is essential to hierarchically 
decompose it into different levels of detail and identify hazards in each of those levels. It is easy 
to argue here that a multilevel analysis might not be necessary and that identifying hazards in the 
lowest levels of detail could be sufficient. However, this argument could not be further from the 
truth. There are two fundamental reasons why hazard identification should not be employed in the 
lowest levels of detail unless higher levels have been previously dealt with. 
 
1. Hazard identification can be an extremely time-consuming process, especially when 
dealing with low-level details. Since time in real-life is one of the most significant 
constraints, hazard identification efforts should be focalized in areas that are most likely to 
lead to hazards. Conducting hazard identification at a higher level of detail will indicate 
whether a deeper level of analysis is actually required. 
 
2. The second reason is probably even more important. Higher levels of detail may be 
accumulating hazards that will not propagate to the lower levels. Imagine a system that 
consists of four different components. We can represent the system with two types of 
diagrams. A higher level diagram illustrating the four components and the way they are 
connected to each other and four lower level diagrams, representing details of every 
==PAGE:58==
58 
 
individual component. Utilizing the four low-level diagrams to identify hazards in each 
individual component would most certainly result in an incomplete analysis. Hazards may 
be hiding into the interactions of those components and therefore conducting a hazard 
identification at the higher level diagram would be the only way to reveal them. 
 
On this basis, it was decided that a UML activity diagram would be a sufficient representation to 
describe the DMMS functionality, at least at an initial high level. Figure 7.1 represents the activity 
diagram of the system, based on the specifications that were given. The DMMS is represented here 
through eleven different activities, undertaken by four different actors. More specifically, the 
operations center is responsible for monitoring the plant’s sensors and identifying any new need 
for work orders. If a new need is identified, the OC will propose a work order. The work planning 
center operators will receive the work order proposal and they will prepare all the necessary 
planning procedures, while the main control center will plan all the accompanying safeguards. 
When both activities of planning are accomplished, the work planning center will issue the work 
order; a join symbol (condition) is used here to illustrate that that the work order will not be issued, 
unless both of those activities are actually achieved. A field worker will receive the work order to 
his mobile handheld device and he will request a permit to work from the main control center. The 
main control center will receive the request and if everything is according to plan, a permit to work 
will be issued. The activity diagram ends with the field worker receiving the permit to work. 
 
Now that the dynamics of the activity diagram have been explained, it is time for the actual hazard 
identification process to begin. Since we are dealing with an activity diagram, Table 5.5 will be 
used as a guide for identifying deviations from the design intent. Activities will be regarded as the 
system’s elements of behavior and their meaningful attributes are their occurrence, conditions and 
duration. Starting with activity number 1 “identify need for WO”, each one of the eleven activities 
are analyzed for potential hazards. Table 7.1 illustrates a small segment of the actual analysis. A 
typical HAZOP worksheet is used here, consisting of six columns. The first column has the form 
of: Activity (attribute). The purpose of this column is to explicitly demonstrate the activity and the 
particular attribute of the activity that the hazard identification process is focusing on. 
 
The rest of the columns consist of the guide word, deviation, causes, consequences and 
recommendations, if any. Typically, an “existing safeguards” column is also used, but since the 
DMMS is still at a very early designing phase, it was naturally assumed that no safeguards have 
been designed yet. 
 
Returning to Table 7.1, we notice that the actual interpretations of the deviations are all based on 
Table 5.5. For the attribute of duration, guide word “too slow” is interpreted as “activity is slower 
than intended”, in Table 5.5. As a result, the deviation in the first line of Table 7.1 is stated as 
“need identification process is slower than intended”. Potential causes of this particular deviation 
could be the fact that the operator might initially misunderstand the sensor readings or get 
confused. Hence, there is a delay in identifying the need to propose a work order. Immediate 
consequences include a delay in maintenance, which could propagate into an accident. An 
interesting fact to take into account here, is that not all deviations are meaningful. Depending on 
the context, there are deviations that could simply be not applicable. As an example the second 
deviation in Table 7.1 is “need identification process is faster than intended”. Even if the operator 
acted faster than it was initially expected, it would not be a harmful deviation but a rather positive

8. Quality Assessment of the methodology 
 
In order to properly assess the quality of the methodology that was presented in this thesis, a quality 
assessment was carried out. The assessment was particularly based on three criteria: 
 
1. Usability; advantages and limitations of the approach 
2. Applicability; how applicable is the approach based on the design representations and guide 
word relevance 
3. Validity; how complete are the results 
 
8.1 Usability 
 
Usability refers to all the advantages and limitations that were identified throughout the 
development and application of the methodology. More specifically, the approach introduced three 
main advantages and one particular limitation.  
 
To begin with the advantages, the methodology is applicable from the very early stages of the 
development life cycle, allowing design choices to be made in a safety oriented manner, from the 
start of the design process. In addition, the methodology can be applied to hierarchically organized 
set of design documents, managing in this way issues of complexity. The approach not only 
handles complexity quite efficiently, but also allows analysts and designers to choose from a 
variety of different design representations, supporting actively the benefits of establishing multi-
level analyses. 
 
A second advantage of the methodology lies in its simplicity. Most hazard identification 
approaches are characterized by steep “learning curves”, as they mandate the use of industry-
specific design representations and industry-specific guide word and parameter interpretations. 
The approach presented in this thesis is primarily based on the use of UML behavior diagrams. 
UML models can be easily exploited by non-experts, since their notation is fairly easy to 
understand, regardless the educational background or the experience of the study team. Moreover, 
the guide word interpretations were developed to eliminate any potential ambiguities and therefore 
their application is straightforward. To confirm this argument, the study team behind the DMMS 
analysis included engineers with three different backgrounds (IT experience, risk analysis 
experience, maintenance experience) that easily managed to perceive the basics of the 
methodology and carry out the hazard identification process. 
 
The third and final advantage of the methodology is the fact that it can be applied to a number of 
different domains. The initial purpose of this thesis was to develop a hazard identification 
methodology capable of managing hazardous deviations in distributed communication systems. 
However, since the approach was based on notions of functional modelling and more specifically 
on UML behavior diagrams, any system that can be expressed through those models, can utilize 
the methodology. In fact, the system under investigation does not even need to be a computer-
based one. As long as any of the six exploited UML behavior diagrams are used, a hazard 
identification process can be theoretically carried out. 
 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
Although the approach offers some very important advantages, it is also characterized by one 
significant limitation. In order to fully investigate potential hazardous deviations, during the 
DMMS analysis, a significant amount of time was invested. Even though the process was 
optimized as much as it was humanly possible, considering the time-frame of this thesis, 
exploration of hazards remained a very time-consuming procedure. While this is natural with most 
hazard identification methods, it is still considered a limitation, especially when dealing with 
overly complex systems. Attempts of automating similar hazard identification techniques can been 
found in literature [18], [61] and can indeed prove very useful time-wise, however this was out of 
the scope of this master project. 
 
8.2 Applicability 
 
In theory a methodology might look quite promising. However, the only way to determine and 
confirm its capabilities is through practical use. The applicability or practicality of the approach 
was reviewed based on two criteria: 
 
 design representation relevance, 
 guide word relevance 
 
As far as the design representation is concerned, UML behavior diagrams proved to be more than 
capable of describing a complex distributed communication system. While only two of the 
diagrams were utilized during the DMMS analysis (activity and sequence diagram), that was only 
by choice. The methodology does not mandate or suggest the use of one diagram over another, but 
rather allows the study team to determine themselves how to represent a given system or how 
many levels of analyses are required. As an example, state machine diagrams could have been 
easily utilized to represent aspects of control, although they were not particularly useful in the case 
of the DMMS.

8.3 Validity 
 
The criterion of validity refers to the level of completeness of the methodology. The DMMS 
scenario was used as a form of validation and the results of the analysis speak for themselves. 
Although the DMMS was still at an early designing phase, the analysis identified in total seventy-
nine potential deviations in the first hierarchical level and fifty-six in the second. In addition, forty-
eight recommendations were given. While the case-scenario acted as a form of validation, at least 
in respect to the scope of this thesis, validating formally the level of completeness requires much 
more than that. 
 
A hazard identification methodology is formally complete if the ratio of the number of the 
identified hazards divided by the number of all possible hazards is equal to one: 
 
𝑁𝑢𝑚𝑏𝑒𝑟 𝑜𝑓 ℎ𝑎𝑧𝑎𝑟𝑑𝑠 𝑖𝑑𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑖𝑓𝑖𝑒𝑑
𝑁𝑢𝑚𝑏𝑒𝑟 𝑜𝑓 𝑎𝑙𝑙 𝑝𝑜𝑠𝑠𝑖𝑏𝑙𝑒 ℎ𝑎𝑧𝑎𝑟𝑑𝑠= 1 
 
Taylor [62] who studied the completeness of conventional HAZOP concluded that such an 
absolute measure is not applicable in practice, since the number of all possible hazards cannot be 
defined precisely. Instead he suggested a more practical measure, which he referred to as historical 
completeness.

Literature revealed only a limited amount of relevant case stories and hence only a small set of 
test-cases referring to actual accidents was used as a comparison with the results of the DMMS 
analysis. Attempting to benchmark the methodology against a small sample of test-cases will not 
lead to a statistically-complete comparison. However, it can provide some valuable preliminary 
conclusions, regarding the completeness of the approach. 
 
The test-cases were extracted from [63] and [64] and illustrate some real-life accidents of the past. 
 
8.3.1 Test case #1 
 
“An operator noted temperature alarm lights on a pump bearing and changed the operation to a 
standby pump, placing a work order request for maintenance on the failing pump. The problem 
arose because of a blockage in the pump lubrication system which was common to both pumps, so 
both pumps were damaged. The maintenance planning failed to identify this case as a priority, 
there would have been enough time if the planners recognized the potential for common cause.” 
 
The first test-case illustrates an accident that was caused by delay in maintenance. The reason for 
the delay was a priority error and a lack of identifying the level of criticality of the situation. 
Similar issues of delay and priority error were identified properly during the DMMS analysis. On 
the other hand, the lack of identification of criticality was not explicitly identified as a source of 
failure, since details of priority allocation were unavailable at the early design stage of the DMMS. 
This actually emphasizes the importance of using a multi-level or hierarchical analysis approach 
and of extending the analysis as the designing phase proceeds. 
 
8.3.2 Test case #2  
 
“A permit to work was issued for scrapers on heavy rollers. The technician was allowed to work 
on the live system with guards removed because the look up to the job safety analysis identified 
the wrong equipment type.” 
 
The second test-case involved inaccurate retrieval of standard job safety assessments as well as 
disregarding the use of safeguards. Of those two, only the first one is relevant here, since 
disregarding regulations is out of the scope of the analysis of a communication system. The DMMS 
analysis identified a similar hazard “error in retrieval of the work procedures” which would 
include standard safety precautions. 
 
8.3.3 Test case #3 
 
“A maintenance team was required to remove piping in order to replace a pump. When flange 
bolts were removed, hot oil came out and the men were burned. The pump had been identified 
incorrectly in the work order. The maintenance team did not recognize that there was hot oil in 
the piping system because the pump was not operating and had cooled.”  
 
==PAGE:71==
71 
 
In this test-case an accident occurred because of an error in a work order. In the DMMS analysis, 
a similar error was identified as “wrong work order issued”. For later stages of analysis, it would 
be also essential to represent the process of filling out a work order with an appropriate UML 
behavior model. Such a representation would naturally lead to a more detailed range of potential 
hazards. Nevertheless, the hazard identification at this level was considered sufficient, since such 
information was omitted from the given system specifications. 
 
8.3.4 Test case #4 
 
“A foreman was given a work permit to modify a pipeline. In the late afternoon, the maintenance 
team went home, without having started the maintenance. The foreman checked that the line was 
safe and asked the safety authority for permission to restart use of the piping in order to allow 
production overnight. The next morning, the maintenance team returned and started work without 
realizing that conditions for the work permit had changed. As a result, acid was released, 
fortunately without injury.” 
 
The fourth test-case involved an incident that occurred because of a work permit becoming out-
of-date. Similar deviations were identified during the DMMS analysis and were captured through 
“Permit to work is not requested when not intended”. The analysis did indeed touch on the subject 
here, although failed to identify the exact causes of the incident. If the actual work process had 
been modelled (e.g. with an activity diagram), such an issue of duration of work and potential 
changes during the work process could have been identified. This particular case can be 
characterized as a borderline one to validate the methodology, as the cause of the incident lies 
outside the scope of the DMMS. However, it emphasizes the fact that the scope of the analysis 
should be always considered carefully. 
 
8.3.5 Test case #5 
 
“Internal maintenance of a vessel required workers to enter the vessel. A safety measure for this 
was a requirement to ventilate the vessel and then check for flammable gas and for adequate 
oxygen content. The atmosphere should have been tested by an authorized gas tester (person) but 
the request for the gas tester was delayed in forwarding to the test department. The workers 
became annoyed by the delay, and entered the vessel without testing. One collapsed.” 
 
In the fifth test-case an accident occurred because of workers consciously breaking a safety rule. 
The communications system here indirectly contributed to the accident by delaying the safety 
activity. Similar deviations were identified through “work order safeguards, planned slower than 
intended”. 
 
8.3.6 Test case #6 
 
“Two maintenance tasks were to be carried out in locations close to each other. One involved 
opening a vessel and steaming it out to remove absorbed gas and vapor. The steaming process 
released some hydrogen sulphide. Originally the planning had scheduled the two maintenance 
tasks on different days, but due to delays in work, the two tasks were carried out at the same time. 
The team opening the vessel retreated to a safe distance during steaming out, but the other team 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
was exposed to the toxic gas. The computer based simultaneous operations safety analysis was not 
checked because the work schedule updating was delayed.” 
 
The sixth and final test-case involved an accident that was caused due to a delay in updating the 
work activities database. Similar deviations were captured in the DMMS analysis. However, in 
order to determine the full impact of the delay, more details would be needed regarding the 
description of the work planning process. 
 
From all the above, the methodology successfully identified similar hazardous deviations in test-
cases #1, #2, #3, #5 and #6, while it was partially successful in the case of #4 (mainly because it 
was out of scope). Even though the comparison was limited, it acted as a valuable qualitative 
confirmation of the methodology. 
 
It is important to note here that the methodology also expresses significant improvements in terms 
of “logical completeness”, in comparison to other hazard identification techniques. The term of 
“logical completeness” refers to a theoretical sense of confidence on the methodology’s abilities 
to identify all potential hazardous deviations from the design intent. There are two factors in 
particular that support this sense of confidence. The first factor is the design representations that 
are utilized as input during the hazard identification process. The exploited UML behavior 
diagrams can theoretically represent any aspect of a distributed communication system, if used 
correctly in practice. Moreover, the diagrams are compatible with each other, empowering 
hierarchical analyses which in any case makes the methodology “more complete” than any other 
that is based on a single representation. The second factor is the way that elements and most 
importantly their attributes are selected in each type of diagram. By considering behavior as the 
basic element of each diagram and identifying all its possible attributes, the methodology should 
be able to determine all the aspects of the design that could be subjected to some kind of deviation. 
Someone might argue that the completeness of the attribute list itself might be an open question. 
However, since there is no mathematical formula to calculate the possible number of attributes, 
the choice is based purely on experience. Preliminary results, such as the DMMS analysis and test-
case comparisons clearly endorsed the attributes selected.

9. Conclusion and Recommendations 
 
The focus of this thesis was the identification of hazards, stemming from current and future 
communication technologies implementations. An extensive literature review revealed, that 
despite the fact that various attempts have been made so far on the subject, none of them has been 
able to reach a high level of acceptance within the risk analysis community. 
 
A comparative review of existing hazard identification methodologies, illustrated that Hazard and 
Operability Studies (HAZOP) proved to be the best candidate to work with and exploit, in order 
to enhance the predictive performance of hazard identification in advanced distributed 
communication systems. Based on the limitations of previous attempts and the valuable “lessons 
learned”, the master thesis suggested a novel hazard identification approach. The approach 
focused specifically on identifying appropriate system representations and establishing relevant 
system parameters and lists of parameter deviations. 
 
Regarding the system representations, the methodology took advantage of the capabilities of 
functional modelling to manage issues of complexity and developed an “umbrella-framework”, 
exploiting six UML behavior diagrams. Based on the common feature of “behavior”, that all UML 
behavior diagrams share in common, appropriate system parameters and guidewords were 
suggested. A similar approach was followed by Guiochet [51] – [56], although his work was based 
on exploiting the UML metamodel and the range of representations dealt with was more limited. 
 
In order to evaluate the practicality of the suggested methodology, a distributed maintenance 
management system (DMMS), in a nuclear power plant, was used as a case scenario. The 
functionality of the scenario was based on specifications extracted from developed prototypes of 
the OECD Halden Reactor Project. The analysis of the DMMS demonstrated some of the benefits 
of utilizing UML behavior diagrams, such as empowering hierarchical decompositions to 
efficiently manage complexity. 
 
A quality assessment was carried out to evaluate the methodology based on its usability, 
applicability and validity. Validity in particular proved to be the most challenging criterion to 
satisfy, as it referred to the “level of completeness” of the approach. A set of test-cases was used 
to compare the outcome of the DMMS analysis with real-life accidents. Even though the number 
of test-cases was rather limited, the comparison acted as a valuable qualitative confirmation of the 
methodology.  
 
In addition, the methodology was found to be “logically-complete”. The argument of “logical-
completeness” was based both on the design representations and the way elements and attributes 
were identified to explore potential deviations. 
 
Although the objectives of this thesis were fulfilled, the journey of developing a hazard 
identification methodology, applicable to distributed communication systems, triggered a number 
of recommendations for future work. 
 
Using behavior as a common theme, it was possible to exploit six UML behavior diagrams, 
forming an umbrella framework. The exact same approach could be theoretically used for every 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
functional modeling diagram and for a number of different domains. Future work should consider 
potential expansion to other domains and exploitation of relevant functional modelling 
representations. 
 
One of the limitations of the approach was found to be its time-consuming nature. However, this 
is a natural consequence of every high-quality analysis. Literature has shown various attempts of 
automating or semi-automating similar approaches.  Unfortunately though, while automation can 
be feasible, its value is open to question as many of the automation approaches have resulted in 
poor quality analysis. Future work should investigate the possibilities of more advanced 
automation. 
 
One significant finding that was identified through the test-cases was that the value of analysis 
could be enhanced if the work processes were modelled alongside with the information processes 
and communication systems. In this way, the methodology could identify how communication 
failures actually affect work safety. The scope of this thesis restricted the analysis on the 
communication aspects, but for future work, the integration of analyses should at least be 
considered. 
 
The methodology exploited six different UML behavior diagrams, although only two of them were 
used during the actual analysis. Future work should also evaluate all of the representations, through 
one or more actual case-scenarios. 
 
Lastly, even though the methodology was partially validated through the DMMS analysis and the 
test-cases comparison, the number of test-cases was rather limited. In order to validate the 
approach adequately, validation in the future should be also carried out in a field where extensive 
accident lists are available.
