Abstract 
Due to the rising awareness of depletion of phosphate rock, which does not allow further 
dependence on mineral fertilizers, the novel digestate processing techniques are being 
developed. The methods aim into maximizing recovery of the nutrients and generating 
dischargeable or re-useable water from the biogas digestate. This project focuses on processing  
digestates based on anaerobic digestion (AD) of sewage sludge (GA1) and sewage sludge 
enriched with fish sludge (GA2). The primary separation of the raw digestates into liquid and 
solids fractions is obtained by centrifugation. However, it was proven, that most of the 
phosphorus present in the digestate is organically bounded in particulate form, therefore the 
acidification treatment with 25% sulfuric acid (aiming in dissolving P into liquid fraction) prior 
to centrifugation is also studied and assessed. The acidification step allows to dissolve 
considerable amount of phosphorous from the particulate form, enhancing its abundance in 
liquid fraction after centrifugation. The study shows that digestates, which were subjected to 
acidification contained up to ten times more phosphorous after centrifugation than the not 
acidified ones. Subsequently, four types of liquid fraction: centrifuged GA1 (CGA1), 
centrifuged GA2 (CGA2), acidified and centrifuged GA1 (CAGA1) and acidified and 
centrifuged GA2 (CAGA2) rich in soluble phosphorous and organics were subjected to 
sequential membrane processes involving microfiltration (MF), ultrafiltration (UF) and 
nanofiltration (NF). MF was conducted in the dead-end process mode, which resulted in 
significant membrane fouling. The membrane rejection in terms of TS (total solids), COD 
(Chemical Oxygen Demand), TP (Total Phosphorus), ortho P (Ortho phosphorous) varied 
according to type of digestate. In general, MF process turned out to be not sufficient separation 
process for phosphorus recovery, however served as a pretreatment for further processing. UF 
was performed in cross-flow operation mode. The experiments showed excellent performance 
of CGA2 with average permeate flux equal to 6.98 [LMH] and TP, ortho P and COD rejection 
exceeding 90%. The permeate flux and overall rejections were worse in terms of digestate 
CAGA2, however it was characterized by the highest values of TP and ortho P (985 mg/l and 
955 mg/l respectively) in the retentate, having therefore the highest fertilizer potential. The 
digestates CGA1 and CAGA1 gave less satisfactory results in accordance to all the parameters. 
The final step of processing – NF, turned out to be not suitable method, giving negative fluxes 
and no improvement in terms of phosphorous recovery in comparison to UF.  
Key words: sewage sludge; sewage and fish sludge; biogas digestate; phosphorous recovery; nutrients recovery; 
solubilization of nutrients; solid-liquid separation of digestate; pressure driven membrane processes; 
Microfiltration; Ultrafiltration; Nanofiltration

Motivation 
Since the late 19th century, oil has been the most broadly used energy source worldwide. The 
“black gold” changed the way we live and it is present in every area of our life. Moreover, it 
still plays a crucial role in economic prosperity and has been the cause of many political 
conflicts. However, the resources of oil are not infinite, not to mention how environmental 
hostile. In 1970s the first energy crisis occurred, causing the 400% increase of oil prices, 
indicating the necessity of finding alternatives. Since then, the oil supplies are in gradual decline 
and there is no chance for regrowth [1][2]. In order to stop the dependence on fossil fuels, it is 
of particular importance to develop other options. One of them is a biogas, which is obtained 
by anaerobic digestion of organic matter such as municipal waste, agricultural byproducts, food 
industry leftovers and many more [3]. Another problem, emerging due to thoughtless and long-
term human activity is worldwide nutrients imbalance. In terms of phosphorus (P) we face the 
unique situation, where there is both too much and too little of this precious element [4]. From 
one side the phosphate rock are being irretrievably depleted in order to obtain mineral fertilizer, 
whereas on the other, the phosphorous that has not been absorbed by the soil is washed into the 
water reservoirs causing eutrophication. It is estimated that with current demand for fertilizer, 
the geological reserves will be gone in 90 years and with no other alternatives the mankind will 
have to face a serious food crisis [5]. Both the energy supply and nutrients imbalance problems 
can be approached by wise management of biogas plants. In Europe, biogas installations are 
becoming more and more common. According to EBA (European Biogas Association), by 2030 
the production of renewable gas will reach 30 billion m3 in Europe and will be able to replace 
around 10% of the natural gas consumption [6]. However, the biogas process is not entirely 
waste free. Along the biogas, a considerable amount of digestate is being produced. The effluent 
is rich in nutrients (which makes it a very good candidate for fertilizer and decrease the 
dependence on phosphate rock), however still very diluted which, in many cases means that it 
cannot be directly applied on the fields. Therefore this research will focus on biogas digestate 
treatment, aiming in effective separation of phosphorous and recovering water.

Thesis Objectives 
The main objective of the project is developing a feasible treatment of biogas digestate coming 
from reactors fed with sewage sludge (GA1) and sewage sludge enriched with the fish sludge 
(GA2).  The goal of digestates separation is to obtain a phosphorous rich fraction as a source 
for fertilizer production, together with a water fraction, that can be safely discharged into the 
environment. The most important steps of the research include: 
• 
Finding the suitable parameters for efficient primary solid-liquid separation, using the 
centrifugation  
• 
Investigating the influence of acidification on the biogas effluents. The main focus will 
be put on the phosphorous release from the particulate fraction of the digestates. 
Moreover, the acidic environment is also known to favor the heavy metals release, 
therefore the content of those unwanted pollutants in digestates will be monitored 
• 
Comparing two types of biogas digestates in terms of phosphorus content and 
possibilities of recovery 
• 
Implementing membrane processes for nutrients separation and water recovery 
• 
Assessing digestate on different stages of treatment in terms of plausibility of field 
application in regards to current legislation

Background 
The Norwegian biogas sector is under constant development. The biggest concentration of 
anaerobic digestion plants can be found in eastern Norway, in the proximity of Oslo [7]. 
Moreover, many projects and research regarding developing biogas sector in western part of 
the country are also carried out. Due to the fact, that agricultural area and animal production 
occupy only small percent of the whole country, the biogas production is mainly based on a 
utilizing sewage sludge as a substrate [8]. Utilizing biogas technology in wastewater treatment 
plants (WWTP) is commonly known and proven practice. Sewage sludge is produced as a result 
of wastewater treatment and forwarded to biogas reactor in order to generate energy as shown 
in the Figure 1. Presently, two biogas plants, namely Rådalen Biogassanlegg (Bergen 
Municipality Water and Sewage Agency(BVA, Hordaland)) and Grødaland Biogassanlegg 
(Intercommunal Water, Sewage and Waste services (IVAR IKS, Rogaland) are considering to 
include other substrates (like fish sludge) to their reactors in order to optimize and enhance the 
methane production, as well as implement digestate treatment in order to recover nutrients 
(Figure 1). Taking into account the fact that only in Norway phosphorous available in organic 
wastes and residues is estimated to account for approx. 28,000 tons/year [9], reconnecting 
nutrient recovery with biogas production could provide a sustainable solution that will 
contribute to the circular economy.

 In Norway, fisheries, fish farming and fish processing produces considerable amount of sludge 
(composed of fish manure and fish-feed surplus) that has a big potential as a biogas feedstock. 
The fish wastes are very rich in organic load as well as in phosphorous. Incorporating fish 
sludge to regular anaerobic digestion of sewage sludge can be very beneficial in terms of 
methane production and enhanced phosphorous recovery. Within every anaerobic digestion 
process, a certain amount of digestate (rich in inert biological matter, nutrients and water) is 
being produced. The main issue with the effluent is that it is diluted. Incorporating a suitable 
treatment of the digestate could transform the biogas production process waste into valuable 
product – fertilizer. In order to that, digestate has to undergo a solid-liquid separation processes 
that allow to dewater the sludge and recover the phosphorous rich fraction. Furthermore, Urban 
Waste Water Treatment Directive in Norway dated on 1991 [11] states that  phosphorous 
removal and thus its recovery from the biogas digestate, is the primary goal in accordance to 
decrease the environmental impacts of discharging wastewater to fresh water and marine 
acceptors. Due to the dispersed population and large number of runoffs in Norway, the organic 
load (oxygen demand) is usually quite low, therefore, depletion of oxygen in rivers does not 
pose a problem. On the other hand, eutrophication of lakes due to high loads of phosphorous 
(that concentration can be fifteen times higher than organic matter!) has been reported and 
therefore phosphorous removal should become a primary goal in regards to wastewater 
discharges. This project will focus on methods of solubilization, fractionation and recovery of 
phosphorous from the digestate coming from biogas process based on WWTP sludge as well 
as digestate based on both sewage and fish sludge.

3.1 
Phosphorous 
Phosphorous is an essential and growth limiting nutrient for all living organisms. It is involved 
in various biochemical reactions during energy transport (ATP), it occurs in DNA, RNA, as 
well as in many building structures (biomineral hydroxyapatite in bones, phospholipids as 
structural support) within organisms [12]. Naturally, phosphorus can be found in bedrock, soil 
and sediments. What has to be mentioned, phosphorus in contrast to other major nutrient 
(nitrogen) does not have a significant gaseous fraction. The phosphorus locked up in the rocks 
(like apatite Ca10(PO4)6(OH,F,Cl)2, the most abundant P mineral) is not directly available for 
organisms and in order to convert it to dissolved orthophosphate it needs to undergo series of 
geochemical and biochemical reactions within the global phosphorus cycle shown in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2 Simplified global phosphorus cycle from phosphorus rich mineral rocks (1) to phosphorite sediments (4) via 
chemical and physical weathering as well as marine transport (2),(3). Terrestrial plants and animals produce dissolved and 
particulate phosphate, whereas  marine forms of life accumulate soluble phosphorus and are able to produce sediments 
thorough biomineralization. Red signs indicates negative human  impact on phosphorus cycle in terms of acquisition as well 
as distribution. 
The first step (1) is the tectonic uplift, exposing P rich mineral rocks to weathering, followed 
with (2) physical and chemical weathering of rocks, by acids coming predominantly from 
microorganisms. Solubilized phosphate is available for plants and due to their decay or litterfall 
it results in soil generation and supplying rivers with dissolved and particulate phosphorus.

for P-bioavailability to plants, which had to develop various mechanism to be able to take the 
phosphorus from soil as well as retain it. The next step (3) is transport of the phosphorus, to the 
oceans, mostly by rivers containing phosphorus predominantly in particulate form (90%). 
Dissolved phosphorus can be found in both organic (20-40%) and inorganic form like 
orthophosphate, aluminum oxyhydroxides and clays. The dissolved orthophosphate together 
with carbon and nitrogen is utilized by photosynthetic organisms, that serve as a base for the 
whole marine food web. Biogenic phosphorus which is the product of the photosynthesis and 
following food web, as well as river originated inorganic and organic phosphorus form the 
sediment-water interface. The last stage of cycle (4) accounts for sedimentation of phosphorus. 
It can occur by breaking down the organic phosphorus by microbial respiration (mineralization) 
into its inorganic form, particle settling or decomposition of dissolved phosphorus in aphotic 
zone. The new cycle begins with uplift of sediments [13]. Over the years of increasing 
population, popularity of animal-based diet and blooming bioenergy sector, the natural P cycles 
have intensified, causing remarkable global imbalances. In terms of inputs, demand for 
phosphorus based fertilizers grows, generating depletion of phosphorus rock. Moreover, the 
excessive fertilization leads to P deposition in ground water, stimulating nuisance algal growth 
and causing hypoxic or anoxic conditions destructive or lethal for resident ecosystems [12] [14]. 
In order reverse the negative influence of human activities, it is important to focus on the 
technologies that allow its appropriate recovery and management.  
3.1.1 Phosphorus dissolution  
As mentioned earlier, phosphorus occurs in nature in multiple forms; dissolved orthophosphate 
(most desirable for membrane separation), inorganic (reactive, condensed or acid hydrolysable 
phosphate) and organic bounded form [15]. As to the phosphorus dynamics and mechanism of 
transition along anaerobic digestion, studies involving manure indicate that most of the 
phosphorus in a raw feed is inorganic. Moreover, analyzing samples after digestion it was found 
that along the digestion process organic phosphate is most likely degraded and therefore 
converted to inorganic form as well. However, aspect of P availability in digestate varies greatly 
between different research studies. Some shows the increase in the overall nutrient availability 
after AD, others state no difference or even a decrease [16].  The pH of the digestate varies 
between 7-8, which means that large portion of phosphate is bounded to percipitates with 
calcium, ammonium or magnesium. The digestate treatment focuses on liquid part, whereas 
most of the phosphate is trapped in the solid fraction. Therefore, in order to maximize the

with rising phosphorus dissolution rate, saturation index of both Ca+  and Mg+ cations, forming 
complexes with P also increase at acidic pH, which enlarges the effect on releasing P from 
wastes [19]. However, if metals (including heavy metals) are present in the sludge, they will 
also get dissolved and pose potential obstacle for implementing recovered phosphorus as a 
fertilizer. In terms of organic micropollutants (OMP), acid does not affect them and OMP stays 
in the organic fraction, they can be removed due to some further processing like Reverse 
Osmosis or Nanofiltration [23]. Some sources says that due to chemical dissolution it is possible 
to recover over 90% of the phosphorus, depending on the sludge quality. Even for slightly 
acidified sludges this rate does not drop below 70% [23], therefore acidification approach seems 
promising and feasible in terms of phosphorous recovery process.

3.2 
Biogas  
As the name indicates, biogas is formed due to biological processes. In anaerobic conditions, 
biomass is converted by microorganisms into a gaseous mixture. Naturally, the process occurs 
on the bottom of the oceans, on peatlands,  in manure and in rumen of ruminants. In the past 
years biogas is also commonly obtained in anaerobic digestion (AD) reactors and serve as 
renewable energy. Similarly to fossil natural gas, the essential component, that provides the 
energy content of the biogas is methane (CH4). Depending on the biogas reactor feedstock, its 
amount varies between 50%-75%. The second major component of biogas is carbon dioxide 
(CO2) which accounts for 25%-50%. Other constituents of the biogas are water (H2O), oxygen 
(O2) and residues of sulfur (S2) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) [24]. 
3.2.1 Biogas feedstock 
The feasibility of the biogas production process is dictated by the methane yields, which is 
largely dependent on the type of waste that is being degraded [25]. In principle, all organic 
materials are suitable for digestion, excluding stable woody materials due to its high lignin 
content that microorganisms are unable to metabolize [26]. In order to assess the feasibility of 
the feedstock, different approaches are implemented. If the composition of  biomass is known 
and 100% conversion of the organic material can be assumed it is possible to calculate 
theoretical methane potential [25]. Based on many studies, it was found that in order to ensure 
high methane production, substrate should contain sufficient amount of carbohydrates, fats and 
proteins and the overall nutrient ratio of C:N:P:S should be 600:15:5:1. The microelements like 
iron, nickel, selenium, molybdenum and tungsten are also vital for bacterial development and 
cannot be omitted during selection of the feed. Moreover, C:N proportion should be maintained 
between 10-30. If the ratio is higher (too much carbon), it is not possible to convert all carbon 
and get the predicted methane potential. In contrast situation, where feed contains too much 
nitrogen, it can result in ammonia formation that inhibits the bacterial growth [27]. However, 
in most of the cases the wastes are very diversified and the exact composition is unknown. 
Therefore, laboratory experiments are implemented in order to estimate the methane potential. 
Some of the utilized methods are: measuring volume of the gas under constant pressure, 
measuring pressure growth in constant volume or using chromatography [25].  
The biomass is often classified into groups according to sector that generates them. The most 
abundant source for anaerobic digestion is within the agricultural sector that accounts for animal 
manures, slurries and energy crops. Industrial organic wastes like organic by-products, food,

next stage of methane formation is acidogenesis. In a well-balanced reactor, acidogenes convert 
most of the hydrolysis products directly into methanogenic substrates as acetate, carbon dioxide 
and hydrogen. Degradation of remaining heterogenous organic matter results also in formation 
of higher organic acids such as propionic, butyric, iso-valeric, valeric and acetic acids (VFA), 
alcohols and ammonia. Depending on the process balance and rate of hydrogen consumption, 
organic overload with those chemicals can occur and lead to anaerobic digestion inhibition. 
Therefore it is important to measure VFA concentration in order to keep biogas production on 
a satisfactory level. Acetogensis is the next step of AD. The acetogenic bacteria transform 
higher organics like VFA and alcohols, that were produced during acidogenesis to acetic acid 
and hydrogen. Both acidogenic and acetogenic reactions results in substrates for methanogenic 
bacteria (H2, CO2 and acetate), therefore it is hard to draw a clear line between those steps. The 
acetogenic bacteria are very sensitive to high H2 concentration, therefore it is important to keep 
the partial pressure of H2 at the accurate level, having at the same time enough hydrogen as a 
substrate for methanogenesis, which is the final and critical biogas production phase. Methane 
formation can occur only in strictly anaerobic conditions. Methane producing organisms 
(acetophylic, hydrogenophylic and methylothropic bacteria) are very sensitive and even the 
smallest amount of oxygen can negatively influence already slow process. There are three 
groups of substrates that are available for methanogenesis; first is carbon dioxide type (CO2, 
CO, HCOO-), the methyl type (CH3OH, CH3NH3) and the acetate type (CH3COO). Methane 
can be synthesized by either decomposition of acetate  (round two thirds of produced methane), 
hydrogen (electron donor) and carbon dioxide (electron acceptor) synthesis (remaining 30 %), 
or produce methane from methanol [31][32]. Methane formation yield can be easily disturbed. 
Beside mentioned above high sensitivity towards oxygen, inorganic sulphur that can be a found 
in some feedstocks pose a danger. Sulphur reduction bacteria use the same substrates as 
methane forming bacteria, therefore, with presence of sulphur in the methanogenesis step, 
different bacteria type compete, that results in lowering down the methane formation process. 
The slow methane formation process allows exceeded fatty acid production, that can 
accumulate and be converted into cellulose, changing the balance of the system. 
3.2.3 Process parameters 
The most important ambient conditions influencing the methane formation are oxygen content, 
temperature, pH, feedstock type (described in section 3.2.1) and presence of inhibitors. Each 
bacteria group involved in the process has different environmental preferences [31]. In terms 
of anaerobic conditions, methanogens are strictly anaerobic, and even small amount of the

oxygen can destroy them,  however some of the hydrolysis, aceto- and acidogenic bacteria can 
survive in aerobic conditions. It is very convenient for the process because it is nearly 
impossible to eliminate all external electron donors from the reactor and all the facultative 
anaerobes can utilize the oxygen present in fermentator, thus saving microorganisms that does 
not tolerate aerobic conditions. The next important aspect is temperature. There are three main 
temperature ranges for anaerobic digestion: psychrophilic (25°C), mesophilic (32-42°C) and 
thermophilic (50-57°C). The advantage of the fist range is stability and the fact that external 
heat exchangers are not needed, because heat generated by bacteria along process is enough. 
However, it is commonly known that most  of the chemical reactions rate increases with 
temperature, in case of methane formation 25 °C  is relatively low and therefore doesn’t result 
in sufficient biogas production. On the other hand, thermophilic operation is the most favorable 
for methanogens and the gas yield are the highest, however it requires a lot of external energy, 
and under those conditions bacteria are very sensitive for disturbances within feed supply which 
complicates the operation. The answer for the problems emerging within low and high 
temperatures is mesophilic condition that provides high gas production with good stability. 
Therefore, most of the installation around the world are working within this moderate range. 
The acidity is another important parameter for different consortia of bacteria. Whereas 
hydrolyzing an acidifying bacteria prefer pH between 4,5-6.3, for acetate and methane bacteria 
the range varies between 6.8-7.5 [27]. If the AD is held in one reactor, the pH of the whole 
system needs to be set within methanogens range. There are two main buffering systems in the 
reactor; carbon dioxide/bicarbonate/carbonate that provides excessive acidification and 
ammonia/ammonium to avoid week acidification [27][25]. Sometimes production rate falls and 
it can be caused by different factors. On the one side it could be a technical problems of the 
installation, on the other hand the reason can be found in the inhibitors, which are substances 
that are toxic to bacteria and disrupt the digestion process. They can be divided in two groups: 
the one that enter the reactor within the feedstock and substances that are byproducts of the 
reactions taking place during AD. The biggest danger for the bacteria pose the uncontrolled 
concentrations of antibiotics, disinfectants, solvents, herbicides, salts of heavy metals or heavy 
metals themselves that can hider bacterial growth. Furthermore, in case of inappropriate dosing 
of the feed, excess of microelements can have a bad influence on the process. However, to some 
extent bacteria are able to adjust to new environment and some of the inhibitors are devastating 
only in particular situations and configurations. For example, heavy metals inhibits the 
reactions only in free form, otherwise H2S present during fermentation can bond and neutralize 
them. In terms of inhibitors forming during digestion, one of the most important is ammonium,

interaction influence the final reaction rate. It is very difficult to predict the behavior of the 
reactor, therefore process parameters have to be constantly monitored and corrected before the 
biogas production will be stopped. The other strategy, being recently developed is the genetical 
modification of the microbes in order to enhance the metabolic pathways efficiency and

3.3 
Biogas digestate  
During biogas production a significant amount of biogas effluent, so-called digestate is being 
produced. Digestate represents the digested substrate removed from AD reactor consisting of 
indigestible material like nitrogen, phosphorous or potassium, as well as dead micro-organisms. 
In terms of digestate composition, parameters varies according to the type of feedstock. The 
most important physical characteristics are solids content, density, viscosity and particle size 
distribution [34]. During the AD, 20-95% [35] of organic matter is being degraded, reducing  
the total solid (TS) content, total organic carbon (TOC)  and carbon to nitrogen ratio (C:N). The 
scope of reduction, largely depends on the feedstock employed. The biomass containing lignin 
has low digestibility, whereas materials rich in fats, sugar and alcohols are digested fast and 
more efficient. The complexity of biochemistry along AD process causes changes in effluent 
pH. The average pH of the raw substrate is about 7.1, whereas pH of fresh digestate can vary 
from 7.5 to 8. Increase of pH can be caused by many reactions like consumption of VFA, 
transformation of CO32- and 2 H3O+ to CO2 and H2O  as well as formation of ammonium 
carbonate ((NH4)CO2). In contrast, precipitation of calcites and iron phosphates increase 
acidity. It was found that higher pH favors ammonia volatilization which can cause problems 
with digestate storage [36]. L.Masse et al [37] shows that lowering the pH with i.e sulfuric acid 
can enhance TAN retention. In case of membrane separation of digestate, acidification can 
additionally improve the water flux recovery rates and decrease fouling. The nutrients content 
and availability also differs according to the feedstock type. Along the AD process, organic 
nitrogen compounds are mineralized into ammonium NH4-N. The inorganic form of nitrogen 
partly serves the microorganism in the reactor as a feed and remaining portion is intended for 
the plants if digested will be used as a fertilizer. Poultry and pig slurry or cereal grains which 
are easily degradable and rich in nitrogen, are known to have high NH4-N and total nitrogen 
(TN) content in the digestate. On the other hand, substrates like cattle manure, organic/industrial 
wastes or brewers spent grains, are rather poor in nitrogen, which will be reflected in the 
digestate composition. In terms of phosphorus, some studies states enhanced P intake after 
digestion [38], however more sources mention no relevant difference or even possible negative 
impact on P-availability after AD. As mentioned earlier, pH of the digestate is higher than the 
original substrate and this basic environment favors the formation of phosphate and its 
precipitation as calcium- or magnesium- phosphate, which causes partial retention in digesters. 
In case of solid-liquid separation of the digestate, the liquid fraction is left with very low 
phosphorus concentration and low fertilizer value. It has to be taken into consideration that

3.3.1 Quality characterization parameters 
In order to quantify the biogas substrates and effluents, certain parameters regarding physical 
and chemical composition need to be introduced. The physical parameters allow to plan the 
efficient solid-liquid separation system. On the other hand, the chemical characterization is 
mostly used to define the nutrient content and its fertilizing value. Moreover, analysis such as 
heavy metals concentration, COD, BOD levels, microbiological pathogens allow to assess if 
for a given post-treatment method the samples are consistent with discharge and fertilizing 
criteria. 
From the physical point of view, the digested sludge can be described as interacting particles 
in a suspension [39]. The factor that plays a key role in solid-liquid separation is a particle size 
distribution, that varies according to the type of feed. It was concluded that this variable is the 
most important in order to assess filterability and the possibility of sludge/digestate dewatering 
[40]. Generally, the ability to filtrate decrease together with decreasing particle size. Moreover,  
it was found that particle size can be modified by the anaerobic digestion process itself. The 
small particles are more digestible, therefore the AD process results in mineralization of small 
particles and shifting particle size distribution to the bigger sizes. The same effect can be 
observed in terms of acidification, which affects surface charge of the  colloidal particles. The 
reduction of negative charge makes the particles to attach to each other which results in flocs 
formation and easier filtration. 
The most common analysis of the digestate and its derivatives along solid-liquid separation 
processes is the total solids (TS) content. TS stands for all of the solids present in the sample.  
The most important information regarding the digestate is the nutrients concentration and 
identification of their chemical form. The rich content of plant macro-nutrients, micronutrients 
and organic matter makes digestate an excellent candidate for natural fertilizer [36]. Nitrogen, 
potassium and phosphorous are relevant nutrients for plants growth and is where the focus 
normally relies for a digestate of good nutrient quality. The main focus of this research is the

organisms [41].

polluted by inorganic species (such as plastic, glass, stones, etc.), heavy metals, inadmissible

Due to its origin, sewage sludge tends to contain higher concentrations of heavy metals than 
other biogas substrates like animal manure. Moreover, its predicted that along AD process, the

There is no clear definition of a heavy metal, however, the most common defining property of 
the element is density that in case of heavy metals has to be bigger than 5 g/cm3. The term 
“heavy metals” often is associated with adverse impact on environment, however it needs to be 
mentioned that the effect of any substance on the living organism is a matter of concentration. 
Therefore, several metal ions as Cr, Fe, Co, Cu, Zn (often referred as micronutrients) in small 
doses are crucial for maintaining biochemical and physiological functions in cells. The problem 
arises within exceeding specific threshold concentration. The biggest threat to environment and 
human health pose lead, cadmium, mercury and arsenic. For many years human activity 
including mining, burning the fossil fuels, developing chemical industry, inappropriate 
discharge of batteries, body care products, plastics and medicines led to accumulation of toxic 
heavy metals, endangering the biosphere [46][47]. High concentrations of heavy metals affect 
microbial growth and survival, therefore having a big impact on the condition of the soil. The 
toxicity of the metals in regards to soil can be graded as: Cd > Cu > Zn > Pb [48][49]. Elevated 
heavy metals concentrations can enhance litter decomposition, which in long-term period will 
cause decrease in fertility of the soil. In terms of carbon mineralization by organic matter, high 
concentration of pollutants as heavy metal can reduce the effectiveness of carbon cycle, 
endangering the soil health. The pollutants can negatively influence the N-immobilazation and 
accumulation, leading to imbalances within the N-cycle. Furthermore, enzymes activity can be

3.4.1 Primary treatment - Centrifugation 
Centrifugation is a relatively simple separation technology that distinguish particles from the 
solution based on their particle size, density, viscosity and shape by rotor speed. The advantage 
of centrifugation is that it  increases the gravity forces on particles and therefore broaden the 
sedimentation range to finer particles or emulsions that in normal condition would be stable 
[58].  The medium is placed in  a centrifugation cell which rotates at defined speed and 
generates centrifugal force that makes the natural process of sedimentation much faster. The 
time of the process usually does not exceed 30 minutes, however it can vary for different 
sediments [59][60]. Centrifugation was chosen as a first step in solid-liquid separation of the 
digestate. The idea of the process is to separate most of the solids from the raw digestate. It is 
predicted that solid fraction should contain from 20-30% TS and considerable amount of 
nutrients. In case of appropriate storage and further stabilization like composting or drying, 
solid fraction should be applicable as bio-fertilizer. On the other hand, remaining liquid (around 
85-90% of the digestate after centrifugation) still contains significant amount of suspended 
solids and nutrients [36]. Further processing of this fraction is vital to separate nutrients as well 
as recover water and will be covered in detail along this project.

3.4.2 Complete processing - Membrane Technology 
Any attempt of separation of mixture conducted with help of semipermeable barrier that allows 
one of the solute to pass through and retain the other can be classified as membrane process. 
The most widespread operations in terms of liquid separation are Reverse Osmosis, 
Nanofiltration, Ultrafiltration and Microfiltration. The conventional gaseous operations are 
being replaced by Pervaporation, Membrane Distillation, or Gas Separation. The most recent 
technologies utilizes also membrane reactors [56][62].  
3.4.3 Types of membranes 
Synthetic membranes can be divided into two main categories: symmetrical (isotropic) and 
unsymmetrical (anisotropic). The most abundant type of symmetrical membranes are 
microporous membranes, which in structure and function resembles the conventional filter, 
even though they are able to retain much smaller solutes (0.1-10 um). The separation 
mechanism is solely based on a molecular size and pore size distribution.  
The other type of isotropic membranes are nonporous dense membranes, which are made of the 
thin film. The transport throughout the membrane accounts mostly for diffusion mediated by 
concentration, pressure or electrical potential gradient. The advantage of dense nonporous 
membranes is the fact that they can separate solutes of similar pore sizes if they are 
characterized by different solubility. 
The last type of homogenous structures are electrically charged membranes that can be both 
porous and dense. The key element of those membranes are fixed charged ions and the 
separation is based on a electrostatic attraction principle [53] [62].  
3.4.4 Membrane materials and modules 
Membrane material should provide high permeability and high selectivity, combined with 
sufficient mechanical endurance. The most common materials used in pressure driven processes 
are organic polymers. Nowadays most membranes has an asymmetric structure and therefore 
are classified as Thin Film Composite membranes (TFC). Cellulose acetate, polysulfone, 
polyvinylidene fluoride, polyethersulfone, polyethylene, polypropylene, polycarbonate, 
polyetherketone and polyamide are the most frequent due to their chemical properties like water 
affinity and flexibility as well as low cost prices [63][64]. On the other hand, the fluxes are

3.4.5 Flux decline in a pressure driven membrane processes 
Fouling is consider as one of the biggest limiting step in membrane technology. Its presence is 
mainly reflected in flux decline during the operation, where all the process parameters are kept 
constant. The phenomenon leads to hindrances such as lower productivity caused by flux 
decline, as well as cost increase due to higher energy demand, cleaning agents, frequency of 
maintenance, membrane replacement and overall shorter membrane lifespan [66][67]. Fouling 
can be induced by several reasons such as: adsorption caused by specific interaction between 
membrane and solutes, concentration polarization leading to gel layer formation and plugging 
the pores by big solutes, that can form a cake layer and clog the membrane. Above mentioned 
factors, contribute to increased resistance on the feed side of the membrane, decreasing the flux. 
The scale of the phenomena is inherently connected to the type of the membrane process. For 
example flux decline is quite severe in case of MF, UF or ED, on the other hand, processes like 
gas separation or pervaporation are not so strongly affected. For pressure driven processes, the 
convective flux of the solution  is proportional to the pressure difference on the two sides of the 
membrane and inversely proportional to the total resistance (consisting of factors mentioned 
above) and the viscosity of the solution. It can be described by equation:

3.4.7 Fouling prevention and eradication 
Fouling negatively influence the economical side of the membrane process and therefore, pose 
one of the biggest obstacle in implementing membrane processes on industrial scale. There are 
several ways of tackling this problem. 
The preventive approach account for feed analysis in terms of salinity, pH, COD, BOD or 
biofilm formation which can provide an useful information in terms of probability of fouling. 
For the processes like NF or RO, an efficient pretreatment is the key to the well prospering 
membrane system.   
As mentioned above,  concentration polarization is the main factor causing the overall 
membrane resistance and what is more it largely contributes to other types of fouling. In order 
to minimize CP, the hydrodynamic aspect of the process should be studied in detail. As shown 
earlier, the CP is mostly dependent on flux and mass transfer coefficient. The second one can 
be moderated by changing hydrodynamics, therefore incorporating the turbulence promoters 
(feed spacers), breaking the boundary layer, using pulsating flow can help achieve smaller CP. 
Another approach is to minimize the increase of transmembrane pressure (TMP) which is the 
pressure difference between both sides of the membrane. According to theory, along the 
pressure-driven membrane process, certain TMP is formed. The TMP tends to increase during 
the process, which indicates that membrane gets fouled. However, J.A Howell et.al [69][70] 
showed that it is possible to establish critical TMP  that should provide a constant flux and 
prevent irreversible fouling. What needs, to be mentioned, TMP value is related to flux, 
therefore, setting correct initial TMP can help to achieve critical flux Jc, that is constant and 
below which the fouling should not occur. On the other hand, many experimental studies

consequence, the solute that is being retained by membrane and starts to accumulate on the 
membrane and forms so-called cake layer that grows along the process [62]. The membrane 
works as long as it will not get completely clogged. In terms of polymeric products the 
regeneration of the membrane’s material is practically impossible, however the steel or ceramic 
filters can be reused. Because of its simplicity, the dead-end filtration can find application in 
separation of diluted media, microbiological stabilization, sanitization and pre- or post-

of the membranes makes the dead-end filtration not economically feasible. 
The more fouling resistant filtration technique is known as a cross-flow filtration. In this type

distinguished into various mode: co-current, counter-current, cross-flow with perfect permeate 
mixing and perfect mixing. This operational mode consists of one inlet stream (feed) and two 
separate outlet streams: permeate (present on the other side of the membrane) being most likely 
the desired product and retentate (concentrated fraction, retained by membrane) that is very 
often recycled to the system. The permeation rate of the system does not decrease as fast as in 
case of dead-end filtration, however flux decline is inevitable and sooner or later the membrane 
needs to undergo the cleaning process or be replaced [74].

3.4.9 Chosen membrane techniques 
 Microfiltration (MF) and Ultrafiltration (UF) 
MF and UF are low pressure filtration processes, used for removal of microorganism as well as 
suspended and colloidal particles from the treated medium. The main difference between 
processes lays in the membrane pore size, which for MF vary from 0.1 μm to 10 μm and for UF 
ranges from 0.001 μm to 0.1μm [76]. The driving force of the processes is pressure (1-10 bar) 
and the transport of solutes is based on sieve mechanism, meaning that flux through the 
membrane is directly proportional to the applied pressure (𝐽= 𝐾∆𝑃) where K stands for

therefore smaller pressure is needed to achieve separation [62] [63].

first one has a quasi-uniform pore sizes along the membrane, so the particles bigger than pore 
diameter are immediately captured on the top surface, consequently clogging the membrane 
very fast. The other type is characterized by more complex structure, with bigger pore size on 
the top surface, which does not capture particles on the top layer. This design, let the majority 
of the particles pass through, so they can be absorbed in the inner surface of the membrane. In-

established, need for the low pressure membranes that can assure bacterial and dissolved 
particles removal rapidly increased. Moreover, MF modules began to play important role in the 
separation of cells from the biologically originated processes [75].  
Moreover, the growing demand and shrinking resources for fresh water led to establishing the 
technologies like RO for salt water desalination. MF and UF membranes play the key role as a 
very efficient pretreatment, therefore being a very important technology in the modern world.

 Nanofiltration (NF) 
Nanofiltration was first identified in late 80s as a bridge between ultrafiltration and reverse 
osmosis. Since then it is known as an effective tool in separation of small molecules and 
particles. The process can be applied in many different areas like water and wastewater 
treatment, desalination, food processing and pharmaceutical and biotechnological industries. 
The pore size of nanofiltration membranes is kept around 1nm which corresponds to MWCO 
between 200-1000 Da. NF membranes can be distinguished from RO membranes by rather low 
rejection of monovalent ions, higher fluxes and presence of ionizable groups on the surface that 
dissociate in contact with solution [67][79]. 
Separation mechanism of NF membranes is very complex. It consists of size exclusion for 
uncharged components as well as charge effects between membrane surface and charged 
molecules (mostly multivalent ions). The charge interactions involve Donnan effect, dielectric 
effect and steric hindrance. Characterization of membrane material is important when 
predicting the selectivity and fouling behavior. One of the most important information is 
isoelectric point (IEP) of the membrane. It corresponds to a specific pH of the solution 
depending on which the net-charge of the membrane becomes zero. The pH value of the feed 
solution has a great impact on membrane surface, therefore changing its selectivity towards 
specific ions (when pH of the feed solution is low – polarity of the membrane is positive, in 
case of high pH, polarity turns negative). Previous studies showed that salt rejection is at its 
minimum close to the IEP of the membrane [62]. It has been observed that when several co-
ions are present in the feed solution, the ones with higher mobility or less charge tend to pass 
through the membrane causing decline in the rejection or in some of the cases negative 
retention. Broadly speaking, salt rejection grows with membrane charge and is minimal at the 
isoelectric point (IEP) – when membrane is not charged at all. IEP influence on a different ions 
is illustrated in Figure 5.

Along the study, LCK350 Hach Lange test kits were used to evaluate total phosphorous and 
orthophosphate concentration in the samples. Each sample had to be diluted with various 
dilution factors, before the analysis to fit in the range. For Ortho P, first adequate amount of 
sample and reagent was added to the kit. Then, cuvette was closed with dosi cap, shaken and 
after 10 min, the result was read on spectrophotometer. For TP, before addition of indicator, 
sulfuric acid was added to the sample and digested for 15 min, rest of the operation was the 
same as in case of ortho P. The procedures are illustrated in the Appendix B 
 COD 
In this project the COD in the samples was measured using Hach Lange kits. The principle of 
the method is the addition of strong oxidant (potassium dichromate) to sulfuric acid with the 
presence of a catalyst. The mass of potassium dichromate that reacts with oxidizable substances 
in the certain time and temperature (15 min in high temperature) is equal to oxygen mass that 
indicates the COD value. The mercury sulphate, silver sulphate are the auxiliary reagents. The 
green coloration of ion Cr3+ after the digestion is measured in spectrophotometer. The method 
was chosen due to its convenience. The cuvette test uses up to 90% less reagents, which are 
pre-dosed which eliminates the error connected to this, analysis takes less time than the standard 
titration method and the results are precise and reliable [83][84][85].  
Most of the measurements were conducted, using LCK 514 kits, for range between 100-2000 
mg/l O2. In order to obtain results within the range, samples were diluted with different dilution 
factors.  First of all the testing kit was shaken, then the adequate amount of sample was added. 
Subsequently the kit was carefully screwed, using the additional cloth to carry the kit due to the 
very rapid exothermic reacting taking place inside. Next, kit was placed in the thermostat set 
for 15 minutes digestion. Afterwards, each kit was shaken again and cooled down, shaken again. 
Then the probes were wiped and put to the spectrophotometer to obtain the results. After 
finished analysis the samples with the kit were stored in special containers for hazardous wastes. 
The procedure scheme can be found in Appendix B

• Introductory part, showing the dynamics of TS, COD, TP and ortho P concentrations

Summing up, during AD, phosphorous attached with the organic  matter is being released to 
the digestate. Its release is most likely caused by hydrolysis of polyphosphate and degradation 
of organic matter. Moreover, partitioning of the P to the solid fraction is linked to the 
development of inorganic phosphorous species or adsorption of these compounds on the surface 
of solids [86]. Since phosphorus stays  in inorganic form after hydrolysis and organic matter 
degradation, it is possible to re-dissolve compounds at low pH. In this study, 25% H2SO4 was 
chosen as dissolution medium. In case of GA1 and GA2, the phosphorous release was the most 
efficient at pH 1.9 and more acidification did not have a significant effect on release or even 
worsen it. The amount of acid needed to reach desired pH, was kept around 2 mass % of the 
acidified sample, however it increased with increasing volume of the sample. In general 
digestate GA2 needed slightly higher amount of acid (Figure 23). The addition of the fish sludge 
(rich in nutrients and organics) in case of digestate GA2 improved the phosphorous release in 
comparison to digestate only based on sewage sludge (GA1). As preliminary acidification 
experiments showed, hydrochloric acid was not a suitable acid source for  studied digestates 
GA1 and GA2.

Conclusions 
Nutrients recycling and depletion of mineral phosphorous are receiving more and more 
attention. Biogas digestate is a valuable source of phosphorus and therefore feasible 
technologies regarding nutrients recovery should be developed and implemented. 
In this project two types of digestates, namely sewage sludge digestate (GA1) and sewage 
sludge enriched with fish sludge digestate (GA2) were investigated. The preliminary chemical 
analysis of the raw digestates in terms of TS, COD, TP and ortho P showed that addition of the 
fish sludge has a considerable influence on the digestate parameters. Both TP and ortho P values 
were elevated, which indicated that the treatment aiming in P recovery could be more promising 
for digestate GA2. The first step of the treatment – centrifugation, significantly lowered the TS 
content in both GA1 and GA2 digestate. The operation was more efficient for the digestate 
GA2. The centrifugation experiments showed as well that the vast majority of the phosphorous 
in digestate remains in particulate form, leaving the liquid fraction of the digestate with very 
little concentration of dissolved P. As a solution to this problem, acidification step prior to 
centrifugation was introduced. Based on literature, two dissolution media were tested: 8% HCl 
and 25% H2SO4. The first one had an adverse effect on the tested digestates, however the second 
one turned out to have a great impact on the phosphorous release. The pH of value 1.9 was 
proven to be the most optimal for the digestates tested particularly in this study. After 24h 
period of dissolution and subsequent centrifugation, the TP concentration in a liquid fraction in 
comparison to the samples that were subjected just to centrifugation raised from 96 mg/l to 473 
mg/l in GA1 and from 73.8 mg/l to 720 mg/l in GA2. What is worth mentioning, the remaining 
solids from acidified digestates were more easily separated by centrifugation than in case of the 
raw samples. This however, left the liquid fraction with considerable amount of dissolved solids 
which could pose a problem in further processing. Next, not acidified samples (CGA1 and 
CGA2) and acidified ones (CAGA1 and CAGA2) were forwarded to more detailed solid 
analysis. The biggest difference between not acidified digestates CGA1 and CGA2 was the 
occurrence of total dissolved solids (TSS) which in case of CGA2 was only 0.069 g/l and for 
GA1 0.7 g/l. In terms of acidified samples, analysis has confirmed that most of the solids 
occurred in dissolved form and was equal to 17.02 g/l for CAGA1 and 18.14 for CAGA2. The 
filtration through 11 μm, 5μm (fine particle filtration) and 3μm (MF) filters were conducted in 
dead-end configuration mode and membrane rejection in terms of TS, COD, TP and ortho P 
were rather poor for all the digestates. Only the COD levels were considerably lowered in case 
of all the digestates, but other parameter were barely changed with exception of CGA1 for 
which the TP and ortho P rejection reached over 50%. This result however was more of a

disadvantage, since the precious phosphorous was removed from the digestate, and because the 
operation was held in dead-end mode, no concentrated solution was collected. What also needs 
to be noted, membranes were clogging were fast due to the high organic concentration in all of 
the digestate types. The UF experiments utilized cross-flow flat sheet unit. Not acidified 
digestate CGA2 was characterized by the highest average flux (6.98 LMH), over 90% rejection 
for COD, TP and ortho P and 50% rejection of TS. The results were impressing, however the 
initial value of TP was only 73 mg/l and the concentrate consisted 75 mg/l, which was not a. 
Performance of the acidified CAGA2, was also very good. The average permeate flux equaled 
to 4.98 LMH, and TP and ortho P concentrations in the concentrate were: 985 and 955 mg/l 
respectively. The digestates based on sewage sludge: CGA1 and CAGA1, showed worse 
performance in terms of flux 3.72 and 3.17 as well as rejection. For CAGA1 the negative TP 
rejection occurred, which might have been caused by specific chemistry and low pH of that 
solution. The aim of the study was to not only obtain the phosphorous rich fraction but also 
provide discharchable water. Unfortunately, none of the permeates after UF was fulfilling 
discharge criteria. It was also found that NF is not a suitable treatment for the digestates. The 
rejection were negative in almost all of the cases. While processing CGA1 and CGA2, 
membranes were permeable but not selective, probably due to interactions of ions in the 
solution with the membrane surface. The complete chemistry of solutions was not studied, 
however it is predicted that Donnan effect (typical for polymeric membranes with sulfuric 
groups) largely contributed to the uneven distribution of the ions which affected the rejection. 
In case of acidified samples it was impossible to carry out the process, which can be explained 
by combination of Donnan effect, high salinity and low pH of the solution (which could be 
close to IEP, which negatively affects NF membrane efficiency [79]. 
One of the concern regarding origin of sewage sludge was high concentration of heavy metals, 
which can pose problems in utilization of digestate as fertilizer. The analysis showed that the 
acidification step affected the occurrence of metals in the samples, however the changes were 
not dramatical and in most of the cases the concentration stayed in the same range as for not 
acidified ones. Moreover,  fish enriched digestate GA2 after centrifugation could be a suitable 
for on filed application in terms of heavy metal content, however as mentioned earlier its 
fertilizing potential (concentration of phosphorus) was rather low. In other cases the metal 
content exceeded the safe range. Surprisingly, the samples after UF showed very high 
concentration of Cu and Zn. In order to cope with metal present in  the digestates, in the future 
research a participation method could be introduce to eliminate them from the samples that 
have a big fertilizer potential (CAGA1, CAGA2, CAGA2 after UF,).

Summing up, it was found that digestate based on sewage and fish sludge (GA2) was 
performing better in all of the stages of treatment. Moreover, it was proven that acidification 
step allowed to significantly increase the levels of dissolved phosphorous in the liquid fraction 
after centrifugation, elevating the fertilizer potential of the samples. The sequential filtration 
involving MF, UF and NF was not the most feasible treatment for phosphorous recovery, 
however UF was a promising pretreatment step, which could be used for further research.

Future work 
Given that last stage of the membrane filtration (NF) did not gave a satisfactory results, it could 
be taken into consideration to focus on optimization of NF process or utilization of a different 
membrane process such as RO [52] or combination of FO and MD [57]. Moreover, obtained 
liquid fraction could be further processed for metal removal and chemical precipitation of 
phosphorous as a magnesium ammonium phosphate hydrate (MAP or struvite). The metal 
removal could be conducted by incineration of a digested sludge, implementation of ceramic 
UF membranes [91] or by using photocatalytic methods [92]. Another aspect could be a careful 
economic evaluation of the technology. It was demonstrated that the acidification step is very 
effective in terms of phosphorous release from particulate form in the digestate, however 
obtaining MAP requires elevating pH to around 8.5 by using NH4Cl and Mg Cl2 [20]. Taking 
into consideration the amount of steps and drastic changes in terms of pH, financial assessment 
of this labor-intensive technology should be assessed.  Furthermore, the project focuses only 
on the phosphorous recovery, whilst digestate contains other valuable nutrients like nitrogen or 
potassium. Their accessibility and recovery potential could be studied.
