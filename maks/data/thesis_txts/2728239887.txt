Abstract
This study delved into an intricate analysis of water surface elevation dynamics
within the lower Chao Phraya River Region, employing a combination of hydraulic
models and ICESat-2 data. The application of ICESat-2 measurements facilitated
the estimation of water surface elevation and slope along a designated stretch of the
river. Integrating ICESat-2 data, hydraulic models were improved and validated.
The construction of SQ and SFQ rating curves elucidate the interrelation between
river stage, fall, and discharge, especially in the context of backwater effects. The
investigation further delved into the interplay between river discharge and tides, un-
raveling both water surface elevation dynamics and the propagation of tide waves
under the influence of damping effects.
The outcomes of this research hold im-
plications for predictive modeling, environmental stewardship, and the sustainable
management of estuarine ecosystems.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
An estuary, categorized as a partially enclosed coastal water body, retains its brackish quality
due to its linkage with the open sea and interaction with rivers or streams. This region serves as
a transitional zone between the river and maritime ecosystems [1]. A key feature of estuaries lies
in blending freshwater and saltwater, establishing a unique ecological habitat with distinctive
characteristics. Mixing occurs at the freshwater-saltwater boundary, influenced by tides, winds,
and internal waves [2].
Tides hold significant importance in estuarine hydrology. The predominant driving forces be-
hind tidal water movements in most estuaries are the Moon’s and Sun’s gravitational effects
on the ocean, resulting in periodic rise and fall of the sea level. The intricacies of tidal dy-
namics reflect the combined influences of coastal processes and human activities on essential
aspects of estuarine systems, including salt-fresh water blending, circulation patterns, sediment
deposition, nutrient transport, and primary productivity [3] [4]. Thus, understanding tides be-
comes crucial for comprehending the broader ecological dynamics within estuarine environments.
The dominant mechanisms governing salt transport in estuaries arise from the combined influ-
ence of river discharge and tides [5]. The interaction between rivers and tides within estuaries
carries great environmental and ecological importance. Understanding tidal wave propagation
and estuarine tidal properties is crucial for predicting tides, managing navigation channels, and
conserving estuarine ecosystems. Tidal waves are influenced by shallow water friction, basin
topography, and river discharge as they propagate from oceans to estuaries [6] [7] [8]. This
knowledge aids in comprehending estuarine dynamics, facilitating effective conservation and
management strategies for these vital transitional zones where freshwater meets seawater.
Hydraulic model can be used to simulate the water behavior in estuarine areas. Alebregtse et
al. [9] developed an idealized model to analyze tidal propagation and net water transport divi-
sion in an estuarine network. The model, governed by cross-sectionally averaged shallow water
equations, incorporates tide and river discharge as boundary conditions. Other state-of-the-art
numerical modeling tools can also be used in hydraulic modeling. Guo et al. [10] developed a
1-D numerical model using Delft3D to study the morphodynamic development of a 560 km long
estuary resembling the Yangtze River Estuary. They investigated the effects of various combined

tidal constituents and river discharges on estuarine morphodynamics in long-term (millennial)
simulations, focusing on multiple tidal asymmetries and river-tide interactions. In addition, Hu

However, constructing a robust hydraulics model often requires abundant in-situ data, which
may not always be readily available. To address this limitation, satellite earth observation has
emerged as an alternative method for monitoring surface water resources since the 1970s. The pi-
oneering Landsat-1, launched in 1972, impressively transmitted over 100,000 images, effectively
covering more than 75% of the Earth’s surface [12]. Subsequent years witnessed the deployment
of numerous satellites, and satellite altimetry has been widely used in in-land water monitoring.
Fundamentally, satellite altimeters measure the two-way travel time of an electromagnetic pulse
between the water surface and the antenna, translating it into distance. This innovative tech-
nique overcomes traditional water level stations’ limitations, including limited spatial coverage,
high construction and maintenance cost, inaccuracy due to local conditions, and difficulty in
remote areas. Table 1 shows the outline of characteristics of some satellite altimetry missions

1.2
Research objectives
The objectives of this project are stated as follows:
• Analyze hydrodynamics pattern: Investigate and characterize the hydrodynamics pattern
in the area of interest, including water surface elevation and water surface slope, river
discharge, and tidal amplitude variations, and understand their interaction.
• Evaluate ICESat-2 and other satellite altimetry missions: Assess the performance and
capabilities of ICESat-2 and other new satellite altimetry missions in monitoring surface
water resources.
• Understand the contributions of Earth observations to hydrodynamic modeling: Inves-
tigate how data from ICESat-2 can contribute to improving and refining hydrodynamic
models, enhancing predictions of water surface elevations in the area of interest.

Case studies and data sets
2.1
Study area
Thailand has 22 river basins with 254 sub-basins, relying heavily on rainwater as a vital water
source [19]. Among its river systems, the Chao Phraya River and the Mekong are together
serving as lifelines for Thailand’s agricultural economy. This project specifically focuses on the
Chao Phraya River, which dominates the largest river basin in the country and represents one
of the most significant agricultural and industrial areas in Southeastern Asia. Flowing through
Bangkok and ultimately reaching the Gulf of Thailand, the river spans a total length of approx-
imately 372 kilometers [20].
Thailand is a tropical country. In relation to the climate conditions in Thailand, the Thai Me-
teorological Department (TMD) [21] classifies the country’s climate into three distinct seasons:
rainy, winter, and summer. The Rainy Season, also known as the Southwest Monsoon Sea-
son, usually spans from mid-May to mid-October. During this period, the Southwest Monsoon
pattern prevails over the central and northern regions, leading to peak precipitation levels in
August and September. It occasionally causes inflow runoff to exceed the upstream reservoir
storage capacity and results in flood events and overflow [22]. In the period of November to
April, known as the dry season, the climatic conditions are shaped by the northeast monsoon
from China and tropical storms from the South China Sea [19].
The water level of the Chao Phraya River is regulated by the Chao Phraya Dam. Historical
hydrological data from the Royal Irrigation Department (RID) [23] reveals that the average
release from the Chao Phraya Dam between 2007 and 2021 is approximately 400 m3/s, while
the highest recorded release reaches 3721 m3/s. This dam plays a crucial role in regulating the
discharge of the river, and its operation involves the diversion of irrigation water to both the
left and right banks of the river [22].
For this study, a specific segment located approximately 123 kilometers from the river’s outlet
was chosen for research, encompassing chainage points from 256,672 meters to 380,140 meters.
The location of the study area and how chainage points were assigned are shown in Figure 1.

2.2
Satellite altimetry data: ICESat-2
ICESat-2, known as the Ice, Cloud, and Land Elevation Satellite-2, measures the height of a
changing Earth with a 91-day exact repeat orbit. It was launched on September 15, 2018, with
a staggering rate of 10,000 laser pulses per second. Equipped with a photon-counting laser al-
timeter, this satellite empowers scientists to obtain highly detailed elevation measurements of
ice sheets, glaciers, sea ice, and various other land features [17].
The NSIDC DAAC ICESat-2 data collection includes data products derived from the Advanced
Topographic Laser Altimeter System (ATLAS) instrument aboard the ICESat-2. ATLAS em-
ploys a unique configuration of a single laser divided into six beams strategically arranged in
three pairs. This design facilitates precise assessments of Earth’s surface slope, enabling sci-
entists to acquire detailed and accurate measurements across various terrains [24]. Figure 2
depicts all the IceSat-2 tracks available over the area of interest, as well as one example of how
these parallel beams appear. Among the 6 beams, gt1l, gt2l, and gt3l are strong beams, while

2.2.1
ATL03
The ATL03 data product from ICESat-2 is a vital intermediary between lower and higher-level
products.
It provides time, latitude, longitude, and height information for each downlinked
photon, which acts as a comprehensive source of photon data for subsequent analyses, enabling
the generation of surface-specific products such as land ice height and sea ice freeboard. ATL03
includes surface masks and photon event classifications and applies geophysical corrections to
enhance accuracy. Additionally, it supplies spacecraft and instrument information required by
higher-level data products. Its role is crucial in facilitating streamlined data processing and
serving as a unified source for further scientific investigations [25].
2.2.2
ATL08
The ATL08 data product derived from ICESat-2 measurements provides estimates of terrain
heights, canopy heights, canopy cover, and other descriptive parameters at fine spatial scales in
7
==PAGE:13==
Technical University of Denmark
the along-track direction. A fixed segment size of 100 m was chosen to provide continuity of data
parameters on the ATL08 data product. Height estimates from ATL08 can be compared with
other geodetic data and serve as input for higher-level products like ATL13 (inland water-related
heights) and ATL18 (terrain and canopy feature maps) [26].
2.2.3
ATL13
The ATL13 algorithm processes global inland water body height products and associated georef-
erenced photons from ATL03. The data products include mean height, significant wave height,
slope, attenuation coefficient, and bottom anomaly. ATL13 data products are reported for each
beam in the along-track direction, with variable short segment(around 75-100 signal photons)
lengths due to water and meteorological conditions. The algorithm accounts for irregular water
body shapes, treating separate beam crossings as independent analyses. Long segment lengths
are used for height and attenuation estimation. Release 5 data products cover global inland
lakes larger than 0.10 km2 with sufficient recorded signal photons from ICESat-2 [27].

Methodology
3.1
Process overview
The project workflow is illustrated in Figure 7. Firstly, hydraulic models were developed for
the lower Chao Phraya River, and their validity was ensured through validation with data from
ICESat-2 and in-situ measurements. Improvements were then implemented to refine the model’s
accuracy.
Using the model outputs, rating curves were constructed to explore the complex
relationships among water surface elevation, water surface slope, and discharge along the river.
Subsequently, tidal analysis was performed to investigate the tidal patterns in southern Thailand.
Finally, a thorough investigation was carried out to understand the interaction between river
discharge and tides, providing valuable insights into tides wave propagation processes and the
water surface elevation dynamics in this estuarine area.

3.2.1.1
ATL13 data processing
In this study, ATL13 data were employed to estimate water surface elevation and slope. Initially,
granules were processed to extract information, including longitude, latitude, surface height, and
time. To ensure data accuracy and relevance, only measurements within a 1.5-kilometer distance
from the river’s centerline and with water occurrence probability higher than 95% were retained.
Subsequently, outlier values were effectively removed using the Hampel filter. The assignment of
chainage points followed the same convention as the hydraulic model for consistency. Afterward,
orthometric height calculations were performed, referencing the TGM2017 geoid model, using
Equation 1 to establish accurate measurements of vertical elevation.
H = h −N
(1)
Where H is orthometric height, h is ellipsoid height, and N is geoid height.
To assign one surface height value for one satellite crossing, DBSCAN clustering was performed
to group the data automatically based on their location. The average surface height and geoinfor-
mation values were computed for each cluster. All the clustering results were checked manually
to ensure accuracy. Finally, the water surface slope was calculated with the help of linear re-
gression using distance and water surface elevation as input. Here, the water surface slope was
assumed to be constant along the river.
3.2.1.2
ATL03 data processing
ATL03 data was processed using the same method as ATL13 data. It served as a valuable refer-
ence in this study, primarily employed to corroborate the findings derived from ATL13 data. As
ATL03 constituted a lower-level data product, it played a crucial role in cross-validation. When
the ATL13 data exhibited peculiar or anomalous patterns, a comparison with the correspond-

3.2.2
In-situ data processing
The research period was defined as January 18th, 2019, to October 12th, 2022, aiming to identify
the optimal satellite path. Consequently, only in-situ data falling within this timeframe was
retained for analysis.
Moreover, to ensure consistency in the dataset, all the measurement
data were converted to Coordinated Universal Time (UTC). Furthermore, all data points were
transformed into hourly averages to facilitate a consistent and standardized dataset. Rigorous
data cleaning procedures were implemented, involving manual removal of implausible values.
Notably, the tides data exhibited instances of NAN values, which were subsequently filled with
predicted values to enhance the performance and accuracy of the model.
3.3
Hydraulics model setup and validation
3.3.1
Hydraulics model setup
Hydraulic modeling was conducted using MIKE Hydro River to simulate water surface elevation,
water surface slope, and discharge. For the time period spanning from 2018 to 2020, a 1D model
was employed. However, for the year 2021, to enhance flood prediction capabilities, a combined
1D/2D model approach was utilized. This technique helps facilitate a better understanding of
the complex hydraulic processes occurring during flood events in the study area. For both the
1D and the combined 1D/2D models, upper boundaries were set as the dam release from Chao
Phraya dam and RAMA XI dam, while downstream boundaries were set as sea water level. The
horizontal plan of the hydraulic model was shown in Figure 9, showing that the Chao Phraya
River network exhibits a complex configuration featuring shortcuts. Furthermore, as the model
outputs are in Bangkok time, it was essential to maintain dataset consistency by converting
them to UTC.

3.4
Rating curves construction
In hydrology, rating curves represent the hydraulic behavior of a channel section or reach and
are the key to providing sequential estimates of discharge for hydrology and water resources
investigations [30]. Two types of rating curves were used in this report. The first one included
stage (water surface elevation) and discharge (SQ rating curves), and the second one included
stage, fall (water surface slope), and discharge (SFQ rating curves).
3.4.1
SQ rating curves
In the context of deriving rating curves, a commonly adopted approach involves employing a
single power function derived from the Manning equation. This preference is attributed to the
limited count of parameters involved. The subsequent section outlines the conversion process

Although SQ rating curves are commonly used worldwide at gauging stations, their validity

5
Future work
This chapter outlines potential avenues for future research and enhancements to the current
study’s methodologies. The following points present key areas of focus for further exploration:
1. Hydraulic models improvement
The primary concern within this study revolves around the datum problem, which is emerging as
a pivotal issue. Notably, there lacks explicit documentation confirming that the input data uti-
lized for the hydraulic models relative to TGM2017. Moreover, the comparative analysis reveals
a consistent trend wherein the simulated outcomes are consistently lower than the ICESat-2
observations. This phenomenon potentially suggests that the discrepancy could be attributed
to datum inconsistencies. Addressing this significant challenge holds the potential to enhance
the accuracy of water surface elevation estimations substantially.
Furthermore, the incorporation of supplementary cross-sectional data obtained from both in-
situ measurements and satellite resources can be used to improve the performance of hydraulic
modeling. The inclusion of as much as possible cross-sectional data is important in accurately
delineating river topography and thus reinforcing the hydraulic modeling process. Particularly
in regions with limited in-situ measurement availability, there exists an opportunity to use Earth
observational data to simulate and establish additional cross-sections, subsequently updating the
hydraulic model.
2. Hydrodynamic modeling enhancements
In this project, the construction of both 1D and combined 1/2D hydraulic models has been
accomplished. To further enrich the scope, an extension of this current study into the realm of
3D hydrodynamic modeling is proposed. This advanced approach would entail the integration
of vertical flow and water surface dynamics variations, offering a more comprehensive grasp of
river dynamics and tidal processes [42] [43]. Additionally, a future exploration involving sedi-
ment transport modeling can be applied. By delving into the intricate interplay between water
surface elevation dynamics and sediment migration within the river framework, a profound com-
prehension of estuarine evolution can be gleaned.
3. Tidal constituent analysis
36
==PAGE:42==
Technical University of Denmark
In this study, a basic harmonic analysis was performed to investigate the interplay between river
discharge and tidal effects. However, different tidal constituents exhibit varied behaviors in the
propagation of tidal waves, leading to diverse interactions with river discharge [10]. Moreover,
the interactions among these different tidal constituents can give rise to compounded overtides,
contributing to tidal asymmetry and introducing complexity to the study of tidal river behav-
ior [7]. Thus, a thorough examination of tidal constituents becomes pivotal, offering deeper
insights into their impacts on the dynamics of water surface elevation. To achieve a more com-
prehensive understanding of estuarine hydrodynamics, an expanded scope of tidal components
should be incorporated.
4. Study area extension
The focus of this study was a river segment approximately 120 kilometers inland from the sea.
However, the analysis revealed that the majority of the study area is dominated by tidal in-
fluence, making it challenging to distinguish the fluvially-controlled portion of the river. Thus
expanding the study upstream would provide a more comprehensive insight into the dynamics
between river processes and tidal influences.
5. Additional data supplement
In this study, the establishment of cross-sections heavily relied on the utilization of water masks.
Only satellite data points with water occurrence probability exceeding 95% were retained. This
approach was adopted due to the close proximity of water surface elevation to land elevation
in the study area. There are instances when water surface elevation surpasses land levels, ren-
dering the differentiation between river bed and water segment challenging without the aid of
water masks. Given the significance of accurate water masks, employing multiple masks and
employing cross-validation to identify water bodies could enhance accuracy.
Furthermore, in order to accurately quantify the proportion of tidal discharge in relation to
river discharge, it would be better to acquire in-situ data concerning tidal wave velocities and
bathymetry information at the river mouth.
For example, Leonardi et al. [44] deployed an
Aquadopp Nortek Acoustic Doppler Current Meter (ADCP) at the mouth of a distributary
channel to measure both horizontal and vertical water velocities. These data were then used to
investigate the combined effect of river hydrograph and micro-tides on the hydrodynamics of a
delta distributary.

6. Integration of other satellite data
Additional satellite data sources can also be incorporated into this study in the future. For
instance, the Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) mission [45], launched on December
16, 2022, aims to achieve several objectives. These include conducting the first inaugural global
survey of Earth’s surface water, capturing fine details of ocean surface topography, and tracking
terrestrial surface water bodies change. Functioning as a swath-based Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR) altimetry mission, its unique capability to penetrate cloud cover enhances the acquisition
of abundant data suitable for hydraulic modeling purposes.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this project delved into the analysis of intricate water surface elevation dynamics
in the lower Chao Phraya River Region using hydraulic models and ICESat-2 data. Through
a meticulous analysis of water surface elevation, water surface slope, river discharge, tidal am-
plitude, and phase lag variations, valuable insights were gained into the interplay between river
discharge and tidal processes.
Firstly, the study highlights the utility of remote sensing data, exemplified by the effective uti-
lization of ICESat-2 measurements. In the first part of the project, the application of integrating
ICESat-2 satellite data within the framework of hydrodynamic modeling was investigated. The
altimetry measurements were used to estimate water surface elevation and slope in a stretch of
the Chao Phraya River. A comprehensive evaluation of the hydraulic model’s performance was
undertaken through the utilization of ICESat-2 data. This analysis yielded an RMSE value of
0.3 meters concerning water surface elevation and 0.4 cm/km concerning water surface slope,
effectively corroborating the robustness and credibility of both the hydraulic model and the
ICESat-2 observations. The integration of satellite observations provided a comprehensive view
of water surface elevation dynamics, which is really important for areas that lack in-situ monitor-
ing stations. Furthermore, this project shows that Earth observational data holds the potential
to enhance the efficacy of hydraulic models and facilitate their validation.
Secondly, the construction and examination of SQ and SFQ rating curves showcased the rela-
tionship between river stage, fall, and discharge, particularly in the context of backwater effects.
In addition, in the downstream region, unique phenomena arise where water surface slope and
river discharge display contrasting behaviors. Further investigation reveals intriguing correla-
tions with tidal patterns. These observations illuminate the complex interplay of water stage,
discharge, and slope, enriching our understanding of hydrodynamics and interrelated processes
in the lower Chao Phraya River. These findings also shed light on the need for accurate rating
curve development to ensure precise discharge estimations in estuarine environments.
Finally, the project explored the interplay between tides and river discharge, revealing the im-
pact of bottom friction brought by river discharge on tidal hydrodynamics. The ratio of riverine
flow to tidal flow determines whether the outward or inward river current gains dominance,
thereby delineating the sections primarily governed by tides and those influenced by riverine

flow. Furthermore, the investigation into phase lag, tidal amplitude, and damping number vari-
ations offered novel insights into the damping effect in the process of tidal wave propagation.
The investigation unveiled the profound impact of river discharge on tidal behavior, demonstrat-
ing the significance of considering this factor in hydrodynamic modeling.
In essence, this study’s comprehensive exploration of tidal dynamics, river discharge direction
change, and water surface elevation variations contribute to a deeper understanding of estu-
arine hydrodynamics. The findings hold implications for predictive modeling, environmental
management, and sustainable riverine ecosystems. As advancements in remote sensing tech-
nology continue, the research underscores the potential for enhanced monitoring and predictive
capabilities, fostering a more comprehensive understanding of complex estuarine systems.
