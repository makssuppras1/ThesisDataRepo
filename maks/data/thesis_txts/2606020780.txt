Introduction
The purpose of this project is to provide a detailed description of bifurcations
of geodesics in Riemannian manifolds (M, g) where the manifold M always
is assumed to be smooth and real in Riemannian geometry. Also, the metric
g in such real and smooth manifolds M is deﬁned by an inner product on
each tangent space TpM that depends smoothly on some point p ∈M in a
Riemannian setting. We shall ﬁrst show in this thesis that there always exists
at least one geodesic connecting two distinct points p, q ∈M in all connected
and complete Riemannian manifolds (M, g). In general, there exist for ’almost’
all pairs of distinct points (p, q) a unique geodesic connecting those points, but
this is not always true. For instance, there may exist multiple geodesics starting
from some point p that may connect another point q from a set of points along
some curve in some Riemannian manifolds (M, g). This leads to the following
deﬁnition of a cut locus of some point p
Deﬁnition 1.1. Suppose (M, g) is some Riemannian manifold. Then the cut
locus of a point p is the set of all points that are connected to p by more than
one minimising geodesic.
We emphasise the importance that the geodesic minimising, because not
all geodesics connecting two distinct points p and q are necessarily minimising
curves between two. Of course, We shall soon see that a curve being a geodesic
is a necessary condition to minimise the path connecting two distinct points
p and q, but this is not a suﬃcient condition. Also, we should also add that
determining the cut locus of some point is in general very diﬃcult, thus we shall
restrain our attention to surfaces in R3 whenever appropriate.
When the determination of the cut locus of a point p on the surface in ques-
tion is completed we shall as well examine the possible structural bifurcations
of the cut locus of p as you vary parameters involved in the parameterisation of
the surface in question. Since the determination of the cut locus of some point
p is carried out on surfaces, thus we compute the structural bifurcations.
We have alluded to the fact that a Riemannian manifold is a (real) smooth
manifold M that is endowed with a metric g. In fact, it turns out that all
smooth manifolds may be equipped with a metric in a Riemannian setting which
we formulate as a theorem as follows
Theorem 1. Any smooth and real manifold M may be endowed with a metric
g in a Riemannian setting.
We omit the proof of this result since it is somewhat cumbersome to prove
without in-depth knowledge of topology, and due to the fact that the important
message is that any smooth and real manifold may be equipped with a metric
in Riemannian geometry.

in a Riemannian metric. We can quite easily derive length formulas for comput-
ing lengths of curves in any arbitrary (Riemannian) manifold M of dimension
N ∈N, but due to clumsy and large expressions that is too much a hassle.

Riemannian setting.
The length formula (2) with its corresponding metric (3) provides the length
of curves between two distinct points p and q. In general, the length of some
chosen curve γ is rarely minimised by choosing γ randomly. Neither does it
tell us what curve γ to choose in order to minimise the distance between p and
q. Therefore, we must resort to some tool which can achieve this goal which is
exactly where variational calculus comes to our rescue. The ﬁrst variation of
calculus of variation seeks to make sure the curve γ chosen is the ’straightest’
curve segment between p and q that are all characterised by having geodesic
curvature zero. Such ’straightest’ curves are known as geodesics. The second
variation seeks to assure that the geodesic obtained is minimised. Locally, all

geodesics are (of course) minimising as we soon shall prove, but this is generally
not true globally. Therefore, the second variation is indeed very important for
computing the shortest possible path from two points in Riemannian manifolds.
Especially, by means of second variation we prove that no geodesic segments
γ : [p, q] −→S with interior conjugate points along it can possibly be minimising
but the second variation does not always assure that some chosen geodesic γ is
minimised due to the eﬀect I call ’cylinder eﬀect’. The combinations of these
eﬀects is one of the main reasons that determining the cut locus of some point
p typically is very diﬃcult.

Remark 1.1. It is important to note that geodesics only are guaranteed to be
minimising locally. Globally, all geodesics are still straightest curves, but not all
geodesics are necessarily minimising. To be precise, we say that a curve being
a geodesic is a necessary condition to be a minimising geodesic between two
distinct points p and q. We shall later see, for example, that all geodesics on
2-spheres follow segments of a great circle. Assuming that two distinct points p
and q are not antipodal there exist exactly two geodesics connecting those points,
but one can show that only one of these geodesics is a minimising geodesics.
Remark 1.2. We could easily extend the length formulas to any dimension
N ∈N, however, in this thesis we shall only treat examples of lengths of curves
on surfaces in R3 which thus makes it appropriate to simplify the length expres-
sions.

1.5
Bifurcations of geodesics
As the name of the title of this thesis clearly indicates we should make sure how
exactly we understand bifurcations of geodesics in Riemannian manifolds. The
very concept of bifurcation loosely means dividing something into two branches,
but what exactly do we mean by such two branches? The type of bifurcation we
mainly shall treat in this thesis is the shift from a unique geodesic to multiple
geodesics on cut loci and back to a unique geodesic with a diﬀerent direction
than the ﬁrst one when going through a point on the cut locus, or when changing
the surface and thence the metric. First, we state precisely what is we mean by
the cut locus of a point
Deﬁnition 1.2. A cut locus of some point p in a Riemannian manifold (M, g)
is another distinct point q for which there exist multiple (minimising) geodesics
connecting points p and q.
Second, we state precisely what may happen the cut locus of some point p
as you move it somewhere else on the smooth manifold M or as you vary some
parameters of the metric g.
Deﬁnition 1.3. Suppose (M, g) is a Riemannian manifold and p is some point
on the manifold M. A bifurcation of a cut locus of some point p may occur
if the set of all points that constitute the cut locus of p will be distributed
’signiﬁcantly’. If this bifurcation occurs by moving the point p continuously
somewhere else in the smooth manifold M we say that a point bifurcation takes
place.
In case this bifurcation occurs by varying a parameter of the metric
continuously we say that a surface bifurcation takes place.

Closed geodesics on surfaces in R3 - Contributors to
Cut(p)
Closed geodesics are very interesting for the analysis of cut loci of points on
surfaces in R3. First, we provide a deﬁnition of a closed geodesic.
Deﬁnition 2.3. A closed geodesic is a geodesic γ(s) for which there exists a
δ > 0 such that γ(s + δ) = γ(s) for all s ∈I.
Since we now clearly understand the notion of a closed geodesic we proceed with
formulating an important result that always holds for true for any surface
Theorem 15. Consider some point p ∈S on a two dimensional surface in R3
suppose that there exists a closed geodesic emanating from this point. Then the
cut locus of point p, Cut(p), contains at least one point.
Proof. Imagine any closed geodesic emanated from point p, and assume that
this geodesic has travelled a distance of L when it returns to p. Then one cut
locus of p must be the point where then geodesic has travelled a distance of
exactly L/2. Hence, the cut locus of point p contains at least one point if we
assume that there exists a closed geodesic emanated from p.
It is important to notice that determining contributions to Cut(p) is par-
ticularly easy for simple closed closed whereas it it not very easy to determine
Cut(p) if the closed geodesic in question is not simple. We only thing we are
guaranteed for nonsimple closed geodesics is that they contribute Cut(p).
We provide a brief example of some elementary closed geodesics on simple
surfaces in R3
Example 2.1. We mention without proof that one can prove the following facts
by the Clairaut theorem for surfaces of revolution (combined with spherical
symmetry for 2-spheres)
1. All meridians on tori of revolution are (simple) closed geodesics.
2. All parallels on cylinders of revolution are (simple) closed geodesics.
3. All geodesics on 2-spheres are (simple) closed geodesics.

Theorem 20. Suppose S is a suﬃciently smooth and complete surface of revo-

this theorem.

9.1
The three-body problem
We brieﬂy introduce the three-body problem which is a problem that can be
traced back to 1687 with Isaac Newton when he discovered the governing equa-
tions of motion of celestial bodies involving two bodies. The three-body prob-
lem was the natural next step after having solved the two-body problem, known
as the Kepler problem named after Johannes Kepler, who arguably was the
ﬁrst to understand it, which Isaac Newton later managed to explain in detail.
However, the three-body problem turned out to be way more complicated to
solve compared to the Kepler problem. According to Newtonian mechanics the
orbits of two interacting bodies are conic sections, either periodic orbits like el-
lipses/circles or non-periodic orbits such as parabolas/hyperbolas. This is more
or less the entire story of the Kepler problem. However, when you add a third
body to the problem then it all gets stunningly diﬃcult.
In the three-body problem we have a large variety of solutions, albeit only
a few analytic solutions in specialised cases. The most famous specialised three
body problems are probably the ones due to Leonard Euler and Joseph La-
grange whose solutions give rise to the famous ﬁve Lagrangian points which are
stable periodic orbits. In the days of Euler and Lagrange, these solutions were
practically not very useful, but they are very important today as we have placed
several artiﬁcial satellites orbiting at the Lagrangian points, especially the ﬁrst
and second one.
We know that by 2019 that there exist more than 2000 diﬀerent types of
collisionless periodic orbits for the three body problem which have been deter-
mined by advanced numerical methods, see (6). However, most of them that
exist show strong signs of chaotic behaviour, and it turns out that there is a
signiﬁcant probability that the three-body system will disintegrate into a single
escaping star and then leave behind a stable binary system, see (7).
The Kepler problem and the three-body problem are two examples where
bifurcations of geodesics are present as either periodic orbits or aperiodic or-
bits depending on some parameter, for example the eccentricity in the Kepler
problem. In the three-body problem we have just stated important results and
progress for this problem without examining the exact nature of the bifurcations
of geodesics in the three-body problem, because it is way beyond the scope of
this thesis, and due to the fact that it is worth mentioning the possible study
of bifurcations of geodesics in three-body systems.
