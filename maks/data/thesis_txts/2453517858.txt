Abstract
NewSpace has made it possible to develop a small but complex satellite mission in a short
time frame. This allows universities, and students, to participate in space development
in the form of CubeSat programs.
This master thesis seeks to propose a student-run Earth Observation (eo) mission un-
der the dtusat initiative. Developing the mission from the initial concept phase through
to the Preliminary Requirements Review (prr).
Systems engineering forms the basis for the methods used to develop the CubeSat.
An iterative approach was utilized for designing the requirements. The requirements are
defined based on simulations and analyses of individual subsystems. Furthermore, risk
assessment has been performed for both the spacecraft and the mission.
The proposed mission will measure the ice velocities at Jakobshavn Isbræ, at least
once per week. Measuring the change in ice velocities can help estimate the impact and
effects of climate change.
The analysis deems it possible to measure ice velocities once a day at Jakobshavn
Isbræ, using a Synthetic Aperture Radar (sar) enabled CubeSat, with a mass of 8 kg.
Additionally, 12 images can be captured allowing for secondary objectives.
This thesis includes the preliminary work for the dtusat-4 concept. The spacecraft
proposed is detailed to the subsystem level, although lacking analyses within certain
engineering disciplines.
CubeSat sized spacecrafts are now mature enough to allow for complex eo missions,
albeit specialized compared to traditional missions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Developing spacecrafts has traditionally been reserved for space agencies and large com-
munication corporations. This is changing with the rise of NewSpace. The space sector
has traditionally produced large spacecraft, with development spreading over decades to
go from idea to operations.
NewSpace is about decreasing the cost while increasing the development pace. The
use of Commercial Off-The-Shelf (cots) and smaller spacecraft, made feasible thanks
to the miniaturization of electronics, are among some of the tools used by NewSpace.
The price of launching spacecraft to orbit has likewise decreased with companies such
as Space-X entering the market with their falcon rocket.
The reduced barrier to entry has attracted new actors to the space industry, such as
Earth Observation (eo) companies like Capella Space and iceye. Both are examples of
companies selling radar images which can be delivered within a matter of hours, once
their constellations are fully operational. Their spacecrafts’ are significantly smaller than
the current imaging radar eo spacecraft like Sentinel-1 and RadarSat-2. They are what
is known as SmallSats, satellites with a mass below 100 kg.
Accessing space has become easier than ever before, which can be seen by the amount
of objects launched into space. The Ørstad sattelitte was launched back in 1999 with the
North American Aerospace Defense Command (norad) catalog number 25 635. While
Atmosphere-Space Interactions Monitor (asim) was launched onboard the SpaceX crs-
14, in 2018, which has number 43 267. Space is getting crowded.
The first nanosatellites, satellites with a weight under 10 kg were launched back in
1998 [1]. The CubeSat format was then defined around 2000 [2], and are typically catego-
rized by their size, measured in units, denoted U, which correspond to 100 × 100 × 100 mm
with a mass less then 1.33 kg. They are cheap, small and fast to develop.
The CubeSat format is also used for testing and qualifying new small equipment to
great effect. Modern companies specialize in the CubeSat format, providing parts for
them and/or whole solutions, like the Danish GomSpace.
Since 2003 the number of CubeSats have increased exponentially. Figure 1.1 shows the
amount of launched nanosatellites from 1998 until June 2019, including pre-announced
launches and predictions of launches to come.
The first interplanetary CubeSats, Mars Cube One (marco), were launched in 2018
together with the Insight lander. marco’s job was to relay back data to Earth during
Insight’s landing. They were 6 U. The CubeSat format is still novel and constantly being
explored.

Radars employed in space have some advantages over optical payloads. They can
penetrate clouds and measure without sunlight. Multiple types of radar instruments
are flown in space. Those include altimeters, scatterometers and Synthetic Aperture
Radar (sar). Altimeters measure the height, which can be used to determine sea level
and the height of ice sheets among other things. Scatterometers can measure the speed
and direction of wind, based on ocean waves. sar is an imaging radar, the images
can be used for 2D or 3D mapping. Additionally, the images can be used for a variety
of measurements, such as crop classification or estimation of geophysical parameters
through inverse problem theory.
sar has the advantage that its resolution in the flight direction, known as azimuth
resolution, is independent of the distance, thanks to post-processing. The spatial resolu-
tion cells are typically in the order of meters. sar relies on advanced signal processing
to provide these high resolutions.
Space missions are complex systems that require the interaction and cooperation of
numerous fields and disciplines. Systems engineering as a discipline focuses on the design
and management of these complex systems. Systems engineering closely mirrors project
management, in that it touches all aspects of a project and coordinates the combined
efforts. It differs however, because the focus lies on the technical aspects of the project
rather than the business aspects.
Many different methods and tools exist within the systems engineering repertoire.
One of the greatest is the life-cycle breakdown of projects. Different fields have differ-
ent implementations, but three overall stages exist concept: development, engineering
development, and post development. During the concept development the project needs
are analyzed, concepts are explored and a final concept is chosen. The engineering de-
velopment then proceeds to further develop the concept, design the requirements and
components of the project, and integrate the many subsystems whilst evaluating the
results. The post development stage then sees to the production and operation of the
product, be it a singular product such as a satellite, or mass produced like a new car.
1.1
Problem formulation
This master’s thesis seeks to develop a student run eo mission from the initial concept
phase through to the preliminary requirements design. Core systems engineering con-
cepts are implemented throughout the development process. Adherence to the European
Cooperation for Space Standardization (ecss) standards are desired, to the extent they
are relevant.
The final goal of the project is to deliver the documentation required for a successful
Preliminary Requirements Review (prr) and form the foundation for a future dtusat-4

1.1.1
Assumptions
Many assumptions have been made during the course of this project. The Preliminary
Requirements Document (prd) presents the assumptions in conjunction with the theory
and analysis, where relevant. The broader assumptions that cover the entire project are
as follows.
It is assumed that dtu restarts the dtusat initiative. Currently no official plans
exist for dtusat-3, but multiple student projects exist relating to a potential dtusat-3
mission. It is therefore expected that a future dtusat-4 is feasible.
A sar enabled eo CubeSat is an ambitious goal, especially compared to the complex-
ity of both dtusat and dtusat-2, of which neither were fully operational. The assumption
that student involvement is possible is therefore established.
1.1.2
Limitation
To limit the scope of the project, as well as try to reduce potential scope creep, a number
of limitations have been implemented. Further discussion of some of the limitations can
be found in chapter 6.
The systems engineering, including analysis and requirements definition, is limited
to the spacecraft. This means that aspects such as the ground station, operations, and
processing of the data products are disregarded.
Analysis relating to the mechanical disciplines, such as material properties, thermal
design, and structural analysis are disregarded. Some generic requirements, including
thermal requirements, are included, but are based only on comparable spacecraft and
norms.
As this is only a preliminary design, component specific analysis and specifications
have not been included. Requirements relating to interfaces have therefore not been
specified. The spacecraft architecture is likewise not defined beyond which subsystems
are present.
Space environmental effects, such as outgassing and radiation, are only briefly in-
cluded. No in-depth analyses have been performed to the level needed in regards to
Product Assurance (pa).

CHAPTER 2
Method
There are numerous methods and techniques for systems engineering. The methods and
processes used for this project are heavily based on two sources Systems Engineering
Principles and Practice, and Space Mission Analysis and Design. Systems Engineering
Principles and Practice, by Kossiakoff and Sweet, is used as the basis for the systems
methodology, while Space Mission Analysis and Design, by Wertz and Larson, is used
as the basis for the spacecraft design.
The ecss engineering standard for systems engineering, ECSS-E-ST-10C, is used as
the backbone for structuring the project. ecss standards have been used where applica-
ble, and adapted when not.
After an introduction to systems engineering, a brief description of the life cycle
phases used by esa is presented. A further description of phases 0 to B is given, as
the original aim of this project was to reach the System Requirements Review (srr).
Following the esa phases, the original project schedule is presented along with the final
schedule. Lastly, a description of the tools used during the project is given.
2.1
Systems Engineering
Kossiakoff and Sweet define the function of systems engineering as “[…] to guide the
engineering of complex systems.” This means that systems engineering focuses on the
system as a whole. It ensures its total operation, by assessing the system from multiple
angles, including interactions outside the system, which could be other systems or the
environment, as well as from inside. Systems engineering goes beyond engineering design,
as it also identifies customer needs, system operational environment, interfacing systems,
capabilities of operators, logistic support requirements, and similar factors which must
be represented in the system requirements and design. The ecss have illustrated the
role of the systems engineer and how they intersect at the crossroad of many disciplines
in figure 2.1. Another element of systems engineering is to bring traditional engineering
disciplines together. Many different engineering fields need to be involved to design
a complex system. For a system to perform, each subsystem element must function
properly in combination with other subsystems. This means that related subsystems
have to interact and function together, and therefore cannot be designed, produced and
implemented individually. Systems engineering must coordinate and guide the design
of each element to the extent needed, assuring that interfaces and interactions between
systems are compatible and support each other. Systems engineering is about finding the

The systems life cycle is a step-wise process that iterates the system through concept
development, engineering development, and post development. Space missions separate
the post development phase into individual phases for production and operations, due
to their long operational periods. This means that the space mission life cycle has four
phases, which depending on the organization is further split into sub-phases. According
to Wertz and Larson, these are: concept exploration, detailed development, production
and deployment, and operations and support. Concept exploration is the initial study
phase, which results in a broadly defined space mission and its components. The detailed
development is the formal design phase, which results in a detailed definition of the
components of a system. In large projects, test-hardware and -software is also developed
during the development phase. The production and deployment phase produces the

Different organizations have different system life cycle models. The phases are similar

2.1.1
ESA’s Mission Life Cycle
esa further splits the four overall mission life cycle phases into six phases 0, A, B, C,
D, E, and F. Each phase contains a review, which must be passed before continuing
with the next phase. Some tasks are part of multiple phases, such as requirements and
verification. Figure 2.4 shows the phases, including when different task and reviews are
carried out.
The initial phases 0, A and the first half of B are part of the concept exploration. This
is where the mission and requirements are defined, constraints are set, the resources to
be used in the development are identified, initial risk are assessed, and pre-development
occurs to mature required technology. Project rejections will typically happen in these
phases. At this point only around 8 % of the budget is spent, but decisions made up
until this point affect up to 70 % of the budget [8], again as seen in figure 2.2.
The second half of phase B along with C constitute the detailed development phase.
Starting after the srr the initial design is defined which is further refined up until the
Critical Design Review (cdr). This results in a detailed definition of all the systems.
Phase D and the start of phase E is the production and deployment phase. Phase
D being dedicated to the production and ground qualification of the spacecraft, ground

Phase 0
Phase 0’s primary tasks are mission analysis and needs identification. Only
a few people commonly work on this phase. It includes the project initiator, top-level
customers and representatives of the end-user. Phase 0 ends with the mdr. The mdr
approves the mission statement and the initial preliminary technical requirements.
Phase A
Phase A concerns the feasibility of the mission. It is an activity which pri-
marily includes the top-level customer and some of the first level suppliers. The outcome
shall be reported back to the project initiator and representatives of the end-users.
Phase A concludes with the prr. The main points of the prr are to provide pre-
liminary management plans, system engineering plans, product assurance plans, release
technical requirements specifications, and confirm the technical feasibility of the mission
concept. Furthermore, the system and operation concepts and technical solutions must
be selected, including model philosophy and verification approach, which are to be used
in phase B.
Phase B
Phase B focuses on preliminary definitions such as systems requirements and
preliminary design. This is quite broad and thus this phase is often split into two B1
and B2. B1 is dedicated to the systems requirements and B2 the preliminary design.
The main objectives are to establish system technical specifications, and demonstrate
that the technical solution meets the requirements, schedule, cost, and organizational
requirements.
Phase B1 ends with the srr and B2 with the pdr. The primary objectives of the srr
are to release updated technical requirement specifications, and assess the preliminary
design definition and verification program. The outcome from the pdr is the verification
of the preliminary design, concept, and technical solution, against defined requirements.
Afterwards, the final versions of management, engineering and product assurance plans
are released. The product tree, work break down structure and specification tree are also
released.

2.2.2
Executed Schedule
At the outset, we planned to do landslide monitoring. This turned out to require a lot
of monitoring and provided only limited science. We ended up investigating multiple
alternative missions and concepts. After researching the ideas, a decision was made to
go with ice velocity measurements. Different measurement tools were assessed, user and
mission requirements where defined, and the mdr was held on the 26th of March, with
our supervisors.
Phase A required substantially more time than anticipated. Analyzing each subsys-
tem and understanding how to scale them appropriately was very time consuming. Our
expectation was that the initial analysis of all the subsystems would be done within three
weeks. Instead they were spent on finding suitable software and consulting experts. The
dependency between subsystems made the iterations less distinguished then anticipated.
10 weeks were spent researching, writing and analyzing the theory. It was during the
writing of the theory and analysis, we realized the project’s scope needed to be decreased.
This meant dropping the strict adherence to the ecss deliverables, and scaling back the
expected end goal to the prr rather then the srr. It was not until the last week of
the analysis that concrete requirements were formulated. Up until then they had simply
been held in our thoughts and expressed through the analysis.
After the analysis was mostly complete, the development of auxiliary documents
began. This included the risk assessment both for the spacecraft and the mission as a
whole, and requirements traceability. The development of the auxiliary documents re-
vealed shortcomings of the current requirements and analysis. Three weeks were therefore
used iterating over all the analysis, requirements, and their associated risks.
Two documents were completed by the end of the analysis, the mdd developed dur-
ing phase 0, and the prd developed during the analysis. A third document was under
development that combined conclusions drawn from both previous documents, meant to
be the document delivered to the srr, but as the goal changed it morphed into the final
thesis. After the auxiliary documents were completed full focus was put on finishing the
thesis.

3.1.1
Urban Analysis
Remote sensing allows for a multitude of measurements with regards to urban analy-
sis. Fifty-five percent of the world’s population live in urban environments[12]. Urban
analysis is therefore more important than ever before. From Jensen and Im multiple
techniques and potential products from remote sensing of urban environments are given.
Some examples of output are mapping, measuring, and modeling of urban growth, as well
as estimating urban population. More traditional urban change analysis includes land
cover/use and urban development. Optical images are often used for change detection.
sar images are also capable of change detection. Interferometric Synthetic Aperture
Radar (insar) allows for the measurement of urban subsidence. Subsidence is the ver-
tical movement of the ground often caused by changes in the underground and the
development of new buildings. It can have large effects on buildings, as sinking ground
can have catastrophic consequences for structures.
Sentinel-1 satellites currently provide insar data for urban subsidence analysis for
some regions. Terrasar-x also provides insar data usable for urban analysis.

3.1.3
Topology Changes
Sand dunes have typically been mapped using optical images. insar images provide
additional information and should be particularly good at detecting changes based on
coherency [19]. Havivi, Amir, Schvartzman, et al. found that with a time series of insar
data it is possible to assess the stability of the dunes, which is very difficult to do using
optical images. The studies were made using Terrasar-x [19].
Dunes are primarily moved by the wind or the ocean. Large changes seem to occur
in conjunction with extreme weather. Local authorities monitor changes in populated
areas (like the west coast of Denmark).
3.1.4
Ice Velocities
Ice velocities provide insight regarding the melting/loss of ice. The arctic area has been
monitored regularly for the last three decades by esa’s remote sensing satellites (ers,
envi-sat, and Sentinel-1).
Many in-situ measurement stations are placed around Greenland to measure the ice.
Some sensors are placed directly on ice, which measure the flow of that specific spot.
Some sensors measure the elevation change of the bedrock, and from that ice-loss can
be derived. Airplane campaigns are also carried out. However, only satellite-based sar
methods provided coverage for entire areas, at regular intervals.
The current eo satellite mission measuring ice velocities on a regular basis is Sentinel-
1 [20]. The current ice velocity is primarily based on off-set tracking, but could also be
done by using insar. The resolution of Sentinel-1 is medium, around 5 × 20 m. It has a
repeat cycle of 6 days, using both satellites. Its wide swath makes it possible to cover the
area often. In 2020 the joint mission nisar will be launched by nasa and Indian Space
Research Organization (isro), which is also going to measure ice velocities, as well as
natural hazards and eco-system, using sar [21].
Ground-based Global Positioning System (gps) measurements are carried out around
Greenland. They directly measure the movement of Earth’s crust, and can from that
estimate the mass loss of Greenland [22]. A grid of measurement stations are placed
around Greenland, providing high temporal resolution at their stationary points.
Deficiencies in the current systems are the low temporal resolution of surface ice
velocity together with a lack of high spatial resolution of surface ice velocities.
3.1.5
Volcanoes
Volcanoes can move when they are close to an eruption. This movement can be mapped
using insar. Figure 3.2 shows a number of volcanic eruptions where the movement was
tracked before and after the event [23]. According to Biggs, Ebmeier, Aspinall, et al. the
biggest limitations of insar are loss due to water vapor, atmospheric loss and the loss
of signal coherence around snow-capped summits, dense vegetation, or steep-slopes.

3.1.6
Ice Sounding of Greenland
Ice Sounding makes use of low frequencies to penetrate the ice. The only viable frequency
to use from space is in the P-band, which is the lowest band allowed for active remote
sensing on Earth [24]. Penetrating deep into the ice requires a large amount of power
and utilizing P-band requires a very large antenna to work reasonably well [25].
No mission has done ice sounding from space, as of writing this. Feasibility studies
have been carried out to use the Biomass satellite for ice sounding during its eol phase,
by changing the antenna to point at nadir [26].
3.1.7
Trade-off
In an effort to weight these different science goals, criteria were defined to choose a
mission, those included complexity, science, personal attraction, and uniqueness. Com-
plexity is a measure of our perceived complexity of such a mission. The science score
describes how big a science improvement, a new instrument could provide. Personal at-
traction is based on how, for lack of a better description, cool, exciting and relevant a
mission feels for each of the project’s participants. Uniqueness describes if something
like this has been done before.
The scores are given such that 5 is the best and 1 is the worst. A score of 5 in

The melting of Greenland’s ice is affected by and impacts the global

Preliminary Satellite Design
In this chapter a description of all the subsystems onboard the spacecraft is given, along
with a brief look at the interfaces present. A review of the system drivers follows. Lastly,
a subset of requirements, from appendix A, are further analyzed to provide context. The
Preliminary Requirements Document (prd) collects all the theory and analysis relevant
for each subsystem and is the document from which all requirements are derived.
4.1
Satellite Systems
The spacecraft consists of six subsystems. The sar payload, that provides the scientific
measuring capability. The Electric Power System (eps), which generates, stores and
distributes the power to the rest of the spacecraft. The Comm. allows for downlink of
the collected data and interactions with the spacecraft. The On-board Processor (obp)
functions as the brain of the spacecraft controlling the current tasks and the other
subsystems. The Attitude and Orbit Control System (aocs) determines the orbital
position and attitude, and corrects the attitude when needed. Last, is the platform that
contains all the systems and provides structural support and limited shielding from space
environmental effects. Additionally, an auxiliary subsystem is defined in the spacecraft
orbit. Despite it not being a physical system, the choice of orbit has massive implications
and is therefore considered an auxiliary subsystem.
The interfaces between the subsystems are subsequently analyzed using a dsm, de-
termining either a structural, energy flow, or data and control relationship.
4.1.1
Systems
A functional analysis, based on the information flow required to capture ice velocity maps,
is presented in the mdd and elaborated on in the prd. From the functional analysis, a
set of systems have been defined. All these systems serve to capture the data required
to develop the final ice velocity products. Figure 4.1 shows a diagram of the spacecraft’s
subsystems.
A brief introduction to each subsystem is given here, along with the essential specifi-
cations. A thorough collection of theory, analysis, and specifications for each subsystem
can be found in the prd.

SAR Payload
The Synthetic Aperture Radar (sar) is the scientific payload enabling
the measurement of ice velocities. A sar is an imaging radar. It is capable of imaging the
ice independent of the weather conditions, as the microwaves it utilizes can penetrate
clouds. The method of offset tracking will be used to determine the ice velocity for the
glacier by determining the relative motion of features between captures.
The sar will use the X-band, with a sensitivity, measured in Noise-Equivalent Sigma
Zero (nesz), equaling −17 dB to distinguish the ice. It will have a mean looking angle
of 25 °, record images of 30 × 45 km, and generate 151.4 MB of data. The antenna has a
size of 0.3 × 1.9 m. The peak transmitted power is going to be 650 W, with an average
transmitted power of 91 W.
Electric Power System
Just as any other machine, a satellite requires energy to func-
tion. This is managed by the Electric Power System (eps). It generates power through
solar panels. It then distributes the power to all the systems requiring energy and stores
the excess energy in batteries, such that it may be used when the solar panels are unable
to deliver the required power.
The eps will have a battery capacity of at least 31 W h. It is designed to use 19 body-
mounted solar arrays, providing an average power of 5.7 W after 3 years. The spacecraft’s
average power consumption is expected to be 4.39 W. An inherent degradation of 77 %

Communication
Although the spacecraft will have some autonomy, the majority of
the spacecraft’s schedule and tasks will be given by ground. This, coupled with the
necessity of downlinking the captured data, leads to the requirement for communications,
or Comm. for short, onboard the spacecraft. A low data rate, but highly reliable, Ultra
High Frequency (uhf) radio, called the ComLink-R, is employed for interactions with
the spacecraft. While a high data rate S-band radio, called the ComLink-H, is employed
for data downlink. Error correction schemes are utilized for both systems to reduce Bit
Error Rate (ber).
Comlink-R will be using uhf-band and omnidirectional whip-antennas. Comlink-R
will transmit a beacon once every minute and have a data rate of at least 10 kbit s−1. It
will transmit with a power of 0.2 W.
Comlink-H will be using S-band and a patch antenna. Allowing it to achieve a data
rate of at least 3 Mbit s−1. It will only include a transmitter, as all commands will be
uploaded using CommLink-R. CommLink-H will transmit with a power of 1.0 W.
On-Board Processor
The On-board Processor (obp) is the brains of the spacecraft.
It is linked to all other subsystems and controls the spacecraft’s task according to the
schedule. If preprocessing of the data is needed before transmission to the ground station
it will also be handled by the obp. Lastly, it is also the obp that handles any failures
that may occur onboard the spacecraft. Either handling the error itself or by enabling
fail-safe mode ensuring the spacecraft’s survival until operators can manually step in.
It is designed to have mass storage of 1.33 GB. Allowing it to capture 7 images
between downlinks, never exceding 80 % capacity under nominal conditions.
Attitude and Orbit Control System
The Attitude and Orbit Control System
(aocs) has two tasks. The first is determining the spacecraft’s attitude and position.
For this, star trackers and sun sensors are used. The second task is to control the atti-
tude and position. Momentum wheels and magnetorquers are used for attitude control,
but due to the limitations of a CubeSat, no active propulsion has been included making
positional control impossible.
The aocs is designed to have a Root Sum Square (rss) mapping error of less than
1500 m. This requires it to have an attitude error less than 0.1 °, a positional error less
than 100 m and a clock error less than 0.5 s. To ensure that the spacecraft can get out of
the stable attitude a minimum peak torque of 13.7 µN m is required. This includes the
torque generated by the gravity gradient and the aerodynamic torque at an altitude of
250 km.
Platform
All spacecrafts consist of a platform that provides the structural integrity
for all the subsystems. The on-board electronics are also temperature sensitive, some
more than others, the platform must therefore also keep the thermals in check. The

also considered a part of the platform. These three components, structure, thermals,
and mechanisms, combine to become the platform. Lastly, the platform also protects

Orbit
Although not an actual subsystem onboard the spacecraft, the orbit is, nonethe-
less, critical for the mission. The choice of orbit influences the altitude of the measure-

inclination also determines the revisit time and ground track while passing Jakobshavn
Isbræ.
A starting altitude of 525 km is assumed in all calculations, making the estimated
lifetime slightly over 5 yrs. The inclination is 72.5 ° to pass Jakobshavn Isbræ parallel to
the latitude. The satellite is expected to naturally deorbit after 5 yrs.

Size
The largest limiting factor for all subsystems is the overall volume available. Ei-
ther it is through direct size constraints or secondary effects such as limited power.
The size limitation is a byproduct of the student involvement, as it is unrealistic to
expect a traditionally sized sar satellite be entirely developed and operated by students.
The CubeSat format provides an accessible platform for student-developed satellites,
although it carries its own limitations with it. Only discrete sizes, in the form of units
(10 × 10 × 10 cm), and certain configurations are supported by industry, e.g. launch
providers. Developing a uniquely sized or nonstandard configured satellite would, there-
fore, increase the mission cost greatly.
This size limitation on the antenna dictates the need for an X-band sar, with shallow
penetration depth. The sar penetration depth of ice depends on the frequency used.
The lower frequency bands provide a larger penetration depth, increasing the coherency
between acquisitions. This means that frequency bands such as C- or L-band would be
preferable in this regard. The downside of lower frequency bands is, they require a much
larger antenna to maintain the high resolution.
Spacecraft Mass
The CubeSat format has a mass limitation of approximately 1.33 kg
per unit [27]. This limits the 6 U satellite’s mass to 8 kg. Some launch providers allow a
6 U CubeSat to weigh as much as 12 kg [28], but launch providers generally charge 30 ke
per kilogram launched [29], making this an expensive luxury.
From the baseline model described in table 6.1, it can be seen that the sar uses over
50 % of the allotted mass. The mass of the sar is quite uncertain. The mass is only
known for a single similar system. If the required mass cannot be allocated to the sar,
then the performance is expected to decrease. This could be due to thermal issues or
due to the lack of components with the required specifications.
Although not specifically analyzed, the spacecraft mass has a large influence on the
survivability of the spacecraft. The shielding required to minimize effects from radiation
and micrometeorites is proportional to shielding thickness, and thereby mass, as seen in
figure 3.6 in the prd. As this is a CubeSat, a higher risk is expected, and the shielding
is therefore much more limited than with traditional satellites.
Power
The available space for solar arrays is limited by the spacecraft’s size and mass
allowance. For body-mounted solar panels, it is mostly the available area that limits
the power generation. If deployable solar arrays are used, the deployment mechanism
would take up valuable size and mass. Power storage is limited by the energy density
of the batteries. Although modern Li-ion batteries are utilized, mass is still the largest
limitation to the storage capacity.
The sar performance is directly proportional to the average power transmitted, as
is the ground resolution. This means that if we wish to half the resolution cell size, twice
the average power must be transmitted. Likewise, the captured area is also affected by
the available power, as the sar must remain at full power while recording.
The aocs is currently assumed to be constantly active as it must continuously correct
the attitude. With that assumption aocs single-handedly accounts for ≈40 % of the total

power, as seen in table 6.1. Until a more detailed attitude analysis is conducted there
will be a high risk associated with the pointing accuracy and power.
Data Rate
The Eb/N0, energy per bit to spectral noise density ratio, is proportional
to the ber. That means that for a lower ber, especially for higher-order Phase-Shift
Keying (psk), more power is required. The antenna size, or rather the antenna gain, also
affects the Eb/N0.
S-band is used for ComLink-H, the high data rate antenna. Increasing the frequency
from S-band to X-band would increase the available bandwidth and thereby the data
rate. That said, the hardware required to utilize X-band communication has an enormous
mass and power cost associated with it.
Onboard data storage is of reduced concern, thanks to modern mass storage allowing
the data to be transmitted to ground over several passes. Nonetheless, the assumption
that the spacecraft only uses the dtu ground station creates a requirement for the
minimum downlink data rate. Cooperating with additional ground stations would allow
for more frequent communications, but at the cost of power.
A high data rate is desirable as it is a bottleneck in regard to the collectible science.
sar compression can reduce the data needed to be transmitted, but at the cost of detail.
The current estimation for data collected during a single pass is 151 MB, as detailed in
the sar analysis in the prd. With a data rate of 3 Mbit, as specified by the requirements
for ComLink-H, a total of 403 s transmission time is required. With the assumption of
250 s effective transmission per ground station pass, a total of two passes are required
for single image transmissions.
Pointing
Pointing correctly requires attitude actuators. The spacecraft needs reaction
wheels to control the pointing. They must occasionally be de-spun, which is done using
magnetorquers. Reaction wheels utilize mass, diameter, and rotational velocity to pro-
duce torque. Magnetorquers however, convert power into magnetic fields. Size and mass
therefore, affects the torque produced by reaction wheels, while power mostly limits
magnetorquers, although also reaction wheels.
Attitude determination requires a high on-time, to ensure the spacecraft knows its
orientation. dtusat-4 is designed to stay in an unstable equilibrium point, which requires
the altitude control to make constant minor corrections. The high on-time of the aocs
means that power is very limiting, as previously mentioned.
The pointing accuracy, or rather the mapping accuracy, has a direct impact on the
data collected swath width of the sar. If mapping accuracy is decreased, a larger margin
needs to be recorded to ensure the roi is measured. This increases the data amount which
further stresses the data rates.
ComLink-H uses a directional patch antenna. It requires a certain pointing to function
optimally. This means that reduced pointing accuracy both increases the data rate, but
also reduces the feasible transmission rate.
Solar arrays provide the power generation. Undesirable pointing consequently limits

Altitude
The launch vehicle determines the final altitude. Launch vehicles have cer-
tain limitations regarding the size and mass they can insert into a given orbit. Launch
providers typically allow for certain fixed-sized CubeSats, which thereby limits the size.
The same goes for mass.
Drag and other effects slowly degrade the altitude over time. Meaning a minimum
altitude is required to fulfill the mission duration. At the same time, space debris must
be minimized, requiring that the spacecraft has a low enough altitude that it naturally
decays by the eol. Figure 4.3a shows the estimated lifetime depending on initial altitude.

craft must also stay below the innermost Van Allan belt. Otherwise shielding would be
required, thereby increasing the overall mass.

a higher altitude would result in a decreased sensitivity, making it more difficult or
even impossible to measure ice velocities. Although, the sar illuminated area also scales
with altitude. Decreasing the altitude too much will thus result in not all the roi being
recorded and decrease the ground range resolution.

This requirement defines the functionality of the communications. Two

Mission Risk
This chapter details how risk influences the mission. First, the risk management for the
mission is detailed, this is an abridged version of the rmp found in appendix F. Next,
each high-level risk found during the fmeca and mission risk assessment is examined.
Lastly, the overall risk for the mission is assessed.
5.1
Risk Management
Once a satellite is sent to space, it is almost impossible to provide physical support
or maintenance. Components need to be reliable, which means that they can perform
their required function, for the given operating condition for the specified time interval.
The system availability refers to the percentage of time in which the system operates
nominally during the mission. The three concepts, alongside safety, make up Reliability,
Availability, Maintainability, and Safety (rams) and together describes dependability,
which is part of the pa discipline. Maintainability is lacking for spacecraft. It is therefore
important to have considered the many things that can go wrong, then work to either
prevent them or prepare plans for the inevitable failures. For this, risk management is
an indispensable tool, as it works to mitigate potential failures. It is important for all
projects, but as noted earlier it is especially important for space missions.
Risk management touches all aspects of the mission. It is just as important for
project management to ensure that the budget and schedule are kept, as for the systems
themselves. The latter is often achieved through what is called Failure Mode, Effects,
and Criticality Analysis (fmeca).
As this is a low-cost student-run satellite mission, the resources are limited. This
means that the focus will be on analyzing and finding contingencies for only the largest
of potential failures.
Redundancy is one of the traditional ways of increasing reliability but is of limited
use for this mission. This is because the CubeSat format lends to cheaper satellites and
faster development time but at the cost of risk mitigation and redundancy.
The full rmp can be found in appendix F. It describes a tailored version of the risk
method laid forth in ECSS-M-ST-80C. The risk method details the process from defining
a risk through eventually accepting the risk. The Risk Index (ri) scheme has also been
tailored from the original and is also discussed in the rmp. ri combines the severity
of a risk and the likelihood to allow for easier categorization of risks. Differing risk
categories, referred to as risk magnitudes, are acceptable for the mission. The tailored

Feasibility
Verification and validation are processes that take place to ensure the product works as
intended, broadly stated. The two terms are highly related. Verification can roughly be
reduced to: Does the product live up to the requirements. Whereas validation can be
explained as: Does the product live up to the expectations of the customer.
The ecss defines verification as “[the] process which demonstrates through the pro-
vision of objective evidence that the product is designed and produced according to its
specifications and the agreed deviations and waivers, and is free of defects”, and vali-
dation as “[the] process which demonstrates that the product is able to accomplish its
intended use in the intended operational environment”[35]. It is important to notice that
verification is a pre-requisite for validation.
The mission is still in its early phase. It is not possible to verify or validate the
product in its intended environment, as the spacecraft has yet to be produced. But, some
of the requirements can be verified through analysis of available cots. Section 6.1.1 goes
through a baseline model, using primarily cots, to confirm the physical constraints of
the spacecraft.

Requirements
The obp requirement D–08 defines that housekeeping data must be collected with a
minimum rate of once per second. The rate of once per second is arbitrarily chosen as
an estimation of the required frequency. Alternatively, it could have been declared as
To Be Confirmed (tbc), but this would have introduced ambiguity for the subsystems
==PAGE:70==

6 Discussion
as to how often they, in reality, are required to deliver housekeeping data. By choosing
an arbitrary value everyone must conform with, assures that a minimum frequency is
upheld. If any objections occur, because the frequency either is not generating enough
data to be useful or producing too much, it provides an opportunity to redefine the
requirement.
Requirements are commonly separated, to the degree it makes sense. All the obp
requirements have been defined together, both hardware and software. These would
usually be separated, as design and production of each subsystem would be performed
by separate teams. The current requirements structure makes it undesirable to split the
obp software from the hardware, but a future iteration should further divide the obp
into a dedicated hardware and software subsystem.
The requirements regarding the functionality of the obp would form the basis for
the software requirements. The rest of the requirements would then form the basis for
the hardware in terms of components, interfaces, and manufacturing.
Different effects of the harsh environment in space can affect the obp and can cause
different types of problems. Bit flips and corrupted memory can be devastating. To
reduce this risk, the obp typically includes Error Detection and Correction (edac) in
some fashion. This can be accomplished in different ways. The overall techniques are
either software- or hardware-based edac. Hardware edac can be implemented by using
multiple processors, where some process the command and one checks if the parity bits
are correct. We assume it is difficult to find cots which are suitable for hardware edac
on a CubeSat. We also assume that writing edac code for a CubeSat obp would be a
major task. We do not feel that we have the necessary knowledge to assess this trade-off
between hardware and software implementations of edac. It shall, therefore, be part of
future work.
It is assumed that with edac, both bit flips and corruption have negligible effects
during the majority of the orbit. Although the South Atlantic Anomaly is expected to
create interference to such a degree that a forced reboot occurs.
A last note regarding requirements, is that no definition on how to determine confor-
mance with the requirements has been included in the report. ecss specifies that each
requirement shall have specified how it is to be verified. This can be done by one or more
of the following methods: test, analysis, review of design and/or inspection [38].
The requirements have not been specified how they shall be verified, due to time
constraints. This must, therefore, be done as future works, to be able to continue into the
next phases, and proceed through the V-model. Documentation for closing requirements

ecss. Requiring ecss conformant documentation would most likely doom dtusat-4, as
it needs to be carried on student involvement. Keeping engagement high is required for
the success of the mission.

Risks
From section 5.3 it was found that the overall risk for the spacecraft was low, and mission
risk was medium, equaling a ri of B4 and C4 respectively. From the rmp, in appendix
F, these are both acceptable risks. The original rmp from ECSS-M-ST-80C shows that
a risk magnitude of medium risk is unacceptable in a traditional ecss compliant project.
The ecss proposes that medium risks “aggressively manage, consider alternative team
process or baseline” to reduce the risk to an acceptable level of low risk.
The main goal of the dtusat initiative is student involvement and a higher risk is
therefore expected and tolerated. Nonetheless, the rmp proposes that medium risks “seek
to reduce the risk further”. One such way that seeks to reduce the risks significantly is by
implementing traditional project management. As discussed briefly in section 5.2.2 risks
such as R-038 and R-092 both relate to students and how the university schedule may
influence the project. If appropriately accounted for in the management of the mission,
those risks could be reduced, lowering the overall mission risk.
As detailed in the rmp, in appendix F, the overall risk is determined using a Center
of Mass (com) approach with the weighting based on the risk magnitude. The weighting
scales linearly from 1 to 5 for very low risks to very high risks respectively. Alternative
weighting schemes could be used for different overall risk calculations. An exponential
weighting scheme would provide a more conservative overall risk.
More extensive risk analysis may also produce a larger proportion of lower magnitude
risks. This is because the number of irrelevant risks would most likely increase as the
pool of identified risks increases. An exponential weighting scheme may then provide a
more accurate evaluation of the overall risk.
The linear weighting scheme has been deemed acceptable for the current project
phase. In the future when more detailed risk assessments have been made, the use of a
different weighting scheme may be beneficial.
Risk assessment, of both the spacecraft and the mission, is a great tool to determine
potential problems. The assessment often leads to missing knowledge being unveiled,
which reveals missing analyses or assumptions. One problem with risk assessment is
that it only assesses known factors within the problem domain, that is, it only finds the
known-unknowns. It does not find unknown-unknowns, affectionately called unk/unks.
==PAGE:72==
58
6 Discussion
They represent everything outside of the sphere-of-knowledge, the things we do not know
that we do not know.
Unk/unks are impossible to remove from the mission entirely. Only testing and anal-
ysis can uncover potential unk/unks, but due to their nature, they appear sporadically.
The low budget, fast development cycle, and reliance on students mean a large num-
ber of unk/unks are expected for the mission. Hopefully, the experience from academic
supervisors can help reveal the unk/unks.
Risk assessment is not an exact science. This means that there will always be risks
and failures not accounted for or misrepresented, like with unk/unks.
An example of a risk that proved to come true but had been mis-assessed is the
resolution of the sar. The mission risk assessment found risks R-001 to R-006 all relating
to the sar not meeting requirements. However, only risk R-001 and R-006 had the cause
of too stringent requirements, and neither correctly assessed the effect. Risk R-001 states
that the requirements being too stringent for the sar would make miniaturization of the
sar unable to reach maturity and therefore cancel the mission. Risk R-06 says that if
a subsystem is unable to comply with requirements the spacecraft will grow larger and
the cost increase. In practice, the too stringent sar sensitivity resulted in a lowering of
mission requirements, which was not a foreseen possibility.
The misrepresentation of risks shows that risk assessment is not a singular activity,
but rather a continuous task. The lack of foresight with regards to sar sensitivity came
from the lack of knowledge, and ultimately incorrect understanding, of sar sensitivity.
This was not exposed until the feedback from experts. The participants of each subse-
quent level of depth in the V-model must therefore also participate in the risk assessment,
as the degree of specialization increases.
Dimensioning
Requirement M–03 defines a minimum of one picture a week of Jakobshavn Isbræ. The
spacecraft is currently designed such that 13 pictures can be acquired each day. This is
partly to justify such a mission, in regards to costs. But, also to satisfy the secondary
mission objectives, which would require additional observational time. A high temporal
resolution would make it possible to identify cycles that occur more frequently than
every sixth day. The science impact of this is difficult to estimate.
The spacecraft has been scaled such that it can operate for five years. Because the
glacier has seasonal variation, multiple years need to be measured to understand the an-
nual cycle. More data provides a better foundation for discovering unknown phenomena.
Ice velocities are interesting to measure, due to seasonal variation, but also due to the
longer trends. Greenland has a yearly ice loss, due to climate changes. It is measured
many different ways, like surface uplift and surface velocity. By following, trends over
a long period, insight into different effects can be obtained. The three-year goal, with a
two-year extension, is based on the lifetime of similar spacecrafts. Assuming the bath-
tub curve for reliance, designing all the subsystems to handle five years will increase the
reliability for the first three years.

Performance
Altitude is the parameter with the largest clash of interest. Orbit lifetime is positively
correlated with altitude, while sar sensitivity is negatively correlated. Much debate has
been had regarding what the altitude should be. Conservative estimates are preferable at
this stage in development, but it is simply not possible regarding this particular trade-off.
The analysis shows that the minimum starting altitude must be above 524 km for the
spacecraft to orbit for 5 yrs. This estimation method has an accuracy of a few percent
the lifetime [10], a starting altitude of 550 km was therefore chosen.
At an altitude of 525 km, and using the current hardware parameters, the sar sen-
sitivity gives a nesz of −17.04 dB, which is only slightly better than what requirement
P–05 specifies. Increasing the altitude by 25 km results in decreased sensitivity of 0.5 dB
but minimizes the risk of early reentry.
The defined requirement for the sar sensitivity is right on the edge of what is cur-
rently possible. It seems unrealistic to push the hardware parameters further than they
currently are. The altitude however only affects the mission duration. Requirement M–
07 along with Note 1 specifies that the mission must last for 3 yrs with a possible 2 yrs
extension. Requirement S–05 however defines that it is only during the initial 3 yrs that
it must work nominally. The change from a starting altitude of 550 km to 525 km, po-
tentially reducing the lifetime, is necessary to ensure a satisfactory sar sensitivity. It
has therefore been decided that the initial start altitude should be around 525 km.
The temperature of the sar has been conservatively estimated. An internal temper-
ature of 60 °, the sun heating the antenna to 100 °, and Greenland having a brightness
temperature of −20 ° have been assumed. Measurements can also be performed during
an eclipse, which reduces the antenna temperature and thereby increases the sensitivity.
The increased sensitivity could be used to increase the bandwidth to acquire a higher
range resolution. This could be done by utilizing a programmable transmitted bandwidth.
It is done by many other sar missions to adjust the resolution, such as Sentinel-1 [40].
The downside of a programmable bandwidth is that it increases the complexity of the
sar because the waveform generator must include additional functionality, compared to
the original requirement.
Quantization level specified in requirement P–23 states that the sar shall use 16 bit
Analog-to-Digital Converter (adc) to represent both the I and Q channel of the complex
modulator. This was based upon Sentinel-1’s product specification [41], concerning the
level 1 product. It has since been discovered that they record with 10 bit adc [41],
but represent the level 1 data using 16 bit unsigned integers, as computers use 2N bit
representation. This means that our dynamic depth is much higher than Sentinel-1.
Using 16 bit, our dynamic range would be close to 85 dB, whereas Sentinel-1’s is closer

Another concern with the sar is the high data rates. Compression algorithms are

should be considered for the final design, but due to time constraints, it has not been
investigated.
Multiple options for how the solar cells are mounted exist: body-mounted, deployable
solar array, and solar cells mounted to the backside of the antenna. Body mounted are
favorable due to their simplicity. Deployable provides higher cell utilization but have
a higher complexity. The antenna mounted solar cells allow for a larger surface to be
used. The power budget as of now allows for body-mounted solar cells. But, in case
the power requirement is too stringent, one of the alternative solutions may need to be
implemented.
Universities worldwide have CubeSat programs. Cooperation with their ground sta-
tions would be beneficial in regard to transferring data. A bottleneck in the current
system is the downlink data rate. Having access to multiple ground stations would
make it possible to capture more measurements. It could also be beneficial regarding
troubleshooting the spacecraft. The spacecraft transmits a beacon on regular intervals,
which gives information regarding its current state. More ground stations would lead
to more data. This could help locate errors thereby making it possible to correct them
earlier.

The tid requirements are based on assumptions regarding shielding material, thickness
and orbit. The online tool spenvis was used for the models and simulations. As the de-
sign evolves, better estimates can be made of the tid. The current tid of 3.940 × 104 rad
is assumed to be a conservative estimate, using a shielding thickness of 1 mm aluminium.

The shielding thickness is going to vary depending on the final architecture. Compo-
nents can also act as shields in some directions for other components. As a result, the
tid will be component-specific, but a CubeSat is small compared to regular satellites,
limiting the overall effect of mutual component shielding. This suggests that the use of
a general tid for all electronics is beneficial.
Mutual shielding may only result in limited effect. The effect could nonetheless be

Outgassing must also be taken into account. Outgassing can cause different kinds of
problems. One of them being condensation, which keeps to the spacecraft like a cloud.
It can cover the camera lenses, thereby affecting altitude control.
Outgassing can be limited by not using restricted materials, which are known to have
high Total Mass Loss (tml). Restricted materials such as pvc plastic and superglue, as
they have a tml ≥15 % [42]. An acceptable tml limit should be set for materials. The
materials used could be screened in outgassing tests to estimate their usefulness. Some
materials could be baked out, to reduce the outgassing. Additionally, a protective coating
could also be applied to critical materials to reduce their tml. Baffles could be used on
lenses to reduce outgassing effects.
Outgassing and material restrictions have not been analyzed. Requirements have,
therefore, not been set and further pa is required for future works.
The space environment near Earth contains different molecules of which some are
more problematic than others, e.g. atomic oxygen is highly corrosive. Commonly eroded
materials are silver, Kapton, and polymers, which should be avoided or coated if used
on the outside of the spacecraft. Most metals will not be eroded [42]. It mainly affects
the surface properties of materials and not structural integrity.
Radiation, outgassing, and atomic oxygen are not the only concerns in space. Many
small objects are also present in the Near-Earth environment. This includes orbital
debris and micrometeorites, which can collide with the spacecraft at high velocities.
A large particle, ≥0.5 mm, colliding can penetrate the spacecraft and thereby cause
catastrophic damage [42]. Smaller particles, of µm size, can cause localized damage to
external materials. Common effects can be cracking and melting. Susceptible materials,
such as solar cells, must be covered with glass. Some of the lesser effects can be mitigated,
but if the spacecraft is hit by debris, then it will most likely end the mission.
dtusat-4 has a relatively large surface area compared to its mass, due to the antenna.
That said, compared to traditional spacecraft dtusat-4 is only a small target. Impacts
are therefore not expected to be a big issue.
It is clear that thermal cycles will occur often for the spacecraft, as it is placed in Low
Earh Orbit (leo). Figure 7.3.b in the prd shows eclipse durations for a year. The mode
eclipse duration is ≈2000 s, the maximum eclipse is ≈2500 s during lunar eclipses, and
some orbits have no eclipses. These different conditions affect the spacecrafts thermal
cycles. Materials such as solder joints, adhesive bonds, composite substrates and coatings

are susceptible to thermal cycles. Stresses can be induced due to Coefficient of Thermal
Expansion (cte) mismatch, causing cracking and delamination.
The large variation of eclipse time can impose an issue regarding thermal dissipa-
tion. Requirement S–06 specifies that thermal control shall be passive, i.e. heat shall be
radiated away from the spacecraft without dedicated hardware. Some components, like
the sar amplifier, are going to generate a significant amount of heat while active. Heat
generation depends on component power consumption, efficiency, and on-time. Choosing
components with high efficiency will be important to reduce unnecessary heating. The
performance of certain components depends on temperature, such as receivers, which
should have a stable temperature and have their temperature logged often for calibra-
tion purposes. Keeping the temperature stable could be an issue, but as long as an
internal calibration loop exists for the sar, the effect is assumed to be minimal.
Long eclipse time can result in a relatively cold spacecraft, which affects some parts
negatively, e.g. the batteries. For these components, heaters will be needed in order to
keep the temperature stable during eclipses.
The thermal cycle is unavoidable and is thus important to design for it. No thermal
simulation has been performed for the system. That does not mean it is not a concern
and a vital element of a space mission. This must be part of future work to ensure a
working design. Future contributors must be aware of cte mismatch during the design
phase. Thermal considerations were not taken into account when designing the spacecraft
orbit. dtusat-4 will be in leo as most CubeSats, thus it is assumed that the thermal
environment can be designed for.
Spacecraft Design
One of the initial future works is the determination of the spacecraft’s architecture, i.e.
a model of the spacecraft with all subsystems properly sized and placed appropriately.
From the spacecraft architecture, more accurate dimensional requirements can be deter-
mined for each subsystem, and dimensioning of the interfaces will give more accurate
mass and power estimates in regards to wiring.
Currently, the baseline satellite uses liquid volume to determine the volume budget,
instead of the spacecraft architecture. The volume of each subsystem is based upon the
outer dimensions, ensuring a built-in margin. The final subsystems can be dimensioned
to meet slightly anomalous shapes to meet the final volume restrictions. The combination
of built-in margin and ability to customize dimensions makes us confident in the volume
budget.
Multiple components made for the CubeSat format were investigated. A vast amount
of them follow a portion of the PC/104 standard, which is a standard for embedded
computers. It defines the size, computer buses and interfaces of stackable electronics. It
specifies a dimension of 90 × 96 mm. This fits within the standard CubeSat unit. One
of our concerns regarding this standard is that our CubeSat only has half the width
of a tradition CubeSat. We expect dtusat-4’s main body to have the dimension of
50 × 200 × 300 mm. This means that six PC/104 components can be placed next to
==PAGE:77==
6.2 Mission-Technical Discussion
63
each other but only a few can be stacked due to the 50 mm width. This would mean that
the stackable feature loses much of the initial attraction. Another concern regarding
the PC/104 is that some systems may not match the standard very well. The sar
hardware is thought to be designed as a single unit. The aocs components that produce
torque need to be oriented in different directions, which does not necessarily match the
stackable concept. It might be too strict to require all subsystems to fit into the stackable
setup, but the stackable setup could be a great asset when it comes to integration and
verification, as they would allow for easy integrations. Some systems could very well be
defined to fit such a format, like obp and eps.
The dsm provides the start of the interface determination and management. The
determination of specific interfaces is still needed. The types of power supply chan-
nels must be investigated further. At the moment, it is anticipated that 3.3 V, 5 V and
28 V channels would be appropriate. The 28 V channel is for the sar, which has a high
peak power requirement. Likewise, the protocols used to communicate data between sub-
systems must also be determined. The CubeSat Space Protocol (csp) is a lightweight
opensource protocol, briefly mentioned in section 6.2.8 of the prd, developed and main-
tained by aau and GomSpace, and a potential candidate. Controller Area Network
(can) bus is also commonly used in the space industry. Lastly, the physical interfaces in
the form of connectors must also be found. They must both live up to the environmental
requirements, as well as the power and protocols that are chosen.
The lack of knowledge within the field of both electronics and computer science has
meant the interface determination has been postponed to future works.
Emerged Revisions
The main design driver for the sar was, in the beginning, a distributed target snr of 8 dB,
which was chosen based on the common use of snr as a design driver. In order to design
the system, other values need to be assumed, which are described in the prd table 5.1.
Late in the analysis process, it was brought to our attention that those assumptions were
not optimal. nesz was therefore switched to as the main design parameter for the sar.
To fulfill the new requirement, many parameters had to be pushed to what we considered
an optimistic but realistic limit. The increase in sensitivity brought by further increasing
some parameters was not enough, the ground range resolution was therefore decreased
to 20 m to get the target nesz of −17 dB. Our initial requirement was a resolution cell
of 5 × 5 m. The range resolution comes from the transmitted bandwidth and incidence
angle. The transmitted bandwidth is equivalent to the received bandwidth and thereby
affects noise power. In order to achieve the desired nesz, the bandwidth was reduced,
which results in a decreased range resolution.
One of the initial goals was to be better than Sentinel-1 in the azimuth direction.
This is still achieved. Sentinel-1 has an azimuth resolution of 20 m, where dtusat-4’s
will be 5 m, which comes from the Pulse Repetition Frequency (prf).
Ice velocity maps with a resolution of 50 × 50 m are required, as defined in require-
ment M–13. sar processing often involves multi-look processing, which is when multiple
==PAGE:78==
64
6 Discussion
resolutions cells are averaged. Often a quadratic cell is desired. This can be done by aver-
aging 8 cells in the azimuth direction and 2 in the range, resulting in a cell of 40 × 40 m.
Thus, the result of changing the sar design driver is limited to reduced low-level perfor-
mance, while still producing an acceptable end product.
As previously discussed, the ground range resolution was decreased due to the sensi-
tivity of the sar. This meant changing requirement M–02 from ≤5 m in both directions
to ≤5 m in azimuth resolution, and ≤20 m in the ground range resolution.
Following the formal ecss procedures, a change to requirement M–02, would require
an official appeal. ECSS-M-ST-40C defines four such appeals: Change Request (cr),
Change Proposal (cp), Request for Deviation (rfd), and Request for Waiver (rfw).
They are detailed in ECSS-M-ST-40C annex G to J. crs are initiated by the customer,
while cps are initiated by the supplier. rfd are likewise planned departures from the
requirements or design, while rfw are unplanned departures. Had the need for lowering
the resolution first been established by the dedicated sar team, it would therefore have
been either a cp or a rfw, depending on the severity, in this case a cp was performed
internally.
Our initial plan was to use insar to measure detailed surface movements. insar
requires two measurements taken from the same vantage point, where the difference in
received phase is compared to measure displacements. This allows for the measurement of
subsidence, uplift and much more. It can only measure the phase modulus 2π. Measuring
areas with displacement often results in phase wrapping, which must be unwrapped. To
use insar, a very stable oscillator must be used to ensure high coherency of signals,
which we assume can be designed based on sri-cires’s sar [36]. The measured phase
is also proportional to the perpendicular baseline between the measurements, meaning
a narrow orbital corridor is required.
The biggest issues, for dtusat-4, is the orbital precision. Sentinel-1’s requirements
define that it shall be within a 100 m orbital corridor, which results in the necessity
of a propulsion system. SmallSat sars like iceeye also have similar orbital constraints,
including a propulsion system. sri-cires is likewise including propulsion on their mission
[36]. dtusat-4’s current design should be able to do insar, it is however not able to
control the perpendicular baseline, thereby making the resulting insar questionable.
This means the possibility to perform the secondary objectives is reduced, compared to
the initial expectations.

Section 2.2 details the project schedule, both in its original incarnation and its final
iteration. Following is a more detailed discussion of why the changes occurred, how the
changes affected the project, and some general takeaways from the project.
It quickly became apparent that the initial mission objective of landslide monitoring
was not the best choice for the mission. This was partly because of the limited amount
of science available, but also because of the overambitious scope. The sudden lack of a

mission objective turned the phase 0 mission analysis from a formality into an actual
search for a mission objective. With only a week delay, the mission objective was chosen
and the mdd was completed.
With phase 0 completed, phase A could commence. All the subsystems were defined,
and a week was set aside per subsystem for the first iteration. The plan was to distribute
the subsystems and upon each iteration swap subsystems, such that each member had
a minimum of one iteration for each subsystem. This never came to fruition. The de-
velopment of the prd’s theory section and subsequent analysis took much longer than
expected. The bulk of the project was spent understanding and analyzing each subsys-
tem. This meant that the initial division of subsystems stayed true throughout the entire
project. Many aspects were developed and discussed in collaboration, but the time re-
quired to get reacquainted with the theory for each subsystem proved too large for it to
be feasible to swap subsystems on iterations.
After the analysis of all the subsystems was completed, the requirements were for-
malized. The formalization of the requirements helped greatly in defining the system
as a whole and outlined incompatibilities that had not been unearthed during the anal-
ysis. The project lacked an actual iteration cycle until the requirements started being
formalized. Each analysis simply affected each other in the sequence they naturally oc-
curred. Staying true to the initial plan of defining requirements for each system within
a given time frame, and then subsequently iterating over them, would have provided
some desired structure to the project. That being said, it was a chicken and the egg situ-
ation; we needed the knowledge from our deep dive into theory and analysis to have the
understanding required to make the requirements, meanwhile, the requirements helped
inform the development of the analysis. Ultimately, the unstructured analysis of each
subsystem was a necessary evil, providing some of the intuition so often relied upon by
system engineers during the early phases of development.
Another aspect eluded to, in section 2.2, that never came to fruition was the amount
of documentation to be completed. Following the original project schedule, most of the
documents seen in appendix E, were to be created and maintained for each review.
Because the development of the theory and analyses for the prd took as long as they
did, the choice to not produce the deliverables was made. Many of the aspects the
documents represent have nonetheless been incorporated into the documentation, albeit
not as independent as the ecss would require.
Through the many iterations, numerous recalculations, and verbal discussions some
of the information found throughout the mission’s documentation is bound to be out-
dated. Despite our constant effort to update all relevant sections and calculations once
new parameters have been set, or old ones have been redefined. It is impossible to en-
sure everything is fully amended. That is, however, to be expected, as this is only the
preliminary requirements definition. The continued development of each subsystem, by
dedicated teams, will highlight some of these errors. It is then the system engineers’ job
to disseminate these findings, update budgets and keep a birds-eye view of the mission
as a whole. A lesson to take away from this is the need for a structured collection of
subsystem parameters, i.e. simplified documentation that is easily updated and reviewed.

Conclusion
We have determined that it is possible to make an eo sar 6 U CubeSat for ice veloc-
ity measurements. Technological capabilities have increased over time, making many
components smaller, and better compared to the past.
The NewSpace era has introduced a vast amount of companies, which produce hard-
ware dedicated to SmallSat platforms. The fierce competition has resulted in a large
number of similar components present on the market. The number of solutions that
exist on the market can inspire the hardware design for our proposed spacecraft.
The increased use of SmallSats and CubeSat have resulted in more and more ambi-
tious projects being launched, such as the interplanetary relay CubeSats marco. Com-
mercial SmallSat eo sar constellations, Capella Space and iceye, are under implemen-
tations, which shows the maturity of the concept.
The development of SmallSat sar is at the forefront of technology. When we started
on the projected, back in February, iceye had launched two test satellites. iceye-x1
was launched in Januar 2018 and iceye-x2 was launched in December 2018. They have
since launched two additional spacecrafts on the 5th of June 2019, which are part of
the final constellation [44]. Capella Space has launched a test spacecraft named Denali.
More have been scheduled to be launched, although no date has been publicized as of
writing. Hence, the race for launching the first SmallSat sar constellation is currently
taking place.
dtu has previously been at the forefront of the CubeSat format. It now has the
opportunity to once again work with the cutting edge of technology and regain its
position as a torchbearer within CubeSat development. We therefore propose this eo
sar CubeSat, which combines science and engineering specialities found at dtu space.
A student project of such a scale will give students a true feeling for the complexity of
real space missions but at an affordable level.
7.1
Mission Summary
The final mission requirements as specified here and in appendix A have been iterated
over since their initial creation. The initial requirements were overly ambitious and
through analysis have been adjusted to be both satisfactory and realistic.
The mission will measure ice velocities, as well as other surface displacements. The
roi is Jakobshavn Isbræ, which shall be measured at least once a week. It is a glacier
==PAGE:82==
68

located in West Greenland, with the largest ice flux of any glacier in Greenland. 3 yrs is
the planned mission duration, with a possible extension period of 2 yrs.
An X-band sar payload will be used to capture the ice velocities. The choice of a
sar allows it to measure all year round regardless of sunlight and the microwaves allow
it to penetrate the often cloud-covered roi. The ground resolution will be 5 × 20 m in
the azimuth and range directions respectively. The spacecraft will be in a leo, with an
expected initial altitude of 525 km, to ensure it can survive through the extended mission
period. The expected inclination of 72.5 ° will be used as it allows for good coverage of
the roi. The satellite will be a 6 U CubeSat, making it a relatively large CubeSat. The
size, however, is still much smaller than a traditional eo satellite.
The mission will be student-run. This entails students designing, producing and ad-
ministering the entire mission, through student projects and extracurricular activity.
The mission will be part of the dtusat initiative, with mission control located at dtu.
The current dtusat ground station and control center are expected to be utilized. Op-
erating the spacecraft will be done primarily by students, as well as a board of scientist
preferably from dtu and/or other Danish national organizations. The data gathered will
be made available for researchers and emergency services, in the form of raw sar data
and ice velocity maps.
7.2
Satellite and Requirements
The spacecraft has been designed with six subsystems: the scientific payload, eps, Comm.,
obp, aocs, and the platform. The analysis and their corresponding theory that form
the basis for the requirements can be found in the prd. As this is only the preliminary
requirements many assumptions and conservative estimations have been performed dur-
ing the analysis. The current specifications are therefore expected to be refined as the
mission progresses. Likewise, certain disciplines have yet to be fully analyzed.
The requirements are defined to a subsystem level. The full list of all the requirements
can be seen in appendix A. Requirement traceability has been made and can be seen in
appendix B. All the requirements originate from the mission requirements.
The scientific payload is a sar that will utilize the X-band. It will have a sensitivity
of −17 dB allowing it to distinguish ice. The mean looking angle is 25 ° with a swath
width of 30 km and records for a duration equaling a length of 45 km. Each image will
generate 151.4 MB of data. The antenna’s size is 0.3 × 1.9 m. The sar will transmit a
peak power of 650 W.
The eps is going to use secondary batteries for energy storage in the form of lithium-
ion batteries and solar cells for power generation. The battery capacity is designed to be
31 W h. The spacecraft will be mounted with 19 body-mounted solar cells, and is sized
to deliver 5.7 W after 3 yrs. The average power consumption is estimated to be ≈4.39 W.
The Comm. subsystem will use two independent subsystems with separate bands,
uhf for the Comlink-R and S-band for the Comlink-H. Comlink-R will transmit an
informational beacon and will be used for receiving commands and debugging the space-

craft. It will have a data rate of 10 kbit s−1, achieved with whip antennas to provide an
omnidirectional link. ComLink-H is designed to only transfer the science data to ground
at a rate of 3 Mbit s−1, using a patch antenna.
The obp will control the sar, Comm., and aocs. It will provide a fail-safe mode to
ensure the spacecraft’s survival if any failures occur. It will have a mass storage of at
least 1.33 GB to allow for the collection of the large sar images.
The aocs is designed to have a rss mapping error of less than 1500 m. This means
it must have an attitude error less than 0.1 °, a positional error less than 100 m, and a
clock error less than 0.5 s. With a minimum peak torque of 13.7 µN m to ensure that it
can escape the stable attitude position.
The platform subsystem combines the thermal, structural and mechanical aspects of
the spacecraft. The internal mean temperature is expected to be 25 ◦C, with a 3 σ range
of −20 ◦C to 70 ◦C. The spacecrafts overall dimensions are 300 × 200 × 100 mm equalling
a 6 U CubeSat. Electronics must be able to withstand a tid of 40 krad to survive a 5 yr
mission in leo.
7.3
Mission Risks
Two types of risk analyses have been performed. The spacecraft’s and the mission’s risks
have been assessed. The risks have been assessed based on the defined rmp, found in
appendix F.
During the risk assessment of the spacecraft a total of 239 risks were found. Of those,
two are very high risk, six are high risk, 49 are medium risk, 92 are low risk, and 90 are
very low, resulting in an overall risk magnitude of low risk. This is an acceptable risk
magnitude, based on the rmp.
The mission risk assessment revealed a total of 92 mission risks. Only one is very
high risk, 14 are high risk, 18 are medium risk, 32 are low risk, and 27 are very low risk,
resulting in an overall mission risk magnitude of medium risk. This too is an acceptable
overall risk magnitude, a reduction of the risk should, however, be pursued. The overall
mission risk is expected to decrease once proper project management is implemented.
The risk assessments are not static documents. Risks will continuously be discovered
and documented. For the spacecraft the risks will primarily come from their dedicated
teams. Likewise, the mission risks will primarily come from the project managers. The
systems team shall then integrate and to mitigate them appropriately.

space mission. Following esa’s project cycles, phase A is completed. Going forth V-model

The obp should define two subsystems one dedicated to the hardware and one the

The integration of edac with the obp, must also be done. A trade-off analysis should

APPENDIX C

C.1
ECSS
The European Cooperation for Space Standardization (ecss) is in their own words “an
initiative established to develop a coherent, single set of user-friendly standards for use
in all European space activities.” [39]. The most prominent of their users is esa, which
sits on the steering board, and requires all their subcontractors to implement ecss.
The ecss is organized using a tree structure, seen in figure C.1. It is primarily the
engineering branch that is relevant for the development of preliminary requirements.
That said, all branches must be used to a certain extent.
Multiple document types exist in the ecss standards, handbooks and technical mem-
oranda. The standards normative documents are meant for use in business agreements.
The handbooks are non-normative and can provide guidance on how to satisfy standards,
and some handbooks are simply a collection of data usable as a common reference. Tech-
nical memoranda are again non-normative and provide useful information or data that
lacks maturity to become a standard or handbook.
You cannot use the ecss in isolation. The requirements are objective, as they stray
away from giving certain solutions. Some requirements are contradictory and must be
tailored to meet the specific applications. Other requirements simply define that a process
must be completed during the project.
The ecss is built on the expertise and past experiences of numerous space authorities
over several years. This means that, while not all applicable, all standards have a reason
for existing, which is often based on past mistakes.
C.2
Tailoring of ECSS
ecss standards are not meant to be implemented wholeheartedly. Instead the standards
most be tailored to the specific mission. This entails selecting which standards are rele-
vant to the overall project, then assessing the applicability of each requirement within
the standard, leading to the development of an earm. In the earm each requirement is
scored in applicability, see table C.1, and if required, the modification or new require-
ment is written next to it and a justification is given.
The vision of the ecss standards is to facilitate easier collaboration between compa-
nies through a common language in business agreements. That is, the development and
use of standards is intended for use in customer-supplier contracts to minimize mission

APPENDIX F
Risk Management Policy
Once a satellite is sent to space, it is almost impossible to provide physical support
or maintenance. Components need to be reliable, which means that they can perform
their required function, for the given operating condition for the specified time interval.
The system availability refers to the percentage of time in which the system operates
nominally during the mission. The three concepts, alongside safety, make up rams and
together describes dependability, which is part of the pa discipline. Maintainability is
lacking for spacecraft. It is therefore important to have considered the many things that
can go wrong, then work to either prevent them or prepare plans for the inevitable
failures. For this, risk management is an indispensable tool, as it works to mitigate
potential failures. It is important for all projects, but as noted earlier it is especially
important for space missions.
Risk management touches all aspects of the mission. It is just as important for
project management to ensure that the budget and schedule are kept, as for the systems
themselves.
The risk management policies and plans are heavily based on ECSS-M-ST-80C [31],
the ecss standard for risk management.
F.1
The Risk Method
A four-step approach to risk management is implemented heavily based on the ECSS-M-
ST-80C standard, [31]. The four steps are further subdivided into seven tasks (task two
is skipped from the original standard as it is more management technical). The first step
is part of the initial setup and the following three steps are repeated multiple times as
part of the risk management cycle. The steps and associated tasks can be seen in figure
F.1.
Step 1 defines the rmp. Defining the overall risk management ethic for the project
includes all the scoring methods, as well as how the risks are evaluated. The rmp is
where the acceptable risk for the project is defined, as it is used as a basis for all further
risks. According to the ecss, an additional task is to prepare the risk management plan.
This has been considered a project management task and is therefore skipped in an effort
of focusing on systems engineering.
Step 2 identifies and asses the risks. This is split up into two tasks, identification
and assessment. Task 2, identify risk scenarios, is where the risk is identified, but is also
where the causes and consequences should be determined. A method for determining

F.2
Resources
As this is a low-cost student-run satellite mission, the resources are limited. This means
that the focus will be on analyzing and finding contingencies for only the largest of
potential failures.
Redundancy is one of the traditional ways of increasing reliability but is of limited
use for this mission. This is because the CubeSat format lends to cheaper satellites and
faster development time but at the cost of risk mitigation and redundancy.
The goal is to identify all risks to the extent it is possible and reasonable. Once iden-
tified and assessed the risk should be documented, but unless it constitutes considerable
risk to the mission, no further work is required.
F.3
Schemes
The increased overall acceptable risk is reflected in the following schemes. As previously
stated, they are all based off [31], but have been modified to allow for higher risk.
The severity of the risk is scored on a 5-score scale ranging from negligible to catas-
trophic. The severity scores can be seen in Table F.1. The severity of consequences are as
follows: No impact. Minimal impact, failures that have sufficient mitigation that it has
no impact, e.g. bit flipping in communications with excessive error correction. Functional
impact, failures that result in temporary loss of function, e.g. bit flipping in communi-
cations with no error correction. Operational impact, failures that result in permanent
loss of current objections, e.g. wrong orbit where new targets are required. Leads to
termination of the project, e.g. fatal collision with a micrometeorite.
The likelihood of a risk manifesting is also scored on a 5-score scale. The likelihood
scores can be seen in Table F.2. The likelihood of occurrence ranges from almost never
occurring, i.e. 1 in 10 000 or more, to certain to occur once or more during the lifetime.
Together Tables F.1 and F.2 represent the scoring attributes used for all identified
risks. The combination of the severity and likelihood is called the ri.

the risk magnitude based on ri. It illustrates, as well as determines, the overall acceptable
risk. Table F.3 shows the proposed actions for the different ri. The table should be used
