ABSTRACT 
 
It is anticipated that climatic changes of extreme rainfall and urbanization will lead to climate 
changes that will have a negative impact on the environment and human life. It is of a great 
importance to find adequate climate adaptation solutions to maintain an acceptable risk of 
flooding as well as strategies that will improve the quality of life.  
This project presents cost-benefit analysis including costs and benefits for the green-blue 
infrastructure (BGI). The benefits include the benefits from flood risk reduction and the co-
benefits. The latter are the results of BGI that increase the total benefits and may substantially 
influence the outcomes of decision process (Hamilton et al., 2010), as they are rarely 
considered in quantitative decision-support framework due to lack of sufficient 
methodologies. BGI is currently very topical in city planning, as it provides valuable ecosystem 
services (ESS) that contribute to improving social life and human-environmental relations (co-
benefits). Therefore, this project focuses on quantification these co-benefits of ESS, and the 
following process has been employed. 
Firstly, the conceptual framework is established to identify ecosystem services and their co-
benefits based on Climate Adaptation Plan and Cloudburst Management Plan prepared by 
Copenhagen and Odense Municipality. Secondly, review of existing literature is used to 
evaluate different economic methods utilized to quantify and monetize the co-benefits. The 
key co-benefits used for quantification are: noise reduction (N), air quality (A), safety (S), 
urban farming (U), recreative value (R), physical health (H), and water quality (W). Thirdly, the 
use of the established framework is applied in a case study in Odense. The case study indicates 
that the introduced framework presented in this project can be considered as a significant 
decision support tool that develop existing decision practices to climate adaptation measures. 
Finally, the established framework is adopted by integrating climate change impact 
assessment, flood inundation modelling, economic tool, and risk assessment.  
The results demonstrated that the integration of co-benefits can alter the outcome of 
economic assessment, but it depends on what kind of land cover is chosen. In general, it has 
shown that the air quality, physical health, and safety have obtained the highest benefits for 
the investigated area, following by the noise reduction, recreative value and UHI.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Climatic changes of extreme rainfall and urbanization are major drivers of ecosystem change 
on the global scale (Sandhu et, al., 2013). They are associated with several negative aspects 
like the environmental degradation including water quality and quantity. It has a tremendous 
impact on people’s life quality, lifestyles, social interaction, loss of green spaces, effect on 
public health and water quality (Rambøll, 2016).  
At the same time, cities are facing challenges related to climate changes including flooding, 
draught, and heat waves. Both urbanization and climate change have a negative impact on 
the local hydrology and biodiversity. The surface perviousness is reduced due to rapid 
urbanization, by turning green areas into impervious spaces and increasing urban runoff at 
the same time (Rambøll, 2016; United Nations, 2014).  
Ecosystem services (ESS) are deﬁned as ‘‘the beneﬁts human populations derive, directly or 
indirectly, from ecosystem functions’’ (Bolund et al., 1999). Natural and modified ecosystem 
services provide quality of life to society and the individuals as well as improve cities economic 
and social sustainability (Københavns Kommune, 2016; Sandhu et al., 2013). The Common 
International Classification of Ecosystem Services (CICES) was developed by the European 
Environment Agency (EEA). They divided ecosystem services into three categories: 
provisioning, regulation, and maintenance and cultural (Haines-Young et al., 2018).  
In cities ESS, such as urban parks, forests, wetlands, and green belts play a fundamental role 
for increasing the quality of life. They provide essential services such as air and water 
purification, flood protection, and noise reduction. At the same time, they improve social and 
cultural services such as well-being, and provide recreational services for residents (Demuzere 
et al., 2014; Sandhu et al., 2013).   
Previous studies have developed different methodologies to valuate ecosystem services. They 
are divided into market and non-market techniques (Kaval et al., 2013). Market values are 
usually measured as direct use values, focusing on the actual use of a resource. Market values 
can be traded in formal markets, for example a decrease in productivity of a fish stock caused 
by an oil spill leading to an earnings loss of an angler.  
Methods for valuating non-market values consist of revealed preference and stated 
preference methods. Revealed preference approaches are based on observations of a 
behaviour, and this can give an information how individuals value changes in environmental 
quality. Stated preference approaches are based on survey questions. Commonly used non-
market valuation methods include the contingent valuation method, choice experiments, the 
travel cost method, and the hedonic pricing method (Kaval et al., 2013).  
 
Another valuation method commonly used in ecosystem services is benefit transfer or value-
transfer approach (Sandhu et al., 2013). Most of the economic studies which are carried out 
on ecosystem services are rather case specific and it is difficult directly transfer to other cities, 
regions or countries. Therefore, the valuation of ESS needs to be chosen carefully and ensure 
reliable and valid estimates provided to decision-makers (Kaval et al., 2013).

In this project, ecosystem services and their benefits are categorized that reflect mainly green

Project objectives 
 
This project has three focus points: 
1. Establish a framework to identify ecosystem services and their co-benefits for Odense 
Municipality. This part of the project reviews Climate Adaptation Plans prepared by 
Copenhagen and Odense Municipality, and The City of Copenhagen Cloudburst 
Management Plan (2012) to establish a framework what would fit to Odense context.  
2. Review existing literature to evaluate different economic methods applied to value 
co-benefits from step 1.   
3. Testify the feasibility of the established framework in a case study and assess the co-
benefits of implemented adaptation strategies. 
 
The next Chapter introduces to the background and the methodology of the project. The 
Chapter is structed as following: the first three chapters introduce to climate impacts, flood 
risk assessment including hazard and vulnerability analysis, and climate adaptation measures. 
The following chapter provides an information about ecosystem services and their co-benefits 
and presents the selected ESS for Odense Municipality.

2.2. CLIMATE CHANGE IMPACTS  
 
It is anticipated that Northern Europe will encounter more frequent and severe storms 
owning to climate changes (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2009). It is expected, that the average 
temperature will increase by 3.2 degrees within the next century. The total rainfall will rise by 
8-20%. Cloudburst is expected to increase by 19-31%. The level of sea water will be increased 
up to 1.4 (Odense Kommune 2014; DMI, 20.04.2018). Climate changes including flooding, 
draught, and heat waves, associated with urbanization process, have a negative impact on 
the local hydrology and biodiversity. The surface perviousness is reduced due to rapid 
urbanization, leading to an increased risk and intensity of flooding as well as rise pollution on 
the surface (Rambøll, 2016). The urbanization process has also a tremendous impact on 
people’s life quality, lifestyles, social interaction, loss of green spaces, effect on public health 
and water quality (Grimm et al., 2008). For instance, Odense is a city under development, it 
is expected to grow with 10 000 dwellings, which means that more than 200 000 residents 
are predictable to settle (Odense Kommune, 2014). Meaning that green spaces will turn into 
impervious spaces and increase urban runoff at the same time.

It acknowledged that anthropogenic climate modification leads to the urban heat island (UHI) 
effect, as cities tends to have higher air and surface temperatures compared to their rural 
surroundings (Grimm et al., 2008). The phenomenon of UHI in urban areas has a negative 
effect on the quality of life for the residents and may lead to increased public expenses on 
health and energy consumption (København Kommune, 2011).  UHI is not considered a severe 
problem for Odense as the weather very rarely presents periods of heat waves where 
temperatures increased up to 28 degrees. However, the climate adaptation plan recommends 
that the city planners will take into consideration when the city is developed, as the city’s 
green infrastructure are used to prevent UHI.  
Climate change challenges including flooding caused by storm surges and heavier rainfall, UHI 
and the phenomenon of urbanization will cause direct and indirect consequences for human 
and nature. Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan (2011) paid attention to the following 
indirect consequences: diseases, pollen allergies, biodiversity, air pollution and health effects. 
 
Diseases 
 
The warmer and damper weather my spread of salmonella infections and legionella bacteria.  
Great volumes of untreated wastewater (CSO) are discharged into watercourses, lakes, and 
harbours (Københavns Kommune, 2011). Consequently, CSO has negative impacts on water 
quality, pathogens, eutrophication, oxygen depletion, and all these processes effect life 
conditions for living organisms. Consequently, eutrophication leads to a reduction in the 
economic value of fishery (Københavns Kommune, 2012b).  
 
People will be exposed to infections from sewers, lakes, sea, and rivers due to excessive 
volumes of CSO, leading to an increased risk of infection in the respiratory organs and the 
gastrointestinal tract.  
More frequent extreme rainfalls may pose damage to buildings, and hence, this will lead to 
an increased humidity causing house dust mites and mould fungi (Københavns Kommune, 
2011). 
 
Pollen allergies 
 
The rise of temperatures can lead to an extension of the pollen season. This is a great of 
importance to allergy suffers. The Copenhagen Municipality recommends that the city 
planners will take into consideration the pollen problems while planning to plant trees and 
develop a new recreational green space (Københavns Kommune, 2011). 
 
Air pollution and health effects 
 
Air pollution causes diseases in humans and harm to the environment. Rising CO2 emissions 
causes acidification and increased nutrient loadings in runoff leading to eutrophication. The 
air pollution measured in cities comes primarily from the traffic. The associated air pollution 
emissions have a negative impact on human health including pre-mature mortality and 
morbidity from cardiovascular and respiratory reasons (Selmi et al., 2016).

Biodiversity  
 
Climate changes like higher temperatures and more intense precipitation can have a negative 
impact on city’s biodiversity. More frequent volumes of CSO can disturb the biological balance 
in watercourses. Previous studies carried out on how to protect and enhance the biodiversity 
recommend the implementation of more green initiatives which can also serve as stormwater 
removal. It is important to select plant and tree species that can tolerate the periods of 
intensive rainfall (Københavns Kommune, 2011).

analysis of hazards and vulnerabilities. The results from hazard and vulnerability analysis are 
integrated in Geographical Information System (GIS). This GIS-based risk model is also applied 
in this project, and the overview of the flood risk assessment framework is illustrated in Fig.2, 
based on Zhou et al., (2012).

The hazard assessment is used for the characterisation of floods, including the probability of 
flood occurrence of various magnitudes, analysis of overland flow paths, maximum water 
depth, and velocity. The most significant indicators of flood damage are flood extent and 
water depth, often called ‘depth-damage curves’. To analyse the hazard of the flood, the 
following components are needed: the extreme external loading of the system, such as 
rainfall intensity, the rainfall-runoff relationship, and the physical layout of a catchment (Zhou 
et al., 2012).  
The vulnerability analysis quantifies the damages due to flooding (Zhou et al., 2012). 
Hammond et al., (2015) presented definitions of direct and indirect damages caused by urban

contact of flooding with humans, property and the environment”. However, the indirect 
impacts may occur beyond the limits of the flood area, such as losses in cultural values. Other 
authors distinguish flood damages to tangible and intangible. Tangible damages can be 
monetized, while the intangible impacts cannot be direct quantified in monetary terms 
(Hammond et al., 2015).

2.4. CLIMATE CHANGE ADAPTATION MEASURES  
 
Cities are facing many complex challenges such as keeping the balance between urban 
development and its impact on the environment. It is expected that the world’s population 
will increase up to 66% by 2050 (UN, 2014), thus this demands continuously the supply for 
new infrastructure construction (Rambøll, 2016). 
Adaptive measures against extreme rainfall events should be designed that the sewer 
discharge reaches a ground level once every ten years, and average water levels can exceed 
ground level by 10cm once every 100 years (Københavns Kommune, 2012b).  Previous 
research confirmed that there is no simple solution to climate change challenges, and the 
adaptation strategies are often depending on the scale (Skovgård Olsen et al., 2015). It is also 
not possible to protect the entire city against extreme rainfall events covering the whole scale 
of severity (Københavns Kommune, 2012a). 
 
Infrastructures expansion due to climate change and increasing population are associated 
with a huge cost. Therefore, solutions which can solve more than one problem at the same 
time are needed. Solutions which keep the cost low, secures population and environment 
against climate change challenges, and bring additional value to the society (Rambøll, 2016). 
The Cloudburst Management Plan in Copenhagen (2012a) focuses on combination of 
measures that can handle extreme rainfall as well as reduce the pressure on the drainage 
system during heavy rainfall. 
Municipality of Copenhagen and Odense in their Climate adaptation strategy focus on green 
and blue elements adapted in the urban landscape which make both cities more attractive 
and improve life quality of residents. Climate adaptation strategy should promote integrated 
planning of the city and its infrastructure, so the population and the environment will benefit 
from it. For instance, an integrated development could be a green open space which 
contributes to reduction of heating, management of stormwater and increase recreational 
facilities (Københavns Kommune, 2011; Odense Kommune, 2014).  
These green and blue elements are defined by Rambøll as Blue-Green Infrastructure (BGI). 
BGI in urban areas meets the demand for both climate change adaptation and ecosystem 
services. Combination of blue and green elements is a superlative example for improving 
urban ecosystem services. BGI combines urban hydrological functions (blue infrastructure) 
with vegetation systems (green infrastructure) in urban environments. BGI contributes to 
various of hydrological functions such as reducing and slowing down runoff, local detention, 
groundwater recharge by infiltration of rainwater, evaporative cooling and improving water 
quality (Rambøll, 2016). 
BGI provides social-economic benefits, strengthen urban ecosystems, and combine the 
demand for sustainable water and stormwater management with the demands of urban life 
(Rambøll, 2016).

Blue and green services can be very efficient on different scales, depending on urban context, 
topography, and climate. In this project, BGI services are investigated only in Odense, 
Denmark.

2.5. CO-BENEFITS WITH FOCUS ON ECOSYSTEM SERVISES  
 
Co-benefits are defined as the benefits in the local scale as a results of adaptation strategies 
that are aimed at addressing global climate change (Hamilton et al, 2010). From the economic 
point of view, co-benefits are the results of climate adaptation actions that increase the total 
benefits (Hamilton et al., 2010). 
Hamilton et al., (2010) reviewed that the most significant environmental co-benefits account 
for energy, transport, agriculture, forestry, ecosystems, and biodiversity. Urge-Vorsatz et al., 
(2014) discussed co-benefits in terms of human health, ecosystem services, macroeconomic, 
social side effects. 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
11 
 
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) determined co-benefits as positive effects 
on human health in association with reductions in greenhouse emissions. IPCC discussed that 
co-benefits include health gains from strategies that are directed at climate adaptation 
strategies from well-chosen policies for health advancement. The literature on co-benefits 
related to health and climate change mitigation strategies includes reduction of greenhouse 
emissions in association with changes in energy sector, and the increase in urban green spaces 
(Miljøministeriet, 2014). 
With the increasing urbanization process, ecosystem services have received a special 
attention. Many researches focus on the increased understanding of benefits from ESS. For 
instance, ecosystem services produce plenty of benefits that are valuable to human well-
being. Carbon storage and sequestration are considered as ESS, provides the benefit of 
decreased CO2 emissions. Other services such as regulation of climate provide the benefit of 
the improved human thermal comfort (Demuzere et al., 2014). Andersson -Skold et al., (2018) 
reveals that the main purpose of valuation co-benefits of ESS is to define the interaction 
between ecological structures and human society.  
The Common International Classification of Ecosystem Services (CICES) divided ecosystem 
services into three categories: provisioning, regulation, and maintenance, and cultural. The 
full list of ecosystem services can be found in Haines-Young et al., (2018). Table 3 shows

refers to wetlands, harbour, lakes. BGI indicates the combination of blue and green elements.

2.6. LITERATURE REVIEW OF ECOSYSTEM SERVICES AND CO-BENEFITS 
 
In the first part of the procedure, 16 ESS and 3 SS are identified for Odense Municipality based 
on Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan (CCAP), Odense Climate Adaptation Plan (OCAP), and 
The City of Copenhagen Cloudburst Management Plan, 2012, see Fig. 1. The following step of 
the procedure included the review of existing literature to evaluate different economic 
methods utilized to quantify and monetize the co-benefits of ESS.  
In order to select and analyse the literature, the author conducted a search on Google and 
DTU Library, using terms regarding to co-benefits from ecosystem services, BGI, and urban 
green infrastructure according to Table 3. This process created 82 literature reviews including 
reports and scientific papers. The final findings were compared to the method implemented 
in Kildeskovsrenden in Gentofte Municipality. Kildeskovsrenden project was carried out by 
Rambøll (2017).  
The categorization of ESS and their co-benefits is challenging owning to multi-functional 
nature of BGI, and the multiplicity of interactions between these services and benefits 
(Demuzere, et al., 2014). For instance, an aesthetic detention pond provides ESS as a flood 
protection, but it may offer recreation value by improving the physical health, and practical 
education for climate adaptation.   
Different approaches can be used to estimate co-benefits, however they are rarely measured, 
qualified and monetized. They often stay at the rhetorical level, even though co-benefits 
inclusion in the quantities decision-making frameworks may significantly influence the 
outcomes (Urge-Vorsatz et al., 2014). Derkzen et al., (2015) provided non-economic method 
how to quantify six ESS co-benefits: air purification, carbon storage, noise reduction, run-off 
retention, cooling, and recreation. She conducted a spatial distribution analysis of these co-
benefits in Rotterdam. Andersson -Skold et al., (2018) also depicted non- economic method 
using Gothenburg city as a case study. She estimated effects of regulating and cultural ESS. 
The findings showed that the leaf area index is applicable for the regulation ESS, while 
diversity of species is relevant to the cultural ESS. 
This project aims to measure the economic value of co-benefits by using literature review. To 
find the best method for all co-benefits is not feasible as a diverse set of measures can be 
used for different scales of urban areas. For some indicators will be easier to find the best 
suitable method on larger regional scale or city scale, for others on local community scale 
within local neighbourhoods (Rambøll, 2016).

Values of ESS can be determined in monetary terms by using the direct approach such as 
current market prices of nature gas. However, this approach cannot be used to estimate an 
aesthetic value. For instance, questionnaire analysis including contingent valuation method 
have gained recently popularity. It is a stated preference method, meaning that a person 
“states” the willingness to pay (WTP) for something.

2.6.1. Urban farming 
 
Cultivated crops belongs to provisioning ecosystem services, and urban farming is the 
measure that is used for quantification.  In this project, the focus is on urban faming which 
can contribute to nutrition, food security, physical activity, mental health, and social and 
physical urban environments (Bellows et al., 2005). It is assumed that urban farming functions 
as community gardens which can be located only on green roofs.  
It is not easy to find a method to quantify community gardens. The majority of the previous 
studies used surveys, literature reviews, and case studies to document the environmental, 
and social impacts (Golden, 2013).  Environmental impacts of urban farming consist of 
managing storm water in a way of absorbing water. For example, the green roofs can absorb 
water from 50-80% of the annual rainfall, delay water before reaching the sewer system. This 
rainfall reduction depends on the green roof structure such as layers and depth, as well as 
climate conditions. In addition, green agriculture reduces temperatures in cities, decrease the 
feeling of UHI. Vegetation reduce temperature in buildings and at the same time increase the 
functionality of buildings, and cities by transforming the roofs to recreational areas like 
vegetable gardens (Københavns Kommune, 2012b). Urban farming creates safe spaces, 
improve the physical space of the neighbourhood, and has significant impacts on human 
relationships (Golden, 2013).   
 
There is a limited literature describing urban farming in economic terms.  Voicu et al., (2008) 
presented a few economic impacts: job creation and increased home values. He estimated 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 
16 
 
the effect of community gardens on neighbourhood property values, using a hedonic 
regression model. He found, that community gardens have positive impacts, in particular in 
the poorest neighbourhoods, as well as the higher quality gardens have the better positive 
impact.  
 
To estimate the economic value of urban farming, it was decided to use the benefit transfer 
method from the study conducted in Newark in 1996. The study revealed that community 
gardens produced 458 354 DKK per ha/year (Bellows et al., 2005). Newark city is a very 
populous city, having almost 100 000 more residents compared to Odense. However, the 
study was conducted 22 years ago, therefore it was decided to use this same value for 
estimation the economic value of urban farming.  
 
Community gardens placed on roofs have a better function in more congested places, like city 
centre, where residents have no direct access to green places where they can cultivate 
vegetables, fruits or herbs. As Voicu et al., (2008) estimated that the community gardens have 
a positive effect especially in the poorest areas. In Odense there are two poor areas called 
“exposed areas” in Solbakken mv and Vollsmose (Transport-, Bygnings- og Boligministeriet). 
 
2.6.2. Noise reduction 
 
Noise reduction is one of the co-benefits used to quantify regulation and maintenance ESS.  
In cities traffic is main source for noise that can cause health problems to people. The total 
cost of noise has been estimated of 0.2-2% of GDP in the EU (Bolund et al., 1999). In Denmark, 
maximum noise levels have been estimated by 58 dB for recreational areas in cities (parks, 
allotment gardens), and residential areas (Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark, 
23/04/2018).  
Exposure to noise pollution is linked to severe impacts on health such as communication 
problems, headaches, sleep disturbance, stress, high blood pressure, an increased risk of 
heart disease and hormonal effects (Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark, 
23/04/2018). Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark reveals that traffic noise is a 
source of 200-500 premature deaths on annual basis resulting in high blood pressure, heart 
disease and strokes. 
The question is how to minimize the noise level. In particular, trees contribute significant to 
the noise reduction. An important factor of noise reduction is the distance. By doubling the 
distance to the source of the noise, it decreases the equivalent level by 3 dB. Secondly, a soft 
lawn compared to a concrete pavement, decreases the level by another 3 dB. Thirdly, 
vegetation has also a positive impact on the noise reduction. Some sources claim that 50 m 
wide plantation can reduce the noise level by 3-6 dB, and the others argue that 100 m of 
dense vegetation lower noise level by 1-2 dB (Bolund et al., 1999).  
Noise is expressed as DKK per SBT (støjbelastningstallet) and marginal external cost of cars, 
trucks, buses and trains (COWI, 2010). SBT consist of inconvenience costs and health costs. 
Inconvenience costs are expressed as the willingness to pay (WTP) of the population to avoid

2.6.3. Safety (Flood risk reduction)  
 
Urban flooding can cause substantial disruption to services such as energy, water provision, 
transportation, housing, education, and employment. All services that people are reliant on. 
Therefore, cities need climate adaptation measure which will lower flood consequences. It is 
crucial to understand the impacts of flooding including the risk to life, damage to property, 
infrastructure such as transport and electricity (Hammond et al., 2015). 
Lamond et al., (2006) presented a study about the impact of flooding on the price of 
residential property. The study concerned the impacts of fluvial flooding in the UK and the 
analysis are based on the flood event from 2000. The impact of flooding is expected to 
increase due to climate change and human actions. The major conclusion from the study is 
that the impact of flooding on property value is temporary. In general, the future buyers of 
properties are unaware of possible risk to flooding to their properties.  
Cloudburst Management plan (2012a) recommends green and blue climate adaptation 
solutions that protects the city and residents against pluvial flooding. These solutions have 
positive effects on water-related ecosystems services and are divided into four typologies: 
stormwater roads, detention roads, green roads and detention areas (Københavns Kommune, 
2016).  
To quantify the safety co-benefit, the unit price (DKK/measure/year) of a climate adaptation 
measure will be used based on flood consequences described in Chapter 2.2. Firstly, EAD is 
calculated for the reference scenario. Secondly, the same process will be repeated for the 
scenario with the implemented measure, and the two scenarios are compared.  
2.6.4. Air Quality  
 
The third co-benefit that quantifies regulation and maintenance ESS is air quality. The major 
environmental and public health problem in cities is air pollution, which is caused by 
transportation and heating (Bolund et al., 1999). The emissions of O3 and ammonia (NH3) have 
negative impacts on urban ecosystems in forms of eutrophication, acidification, and damage 
to vegetation. Air pollution is the top of the environmental cause of premature death and 
morbidity from cardiovascular and respiratory causes (Selmi et al., 2016; EEA, 2014). In 2011, 
fine particulate matter (PM2,5) caused about 4000 premature deaths in Denmark (EEA, 2014).  
Previous research showed that there is a connection between the increased air pollution and 
hospital admission. In particular, the most exposed group are children between 2 and 14 years 
of age. Improved air quality will decrease respiratory illnesses, increased physical activity and 
at the same reduce obesity, cardiovascular disease, and social isolation while also improving 
mental health (Sharon et al., 2011).

2.6.5. Urban Heat Island 
 
Urban heat island is the last of the co-benefits that quantifies regulation and maintenance 
ESS. 
UHI is mainly caused by the anthropogenic heat released from industrial activities, vehicles, 
power plants, and the sun in the form of solar radiations. Generated heat during the day is 
stored in the structures, and then is released to the environment after the sun starts settling 
down (Voicu et al., 2008). The main problem occurs in the heavily built-up areas in a city, 
where spaces are covered with hard surfaces. Urban heat island effect has a negative impact 
on the quality of life for the individuals and may increase in public cost on energy consumption 
and health (Københavns Kommune, 2011). In Denmark there is approximately 0,5-1 °C 
warmer on annual basis in urban areas compared to the surrounding landscape, and in the 
summer period the temperature can increase in the range 3-10 °C (Miljøministeriet, 2008).  
 
Different studies have been carried out on the cooling effect of urban parks reflecting in the 
lower temperatures in the surrounding. For instance, Ca et al., (1998) tested that by planting 
a 0.6 km2 park could decrease air temperature by 1.5 C, and at the same time save of 4000 
kWh in an hour of a summer day.  The study was conducted in the Tama new Town in Japan. 
The temperatures are higher compared to Denmark, therefore the data cannot be directly 
transferable.

effect and moderate to strong indication for reduced temperatures. Meta-analyses used in 
his research demonstrated that on average a park was cooler by 0.94 °C compared to 
surrounding urban environments.  
 
McPherson et al., (2007) in his paper focused on planting trees which have significant

2.6.6. Recreative value          
 
Another measure to estimate the cultural ESS is the recreative value.  
The demand for more urban parks is growing due to the increasing urban population. There 
are more paved places as there is a bigger demand for new buildings and roads.  The 
remaining small green spaces in urban environment are being shared among an increasing 
number of individuals (Poudyal et al., 2008). 
Open space as well as public recreation improve the quality of life and the economy in urban 
areas by adding values to ecosystems services like air quality, recreational and aesthetic 
values. The size of an open space is a significant indicator, it affects not only the recreational 
potential and aesthetic value, but also determines the quantity and quality of ecosystem 
services provided to the neighbourhood (Poudyal et al., 2008). Urban green spaces have 
significant amenity values that providing leisure opportunities, outdoor recreation, and 
aesthetic enjoyment (Bjørner et al., 2014; Kong et al., 2007).    
Previous researches show that the price of a house increases with its distance to green areas, 
and the bigger the size of a green area increases the property values nearby. The latter has 
been estimated by the willingness to pay for proximity to planned urban green areas (Poudyal 
et al., 2008). 
Poudyal et al., (2008), presents the method using a two-stage hedonic framework to assess 
how the parks will increase the welfare of the society. The study was conducted in Roanoke, 
Virginia showing that both the proximity and the size of urban recreation spaces have a 
significant result on property values. The study also revealed that by increasing the present 
mean size of urban forest acres by 20% it will increase the customer surplus by 160 USD per 
household.   
Another research has been carried out in Mols Bjerge in the Eastern part of Jylland in 1996.The 
purpose of the project was to investigate WTP for accessibility for outdoor recreation. 3 300 
visitors have been questioned, and the estimated average WTP accounted for 30-50 kr. 
Dubgaard et al., (2003) used 40 kr. per visitor in his research, giving approximately 4 MDKK 
on yearly basis. 
Bjørner et al., (2014) estimated the value of recreational sites in Denmark, the method a two-
stage multiple-site travel cost has been used including spatial data on recreational trips,

demographics and socioeconomics for the population, and the characteristics of recreational 
places such as forests, open areas, beaches and parks in larger city. The whole description of 
the method is presented in Bjørner et al., (2014). They showed that the average annual

determines the value of recreational sites is the proximity to densely populated areas.  
Rambøll (2017) categorized the recreative value based on the quality of the area, the size of

2.6.7. Physical health

reduced of premature death, risk of coronary heart disease, hypertension, colon cancer, and 
non-insulin-dependent diabetes (Sherer et al., 2006). In Denmark, the physical inactivity costs 
the society 4,2 mia. DKK on annual basis, and approximately 3,1 million people are absent 
from work related to the physical inactivity.

Poor air quality, water pollution and urban heat island have negative effects not only on the 
physical but also the mental health. An urban green area can offer an aesthetic place for social 
and recreational opportunities, which enhance social relationships and promotes mental and 
physical improvement. Nutsford et al., (2013) discussed three main theoretical pathways 
which point out the connection between green spaces and mental health. Firstly, green 
spaces encourage to the physical activity which reduces stress levels. Secondly, social 
activities often take place in public green spaces, and this has been revealed to improve 
mental well-being. Thirdly, green places can serve as places for relaxation and recuperation 
from stressful situations. 
A research conducted by Stigsdotter et al., (2010) found that people living more than 1 km 
away from green space were 1.42 times more likely to experience stress than residents living 
in a distance smaller than 300 m from the green space.

3. RESULTS AND APPLICATION OF FRAMEWORK 
 
The results are structured as following: the first chapter provides the introduction to the study 
area, Dyrup. The following chapter provide assessment of benefits and costs. Subsequently, 
flood risk assessment is provided for the entire catchment Dyrup, and a climate adaptation 
measure will be implemented in a focus area. Note, that the climate adaptation measure is 
divided into technical cover (TC) and land cover (LC). According to Table 1 (Chapter 2.1), 
benefits and costs will be calculated for both TC and LC. The final findings from CBA will be 
presented in the conclusion of a case study. The last chapter depicts the developed 
framework for Odense Municipality based on Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan (CCAP), 
Odense Climate Adaptation Plan (OCAP), The City of Copenhagen Cloudburst Management 
Plan (2012a), and the literature review.  
3.1. 
INTRODUCTION TO THE STUDY AREA IN DYRUP 
 
A Danish case study is used to evaluate the feasibility of the framework for identifying 
ecosystem services and their benefits in different climate adaptation strategies.  
The study area is situated in the southern part of Odense, which is the third largest city in 
Denmark. Odense has a population of around 180 000 (Danmark Statistik, 2017), and lies on 
the island of Funen. The catchment size, Dyrup is 503 ha, and the main land use consists of 
residential, commercial, and public buildings. The study area is shown in Fig. 5 (with buildings, 
roads, green areas, river, and lakes). The mean annual precipitation for this area accounts for 
684 mm, and the mean exceedance daily rainfall consists of 27,7 mm/day.  
The area is fully developed, and it is assumed no further changes will occur apart from the 
suggested climate adaptation strategies. There are several lakes and green areas which 
provide a good possibility for potential water detention giving co-benefits to the area. The 
elevation in the catchment varies from 10 to 44 m above mean sea level.

4. DISCUSSION 
The aim of this project was to develop a method that can be used to assess co-benefits of ESS 
in Odense Municipality. The estimated co-benefits were included in cost-benefit analysis. 
The results indicated that the implemented technical cover was cost efficient in terms of flood 
risk reduction. It should be noted, the estimated costs of the open channels, pipes, detention 
basin, and the cost of building a new green area are based on different sources, advises of 
experts, and it should be viewed as an exemplification of the overall cost of investments. The 
damage cost of flooded buildings, roads, infrastructures, and green areas are taken from 
Skovgård Olsen et al., (2015), where the prises are giving an overview of the damage classes.  
Three strategies with different land covers were tested to quantify the co-benefits. Results 
presented in this project show that the forest scenario for Case A increased the outcome in 
economic flood risk assessment compared to baseline scenario. The dominant co-benefit for 
Case A was the air quality. It was assumed that the area covered by forest does not have a 
recreative value, thus it does not contribute to physical health. However, others can argue 
that trees can generate substantial recreational values, and thus contribute to the physical 
health. There are many who enjoy running, walking among trees. If the quality of forest was 
classified as the quality of greening area, i.e.  33% of the full price of the quality area (Table 
12), then calculated NPV for 50 years with 3% discount rate would account for 32 MDKK.  
The strategy with the green area with recreative value also obtained a positive value of NPV, 
however, it is lower compared to the reference situation. The green area selected for the 
adaptation strategy, consists of 3ha, therefore removing the soil, planting new trees, and 
rebuilding the park from the beginning is a very costly investment. It is recommended to 
prioritize tree preservation over the tree planting (Groth et al., 2008). Diverse literature 
review confirmed that older trees have greater benefits for cooling, air pollution reduction 
and stormwater management. However, if the selected area for the adaptation measure 
would be covered by, for instance, a parking lot without any vegetation.  Thus, the value of 
co-benefits would be much higher.  
This project focused on the quantification of the co-benefits with regards to climate change 
adaptation. The project demonstrated that that there is a huge variety of literature that can 
help to estimate the co-benefits of ecosystem services. The analysis also showed that it 
remains difficult to estimate the co-benefits, as in many cases value transfer from other study 
sites are not relevant for Odense Municipality. There is a big difference between results 
regarding to the air quality. Benefit transfer method has been used to estimate the air quality. 
The result of the air quality based on data obtained from Strasbourg (Selmi et al., 2016) is 
much lower compared to data from Virginia Beach (The Trust for Public Land, 2011). It shows 
that different cities generate various results based not only on tree covers, age and tree 
condition but also on variances in ambient air quality and preferences of the people living 
there. Therefore, it is recommended to conduct research focusing on air pollution removal by 
urban trees to quantify ecosystem services across Denmark.  
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
 
50 
 
Two cases were tested to measure health (H) and recreative value (R). In case A, the values 
from two distances were summed, giving a higher value of co-benefits for the park (Table 21). 
In Case B, the mean values were considered. Assuming, the pollutant removal rate of urban 
trees from Strasbourg, the only beneficial adaptation strategy would be the green area with 
the recreative value. However, the calculated NPV for 50 years including costs and benefits, 
for baseline scenario is still higher. In this situation, it would be advisable to not modify the 
entire land cover, just implement the climate adaptation solution i.e. the technical cover.  
Urban farming was chosen for the framework, as it is the only indicator that can be used to 
quantify provisioning ESS for Odense Municipality. However, urban farming was not 
considered in the case study in Dyrup, as the catchment consists of mainly residential and 
industrial buildings. It means, that the residents have their own gardens, and by implementing 
a community garden would not provide a high value to this area.

centrum of Copenhagen, see Fig.17. It is recommended to conduct research focusing on 
collecting data from this place regarding to the production of vegetables, fruits, employment, 
education, and increased home values, according to Golden (2013). Consequently, this data 
could be transfer to other potential places in Denmark.

4.3. MASLOW’S HIERARCHY OF NEEDS  
 
Maslow’s hierarchy of needs can be adapted as an additional framework for the assessment 
of the priority of co-benefits in climate change adaptation based on Yawson et al., (2009).  
Many cities have developed risk management plans which include adaptation strategies to 
minimize hazards and vulnerabilities due to climate change. For example, In Odense and 
Copenhagen there is a big focus on blue-green solutions defined also as BGI by Rambøll. These 
solutions contribute to various hydrological functions such as reducing and slowing down 
runoff, local detention, groundwater recharge by infiltration of rainwater, evaporative cooling 
and improving water quality, which are crucial for flood risk management. In addition, these 
solutions have a tremendous impact on human well-being and health.  
It occurs frequently that co-benefits of climate adaptation strategies are ignored in decision 
making due to lack of sufficient information. Maslow’s theory of needs could help individuals 
and policy make decisions based on choices and prioritization.  
Fig. 18 shows the classification of five human needs: psychological, safety, belongingness and 
love, esteem, and self-actualization. It illustrates the hierarchical order in which these needs 
are accomplished and give satisfaction to an individual. How Maslow’s need can be applied 
to the prioritization of ESS co-benefits? 
1. Psychological needs refer to the most fundamental needs by which human being can 
survive like water, food, shelter, clothing, and others. In terms of co-benefits, at the 
lowest level it can be represented by water and air quality, health, urban agriculture. 
 
2. Safety needs relate to desire for security and protection from harm. Co-benefits can 
be seen in their needs for protection of flood, vulnerable areas due to climate change, 
microclimate regulation (UHI), noise protection, exposed urban areas. Improving living 
conditions lead to the enhancement of national and individual incomes.  
 
3. Belongingness and love needs refer to the need for affection. When individuals fulfil 
their basic needs such as survival and safety, they move on to satisfy the need for 
companionship and care. Here, belongingness can be seen as global or regional 
collaboration between supply companies, municipalities, other countries. 
 
4. Belongingness and love needs refer to the need for affection. When individuals fulfil 
their basic needs such as survival and safety, they move on to satisfy the need for 
companionship and care. Here, belongingness can be seen as global or regional 
collaboration between supply companies, municipalities, other countries.  
 
5. Esteem needs characterised by prestige and feeling of accomplishment. Here, the 
need for esteem can be considered as branding and tourism.  
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
 
52 
 
 
6. Self-Actualization is the peak in Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, and refers to as 
transcendence, appreciation of beauty and nature. Here, the need for self-
actualization can be seen as maximum satisfaction of achieved growth, sustainable 
development. Co-benefits like aesthetic value, accessibility to outdoor recreation, 
recreational services, sensory experience. 
 
Figure 18: Hierarchy of co-benefits based on Maslow's hierarchy of needs (Yawson et al, 2009).  
By adopting Maslow’s hierarchy of needs as a framework for prioritization of co-benefits, 
could help effectively and efficiently allocate and manage flood risk management to achieve 
desirable goals.  
This project focused on the psychological and safety needs in terms of Maslow’s hierarchy. 
The findings in this paper and Maslow’s hierarchy can confirm that co-benefits such as urban 
farming, air quality, UHI, noise reduction, safety, water quality, physical health, and recreative 
value are the fundamental needs for existence. All these needs can be fulfilled by 
implementing a green space. However, in the current urban development, it is not enough to 
build a green space with trees, but the aesthetic aspects play a vital role. Therefore, this value 
should be closer investigated.

5. CONCLUSIONS 
 
The purpose of this project was to quantify co-benefits on improving social life and human-
environmental relations to the implementation climate adaptation strategies. Additionally, 
the quantified co-benefits were included in CBA. 
The project demonstrated that there is a huge variety of literature that can help to estimate 
ESS co-benefits in relation to climate change adaptations. Many studies provide empirical 
evidence that design of the green infrastructure can decrease the vulnerability of urban areas 
as well as enhance ecosystem services (Demuzere et al., 2014; Derkzen et al., 2015; 
Andersson-Skold et al., 2018). Others believe, green services are not recognised as a valuable 
opportunity to create multifunctional landscapes with focus on ecological, socio-economic 
benefits and sustainable urban storm water practice. The explanation for this is that the 
implementation of green infrastructure goes beyond the technical solutions (Rambøll, 2016).  
The methodology used in this project can help answer the following questions: What ESS are 
relevant for Odense Municipality? Which climate adaptation strategy contributes the most to 
ESS? Having this knowledge, Odense Municipality have the possibility to improve the 
liveability and attractiveness of the city for their residents.   
The main conclusions of this project are:   
• The analysis in this paper showed that green infrastructure contributes the most to 
ecosystem services and offer the highest social-economic benefits. The suggested 
climate adaptation measure combines the demand for sustainable water and 
stormwater management with enhancing community activities and recreation. 
 
• The conceptual framework was established to identify ecosystem services and their 
co-benefits for Odense Municipality. The key co-benefits used for quantification of ESS 
in this project are: noise reduction (N), air quality (A), safety (S), urban farming (U), 
recreative value (R), physical health (H), and water quality (W).  
 
• The use of the established framework was applied in a case in Dyrup. A case study 
indicates that the introduced framework presented can be considered as a significant 
decision support tool that develop existing decision practices to climate adaptation 
measures. 
 
• The analysis also showed that it remains difficult to quantify the co-benefits. The most 
common method used to estimate the co-benefits is the value transfer. However, 
benefit transfer method from other studies is challenging as the obtained values 
depend on topography, climate, culture, and economic situation.  
 
 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
 
 
54 
 
• The literature review demonstrated evidently that urban vegetation is the most 
effective technique to reduce UHI, noise pollution, and improve the air quality, in 
particular when trees are involved. It has proven that urban vegetation is an important 
characteristic to quantify regulation & maintenance ESS.  
 
• The analysis of co-benefits confirmed that bigger areas of green spaces are more 
valuable than the small-sized parks. However, increasing green spaces in urban 
landscapes will lead to a reduced space for the other significant urban functions.   
 
• The results demonstrated that the integration of co-benefits can alter the outcome of 
economic assessment. It has shown that the air quality and physical health have 
obtained the highest benefits for the investigated area, following by the noise 
reduction, recreative value and UHI. Safety co-benefit was calculated as a flood risk 
reduction (ΔEAD) and it is recommneed to find another method to quantify this 
benefit.  
 
• The project demonstrated that it is more beneficial to plant forest, or not to 
implement changes to the entire existed green space as there is a great loss of co-
benefits in relation to the cost. To avoid this loss, it is suggested to conduct a spatial 
assessment of ESS co-benefits in Odense Municipality. The purpose for this is to 
identify uneven distribution of co-benefits in order to find the suitable allocation for 
green infrastructure. This spatial assessment of ESS will give an understanding to city 
planners how the ESS co-benefits are distributed in Odense.   
 
 
• Future research should provide investigation on the impact of urban trees and green 
spaces on local air quality in Denmark to ensure air purification and sustain human 
health in urban landscapes.
