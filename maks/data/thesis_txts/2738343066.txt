Abstract 
This thesis explores the landscape of scaling strategies to achieve positive societal impact, focusing 
on the transition from startup to scale-up. The research addresses a relatively unexplored area 
(Francois & Goi, 2023), emphasizing the critical need for evolving impact strategies. These strategies 
aim to serve organizations with the substantial mission of contributing to economic and social well-
being. Several researchers have highlighted social entrepreneurship as a crucial contributor to these 
changes (Bloom & Smith, 2009; Kamaludin et al., 2024). However, many startups struggle to scale 
effectively while staying true to their initial mission and core values (McLean & Garrgani. 2019). 
Throughout this research, a qualitative methodology has laid the foundation for extending existing 
theoretical observations with new insights from various key stakeholders working towards positive 
change in both existing and new industries. The analysis has evolved using the Gioia methodology 
(Gioia et al., 2012) to systematically code the empirical findings and create a data structure 
incorporating new theory around scaling impact. The findings highlight the essential role of 
leveraging unique organizational capabilities and strategic market engagement, utilizing market 
opportunities through continuous improvement. 
 
Moreover, based on the results of the analysis, the research´s implications have created a strategic 
iterative framework. This framework visualizes the interplay between strategic orientation, dynamic 
capabilities and scaling forms, providing a holistic overview to empower individuals to sustain 
businesses with a long-term focus. It emphasizes strategic patience in scaling through constant 
assessment of values, needs, and resources to lower risks and heighten the chances of a successful 
scaling process that serves a broader purpose. A combination of theoretical observations and factual 
work has established a framework that offers practical guidance for scaling impact-driven ventures.

1 Introduction 
The first section of this thesis is dedicated to establishing the underlying awareness and interest in 
exploring scale-up strategies for positive change and conducting the research’s path and structure. 
 
1.1 Background 
In today's rapidly changing world, scaling appears as a straightforward replication of a technology or 
innovation across different locations. However, the concept must be more genuine for social 
entrepreneurs driven by a mission to effect substantial change. Standing in the middle of the 21st 
century, the planet and its people are witnessing profound transformations, some for the better and 
others for the worse. The United Nations secretary-general, António Guterres, states that "everything 
we do affects the world around us," illustrating our deep interconnections and that no humanity stands 
alone (United Nations, n.d).  
 
The global demand for startups is growing, with social entrepreneurship being recognized as a key 
contributor to economic and social well-being. Research from the World Economic Forum (2024) 
reveals that there are over 10 million social enterprises worldwide, accounting for 3% of all global 
businesses. These entities stand out from traditional organizations by their dual focus on creating 
social and environmental value alongside economic returns. Better known as scaling impact, this dual 
focus meets the strategic aim that scaling is not limited to increase profitability, but to enhance quality 
and innovate enduring benefits for society and environmental purposes to scale the level of need (Han 
et al, 2023; McLean & Gargani, 2019). 
 
The challenges and risks associated with scaling are not to be underestimated and include the need 
for adaptive strategies that can meet the unique demands of varied local contexts (Dees et al, 2004). 
According to Swanson & Di Zhang (2010), entrepreneurs do more than simply act in a socially 
responsible manner because they include social and environmental change as part of their missions. 
This underscores the complexity of scaling and the need for in-depth research and understanding to 
overcome these challenges. Despite the growing attention on impact, many startups struggle to scale 
effectively (McLean & Garrgani. 2019), highlighting the critical need for evolved scaling strategies 
that maintain core missions, while scaling, inspiring a new generation of entrepreneurs.

2.1.1 Social Entrepreneurship 
Social Entrepreneurship (SE) has been a well-known and used area of research in the 21st century, 
where elevated attention to acting as a catalyst for social change has contributed to environmental 
and socio-economic justice and development (François & Goi, 2023). It is important to emphasize 
that there are identified additional definitions of SE, and there is not any unified definition: however, 
one of the critical factors stated is the differentiation from traditional entrepreneurship, where the 
primary goal is to create social value rather than gaining private economic welfare (Gupta et al., 
2020). The European Commission acknowledges Gupta et al.'s (2020) perspective of social 
entrepreneurship and emphasizes the importance of creating social impact (SI) rather than building 
profitable businesses (European Commission). Additionally, Zahra et al. (2009) recognize SE as "the 
activities and processes undertaken to discover, define, and exploit opportunities in order to enhance 
social wealth by creating new ventures or managing existing organizations in an innovative manner." 
This perspective can argue that SE's purpose lies in building activities and processes that maximize 
impact through a scalable business model (BM), where innovation is crucial (François & Goi, 2023).  
 
The difficulties occur in the usage of SE as a theoretical framework due to the complexity of placing 
a single value creation on the contribution made by a social enterprise (Weerawardena & Mort, 2006). 
To form a unique area of SE, Weerawardena and Mort (2006) indicate that behavioral 
entrepreneurship depends on both the social mission and continued efforts of sustainability that 
cannot exist without each other.

2.1.2 Sustainability 
Sustainability is a debated term that, like SE, can be difficult to define; however, according to 
Kamaludin et al. (2024), the core aspect stands clear in its coverage. Disendorf (2000) argues that 
sustainability is an endpoint of a sustainable development process. The Brundtland report from 
WCED (1987) supports that "sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the 
present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." This 
definition of sustainability highlights the long-term perspective of an ethical principle between the 
present and future generations (Disendorf, 2000).  
 
Additionally, sustainable development can be defined as numerous types of economic and social 
developmental initiatives that promote and protect the natural environment and social equity. 
Positioning social entrepreneurship to sustainability, social organizations need to demonstrate the 
ability to both accomplish impact and operational outcomes to express sustainability. Potentially, 
social organizations can achieve sustainability by reducing operational costs as they increase their 
impact; Kamaludin et al. (2024) state that “social entrepreneurship is the most sustainable and 
impactful way to bring change.” Moreover, the integration of social, environmental, and economic 
goals expresses the connection between SE and sustainability. Organizations that continuously 
improve operational efficiencies by monetizing social value achieve sustainability, aligning 
profitability with social and environmental impact (Kamaludin et al., 2024). 
 
2.1.3 Scaling Impact 
As we delve into the review of social entrepreneurship and sustainability, it is crucial to understand 
the theoretical background that underpins the process of scaling impact. McLean and Gargani (2019) 
emphasize that scaling impact is not a simple task but a complex journey filled with challenging 
decisions. It is a process where trade-offs and scaling values must be carefully considered. The 
essence of scaling is not just resource allocation but adopting broader ways of thinking that can 
navigate the uncertainty of scaling (McLean & Gargani, 2019). 
 
We have, through decades, experienced the conventional efforts of scaling up that reflect a procedure 
in which organizations, rather than impact, are scaled up, where bigger is better, and where 
organizations are driven by commercial success (McLean & Gargani, 2019).

The lean scaling development process is a well-known paradigm used in the 21st century (Ries, 
2011). The core principle revolves around building a minimum viable product (MVP), bringing it to 
market, learning about customer behavior, modifying, pivoting, and repeating. This paradigm is 
developed to achieve commercial success rather than impact. According to Austin et al. (2006), 
growth for the sake of growth can be a waste of resources and potentially lead to an insignificant 
impact. Scaling in this context is identical to growth, where more seems better; however, McLean 
and Gargani (2019) see the existing paradigms as valuable when they apply to impact but rather 
incomplete. The interest in scaling impact arises when developing scaling efforts – in this case, more 
is not always better. This concept is what they call Scaling Science that will be expanded in section 
2.5 (McLean & Gargani, 2019).  
 
2.1.3.1 Scope of Social impact 
The scope of social impact is set by the fundamental purpose of social entrepreneurship and created 
by social organizations, which place the mission of the individual social organization (Kamaludin et 
al., 2024). While commercial companies can beneficially improve and provide social value, the 
significance stands out, balancing the commercial and social mission to enhance the social impact 
(Austin et al., 2006). According to Ebrashi (2013), social entrepreneurs can, through network 
environments, learn and understand their social missions to create sustainable change, where change 
should focus on the level of impact rather than the organization's potential outcome (Kamaludin et 
al., 2024). However, the scope of social impact goes beyond the ideation of tackling immediate 
problems; instead, it sees the bigger picture of creating long-term systemic change. 
 
2.1.3.2 Scope of Scaling  
This literature review of scaling aims to manifest the differentiation of what scaling has been defined 
as “scale what works” in a business context to now narrow the understanding towards increasing the 
impact. While the initial definition of scaling highlights the economic benefits and exponential 
growth (Bohan et al., 2024), scaling impact differentiates itself by expanding impact rather than 
growing the organization (Uvin et al., 2000). McLean and Gargani (2019) highlight that scaling 
impacts bigger and faster might not always be for the better. In this manner, scaling science has 
created a new approach focusing on impact over actions to create an optimal scale. As a result, the 
core of its purpose can create challenges in successfully generating the intended impact on a larger 
scale, as the scaling term changes its purpose and builds higher uncertainty due to limitations in

2.2 Three Levels of Transformational Mechanisms  
Adding to the overview of conceptual theoretical background, Horne and Fichter (2022) have created 
a multi-level framework that highlights two main perspectives by exploring existing entrepreneurship 
and strategic management literature. They discuss the internal and external enablers that assist 
startups in achieving sustainable growth, focusing on aligning business models with sustainability 
goals. They emphasize that critical paths will follow when implementing sustainable business 
innovations, which works closely with the research question on how startups can transition to impact 
scaling. Their point of view considers that every startup with better solutions than the existing 
circumstances in the socio-technical regime is favorable for sustainable development (Horne & 
Fichter, 2022).

In Figure 1, Horne and Fichter (2022) illustrate the three levels of transformational stages on the 
vertical line. The Transformational mechanism (TM) explains the collective effects of multiple 
ventures (Miso-level), markets (Meso-level), and grander institutional landscapes (Macro-level).  
These levels are dynamically interconnected, showing how individual startups influence and are 
influenced by broader market trends and institutional policies. This approach allows a deeper 
understanding of how startups respond to external pressures and act as agents of change, shaping or 
being shaped by the ecosystems (Horne & Fichter, 2022).  
 
The first step in the multi-level framework is when an idea goes from the protected niche to the second 
step, where it matures and outgrows and is activated by a change agent or, for this research's purpose, 
a startup (TM 1). In the third step, the startup transforms from within the higher level of miso to scale 
the operations into the socio-technical regime (TM 2). The socio-technical regime indicates how the 
organization can meet the competitive pressure. Now, if the startup successfully gains market share, 
it can transform the market by contributing to change in the market on a meso-level (TM 3). This 
framework's positive impact refers to sustainable net benefits, where the increase in market share 
contributes to sustainable transition (Step 5). Lastly, the net benefits of sustainability can bring the 
organization to a macro level, where the given value contributes on a larger scale (Horne & Fichter, 
2022). 
 
While Horne and Fichters' (2022) conceptual framework gives a simple transformational overview, 
they argue that in the real world, this pathway would possibly be more like winding paths. These 
winding paths can be affected by external and internal enablers that magnitude the likelihood of 
successfully scaling impact. 
 
The area of external enablers (EE) builds on the circumstances that can enhance entrepreneurial 
endeavors. Davidson (2015) defines EE as encompassing new technologies, regulatory changes, 
sociocultural shifts, economic trends, and environmental factors. These dimensions can vary 
depending on the context but reinforce a trigger to shape new business models and enhance 
entrepreneurial success. In this conceptual framework, EEs can create positive and negative effects, 
where entrepreneurial activities depend on such opportunities and barriers. Given the context of each 
step, if the transformational levels are successfully met, EE will affect the impact, which is why EE 
is crucial for potential growth and the impact of startups (Horne & Fichter, 2022).

Inspired by the resource-based view (RBV) from Barney (1991), the internal factors build on the 
effect resulting from valuable resources, which are rare, difficult to imitate, and challenging to 
substitute. To extend the RBV, Horne and Fichter (2019) have incorporated the dynamic capabilities 
perspective (Teece et al., 1997), which enables the practical decision-making and resilience of 
ventures. To frame the enablers, the European Federation of Quality Management (EFQM) has 
outlined five types of specifications for the sustainability assessment of startups: (1) Team; (2) Vision 
and Strategy; (3) Processes; (4) Partnerships and resources; (5) Product/services and market position. 
Horne and Fichter (2019) further extend the scope of these five types, highlighting how they can vary 
based on the organization's stage and focus. This aspect underscores how crucial understanding and 
leveraging these enablers are for positioning startups for successful impact and competitiveness in 
their respective markets (Horne & Fichter, 2019).  
 
2.3 The SCALERS Model 
In social entrepreneurship, scaling social impact has gained recognition, signifying innovative change 
strategies that diverge from traditional approaches. However, challenges have occurred in replicating 
the initial ideas on a larger scale. In that manner, Bloom & Chatterji (2009) have developed the 
SCALERS model that identifies seven organizational capabilities: Staffing, Communicating, 
Alliance-Building, Lobbying, Earnings-generation, Replicating, and Stimulating Market Forces (see 
Figure 2). Bloom and Smith (2010) argue that these drivers have the potential to provide new 
directions for theoretical and empirical studies on scaling social impact. As with Horne and Fichter 
(2019), the SCALERS model has adopted the dynamic capabilities (Teece et al., 1997) that define 
the organization's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external capabilities to 
accommodate changing conditions.

Staffing, the initial element in the acronym, refers to the organization's capability to attract suitable 
candidates for appropriate roles. This aspect highlights the organization's meticulous personnel and 
human resource management (HRM) prioritization. Bloom and Chatterji (2009) acknowledge the 
importance of human resources in supporting organizational growth, which has been recognized for 
a long time in management literature. The efficacy of HRM significantly impacts organizational 
performance based on the human resource policies and practices inside an organization. Such 
capabilities are crucial for increasing the ability to attract, retain, and motivate their employees. 
However, Bloom & Chatterji (2009) also acknowledge that the role of staffing in driving scaling 
varies depending on the specific needs and the strategic requirements of the change strategy. 
Moreover, adequate staffing is recognized to improve effectiveness across all other SCALERS. 
Synergies and effects between the SCALERS exist, which can influence the organization's capacity 
to scale its social impact (Bloom & Chatterji, 2009). 
 
The SCALERS' following capability is Communicating, which refers to effectively convincing key 
stakeholders to support and adopt the change strategy. Bloom & Chatterji (2009) manifest that 
improving communication about an organization's services and change strategy can significantly 
enhance its capacity to scale. This aspect of the SCALERS relies on the persuasion that what the 
organizations are doing has value to those they are targeting. Scaling social impact can be challenged 
by inactivity, where it is necessary to break people out of old habits to achieve social change. This 
challenge underscores the importance of finding the "right" framing, where understanding culture and 
audience needs is established to generate a faster scale and more significant impact. For social 
entrepreneurs to create a practical understanding of the conditions, research is central. According to 
Bloom and Smith (2010), social entrepreneurial organizations often rely on intuition rather than 
rigorous research. As with staffing, the communicating aspect can become redundant for driving 
successful scaling, which can occur when there is already a public extent of the change strategy. 
Therefore, organizations in those situations depend more on their effectiveness at alliance-building, 
lobbying, and replicating (Bloom & Chatterji, 2009). 
 
Alliance-building is the third capability of the SCALERS, which corresponds to the linkages of 
partnerships, coalitions, joint ventures, etc. In this aspect of the SCALERS model, an organization 
goes beyond operating by itself and seeks to leverage the advantages of collaborative efforts. Bloom 
and Chatterji (2009) state that research shows alliance-building significantly impacts successful

Earnings Generation refers to the organization's ability to effectively orchestrate revenue building

legitimacy and affects the possibilities of empowering change (Bloom & Smith, 2010).

2.5 Four Guiding Principles for Scaling Impact 
In further research on potential positive change, McLean and Gargani (2019) explored the four 
principles for scaling impact. The essence of building upon different theoretical frameworks rests on 
capturing innovators' considerations for scaling, both before, doing, and after. Scaling impact is a 
complex process, where the bounded nature of frameworks can appear risky with innovation present, 
as the path can change radically to seek desirable impact and where scaling matters (McLean & 
Gargani, 2019). 
 
Guiding Principle 1: Scaling is a choice that must be justified – In the literature surrounding the 
theoretical framework of the four guiding principles of scaling, justification emerges as a crucial 
cornerstone. In the act of achieving impact, substantial pressure, such as the desire to help others, the 
urgency of a crisis, the ask of the funders, and the motivation for a particular form of reward can 
occur. This pressure has merged into a norm, where scaling becomes a requirement instead of a choice 
(McLean & Gargani, 2019).

The striving is displayed by more being better, as the belief is built upon scaling imperative, where 
"big problems require big solutions" (McLean & Gargani, 2019). To avoid the scaling imperative, 
the decision to scale must be justified. As an entrepreneur, you are responsible for the actions of your 
inventions and the people affected by the innovation. This may cause uncertainty around the fact of 
scaling; however, this is a balance of anticipating the potential benefits and risks and learn what risk

to success. Optimal scale refers to a multidimensional concept emphasizing the importance of a 
nuanced collection of quantitative and qualitative aspects that collectively determine a meaningful 
magnitude for addressing social and environmental challenges. This concept can make wonder about 
who defines what is good change, where a way to accommodate the uncertain definition is to ask the 
affected and is a method to reconcile various perspectives that leads to an inclusion of stakeholders 
throughout the scaling process (McLean & Gargani, 2019). 
 
Drawn upon the first guiding principle, justification, the holistic concept of optimal scale can endorse 
a comprehensive framework that justifies the selection of innovation and creates a more profound 
understanding of potential impact and viability across contexts and stakeholders. As a result of scaling 
innovation, new possibilities occur both intended and unintendedly as the collection of impacts 
changes. Infrequently, scaling has only been involved in the first declared goal of impact, which 
optimal scale can unveil by the four dimensions of impact (Magnitude, variety, sustainability, equity) 
using the DIA model that helps place the impact in categories (see Appendix A). To achieve optimal 
scaling for impact, stakeholders should endorse the balance of interests, values, needs, and resources.

This section follows a theoretical background of the five R´s: readiness, receptivity, resources, risk, 
and returns. In the area of scaling social impact, Lisbeth Schorr observed that "if we are serious about 
tackling social problems on a large scale, we need to develop more effective tools to address this 
challenge. " Dees et al. (2004) concluded that if we want to create social impact, taking a step back 
is necessary, following the need for adopting more systematic and strategic approaches that allow

assessment of how social entrepreneurs can find the best path for them before jumping to decisions.  
In the research of finding the most promising strategy for social impact, there are three mechanisms 
for expanding impact strategies – dissemination, affiliation, and branching. Dissemination strategies 
are like an open-source approach, where the central advantage is rapid expansion possibilities through 
low costs and efforts. However, the lack of control can appear in utilizing the dissemination due to 
the high opportunity of replication from other organizations and whether the quality is maintained 
while scaling. Affiliation is defined as a collaboration between a parent organization and other parties. 
Within the affiliation mechanism stands two approaches towards scaling: loose cooperation, where 
organizations share the same mission in affiliate systems, and closer affiliations that work like 
business franchises. Compared with dissemination, affiliation takes longer to establish, where more 
resources are needed, and evaluation of readiness and receptivity are deliberate. Branching represents 
the most controlled strategy of the three mentioned. This strategy is built upon local sites that are 
operated by one organization (Dees et al., 2004).

Dees et al. (2004) state, "The initial exploration of scaling options begins and ends with 
considerations of readiness." This first aspect of the five R's discusses the readiness of innovations 
to be scaled and disseminated to new areas. Readiness is highlighted by the need for clear, objective 
evidence to drive success that relies on something other than unique leadership or a set of conditions. 
This emphasis on evidence-based decision-making creates confidence in the scaling process, as it 
ensures that success is not dependent on individual factors but on the proven effectiveness of the 
innovation. It is argued that those leading the scaling efforts need to have a deep understanding of the 
innovation and identify what drives the desired impact. Improving the circumstances in a single 
market is one thing, but to shift focus on a larger scale, it is essential to effectively communicate the 
innovation and adapt to possible changes to demonstrate readiness (Dees et al, 2004). 
 
Building on the elements of readiness lies an assurance of receptivity, which is about the willingness 
to adopt and accept new ideas. For an innovation to be receptive, the target audience must understand 
and see its relevance and applicability to their own context. Innovations that highly differ from the 
current solutions can experience resistance due to the complexity and unfamiliarity. Dees et al. (2004) 
state that "scaling innovations should prioritize less central coordination" to provide an adaptive 
process approach. However, openness alone does not necessarily create a market demand. If the 
leading market players recognize the unfulfilled need, it will be easier to connect with the local 
demands and execute strategies effectively (Dees et al., 2004). 
 
After addressing the aspect of receptivity, the next crucial element is the requirement of resources. 
Scaling impact stands in the balance of planning ahead to systematically allocate the resources 
efficiently with the strategies. The first part is to understand the needed cost based on the innovation 
possibilities to lower costs while maintaining value. Dees et al. (2004) suggest that scaling the 
innovation could be done through a collaboration with existing companies to reduce costs and remove 
potential barriers. The second part follows the opportunities to create sound revenue streams. Once 
again, the potential rests on local demands to deliver value to the market successfully.

 If the demands are higher to cover the implementation, it can lead to a risky scaling, where long-
term securement falls short. Overall, the resources assist other aspects of the five R's framework to 
generate scaling strategies that effectively leverage these assets to enhance the impact and 
sustainability of the scaling efforts (Dees et al., 2004). 
 
Risks, the fourth aspect of the five R's framework, underscore the need to thoroughly assess the 
consequences of potential failures in the context of scaling efforts. This assessment is not just critical; 
it's crucial, as scaling businesses increases the exposure to greater risks and requires substantial 
investment of resources. As a business scales, it not only faces financial and operational risks but also 
growing expectations and responsibilities from key stakeholders and customers. Dees et al (2004) 
argues that managing risks in the context of scaling is about balancing growth opportunities with the 
anticipation of obstacles that could disrupt progress. Risk assessment is not only essential for the 
survival of the business but also for the sake of maintaining the trust and commitment of customers, 
employees, and key stakeholders (Dees et al., 2004).  
 
Moving into the last aspect of the five R's, it becomes clear that returns are not just about financial 
profitability but also about enhancing the quality of impact. "Impact is not just about serving more 
people and communities but about serving them well" (Dees et al., 2004). This insight underscores 
that returns from scaling impact involve not only extending reach but also providing a higher-quality 
impact than existing alternatives. In this area, there may be different pathways that seem promising, 
but they require a reevaluation of the business's readiness to ensure that any scaling efforts align with 
the ability to deliver superior quality (Dees et al., 2004).  
 
While dissemination and loose affiliation strategies can lead to rapid expansion, the true value lies in 
the ability to maintain high quality. According to Dees et al (2004), coordinated approaches with a 
tighter affiliation and branching strategy can yield higher-quality impact. However, choosing a 
coordinated scaling path may slow the process and typically require more resources, which may only 
sometimes be feasible. The overall considerations about returns in scaling strategies underscore that 
success is more than expansion; it's about improving quality through intended and strategic growth 
(Dees et al., 2004).

3.1 Research Philosophy 
This thesis aims to explore the landscape of startups empowering their innovations to scale impact 
through strategic exploration and development. Like the scale of businesses, the research philosophy 
highlights the intricate relationship between the questions posed and the insights discovered 
(Schwandt, 1994). In scientific research, the need for a clear vision of paradigms is central to provide 
a methodological foundation to connect research aims and questions (Žukauskas et al., 2018). 
According to Kuhn (2015), a scientific paradigm supports the understanding of people's knowledge 
(epistemology) and reality (ontology). In research philosophy, two common methods, positivism and 
interpretivism, are made to approach the study of phenomena and extract valuable insights. Positivism 
emerges from empiricist philosophy that relies on the empirical foundation to find the truth of 
theories. The concept of positivism builds on the findings of logical connections and recognizes that 
philosophical systems can only be scientifically verified based on statistical results and 
generalizations (William, 2024). Opposite, interpretivism is driven by actions and interactions based 
on the assumption that social structures are not naturally formed (William, 2024). Interpretivism is 
closely related to constructivism; according to Schwandt (1994), these methods aim to understand 
and experience the world from the point of view of those who live it. Interpretivism states how 
interpretations shape understanding, and constructivism delves into constructing individuals' 
understanding of the world based on experience and interactions (Junjie & Xingxin, 2022). 
 
In the light of researching scaling strategies for positive change, the methodology around 
interpretivism and constructivism has been used in the qualitative methodology to gain deeper 
insights into the experiences and interpretations of individuals involved, as well as to understand the 
social constructs and meanings attached to scaling initiatives and their impact on positive change 
(William, 2024). Through constructivist research, it has been possible to understand the complexity

explain behaviors. Both GM and grounded theory require precise procedures to follow, which aim to

The Gioia theory is based upon three steps of coding (see Appendix D): 1st-order concepts that have

data through open coding, where valuable quotations from each interview were collected. Secondly, 
the quotes were sampled to first-order concepts and were gathered in separate focus areas (see 
Appendix E). In this step, it is essential to maintain objective rigor through the process of identifying 
and labeling first-order concepts (Gioia et al., 2012).

4.1.1 Resource Leverage 
Resource leverage captures an essential second-order theme for the organizational capability and 
resilience aggregate dimension, reflecting the necessity to drive organizational robustness and adapt 
to changes. For several stakeholders interviewed, allocating resources is seen to be set in place for 
the scaling process to accelerate. In the Five R's framework, resources are identified as an assisting 
aspect to enhance the impact of the scaling efforts (Dees et al., 2004). Moreover, resource leverage 
involves optimizing the use of existing resources to achieve maximum impact with minimal 
investment. 
 
Spirii is an example of mistaken prioritization of resource leverage. The organization initially focused 
on broad expansion without leveraging the existing resources. This fault led to operational 
inefficiencies and prevented scaling efforts. Tore Harritshøj from Spirii highlights the critical need 
for strategic resource management, a learning that has significantly improved their scaling efforts. 
Other informants also highlight that their inability to effectively distribute resources has impeded the 
fundamental mechanisms necessary to achieve their desired scaling process.  
 
Following the importance of resource leverage, the resource-based view (RBV) argues that the most 
substantial effect comes from resources that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and hard to substitute 
(Barney, 1991). For the resource-based view on startups, Horne and Fichter (2022) have extended the 
RBV by introducing the concept of dynamic capabilities. The perspectives can intentionally improve 
the usage and base of resources. This perspective underscores the importance of capability and 
resilience in startups, which is not just about having good resources but also using them carefully and 
adapting them in response to change.

4.1.2 Leading The Activities of Impact 
Related to resource leverage, this second-order theme emerges from the importance of the human 
element when executing business strategies in growth phases. Effective leadership is especially 
central for navigating the challenges of scaling, where team dynamics are the effect of effective 
leadership and are necessary to adapt to evolving business needs. As the startup grows, the complexity 
of operations increases, which requires a more structured approach to management and a more 
evident distribution of roles and responsibilities. Just like the timing of market entries, the internal 
operations have to be well-timed and communicated to maintain efficiency.  
 
Within the internal enablers, Horne and Fichter (2022) argue that "IEs can also have different 
characteristics in their scope, and they can play different roles depending on the maturity and focus 
of the entrepreneurial endeavor." This connection between leadership and internal enablers can be 
critical to leverage the operational element to scale. Serial Entrepreneur Henrik Isaksen recognizes 
how leaders play a pivotal role in activating these capabilities and are not only for strategic insights 
but to recognize when specific enablers should be activated to maximize impact. Beyond the timing 
of market entrance, leaders must initiate these enablers at the right moment and ensure that they are 
implemented effectively. 
 
Another dimension to this is how the team utilizes individual skills and competencies. Both Frederik 
Riber from Arriber and Mikkel Skou from NewC identified the need for alignment through their close 
relationship with the team, emphasizing that each team member's unique skills should complement 
each other to minimize weaknesses (Bloom & Chatterji, 2009). Further, this anticipation is recognized 
through RBV. It is built upon unique individual strengths (valuable and rare resources) and strategic 
collaboration (inimitable and non-substitutable practices) to achieve common goals (Barney, 1991).

4.1.2.2 The Impact of Founder Involvement on Startup Growth 
Closely aligned with the former first-order concept, this concept outlines the importance of self-
realization. When scaling occurs in startups, there will come a time when the founders who have

4.1.3 Lifecycle Awareness 
The third second-order theme of the first aggregate dimension encapsulates the previously identified 
logic of capabilities clarifying the organizational factors essential for growth and sustainability. 
Horne and Fichter (2022) argue that a combination of internal and external enablers influences the 
potential of a startup to grow and create sustainability net benefits. For leaders to time and execute 
these enablers, the proper lifecycle must be well-structured. This transformational lifecycle is 
illustrated in the Theoretical Background section (see Figure 1), which outlines a three-stage approach 
that impacts startups use to scale and achieve their sustainability goals. Each level requires different 
strategies, resources, and enablers, reflecting the challenges and opportunities at each phase of the 
lifecycle. Horne & Fichter (2022) underscores that "this step-by-step description may give the 
impression of a simple, linear path, but in reality, startup growth is often not linear and follows much 
more winding paths." This statement highlights the unpredictability that cannot guarantee a 
straightforward journey. However, it emphasizes that a well-defined lifecycle can enhance the 
chances of achieving sustainable impact. Likewise, Henrik Isaksen recognize that a clear pathway is 
essential for sustaining long-term growth built on anticipating changes, preparing for challenges, and 
taking advantage of opportunities at each stage.  
 
Nevertheless, Peter Vodder experienced a clear differentiation in pursuing an unexplored market, 
where he, as an entrepreneur, took part in transforming the market conditions. He sees Monta's 
foundation based on their strong network and market knowledge, but whether they could fly still 
stood uncertain. Following the winding paths (Horne & Fichter, 2022), Henrik Isaksen recognizes 
the importance of being aware of the lifecycle through scaling impact; however, operating in a new 
market that still has not shown high potential yet or an existing market where the product or service 
you present stands out from the crowd, creates several uncertain points to scale, and have led both 
informants in different pathways that were not part of the original strategy.  
 
Following the receptivity from the Five R's framework (Dees et al., 2004), this second theme 
emphasizes how external enablers significantly affect the willingness to adopt and accept new ideas, 
where less central coordination is suggested to reinforce an adaptive approach. The same ideation 
goes into McLean and Gargani's (2019) third guiding principle, which evaluates the enablers and the 
whole scaling system. As highlighted in the first two second-order themes, resources and leading 
execution of those enablers significantly affect whether scaling impact can be possible.

4.1.4 Impact Integration 
Extending the lifecycle awareness, the fourth theme within the first aggregate dimension encapsulates 
that impact is not just an orientation but needs to be integrated into all levels of scale to ensure that 
business decisions contribute to broader social and environmental goals. This integration emphasizes 
that startups focus not entirely on expansion but on maintaining and deepening their impact, which is

startups, Horne and Fichter (2022) assert that "as startups scale, they must continuously weave their

contributes as much to sustainability benefits as it does to their bottom line." 
 
Informant A particularly sees the differences between traditional scaling methods and impact scaling

environmental values. This integration emphasizes maintaining and enhancing a business's positive 
contribution, which is reinforced in every strategic move. Further, this effect requires understanding 
how different aspects of the business interact with local and global systems.  
 
Moreover, challenges occur in scaling activities, where Informant B outlines that taking responsibility 
for how impacts change is crucial while scaling. In this manner, the learnings need to be used to help 
place the actions between scaling and impact achievements. McLean and Gargani (2019) do this 
through dynamic evaluation that enhances the chances of adjusting the conditions through continuous 
learning.
