Abstract
The formulation of wave loading from breaking waves on offshore structures has been
the main topic of many experiments and research works. Generally, this broad literature re-
garding the slamming loads does not, however, cover the response of the structure subject to
these impulsive loads and this MSc thesis aims to fill this gap by analysing the response of an
offshore wind turbine subject to slamming loads and including different soil characteristics.
Stemming from a review of the available slamming load methodologies, this report fo-
cuses on three of these methods and by computing them in a simplified numerical MATLAB
model it will be demonstrated that even though the formulation proposed by Wienke and
Oumeraci [28] results in the largest slamming load and tower accelerations, it is the load pro-
posed in Paulsen et al. [21] the one that derives the largest force impulse.
The third formulation computed in this report is the Pressure Impulse model (PIMP)
[13], developed at DTU Wind Energy, and after examining the response obtained, it is rec-
ommended to be used not with a single breaking wave event as in this MSc thesis but as a
tool to compute a set of loads from different breaking waves for a specific sea state followed
by the selection of an appropriately conservative design slamming load.
Overall, the slamming load applied to the structure will triggered high oscillation modes
in the response signal, this report covers up the the 4th mode, and this impulsive feature will
characterised the dynamic response of the offshore wind turbine at the breaking instant, spe-
cially the acceleration. This peculiarity in the structural response will be presented visually
in a time-frequency analysis by means of the Continuous Wavelet Transform in MATLAB.
The outcome of the sensitivity analysis of the soil characteristics carried out in the HAWC2
software will indicate that the damping input in the soil model of the embedded pile foun-
dation is relatively significant depending on the vibration modes governing the response as
the low modes will be damped to a larger extent than the high ones. On the other hand, in-
creasing the soil stiffness generally will lead to lower deflections and velocities of the tower
although to higher accelerations at tower top as well. Similarly, the mudline overturning
moment and inline force will also rise with the soil stiffness.

Introduction
1.1
Offshore wind energy overview
With over 700 GW installed capacity, wind energy has become a key player in the decar-
bonization of the electricity system. Within the wind energy sector, the offshore wind energy
share has seen an increase in the recent years (see Figure 1.1) and its popularity has grown
in part because some of the concerns existing in onshore wind power such as noise pollution
and landscape disruption are not an issue in the offshore environment. Additionally, off-
shore wind energy presents other advantages given that the energy production is generally
higher, with faster and more consistent offshore wind speeds.
Figure 1.1: Historic development of total offshore wind energy installation (GW). Source:
Global Wind Report 2021 [1]

Chapter 1. Introduction
Additionally, the scaling up of the wind turbine size in offshore wind farms allows the
development of more cost-efficient offshore wind turbines (OWT) mounted on various foun-
dation types on the sea bed which makes wind energy more competitive when looking at the
whole electrical market dominated by more traditional sources of energy in the past.
On the other hand, OWT are exposed not only to strong wind loads but also loads from
the ocean waves and therefore, understanding the hydrodynamic loading on OWT is essential
for designers to ensure the integrity of the offshore structures.
In this regard, the broadly accepted Morison equation is used in the industry to predict
the quasi-static part of the hydrodynamic inline force on a circular cylinder. However, even
though loads on slender cylinders due to steep breaking waves have been investigate for
decades there is not a common agreement on a precise method to estimate the wave slam-
ming load on the structure due to breaking waves. Stemming from simple methods such
as Wagner [27] to more state-of-the-art complex formulation backed up by CFD models, ex-
tended literature covers this topic and yet many uncertainties and discrepancies arise when
comparing the existing slamming load formulations. This inaccuracy justify the continuation
of the research on this topic to try to find an accurate and consistent method to assess the
slamming loads when breaking waves hit the offshore structures.
1.2
Objectives and motivation
Breaking waves occur when the crest of the wave becomes so steep that it curls over and
drops suddenly inducing very short, but highly intense, slamming loads that lead to stresses
and displacements in the foundation, tower and nacelle as well as high accelerations. The
response due to these highly intense loads becomes more critical when the wind turbine is in
idling mode due to the non-existent aerodynamic damping, and consequently, the accurate
prediction of these loads as well as the structural response is pivotal during the design phase
of the OWT.
However, the evaluation of the structural response not only rely on the structure and
loads themselves, instead the characteristics of the surrounding soil model could greatly in-
fluence the intensity and duration of the response. On this subject, the objective of this MSc
thesis is to investigate the effect of the soil characteristics on an OWT mounted on a monopile
subject to wave slamming load. The sensitivity analysis of the soil stiffness and damping
carried out in this report is proposed to be a guidance for designers to assist them in the
prediction of more realistic structural response depending on the soil model used.
In terms of the slamming loading, as mentioned above many research studies and exper-
imental data exist. Nonetheless, the available literature mainly focuses on the formulation
of the load itself and the investigation of the time and frequency responses of the structure
subject to such impulsive loads is rather unexplored. In this regard, this MSc thesis aims to
provide a detailed analysis of the dynamic response of the OWT in both time and frequency
domain when several slamming load models are applied, revealing the role played by high
modes that will cause severe response. This analysis is done by taking two approaches to
model the OWT, namely a simplified numerical model in MATLAB and a more detailed one

Contributions
Apart from my own work, this report contains research work made by others that I have
utilized and/or adjusted to carry out the wind turbine soil sensitivity analysis. The paragraphs
below elaborate on this.
The geometric characteristics of the 15 MW OWT were taken from the IEA Wind Task 37
open repository [15]. To assemble the stiffness and mass matrices, I adjusted the MATLAB
code from Schløer et al. [23] in order to take into account not only the first fore-aft harmonic
included in the original code by Schløer but also the second, third and fourth fore-aft modes.
Then, I obtained the eigenfrequencies, mode shapes and the generalised terms GK, GM and
GD according to the adjusted MATLAB code.
I computed the slamming load formulations following different approaches explained
in section 3.3 and I applied them in the wind turbine model that included the structural char-
acteristics derived as above. After this, I calculated and analysed the dynamic response of the
structure subject to the slamming loads by applying the knowledge I acquired in the Offshore
Wind Energy DTU course and by reading various scientific papers related to the topic.
Once this was done, in the second part of this report I modelled the wind turbine in the
Aeroelastic simulation tool HAWC2, I applied the slamming loads and I tested different soil
parameters. In this case, I adjusted the wind turbine model fixed in translation and rotation
at the bottom of the tower as provided in [15] to fulfil the purpose of this MSc thesis. The
wind turbine data, namely the geometry, mass, material characteristics, structural stiffness,
moments of inertia, center of gravity (COG) of each body, etc, was input as provided in the
same report [15].
I added the embedded pile as well as the soil model to the OWT in HAWC2 and I mod-
ified the original constrains from the fixed model accordingly to represent the addition of
these two elements.
To implement the soil damping in the HAWC2 model I sought assistance by contacting
the HAWC2 support team and I chose the soil stiffness in such a way that the first eigenfre-
quency laid within the 1P and 3P frequencies specified in the wind turbine report.

2.3
Paulsen et al.
In a more recent study, Paulsen et al. [21] studied both, the probability of waves breaking
in random seas and the consequent impact on OWT. Opposite to previously seen theories
such as Goda, Von Karman or W & O where it is assumed that either the impact force is
constant during its duration or that the force decreases from its maximum value given at
t = 0, this method proposes a different time development of the impulse force. This way,
the force does not rise in an infinitely short time, but rather the impulse load builds up over a
finite period of time. This method was derived by carrying out curve fitting to the experiment
results whereas other methods such as the one proposed by W & O are based in mathematical
formulation. In Figure 2.3, the time development of the slamming load is shown for different
theories and it can be observed that in Paulsen et al., the slamming load has a duration which
is twice the duration calculated in the rest of the theories.

states with random irregular waves based on JONSWAP spectral power density.
One of the findings in this work was that among the events analysed, the ones with
larger return period corresponding to more critical sea states have a larger number of break-
ing waves.
In addition, it was found that there were more slamming events in the 2D than in the 3D
case. To explain this behaviour, in the 2D case, all the waves come from the same direction and
the faster waves catch up with the slower ones to create larger waves (dispersion focusing).
On the other hand, in the 3D case, apart from the dispersion focusing, waves that come from
different directions superpose to create larger waves which is known as directional focusing.
However, this happens slower than in the 2D case, therefore larger waves and consequently
more slamming events are seen in the unidirectional 2D case.

load for a particular sea state since they incorporate the significant wave by using the breaking
wave height Hb = 1.4·Hs in the formulation of Fs. On the other hand, the PIMP model and the
formulation proposed by W & O take into account the free surface elevation of the breaking
wave ηb and this means that these methods could be used to analyse the impulsive load due

count when the parameters in the respective slamming load formulation were calibrated,
providing conservative enough results for designers to incorporate in their calculations. On
the contrary, the PIMP model has been developed to be used for a specific slamming event
and wave celerity. The purpose of this method is to compute a large set of slamming events,
generate a slamming load distribution using the PIMP model, and by engineering judgement
and analysis of the results, a conservative design slamming load should be selected among
them. However, when this method is applied with U = LB/TB and only one single pressure

4.1
HAWC2 Software
HAWC2 stands for ’Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation’ and
it is an aeroelastic code intended for calculating the wind turbine response in time domain.
It was developed within the years 2003-2006 at the aeroelastic design research programme at
Risø, National laboratory Denmark, and since then, further improvements and new features
have been incorporated in this tool.
The structural part of the code is based on a multibody formulation where each body
is an assembly of Timoshenko beam elements. This gives the software a great flexibility in
terms of wind turbine and foundation configurations [18]. In addition, it allows to represent
the soil where the foundation is embedded and this feature will be utilize to carry out the soil
sensitivity analysis which comprises the main purpose of this MSc thesis report.
4.2
Wind turbine model and soil modelling in HAWC2
The wind turbine object of this analysis is shown in Figure 3.1. It was model in HAWC2
by creating and assembling different bodies, namely the embedded pile, the monopile, the
tower, the tower top, the connector, shaft, hubs and blades.
Regarding the damping applied, in an OWT there are different types of damping sources:
ζTotal = ζAero + ζStruc + ζHydro + ζSoil + ζDamper
(4.2.1)
According to [4], the hydrodynamic damping, ζHydro, is extremely low when compared with
other sources of damping and consequently it can be neglected for simplicity. Additionally,
37
==PAGE:42==

slamming loads
given that in the simulations the wind loads have been ignored in this study, ζAero = 0.
The structural damping, ζStruc, of the embedded pile, monopile and tower was set to
be stiffness-proportional only Rayleigh damping type and it was tuned to 2% logarithmic
decrement on the 1st fore-aft natural frequency as suggested in the wind turbine report [15].
In HAWC2 the simulation started with a transient motion. To obtain representative re-
sults of how the isolated slamming load affects the structure, the slamming load was intended
to be applied when the structure was in equilibrium. In order to reach equilibrium faster and
to save some computation time, an additional external damping was applied to the model
during the first 35 seconds of the time series. This damping could be denoted as ζDamper
in Equation 4.2.1. The constraint of the motion product of applying this ζDamper caused the
structure to reach equilibrium at approximately t = 40 s and then the slamming load could
be applied at t = 50 s. Notice that the x-axis in the plots in this section have been offset by 40
s for clarity.
Lastly, the soil damping ζSoil is represented in HAWC2 by:
C(x) = µ · K(x)
Where, 0 ≤µ ≤1 is the damping factor and K(x) means that the stiffness of the soil is
interpolated with the embedded lateral pile displacement. In this thesis work, values of µ
from 0 to 1 with a 0.1 interval have been given in the computations.
The soil model was done by approximating the stiffness of the soil-pile system with 10
independent nonlinear soil springs evenly distributed along the length of the embedded pile,
see Figure 4.1. This method has been widely utilized for many years by the oil and gas indus-
try and even though the loads on the wind turbine supporting structure are not the same than
on an oil platform, this method is accepted and recommended by the IEC and DNV standards
to model the surrounding soil of offshore wind turbines.

The lateral resistance of the pile is represented by p-y curves, where ’p’ denotes the lateral
resistance of the soil and ‘y’ the lateral displacement of the pile. This curve is defined by a
tanh(y) shape.
In this project, a detailed geotechnical report of an offshore site of interest was not avail-
able hence, the p-y curves describing the four soil studied were assembled without represent-
ing any site in particular.
The process of establishing the p-y curves plotted in Figure 4.2 was rather iterative. Start-
ing by defining the first set of p-y values for Soil 1, (p1, y1), and building the curve following
the tanh(y) shape, several values were tested. It was found that values of p1 < 200 kN/m for
y < 0.01 m and the corresponding tanh(y) shape, led to a first natural frequency fN,1 < 15
Hz. Since the first natural frequency of the system is specified in the IEA 15 MW WT report
[15] to be 17 Hz, to have model that did not deviate much from the original, starting with a
lateral resistant of p1 = 200 kN/m was deemed to be appropriate with fN,1 = 0.16 Hz.
Then, Soil 2, 3 and 4 were calculated as multiples of Soil 1 to complete the soil stiffness
sensitivity analysis, see Table 4.1. The difference between the fN,1 = 0.17 Hz in the IEA 15
MW WT report and fN,1 = 0.16 Hz obtained in the model may be because in the WT report
the OWT is fixed at mudline and the soil is not modelled. By including the soil model in this
thesis work, it may induce some flexibility in the system that may affect the motion of the
structure and slightly decrease the natural frequencies.

4.4.3
Soil damping sensitivity analysis
As explained in Section 4.2, the soil damping ζsoil is introduced in the wind turbine model
in HAWC2 by means of the damping factor µ, with 0 ≤µ ≤1. In this regard, Figure 4.23 and
Figure 4.24 aim to illustrate to what extent the oscillating modes will be damped depending
on the input µ for both soils considered, namely Soil 1 and Soil 4. The total damping ratio
ζTotal was obtained by performing an eigenanalysis of the structure in HAWC2.
Given that in this case the model only comprises structural and soil damping, ζTotal =
ζStruc + ζSoil. Then, when µ = 0, ζSoil = 0 and this means that the damping ratio result of
the eigenanalysis will correspond to the structural damping ζTotal = ζStruc. This can be seen
in Figure 4.23 and Figure 4.24 where ζStruc has been indicated with a dashed line for each
vibration mode. Consequently, ζSoil can be seen at a glance by subtracting ζTotal −ζStruc for
each nonzero value of µ.
Initially, values from 0 to 1 incrementing by 0.1 were given to µ and it was seen that
the higher the vibration mode, the faster the damping ratio become constant, generally for
µ > 0.1 −0.2. In other words, only small values of µ induce a variation in the total damping
ratio of the high modes. To analyse how the damping ratio develops for such a small µ,
0 ≤µ ≤0.1 incrementing by 0.01 was also applied in the model and a zoom-in plot of this
can be seen on the right hand side of Figure 4.23 and Figure 4.24.

4.5
Comparison of MATLAB and HAWC2 WT models
In this section, some differences between the simplified model in MATLAB and the more
detailed in HAWC2 are highlighted. To do this analysis, the HAWC2 wind turbine model
fixed at mudline provided in the IEA 15 MW WT repository [15] was used. This model does
not include an embedded foundation in the soil which makes the comparison more precise
since the MATLAB model does not include a soil model either. Nevertheless, some differ-
ences have been identified between the two models and they are listed below:
• The HAWC2 model comprises different bodies such as pile, monopile, towertop, shaft,
hubs and blades. All these bodies are input with their own stiffness, inertia and mass
applied to their individual COG and the COG of the NRA is not in line with the tower
COG. In MATLAB the model consists of a cantilever beam and the NRA is input by
adding a single tower top lumped mass whose the COG is in line with the COG of the
tower. The difference of the NRA COGs in both models explains the offsetting in the
deflection plots, see Figure 4.35.
• In MATLAB the computations are based on a simple cantilever Euler-Bernoulli beam
numerical model with a lumped mass and inertia representing the NRA. In HAWC2,
each body is an assemble of Timoshenko beam elements. According to [25], the Tim-
oshenko beam theory is more accurate than the Euler-Bernoulli beam theory because
it includes shear deformation and rotational bending effects. Hence, the Timoshenko
beam theory is more appropriate than the Euler-Bernoulli beam theory for calculating
transient responses and high frequency vibrations.
• In MATLAB the blades are not modelled whereas in HAWC2 the stiff blades are mod-
elled as Timoshenko beams. This means that the blades in the HAWC2 model could
influence the motion of the overall structure.
• In MATLAB the coupled translational and rotational spring was set to be very stiff to
simulate a fixed foundation at mudline. In HAWC2 the soil module provide flexibility
in terms of the characteristics of the soil surrounding the embedded foundation.
The plots below illustrate the similarities in the response obtained with both models.
As mentioned above, it exists an offset in the deflection in the HAWC2 model in Fig-

Chapter 5
Conclusions
Among the methods commonly used in the offshore wind energy industry to compute
slamming loads on piles, two methods namely the one proposed in Paulsen et al. [21] and
Wienke and Oumeraci [28] are analysed in this MSc thesis work as well as the Pressure Im-
pulse model [13], a more research approach. Two models are used to carry out the analysis,
a simplified beam model in MATLAB and a more exhaustive one in HAWC2. The use of the
W & O method in the HAWC2 WT model leads to extremely large accelerations at tower bot-
tom, over 20 m/s2, which could compromise the safety and structural integrity of the wind
turbine if this is not taking into account during the design phase.
The time-frequency analysis performed with the Wavelet MATLAB tool determines that
generally, at tower bottom the dynamic response is mainly governed by high harmonics char-
acterised by their short period while at tower top the main component of the deflection and
velocity signals is the 1st oscillation mode lasting for the entire time series computed.
To model the soil in HAWC2 as a set of independent nonlinear springs along the length of
the foundation, 4 soils with increasing stiffness are computed and the results from the softer
and the stiffer one are analysed.
The sensitivity study of the soil characteristics carried out in this report reveals that the
soil damping is not the main driver of the dynamic response of the structure, on the contrary,
only the 1st mode component is considerably affected by it while higher modes are only sen-
sitive to a small range of values of the soil damping factor.
In terms of the soil stiffness, stiffer soil models will result in lower deflections and veloc-
ities, however, the opposite trend is seen in the tower top acceleration while at tower bottom
the stiffness of the soil does not influence the acceleration response.
These and other findings related to the topic are summarized below:
• The slamming loads from breaking waves have enough frequency content to excite high
modes, and in some locations along the tower they can even be the main drivers of the
dynamic response.
• The 1st mode is the most sensitive to the variation of the soil damping factor. Higher
modes are only sensitive to small values of µ, becoming constant for µ > 0.1 −0.2.
61
==PAGE:66==
Chapter 5. Conclusions
• The effect of the soil damping will be amplified in softer soils in comparison to stiffer
ones.
• The deflection and velocity are larger in softer soils than in stiffer ones, however, the
acceleration increases if the soil is stiffer.
• The stiffness of the soil affects very little when the response is governed by the 1st mode
and responses dominated by high oscillation modes are more sensitive to the change of
soil stiffness.
• The maximum values of the inline force and overturning moment at mudline are larger
when stiffer soils are considered.
• Similar results are obtained in the MATLAB and the HAWC2 models in the deflection.
However, the velocity and acceleration computed in HAWC2 is fairly larger. The time
series computed with both models reveals that the HAWC2 model is able to damp im-
pulsive response faster than the numerical model in MATLAB.
