Abstract
In recent years a great number of registrations were made to the European Unions (EUs)
REACH for UVCBs (substances of unknown or variable composition, complex reaction
products, or biological materials).
The EU regulations and guidelines require that all
substances should be tested for hazard and risk assessment, but the properties of the
mixtures often cause diﬀiculties.
Essential oils make a prominent group of UVCBs with major yearly production values,
making a large fraction of cleaning and personal care products.
Such down-the-train
products, often end up in wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) and it is important to
investigate if they are biodegradable in sludge suspensions.
The goal of the study was to design an aerobic, closed biodegradation kinetics testing
platform with WWTP activated sludge as an inoculum. A major concern in such tests
is the high oxygen depletion, since the microorganisms in the sludge consume significant
amounts. Hence, oxygen depletion tests were conducted.
ThOD (theoretical oxygen demand) and oxygen depletion kinetics rates were calculated
for activated sludge taken from Lundtofte WWTP and Avedøre WWTP. Generally, data
showed that oxygen depletion depends on microbial density in the system and the headspace
fraction. It was assumed that the closed activated sludge system with a 75% headspace
fraction was the optimal solution for designing a biodegradation kinetics testing platform.
Data obtained from biodegradation kinetics testing showed no significant indication of
biodegradation. Further development of the biodegradation kinetics testin platform for
UVCBs in the sludge suspensions should be done and efforts should mainly be put into
designing the closed, aerobic systems.
UVCBs are diﬀicult to test substances that are not properly researched. The need for
changing the current guidelines and regulations is high since they are mostly not appro-
priate for testing such substances. The importance of designing advanced experimental
platforms lies in discovering new approaches for testing complex mixtures.

1
Introduction
The importance of classification and identification has risen significantly, due to the in-
crease in the production and consumption of chemical substances. Environmental aware-
ness and sustainable development call for the risk assessment of the chemicals. Regulatory
bodies in European Union (EU) are putting their efforts into creating the systems for
proper evaluation of the chemicals that will have the function of protecting human health
and the environment. For manufacturers and importers of chemicals in the EU, the com-
pliance with regulations such as the REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and
Restriction of Chemicals) and ROHS directive (Directive on the Restriction of the Use of
Certain Hazardous Substances in Electrical and Electronic Equipment) is mandatory [1,
2].
In recent years, a large number of registrations to the EU’s REACH have been made for
substances of unknown or variable composition, complex reaction products, or biological
materials, commonly known as UVCBs. Recent reports indicate that 45% of registered
UVCBs substances are produced or imported in volumes >100 tonnes per annum [3].
While conducting hazard classification and risk assessment of UVCBs, challenges arise
due to their complexity and variable composition [4].
Depending on their source and
manufacturing process, most of the time it is not even possible to define all the constituents
contained in the substance, leading to issues with persistence, toxicity or bioaccumulation
testing. Such tests are generally necessary for risk assessments, appropriate classification
and labelling [5].
UVCBs include a large group of substances from different sources and some of the most
known examples are petrochemical mixtures, biofuels, vegetable oils and their derivatives
[5]. In this study, essential oils were chosen as the representative group for biodegrad-
ability testing with a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) activated sludge in aerobic
conditions. Using sludge as an inoculum for biodegradation kinetics testing with volatile
and hydrophobic chemicals in the closed aerobic systems is a novelty and, to our knowl-
edge, hasn’t been thoroughly researched yet.
In general, UVCBs are not comprehensively investigated and there is a need for changing
the current guidelines and regulations since they are not appropriate for testing such sub-
stances. Not knowing the impact on the environment of certain chemicals, raises serious
concerns which could affect the current and future generations. Therefore, designing and
validation of models and experiments are needed for predicting the persistence, bioaccu-
mulation, fate and toxicity properties of UVCBs constituents.
1.1
Goals and scope
Pre-experiment was conducted to get an idea of how to design the biodegradation kinetics
testing platform, by comparing oxygen depletion in the vials with different amounts of
sludge.
The purpose of the first experiment was to investigate if oxygen from the headspace was
suﬀicient for the maintenance of the aerobic conditions in various closed WWTP sludge
test systems, spiked with 1-µL ethanol. Ethanol was considered an appropriate example
of cosolvent which often affects higher oxygen consumption in the systems. Oxygen de-
pletion was measured for different WWTP sludge systems and oxygen consumption by

2
Theoretical background
2.1
Biodegradation testing
The term environmental fate describes the distribution and transformation, of each UVCB
constituent, by various processes after release into the environment. Constituent’s prop-
erties have a major influence on the degree of degradation and partitioning into the envi-
ronmental compartments such as water, air, soil, sediment and biota [5].
Degradation can be described as the loss or conversion of chemicals in the environment, it
can be abiotic or biotic. The most common abiotic processes are hydrolysis, oxidation and
photolysis. On the other hand, biotic or biological degradation is called biodegradation,
which can be anaerobic or aerobic. According to Organisation for Economic Co-operation
and Development (OECD), biotransformation (primary degradation) is an ”...alteration in
the chemical structure of a substance, brought about by biological action, resulting in the
loss of a specific property of that substance ” [8]. Further, ultimate biodegradation leads
to the complete degradation of the test chemical by microorganisms, and eventually to
the production of CO2, water, mineral salts and biomass (microbial cellular constituents)
[9].
2.1.1
Guidelines and regulations
REACH (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals, 2007) is
the EU regulation focused on the protection of human health and lowering any potential
environmental risks that may be caused by chemicals. Additionally, it is adopted to raise
the quality of the chemicals on the market, by making EU industries more competitive
and to lower the number of tests on animals. To be compliant with REACH, the company
must identify and manage the risks of the chemicals they produce and market in the EU.
Companies are required to register information about the physical and chemical properties
of the manufactured substance, exposure, uses and risk management measures, alongside
chemical safety assessment. Regulatory bodies can restrict the use of certain substances if
it is found that they pose a serious risk to the environment and request substitution with
appropriate alternatives [1, 10].
Reports on substances’ biodegradability mostly contain information obtained from stan-
dardized tests for ready biodegradability, alongside tests simulating the biodegradation
in water, aquatic sediment and soil. Additionally, facts acquired from sewage treatment
plant simulation testing, inherent biodegradability tests and anaerobic biodegradability
in seawater testing can also be used for building the chemical dossier. Such data may be
used for hazard, risk and/or persistence assessments. Choosing an appropriate test can
be based on partitioning properties and realise or emission pathway [11].
The 301 series of ready biodegradability tests are aerobic screening tests which measure
ultimate biodegradation. Production of Dissolved Organic Carbon (DOC), Biochemical
Oxygen Demand (BOD) and production of carbon dioxide are commonly measured param-
eters. For such tests, high concentrations of the test substance are used (2 to 100 mg/L).
The requirements for passing the test are 70 % removal of DOC and 60 % of ThOD ( The-
oretical Oxygen Demand ) or ThCO2 (Theoretical amount of Carbon Dioxide) production
for respirometric methods [8, 11].

If the ready biodegradability test is negative, the inherent biodegradability test (302 series
of OECD tests) could be the next choice in determining chemicals’ potential for environ-
mental persistence. The inoculum used for this kind of testing contains a high density
of microorganisms (e.g.
sewage sludge), and it is conducted under aerobic conditions.
Biodegradation extent and/or rate are measured [12, 13].
Simulation tests (aerobic/anaerobic) are relevant for obtaining information about biodegra-
dation in predetermined environmentally relevant circumstances.
Such tests simulate
degradation in particular surroundings and show the relevance of sorption of the sub-
stance to different environmental compartments [9].
Anaerobic biodegradability of the organic substances in anaerobic digesters at the WWTP
is covered by Test No. 311 OECD Guideline. In this case the production of methane and
carbon dioxide is measured, under the specific conditions. The exposure of sludge to the
test chemical lasts for 60 days and complete anaerobic degradation is assumed when 75 %
to 80 % of gas is produced (methane or carbon dioxide) [14].
2.1.2
Challenges
Standardized biodegradation tests are not suitable for UVCBs, as most focus on single
chemicals and do not consider mixture effects.
Additionally, the concentration of the
chemicals used for regulatory biodegradation testing is often much higher than what it
appears to be in the environment. Recent studies have shown that it is possible to conduct
biodegradation testing for the mixtures in the low concentrations (range: ng/L to µg/L),
which can be a step closer to obtaining more environmentally relevant data [15].
Volatile chemicals are defined as ”diﬀicult” test chemicals, according to OECD [16]. Hence
it is important to develop new biodegradation testing platforms for such compounds, as
their tendency to partition into the headspace can cause diﬀiculties. Tests in air-water
systems with such compounds should be conducted in the closed environment as the major
fraction of volatile compounds will reside in the air. During the biodegradation process,
the removal of dissolved test chemicals will control the distribution of remaining in the
headspace and water. In the other words, the water fraction will constantly be replenished
with the test compounds from the air. Also, if the chemical is hydrophobic, it is necessary
to keep in mind the partitioning into dissolved organic matter, sediment and suspended
matter or dissolution from water, while designing biodegradation tests [17].
Birch et
al.(2017) focused on biodegradation testing of chemicals with high Henry’s law constants
in closed systems. They emphasized the importance of inclusion of only the dissolved
chemical mass (effective concentration) in biodegradation kinetics calculation, rather than
the total mass in the system [17]. Another study from the same department investigated
the connection of inoculum source on biodegradation kinetics of hydrocarbons. Automated
SPME - GCMS was used to analyse the systems and gain biodegradation information.
Gathered data showed the biodegradation kinetics rates (first order) generally depended
on the microbial density and the previous hydrocarbon inoculum exposure [18].
Dosing methods and exposure scenarios can have a significant influence on biodegradation
testing and its outcomes. Currently, three methods are commonly used; solvent spiking, a
water-accommodated fraction (WAF) and passive dosing. In this project, cosolvent spiking
was chosen to quantitatively transfer an essential oil to WWTP activated sludge samples,
since it is assumed, that is the appropriate simulation of down-the-drain products reaching
the sewage systems. It is also the method that is most commonly used. Several concerns
are present if solvent spiking is used as a method, such as toxicity of the cosolvent, modifi-
cation of the toxicity in the test chemical, stimulation of biomass growth and high oxygen

data on primary biodegradation kinetics at environmentally relevant concentrations [20].
2.2
Sorption and Biosorption to Activated Sludge
The sorption of constituents into activated sludge is an important removal process in
WWTP [21]. It can be described by two different processes, adsorption and absorption.
Absorption includes distributing the chemical into liquid or solid absorbent. The adhesion
of a substance to a surface, while forming a film at the interface between two phases
is called adsorption [22].
Biosorption is adsorption to the biological matrix, in other
words, the ions bind from liquids to functional groups present on the biomass surface [23].
Banihashemi & Droste distinguished between physicochemical sorption and biosorption in
their experiments with activated sludge and 3 different organic chemicals (bisphenol A,
triclosan and 17￿-ethinylestradiol) in the ￿g/L range. They noticed that physicochemical
sorption is initially rapid until it reaches equilibrium between the solid and aqueous phase,
followed by biosorption or biodegradation [21]. Generally, it is assumed that the sorbed
fraction of the chemicals is not bioavailable and that microorganisms have a tendency to
utilize dissolved compounds [24, 25]. Zhang et al. describe sorption to activated sludge as
the main process for immediate removal of organic pollutants from WWTP, followed by
biodegradation. Furthermore, they also introduce the possibility of utilization of organic
matter sorbed onto activated sludge for biomass growth [26]. A recent study from Runjevac
et al. indicated that the morphology of the activated sludge was not changed due to the
acclimatization period during biosorption [27].
2.3
UVCBs - Essential Oils
Unknown or variable composition, complex reaction products or biological materials (UVCBs)
is a group of substances which contains a large number of different constituents. Some
of the components are sometimes unknown, thus it can be demanding to predict UVCBs
composition. Additionally, the identification and characterisation of such complex chem-
ical mixtures can also be a challenge. UVCBs composition mostly depend on their source
material or manufacturing process and it can vary due to fluctuations in production, and
seasonal or temporal changes [5]. According to REACH regulations, it is important to
identify the substances and describe their structural composition to have an overview of
the possible negative impacts that chemicals may have. Ecotoxicological, fate and physico-
chemical properties information is used for substances hazard and risk assessment. UVCBs
can be assessed based on the individual constituents in the mixture, the substance as a
whole, after fractionation or by combining different approaches [28, 5].
Assessment of all the individual constituents in the mixture often requires a lot of resources
and effort, especially when hundreds are present. Therefore, this approach is mostly used
for assessing the few constituents that represent the whole mixture. Components that are
observed often make up more than 0.1% of the substance. Occasionally, the ones that can
be found in lesser concentrations are also identified due to their relevance for classification,
labelling and PBT (persistent, bioaccumulative and toxic) assessment, which is regulated
by REACH [29, 30, 31, 32].

One of the significant disadvantages of constituent based assessment is the lack of inclusion
of the rest of the mixture components that can be relevant for risk and hazard assessment
purposes. If the UVCB is poorly characterised, the whole mixture approach can be used to
provide valuable data. It is feasible for determining unidentified chemicals in the mixture,
alongside any interactions among components. Since it is often complicated to reproduce
the environmental conditions in the laboratory (e.g., temporal changes and various inter-
actions in real-world exposures), untargeted analysis is not always recommended. In such
cases, fractionation may be the optimal solution [29, 30, 31].
Essential oils make a prominent UVCBs group with major yearly production values. They
are used mainly in the manufacturing of cleaning, personal care, food, health and cosmetic
products. More than 150 000 tonnes were produced in 2017 and the current expected
market size growth is from $12.36 billion (2021) to $21.85 billion (2026) [33]. Essential
oils generally consist of a large fraction of volatile hydrocarbons and can be retrieved from
a natural product by (steam) distillation or expression (for citrus fruits). Oils carry a
specific odour (essence) of the plant [34, 35].
Additionally, their hydrophobicity, alongside volatility, hinders conducting the biodegra-
dation tests since most constituents tend to escape to the headspace. Chemical properties
allow essential oils to possess antibacterial, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, repellent and
insecticidal activities [36].
2.4
Sludge and WWTP
Primary treatment of WWTP is mainly based on the removal of settable solids and residual
floating matter, where approximately 60% of the TSS (total suspended solids) and 35 %
of BOD (biological oxygen demand) are removed. The process consists of several sections;
screening, comminution, grit removal and sedimentation. Primary sludge comes from the
primary clarifier or settling tank (see Figure 2.1).
It contains a significant amount of
organic matter and a great number of pathogenic organisms (they tend to attach to solid
particles) [7, 37].
Secondary treatment can be done using one of four different methods; the trickling filter,
the activated sludge process, oxidation pond and rotating biological contacter. Generally,
wastewater is treated with activated sludge for the removal of high biodegradable organic
matter content (more than 85% TSS and BOD) which is done in a series of tanks; aerobic
(oxygen present), anoxic (nitrate present, no oxygen) and anaerobic (no oxygen, no nitrate)
tank [38]. In this process, biomass, containing flocs (biological aggregates), is mixed with
wastewater (see Figure 2.1) [7, 37].
The biodegradation of organic matter is followed by the growth of biomass.
Waste-
activated sludge or secondary sludge contains organic matter, inerts and a colossal num-
ber of microorganisms, such as the aquatic culture of bacteria, protozoans and helminths.
Most of the biodegradation is done by aerobic, heterotrophic bacteria. On a micro-level
two processes are relevant; the first is the oxidation of organic chemicals to carbon dioxide
to obtain energy for the microorganisms. The second process is called synthesis and it
includes the incorporation of remaining organic material into new microbial cells. After
leaving the series of aerobic and anaerobic tanks, the wastewater reaches a secondary clar-
ifier (cell separator) where separation from bacterial flocs is usually done by sedimentation
[7, 37].
Activated sludge, highly dense with microorganisms, is separated into two streams. A
fraction of the sludge returns to the aeration tank. This is a vital part of the process, as

sludge circulation ensures the high density of the microorganisms to ensure biodegradation
[7, 37, 22].
The tertiary WWTP process removes significant amounts of impurities but is mostly used
under special circumstances for chemical nutrient removal. Sometimes, the final step of
wastewater treatment is chlorination or ultraviolet radiation for disinfection [7, 37, 22].
Using activated sludge as an inoculum for the biodegradation kinetics tests, can simulate
(to some extent) environmental conditions and can provide the presence of a significant
number of organisms. Although such inoculum can provide more representative results
in biodegradability studies, geographical and temporal difference affects the microbial
communities. Therefore, activated sludge composition can affect the reproducibility of
tests [40].
Even though the experiment in this project is not simulating exact WWTP conditions,
essential oils (as most UVCBs) are down-the-drain chemicals and it is important to obtain
the biodegradation kinetics data in activated sludge systems. Novel strategies need to be
developed to test complex mixtures and the suggested platform can be used as a starting
point.

Materials

3.2
WWTP Sludge
WWTP activated sludge was sampled three times.
The first sample was taken from
Avedøre WWTP (BIOFOS A/S, Copenhagen, Denmark) on the the 15th of February 2022
at 8.40 a.m. and was used to conduct the pre-experiment. Lundtofte WWTP (Mølleåvær-
ket A / S, Lyngby, Denmark) was the second location where the sample was taken for
oxygen depletion testing on the 19th of April 2022 at 10 a.m. For biodegradation kinetics
testing activated sludge was sampled on the 10th of May 2022 at 7 a.m. from the Avedøre
WWTP (BIOFOS A/S, Copenhagen, Denmark) which treated 22,9 million m3 of water

Cleaning and cosmetic products often contain essential oils and tend to end up in wastew-
ater treatment plants [42, 43]. Therefore, activated sludge was chosen as an appropriate
inoculum to use for biodegradation kinetics testing.
3.3
Pre-experiment
Preexperiment was conducted to get an approximate overview of the oxygen depletion in
the various sludge systems, designed for the biodegradation kinetics experiment. As sludge
contains abundant amounts of microorganisms and organic matter, it was assumed that the
amount of the consumed oxygen in the system would be significant [7, 22]. Additionally,
concentration of the oxygen is generally higher in the air, compared to water. Therefore,
by increasing the headspace fraction in the system, the amount of oxygen available for
microorganisms to utilize also rises.
3.3.1
Oxygen sensor meter and spots
The oxygen meter includes two parts, the sensor and the meter. In this study, oxygen
sensor spots were used to measure dissolved oxygen concentrations based on the quenching
of luminescence (decreasing the fluorescence intensity of the molecule) in the presence of
oxygen [44]. Spots were glued on the inner part of the glass vials to be in constant contact
with the sludge. The meter is used to receive optical signals from the sensor spots.
3.3.2
Testing
The pre-experiment was set up to investigate the optimal amount of sludge in the vials for
designing a biodegradation kinetics experiment. Four different systems were set up in 20
mL gas-tight vials (Mikrolab Aarhus, Denmark) with different WWTP activated sludge

Biodegradation Kinetics Test
Biodegradation testing was performed to obtain primary biodegradation kinetics of seven
Cedarwood Virginia Oil constituents. The experiment was set up in the 20 mL vials, with
5 mL sludge and 15 mL headspace. Prior to conducting the experiment stock solution
was prepared by adding 7.5 µL pure Cedarwood Virginia Oil to 15 mL ethanol to obtain
the concentration of 500 mg/L. Using cosolvent spiking, 5 µL stock solution was added
to 5 mL of sludge. The concentration of Cedarwood Virginia Oil in the sludge was 0.5
mg/L, and the concentration of ethanol was 790 mg/L. The applied experimental platform
was developed for biodegradation kinetic testing of hydrophobic chemicals below their
solubilities (See Table 3.4) [20]. The biodegradation was stopped, by adding 5 mL ethanol
(100%) to the vials. Triplicates were made for every stop time. Samples were immediately
put in the freezer at -20 °C. The day before the analysis, samples were left to thaw and
filtered with 0.2-µm filters. The filtered sample was diluted with 15 mL water and the end
concentration of Cedarwood Virginia Oil was 50 ￿g/L. Vials were kept in the 4 °C room
overnight until the start of the analysis.

Biodegradation kinetics testing
The biodegradation kinetics testing was conducted in the system containing 75 % headspace
(5 mL sludge), which was decided from the pre-experimental data.
After obtaining the results from biodegradation kinetics testing the results shows un-
expected discrepancies. These results suggest that the biodegradation process occurred
under abiotic conditions. Investigation into this occurrence led us to believe there has
been a mistake in the protocol.
The abiotic conditions were confirmed by calculating the ThOD for 5 µL ethanol, following
Equations 4.1 and 4.2.
The oxygen amount in the air (m O2,headspace) was calculated:
m O2,headspace = V headspace · ρ · φ = 15 mL · 0.2095 · 1.309 mg/mL = 4.11 mgO2
(4.1)
The amount of O2 needed for degradation of 5-µ L ethanol ( ThODNO3, 5-µL EtOH), which
corresponds with 3,95 mg:
ThODNO3,5-µEtOH = ThODNO3,1-µL EtOH · mEtOH = 1.64 mgO2 · 3.95 mg = 6.48 mgO2
(4.2)
As can be seen the available amount of oxygen in the headspace is lower than the needed
amount of oxygen to degrade ethanol. Ethanol will be rapidly degraded in such environ-
ment, hence we predict that the system was anoxic quickly.

4.3
Improvements of UVCB biodegradation kinetics testing with
sludge
There are several challenges that this study faced while performing the experiment.
It is known that solvents have a significant influence on oxygen depletion rates, therefore
recent studies call for the regulatory changes of the threshold values in biodegradation test-
ing. In this study, it was shown that the high concentrations of cosolvent can cause rapid
oxygen depletion and anoxic conditions. For the future development of the biodegradation
kinetics tests, we recommend using low concentrations of the cosolvent and/or calculation
of the appropriate volumes.
Only biotic test systems were observed, to learn about the possibility of biodegradation
of the chosen constituents from Cedarwood Virginia Oil. From this, a distinction between
biodegradation and degradation (or sorption to the organic matter) could not be made,
since abiotic systems were not included in the study. Additionally, introducing abiotic
systems into such experiments would give a better overview of primary biodegradation
kinetics in WWTP activated sludge.
Targeted Multiple Reaction Monitoring and nontargeted scan method combined with
UVCBs biodegradation testing were used to obtain satisfactory data.
However, infor-
mation about the mineralisation of the constituents and degradation products cannot be
produced, which is important for further persistency and toxicity assessment. The used
method could be combined with other analytical instruments to assess the remaining com-
pounds at the end of the biodegradation testing.
Since sorption is often the driving process in the removal of organic impurities in the
wastewater, it would be desirable to do further testing with WWTP activated sludge, by
using SPME as an appropriate method.

5
Conclusion
This study was focused on obtaining the relevant information for oxygen depletion and
biodegradation kinetics testing. The oxygen depletion test included sludge systems and
ethanol as a test chemical. The optimal headspace to sludge ratio was shown to be 3:1
(vol:vol), with the 75 % headspace (air), to maintain the aerobic conditions for 48 h, if
1-µL ethanol is added. The oxygen depletion kinetics values for Lundtofte WWTP and
pre-experimental sludge systems indicated that more oxygen is utilized per hour if a higher
amount of sludge is present in the system.
Additionally, it was noticed that the activated sludge samples from the different WWTP
do not show similar oxygen depletion kinetics data for the same amounts of sludge in the
system. This can be explained with the different sludge quality, which can depend on the
sampling time in a day, the season or the sampling location in the WWTP.
The distinction between the consumption of oxygen with and without test chemical was
also observed.
In both cases - for Avedøre WWTP and Lundtofte WWTP, the same
amount of oxygen was consumed at the end of the experiment and the values for the
oxygen depletion rates were similar.
The biodegradation kinetics testing showed unexpected results since the anoxic conditions
were quickly achieved. The test was made with Cedarwood Virginia Oil and its main
constituents were observed: α-Cedrene, Cedrol, Thujopsene, β-Cedrene, β-Chamigrene,
β-Himachalene and Cuparene. For the identification and analysis of the compounds, the
SPME-GC/MS was used. According to the results, no clear indication of biodegradation
or anaerobic degradation was found.
The microorganisms in the sludge utilize significant amounts of oxygen and it is necessary
to develop a platform for closed systems which will stay aerobic and allow the biodegra-
dation kinetics testing.
