Abstract
The objective of this study was to determine the minimum requirements for a ventilation
system to achieve optimal indoor climate with low energy use in ﬂexible oﬃces with ﬁxed
building envelopes. The project investigated ﬂexibility in an oﬃce building by testing the
adaptability to accommodate changes in oﬃce layouts and building programmes.
In collaboration with Rambøll, a case building in Carlsberg Byen was used to create ﬁve
layout variations and four building programmes. The layouts varied in distribution of
open oﬃces and ﬂexible workspaces, while the building programmes varied in occupancy
presence and internal gains. Furthermore, the ventilation system was designed to meet
two diﬀerent indoor climate categories.
Floor plans were modelled in Revit with the
ducting layout for the ventilation system, and energy and indoor climate were evaluated
with IDA ICE. Initial and operational costs were estimated for the DCV system used to
ventilate the oﬃce space.
The hypothesis of the study was that it was possible to achieve optimal indoor climate in
all scenarios, and that ﬂexible oﬃces would achieve lower energy consumption and costs
for the ventilation system.
The results showed that all scenarios complied fully with the air quality evaluated on
CO2 concentration, while thermal conditions did not. Flexible workspaces met the re-
quirements for the thermal conditions more than open oﬃces did in the occupancy time.
Energy simulations showed that the scenarios with most ﬂexible workspaces had lower
energy consumption for the building, but higher for the ventilation system when com-
paring with the reference model. Open oﬃces followed a reversed pattern. Total costs
showed no signiﬁcant changes between the scenarios, unless the operational cost based on
the energy consumption, was considered over a longer investment period.
The developed scenarios all showed how the change in layouts and building programmes
aﬀected the energy consumption of the building and ventilation system, the related ven-
tilation costs and that trade-oﬀs were required to maintain optimal indoor climate con-
ditions, achieving the predicted results. This study concluded that it was possible to
design oﬃces that could be adaptable for changing needs for ventilation demands based
on indoor climate criteria.

Introduction
In today’s modern society, we spend up to 90% of our time indoors. A big fraction of the
work force spends up to 8 hours every weekday in oﬃces (Exhausto, 1). Large corporate
companies strive to push the eﬃciency higher by spending lots of money and resources on
organisational changes and improving their facilities within work environment, allowing
employees much higher ﬂexibility regarding how and at what time of the day they work
at the oﬃce.
Open-plan oﬃces were introduced to bring value to the business strategy of a company
by reducing overhead costs, increasing space eﬃciency and improving the oﬃce ﬂexibility,
as open oﬃce plans were more resistant to layout changes than traditional cell oﬃces.
Changing the oﬃce layout to modernise the work environment allows better interaction
between the employees and the oﬃce environment and increase ﬂexibility (Rolfö et al.,
2017).
Open-plan oﬃces lack on privacy, have acoustic issues, and an overall reduced satisfaction
and performance due to low auditory and visual privacy compared to a single cell oﬃce.
Changing from open-plan oﬃces to activity-based workplaces might mitigate some of the
drawbacks from open-plan oﬃces. However, activity-based oﬃce spaces still lack research
and requires more attention. Activity-based oﬃces have a diversity compared to open-
plan that can improve communication and teamwork quality, whilst support privacy in
quiet spaces (Rolfö et al., 2017).
Moreover, not only the the oﬃce layout has an impact on the worker performance; there
are also concerns of indoor environment quality on workers well-being and productivity
as well as increasing energy costs. Another study compared a traditional oﬃce focusing
on the user in control with an open-plan oﬃce with limited user control. The traditional
oﬃce had an increase of 35% user satisfaction and 20% user comfort compared to the
open-plan oﬃce, however, the energy consumption was more than 10 times larger in the
traditional oﬃce than in the open-plan oﬃce (Shahzad et al., 2016).
It is a challenge to balance between building energy and comfort; thermal control has
proven to improve user comfort and reduce energy consumption. User control of thermal
environment is recognised as an important factor in the future, while it may also be seen
as unnecessary due to the ﬂexible workways and allocation of workstations that shifts
occupants and therefore it would be impractical to implement (Shahzad et al., 2016).
There will always be trade-oﬀs in creating the balance of energy consumption and occu-
pants comfort, making it diﬃcult for a company to choose the best strategy. Therefore,
the present study wanted to approach ventilated oﬃce spaces, integrating thermal com-
fort, air quality, and energy eﬃciency altogether within a feasible budget.

2.1
Purpose
The purpose of this study is to determine the minimum requirements for a ventilation
system for achieving optimal indoor climate with low energy use in ﬂexible oﬃces with
ﬁxed building envelopes.
2.2
Issues
It is a challenge to balance indoor climate and energy performance with cost when design-
ing oﬃce buildings, while maintaining and utilising a ﬂexible work environment. When
large corporate domiciles make organisational changes towards a more ﬂexible oﬃce lay-
out, it is uncertain how the changed design will inﬂuence the indoor climate and energy
consumption. There will be trade-oﬀs in creating the balance between energy consump-
tion and occupants comfort, however the design of oﬃce spaces should also be ﬂexible to
keep the ventilation design and operation resilient for changes in oﬃce layout and building
programmes.
In respect of the present study, oﬃce ﬂexibility was deﬁned as oﬃces being adaptable to
comply with diﬀerent indoor climate requirements, changes in oﬃce layout and changes
in building programme. Changes in oﬃce layout entailed varying distribution of open
oﬃces and ﬂexible workspaces, while the building programme consisted of occupancy and
equipment schedules as well as the magnitude of internal gains.
There are two fundamental questions that need to be addressed:
• How should ﬂexible oﬃces be designed to make them adaptive for future layout
changes and diﬀerent building programmes?
– How are oﬃces designed today and what are the challenges?
– Which oﬃce layouts and building programmes allow ﬂexible oﬃces to perform
best to meet indoor climate requirements?
• How should the ventilation system be designed to optimise and balance indoor
climate, energy performance and cost?
– How can the ventilation system be modelled and veriﬁed?
– Which recommendations can be proposed?
All of the questions are answered throughout the report.

2.3
Hypothesis
The hypothesis tested in the present study was that it is possible to achieve an acceptable
indoor climate with varying oﬃce layouts and building programmes.
A lower energy
consumption and cost of the ventilation system was expected in ﬂexible oﬃces compared
to open oﬃces.
2.4
Method approach
Flexibility of the oﬃce spaces were examined by following a step-by-step procedure used
to answer the thesis statement and accept or reject the hypothesis as following:
1. Build a reference model based on a case project.
2. Deﬁne several rearrangements of the oﬃce into new design by following the concept
for making ﬂexible oﬃces by changing layouts, schedules, and internal loads.
3. Run simulations to determine minimum requirements for the ventilation system of
the reference model build in (1), to deliver optimal indoor climate conditions with
low energy use in scenarios deﬁned in (2), and conduct a cost estimation of the
ventilation system.
4. Evaluate and compare scenario performances in indoor climate, energy and costs.
5. Propose recommendations.
2.5

step was to build a reference model of a case building provided by Rambøll. The case

Background
Companies require diﬀerent and unique oﬃce layouts in order to perform on the high-
est possible level, and the proper combination of work place design and organisational
strategies can increase the business value (De Paoli et al.). Over time, companies can
develop, expand or decrease, move to new locations, or have changing requirements and
needs in-house, and thus highly adaptive oﬃces can be a solution when designing new or
renovating existing oﬃces.
Personalisation also has a highly essential organisational and management perspective,
since it is "the amount of freedom and control the organisation allows the individual to
exert over to the workspace" (Sundstrøm, 1986).
Early in this project, it was vital to understand how oﬃces are designed today, but also
why they are designed in a certain way so that future oﬃce designs could be approached.
4.1
Oﬃce Types
A vary of oﬃce types are used in the industry, as they bear diﬀerent characteristics and
thus support diﬀerent work environments. The oﬃce types ranges between the ﬁrst ex-
treme being private oﬃces, to the other extreme being open, shared oﬃces. There are
beneﬁts as well as disadvantages linked with the diﬀerent oﬃce plans, as the physical
environment can be diﬀerent in diﬀerent oﬃce plans and it is aﬀecting the workers’ sat-
isfaction, work eﬃciency, and behaviour.
Oﬃce typologies were reviewed in order to
recognise future needs, the best possible oﬃce environment, to deﬁne ﬂexibility, and to
create realistic layout variations. The most prevalent and deﬁned oﬃce typologies are
presented in the following, including traditional private oﬃce, open-plan oﬃce and activ-
ity based oﬃce.
The indoor environment include thermal conditions, air quality, light, and noise, however,
noise is highly related to privacy, thus the psychological responses can be divided into the
three domains ambient factors, noise and privacy, and design-related factors (Danielsson
and Bodin, 2009).
4.1.1
Traditional Private Oﬃce
In terms of traditional oﬃce layouts, the typical oﬃces consist of single or shared-cell
oﬃces and are characterised with a ﬂoor plan divided by a corridor, see ﬁgure 4.1. The
rooms usually have access to one window. Most of the equipment is in the oﬃce.

The single cell oﬃce describes one extreme of oﬃce types and promotes highly concen-

and can fairly be fairly easy addressed.
The shared-room oﬃces are shared by two to three workers, typically. Shared-room oﬃces

however the workers tend to have the same work assignments (Danielsson and Bodin,
2009). Most of the equipment is typically outside the oﬃce.

In the 1950s, open-plan oﬃces were proposed to create ﬂexible space and reduce cost
in construction and maintenance because of the absense of internal physical barriers
(Brennan et al., 2002).
Typical open-plan oﬃces are deﬁned by employees sharing a
common workspace, see ﬁgure 4.2. There are no walls between the workstations, however,
screens can be used to separate workstations and to reduce noise create some privacy.
Most workers do not have access to individual windows.

In open-plan oﬃces, workspaces can be reconﬁgured to meet changing needs, because the
open areas are structurally less hindered. Diﬀerent deﬁnitions of open-plan oﬃce types
were found. The number of workers in the room deﬁnes the open-plan oﬃce as either
small, medium or large (Danielsson and Bodin, 2009), or the design complexity ranging

4.2
Ventilation Types
Categorising the diﬀerent ventilation types used in oﬃces, ventilation systems ranges from
natural ventilation systems to a partially or fully mechanical ventilation system. The
indoor climate is aﬀecting the workers’ eﬃciency and overall well-being, and a proper
ventilation system forms a great basis for a good indoor climate. There are beneﬁts as
well as disadvantages linked with the diﬀerent ventilation systems in terms of both indoor
climate and energy performance.
4.2.1
Natural Ventilation
Using a natural or passive ventilation system, natural forces are used to ventilate the oﬃce,
e.g. buoyancy forces or winds. The ventilation is often controlled by opening or closing
windows or doors, however, the ventilation can also take eﬀect because of inﬁltration, grills
or other openings in the façade. The obvious reason for utilising a natural ventilation is,
when successfully designed, reduced energy consumption. It is possible to achieve a high
performing ventilation system with little or no energy use, however, maintaining a high
indoor climate complying with the requirements is diﬃcult to achieve.
Using natural ventilation will mainly be plausible in a private or shared-room oﬃce, where
the worker has access to an operable window. In terms of this project, natural ventilation
was not considered to be available in the case building.
4.2.2
Mechanical Ventilation
Using a mechanical ventilation system, mechanical fans are installed in the oﬃce to supply
and/or exhaust air. The air is supplied or exhausted by the air terminal devices through
ducts that are connected to the air handling unit (AHU). Utilising a decentralised venti-
lation system can simplify installation cost and reduce ductwork, however, a centralised
systems tend to have a higher energy eﬃcieny and bear a greater load potential. Me-
chanical ventilation systems will allow less noise pollution to the building through open
windows, however, the mechanical ventilation system needs to be dimensioned properly
to avoid noise issues from the ducts and air terminal devices. Achieving and maintaining
a high indoor climate complying with the requirements is the main beneﬁt from installing
a mechanical ventilation system, but the energy consumption will also be higher.
Three principles of ventilation control are used: constant air volume (CAV), variable air
volume (VAV), and demand controlled ventilation (DCV). In a CAV system, the supply
air ﬂow rate is constant, but the temperature can vary, thus CAV systems are mostly used
in single zones, or when the ventilation requirements are the same in the diﬀerent zones.
VAV systems however vary the airﬂow, and will potentially reduce the fan energy con-
sumption. In a VAV system, the airﬂow is controlled by a schedule for the oﬃce use, but
does not adapt to the usage. In a DCV system, the airﬂow is controlled by the demand
in the oﬃce, e.g. regulating the airﬂow on changes in CO2 concentration or temperature.
DCV systems have a clear advantage when load diﬀerences are signiﬁcant. Figure 4.4
presents the diﬀerent ventilation control concepts.

4.3
Modern Oﬃce Buildings
A modern oﬃce building mostly uses state of the art solutions and may even introduce
innovation with futuristic concepts or new technologies.
In the present study, it was
mostly important that the modern oﬃce building entailed having a sustainable indoor
environment associated with the following traits (lecture 20 in ventilation and climatic
systems course):
• Well insulated and tight buildings with good solar shading.
• Mechanical air intake with good outdoor air ﬁltering.
• Demand based ventilation.
• Right temperature, high air quality, low velocities and noise in the space.
• Low energy cooling and heating technologies and renewable energy.

Energy Performance
The total energy demand of the oﬃce building for supplied energy for heating, cooling,
domestic hot water and lighting pr. m2 must not exceed 41.0 kWh/m2 a year, with an
additional 1.000 kWh a year divided with the total heated area (BR18).
Primary energy factors are applied to electrical, fuel, and district heating or cooling to
express the relation between the produced heat or electricity and the amount of fuel that
were needed to produce it. Primary energy factors are used to calculate the total energy
consumption in the building. The primary energy factors are 1.80 for electrical, 1.00 for
fuel, and 0.60 for district heating or cooling, when dimensioning for a class 2020 building
(SBI 2017:04).
The Danish Building Code BR18 requires a speciﬁc fan power (SFP) for the ventilation
system and cannot exceed 1,800 J/m3 for CAV systems and 2,100 J/m3 for VAV systems.
The Danish Building Code BR18 also states the EU regulation of heat recovery in venti-
lation systems which requires an eﬃciency of the heat recovery of at least 73%.

5.5
Expectations
The building programmes were designed to propose diﬀerent oﬃce operation, and thus
building programmes were expected to perform diﬀerently, due to the diﬀerent internal
loads and schedules.
The layout variations were designed based on the same principles to prevent external
forces to signiﬁcantly aﬀect the comparison between open oﬃces and ﬂexible workspaces.
The outcome were therefore expected to be a result of the varying building programmes
and area of the diﬀerent room types, i.e. diﬀerent loads and schedules.
5.5.1
Expected Building Programme Performance
The ﬂexible workspaces were dimensioned with a higher density than the open oﬃces,
however, the ﬂexible workspaces had reduced internal loads and generally less people
present at the same time. For these reasons, ﬂexible workspaces were expected to per-
form better than open oﬃces, as ﬂexible workspaces were more likely to achieve the
required indoor climate with less energy.
Building programme A had the highest internal loads as well as it was dimensioned to
be responsive to the most occupants being present at the same time, thus building pro-
gramme A was expected to have the highest energy demand.
Building programme B, C, and D were using the same schedule conﬁguration in terms of
breaks, and when the occupants were meeting or leaving the building, thus they should
be very comparable. Building C was expected to result in the highest energy consump-
tion, performing slightly better than building programme A. Building programme D was
expected to achieve the best results, while building programme B would ﬁt in between
building programme C and D in terms of energy.
5.5.2
Expected Scenario Performance
Among all of the scenarios, scenario 0 was expected to perform averagely, as open oﬃce
and ﬂexible workspace areas were allocated equally, i.e. 50% open-plan oﬃces and 50%
ﬂexible workspace areas.

General Ducting
In general the ducting was simpliﬁed to make all scenarios comparable using the following
guidelines:
• Straight and symmetrical layout, meaning as few bends as possible and that branches
had equal air ﬂows and pressure losses for ensuring a self-balancing system.
• Ducting was simpliﬁed and designed to run at room level and not include the air
terminal devices, i.e. inlets and outlets, by following the concept in ﬁgure 6.11.
• Fittings were limited to elbow bends, tees and reductions:
– Elbows were mostly 90 degree turns, however a few exceptions of 60 degree
turns were necessary due to the skewness of the ﬂoor plan layout.
– Tees could include a reduction if the straight duct connecting to the tee would
change dimension. This reduction was included in the tee single pressure loss,
but the cost was calculated made for both components.
– Reductions were made between rooms, but were also allowed every 10th meter
in the long spanned rooms, in order to adjust to the decreasing airﬂow across
the room length.
– Reductions decreased maximum three dimensions down, e.g. from 400 mm to
200 mm but not to 160 mm and below.
• Other installations layout were not included, however ducting was within the sus-
pended ceiling and respected the present structural system, i.e. columns.

Supply and Return Ducting
The supply and return ducting followed these guidelines:
• Supply ducting were ﬁrst dimensioned and then return ducts followed the supply
as the air ﬂow rates through both were equal.
• For simpliﬁcation, supply ducts ran in the middle of the rooms and ended 2 meters
from the back of the last room supplied to or at least halfway through the room (in
case of a short room).
• The return ducts ran parallel to the supply ducts, but closer to the building core in
the center of the corridor space or otherwise 1 m from the supply duct or a distance
to avoid clashes with columns. The return ducts ended 1 m before the supply or
immediately if it ends in a short room.
• Supply and return ducts were placed in separate heights to avoid any clashes be-
tween them.

6.4
Determining Pressure Loss
To determine the pressure loss a theoretical and practical calculation method were used.
The practical solution was based on read oﬀs from Lindab product catalogues found in
appendix G, while the theoretical solution was calculated from equations based on the
lecture note from DTU, Komfortventilation
The accuracy between the theoretical and
practical calculations were compared for the reference model calculations, and a decision
was then evaluated on which approach to use for the rest of the models. Pressure loss
calculation was performed on the model level of indoor climate categories similar to the
ducting layout.
In the DCV system the volume air ﬂow varied and thus the pressure losses. The total
pressure was calculated from where the farthest diﬀuser was located and back through
the ducts and ﬁttings all the way to the shaft where the AHU and total air ﬂow for the
section came from. This process required the ducting branch to be broken down into
sections calculated one at a time, a ducting section example is seen in ﬁgure 6.18.
Figure 6.18: Ducting sections example and notation.
When two separate ducting routes met at the same point, the maximum pressure loss was
used when calculating further back the ducting sections, while the lowest pressure would
need a damper to balance the diﬀerence in the pressure loss.
Branch sections were deﬁned by a duct piece and ﬁtting. Through the duct piece, the
pressure loss came from a friction loss calculated using equation 6.2.

Initial Costs

summarised in schedules including lengths, counts and dimensions. The data was then

were calculated on the model level of indoor climate categories, one estimation for each
ducting layout.
The initial cost included pricing of the ducting based on the length

Prices for the ducts and ﬁttings were based on the Lindab products and can be found in

in appendix H, and the products used for price calculations are listed here:
• Ducting: Spiralfalsede rør (SR)
• Elbows: Bøjninger - presset (BU) for small dimensions up to 200 mm, and Bøjninger
- falset (BFU) for larger dimensions from 250 mm and up.
• Reductions: Reduktioner (RCFU)
• Tees: T-rør (TCPU)
Price estimating the SR ducting was simpliﬁed to being only the price pr single meter
(kr./m in the price catalogue) and disregard other meter lengths of pricing and added

9.1
Indoor Climate
All scenarios were designed and modelled to achieve optimal indoor climate for the indoor
air quality which was measured by the CO2 concentration.
Thermal conditions were
not necessarily optimal and was investigated, but ﬁrst it was established if there was
optimal air quality. To limit the analysis to the key parameters, overheating was not
investigated further than it was simulated in IDA ICE as the building envelope was kept
ﬁxed and was deemed irrelevant for comparison. The overheating hours were reﬂected
in the temperature as overheating hours are outside the interval from the requirements.

10
Discussion
This section summarises and discusses the key ﬁndings on layout design, building pro-
grammes, indoor climate, energy, and cost.
10.1
Layout
Scenario 0 was based on the case building in order to have a realistic layout as a bench-
mark. Changes were made to the oﬃce layout, e.g. the meeting rooms were moved away
from the outer zone to the inner zone to make the critical rooms less critical by potentially
reduce overheating. The changes made were done to create an overall more realistic and
useful model build on general principles used in oﬃce environments, and thus conclusions
drawn in the present study can be used for other oﬃce environments. To this extent,
scenario 0 presented a realistic contemporary oﬃce layout.
Scenario 4 allocated only open oﬃces and might represent realistic oﬃce layout, as open
oﬃces are very common. In contrast, scenario 2 consisting of only ﬂexible workspaces
might not represent a contemporary oﬃce layout, however, it might not be unrealistic.
Based on literature found for this study, ﬂexible workspaces are preferred over open oﬃces
because of a decreased satisfaction with the physical environment in open oﬃces. This
study also found advantages for ﬂexible workspaces, pointing that this oﬃce type could
be preferred in future oﬃce layouts.
This study looked at the eﬀects of layout changes in regard to oﬃce area, but changing the
numbers and locations of meeting rooms would have made the analysis broader for typical
oﬃce layouts as meeting rooms is a necessary room for an oﬃce building to function and
they also require more ventilation than said oﬃce rooms.
10.2
Building Programmes
The building programmes were designed to represent diﬀerent ways of dimensioning of-
ﬁces, and to illustrate diﬀerent oﬃce operations. Building programme A was based on
the case building, and was dimensioned for all occupants being present at the same time.
After consultancy with Rambøll, it was discussed that it might not be the case to dimen-
sion for a 100% concurrency. Building programme A was however a great indicator of
how much the internal gains aﬀect the overall system.
Considering breaks is recommended, and this reduces the overall energy consumption.
Based on the literature, building programmes for a regular, high, and low number of oc-
cupants present were scheduled. Building programme C might over dimension the system
in practice, whilst building programme D might be to risky for its low occupancy. Build-
ing programme B represented a regular work day and would be the most representative
building programme for an oﬃce building project.
Dimensioning for a lot of people present at the same time aﬀected the energy performance
and cost negatively, however, it would be more resistant to diﬀerent loads, i.e. capacity
changes.

Ventilation
10.3.1
Ventilation Rates
The required ventilation rates set for the room types of open oﬃce, ﬂexible workspace and
meeting room were based on occupancy densities of 8, 6 and 2.5 m2 per person, diﬀerent
from the standard DS/EN 15251. A comparison is seen in table 10.1.
Table 10.1: Comparison of occupant density.
Open oﬃces were much more dense than deﬁned from the standard, which was more
realistic when designing the oﬃce spaces. The ﬂexible workspaces were deﬁned to be
denser because of the workstation being deﬁned with less permanent equipment, however
the presence level of occupants was lowered to compensate. It can be diﬃcult to esti-
mate the density and presence of people in the oﬃces, which was why multiple building
programmes were investigated. The variations showed that changing the occupant and
equipment schedules made a signiﬁcant impact on the operational cost and energy con-
sumption of the ventilation system, in order to keep the air quality level optimal.
From chapter 8, it was found that the ventilation rate was increased by 40% when ven-
tilating for IC category B to A, which was expected because of the criteria set for each
category. The ﬂexible workspace was ventilated with 2.7 l/s pr m2, while the open oﬃce
was ventilated with 2.3 l/s pr m2 in IC category A. For IC category B, the ventilation
was 1.9 l/s pr m2 and 1.6 l/s pr m2 for ﬂexible workspaces and open oﬃces, respectively.
Therefore, it was expected for Scenario 1 and 2 both with most ﬂexible workspaces to have
higher ventilation rates, and Scenario 3 and 4 with open oﬃces to have lower ventilation
rates compared with Scenario 0. Results showed the same trend as predicted, however
the magnitudes only varied up to 400 m3/h in the range of 2,700 and 5,900 m3/h in total.
10.3.2
Ventilation System
There was a lower pressure loss for ducting in IC category A compared to B, likely to be
because of category A having larger duct sizes reducing the pressure gradients. The duct-
ing principle used in the present study also impacted the pressure losses and in the end
caused inconsistent duct size increases and decreases because of air ﬂow varying between
room borders. The ducting principle was simpliﬁed but did not necessarily reﬂect a real-
istic ducting system. Pressure losses were small in the range of 70-100 Pa and diﬀerences
might have been larger if the actual air terminal devices were included in addition to other
ventilation components such as dampers and silencers. For both supply and return air, it
was seen that the ducting pressure loss had no correlation with the total air ﬂows, again
probably caused by the ducting principle which specially resulted in high single losses.

In the AHU the pressure had a diﬀerent trend as category B was much lower than cate-
gory A. There was a high correlation between AHU air ﬂows and pressures on both the
supply and return air sides, meaning that the AHU was a crucial component compared
to the ducting system. The AHU were all modelled as the same type but had large vari-

performances of the ventilation system. Modelling the DCV system in IDA ICE proved

In practice the heat exchanger would diﬀerentiate much more because of variying air ﬂows

10.4
Indoor Climate
10.4.1
Air Quality
The air quality was modelled to be optimal in all scenarios, as the DCV system was
controlled with a CO2 setpoint 10 ppm below the requirement, and thus ensuring 100%
compliance in all scenarios. Maximum CO2 concentrations were presented for all scenarios
and showed that they did not vary signiﬁcantly, except that scenario 1 and 2 with most
ﬂexible workspaces had an upper value of CO2 of 100 ppm higher than the other scenarios.
As all scenarios complied with the requirement within each category, the resulting CO2
concentration did not mean much for the diﬀerent scenarios.
10.4.2
Thermal Conditions
The thermal conditions were analysed between the open oﬃces and ﬂexible workspace in
scenario 0, 2 and 4. It was clear that the ﬂexible workspaces stayed within the required
temperature range for more time than for the open oﬃces. Comparing the means of all
scenarios showed that ﬂexible oﬃces had 20% better thermal conditions than the open
oﬃces. Open oﬃces had the worst thermal conditions in Scenario 1 where there were
more ﬂexible workspaces, while ﬂexible workspaces had the best thermal conditions in
scenario 3 where there were most open oﬃces. This results implied that despite being
opposite in distribution, the layout mattered as the orientation of the rooms were exposed
to diﬀerent external heat gains. Overall, the ﬂexible workspaces performed best in terms
of thermal conditions.
Limitations for the thermal conditions was that the investigation was bound in just the
operative temperature for measuring the thermal comfort. Setpoints in IDA ICE were
critical because the heating and cooling system and ventilation system controlled the
temperature. but the results were comparable though as the models were constructed in
the same way, and thus the results can be justiﬁed. Opening of zones were simpliﬁed in
IDA ICE, having walls surrounding all the zones, when in fact they should be open. In
order to balance pressure diﬀerences between the zones, doors were added to the walls to
have a leakage area, and therefore the airﬂow between the intended open zones would be
diﬀerent. PMV and PPD were neglected because they already depended on the operative
temperature, and since the air ﬂow within the zones was modelled diﬀerently because of
the openings, it was deemed reasonable to evaluate thermal comfort on just the operative
temperature.

10.5
Energy
The building energy did not comply with the energy frame requirements, however, the
models were not optimised to do so. The building case was simpliﬁed in IDA ICE, and
to answer the problem statement within the project scope, the relative diﬀerences be-
tween the scenarios were subjects for investigation. The modelled ventilation system was
designed to comply with guidelines for ventilation rates, pressure loss and air velocities
in the ducting system. The ventilation system and its energy performance was the only
variable parameter throughout the scenarios which was inﬂuencing the energy consump-
tion, while the relationship between the energy consumption of the ventilation system
and the building energy showed diﬀerently. The correlation between them was examined
and it was found that there was no relationship between the two. From the comparison
of scenarios, it could be seen that scenario 1 had a distinctly higher ventilation energy
consumption compared to the other scenarios, while at the same time it also had the low-
est total building energy consumption. This trend in all the building programmes were
contradicting and could be explained by one of two things. Either the ventilation energy
was lower in the other scenarios because the heating and cooling system of the building
took over what the AHU was in control of, or that the simpliﬁcation of the building case
made its energy consumption unreliable. The latter could be optimised by adding solar
power, changing the heating and cooling system or improving the building envelope.
As scenario 2 and scenario 4 were opposite in layout, they were used to compare the energy
demand, because they had the same external loads in every zone. For the ventilation
energy, scenario 4 with open oﬃces were performing up to 8% better than scenario 2
with ﬂexible oﬃces. However as previously discussed, it was the other way around when
looking at the whole building energy, where the diﬀerence was up to 15% in the high
demanded building programme A in favour of scenario 2.

Recommendations
It was found that the ducting system had almost no signiﬁcant impact on the energy
performance and cost of the ventilation system. So it can be recommended to focus on
choosing the correct AHU and evaluate the required ventilation rate to each room as
the resulting total air ﬂow deﬁned the energy consumption of the ventilation system. It
would also highly impact the cost to deﬁne the investment strategy to estimate how long
the ventilation system should be operated, and how high ﬂexibility is rated in the oﬃce
building.
Based on the discussion of the occupant density and required ventilation rates in the oﬃce
room types, recommendations can be made to determine their magnitudes by settling it
with the client. It should open a discussion on how to adjust the design criteria according
to the building programme by its demands including when and how many people work,
what type of work employees perform and with which tools, e.g. desktop PC’s and tablets.
Another consideration should be on how the oﬃce is furnished and the arrangements of
tables and seating areas, since open oﬃces can lead to acoustic issues which could be
solved by having a mixture of ﬂexible workspace with integrated quite spaces.
For the building programmes, it was discussed on how to dimension the oﬃce. A high
intensity building programme might oversize the ventilation system, while a low occu-
pancy building programme could increase the risk of a not well-performing system with
dissatisﬁed occupants. It can be suggested that if the building client wants a resilient
solution, they should aim for designing a building programme that can accomodate ca-
pacity changes such as building programme C.
Building programme B proved that it could be advised to change the design criteria from
category B to A, thus improving the indoor climate quality level sacriﬁcing cost and energy
consumption for the ventilation system for an increase of 10% and 25%, respectively.

Conclusion
Studies show that open-plan oﬃces does not perform as intended, whilst activity based of-
ﬁces on the contrary might mitigate some of the drawbacks from open-plan oﬃces. Based
on literature regarding the open-plan and activity based oﬃces, two oﬃce were designed
and introduced: open oﬃces and ﬂexible workspaces.
Flexible oﬃces were investigated to determine how the ventilation system should be de-
signed to achieve optimal indoor climate and a low energy consumption with a feasible
cost. A reference model for an oﬃce plan was build from a case building to create ﬁve
diﬀerent layouts by changing the distribution of open oﬃces and ﬂexible workspaces in the
outer zones. The ventilation system was a demand control ventilation system designed
to meet category A and B, and its performance was simulated for four diﬀerent building
programmes, varying in occupancy schedules and internal gains.
The occupant density and ventilation rates were higher than in the standards for the oﬃce
areas. Flexible workspaces were ventilated more than open oﬃces, but had fewer people
present at the same time. The total ventilation rates for the investigated building section
did not change signiﬁcantly for the ﬁve layouts. Changing the required ventilation from
category B to A, increased the ventilation rate by 40%.
For the ventilation system, it was found that the ducting system had a small impact
on the energy performance because the performance of the air handling unit was relying
more on the total air ﬂows than on the pressure loss from the ducting system. The duct-
ing system only made up 25% of the initial cost, while the air handling unit cost 75%
of the total, which did not change signiﬁcantly between the scenarios. A higher energy
consumption of the ventilation system lead to higher operation costs which amounted to
8-23% of the total cost on a 5 year investment period.
It was possible to achieve high indoor air quality in all scenarios with varying energy
consumption and costs. Flexible workspaces complied with the thermal conditions 20%
more than open oﬃces. The ventilation energy consumption did not impact the thermal
conditions. Scenario 1 and 2 with most ﬂexible workspaces had lower energy consumption
at a building level, but higher for the ventilation system compared to the reference model.
Scenario 3 and 4 with most open oﬃces had the opposite tendency.
The highest energy consumption was found in building programme A, as it had the highest
loads and concurrency, whilst building programme D had the best energy performance.
Keeping the same air handling unit for all scenarios proved to be cost eﬃcient as the
varying operational costs did not out outbalance the initial cost.
All building programmes showed that the scenarios reacted equally changing from cat-
egory B to A demands, meaning that the oﬃce could be designed to accommodate for
diﬀerent layouts, changing occupant schedules and internal gains. For the regular building
programme B, an increase in quality from category B to A resulted in 24-26% increase in
ventilation energy consumption, and 9-10% increased costs for the ventilation system.

Future Work
The present study was constricted by the given time frame and only allowed investigation
of a focused part of investigating indoor climate, energy and costs for the ventilation
system in ﬂexible oﬃces as described in the project scope in section 2.5. Future research
and work on relevant topics for designing ﬂexible oﬃces could include any of the following:
• Comparison of single oﬃces with open-plan and activity based oﬃces.
• Quantify the qualities of the ﬂexible workspaces, by answering how ﬂexibility can
be evaluated when designing oﬃces?
• Conducting measurements to validate indoor climate in either lab based environ-
ment or real oﬃce buildings.
• Introducing ventilation system types as another variation parameter, including dis-
placement ventilation, VAV and CAV systems, underﬂoor ventilation and personal
or local ventilation systems.
• Perform similar simulations with a tool, where the open zones can be modelled.
• CFD analyses for detailed indoor climate results in relation to air movement and
local discomfort at occupant level.
• Multiple buildings with varying use and layouts for further comparison, even intro-
ducing the building envelope as another variation parameter.
• Comparing old versus new oﬃce buildings, as newer buildings are more likely to
have better space usage and generally higher ﬂoor heights, where as renovating old
building will give constraints in the space given.
• Further details of the ducting system including all ﬁttings and detailed cost estima-
tion for maintenance and replacement pieces etc.

Safe-systemet
• Safe er et hurtigt monteret system til runde
ventilationskanaler.
• Safe er typegodkendt til klasse D af Kiwa Sverige AB, nr.
1105.
• Hele programmet har dimensioner i henhold til Dansk
Standard DS-EN 1506.
• Systemet bygger på en dobbelt, fabriksmonteret tæt-
ningsliste af EPDM-gummi. Listen, som er meget
modstandsdygtig og er upåvirket af temperatursving- 
ninger, giver en meget lufttæt samling.
Fordele ved Safe-systemet
• Hurtig montage.
• Fabriksmonteret tætning uden løse dele.
• Kan vrides og finjusteres uden at tætheden påvirkes.
• Monteres uden tætningsmasse
• Kan anvendes i al slags vejr.
• Tætningslisten kan klare undertryk på 5.000 Pa og
overtryk på 3.000 Pa, uden at tætheden forringes.
• Kanalens modstandsdygtighed overfor kollaps adskil-
ler sig fra disse tryk, og kan ses under rør SR.
• Typegodkendt til tæthedsklasse D.
Typegodkendelse
Godkendelse nr. 1105 betyder, at Safe-systemet opfylder 
kravene til tæthedsklasse D i henhold til EN 12237. 
Godkendelsen gælder kun under forudsætning af, at alle 
faconstykker er af typen Lindab Safe, og at de er monteret i 
henhold til vores montagevejledning.
En anden forudsætning er, at der er et “normalt” forhold 
mellem rør og faconstykker dvs. antal samlinger (se evt. DS/
EN 12237)
Tæthedsklasse D gælder for systemer udført i galvaniseret 
plade.
Eurovent certificering
Lindabs cirkulære kanalsystemer Lindab Safe og Lindab 
Safe Click er af Eurovent certificeret i forhold til styrke og 
tæthed i tæthedsklasse D i henhold til Eurovent Certified 
Performance program for cirkulære kanalsystemer, fremstil-
let af metal (DUCT – MC). Certifikaters gyldighed kan altid 
kontrollers på:
www.eurovent-certification.com
Lindabs produkter, som er omfattet af Eurovent certificerin-
gen, har Eurovent logoet på katalogsiderne.
Bemærk: De fleste af vores Lindab Safe og Lindab Safe 
Click produkter er tæthedmæssigt bedre end tæthedsklasse 
D. Selvom enkelte produkter ikke kan opfylde klasse D i
henhold til EN 15727, kan det samlede kanalsystem godt
overholde denne klasse under forudsætning af, at disse pro-
dukter er monteret i begrænset omfang. Det fremgår af
katalogsiderne, hvis et produkt kun klarer tæthedsklasse C.
Tæthed
Et kanalsystem bliver aldrig “helt tæt”. Normalt lækker 
systemet ved samlinger mellem rør og fittings. Lækagen vil 
desuden stige ved større trykforskel mellem den indvendige 
og udvendige side af kanalsystemet.
Lækagefaktoren (l/s)/m2 angives derfor altid i forhold til trykfor-
skellen i Pa. (Enheden (l/s)/m2 angiver lækagemængden i l/s 
ind eller ud af systemet i forhold til kanalarealet i m2.) Neden-
stående diagram viser lækagefaktoren for tæthedsklasserne 
A–D som funktion af trykforskellen (ref. DS/EN 12237)
Diagrammet viser, at tæthedsklasse D er 3 gange bedre end 
klasse C, som på sin side er 3 gange bedre end klasse B 
osv. Klasse D medfører således ikke blot krav til tætningsli-
sten, men også til komponenterne, og til hvor godt systemet 
er monteret.
Det er bl.a. derfor vi har forsynet alle faconstykker med en 
ombøjet kant og forsynet endnu flere faconstykker med stop-
sick.
Derigennem får vi stabile produkter, som bedre tåler håndte-
ring på byggepladsen, samtidig med at risikoen for skæv 
montering reduceres.

Økonomi – Tæthed
Med de høje krav, der i dag stilles til indeklimaet, følger en 
kostbar behandling af luften. Lækage medfører uøkonomisk 
drift, indreguleringsproblemer samt overdimensionerede 
anlæg. Det er derfor vigtigt, at ventilationssystemer er meget 
tætte for at holde de totale omkostninger nede. Derfor 
afhænger myndighedernes krav om tæthed også af syste-
mets størrelse og anvendelsesområde.
Kontrol/Afprøvning
For at Safe skal opfylde kravene til tæthedsklasse D har vi 
konstante kontrolprocedurer, hvor vi foretager daglige stik-
prøver. Kontrollen udføres dels som ankomstkontrol af varer 
modtaget fra underleverandører og dels vor egen produk-
tion af rør og faconstykker.
Modtagelseskontrollen følger Svensk Standard både med hen-
syn til afprøvningsmetoder og acceptniveauer. Kontrollen 
omfatter følgende punkter:
1
Kontrol af tætningslistens indvendige diameter. Dette er 
specielt vigtigt for gummiens ældningsbestandighed. Jo 
mere gummilisten er belastet, enten af træk eller tryk, jo 
hurtigere ældes den, med sprødhed og revner til følge.
2
Gummitætningens profil måles i en profilprojektør, hvor 
tætningslistens dimensioner kontrolleres i h. til gæl-
dende tolerancer.
3
Tætningslistens materiale testes gennem accelereret 
påvirkning i varmeovn.
Fremstillingskontrollen registreres. Kontrollen omfatter en dia-
meterkontrol af rør og faconstykker, en kontrol af det spor, hvor 
tætningslisten fastgøres, samt en kontrol af fastgørelsen af 
disse. Prøvetryk foretages i vort lufttekniske laboratorium for at 
kontrollere lækagemængden fra vore produkter. Dette giver 
imidlertid ikke et komplet billede, så den bedste kontrol af 
Safe-systemet er den tryktest, som foretages på vilkårligt 
udvalgte produkter. Ved alle disse trykafprøvninger har Safe-
systemet altid ligget over de gældende tæthedskrav.
Faconstykker
Produkter under fanebladet Safe og faconstykker med Safe-
tætning under fanebladene Lyddæmpere, Spjæld, Måling og 
indregulering samt isol indgår i typegodkendelsen for tæt-
hedsklasse D.
Enkelte faconstykker med Safe-tætning kan kun klare op til 
tæthedsklasse C. Dette er da angivet ved de respektive pro-
dukter.
Faconstykker i dette katalog med et “U” i deres betegnelse er 
med ganske få undtagelser forsynet med Safe-tætning.
Undertryk
Ved store undertryk er der risiko for, at et ventilationssystem 
kollapser. Denne risiko er større, jo større dimensioner man 
har.
For at øge kanalernes styrke kan man f.eks. øge deres pla-
detykkelse. Dette er en enkel måde, men effekten er ikke så 
stor. Der findes andre måder med bedre virkning. For store 
dimensioner kan kanalerne da blive stærkere end faconstyk-
kerne.
For at øge faconstykkernes styrke er tillige andre måder end 
øget pladetykkelse mere hensigtsmæssige.
Lindab har erfaring og viden om dette og hjælper gerne med 
at løse specielle opgaver.

Konstruktion
Vores tætningssystem Safe er baseret på en U-profil af 
homogent gummi. Tætningslisten ligger i et spor på facon-
stykkeenden og er fastgjort med et stålbånd.
Som standard leveres Safe-faconstykker altid med en tæt-
ningsliste af EPDM-gummi (Ethylen-Propylen gummi). Materia-
let er valgt på grund af dets lange levetid og store 
modstandsdygtighed overfor ozon og UV-stråler. Det er også 
meget ufølsomt overfor temperatursvingninger. Under normale 
betingelser, klarer listen:
-30 °C til +100 °C kontinuerligt
-50 °C til +120 °C kortvarigt
Som speciale til anlæg med krav om høje temperaturer og 
noget bedre oliebestandighed kan Safe-faconstykkerne for-
synes med en speciel liste af siliconegummi. Denne liste kan 
genkendes på den blå farve.
Temperaturtolerance:
-70 °C til +150 °C kontinuerligt
-90 °C til +200 °C kortvarigt
Når faconstykket monteres i kanalen, bliver tætningslistens 
læber bøjet bagud. Derved vil tætningen bedre kunne klare 
undertryk end overtryk, da undertrykket vil presse læberne 
hårdere mod kanalens indvendige side. For at overholde 
tæthedsklasse D må følgende trykforskelle ikke overskrides.

dimensioner, har vi valgt at anvende successivt større tæt-
ningslister i takt med at kanaldimensionerne øges.

Undertryk:
Ved anlæg med kraftigt undertryk i forhold til atmosfæren kan 
der være risiko for, at ventilationskanaler "klapper" sammen. 
Det starter ved kanalens svageste punkt, som oftest en bule 
eller lignende fra transport eller håndtering. Det er derfor 
vigtigt, at kanaler er helt intakte, såfremt driftstrykkene 
nærmer sig de kritiske tryk.
Overtryk:
Ved store overtryk vil der opstå støj ved tætningslisterne i 
kanalsystemets samlinger. Ved endnu større tryk vil sam-
lingerne deformeres. Hvis man har haft succes med at tætne 
tilslutningerne / samlingerne meget godt, vil rørene åbne sig i 
falsen, men dette fænomen intræder først ved tryk, som ikke 
er aktuelle i ventilationsanlæg.
