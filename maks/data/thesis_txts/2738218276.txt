Abstract
It is well-known that ANNs are universal approximators. Recently, PINNs have
emerged as a method of solving partial differential equations (PDEs). Their per-
formance has been well-studied for lower-order equations, but literature is sparse
for their performance on higher-order equations. In this master’s thesis, we aim to
expand current knowledge on their behavior for higher-order problems. We present
the theory of artificial neural networks (ANNs), physics-informed neural networks
(PINNs) and deep operator networks (DeepONets). We apply it to a plane stress
problem derived from a linear elastic plate with a small circular hole. The resulting
boundary value problem consists of the homogeneous biharmonic equation together
with a number of second-order conditions on the boundary. For practical use, we
would like the models to have a maximum L2 relative error of 1%.
Our results show that the problem cannot easily be solved with the basic PINN
framework presented in [1].
We need to apply additional techniques to improve
convergence and lower the training time. After thorough testing of numerous tech-
niques, we end up with a much more accurate model with a relative L2 error of
0.09% for the von Mises stress. If provided with data in addition to the physics, the
relative L2 error decreases to 0.02%.
Besides the thorough investigation of the plane stress problem, the project will hope-
fully contribute more generally to the area of scientific machine learning (SciML)
as a guide to applying more novel techniques to physics-informed and data-driven
models.
Future research objectives include evaluating the the performance of PINNs on plane
stress problems with more complex geometries, as well as fully examining the ca-
pabilities of DeepONets to solve plane stress problems with varying geometries and
boundary conditions. Further research also involves extending the applicability of
SciML to nonlinear elasticity and exploring three-dimensional domains.

Introduction
Partial differential equations (PDEs) are ubiquitous in science and engineering, and
for many decades, researchers have looked for efficient ways of computing solutions.
Many numerical methods have been developed, and they each have their strengths
and weaknesses.
Prominent examples are finite element methods (FEM), finite
difference methods (FDM), finite volume methods (FVM), and spectral methods.
These are what we would usually call classical numerical methods. They are well-
established and popular, because they are based on rigorous theory which guarantees
convergence in one way or another. In many applications, it is essential to be able
to determine the accuracy of the calculations done.
Recently, another approach to solving PDEs has become popular.
It is the uti-
lization of deep learning (DL), in particular artificial neural networks (ANNs), for
constructing surrogate models. The reason is that ANNs are very flexible in terms
of function approximation, and evaluation of a trained neural network is fast. The
model fitting is typically done with gradient-based optimization, which is feasible
due to automatic differentiation (AD). Purely data-driven models do, however, as
the name implies, require data, and often a lot of it. In many situations, data is not
available or only available in small amounts. One way to circumvent this is by incor-
porating physics directly into the optimization problem. Work such as [2, 3, 4] has
contributed to the development and eventually, this has led to the physics-informed
neural network (PINN) [1], which is the central framework in this project. With the
PINN, we are able to utilize AD to incorporate physical law described by differential
equations directly in the loss function. Since this milestone, the development has
accelerated remarkably.
1.1
Previous Work
From the initial development of the PINN [1, 5, 6], many extensions to the frame-
work has been produced. Each of these extensions build on the core principles of
the PINN, but add other computational techniques.
Conservative PINNs (cPINNs) [7] can solve conservation laws utilizing domain de-
composition with enforcement of flux continuity at the domain interfaces. Extended
PINNs (XPINNs) [8] generalize this to any type of PDE and simplifies the interface
conditions.
Multi-fidelity PINNs (MPINNs) employ three neural networks. The first approxi-
mates low-fidelity data, and the other two learn linear and nonlinear correlations,

respectively, between low- and high-fidelity data.
There has also been variational formulations, such as VPINNs [9] and hp-VPINNs
[10], as well as FEM-inspired approaches like Finite Basis PINNs (FBPINNs) [11]
which make use of basis functions with overlapping compact support.
Fractional PINNs (fPINNs) [12] can solve equations of fractional order, and gradient-
enhanced PINNs (gPINNs) [13] impose further conditions on the gradients of the
PDE residuals in order to speed up convergence.
It seems there are endless variations of the PINN, and it is often even possible to
combine these extensions.
The PINN falls under the emerging area of scientific machine learning (SciML)
[14], in particular physics-informed machine learning (PIML) [15].
This area of
computational science is concerned with achieving better computational models by
combining data and physics using various tools from machine learning (ML). It is ex-
periencing rapid development because of all the applications of fast surrogate models.
Since the PINN is still a relatively new framework for solving PDEs, many open
questions about it remains. One of these is its capabilities when it comes to solving
PDEs of higher orders. Many results show its strengths and weaknesses by solving
low-order PDEs such as advection-diffusion problems and Poisson problems, but
problems involving PDEs of higher orders than two are less frequently examined.
Thus, while much of the theory of PINNs generalizes to PDEs of any order, the
practical feasibility of the methods on these problems is not well-established.
An important development in SciML is operator approximations.
Recently, the
deep operator network (DeepONet) [16] has been developed. This has made it pos-
sible to approximate operators, which in turn can be used to learn entire families of
PDEs. This is in contrast to the PINN which, at least in its original formulation,
only learns a single PDE. Because of this, we can train operator networks on many
PDE problems belonging to a certain family and then expect to see good approxi-
mation of previously unseen PDEs.
Other operator learning frameworks have since been developed. One example is the
Fourier neural operator (FNO) [17] in which the integral kernel is parameterized in
Fourier space. There is the physics-informed DeepONet (PI-DeepONet) [18] which
makes it possible to incorporate prior knowledge of the physics into the model in the
same way as PINNs. The multiple-input operator network (MIONet) [19] makes it
possible to learn even larger families of solutions to PDEs by extending the universal
approximation theorem to operators on products of Banach spaces.
Neural operators are particularly interesting, because we want fast surrogate mod-
els, which can make accurate predictions of solutions to previously unseen problems,
e.g. problems with new boundary conditions.
1.2
The Focus of This Project
Our focus lies in the domain of linear elasticity. Linear elasticity problems are rel-
evant in numerous areas of science. They are important in mechanical and civil
engineering, where knowing the strength of materials is crucial. They are useful in
so many situations, from small-scale problems such as faster 3D-printing to large-
2
==PAGE:15==
1.3. Outline
scale infrastructure problems such as building tunnels and bridges. Therefore, fast
and accurate computational methods are highly desirable.
We examine a plane stress problem involving a plate with a circular hole. This
problem can be formulated as a boundary value problem involving the biharmonic
equation, a fourth-order PDE. Similar biharmonic problems, though not the exact
same, have been examined in [20, 21, 22]. The problems in these papers differ by
the type of boundary conditions and mostly also by the geometry of the domain. By
considering a problem slightly different to those in one way or another, this project
might also give the reader a better understanding of the variation in difficulty of
problems that may seem comparable at first glance.
The techniques tested will
further underline the large variation in performance of these methods. We will em-
phasize both the theory of the methods and apply them to the plane stress problem.
The focus is thus split in two: A thorough understanding of the theory of PIML, and
a concrete application highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of the methods on
higher-order equations.
The implementation of the models will be done in JAX [23] and libraries built on
top of it [24, 25]. JAX is a modern Python framework for scientific computations,
and its features make it especially useful for deep learning. Among other things,
it implements just-in-time (JIT) compilation and AD, and it utilizes the acceler-
ated linear algebra (XLA) compiler for fast computations on accelerators such as
graphics processing units (GPUs) or tensor processing units (TPUs). The appendix
contains a chapter showing the central ideas in the JAX framework. These include
the functional programming paradigm and the idea of stateless computations. We
also include examples of how to implement PINNs in the appendix.
1.3
Outline
The thesis is structured in two main parts, a theoretical one and an experimental one.
In chapter 2, we go through the general theory of neural networks, including a
presentation of popular architectures, their approximation properties, how auto-
matic differentiation works, as well as the basics of training and optimization in
deep learning. This chapter is mostly concerned with the basics of classical deep
learning, although we mostly include theory that is also relevant in PIML.
In chapter 3 we introduce the PINN framework, starting with the general idea and
then moving on to the mathematical formulation. Simple examples are provided
along the way to demonstrate how the theory translates into practice. We explain
the weaknesses of PINNs and present techniques that aim to circumvent these.
In chapter 4, we briefly present the theory of linear elasticity before stating our
main problem and its analytical solution. We describe our experiments and provide
results showing the effects of the techniques presented in chapter 3 on the main
problem. Along the way, we assess the importance of each technique.
In chapter 5, we discuss the overall results from the numerous tests done.

1.4
Contributions
While the main problem is to solve the biharmonic equation, we try to emphasize
valuable and important theory and useful techniques for PINNs in general. Con-
sequently, we include examples and illustrations in order to facilitate easy under-
standing of the concepts. We then hope people reading this thesis will gain a better
understanding of the mathematics in deep learning and as a result be equipped with
more useful tools for solving PDEs using physics-informed machine learning.
The master’s project includes the following main contributions:
• An extensive theoretical overview of neural networks and automatic differen-
tiation, as well as a thorough introduction to the PINN framework, including
mathematical formulations, examples, and valuable techniques for handling
slow (or lack of) convergence.
• The application of PINNs to a linear elastic plane stress problem involving the
biharmonic equation.
• A detailed investigation of the challenges emerging when applying the PINN
framework to higher-order differential equations, as well as the effects of nu-
merous techniques on performance.
• A large JAX-based framework for easy creation and tweaking of physics-
informed and/or data-driven models, including base models such as PINNs
and DeepONets. Although most implementations are for linear elasticity, the
framework is flexible and allows for easy implementation of other problems.
• An elaboration on more advanced techniques for higher-order automatic dif-
ferentiation in the appendix.
Overall, the project contributes to the rapidly developing area of scientific machine
learning by familiarizing the readers with the most important theoretical concepts
so that they can apply them to their own domain of interest. The theoretical part of
the report can also be used as a first introduction to PINNs and the mathematical
foundation of deep learning in general. Furthermore, the elaborate examination of
the plane stress problem can help identify where the main challenges in PIML lie,

Neural Networks
The artificial neural network (ANN), or just neural network (NN), is something
most people have heard about lately, due to large language models (LLMs) becom-
ing available for practical use. However, neural networks have existed for a long
time in various forms. In this chapter, we explain the basics of neural networks and
why they are useful.
We build up the intuition behind neural networks, starting with the first approaches.
We then explain the universal approximation theorem for neural networks, which is
paramount to the development of the physics-informed neural network. Addition-
ally, we give examples of prominent network architectures relevant to the project.
When this theory has been established, we present one of the defining algorithms in
deep learning, namely the backpropagation algorithm, and how it is made feasible
by automatic differentiation.
2.1
The Artificial Neuron

2.1.2
From Binary Numbers to Real Numbers
The perceptron is the precursor to what we typically denote neurons or units of a
neural network today. The inputs and outputs of these units are not necessarily
binary numbers. Typically they are real numbers, and in some cases even complex
numbers. We are only concerned with the real case in this project, but many results
and theorems are extendable to the complex case.

2.2
The Multilayer Perceptron
The multilayer perceptron (MLP), also known as a feed-forward neural network
(FNN) is a term for a neural network consisting multiple layers of neurons.
In
general, it does not consist of the original perceptron units, so the name is a bit
of a misnomer. Nevertheless, it is often used. When we refer to the MLP, we do
not refer to a network of perceptrons, but just a network of modern artificial neurons.
The layers of the MLP are ordered, and information flows one way. This is the
reason for the alternative name, the feed-forward neural network. A simple MLP
is illustrated in figure 2.2. The neurons of a layer in the MLP are not connected
with each other, but to the neurons in the previous and next layers. The input to a
neuron is a weighting of the outputs of all neurons in the previous layer. The output

2.3
Other Variants of the Neural Network
The last couple of decades, development of deep learning models have accelerated
significantly. Lots of different architectures have emerged, and many problems have
been tackled with deep learning. One example is DeepMind’s AlphaFold models for
protein structure prediction [27, 28, 29]. These models are not just large MLPs, but
rather consist of multiple different network modules combined with other machine
learning algorithms. So even though deep learning models are often referred to as
black boxes, they must often be tailored to the specific problem that is to be solved.
In the field of natural language processing (NLP), one must encode the words, i.e.
convert them to numerical data in a specific way, such that the main model can

2.3.1
The Convolutional Neural Network
The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a neural network which is very popular
in areas such as image classification and computer vision. It is the result of decades of
research and development (see e.g. [30, 31]), but has gained tremendous popularity
in the later years [32]. The CNN utilizes convolutions in at least one of its layers.
The convolution is a linear operation like matrix multiplication. It generalizes to
multiple dimensions which makes it useful for processing data structured in grids,
e.g. images. The input to a convolution layer is convolved with a kernel. The kernel
dimensions are typically much smaller than the data grid, reducing the number
of required weights significantly. At the same time, this design of shared weights
makes the convolution layers shift-invariant.
This is desirable, because in most
image recognition applications, we do not want to classify an object based on where
it is in the picture. Figure 2.3 illustrates a two-dimensional convolutional layer with
a 3 × 3 kernel.

2.5. Automatic Differentiation
2.5
Automatic Differentiation
Differentiating functions is central in many engineering tasks, and there exists a
handful of ways to do this. The first method is to differentiate by hand. This is
very manual and mostly useful when considering very specific problems. Another
approach is symbolic differentiation which is implemented in many CASs such as
Maple [37] or the Python package SymPy [38]. When doing large numerical calcu-
lations, these methods are generally infeasible or at least highly inefficient. Instead,
finite difference methods are often used. These methods are not exact, however,
since they are based on a truncation of the function’s Taylor series.
While these methods all have their uses, they are not that useful in deep learning,
because neural networks are large function compositions with many parameters.
Differentiating them with these methods are either extremely tedious or inaccurate.
The approach used in this case is instead what we call automatic differentiation
(sometimes also called algorithmic differentiation or computational differentiation).
Automatic differentiation (AD) relies on repeated use of the chain rule and well
known derivative rules for primitive operations. As seen in section 2.2, in particu-
lar equation (2.9), deep neural networks are function compositions of many simpler
functions. The derivative can then be obtained using the chain rule for each com-
position of these simpler functions. Consequently, while AD is not specific to deep
learning, it is highly useful in this domain.
2.5.1
The Univariate Chain Rule
Though neural network components typically are multivariate and vector-valued, we
first consider the univariate case of the chain rule before generalizing the concepts.
The chain rule is a powerful mathematical identity that allows easy computation of
derivatives of composite functions. If the univariate function f is the composition of
two univariate functions g and h, such that f = g ◦h, then, according to the chain
rule,
f ′(x) = (g ◦h)(x) = g′(h(x)) · h′(x).
(2.28)
It is a simple rule, but it can be applied over and over. Imagine, for example, that
g and h themselves are composite functions. Then we can apply the same rule to
these parts of the total function. This way, we can break down a function to its
simplest parts. These are often denoted as primitives.
As a simple example, consider a scalar function of a single variable x, i.e. f : R →R,
where f = a ◦b ◦c, and a, b, c are all scalar functions from R to R. The (univariate)
chain rule states that

While we have now established the multivariate chain rule as repeated multipli-
cation of Jacobians, the associativity of matrix multiplication leaves us with an
important choice: In which order should we multiply the Jacobians? While we get
the same result in the end, the computational complexity depends on this choice.
The number of ways to multiply a number of matrices together is a combinatorial
problem, but in general we consider two main ways: Repeated multiplication from
the right and repeated multiplication from the left. The first is known as forward
accumulation, and the second is called reverse accumulation.
2.5.3
Forward and Reverse Accumulation
Let us consider the Jacobian matrix J of some composite function f : Rn →Rm
as a matrix product of L Jacobians, and denote the intermediate Jacobian matrices
Dfℓ(fℓ−1) as Jℓ:
J = JLJL−1 · · · J2J1.
(2.38)
Repeated multiplication on the right yields intermediate Jacobian products with n
columns, since J1 has n columns. Thus, if n is small, this way of calculating J
is efficient. If n = 1 (i.e. f is a vector-valued function of a single variable), then
the calculation of J is just repeated matrix-vector products. Thus, forward accu-
mulation is efficient for functions with low-dimensional input and high-dimensional
output.
When repeatedly multiplying on the left, the intermediate Jacobian products all
have m rows since JL has m rows. Calculating the Jacobian this way is therefore
efficient if m is small. If f is a scalar-valued function, such that m = 1, then all
multiplications are vector-matrix products.
In many situations, the Jacobian is calculated to be used in a matrix-vector prod-
uct. In such cases, one can directly calculate the Jacobian vector product. Then
the size of the Jacobian does not matter, because the forward/reverse accumulation
is extended by a matrix-vector product. If we want to calculate the product Jv, for
v ∈Rm, we will just multiply v by each intermediate Jacobian. This is just a case
of efficient forward accumulation. Similarly, we might want to calculate a vector-
Jacobian product v

can efficiently reverse accumulate. This trick is useful when we want to calculate a
part of the Jacobian. We can multiply it by a basis vector from the right to get a

Higher-Order Derivatives and Tracing
Mathematically it is straightforward to calculate higher-order derivatives. To get
the next derivative, we just need to apply the chain rule to the result obtained from
applying the chain rule in the first place. In the example from section 2.5.1, we
calculated the derivative (2.33) of the function (2.31) by applying the chain rule.
We could just repeat the procedure on (2.33) to get the second-order derivative, and
so on.
The standard way of implementing AD is by some sort of tracing. Each part of
the function is traced in order to calculate the derivative. This requires a call to the
function and is usually called a forward pass. We go through the function and break
it down into a sequence of primitives called the trace. From here, the derivative
function is built. Importantly, this function is also traceable. Higher-order deriva-
tives can therefore be calculated by repeatedly applying first-order AD. In chapter
3, we will see how traceable derivative functions are essential in the physics-informed
neural network.
While higher-order derivatives are easy to calculate, repeated first-order AD is not
necessarily the most efficient way. Calculating higher-order derivatives is a combina-
torial problem involving lower-order derivatives. In some cases, a lot of redundant
computations can be avoided by so-called Taylor-mode higher-order AD [39, 40].
See appendix B for more details on this.

Physics-Informed Neural Networks
The physics-informed neural network (PINN) [1, 5, 6] has gained popularity quickly
in computational science. One of the reasons is the fast evaluation of the trained
model, which in some areas is of great importance. In such situations, many clas-
sical numerical methods can be too slow. This DL-based technique carries other
challenges, however. In this chapter, we will establish the PINN by building on
top of the artificial neural network covered in the previous chapter. We will also
cover a range of novel techniques for improving various lacking aspects of the PINN
framework, mostly related to the training time and rate of convergence.
3.1
Central Principle
A classical ANN is trained for regression or classification by applying some sort of
gradient descent optimization on a loss function with respect to the network pa-
rameters. This loss function is typically constructed such that it is minimized when
the predictive accuracy of the network is as high as possible, based on some set of
test data. When solving differential equations, we do not know the values of the
solution function(s) in the domain. We only know the equations that should be
satisfied. Letting the neural network act as a surrogate for this solution function,
we can differentiate it with respect to the input using AD. The relevant derivatives
are then applied to the differential equation to see if it is satisfied. From here, we
typically get a nonzero residual, and this is what we want to minimize. In other
words, we want the ANN to satisfy the differential equation on the specified domain.
One clear difference compared to a purely data-driven approach is the ability to
sample the domain without requiring labels in the form of function values at those
points. If we are dealing with unknown source terms, though, we do not have ac-
cess to an umlimited number of residuals. A similar situtation can happen on the
boundary of the domain, where the true function could be unknown, but we have
access to a limited amount of data. But these situations are also good examples of
why PINNs are so flexible. They can work on a mix of data and physics. Thus, by
incorporating prior physical knowledge, we can reduce the amount of data needed
significantly in some cases.

3.4.1
The Influence of Sampling on Convergence
The purpose of sampling in PINNs is to guide the model when updating its param-
eters. By determining the residuals of the model at some point, the parameters can
be moved in the optimal direction in parameter space for minimizing this residual.
We do this typically by minimizing a loss function that is convex in the residual, as
discussed previously in section 2.6. The challenge is to minimize the loss in every
point in the domain.
The result of a good sampling technique must be a training set of as few points
as possible such that the important features and characteristics of the solution can
be represented by the solution values in those points. This means we should be able
to smoothly interpolate between these points in some way such that this interpola-
tion does not deviate significantly from the true function. If we cannot do this, the
model will most likely not catch the true solution behavior, because it has no way of
knowing the right values between the sampled points. Figure 3.5 illustrates this idea
on a simple sinusoid. By knowing the values of the true function in a few sampled
points, we are able to recreate the function relatively well in the low-frequency case.
In the high-frequency case, however, we cannot recognize the original function when
interpolating between the sampled points and values. Such an interpolation is not
necessarily the approximation we would see. In general, the approximation depends
just as much on the architecture of the network. The architecture determines the
approximation space in which we find the most optimal model. If we directly re-
strict this space to sinusoids, we might get a more accurate model, but this would
require prior knowledge of the solution. If we once more consider the Poisson prob-
lem (3.12)-(3.13), but this time let a1 = a2 = 10, corresponding to a high-frequency
solution, the same setup would not make the model converge to the solution. This
is probably also due to the small network size, but the sampling seen in figure 3.2
is not dense enough for the model to capture the highly oscillating behavior.
The performance of a standard universal approximator such as the MLP depends on
the information given to it in the sampled points. If the sampling is dense enough,
the model accuracy is often adequate for interpolation, so it can be used for predic-
tion in other points in the domain. However, this concept of the model only knowing
the solution behavior close to the training samples implies the incapability of ex-
trapolating the solution in any meaningful way. In all fairness though, we might not
be interested in solution values outside the domain, or the solution function could
even be undefined, so we should not expect any miracles.
To summarize, we have two major requirements for the model.

3.4.3
Leveraging Prior Knowledge of the Solution
We usually sample the domain uniformly, because we do not expect any part of it
to contain more information than other. If we, in fact, do know something about
the behavior of the solution, we can incorporate this.
For example, if we know
the location of a discontinuity, we can sample very densely in the area around it
in order to tell the model that this area demands special attention. As discussed
earlier, more sampling points are needed to resolve high frequencies, because the
gradients are larger. Moreover, neural networks suffer from the so-called spectral
bias, meaning they fit lower frequencies before the higher ones [51, 52, 53]. In [11],
the authors show that a high-frequency sinusoid requires a much larger network for a
good approximation, even if the number of sampling points per wave period remains
the same. The key point here is that we cannot just increase the number of sample
points alone and expect good convergence. Many other factors are relevant as well.
3.4.4
Adaptive Sampling
During training, we can resample the training points after a given number of epochs.
In some cases, this can possibly prevent the model from overfitting, as it acquires
new information every so often. The resampling can be with or without replacement.
Which training points to replace can be chosen e.g. based on the value of the loss
in the points. If the loss is already low in a part of the domain, then we might
want to have fewer training points there for computational efficiency and for the
total loss to put more emphasis on other parts of the domain. The same idea can
be used when sampling new points. If we want to add s new points to the data set,
we can sample 2s points, add the s points with highest loss, and discard the rest.
The higher the proportion of discarded points, the more relative emphasis on the
areas with high loss. There are many parameters to tweak here, as one can probably
sense. Another strategy is curriculum training [54] in which the problem is broken
33
==PAGE:46==

down into subtasks of increasing difficulty. As an example, solving an initial value
problem with a PINN might require the model to approximate the initial values well
before propagating information further in time. In the curriculum based approach,
we first train the model on the part of the domain close to the initial condition
and then gradually extend along the temporal dimension. A thorough treatment of
adaptive sampling strategies is given in [55].
3.5
Error Analysis
Often, the performance of a PINN is highly dependent on the problem. On some
problems it will perform great out-of-the-box. On others, it will struggle a lot. The
possible reasons are many. So far, there is no universal formula telling us how easy or
how difficult a specific problem is to solve with a PINN. The field of SciML is evolv-
ing fast, and a large effort goes into shedding light on the black box of deep learning.
An example is error estimates. Normally, it is difficult to estimate the prediction
error of a model if no reference solution is available. The loss might give an indica-
tion, but the relationship between the loss, e.g. the MSE, and the absolute error can
be complex, because the loss is defined in terms of a differential equation. Consider
the Poisson example (3.12)-(3.13). For large a1, a2, the values of the Laplacian of u
is much larger than those of u itself. Then the loss of the model ˆu could be large,
while the prediction error is small. As a result, we will possibly struggle with lower-
ing the loss to the orders of magnitude often seen with pure function approximation.
In [56], the authors establish general error bounds and stability criteria, using a
perspective resembling the one often used in classical numerical methods. A similar
thing is done in [57]. Assuming linearity, the authors set up a PDE for the error
with source term equal to the residuals of the original PDE. This results in an upper
bound on the error which involves constants depending on quadrature rules used.
They provide an insightful analysis of the source of each term in the error bound.
Nevertheless, the error bounds are difficult to apply in practice, and the involved
constants mostly depend of the solution or the model. Although the results are
difficult to use in a concrete setting, error bounds depending on quadrature rules
indicate that uniform random sampling might result in more varying results, hence
quasi-random sequences such as the Sobol sequence are preferable in most cases.
To summarize, some error bounds can be shown based on residuals and can be
used for other general results, but the bounds are mostly difficult to use in practice.
3.6
Multi-Objective Optimization
The usual way to optimize multiple losses in the PINN framework is to add together
the loss functions in order to construct a single overall loss, just as in (3.7). This
sum of losses still has the property that minimizing it is equivalent to solving the
original problem. Even so, the convergence might be much worse, if some parts of
the problem converge slower to the optimal values. We can imagine that the total
loss is a sum of several loss terms, e.g. a residual loss term for each scalar equation
on the domain, and a loss term for each boundary condition.

Non-Dimensionalization
Many machine learning applications normalize data before processing it. The idea
of non-dimensionalization is similar. One or more of the variables are scaled to a di-
mensionless quantity with a small given range, e.g. [0, 1] or [−1, 1]. The arguments
in [59] for using non-dimensionalization are the following. First, effective initial-
ization schemes are often based on the assumption that the inputs are in a decent
range, for example close to zero mean and unit variance. This is why data is usually
standardized in classical ML. Second, it can help with imbalances in variables scales.
If one variable is scaled much differently than another, they can have much different
effects when training the model.
3.7.4
Coordinate Transformations
Sometimes the solution may be expressed in a simpler way if we do a coordinate
transformation. For example, in a problem with a circular geometry, it could be
easier to express the solution in polar coordinates. In some cases, the PDE itself
can be transformed such that the PINN is implemented differently. In other cases,
the coordinate transformation will just be a preprocessing step before the data is
passed to the main model. If the PDE is not transformed as in the first case, it
is important to remember to differentiate with respect to the original variables.
In other words, the coordinate transformation is part of the forward pass in the
second case. Sometimes, the problem can even be simplified to a lower-dimensional
problem. For example, if the problem is axisymmetric, we can remove an angular
component and only consider the radial component.

3.7.6
Changing the Network Architecture
Instead of using a standard MLP, we can use other network architectures to capture
desired model behavior or mitigate specific problems. Trying out a different network
architecture is sometimes a good idea. The ResNet or the modified MLP can be
tested if one uses deeper networks and suspects poor convergence due vanishing
gradients or similar. In some cases, these residual connections can help speed up
training. Recently, the Kolmogorov-Arnold network (KAN) was introduced in [66].
This network is fundamentally different from the MLP and its extensions. The KAN
is based on the Kolomogorov-Arnold representation theorem, which states that we
can represent multivariate functions as superpositions of univariate functions. The
network represents these univariate functions with some basis, e.g. splines.

3.7.8
Early Stopping
An important part of machine learning is prediction using unknown data. We would
like our models to accurately predict outcomes given previously unseen data. Typ-
ically, we split a data set into two parts: One for training and one for evaluation.
The question is then how well the model will predict data on which it has not been
trained.
An often used strategy is to train a model until the loss of the evaluation data
starts to increase. At this point, the model is only adapting to the training data,
but it is not improving its prediction capabilities. We call this overfitting. This
seems to be a bigger problem when we are dealing with noisy data where a mini-
mized training loss would mean fitting the noisy data perfectly. In such applications,
early stopping is used to prevent the model from fitting the noise.
In the PINN framework, fitting the training points perfectly might lead to over-
fitting, but in less degree, because often there is no noise misguiding the optimizer.
Early stopping is therefore not as crucial as in other ML applications, but it can
still be used if we want to save computations that are not contributing significantly
to improved accuracy of the model.
3.7.9
Batching
The authors of [59] also recommend using mini-batches when training. They argue
that full-batch sampling requires more memory and is computationally more expen-
sive per iteration. According to [67], larger batch sizes seem to be preferable, though.
In addition, from the implementation in [68], their concept of batching seems to be
different than the classical one. In classical deep learning, we have a fixed set of
training data, and a batch can be anything between a single sample and the whole
set. When we have done SGD on each batch once, we say that the model has been
trained for one epoch. In this case, however, it appears that their definition of a
batch is just an entirely new set of samples each iteration and not a random subset
of a fixed total data set. This is possible when we are able to sample the domain
without any theoretical limit, as discussed in section 3.4. It is therefore equivalent
to a full-batch sampling strategy using fewer sample points combined with a full
resampling each iteration.
When training PINNs, we usually have multiple sets of data corresponding to initial
data and/or boundary data and residual points in the domain, and these sets are
not necessarily equal in size. With classical batch training, we could therefore run
out of batches in one data set before the other. There are lots of ways to tackle
41
==PAGE:54==

this. One way is to divide each data set into an equal number of batches resulting
in different batch sizes between data sets. Another strategy is to only batch one
data set and use full-batch training for the others. The method used in [59] appears
to be the strategy mentioned above for residual points and full-batch training for
initial/boundary points, according to the implementations in [68].
3.7.10
General Hyperparameter Recommendations
Every problem has different optimal hyperparameters, but most of the time, we do
not know these from the start. Therefore, we must use general estimates to begin
with and then tweak them afterwards. For optimizing, the authors of [59] suggest us-
ing the ADAM optimizer and an initial learning rate of 10−3 with exponential decay.
The optimal neural network hyperparameters typically varies much more, and it
takes some experience to choose a good configuration for a given problem right
away. High-frequency functions require more degrees of freedom to represent, but
this characteristic is not necessarily known in advance. As a rule of thumb, a prob-
lem that is challenging to solve using classical methods would require a larger and
more flexible network for adequate approximations.

Data Generation
The original DeepONet is purely data-driven, meaning it incorporates no physics.
Therefore, we have to generate data in order to train it. This can be done in many
ways. The authors of [16] suggests Gaussian random fields and Chebyshev polyno-
mials for generating samples of u. When the set of functions is generated, we need
to calculate numerical solutions in order to obtain labels for the data. The authors
use basic methods such as Runge-Kutta schemes and FDMs. Since each data point
is a combination of a sample of u and a sample of y, the total dataset can be large
with few random functions and few coordinates. This also means that it takes much
longer to train as well though.
The implementation of a data-driven DeepONet is straightforward if one has im-
plemented neural networks for function approximation before. The data generation
might be the most complicated part of the implementation, especially if the domain
geometry is very complex. Given a data set based on e.g. Gaussian random fields
or random sampling of orthogonal polynomials up to some order, the model can
be trained as a standard MLP. For each triplet (u, y, G(u)(y)), we train the model
using classical supervised learning.
3.8.2
Physics-Informed Deep Operator Networks
Instead of relying entirely on data, we can incorporate physics in the loss function
of the DeepONet just like we did with PINNs. The physics-informed DeepONet
(PI-DeepONet) [18, 70] is essentially a parameterized PINN. In addition to the
coordinates, we give parameters in the form of function values at the sensors. This
removes one of the biggest weaknesses of the PINN, namely the fact that it can only
approximate a single function. If trained properly, the DeepONet can approximate
solutions to large families of PDEs, and the PI-DeepONet can do this without ever
using true solutions for the training. Training a PI-DeepONet is often significantly
more time-consuming, though. This makes sense, because in addition to training the
model on large amounts of data as the data-driven DeepONet, we must differentiate
it with respect to the coordinates y. This is analogous to the way training of a
PINN is slower than that of a standard ANN. The PI-DeepONet can be trained by
incorporating known physics into the loss function. When applying AD with respect
to the input, we are most often referring to y, because these are the coordinates of
the training points.

3.8.3
Operator Networks with Multiple Inputs
The DeepONet can be extended with more branches to model operators defined
on products of Banach spaces. This is called a multiple-input operator network
(MIONet) [19]. The universal approximation theorem can also be extended to in-

to approximate even larger families of PDEs. We can, for example, have one branch
network encode the initial condition of a problem, and use another branch network
for the boundary conditions, and we can also have a third one for a source term.
The possibilities seem almost endless. Even the geometry can be an input [71].

4.4
The Process of Improving the Model
As seen in the previous section, the first test has not resulted in a highly accurate
model. This is generally to be expected, hence we need to test additional techniques
in a fairly systematic way in order to find what works best. There is most often
mathematical reasoning behind each technique, but there is still no guarantee that
each one will improve the accuracy. To thoroughly test them, we ought to run the
model multiple times with different random seeds and collect statistics, but this is
infeasible due to the limited amount of time for this project. Meanwhile, the search
space is huge, growing exponentially with the number of techniques to test. As a
consequence, we choose to run each model once with the same random seed. Fur-
thermore, we must lower the search space using a heuristic method. We decide to
use a greedy approach, where we test each technique one at a time. The configura-
tion that results in the best-performing model is then kept fixed when testing the
next technique. With this procedure we might miss the optimal configuration, but
this argument is inapplicable anyway when we are not doing a statistical analysis
of the performance. Besides, most of the techniques are quite different from each
other, leading us to believe that their effects will not interfere much.
We start with a basic analysis of gradient accuracy.
With this, we aim to bet-
ter understand the effect of function accuracy on derivative accuracy and vice versa.
Then, we test a simpler two-dimensional biharmonic problem to observe if the bi-
harmonic equation is the main source of slow convergence. From here we consider
the main problem again. We begin with basic tweaking of hyperparameters such
as network depth and width, sample size, activation functions and loss balancing.
Using the best configuration found, we test the modified MLP to see if this archi-
tecture works better for the problem. Then we rewrite the biharmonic equation as
two Laplace equations. This has also been done in other works such as [20, 21]. We
hope to speed up training with this approach, since it will only require second-order
AD.

We also test if the network learns the solution more easily in polar coordinates.
Furthermore, we test the techniques summarized in [59]: Random Fourier features,

the effect of floating point precision, available data in different amounts, and the

4.5
Testing Gradient Accuracy in One Dimension
In this section, we aim to show how constraints in the PINN formulation affects the
accuracy of the surrogate model as well as its derivatives. The goal of this is a clearer
perception of the accuracy we can expect from the second-order derivatives of the
surrogates (i.e. the internal stresses) when constraining the fourth-order derivatives
on the domain (i.e. the biharmonic equation) and the second-order derivatives on
the boundary (i.e. the external stresses).
For some known function, we train a model to approximate the function and eval-
uate the accuracy of the derivatives. We then train a PINN based on a first-order
equation with boundary conditions, a second-order equation with boundary con-
ditions, and so on up to fourth order. Lastly we train a PINN on a fourth-order
equation with second-order boundary conditions and seek to accurately approximate
the second derivative without constraining the first derivative and function values.
This can be seen as a one-dimensional equivalent to the main problem where we aim
to approximate the stresses without constraining the potential. For each of these
PINNs, we examine the accuracy of the model.
We do these tests on two functions, sin(x) and exp(sin(x)). The function sin(x)
and all its derivatives are simple trigonometric function and should not be difficult
for a neural network to approximate. The function exp(sin(x)) has more compli-
cated derivatives, and their L∞norm increases with the order. In general, we would
expect a network to approximate the first of the two functions more easily.
There are many hyperparameters that can be tuned to increase model performance,
such as the sample density, network architecture, network width and depth as well
as the learning rate and loss weights. To keep the experiments brief, though, the
network architecture used is a standard MLP with four hidden layers of 32 neurons
each, as this should be sufficient to express the solutions, from our experience. We
use a learning rate of 10−3 with an exponential decay of 0.95 every 2000 epochs. No
weighting scheme is used.

4.6. Examining the Biharmonic Equation
There are several trends to notice here. The first is that the composite function with
more complicated derivatives is harder to approximate, i.e. slower convergence. Sec-
ond, we notice that the convergence of the model is generally improved by sampling
more densely. If the sampling is already appropriately dense enough to resolve the
problem, increasing the density will not necessarily increase convergence, but also
will not worsen it considerably. Another clear trend is that the PINNs converge
slower the higher order the differential equation is. For example, a PINN solving
a fourth order equation will be harder to approximate compared to a second order
PINN. Lastly, for the case of the fourth order differential equation where we seek
to approximate the Hessian with only second order boundary conditions, we see a
decrease in convergence in most cases, compared to a regular PINN of fourth order
with constraints on the function values and first order derivatives.

This is a method of manufactured solution of a biharmonic equation, without the
extra complexity of a hole in the middle, with ϕ(x, y) = sin(x) sin(y) as the true
solution. By trying to solve this problem, we hope to to gain some insight into the
convergence of a biharmonic equation in two dimensions on a simpler and presum-
ably more well-behaved problem to which we have an analytical solution. A PINN,
consisting of a standard MLP with two hidden layers with 128 neurons in each layer,
is deployed to solve this problem. It is trained for 50,000 epochs using a learning
rate of 10−3 with exponential decay of 0.95 every 2000 epochs. No loss-weighting
scheme is used. 8000 Sobol-generated collocation points and 2000 uniformly sam-
pled boundary points (500 on each side) are used to train the model with full-batch
gradient descent. The result of this can be seen in figure 4.12. From this we see
that the biharmonic equation itself is not problematic to solve, though fourth-order
AD is time-consuming. It seems that we cannot ascribe the convergence difficulties
solely to the biharmonic equation or differentiating the model output twice to ob-
tain the stresses. Together with the results of the previous experiment, we conclude
that the problem is challenging due to the decreased accuracy, but not impossible.
Starting from the next section, we search for a better-performing model by tweaking
hyperparameters and testing techniques.

4.7.2
Sample Size
Now that we have established a fitting network size to express the solution, we can
move on to the next tuning parameter, being the sampling. In this case it is very
important for information to be propagated into the domain from the boundary,
as we have a homogeneous right-hand side to the PDE. Therefore it is important
that the boundary is sampled densely enough to resolve all the boundary conditions
properly. This is even more important since they are second-order conditions and
might therefore need denser sampling than e.g. Dirichlet conditions.
We use Sobol sequences for sampling 8192 points in the rectangular domain in-
cluding the hole in the middle. Then we remove sample points in the hole. This
typically leaves us with a little less than 8000 points. We can then repeatedly sample
more points and remove the points in the hole until there is at least 8000 points in
the domain, and then we can remove the surplus, so we have exactly 8000 points.
As a second strategy we can also stop the process after removing the points from
the hole and simply accept that there will not be exactly 8000 points. We get bet-
ter results with the first strategy, so we will use this in the following tests. Using
batching and resampling does not yield better results, so we will not use these. We
might expect the tensions to be greater near the circle, and it might therefore be
an idea to sample additional points close to the circle in order to better resolve the
potentially high gradients. However this yields worse results, hence we will move
forward without this strategy.
To find the appropriate amount of boundary points we fix the amount of collocation
points at 8000 and then tweak the amount of boundary points that the model is
trained on. The results can be seen in table 4.4. The best configuration appears to
be 1000 points on the circular boundary and 2000 on the rectangular one, i.e. 500
on each side. From now on, we will use this configuration together with the 8000
collocation points, unless otherwise stated. The von Mises stress corresponding to
this configuration can be seen in figure 4.14.

4.11
Testing Additional Techniques
In this section, we test the effects of some of the techniques from [59] as well as the
effect of using double-precision floating point numbers. The results are all gathered
in table 4.12.
4.11.1
Random Fourier Features
The idea of random Fourier features is to more easily catch the high-frequency be-
havior of the solution. The solution is thought to be fairly smooth, so we do not
expect any miracles from this technique, but the results from [59] are positive.
Adding random Fourier features to the model does not result in better accuracy.
It instead leads to large oscillations, probably due to high frequencies trying to
compensate for each other.
4.11.2
Random Weight Factorization
We test random weight factorization based on the suspicion that the model easily
gets stuck in local minima of the loss landscape. This decreases the error slightly, but
upon further testing by applying it along with other techniques and with different
network sizes, this is not generally the case, so we do not use it going forward.
4.11.3
Non-Dimensionalization
Non-dimensionalization is tested based on the hypothesis that standardized inputs
will be easier to learn from, with the usual parameter initializations.
Since our
domain is centered around the origin, we normalize it to inputs in [−1, 1] × [−1, 1]
by dividing by 10. The model accuracy does not improve from this modification.
4.11.4
Double Precision
Many machine learning applications use low-precision floating point numbers. The
low precision is often acceptable because the models are not meant to approximate
anything to a high precision in the first place. However, when we start using these
methods for scientific problems requiring high precision, and where the available
data might be noiseless, the precision available with single precision numbers might
not be adequate.
Another reason for testing higher precision is due to possible
error buildup from backpropagation through the network each time the chain rule
is applied. This test shows that this is not the case, however, or that it is at least
not the main cause for the size of the errors.
4.11.5

A Deep Operator Network
The last model we consider is a DeepONet, which we test in two ways. The first is
not exactly the original DeepONet, since we do not evaluate a function in a fixed
amount of sensors. We input the external tension T applied to the plate. A single
input like this should be sufficient if we are only interested in cases where the tension
does not vary along the boundary. Additionally, we input the radius in the branch
to test the model’s ability to generalize the solution to plates with circular holes of
other sizes.
The second approach is more along the lines of an original DeepONet, since we
evaluate a function on a fixed amount of fixed sensors. We pass the tension of the
analytical solution at the boundary as input in the branch network. This means that
we place fixed sensors on the boundary, and then for each sensor evaluate either the
pair (σxx, σxy) or (σyy, σxy) depending on which boundary the sensor is located at.
The stress pairs are then passed as input to the branch network.
The experiments conducted on the PINNs throughout the report have been ex-
tensive and there has not been enough time to conduct a similar extensive analysis
and tuning of the DeepONet. We include the limited amount of results here to
show their potential for the plane stress problem. We examine the performance
of physics-informed DeepONets, data-driven DeepONets and a DeepONet being
trained on both physics and data.

Summary
In this chapter, we have shown the results of adding extra techniques to the PINN
framework for solving the plane stress problem given by (4.41a)-(4.41f). Some make
a positive difference, some make a negative difference, and some are insignificant.
The difference between two- and four-layer networks is not that big. There is no
clear pattern for the width of the network either. A single configuration of the ones
tested stands out a bit, so this is chosen as a new baseline model. When tuning the
number of sample points, the most clear improvement is achieved by increasing the
points on the rectangular boundary by 1000 (250 on each side) to a total of 2000
(500 on each side). Moving on to the activation tweaking, the clear winner is the
SiLU activation. The sigmoid is not an improvement to the model. This is antici-
pated since it is closely related to the hyperbolic tangent, but with lower values and
gradients. The periodic activations are the worst-performing.
When testing loss balancing methods, we find that the unweighted one actually
performs the best. Apparently, this problem does not suffer significantly from loss
term imbalances. The parameter initialization does not make a big difference. Even
though the difference might be statistically insignificant, the He-normal initialization
is a bit better than Glorot-normal initialization, so we use this one going forward.
We also test another network architecture, namely the modified MLP. It yields a bit
better results, but the training takes much longer.
Next, we rewrite the biharmonic equation as a system of two Laplace equations. The

5
Discussion
As we have seen in the previous chapter, the choice of model is paramount when
tackling challenging problems with data-driven and physics-informed models such
as PINNs and DeepONets. When dealing with toy problems such as the Poisson ex-
ample, we can get an accurate solution in 15 seconds using the basic PINN without
applying any exotic techniques. As the problem gets more complicated, the need
for these techniques increases rapidly, and the training times become longer.
In the following sections, we discuss the results in more detail, starting with problem-
specific ones. Then we go through the effect of each technique applied. Lastly, we
suggest several ideas to be explored in the future.
5.1
Problem Formulation
The performance of the PINN depends heavily on the specific problem. For example,
if a solution contains many high frequencies, spectral bias will cause slow conver-
gence of these. Some problems lead to ill-conditioned Hessians, as examined in [34].
Therefore, it often takes a more thorough investigation of a specific problem before
we can determine if it is easy or difficult to solve. For this reason, we cannot expect
to see the same performance in the biharmonic problem as other authors have seen
in similar setups. For example, most papers on the biharmonic equation considered
problems with Dirichlet conditions on the boundary. In [20], they considered the
Lamé problem where they were able to reduce the dimensionality of the problem due
to axisymmetry. Seemingly small modifications of the problem can make a notable
difference.
The formulation of the problem from chapter 4 is a bit odd compared to the ones
generally used in articles on PINNs. We only have second-order boundary condi-
tions, so the surrogate itself is not uniquely determined. We are not sure if this
makes the problem more difficult. Based on our experiments, it is not a direct cause
of slow convergence, however it makes many methods more complicated, e.g. rewrit-
ing the problem to a system of equations or finding an appropriate solution ansatz.
At the same time, the specific boundary conditions are tough to work with. Other
papers such as [21] use Dirichlet conditions and second-order conditions expressed
by the Laplace operator. This gives rise to a much more straightforward reformu-
lation of the problem where the authors can impose Dirichlet conditions on both of
the functions ϕ and ψ of the system (4.50a)-(4.50b). Even though two formulations
of a boundary value problem are mathematically equivalent, they might demand

totally different efforts to solve with PINNs or similar methods.
In section 4.6, we show that we can approximate the biharmonic operator better
than our initial experiment indicates. Furthermore, we have examined the gradient
accuracy in section before it, so instead of concluding that the problem is too diffi-
cult to solve with a PINN, we accept that the main problem requires more than the
basic formulation from [1] to achieve high accuracy. This is then confirmed in the
rest of the chapter.
5.2
Effects of Various Techniques
Throughout chapter 4, we have tested many techniques in an effort to improve the
performance of the PINN. We have tweaked the usual hyperparameters such as net-
work width and depth, activations, parameter initialization, and so on. As with
most problems, these hyperparameters do make a difference. Most notably, we see
that more sample points on the rectangular boundary improves the performance
significantly, but only to a certain point. We also observe a clear improvement from
using the SiLU as activation instead of the hyperbolic tangent. Using this activation
might also be the reason for the small improvement He initialization provides, since
SiLU and ReLU are similar.
Interestingly, adaptive loss balancing does not make a big difference. No matter
which scheme we use, the loss for the biharmonic equation is the highest followed
by the loss on the circular boundary. Based on the loss histories in figure 4.16, we
presume that the learning rate is appropriate. With so many loss terms, we had
expected this to make a bigger difference, but that is apparently not the case. Based
on the findings in [59], we had expected the modified MLP to perform better, but
the indifference between it and the standard MLP might be due to the low num-
ber of hidden layers. The other techniques also do not improve the model, even
though the authors of [59] present them as almost necessary to achieve good results.
Random weight factorization does perform acceptably, but we find that random
Fourier features it not always advantageous if the solution does not contain many
high frequencies. It might also be unstable due to the high order of the problem.
Non-dimensionalization might be more important if the x- and y-dimensions were
scaled differently.
Rewriting the biharmonic equation to a system of coupled Laplace equations makes
a big difference in terms of training time. Even though we have not done any thor-
ough analysis of training times, we have noted that some methods definitely are
faster than others. For example, more complex network architectures generally ex-
tend the training time while lower orders of AD reduces it. The improvement due to
the rewriting is a clear sign that it can be tremendously beneficial to apply purely
mathematical techniques rather than just relying on modifications of the surrogate
architecture. This may be a general trend for higher-order differential equations.
We have seen that both training time and accuracy can be challenges with PINNs
for higher-order PDEs, so different techniques should be in focus rather than the
ones generally recommended.

5.3. Advantages and Disadvantages
One can then argue that we are applying the methods in the domain of linear
elasticity, so that a different formulation of these equations should be used. This is
a valid argument, and it would be interesting to apply the PINN framework to e.g.
the displacement formulation. However, we have chosen to keep the focus on this
formulation in which the biharmonic operator appears. We have used the equations
of linear elasticity more as an interesting application where higher-order derivatives
appear and additionally where the boundary conditions are different from the ones
we usually see in other articles on PINNs.
We are impressed by the improvement of the model due to the solution ansatz.
It is not obvious that it can improve the performance since we still need to use all
the loss terms. Adding data to the model is of course expected to at least not worsen
the performance. But it is interesting to see just how little data one actually needs
to add to a physics-informed model in order to see significant improvements.
5.3
Advantages and Disadvantages
At this point, the reader has likely sensed that there are both advantages and disad-
vantages of models such as PINNs and DeepONets. They are not meant to replace
other numerical methods everywhere. It is about finding the areas where they are
more useful. We have seen how the biharmonic problem requires more than just
standard formulation from [1].
The need for these techniques arises due to the nature of deep learning and the
accuracy requirements in scientific simulations. In many applications, deep learn-
ing is used as an alternative to classical statistical methods which are not intended
to show more than general tendencies in data. An example is smart advertising
or movie recommendations based on previous user behavior. Classical numerical
methods such as FEM has been used for simulation of scientific phenomena with
remarkable success due to the guarantees of accuracy and a clearer connection be-
tween theory and practice. PINNs and classical numerical methods share the same
abstract framework consisting of a way to formulate the problem and a way to rep-
resent the solution. Element-based methods and spectral methods often represent
solutions with orthogonal basis functions. For PINNs, the solution is represented
using a neural network which essentially is a trained basis with the last affine trans-
formation constituting the basis coefficients. While this basis is more flexible, we
lose some of the nice properties at the same time, e.g. orthogonality. This adds
to the difficulty of concluding anything using established mathematical results. As
mentioned in section 3.5, it is quite challenging to apply error bounds in practice
to deep learning models. The large gap between the theoretical capabilities of the
models, shown by the universal approximation theorems, and an actual model with
that performance, makes it tough to compete with other numerical methods. In gen-
eral, it is difficult to guarantee anything when the method is based on non-convex
optimization. It is therefore important to clarify why we want to find alternatives
to classical numerical methods, and determine where they are most useful. Until
we find better methods for controlling the error, these models will mostly be used
where high accuracy is not a strict requirement.
97

Chapter 5. Discussion
A major reason for choosing surrogate models is their fast evaluation time. Simula-
tion times can be extensive with classical methods, but a forward pass in a neural
network is fast. If the training is carried out in advance, PINNs and DeepONets
can evaluate a solution very fast. The PINN can only approximate a single solution,
though, making it less interesting for this purpose. DeepONets can be made to
approximate solutions to all relevant types of PDEs. Another reason for choosing
PINNs or DeepONets is their flexibility in regards to geometry. It is often much
easier to set up PINNs for problems on oddly-shaped domains, because the method
is mesh-free and relies on sampling. Grid-based methods such as FDMs are not
easily applied in such cases. Lastly, the ability to easily combine data and physics
is a major advantage of PINNs and DeepONets.
5.4
Future Work
Even though we have tried a plethora of different techniques and combinations of
them, there are still many others to try. With so many modifications proposed to
the original PINN, there seems to be an endless amount of things to try out. In this
section, we suggest some of the ideas, we think could have been interesting to try,
if it was not for the limited duration of this project.
Since the behaviors of the solution close to and far from the hole differ quite a
lot, a domain decomposition approach, e.g. the XPINN, could be interesting to ex-
amine. It could potentially circumvent problems arising due to spectral bias, and it
might also make three-dimensional problems and domains with more oddly-shaped
holes more accessible.
Seeing how a small amount of data has a positive effect on the convergence, we have
thought about a hybrid approach utilizing classical methods such as FEMs for low-
fidelity simulations guiding the physics-informed model to a better solution. This is
closely related to MPINNs [75].
In the long term, we would like a more thorough investigation of DeepONets than
time has permitted in this project. With DeepONets, particularly MIONets, we
might be able to approximate larger families of solutions to PDEs, where we can
specify geometry, tension, and other relevant parameters in more detail.
It would also be interesting to consider other formulations of linear elasticity, e.g.
the displacement formulation. This project has focused on the biharmonic equation
and higher-order PDEs, but as we have seen, the training time grows fast with higher
orders of AD. So if the goal is better models for linear elasticity specifically, we might
want to look into alternative formulations. If we are interested in the biharmonic
equation in general, then Taylor-mode AD, described in appendix B, is interesting
to investigate further. The JAX implementation of this is still in its experimental
phase, but hopefully in the near future, it will be part of the stable releases. There
are so many extensions to the PINN that it can be tough to choose which one to
apply to a problem. However, given more time, the variational approach in [21], and
the methods of [9, 10] in general, would be very interesting to investigate further
and perhaps combine with the properties of DeepONets.

Conclusion
In this project we have presented the mathematical theory behind neural networks
and physics-informed machine learning, including PINNs and DeepONets. There
are many ways to improve the performance of these models. We have presented sev-
eral recently developed techniques for improving convergence, and we have provided
examples of improvements for a simple Poisson problem.
We have tested the methods on a linear elastic plane stress problem involving the
biharmonic equation and second-order boundary conditions. The basic PINN for-
mulation does not result in an accurate model. By applying each of the techniques
one at a time in a greedy manner, we have found a good combination for a satis-
factory solution to the problem with an L2 relative error of 0.09% for the von Mises
stress. Rewriting the biharmonic equation to a system of coupled Laplace equations
has been particularly helpful for speeding up the training. The solution ansatz has
enabled much better convergence.
We find that many techniques presented as crucial for optimal convergence actu-
ally do not improve the performance of the PINN for this specific problem. It might
be due to the higher order of the problem, the fact that we are actually interested in
the Hessian of the surrogate, or the distribution of frequencies in the solution. We
have also shown that it is possible to train DeepONets to approximate this type of
plane stress problem, but this still needs a more thorough investigation in a future
project. Likewise, the vast number of extensions to the PINN will be interesting to
test.
Overall, the project demonstrates how SciML is contributing to computational sci-
ence with lots of novel methods and creative ways to utilize available data and
incorporate known physics. Though it is not a mature field, it has tremendous po-
tential in numerous areas of science, and we are excited to be part of the ongoing
development.

A
Programming in JAX
JAX [23] is a framework developed by Google. One could compare it to frameworks
such as PyTorch [81] and TensorFlow [80], but they do not quite implement all
the same functionalities, and the implementations differ significantly in some parts.
While all three frameworks utilize AD and XLA, the style of programming is dif-
ferent. First of all, JAX is not only a deep learning library, though it is one of its
applications. Rather, it is a more general library implementing the tools used in
deep learning and high-performance computing. Then there are multiple packages
built on top of it. These include Flax [24] which implements modules (almost) as we
know them from PyTorch, Optax [25] which implements optimizer for training the
models, and Orbax [84] which takes care of saving and loading model checkpoints.
Flax is not the only neural network package built on top of JAX. Other libraries
include Haiku [83] and Equinox [82]. We use Flax in this project.
In this chapter, we highlight the most important features in JAX and how they
compare to functionalities in other deep learning frameworks. For a more thorough
walkthrough of the features, we refer to JAX’s own documentation containing both
detailed and intuitive explanations.

for details).
A.2
NumPy and SciPy in JAX
NumPy [85] and SciPy [86] are some of the most used Python packages for numerical
computing, but they are written for computations on the CPU. For convenience,
JAX has adopted a similar interface. Instead of importing numpy, we can import
jax.numpy, and similarly for SciPy. Most operations in the numpy library have a
JAX-equivalent. It is therefore easy to get started with JAX, if you already are
familiar with numerical computations using numpy. While jax.numpy provides a
familiar interface, it is essentially a high-level wrapper for the jax.lax API. In
turn, jax.lax operations can be seen as wrappers for XLA operations. The high-
level wrappers are very convenient, since jax.numpy arrays can be used in most
functions that use numpy arrays, e.g. functions for plotting.
1 >>> import jax.numpy as jnp
2 >>>

5 >>> print(jnp.sin(x)**2 + jnp.cos (2) **2)

0.5 1. ]
[1. 1. 1.]

A.4
Stateful and Stateless Computations
Many computations in PyTorch and TensorFlow are stateful, meaning they modify
the (global) state of the program. A stateful program has side effects, meaning it
either affects the global state in other ways than through its return value(s), or it is
itself affected by the global state in other ways than through its input argument(s).
Consider the generation of random numbers. PyTorch and TensorFlow can both
operate with a global seed, which means that calling a random number generator
twice in a row will produce two different results. This is an example of a stateful
computation, i.e. it changes the state of the program. In other words, the function
generating random numbers has side effects.
JAX has deemed such side effects
undesirable for multiple related reasons.
We would like to write parallelizable and vectorizable code with reproducible results.
Setting a global seed and then generating a sequence of random numbers might give
the same results if run twice, but this is only really the case in serial programs.
Let us illustrate this with a simple program utilizing the NumPy package, which
also supports setting a global seed. The following simple program defines a function
for generating a list of random numbers, then it calls the function to generate four
random numbers:

One of the main features of JAX is its JIT compiler. It makes it straightforward
to accelerate computations by transforming them to efficiently executed XLA code.

XLA code instead of recompiling or tracing the function again. This means that
side effects are not taken into account during subsequent calls to a JIT-compiled
function.
If a JIT-compiled function uses variables defined outside of the scope
of the function and they are not passed as arguments, the cached XLA code will
use the values the variables had when the function was compiled, and subsequent
changes to the variables will not affect the compiled code. For this reason, it is
advised to use JIT-compilation on pure functions only. In the case where one of the
inputs changes the trace depending on the value or the shape, it can be marked as
static. This ensures that JAX can JIT-compile the function as usual, but retrace
the static argument every time it changes. This way, it only recompiles or retraces
the operations affected by the change in the static argument, instead of recompiling
the entire function.

Implementation of Models
Here, we include some implementations of the models discussed throughout the re-
port. To support the numerous amount of different tests presented, we have created
a JAX-based framework for physics-informed machine learning. It takes care of most
of the setup, so that tweaking models becomes easy.
The framework has a standard hierarchical structure, meaning we have as base
class Model which is the skeleton containing functionalities used by all the mod-

concrete model classes from here. For example the PINN class implements func-

DeepONets. For the specific plane stress problem considered in chapter 4, we have
made a PlateWithHolePINN class which inherits from the PINN class. Since this
problem is the focus of the project, we have implemented many functionalities spe-
cific to the problem in this class. From here, we can set up even more specific classes
for testing the performance of various techniques.
