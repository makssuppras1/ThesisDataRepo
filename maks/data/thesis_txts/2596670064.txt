Abstract
Most of Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) patients (60-70%) exhibit anti-citrullinated protein
autoantibodies (ACPA) which also serve as biomarkers of important diagnostic and prognos-
tic value, due to their high disease-speciﬁcity. The production of ACPA depends on T cells
and it is associated with the shared epitope (SE)-containing HLA-DRB1 alleles. Vimentin
is a satisfactory candidate protein for T cell recognition, since >90% of ACPA positive RA
patients that are responsive to citrullinated vimentin carry SE-containing HLA-DRB1 alle-
les.
This project focused on the synthesis and analysis of peptides derived from citrullinated
vimentin, aiming to give a contribution for a better understanding of RA.
Three diﬀerent forms of peptides derived from citrullinated vimentin were synthesized by
solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS), biotinylated for biochemical assay purpose, character-
ized and puriﬁed by MALDI TOF MS and Reversed-Phase HPLC. Subsequently, the received
products were applied in vitro for testing RA sera samples, performing ELISA screening and
Aﬃnity Chromatography.
In total, ﬁve peptides derived from citrullinated vimentin were successfully synthesized and
puriﬁed, achieving values of purity suitable to in vitro applications.
Afterwards, ELISA assay was performed using two of the synthesized citrullinated vimentin
peptides, in linear form, and testing ACPA positive RA samples (n=12) and ACPA negative
RA sera and synovial ﬂuid samples (n=25). However, just one of the two peptides showed
activity in ACPA positive RA, which was also signiﬁcant for ACPA negative RA sera sam-
ples: this subset does not present ACPA but biotinylation, high coeﬃcient of variation, and
limited cohort may have altered the results.
Lately, antibodies in ACPA positive RA sera samples (n=7) were aﬃnity puriﬁed, employ-
ing linear and cyclic peptides derived from citrullinated vimentin as antigens. Diﬀerences
were observed between the two peptides, but further conﬁrmation and extended studies are
required for better deﬁning the responsiveness of RA patients to these peptides.

Introduction
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a common autoimmune disease, primarily aﬀecting joints,
and characterized by symptoms of inﬂammation and pain. RA can severely aﬀect patients’
quality of life: disability, systemic complications, and early death are associated with this
chronic disease [1]. Even if RA is the most common inﬂammatory arthropathy [2], several
unfulﬁlled needs persist.
Over the years, some progresses have been achieved: actually, the main targets for the au-
toimmune attack in RA have been identiﬁed. Those targets are citrullinated proteins, and
their characterization helped to understand that the disease is the result of speciﬁc autoanti-
bodies, of which response is given by an interplay of genetic and environmental risk factors:
these autoantibodies are called Anti-citrullinated proteins antibodies (ACPA).
Likewise, it is important to underline that RA is a heterogeneous disease spanning several
subsets. The most crucial subdivision is deﬁned by the presence or absence of these anti-
bodies: respectively, they are called ACPA-positive and ACPA-negative RA [3].
In particular, in this project, citrullinated vimentin has been chosen to be synthesized
and then tested aiming to give a contribution for a better understanding of RA.
It was previously shown as the CD4+ T-cells response is stimulated by immunization of HLA-
DRB1*0401 transgenic mice with the citrullinated form of vimentin. Based on this evidence,
Wegner et al.
[4] proposed a vimentin peptide sequence, derived from human vimentin
with leucine-69 replaced by alanine, having a higher aﬃnity of binding to HLA-DRB1*0401
than in its native form. The idea of substituting a leucine for alanine was applied to lock
the contiguous arginine/citrulline in a more stable position in HLA-DRB1*0401 groove.
The vimentin peptide sequence is 65SAVRAcitSSVPGVR77 in which the citrullination is at
position 70. In this study, the same sequence was reproduced, together with two cyclic forms:
the ﬁrst cyclic form was obtained by a disulﬁde bridge, the second one was a backbone cyclic
peptide. Previous studies mainly focused on the analysis of linear citrullinated vimentin. As
a consequence, the choice of synthesizing also two cyclic forms of citrullinated vimentin was
suggested by the scarcity in the literature of tests based on them and also for the well-known
properties of cyclic forms: higher biological activity compared to their linear counterparts
thanks to their conformational rigidity [5].
The sequences of all the three peptides are
illustrated in Table 1. The three sequences were synthesized by Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis
(SPPS). Modiﬁcation of the synthesized peptides was also performed: coupling Biotin to the
sequences. This process is called Biotinylation, and it’s commonly practised for isolating and
purifying proteins. Indeed, biochemistry assays were also performed: ELISA and Aﬃnity
Chromatography. The synthesized products were used as antigens and they were tested with
human sera samples, to understand possible interactions with antibodies produced by RA
patients.

To sum up, this project aims to evaluate the potential of the synthesized products as
future biomarkers, to be used for assessing the biological response to them in human sam-

between the linear and cyclic structure could help to deﬁne the antigen that presents the
most eﬃcient interaction with antibodies, in ACPA positive RA samples. The hypotheses
are that, since the synthesized peptides are citrullinated, they should promote the immune
response in ACPA positive RA, but not in ACPA negative RA, since it is well known that
this subset lacks of antibodies against citrullinated proteins. Likewise, the higher conforma-
tional rigidity characteristic of a cyclic structure than its linear counterpart should play a
role into promotion of a strongest binding aﬃnity between the cyclic citrullinated vimentin
and the antibodies.

Scientiﬁc background
The immune system is essential for human survival [6]. Thanks to it, the human body
is protected from pathogens’ attack through the action of several biological structures and
processes within the organism. Immune system becomes stronger when exposed to invading
microorganisms. This process is called "immune memory" and it refers to the ability of
the immune system to recognize and remember pathogens previously detected, producing
a faster and more robust response than the ﬁrst encounter [7]. Based on the latter, from
the 20th century, vaccination gives a huge contribution on prevention of diﬀerent diseases
[8], inducing antigen-speciﬁc memory T cells that endures long after the antigen has been
eliminated and allowing protection against consecutive infection [9].
2.1
Innate and Adaptive Immunity
In general, the coordinated response of the cells and molecules of the immune system is
called the immune response. There are two diﬀerent mechanisms that cooperate to protect
the body: the innate and adaptive immunity. The ﬁrst line of defense is the innate immunity
which acts immediately, within minutes to hours, as a barrier against microbes. Instead, the
adaptive immunity functions slower but it is more eﬃcient than the innate response.
The adaptive immune response consists of two classes of lymphocytes, B lymphocytes (hu-
moral immunity) and T lymphocytes (cell-mediated immunity), including antibodies. Sub-
stances that promote the activation of the adaptive response are called antigens. The anti-
gens can be classiﬁed in two types: antigens originating in the body are known as self-
antigens, while those that do not originate in the body are called non-self antigens. Lym-
phocytes recognize non-self antigens and produce antibodies that bind speciﬁcally to each
antigen [10].
2.1.1
Immunoglobulins
Immunoglobulins, or antibodies, are able to speciﬁcally recognize and bind to antigens.
These molecules have a characteristic "Y" shape, formed by two identical light chains and
two identical heavy chains. Both light and heavy chains have constant and variable regions.
The constant regions have sequences of amino acids similar to antibodies of a speciﬁc class of
immunoglobulin. Conversely, the variable region contains the antigen binding sites (Figure
1). There are ﬁve classes of immunoglobulins: IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD, and IgE. Immunoglob-
ulin G is the most abundant of the immunoglobulins, and it can be found in body ﬂuids.
Immunoglobulin M is the ﬁrst circulating immunoglobulin to respond to an antigen [10].

Unfortunately, immune system may cease to function properly. A breakdown in the
self-tolerance process, in other words the ability to diﬀerentiate between self- and non self-
antigens, induces to the onset of a group of disorders called autoimmune diseases [10].
2.2
Autoimmune Diseases
Autoimmune diseases (ADs) are conditions bringing serious health problems at the
level of the individual patient. The origin of ADs is an interruption of immune tolerance
of self-antigens [12].
Unknown mechanisms related to over-activity to self-antigens may
lead to the development of autoimmunity. However, most of the ADs share a considerable
degree of immunopathology, represented by a boost in activity of auto-reactive CD4+ T-
cells secreting inﬂammatory cytokines and loss of regulatory T-cell (Treg) function [13].
In addition, several common autoimmune diseases, such as Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA),
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) and Multiple Sclerosis (MS), are also genetically linked
to distinct human major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules.
According to several studies, complex interactions between genetic variation and environment
characterize the ADs’ onset [14]. In general, it is possible to state that ADs develop when a
genetically-susceptible human being meets an environmental trigger that could be the reason
for autoimmunity initiation [15]. So, the appearance of this condition not only depends on
a particular predisposition of immune system to this deviant responsiveness but also from
environmental factors (Figure 2) [16].

Noteworthy, various ADs show clear sex diﬀerence. Females are generally more fre-
quently aﬀected than males [18]. Precisely, previous studies highlighted how this diﬀerence
is stronger in certain diseases than others: for example, a very high (9:1) gender bias to-
ward females is present for SLE, which does not depend on country of assessment [19][20].
However, sexual dimorphism in ADs hasn’t been explained yet. Numerous are the causes
that can bring this diversity in immune system activity. First of all, female body presents
a higher immune reactivity due to a greater cell responsiveness [21]. In addition, it is nec-
essary to consider the hormonal alteration during women life, such as puberty, pregnancy,
menopause. Another factor is the diﬀerence in hormones: estrogen, progesterone, and pro-
lactin for women and androgens for men [12].
More than 80 chronic conditions have been identiﬁed as ADs [22]. Generally, ADs can
be categorized into two types of disorders [23]:
1. Systemic autoimmune disorders: immune tolerance interests systemic antigens. In this
case, disease symptoms manifest in diﬀerent sites of the body. Examples are RA, SLE,
and APS (Antiphospholipid syndrome) .
2. Organic-speciﬁc autoimmune disorders: autoimmunity principally involves tissue-speciﬁc
elements. Examples include Type 1 Diabetes (T1D), Psoriasis (PS) and MS.
2.2.1
Animal models of ADs
Thanks to their physiological resemblance to humans, animal models are widely used in
order to understand pathology, etiology and signaling pathways of human disease. Animal
models are particularly convenient also for their shorter life span: in this way, disease course
can be analyzed in a shorter interval of time. The most common animal models for human
diseases are rodents, in particular mice, rats and hamsters: they represent the 90% of the

Two groups of animal models of ADs are available: spontaneous and induced models. In the
ﬁrst group, disease develops spontaneously, instead, in the second group, disease is unnatu-
rally promoted. For the latter, three diﬀerent kinds of strategies are commonly applied for
induction of experimental ADs: immunization with autoantigen, transfer of autoimmunity,
and induction by environmental factors.
As previously mentioned, mouse model is broadly utilized, especially for studying ADs. In
general, some considerations are necessary in order to select a proper model. In the ﬁrst in-
stance, a mouse strain with a susceptible MHC must be preferred for induction of disease by
immunization. This is a consequence of the fact that most of ADs in humans are associated
with precise HLA loci, instead mouse models of ADs induced by immunization are usually
MHC-dependent. Unfortunately, spontaneous genetic mutations can alter the course of ADs
in animals.
Despite of the role of animal models in the study of ADs is remarkable, limitations need to
be considered. Due to discrepancies between humans and animals, some ADs still remain to
be modeled. In addition, since ADs in humans present diﬀerences, each animal model can
represent only a side of the total mechanism behind a particular disease [25].
2.3
Rheumatoid Arthritis
As recurrently mentioned above, RA is a chronic, auto-immune and inﬂammatory dis-
ease, aﬀecting synovial joints. First documentation of RA in Europe dates back to the early
17th century art and the ﬁrst reported case was published in 1676. It is the most common
inﬂammatory arthropathy and it is a major cause of disability [26]. Nowadays, the pervasive-
ness of RA remains high, with over 1% aﬀected of the population worldwide [27]. Statistics
showed as one in 12 women and 1 in 20 men will develop RA during their lifetime [28].
2.3.1
Etiology and Pathogenesis
This disorder is characterized by synovial inﬂammation and hyperplasia, autoantibodies
production, cartilage, and bone destruction [1]. Unfortunately, causes remain obscure.
As for all the ADs, RA involves an interaction among genotype, environmental factors and
autoimmunity triggers. Together with common environmental risk factors, such as smoking,
silica or textile dust, an interplay between environmental and genetic factors at mucosal
surfaces may originate RA. Sites as lungs, oral mucosa and gastrointestinal tract have been
associated with the disease [2]. This mechanism is not totally understood, but it is believed
that local tissue stress drives to post-translational modiﬁcation of peptides with consequent
antibodies generation.
Genetic factors include class II major histo-compatibility antigens/human leukocyte antigens
(HLA-DR).
The most important genetic risk allele for RA is located in MHC locus: it accounts for about
40% of the genetic inﬂuence. It was already conﬁrmed as HLA-DR4 alleles, containing the
"susceptibility epitope" SE, are associated with vulnerability to RA. The sequence associ-

ated with disease is generally glutamine-lysine-arginine-alanine-alanine (QKRAA), which is
present in some DR4 and DR14. The role of SE may be in shaping the T cell repertoire
in the thymus, modifying intracellular HLA-DR traﬃcking and antigen loading. Thanks to
recent informatics-based studies, it is possible to state that HLA-DR risk for RA is based
on the increased eﬃciency of antigen presentation for altered peptides rather than native
proteins. For altered, it is meant citrullinated peptides. Indeed, RA-associated alleles are
able to present citrullinated peptides eﬃciently to T cells, which, in turn, produce more
cytokines IL-17 and IFN-γ than native peptide [29].
Interestingly, HLA have also been linked to autoimmunity to citrullinated proteins. The
presence of serum anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPAs) is highly speciﬁc for RA:
70 % of patients with RA present ACPA [29]. Moreover, ACPAs are a useful marker for RA
diagnosis as they can be present several years before clinical onset [30].
However, previous studies carried out as some of the RA patients lack in these antibod-
ies. This is the reason why researchers have reached the agreement over the possibility to
divide RA into two major subsets based on the presence or absence of ACPA. Consequently,
RA individuals can be grouped as ACPA-positive and ACPA-negative patients. Their dis-
ease course is diﬀerent, which is more persistent and destructive for ACPA-positive patients
[31]. Their diﬀerent underlying pathogenesis supports the assumption that they can be con-
sidered as two diﬀerent illnesses, to be treated with diﬀerent strategies.
In spite of recent studies tried to address questions regarding these two contrasting sub-
sets, ACPA-negative disease is still poorly understood. However, in the last years, research in
this ﬁeld is moving forward, trying to ﬁnd explanations from analysis of genes. Previously, it
was mentioned as presence of ACPA can be predictive of the clinical status of patients. This
prediction may be arduous to achieve for ACPA-negative subjects so diﬀerent methods are
necessary to obtain the same information. Interesting is the research conducted by Boeters
et al.[31], in which they evaluated whether serological biomarkers at disease outbreak can
be predictive of achievement of sustained DMARD-free remission. Management of RA is
typically achieved by Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic drug (DMARD), and often by co-
administration of the latter with glucocorticoids (GCs) [32]. Thanks to this treatment, some
patients can attain sustained remission. Multi-Biomarker Disease Activity (MBDA) score,
based on measurement of the concentrations of 12 serum biomarkers, is a technique devel-
oped to measure RA disease activity [33], to make prediction for radiographic progression
[34] and to track response to treatment with several DMARDs [35]. As a result, the scientists
found out that ACPA-negative RA patients who achieved sustained DMARD-free remission
displayed a moderate or high MBDA score at the moment of diagnosis. The relevance of
this study is given by the fact that it was the ﬁrst one highlighting that, at disease onset,

2.3.2
Diagnosis of antibodies and use of synthetic antigens in RA
A prompt diagnosis of RA is paramount, in order to appeal to treatment as soon as
possible, avoiding joint damage and improving prognosis. One of the criteria of American
College of Rheumatology (ACR) for diagnosing RA is Rheumatoid Factor (RF) analysis. RF
is an antibody acting against the Fc portion of IgG, commonly used as a diagnostic marker
for RA. Unfortunately, RF is not fully trustworthy, since it can be present in healthy indi-
viduals or other patients with other ADs [38]. Consequently, European League of Arthritis
and Rheumatism (EULAR) recommended a combination of RF and ACPA, in particular an-
tibodies against cyclic citrullinated peptides (CCP), as a more reliable serological criterion
[39]. In fact, anti-CCP antibodies have the same sensitiveness but higher speciﬁcity than
RF for diagnosing RA and, the fact that they can be found in RF-negative RA patients is
worthy of note. Indeed, for detection of ACPAs in patients, most of the tests are executed
by assays that apply synthetic CCP as antigens. The former one are available in the market,
but, it is unfortunate that the speciﬁc antigens utilized in these assays are not declared by
the manufacturers [40].
Nowadays, citrullinated antigens are considered the most speciﬁc RA antibody.
Despite
studies have found out new antibodies for RA, diagnosis process hasn’t included them yet,
due to technical diﬃculties in their detection [38].
RA showed evidence of pathogenic disease-speciﬁc autoimmunity to citrullinated pro-
teins. CD4 T cells recognize modiﬁed self-epitopes, promoting the production of autoan-
tibodies that form immune complexes and deposits causing inﬂammation.
One of these
common modiﬁcations of self-epitopes is the citrullination of arginine, implicating the alter-
ation of the positively charge aldimine group (=NH) of arginine to the neutrally charged
ketone group (=O) of citrulline [41]. Expression of citrullinated proteins is abundant in
the synovial ﬂuid, an extracellular compartment within the joint [42]. The speciﬁc antigens
driving the immune response in RA are four citrullinated antigens, which are present in the
joint: citrullinated ﬁbrinogen, vimentin, collagen type II and α-enolase [43][44][45][46].
In particular, vimentin is a ﬁbroblast intermediate ﬁlament mainly found in non-muscle cells,
such as ﬁbroblast, endothelial cells, macrophages, melanocytes, Schwann cells, and lympho-
cytes. Historically, citrullinated vimentin was known as Sa antigen and it was identiﬁed in
circa 40% of patients with rheumatoid diseases. Subsequently, the real identity of Sa antigen
was revealed by Vossenaar et al [47].
It was demonstrated that anti-Sa antibodies react with citrullinated vimentin and they have
a similar speciﬁcity to anti-CCP but lower sensitivity. Consequently, studies demonstrated
how recombinant Mutated Citrullinated Vimentin (MCV) is a more sensitive autoantigenic

From the previous lines, it is understandable that synthetic antigens are valuable tools
used in immunological research, especially for diagnosis of autoimmune diseases, such as RA.
They are usually linear or branched polypeptides, prepared by polymerization of N-carboxy-

i.e. the ability of a substance to initiate an immune response in higher animals. A better
understanding of many immunological phenomena can be achieved by synthetic peptides,
for explaining the complex processes initiated by antigens that lead to a speciﬁc immune
response. As mentioned above, synthetic CCP are used as antigens for RA diagnosis, but cit-

vimentin has been identiﬁed as one of the speciﬁc antigens driving the immune response in
RA patients. For this reason, in this project, it was decided to synthesize linear and cyclic
peptides derived from citrullinated vimentin.
2.3.3
Cyclic peptides
As stated before, in this project, it was decided to synthesize also two diﬀerent cyclic
forms of the linear citrullinated vimentin. Peptides are attractive compounds, widely used
for drug development and for biochemical research. However, when using linear peptides,
there are diﬀerent limitations to deal with: poor membrane permeability, high susceptibility
to proteolysis, susceptibility to oxidation, short half-life and fast elimination [49].
Con-
versely, cyclic peptides show higher biological activity thanks to the their conformational
rigidity. Higher rigidity of cyclic peptides means a decrease in Gibbs free energy, improving
the binding with target molecules. As well, the cyclic structure is more resistant to both
exopeptidases and endopeptidases, since it lacks in both amino and carboxyl termini and
its structure is less ﬂexible than linear peptides [5]. These features make the cyclic peptides
potential tools to overcome linear peptides limitations.
Diﬀerent strategies can be used for chemically synthesized cyclic peptides. In this study,
it was decided to synthesize a backbone cyclic and a cysteine-bridged peptide. Both the
structures were obtained starting from the linear form, synthesized on beads using Fmoc
chemistry. In the case of backbone cyclic structure, the ﬁrst amino acid bears a protected
carboxylic acid. Then, the protecting group (Fmoc) is deprotected to release a free carboxyl
group for forming a lactam with the N-terminus of the linear peptide. On the other hand,
cyclization through disulﬁde bond formation is achieved by cysteines residues on the two
extremities of the linear peptide.

2.4

2.4.1
Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis (SPPS)
SPPS is a common technique for synthesizing peptides.
The chemistry in SPPS is
deﬁned by the temporary protecting group in the amino acid N-terminal amino group. In
this study, ﬂuoren-9-ylmethyloxycarbonyl (Fmoc) group is used. The synthesis starts from
the attachment of the ﬁrst amino acid (AA), from the C-terminal residue, i.e. carboxyl
group, and keep on coupling each amino acid of the sequence till the N-terminal end. The
coupling site is the alpha amino group and a temporary protective group is located in the
reactive side chains. In order to promote the coupling to the resin, the carboxyl group of
each amino acid must be activated. Then, the resin can be ﬁltered and washed to remove
reagents in excess.
Resins
There are diﬀerent types of linkers which determine the properties of the ﬁnal
product and what chemicals the resin-bound peptide complex can be exposed to during
reactions and cleavage. Two diﬀerent resins are utilized for this study: Rink amide resin
in Peptide 1 and Peptide 2, and, on the other hand, 2-Cholorotrityl resin for Peptide 3
(Figure 3). The diﬀerence between the two resins is that 2-chlorotrityl chloride results in an
carboxylic acid C-terminus, while rink amide resin gives an amide C-terminus [50][51].
Figure 3: Resins for SPPS used in this project [50][51]
Solvents and Swelling
The most common solvents for Fmoc SPPS are dimethylfor-
mamide (DMF) or dichloromethane (DCM). Prior to initiating a synthesis, the resin should
be swelled in the synthesis solvent. This is an important step: since resins are like a sponge,
swelling allows them to expand and boost the exposure of their functional groups to the
incoming reactants [50].
Amino Acids and Protecting Groups
Amino acids are compounds that consist of a
basic amino group, an acidic carboxyl group, and a unique organic side chain. They can be
considered as "building blocks" that form polypeptides and ﬁnally proteins. The end of the
peptide with the amino group is known as the N-terminus (amino terminus), and the end
of the peptide with the carboxylic acid group is known as the C-terminus (carboxylic acid
terminus) [52]. Peptide sequences are typically written in the N to C direction. However,
peptides are attached to resin from their C-terminus, so the synthesis is from the C to N di-
rection. In order to attach one amino acid at a time, the amino group must be protected with
Page 11 of 64
==PAGE:17==
Master thesis project
2.4
Synthesis of Peptides
an Fmoc protecting group to avoid indeﬁnite forming chains. However, it is also necessary
to protect the unique side chains, since some of them contain reactive groups.
Deprotection
Deprotection is the ﬁrst step in a synthesis cycle, aiming to the removal of
the Fmoc protecting group from the rink amide resin. Typically, 20% piperidine in DMF is
used for removing Fmoc groups.
Coupling
Activation is necessary for promoting the coupling of the carboxylic acid of
an amino acid to the amino group of another amino acid. In this study, HATU is used as
activator. Additionally, HATU needs a basic solution to work, such as Diisopropylethylamine
(DIPEA). The ﬁnal purity of the peptide depends on the coupling eﬃciency.
Coupling
reaction time may range from 1 hour to 2 hours.
Washing
Washing of the resin needs to be performed between each deprotection and
coupling steps for eliminating residual piperidine that could remove the Fmoc group from
the amino acid just added [50]. Washing can be performed with DCM and DMF .
Final deprotection and Cleavage
Once synthesis is accomplished, the ﬁnal steps are the
removal of the last Fmoc and side chain protecting groups and peptide’s cleavage from the
resin. The latter one is performed with triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA) or hexaﬂuoroisopropanol
(HFIP) and it is also common to add further reagents, called scavengers, to clear out side
chain protecting groups. A typical scavenger is TIPS (Triisopropylsilane). Afterwards, pep-
tides may be left in a fume hood to dry with nitrogen ﬂow overnight, in order to remove any
possible excess of TFA/HFIP and then lyophilized for storage [53].

2.4.2
Biotinylation
Biotinylation, also known as biotin tagging/labeling, is the covalent coupling of biotin
to an amino acid or carbohydrate moiety of the protein.
Biotynilation can be found in
nature, in organisms such as bacteria, plants and animals, but it can also be obtained in
in vitro chemical synthesis thanks to its versatility: not only biotin can bind to any reac-
tive functional group on the protein/peptide, but also this coupling is not even restricted
to a single residue. Chemical biotinylation of proteins is extensively used in cell biology
and biochemistry for detection, assay and puriﬁcation. Noteworthy is the strong interaction
of biotinylated proteins with avidin or streptavidin: this interaction is one of the strongest
non-covalent interactions existing in nature [54]. Thanks to this aﬃnity, it is possible to trap
speciﬁc proteins: the tagged protein/peptide is bonded to biotin that, in turn, is tightly at-
tached to avidin or streptavidin. Thereby, the unbound proteins are washed away from solid
support and just the proteins with high aﬃnity remain attached. The biotinylated protein
is obtained by mediation of polyethylene-glycol (PEG), that is attached to the N-terminal
of the peptides prior to biotin coupling. Indeed, it is preferable to avoid direct attachment
of biotin to the peptide’s N-terminal because the PEG-based spacer enhances the reduction
in steric hindrance, which is optimal for biotinylated product due to an improvement of
streptavidin binding to the biotin molecule [55]. Because of that, the peptides synthesized
in this project have been biotinylated, using PEG2 as linker.
2.5
Analysis of Peptides
After the synthesis of the linear and the two cyclic vimentin peptides, it is necessary to
proceed with the characterization and puriﬁcation of those ones. RP-HPLC is performed to
remove truncated synthesis products and reﬁne purity. Additionally, masses of peptides can
be conﬁrmed by MALDI-TOF MS.
2.5.1
Yield
Percent yield can be calculated by dividing the dry mass of the peptide by the theoretical
yield of the peptide. The theoretical yield of the peptide (in mg), is obtained by multiplying
the scale (in mmol) by the molecular weight of the peptide product (in mg/mmol).
2.5.2
Reversed phase HPLC
The percent purity of the product can be determined using reversed-phase HPLC anal-
ysis.
Reversed-phase High Performance Liquid Chromatography (RP HPLC) exploits the hy-
drophobicity of molecules in order to separate them. The more hydrophilic a molecule is,
the faster it elutes, meaning that a strong hydrophobic molecule elutes later. The chromato-
graphic mode that is commonly used for reversed-phase separation of peptides is gradient
elution, with two mobile phases. So, the sample is dissolved in a hydrophilic eluent and
then ejected into a column that is equilibrated with the same eluent. On the basis of that,
a hydrophobic molecule will bind to the column, since the aﬃnity to the column is higher
Page 13 of 64
==PAGE:19==
Master thesis project

than the aﬃnity with the hydrophilic mobile phase. Subsequently, the concentration of a
hydrophobic eluent is increasing following a gradient: when the aﬃnity of the molecule to
the mobile phase is larger than the stationary phase, the molecule can elute. Small proteins
(<50-100 AA), peptides, oligonucleotides can be puriﬁed by RPC, since their hydrophobic
moiety [56]. Peptides are usually dissolved at a concentration of 3 mg/mL in water or buﬀer
A. The buﬀer system used in this study for analysis of peptides consisted of Buﬀer A=0.01%
formic acid in water, and Buﬀer B =0.01% formic acid in acetonitrile. The peptide samples
are commonly analyzed and puriﬁed by increasing the gradient of buﬀer B from 5-95% or
5-50% over 30 or 45 minutes in a column. Peptides are detectable at 214-220 nm. Finally,
the % purity is calculated by dividing the area of the product peak by the total area and
multiplying by 100%.
2.5.3
MALDI-TOF MS
Then, the molecular weight of the product has to be veriﬁed and it can be done by mass
spectrometry. Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-ﬂight mass spectrometry
(MALDI-TOF) is a powerful method to determine in an accurate way the masses of peptides
[57]. In MALDI-MS, the sample is mixed to a small organic compound, called matrix, in
order to install it in a crystalline structure. In this project, 2,5-Dihydroxybenzoic acid (DHB)
matrix was used. Then, the mixture is deposited on a conductive plate. The analysis of the
samples starts when the plate is inserted in the device. A laser beam irradiates the crystals
in a order of time of nanoseconds. The beam may be an ultraviolet (UV) laser of which
characteristic wavelength is 266 or 337 nm. Due to the laser energy, the crystal is subjected
to decomposition, generating a particle cloud, called the plume, from which ions can be
extracted by an electric ﬁeld. The latter gives acceleration to the ions, which are able to
drift through a ﬁeld-free path and ﬁnally reach a detector. Next, ion masses (mass-to-charge
ratios) are calculated by measuring their time of ﬂight (TOF). Longer is TOF, larger are the
molecules. Finally, a spectrum shows the ion masses which are also available in numerical
data for direct processing and analysis. The range of TOFs measured during MALDI test
are about several microseconds. Very important is the choice of the matrix compounds, since
there is a strong correlation between them and quality of spectra. The optimal procedure
is to pipette a small volume of the sample, circa 0.5 µL, to same amount of matrix solution
and let the mixture dry out [58].

2.6.1
Indirect ELISA
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is a method that uses the immunology
concept of an antigen binding to its speciﬁc antibody, allowing the detection of antigens such
as proteins, peptides, hormones, or antibody in a ﬂuid sample [60].
This method is called "indirect" due to the presence of an antibody placed in between the
primary antibody and the antigen. Indeed, the diseased serum is added to the wells that
have been previously antigen-coated. During incubation, an antigen-antibody complex is
formed from the onset of antibodies against the antigens in the serum. The addition of
a secondary antibody, recognizing the antibody in the serum, makes the antigen-antibody
complex visible.
Subsequently, the substrate of the enzyme is added to the medium to
produce color and the concentration is determined by absorbance (Figure 4).
In this study, ELISA assay (IgG, IgM used as secondary antibodies) was performed using
multiple antigens to test a group of healthy, ACPA positive RA and ACPA negative RA sera
and synovial ﬂuid samples. As control diseases, sera samples with SLE, and AntiPhospholipid
Syndrome (APS) were included in the assay. All the chemically diverse antigens were applied
on the same ELISA plate. The 8 disease-related antigens are: D4, D5, HUV, mtDNA1,
mtDNA2, mtRNA1, CLox, and Peptide 1 (called cit2 in the assay) and Peptide m1 (called
cit5 during the assay), two forms of citrullinated vimentin synthesized during the project
. All of them are strongly associated with RA and SLE. Indeed, D4 and D5 are sequences
previously used for SLE studies [61].
It has already been recognized as mitochondrial and nuclear nucleic acids are present in
human blood, especially in patients suﬀering some kinds of pathologies, such as RA. In
fact, studies have conﬁrmed that mitichondrial RNA (mtRNA) and mitochondrial DNA
(mtDNA) induce proinﬂammatory response in human cells [62]. This is the reason why
it was decided to include mtDNA1, mtDNA2 and mtRNA in the antigens cohort for this
ELISA assay. Lastly, it has been demonstrated that CLox (oxidized cardiolipin) is useful
for research and diagnostics of human autoimmune disorders, in particular APS, for the
identiﬁcation of autoantibodies directed against cardiolipin, an important component of the
inner mitochondrial membrane in mammalians [63].

One of the most widely used method for antibody puriﬁcation is Aﬃnity Chromatog-

antigen, also called ligand, used for capture of antibody.

coupling of required ligands. Then, a biospeciﬁc ligand is needed for binding to immunoglob-
ulin. Essential characteristics of a ligand are its aﬃnity to the target and retention of the
target binding capacity after attachment to the matrix [64].
In substance, aﬃnity chromatography consists of four steps: preparation of absorption me-
dia, sample adsorption, washing and elution for achieving maximum yield and purity (Figure

2.6.3
Bradford protein assay
Bradford protein assay is a useful tool for assessing the protein concentration.
This method consists in binding of the dye Coomassie Blue G250 to protein. This free dye is
available in four diﬀerent ionic forms for which the pKa values are 1.15, 1.82 and 12.4. In the
acidic assay reagent solution, the more cationic red and green forms of dye have absorbance
maxima at 470 nm and 650 nm, respectively. On the other hand, the more anionic blue form
of the dye has an absorbance maximum at 590 nm. The latter is binding to the protein [65].
In this project, during the Bradford protein assay, protein concentrations were determined
by the absorbance at 595 nm thanks to the binding of Coomassie brilliant blue G-250 to
proteins.

4
Discussion
Solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) has demonstrated valuable success for synthesis of
a variety of natural and modiﬁed peptide sequences. The most eﬀective method currently in
use is the Fmoc chemistry, which has oﬀered a chemically mild alternative to the acid-labile
Boc group. Even though SPPS is substantial for peptide production, it has also challenges
to be faced [67].
If each step of the coupling process does not proceed with 100% eﬃciency, the ﬁnal product
will be contaminated with a mixture of shortened peptides, called deletion peptides. In this
project, the highest yield that has been achieved is 12.81%, 5.8 mg, which is not excellent,
taking in account normal yield values for SPPS with Fmoc chemistry [68]. There are sev-
eral peptide-related impurities that may aﬀect the purity and the ﬁnal yield of the product:
deletion and insertion of amino acids related to ineﬃcient Fmoc-deprotection and surplus
of amino acid reagents.
Furthermore, unprotected side chains may result into peptide-
protection adducts and they can also react with reagents used in the synthesis.
Lastly,
contamination may deﬁnitely play a role into the synthesis of the desired product [69]. In
the same way, the calculation of the theoretical yield was idealistic because it did not take
into account all the potential side reactions that occur during typical synthesis: this factor
aﬀected the calculation of the % yield.
Fortunately, RP-HPLC dealt with the puriﬁcation challenges, and products with high
purity could be obtained. Generally, a >80% purity is satisfactory with the production,
puriﬁcation and testing of antibodies, and products with this purity can be safely used with
ELISA and aﬃnity puriﬁcation [70]. In this project, indeed, values higher than 80% have
been achieved, and the highest purity is 100 %.
ELISA assay showed interesting results.
Both the citrullinated peptides 1 and m1
showed signiﬁcance in APS, for both IgM and IgG. Indeed, Perricone et al. [71] have showed
as Anti-MCV (Anti-mutated citrullinated vimentin) antibodies are present in a high per-
centage of APS patients (26.6%). According to this study, Anti-MCV antibodies can be
found in APS patients and they are associated with the presence of arthritis, meaning that
citrullination of vimentin occurs in APS, participate in disease pathogenesis, and inﬂuence
clinical picture.
Peptide m1 showed also signiﬁcance in ACPA positive RA and ACPA negative RA sera, for
IgG. Interestingly, the citrullinated Peptide m1 showed antibodies activity in ACPA positive
RA, but the same was not observed for Peptide 1. As MM2 in Chem3D demonstrated, the
citrulline residue can be less available for ACPA binding due to steric hindrance. Conse-
quently, this may have aﬀected the binding of ACPA to Peptide 1. On the other hand, the
activity in Peptide m1 may be also related to its modiﬁcation by biotinylation: biotin binds
non-speciﬁcally to plasma protein and biotin-reactive antibodies can occur in human sera
[72].
Several antigens showed signiﬁcance in ACPA positive RA, for IgG: D4, D5, HUV, mtDNA1,
mtDNA2, mtRNA and, CLox.
Page 35 of 64
==PAGE:41==
Master thesis project
HUV is a cytomegalovirus and only few studies were found that have determined HUV sero-
prevalence in RA patients [73].
Regarding mtDNA1, mtDNA2, and mtRNA, it has already been recognized as mitochon-
drial and nuclear nucleic acids are present in human blood, especially in patients suﬀering
some kinds of pathologies, such as RA. In fact, studies have conﬁrmed that mitochondrial
RNA (mtRNA) and mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) induce proinﬂammatory response in hu-
man cells [62]. At the other hand, mtDNA2 showed also signiﬁcance in ACPA negative RA
synovial ﬂuid samples, both for IgG and IgM: this conﬁrmed previous ﬁndings which stated
that, in RA, endogenous nucleic acid compounds might participate in joint inﬂammation by
activating immune cells in the joints to produce proinﬂammatory cytokines [74].
Finally CLox, not only was signiﬁcant for ACPA positive RA, but also for ACPA negative
RA sera samples: this result agrees with previous ﬁndings that conﬁrmed that anticardi-
olipin antibodies (ACA) are present in both ACPA positive and negative RA patients, even
if these antibodies are usually present in larger proportion in the positive subset than in the
negative one [75]. In the case of IgM, CLox was also signiﬁcant for both ACPA negative RA
sera and synovial ﬂuid samples.
This test was a pilot study and the amount of samples was limited, especially for healthy
and ACPA positive RA samples: this factor may have caused results’ alteration. Moreover,
it wasn’t possible to assess the binding activity of Peptide 1 and m1 in ACPA positive RA
samples for IgM, due to scarcity of samples .
Another factor that may have compromised the outcomes is CV. The duplication of the
plates allowed to estimate the coeﬃcient of variation: acceptable CV is lower than 15%.
During this assay, CV for Peptide 1 and m1 were out from the acceptable range. The next
study should aim to values under 15%, to be considered as acceptable. The high CVs may be
related to pipetting. Given the promising results, the assay should be extended, increasing
healthy, ACPA positive and negative RA, and APS samples and also improving the CV.
The stability of Peptide m1 and Peptide m3’s structure was veriﬁed through MM2 in
Chem3D: a lower free energy was observed in Peptide m1. Consequently, Peptide m1 has
a more stable structure and this should be reﬂected into a tighter bond to the antibodies.
It is well-established that cyclization enhances the selective binding, uptake, potency and
stability of linear precursors. This means that the two sequences have to be identical: just
in this case, the cyclic peptides show higher stability compared to their linear counterparts.
It could result surprising that, in this project, Peptide m1 is more stable than Peptide m3,
but it is necessary to remember that the two sequences diﬀer: in Peptide m3, alanine was
replaced by lysine, for consenting coupling of biotinyl. This little diﬀerence may have inﬂu-
enced the free energy, and so the stability, of Peptide m3.
In order to study the characteristics of the ACPA molecules, biotinylated citrullinated
vimentin linear (Peptide m1) and cyclic (Peptide m3) were used as antigens, to aﬃnity purify
antibodies from human sera samples (ACPA positive RA, n=7).
Thanks to this assay, it was possible to assess the diﬀerent behaviours of the two peptides.
The elution time of the two antigens was diﬀerent for 4 out 7 patients. It was expected to
see a later elution in Peptide m1, due to its lower free energy veriﬁed by Chem3D, resulting
Page 36 of 64
==PAGE:42==
Master thesis project
into a higher biological activity and, consequently, into a stronger bond between antigen and
antibodies: this was observed in three samples (patient 1, 4 and 6), in which antibodies were
released later by Peptide m1 than Peptide m3. Surely, through this assay, it was possible to
purify the antibodies which recognize citrullinated vimentin, and thanks to Bradford protein
assay, it was also possible to deﬁne the amount of protein contained in the elution, but
further studies need to be accomplished, for conﬁrming the responsiveness of ACPA postive
RA to those antigens. The next step would be Gel Electrophoresis, for separating proteins
according to their molecular size. In this way, it would be possible to deﬁne if antibod-
ies, such as IgG, are present in the elution obtained during aﬃnity chromatography. Then,
the binding between antigen and antibodies can be conﬁrmed by ELISA assay, and, lastly,
a macrophage assay can verify inﬂammation: antibodies and citrullinated peptides would
form an immune complex, which should stimulate release of RA macrophages and cytokines.
Additionally, increasing the number of samples would be ideal, for eﬀectively determine the
diﬀerences of the two antigens binding to antibodies of ACPA positive RA samples.
In conclusion, even if the results were altered in both the biochemical assays, due to
limited cohort for healthy, ACPA positive RA, and high CV for ELISA screening, future
studies could also try to improve the synthetic strategy of peptides. For example, Szarka
et al.[76] demonstrated that IgG puriﬁed on citrullinated vimentin are able to recognize
also other peptides such as ﬁlaggrin, even without Ala-Cit motif, which is characteristic of
vimentin. It means that antibodies speciﬁc for the Ala-Cit motif cross-react with Cit-Gly.
Taking in consideration this ﬁnding, implementation of the peptide sequences by adding
a Cit-Gly motif may be beneﬁcial.
It was demonstrated that this peptide has a higher
sensitivity to detect serum ACPA than peptides containing just Ala-Cit motif, it has a
similar binding aﬃnity to peptides with Cit-Gly motif, and, in vitro, it can also target the
peptide-speciﬁc B cells of RA patients.

5.2.1
General procedure of SPPS on Rink resin - GP1
All the following reactions are performed in standard SPE ﬁltration columns.
Coupling of ﬁrst amino acid
After coupling of the ﬁrst amino acid, the Fmoc group is
removed from the Rink linker using 20% piperidine in DMF. The coupling is achieved with
preactivation of Fmoc amino acid (4 eq.) with HATU (4 eq.) and DIPEA (6 eq.) in DMF.
Subsequently, the activated reagents can be added to the resin. The reaction time is 1 hour
with manual stirring every 15 minutes and coupling is performed 2 times. Washing of resin
between all reactions is carried out with DMF, DCM and DMF.

5.2.2
General procedure of SPPS on 2-chlorotrityl resin - GP2
All the following reactions are performed in standard SPE ﬁltration columns.
Coupling of the ﬁrst amino acid
Through preactivation (5 mins) of Fmoc amino acid
(4 eq.), with N,N -diisopropylethylamine (DIPEA) (6 eq.) in DCM, it is possible to carry
out esteriﬁcation of the 2-chlorotrityl linker with Fmoc amino acids. Once the reagents are
activated, they’re added to resin swollen in DCM. Reaction time is 1 hour with gentle stirring
every 15 minutes. It is important to perform coupling of ﬁrst amino acid twice to guarantee
complete reaction with the linker. Resin is washed between all reactions with DMF, DCM
and DMF respectively. After coupling, Fmoc cleavage is performed with 20% piperidine in
DMF.
Elongation of peptide
The coupling of amino acids is achieved with preactivation (5
mins) of Fmoc amino acid (4 eq) with 1-[bis(dimethylamino)methylene]-1H-1,2,3-triazolo[4,5-
b]pyridinium 3-oxid hexaﬂuorophosphate (HATU) (4 eq.) and DIPEA (6 eq.) in DMF. Once
the reagent is activated, it is added to the resin swollen in DMF. Reaction time is 1 hour
with manual stirring each 15 minutes. The coupling is performed two times. Subsequently,
Fmoc cleavage is performed with 20% piperidine in DMF. Again, washing of resin between
all reactions is achieved with DMF, DCM and DMF.
5.2.3
General procedure of kinetic oxidation for disulﬁde bond formation - GP3
DMSO oxidation is very mild and works at pHs from 3 to 8. The following procedure
was applied in this study [77]:
1. Dissolve the peptide in acetic acid (AcOH)
2. Dilute the compound to 0.1−10 mg/mL with MQ water
3. Add ammonium carbonate till pH 6 is reached, followed by Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)
(10% by volume).
4. Stir the mixture for at least 24 hours.

5.2.4
General cyclization procedure - GP4
Peptide preparation
In order to perform cyclization, the peptide needs to be synthesized
so that only 1 free amine and 1 free acid is available for reaction. This can be achieved,
using 2-chlorotrityl as resin and cleaving the peptide by Hexaﬂuoroisopropanol (HFIP) (20%
in DCM). Once the peptide has been cleaved, all the acids must be removed from the crude
peptide. Additionally, HFIP has to be removed by freeze-drying.
Reaction set up
Dissolved peptide is slowly added to a solution containing a high excess
of coupling reagent. The reaction is performed in vials or small round bottom ﬂasks: it is
important to seal oﬀthe vial or ﬂask with septum for maintaining argon atmosphere. The
peptide is dissolved in DMF (2 mL per 0.1 mmol peptide). Since the coupling solution is
unstable, it has to be prepared immediately before use it (5 mL DMF per 0.1 mmol peptide,
16 eq. HATU, 24 eq. DIPEA). The dissolved peptide is added dropwise over the course of
30 minutes after which the reaction is left for an additional 24 hours.
Work-up
The cyclization can be conﬁrmed by MALDI, Then the reaction mixture is
transferred to a separating funnel. Brine (50 mL) is added and the product is extracted
using DCM (3 x 50 mL). The combined organic phase is reduced by rotary evaporator due
to the presence of some DMF. The crude product is then ready for sidechain deprotection
or puriﬁcation.
5.2.5
General Alloc deprotection on 2-chlorotrityl resin procedure - GP5
Thanks to the following procedure, deprotection of Alloc as protecting group can be
performed [78]:
1. Wash the resin in a polyprep column 3 times with Dichloromethane (DCM)
2. Based on loading, dissolve 0.1 equiv. of tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0) in 6
ml of DCM
3. Add 20 equiv. of phenylsilane (density 0.878 g/cm3) to the resulting solution
4. Transfer the solution to resin in a poly-prep column in Argon environment
5. Leave the solution for 20 minutes, always keeping an Argon environment, if necessary,
add DCM to the resin
6. Remove the solution from the poly-prep column through vacuum

5.2.6
General procedure of TFA cleavage - GP6
In order to obtain a deprotected peptide, the cleavage from the resin is performed by
TFA (Triﬂuoroacetic acid) with scavengers. The TFA mixture is composed by TFA 88%,
H2O 5%, Phenol 5%, TIPS (Triisopropyl silane) 2%. The mixture is added 3 times to the
resin per 60 minutes. Once the 60 minutes has passed, with the help of nitrogen ﬂow, the
solution is transferred to a vial that has been previously weighted, in order to deﬁne the
weight of the crude product after lyophilization. The extracted product is reduced under
nitrogen ﬂow.
Note:
• Additionally, wash the resin with DCM for 3 times, in order to remove possible prod-
uct’s residues from the resin.
5.2.7
General procedure of HFIP cleavage - GP7
Cleavage from the resin is performed by HFIP (hexaﬂuoroisopropanol) to maintain
sidechain protecting groups. 20 % of HFIP is dissolved in DCM and added to the resin, 3
times per 30 minutes. Once the 30 minutes has passed, with the help of nitrogen ﬂow, the
solution is transferred to a vial that has been previously weighted, in order to deﬁne the
weight of the crude product after lyophilization. The extracted product is reduced under
nitrogen ﬂow.

possible to use the linker PEG2 as mediator for D-Biotin coupling, since Fmoc removal
couldn’t be performed. Another Fmoc group was already in the chain, at the N-terminal,
since it hadn’t been removed from the last Arginine. If linker PEG2 was coupled to the
chain and then, Fmoc removal was performed, we couldn’t know which of the two Fmoc
groups would have been removed, meaning that the D-Biotin coupling position would also
have been unknown. Once Biotinyl-AEEA was coupled to the sequence, it was possible
to proceed to the elimination of Fmoc group in the amino acid N-terminal amino group.
After the resin was cleaved by HFIP treatment, following the general procedure of HFIP
cleavage (GP7), the extracted product was reduced under nitrogen ﬂow, in order to remove
solvent. Lately, it was dissolved in 70% ACN in MQ-water with 0.1% FA and it was freeze
dried. Lately, the cyclization of the structure could be performed. In this case, the general
cyclization procedure (GP4) was followed. Then, the global deprotection of all side chains
was performed by TFA cleaving mixture (TFA 88%, H2O 5%, Phenol 5%, TIPS 2%): 5
ml of mixture were added to the product and left for approximately 6 hours, occasionally
stirring it manually. The product was reduced under nitrogen ﬂow and then dissolved in
75% ACN in MQ-water with 0.1% FA. After freeze dry, the correct mass of the received
product was conﬁrmed by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry. The received compound was
puriﬁed by preparative HPLC utilizing gradient elution 5-95 %, and, then, characterized by
MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry. The mass was received with a good intensity.

Enzyme Linked Immunoassorbent Assay - ELISA
The ELISA screening was carried out by the following procedure.
Buﬀers
Before starting the ELISA, it was necessary to prepare the buﬀers. All the buﬀers
were prepared using MQ water. The buﬀers’ preparation methods are given below [59]:
1. Phosphate buﬀered saline (1X PBS): this buﬀer was used as washing buﬀer and it was
prepared mixing 8 g of NaCl, 200 mg KCl, 1.44 g Na2HPO4, 240 mg KH2PO4 in 1 L
of MQ water. Then, the buﬀer was adjusted in pH to 7.4.
2. PTB buﬀer: this buﬀer was used as blocking solution and it was prepared mixing 20
g of Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) in 1 L of 1X PBS, followed by 50 µl of Tween 20.
3. Diluent: it was prepared adding 2 g of Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) in 1 L of 1X
PBS. 50 µl of Tween-20 were added to the solution.
4. Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) substrate: this buﬀer was used for incubation and it was
prepared dissolving 3 mg of analytical grade TMB in 5 ml of biological grade Dimethyl
sulfoxide (DMSO) and the volume was adjusted to 50 ml with 0.1 M acetate buﬀer.
Then, 3 µl of H2O2 were added to the buﬀer and it was stored ad +4◦C
5. Stopping solution: the analytical grade sulfuric acid H2SO4 was diluted to gain the
concentration of 1 M.

Conclusion
Prompt diagnosis and punctual treatment are indispensable for the prevention of un-
desirable disease outcome in RA, such as disability and systemic complications. Nowadays,
between the classiﬁcation criteria for RA, autoantibodies are included, especially ACPA. In
fact, production of novel autoantibodies can be encouraged by citrullination of amino acids
that cause new epitopes. Even if RA is the main focus for several studies, the pathogenic
role of these autoantibodies is not totally understood.
In this study, aspiring to give a contribution to a better understanding of ACPA posi-
tive and ACPA negative RA subsets, three diﬀerent forms of citrullinated vimentin and their
modiﬁcations have been synthesized. The choice of synthesizing two cyclic forms was driven
by the scarcity in the literature of tests based on cyclic citrullinated vimentin. In particular,
two linear peptides and a cyclic one were tested by ELISA and aﬃnity chromatography, for
evaluating the possibility of using them as promising biomarkers.
These three peptides, the linear, the biotinylated linear and the biotinylated backbone
cyclic forms of citrullinated vimentin were obtained with poor yield, but their purity was
satisfactory and applicable for in vitro studies. The received purities were 98,70%, 87,92%,
and 81.81%, respectively.
Lately, ELISA screening was conducted, employing the linear form, Peptide 1, and the
biotinylated linear form, Peptide m1, as antigens. The binding diﬀerence of ACPA positive
RA and ACPA negative RA patients with healthy controls was assessed by one-way ANOVA
test. The test was not positive for Peptide 1. On the other side, Peptide m1 showed signif-
icance in ACPA positive RA, but, unexpectedly, also in ACPA negative RA sera samples,
in IgG. Unfortunately, ACPA positive RA could not be tested for IgM. Biotinylation, high
CV, and limited healthy and ACPA positive RA sample cohort may have aﬀected the results.
For a better interpretation of the aﬃnity chromatography’s results, the stability of Pep-
tide m1 and Peptide m3 was checked by MM2, using Chem3D. It resulted that Peptide
m1 has a more stable structure than Peptide m3, due to its lower free energy, which was
surprising since cyclic structures are well-known for their conformational rigidity. However,
the sequence of the two peptides is not identical and this may have played a role.
Lastly, antibodies binding speciﬁcally to Peptide m1 and Peptide m3 were puriﬁed by
aﬃnity chromatography, from n=7 ACPA positive RA samples. Analysis of the elution,
performed by Bradford protein assay, showed that, in some patients, the two antigens have
diﬀerent outcomes. In overall, Peptide m1 showed a later elution time in three patients
out of seven, suggesting that this peptide may have a tighter bond with the proteins than
Peptide m3. However, the results are still vague to draw a conclusion. The assay needs to
be conﬁrmed by further tests and also extended with a larger cohort, to deﬁne the potential
as antigens of the two peptides.
