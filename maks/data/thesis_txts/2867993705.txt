Abstract
In this study, we extend the principles of life2vec, originally developed for modeling human
life trajectories, to analyze and predict company lives. By representing companies as structured
sequences of life events, we develop a symbolic language capable of capturing the complexity
of corporate trajectories. Utilizing over 21 million event registrations from more than 400,000
Danish firms spanning 2013–2023, we present company2vec, a model that learns semantically
meaningful representations of these sequences using state-of-the-art methods from language
modeling. Our approach leverages the full scope of company life trajectories, enabling predic-
tions of firm dynamics, including bankruptcy, capital increases, relocations, and changes in em-
ployee levels. The resulting embedding space, the vector representations of our symbolic lan-
guage, demonstrate robust and logical structure, clustering industries, geographic regions, and
numeric data from financial reports in ways that reflect real-world relationships. This frame-
work removes the need for multiple task-specific prediction models, as it can be fine-tuned for
a variety of downstream applications. The findings highlight the potential of company2vec as
a versatile tool for predicting corporate outcomes and exploring the broader dynamics of eco-
nomic systems. This work lays a foundation for deeper insights into corporate behavior and the
mechanisms driving firm success or failure.

Introduction
Companies, like people, live a life with a beginning and an end. They are born, grow, form
relationships, and adapt to their environments. Some achieve great success and leave legacies,
while others face challenges that lead to decline or reinvention. A life can be viewed as a series
of events collectively forming a trajectory. The life-events experienced by companies and indi-
viduals share more similarities than one might expect. Humans and companies are subject to
financial considerations at almost every major decision point in their lives. For instance, just as a
person might choose to switch careers to improve their prospects or align with new opportuni-
ties, a company may alter its business model to focus on a more promising industry. Similarly,
the decision to move to a new city, whether by an individual or a company, often stems from
similar motivations, such as access to certain things or pursuing new opportunities. Whether
it is registering a new industry, relocating, hiring new employees, or publishing audit reports,
company lives are characterized by a rich series of events that are common across all compa-
nies. Some of these events occur regularly, and others happen less frequently, but together,
these events shape a company’s life, forming a unique sequence that reflects a company’s pro-
gression and decision-making. Consequently, we theorize that no companies follow the exact
same path - each company has a distinct life trajectory. However, there might exist similarities
across life trajectories for companies experiencing rapid growth, decline, or bankruptcy. These
hidden patterns could be the key to understanding and predicting firm dynamics.
In Savcisens et al. (2023), life2vec was introduced - a novel architecture capable of effectively
understanding and predicting human life trajectories. The architecture relies on Transform-
ers, a neural network architecture stemming from language modeling. Transformer models
have rapidly gained popularity in the field of machine learning due to their exceptional per-
formance across a wide range of prediction tasks, particularly with the rise of large language
models. These models excel at understanding sequences of data, such as sentences, music, or
other temporal patterns. The life2vec model used a Transformer model to predict human life
expectancy and personality traits on an individual level (Savcisens et al., 2023). This application
was enabled by converting a large labor and health registration dataset into a symbolic language
representing human life-events. This language representation enabled the Transformer to effec-
tively leverage its ability to identify patterns in sequential data.
Much like a person’s life, a company’s trajectory consists of significant events, which we can
formulate into a symbolic language. Through such a language, we can analyze the ”life” of a
company similar to how life2vec analyzed human life sequences. Thus, this project aims to
create a company2vec model following the methodology presented by Savcisens (2023a). Pre-
dicting company outcomes, such as growth or bankruptcy, has historically relied on calculations
based on financial statement analysis or other traditional statistical models within economet-
rics. Our motivation is thus twofold: (1) to determine whether the life2vec methodology can
be applied to a different life trajectory domain and (2) to develop a model that extracts signals
from company life sequences and predicts firm dynamics. This eliminates the need for sev-
eral task-specific models within the corporate domain. Furthermore, by utilizing Transformer
models to encode company event sequences, we can uncover life-event patterns that may not
be apparent through traditional methods.

Specifically, we aim to describe events such as:
1. Bankruptcy
2. Changes to capital structure and investments
3. Firm relocation
4. Development in employee levels
We will develop the company2vec model using data from the Danish Central Business Register.
This data will serve as the basis for constructing a vocabulary that defines key events throughout
a company’s lifespan. Using Transformer models, the company2vec model will embed these
events into a vector space, potentially capturing essential patterns of company development.
The project will analyze the embedding space to uncover latent structures and relationships
between different business events.
Our research is organized into four main parts. First, in the Literature Review, we discuss the
relevant literature and methods closely related to our work. Second, in Data, we outline the
process of extracting raw data from the Danish Central Business Register, analyzing firm data,
and transforming it into a symbolic language. Third, in Methodology, we provide a detailed de-
scription of the company2vec model architecture, the fine-tuning process, our selected bench-
mark models, and the evaluation methods used. Finally, in Results, we examine the embedding
space of company2vec, evaluate its robustness and meaningfulness, and present the outcomes
of our firm dynamics prediction tasks.

Literature Review
This chapter aims to introduce the reader to the literature relevant to our study. This includes
introducing the core paper that inspired our study, life2vec. Additionally, we will introduce
literature detailing other uses of Transformer models for sequence-based tasks in recent years.
These will showcase the advantages of using Transformer-based models and help explain the
increasing interest in using them due to their state-of-the-art performance. This literature re-
view will also present research relevant to ours in the domain of corporate predictions. We
will review the key areas used to describe firm dynamics. In particular, we will provide a re-
view of the traditional accounting-based approaches as well as newer market-based approaches
for predicting bankruptcy. Furthermore, we will present examples of research detailing other
firm dynamic prediction models. Finally, we will also present the newest advances in corporate
predictions using machine learning methods to describe different firm dynamics.
2.1
Using Sequences of Life-events to Predict Human Lives
The core inspiration for our project is the 2023 paper ”Using Sequences of Life-events to Predict
Human Lives” that presents the life2vec model. The paper by Savcisens et al. (2023) was pro-
duced as a part of the Ph.D. project ”Life Trajectories as Symbolic Language” completed by Ger-
mans Savčišens. We will refer to the work presented in the published article and the doctoral
dissertation collectively and interchangeably as life2vec.
The aim of the life2vec project was to model human life trajectories using Danish registry
data and Transformer-based methods to capture patterns in these trajectories and predict future
life-events. The data source for the project includes information such as personal addresses,
health data (e.g., hospital visits, diagnoses), and labor data (e.g., monthly salary and employer
information) for approximately 6 million individuals. The project adapted the well-recognized
BERT language model to learn representations of labor and health concepts and create person-
level summaries (Savcisens, 2023a).
Savcisens (2023a) highlights that a key motivation behind the architecture chosen for the life2vec
model is its ability to utilize the full, rich sequential signal of life-event data. This is in contrast
to traditional statistical models like regression or tree-based methods, which require the data
to be reduced into a rigid tabular format. While these traditional models are inherently inter-
pretable, allowing for straightforward explanations of predictions, they require extensive data
preprocessing and feature engineering, which, as Savcisens (2023a) points out, may lead to the
loss of temporal information and other critical details enclosed in a sequence of life-events.
Instead, life2vec uses the principles of representation learning, where the focus has shifted
from understanding and explaining a model’s parameters to understanding the numerical rep-
resentation of different phenomena. By adopting this approach, Savcisens (2023a) creates a
model that may understand patterns in human life trajectories otherwise beyond our compre-
hension. This results in a so-called foundational model, as Savcisens (2023a) denotes it, which
can tackle different prediction tasks related to a human life trajectory.

The general approach presented by Savcisens (2023a) includes three overall steps:
1. Translate raw event data into a symbolic language, which is a text-like representation of
human life trajectories.
2. Pre-train a language model on the symbolic language to create an embedding space1 that
can distinguish between different life-events.
3. Fine-tune the model with a specific prediction task (e.g. early mortality).
A key element in the methodology presented by Savcisens et al. (2023) is the task of translat-
ing raw data describing sequences of life-events into a symbolic language. Without this step,
a Transformer-based model will not understand the sequence of life-events. Savcisens (2023a)
describes how they draw inspiration from the world of music, where musical pieces are trans-
lated into a machine-friendly language, specifically in the Musical Instrument Digital Interface
(MIDI) format. In this format, a piece of music is stored as a sequence of events, including the
time of a note, its velocity, pitch duration, etc., instead of storing the actual audio. This data
may be transcribed into a symbolic language, resulting in a document you can pass to a Trans-
former detailing the musical piece. Savcisens (2023a) refers to Huang and Yang (2020), who
used this methodology in their paper ”Pop music transformer: Beat-based modeling and generation
of expressive pop piano compositions”, where they used Transformer-based models to generate new
compositions.
The life2vec model produces an embedding space, which clearly distinguishes between dif-
ferent concepts. The resulting embedding space is clearly separated with a meaningful ranking
of numerical tokens and clustering of concept tokens in meaningful categories. This proves the
model’s grasp of the life sequences, allowing it to make predictions of future events in a life
trajectory.
One of life2vec’s prediction tasks is predicting early mortality (the ”survival task”). This is a
binary classification, which predicts whether a person dies within a prediction window or not.
Additionally, Savcisens et al. (2023) shows that the life2vec model may be adapted in order to
predict personality nuance, which is a multi-label prediction. Savcisens (2023a) compares the
life2vec performance against several baselines, and he finds that life2vec outperforms all
of them for both the early mortality prediction as well as personality nuance prediction. This
shows how the life2vec model has learned patterns in the pre-training phase regarding the
phenomena in life trajectories that other models cannot capture. Savcisens et al. (2023) thus
succeeded in creating a so-called foundational model, which is a model with core concept un-
derstanding that can be fine-tuned to specific prediction tasks within that domain.

Natural Language Processing
The Transformer architecture was quickly adapted for further research and development. In
the 2018 paper ”Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training” by Radford et al.
(2018), OpenAI introduced the first GPT model (short for Generative Pre-Training). This model
used variants of the Transformer to operate in a unidirectional manner by processing text se-
quentially from left to right in order to predict the next word in a sequence of text. The paper
presented a semi-supervised approach to language understanding: the model first undergoes
generative pre-training by learning patterns from a large, unlabeled corpus to build a language
model, and is then fine-tuned for specific discriminative tasks using labeled data. These in-
clude tasks such as natural language inference, question answering, semantic similarity, and
text classification.
The fine-tuning approach, as presented in Radford et al., 2018, which combines pre-training on
a large dataset with fine-tuning on a smaller, task-specific dataset, was further developed in De-
vlin et al. (2019). In the paper ”BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language
Understanding”, it was argued that existing fine-tuning methods were limited because they re-
lied solely on unidirectional pre-trained models. Fine-tuning a unidirectional model to tasks
such as question-answering would be challenging due to a lack of sentence-level understand-
ing. As an alternative, they introduced BERT, a model built on a bidirectional Transformer
architecture. BERT was pre-trained using two unsupervised tasks: (1) Masked Language Mod-
eling (MLM), a task where random words in a sentence are masked, and the model predicts the
masked words, and (2) Next Sentence Prediction (NSP), a task that teaches the model to identify
the correct order between two sentences in a pair. Unlike GPT, which predicts the next word in
a sequence using a left-to-right approach, BERT is trained to predict a randomly masked word
in a sentence using a bidirectional attention mechanism, allowing it to simultaneously consider
all words in a sentence. This bidirectional processing allows BERT to outperform GPT in tasks
requiring language understanding across an entire sequence, such as question answering and
sentiment analysis, while GPT remains stronger for text generation tasks.
These two papers have arguably become some of the most influential papers within modern
NLP, laying the foundation for the widely spread use of LLMs today. In addition, they helped
solidify the effectiveness and impact of Transformers in terms of predictive performance. Due
to their success in NLP and ability to capture complex relationships in data, Transformers have
been adapted for use in other domains beyond text.

2.3
Corporate Predictions
Understanding and predicting the trajectories of companies has long been an area of study in
economics and econometrics, with societal implications for financial institutions, investors, pol-
icymakers, and researchers. Companies operate within complex, dynamic systems shaped by
market forces, industry trends, and the decisions and behaviors of individuals within organi-
zations and society as a whole. These complexities make corporate behavior both challenging
to analyze and fascinating to study, as it provides insights into economic trends and societal
outcomes.
Traditionally, corporate predictions have focused on modeling credit and bankruptcy risk - areas
of critical interest to financial institutions needing robust evaluations of a firm’s ability to repay
loans. The foundational data for such predictions often came from financial metrics drawn from
annual reports, i.e. a company’s profit and loss statement as well as balance sheet. However, as
the availability and diversity of data have expanded, so has the scope of corporate predictions.
Today, corporate predictions expand beyond credit and bankruptcy risk into areas such as fore-
casting revenue growth, start-up success, employee turnover, etc. In the context of our project,
we consider these predictions different kinds of firm dynamics - i.e., the corporate predictions
cover a broad range of company characteristics and life-events. In this section, we will review
examples of traditional as well as more modern corporate predictions.
2.3.1
Traditional Approaches for Bankruptcy Predictions
The first attempts at creating bankruptcy prediction models were completed by Altman (1968)
and Ohlson (1980). The two approaches were both based on using financial ratios as the vari-
ables to which they fitted their models. In this section, we will dive into the two original ap-
proaches, which can be divided into the accounting-based and market-based approaches. In
addition, we will discuss some of the empirical literature that investigates bankruptcy predic-
tion models.
Accounting-based Approach
In order to determine whether a company is creditworthy, traditionally, companies would be
evaluated based on their financial reports (Altman, 1968). Specifically, they would be evaluated

on certain ratios of key financial values, i.e. ratios measuring the company’s liquidity, profitabil-
ity and solvency. Naturally, these measures were interpreted as a sort of bankruptcy indicator
and thus used in evaluating a company’s bankruptcy potential.
In 1968, Altman (1968) published his influential paper, ”Financial Ratios, Discriminant Analysis
and the Prediction of Corporate Bankruptcy” in the Journal of Finance. This work has since become
a seminal paper in the field of bankruptcy prediction. The paper evaluates the effectiveness of
ratio analysis as an analytical tool and aims to bridge the gap between traditional ratio analysis
and modern statistical techniques. Traditional ratio analysis has a univariate approach and
had, among theorists, gained a reputation of being ”arbitrary rules of thumb” (Altman, 1968).
As an alternative, Altman (1968) introduced a methodology based on multiple discriminant
analysis (MDA), which derives a linear combination between multiple company characteristics
- the independent variables also referred to as ”ratios” - that best distinguishes between two
groups: bankrupt and non-bankrupt companies. This results in a sum, or discriminant score,
which Altman (1968) denotes the ”Z-score”:

This Z-score serves as a threshold to predict whether a company is likely to go bankrupt and is

These ratios are:

2. Retained earnings / Total assets

5. Sales / Total assets
Altman (1968) describes how these five ratios are chosen, as they each provide nuanced insight
into a company’s performance and trajectory. It is important to use several ratios in order to
obtain a nuanced prediction - as an example Altman (1968) states how low profit on its own is
not necessarily a sign of impending bankruptcy if liquidity is above average.
Altman (1968) finds that the bankrupt and non-bankrupt firms get statistically significant dif-
ferent Z-scores, and with lower discriminant scores follows a greater bankruptcy potential. For
predictions, the classification rule is to assign a firm to the group (bankrupt or non-bankrupt)
with a group mean closest to its score. Altman (1968) finds that the bankruptcy prediction
model is an accurate forecaster up to two years prior to bankruptcy, however, with rapidly de-
clining performance after the second year. Specifically, the model achieves a 94% accuracy on
an in-sample dataset of 66 firms, specifically publicly held manufacturing corporations (50/50
split on bankrupt/non-bankrupt firms). Despite its simple structure, the Z-score is still used
as a benchmark model for bankruptcy predictions to this day, although not always in its orig-
inal form. We should note that the model has been revised since 1968. In 1977, Altman et al.
(1977) presented the ZETA model, an extension of Altman’s original work with a total of 28
independent variables used. In 1980, Ohlson (1980) presented another alternative but similar
approach to that of Altman. His work uses logit regression as an alternative to MDA to predict
the probability of bankruptcy within one year. Similar to Altman’s original approach, Ohlson’s

Market-based Approach
Another approach to firm predictions, including bankruptcy forecasting, is market-based meth-
ods. These methods can use market data, such as stock returns and market size, along with
financial metrics to estimate the likelihood of bankruptcy. One idea of creating market-based
models was first presented by Merton (1974) in the paper ”On the Pricing of Corporate Debt: The
Risk Structure of Interest Rates” published in the Journal of Finance. The ”Merton model” assesses
corporate credit risk by pricing corporate debt, and it does so by considering both a firm’s as-
sets as well as the volatility of its assets. The big difference between the Merton model and
Altman’s z-score is that Altman considers historical financial statements, while Merton intro-
duced the idea of using current data consisting of market valuations and different volatility
measures. The Merton model is based on market expectations and is thus a dynamic assess-
ment of a firm’s creditworthiness. However, it should be noted that the Merton model was not
designed to be a bankruptcy prediction model like Altman’s z-score. Under certain conditions
with assumptions, it may be used for bankruptcy predictions (Merton, 1974)
An attempt to combine Altman’s methodology with market-based methods was presented by
Shumway (2001) in the paper ”Forecasting Bankruptcy More Accurately: A Simple Hazard Model”
published in the Journal of Business. Shumway’s motivation was based on the fact that since
Altman, researchers had been using static models, i.e. single-period predictions relying on sam-
ple data from several years. Shumway (2001) argues that this leads to biased results, as these
static models ignore changes in sample data through time. Instead, he presents a simple hazard
model, or so-called ”survival model”, which specifies the likelihood of a ”failure/death” event
(in this case bankruptcy) at a given time t throughout a firm’s life. As the independent vari-
ables for his model, Shumway (2001) uses financial ratios, including those presented by Altman
(1968), combined with market variables such as firm market capitalization, the standard devia-
tion of stock returns, etc. Shumway (2001) finds that the hazard model is unbiased whereas the
static model is biased. Moreover, he finds that the hazard model classifies better than the static
model when using identical variables.
Empirical Research
In the paper ”Comparing the performance of market-based and accounting-based bankruptcy prediction
models” published in the Journal of Banking & Finance, Agarwal and Taffler (2008) compares
the market-based and accounting-based approach to bankruptcy prediction. The paper was
motivated by the introduction of the then-new Basel II requirements (2006), where the mini-
mum capital requirements for banks were raised. For context, the Basel framework is the in-
ternational regulatory standard for banks, and the Basel II framework renewed the interest in
credit risk assessment. Agarwal and Taffler (2008) specifically compares a so-called KMV model,
which is a model derived from the Merton model, with a UK-based z-score model. Agarwal and
Taffler (2008) finds that the two models perform very similarly in terms of predictive accuracy.
However, Agarwal and Taffler (2008) also finds that neither model is a sufficient statistic for
bankruptcy prediction. Moreover, both models carry unique information about firm failure, so
according to Agarwal and Taffler (2008), one model cannot necessarily said to be preferred over
the other.
While understanding the similarities and differences between bankruptcy prediction approaches
is insightful, both methods discussed here focus primarily on economic values, whether finan-
cial ratios or market variables. However, there are other parameters to be aware of when assess-
ing a firm’s likelihood of bankruptcy. A growing body of research explores how non-financial
factors contribute to a firm’s stability and financial health. One example of this is presented by

Supervised Learning in Corporate Predictions
With the rise of statistical learning, the use of supervised learning for corporate prediction in-
creased as well. Bargagli Stoffi et al. (2020) provide a review of supervised machine learning ap-
plications for predicting firm dynamics, such as startup success, firm growth, and bankruptcy.
They argue that the non-parametric8 nature of supervised learning techniques enables them
to uncover hidden patterns in large datasets that traditional econometric methods might miss.
They divide the review of prediction models into three categories of firm dynamics, where they
highlight (1) entrepreneurship and innovation, (2) firm performance and growth, and (3) finan-
cial distress and firm bankruptcy.
Entrepreneurship & Innovation: Based on their review of papers, the authors note that startups
are an important part of the economy, but only 10% generally remain active in the long-term.
This makes predicting startup success an interesting target, which has been explored widely
in the literature, where supervised learning methods have been shown to perform well, with
performance varying based on the dataset at hand. The authors note that startup success can be
defined in different ways: (1) companies receiving a large amount of external funding, (2) com-
panies that simply survive a given number of years, (3) companies that are merged or acquired
in a M&A activity, (4) companies with project-based success, e.g. FDA approval of clinical trials
for pharmaceutical companies.
Firm Performance & Growth: In their review of contributions, Bargagli Stoffi et al. (2020) find
that predicting company growth remains a difficult problem with recent approaches having
little predictive power. They find that good predictors vary across firms and that predicting
growth and future performance may be difficult based on historical information, such as balance
sheet data. Generally, they find that growth can depend on interrelated variables, making the
prediction task difficult.
Financial Distress & Bankruptcy: Bargagli Stoffi et al. (2020) find that the usage of unstructured
data (e.g., text from audit reports) can improve the prediction of financial distress, and when
large amounts of data are used, supervised learning can outperform standard econometric mod-
els. Additionally, Bargagli Stoffi et al. (2020) find that including ”missing” information is rel-
evant as well, as financial information is not always disclosed in times of financial distress. In
their review of contributions to the literature, it seems that predictions of financial distress and
bankruptcy are often completed on datasets with primarily larger corporations.
Overall, Bargagli Stoffi et al. (2020) provide lengthy lists of papers within these three categories.
In the context of our project, we are mostly interested in diving further into research within the
latter two categories: firm performance and growth, as well as financial distress and bankruptcy.

M&A Activities

In Andrade and Stafford (2004), the authors investigate the economic role of mergers and find

There is a substantial amount of empirical research detailing company behavior in their deci-
sions regarding relocation. In Brouwer et al. (2004), which is an empirical investigation, the
authors state that there are three well-established and recognized factors that influence a firm’s
decision to relocate. Specifically, these are (1) internal factors (e.g., firm size), (2) external factors
(e.g., market size), and (3) location factors (e.g., region) (Brouwer et al., 2004). They investigate
these through an analysis using data on European companies, focusing on larger firms with
200+ employees. They find that the tendency to relocate decreases with the size of the firm.
Moreover, they find that firms involved in a merger or an acquisition are much more likely to
relocate, which intuitively is very reasonable. Based on this finding, Brouwer et al. (2004) con-
clude that ”external growth” factors are very important in understanding relocation behavior
for larger firms. Finally, the authors find that firms serving larger markets are more inclined to
relocate compared to firms serving smaller markets.

Stam (2007) investigates the behavior of entrepreneurial firms in particular. They state that en-

Employee Forecasting
In the literature, the majority of research on employee forecasting focuses on predicting em-
ployee turnover or attrition. As a result, the emphasis is primarily on forecasting the loss of
employees, whether voluntary or involuntary, with less attention given to predicting company
growth in terms of employee acquisition.
Punnoose and Ajit (2016) successfully used the extreme gradient boosting algorithm (XGBoost)
for predicting employee turnover. The authors argue that this is an important tool in enabling
data-driven decision-making for firms regarding their employee retention and succession plan-
ning. The authors argue that employee turnover affects workplace productivity as well as a
firm’s long-term growth strategies, making their results very useful for real-world application.
Another example is in Ozdemir et al. (2020), where they successfully used 11 different clas-
sification algorithms to predict employee attrition using an IBM HR dataset. Their findings
highlight that data mining methods are highly effective for predicting employee attrition and
point to significant automation potential for HR departments across organizations.
2.3.5
Transformers in Corporate Predictions Settings
The literature on company predictions utilizing Transformer-based models and representation
learning remains relatively sparse, with notable exceptions in the stock market domain, where
Transformers have been extensively applied and tested. Some studies have explored fine-tuning
BERT models for prediction tasks in adjacent areas using strictly textual data. For instance,
Bhattacharya and Mickovic (2024) use the ’Management Discussion & Analysis’ sections from
SEC filings9 as input for fraud detection. Other approaches use pre-trained Transformer models
as components within larger architectures. An example is Lombardo et al. (2024), where a BERT
model extracts document-level embeddings from annual reports, which are then combined with
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)-processed financial values to predict bankruptcy.
Further studies focus exclusively on financial values, such as Korangi et al. (2023), who devel-
oped a Transformer-based default prediction model specifically for mid-cap companies (market
capitalization under USD 10 billion). Their dataset comprises approximately 17,000 companies
over a 30-year span (1990–2020) and includes features such as quarterly accounting data, gen-
eral market performance (e.g., S&P index data), and daily stock prices. Their approach employs
a Transformer encoder tailored for panel data classification, where numeric panel data at time
푡is processed through convolutional layers before being passed into the Transformer - a slight
variation of the embedding approach of the original Transformer. They achieve better perfor-
mance on short prediction intervals than long, achieving an AUC-score of 0.863 on a 3-month
horizon versus 0.833 for a 2-year interval.

Data
In this chapter, we describe the data which is used to study the life trajectories of Danish com-

than 400,000 companies in the period between 2013-2023. The data is nationwide and fetched
from the Central Business Register provided by the Danish Business Authority. We provide an
overview of tendencies in the data through an exploratory analysis, specifically investigating
firm dynamics. We describe our four chosen prediction tasks based on this analysis and previ-
ous literature review. We then explain how the company data is transformed into a symbolic
language resulting in text-like event sequences, which may be used for modeling life sequences.
In Denmark, a central business register was created under the CVR Law of 1996. The purpose
of the Danish Central Business Register (CVR) is to provide data about the legal entities in Den-
mark. Additionally, the purpose of the register is to store standardized unit numbers for the
legal entities (Erhvervsministeriet, 2019). With its introduction, the register made it possible
to digitalize information regarding companies in Denmark. Today, companies must use digi-
tal self-service tools to register any structural changes to their company and annual financial
results, among other things. Generally, the company must use digital tools to interact with
the public sector. Since the turn of the millennium, Denmark’s governments at all levels have
coordinated efforts to lay the groundwork for a digital public sector (Agency for Digital Gov-
ernment, 2024). Over two decades later, this has resulted in uniquely dense databases with data
on individuals and businesses in Denmark. The comprehensive data register offers a rare op-
portunity to model the life trajectories of companies and understand what events characterize
a company’s life.
3.1
The Life of a Company
Similar to human life, a company’s life has a start and an end, with a range of life-events spread
throughout. We consider the founding date of a company its birth, and correspondingly, we
consider the dissolution date of a company its death. As with a human life, the path from
cradle to grave for a company varies greatly. However, the journey is characterized by different
events that we will describe in this section.
3.1.1

To found a company in Denmark, you must register your company, request a CVR number, and
select a legal form - of which several types exist. We refer to Table 3.1 for the full overview
of the currently available company types in Denmark with corresponding descriptions. These
company types can be divided into two main categories: personally owned businesses, where
the owner has full liability, and companies, where the owner has limited liability. Some of
the most commonly used company types include A/S (public limited liability company) and
ApS (private limited liability company). Previously, the company type IVS (entrepreneurial
company) was available as well and widely popular. Originally, it was designed to support
startups with lower capital requirements than a standard ApS. It was abolished in 2021, and all
companies with the form IVS were thus required to either restructure as an ApS or be dissolved.

3.1.2
Company Equity & Capital
Depending on the company type, starting capital may be required when a company is founded.
This applies to ApS, A/S, and P/S legal forms. It also used to be the case for the now phased-
out IVS type, which was specifically designed for entrepreneurs with its uniquely low capital
requirement of just DKK 1. The starting capital requirements are much higher for the remaining
company types. Specifically, for an ApS, the initial deposit must be at least 40,000 kr., and for
A/S and P/S, the deposit must be at least 400,000 kr (Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2025). Generally, there
are no capital requirements for personally owned businesses.
The registered capital may change throughout a company’s lifetime, either through internal de-
posits or via external investments in exchange for equity. A company may also invest in other
businesses, thereby acquiring ownership in external entities. When an investment is made, it
is done at a specific rate, determining the number of shares the investment yields. We consider
changes in a company’s internal capital structure as well as its equity stakes in other businesses
as significant company life-events, as these changes often reflect important financial develop-
ments.
3.1.3
Annual Financial Reports
When a company is founded in Denmark, it is mandatory to report the cycle of its financial year.
A financial year can either follow a calendar year from January to December or be offset, e.g.,

May to April. All companies, except smaller, personally owned businesses, must provide yearly
financial reports1. The financial reporting requirements are determined based on the company
type. This means that from the time a company is founded, its financial year and reporting

There are four different reporting classes: A, B, C, and D. The requirements become stricter, and
the level of financial detail required increases as a company moves up to a higher reporting class.
Businesses in reporting class A, which typically include smaller businesses, are not obliged to
follow the legal requirements stated in the Danish law governing annual audit reports, but they
can choose to do so. Reporting class B is further divided into micro-businesses and other small
businesses. Class C includes medium-sized and large companies, while class D is reserved for
publicly listed companies (Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2024a). A company’s placement in a reporting
class may change as it depends on its yearly net revenue, balance sheet total, and number of
full-time employees assessed on consecutive years.
Companies use the XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language) or IFRS (International Fi-
nancial Reporting Standards) taxonomy to report their financial numbers. Additionally, a Denmark-
specific taxonomy called ÅRL (Årsregnskabsloven) is meant for companies following the Dan-

may vary, all audit reports contain a Statement of Profit and Loss and a Balance Sheet. The
Statement of Profit and Loss shows the company’s profit or loss and provides a breakdown of
income and costs with varying levels of detail. In contrast, the Balance Sheet details the com-
pany’s assets and liabilities. The amount of financial detail varies as the detail required in a
company’s public annual audit report depends on the reporting class it belongs to.
We consider annual financial reports submitted digitally and made public in the CVR database
to be company life-events. These reports provide insight into a company’s financial health and
stability, reflecting its performance and growth over time. The report for a given financial year
is usually published within 4-6 months of the end of the financial year - the specific deadline
depends on the company’s legal form. Either way, the financial report reflects a firm’s perfor-
mance in its most recent financial year, which may not necessarily reflect its current performance
and economic state.
3.1.4
C-Suite & Board Members
Depending on the company structure, a business may have both an executive board and a board
of directors. We refer to members of the executive board as C-level executives and members of
the board of directors as board members. Regardless of size, all companies must have at least
one executive appointed. A P/S or A/S requires an executive board and a board of directors,
whereas an ApS only requires an executive board. In larger companies, executives are appointed
as part of a business strategy or when a position among the executives becomes available. While
a board member may serve indefinitely, the composition of a company’s board of directors often
changes with varying frequency for reasons similar to those affecting executives.
We consider individuals who take on the roles of executives and board members as participants
in a company. The entries and exits of these participants are recorded digitally, meaning that
the CVR database maintains a timeline for each company detailing changes in its participants.
Changes in a company’s participants are also considered life-events. We expect that the size
of a board and the individuals serving in these roles can provide insights into a company’s
trajectory. The combined experience of executives and board members may influence a firm’s
performance, as these individuals make budget decisions, business strategy, and operational
planning decisions.

3.1.6
Production Units
For a company to operate in Denmark, it must have a production unit (p-unit) - an entity that
describes where the company performs its activities. Some businesses may operate in multi-
ple locations, necessitating multiple p-units. Each p-unit is linked to a specific address and an
industry code that defines its operating industry, which may differ from that of its parent com-
pany. Additionally, every p-unit has a start date and, if closed, an end date as well. We consider
the establishment and closure of p-units company life-events as they signify either expansion
or contraction in its operations.
3.1.7
Company Status & Dissolution
A company maintains a registered status in the CVR database throughout its life. When ”alive,”
a company may have the status ”active” or ”normal.” Additionally, it may have a status indi-
cating financial distress or an impending voluntary dissolution. Specifically, a company can be
”alive” yet have one of the following temporary statuses: 1) in resumption, 2) in voluntary dis-
solution, 3) in reconstruction, 4) in forced dissolution, or 5) in bankruptcy. A company enters
resumption if more assets are discovered after it has been dissolved and liquidated. Recon-
struction occurs when a company on the verge of bankruptcy seeks to avoid dissolution by at-
tempting to resolve its financial issues. A court can forcibly dissolve a company if it has failed to
meet legal requirements. Lastly, if a company is insolvent, it will enter bankruptcy proceedings,
which can be initiated by itself or a creditor.
Some of these statuses are only temporary. For example, if a company successfully completes
reconstruction, it may return to a normal status, i.e., ”active” or ”normal”. Every status change
is logged in the CVR database, allowing for the tracking of financial distress and changes in a

3.2
Data Collection & Processing
The foundation for this project is data extracted from the Danish Central Business Register,
which is made available upon request by the Danish Business Authority (Erhvervsstyrelsen).
The register is administered by the Danish Business Authorities in collaboration with Statistics
Denmark (Erhvervsministeriet, 2019). In this section, we will explain in detail what data we
extract from the CVR database as well as the criteria we use for our data collection.
3.2.1
Data Criteria
For this project, we collect data exclusively for companies from 2013 to 2023. The Danish Busi-
ness Authority did not require companies to submit financial reports in a digital format before
2012, and there was a grace period allowing companies time to adapt to these new requirements,
extending into 2013 (Erhvervsministeriet, 2024). As a result, the majority of companies did not
consistently deliver reports in an accessible XBRL format until 2013. Thus, we select 2013 as the
starting year for data collection. We choose 2023 as the final year as it is the most recent year
with finalized data.
Additionally, we limit the data used for this project based on two criteria:
1. The company has two or more financial reports published during the period from 2013 to
2023.
2. The company has operated as an A/S, ApS, or IVS at some point in its lifetime.
We focus on companies that exhibit life-events within the selected period. To ensure this, we
require companies to have been operational across multiple years, which we anticipate will
increase the likelihood of capturing life-events. Therefore, we include only companies with at
least two years of financial reports, as this indicates at least two years of active operation.
Moreover, we aim to study companies with enough recorded life-events to enable meaningful
analysis. For this reason, we limit our dataset to companies with specific forms: A/S, ApS, or
IVS. These represent the most common company types with operational activities, each likely to
have registered activities in the CVR database. We include IVS due to its popularity prior to its
abolishment in 2021, after which many IVS companies restructured as ApS or were dissolved.
Under these criteria, we allow firms to ”enter” and ”exit” during the period between 2013-2023,
i.e., we do not require firms to be born prior to the period or remain alive until a cutoff date.
This method is different from life2vec, which necessitates that individuals be alive and of a
specific age at the commencement of their data period, in addition to remaining alive for a set
number of years (Savcisens, 2023a). We chose these more flexible criteria, where companies may

3.3
Analysis of Firm Dynamics
We have now collected data that describes different events in a company’s life. Before we can
construct the symbolic language that will represent a company life, we want to better under-
stand the importance of different events, as well as discover any underlying patterns in the data.
In order to do so, we complete an exploratory data analysis of a firm’s typical life course. Specif-
ically, we focus on understanding patterns in bankruptcy and the timing of bankruptcy, as this
is the natural end for all companies. Furthermore, to understand other firm dynamics, we will
also investigate performance and growth proxies for firms.
3.3.1
Bankruptcy Dynamics
In order to investigate the dynamics of bankruptcy, we must first start by determining what
constitutes bankruptcy in our framework. As previously discussed, there are several different
ways to reach company closure, some voluntary and some involuntary. For bankruptcy predic-
tions, we focus exclusively on involuntary closures, as these are typically not intentional and are
presumed to be preventable through timely warnings and sound decision-making. A firm will
have reached an involuntary closure when gaining the status ”forced dissolution” or ”dissolved
following bankruptcy”. However, as there are intermediate statuses describing temporary pro-
cesses, e.g., ”in bankruptcy”, we anticipate that our true bankruptcy target might occur prior to
the two final involuntary closure statuses.

3.4
Symbolic Language Engineering
In this section, we aim to define the sentences that a company’s life story comprises - in essence,
defining a symbolic language consisting of pre-determined sentence structures and an accom-
panying vocabulary. This is done by creating a system of symbols that represent the key events,
processes, or states within a company. These symbols do not rely on spoken sounds or pho-
netic rules; instead, each symbol is assigned a specific meaning tied to an event or concept. The
language engineering process enables us to map our event data to a format that is suitable for
Transformers to model the life sequences of companies.

3.4.1
Language Considerations
The process of creating a symbolic language lies in defining the key components of the lan-
guage. Akin to a human language, our symbolic language consists of morphemes, the smallest
unit of language, and a syntax to bind these units into meaningful sentences (LibreTexts, 2023).
Whereas a human language like English might combine morphemes to adjust or create new
meanings of words, i.e. the core morpheme ”dance” can adjust its tense to ”danced” or ”dances”,
the symbolic language relies on a set of immutable morphemes, which we will refer to as to-
kens. In terms of syntax, we will not introduce extensive syntactic rules but rather define a
specific sentence structure for the relevant life-events that we wish to include. In other words,
we will not allow the odd combination of life-events; thus, each life-event is presented in se-
quence based on the date of the events. We will not use tokens in the language itself to express
date information; instead, we will utilize that the temporal aspect can be efficiently encoded in
the Transformer model. A detailed explanation of this is provided in the Methodology section.
Sentence Structure
In many ways, the symbolic language is similar to that of a small child, who only needs to ex-
press ”Daddy, water!” in order to convey what a very elaborate adult might express as ”I am
dying of thirst, could you please pass me the water, Dad”. In a similar way, our symbolic sentences
convey information in a compressed manner. Using the example, if we were to define a stan-
dard symbolic sentence for the action of asking for a beverage from a given person, it could be
expressed as:
[PERSON][DRINK]
where PERSON and DRINK are placeholders for a limited set of tokens. Now, every time we want
to express such a sentence in our symbolic language, we rely on this fixed sentence structure.
Expressing that a given person wants to be handed water from their dad or a soda from their
mom is as simple as:
[DAD][WATER] or [MOM][SODA].3
The hope is that many simple sentences describing company life-events in sequence are enough
to capture the complexity of a company’s life trajectory.
Vocabulary
Naturally, the vocabulary determines how much information our language can convey, and a
larger vocabulary generally enables more expressive sentences. In a symbolic language like
ours, we can think about the expressiveness of a vocabulary in two ways. Firstly, a vocabu-
lary can be expressive in terms of the amount of token categories it contains. An example of a
token category is PERSON - without this token category, we would not be able to express who
we specifically are asking for a drink in our generic sentence, but merely that we are asking.
The token categories help to control the amount of information types presented in a sentence.
Secondly, a vocabulary can be expressive in terms of the number of available tokens within the
categories, as this controls the granularity of information. Increasing the granularity within a
category comes with a key consequence: Tokens become less frequent as the distinctions grow
finer. As a result, determining the words in our vocabulary has an inherent trade-off: We want
to convey as detailed sentences as possible while ensuring that the tokens in our vocabulary are
used frequently enough for their meaning to actually be captured by the model. Thus, token
categories that are present in highly frequent sentences are allowed to be more expressive if
needed than a category that only occurs in an infrequent sentence.

The process of defining our vocabulary is thus a somewhat handheld process where we have to
decide which information we deem relevant and if certain information is better conveyed in a
less specific manner. Typically, these decisions involve determining whether a list of possible to-
kens within a category can be consolidated into a smaller set of groups. If the [PERSON][DRINK]
sentence is sparsely represented and we generally ask for a beverage from either a parent ([MOM]
or [DAD]) or a sibling ([BROTHER] or [SISTER]), we can choose to reduce the available tokens
within the [PERSON]-category to [PARENT] and [SIBLING]. Thereby creating a token category
with higher token frequencies.
Continuous Variables
Consider the case where we ask a person for a drink and specify what amount we want in [mL].
How should this continuous variable be represented in our vocabulary? Creating a token list of
all possible amounts between 0 and infinity is hardly a viable solution if we want sufficient token
frequencies. Instead, we turn to discretizing the variable by binning it into quantiles. Thus, we
drastically decrease the number of tokens while ensuring sufficient coverage of all tokens. A
person-drink sentence modified to specify a ’normal’ (50th percentile) volume of water could
look like this:
[DAD][WATER][VOLUME-50TH].
Having introduced our general approach to designing a symbolic language, the rest of this
section serves to explicitly define the sentences needed to express the complexity of a company
life. This involves looking at the collected company data from the tables presented in Figure 3.4
to decide:
• Event-level: Which life-events are meaningful to express as sentences?
• Sentence-level: Which token categories do we need to express these events?
• Token-level: How granular should the tokens within these categories be?
Once the sentences are defined, we will present an overview of the structure and distribution
of the full symbolic language, outlining the sentence types, the token categories used, and their
respective frequencies.
3.4.2
Sentence Definitions
Employee Sentence
Changes in recruitment and termination of employees often signal shifts in a company’s growth
and direction. Sudden changes, like a hiring spree, might suggest rapid expansion or new
opportunities, while layoffs could indicate restructuring or financial pressures. Beyond these
shifts, the size of a company’s workforce itself arguably reveals much about its scale and market
presence, as we saw in our analysis of firm dynamics (Figure 3.10). As a result, employee counts
are an essential part of the story of a company.
Employee count registrations are some of the most frequent in the CVR database, which al-
lows us to create expressive sentences with several token categories. In the life2vec approach,
where the most frequent sentence is the monthly ’income’ event, the sentence captures salary
and other attributes such as job function, geographical area, and industry. Inspired by this ap-
proach, we apply a similar methodology of displaying related attributes within the monthly
recurring employee counts. Specifically, we allow for attributes regarding a company’s cur-
rent status, type, main industry, and municipality of its primary address. The values of the
attributes are found by looking up their latest value at the time of employee registration. As
such, our general sentence structure can be written as:
[COMPANY-TYPE][INDUSTRY][COMPANY-STATUS][MUNICIPALITY][EMPLOYEE-COUNT]

Annual Report Sentence

report offers a more detailed, albeit delayed, yearly reflection of the state of a company. Choos-
ing which financial figures to include in the sentences is a decision with many trade-offs. Many
financial metrics are highly correlated or are sub-components of others. Additionally, not all
companies are required to disclose the same information due to variations in their audit classi-
fications, as discussed previously. Therefore, we need to be mindful of avoiding financial fig-
ures that might be inconsistently reported across companies. For example, while a company’s
short-term debt to banks is an interesting figure, it is rarely reported and often accounted for
within the broader liabilities equation. In addition, we have to strike a balance between the in-
formation conveyed and the vocabulary size. Since financial variables are continuous, adding a
single token category can yield a hundred new tokens in the vocabulary if the variable is binned

Status Sentence
The employee and annual report sentences lack a key feature in terms of company status reg-
istrations. To truly capture the life trajectory of a company, we have to also represent times
of hardship. However, if a company goes bankrupt or is temporarily under dissolution, they
often refrain from reporting employee counts or publishing annual reports, which means that
the company status in this period of time will not be encapsulated. In other words, this would
merely be a gap in the company’s life sequence. Furthermore, should a company be dissolved
for some reason, the final status of ”dissolved due to X” will not be portrayed in its life sequence.
As a result, it is necessary that we have a separate sentence to indicate status changes:
[COMPANY-STATUS]
This short sentence contains only the new company status. This company status will then later
be reflected in the sentences for employee counts and annual reports. While the company sta-
tus token in the employee and annual report sentences now might appear redundant, it does
serve a purpose. The repetition in both sentences enhances token frequencies and prevents the
model from relying on a company status token that might have been introduced far back in the
sequence.
Leadership Sentence
As highlighted in the literature review, the success or demise of a firm can be influenced by its
board of directors and C-suite. The management group is thus an important variable to include
in our company life-events. However, reducing the management group to a sentence proves to
be a less trivial task when seeking to preserve as much information as possible.
Encapsulating the uniqueness of each board can, in theory, be done by dedicating a token to
each unique professional in Denmark. However, as already argued, it is paramount that we
have sufficient token frequencies if the approach is to work. Given that business professionals
are never a part of thousands of management groups, we are left to rely on more generic descrip-
tions of the management. Instead, we opt to refer to each board member merely by their title.
By doing so, we can express generic expressions about the firm’s current CEO, board members,
chairman, or directors.
We hypothesize that the management group has two important traits relevant to the company’s
life trajectory. Firstly, changes in management can be a valuable signal of the state and outlook
of the company. There are numerous examples of CEOs and board members being replaced
after a period of poor company performance or when the company has decided to pursue a
new strategic direction. This temporal aspect should be reflected in the sentence structure. An
approach could rely on a sentence that expresses when an individual enters and/or exits the
group. However, it quickly becomes difficult to keep track of the current members when the
primary descriptor of the person leaving is their title. We instead opt for a different approach
by having a yearly statement showing the current board members with an according experience
measure. This is a list of variable lengths depending on the number of people in the manage-
ment:
[LEADER-TYPE][EXPERIENCE] x each member of management

original method failed to reflect changes when a similarly experienced individual replaced an-
other clearly. Therefore, experience is now calculated as the number of years an individual has
held a particular management role in a company, with new members having zero experience.
Equity Stakes Sentence
Investing in equity stakes is crucial for many companies, especially investment or holding firms,
whose business models rely on acquiring and managing ownership in other companies to maxi-
mize returns. External investments serve strategic purposes for others, like expanding into new
markets or funding innovation. Conversely, receiving an equity investment can reflect external
confidence in the company’s trajectory, like signaling growth potential or market promise. It
provides capital to scale, fund R&D, or enter competitive markets. To capture the dynamics of
investments and firm ownership, we define the following sentence:
[OWNER-TYPE][SHARE][INDUSTRY][EMPLOYEE-COUNT]
where
• [OWNER-TYPE] is either internal or external - signaling whether the company is investing
externally or receiving investments in their internal ownership group.
• [SHARE] is the equity stake acquired - discretized to either 0-10%, 10-20%, ..., 90-100%.
• [INDUSTRY] is the industry of the firm acquiring the equity stake or the industry of the
firm being acquired (depending on internal/external owner type).
• [EMPLOYEE-COUNT] is a binned variable with 100 bins showing the latest employee count
of the firm acquiring the equity stake or of the firm being acquired (depending on inter-
nal/external owner type).
• [ASSETS] is a binned variable with 100 bins showing the latest Assets financial figure of the
firm acquiring the equity stake or of the firm being acquired (depending on internal/ex-
ternal owner type).
This sentence is thus twofold in what it is capturing, with the point of view determined by the
[OWNER-TYPE] token. Given a company, the sentence can describe outside firms entering the
company’s ownership group (internal ownership), or it can describe the company’s outside in-
vestments in firms (external ownership). A given company can thus be either an investor or
an investee. Naturally, we would like to encode as much relevant information about the in-
vestor/investee as possible. We choose to include three attributes about the investor/investee:
Employee count, industry code and size of assets. When a company is the investee, this in-
formation describes the external investor and vice versa. As such, a context is provided about
either who has invested in you or who you are investing in.

The Firm Dynamics Prediction Tasks
In our literature review, we found that bankruptcy is a well-researched prediction task within
the field of corporate predictions. Other aspects of firm dynamics, such as startup success, com-
pany growth, and employee turnover, have also accumulated interest in research on corporate
predictions. However, we observed that the comparative literature on bankruptcy predictions
far exceeds that of any other prediction task. In our project, we aim to model bankruptcy as well
as firm growth and performance, for which we will use three proxies: changes in the number
of employees, address changes, and raised capital. The first two can serve as proxies for firm
growth or decline, while the third can indicate firm growth but may also reflect startup success
specifically.
For the prediction tasks, we establish a cohort of companies for which we aim to predict whether
a given event occurs during the period from January 1, 2022, to December 31, 2023 based on the
2013-2021 symbolic sequence event data. We select a two-year prediction window to account
for the longer timeframes required by certain prediction tasks, such as bankruptcy which often
involves gradual financial decline. At the same time, we aim to expose the model to as much
of the life sequences as possible prior to making predictions. These cohorts are a subset of the
”full dataset”, filtered according to criteria specific to each prediction task.
We focus on four distinct prediction tasks collectively referred to as firm dynamics: (1) Bankruptcy,
(2) Capital Increase, (3) Relocation, and (4) FTE Development. For each task, we define a corre-
sponding sub-population, which will be described in detail below.

• Must be registered with company status normal or active as per 1st of January 2022.

Methodology
The previous chapter emphasizes that companies are shaped by more than just their latest at-
tributes; their current state is the result of a series of individual events, which can be described
as a life sequence. Additionally, exploring these life sequences reveals that companies’ progress is
not entirely random - there are general patterns that they tend to follow. The sequential nature
of life events makes them particularly interesting to model using language modeling techniques.
This is why we formalized a symbolic language to describe the life-events of a company in the
previous chapter. This methodology section aims to develop a model to learn from these se-
quences. Ultimately, if a model can build an understanding of our symbolic language, it will,
in turn, understand the patterns of company lives.
Thus, this section presents our approach to developing the company2vec model - detailing its
design, implementation, and evaluation on downstream prediction tasks. The company2vec
methodology replicates the life2vec manuscript presented in Savcisens (2023a). The general
purpose of the methodology is to pre-train a model that achieves a foundational understanding
of its domain, which in the case of life2vec is human lives. Thereafter, the foundational model
may be fine-tuned for specific prediction tasks within the domain, which in our case is the four
firm dynamics tasks. To assist the reader, we offer a brief overview of the purpose of each of the
sections in this chapter:
1. Natural Language Processing: This section introduces the foundational concepts of natu-
ral language processing that company2vec relies on, including the representation of words
as vectors and the advancements in model architectures such as RNNs, Transformers, and
BERT. These topics form the theoretical basis of the life2vec architecture and, by exten-
sion, company2vec.
2. Model Architecture: Here, we describe the architecture of the company2vec model, guid-
ing the reader through its embedding layer and encoders.
3. Pre-training company2vec: This section serves two purposes: (1) to expand on the model
architecture by detailing task-specific decoders and (2) to explain the pre-training process
in detail.
4. Fine-tuning company2vec: This section explains the company-summary decoder used to
generate predictions. It also outlines the fine-tuning process for company2vec, introduces
baseline models, and discusses the evaluation metrics employed.

Transformers
With the introduction of the Transformer in the seminal paper ”Attention is all you need” by
Vaswani et al. (2023), new standards have been reached within language modeling. As outlined
in Section 2.2, while the architecture originates in NLP, it has been widely adopted in various
domains with excellent results. We will introduce the theory behind the Transformer architec-
ture here to provide a foundation for understanding its application.
A core idea of the Transformer is to build contextual representations of the tokens in the input
sequence by enhancing the representation of each token, layer for layer (Jurafsky and Martin,
2024). With static word embeddings such as word2vec or GloVe, it quickly becomes difficult to
understand ambiguous contexts. Consider the sentence:
The company did not invest in the startup because it was not mature.

4.1.4
BERT: The Paradigm of Pre-training & Fine-tuning
Bidirectional Encoder Representations for Transformers (BERT) is a Transformer-based method for
achieving language understanding, as introduced by Devlin et al. (2019). It features a bidi-
rectional3 architecture composed of Transformer encoders and employs a pre-training approach
that includes two tasks focused on sentence-level understanding. This is followed by an application-
specific fine-tuning phase. Similarly, life2vec is a BERT-like model inspired by both the archi-
tecture and training methodology described by Devlin et al. (2019). In this context, we will
outline the relevant methods from the paper.
Pre-training
Prior research had relied on unidirectional language representations in the pre-training phase,
potentially harming any downstream applications that may require sentence-level context. As
a result, a bidirectional pre-training phase was introduced, which simultaneously trains on two
tasks: Masked Language Modelling and Next Sentence Prediction.
In Masked Language Modelling, tokens are replaced at random with the special [MASK] token,
which hides the original token at the given position. Thus, language understanding is achieved
by minimizing the loss of the classification task of predicting which tokens are behind the masks.
The company hired a new employee →The company [MASK] a new employee
In the original implementation, 15 % of tokens are masked. However, to alleviate that the
[MASK] token will not be present in any fine-tuning applications, 80 % of the masked indexes
are replaced with [MASK] and the remaining are either left unchanged (10%) or substituted
with a random token (10%). This decreases the difference between data distributions of the
pre-training and fine-tuning steps.
In Next Sentence Prediction, the model is presented with a range of sentence pairs and is tasked
with predicting whether the second sentence is a continuation of the first. This trains the model
in learning sentence relationships. Thus, 50% of sentence pairs are the actual continuation from
the corpus, and the rest are random corpus passages. For the model to be able to discern the
sentences in the sentence pair, a learnable segment embedding is introduced (either A or B),
and the sentences are interleaved by the separator token [SEP]. The [CLS] token marks the
beginning of each input sequence and is used as the aggregated sequence representation for
downstream classification tasks (Devlin et al., 2019).
The two pre-training objectives are done at once, yielding training examples on the form:
[CLS] The company [MASK] a new employee [SEP]. The [MASK] was soon promoted
The objective is twofold: the model must predict the likelihood of each token in the vocabulary
filling the mask and determine the likelihood that the second sentence continues the first. The
training loss is calculated as the sum of the mean likelihoods for these tasks.
Fine-tuning
In the fine-tuning stage, the model is initialized with the final weights of the pre-trained model,
and the decoder is substituted with a task-specific decoder, typically consisting of several feed-
forward layers. The objective is to adjust the pre-trained weights and the new decoder head so
that the model performs well on the task. Devlin et al. (2019) find that pre-training for language
understanding followed by a simple decoder architecture performs better than with no pre-
training or task-dependent output layers.

The model is trained for 30 epochs, with each epoch comprising 3,000 batches, where each batch
contains 8 life sequences. Gradient accumulation is employed, updating model parameters after
every 8 batches. This approach effectively results in a batch size of 64. During the pre-training
phase, the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) is utilized, with the learning rate and
momentum adjusted according to a single cycle of cosine annealing. For a detailed visualization
of the learning rate and momentum variations across epochs, we refer the reader to Savcisens
(2023a).
Dropout layers are applied after the embedding matrix, as well as in the attention, position-wise,
and decoder layers, all with a dropout rate of 10%. In addition, we apply data augmentation
techniques to introduce variability into the training data, helping the model generalize more
effectively and learn robust representations (Savcisens et al., 2023). We perform the following
augmentations:
• Shuffling: Shuffle the tokens within each sentence to prevent the model from relying on
their original order, ensuring it learns the semantic relationships between tokens rather
than overfitting to positional patterns.
• Event Removal: With probability 푝= 0.1 we remove between 25 % and 50 % of the events
in the life sequence.
• Token Removal: For each event, we drop a single random token from the sentence with
probability 푝= 0.01.
• Temporal Noise: With probability 푝= 0.1 we add noise to the life sequence by adding
between [−5, 5] days to the absolute position of each event. The noise is drawn from a
uniform distribution and is separate for each event in the chosen sequences. With noise
on the temporal dimension, the model relies less on that specific events might occur on
specific days, e.g., employee counts being disclosed as per the first day of each month.
The original paper employs a significantly higher event removal probability of 0.4. In our case,
this probability has been reduced to account for the smaller size of our dataset. Augmentations
are applied exclusively to life sequences containing more than 12 event sentences. This ensures
that companies with shorter life spans - such as those existing for less than a year, assuming
monthly event registrations - are not made even shorter.

4.4.2
Fine-tuning Procedure
We focus on building a model that relies on the learned representations from the pretraining
stage. To ensure company2vec makes effective use of these representations, we implement two
key measures.
First, we freeze the concept token embedding matrix, preventing any updates to the initial con-
cept token representations. This ensures that the foundational learned representations remain
intact. However, we allow adjustments to the weighting among the different embedding di-
mensions, including segment, concept tokens, age, and absolute position, enabling the model
to refine its understanding of the relationships between these features.
Second, we employ a gradual reduction in the learning rate across the encoders, starting from
the topmost encoder and decreasing as we move back toward the embedding matrix. The closer
an encoder is to the embedding matrix, the lower its learning rate. This progressive adjustment
reduces the model’s ability to modify the learned token representations while still allowing
some flexibility in the higher layers. Specifically, the learning rate is scaled down by a factor
of 훾= 0.95 for each successive encoder layer. The global learning rate decreases exponentially
with a factor of 0.8 after every epoch with an initial learning rate of 0.005. We use consistent
hyperparameters for company2vec across all four prediction tasks without tuning them. Specifi-
cally, we set the weight decay to 0.001 and the decoder weight decay to 0.01. We apply a dropout
rate of 5 % to the attention layers, positionwise layers, decoder layers, and embedding layers.
We also use early stopping, which further aids against overfitting. With early stopping, the
model runs at least 6 epochs and at most 30, with the training loop terminating if the optimiza-
tion metric does not improve 5 epochs in a row. We use the area under the ROC curve7 as
the optimization metric to determine when to terminate training. In the fine-tuning setting, an
epoch consists of 1200 batches with 8 samples in each batch.

4.4.3
Baselines
In order to compare the performance of our company2vec model, we choose four different base-
line models of varying complexity and with varying temporal understanding. We use Logistic
Regression, a simple Feed Forward Neural Network (FFNN), and an RNN-GRU model across
all four prediction tasks, as these models were all used in life2vec (Savcisens, 2023a). In ad-
dition, we use a simple ”dummy” baseline, which differs slightly depending on the prediction
task:
1. Logistic Regression is trained on a Bag-of-Words representation of the company life se-
quence.
2. FFNN is trained on the Bag-of-Words representation of the company life sequence.
3. RNN-GRU network takes an input identical to that of company2vec. The architecture of
its decoder is also the same as that of company2vec.
4. SALY (same-as-last-year) is used as a dummy for relocation, capital changes, and employee-
level predictions. For all three, the dummy predicts the observation from the year prior
to the test period (2021).
5. Ratio is used as a dummy for bankruptcy prediction and predicts bankruptcy based on a
financial ratio.
Only the RNN-GRU model takes the input data in exactly the same format as company2vec.
The two other trainable baselines use a Bag-of-Words (BoW) representation of the company life
sequence. BoW is a specific method of creating a numerical representation of a document by
counting words (Jurafsky & Martin, 2024). This is a way of creating a very simple ”embedding”
of a document. Our symbolic language is thus transformed into a BoW and used for the models
without temporal understanding. Although the representation is simplified, this method allows
us to use the same data foundation for all three baselines and company2vec.
The trainable baselines follow the same epoch, early stopping, and task imbalance strategy as
the company2vec model. The FFNN has four tunable parameters: hidden size, number of lay-
ers, dropout rate, and learning rate. The RNN-GRU network also has four tunable parameters:
hidden size, number of layers, dropout rate, and bi-directionality (binary). All baselines are
hyperparameter tuned, which is added as supplementary material in Appendix H.
Our SALY baseline is very simple - it predicts the same occurrence as what was observed in the
data the previous year. This means that a company is predicted to experience a capital change
or a relocation if it did so in the year prior to our test period, which is 2021. For employee-level
development, a company is predicted to have stability, an increase, or a decrease in its employee

Results
Now that we have established the life2vec approach, and by extension company2vec, we ar-
rive at a critical point in the thesis. We have developed a sophisticated Transformer-based model
whose training approach we know to be effective in modeling human life trajectories as a sym-
bolic language. We have processed an extensive dataset containing millions of event registra-
tions for several hundred thousand companies and mapped these to a symbolic language fit to
describe these registrations as life-events. The question now remains: Can a model be trained
to capture the complexity of company trajectories through symbolic language, and moreover,
can this understanding be used to predict company outcomes?
In this chapter, we present the results from applying the life2vec approach, as detailed in the
Methodology chapter, to the symbolic company event language introduced in the Data chap-
ter. First, we present the results of the pre-training phase, where a thorough hyperparameter
search leads to the final architecture of the company2vec model. We evaluate the model’s abil-
ity to capture the complexity of the symbolic language through qualitative inspections of the
learned representations and, furthermore, assess their robustness. Later, we present the results
of the fine-tuning phase. Here, we assess the model’s ability to utilize the patterns of the life
trajectories to predict company outcomes in a suite of tasks, collectively termed firm dynamics.
5.1
Pre-training of company2vec
Here, we present the results of the pre-training stage of the company2vec model. In the pre-
training phase, we use our full dataset from the period 2013-2021. This learning set is randomly
split into training (70%), validation (15%), and test (15%) sets. We conduct extensive hyperpa-
rameter tuning to identify the model configuration that achieves optimal performance on the
data. Following this, we analyze the learned representations by applying dimensionality re-
duction techniques on the resulting embedding space to assess whether tokens are arranged
meaningfully and capture any underlying structures of the data. Additionally, we evaluate the
robustness of these representations across various model configurations, initializations, and
data splits.
5.1.1
Model Selection
As previously presented in Table 4.1, the model architecture is flexible with respect to seven
different parameters. We cannot assume that the final life2vec-configuration is appropriate
for our data, so we perform a hyperparameter search to find the configuration that achieves the
best language understanding on our data - as measured on the language modeling tasks MLM
and SOP. We aim to explore a wide range of configurations; however, running a single configu-
ration for 30 epochs takes several hours on a GPU. To save computation time while thoroughly
exploring the configuration space, a hyperparameter search is performed in two stages. First,
an initial search is conducted, testing an extensive list of configurations but limiting the train-
ing duration to 10 epochs per configuration. The tested configurations and their corresponding
losses are presented in Table 5.1. The configuration parameters are selected using Bayesian op-

space with the exploitation of promising regions based on prior results. To evaluate the perfor-
mance of the architecture derived from the life2vec model, we include ID 0, representing the
final configuration used in that model. Additionally, eight configurations are randomly sam-

configurations for subsequent runs.

5.1.3
Robustness Test of the Embedding Space
We have established that the embedding space exhibits semantic qualities and that these quali-
ties are consistent across different projection methods. However, an important question arises:
if the model was trained again with slight changes, would we arrive at similar projections? Is
the observed projection a preferred semantic subspace influenced by the model architecture, or
is it an inherent result of the symbolic company life trajectories in our corpus? Specifically, we
aim to assess whether the embedding space is robust to changes in parameter initialization and
variations in data subsets - a robustness measured by the similarity of the resulting embedding
spaces. To do so, one must determine how to measure the similarity of embedding spaces, es-
pecially considering that there are no guarantees that projections align at the same coordinates.
The life2vec project addresses this issue by proposing that similar embedding spaces can be
identified through similar distance matrices. To this end, the authors employ a matrix permu-
tation test as outlined in Algorithm 1 to validate whether two distance matrices are correlated
(Savcisens, 2023a). This permutation test is applied to models trained on various subsets of the
original data.2

5.2
Fine-tuning of company2vec
We have demonstrated that the company2vec model effectively captures the complexity of the
symbolic language, producing an embedding space that is both semantically meaningful and
robust to variations in data subsets, weight initializations, and model configurations. This raises
a critical question: If the learned representations truly encapsulate the life trajectories of com-
panies, can they be leveraged to predict these trajectories? In this section, we aim to address
this question by fine-tuning the company2vec model on several firm dynamics prediction tasks
and benchmarking its performance against a range of hyperparameter-tuned baselines3.
For all tasks, the models are trained using company life sequences from 2013 to the end of 2021.
The goal is to predict how certain company characteristics will develop during the following
two-year period, from January 1, 2022, to December 31, 2023. The data for the prediction sub-
populations is filtered according to the criteria outlined in Section 3.5. Across the various pre-
diction tasks, the data is divided into three sets randomly: training (70%), validation (15%), and
test (15%). For each prediction task, we evaluate on the test set and report a series of metrics
as outlined in the Methodology section. We compute a 95% confidence interval using a strat-
ified bootstrapping4 of 1000 samples. Furthermore, we investigate the performance in certain
subsets of the data.

6
Discussion
In the Results chapter, we demonstrate that our comprehensive data and modeling setup pro-
duces results showing that the company2vec model effectively captures the complexities of com-
pany dynamics and does so more effectively than comparable models in predictive contexts.
However, the model excels in specific applications and less so in others. Why might this be?
Are some of our specified firm dynamics tasks inherently more difficult than others, or has the
model only captured specific patterns of the life trajectories? Furthermore, the results leave us
in an interesting position to discuss the general applicability of the life2vec approach. What
does it tell us that the approach can be successfully applied in other ”life-trajectory” domains?
Are human and company lives more similar than one might expect, or is this only the case
through the eyes of the Transformer? We will discuss the difference in the results of the two
methodologies and, thus, the difference in the two domains. Lastly, we examine what we could
have done differently and how our choices have affected our obtained results.
6.1
Model Performance
Our results showed that the company2vec model produces an embedding space with token cate-
gories well-separated and ordered meaningfully. Moreover, we found that the clusters of tokens
have sensible neighbors - for instance, industry codes with similar business models. Addition-
ally, in our test of the robustness of the embedding space, we found that different model config-
urations and data subsets resulted in similar embedding spaces. However, while company2vec
achieved superior performance in the firm dynamics predictions, achieving the highest AUC-
score across all tasks, the margins to the baselines varied a lot from task to task - with some
being negligible. This brings us to a discussion on what factors might contribute to the varying
levels of difficulty across tasks and why the company2vec approach performs better in certain
cases.
6.1.1
Assessing the Performance of company2vec
In our review of the results, we observed varying performance across the firm dynamics pre-
diction tasks. Specifically, the predictions for firm relocation and employee-level development
were particularly challenging for all models. While company2vec outperformed the baselines,
the overall task difficulty was evident, as AUC-values hovered around 0.6, which is only slightly
better than a random classifier (AUC of 0.5). Conversely, our bankruptcy and capital increase
predictions were handled more effectively by both company2vec and the baseline models. These
two tasks also resulted in the largest margins from company2vec to its nearest baselines if mea-
sured by AUC. If we inspect the MCC-values, the increase in mean MCC from the nearest base-
line to company2vec is 10.97% and 6.45% for the bankruptcy and capital increase tasks, respec-
tively. Thus, the bankruptcy task seems particularly well-suited to the company2vec model,
outperforming the baselines with no overlap in the bootstrapped confidence intervals.
We argue that the patterns indicating whether a company is facing imminent closure may be
more clearly reflected in our data than the patterns associated with our other prediction tasks.
In the case of traditional bankruptcy, it is often the result of financial problems that develop
over a longer period, which are then evident in the financial reports. In cases of involuntary
closure due to forceful dissolution, it is typically triggered by non-compliance with specific reg-
ulations, which may be indicated by missing registrations. Given the clearer trajectory signals in
the data, we argue that the attention mechanism of the transformer model enhances the ability

of company2vec to capture these patterns effectively compared to the benchmark models. Orig-
inally, we anticipated that the bankruptcy prediction task might be somewhat challenging due
to delays in financial reporting. The model is tasked with predicting whether a company will
experience bankruptcy during 2022–2023, but the most recent financial information available to
the model comes from annual reports published in 2021. These reports reflect the fiscal year
leading up to the publication, i.e. the fiscal year of 2020/2021, meaning the financial data can
be up to a year old by the start of the prediction period. Even though company2vec outperforms
the baselines, this delay makes the bankruptcy task inherently more difficult.
However, while the company2vec model outperforms other architectures on the same dataset,
we cannot necessarily conclude that company2vec achieves state-of-the-art results on bankruptcy
prediction. This limitation arises because we have not compared it against models that utilize
different data structures or methodologies. The FFNN and Logistic Regression models, for ex-
ample, might be disadvantaged by the BoW approach, which provides them with life sequence
representations that lack temporal information. We do not know the performance of a Logistic
Regression model trained with more deliberate feature engineering based on expert knowl-
edge or statistical approaches like the survival hazard model discussed in the literature review
(Shumway, 2001). We cannot make definitive claims about relative performance without testing
such alternatives.
That said, while other models may excel at specific subsets of data (e.g., market-based meth-
ods for large corporations with extensive mandatory disclosure requirements), the sequence-
based approach of company2vec offers a distinct advantage: Its design is inherently versatile,
functioning as a one-size-fits-all method that accommodates firms of varying sizes and handles
challenges like missing data.
6.1.2
Difficulty of the Firm Relocation Task
As outlined in the relocation prediction section, the company2vec model did not significantly
outperform its counterparts. One possible explanation is that the moving prediction task is
inherently challenging due to constraints in both the available data and the definition of the
target. In a traditional office-worker context, key indicators for predicting company relocation or
expansion might include the size of the current office, associated costs, and trends in employee
numbers. The underlying hypothesis is that companies are primarily motivated to move when
their current space is either insufficient or excessive for their needs or when the lease is deemed
financially unsustainable. Such factors are a valuable context for the relocation prediction task
but may not be fully captured in our current data.
Currently, the model is not provided with explicit data on address changes. While the latest mu-
nicipality of a company’s address is provided alongside employee counts and financial data, the
model does not include specific ”address change” sentences. In theory, such sentences could
integrate attributes from the Danish BBR register1, such as the type, size, and other character-
istics of the building being moved into. Including structured data on address changes could
enhance the model’s ability to capture patterns related to company relocations.
In addition, expansion does not always equate to changing the main address. Companies may
open additional offices while retaining their primary location. However, the current target only
tracks modifications to the main address recorded in the CVR register. Opening a new office
is registered as the creation of a new production unit rather than as an address change. As
a result, any company which might show characteristics of relocating soon but instead selects
to open a new office branch will be a somewhat noisy sample in our targets. While it would
be possible to extend the target to include new production units, not all of them correspond

6.1.3
Prediction Window
We must also consider the implications of using a two-year prediction window (2022–2023) for
our firm dynamics prediction tasks. Our goal is to predict the occurrence of specific events within
this two-year period following the training period from 2013–2021. For some tasks, this window
seems appropriate. For example, in bankruptcy prediction, forecasting bankruptcy within two
years aligns with existing literature and provides practical value. Similarly, a two-year window
is reasonable for tasks like predicting capital increases and firm relocations since these events,
even if recurring, only need to be predicted once during the window.
However, the two-year window introduces challenges to predicting employee-level develop-
ment. The targets for this task (stable, increase, decrease) are calculated based on the difference
between the final FTE value reported in 2022–2023 and the last FTE value from 2021. This ap-
proach creates a potential issue: a company could exhibit an early signal for FTE growth in
2022, achieve that growth, but then experience a decline by the end of 2023, resulting in a final
FTE value lower than in 2021. In this scenario, our model might misinterpret an early growth
signal as the opposite due to the final decrease by the end of 2023. Such fluctuations within the
extended window can create confusion, as employee levels are inherently more dynamic and
subject to rapid changes. Thus, our prediction window could explain why predicting employee-
level developments appears to be particularly challenging, not only for company2vec but also
for the baseline models.
6.2
Data Foundation
In assessing our model, it is essential to examine the data foundation upon which it is built.
This involves the choices made regarding the data we include, the data we deliberately exclude,
and how we choose to structure the data. Additionally, we should consider which additional
data could further enhance the model’s performance.
6.2.1
Choices in Sentence Engineering
Our results are inherently dependent on the data we have used. The core idea behind the
sequence-based approach of company2vec/life2vec is to avoid manual feature engineering
and instead rely on the model to extract meaningful feature representations of life trajectories.
This is the strength of the approach: using a single dataset, we fine-tune the model for four
vastly different tasks - each of which would typically require constructing task-specific datasets.
However, compared to the original life2vec approach, we have had to make more deliberate
decisions about the data included in our company life sequences. At the outset, we selected a
series of sentences based on notions of importance drawn from existing research and intuition.
While this approach yielded positive results during the later modeling stages, we cannot be cer-
tain that these sentences were the right ones to include or that they were specified in the optimal
way. These decisions may have either strengthened or weakened our training and prediction
outcomes. One might raise the question: Is there a single ”correct” way to represent company
trajectories? Given the flexibility of Transformer models, informative feature representations
can probably be derived from various sentence configurations. However, it is most definitely
possible to end up with suboptimal data setups, which could negatively impact model perfor-
mance.
In our current setup, the embedding space exhibits a certain structure, with different sentence
types occupying distinct, non-overlapping regions. However, which specific sentences con-

remains unclear. If time and resources had permitted, conducting ablation studies could have
provided valuable insights into this area. By adding or removing specific sentence types or ad-
justing parameters like the number of bins for continuous variables, we might have uncovered
their impact on the model’s predictive accuracy.
6.2.2
Drawbacks of the Sequential Approach & Potential of Network Data
While sentences offer flexibility for representation, certain types of registrations are inherently
poorly suited to being expressed in sentence form. Specifically, data points that exhibit network
structure contain much information that is not explicitly used in our life trajectories. company2vec
has several of such life-events, which are more meaningful if the underlying network graph is
expressed. Here we present a few.
Understanding management networks - the graph formed between individuals who serve on
the same management teams across companies - could possibly enhance the model’s compre-
hension of company life trajectories. Based on our literature review, we have established that
company default risk may increase if a firm’s management shares connections with the manage-
ment of recently defaulted companies (Tobback et al., 2017). Additionally, it is established that
specific board governance variables contribute to better prediction performance in bankruptcy
models as well as better explanatory power (Almaskati et al., 2021). Thus, knowledge of these
networks could improve downstream bankruptcy prediction applications and contribute to a
more nuanced understanding of company trajectories. If the model knew which companies are
connected through shared management, it could further enhance the concept space, e.g. in-
dustry code embeddings - particularly if professionals tend to transition between companies in
similar industries.
Another data point that is poorly represented in the sentences is investment and ownership reg-
istrations. These relationships are best understood as a directed graph, where an edge points
from company 푥푖to company 푥푗if 푥푖owns a share of 푥푗. While the investment size itself is infor-
mative, its true significance becomes apparent when paired with contextual knowledge about
the investing entity. For example: Is the investor an established, experienced firm with many
investments? Do they exhibit a pattern of investing within specific industries? Do investments
historically lead to company growth? Moreover, the health and performance of an investor can
have direct implications for the invested company. If the investing company faces financial dif-
ficulties or fails altogether, this would likely impact the recipient company. Incorporating this
type of information about the investment network could further enhance the model’s under-
standing of the dynamics of investments.
In both the management and investment domain, we try to make the data fit a sentence structure
that may not be entirely suitable. In ownerships, we create a specific token to signify whether
the ownership sentence is related to internal or external changes in ownership stakes. In man-
agement, we compute an experience measure as the tenure at the company. These handheld
features could be avoided with more network-based data introduced.
In conclusion, the notion that success or hardship can propagate through company networks is
not far-fetched. However, the current model does not capture these intricate interactions, which
are likely critical for fully understanding the complexities of company trajectories. Incorporat-
ing such network effects could be an important avenue for future improvements. However, it is
unknown how it can be done and whether it is even possible within the company2vec-paradigm
or requires a whole different modeling architecture.

6.3
A Comparison with life2vec
As our approach replicates that of life2vec, we must discuss our results in relation to those of
Savcisens et al. (2023). Our bankruptcy prediction task achieved the largest margin over base-
lines, comparable to the survival prediction task in life2vec. However, while we have demon-
strated that the life2vec methodology can be successfully applied to corporate predictions, its
performance in this domain seems less pronounced than in predicting human life outcomes.
Why might this be?
Human vs. Corporate Variability
One perspective is that human lives exhibit less variance than corporate trajectories. Individu-
als tend to follow relatively predictable patterns - growing up, gaining education, entering the
workforce, retiring, etc. These consistent life stages might make it easier for models to detect
signals. In contrast, companies experience a wider range of outcomes and less standardized
paths, with fewer rigid sequences from A to B. In theory, companies can exist indefinitely, and
even when they do close, the reasons for closure can vary widely. This increased variability
could make corporate predictions inherently more challenging. At the same time, the variance
present in human lives may be better captured in the life2vec dataset due to its significantly
larger population. Conversely, the company2vec dataset focuses solely on specific company
types (A/S, ApS, and IVS), with a relatively large share of holding companies, which exhibit
much less activity than a traditional company with employees. Thus, the dataset’s more limited
scope, with a large share of less active trajectories, might hinder the model’s ability to capture
the patterns of company lives.
Pre-training and Embedding Space Dynamics
Another cause for the difference in performance could be the pre-trained model and, as a re-
sult, the structure of the embedding space. Our embedding space shares similarities with the
embedding space of life2vec, as it is also well-separated, providing a visual indication that
it captures and distinguishes between different company life-related concepts. However, we
must acknowledge the possibility that our embedding space could appear deceptively well-
structured due to the many numerical features, which the model effectively ranks. While some
of our categorical features seem meaningful upon inspection, they do not form clusters as dis-
tinct as those observed in life2vec. For instance, life2vec produced job embeddings that were
neatly divided into clear clusters, such as ”professionals”, ”managers”, ”technicians”, and ”el-
ementary occupations”.
The company2vec model configuration employs strictly local attention compared to the life2vec
configuration, which has a mix of global and local attention heads. While this design may be
suitable for the company domain, it appears less optimal for the SOP task in the pre-training
phase. The SOP task relies on constructing a meaningful representation of the first token (the
[CLS] token). A strict reliance on local attention restricts the model’s ability to incorporate
broader contextual information in each token, as it limits the number of tokens the model can
consider. This constraint may hinder the effectiveness of the [CLS] token’s representation in
capturing the overall context and, in turn, make the SOP task more difficult for our model to
master compared to life2vec.
External Factors
A further distinction between the two use cases lies in their sensitivity to external factors. Events
such as wars, natural disasters, pandemics, or financial crises impact both company and human
lives, but companies tend to experience these shocks more directly in economic terms. Individu-
als, particularly in a country like Denmark with a robust welfare system, are somewhat shielded
from the immediate effects. For example, while a person losing their job due to a company’s
bankruptcy may feel the ripple effects of a crisis, they are impacted due to the company’s direct
82

vulnerability to the event. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Danish companies could apply for
government support, such as subsidies to cover salaries, while operations were halted due to
the lockdown. However, the eligibility for this aid was tied to specific company requirements.
It is possible that this governmental support extended the survival of some companies beyond
what might have been expected under normal circumstances. As a result, the pandemic intro-
duces a degree of uncertainty into the data used to train our model. The years 2020 and 2021,
during which the pandemic had the most significant effects, are included in our training set.
Consequently, we cannot rule out the possibility that our model has learned biased patterns
during this period. Furthermore, the following 2-year period may be particularly difficult pre-
dict firm dynamics due to the unpredictable nature of how the economy recovers from a global
pandemic.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we have successfully developed a symbolic language to describe the life-events
of companies. Using this language, we have documented the life stories of over 400,000 Dan-
ish companies over a decade-long period. Our findings demonstrate that it is possible to train
a Transformer-based model on this symbolic language. By understanding the language, the
model effectively captures certain patterns in company life trajectories.
This capability is reflected in the model’s learned representations - the embedding space - of
the concepts used to describe company life sequences. We find that the model has developed
semantically meaningful structures for the symbolic tokens representing company life-events.
The embedding space reveals an understanding of key aspects of company data. For exam-
ple, it organizes annual report values as numeric entities arranged logically and ordered. It
also groups industries into meaningful clusters and positions municipalities near other geo-
graphic areas with similar business characteristics. Furthermore, the embedding space is ro-
bust to changes. Models trained on subsets of the data or with different configurations produce
consistent structures, indicating that the patterns identified by the model are not random but
reflect inherent regularities in the data
In addition, we show that the learned representations are, in fact, useful in predicting firm dy-
namics. The company2vec model outperforms all baseline models across the four prediction
tasks: (1) bankruptcy, (2) capital increase, (3) firm relocation, and (4) employee level. company2vec
achieves the greatest margin to the baseline models on the bankruptcy task. For the remain-
ing prediction tasks, we observe overlapping confidence intervals across evaluation metrics for
company2vec and baseline models. To that extent, we find that some firm dynamics prediction
tasks are inherently more difficult than others. In particular, we find the firm relocation and
employee-level prediction tasks difficult to solve for any model. We argue that the inherent
difficulty of certain tasks arises from multiple factors: the data available, the structure of the
sentences, and our chosen prediction window format.
Further directions for company2vec include investigating how to represent the registration data
in the best way possible. This includes network-based data, as this could potentially enrich
the model’s understanding of the dependencies among companies. Specifically, incorporating
the networks of executives as well as ownership links. Additionally, we recommend exploring
alternative sentence structures that better capture phenomena beyond bankruptcy. With more
time, we would have carried out ablation studies in order to understand how our data and
choice of sentences impact the model. Specifically, ablation studies could help clarify which
sentences are important in achieving a meaningful embedding space.
Overall, our results demonstrate that the life2vec approach can be successfully applied in the
domain of company lives. Thus, the strength of the methodology is further underlined by our
research. Just as life2vec presents alternative ways for researchers to study socioeconomic and
health challenges, company2vec provides new avenues for researching economic and corporate
phenomena.
