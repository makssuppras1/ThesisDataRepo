Abstract, Resumé
Patient motion during magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) poses an enormous issue
for diagnostic conﬁdence.
The current state of the art methods in prospective
motion correction, approach the problem of patient motion diﬀerently. The choice
of correction method depends on the type of scan. It is desirable to keep developing
new low-cost methods that can assist in reducing motion artefacts during MRI. The
general idea of prospective motion correction is to update the scanner continuously
so that the imaging volume, i.e. the ﬁeld of view (FOV), follows the head movement.
To track motion in this phantom based study, a previously validated motion
tracking method were used. The motion tracking technique utilises a spatial infor-
mation contained in gradient artefacts that are commonly considered as unwanted
noise in electroencephalography (EEG) signals recorded during simultaneous MRI.
The EEG recordings were transferred real-time from the recording software to the
computer in charge of the motion tracking and correction algorithm via a remote
data access (RDA) client. In this way, RDA enabled the real-time estimation of
motion parameters.
The estimated motion parameters were translated into the
scanner frame of reference and used to correct the scanner geometry accordingly.
The communication between the scanner and the computer that estimated the mo-
tion parameters were handled via an eXTernal Control application (XTC) and a
TCP/IP connection.
Problems were experienced with both the RDA client and XTC application
that resulted in an obstruction of completing the initial experimental procedure.
Simpliﬁed experiments were set up to test the principle of this prospective motion
correction method. Results showed that the FOV followed the moved phantom.
XTC was also shown to be able to assist this process. Possible solutions to the
minor issues that obstructed the initial method were presented to initialize further
research.
This thesis demonstrates proof of concept of a novel prospective motion correc-
tion method that uses a previously validated tracking method. It is demonstrated
how XTC can assist the correction method described in this thesis by updating the
scanner geometry, based on estimated motion parameters.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Patient motion during magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) continues to be an enor-
mous issue. It can cause image artefacts such as ghosting or blurring. Image arte-
facts can decrease the image quality to such a degree that will render the images
useless in a diagnosis. Eﬀective reduction of these artefacts is vital for improving
the diagnostic conﬁdence. Every day magnetic resonance (MR) scans are being
repeated or discarded due to patient motion corrupting the images. Time lost due
to patient motion is not only annoying to the patient and the staﬀ, but this is time
that could be spent on other patients. It is in the interest of all to make the best
of the hospitals resources.
Motion tracking and correction techniques are numerous and can help reduce
the motion artefacts. Prospective motion correction involves real-time updating of
scanner acquisition parameters, i.e. the scanner ﬁeld of view (FOV) and prevents
motion artefacts from ever appearing if performed correctly. According to [8], the
ﬁeld of prospective motion correction can be divided into three areas; navigators,
ﬁeld detection systems, and optical systems.
During, for example, functional MRI (fMRI) and simultaneous electroencephalog-
raphy (EEG) recordings, gradient artefacts corrupt the EEG signal. The study
conducted in this thesis is based on the studies in [18], where a motion tracking
method was developed using an EEG hood. The EEG hood electrodes are in pairs
short-circuited to make up a wire loop. If the patient moves or when the gradients
switch during an imaging sequence, signals are induced in the wire loops due to
Faradays law of induction. These signals that under other circumstances are con-
sidered as gradient artefacts withholds spatial information that are used to track
the motion.
In another study, it has been shown how to use EEG recordings and the spatial
information within to correct for image artefacts retrospectively due to subject
motion [22]. However, a prospective correction method has still not been developed
using EEG monitoring equipment.
All experiments in this thesis are performed on a Philips 3 Tesla Achieva scanner.
Philips provides an eXTernal Control (XTC) application that allows the user to
control the scanner externally. XTC is used in this study because it enables regular
real-time scanner adjustments, such as updating the FOV to follow the movement

2.1
Magnetic resonance imaging
Hydrogen nuclei (protons) possess the property of nuclear spin, which means the
protons can be thought of as small rotating magnets inside the body. There are
other atomic nuclei that possess the same property, but due to the abundance of
water in the human body makes hydrogen the most relevant atom. Inside a high
static magnetic ﬁeld (commonly denoted as B0) the magnetic moment of these
nuclei tends to align in the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld. In general there will be
nuclei orientated in all directions but the net magnetisation of all nuclei will align
with B0. In addition to the alignment, the nuclei will precess1 around B0 with a
particular frequency called the Larmor frequency or resonance frequency, f ([5]):
f = γB0
(2.1)
where γ = 42.58 MHz/Tesla, [12], is the gyromagnetic ratio for protons (unique
constant for each type of atom). As an example, a proton will in a 3 Tesla B0 ﬁeld
precess at the frequency of approx 127.74 MHz. Electromagnetic radio frequency
(RF) pulses from an external source can be used to excite speciﬁc nuclei. This
external magnetic ﬁeld, produced by an RF coil, is typically referred to as the B1
ﬁeld. As long as B1 is on, the electromagnetic RF pulse will "push" the protons
away from their initial orientation. Although, if one pushes a person on a swing,
the push will need to be in synchrony with the swing pattern in order to gain max-
imum height. It is the same principle in MRI, where only the protons precessing
at the particular frequency of the RF pulse will be pushed away from the initial

2.1.1
Spatial localization
Gradient coils are electromagnets used to encode the nuclei positions spatially by
producing a linear variation of the ﬁeld strength along the gradient direction. Ap-
plying only the slice selection gradient will variate the magnetic ﬁeld strength along
this direction so that diﬀerent cross sections will have diﬀerent resonance frequen-
cies. This way one can excite a single slice (cross section) of the human brain by
adjusting the RF pulse accordingly. The thickness of the slice depends on the band-
width of the selective pulse, where the bigger the bandwidth is, the thicker the slice
will be. The magnetic ﬁeld that varies with the z-direction (often the slice selection
gradient is chosen to be along the z-direction) can be described by Bz ([12],[14]):
Bz = B0 + Gzz
(2.4)
where Gz is the gradient in the z-direction and z is the position along z-axis. This
linear variation aﬀects the resonance frequency along the z-direction:
f(z) = γ (B0 + Gzz) = f0 + γGzz
(2.5)
Two additional gradient coils, frequency encoding (FE) and phase encoding (PE),
can be used to further spatially localise the positions of the protons within a slice.
The PE gradient is used to induce dephasing to the spin frequencies before sampling.
If one considers a slice of protons, then each proton will spin at the same frequency
but the phase will vary along the PE gradient. The FE gradient is used to vary the
spin frequencies of the protons during sampling of the signal. Figure 2.1a shows

2.1.2
Image formation
Sampling of the MR signal happens in the spatial frequency domain, Fourier space,
as the MR signal can be decomposed to a mix of RF waves with diﬀerent temporal
frequencies and phase. In MR, Fourier space is normally referred to as k-space.
MR data is spatially encoded by the diﬀerences in frequency and phase, as brieﬂy
described above. The sampling is performed by moving around in k-space and sam-
ple the data. Each sample in k-space encodes for spatial information of the image
and every point in the image is a combination of the spatial information from all
samples in k-space. The PE and FE gradients control the movement in k-space,
which depends on the strength of the gradient and the duration of which they are on.
Low spatial frequency components will be near the center of k-space and contain
information about the general shape and contrast in the image. High spatial fre-
quency components will be more distant from the center of k-space and contain
information about the image details, hence the edges in the image. So the spatial
encoding achieved by the gradient coils results in spatial frequency information in
k-space. A two-dimensional (2D) inverse Fourier transform of this k-space data
reconstructs the image. K-space is sampled in accordance with a trajectory, which
is the "road map" for moving around in k-space and collect data. The k-space tra-
jectory is controlled by the gradients and depends, for example, on what kind of
image contrast is wanted. The entire approach to image formation is controlled by
the sequence. For instance, the echo planar imaging (EPI) sequence used in this
thesis can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2. Here it is seen how all of k-space is traversed in
a single slice excitation. In ﬁgure 2.3 the same can be seen for the fast low angle
shot (FLASH) sequence used in this thesis, but here only a single line in k-space
is sampled per excitation. With the FLASH sequence, it takes more excitations to
acquire a single image slice, but the ﬂip angle is much smaller than the on used in
the EPI sequence. This will be explained later in section 3.7.

Navigator methods
As mentioned earlier, the ﬁeld of motion tracking can be divided into three cat-

within each category will be presented.
MR navigators are the traditional way of correcting for motion during MRI and
only use the MR scanner. Navigators can be either k-space-based, image-based or

recovery spoiled gradient (3D IR-SPGR) and a 3D fast spin echo (3D FSE) pulse
sequence. The SP-Nav pulse sequence consists of three pulses, with a small ﬂip angle

Optical methods often take advantage of technologies used for other purposes, such

sight from the subject to the camera. The line of sight can be a limiting factor in
cases where, for example, closed coil systems are used. Another issue regards the
tracking markers, as these need to be attached to the subject in a ﬁxed position
throughout the scan. If the marker is attached to the skin, there will be a risk

perfectly still but moves their skin a tiny bit, the system will take this as motion
and correct the imaging volume. This problem is accounted for by mounting the
marker to a mouthpiece, which should be coupled with the subjects skull in a more
rigid manner. However, this gives rise to new issues for two reasons. First of all, the
subjects comfort decreases. Second of all, potential movement with the mouth (for
example chewing or swallowing) could cause the same problem as before. Main-
taining a clear line of sight can in some MR systems be very diﬃcult to ensure, as
closed coil systems can disrupt this. Even though the precision is much better than
other existing systems, the problem of marker ﬁxation and camera line of sight are
areas that need further improvements.

Field detection methods
The method described in this thesis falls under the ﬁeld detection methods. Field
detection methods utilise the spatially encoding from the gradients. Markers used
in this technique are popularly referred to as ’active markers’( [13]) or in some
instances ’semi-active markers’ ([15]). In [13] and [15], they use nuclear magnetic
resonance (NMR) probes to track motion, at 1.5 Tesla and 7 Tesla, respectively.
The invention described in [11] regards a small sensor consisting of three orthogonal
coils with known geometry. As this sensor changes direction in a static magnetic
ﬁeld or the gradient coils switch, a current will be induced in the coils according
to Faradays law of induction (will be described later). Estimating the position and

orientation of this sensor can give valuable information about the motion of the
sensor, i.e. whatever is attached to the sensor. Amongst various applications, the
technique can be used to track and correct for motion during MRI. The magnitude
of the induced current is related to the time derivative of the ﬂuctuating magnetic

The amplitude of the actual magnetic ﬁeld is calculated by multiplying the time

A search algorithm ﬁnds the location of the estimated magnetic ﬁeld from the step
before. The search algorithm ﬁnds the location of possible magnetic ﬁelds, which
has the same magnitude as the magnetic ﬁeld found from the step before during the
gradient activation. Some problems of this search algorithm are that it involves a
minimization of a cost function, usually leading to multiple solutions. Minimizing
the number of solutions requires additional information.
This method is also not without challenges. The accuracy of the estimation
depends on the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and can be dangerous as only three
samples are used. Multiple equivalent minima exist due to cost function symme-
try (which would be standard in most modern MRI systems [11]) and ultimately
resulting in multiple solutions. It requires additional data to reduce the number of
solutions, which increases the computational time.
It should be noted that [11] describes an invention that could have multiple
functions. It describes how to estimate the position and orientation of a sensor
within an MRI system. Robin Medical Inc. have used this to develop their prod-
uct MotionScout, which is speciﬁcally intended for motion tracking and correction
during MRI. Other applications are mentioned, for example, tracking of surgical
instruments (such as catheters), EndoScout, inside an MRI system. However, in
this thesis the focus is only on the application of motion tracking and correction.
Here follows a brief summary of the state of the art in prospective motion correction.
Not a single method fulﬁl all three main requirements for motion correction, hence
the empty ﬁeld in the middle of ﬁgure 2.6. A common approach is to measure
the motion in one direction at a time, e.g. "nodding" in the feet-head direction or

during free motion in all six DOF, as they do in [21].

After calibration, the changes in gradient artefact amplitudes (seen in ∆w) can be
translated to a head position and orientation via the approximation from equation
(2.4). The calibration should continue to be updated during the scan, as it otherwise
will be outdated after the ﬁrst scanner update. Updating the scanner happens by
changing the direction of the slice selection, phase encoding and frequency encoding
gradients so that the FOV follows the head movement. How this is done in practice
will be explained in a later section but essentially it is a rotation of the gradient
directions as explained in [1] by:
Gnew = RG
(2.15)
where R is a rotation matrix. Updating the imaging volume, i.e. FOV, due to
translation is in the slice selection direction a matter of altering the frequency of
the RF excitation pulse. In the readout direction, the centre of the FOV will be
deﬁned by the centre frequency that results from demodulation of the received RF
frequency. For the phase encoding direction, the receiver phase is modulated.[10]
This is a very important aspect of prospective motion correction, because the
change of FOV result in new directions of which the gradients are played out. With
the new geometry, the gradient artefact will look diﬀerent than the ﬁrst version
recorded before the calibration. Instead of measuring G again it should suﬃce to
rotate G as described above.

Use of an eXTernal Control application
The eXTernal Control (XTC) application is a bi-directional scanner interface that
allows the user to control the scanner externally in a safe manner.
XTC was
invented by Philips to be used in, for example, interventional MRI but it can also
be used for motion correction [16]. XTC communicates with the reconstruction
and scanner processes on the MR scanner through a TCP/IP connection with the

updating the scanner geometry (i.e. the FOV) continually while performing a scan.
The XTC functions make it a very attractive and suitable tool for this study.
In the set-up of this study, the XTC software was installed on the scanner per-

Estimating the gradient sequence diagram
The ﬁrst step of the tracking process is to get an estimate of the gradient sequence
diagram. In this section, a diﬀerent approach than the ICA method will be taken
to estimate G from equation (2.4). It will be described how the estimation is done

3.2.1
Methods
To ensure that the estimated gradient sequence diagram is the best possible rep-
resentation of the real gradient sequence diagram, the scanner was reprogrammed
such that the user can exclude all but one gradient (Scanner reprogramming was
performed by Mads Andersen). Now, running the same scan three times in a row,
while keeping only one of the gradients turned on at a time, yields that particular
gradients contribution to the overall gradient artefact.
The goal is to update the scanner geometry once every imaged volume. Meaning

motion parameters. In order for the algorithm to keep track of time and place in the
imaging sequence, there is a need for a trigger telling when a new imaging volume
or slice begins. Triggers indicating the start of every slice was also programmed into
the imaging sequence (again performed by Mads Andersen). Here, an extremely
fast trigger pulse with a length of 1 ms was inserted just before each new slice
excitation.

3.3. The importance of synchronous measurements
3.2.3
Discussion
Getting an estimate of the gradient sequence diagram is essential as the change in
amplitude reveals spatial information as already discussed in section 2.4. Diﬀerent
approaches exists and as already mentioned in section 2.4, the ICA method used in
[18] does not seem to be the best way of estimating the gradient sequence diagram.
The gradient artefact is a combination of all three gradient time series, but ICA
decomposes this signal into six components (as it was in [18]). There are no proof
that three of these decomposed signals are true representations of the gradient
time series. In addition to the fact that the user needs to choose manually which
components to use, makes this method a bit too lengthy. One could argue that
this seemed to work just ﬁne in [18], but there cannot be any doubts of which
components to choose, when looking at the big picture. Another problem with the
ICA approach is that the order of which the components are estimated are not
consistent with repeating analyses, which also complicates any automated selection
algorithms.
Estimating the gradient sequence diagram by running the scan thrice, with
only one active gradient at a time, yields a more conﬁdent estimation of the actual
gradient artefact compared to the ICA method. In addition, the risk of choosing a
wrong representation is eliminated, and semi-automated gradient sequence diagram
estimation is possible. The ICA ambiguity is replaced with a precise estimate of
the gradient sequence diagram.
Having a trigger per slice makes it possible to accelerate the method to update
the scanner geometry per slice instead of per volume, which in time would be
preferable.
3.3
The importance of synchronous measurements
After estimating the gradient sequence diagram, it was found that the gradient
artefact was not sampled the same way for each TR, which resulted in asynchronous
measurements. The gradient sequence diagram is identical for each TR and is a
fundamental criterion for this tracking method to work. If no motion occurs, the
gradient artefact will remain the same for each TR. This section goes through the
important factors that should be considered to obtain an identical sampling pattern
of the gradient artefacts.
3.3.1
Methods
The signals are sampled digitally, and it is crucial that the gradient artefacts be
sampled the same way for each TR. If not, the gradient artefacts will variate re-
gardless if motion occurs or not.
In [9] it was pointed out that synchrony between the MR sequence and the
sampling pattern of the EEG recordings could be obtained by setting TR to be a
multiple of the sampling interval. In this thesis, the sampling frequency was 5 kHz
and corresponds to a sampling interval of 0.2 ms. For instance, this means that
a TR of 1000.4 ms should yield synchronous measurements between two measured
gradient artefacts, whereas a TR of 1000.5 ms should not. In the exam card of the
scanner user interface, it is possible to deﬁne a TR value, as long as it is minimum
the value of the shortest TR possible for that particular sequence. The TR was

3.4
Calibration
Approximating the motion parameters from amplitude changes of the gradient in-
duced signal requires the transformation matrix A that can be found by calibrating
with SPM motion parameters. In this section, the calibration step is described, and
it is examined how XTC inﬂuences this procedure.
3.4.1
Methods
As described earlier by equation (2.4), amplitude changes in the gradient artefact
(i.e. the diﬀerences between weights) are used to approximate motion parameters
via the transformation matrix A.
The calibration procedure is to scan an entire volume, one at a time, and ﬁx
the phantom in a diﬀerent position for every new volume. Afterwards, the images
are realigned by the realignment function in SPM and the motion parameters are
obtained with reference to the ﬁrst image (ﬁrst volume). As six motion parameters
needs to be estimated, it requires at least six sets of SPM parameters.
In this
thesis, both eight and nine sets has been used in two diﬀerent experiments. Eight
sets were used in the experiment described later in section 3.5, and nine sets were
used in the experiments described later in section 4.3.
One goal of this thesis was to use the XTC application to control the scanner
externally during a scan, which also means during the calibration. Utilising XTC
makes the scan pause after each volume, where the scanner awaits orders from the
user via XTC. The pause is used to reposition the phantom, and then the scanner
is given the message to proceed. The calibration procedure was tested by allowing
XTC to control the scanner pausing and scanner starting to see whether or not it
works.

3.7. Choice of MR sequence
3.7.1
Methods
An EPI sequence is attractive as it yields the fastest imaging acquisition there is, but
the FLASH sequence is also quite fast. The motion tracking and correction method
described in this thesis was intended to be used during fMRI and simultaneous EEG
measurements, which is why EPI would be the preferred sequence. An experiment
was set up to investigate if the image distortions could be eliminated by using the
parallel imaging technique SENSE together with the EPI, or by using the FLASH
sequence instead. The same phantom was scanned thrice, one with each of the
sequences EPI, EPI with SENSE, and FLASH with SENSE.
Single shot echo planar imaging sequence
Single shot EPI is a very well known MR sequence. Most MR sequences go through
one line in k-space per excitation, whereas EPI traverses "all" of k-space in one
excitation, which makes this a very fast imaging sequence. The fast imaging acqui-
sition results in limited spatial resolution. However, the EPI sequence applies to
several image acquisition techniques such as diﬀusion imaging, perfusion imaging,
and fMRI. The EPI k-space trajectory used in this thesis can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2.
Apart from the image distortions that came with the use of the EPI sequence, it
was also not possible to use the a single shot EPI together with XTC.
Fast low angle shot sequence
The FLASH sequence was due to the image distortions that came with the use of
EPI considered as an alternative to the EPI sequence. By choosing a small ﬂip
angle, around ten degrees, it is possible to do rapid MRI one line in k-space at a
time. After acquiring one line in k-space, the leftover transversal magnetisation is
spoiled by gradients to get ready for a new excitation. In ﬁgure 2.3 the k-space
trajectory of the FLASH sequence can be seen.

image distortions that were experienced with the use of the EPI sequence aﬀected
both the motion parameter estimation and the translation of SPM coordinates to
scanner coordinates. As a consequence of this, a FLASH sequence will be used in
all future experiments in this thesis.

4.2
Prospective motion correction using remote data
access application
In this thesis, the EEG recording system from BrainVision comes with a computer
program Recorder, which handles all incoming EEG data. It is designed to monitor
mainly and save incoming EEG signals.
Remote data access (RDA) is an application in Recorder, which allows the user
to access the data in real-time through other applications or computers. RDA is a
very useful tool as the recorded gradient artefact needs to be accessed in real-time.
In this section, there are no results due to unresolved problems with the ex-
periment. However, the method will be described, and the lack of results will be
discussed.
4.2.1
Methods
RDA is used to live stream the gradient artefact signal into MATLAB, where all
other computations involved in this thesis are run. Existing MATLAB code is avail-
able, to establish a connection between the recording program (Recorder) and the
analysing PC handling the motion tracking and the prospective motion correction
algorithm. First, a TCP/IP (Transmission control protocol / internet protocol)
connection is established. Whenever Recorder is on monitoring mode, the data can
be accessed through the connection in small blocks of data. These blocks come,
35/83
==PAGE:58==
4.2. Prospective motion correction using remote data access application
Prospective motion correction using an EEG hood
in the test done in this thesis, in sizes of 100 samples at a sampling frequency of
5 kHz. The sampling interval is in this case 200 ms and not a single sample will
get lost. It seems like the Recorder software continuously read incoming data and
package the data in small blocks and when the block is full; it is made available
through the TCP/IP connection. When the RDA client is running in MATLAB
it calls Recorder as quickly as it can to see if a new block of data is ready. Time
is, in this case, a limitation and the aspect of real-time data processing can be
discussed. Although, this is as close to real-time as one can get when transferring
data in this manner. In this thesis, one of the goals is to update the scanner before
a new volume is being scanned. The time lag is in this sense irrelevant as long as
the software can estimate motion and calculate the changes in the imaging volume
before a new volume is to be scanned.
Running the RDA client on an external PC is done through MATLAB, where
the code for establishing the connection and reading data from the source, is pro-
vided by BrainVision. The source code has been modiﬁed to do the computations,
which will calculate the motion parameters and save the results in a geometry ﬁle1
to be available for XTC to handle.
For volume to volume update the steps are as follows:
• Data is continuously received by the RDA client, and each block of data comes
with messages. These messages tell whether or not there are any markers
indicating the excitation of a slice (this marker is also called a trigger) within
that speciﬁc block.
• As the ﬁrst block containing a trigger is read; the data from the trigger
position and the rest of the data block is stored in a matrix.
• The following blocks are also stored in an extension of the ﬁrst block until
the entire volume (or a large part of it) has been recorded.
• At this point, the motion correction algorithm is initiated and the motion
parameters are estimated and saved to a ﬁle. This ﬁle is then transferred to
the scanner via XTC, as FOV geometry updates.
• After this, the matrix containing the stored data is set to zero in order to be
ready for a new volume.
• The entire routine is then repeated.
An experiment was performed, where these steps were followed while scanning a
phantom using the FLASH sequence as mentioned earlier (forty slices per volume,
TR = 12 ms). Total scan time for one dynamic volume is approximately 55 s.
The phantom was not moved during this scan as it were more interesting to ﬁnd
out whether or not this whole set-up would work. The goal of the experiment was
to ﬁnd out if it is possible to live stream the gradient artefact in MATLAB while
estimating parameters of motion. A more complex experiment could incorporate a
movement of the phantom.

4.2.2
Discussion
The RDA client turned out to work ﬁne in regards of transferring data from the
EEG recording program to the external PC. Data was continuously received during
the scan and blocks containing triggers were also recognized and dealt with the
intended way. Motion parameters could be estimated and also be written to a ﬁle.
However, problems arose with data overﬂow, and it is believed to be because of too
heavy computations. Blocks of data are continuously made available through the
TCP/IP server, but this server has limited space. If the time between grabbing
the data blocks gets longer, the server ﬁlls up, and one of the two things happen.
One, once the server is full, any new block of data will overwrite one of the blocks
in the server. Two, any new block of data will get dismissed if the server is already
full. Which one it is, is unknown, but either way important data will be lost and
unrecoverable. Data overﬂow was experienced every time this experiment was run,
and it ruined the experiment every time.
Possible ways to eliminate the risk of experiencing data overﬂow could be to
either optimize the code to run faster or set up a new buﬀer and store all incoming
data there before it is processed. The TCP/IP server buﬀer size does not seem to
be able to enlarge, but if this is possible then it would be the preferred option. The
biggest problem is the loss of data.
On the bright side, it is a problem that is relatively easy to ﬁx, as there are
several ways to make one’s own buﬀer for incoming data. One way is to have two
sessions of MATLAB open, where one session receives data via the RDA client and
stores it in a ﬁle. The second session can handle that ﬁle and perform the motion
estimation and correction.

to control the scanner externally by transferring various commands to the scanner.
These commands are delivered in simple text ﬁles and can be to either start, pause
or stop the scan, but it can also be to update the scanners FOV geometry. XTC
looks for commands in a single folder on the PC, where XTC is run. It has already
been established that XTC should not be used while calibrating the tracking system.

4.5. Summary and reﬂection
54/83
==PAGE:77==
Chapter 5
Further research
As it has been discussed, there are a few areas of the prospective motion correction
method described in this thesis, where further research is needed to make this a
viable method. XTC showed to have a negative inﬂuence on the system calibration,
where SPM is used to realign the images. The intention is eventually to update
the calibration continuously during the scan to make sure that the estimation of
the motion parameters are as exact as they can be. If XTC is included in future
studies, and it is this author’s opinion that it should be, then this problem will
need to be ﬁxed. To solve this problem it might help to use a diﬀerent header ﬁle,
so that the customized pixel scaling can be eliminated.
With regards to the RDA method described in section 4.2, a solution to the prob-
lem of data overﬂow was brieﬂy discussed. Data overﬂow would be a very vital
problem to solve as the RDA method is believed to have excellent potential. As
also mentioned, one can solve this problem in several ways. One approach could be
to have two MATLAB sessions open at once and split the computational steps in
two. The ﬁrst session could read data via the RDA client and write it to a ﬁle that
is shared with the second session. The second MATLAB session could read the
data from the shared ﬁle and estimate the motion parameters and write the FOV
parameters to a geometry ﬁle. XTC could be used to update the FOV continuously
every time a new geometry ﬁle is generated. This concept of generating a new
buﬀer can be approached many diﬀerent ways, and the most suitable method can
be found through testing. The preferred method will be the one that generates the
smallest amount of time lag.
A simple problem that was described in section 4.3 is the hypothesis regarding one
of the wire loops to have a broken connection that causes the gradient artefact to
look clipped. After the experiment, the EEG hood was inspected. Here it was
realized that one of the electrode pairs was not attached to each other anymore. In
addition to this there seemed to be problems with the wiring, which will need to
be ﬁxed to be able to use all six channels again.
It was after the EEG experiment described in section 4.3, that the transformation
of SPM parameter to FOV parameter was realized to be more complicated than

ﬁrst assumed in section 3.6. This transformation needs to be further tested, as
there are no data to support this hypothesis at the moment of writing this thesis.
An experiment could be set up to investigate if the SPM parameters do not match

In this
experiment, the reference FOV should not be zero in all parameters. If this conﬁrms
that the initial translation is incorrect, then it would be necessary to look at the

==PAGE:79==
Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis has described a method that involves a validated motion tracking
method in coherence with a real-time data streaming client and a bi-directional
scanner interface.
The focus has been on the gradient artefacts that appear in
EEG signals during fMRI and simultaneous EEG measurements and the spatial
information that these artefacts contain. A method, which were developed in [18],
of tracking patient motion inside an MR scanner from the spatial information that
is contained in the gradient artefact has been presented, and a novel prospective
correction method has been investigated.
The purpose of this thesis was to develop a novel motion correction method that
via an EEG hood and the use of XTC could update the scanner FOV prospectively
during a scan. The goal was not reached entirely, but proof of concept was shown
through multiple experiments. It was also shown that XTC can assist this tech-
nique as long as the user is aware of its limitations.
So far, the limitations of this method lie with; the data overﬂow that was ex-
perienced with RDA; XTC disrupting the calibration; and the complexity of the
SPM parameter to FOV parameter translation. However, all of these limitations
have been evaluated, and possible solutions to the problems have been formulated.
Therefore, these limitations are considered to be minor problems. It is diﬃcult
to determine if ﬁxing these minor problems will result in success. Still, it is this
author’s opinion that this method has the potential to become a quality prospec-
tive motion correction technique that eventually will help reduce motion artefacts
during MRI.

6
Time resolution limits
The inversion equation (21) requires GT G to have full rank (=3), which is
the case if there is gradient activity in all three directions during the time
series. If all weights had to be estimated for each position estimation, the time
resolution of the tracking system would thus be limited to how often the least
used gradient is used - this would often be the slice selection gradient, Gz.
However, when changing a loop’s position and orientation, it seems from
equations (11)-(12), that wx,wy will change, no matter how the loop is moved,
this is because of the concomitant ﬁeld changes 1 . Thus, 2 out of 3 weights are
enough to detect motion in all 3 directions, due to the concomitant ﬁeld changes
and the time resolution can thus be increased to the time interval for the second
most used gradient if having at least 6 loops (this is to make inversion (18)
possible). To increase time resolution, the problem could thus be reformulated
without e.g. wz and ∂Gz
∂t for the time points where the Gz gradient is not active,
and still it would be possible to solve for all the elements in ∆rh.
The higher time resolution is needed to make a geometry update per shot
in a multishot sequence where not all gradients are changed during one TR.
For singleshot, multislice EPI, the lower time resolution using all three weights
seems suﬃcient, however.
