Abstract
When the Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) satellite launched in De-
cember 2022, a new era in high resolution satellite altimetry began. In this thesis,
we investigate the first data released from the SWOT mission; the initial 3-months
of 1-day repeat orbit, and the first cycles of the 21-day science orbit. We look at the
spatial resolution and noise level of the ocean height as determined from SWOT, and
from this estimate the wavenumber spectral slope in the East/Japan Sea. From the
observations, the surface slope and currents are estimated, and validated against nu-
meric models and other altimeters. This preliminary analysis of SWOT data shows a
large improvement of ocean height determination, compared to conventional radar
altimeters. We also see a large potential in geodetic application of SWOT data,
as well as for observing oceanographic features. However, the novel sampling pat-
tern of SWOT requires new filtering techniques to fulfill the full potential of the
observations.
Dansk Resumé
Da satellitten ”Surface Water and Ocean Topography” (SWOT) blev opsendt i De-
cember 2022, begyndte en ny æra inden for høj-opløsnings satellit altimetri. I dette
speciale vil vi undersøge det første data der blev udgivet fra SWOT missionen: de
første 3-måneders 1-dags kredsløb, samt de første cycler af 21-dags kredsløbet. Vi
ser på den rumlige opløsning og støjniveau af havoverfladen som set fra SWOT, og
herfra bestemmer den spektrale hældning i Det Østlige/Japanske Hav. Fra disse
observationer, bliver hældningen i havoverfladen samt havstrømmene estimeret og
valideret mod numeriske modeller samt andre altimetre. Denne indledende under-
søgelse af SWOT data viser en stor forbedring af bestemmelsen af havoverfladen,
sammenlignet med konventionelle radar altimetre. Vi ser også et stort potentiale for
geodætiske applikationer af SWOT data, såvel som observationer af oceanografiske
træk. Dog vil der med de nye observationer fra SWOT kræve nye filtreringsteknikker
før det fulde potentiale af observationerne kan opnåes.

Introduction
The ocean covers more than 70% of the surface of the Earth, and plays a vital
part in the climate, ecosystem, economy and as a source for food around the globe
(Stewart, 2008). Ever changing and remote, obtaining data on the surface features
as well as internal structure has been a challenge for centuries. As we mainly observe
the ocean surface, we see the combined effects of a multitude of features, including
atmospheric, currents, tidal forces, and gravity. Oceanographers have used ship-
borne observations, in-situ buoys, or theoretical studies of ocean flow and circulation
to try to better understand the global ocean (Charney, 1971; Stewart, 2008; Caldwell
and Merrifield, 2015; Jayne et al., 2017). The sparsity of the observations made it
challenging to study the ocean. It was in this light that GEOS-3 was launched in
1975 and subsequent launch of Seasat in 1978, GEOSAT in 1985 and ERS-1 in 1991,
with the goal of observing the ocean topography.
However, these systems shared the same fundamental challenge; the total measured
height error was greater than the small ocean variability wished to observe, resulting
in only features with length scales larger than 100-200 km being distinguishable
(Gordon and Baker, 1980; Fu, 1983; Sailor and LeSchack, 1987; Abdalla et al.,
2021). The launch of TOPEX/Poseidon in 1992, was a breakthrough in satellite
oceanography. The orbital error had been reduced to levels below other error sources
(Stammer and Wunsch, 1994), and preliminary studies achieved accuracy at 5-10
cm level. As subsequent error sources were addressed, this accuracy was improved,
obtaining 2-3 cm accuracy in 1997, and the final re-processing of the data being
released as late as 2023 (Stammer and Wunsch, 1994; Stammer, 1997; AVISO,
2023).
The focus on error reduction has improved altimeter observations greatly, with cur-
rent noise levels of instrumental <20 mm and orbital <10 mm, with CryoSat-2,
Jason-3, SARAL/AltiKa, Sentinel-3A and Sentinel-3B as well as Sentinel-6 Michael
Freilich (MF) all in current operation (Abdalla et al., 2021; Srinivasan and Tson-
tos, 2023). With the increased accuracy, followed the ability to accurately observe
mesoscale features and it was in this light that satellite altimetry was deemed to
have ”reached maturity” (Abdalla et al., 2021). The altimetry data was used in an
increasing amount of studies; global sea rise being observed and consolidated (Nerem
et al., 2018; Veng and Andersen, 2021), the global mean sea surface achieving higher
resolution (Andersen et al., 2023b; Schaeffer et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023), mapping
of bathymetry (Yale et al., 1998; Sandwell et al., 2006), hydrographical/inland-water
applications (Nielsen et al., 2020; An et al., 2022) and mapping of the cryosphere

(Helm et al., 2014; Tilling et al., 2018).
Subsequently focus was set on the next step: studying and mapping the submesoscale
ocean processes. Theoretical work suggested that the processes at this scale have
a fundamental role in the exchange between the ocean surface and the deep ocean
(Fu and Ferrari, 2008; Su et al., 2018). Understanding this process would improve
climate models, further the understanding of the carbon pump as well extend our
knowledge about ocean processes.
However, the noise level is still too great to
observe these. Simple theoretical estimates suggested the noise level at a 10 km
wavelength needed to be better than 1 cm (Fu and Ferrari, 2008).
Even with the development of synthetic aperture radar (SAR) altimeters, new tech-
niques were needed to resolve the submesoscale processes (Wingham et al., 2006).
Interferometric altimetry utilizes two antennas to observe the surface, enabling ob-
servations off-nadir. This would enable observations in a swath, as opposed to the
1D profile obtained by conventional nadir altimetry. The successful development
of swath altimetry would revolutionize the ability to monitor the ocean topography
and the derived features. This concept was first proposed as the Wide-Swath Ocean
Altimeter (WSOA) in 2004 (Fu and Rodriguez, 2004), and subsequent studies and
technical maturing resulted in the Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT)
satellite (NASA, 2007; Durand et al., 2010). A statement from the 25th Altimetry
for the Future symposium in 2021 tells us about the prospects of SWOT;
”The ability to do swath altimetry is such a major advancement (...) that it
might be the most important technological breakthrough in satellite altimetry
since TOPEX/Poseidon’s capability to reach centimeter-level accuracy.”
- International Altimetry Team,
Altimetry for the future,
(Abdalla et al., 2021).
This leaves no doubt in the exciting possibilities obtained from swath altimetry, with
the field preparing for the first data release from SWOT with multiple studies done
on simulated data from SWOT (Qiu et al., 2016; Ruggiero et al., 2016; Chelton
et al., 2019; King and Martin, 2021).
SWOT was launched in December 2022 and, during the time this thesis has been
prepared, SWOT has passed through the initial commissioning phase, the 1-day
repeat calibration phase, and now finds itself in the 21-day repeat science orbit
(NASA, 2023). We had therefore a unique opportunity to get first-hand experience
with the new SWOT data, even before oﬀicial calibrated data versions have been
released. In this thesis, we aim to determine the first-hand performance of the SWOT
satellite and develop methods to utilize this product in oceanographic research.
As SWOT uses a new sampling technique, the development, usage and validation is
of vital importance for using the data to improve our understanding of the global
oceans. Understanding the new challenges, and how to account for these, is the
key to get the best out of the new data. We will look at preliminary analysis of
the SWOT data, and perform preliminary studies of data performance and science
returns.
We focus on the Sea Surface Heights (SSH), and the first derived oceanographic
features, the Sea Surface Slope (SSS) and the Sea Surface Currents (SSC). We

2.1
Temporal and Spatial Resolutions
The spatial scales of geodetic and oceanographic features are commonly divided
into large-scale O(> 1000km), mesoscale O(100−1000km) and submesoscale O(1−
100 km).
Satellite altimetry has enabled the study of large-scale and mesoscale
features with accurate data, however, the study of submesoscale is still a challenge.
To get a sense of oceanographic phenomena across different scales, we see an illustra-
tion in figure 2.1. As the sea surface we observe is a sum of a variety of phenomena
across a large amount of temporal and spatial scales, we want to be able to classify
them to be able to determine what we expect to see. The very long time-scale ef-
fects we see are either of interest in geodesy, namely the bathymetric features, or
in climate science with global warming. We see they share similar time scales, but
an entirely different spatial scale, with climate change having a global effect, and
bathymetry varying widely over longer distances. This influences our data collection
strategies, in how to best observe this.
At shorter time scales, we start to see other effects. In the time-scale of O(1−100yr),
we see larger seasonal cycles and planetary waves or Rossby waves. In the mesoscale
O(100 −1000 km) in the time region of O(1 month −10 yr) we see the dominant
features are the large Rossby waves, barotropic motion, and smaller scale eddies.
While larger scale features are quite well mapped from satellite observations today,
we are still lacking reliable observations of the features being most predominant in
the submesoscale (Rodriguez et al., 2017).
In the submesoscale O(10 −100km) and shorter scales, we primarily see short time-
scale features O(< 1yr). Complex phenomena with large topographical dependency
or interactions are predominant in this region (Lee and Kim, 2021).

The Geoid
The geoid is the hypothetical equipotential surface at which the sea
surface at rest would lie. Modeling the geoid is important as this is the predominant
signal in ocean heights, with approximately 99% of the variance being described by
the geoid (Stewart, 2008). Using the geoid as a reference surface is therefore often
a key to discovering the more fine-scale structures in the ocean surface.

Sea Surface Heights (SSH)
The Sea Surface Height (SSH) is the directly observed
height h(x, y, t) at a location x, y and time t of the ocean surface, as referred to a
reference ellipsoid W. The majority of the signal in the SSH is therefore of the geoid
N(ϕ, λ), which is why observations of the SSH are important in marine geodesy.
Even though the majority of the observed signal is due to the geoid, oceanographic
signals are still present.

2.3.2
Ageotrophic Currents
The description of geostrophic currents explains large parts of the observed sea sur-
face currents at a larger scale. As we saw, a lot of approximations are needed for
these equations to hold and are generally valid in mesoscale regions. However, when
getting closer to the submesoscale, unbalanced ageostrophic motion begins to domi-
nate. The length scale at which ageostrophic motion dominates geostrophic motion
is termed the transition scale Lt and is a current scientific interest to determine
more accurately (Vergara et al., 2023). A better understanding of Lt would enable
the scientific community to better utilize high-resolution SSH data, and understand
when our assumptions break down (Vergara et al., 2023).
Global studies of the transition scale currently show Lt to have a larger value O(100−
150 km) at low latitudes, with lower values O(50 −80 km) at high latitudes, with a
large locational-effect in highly energetic areas (Vergara et al., 2023). This shows a
larger part of the current explained by geostrophic balance at higher latitudes.

2.5
Ground Truth
Tide Gauges
While we can obtain the equations for what we expect to observe,
validating it is quite hard. For the same reason, satellite altimetry has become so
important for oceanography, and is also one of the challenges of validation; obtaining
global observations of the state of the ocean. While there are many tide gauges
globally, they are mainly located at the coasts, at islands, at northern latitudes, or
in the shallow seas, and lack observations at the open ocean (Caldwell and Merrifield,
2015).
Argo Floats
Another data source is the Argo float program. We don’t use these
data in the thesis, however, they are an important source of data for the numerical
models, which we use for validation later. The program consists of almost 4,000
floats obtaining data profiles in the upper 2 km of the water column distributed
globally with nominal coverage of at least one float per 3◦×3◦degrees in the ice-free
oceans (Jayne et al., 2017). This has contributed significantly to globally distributed
data on the open oceans and is widely used in the scientific community. Due to its
10-day sampling pattern, and distribution of one float per 3◦×3◦degrees nominally,
it is a key component in constraining mesoscale processes and determining the ocean
surface to interior link, however, obtaining finer temporal validation with satellite
altimetry is not feasible.
Numerical Models
If we compile everything together, we can use numerical models
to determine the behavior of the oceans from the known governing equations, and
constrained by the data obtained from satellite altimeter, floats as well as additional
data sources, we obtain an inverse problem, and can determine the sea state from
this; we obtain an assimilation model (Stewart, 2008). These models can become
highly complex and can run analyses of the ocean state as well as forecasts, such
as the Massachusetts Institute of Technology General Circulation Model (MITgcm)
(Marshall et al., 1997). Reanalysis models achieve the highest resolution as both
data from before and after the wished time can help constrain the model. One of the
providers of the reanalysis data is the Copernicus Marine Environment Monitoring
Service (CMEMS) with Global Ocean Physics Analysis and Forecast, from which
we will use data to validate our methods and observations (Service, 2022).

3.3.4
Ocean Effects on Radar Interferometry
The analysis of surface characteristics in radar returns is a large research field, and
important for understanding the behavior of the interferometric images (Rodriguez
and Martin, 1992; Rosen et al., 2000).
In the context of ocean observations we
describe some of the main effects.
20
Chapter 3.
Altimetry
==PAGE:30==
Ocean surface waves for instance can influence the radar return in a multitude of
ways. In its simplest form, if the ocean waves are much smaller than the radar
resolution cell, the effects simplify to a volume scattering effect as mentioned before,
as well as the well-known EM bias. However, in the case of larger waves, different
effects need to be taken into account (Peral et al., 2015).
One effect stemming from the off-nadir observations happens when the angle of
incidence is smaller than the slope of the ocean waves. The range plane will thereby
”cut” the wave field at multiple points (Peral et al., 2015). This is similar to layover
and is the main effect that limits the near-nadir observations in the interferogram
(Rodriguez et al., 2017).
Another effect similar to that of the EM bias is wave bunching. As the interferogram
is created from the phase observation, a radial velocity will result in a height dis-
placement in the interferogram. Due to the ocean surface being in constant motion,
this will lead to non-equal mapping of the troughs and crests of ocean waves and
will lead to a biased sea surface height if not corrected for (Rodriguez et al., 2017).
Given the averaging steps taken by the onboard processor, the resulting image is
often averaged down to a lower resolution and decimated to a given posting, ex. a 2
km grid. This should combat most problems with large ocean swell waves, as most
of the wave energy is below wavelengths of ∼300 m (Peral et al., 2015). However, if
the angle of propagation of the waves is perpendicular to the along-track direction,
the ocean waves can be aliased into larger wavelengths (Villas Bôas et al., 2022). The
filters used for this averaging also experience side-lopes below the cutoff wavelength,
and the effect might not be fully removed (Peral et al., 2015).

4.1.1
SWOT Data Formats
We saw that there are many steps needed to be taken before viable height obser-
vations are made, to tradeoffs between data spacing and height uncertainty. The
SWOT mission therefore provides many different data formats, and we use some of
them in this thesis. The data formats used are from the Level 2 processing step,
which is based on the Level 1-B data: the SLC. From L1B, the Level 2 data products
are created, where the hydrological (or finer resolution data) processing chain can
be seen in the appendix in figure A.4, and the oceanographic data can be seen in
the appendix in figure A.3. The three main data formats used in this analysis are
described here.
The data formats are unique to altimetry, as we now have distributed data in the
entire swath. The data format is therefore more similar to image data than the 1D
SSH profiles usually obtained.
L2 High Rate PIXC
The high rate (HR) data provided, is formatted as a distributed
pixel cloud. No gridding has been provided, and there are purely geolocated heights
from the interferogram. Determining a spatial resolution from this data product
is therefore diﬀicult, as it depends on the resolution of the interferogram, which
changes depending on the location in the swath as well as the quality of the data. It
therefore needs to be manually gridded. It provides the finest resolution SSH data
we have access to and due to its large data size is only provided at specific Cal/Val
locations, and crossover points in the 1-day Cal/Val orbit. See the L2 HR PIXC
Product Description (Williams and Desroches, 2022).
L2A Low Rate 250 m
The L2A low rate (LR) data is provided in the native KaRIn
grid, which means the grid depends on the actual location of SWOT at sampling
(which can deviate up to 2.5 km from the reference ground track (Desai et al., 2018)).
The data is gridded, and provided as a 2D array divided into left/right swaths, and
are indexed as; nadir and outwards and in along-track distance. Even though the
grid spacing is 250 m the half-power gain of the point target response indicates an
approximate 500 m resolution, such that the data is oversampled by a factor of two.
No geophysical corrections are available in this file, and interpolation from other
sources is therefore needed. See the L2A 250 m Product Description (Stiles et al.,
2022).
L2B Low Rate 2 km
The L2B LR data is a further development of the L2A data.
Gridded in a fixed-geographical grid at 2 km spacing, filtered and decimated from
the L2A data using a Hamming filter, it provides stackable data independent of
deviation from the reference ground track (Stiles and Dubois, 2023). This often
degrades the outermost pixels in the data array.
The data is provided in a 2D
array as with L2B, here combining left and right swaths to an array without data in
the nadir gap. The array is indexed: from left to right and increasing along-track.
Geophysical data products are interpolated into the L2B 2 km grid spacing and are

4.3
Beyond SWOT
As SWOT has been many years under development, being launched in 2022 a mission
proposal paper in 2010 (Durand et al., 2010) and a concept study in 2004 (Fu and
Rodriguez, 2004), active study into future missions is already being conducted.
A mission proposal called SEASTAR was published in 2019, tackling the geostrophic
assumption needed to determine SSC from satellite altimetry by Along-Track Inter-
ferometry (ATI). With ATI the Doppler shift acquired from the SAR images enables
measurements of directional sea surface motion directly. This would expand the
study of the ocean surface into the submesoscale (Gommenginger et al., 2019).
Another approach for obtaining the directional sea surface motion is the Winds and
Current Mission (WaCM) (Rodríguez et al., 2019). By utilizing the proven method
of scatterometry, they propose a Ka-band scatterometer to observe the Doppler shift,
and thereby observe the surface motion. Just as with WaCM, the proposed mission
Sea surface KInematics Multiscale (SKIM) would carry a Ka-band scatterometer,
in a conical scanning approach, thereby observing the directional surface motion
(Ardhuin et al., 2019).
Another approach to allow for a wider swath would be to consider a bistatic radar
where the transmitter and receiver are separated into two distinct satellites (Freeman
et al., 2010). The receiver would then measure the forward scatter of the ocean, as
opposed to the backscatter on conventional or monostatic radar systems. A variant
of this approach would be to utilize the GPS signals reflected off the ocean surface,
by multiple satellites orbiting at a low altitude (Freeman et al., 2010).

5.3
Spatial Resolution
The separation of altimetric noise and oceanographic signal has always been a major
undertaking in oceanographic satellite altimetry. Nadir altimeter data, especially
with the introduction of delay-doppler altimetry with CryoSat-2 (Wingham et al.,
2006), have fine along-track measurement spacing however the altimeter noise level
limits the ability to observe short wavelengths (Le Traon et al., 2008). This limit
depends on altimeter development, but also on geographical location. At favorable
conditions, studies show this to be down to 40 km for SARAL, 45 km for CryoSat-2,
and 50 km for Jason-2 (Dufau et al., 2016; Vergara et al., 2019).
The wavelengths able to be resolved by conventional altimeters, enable most mesoscale
features to be studied. However, the improved spatial resolution provided by wide
swath altimetry would resolve finer structures than before (Fu and Rodriguez, 2004).

trade-off with spatial resolution, compared to vertical height accuracy.

the swath altimeter. While we see the distinct altimetric noise at roughly 50 km

5.4
Wavenumber Spectral Slopes
Earlier we used the spectral slope to help determine the noise level of the altimeter.
However, the spectral slope is an important parameter telling us about the energy-
cascades, turbulence, and flow (Charney, 1971). One of the benefits of observing this
spectral slope as a function of time is then to be able to develop a more accurate
understanding of the time dependency of the ocean surface. As SWOT was in the 1-
day repeat orbit, we have a unique possibility to determine if it is possible to estimate
the spectral slope as a function of time, with a very high temporal resolution.
The spectral slope is determined from an ensemble of PSDs, with both cycle 6 and
21 used in the estimation, due to them both covering the East/Japan Sea. Each
estimation of the spectral slope thereby contains 14 cycles in total, thereby resulting
in an approximately 7 day average. This is then continued per 2 cycles, thereby
having 1-day overlap for each 7 day average.
Because the spectral slope is very sensitive to our choice of range on which we fit,
(when determined only on a subset of all observations), we determine a varying
amount of slopes. From these fits, we can determine statistical bounds where our
slope should lie. With this method, we show the uncertainty in slope estimation as
well as remove some of the bias associated with arbitrary choices of bandwidth. The
varying range where we determine the slopes is defined as:

see this phenomenon in the data, as opposed to a smaller SWH which would be

be possible to resolve. This would make it possible to supplement high-resolution
coastal studies acquired from high-frequency radar (HFR) (Yoo et al., 2018).
5.6.1
Ocean wave spectrum
To determine if what we see actually is the ocean gravity waves, we determine the
ocean surface spectrum. Studies have shown what we would expect the spectrum in
a field with ocean swell to behave like (Yu et al., 2021a; Villas Bôas et al., 2022). If
we determine the along-track and cross-track spectrum from the sea surface seen in
figure 5.13, we obtain the spectrum plotted in figure 5.14. We see a clear peak at
around 500 m wavelength in the cross-track direction. This same peak is however
very faint in the along-track and shifted somewhat towards longer wavelengths. This
peak is located where we would expect swell waves to be present in the wavenumber
spectrum. The shift and change of the peak is something we expect, due to the
directionality of the waves. We will discuss this in the next section.
This feature was also seen at other locations in the SWOT high-rate data product,
with varying magnitudes. However, due to the prominent waves seen in the Southern
Ocean, we have chosen to show the analysis at this location.
The effect of these ocean waves is important to understand, as they also can affect
the measured power at higher wavelengths, due to aliasing. This means this effect,
if not properly handled, can contaminate the ocean spectrum at wavelengths much
longer than the swell waves.
Several filtering techniques have been discussed to
combat this problem, something which will become more and more prominent when
we start observing smaller submesoscale features (Jones et al., 2023). However, it
seems the lower rate data products with 2 km grid posting should remove most of
the effect of ocean waves, while this effect might still be present at the 250 m grid
posting (Villas Bôas et al., 2022).

Sea Surface Slopes
Observations of the sea surface slopes (SSS) can enable us to map the marine grav-
ity field. It can be shown that the slope of the geoid relates to the gravitational
anomaly (Sandwell et al., 2006). However as the SSH contains both gravitational
and oceanographic signals, care must be taken.
To determine the SSS we computed the spatial derivative of the SSH, by computing
the centered differences in the KaRIn sampling grid. This means the derivatives are
in the KaRIn grid δ∥and δ⊥and must be converted to north-south and east-west
SSS, as described in section A.3 in the appendix.
The derivates are here done in relation to the SSH h to determine if we can see a
geodetic signal in SWOT data. As we saw in section 2.3 the derivative of the sea
surface is also used to determine geostrophic currents. We will consider this in the
next chapter, where we use the derivative of the OST η.

ICESat-2 Slope Comparison
Another satellite that is capable of cross-track slope determination, is the Ice, Cloud
and land Elevation Satellite-2 (ICESat-2), with three laser beams obtaining height
data in parallel across the track (Markus et al., 2017). From these parallel height
data, as well as along-track data, the total SSS can then be determined along the
track of the satellite, as illustrated in figure 6.7. We have developed a method that
computes these slopes while handling the noisy data. Another approach of obtain-
ing the directional SSS from ICESat-2 was published by (Che et al., 2021). The
preliminary results of the comparison between SWOT and ICESat-2 were presented
at AGU23 (Andersen et al., 2023a).
To determine the sea surface slope from the ATL12 data product, we need to account
for the specific sampling geometry. In practice, the adaptive algorithm of ATL12
changes the sampling of the data to between 70 m and 7 km, depending on the
number of return photons in each segment (Morison et al., 2021). This complicates
the cross-track slope detection, as illustrated in figure 6.7. We see here how the
individual photons (green dots), as given in ATL03, are distributed along each beams
groundtrack. Here we only see the two outermost beams, gt1 and gt3, with the mean
being denoted as gtx. The middle beam, gt2, also samples the sea surface and lies
approximately where gtx lies, but to keep the description simple we only see the
outermost cases. At areas with optimal conditions (clear skies etc, see more at the
ATBD (Morison et al., 2021)), we get many photon returns. However, at locations

Summary & Discussion
In the previous chapters, we analyzed the sea surface heights, slopes and surface
currents as obtained from the SWOT KaRIn instrument. We determined the spatial
resolution, and looked into differences between data formats. We have discussed the
primary error sources, as determined in the simulated data, and compared to the
initial Beta Pre-Validated we have access to.
A major error source was the roll error, which should be compensated from crossover
validation. However, due to the low number of crossover-validation points in the 1-
day repeat orbit, the initial validation does not account for the entire roll-error. Even
though the majority is compensated for (from 278 cm rms at crossover locations,
to 15 cm), the discrepancy between passes could still be reduced by simple local
cross-track detrend (from 15 cm to 5.8 cm). This is, however, something we believe
would be mitigated in the 21-day science orbit, to a minor level as determined in
(Rodriguez et al., 2017).
We determined the noise level of the altimeters using several different methods,
due to the intrinsic assumptions made when assessing the noise. At these methods
we see that the new KaRIn instrument has a significantly lower noise level than its
counterpart, the Poseidon-3C Nadir altimeter (0.51 cm vs 1.22 cm for oceanographic-
signal corrected rms). However the estimated noise is made under the assumption
that we can model the oceanographic signal, as well as the resulting energy being
from pure noise.
As we look into shorter wavelengths other error sources might
influence the SSHA, such as unresolved features in the geoid or MSS below O(10km)
wavelength (Pavlis et al., 2012; Schaeffer et al., 2023)).
Our best estimate for determining the spectral slopes with this noise was the simple
parametric model, as 1 Hz sampled nadir altimeters contains the spectral hump at
shorter wavelengths (Dibarboure et al., 2014). This is combined into this model,
and the effect on the determination of the spectral slope is reduced (Vergara et al.,
2019). We therefore saw that the agreement between the nadir altimeter and the
KaRIn instrument were much more aligned. However the assumptions made for this
model did not match the data from KaRIn, which influenced the noise level estimate.
Further research into parametric model fitting for this data would be beneficial.
As we have two passes located in the East/Japan Sea, it was possible to make a
time series of the spectral slope over the three month period, to see if it was possible
to determine the sea surface characteristics over time.

A great benefit of SWOT was the large area covered for each pass as we could map
almost the entire Ojin sea mount from only a single cycle of data over 13 days.
The benefit of the swath approach was also to be able to determine the north-south
and east-west component simultaneous, which is beneficial for both SSS and SSC
determination (Fu and Rodriguez, 2004; Stammer, 1997). East-west components of
the SSS has been challenging to determine, especially at low latitudes.
Handling the noise level when determining the SSS and SSC is of great importance.
As we saw that the SSS was determined from the SSH slope, and acting like a high-
pass filter, we need to suppress the noise. However traditional filtering techniques
involving convolutional filters have a dual problem; our limit of kernel size when
using long cut-off wavelengths, as to fit in the swath-width of SWOT, as well as
the impact on the amplitude of the signals we want to determine. We were able to
use a 50 km low-pass filter in our comparison of geostrophic currents with CMEMS,
however, this is the limit of the conventional convolutional filters due to the 50 km
swath width. We also saw how our filtering choices impact the observed amplitude,
when we determined the SSS at the Simpson seamount.
The determination of the SSC was inherently more challenging, as the noise in the
SSH is enhanced in the SSS, while our geostrophic assumptions breaking down in
the submesoscale region. Nevertheless, we could reach a similar spectral slope in
the region, from k−5/3 to k−2.5. We also saw some agreement between CMEMS
and SWOT, however not as well as we did in the SSH region. Further research into
the high-resolution SSH from SWOT concerning chlorophyll concentrations and sea
surface temperature might be of interest for the analysis of the ageostrophic features
of the SSH observations seen with SWOT.
With convolutional filtering, we achieved a good preliminary analysis of the KaRIn
instrument, and determination of the SSC. Multiple proposals for more advanced
filtering techniques have been proposed. A proposal designed to combat the prob-
lem of amplification of the noise when taking the derivative of the SSH, is to use
Tikhonov-regularization (Gómez-Navarro et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2023a).
Tikhonov-regularization makes it possible to obtain noise-reduced estimates of η, by
solving a least-squared problem, with regularization terms added to the cost func-
tion. We can specifically penalize large amplitudes and changes in the derivatives
of η. The cost function can be written as
J(η∗) = 1
2∥m ◦(η∗−η)∥2 + λ1
2 ∥∇η∗∥2 + λ2
2 ∥∆η∗∥2 + λ3
2 ∥∇∆η∗∥2,
(8.1)
where η∗is the found regularized heights, η is the observed noisy field, ∇= (∂/∂x, ∂/∂y),
∆= ∂2/∂x2 + ∂2/∂y2, m is a mask and ◦is elementwise matrix product (Gómez-
Navarro et al., 2020). In the case here, we are observing the fit of η∗to η, as well
as penalizing the first and second derivatives, as well as the gradient of the second
derivative. These relate to the geostrophic velocity, vorticity and gradient of the
vorticity. The mask m handles gaps in the SWOT data due to various features,
something a naïve convolutional approach lacks. Here the regularization variables
λ1, λ2 and λ3 need to be determined, which can be done globally, but also in a
specific spatial or temporal region, which makes this solution very adaptable.
Other approaches with potential for analyzing SWOT data are advanced image anal-
ysis techniques, which have advanced considerably in the last decade. With the new
swath-altimetry format, these tools would be applicable and could tackle the noise

problem, without impacting the amplitude or the edge-case problems with conven-
tional filtering techniques. The commonly well-performing methods usually involve
convolutional-neural-networks (CNN), usually arranged in the U-net architecture.
This structure has also been proposed for the SWOT mission as a noise mitigation
technique (Febvre et al., 2023).
A recurring problem is establishing a ground truth. How to discern what is noise?
Here we have used both in-situ gauges as well as assimilation models to determine
the performance of SWOT, however, in-situ gauges are very sparsely distributed and
models might not capture the ground truth at the moment of observation.
The construction of simulated data, using assimilation models as ground truth, and
then adding simulated errors, has created an environment well-suited for the train-
ing of neural networks. This has led to noise mitigation algorithms that currently
outperform the Tikhonov-regularization described earlier and are provided natively
in the future Level 3 data as a denoised SSH data product (Tréboutte et al., 2023;
PODAAC, 2023). Further work on deep learning techniques has shown the possi-
bility of designing these models to combat specific known noise in the same scale
as the target feature, as shown with noise reduction in global geostrophic currents
(Gibbs et al., 2023).
The development and handling of noisy data to achieve the highest possible res-
olution from the SWOT data is clearly a requirement for achieving the best ob-
servations from this mission. New techniques and methods need to be used as we
are provided with new data formats moving away from the conventional 1D pro-
file altimeters. While we have looked at the data resolution in SSH, SSS and SSC
and have achieved considerable improvement from earlier missions, we see that to
bridge the gap between high-resolution small-scale observations (HF-radar, optical,
airborne) and altimetry, development of these new algorithms need to be considered
(Soh and Kim, 2018; Lee and Kim, 2018; Villas Bôas et al., 2022).
Future mission concepts directly observing the SSC, such as SEASTAR, WaCM, and
SKIM might combat some of these filtering challenges as we are no longer relying
on SSH resolution to determine the geostrophic currents, however eliminating noise
to determine the geodetic signal would still be a priority for high-resolution gravity
mapping of the sea (Gommenginger et al., 2019; Rodríguez et al., 2019; Ardhuin
et al., 2019).

9
Conclusion
From the analyzed Beta Pre-Validated data from SWOT, we computed the SSH,
SSS, and SSC. We see how the altimetric noise level is reached at a far shorter
wavelength than with conventional altimetry and with the ability of spatial filtering
to improve vertical accuracy. The 3-month 1-day-repeat orbit has enabled a unique
view into short-time ocean dynamics and enables us to create a time-series of SSH
and SSC spectral slopes in the East/Japan sea, as well as observing the movement
of SSH features.
From the SSH obtained from SWOT, we see that it is possible to determine the SSS.
Geodetic signals is readily observable, where a near total map of SSS at the Ojin
seamount (∼200 × 250 km region) was obtained after only 13 days in the 21-day
science orbit. Initial validation against ICESat-2 SSS shows good performance.
Directional geostrophic currents are recoverable from the SWOT data, as we have
obtained SSS in both along and cross-track directions. Inherent noise in the data,
as well as the swath width of 50 km each side of nadir, results in challenges when
computing the geostrophic currents. Convolutional filtering of the SSH has enabled
SWOT data to match the observed data from CMEMS, while further development
of filtering techniques would be needed to utilize the full potential of the SWOT
data.
With the initial performance determined from the Beta Pre-Validated data, we
should expect the introduction of SWOT data to the current models of the geoid,
MSS, and global ocean currents to be a great improvement. Given the improved
calibration and design of methods utilizing the novel sampling geometry of SWOT
we can introduce data of unprecedented accuracy to the oceanographic models.
As the data from SWOT was released during the work of this thesis, we are expecting
the release of the Cal/Val analysis of the performance of SWOT soon and can expect
the scientific community to produce exciting new research using this data.

A.2
2D Fast Convolution
Convolution is a computationally complex operation, in a 1D case with O(N2) and
in the 2D case with O(N2M2). A more computationally effective way of doing this
convolution is by multiplication in the spectral domain. If we transfer our data as well
as our kernel to the spectral domain, we can simplify the computation extensively. The
mathematics are expressed in the appendix. This reduces the computational complexity
to O(N log N) for the 1D case, and O(NM log N log M) for 2D, which is significantly
faster. We therefore employ this method in this thesis for performing convolutions.
Therefore, for data x ∈CN×M (where C denotes the complex numbers) and a kernel

would be

A.3
Conversion from KaRIn coordinates to ENU
Observations done in the along-track and cross-track directions of the KaRIn swath
are orthogonal. This enables a full description of the observed surface, however, these
observations need to be converted to geographic coordinates in order to be applicable
for use with other systems.
Figure A.2: SWOT KaRIn L2B Coordinate System in relation to the geographic
coordinate system.
In the current configuration, SWOT passes have two ”modes” northward orbit or south-
ward orbit. This configuration is seen in figure A.2, where the angle θ indicates the hor-
izontal (to the earth’s surface) velocity component of the spacecraft to the geographic
north. In practice, this means θ might be between 10-20 degrees when in northward
orbit, or 160-170 degrees when in southward orbit. This does of course not hold at the
xiv
Appendix A. Appendix
==PAGE:100==
minimum and maximum latitude when the orbit switches direction, but this is a fair
approximation at latitudes closer to the equator.
In these two configurations, we can decompose the along-track (A) and cross-track (C)
observations into their two components; Ax, Ay and Cx, Cy, as indicated in figure
A.2. An important note is done when keeping the directionality of the observations.
As the KaRIn coordinate system rotates along with the spacecraft, but our geographic
coordinates keep still, we need to keep the N-E directionality in our observations. So
we need to add or subtract some components to allow this. The equations used are
indicated below.
If the angle θ is smaller than 90 degrees;
Ax = A sin(θ)
Ay = A cos(θ)
(A.10)
Cx = C cos(θ)
Cy = −C sin(θ)
(A.11)
If the angle θ is greater than 90 degrees;
ϕ = 180◦−θ
(A.12)
Ax = A sin(ϕ)
Ay = −A cos(ϕ)
(A.13)
Cx = −C cos(ϕ)
Cy = −C sin(ϕ)
(A.14)
The observation components are then finally combined in the geographical coordinates
Obsx = Ax + Cx
(A.15)
Obsy = Ay + Cy.
(A.16)
These steps are important when using SWOT data in geographic models or in relation
