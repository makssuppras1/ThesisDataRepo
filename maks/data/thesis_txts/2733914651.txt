Summary
This thesis consists of designing a network of services and implementing a method
to solve the liner shipping network design problem. To align with real-world sce-
narios is, the following included in the model: transshipments, rejection of de-
mand, weekly frequency, variable speed on each service and butterfly services are
allowed. The services for the network are established based on various factors,
including demand and location. There are presented 2 models: an arc formu-
lation and a path formulation.
Both models are formulated as mixed integer
programming problems where the objective function is to minimize the opera-
tional cost for the network. The arc formulation is solved with an MIP solver
in Julia, while the path formulation is solved with a greedy heuristic method
using column generation. The computational results are reported for the first 4
instances in the data set of LINER-LIB. Both methods report negative solutions
for the first 2 instances. The MIP model performs well on the smallest instance,
but the computational time increases rapidly for larger instances. The heuristic
solution has a faster computational time and reports results for all 4 instances.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Maritime transportation is an important factor for the international cargo trade
since over 80% of international trade volume is transported by sea. Compared
to other transportation modes, sea transport is suitable for long distances, can
travel between several continents, connecting ports worldwide, and has a large
capacity. However, this form of transport is very time-consuming because of the
large distances and possible waiting times associated with port calls. The weather
is also an important and unknown factor when sailing.
In 2022, was 12,027 million ton trade volume transported by sea, and UNCTAD
(2023) expects continued moderate growth in maritime trade volume from 2024-
2028. An average annual growth rate of 2.1% is expected during that period.
Furthermore, is the containerized trade volume expected to increase by 3.2% in
2024. Figure 1.1 illustrates the development of global containerized trade from
1996-2023 in million TEU annually and the percentage annual growth. We can
clearly see that the transportation of containers has increased over the years,
with some fluctuations.

1.1
Liner Shipping Network Design
The liner shipping business transports containers with cargo on vessels from one
destination to another based on demand. The liner-shipping company is referred
==PAGE:13==
1.1 Liner Shipping Network Design
3
to as a carrier, while the cargo owner is denoted as a shipper. The containers
are transported via a network of services. A network must be competitive and
eﬀicient since we are interested in a minimal cost.
A service is a cyclic sequence of ports sailed by a number of vessels.
In or-
der to give customers regularity and consistency, a service usually has weekly or
biweekly departures. A network can contain services where the demand can be
transported by multiple routes in order to respect the vessel’s capacity. Some
services might only connect to larger ports carrying large amounts of cargo. At
the same time, other services connect smaller ports to larger ports. Low transit
time, which is the time cargo travels from its origin to its destination, and few
transshipments are important in a network. Transshipment between services is
especially needed in large networks in case no service connects the origin and
destination. However, transshipments have a cost and take time. The demand
can vary over the year and can, for example, depend on harvest seasons, holidays
or summer vacation. The varies can have a global effect or affect a few ports or
trades. Christmas could be a rise for higher demand. The demand comes from
the shipper and estimates what needs to be transported from an origin port to a
destination port. Each demand has a revenue value that the carrier earns from
the transport. The vessels used in a network can be divided into vessel classes.
The capacity and speed are some of the main features, and another important
feature is the draft. The draft dictates which ports and canals the vessel can visit.
The containers transported are in two sizes: a 20-foot equivalent unit (TEU) and
a 40-foot equivalent unit (FFE). The FFE is used in this thesis.

The number of vessels needed to keep the weekly frequency depends on the total
travel time for a service. Suppose the travel time is 14 days then two vessels
are needed, sailing one week part. If the travel time is 5 days, only one vessel is
needed, however, to provide the weekly frequency, the vessel needs to be idling
the remaining 2 days somewhere along the route.
When creating a network, the following four aspects are important to consider in
order to relate to the real world.
• Transit time constraints.
Each demand has a time limit that must be
respected. Important when transporting fresh food.
• Transshipment cost. The cost when transshipping containers between ser-
vices. It is important to be correctly handled, relating to the real world.
• Rejected demand. Allowing the model to reject demand by generating a
penalty.

1.2
Literature review
Agarwal and Ergun (2008) present a mixed integer model of the ship-scheduling
and cargo-routing problem.
The model generates multiple routes, allows for
transshipment and includes weekly frequency by grouping vessels into vessel
classes. However, the transshipment cost is not included. Agarwal and Ergun
state that it is diﬀicult to include when the network is unknown. Agarwal and
Ergun were the first to use a two-stage algorithm for the liner shipping network
design problem. The paper presents three different solution methods: a greedy
heuristic, column generation, and Benders’ decomposition. Column generation
and Benders decomposition generate solutions of similar quality. However, the
column generation algorithm has a much longer computational time, which in-
creases with the size of the problem.
The two solution methods report good
results for up to 20 ports and 100 vessels.
Álvarez (2009) present a method to solve the problem of joint routing and deploy-
ment of a fleet of container vessels. Compared to Agarwal and Ergun, Álvarez
includes transshipment costs and butterfly routes are allowed.
However, the
transshipment costs are not correctly calculated because the model can not de-
tect transshipments for ports that have been visited multiple times. Álvarez uses
a two-stage algorithm to solve the problem. The paper presents a tabu-search
combined with a column generation procedure and A MIP solver to solve a case
study with up to 120 ports.
Contrary to Agarwal and Ergun, and Álvarez, Plum et al. (2014) introduced
a single-liner shipping service design problem, where a single route is designed.
An arc-flow formulation is presented, and Danzig-Wolfe decomposition is used to
formulate a path-flow problem. A branch-and-cut-and-price method is proposed
to solve the path-flow problem. The solution method is able to solve problem
instances with up to 25 ports and some in less than 3600s. However, the compu-
tational time increases with the size of the problem.
Brouer et al. (2014) give a thorough introduction to the liner shipping network
design operation research and introduce the LINER-LIB benchmark suit, which
is data based on real-life data from ”Maersk Line”, industries and stakeholders.
The integer-based model presented is an extension of Álvarez. The model includes
butterfly rotation and ensures weekly or biweekly frequencies. Furthermore, it
accounts for transshipment costs, so it is suited for butterfly rotation. Transit
time at ports, maximum transit time for each commodity and repositioning of
empty containers are not considered. As for Agarwal and Ergun, and Álvarez the
problem is solved in two stages. At every iteration, a multi-commodity flow prob-

lem is solved, generating new rotations based on the resulting flow. The problem
is solved using a heuristic column generation approach, applying a tabu-search
algorithm selecting a subset of rotations for the network. There are reported
results from 6 out of 7 instances.
Brouer, Desaulniers and Pisinger (2014) propose a matheuristic solution to the
LSND problem using integer programming.
The matheuristic uses four algo-
rithm components: Construction (C), improvement (I), reinsertion (R) and per-
turbation (P) heuristics. They use a greedy construction heuristic to create ten
solutions, and the best is chosen as the initial solution. The improvement heuris-
tic improves the current solution by solving an integer program to find a set of
improving port calls and removals for each service. The container flow for the
resulting solution is evaluated by a warm column generation algorithm, while a
simulated annealing scheme decides if the solution is accepted. The reinsertion
heuristic creates butterfly routes in the current solution to eﬀiciently utilise the
capacity of the network. The algorithm identifies, for each service, ports where
the outgoing voyage arc has no residual capacity and rates each port based on the
residual demand. The port with the highest rating is reinserted into the service
and is accepted if it improves the objective value or increases the amount of cargo
transported. The perturbation heuristic changes the composition of the current
service set. They test five scenarios using the LINER-LIB benchmark suite to
see if the four components contribute to improving the solution: C, CI, CIR,
CIP, and CIRP. First, testing the contribution heuristic alone and ending with
the full matheuristic. CI is an improvement from C, while adding P (CIP) has
a larger improvement in almost all cases compared to when adding reinsertion
(CIR). The full matheuristic (CIRP) performs best in all instances except for
WAF and WorldSmall where CIP has a better objective value. The paper com-
pares solutions to Brouer et al. (2014) and concludes that the matehuristic can
find improved solutions for the 3 of the large instances (Pacific, WorldSmall and
AsiaEurope). However, the results can not be directly compared since Brouer et
al. (2014) allow for weekly and bi-weekly frequencies, and this paper only allows
weekly frequencies.
The paper by Brouer et al. (2015) presents a mathematical model considering
transit time restrictions on the cargo flow. The model also allows for the rejec-
tion of demand and enforces weekly frequency. The algorithm used to solve the
LSNP-TT is an extension of the matheuristic presented in Brouer, Desaulniers
and Pisinger (2014).
In this paper, the improvement heuristic is the central
component. An integer program is solved as a move operator in a large-scale
neighbourhood search.
They use a method which estimates the impact of a
change in the flow due to insertion or removal of port calls. The change in the

flow and revenue is estimated by solving a set of shortest-path problems on the
residual graph of the current network. The algorithm finds profitable solutions
for 4 out of 6 instances, and for 5 of the instances, is 85% - 95% of the available
cargo transported.
Karsten et al. (2015) analyse the multi-commodity network flow problem with
transit time constraints and look at the consequences of excluding the transit
time restrictions. The transit time constraint limits how long the commodity
can be under transit in the network. The paper does not consider network de-
sign but only focuses on the flow of cargo through the network.
They use a
delayed column generation to solve the multi-commodity flow problem. The pa-
per thoroughly reviews how to handle transshipments and presents results from
3 different structures. The computational time with and without transit time
limits is compared, and there is no high increase when including transit time
constraints. However, the volume shipped decreases clearly when there is a limit
on travel time. The paper emphasizes that by omitting transit time constraints,
cargo might be transported by services that would not be accepted in practice.
Balakrishnan and Karsten (2017) consider service selection and cargo routing
with transshipment limits. They suggest a heuristic solution method by having
a list of candidate services as input, which means changing the problem from
service design to service selection. The model includes a limit for the number of
transshipments for each container and allows rejection of demand. There is pre-
sented a multi-commodity model established from flows along sub-paths in the
network. The method is tested on four instances from the LINER-LIB bench-
mark suite, with a limit of 2 transshipments per container.
Karsten et al. (2017) present a matheuristic for the competitive liner shipping
network design problem, CLSNDP. The paper considers transshipment time lim-
its for all demands and individual sailing speed on each service leg by enforcing
that the network is competitive on transit time and cost. The CLSNDP is based
on the model for the LSNDP in Brouer et al. (2014). To include transit time
restrictions, the multi-commodity flow problem is decomposed into a path flow
problem, where only paths respecting the transit time restrictions for a given
demand are feasible. The initial service is found by a greedy algorithm based on
distance and trade volume between ports. The key component of the matheuris-
tic is an IP, which considers which ports to insert or remove from a service, and
an IP adjusting the speed. The IP is extended to consider how inserting or re-
moving ports affects the transit time of the existing cargo flow. Furthermore, the
time-constrained multi-commodity flow is solved using column generation. The
paper presents results for both constant and variable speed, using the data from

The research paper of Krogsgaard et al. (2018) approaches the 2-stage method
backwards by first flowing the containers through a relaxed network based on
the demand from each commodity.
Designing the services for the network is
the second stage. The method is called backbone flow in Christiansen, Hellsten,
Pisinger, Sacramento and Vilhelmsen (2019). The initial solution is improved by
a variable neighbourhood search method. In each iteration of the local search, a
complex multi-commodity flow problem is solved using a fast Lagrange heuristic.
The Lagrange heuristic for flowing containers is 2 −5% from the optimal solu-
tion but is used as a guide for the variable neighbourhood search method when
designing the network. The paper uses LINER-LIB provided by Brouer et al.
(2014) and reports solutions for all 7 instances. Krogsgaard et al. (2018) is the
first heuristic to report results for the WorldLarge instances.
In the paper of Koza et al. (2018) is, a MIP-model presented. The model in-
tegrates service scheduling into the liner shipping network design problem. The
model allows for different speed between port calls in a service. Cargo transit
time limits are modelled more precisely instead of being approximated by a con-
stant. Furthermore, the model allows for any type of service, not just simple and
butterfly service types and assumes a weekly frequency for all services. To solve
the model, they propose a column generation matheuristic which combines linear
programming techniques with heuristics, referred to as the LSNDSP-CGM. They
present results from 6 of the 7 instances in the LINER-LIB benchmark suite,
solving instances with up to 114 ports. The objective value and cargo volume
are compared between the liner shipping network design and scheduling prob-
lem called LSNDSP with exact transshipment time, and the unscheduled liner
shipping network design problem denoted ULSNDP with 48 hours transhipment
time. The ULSNDP gives significantly better results for the 4 largest instances.
This is, however, expected since the optimal solution, under the assumption of
constant 48-hour transshipment time, is a lower bound of the optimal solution
for the LSNDSP. The results are compared to the research paper of Karsten et
al. (2017), showing that the results for the ULSNDP found by the LSNDSP-
CGM are better except for the average objective value for the instance with the
largest number of ports. However, the run time per iteration is longer compared
to Karsten et al. (2017). The paper concludes that integrated LSNDSP should
be considered over ULSNDP if competitive cargo transit times are relevant.

Contribution
This thesis aims to design a network of services which will allow cargo to be
transported between two ports not directly connected by any existing service.
Additionally, implement a solution method to solve the LSNDP. Two models
are presented as an attempt to address the LSNDP. The first utilizes a Mixed-
Integer programming (MIP) solver, while the second uses heuristics. Agarwal
and Ergun (2008) did not include transshipment cost, while transshipments were
not correctly accounted for in Àlvarez (2009). The models presented in this thesis
strive to correctly handle transshipments.

Data
Brouer et al. provide a data set for the liner shipping network design and the fleet
deployment problem. The data set has already been used by multiple research
papers. Christiansen et al. (2019) uses the data set to compare performance of
methods introduced in Brouer et al. (2014a), Brouer et al. (2014b), Balakrish-

3.1
Design of services
Designing the services for a liner shipping network from scratch can be diﬀicult
when either existing services or cargo flow is available to start. That leaves the
origin-destination pairs and the distance matrix to work with. This section goes
through 4 ways to design services.
Nearest neighbor
The first approach of generation services is based on the nearest neighbour tech-
nique. A random port is chosen as the start port, and the nearest neighbour
is found and added to the service. Then, the nearest neighbour to the recently
added port is found. A port already visited once can not be visited twice. When
the desired length is reached, the rotation returns to the start port. The method
does, however, only connect services close to each other. To update the method
and avoid creating similar services, the 4 nearest neighbours are considered, and
a probability for choosing the port is included. Ports with the shortest distance
have the largest probability of being chosen, while the ports with the largest
distance have the lowest probability.
Demand
When considering the origin-destination pairs, it can, for small instances, be
useful to make direct connections between the pairs with the highest demands.
Services with direct connections also avoided transshipment and the extra cost
associated with it. This is not suﬀicient for late instances where transshipments
are necessary. Another approach is to divide ports into 3 categories high, average
and low, depending on the demand each port should receive. Ports within the
==PAGE:30==
20
3 Design of services and network graph
same category are then connected. It is not enough only to connect ports within
the same category since, in many cases, the origin and destination ports are not
part of the same category. Hence, there needs to be services which include ports
from 2 or all 3 categories.
Area
Instances with ports there scattered over longer distances need services which
travel larger distances. The ports are then categorised after areas. A chosen
number of ports from each area is then connected in a service, ensuring vessels
sailing through different areas also visit ports on the way.
Butterfly services
Butterfly services where a port is visited more than once in the same rotation
can be of importance for instances with a smaller number of vessels available.
The port included twice is decided depending on high demand or if the instance
has a hub port.
As example are Baltic and WAF single-hub instances with
Bremerhaven in Germany and Algeciras in Spain as hub ports, respectively. The
butterfly services are for Baltic and WAF created such that the service, e.g. starts
in the hub port and visits 2-3 ports, then returns to the hub and visits 1-3 ports
before the rotation ends back in the hub port. In the Mediterranean and Pacific
instance is the port with the highest demand chosen to be included twice.
Improving services
When the first set of services is generated, and the models introduced in section
chapter 4 are solved, then are the services which are not included in the solution
analysed to find which ports should be removed, added or replaced? Furthermore
is the flow of demand analysed focusing on origin-destination pairs with the
highest rejected demand. New services where either both ports are included or
services where the demand can be delivered by transshipments.
Vessel types
When creating a service, it is important to keep in mind that some vessels are
too large to visit some ports. This depends on the draft size introduced in the
data section 2. For Baltic and WAF, which have 2 vessel types is a set of services
created without considering the vessel types. Each service is then created for
both vessel types. The draft size is considered after, and services that contain
ports the vessel type can not visit are removed. The Mediterranean and Pacific

CHAPTER 4
Mathematical Model
Christiansen et al.
(2019) define the liner shipping network design problem
(LSNDP) as follows: Given a collection of ports, a fleet of container vessels
and a group of origin-destination demands, a set of services is constructed for
the container vessels such that the overall operational expenses are minimized,
while ensuring that all demands can be routed through the resulting network
from their origin to their destination, respecting the capacity of the vessels. As
mentioned earlier, are we allowing rejection of demand in order to get a feasible
solution even though not all demand can be transported.
Christiansen et al. (2019) introduce four main algorithms for the LSNDP
• MIP-based algorithm - this approach is based on a MIP model which designs
services and flows containers through the resulting network.
• Two-stage algorithm - start by designing the services and then flowing con-
tainers through the resulting network.
• Subset of routes - generating a network by selecting a subset of sailing
services from a set of candidate services given by expert planners. This
approach avoided the processes of designing services and instead selected
services. This is called the liner shipping planning (LSP) problem.
• Backbone flow - this approach is in reversed order compared to the two-
stage algorithm. Starting by finding the flow through a network with all
connections between ports available. The needed services are found after-
wards.
In this thesis, we take inspiration from the MIP-based algorithm and the two-
stage algorithm. From the two-stage algorithm, we divide the process into two
tasks. Designing the services for the network and flowing the containers through
the resulting network. The process of designing services is, however, revisited
multiple times with the purpose of designing more suitable services to fit the
demands.

Column Generation
This section introduces the column generation technique used to solve the LSNDP
and presents the master and sub problem. Column generation is a technique for
solving (mixed) integer programming models with a larger number of variables
and is often used on large-scale problems. The following papers, Agarwal and
Ergun (2008), Álvarez (2009) and Brouer et al. (2014), use column generation
in the solution method. The column generation algorithm consists of solving a
master problem and one or more subproblems.

5.4
Solution method
This thesis describes a heuristic approach used to solve the LSNDP. Column
generation is used as a solution method for the path formulation. Since column
generation does not include all possible paths, the number of variables and con-
straints is reduced, providing a smaller model than was first intended. Column
generation is often used when solving large-scale problems. The disadvantage of
column generation is that an LP-relaxation is needed, allowing the solution to
have fractionally y-values. However, an integer solution is still required for the
LSNDP. To reach that is, the following two problems defined, which are used in
the solution method to find the integer solution.
• Problem A: The y-variable with the highest value is chosen based on the
latest executed column generation. The LRMP is resolved, and the chosen
y-variable is fixed to 1 while the remaining is fixed to 0.
The column
generation algorithm is executed, and the found objective value is saved.
• Problem B: The LRMP is resolved, and the y-variable found in problem
A is fixed to 1, and the rest remains untouched and can still be fractional
values. The column generation is then executed.
A y-variable can not be chosen if the vessel capacity is exceeded when doing so.
The heuristic solution method is described fully in the following paragraph. First,
the LRMP is solved, and the column generation algorithm is executed, providing
the mentioned fractionation solution. There are multiple approaches to choosing
which y-variables to fix to 1. In this thesis, is a form of greedy heuristic method
used. Instead of selecting the y-variable with the highest value, is there a selec-
tion between the three highest y-values in the following way. The A problem is
executed three times; for each run, the highest chosen y-variable is marked as
temporarily chosen, so a different y-variable is chosen in each run. The three
objective values are compared, and the y-value with the smallest objective is per-
manently fixed to 1, and the corresponding objective value becomes the upper
bound. The B problem is then executed, providing a new fractional solution. For
analysing is, the y-variables temporarily set back to being binary, and the RMP
is solved using a Gurobi solver in Julia, based on the paths generated in problem
B. This will show which services the MIP solver chooses as more y-variables get
fixed to 1. This process continues until no fractionally y-variables are left. This,
however, might not provide the best solution. If the upper bound at some point
keeps getting worse as y-variables are fixed to 1, then including a stopping cri-
teria would be of interest. Hence, if the upper bound three times in a row has
not improved, the algorithm stops. In this approach, the paths created with the
column generation are not saved, but new ones are found every time the column

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to set up a network of suitable services allowing
flow for cargo between ports not connected in a service and implementing a
solution method which handles transshipment correctly for the liner shipping
network design problem (LSNDP). Two models have been presented; the arc
formulation and the path formulation, both as minimization problems. The arc
formulation model is solved using a Gurobi solver in Julia and presents an optimal
solution for the given set of services. The model is tested on the Baltic and WAF
instances, obtaining profitable solutions in both cases. The model performs well
for Baltic with a computational time of 304.98 seconds. The number of decision
variables is, however, of concern.
The number of y-variables depends on the
number of services, while there is an x-variable for each arc for each commodity.
The model size increases rapidly when the set of commodities is increased. Baltic
has 554,730 x-variables and 87 y-variables with a set of 22 commodities. The
WAF instance had 1,151,625 x-variables, 120 y-variables, a set of 37 commodities
and a computational time of over 19 hours. It is, therefore, of interest to find
another solution method since the arc formulation is not suitable for large-scale
instances. The path formulation is solved with a greedy heuristic solution using
column generation. The heuristic method performs much better for the WAF
instance with a computational time of approximately 2.32 hours. The method
obtains profitable solutions for the Baltic and WAF but not for the Mediterranean
and Pacific instances. The obtained solution for Baltic is identical to the MIP
solution found for the arc formulation, which was an optimal solution. There
is, however, no way of knowing if the heuristic solutions are optimal.
Since
not all paths are considered and the chosen y-variables might not provide the
best solution. Additionally, the solution depends on the services provided to the
model. If the right connections between ports are not established or the needed
transshipments are not created, then a profitable solution might not be found.
The services offered for the Mediterranean and Pacific instances may not suﬀice
to achieve a profitable solution. Furthermore, including transshipment has been
correctly handled by introducing the complete transshipment structure for ports
with multiple services visiting, allowing for transshipments between ports. The
added arcs and nodes allow for transshipment cost to be included, which is of

7.1
Future work
This thesis has presented solutions for the first 4 instances of the LINER-LIB
data for future work would it be of interest to obtain solutions for the remaining
3 and larger instances. The arc and path formulation model have included trans-
shipment cost, allowed for rejected demand and has variable speed on services. It
would be relevant as a next step to include transit time constraints and observe
which influence it has on the solution values. In the same context, would it be
reasonable to allow speed optimization on each individual leg in a service. The
heuristic solution method runs the column generation algorithm multiple times,
however the previous paths are not saved. In future studies, would it be relevant
to save all paths so the set of paths the solution is found from is larger. Addition-
ally to the heuristic solution method, could it be an idea to include the branch
and bound method. A simplification of the network graph with either the star or
ring transshipment structure introduced in section 3.4 could be a way to decrease
the number of arcs and nodes related to a butterfly node, which could possibly
lead to lower computational times.
