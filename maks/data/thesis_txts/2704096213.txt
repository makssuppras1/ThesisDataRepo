Abstract
Heat makes up nearly half of the world’s energy consumption, about 2.5 times as much as
electricity. Yet, most discussions regarding energy and climate focus on electricity-driven so-
lutions. Recently the Danish start-up Heliac has introduced a new method for producing high
and low-temperature heat eﬀiciently. This was achieved by employing a novel foil-based solar
concentrating lens, which produces heat based on a principle otherwise referred to as Concen-
trated Solar Power (CSP). This thesis presents a novel approach for the design, operation and
optimization of the foil-based concentrating solar collector field. Due to the complexity of the
system, simulation has been deemed essential for assessing the operation and optimization of
the novel solar plant. Although preliminary work carried out on the simulation of large solar
collector fields exist, commercial tools such as TRNSYS showed several limitations, such as not
being able to solve for the flow distribution in hydraulic networks and lacking the flexibility to
rapidly simulate the effects of design changes. Therefore, a custom Python-based simulation
tool was developed, capable of performing a static and dynamic simulation of the solar plant.
The simulation tool is employed to perform a series of case studies, of which the first one is to
mimic an existing demonstration plant and compare the results with its real operating data.
It is found that the simulation tool is capable of simulating the complex interaction that takes
place within the demonstration plant to high precision. Additional case studies are performed
in order to optimize the existing liquid system design and operating strategy. As a result of
this, concrete proposals are specified for improving the current solar field’s engineering design,
such as changing the flow direction of the field and employing a feedforward control strategy.
In addition to this, proposals for future improvements are presented based on analysis of the
simulation results, such as the use of a buffer storage.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In the last several decades the world’s energy consumption has increasingly been supplied
through the use of renewable energy sources. In fact, with an expected increase by an average
of 2.3%/year between 2015 and 2040, renewable energy will be the world’s fastest-growing
energy source. Despite all this, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) forecasts
that only 23% of the world’s total energy demand will be supplied by renewable energy sources
by 2040, which includes renewable fuels such as biodiesel and biogas (Figure 1.1) [1].
Figure 1.1: World energy consumption by energy source where 1 kW = 3414 Btu. Source:
EIA 2017 [2] (edited)
When considering the world’s energy consumption, heat is likely to be the most overlooked
opportunity when increasing the share of renewable energy. According to the Renewable Energy
Policy Network (REN21), heat accounted for nearly 50% of the world’s energy consumption by
2015 (Figure 1.2). Of this, only 27% consisted of renewable heat, of which more than half was
generated using the highly disputed biomass [3]. Even though heat takes up nearly 2.5x as much
energy as electricity, most discussions regarding energy and climate focus on electricity-driven
solutions such as photovoltaic (PV) panels and wind turbines.
==PAGE:17==

The reason for this might be the diﬀiculty of generating cost-effective heat sustainably. Ac-
cording to the Energy Watch Group an estimated 75% of the global heat consumption, which
is a third of the world’s energy consumption, is used for industrial processes below 400°C [4].
Besides burning fossil fuels and biomass to fulfil this global demand, the options have shown
to be scarce. Feasible solutions in the lower temperature regions (60-70°C) exist, such as solar
flat panels. For higher temperatures evacuated tube panels can be used (up to 130°C), but are
much more expensive and have therefore shown to be less feasible [5]. On the more positive
side, Jesper et al. have shown how large-scale heat pumps powered by renewable electricity
are a strong contender for decarbonizing the industry [6]. However, technological diﬀiculties
must still be dealt with in the near future in order for this solution to be widely implemented.
So far, Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) systems have been shown to be one of the primary
market contenders for producing sustainable high-temperature heat (up to 200-400°C) [7]. Until
recently these systems worked by using mirrors to concentrate large amounts of sunlight on a
small area, called a receiver, and thereby greatly increasing the temperature. In turn, steam
is produced in order to power a steam turbine and generate electricity. Recently, the Danish
start-up Heliac has introduced a new method for producing high (and low) temperature heat
at better eﬀiciencies than its mirror-based competitors. The start-up from Denmark develops
solar fields with solar trackers, which carry foil-based concentrating lenses, that operate similar
to magnifying glasses.
Due to the complexity of this novel CSP-based solar field, the use of ballpark estimates for its
engineering design is only limited. The engineering design of the solar field entails among other
things the layout and dimensioning of the liquid distribution network, as well as the control
and operational strategies.
By making it possible to accurately predict the behaviour and
performance of the solar field, the impact of the chosen design parameters can be evaluated
and improved.
As well as the avoiding of expensive interventions later on.
Commercially
available simulation tools have exposed a number of limitations when targeting the various
design parameters of the novel solar field. Hence, a custom Python-based simulation tool is
developed.
The simulation tool allows for the study of various liquid network designs and
operating strategies, faster than its commercial counterparts.

Chapter 2
Theory
When discussing the solar plant and its operation, it is meaningful to start out by gaining
some basic knowledge and understanding. This chapter elaborates on the basic definitions and
operation of the solar plant, as well as the engineering design parameters that cause its unique
behaviour. To make matters less complicated, the solar plant is divided into three subsystems.
These subsystems are treated in order of magnitude and are the solar tracker, solar field and
the pump station and secondary side.
2.1
Solar tracker
A Heliac solar plant is built up out of solar trackers, whose primary goal is to transform the
solar irradiance into usable heat. A CAD model of the latest novel solar tracker prototype is
shown in Figure 2.1 down below [8]. The solar tracker carries a unique polymer-based Fresnel
lens which concentrates the solar irradiance onto a receiver that is positioned behind the lens.
These receivers are connected in series by a number of distribution pipes, through which runs
a heat transfer medium such as water or oil.
Figure 2.1: CAD model of the latest solar tracker prototype
==PAGE:20==
2.1 Solar tracker
5
In the current solar tracker design, 8 lenses of 1.6x1.4m are mounted on an aluminium frame
that is moved by 2 electric motors, giving the solar tracker 2 degrees of freedom (DoF). This
way, the sun is traced over the course of the day by facing the lens surfaces normal to the
sun. In this orientation, only the solar irradiance that is received directly from the sun without
having been scattered is utilized, and is denoted as the beam irradiance (Bn) or Direct Normal
Irradiance (DNI) [9]. The DNI varies during the day as a result of the height of the Sun above
the horizon, as well as the atmospheric conditions. Figure 2.2 shows the DNI versus time for
a largely clear day in June and April of 2021 in Copenhagen, Denmark.
Figure 2.2: DNI for a largely clear day both June and April of 2021 in Copenhagen, Denmark.
Source: DMI [10]
The solar concentrators, or lenses, are made from polymer foil sheets that contain micro-sized
Fresnel lens structures. These lens structures are laminated onto a sheet of glass using silicone.
This is different to conventional Fresnel lenses, which are obtained by dividing the glass lens
into a set of concentric annular sections and removing the unnecessary inner parts. This allows
for a significant weight and size reduction, and is shown schematically in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Schematic of the intersection of a conventional Fresnel lens (left) and Heliac’s
silicone lens (right)
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Solar field

As opposed to the conventional Fresnel lens, the polymer-based Fresnel lens from Heliac sees a
slight eﬀiciency decrease induced by its production process. The lens structures of the polymer
lens have small non-perpendicular angles with respect to the glass.
This can be observed
graphically by the grey area in the figure, which illustrates how the structure of the lens does
not allow for full utilization of the incoming solar irradiance.
The total eﬀiciency of the solar tracker can be defined as ”the proportion of solar irradiance
that is converted into available heat, and the reluctance to lose it once it is absorbed” [11].
In addition to the losses in the lens as a result of the lens structures, there are several other
factors leading to an eﬀiciency decrease of the solar tracker. These losses can be grouped into
two different categories:
• Optical losses
These are the losses induced by the lens, such as the losses due to the ineﬀicient lens
structures, as well as the reflection on the glass pane and the receiver.
• Thermal losses
These are the losses due to heat absorption of the lens and solar collectors, as well as
heat losses (or convection) in the receiver and piping on the solar tracker.
In 2020 the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) performed a thermal performance test
according to ISO 9806:2017 in order to obtain the standardized tracker eﬀiciency [12, 13].
The test was performed using the latest tracker prototype at the time, and concluded for
a total tracker eﬀiciency (ηtr) of 60.2%. The ISO method considers the solar tracker as an
open system, with incoming and outgoing mass and energy flows according to the first law of
thermodynamics.
2.2
Solar field
In 2020 the first full-scale demonstration plant was built on Møn, an island in south-eastern
Denmark, and is used to supply warm water to the local district heating (DH) network. The
prototype plant has a peak power (Pp) production of approximately 1.6 MW, which is the
production under clear sky conditions and a DNI of 1000 W/m2. This is approximately the
best-case scenario in Denmark. A simplified schematic of the solar field’s piping network is
shown in Figure 2.4.
Under normal operating conditions a solar tracker only generates several degrees of temperature
increase between its in- and outlet. In order to obtain a larger temperature increase, the solar
trackers are connected in series, which is referred to as strings. These strings of solar trackers
are in turn linked in parallel to increase the production capacity of the solar field. The in- and
outlets of each string are connected to a supply and return line, respectively. In the schematic
down below the supply and return lines are represented by the blue and red lines, respectively.
The supply line distributes the cold heat transfer medium that is coming out of the pump
station into 24 individual strings: 12 on the North side and 12 on the South side of the field.
Once heated up, the warm heat transfer medium coming out of the 24 strings is collected in
the centre of the field and returned back to the pump station. In the pump station, the heat
in the return line is transferred to the system for which the heat is produced.

2.4
Temperature control and controllers
The control strategy of solar energy systems is a complex topic to which entire books are
being devoted [14]. When operating a solar plant, reaching and maintaining a constant outlet
temperature is seen as one of the biggest challenges [15]. However, it can be achieved by using a
proper control strategy in combination with a number of actuators such as pumps and valves.
In order to reach the desired set-point temperature, the solar plant makes use of electronic
controllers. These controllers have to account for system disturbances in order to reach the
desired set-point. The most critical disturbances are:
• Variation of the DNI. These variations cause fluctuations of the power produced by a
solar tracker, and therefore changes its outlet temperature. This is the primary cause of
disturbance for which the control and operational strategy is optimized.
• Complex interaction between strings. Due to asymmetries, the path through the solar
field is different for nearly all strings, creating a complex interaction between the in- and
outlet of the solar field.
• Temperature fluctuations on the secondary side. Fluctuations of the inlet temperature
cause temperature fluctuations at the outlet, and are a result of the processes outside of
the solar plant.
• Flow rate disturbances on the secondary side. These are again caused by the processes
taking place outside of the solar plant.
• Fluctuating pressure differences caused by the change of temperature gradients in the
solar field
The biggest challenge when controlling a solar energy system, such as the novel solar plant from
Heliac, are the high dead times which are caused by the large capacity and fluid content of the
solar plant [16]. Especially under conditions of low DNI, and therefore low flow rates, it proves
hard to reach and maintain the desired set-point temperature. Due to the novelty of the solar
field, it is currently run using feedback control (see Figure 2.6). In feedback control, the mass
flow rate through the system is controlled with respect to set-point deviations. This strategy
generally yields long settling times and many fluctuations before a steady outlet temperature
is reached, despite being highly accurate.
Figure 2.6: Block diagram of a feedback control system for a solar energy plant
==PAGE:25==

Feedforward controllers (see Figure 2.7), in contrast to feedback controllers, control the outlet
temperature by evaluating the system model equations for the relation between the flow rate,
in- and outlet temperatures of the solar field and the DNI. When disturbances occur, the
necessary solar field flow rate will be set according to the predefined characteristics of the
solar plant. The biggest disadvantage of feedforward controllers is that they do not react to
set-point deviations. Only a highly accurate system model would allow the system to operate
satisfactorily. Therefore it would be more realistic for a feedforward controller to account for
set-point deviations by means of including a feedback control loop.

Chapter 3
Simulation tool background and
motivation
In order to perform more far-reaching optimization of the solar plant design and its operation,
the use of simulation tools has been deemed essential. Over the course of the last years, several
papers have been published on the simulation of solar collector fields. This section will discuss
the relevant work that has been performed in this field, as well as the limitations that have
been observed. By these means, the goals related to the development of a novel simulation tool
can be clearly outlined.
3.1
Literature review on solar field simulation
Even though this study focuses on the development of a novel simulation tool, preliminary work
on the simulation of solar collectors fields is existing. In Solar District Heating Guidelines, Le
Denn [17] distinguishes between 2 types of tools that are available for the simulation of large
solar collector fields for DH applications: dedicated and generic tools. Here, the first category
contains of dedicated solar DH tools mostly used for feasibility studies and rough estimations of
the system performance. The second type of tools that are discussed are suitable for performing
more detailed dynamic simulations. In this category, TRNSYS [18] is generally identified as
most suitable for this specific use case. This is confirmed by a range of publications where
TRNSYS is used for the simulation of large-scale solar fields. Raab et al. [19] developed and
validated a TRNYS model used for simulation of a solar assisted DH network in Hannover,
Germany. Similarly, Bava et al. [20] developed and validated a TRNSYS-Matlab model for a
large solar collector field used for DH purposes in Denmark. However, they identify a number
a limitations when it comes to using only TRNSYS for these purposes:
• TRNSYS cannot solve problems of flow distributions in hydraulic networks. Therefore,
Bava et al. [20] made a MATLAB model to communicate with TRNSYS which can solve
for the flow distribution in the hydraulic network.
• Many component models (e.g. specific types of solar collectors) do either not satisfy the
desired precision or do not exist at all and must therefore be custom integrated.
• As TRNSYS has a very wide use-case, and is not specifically developed for large solar
fields, there is only limited flexibility for low effort comparisons of different solar field
designs.
==PAGE:27==

• Simulation time is considerably high, likely due to the pipe models that are used, which
allow for very high precision in dynamic simulations [21]. There are however simpler pipe
models available which could potentially reduce simulation time significantly while still
maintaining a high level of precision [22].
3.2
Goal for a novel tool
In order to account for the limitations that are induced by TRNSYS, as well as allowing for the
integration of additional desired features now and in the future, it is justifiable to investigate
the development of a novel simulation and design tool as will be executed within this study.
Python will be the programming language of choice as it is open-source and will allow for
complete freedom when developing the simulation tool. As of 2020, StackOverflow’s annual
Developer Survey once again showed that Python is one of the most endorsed programming
languages worldwide [23].
The simulation tool should allow for the accurate prediction of the behaviour and performance
of the solar field, which is a result of the chosen liquid distribution network design and oper-
ational strategy. These design and operational parameters influence among other things the
pressure losses, heat losses, field eﬀiciency and general dynamic behaviour of the solar field.
In order to study the operational performance and optimize the solar field, it is identified that
the simulation tool must perform a static and dynamic simulation, respectively. These two
types of simulations are in line with the two types of simulation tools that were presented in
Section 3.1. The objectives for both of the two types of simulations are presented in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: The two types of simulation and their respective objectives
In order to improve the process of designing and optimization of the solar plant, the simulation
tool will be set up such that changes in the liquid system design and operational parameters can
be rapidly applied. This means that the routines and algorithms will not be hard-coded, but
rather be set up such that any variety of the liquid system design and operational parameters
can be solved for and simulated.
3.2.1
Static simulation
The primary objective of the static simulation is to develop a model which can solve the flow
distribution within the solar field.
The flow distribution model will then also be used for
performing a more accurate dynamic simulation. Once the flow distribution is solved, it can
==PAGE:28==
3.2 Goal for a novel tool
13
also be used to calculate other static system parameters. This includes the calculation of the
pressure drop in the system, the circulation time of the liquid between the in- and outlet of all
strings, and the variation in outlet temperature between strings of solar trackers.
The results that are obtained by the static simulation are calculated for a given set of operating
conditions, which is generally the worst-case scenario.
This worst-case scenario is defined
as the scenario in which the DNI is the highest it is likely to get in a specific geographical
location. Which in turn yields the largest pressure drop that is to be expected within the
liquid distribution network.
3.2.2
Dynamic simulation
The primary control parameter of a solar field is the outlet temperature of the plant, which is
regulated by controlling the flow through the field. Due to the mechanics of the solar trackers, a
relatively low flow is required to reach the desired outlet temperatures. This low flow rate yields
high death times within the system, making it challenging to control for a steady-state outlet
temperature. Additionally, fluctuating operating conditions (i.e. changes in inlet temperature
and solar irradiation), as well as pathways of different lengths through the distribution network,
lead to complex behaviour and further increase control challenges.
The objective of the dynamic simulation is to develop a realistic model of the solar field which
allows for simulating its dynamic behaviour. This way, the effect of the liquid distribution
network design and operating strategy can be assessed and further improved.

Chapter 4
Static simulation
This chapter presents the background, theory and methods that have been used for performing
the static simulation of the solar field. This includes among others the solar field performance,
heat losses of the distribution piping and water distribution modelling. The exact sequence of
operation and integration of the theory and methods into code is not treated.
4.1
Solar field performance
In Chapter 2 it was discussed how the performance of the solar plant is influenced by the losses
in the solar tracker and solar field. Under normal operating conditions the eﬀiciency of the solar
tracker (ηtr) is found to be 60.2% [12], while the eﬀiciency of the solar field is characterized
by the heat losses in the distribution piping. Knowledge about these losses can be used to
calculate the instantaneous performance of the solar plant. The instantaneous performance of
the solar plant is the performance under some fixed/stationary operating conditions. During
the design procedure the most relevant case is generally the case during peak load, or full load,
operating conditions. These extreme operating conditions are most relevant as it produces the
highest power, flow rate rate and pressure drop, whilst having the lowest eﬀiciency.

4.3
Water distribution modelling
Thus far, the liquid in the solar plant has been denoted as heat transfer medium.
In the
context of the solar plant, this can refer to among other things water, a water-brine mixture
or synthetic oil. In the current solar plant design, where the set-point temperature is below
200°C due to mechanical limitations, water is set to be the heat transfer medium of choice.
Although synthetic can oils have preferable thermal properties, spillage into the environment
due to leakages of the solar plant is highly undesirable and could cause trouble with local
legislators [28]. This section, therefore, focuses on water distribution modelling, as this will be
the heat transfer medium of choice. Besides several minor differences the theory largely applies
to other heat transfer media regardless.
4.3.1
Goal and background of water distribution modelling
In water distribution modelling, the goal is to obtain the flow distribution in the piping network.
In case of the solar field design, operation and optimization this is primarily relevant for the
following two reasons:
• Once the flow distribution is solved it can be used to calculate the pressure drop in the
solar field
• A variation of the flow between strings causes a difference in the outlet temperature
between the strings, which influences the dynamic operation of the solar field
==PAGE:34==
4.3 Water distribution modelling
19
There are several analysis methods for solving of the water distribution in piping networks.
According to Kelly at al. [29] the three predominate analysis methods that have been used
over the course of the last 40 years are the Newton-Raphson, Linear Theory and Gradient
methods.
All three of these methods have been widely adopted by a range of commercial
tools [30]. However, in order to achieve complete integration between the static and dynamic
simulation, as well as include the effect of custom components such as the receiver, a custom
tool is developed. The method that has been adopted for solving the flow distribution in the
solar field is the Hardy Cross method, which is an iterative algorithm that can solve for the
flows in a system where the in- and outlets are known. Although the method was first presented
in a publication by Hardy Cross in 1936 [31], it is still one of the most widely used methods
for the solving of flow distribution in piping networks [29].

4.3.3
The Hardy-Cross method
The Hardy-Cross method has been adopted to find the flow distribution and pressure loss of
the solar field. This section will present the concepts, iterative steps and integration, as well
as an example of the method.
Concepts
In his original 1936 paper, Hardy Cross [31] starts with describing the two major conditions
his method was based upon:
1. Continuity of flow
The total flow into any node or junction is equal to the total flow leaving that same node
or junction (∑Qin −∑Qout = 0). This principle is commonly known as Kirchoff’s First
Law, and was originally introduced for the use in electrical circuits.
2. Continuity of potential
The total potential change along any closed path must be equal to zero (∑h = 0, where
h is head loss), which is a reference to Kirchoff’s Second Law. In practice this means that
the head loss between two nodes is equal, regardless of the path that has been taken.
This is the primary cause for the outlet temperature difference between the strings, and
is one of the most important phenomena describing the behaviour of the solar plant.
The algorithm and its integration
There are two different solution methods, referred to as the loop method and the node method.
The first method is used when the in- and outlet flows are known, while the latter is used when
the in- and outlet pressure heads are known. In the case of the solar field, the in- and outlet
flow of the solar field is given, and therefore the loop method is used. In this method, the
equation for head loss is generalized as:
h = rQn
(4.19)
where: h : Generalized pressure head losses
r : Head loss per unit flow
n : Flow exponent (2 in case of Darcy-Weisbach)
The r-factor can be derived from Equations (4.14), (4.16) and (4.18).
The iterative steps of the Hardy-Cross loop method are listed below:
1. Assume flow distribution
Assume the quantity and direction of the flow, maintaining the continuity of flow accord-
ing to Kirchoff’s First Law.
2. Determine each closed loop
All closed loops in the system should be detected. In order to allow the simulation tool to
quickly analyze various pipe network designs, a graph theory algorithm has been utilized
which allows for finding the fundamental set of cycles (or loops) of a graph [36].

4. Calculate the total R (potential) around each loop
Without reference to direction (all R’s should be positive), find ∑R = ∑nrQ(n−1)
5. Calculate the change in flow

5.2
Pipe model
In operational solar plants, temperature fluctuations due to disturbances are commonplace. As
these temperature waves propagate along the distribution network they get dampened, elon-
gated and further disturbed due to the mixing and splitting of flows. The accurate prediction of
the wave propagation phenomena is essential when performing an accurate dynamic simulation
of the solar plant. Accurate finite difference and finite volume pipe models for the dampening
of pipes do exist, but these can often only be applied for fixed flow rate conditions [37]. The
discretization of the pipe into a finite set of nodes does generally not allow for conditions where
both the flow rate and the temperature fluctuate over time, as is the case with the solar field.
Furthermore, despite these models being highly accurate they have a long computation time
as it takes a large number of iterations to solve for a single time step of a single pipe section.
5.2.1
The node method
In order to deal with these issues, the so-called node method has been implemented and plays a
central role in the dynamic simulation. This method was first introduced in 1991 by Benonysson
[38] as a way to study the dynamic operation and optimization of district heating systems. In
2008, Gabrielaitiene et al. [22] further examined and validated the node method as a way of
evaluating wave propagation in district heating pipelines.
The node method considers the pipe to consist of two nodes, namely, at the in- and outlet of the
pipe, and is demonstrated using Figure 5.2. For every time step, a block with a given volume
and temperature is inserted into the inlet of the pipe. At the outlet node, a weighted average
temperate is obtained from a block or combinations of blocks that have the same volume as the
one inserted into the inlet, thus taking into account the flow time and changes in flow velocity
over time. The obtained temperature is then corrected to account for the effects induced by
the pipe wall heat capacity and heat losses in the pipe.

The relevant background and theory concerning temperature control of the solar field was
touched upon in Section 2.4.
This section focuses on the feedback control method that is

5.4.1
Current control method
As of now, the pump speed of the solar field is controlled using a proportional-integral (PI)
controller, where the speed of the pump is regulated with respect to temperature set-point
deviations. In order to perform a realistic dynamic simulation of the existing demonstration
plant, a similar feedback control loop to the one of the solar field is used. The block diagram
of this control loop is shown in Figure 5.6.
Figure 5.6: Block diagram of the feedback control loop used in the dynamic simulation
==PAGE:48==
5.4 Control strategy
33
When large and long-lasting disturbances occur the error term can grow very large for a sig-
nificant period of time. This causes the integral term to start accumulating a significant error,
and is known as a phenomenon referred to as integral windup [39]. Once the accumulated error
grows very large, the actuator, in this case the main pump of the solar field, cannot follow the
given command and saturates. The first measure that is taken to prevent the controller from
giving an unobtainable pump speed command, is to cap the output of the controller by limiting
its output. This is done by using a saturation check, referred to as the pump speed limit on the
block diagram. This saturation limiter makes sure that the pump speed command cannot go
below the minimum ( ˙mmin) and maximum ( ˙m100) pump speeds, respectively.
However, this does not yet prevent the integral windup problem from occurring.
In order
to prevent this from happening, an anti-windup protection measure can be taken, where the
integral term is being limited between an upper and lower bound. This way, the control loop
will immediately respond once the sign of the error changes. The anti-windup method that
is currently used is referred to as clamping, which essentially turns the integrator off once it
satisfies both of the following two conditions [40]:
• The output of the PI controller is saturating
• The error has the same sign (negative or positive) as the output of the PI controller
On the solar field there are several temperature sensors available that can be used for the feed-
back signal of the feedback loop. As of now, 3 methods for determining the outlet temperature
have been considered:
1. True solar field outlet temperature
This method uses the temperature sensors at the solar field outlet, whose physical placement
is in the pump station, right in front of the primary heat exchanger. Even though this gives
the most accurate outlet temperature, extra delay times are induced due to the long path
length from the string outlets to the pump station.
2. Average string outlet temperature
This method utilizes the temperature sensors placed right at the outlet of each string. By
taking the average outlet temperature a prediction is made of the outlet temperature that
will, after some delay, reach the pump station at the outlet of the field once all branches are
joined back together. By using the instantaneous average outlet temperature of the strings
extra delay times are prevented. However, heat losses between the strings and solar field
outlet fluctuate depending on the operating conditions, and can therefore potentially cause
control instabilities.
3. Maximum string temperature
Instead of taking the average string outlet temperature, this method uses the maximum
string outlet temperature for calculating the set-point deviation. The primary reason for
this method to exist is due to practical considerations. During the operation of the solar
plant, disturbances may cause only several strings to overheat massively, which is highly
undesirable due to the mechanical properties of some solar tracker components.
Because it is highly undesirable for an individual string to overshoot in temperature, the latter
maximum string temperature method is currently used to operate the solar field.

Although Equation (5.15) presents a relatively accurate inverted model of the system, no model

normally uses the average temperature for set-point deviation calculation, and only resides to
the maximum string outlet temperature if a certain threshold is reached.

5.6
Operational strategy
The operational strategy of the solar plant entails the control of actuators such as pumps and
valves, that allows for the most stable and desirable operation. In practice, there are many
factors that can cause a deviation from the most desirable operation, and for which certain
protection mechanisms are put in place. For example, the case in which the pressure in the
system increases rapidly, action is being taken by opening a valve and letting some liquid out
of the system.
In the case of the solar field simulation, the most important mechanism to replicate is the
bypass on the secondary side, as this has far-reaching effects on the behaviour of the solar
plant. During the start-up of the solar plant in the morning, as well as during the occurrence
of large disturbances when in operation, the set-point temperature is not always satisfied. This
is a highly undesirable situation for most processes for which the solar field is producing heat.
When the solar field produces a temperature that is too high. the flow rate on the secondary
side is increased, as was treated in Section 5.5. However, when the outlet temperature is too
low, the bypass on the secondary side might be used in order to redirect the secondary outlet
flow back into the secondary inlet side.
A simplified but representative illustration of the
temperature in the distribution network during start-up is presented in Figure 5.10, as well as
an enlarged image is made available in Appendix A. The illustrations present a time sequence
consisting of the following steps:
1. All liquid in the solar plant is in equilibrium with the environment.
2. Pumps start running and the solar field starts to slowly produce heat.

4. The lukewarm heat transfer medium has passed the bypass and is now starting to heat up

optimal gains would be to go through the conventional steps of modelling and control design.

process dynamics of a step response. Once such a step response is characterized, the extracted
parameters can be used to calculate the control parameters through the use of a table. Once
the Ziegler-Nichols method has been used to find an initial guess for the PI controller gains,
trial-and-error is used for further narrowing down the optimal control parameters.

6.3
Analysis and interpretation of the results
Once the static and dynamic simulations have been performed the data must be analyzed and
interpreted. This section presents the relevant methods that are used to analyze the simulation
results of the static and dynamic simulations, respectively.
6.3.1
Telos of the solar plant
Before presenting the analysis criteria that will allow for the assessment of the static and
dynamic simulations, another point of discussion is raised. Namely, that of the telos of the
solar plant. Around 350 BC the Greek philosopher Aristotle proposed the idea of telos, which
translates to end, purpose or goal. It refers to “the inherent purpose of each thing, the ultimate
reason for each ting being the way it is, whether created by human beings or nature” [45].
The reason for considering the telos of the solar plant, is that the results of the dynamic and
static simulation must be interpreted in its broadest sense. However, different analysis criteria
might present contradicting suggestions of what seems to be “the most well-operating solar
plant design and operational strategy”. The concept of telos allows for making a distinction
between the different analysis criteria that will be used to describe the behaviour of the solar
plant. This discrimination between criteria can be achieved through teleological thinking, which
is the attribution of the purpose and causes to the solar plant. In an attempt to establish the
telos of the solar plant, a proposal is given as follows:
“To convert the solar irradiance into usable heat as eﬀicient as possible, while
realizing outlet conditions that are as stable as reasonably possible”
6.3.2

is 1000 W/m2. Only in the case of the validation case, case study 1, the DNI will be varied
from the worst-case scenario in order to perform a comparison with the real operating data.

Each of the analysis criteria presented in Table 6.7 relate to a different set of liquid system

considering the cost-benefit of each change of the liquid network design and operating strategy.
6.3.3
Analysis criteria for the dynamic simulation
The dynamic simulation produces a deep insight into the complex interaction that occurs
within the solar plant, as it enables to analyze the temperature, pressure and mass flow rate
of each individual pipe section over time. The data produced by the dynamic situation can
be graphically displayed, and subsequently analyzed. However, even though the graphs will
be used to discuss the behaviour of the solar plant globally, a more rigorous assessment is also
desirable in order to assess the performance of the solar field. To achieve this, a number of
well-known terms from control theory will be used. Figure 6.5 shows a sample step response,
as well as some key performance criteria that are used to assess the behaviour of the signal.
Figure 6.5: Sample step response showing the rise time, overshoot, settling time and steady-
state value. Source: Feedback Systems [46]
The terms that are used are in accordance with the assessment of the performance of a step
response in control theory. Because the PI controller uses an integral term, the steady-state
value (yss) is by definition the set-point temperature of the solar field, and will therefore be
neglected in the assessment of the results. The rise time (Te) is the amount of time required
for the signal to go from 10% of its final value to 90% of its final value. It is, however, possible
to define these limits otherwise if desired. The overshoot (Mp) is the percentage that the signal
rises above the final value, where the zero-point is set to the ambient temperature. Lastly, the
settling time (Ts) is the amount of time it takes to stay within a certain percentage of its final
value. Where, similar to the rise time, limits are arbitrarily chosen. In case of the assessment
of the solar field both the 5% and 2% settling times, Ts,5% and Ts,2% will be considered.

7.1.2
Dynamic simulation
Two dynamic simulations of the start-up phase of the solar plant have been performed. The
case study that was initially proposed has an ambient temperature (Tamb) of 20°C, which
means the water in the whole distribution network starts out at 20°C. However, it could be
observed from the operation data that the water in the real distribution network does not cool
entirely back down to ambient temperature overnight, which in turn influences the behaviour
of the solar plant during its start-up. This is the reason why initially the solar field’s inlet
temperature is higher than its outlet temperature. To account for this phenomenon, a second
simulation was performed with an ambient temperature of 30°C. This might help to explain
some of the behaviour that occurs within the solar field during start-up. The results of the
simulated outlet temperatures of the solar field, as well as the real in- and outlet temperatures
during operation, is presented in Figure 7.4. More elaborate simulation results can be found
in Appendix C.
At first glance, it might seem like there are some considerable differences between the performed
simulations and the real operating data. However, this simulation is performed with the exact
gains (Kp = 5 and Ki = 0.02) that are also used in the real solar field on the 3rd of September,
2020. When deviating from these gains, the behaviour is vastly different, as it makes that
the solar field’s outlet temperature looks nothing like the real operating data. Similarly, the
operating strategy that was used on this 3rd of September used a reduced maximum pump speed
(Nmax) of 80%. This was found necessary in order for the solar field’s outlet temperature not
to start fluctuating uncontrollably. Similarly, some additional simulations showed that when
increasing the limit of the maximum pump speed above 80%, it instantaneously makes the
solar field run out of control. This shows that the dynamic simulation is capable of simulating
the complex interaction that is taking place within the solar field.

The pipe model that is used for simulating the wave propagation in the distribution network
is known to have a number of limitations.
Gabrielaitiene et al.
[22] elaborate on this by
acknowledging that thermal diffusion is not considered in the modelling, and consequently the
rapid changes in inlet temperature result in inadequate prediction of the critical areas in the
temperature profile. This is clearly observed by the fact that the periodic oscillation of the
simulation keeps persisting even when the amplitude is really low. In reality it is more likely
that thermal diffusion causes these low-amplitude fluctuations to even out within the flow.
In contrast, the pipe model has shown to be very effective in predicting the timing of the
temperature wave, which is very important when simulating the complex interaction that
occurs within the distribution network. This positive characteristic of the pipe model is also
reflected by the results from case study 1. Changing the controller gains and pump speed
limits have an effect on the timing of the wave propagation through the distribution network.
Choosing parameters that do not reflect the demonstration plant also causes the simulation
results to diverge from the operating data, to very high sensitivity. In addition to this, as
opposed to some more advanced methods the current pipe model allows for quick computation
of the wave propagation in the whole distribution network.

Case 8: Feedback and feedforward control
The goal of case study 8 is to show the benefits of the newly proposed feedforward control
strategy. This is not done by considering the start-up of the solar plant, as is the case for all
other case studies, but instead by considering a temporary step disturbance in the DNI. This
is a phenomenon that is very common and one of the primary issues when dealing with the
optimal control and operating strategy of the solar plant. To do so, the simulations starts with
increasing the DNI from 0 to 700W/m2, after which the system is allowed to stabilize. Once
stable, the solar field is subjected to a temporary step disturbance from 700 to 500W/m2 that
is held for 10 minutes, after which the DNI is raised back to 700W/m2.

However, the primary strength of the feedforward control strategy is observed when considering
the temporary step disturbance that takes place right on the 1 hour, or 3600 seconds mark.
The feedforward controller predicts the mass flow rate based on, among other parameters,
the DNI. Once the DNI drops, the controller instantaneously reduces the mass flow rate in
order to maintain the desired outlet temperature. Depending on the precision of the prediction
model, the PI controller only needs to make slight adjustments in order to get back to the
desired set-point temperature. In case of the currently used feedback control strategy, it can
be observed that the DNI disturbance causes a large fluctuation for which the PI controller
must guide the solar field back to the set-point temperature over time. Overall, it seems like
the newly proposed feedforward controller is able to cope with DNI disturbances very well.
This is a positive indication, considering the variety of other disturbances whose parameters
are included in the prediction model as well.

Chapter 8
Proposals for optimizing solar field
design and operation
This chapter discusses the proposals that are presented for optimizing the solar field’s liquid
system design and operating strategy.
This involves concrete proposals based on the case
studies that have been performed, as well as suggestions for future work.
8.1
Proposals based on current results
The dynamic and static simulations that have been performed in relation to the 9 case studies
have demonstrated the complex interaction that is taking place within the liquid distribution
network of the solar plant. This complex interaction was first observed when performing the
simulations for the first case study, the validation case, whose goal was to validate the simulation
tool by mimicking the existing prototype plant. When varying the simulation parameters to
be slightly different than the ones used in the prototype plant, the results of the dynamic
simulation instantaneously started deviating from the real-life operation.
The other 8 case studies, case studies 2 to 9, have been performed in order to optimize the
liquid system design and operational strategy of the existing solar plant. It was demonstrated
by the simulation results that changing individual parameters, no matter how minor, results
in a significant impact on the static and dynamic operation of the solar plant. This shows
that the ideal design for the liquid distribution network, as well as finding the ideal operational
strategy, is a complex multi-dimensional problem. This complexity was for example observed
during case study 2 when trying to find the ideal controller gains Kp and Ki.
The initial
suggestion, with Kp = 1 and Ki = 0.004 yielded a satisfactory operation. When increasing
either Kp from 1 to 1.2 or Ki from 0.004 to 0.006, a less favourable dynamic operation was
observed. However, when increasing both gains at the same time, Kp from 1 to 1.2 and Ki
from 0.004 to 0.006, the system suddenly performed better than the initial guess with a Kp of
1 and a Ki of 0.004.
The findings above show that it is nearly impossible to present concrete one-dimensional pro-
posals that allow for the optimization of the solar field design and its operation. However,
the simulations that have been performed so far have already contributed towards a deeper
understanding of the behaviour of the solar plant. This understanding is used to present a
number of guidelines, concrete proposals and suggestions that allow for the optimization of the
solar field design and its operation:
==PAGE:82==
8.2 Proposals for future improvements
67
1. Currently, the flow direction of the field is referred to as out to in. It was found that the
newly proposed in to out flow direction will improve the dynamic performance of the solar
field during start-up. The downside of this flow direction is the relative increase of return
lines, which transports the hot heat transfer medium. Therefore, the insulation thickness
should be slightly increased in this section of the distribution network.
2. The newly proposed feedforward control strategy greatly improves the dynamic performance,
and should therefore be utilized in future solar plants. The primary focus of the case studies
has been on the start-up procedure of the solar field. Here, the feedforward control strategy
already showed a slight benefit in terms of stability and speed in reaching the desired set-
point temperature. However, the most significant improvement was observed when the solar
field is subjected to large disturbances during operation, such as a cloud passing over the
solar field.
3. Increasing the set-point temperature of the solar field can lead to a significant increase of
the heat losses, and therefore a decrease in solar field eﬀiciency. The currently used 50mm
of insulation is too little when going to a set-point temperature much above 100°C. A higher
insulation thickness would therefore be recommended.
4. Minor changes are likely to allow for slight improvements of the solar field, such as finding
the right balance in the sizing of the distribution piping.
However, nearly every design
change of the liquid system causes the complex interaction that is taking place within the
distribution network to change as well. Therefore, the simulation tool should be used to
re-calibrate the controller gains when making minor design changes such as changing the
insulation thickness or pipe sizing. This also applies to changing the operational parameters,
such as the in- and outlet temperatures of the solar field, which might change as a result of
the process for which the heat is produced.
5. The in- and outlet temperatures of the solar field are specified based on the system for which
the heat is produced. Decreasing the temperature difference between the in- and outlet, and
therefore decreasing the required temperature lift by the solar field, increases the mass flow
rate in the system. In this case, maintaining the current configuration with 6 trackers per
string leads to an undesirably high pressure loss, and should therefore be avoided.
6. Depending on the required temperature lift, or the physical space that is available to install
the solar plant, it might be desired to change the number of trackers per string or the
number of strings.
Increasing the number of trackers per string from 6 to 7 creates a
dynamic pressure drop that is too high, and is therefore discouraged. However, decreasing
the number of trackers from 6 to 5 does lead to satisfactory dynamic operation, and could
be considered if desired. In this case, the range of operation of the solar tracker receivers
should be further investigated.
8.2
Proposals for future improvements
When the DNI is low during the start-up procedure of the solar plant, it can take multiple cir-
culations before the desired temperature-lift and set-point temperature is reached. In addition
to this, because the system is running at such a low flow rate during start-up, a large overshoot
of nearly 20% is not uncommon.
==PAGE:83==
8.2 Proposals for future improvements
68
In order to deal with the phenomenon of large overshoot during start-up, it is proposed to
investigate the use of a non-linear gain, also referred to as gain scheduling (GS) [47]. When
employing this strategy the Kp is multiplied by a multiplier based on the magnitude of the
set-point error and chosen gain curve, such as the parabolic gain curve shown in Figure 8.1.
Although this adds some complexity to the control system, the flexibility of the simulation
tool will allow for quickly finding a satisfactory gain curve. The non-linear proportional gain
strategy is likely to work better than adding a derivative term to the PI controller. Slower
systems such as the solar field might cause the derivative term to drift when the error occurs
for a long period of time, as is the case during start-up [46].
Figure 8.1: Proposal of a new parabolic gain curve
A second proposal for future optimization is related to the liquid system design of the solar
plant. Once the solar field stops producing heat by the end of the day, the warm liquid is still
located in the return lines, which can easily be up to several thousands of litres. By adding a
buffer storage on the secondary side, as shown in Figure 8.2, the otherwise wasted heat can be
stored, and used to warm up the plant the next morning. Using the operating temperatures
of the current prototype plant, the payback time of this storage buffer should be less than 10
years. Depending on the operating temperature of the solar plant, it might also prove beneficial
for the overshoot phenomenon. In addition to this, the storage buffer might also prove useful
as a bypass. By adding a coil inside of the storage buffer, it will become thermally stratified,
and therefore dampen the large temperature fluctuations that occur during bypass mode.

Chapter 9
Conclusion and outlook
This thesis presents a novel approach for the design, operation and optimization of a foil-based
concentrating solar collector field. This novel solar field, developed by the Danish start-up He-
liac, employs new technologies based on the Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) principle. Due
to the complexity of the system, simulation has been deemed essential for assessing the opera-
tion and optimization of the novel solar plant. Although preliminary work carried out on the
simulation of large solar collector fields exist, commercial tools such as TRNSYS showed sev-
eral limitations, such as not being able to solve for the flow distribution in hydraulic networks
and lacking the flexibility to rapidly simulate the effects of design changes. Therefore, a cus-
tom Python-based simulation tool was developed, capable of performing a static and dynamic
simulation of the solar plant.
The simulation tool has been employed for performing nine case studies with varying solar field
liquid system designs and operating strategies. The goal of the first case study was to mimic
the existing prototype plant and compare the results with real operating data. It was shown
that the static simulation is capable of estimating the real operation behaviour of the solar
plant, although it seems to slightly underestimate the pressure losses. In case of the dynamic
simulation the tool is found capable of simulating the complex interaction that is taking place
within the liquid distribution network, and well within the precision desired in order to use the
tool for the development of future solar plants.
Eight additional case studies have been performed with the goal of optimizing the liquid system
design and operating strategy of the existing solar plant. It was shown that a deep understand-
ing of the behaviour of the solar field is key for finding the most ideal engineering design, as well
as helping to develop solar fields that can serve their telos. Concrete proposals are specified
for improving the current solar field’s engineering design, such as changing the flow direction
of the field and employing a feedforward control strategy. Additionally, proposals for future
improvements are presented, such as the use of a buffer storage.
Future work on the simulation tool should accommodate the integration of the proposed design
improvements for the solar field’s engineering design. This involves including the pump station’s
liquid system components and the proposed buffer storage into the simulation models. But
also a reevaluation of existing models, such as the pipe model, should be considered. Although
it allows for quick and accurate simulations of the temperature wave propagation, it does
not consider thermal diffusion, and therefore inadequately predicts the critical areas in the
temperature waves. In addition to this, dynamics that are currently not yet considered, such
as the shadowing effect between solar trackers, will allow to further increase the accuracy of
the simulation tool.
