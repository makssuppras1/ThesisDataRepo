Abstract 
Climate change will increasingly impact the building industry, as the globe is continuing to get 
warmer each year. The EU Taxonomy is an attempt at creating a guideline, which will help inves-
tors improve their sustainability, and reduce climate change. In the taxonomy, criteria are set out 
for adapting building to climate change, which has been analyzed in this thesis, to create a stand-
ardized methodology. The EU Taxonomy does not include a minimum requirement for complexity 
or contents, so this minimum requirement is assessed and will be the foundation of the proposed 
methodology. This work is created based on the EU Taxonomy’s annex 1 and 2, a Q&A with the 
EU commission from December 2022, the standard for risk assessments “DS/ISO 14091”, and a 
review of already-existing literature and methods in this field. The physical climate hazards, 
which might impact buildings in Denmark are presented based on literature reviews and the ‘Best 
Practice Guidance for climate risk assessments’, made by Rambøll and the EU commission. To avoid 
the ‘black box problem’, all climate assessment methods will be made using free-to-access data 
and models. 
The minimum requirements for the climate risk and vulnerability assessment were found to be: 
 The expected lifetime of the building. 
 A catalogue of climate hazards, which might impact the building in its lifetime. 
 Three assessments; current climate, 30 years in the future and at the building’s end of life. 
 Climate projections for each assessment, using RCP 8.5 or SSP5-8.5 as a minimum. 
 Quantifiable evaluations of economic and social risk, for each climate hazard assessment. 
 A catalogue of adaptation solutions, which can reduce the found climate risks. 
 A choice of adaptation solutions, which reduce the most important climate risks. 
o For new buildings, these are implemented at the start of operations. 
o For existing buildings, these are implemented in a period of up to 5 years. 
In Denmark, 9 climate hazards that should be assessed at a minimum are presented as: ‘Heat 
waves’, ‘Frost’, ‘Wildfire’, ‘Storm’, ‘Heavy precipitation’, ‘Flooding (coastal, fluvial, groundwater)’, 
‘Coastal erosion’, and ‘Subsidence’. Methods for assessing the economic and social risks, as well as 
likelihood of these climate hazards is presented. Together with these, each climate hazard will 
also have a catalogue of potential adaptation solutions, highlighting trade-offs and co-benefits in 
relation to other hazards. This is a first step in making future projected climate risk assessments, 
at a complexity that is valid for building operators, while being in line with the EU Taxonomy. 
Lastly, potentials for digitizing this work, along with limitations and wishes for future work is 
presented, highlighting the importance of automation and inclusion of risk assessments to the 
environment.

Introduction 
Building industries across the globe, and their respective building regulations, are all trying to 
make sure that buildings stay for a long time and are not a risk to people using them. The buildings 
are made resilient to the context of which they are built, increasing their lifetime, and reducing 
the risks of weather impacts. However, people have realized that we are in the midst of a rapidly 
changing climate, which will prove to be fatal, if we follow the business-as-usual approach. The 
impacts of climate change are already evident, with building owners experiencing the destructive 
consequences firsthand. Flooding from intense downpours, building subsidence and coastal ero-
sion, leading to complete building destruction, are just a few examples. Ignoring these climate-
related damages will only lead to more severe consequences in the future. So, to ensure prepar-
edness, it is crucial to gain a deep understanding of the risks involved and implement adaptation 
solutions to ensure operation of the building in the future. 
The EU commission has realized this, and in response, released a guideline on how to conduct 
sustainable investments, the EU Taxonomy. In the EU Taxonomy, one requirement is to make a 
robust climate risk and vulnerability assessment, to ensure that the future climate is considered 
for investments. Unfortunately, there is currently no universally accepted method for precisely 
identifying these risks, especially related to buildings. Furthermore, many of the current climate 
risk assessments, suffer from the ‘black box’, where it is uncertain, which methods and data are 
used. The approaches are also often qualitative, based on old experience and gut feeling, which 
might have been sufficient in the past, but will be lacking for the future climate. Moving away from 
qualitative approaches to quantitative approaches, with transparent data, provides a common 
ground for comparing climate risk assessments, while increasing knowledge across the industry, 
on this pressing matter. 
In this thesis, an attempt will be made to create a methodology, for conducting a robust climate 
risk and vulnerability assessment, in accordance with the EU Taxonomy. This methodology 
should serve as a minimum required complexity level, for buildings in Denmark, and should be 
supported with additional risk assessments, if the specific building plot requires it. This will be 
done through a deep dive into the EU Taxonomy, its referenced norms, and relevant literature on 
climate hazards in relation to buildings. The relevant climate hazards in a Danish context will be 
explored, along with how to scope the assessment correctly in regard to geographical-, sectoral-, 
temporal-, political-, and risk. For each of the climate hazards, some relevant adaptation solutions 
for existing buildings, and for new building projects will be presented, which can be supplied with 
more if necessary. 
The methodology will be applied to some buildings, managed by PFA, which have been provided 
access to, through cooperation with Sweco. This will be done to assess the potential shortcomings 
of the methodology. 
For each of the climate hazards, it will be discussed how well they are representative for the im-
pact, that they are supposed to illustrate, along with a concluding discussion on the risk assess-
ment framework as a whole. This will include some recommendations for further work, and re-
flections on what could have been improved.  
 
The research question for this thesis is:  
“How can a standardized methodology be made for robust climate risk and vulnerability assess-
ments, as defined by the EU Taxonomy, using free data, in relation to buildings in Denmark?”

Background and Literature review 
In the following chapter, several topics are explored on sustainable finance and climate change, 
related to buildings. This will be explored through important literature, from the EU, UN, Govern-
ment officials, insurers, and other actors in the building sector. This will be used as a foundation 
for the proposed methodology, to assess climate related hazards’ risk on specific buildings, and 
solutions for adaption. 
 
The EU Taxonomy 
On the 5th of January 2023, the corporate sustainable reporting directive, “CSRD”, entered into 
force. It is an EU law that forces all large companies and all listed companies, except listed micro-
enterprises, to disclose information on what they see as the risks and opportunities arising from 
social and environmental issues, and on the impact of their activities on people and the environ-
ment. This is meant to help investors, civil society organizations, consumers, and other stakehold-
ers to evaluate the sustainability performance of companies, as part of the European green deal 
(EU commission, 2022/2464). 
The EU Taxonomy is then an attempt from EU to make a common classification system for coun-
tries in the union, to identify sustainable activities, and direct investments towards more sustain-
able projects. It was created to help the EU-countries, meet the targets and objectives in the  
European green deal and the Paris Agreement. Furthermore, the EU Taxonomy is a tool for  
identifying activities, which must be reported on, as part of the CSRD, as well as a way for inves-
tors to avoid greenwashing. The EU Taxonomy is therefore not a certification on ‘product level’, 
as we are used to in the building sector, but a tool to assess a financial institution’s investments, 
as illustrated in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of the EU Taxonomy vs. common "sustainability" certificates, used in Denmark. 
On the 12th of July 2020, the Taxonomy regulation was published. It established the basis for the 
EU Taxonomy with conditions that an economic activity has to meet, to be eligible for the taxon-
omy. The Taxonomy established six environmental objectives - climate goals -, that a company 
must not do any significant harm to, in their activities, as well as possibilities to provide substan-
tial contribution to these environmental objectives (EU commission, 2020/852). The regulation’s 
article 8 provided information on how to report turnover, capital expenditure, CapEx, and opera-
tive expenditure, OpEx, as sustainable, for activities. 
Then in June 2021, the 1st Delegated Act was released, providing criteria on how to do no signif-
icant harm, DNSH, and how to provide substantial contribution to climate goal 1 and 2, in separate 
annexes for each of the first two climate goals.

Annexes to the EU Taxonomy 
Two annexes are released, at the time of writing this report. One for substantial contribution to 
climate goal 1, along with DNSH criteria for climate goal 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6, and one for substantial 
contribution to climate goal 2, with DNSH criteria for climate goal 1, 3, 4, 5 and 6. Since this report 
focuses on buildings, the activities in the EU Taxonomy, whose criteria that will be presented are: 
 Activity 7.1 Construction of new buildings 
 Activity 7.2 Renovation of existing buildings 
 Activity 7.7 Acquisition and ownership of buildings 
The DNSH criteria for climate goal 2, presented in annex 1 and climate goal 2’s substantial contri-
bution criteria in annex 2 are presented in the following. For each of the three activities, the word-
ing of the substantial contribution- and DNSH criteria are the same and will therefore be pre-
sented for them all, collectively. 
 
Climate change adaptation – Do No Significant Harm criteria 
The DNSH criteria for climate goal 2, climate adaptation, refers to complying with the criteria set 
out in Appendix A to Annex 1. This Appendix A can be seen in Appendix 5, to this report. It can be 
summarized as (EU Taxonomy: Annex 1, 2021): 
1) A screening of which physical climate risks that may affect the performance of the activity, 
during its expected lifetime, from the hazards named in Table 1. 
2) Where there is assessed to be a risk, a climate risk- and vulnerability assessment to assess 
the materiality is made. 
3) An assessment of adaptation solutions that can reduce the identified risks is made. 
4) The economic operator implements the adaption solutions, which reduce the most im-
portant climate risks.

The climate projections for the future climate risks must be at the scale of the activity and it’s 
expected lifespan. If an activity’s lifetime is expected to be less than 10 years, the assessment is 
made, at least by using the smallest appropriate scale of climate projections. Every other activity 
uses the highest available resolution, on future scenarios, including at least, 10-to-30-year climate 
projections for major investments. 
A plan for implementing adaption solutions over a period of up to five years, must be drawn up 
for activities, that already exist. If it is a newly built physical asset, the adaptation solutions must 
be implemented before operations. 
These adaptation solutions are not allowed to adversely affect the resilience to climate risks of 
other people, of nature, of cultural heritage, of assets and of other economic activities. Further-
more, they have to be in line with local, regional, and national plans. Furthermore, nature-based 
solutions should be considered, and blue or green infrastructure should be relied on to the extent 
possible. 
 
Climate change adaptation – Substantial contribution criteria 
The criteria under the substantial contribution section are similar to the above DNSH criteria, the 
main points, that the substantial contribution criteria have, that the DNSH criteria does not are 
(EU Taxonomy: Annex 2, 2021): 
 The adaptation solutions have to be implemented for substantial contribution. 
 The adaptation solutions are monitored and are remedied if they do not work as intended. 
 If the solution itself is physical and can be associated with an activity, listed in annex 2, it 
must comply with the DNSH criteria for that activity. 
 
An important aspect in regard to the substantial contribution criteria for climate change adapta-
tion is mentioned in Article 8 of the Taxonomy Regulation, which states: 
 
An activity’s turnover is therefore not counted as sustainable if it is aligning with the EU Taxon-
omy through substantial contribution to ‘Adaptation to climate change’, since this requires imple-
mentation of adaptation solutions. Only the CapEx and OpEx can then be reported as sustainable. 
“Allowing for turnover from [Climate] adapted activities to count [as sustain-
able] could be misleading: once the ‘substantial contribution’ to adaptation of 
an activity is made (i.e., it is made resilient to climate change), it is questiona-
ble if the turnover associated with that activity (which may or may not have 
environmental benefits) should count as sustainable.”

Future climate change scenarios 
To predict the future trends of climate change, many researchers have tried to look at the past 
and present, and then predict the future, based on different political agendas (Miljøstyrelsen, 
2023a). The main driver behind these future projections is the UN’s intergovernmental panel on 
climate change, IPCC. Their main goal is to release reports, which shares knowledge of climate 
change, while being unpolitical. At the time of writing this thesis, they have released 6 reports, 
the latest being this year, 2023 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2023b). 
IPCC 4th report - SRES scenarios 
In the 4th report, 2007, the UN released the so-called SRES scenarios, which predicts future green-
house-gas emissions, from assumptions of population-, economic- and technology changes. The 
main scenarios are called: A1B, A2, B2 and EU2C. 
IPCC 5th report - RCP scenarios 
In the 5th report, 2014, the UN released their RCP scenarios, ‘Representative Concentration Path-
ways’. In contrast to the SRES, the RCP scenarios are modelled as stabilizing scenarios. This is 
meant to help decision makers see the future impact of their political decisions. These scenarios 
are accompanied by a number, representing the radiative forcing, resulting from various levels of 
GHG concentration in the atmosphere. A summary of the RCP scenarios is seen in Table 3.

DS / ISO 14091:2021 | Guidelines on vulnerability, impacts and risk assessment 
DS/ISO 14901:2021 gives guidelines on how to assess the risk related to potential climate haz-
ards from climate change, as well as a guide on assessing adaptive capacity. The standard de-
scribes different methods for assessing risk, e.g., IPCC’s method in their 5th report, seen in Figure 
5. This is the most simplistic way of visualizing the relationship between the different risk com-
ponents in a risk assessment, that the standard presents.  
 
Figure 5: Relationship of the main components of risk assessment from IPCC's 5th report. 
The figure shows that the climate risk is a combination of exposure and sensitivity of an activity, 
to a hazard, and that there exists two risk-levels. One without any climate adaptations and one 
with implemented adaptations. The methodology has 5 steps for conducting a successful climate 
risk and vulnerability assessment, which in short are: 
1) Establishing the Scope and Objectives of the Assessment: 
Firstly, the goal and scope of the assessment is established. Including defining the geo-
graphic-, temporal-, sectoral- and political scope of the activity. 
2) Collecting Climate Data and Projections:  
Climate data and projections for future scenarios are collected. Identifying credible 
sources of climate data, historical data, and data relevant to the geographic and sectoral 
scope is necessary. 
3) Identifying Vulnerabilities and Impacts:  
Identification of direct impacts, like changing temperatures, sea levels and rainfall, as well 
as indirect impacts, such as market changes and political changes, to the activity. The vul-
nerability, sensitivity, adaptative capacity and exposure is then assessed. 
4) Assessing Risk:  
The risk is identified through vulnerabilities, exposures, sensitivities, and hazards. This 
involves estimating the likelihood of the different climate change impacts occurring and 
the severity of their potential consequences. 
5)  Developing Adaptation and Mitigation Strategies:  
The most appropriate strategies to mitigate the worst climate risks should be imple-
mented, looking at both physical and non-physical strategies. Co-benefits and trade-offs 
from different strategies and the cost-effectiveness of them should be assessed before im-
plementations.

Publications on future climate related risks to buildings and people 
Climate change’s impact on the environment, is not assessed as a risk for building projects in this 
thesis. This could however be done, similarly to an LCA, which takes multiple impact categories 
into consideration, like climate change, biodiversity, ozone depletion, land use, etc. The UN, for 
example, has in a study chosen to tackle the climate and biodiversity together and looks at how 
much of mammal habitats is lost due to climate change (UN, 2022a). Multiple studies have how-
ever found that it is hard to directly quantify the impact to nature, caused by climate change, es-
pecially because of the limited understanding of biological adaptation limits in the changing eco-
systems (Magnan et al., 2021). Furthermore, the EU Taxonomy is using a financial lens, to assess 
sustainability, which can easily be associated with social impacts and economic impacts, unlike 
environmental impacts, which cannot directly be translated to a cost of damage (Magnan et al., 
2021). The definition of risk will therefore, in the next sections only be considered as direct eco-
nomic losses, and social health risks to people. 
To identify which climate hazards that are relevant in relation to costs and people, a review is 
made of publications, looking into future climate risks and hazards for buildings. Publications 
with an economic and socioeconomic focus are chosen from different disciplines that touch the 
building sector or release scientific papers analyzing the sector, such as: Insurers, governmental 
institutions, educational institutions, Investors, nonprofit sustainability organizations and compa-
nies, working in the industry.

A. Lexology – Climate change, extreme weather, and property damage 
This paper focuses on the increasing risks of climate change induced weather damage to proper-
ties in the UK and takes the point of view, of insurance companies. They conclude that insurers 
are likely to see more claims of weather-related property damage, from the following (Brown et 
al., 2020): 
1) Extreme frost, leading to burst pipes in buildings. 
2) Subsidence, leading to cracks in buildings. 
3) Floods, ruining homes, and business properties. 
B. Dr. Michael H Smith - Assessing climate change risks and opportunities for investors 
The information in the report is based on climate impacts in Australia and provides a list of rele-
vant climate impacts for the Australian building sectors, as well as adaption strategies. The report 
focuses on the following climate impacts (Michael H. Smith, 2020): 
1) Heatwaves, covering increased air-conditioning energy and water consumption costs. 
2) Storms, covering damage to construction sites and property. 
3) Heavy precipitation, covering impact damage to buildings from floods and melt water.  
4) Drought, covering reduced water availability for production. 
5) Pluvial flooding, covering the increased costs from construction delays, property asset 
damage and higher insurance costs. 
6) Wildfire, covering increased property asset damage and disruption to essential services, 
because of damage to houses and other buildings. 
7) Sea level rise and storm surge-coastal inundation, covering coastal property damage. 
C. Danish Environmental Protection Agency “Klimatilpasning.dk” 
The Danish environmental protection agency and the ministry of the environment, in collabora-
tion with several ministries, agencies and organizations, have created a website for sharing infor-

nesses. The climate impacts, that the website posts as relevant for buildings in Denmark are 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2023c): 
1) More frequent storms, covering recommendations for both existing buildings and new 
buildings. 
2) Water levels rising, covering damage from water level and groundwater rises in Den-

heavy rainfall and storm surges. 
3) Wetter climate, pointing out that an increase in air humidity and rainfall will increase 
the risks of rotting and other degrading processes of building materials.  
4) Snow, thaw, and frost, covering the damages that comes from thawing snow that can be 
compressed and weigh heavily on a building. 
5) More extreme rain, focusing on flooding, caused by precipitation. 
6) Warmer weather, covering more heatwaves and the increased need of cooling in build-
ings.

4) Flooding from sea levels rising is assessed and the risk is determined by looking at a 
risk table.

During this thesis, on the 29th of March 2023, the EU Commission published a report, providing

further. The engineering consultancy firm, Rambøll, produced the report with support from the

Climate risk and vulnerability assessment - Proposed methodology 
On the topic of climate risk and vulnerability assessment, a few things have become apparent in 
the foregoing chapters. Many stakeholders are involved and interested in climate change related 
hazards, with different points of views on what is important. Most assessments, mentioned in 
literature (Table 5), are based on a qualitative approach, with no direct way of evaluating the 
magnitude of risk. This is likely because experts want to keep their methods for evaluation secret 
from competitors, or maybe because they do not have a standardized approach (Kadijk et al., 
2022). This is a big problem, which the research project ‘ClimINVEST’ also highlights and calls ‘the 
problem with the black box’. The problem with this ‘black box’, is that results from different ex-
perts can’t be compared, when their choice of methodology and data isn’t transparent. The relia-
bility of the results is also sometimes questionable (Jun et al., 2020). Because of this, the proposed 
methodology will be based on free-to-use, open-access data, provided by the Danish meteorolog-
ical institute and other data providers. This is also in line with the EU Taxonomy, which promotes 
and demands transparency. 
 
Figure 7: Illustration of how the 'Black Box' can turn potential good data into a bad output.

The minimum number of hazards assessed, in the following methodology, is therefore based on

also mentions how some climate hazards leads to other hazards, like drought leading to reduced 
water availability and heavy precipitation leading to flooding, so called cascading effects (Dr. Mi-

Landslides are, according to a short interview with ‘associate professor of geotechnics and geology 
at DTU, Irene Rocchi’, not relevant for buildings in Denmark – they do however pose a threat to 
infrastructure. Coastal erosion on the other hand, is a big threat, and acute coastal erosion poses 
similar problems to landslides in other countries. Maine’s department of agriculture even uses 
the words coastal erosion and coastal landslides, interchangeably (DACF, 2015). Landslides will 
therefore, in this methodology, be assesses as a coastal landslide, along with coastal erosion. 
Drought is mostly mentioned as leading to reduced water availability or leading to subsidence 
(NRP, 2022). In Denmark, the groundwater is not at risk of drying up as it is in other countries, 
and we do not have a reduced water availability (Bolius, 2020). Denmark will therefore experi-
ence increased water availability, which is why drought will not be evaluated as resulting in re-
duced water availability. It will however be evaluated and assessed as an affecting factor on sub-
sidence and wildfire.

General risk assessment structure 
To ensure similarity between the different climate hazard’s risk assessment, a general structure 
is made, which will be based on the criteria from The EU Taxonomy, the structure of ‘DS / ISO 
14091:2021 | Guidelines on vulnerability, impacts and risk assessment’ and guidelines from the 
‘Technical guidance’, which have all been explored in earlier chapters. The methodologies in the 
literature matrix (Table 5), won’t be used since they are mostly qualitative assessment methods, 
that still require other ways of estimating the exact materiality of the hazard. Climate risk assess-
ment methods for the social and economic impacts will be analyzed later.

With this, the start of the century to middle of the century represents the minimum required 10-

The political scope of the future, to use for extracting climate data, is not necessarily straight for-
ward. The newest future scenarios, the SSP scenarios, should be used, seen earlier in Table 4. 
Based solely on the Danish political environment, Denmark is seen as a leading country in regard 
to energy and climate, and has reduced its emissions of greenhouse gasses, constantly, over the 
last few years as seen in Figure 8 (Regeringen, 2018). The same is the case for the rest of Europe, 
which is also, in its entirety, reducing greenhouse gas emissions. 
 
Figure 8: Annual CO2 emissions from Denmark and Europe, based on 'Our World in Data’ (Our World in Data, 2021) 
These results are however skewing the Danish population’s perception of how much they actually 
emit. The chosen method for calculating a country’s CO2 emissions, is not taking the CO2 emissions 
from imported products into consideration (D. Moran, 2023). Denmark’s actual emissions are 
much higher, and the Danes are actually emitting about twice as much in other countries, because 
of import-demand, than they do in Denmark. Denmark’s consumption footprint was larger in 
other countries for 8 out of the 11 categories, which are used by ‘Denmark’s Statistics’ to evaluate 
Danes’ consumption. Along with this, emissions for all 11 categories have increased, globally, 
from 2010 to 2020 (Illiev et al., 2021). This increase is furthermore proved by looking at the entire 
world’s climate CO2 emissions, seen in Figure 9. From this it can be seen, that even through Den-
mark is reducing its emissions, within its own borders, CO2 is still being emitted at an increasing 
rate. 
When choosing a future climate scenario, it is important not to blindly look locally, as the whole 
world’s emissions affects the whole climate. Therefore, if a country emits a lot of CO2 emissions 
on the other side of the world, from Denmark, Denmark will still experience the climate change 
hazards, resulting from the global warming effect. Because of this, and the current increase in CO2 
emissions, the ‘worst’ SSP scenario, SSP5-8.5, should be used for future climate projections. This 
is also in line with the EU Taxonomy, which requires the RCP8.5 scenario as a minimum, since 
these two scenarios have similar radiative forcing of 8.5 W/m2.

The vulnerabilities, impacts and risks will be assessed together. This will be done, by looking at

alizations of climate hazards for specific building sites. The EU Taxonomy requires an assessment 
of the materiality of the climate hazard, which will be evaluated through damage functions. These 
damage functions will be based on statistics, and acknowledged theories, when it is possible. 
The damage for each climate hazard will be evaluated as a social and economic damage. The eco-
nomic damage will be a percentage of the building or development’s value, and the risk will then 
be found by estimating the likelihood of the different climate change impacts occurring and the 
severity of their potential consequences. The social risk will be evaluated through an assessed 
damage to people from no injuries to multiple fatalities, as seen in Table 15. The vulnerability of 
the specific building will come in play for the damage functions, where it is necessary to differen-
tiate between different materials, age, and location. Finally, the adaptive capacity of the current 
situation will be evaluated to assess already-existing climate adaptation possibilities that won’t 
require new implementations.

The likelihood of impacts, will mostly be evaluated through ‘events’, which will occur once every

Developing Adaptation and Mitigation Strategies: 
After having assessed the risk from all relevant climate hazards, the most appropriate strategies 
to mitigate the worst climate risks are listed. These are both physical and non-physical adaptation 
strategies, which reduce the risks impact. Co-benefits and trade-offs and cost-effectiveness of the 
strategies should be assessed before implementation. 
Various sources for climate adaptation solutions will be used. Most important is the best practice 
guidance, released by the EU commission, showing adaptation solutions to the minimum required 
climate hazards, with trade-offs and co-benefits towards other climate hazards. Secondly, the 
Danish tool, PLASK, will be used. This tool evaluated adaptation solutions that reduce the impact 
from water-based climate hazards and calculates the expected cost of the chosen solution 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2020). Thirdly, the Dutch application ‘ClimateApp’ will be used, which shows dif-
ferent adaptation solutions, sorted by the scale of the project, the primary climate hazard, project 
type, and the soil type. This is all done, while also indicating which other climate hazards, the 
adaptation solution is also providing co-benefits for (ClimateApp, 2022). 
The adaptation solutions, possible for each climate hazard, will be presented in a catalogue, with 
co-benefits, trade-offs, capacity, and estimated costs. Costs will be based on PLASK, and experi-
ence-prices from dialogue with Sweco engineers. The adaptation solutions should be at a scale, 
matching the project’s scale. The climate hazards’ risk shall then be reevaluated, using the adap-
tation solutions’ capacity of reducing the impacts, and the cost of the solutions must then be com-
pared to the reduced consequence of impact.

Heat waves are rare in Denmark, but they do occur. Heat waves, defined by the meteorological 
institute of Denmark, are when the average highest temperature, across three days, is higher than 
28 °C. Heat waves are especially dangerous for elderly people and can result in heat strokes, hos-
pitalization, and death in worst cases (DMI, 2023a). Heat waves and heat stress are mostly occur-
ring when the weather is hot and dry for a longer period of time, and is an increasing risk in the 
entire world, as the global temperature is increasing. For buildings, an increased cooling demand, 
to avoid overheating issues, will increase the annual energy consumption, resulting in a larger 
electricity bill. 
 
 
 
The historical data in Figure 10 shows that even though the eastern and southern part of Den-
mark, has the highest average temperatures, the western and southern parts are mostly domi-
nant, when it comes to the highest temperature recorded. Furthermore, all parts of Denmark have 
experienced temperatures of above 28 °C, indicating that all parts of Denmark can experience 
heat waves (DMI: weather archive). 
Weather, characterized by heat stress, is, as mentioned, often accompanied with extended peri-
ods of drought. In Denmark, DMI calculates a so-called ‘drought index’, which indicates the 
amount of water in the soil. The drought index shows an increase in dry summers, the past few 
years, seen in Figure 11. This is a result from intense sun, which evaporates the water on the 
earth’s surface, along with no precipitation or precipitation events, which are not evenly scattered 
throughout the month.

Heat wave: Discussion 
The models used for modeling the risk of heat waves are quite different for the social and eco-
nomic risk. Neither of them is looking at the specific geographic area in Denmark, and are there-
fore just giving an estimate, which can be applied to any building. This does make the assessment 
quite easy, and quick to do, with a fine guess as to what magnitude the risk is. If an energy demand 
model, like BE18, for the building is available, it would be much better, to do a simulation of over-
heating hours, and increased energy demand, using a weather file, with data for future projec-
tions. This would give the most realistic result and foundation for assessing the risk from heat 
waves. These types of future climate files are already in use, in Denmark, and was used by Guarino 
et al., in their analysis of energy demand in the future climate. 
The social risk is using data from a northern Chinese province, which does have similar weather 
conditions to Denmark, but the buildings might be built vastly different. A particularly crucial 
factor, to look further into, is the difference in heating and cooling, between Denmark and this 
Chinese province, as well as the difference in isolation and tightness of buildings. This could pro-
vide information on how to better estimate the risk in Denmark. The current estimates of heat-
related sickness are also expected to be larger than what is most likely happening. The estimate 
does not consider the difference in yearly heat-hurt people, inside of buildings and outside of 
buildings. It would be more precise if more data were available on how many people get heat 
stroke, inside buildings, yearly, as well as including what they were doing, just before the heat 
stroke. From experience, most people are suffering the worst from heat, when they are outside, 
directly in the sun, when they are doing physical activity, in a hot environment, and if they are 
directly in front of a window. These indicators should be looked into, in future work. 
The economic risk does build on 16 simulations of different standardized building models in 
BE18, from the ASHRAE Standard (Guarino et al., 2022). The simulations are also using a weather 
file, for Copenhagen with the RCP8.5 scenario, which should give results, the are expected to be 
close to reality. The simulations are however presented as a single result of the average increased 
energy demand. It would be more beneficial, if the author had separated each result into each 
building type, so the increased energy demand could be estimated for residential building, offices, 
restaurants, storage units etc. This would give a more precise model to use, for each building.

Frost is a problem for Danish building owners. Frost damage can occur when water from snow 
infiltrates the foundation and freezes overnight. The freeze expansion can cause the foundation 
to crack and remove surface protection of the building envelope (NRGI, 2020). This is however 
not a notable risk, as it is something a normal building owner, easily can fix themselves (Bolius, 
2021a). Water pipes, however, which are not insulated or are moving outside of the building en-
velope, has an elevated risk of freezing. When a water pipe freezes, the expansion can cause it to 
crack, which leads to immense water damages, when the water starts running again (TopDan-
mark, 2015). 
Especially buildings from the 60’s and 70’s are experiencing frozen pipes and cracks (Thorkild 
Ærø, 2019). A reason for this is likely, that the Danish building code first was introduced in the 
60’s, and before that, there was not a demand to insulate water pipes against frost, nor any rules 
against hiding them in the construction (BR, 1961). Other buildings that are at risk are vacation 
homes, which often are not used, during winter. If the water pipes have not been emptied and the 
heat has been turned off, there is a significant risk, that the water pipes will explode, resulting in 
water damage (Bolius, 2022a). 
Frozen pipes, mostly occur, when the temperature drops below -10 °C. In the period from 2011 
to 2022, all Danish municipalities have experienced temperatures that low, as seen in Figure 14. 
 
Figure 14: Lowest recorded temperatures in Denmark's municipalities, in the period 2011-2022, from DMI. 
(See Appendix 6 for data). 
In Denmark, the risk of frost, in relation to buildings, is mostly economic. Buildings, that are meant 
for people’s use, usually have a heating system, and it is beyond rare, that the indoor tempera-
tures are below 0 °C, unless the building is unoccupied (Codan, 2022). Furthermore, future pro-
jections of temperatures, shows that the lowest temperatures in Denmark, will only get warmer 
in the future, for all climate scenarios (DMI: Climate Atlas, 2023). Therefore, Frost will only be 
assessed as an economic risk.

Frost: Discussion 
Using the proposed method for estimating economic risk to building, caused by frost, is particu-
larly good at estimating the total costs for frost burst pipes, that insurers can expect, but might be 
less in line with what a specific building owner can expect. First of all, it is very uncertain that all 
buildings, in the risk group, are at an equal risk of having frost burst pipes. Some of them might 
already have experienced burst pipes and renovated the house or rerouted the pipes inside the 
building envelope. It is also uncertain whether or not the pipes are actually outside the building 
envelope of all the building from this time. 
The economic risk is also only considered for a single burst pipe, from the point of view, of an 
insurance company. It does not show if people have extra expenses associated with the burst pipe, 
and furthermore, it does not differentiate between the cost of burst pipes underground, in the 
construction and outside of the construction. This is important, as the water damage will depend 
on where the pipe is actually located. The economic data from F&P is also for burst pipes in gen-
eral and does not provide information directly on frost burst pipes. The frost burst pipes are ex-
pected to do much more damage than the proposed economic risk functions, as they are often 
taking place, when people are not in the building. Since no one is in the building when frost burst 
pipes happen, there is no one to stop the water from seeping into every crack of the building, 
resulting in far more damage, than if the accident is stopped quickly. 
For future research, a further investigation into which characteristics buildings have, that suffer 
from frost burst pipes, should be made. The current method is fine for a simple risk assessment, 
but with more data on the buildings, and good guesses as to which buildings are actually in the 
risk group, it could be even better. 
The climate is also changing a lot, and with a “lowest temperature”, above -10 °C, for all RCP8.5 
scenarios, it is uncertain if frost burst pipes are an actual problem for Denmark, in the future. This 
is especially interesting, looking at Figure 15, where a lowest temperature, warmer than -10 °C, 
results in almost no frozen pipes in Denmark.

solution. If the building envelope is leaking a lot of heat, an insurance and moisture alarm could 
also prove useful.

Wildfire: Discussion 
In Denmark, the general risk of wildfires, actually burning buildings, is exceedingly small. Only an 
average of 78 building fires, are annually caused by nature. Because of this, it is uncertain whether 
or not wildfire should be considered a risk in Denmark. The weather is however getting drier in 
the summers, so the risk of wildfires in Denmark is slowly increasing, with an increasing annual 
drought index sum. For the assessment of likelihood, the environment could be considered more, 
than just looking at municipality-level. For future work, it would be recommended to look closely 
at big burnable areas, like fields, bushes, and forests, and making a model that can estimate the 
likelihood of each of these catching fires, and then spreading to a building. Another way of pre-
dicting the number of wildfire cases could also be made, instead of relying on the drought index. 
It is however a good approximation, considering the simplicity of the model. 
The social risk is also a fine approximation of what kind of damage, is associated with fires in 
buildings. Having about 1 hospital visit when there is a fire, is expected, as people are usually 
having a check-up, after being close to such an event. The specific risk could however be further 
analyzed, by looking at escape routes, building material, estimated time of fire, in relation to the 
people’s occupancy etc. These factors could prove to assess the social risk of each specific build-
ing, more precisely. The economic risk should also be reconsidered. The building typology, as-
sessed in the work by Papathoma-Köhle et al. is quite different from the buildings in Denmark. In 
Denmark, buildings are commonly built very resistant to fires, so new damage functions, specific 
to Denmark, should be looked into.

Damages from storms are mostly attributed to two categories of impacts on buildings. Wind-
driven rain, which results in water damages, because of rain being carried into buildings, and 
objects being lifted and blown into buildings. An extreme case of the latter is trees, falling onto 
buildings or façade-elements being torn off, from the wind-suction (NRP, 2022). Another signifi-
cant impact, which can be caused by storms are storm surges, which flood large areas. Storm 
surges will however be assessed as part of the chapter on flooding (See Coastal), because it is very 
hard to split the available data into storms and coastal floods, separately. Most commonly though, 
it is branches, outdoor furniture, rain and rooftiles, that do damage to buildings and people. 
In relation to buildings, storms will in this chapter only be assessed as an economic risk. The 
damage to people from storms, will be assessed as part of the assessment of storm surges, in the 
flooding chapter. This is done, because the damage to people, as a result of storms, is mostly hap-
pening during storm surges, and as mentioned, it is hard to split the data into direct wind-caused 
damage, and flooding damage, separately. 
In Denmark, wind data from the period 2011-2022, is provided by DMI, which can be seen in 
Figure 21. The strongest winds ever recorded in Denmark is however in 1999, where the 10-
minute average reached 41,2 m/s, which converted to an average over 1 minute is 48,2 m/s (Tun 
et al., 2014). The strongest wind-gust was in 2013, which reached 53,5 m/s (DMI: Violent Weather, 
2023).

Heavy precipitation: Discussion 
Using blue spot assessments for assessing the flooding risk from rain, is a commonly accepted 
approach, in climate risk assessments (DK-GBC, 2022). It does however only look at depressions, 
and fails to emulate the flow of water, which moves from one area, down to the depression, which 
could do damage on the way. Furthermore, the assessment does not consider different building 
materials, and just assumes that all buildings are taking damage at 20 cm water depth. This is 
different from reality, where buildings might be made of more or less resistant materials, and 
where some buildings might be flooded already at 10 cm or less. The KAMP map is however par-
ticularly good at assessing specific areas and can be used confidently on specific building assess-
ments. Another economic risk, which could be included is heavy rain or snow, on roofs, which 
might lead to building collapse. 
The social impact is assessed to be what is expected from a home being flooded, but it is uncertain 
if the building will experience that damage, from a single small depression, or if it is the entire 
plot that needs to be submerged. The same goes for the economic risk, where it would be nice to

Flooding is hard to define as a single climate hazard, because flooding can occur from a multitude 
of sources, like rain, rising groundwater levels, or the ocean. According to the flooding directive 
of Denmark, flooding is defined as an event, where normally dry land-area is covered by water 
(K. Andersen, 2022). This is a way too general description, which doesn’t catch the nuances in 
different flooding types that is important to consider, when doing a climate risk assessment. 
For flooding, the EU Taxonomy, however, splits it into four parts, coastal flooding, fluvial flooding, 
pluvial flooding, and groundwater flooding. In the following, each type of flooding will be ex-
plained, except for pluvial flooding. Pluvial flooding is assessed as part of the heavy precipitation 
chapter (see Heavy precipitation). 
 
Coastal flooding 
Denmark is a flat country, with the highest peak, only 170 meter above the water. Denmark has a 
long coastal stretch, compared to land area, and over 70 habited islands, which makes it a country, 
very prone to coastal floods. The worst coastal flood Denmark has seen was in 1634, where a 
storm surge pushed the Danish sea levels, 6,3 meters above normal in Ribe (DMI: Storm surge, 
2018). In Esbjerg, the water levels have been measured to be 4,3 meters above normal, in 1981 
(NRP, 2022). 
In Denmark, the risk of storm-surges and high tides are becoming more common, as the climate 
is changing. The current frequency of dangerous flooding events will change for the worse, and 
the general sea levels will also rise (DMI). In Figure 29, the change in how often a 20-year storm 
surge occurrence happens is seen, for future SSP5-8.5 scenarios. It can be seen that in Bornholm, 
during the middle of the century, the current 20-year incidents will happen close to once a year, 
and that at the end of the century, most of Denmark will experience current 20-year incidences, 
once or twice a year.

Fluvial flooding 
Fluvial flooding, also called river flooding, is when rivers, streams, and other inland bodies of wa-
ter, break their banks and water start flowing out onto the surrounding areas. This mostly hap-
pens when heavy precipitation runoff, exceeds the natural capacity of a river channel, while the 
channel is unable to lead the water to an outlet. High sea levels can for example, impede the flow 
of river water into the sea, as well as manufactured dams or trees and debris falling into the chan-
nel (Floodinfo, 2018). Many areas in Denmark are affected by fluvial flooding, especially during 
storm surges, where the water is pushed from the ocean, into the river channels, sometimes ex-
ceeding the limits of the channel (Andersen et al., 2018). SCALGO and COWI have, together with 
the coastal directive of Denmark, created a national risk assessment of fluvial flooding. This was 
done, looking at a 20-year incident, a 100-year incident and a 1.000-year incident.

Groundwater flooding 
Flooding from the groundwater happens, when the groundwater level is close to the foundation 
of a building or a basement. It usually occurs after prolonged rainfall, which fully saturates the 
soil, raising the ground water level, closer to the ground’s surface. High groundwater levels can 
last for extended periods of time, which often results in significant building damage (Floodinfo, 
2018). Winters are experiencing the highest groundwater levels, in Denmark, because this is 
when there is most precipitation, and summers are experiencing the lowest groundwater levels. 
In some areas, the water is between 1 and 0 meters from the ground, and in others, it is as deep 
as 5 to 10 meters from the ground.

Flood: Discussion 
Using the GIS maps that are available for all types of flooding gives a reliable foundation for mak-
ing a risk assessment. They are considered to be very precise, and can illustrate the different ex-
treme events, through the modeled return values for RCP8.5 and SSP5-8.5. These are ‘just’ show-
ing the median values, and there might be even greater risks at these return timeframes, if the 
uncertainty levels are considered as well. These should be considered as well if the building is at 
a risk of flooding from the upper quartile values. 
The social and economic risks are modelled specifically for coastal flooding, and groundwater 
flooding in Denmark, hence these are expected to be very precise, coupled with the precise visu-
alizations from KAMP. Something that could make it more precise, would be to include damage 
functions for distinct types of building materials. For groundwater flooding, it is uncertain when 
and to what degree, the groundwater will seep into the building, when it touches the foundation. 
Furthermore, groundwater levels vary throughout the year, so it is not clear, when the flooding 
is experienced. A more detailed damage modeling of groundwater levels should be assessed in 
future work, in this field.

Coastal erosion and coastal landslides are in this chapter defined as the natural occurring change 
in coastal profiles, as a result of the sea. The coastal erosion is the chronic erosion that happens 
slowly over time, as the sea levels rise, and the daily waves eat away at the coast. The coastal 
landslide, also called acute erosion, is the result of extreme sea-level scenarios, which remove 
huge volumes of sand and soil, in a brief period of time (Piontkowitz et al., 2023). Denmark is a 
very flat country and has a long history of coastal erosion. During the great storm surge in 1634, 
with a water level of 6,3 meters above normal, Denmark lost a lot of land in south-western Jutland, 
caused by extreme coastal landslides and erosion (See Figure 34). 
 
Figure 34: Historic map of south-western Jutland. Left: year 1634 and Right: year 1362 (Wikipedia, 2023). 
Denmark is known for having one of the world’s largest coastlines, compared to the country’s 
size, and close to 1 million Danes (~17 % of population), live within 1.000 meters from the coast 
(Piontkowitz et al., 2023). Danes know of the dangers from coasts and have already implemented 
many solutions for slope protections on coast lines. The majority of these solutions are however 
old, and even the new ones, might not be enough. According to the Danish coastal directive, the 
newest protection solutions are not dimensioned for future climate events. Furthermore, the 
quality of the solutions is varying to such a degree, that they are not expected to provide much 
protection. They are even expected to collapse in future storm surge events (Piontkowitz et al., 
2023). 
The coastal damages vary across the Danish coastlines. The worst case is the western coast in 
Jutland, where it is expected that 3,8 meters are lost, yearly, as a result of chronic erosion. Some 
coast lines are only losing 0,15 meters coast per year, while others do not change at all (Piontko-
witz et al., 2023). In the figure below, expected lost coasts is seen visualized for the period until 
2050. This is made, assuming no adaptation solutions or coastal protection.

Coastal erosion: Discussion 
Assuming that coastal erosion will destroy the current coastal safety-measures is only applied, 
since the Danish coastal directive is concluding that this will happen. This is however not certain, 
and measures can be taken by the Danish government, to renew and restrengthen the current 
measures. The model from the coastal directive is building on statistical storm surges, with values 
rounded off at different decimals for different return periods. This includes a bit of uncertainty 
for the model but is assumed to be a best guess at estimating the damages at large areas. The 
model is furthermore not considering the surrounding trees, soil types and redistribution of 
coast-material. The model only considers sand coasts, even though clay-soils may be eroded, dur-
ing multiple storm surge events. A GIS map showing landslides, and coastal erosion, for future 
events, including different soil types, and vegetation, would be ideal for this type of assessment. 
The social impacts are just an estimate but are less than what they would be. Since coastal ero-
sions are happening, together with coastal flooding, it can be expected that the social damage 
should be worse for coastal erosion than for flooding, if it happens. Furthermore, people might 
actually be killed, if they are inside a building, which falls of a coast and into the ocean. This is 
however not considered, since it is highly unlikely that a building on the edge of a cliff would be 
occupied during an acute coastal erosion event. People would realize the risk beforehand and 
leave the building. 
The economic damage functions are quite simple, and were modelled by the coastal directive, to 
give an average damage from distinct types of buildings. It could however be interesting to esti-
mate how many people would actually try to repair their coastline, before their building is unus-
able, and if they would actually leave it at 20 % and 40 % for commercial and residential buildings,

Subsidence happens when the soil underneath a building changes volume, resulting in an uneven 
ground. Subsidence most often happens when the building sinks, ’subsiding’, into the ground. Sub-
sidence can also happen when the ground increases in volume, creating a lift in some areas of the 
building, and not in other. Especially buildings with a shallow foundation are prone to subsidence, 
typically found in low-rise- and historic buildings. Subsidence damages, are seen as cracks, run-
ning through the foundation, and building walls, and can vary much in repair costs (Bolius, 
2019a). Subsidence is in Denmark, caused by three main dynamics (EU commission, 2023b): 
 Poor soil quality: Sometimes, the soil that a building is built on is soft and cannot manage 
the load of the building’s weight. This will result in the soil being squeezed underneath 
the building, like a sponge, resulting in the building sinking over time. 
 
 Groundwater-induced subsidence: Rainfall increases the groundwater level and water 
content of the soil. This can result in soil-swelling, creating an uplift under specific areas 
of the building. 
 
 Drought-induced subsidence: Long dry periods evaporate the water in the soil, causing 
it to sink. Furthermore, during droughts, tree roots will absorb water from underneath 
the house, if they are closely planted, which can magnify the drought-induced subsidence 
impact. 
 
Figure 37: Areas with increased risk of subsidence (left) and geological map of Denmark’s soil (right)  
(Climate-GIS, 2019) (Link here). 
Soil characterized as poor in quality and prone to subsidence, is soil with a high content of organic 
material, such as peat, or silt. This type of subsidence happens every year, as water contents in 
the soil changes, and is mostly done after 10 years. After 100 years, a building is expected to have 
settled, and the risk of further subsidence, caused by bad soil quality, is expected to be almost 
zero (Bolius, 2022b). An attempt by DTU, to map the areas, prone to subsidence from bad soil 
quality, in Denmark, is seen on the left in Figure 37. Clay is usually regarded as a stable building 
soil, meaning that it will not move easily, but it can also hold a high volume of water, which means 
that it has a risk of swelling and shrinking. Groundwater- and drought-induced subsidence mostly 
happens in soil with high clay-content, which has plastic properties (Tue Patursson, 2020). A map 
of Denmark’s soil is provided by GEUS, seen in Figure 37 on the right, which can be used as a 
screening tool.

In the 1980’s Denmark started building houses with massive concrete, ground floor slabs, and 
deep foundations of at least 0,9 meters, which reduced the risk of subsidence (SparEnergi, 2023). 
This is further confirmed by Morten K. Mathiasen, who states that buildings from before 1980, in 
Denmark, are especially at risk of subsidence (Bolius, 2019a). Furthermore, it has been increas-
ingly common to get a geotechnical report made, before starting a building project, and in the 
current building regulation, it is mandatory to do geotechnical assessments for any geotechnical 
construction (BR18, 2023). 
The groundwater’s effect on the foundation damage, as a result of soil-swelling, have been found 
to be biggest, when the groundwater level fluctuates below and above the foundation depth (Rog-
ers et al., 1985). The future groundwater levels can be determined for the RCP 8.5 scenario, for 
the future, through HIP’s GIS map (link here). 
In the Danish drought of 2018, over 200 homeowners experienced drought-induced subsidence. 
These homeowners created a Facebook group, where many questionnaires have been made. For 
this thesis, the question: ”Did your house, which experienced drought-induced subsidence, have a 
tree close to it”, was asked. At the time of writing, 40 people have answered the question, of which 
70 % answered “yes” (FB Questionnaire, 2023). The idea that trees close to the house is increasing 
the risk of subsidence is further confirmed by GEO (Danish consultancy firm on geology and hy-
drology), who states that the safe foundation depth, to avoid drought-induced soil movement is 
0,9 to 1,5 meters, with no close tree. The safe foundation depth is however as deep as 3 to 5 me-
ters, for houses that are close to trees (Geo, 2018). 
From this information, the following building risk-groups are found, for the three subsidence dy-
namics (Note that a single building can be in multiple risk groups): 
 Poor soil quality: Buildings built from 1900 or later, on soil with high organic content, 
and foundation depth less than 0,9-1,5 meters. 
 
 Groundwater-induced subsidence: Buildings built on soil with plastic clay, where the 
foundation depth is less than 0,9-1,5 meters, and where the foundation depth is between 
the upper and lower groundwater-level fluctuations. 
 
 Drought-induced subsidence: Buildings built on soil with plastic clay, of which the: 
o Foundation depth is less than 0,9-1,5 meters, for buildings without large trees 
close to the foundation. 
o Foundation depth is less than 3-5 meters, for buildings with large trees close to 
the foundation.

Subsidence: Discussion 
The risk of subsidence is hard to estimate using GIS maps, because it depends on variations in soil 
composition, which are not possible to map. An estimate can be made using virtual boreholes, or 
topsoil maps, but nothing is certain from these. Furthermore, the water content and hydraulic 
conductivity might play a crucial role in how fast soil swells and shrinks. Mapping out the precise 
soil compositions, along with groundwater levels, might make it possible to correctly estimate 
subsidence risks. Another problem is the depth of foundations. These depths should be varying 
more, depending on the height of the building, and is therefore not accurate for high-rise build-
ings. The buildings in Denmark are however, usually, made very structurally sound, and since it 
is common custom to assess the geology before building a building, it might be redundant to do 
an assessment like this. However, people might be single-mindedly looking at one risk of subsid-
ence, and not consider things like the future trees’ capacity of drying soil, or the changing ground-
water levels. Therefore, the subsidence assessment is seen to at least be reminding people of the 
most crucial factors, to avoid subsidence-induced damages. The economic damage is also only 
based on one survey, from one year, and should be reevaluated, when F&P has collected more 
statistics on subsidence damages to buildings. 
Subsidence: Adaptation solutions 
When implementing adaptation solutions for subsidence, or are doing repairs, because of cracks, 
it is important to keep in mind, how subsidence works. If the soil has shrunk under a building, 
during a summer drought, reparations should wait until next spring, as the soil will swell up again, 
during the winter. If the building has been made stable during summer, then it will experience 
‘heaving’ during winter. These geological variations should be considered when choosing adap-
tation solutions from Table 46.

Discussion 
Since the EU Taxonomy has changed three times, while writing this thesis, and multiple guides 
and publications have been released in relation to the taxonomy, it is certain that the final Taxon-
omy criteria haven’t been made yet. As the Taxonomy changes, the methodology in this report, 
could become too complicated or too simple, depending on which direction the taxonomy is 
pushed. The proposed methodology does however screen buildings, with free data, in a more 
complex way than most current climate risk assessments, while being easy to do. Furthermore, 
there is a potential of digitalizing this method. Most information used is gathered on online GIS-
maps, which can be freely accessed. Collecting these data and updating the BBR register with 
more information on the buildings, could automate the method tremendously. If this is done, the 
assessment could be conducted on existing buildings, with only a click of a button. 
The chosen climate hazards, for the assessment in the Danish building industry, are relevant in 
varying degree. It is questionable if ‘Frost’ should be assessed, as the damages each year are not 
too bad, and the lowest yearly temperature is getting warmer than -10 °C. This makes the likeli-
hood of frost burst pipes occurring very unlikely. It is however an impact that still happens in 
Denmark, so it should still be kept in mind for old buildings, especially if frost-anomalies can be 
modelled better for future scenarios. The relevance of assessing wildfire is also questionable, as 
there are so few occurrences in all of Denmark, each year. However, since global warming is mak-
ing all of Europe warmer and drier, and there is a general increase in wildfires, it should be kept 
ensuring that the building is future proof. These two climate hazards were the same hazards, 
which the ‘Best Practice guidance’, from the EU, didn’t include. Other climate hazards could also 
become important to assess, in a changing climate. Drought might become relevant, in relation to 
clean drinking water, if the Danish ground water is becoming increasingly polluted by ‘PFAS’ and 
other substances. Saline intrusion, more humidity, and other impacts, which might reduce the 
quality and lifetime of materials are also relevant to investigate further. The current chosen cli-
mate hazards are however assumed to be the most relevant ones, at the current knowledge-level 
of climate impacts on buildings. 
The general risk assessment is precise in relation to the EU Taxonomy, but it is uncertain, if this 
is the best method, from a ‘sustainability’ point of view. The Taxonomy is made for ensuring that 
investments are sustainable but fails to include environmental assessments in the climate risk 
assessment. This is a big negative for the EU Taxonomy, which is already lacking ambition for 
climate goal 1: Climate change mitigation. Even the technical guidance from the EU is only looking 
at social and economic risk, even though many researchers from big institutions like the UN, have 
concluded that climate change’s impact on nature and biodiversity, will have the biggest impact 
on humanity eventually. Climate change is also just one impact category, covered by LCAs, where 
many other categories, might also be important for human life. A method could be to do some-
thing similar to a ‘full LCA’, with all impact categories considered. This should estimate the local 
loss of biodiversity, water, land, ozone, and other impacts, which the adaptation solutions could 
try to mitigate. It is however hard to estimate what the risk is of losing a beetle species or

acidifying soils and water. It is however particularly important to keep this in mind for future 
work, as just one of the LCA impact categories, climate change, is putting the entire world in a 
crisis. Especially looking at ‘tipping points’, where ecosystems become unstable, or soils and wa-
ter become too acidic for life and the properties, that we are used to. Cascading effects from these, 
might be worse than climate change. Not enough research has been put into this, to know for 
certain, though. 
In the proposed methodology by the EU, the likelihood has to be assessed from likely to unlikely. 
This qualitative method leaves a lot to be desired, as it would be easier to compare risk assess-
ments, if the likelihood were based on a numerical value. An approach to this, could be to calculate 
the total impacts, during the building’s lifetime, for each climate hazard. Dividing these impacts 
by the expected lifetime of the building, would then give a number for each climate hazard, which 
could be compared to each other. Haven a risk-value as the average ‘damage per year’, would 
make it easier to compare buildings with each other and could be added up, across climate haz-
ards. This would make it easier to manage portfolios by pension funds and to estimate damages 
to whole cities if a municipality wants to do large-scale risk assessments.

The proposed methods could also easily be improved by using software, that costs money. This is 
however going against the complaints on the ‘black box problem’ but could give more robust re-
sults. Instead of using these though, it should be looking into, how equivalent results can be 
achieved using the data that is available already. Another way they could be improved is to assess 
the reduced lifetime of building materials, caused by increased humidity levels, more drought, 
and other factors. This would increase the frequency of renovations, further increasing the oper-
ational costs. 
For activity 7.1 construction of new buildings and 7.2 renovation of existing buildings, in the EU 
Taxonomy, it would also be interesting to look at the climate during the construction-phase. This 
is especially interesting for large buildings, which take many years to complete, where sheltering 
of the materials against the weather is important. Furthermore, looking at the damage to unfin-
ished buildings or workers during construction, could be what is actually needed for these two 
activities, since their descriptions are regarding the actual construction phases, and not the oper-
ation phases. 
Testing the methodology on the PFA portfolio, shows some important flaws of the methods, but 
is also lacking in building diversity. Ideas, in regard to what can be expected as a result of each 
climate hazard risk assessment, in relation to a specific building type, were made clear, but since 
the buildings were all new buildings, built similarly and large-scale projects, it is uncertain if they 
are representative of all buildings. Another assessment should have been made for a collection of 
buildings, that all vary in size, construction, placement etc., to evaluate the methodology on a wide 
range of buildings. A guide or manual on how to use the proposed methodologies should also be 
made to help teach relevant people, how to use the methods. This should be done with examples 
of a few buildings, for each climate risk, to show differences and similarities between buildings.

Conclusion | Climate adaptive buildings

Conclusion 
The EU Taxonomy will definitely change its criteria for each climate goal, in future iterations. But 
for the current wording of the regulation in effect, the required minimum for a robust climate risk 
and vulnerability assessment, for a building, is: 
 The expected lifetime of the building. 
 A catalogue of climate hazards, which might impact the building in its lifetime. 
 Three assessments; current climate, 30 years in the future and at the building’s end of life. 
 Climate projections for each assessment, using RCP 8.5 or SSP5-8.5 as a minimum. 
 Quantifiable evaluations of economic and social risk, for each climate hazard assessment. 
 A catalogue of adaptation solutions, which can reduce the found climate risks. 
 A choice of adaptation solutions, which reduce the most important climate risks. 
o For new buildings, these are implemented at the start of operations. 
o For existing buildings, these are implemented in a period of up to 5 years. 
In a Danish building context, there are 9 climate hazards that should be assessed at a minimum: 
‘Heat waves’, ‘Frost’, ‘Wildfire’, ‘Storm’, ‘Heavy precipitation’, ‘Flooding (coastal, fluvial, groundwa-
ter)’, ‘Coastal erosion’, and ‘Subsidence’. For Denmark, there is a lack in free climate projections, 
data, and software, which can be used to evaluate future climate scenarios. This makes it hard to 
assess all 9 climate hazards with comparable precision, without increasing the assessment’s com-
plexity to an unintended extent.  
A first attempt at evaluating these climate hazards, in line with the Taxonomy, has been pre-
sented. The hazards, ‘Frost’, ‘Storm’, and ‘Subsidence’ are only having economic impacts, while 
the other climate hazards have both social- and economic impacts. ‘Heat wave’, ‘Wildfire’ and 
‘Storm’ are having the least robust risk assessments, as they are relying on data and models from 
outside of Denmark. The assessment methods with the highest precision are ‘heavy precipitation’, 
‘flooding’, and ‘coastal erosion’. These three climate hazards are the only ones, which building 
developers assess using future climate projections. It is encouraged that the presented method-
ologies are challenged and improved upon, to increase the robustness of the assessments. 
Lastly, there is a potential of digitizing and automating the climate risk assessment process be-
cause they rely on free-to-access GIS-services and datasets, and data from BBR. The risk assess-
ments could also be broadened to assess environmental risk along with social and economic, as 
the environment’s risk might be the most damaging to life on Earth eventually. The risk from 
climate hazards, during the construction or renovation phase, in regard to building materials and 
workers, have not been assessed. This should all be investigated further.
