Abstract
On a microscopic level, the crystalline solids (which includes metals) are made up of
smaller parts known as grains, each of which has an orientation along which the atoms
of the grain are arranged. Knowledge of grains maps and orientation are of interest
to those who wish to derive properties about the material such as its tensile strength.
Grain mapping is also important in the study of crack formation and propagation in
materials.
In order to identify the grains of a crystal, there are many experimental methods,
one among them being diﬀraction tomography, which via high-energy X-rays pene-
trate the sample in question. Parts of the X-rays are diﬀracted upon contact with the
sample, and the angles at which these X-rays are diﬀracted can through mathematical
and tomographic methods identify the sample grains in a non-destructive manner.
The problem of mapping the grains and determining their crystal lattice orienta-
tions is a diﬃcult and ill-posed problem, and state of the art methods use algorithms,
that keep track of the possible sources of diﬀraction spots inside of sample for each
diﬀraction, and use these to estimate the orientation of the grain in said spot. How-
ever, in this thesis we attempt a hitherto untested approach, by using machine learning
methods to solve this problem, in particular conventional supervised learning, and a
very recent regularisation technique based on machine learning methods, known as
deep image prior.

Introduction
On a microscopic level, a crystal is a solid made up of a repeating 3-dimensional
(3D) pattern of atoms. This repeating pattern is known as the crystal lattice. An
ideal crystal has a mono-crystalline (or simply crystalline) structure, which means
that a single lattice permeates the entire crystal. However, in reality crystals have
a polycrystalline structure, i.e. they are made up of multiple lattices with diﬀering
orientations in space. See Figure 1 for a 2-dimensional (2D) example.

Each of the sub-lattices in a polycrystalline structure is known as a grain. The
study of determining the arrangement of atoms, and thereby the orientation of grains,
in (poly)crystalline materials is known as crystallography.
The prior work in the ﬁeld of crystallography is extensive, and there exists many
structural probes. For an overview see [5, section 2]. In this thesis, we focus solely
on the method known as X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) crystallography, which boasts two
advantages. Firstly it is a non-destructive method, meaning that the probing methods
do not tamper with the sample. Secondly, the method is fast compared to tomographic
scanning methods [4].
The XRD framework uses a plane wave X-ray to penetrate the sample. Upon con-
tact with the sample, some of the X-rays are diﬀracted onto a 2D detector to create
diﬀraction patterns. From the 2D diﬀraction patterns for varying rotations of the
sample, the grain characteristics of the sample are reconstructed. Given the diﬀrac-
tion patterns, there are multiple reconstruction algorithms, perhaps most notably
GRAINDEX [5].
In this thesis, we will study the use of machine learning methods to map grains
and their orientations. To the knowledge of the author, this is yet an unexplored class

Crystallography
In order to understand the grain mapping problem, we must ﬁrst achieve a more
detailed understanding of the crystal lattices, which represent the diﬀerent grains
of our poly-crystalline sample, and most importantly have an understanding of the
Bragg condition, which determines how electromagnetic waves scatter on crystalline
solids.
3.1
Crystal lattice
A crystal lattice is an inﬁnite array of points in a translationally periodic pattern
matching that of the crystal it represents.
Each point can be thought of as the
position of an atom in the crystal. Due to the translational symmetry of crystals,
the crystal lattice is not deﬁned by the position of its points, but rather by a set of
primitive fundamental translation vectors, a, b, c ∈R3, s.t. any two lattice points are
connected via the vector combination
R = aℓ+ bm + cn , ℓ, m, n ∈Z
(11)
The translational symmetry is what allows us to pick an integer vector combination.
For a single crystal lattice, there may be multiple sets of primitive fundamental trans-
lation vectors. There are only fourteen symmetrically diﬀerent 3D lattices, known as
the Bravais Lattices. Of these fourteen we focus on the three with a cubic unit-cell.
The unit-cell is the parallelepiped of 3 translational vectors, which span R3, but does
not necessary have an integer vector combination to and from every lattice point.
Of the Bravais lattices with a cubic unit-cell we have cubic, body-centred cubic and
face-centred cubic, see Figure 2.
3.2
Lattice planes and Miller indices
Bragg’s Law models diﬀraction of waves along planes of atoms. In a 3D crystal lattice,
there are a multitude of atomic planes. To denote them, we use the Miller indices

with axis a, b, c ∈R3 onto an arbitrary diﬀerent lattice point. As in (11) we ﬁnd a
set ℓ, m, n ∈Z of projections onto the unit-cell axis, and denote the direction of R by
[ℓ, m, n].

Grain mapping problem
The grain mapping problem can be stated as follows: Given all diﬀraction spots of a
given sample, produced by the forward model, reconstruct a 6D representation of the
sample - a 3D spatial reconstruction of the sample, with corresponding orientations.
The grain mapping problem is an inverse problem. Author’s note: As this is a
topic, I have already covered my bachelor thesis, [14], I shall be very brief. Given some
kind of forward mapping A : X →Y , the inverse problem is to solve the equation
Ax = y for x ∈X, given y ∈Y . This problem is said to be well-posed, if
1. a solution x exists;
2. the solution x is unique; and
3. the solution x depends continuously on y.
If any of these requirements are not met, the problem is called an ill-posed problem.
The grain mapping problem is ill-posed, because as we will see, the forward oper-
ator is not injective, meaning the solutions are non-unique.

Hence for a single diﬀraction point, the grain mapping problem is doubly non-

ill-posed", when the complete set of diﬀraction spots is taken into account, as this
allows to make use of the following.
• Each grain is assumed to have a constant crystallographic orientation. Therefore
in a poly-crystalline solid, we can expect the crystallographic orientation to
remain constant in a neighbourhood about almost any point, and hence the
diﬀraction events will be similar in almost any neighbourhood.
• The same sample point may admit diﬀraction events from diﬀerent sets of Miller
indices (h, k, l) and Bragg orders n. In good cases, this may uniquely determine
the grain orientation.
With multiple diﬀraction spots in play, a new problem with uniqueness occurs in
the form of overlapping diﬀraction spots from diﬀraction events at diﬀerent sample
spots.

This section is a brief introduction to deep learning based on [10][8]. An artiﬁcial
neural network is made up of neurons,
Deﬁnition 6.1 (Artiﬁcial neuron) For an input x ∈Rn, the function
g : x 7→σ (w · x + b) ,
for weights w ∈Rn and bias b ∈R,
(24)
is called an artiﬁcial neuron. The function σ : R →R is called the activation function.
The artiﬁcial neurons are the building blocks of neural networks.
Usually, their
weights w and biases are trained iteratively, while the activation function is cho-
sen prior as part of the network design. Common activation functions are Sigmoid,
Tanh, and ReLU.
By combining neurons in graphs, we get neural networks.
The most common
are the feedforward neural networks, where input is not recursively added to prior
neurons.
Deﬁnition 6.2 (Feedforward neural network) A feedforward neural network is
a function fθ : Rn →Rm determined by a directed acyclic graph G = (V, E). The
nodes are partitioned into n input nodes, m output nodes and |V | −n −m hidden
nodes.
Each node is associated with an artiﬁcial neuron, which takes as input the numbers
passed on from incoming edges, and outputs along each out-going edge the number
computed.

Deep learning is the process of teaching a network fθ its parameters θ s.t. fθ to best
approximate a desired function. In supervised learning, this is done via a dataset of
corresponding {xi, yi}i values drawn from some underlying, often unknown, distribu-

a metric on Y , and often it is chosen based on the type of problem. A few commonly
chosen loss functions are L-norm loss, cross-entropy-loss, negative log likelihood loss,
and Kullback-Leibler Divergence.
The expectation and hope is, that by minimizing the loss for known samples of the
hidden underlying distribution, the network fθ learns to generalise to new hitherto
unseen samples (xi, yi).
In practice the loss in (27) is minimised numerically via some optimiser, most
often a variation of stochastic gradient descent.[11]

Deep Image Prior
Let X ⊂R6 be our sample space, which includes grain-orientation, and Y ⊂R3 the
space of our diﬀraction spots. Then the forward operator A : X →Y sends samples

==PAGE:17==
to their corresponding diﬀraction spots. Let y ∈Y be a measurement, L : Y ×Y →R
a loss functional. Naïvely, we may attempt to ﬁnd a solution x ∈X s.t. Ax = yδ,
where ∥y −yδ∥< δ by minimizing the loss as follows.
x∗= arg min
x
L(Ax; y)
(28)
Since the problem is ill-posed (in our case by lack of uniqueness), we cannot expect
to ﬁnd a good solution. Often to improve odds of a good solution being found, a
problem-speciﬁc regularisation term R : X →R is added.
x∗= arg min
x
L(Ax; y) + αR(x).
(29)
for some regularisation parameter α > 0. As a side note, these regularisation terms
can in some cases be equated to regularisation schemes, which guarantee point-wise
convergence in the case of compact, injective forward operators, for example Tikhonov
regularisation, which is a minimiser for the case R(x) = ∥x∥2.
[6] Our forward
operator is however not injective, which means we cannot use this theory.
The deep image prior makes use of an untrained neural network as an implicit
regularisation term. We deﬁne a neural network fθ : RNinput →X with parameters
θ ∈RNpara, and a random, ﬁxed, input z ∈RNinput. Equation 29 is then replaced by
x∗= arg min
θ
L (Afθ(z); y) .
(30)
The intuition is that the network fθ, while surjective, is adapted better towards
producing certain results. In the original deep image prior paper, [12], it is shown to
work very well in for example denoising of images by using an SGD optimiser with
early stopping (stopping the training process before the loss converges). In [13] it is
shown, that in the setting of inverse problems, the deep image prior can be interpreted
as a tikhonov regularisation of sorts.
8
Experiments and results
It was during the work on this thesis not possible to ﬁnd a solver for the grain mapping
problem. In general no supervised network was able to produce a low loss, even during
training. Similarly, no deep image prior found a good or even satisfying solution. This
section will therefore describe the diﬀerent networks and hyper-parameters, as well
as the problems tested.
We discretise our sample into a cubic grid of N 3
voxels voxels. Each voxel is then
associated with a single grain, which in turn is associated with a 3D orientation.
Additionally, diﬀraction events may only occur at the center of each voxel.
Diﬀraction spots were in early testing represented as 1’s in a sparse matrix in a
discretised 3D space of turntable rotation and ydet, zdet. To estimate the diﬀerence
between a generated set of diﬀraction spots and the ground truth, Kullback-Leibler
14
==PAGE:18==
divergence was used as loss function. This divergence treats the discretized sparse
matrices of diﬀraction spots as distributions and compare their closeness as such.
This representation of diﬀraction spots was discarded in favour of a simpler ap-
proach: Assume (21) always has a solution, i.e. for any set of Miller indices and Bragg
angles, there always exists a pair of turntable rotations ω± which produce a diﬀraction
spot. In testing, diﬀraction spots were occurring often enough, for this assumption
to have a negligible eﬀect on the training. In cases where no turntable rotation was
admissible, the diﬀraction spot was chosen as a arbitrary ﬁxed spot.
This approach simpliﬁes the forward operator, s.t. for each voxel there are exactly
2NhklNBragg diﬀraction spots, which change continuously with change in the voxel’s
corresponding orientation. Here Nhkl is the number of chosen Miller indices to loop
through.
For optimisers, SDG and Adam were chosen, both with learning rates in {10−4, 10−5, 10−6}.
The network parameters were updated in batches of variable sizes between 1 and 2000.
Experiments were done with between 1 and 3 diﬀerent grains for Nvoxel = 10. A
simpliﬁed approach was tested for a single voxel with varying number NBragg and Nhkl.
The simplest of approaches tested was with orientations locked to a 1-dimensional
subspace.
Of networks, simple fully connected linear networks of varying sizes between 5 and
15 layers were chosen. As well as 3d-convolutional networks over turntable rotation
and ydet, zdet.
9
Discussion and conclusions
In this thesis, we have attempted to use machine learning methods to solve the grain
mapping problem, an ill-posed problem due to its non-injective forward operator.
The results of both the deep image prior and supervised learning approaches have
been very poor with a lack of convergence even for the most simpliﬁed setups.
Machine learning methods have been showing promise on other inverse problems,
however it is possible that this particular problem does not suit well for machine
learning approaches.
The large set of hyperparameters inherent in chosing a network architecture, es-
pecially for deep image prior, make it daunting to search for a good solver. It is
therefore also likely that a deep image prior approach for the grain mapping problem
yet exists.
As for supervised learning, additional network architectures should be attempted
in pursuit of convergence.

def discretise_diffsp(diffsp, N_omega = 500, N_y = 500, N_z = 500,
omega_range = [-PI,PI], y_range = [-PI,PI], z_range =␣
,→[-PI,PI]):

def discretise_diffsp(diffsp, N_omega = 500, N_y = 500, N_z = 500,
omega_range = [-PI,PI], y_range = [-PI,PI], z_range =␣
,→[-PI,PI]):
""" discretise diffsp list of (omega,y,z) to 3-dimensional integer tensor
element value indicates how many spots lie within the discretised spot """
output = np.zeros((N_omega,N_y,N_z))
omegas = np.linspace(omega_range[0],omega_range[1],N_omega)
ys = np.linspace(y_range[0],y_range[1],N_y)
zs = np.linspace(z_range[0],z_range[1],N_z)
for spot in diffsp:

output[omega,y,z] = 1
return output
