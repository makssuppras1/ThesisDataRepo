Abstract
Hafnia-Based thin-films have attracted significant attention over the last decade due
to their compatibility with complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor technology.
Doped Hafnia is a versatile material that exhibits electrical, electro-chemical and
electro-mechanical properties. In this work, Hf0.5Zr0.5O2 thin-films are fabricated
and characterized individually in their electro-chemo-mechanical coupling.
Thin-
films of 8 nm thickness are fabricated by means of Pulse Laser Deposition under ther-
modynamic conditions, which reinforce the orthorhombic phase of Hf0.5Zr0.5O2 on
La0.6Sr0.33MnO3 on SrTiO3 : Nb substrates. The substrate termination is controlled
to achieve single termination through chemical etching and annealing. Reflective-
high energy electron diffraction and atomic force microscopy provide insights into
the growth mechanism of the thin-films, while phase identification of the hetero-
structure is confirmed by X-ray diffraction. Characterization of electro-mechanical,
electro-chemical and electrical effect, by utilizing custom made hardware and soft-
ware, reveals a geometric dependence of the electro-mechanical response (pseudo-
piezoelectricity). Whereas, the electro-chemical effect (memristive behavior) is evid-
ent under ambient conditions for both TiN and Pt electrodes. However, the electrical
effect (ferroelectricity) is suppressed likely due to non-optimized deposition paramet-
ers. Although these phenomena were investigated individually, the findings motivate
further research on hafnia-based systems capable of integrating all these effects sim-
ultaneously.

Chapter 1
Introduction and Research topic
Electro-Chemo-mechanical coupling is a rapidly evolving research area in metal oxide
materials. Understanding and decoupling the origins of electrochemical, mechanical
and electrical phenomena remains a major scientific challenge [1]. In defective metal
oxides, piezoelectricity, electrostriction, ferroelectricity and resistive switching can
coexist enabling multi-purpose devices, such as Micro-Electro-mechanical Systems
(MEMS), actuators, transducers and non-volatile memories.
Over the past decade, transition metal oxides have undergone extensive research
on the aforementioned phenomena individually.
For instance, dielectric material
Hafnium oxide (HfO2), depending on the dopant concentration and its final device
structure, exhibits pseudo-piezoelectric effect mediated by electrostriction in the out-
plane regime (electro-mechanical effect). In addition, HfO2 exhibits unconventional
ferroelectricity (electrical effect) mediated by anion-displacement, strongly influenced
by film thickness [2]. Finally, resistive switching (electro-chemical effect), governed
by oxygen vacancy clustering, can be tuned by factors such as surface potential and
thickness [3].
It has been confirmed that by doping HfO2, with zirconia enables electrical, electro-
chemical and electro-mechanical phenomena to exist separately. Demonstrating and
confirming their coexistence within a single device could potentially unlock the multi-
functionality of such a platform.

1.1
Electro-Chemo-Mechanical Coupling
Electro-chemo-mechanics describes the interplay between electrical, chemical and
mechanical properties and the study of adjusting one property through the control
of another. Electro-mechanical coupling is strongly evident in many dielectric ma-
terials, where electrical energy is converted into mechanical energy and vice versa.
Electro-chemical effect, refers to the interaction between electrical driving forces and
chemical processes, such as the drift of charged defects (e.g.
oxygen vacancies).
Therefore, electro-chemo-mechanical coupling integrates these phenomena enabling
simultaneous control to design multi-modal devices [4, 1].
1.2
Electro-mechanical Coupling
Electro-mechanical coupling is the interaction between electrical and mechanical
states, where an electric field induces mechanical deformation or vice versa. Electro-
mechanical coupling is constituted by piezoelectricity, pyroelectricity and electrostric-
tion. The following sub-chapters provide a detailed discussion of these effects.
1.2.1
Piezoelectricity and Pyroelectricity
On the core of the electro-mechanical effect lies piezoelectricity. It was predicted
and experimentally demonstrated in the 1880s by the Curie brothers [5]. Their work
focused primarily on quartz, although other materials were used, such as cane sugar
and tourmaline.
These naturally occurring crystals, when mechanically stressed
along specific crystallographic orientations, intrinsically featured opposite electric
charges (i.e.
spontaneous polarization).
These charges were proportional to the
applied force and ceased to exist with the removal of it (Fig. 1.2a). On the con-
trary Lippmann in 1881 predicted the converse piezoelectric effect on the basis of
thermodynamics [6]. This effect refers to the mechanical deformation of a piezoelec-
tric material when it is subjected to an electric field (Fig. 1.2b) and was observed
experimentally the same year by the Curie brothers.
Piezoelectricity, is exhibited only by insulating materials (dielectrics) which lack a

1.3
Electrical Effect
Electrical effect is the interaction of the material with respect to the imposed electric
field. In this work, ferroelectricity is considered as the electrical effect.
1.3.1
Ferroelectric and Anti-ferroelectric Phenomena
Ferroelectricity, is based on pyroelectricity which in turn originates from piezoelec-
tricity. It has been in the subject of research for many years, particularly for digital
information storage. In the 1950s, D. A. Buck fabricated non-volatile memories using
Barium Titanate (BaTiO3) single-crystals, known as Ferroelectric Random Access
Memories (FeRAM) [8].
In later years, devices such as Ferroelectric Field-Effect
Transistors (FeFETs) and Ferroelectric Tunnel Junction (FTJs) were developed and
extensively studied [9, 10, 11].
Ferroelectricity is the spontaneous polarization of an insulating material that exhibits
two or more discrete stable or metastable states with non-zero values of polarization
in the absence of an external electric field [12]. A defining characteristic of ferro-
electric materials is the ability to switch between these polarization states through
the application of an external electric field. Ferroelectricity, belongs to the broader
class of ferroic phenomena, which also includes ferromagnetism and ferroelecasti-
city. These functional materials, share the defining characteristic of spontaneous
polarization via the application of an external field (i.e. magnetic and stress field)
[13].
Lastly, Anti-ferroelectricity is the spontaneous polarization in which lines of ions (or
sub lattices) in the crystal are polarized in an antiparallel pattern [14]. The ground
state of the crystal structure is the antiparallel pattern of all the domains.

1.3.2
Domain Switching and Hysteresis Loops
Ferroelectricity arises from a slight differentiation of the crystal structure relative
to its primitive, high-symmetry phase. This change in crystal structure, known as
Phase Transition (PT), occurs under specific thermodynamic conditions for each
material. During the cooling of ferroelectric materials, the phase transition proceeds
from a high-symmetry to a low-symmetry (i.e. non-centrosymmetric) structure, and
associated reduction in free energy is compensated by the formation of ferroelectric
domains [13].
The domains are separated by domains walls, which define their boundaries. As
cooling continues, domains may further subdivide into smaller regions due to large
interal stresses that develop. Each material exhibits ferroelectricity only within a
specific temperature range, called Curie temperatures. Depending on the thermo-
dynamic conditions and relaxation pathways of the system, adjacent domains may
differ in polarization orientation by 180o. For example, in a material with tetragonal
symmetry, if two adjacent domains possess polarization vectors along [010] and [010],
respectively, the domain wall lies in the (010) plane. Because both domains share
the same lattice plane (010), the lattice distortion across the wall is minimal. In
contrast, non-180o domain walls involve a change in polarization direction that leads
to observable displacement and strain during their movement.
Hysteresis loops are the outcome of the orientation of the ferroelectric domains.
Each material exhibits a fingerprint like hysteresis loop; characteristic parameters
can be perceived from these-Polarization - Electric field (P-E)-measurements like
the spontaneous polarization (Ps), remnant polarization (Pr), and coercive field
(Er). As shown in Fig.1.4(a-b), the ferroelectric domains in the pristine state are
randomly distributed, resulting in zero net macroscopic polarization. Consequently,
Ec represents the electric field required to bring the material into polar state. With a
gradual increase in the applied electric field, polarization saturation is achieved as the
available ferroelectric domains become fully aligned. The abrupt changes observed
when the polarization surpasses the coercive field arise from domain switching. By
gradually reducing the electric field to zero, leads to a finite polarization, referred to
Introduction and Research topic
7
==PAGE:18==
as the remanent polarization. To fully reverse the ferroelectric domains, an electric
field of opposite polarity, known as the coercive field (or coercivity), must be applied.
Further increase in the reverse field strength leads to the reorientation of the domains.
The ideal ferroelectric system should be symmetric as seen in Fig.1.4a. Both the
positive and negative Ec, Pr are equal. Although, the hysteresis loops are expected
to be symmetric, influencing factors like sample thickness, material composition,
thermal treatment and mechanical stresses could change the shape of the loop [13].
Regarding anti-ferroelectricity, an ideal system exhibits a characteristic double hys-
teresis loop, resulting from the electric-field-induced switching of domains that are
initially oriented antiparallel to the applied field.

1.3.3
Conventional Ferroelectric Materials
The first scientist to experimentally prove the dielectric hysteresis in ferroelectrics
was F. Beaulard in 1900 [15], though dielectric saturation was not observed.
J.
Valasek, was the first to observe hysteresis due to electric polarization in 1920 while
working on Rochelle salt (C4H12KNaO10) [16]. The dielectric constant was measured
from -70◦C to 30◦C, and found to be 50 at -70◦C and 1000 near 0◦C, while the
Introduction and Research topic
8
==PAGE:19==
most abrupt changes of the properties occurred at -20◦C and +20◦C; accordingly he
suggested the two Curie Points. Initially, ferroelectricity was believed to originate
from the hydrogen bonding in Rochelle salt [15]. However, this interpretation was
revised following the discovery of ferroelectricity in Barium Titanate (BaTiO3) in
1949 [17], whose much simpler perovskite structure does not contain hydrogen.
Perovskite oxides constitute the most extensively studied family of ferroelectric ma-
terials over the past five decades. They share the crystal structure of Calcium Titan-
ate (CaTiO3) and conform to the general formula ABO3, where A and B are either
one cation element or a mixture of such elements or vacancies [18]. Depending on the
composition these perovskites can be metallic (e.g. SrNbxTi1−xO3) [19] or dielectrics
(e.g. SrTiO3).
Fig.1.5a illustrates the unit cell of Barium Titanate (BaTiO3). The left structure
corresponds to the paraelectric cubic perovskite phase (Pm3m), whereas the right
structure represents the ferroelectric orthorhombic phase (Amm2). The cubic phase
is stable at high temperatures down to 393 K, where BaTiO3 undergoes a transition
to the ferroelectric tetragonal phase (P4mm). This phase remains stable between
393 K and 278 K, below which the material transforms into the rhombohedral phase
(R3m) [20].
As shown in the Fig. 1.5b, in the rhombohedral phase the Ti+4 cation is displaced
inside the oxygen octahedron framework, meaning the polarity of the material is
based on this cation. In conventional perovskite oxides, ferroelectricity is primarily
mediated by such off-center displacements of B-site cations.
Although perovskite oxides dominate ferroelectric applications, other crystal fam-
ilies like Lithium Niobate (LiNbO3), and Potassium di-hydrogen Phosphate (KDP,
KH2PO4) demonstrate ferroelectricity. Notably, lithium niobate, crystallizes in a
non-centrosymmetric trigonal structure (R3c), at room temperature, as seen in
Fig.1.5b [12]. Conversely, the Curie temperature of KDP is c.a. 123K, transitioning
from paraelectric phase with space group I42d, into ferroelectric phase with space
group Fdd2 [21].

1.3.4
Scaling Effects of Conventional Ferroelectrics
When referring to thin-films, thick-films and bulk materials in canonical ferroelec-
trics, scaling effects are major influencing factors. There is a size limit for how thin
conventional ferroelectrics can become, which is called intrinsic size effect, where
the ferroelectric effect is nonexistent. Scaling effects are the backbone of this limit;
they are not inherent to the crystal, but rather they are related to the adjacent
environment and the structure within the crystal.
Scaling effects can be broadly categorized into two major sub-categories; intrinsic
and extrinsic responses. Intrinsic responses, reflect the behavior of ideal ferroelec-
trics that are uniaxial, single-domain, and single-phase, representing the hallmark of
genuine ferroelectricity. In contrast, extrinsic responses originate from mechanisms
such as domain wall motion and phase transitions [22].

coefficient (d33) and relative permittivity κ33, as a function of thickness which is
visible in lead-titanate and lead zirconate titanate-based ferroelectrics, as seen in
Fig. 1.6(a-b). Regular intrinsic values of permittivity lie in the range of 200-250.

1.4.3
Unconventional Electro-Mechanical Coupling in HZO
Unconventional piezoelectricity, is observed in hafnium-based oxides, although its
underlying mechanism remains unclear [34, 35]. Several hypotheses have been pro-
posed regarding the origin of this pseudo-piezoelectric behavior. In this thesis, a new
mechanism is considered, previously demonstrated by A. Bergne in his Ph.D. disser-
tation [4]. Specifically, HZO exhibits a pronounced electrostrictive response that can
induce significant lattice strain, eventually stabilizing a polar phase and resulting
in a giant piezoelectric-like effect. Such behavior is not governed by intrinsic polar-
ization switching alone but emerges from an electro-mechanical interplay involving
large electrostriction (M = 10−14, m2V−2) and structural reconfiguration.

General Mechanisms for Non-Volatile Memories
Currently, non-volatile mechanisms have been categorized into five main sections;
Oxygen ion movement, Metal ion movement, Phase change memory, Spin-transfer-

1.5.3
Neuromorphic Computing
One of the most prominent approaches to information storage in emerging comput-
ing paradigms is shifting from charge-based storage to resistance modulation. Con-
ventional memory architectures rely on one-transistor–one-capacitor (1T1C) cells,
where the WL controls the gate of the access transistor, connecting the bit line BL
to the storage capacitor. Writing a “1” or “0” corresponds to charging or dischar-

READ operations are destructive and require precharged bit lines and sense ampli-
fiers, which significantly increase energy consumption [41]. In contrast, memristors
integrated into synaptic resistor circuits (synstor circuits) enable a brain-inspired,
Super-Turing computing platform.
Neuromorphic computing emulates biological
neural networks, where presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons communicate through
synapses formed by spike signals. In neurobiological systems, synaptic weights, rep-

1.6.1
Effect of Doping Type and Concentration on Ferro-

As demonstrated in [44], there is a correlation between zirconium concentration,
phase formation, and in-plane strain in ALD-deposited HZO films.
Zr-rich films

and tetravalent dopants in HfO2 films. The study presented in [45], systematically
investigated dopants such as Y, La, Ba, Nd, and Mg, all of which exhibited fer-
roelectric behavior. A notable finding is the correlation between ionic radius and
remanent polarization. Dopants with ionic radii larger than approximately 85 pm
tend to induce significantly higher remanent polarization compared to smaller ions.
Lastly, the thickness as well as the electrode engineering of the film influences the
stabilization of ferroelectricity due to the induced strain applied to the material.

2.1
Film Synthesis Characteristics
For thin-film deposition methods involving vapor-phase transport, a vacuum envir-
onment is essential. In ultra-high vacuum (UHV) systems, the extremely low back-
ground gas pressure minimizes collisions between the evaporated atoms or molecules
and residual gas species, allowing them to travel long distances without scattering.
This ensures a more directional flux and higher film quality compared to deposition
in non-vacuum conditions [51].
Thin-film deposition depends on the fabrication technique and the growth paramet-

the growth direction.
The orientation of the thin-films describes the order and the coherency of the film.

Physical Vapor Deposition
Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) is a vacuum coating process that turns materials

2.3.1
Pulse Laser Deposition
Pulse Laser Deposition (PLD) is a PVD growth technique in which material is ab-
lated from a target onto a substrate by high-intensity laser pulses. The oxide target is
a pressed ceramic pellet, and it is synthesized by conventional powder ceramic tech-
niques that contain the desirable material composition. For this technique, UHV is
of paramount importance due to the need of stoichiometric transfer from the target
to the heater. Plume is the process of the removal of material from the target, which
refers to ejected material cloud containing neutral atoms, ions and clusters that move
through vacuum. The excitation of the atoms originates from the high-intensity laser
pulses which are directed to the target itself [57]. As seen in Fig. 2.3d, the heater is
where the substrate is placed upon, and can reach temperatures up to 850◦C. The
pressure, is controlled through a rough and turbo pump connected in series [51].
Thin-film deposition is primarily governed by three critical factors; the substrate
properties, the deposition temperature, and the substrate-target distance. These

The

Also, defects could be engineered with temperature.

Afterward, the heater is inserted into the chamber, heated, and the deposition of
the buffer layer is initiated. Following buffer layer deposition, the sample is cooled
to room temperature. A shadow mask is then applied to prevent HZO from being
deposited on undesired regions of the buffer layer. Finally, the substrate is positioned
in the chamber, heated to 800◦C, and HZO deposition is carried out. Notably, this
protocol was consistently followed for all fabricated samples.
2.3.2
Sputtering
Sputtering is an indispensable PVD technique in which material is stripped from the
target and travels through vacuum to reach the designated substrate. This technique
doesn’t need UHV but pressures in the regime of 10−6 −10−7 mbar are enough for
the deposition of material. Sputtering requires an inert gas, a cathode, a target, a
heater and a fixed substrate. During depositing, the target functions as the cathode,
as illustrated in Fig. 2.4, while a high DC voltage (up to 1000V) is applied to it and
the rest of the chamber is grounded. The basic inert gas used is Argon and is ionized
close to the target. When ionized it is accelerated to the surface of the target which
eventually creates a plasma and the charged atoms, ions move through vacuum to
reach the designated substrate. In magnetron sputtering, a magnetic field confines
electrons near the target, enhancing ionization and enabling operation at lower gas
pressures [58].

3.2
Microscopy
When growing materials via PLD or sputtering, it is essential to investigate the sur-
face topology ex-situ. Conventional optical microscopes offer limited utility for this
purpose, as their resolution is constrained by the wavelength of visible light (approx-
imately one µm), making it impossible to resolve fine features such as atomic steps
and terraces. In contrast, Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) provides a resolution
down to 0.1 Å, enabling detailed characterization of nanoscale surface morphology.
3.2.1
Atomic Force Microscopy - AFM
AFM is based on the van der Walls interactions. A schematic of the AFM setup is
available in Fig. 3.1d, and it consists of laser, cantilever and photo-diode. The at
the top of the cantilever a tip of few Å thick is available. The laser is positioned
at the edge of the cantilever and its reflection is acquired from the diode.
The
cantilever resonates with a specific frequency (i.e. in tapping mode), and when it
approaches the surface the interaction sets up a potential and the tip will first feel
a force of attraction and then gets deflected. In this manner, it is possible to get
atomic resolution on flat surface or determining the surface morphology on a larger
scale.

Electro-Chemical Effect: Resistive Switching
in HZO with Pt Electrodes
In order to assess the electro-chemical effect, quasi-static I-V sweeps and retention
measurements were conducted. In Fig. 4.8, the voltage sweep characteristics of the
device DK003-a are presented. As shown in Fig. 4.8a, the current response during
the initial cycles (I–IV) remains symmetric under both positive and negative bias.
However, by the eighth cycle, the electroforming process effect becomes evident. As
Results and Discussion
49
==PAGE:60==
thoroughly discussed in Chapter 1.5.2, this phenomenon is attributed to pinned oxy-
gen vacancies, which gradually redistribute during repeated voltage cycling. Prior
to electroforming effect, the cycles follow the I–II–III–IV path, whereas post electro-
forming, the cycles follow the *I–II–III–IV path. In the pre-electroforming regime,
the device exhibits a linear (ohmic) response, as seen by the constant resistance
in Fig. 4.8b. The electroforming effect probably originates from the charge accu-
mulation within the thin-film, where oxygen vacancies acquire sufficient energy to
redistribute abruptly.
The *I–II–III–IV cycles exhibit hysteresis. In terms of band theory, the band gap of
HZO accommodates intermediate electronic states associated with the formation or
reduction of oxygen vacancy pathways. This statement is supported by the current
hysteresis observed along the *I–II and III–IV segments. Comparing the states II
and *I, the dissociation of oxygen vacancies reduces the density of available electronic
states relative to *I. Contrarily, comparing IV to III reveals that oxygen vacancies
have saturated, and conductive pathways have been established and eventually more
electronic states are available.
The DK0003-a device exhibits a thin-film thickness of 8nm, the same as in [36, 3].
Both references exhibit the same layer configurations. But there is a difference in
the growth mechanism with PLD. For DK003-a it was concluded that the growth
mechanism was first epitaxial and then converted to 2D or island-like. This probably,
introduces smaller hysteresis, due to lower number of free oxygen vacancies. Finally,

5.1
Conclusions
HZO is a highly versatile material whose multifunctional properties make it a prom-
ising candidate for next-generation devices which can encapsulate multi-phenomena
into one device. However, further investigation is required to disentangle the indi-
vidual phenomena and optimize them independently.
This work documents the existence of electro-mechanical, electro-chemical and elec-
trical phenomena as distinct yet interrelated mechanisms. Electro-chemical effect
is evident and demonstrated through comparative analysis of devices with differ-
ent electrode materials. Whereas, electro-mechanical coupling, is observed exclus-
ively in the in-plane configuration, as reported by A. Bergne [4], while the out-
of-plane configuration of the samples fabricated during this work, exhibit neither
pseudo-piezoelectric nor electrostrictive effects. This strongly suggests the electro-
mechanical coupling is geometry related. These findings support the existence of
electro-chemo-mechanical coupling, which may be enhanced by geometrical inter-
play.
XRD characterization indicates that LSMO is single-crystalline, whereas HZO ex-
hibits polymorphic behavior. Optimization of deposition pressure and temperature
could enable the manifestation of ferroelectricity in the same device by increasing
the XRD peak intensities corresponding to the o-III and t-phases of HZO. Achieving
59
==PAGE:70==
robust ferroelectricity in HZO would enable synergistic coupling with memristive
and pseudo-piezoelectric behaviors, paving the way for advanced neuromorphic and
adaptive systems.
5.2
Future Research Topics on HZO
Owing to the versatility of HZO, electronics could benefit in numerous emerging
applications.
Future work should explore cantilever-based out-of-plane measure-
ments to validate pseudo-piezoelectricity under high electric fields and optimize de-
position parameters to enable ferroelectricity. Such advancements could guide the
next-generation of multi-functional (multi-modal) devices.
Another interesting idea would be ferroelectricity mediation by means of rapid an-
nealing process of HZO. As discussed in the HZO thin-film engineering sub-chapter,
the o-III phase can be configured via oxygen vacancies, hence by applying topotactic
high-energy bursts through laser pulses under reducing conditions, followed by rapid
cooling, the unit cells can adopt the o-III phase.
In respect to integrated circuits, HZO offers intriguing possibilities. For example,
3D monolithic stacking of electro-chemical mechanisms is an novel process which
can overcome the Moore’s law. Additionally, freestanding HZO membranes provide
a promising platform for exploiting electro-chemo-mechanical coupling.
