Nurse Rostering in the Emergency Department
Abstract
This study examines how implementation of operation research techniques can
impact the cost-eﬀectiveness of nurse rosters at the emergency department of Slagelse
hospital. The problem formulation is generalized, assuming all competences are avail-
able throughout the planning horizon. Nurse rostering is a well known problem within
operations research and earlier studies have revealed various approaches for eﬃciently
solving diﬀerent instances. The applicability of diﬀerent methods is however highly
dependant on the speciﬁc instance, as the formulation of constraints can diﬀer signif-
icantly.
For this instance, a heuristical column generation procedure was considered to be
a desirable approach. The columns of the master problem are rosters for individual
nurses, which are generated using a shortest path heuristic. An integral solution to the
master problem is then found using an eﬃcient cut-and-solve algorithm. The method
is ﬂexible with respect to modiﬁcations in the formulation.
The results show a possibility of savings up to 40%. In addition, the solution
obtained by the heuristic is only 3% from a theoretical lower bound.
The results
revealed potential for future research, including adjusting the problem to relate better
to the real scenario.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Personnel rostering is a classical problem which arises within many professions all around
the world. It is the job of assigning personnel to tasks at certain times to generate a
complete schedule satisfying some pre-deﬁned requirements. Many stakeholders need to
be considered when generating rosters, and their needs and preferences can be conﬂicting.
Therefore it can be a diﬃcult task to generate good and balanced rosters, fulﬁlling the
needs of all stakeholders.
This project is a master thesis at DTU Management Engineering department on nurse
rostering and how it can be improved by implementing operations research techniques. It
is conducted in collaboration with Region Sjælland and considers the rosters generated for
nurses at the emergency department of Slagelse hospital.
When generating the rosters there are three main stakeholders which need to be consid-
ered. First we have the patients, which we want to oﬀer a high service level and security
during their time in the emergency department. To fulﬁll that we need to have skilled
nurses available 24 hours a day carrying out various tasks in the department. The second
group of stakeholders are the nurses working in the department. There are agreements
regarding their working hours and conditions which need to be fulﬁlled, as well as some
individual agreements and preferences. The ﬁnal stakeholder is Region Sjælland, which
would like the rosters generated to be cost-eﬀective.
The rosters are currently generated manually by a planning nurse, who tries to satisfy
the stakeholders needs. This is not an easy task due to the extent of the problem. It
has proven diﬃcult to have the correct competences present at all times, especially for
tasks where it takes time to develop the competences needed. This has resulted in un-
dercoverage, as well as some nurses working relatively more than others. Another aspect
is the cost-eﬀectiveness of the rosters generated. In 2014, supplement salary for nurses
working evenings, nights or weekends was 8% of the total salary cost for all employees in
the emergency department at Slagelse hospital, including doctors and other caretakers.
In 2013, Denmark spent 10.4% of their GNP on healthcare, compared to a 9% average
in the OECD countries. For the same year, the average life expectancy in Denmark was
the lowest out of seven comparison countries, including the United Kingdom and Finland
which spent around 8.5 % of their GNP on healthcare [21]. In 2014, Region Sjælland had
the highest healthcare expenses per citizen of all the regions in Denmark, around 19.500
DKK compared to an average well below 19.000 DKK in entire Denmark [22].
The National Audit Oﬃce of Denmark published a report in March 2015 on the uti-
lization of personnel in hospitals in Denmark. The main conclusion was that the resources
can generally be utilized more eﬃciently within the healthcare sector. It also concluded
that the rosters generated are not always cost-eﬀective and in some cases they do not fully
utilize the agreed working hours corresponding to a ﬁxed monthly salary [19]. Region
Sjælland would like to improve on those aspects and this project is a part of that process.

Introduction
1.1
General problem description
Personnel rostering is applied in many professions, including healthcare, to obtain a bal-
anced schedule satisfying the needs of various stakeholders. Nurse rostering is focused on
generating cost-eﬀective rosters for nurses with a required service level for patients while
fulﬁlling regulations and laws regarding working hours and resting periods.
The regulations and other speciﬁcations of the problem can vary signiﬁcantly based
on each instance. Many restrictions are related to collective union agreements for nurses,
which can diﬀer between countries. Local agreements along with individual agreements
and problem speciﬁc preferences also need to be considered, and the combination of dif-
ferent agreements can become highly complex. Diﬀerent instances often include similar
requirements, but the formulation can however be very diﬀerent. Therefore each instance
needs to be developed based on its own speciﬁcations.
We now consider a three day roster for six nurses as presented in Table 1.1. There are
three shifts each day, shift A from midnight to 8 am, shift B from 8 am to 4 pm and shift C
from 4 pm to midnight. We require one general nurse to work each shift and additionally
one nurse to answer the telephone during shift B. All nurses are qualiﬁed to work as general
nurses, but only nurses 4-6 have the qualiﬁcations needed to work on the telephone. The
nurses are only allowed to work 1 shift within each 24 hours and we assume they all have
identical contracts with respect to their working hours and ﬁxed salary.

Chapter 2
Literature review
The nurse rostering problem (NRP) is a classical problem within operations research and
has been widely studied through the years. Even though all NRPs have the same purpose
the requirements often diﬀer between instances, e.g. due to diﬀerent regulations or agree-
ments. A wide range of models and solution methods have therefore been developed for
diﬀerent instances of the NRP, leading back to the 1960s [15].
In 2003, Cheang et al. [10] gathered diﬀerent modelling techniques and methodologies
which had been published for the NRP, clearly presenting its problem speciﬁc properties.
Most NRPs are highly constrained in nature, including various hard and soft constraints.
All NRPs include some sort of resting requirements for the nurses, but the exact formula-
tion of these requirements is highly dependant on the speciﬁc instance. Another common
constraint is regarding coverage requirements, i.e.
ensuring that the number of nurses
working meet the demand guidelines set by the hospital. The criteria used to evaluate the
quality of the roster generated can also vary. In the simplest cases we can have mathe-
matical programs with a single-objective, but in more complex cases we can have complex
evaluation functions incorporating various penalty factors related to soft constraints.
Various operations research methods have been proposed to solve diﬀerent instances
of the NRP, including diﬀerent mathematical programming models, heuristics and meta-
heuristics. Due to the instance speciﬁc characteristics of the problem it has proven diﬃcult
to compare the quality of diﬀerent solution methods and algorithms. As this is a highly
constrained problem, the trade-oﬀbetween time and optimality needs to be considered
from the hospitals point of view. Obtaining and proving the optimality of a solution can
take a long time, whereas a good enough solution might be generated much faster.
Among the earliest studies considering a weekly time-oﬀfor employees utilized cyclic
scheduling to obtain an optimal schedule for a simpliﬁed problem with a single objective
[6]. Another research applied cyclic scheduling to propose an algorithm to solve a more
generalized problem covering diﬀerent labour constraints. A major advantage of this was
lower computational eﬀorts as well as the possibility of evaluating the roster quality with
respect to the chosen labour constaints [20]. We note that both these studies simplify the
problem, excluding diﬀerent shifts and only focusing on days.
Even though cyclic scheduling does not require as much computational eﬀort as non-
cyclic scheduling, it also has low adaptability with regards to sudden changes which can
be a huge disadvantage. Therefore, with increasing computational possibilities, non-cyclic
scheduling began to be applied to solve the NRP to optimality. In 1998, Millar and Kiragu
[18] compared these two methods using network programming considering 12 hour shifts.
Their result showed that the non-cyclic method used up to 10 times the computational
eﬀorts of the cyclic method, but on the other hand returned much better solutions.
In the same year, Jaumard et al. [16] presented a column generation approach to solve
the NRP. The variables in the master problem contained feasible schedules for single nurses,

which were generated in the subproblem by solving a resource constrained shortest path
problem. The approach was generalized, covering most of the restrictions needed for a
realistic problem. Branching techniques were applied to obtain the optimal solution, but
the authors also suggest terminating after ﬁnding a number of feasible solutions to choose
from.
In 1996, Berreda et al. [7] proposed a multi-objective programming approach, based on
several objectives from other NRP models. They applied both an iterative approach and a
weighting of objectives, as well as introducing a tabu search approach. The ﬁrst two meth-
ods were implemented using a commercial software package and had low computational
time. Tabu search was however very experimental during that time and the computations
took much longer.
Meta-heuristics, including tabu search, have been increasingly implemented from the
late 1990s to obtain good solutions to the NRP within a short computational time. In
2001, Burke et al. [8] applied a memetic algorithm incorporating a tabu search to diﬃ-
cult problems from Belgian hospitals. The computational time was much higher than for
previous research which only included implementation of tabu search. The results were
however much better and more robust with respect to changes in parameters.
Burke et al. [9] also implemented a hybrid approach containing integer programming
and variable neighbourhood search on a decomposed model. In the ﬁrst phase a problem
containing a subset of the soft constraints was solved using integer programming, and
then improved to account for the excluded constraints in the second phase using variable
neighbourhood search. The results show that the decomposition of the model shortened
the computational time signiﬁcantly, compared to including all the soft constraints from
the beginning.
In 2012, Lu and Hao [17] implemented an Adaptive Neighbourhood Search algorithm
on 60 instances for the First International Nurse Rostering Competition (INRC-2010) [14].
The algorithm obtained a solution for all 60 instances, including the best known solutions
for 39 instances and an improvement on the best known solution for 12 instances. The
winners of the INRC-2010 [23], implemented a two-phase integer programming approach,
assigning nurses to days within the ﬁrst phase and arranging a day into shifts in the second
phase. They obtained the best known solutions for 42 instances, and were close to the best
known for the other 18 instances.
In the recent years, eﬃcient column generation algorithms have been implemented to
solve the NRP. Dohn and Mason [12] applied generic programming to create a branch and
price framework for the general staﬀrostering problem. The subproblem was solved as a
resource constrained shortest path problem, breaking the rosters into smaller entities for
increased eﬃciency. The framework was used to solve three instances with diﬀerent rules
for a four week planning period. The approach resulted in the optimal solution for two of
those within 15 minutes and a high-quality solution for the third. Baeklund [5] proposed a
branch and price method for a ward in a Danish hospital, using a constraint programming
approach to solve the subproblem. The method eﬃciently solved the problem for a two
week period, but was unsuccessful for a three week period.

Chapter 3
Problem speciﬁcations
The scope of this project is the rosters generated for nurses at Slagelse emergency depart-
ment, more speciﬁcally Akut 1. The rosters include nurses with a ﬁxed monthly salary,
both full-time and part-time. We consider a general planning period for the roster, i.e.
assuming vacations, sick leave, public holidays and other exceptions do not occur within
the period. A feasible roster ensures that all tasks within the department are covered by
nurses with the appropriate competences at all times, while satisfying various agreements
as presented in Section 3.1. The structure of the tasks and speciﬁcations in the agreements
ensure that we generate rosters which satisfy both the needs of the patients as well as the
nurses. We use real data for the nurses currently working in the emergency departments
along with the shift patterns used for diﬀerent tasks. The data is presented in Section 3.2,
including some adjustments that needed to be made. The overall objective of the project
is to minimize the total cost of supplementary salary, and thereby satisfying the needs
of Region Sjælland. To improve the roster and adjust it even further to the stakeholders
needs, various preferences can be considered in the objective. In Section 3.3 we discuss
some preferences that are relevant for this case.
3.1
Agreements
Most of the constraints for the nurse rostering problem are related to various agreements.
The agreements which need to be considered at Slagelse emergency department are as
follows:
(A) Collective agreement for nurses in Denmark [13].
(B) Announcement regarding resting periods [1].
(C) Local agreement for the emergency department at Slagelse hospital [3].
(D) Preliminary agreement between DSR and Slagelse hospital [2].
(E) Daily plan for the emergency department [4], covered in Section 3.2.
(F) Individual agreements for nurses.
The rosters should be generated for a 16 week period according to (C). According to
(A), each nurse should on average work as close to the number of hours a week that are
stated in (F) without exceeding them. The hours can vary between diﬀerent weeks, as
long as the average remains the same and other regulations are not violated.
Each nurse should get a continuous break of at least 11 hours within each 24 hours
according to (B). At Slagelse emergency department, the nurses can volunteer to reduce

the break to 8 hours at most 2 times every 4 weeks according to (C). Since this is voluntary
it will be excluded from the general roster and a resting period of 11 hours minimum used.
The nurses should also get 2 days oﬀwithin each 7 days according to (B) and (C), i.e.
in total 32 days oﬀwithin the planning period. These can either be two continuous days
(period of 55-64 hours) or two seperate days (each 35 hours minimum). It is possible to
extend the time oﬀto three consecutive days by adding 24 hours, i.e. 79-88 hours oﬀ.
According to (A) the nurses get a supplement salary for working evenings, nights and
weekends. These supplements are a percentage of the nurses hourly rate, which is derived
from their ﬁxed yearly salary. The nurses can choose to either get paid the supplement
or get time oﬀas compensation, but we assume all nurses get the supplement paid. The
supplements are paid by the hour and are calculated for each 30 minutes begun. The
compensations for diﬀerent times are as follows:
• Work during the evening (18.00-23.00) gives a supplement of 27%.
• Work during the night (23.00-06.00) gives a supplement of 32.5%.
• Work during the weekend (Sat 06.00 - Sun 24.00) gives a supplement of 42%.
According to (D) there are additional supplements for covering certain tasks and work-
ing the night shift from Saturday to Sunday.
These supplements are a ﬁxed amount
independent of the nurses salary and all feasible rosters result in those supplements. They
are therefore a constant term in the objective and will be excluded from the problem
formulation.
3.2
Data
There are around 70 nurses working either full-time or part-time in the emergency depart-
ment at Slagelse hospital. Two nurses, the planning nurse and the clinical instructor, are
excluded from the project. These nurses are generally not a part of the group we generate
the rosters for, but they can step in while others are on vacation. All other nurses employed
in June 2015 are included.
The nurses have diﬀerent competences and experience and their monthly salaries are
ﬁxed based on that and the hours stated in their individual agreements. The data for
the nurses comes from two separate data ﬁles, a document gathering the competences of
diﬀerent nurses created by the planning nurse in June 2015 and an overview of the monthly
salaries for February 2015. There are some inconsistencies between those two documents,
as they are not generated at the same time. Due to these inconsistencies the following
adjustments are made:
• Two nurses are not included in the salary document, but an appropriate salary is
estimated based the number of hours they work and their competences.
• Seven nurses have diﬀerent number of hours a week stated in the two documents.
For those conﬂicts, the information from the salary document was used.
The nurses can be assigned to diﬀerent tasks within the emergency department based
on their competences. The coordination nurse is responsible for all nurses working in the
department at a given time. The triage nurse examines the patients when they arrive
to categorize the severity of their condition. The nurses in the emergency room examine
and treat patients with less severe conditions, e.g. broken bones. The nurses employed at
Slagelse hospital can also be sent to Næstved hospital, which is considered a special task.
Finally there is the ﬂow-coordinator, who is responsible for routing the patients to wards

3.3
Preferences
For most rostering problems there are preferences, which the stakeholders would like to
be fulﬁlled but are willing to ignore if the associated cost is too high. Three preferences
came to consideration regarding the emergency department at Slagelse. In this section we
will go through these preferences, how they are currently used and if/how they should be
implemented into this project.
The ﬁrst preference is that at least one of the nurses available at the emergency depart-
ment needs to have much of experience, which we deﬁne as over 10 years worth. This is
not currently considered when creating the plan at the emergency department, but is still
something we would like to implement to some extent. We consider the nurses working
the tasks corresponding to shifts II, IV and VI to be physically present at the emergency
department. In Table 3.3 we can see the tasks this applies to and how many of the nurses
qualiﬁed for each task have 10 years experience or more. We note that the coordination,
triage and treatment are more advanced tasks which the nurses have been specially trained
to conduct. Therefore it is to be expected that these nurses generally have longer working
experience.

If we were to consider all these tasks it is highly likely that at least one nurse working
has 10 years of experience, and therefore implementing that as a preference might not
have any signiﬁcant eﬀect. Therefore when implementing the preference we only include
the more basic tasks, i.e. general and emergency room. That way we focus on maintaining
experience in the tasks the nurses need less speciﬁc training for. Currently, the nurses
which have trained for the more advanced tasks are often hesitant to work the more basic
tasks and therefore the experience at that level decreases.
We will therefore include the following preference: We prefer that at least one of the
nurses covering the general task or the emergency room has at least 10 years of experi-
ence. This preference does not only maintain experience, it also brings us to the second
preference.

as long as a nurse has the competences she can

Chapter 5
Possible solution approaches
Comparing this instance of the NRP to other instances presented to date, two main dif-
ferences can be seen. The ﬁrst is the length of the planning horizon, 16 weeks, which is in
general only 2-4 weeks. Based on this, and the fact that the problem is NP-hard, it can
be assumed highly unlikely that the optimal solution for this instances can be obtained
within reasonable time. Due to the long planning horizon it might become necessary to
break the problem down into shorter periods which would then be combined to form a
roster for the entire planning horizon.
The second diﬀerence lies in the formulation of the weekly time oﬀ, which is often
formulated as a maximum number of consecutive working days and a minimum number of
days oﬀ. If there are requirements regarding a sub-period of the planning horizon they are
generally for each week, e.g. from Monday to Sunday. For this instance we however need
to consider each seven day period within the planning horizon, i.e. Monday to Sunday,
Tuesday to Monday etc. In addition there are diﬀerent options when it comes to time oﬀ,
i.e. whether it should be continuous and the number of hours required.
For this instance it is therefore diﬃcult to join two or more rosters to form a longer
one, as the combination is likely be infeasible due to the weekly time oﬀ. Even for feasible
combinations, many criteria need to be checked to conﬁrm feasibility, which can be time-
consuming. Therefore we would like to generate rosters for shorter periods which could
be repeated to form a roster for the entire planning horizon. To do that we require an
additional property of circular feasibility, i.e. that the ﬁrst week of the roster succeeding
the last week is feasible with respect to the weekly time oﬀand resting time between shifts.
Including this circular property in the solution approach both allows for the 16 week roster
to be generated from shorter rosters, and repeating the best-known 16 week roster when
a new planning horizon begins.
The MIP model presented in Chapter 4 can be modiﬁed to include the circular prop-
erty by changing the conditions of constraints (4.8), (4.11) and (4.13)-(4.20). Solving the
circular version directly with CPLEX gives the optimal 1 week roster within a minute, but
a direct approach running for a few hours is not able to ﬁnd any feasible two week rosters.
Various solution methods could be applicable for this instance, and the quality of
diﬀerent methods can not be fully known without implementing them. In this project we
will implement one solution method, chosen based on the problem structure and likelihood
of success. Based on the properties described above, the ﬁrst decision made was not to try
to reach the optimal solution using exact methods, but rather consider heuristic approaches
to obtain a good roster within a shorter computational time.
One possibility is to implement a metaheuristic to obtain a good solution for the prob-
lem instance. Various metaheuristics have been implemented to solve diﬀerent instances
of the NRP, and many have produced near-optimal solutions within reasonable time as
discussed in Chapter 2.
These instances are however more simple than the rosters at

Slagelse emergency department, e.g. the First International Nurse Rostering Competi-
tion [14] used datasets with up to 2 diﬀerent tasks, 5 diﬀerent shifts and 51 nurses, as
well as the constraints not being as extensive as in our case. The main drawback when
applying metaheuristics is however their inability to handle hard constraints eﬃciently.
As this instance is highly constrained, especially regarding the weekly time oﬀ, it is not
considered desirable to implement a metaheuristic.
Another possibility would be to decompose the problem and use heuristic approaches to
solve it. One option is Benders decomposition, where we include few of the variables, called
linking variables, in a master problem, and move the rest of the variables to subproblems.
Initially the master problem is unconstrained, but we can dynamically generate cuts by
solving the subproblems. The generation of cuts will then terminate when the solution to
the master problem is feasible with respect to all the constraints in the original problem
formulation.
Reducing the number of constraints needed could seem desirable for this
highly constrained problem.
However it does not have a good structure to implement
Benders decomposition as there are no linking variables connecting a majority of the
constraints.
Another decomposition option is Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition, where we deﬁne a mas-
ter problem which includes a few linking constraints and a set of initial variables which
make it feasible.
The rest of the constraints, and the main complexity of the original
problem, is moved to subproblems, which we can solve to generate new variables to add to
the master problem. This process is referred to as delayed column generation. This prob-
lem has an ideal structure for a Dantzig-Wolfe decompositions, as most of the constraints
are speciﬁed for each nurse, e.g. working hours and time oﬀ. The suggested decomposi-
tion would therefore be to generate rosters for single nurses in subproblems, and combine
these rosters to form a feasible plan for the entire emergency department within a master
problem.
The main eﬀorts within a column generation procedure often occur within the sub-
problems, as we require the solutions to them to be integer feasible. Solving large MIP
models directly to optimality can be time consuming, and as we are not looking to prove
optimality it is also unnecessary. We therefore need to look into possible reformulations
or heuristics to eﬃciently solve the subproblems.
A common method is to reformulate the subproblem as a resource constrained shortest
path problem, as Jaumard et al. [16] presented in 1998. A diﬃcult aspect with this formu-
lation is to include time oﬀ, as the feasibility of some arcs is dependant on whether other
arcs are used in the solution, which adds additional constraints to the formulation. Due
to those constraints and the resource constraint regarding working hours, the constraint
matrix is not totally unimodular and therefore solving the LP relaxation is not guaran-
teed to result in an integer feasible solution. To obtain the optimal solution we therefore
would need to use a MIP solver or exact algorithms, e.g. label correcting algorithm for the
resource constrained shortest path problem, which would result in an exponential running
time in the worst case scenario.
In 2013, Dohn and Mason [12] developed a framework where they broke the subproblem
down into 3 phases, solving the NRP to optimality. The ﬁrst phase considered the genera-
tion of on-stretches, i.e. continuous working periods, by solving an all-to-all shortest path
problem. The second phase combined on-stretches with oﬀ-stretches into work-stretches
and the third phase was formulated as a shortest path problem to generate a ﬁnal roster
from the feasible work-strethces. This approach was considered, but it was estimated as
too much overhead work for an eﬃcient heuristic. When creating the on-stretches for each
subproblem there would be up to 20 feasible shift-task combinations for each day. In ad-
dition, a feasible on-stretch starting on day d within the planning horizon can range from
1 to 5 days, making the all-to-all shortest path problem really large. Another problem
would arise in the third phase, i.e. when generating the ﬁnal roster from work-stretches, as

the time-oﬀwithin each 7 days requires a complex formulation. These aspects make the
approach less desirable, specially since we are not looking to prove optimality.
It was decided to solve the subproblems by going heuristically through a resource
constrained shortest path graph. The graph should be set up to include nodes for feasible
shifts and tasks as well as for days oﬀ. The main beneﬁt of going heuristically through
each day from start to ﬁnish, is that when making the decision for day d we know the
decisions made for previous days and therefore which arcs are feasible with respect to the
partial solution. An exception is within the last week due to the circular feasibility, as it
is not enough to know the previous days to ensure that property. The rosters could be
generated as described and a feasibility check performed afterwards to eliminate rosters
that do not possess circular feasibility.
Various criteria can be used to make a decision on each day. One possibility would be
to choose all feasible nodes and thereby enumerate all rosters by going recursively through
the graph. This would result in a very large recursion tree including all possible rosters
which could be added to the master problem. The main drawback of this procedure is its
eﬃciency, as it consumes a lot of time and memory. In addition, having all the columns in
the master problem rather than adding few at a time would result in a very large MIP and
might defeat the purpose of the decomposition and even exceed the computational eﬀorts
of the solver used.
Another way could be to only consider one node for each day, going through the
graph using a greedy criteria, e.g. minimizing cost. A drawback of this approach is that
generating only one column for a subproblem might not have enough eﬀect on the master
problem to support an eﬃcient column generation framework. Another thing that needs to
be considered is the possibility of a partial roster becoming infeasible and the elimination
of rosters that do not fulﬁll circular feasibility. For those cases we do not add columns to
the master and might terminate even though many improving columns might exist.
The third option considered was a combination of these two methods, i.e. an algorithm
that uses a greedy criteria to make a decision each day, but still allowing for more options to
be considered. This would reduce the risk of terminating the column generation procedure
to early, but since the columns are generated heuristically we do not guarantee that no
improving columns exist when we terminate.
When applying column generation we solve the LP relaxation of the master problem
in each iteration. After termination we need to think about how we will force integrality.
Since this is approach generates columns heuristically and will therefore not solve the
problem to optimality, a complete branch and price framework is not considered eﬃcient,
but rather exploring the solution space using branch and bound. The eﬃciency of a branch
and bound procedure is highly dependant on the number of variables, as it can consume
memory and time for larger problems. If needed, various techniques can be used to ﬁnd a
solution more quickly, e.g. rounding variables that are highly likely to be included in the
solution or pruning variables that are not likely to be included.

Column generation heuristic
Based on the block-angular structure of the formulation, a Dantzig-Wolfe decomposition
with a delayed column generation heuristic was chosen as an appropriate solution method
to obtain a good roster for the nurses in the emergency department. When decomposing
the mathematical formulation presented in Chapter 4, the requirement constraints (4.6)
along with the experience constraints (4.21) create a link between all nurses and are kept in
the master problem, as presented in Section 6.1. All other constraints are nurse-speciﬁc and
moved to the subproblems, which are solved using a shortest path heuristic as described
in Section 6.2. Due to the uniqueness of the nurses, only a few can be grouped based on
agreements, competences and salary. Therefore we have 53 diﬀerent subproblems for 70
nurses. An overview of the column generation procedure is presented with Figure 6.1.
Figure 6.1: An overview of the column generation process.
Various decisions can aﬀect the eﬃciency of this process. The ﬁrst decision is regarding
how we include the subproblems in the procedure, and brings forth the following questions:
Do we solve all the subproblems in every single iteration? Do we solve only one of them at
a time? Or do we maybe solve a small subset of them? There is no one correct answer to
these questions, as the choice is highly dependant on the number of subproblems and the
time spent solving them. For time consuming subproblems it might be a waste to solve
to many of them using the same dual values from the master problem and better to solve
only a few in each iteration. On the other hand, if the subproblems take only a short time
to solve then we might want to solve many (or even all) in each iteration to generate more
columns for the master problem in hopes of a faster convergence.
The second decision is regarding the columns we choose to add to the master problem.
How many columns do we add in each iteration? Do we go through the columns to add only
the ones with the most negative reduced cost? Or do we settle for adding any columns with
negative reduced cost? These questions also need to be answered with the computational
time in mind. Adding many similar columns in each iteration results in a rapidly growing
master problem, which might become diﬃcult to solve when the integrality requirement is

Finally there are some further decisions to be made if the column generator is not

the convergence or reduce the eﬀorts spent in the subproblems. As this is a heuristical

greedy method, but rather a sparse recursion tree considering only a few good options

6.3
Forcing integrality in the master problem
When no more negative reduced cost columns can be found in the subproblems, we move
on to ﬁnding integral feasible solutions to the master problem. A classic way to force
integrality in column generation problems is to implement a branch and price framework,
adding improving columns at every node, not just the root node.
When generating columns at every node, we must consider our branching decisions
carefully.
If we were to branch on the master problem variables λij then the rate of
convergence in the branch and price procedure would not be high, as we could generate
similar columns at all nodes. The partitioning of the solution space would therefore not
be too eﬀective. The branching tree would also be highly unbalanced, and it would not be
possible to know the depth beforehand. Therefore it would be a wise decision not to branch
on the master problem variables, but rather to branch on the variables’ characteristics. For
this nurse rostering problem it would be sensible to branch on the arcs generated with the
shortest path heuristic from the subproblems, i.e. the combinations of zeros and ones in
the kdstij parameter. By that branching strategy we can ensure that the ﬂow from a node
on day d to a node on day d + 1 is not split between many arcs. That limits both the size
of the branching tree as we have a ﬁnite number of arcs as well as reducing the number of
columns generated at each node by putting additional constraints on their structure.
As we are aiming for eﬃciency and not trying to prove optimality, we will only use the
columns which have already been generated at the root node rather then implementing
a branch and price framework. Solving the master problem directly to optimality with a
MIP solver using branch and bound is a simple approach. However, it is unrealistic for
larger problems as the branching tree grows rapidly. This can cause problems, not only
with the time spent but also with the memory needed to keep track of all the nodes within
the tree. Therefore some other methods need to be considered to increase the eﬃciency.
In this section we introduce methods which were considered, and in Section 7.2 we will
discuss the eﬀectiveness of those methods for this instance.

The ﬁrst method considered was rounding, where variables which are close to the next
integer value above are rounded up, i.e. given the integer as a lower bound. This method
is not time consuming, as it identiﬁes one basic variable at a time to be rounded, resolves
the LP relaxation with that requirement and then repeats. We choose to set an additional
requirement on the variable choice, i.e. that it has to be closer to the next integer value
above than to the next integer value below. This means that we terminate and move on
to branch and bound when all fractional variables in the basis would be rounded down.
We can also choose to move to branch and bound earlier, e.g. after a given number of
variables have been rounded.
The main advantage of this method is its speed, but on the other hand we might be
eliminating variables which are needed to get good solutions. When rounding one variable
it has an eﬀect on all other variables for that nurse type, as we only have a ﬁxed number of
each nurse type available. This method is therefore only an approximation, but has been
known to ﬁnd good solutions to large MIP problems.
The second method considered was pruning, which is implemented within the column
generation procedure but not the ﬁnal master problem.
While solving the LP relaxed
master problem in each iteration we also consider the columns we are using and which
columns we have been using. If a column has not been used in the LP optimal solution
for a certain number of iterations we assume that it will not be used in the ﬁnal solution
and delete it, hoping that fewer variables will be a part of the branching tree in the ﬁnal
master problem.
Generally this is not an invasive procedure, as the column should be regenerated if it
were to have negative reduced cost. For this instance we are however using a heuristic
to solve the subproblems and therefore we do not guarantee that the columns we might
need would be regenerated. We note that we never prune the empty columns, i.e. a nurse
working no shifts, as they might be needed for feasibility and will never be regenerated
due to restrictions in the subproblems.
The third method considered was a cut-and-solve algorithm, presented by Climer
and Zhang in 2006 [11]. The aim of this method is to obtain the optimal solution to a
large MIP relatively quickly by breaking it down and solving many smaller MIPs rather
than one large. In each iteration we divide the solution space by adding piercing cuts as
shown on Figure 6.4.
Figure 6.4: In each iteration i we divide the solution space into a small set Ai, which
we solve to integer optimality, and a larger set Bi, where we solve the LP
relaxation of the problem [11].

In each iteration i we go through the following steps:
(1) Identify a cut and deﬁne the set Ai.
(2) Solve the problem to integer optimality over Ai.
(3) Update the best MIP solution found if needed.
(4) Add a piercing cut to deﬁne the set Bi.
(5) Solve the LP relaxation over Bi.
The solutions to Ai are integer feasible and provide an upper bound on the optimal
solution, while the solutions to Bi are fractional and provide a lower bound. When the
lower bound for Bi exceeds the best integer feasible solution found, then we have proven
that the optimal integer solution for the entire solution space has been found and we
terminate. This process is displayed on Figure 6.5. The algorithm can also be terminated
earlier without proving optimality, e.g. when we are satisﬁed with the gap or with the best
integer feasible solution found.

Testing
The solution approach described in Chapter 6 was implemented in Java using ILOG CPLEX.
In this chapter we present the adjustments and tuning applied when implementing the
approach as well as the results. As the method is deterministic we only need to run it
once when tuning our parameters.
Regarding the implementation, Section 7.1 focuses
on the generation of columns, while the integrality conditions in the master problem are
considered in Section 7.2.
Section 7.3 then presents the results for diﬀerent penalties
associated to soft constraints. The best solution is then further discussed in Section 7.4
and the quality of it evaluated in Section 7.5 by comparing to a theoretical lower bound.
The 16 week roster generated in this project is obtained by repeating a 2 week partial
roster 8 times. When choosing the length of the partial rosters two options were considered,
i.e. 2 weeks and 4 weeks. The column generation was applied for both cases, without
ﬁnding integral solutions to the master problem. By increasing the roster length from 2
weeks to 4 weeks the number of columns generated quadrupled and the time spent exceeded
an hour, compared to only 5 minutes. In addition, there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in
the lower bound for these two instances.
The complexity of the master problem also increases when the roster length is increased
from 2 weeks to 4 weeks, as the number of constraints is almost double. No feasible 4 week
rosters were not found within 24 hours, while a good 2 week roster could be found within
a couple of hours. Due to these signiﬁcant diﬀerences in time it was decided to continue
with a 2 weeks roster.
7.1

Like presented in Chapter 6, various aspect of the column generation procedure along with
the subproblem can be tuned to get the most eﬃciency. The column generation procedure

iteration. We hope that the additional time spent ﬁnding these speciﬁc columns results
in fewer columns generated and less eﬀorts when ﬁnding integral solutions to the master
problem.

7.2
Integral solutions to master problem
Finding integral solutions to the master problem proved to be the most diﬃcult and time
consuming part of the solution approach, as the number of columns generated was sub-
stantial and the size of the branching tree became an issue. The three methods introduced
in Section 6.3 were implemented with hopes of reducing the size of the branching tree to
eliminate memory errors and ﬁnd a good integer feasible solution more rapidly.
The simple methods, rounding and pruning, were both unsuccessful as there was ex-
treme sensitivity w.r.t. parameter choices. In both cases rounding too many or pruning
too often led to infeasibility, while not rounding or pruning enough led to memory errors
within the branching tree. No integer feasible solutions were found for any parameter set-
tings with rounding or pruning. These methods were also implemented as one combined
method without any results.
We believe that there are two main reasons for these methods to fail. The ﬁrst one
is the high number of variables with low fractional values in the LP solutions, as well
as the number of variables with value zero in the basis. Applying these simple methods
seems to eliminate many variables with low reduced cost, which aﬀects the eﬃciency when
searching for a good integral solution. We believe that the uniqueness of diﬀerent nurses is
the second reason for these methods failing. Some tasks, e.g. ﬂow-coordination, can only
be assigned to few nurses. Therefore rounding or pruning columns relating to those tasks
and nurses can have a large impact on feasibility.
Cut-and-solve was the third method considered, as it reduces the size of the branching
tree based on diﬀerent criteria than the other two. This method is also highly sensitive
w.r.t. parameter choices, but it is able to ﬁnd feasible solutions for some parameter set-
tings. Due to the sensitivity, the cut-and-solve algorithm must be modiﬁed to account for
infeasibility or memory errors as is presented with Algorithm 3. The main diﬀerences from
the general cut-and-solve algorithm occur in lines 6-13 where we redeﬁne the α value if
problems occur within an iteration, and in line 18 where we decrease the α value between
iterations.
The choice of increase or decrease as well as the initial α was tuned based on the
number of errors occurring and the eﬃciency when ﬁnding integer feasible solutions over
Ai. It was chosen to decrease α by 50% between iterations. If an error occurred within an
iteration then the ﬁrst change was an increase or decrease by 20%. If more than one error
occurred then the update was reduced gradually.
Another adjustment made was allowing a certain gap when ﬁnding integer solutions
over Ai to reduce the time spent proving the optimality of the solution and to reduce
the chance of memory errors occurring. The allowed gap was chosen as 3%. Having this
allowance lower gave worse results, as memory errors often occurred and more time was
spent on redeﬁning Ai and resolving the problem. To increase the eﬃciency even further
we emphasised feasibility over optimality in our branch and bound search tree.
Since we allow for a gap when ﬁnding integer solutions, we also need to allow a gap in
the termination criteria. It was decided to terminate the cut-and-solve algorithm when the
best integer solution found was less than 5% from the lower bound. After these adjustments

7.4
Postprocessing
As mentioned in Section 6.1, the problem we have solved is a relaxation of the original
problem allowing for excess nurses to be assigned to all tasks and shifts. In some cases
we can appreciate overcoverage, e.g. for the general task. In other cases it is however not
desirable and might become problematic, e.g. if more than one nurse is coordinating the
emergency department.
We therefore need to modify the rosters generated to eliminate overcoverage where it is
not desired. As a rule of thumb we keep the nurses with the lowest hourly rate in the roster
to minimize the cost. An exception is for shifts I and II on weekdays, as they do not result

night or weekend shifts for a single nurse by switching some of her shifts with another
nurse. The cost of such a modiﬁcation is dependant on the salary of those two nurses.

the nurses is highly dependant on the ﬂexibility we have along with which preferences we
include and the emphasis on diﬀerent preferences.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to analyse the cost-eﬀectiveness of nurse rosters at Slagelse
hospital’s emergency department, and evaluate the savings possible with better resource
allocation. The problem was formulated as a mathematical model covering various aspects
of the problem, including union agreements, shift patterns and required competences as
well as some preferences. Desirable solution approaches to the nurse rostering problem are
highly dependant on the speciﬁc instance, and the most diﬃcult aspects of this instance
were the formulation of weekly time oﬀas well as the length of the planning horizon.
To make the problem more manageable, it was broken down into shorter periods of
two weeks which were repeated to form the entire planning horizon of 16 weeks. This
was considered more eﬃcient than combining diﬀerent partial rosters to form the entire
planning horizon due to the diﬃcult formulation of weekly time oﬀ.
Based on the problem characteristic, a heuristical column generation was chosen as
the most promising solution approach.
The columns were generated using a shortest
path heuristic going through each day from start to ﬁnish choosing the cheapest feasible
option each day. The rosters not repeatable with respect to the weekly time oﬀwere then
eliminated. The generation of columns had a relatively rapid convergence, while integrality
in the master problem raised some issues. Various methods were considered to ﬁnd good
integral solutions, but the problem was highly sensitive when it came to parameter choices.
The problem was often infeasible over the smaller solution spaces generated, but increasing
them led to memory errors. The only approach which generated any feasible solutions was
the cut-and-solve algorithm after minor modiﬁcations to handle the sensitivity better.
9.1
Future research
The main limitations of the solution approach implemented in this project are twofold.
First it is the fact that the solution is heuristically generated and optimality not guaranteed.
However, the solution obtained revealed possibility of up to 40% savings in supplement
salary for nurses, and was less than 3% away from a theoretical lower bound. The second
limitation is the reduced diversity that occurs when breaking the problem down into smaller
repeatable units.
If the focus is set on increased diversity, one possibility would be to increase the length
of the partial repeatable rosters. It could also be worth looking into the possibility of
combining diﬀerent partial rosters to form the longer one. This was not implemented at
this stage, due to complexity with weekly time oﬀ, but the results regarding feasibility of
the combinations could be interesting.
Future work could also include extending the problem deﬁnition to get even closer to
the real life scenario. The simpliﬁcations made when deﬁning this project were mainly
two, i.e. to assume a general planning period with all competences available and to only
43

include the minimum requirements for a feasible solution to evaluate the maximum possible
savings.
As discussed thoroughly in Chapter 8, it is highly sensible to include some additional
preferences to ensure that the rosters generated are acceptable by the nurses. It could
also be interesting for Region Sjælland to analyse the cost of having more competences
available during rush hour, to increase the service level they oﬀer their patients.
In addition, the eﬀect of assuming a general planning period should be addressed. The
exact conditions can never be fully known before hand, e.g. as sick leave is rarely planned
many weeks or months ahead. These aspects can however be included stochastically by
estimating the unavailability using real data. The additional cost due to public holidays
could also be evaluated in a similar way.
