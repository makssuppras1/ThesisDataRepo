3. Abstract 
Natural products (NPs) are naturally occurring compounds which are a great source of 
therapeutics due to natural selection of their structures. Compounds inspired by NPs have 
been of great interest due to the prevalidated biological activities of the guiding NPs. 
Quinuclidine is part of a group called bicyclic amines which can be found in some NPs, and 
a lot of research has been focusing on analyzing and studying these structures. Interestingly, 
Cevimeline, which is a commercially available drug against dry mouth, has the same NP 
scaffold as quinuclidine. It also has a spirocyclic functionality which will be studied more in 
this project. 
The purpose of this project was to generate a compound library, using the key precursor 
quinuclidine-3-one, which was generated from the commercially available salted 
quinuclidine-3-one (1), which could be used with known spirocyclization reactions. The 
precursor was used to generate a range of different spirocyclic derivatives by 
spirocyclization on the ketone. This gave us the five 2-hydroxyacetophenol (4), four 
dihydroquinazolinone (6), and two Pictet-Spengler (9). The Oxa-Pictet-Spengler (17) 
reaction was also tested but did not yield anything, which was also the case with the Prins 
reaction (13) as well as the three-compound ring-closing reaction (15), this can be seen in 
Scheme 1.

6. Introduction 
6.1 Natural products – a source of therapeutics 
Natural products (NPs) represent an extensive group of diverse compound 
classes that are produced by living systems through primary and secondary 
metabolism. NPs come from all sorts of living creations in nature, like plants, 
microorganisms, and marine organisms. NPs are a significant source of 
inspiration for the discovery of new drugs1,2. They are produced with a specific 
purpose to deliver a particular function to either primary or secondary metabolites. 
Primary metabolites have the necessary functions to obtain homeostasis, and 
secondary metabolites have various meanings. Secondary metabolites are often 
more complex in structure and have a lot more functionality. The size and 
functions of NPs are not the only specific thing; they also have unique structural 
elements, like fused-, bridged-, or spirocyclic rings. They can be complex to 
synthesize by a purely synthetic route, but a biological system can sometimes 
make them accessible.2 
NPs are classified according to their biological functions, structural similarities, or 
source of origin. Primary metabolites are easy to recognize as their structure is 
like amino acids, carbohydrates, fatty acids, and DNA/RNA, that can be seen in 
Figure 1. These are the building blocks of our body as they make up the cells or 
generate energy to the many biochemical pathways. Secondary metabolites 
include compound classes like alkaloids, terpenes, and phenolic compounds, that 
can be seen in Figure 1.

In medicinal chemistry, NPs can be an ideal lead to get inspiration and research 
for potential bioactive compounds or components for drug discovery. With regard 
to drug discovery, a non-planar structure of natural products is well suited for 
adding and changing different substituents to the molecule. By retaining the core 
scaffold of the NP and changing the substituents, you will also change the 
bioactivity of the molecule. But the molecule is still likely to interact with the ligand-
binding site if the scaffold is retained or similar. This will be determined by the 
size and the chemical properties of the substitutes if the binding ligand can match 
that. NPs have been used for millennia; they have been recognized and exploited 
for medicinal purposes. Humans have, throughout our history, used a different 
mix of plants to help certain problems and diseases. Traditional Chinese medicine 
is one of the oldest that have used NP-based drugs, and they have relied very 
heavily on it. It is first many years later, with the help of science, that we know 
how the drugs are working and what in the plants that give this relived to your

them for some of the drugs that we can buy for the therapeutic effect.

6.1.1 Natural products in drug development 
NPs and their derivatives are frequently large, highly functionalized molecules 
with several stereogenic centres. They often occupy chemical space with their

unique structure and particular functional groups that are not seen in purely 
synthetic compounds. The most common functions in bioactive NP’s are 
polyhydroxy groups, esters, epoxides, and α, β-unsaturated systems compared 
with synthetic small-molecule drugs. NPs tend to be more complex molecules 
that fevor more oxygens incorporated into the structure than nitrogen, sulphur, 
and halogen groups; this also increases hydrogen bond donors (HBD) and 
hydrogen bond acceptors (HBA). Because of the complex structure, there are 
fewer rotatable bonds. The complexity of NPs comes from being evolved over 
millions of years so that they can help the host as best as they can; this makes 
them a biologically prevalidated structure. Many NPs have a particular target and 
are therefore a great lead compound for drug discovery. 
However, NPs can also be challenging to work with because of their size, 
complexity, and high functionality. Because they are made through evolution, 
NPs have been selected for a specific target and going through a specific delivery 
route. There are often issues with oral bioavailability, stability, and too rapid of 
clearance in vivo in these compounds. This will lead to optimization on NP-
derived drugs; this is focusing on the issues and enhancing that the NPs have. 
This can be selectivity, potency, safety, ADME (absorption, distribution, 
metabolism, and excretion), pharmacokinetics, and -dynamics in vivo. 
The development of the structure-activity relationship (SAR) gives optimization of 
lead compounds, both for fragment exchange NPs and small molecules. This has 
led to the discovery of bioactive structures, which have the giving inspiration to 
NP-inspired compounds.

6.2 Strategies for synthesis of NP-inspired compound 
library 
In chemical biology, research has shown that various small molecular structures 
have been identified to modify the activity of a wide range of proteins. This has 
shifted the researchers of designing synthetic compound libraries for HTS to 
generating selective small molecules with molecular scaffolds that might display 
unexpected biological activities. This type of compound library takes its 
inspiration from compound classes that are biologically relevant, such as NPs. 
NPs are a significant source of inspiration for NP-derived and NP-inspired 
compound libraries due to their evolution. NP-inspired compounds also help us 
expanding into more small chemical molecules. It is expected that NPs share 
some common structural elements but with different and diverse substituent 
patterns, thus enabling them to have different bioactivity profiles. NP-inspired 
libraries are often used for HTS against new targets because they have increased 
hit rates and various bioactivities compared to the standard combinatorial 
chemistry library. 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
Synthesis of a quinuclidine alkaloid-inspired compound library 
In generating NP-inspired libraries, different strategies can be used depending 
on the compound library's purpose. The general concept is to try designing 
compounds that will mimic the structure and property of the target NPs; this 
concept gives structures that are more likely to be biologically relevant based on 
the guiding principle that similar structures have similar properties and activity. 
NP-inspiration comes from a scaffold that is the core in the leading NP. By 
simplifying the scaffold, it will remove some complicity, make it more hydrophilic, 
reduce molecular weight, and reduce the number of rings and rotatable bonds. 
When creating such as library, one of two different focuses are chosen: either 
diversity-orientated synthesis (DOS) or (BIOS) iology-orientated synthesis. 
 
6.2.1 Diversity-Oriented Synthesis 
The DOS strategy focusses more on diversity rather than biological specificity. 
This means that DOS libraries do not have to be based on a NP structure but can 
have some guiding NPs that can be used as an inspiration to generate a more 
chemically diverse compound library that might have some NP characteristics7,8. 
The DOS is used to generate a compound library that is stereochemically and 
skeletally diverse. The strategy has a workflow that needs to be followed, and the 
workflow is build/couple/pair, this can be seen below in Figure 4. This workflow 
really involves planning the targeting of the compounds that have the right 
functional groups that can be selectively linked in one of the three steps described 
below. 
Build: Asymmetric synthesis of chiral building blocks containing specific sets of 
functionalities suitable for subsequent coupling and pairing steps. 
Couple: This step focuses on the intermolecular coupling, with putting them in 
building blocks of two. This results in compounds with a different array of 
functional groups that can undergo intramolecular reactions in distinct pairwise 
combinations. 
Pair: The intramolecular coupling reaction is performed to join the pairwise 
combinations of functional groups incorporated in the build phase. 
==PAGE:17==

17 
 
Figure 4- The build/couple/pair workflow of DOS 
 
 
The building blocks can be based on a core scaffold, specific structural elements, 
or mimic some structural features of a specific NP in a general sense. Some of 
the often-used building blocks are outdated structural elements and systems 
commonly found in several NPs to give this NP-like characteristic. This will 
increase the change of a bioactivity hit. Chiral building blocks are also standard 
and can be made by preparing enantio- and diastereoselective reactions, or a 
compound can choose from a chiral group. The stereochemical diversity arises 
in the “build” and “couple” steps, whereas the structural diversity arises in the 
“pair” step. 
 
6.2.2 Biology-Oriented Synthesis 
The concept of BIOS originated from the first strategies used to discover NPs; 
this focusses on the structural analysis of proteins and small molecules that leads 
to many discoveries of certain structural elements9. These structural elements 
have validated biological relevance and proven to have a diverse biological

activity in different compounds or compound classes. In BIOS, compounds are 
often selected on these biological prevalidated structures. NP scaffolds are 
mainly based on their biological relevance. By only using the scaffold of the NP 
to generate a library, it will reduce the structural complexity, but at the same time 
keep the biological activity compared to the guiding NP. These scaffolds are often 
used as a precursor to generating NP-inspired analogues10. 
The BIOS strategy uses a hierarchical classification of biologically relevant 
compounds based on structural relationships and their type of bioactivity11. The 
first Structural Classification of Natural Products (SCONP) was the concept of a 
scaffold tree introduced and applied to the Dictionary of Natural Products (DNP) 
by Waldmann et al. (2005). This NP tree categorizes the chemical space of NPs 
in the DNP by classifying and hierarchically arranging the scaffolds of NPs to 
reduce the high diversity of NP structures to a manageable level. The SCONP 
gave guidelines for what scaffolds were selected for its bioactive compound to be 
used as a starting point for developing a BIOS library.

6.2.3 Pseudo-natural product libraries 
The limitations in BIOS can be overcome by expanding into concepts of fragment-
based compound design. Fragments of NPs that have to characterize structures 
and properties are combined in de novo to access novel scaffolds that may have 
retained or improved the guiding NP's chemical and biological characteristics11. 
This fragment-based strategy gives rise to pseudo-NPs that can include several 
structural characteristics across several NP classes, this can be seen below in 
Figure 6; this will make them hard to be accessible through existing biosynthetic 
pathways. The idea of a pseudo-NP is that it may retain the biological activity of 
the guide NP. However, there is the possibility of giving rise to new, unexpected 
bioactivity and giving the pseudo-NP a way to expand into other biological 
spaces.

6.2.4 Examples of NP-inspired compound libraries

compound libraries has become a standard method to explore cellular pathways 
to identify new targets. When starting from an already biological validated 
structure that will bind to a specific protein receptor, the likelihood of finding a 
lead compound in a smaller compound library will increase.

purposes. Kissau et al. (2003)3 reported the generation of a small NP-inspired

linked by one common atom”. Spirocyclic compounds have been slowly growing

suggestion meant that compounds with higher fraction of sp3 (Fsp3 = number of

9. Conclusion 
The commercially available quinuclidine-3-one salt was used to generate the key 
precursor, just the desalted quinuclidine. The key precursor was used to generate 
several spirocyclic-inspired compounds by spirocyclization through the ketone in 
quinuclidine-3-one.Six different spirocyclization reactions were attempted from 
this key precursor, yielding five 2-hydroxyacetophenone derivatives, four 
anthranilamide derivatives, and three Pictet-Spengler derivatives that were 
purified and prepared for biological testing. There was also tested Oxa-Pictet-
Spengler reaction, Prins reaction, and three-compound ring closer; all this gave 
no formation of the product. An overview of the reactions and synthesized 
compounds carried out during the project, along with possible suggestions and 
ideas for future work to expand the compound library, is shown in Scheme 6. 
All the scaffold reactions overall gave a relatively middle yield. There were some 
solubility problems with the anthranilamide derivatives that they only want to 
dissolve in water, DMSO, and HCl in water. This was work around by dissolving 
the salt in HCl in water and adding methanol. Then rotary evaporate the mixture 
till all the HCl in water is going and you are left with a compound that can be 
dissolved the most common organic solvents. The problem in all the experiment

that I need to add triethylamine to my TLC plate with made it impossible to test 
the plate for nitrogen because quinuclidine is not UV accept. This was a 
workaround with extractions because quinuclidine likes to stay in the water 
phase, making it easy to remove. The purification of the derivatives that need it 
was done by flash chromatography, and in some experiment also recrystallization 
was needed. The flash chromatography procedure was always the same; it just 
needs small column silica and starts to wash with ethyl acetate to the first 2-4

None of the scaffold reactions was optimized, only the purity of the compounds 
was optimized, and a different approach was taking if the reaction did not work. 
This was done to create as many different scaffolds as possible and analogues 
with high purity to be biologically tested. 
Quinuclidine is an excellent scaffold to work on if you do chemistry on the ketone 
position because of the molecule's structure. There are no chiral centres, and you 
are not going to get one. You will only get one isomer out of the reaction, making 
the purification go a lot faster.
