Abstract 
This study investigated the feasibility of using TiO2 and TiO2/ZrO2 sol-gels to enhance the 
mechanical strength and surface protection of Al2O3 ceramic membrane discs. The results 
showed that the TiO2 sol-gels, specifically sol-gel #1 and sol-gel #5, exhibited higher stability 
and achieved better mechanical strength in the breaking point test compared to the TiO2/ZrO2 
sol-gels (sol-gel #6 and sol-gel #7). Furthermore, the TiO2 sol-gels demonstrated superior 
surface protection capabilities, indicating that they were more effective in safeguarding the 
ceramic discs from damage and wear during the cleaning cycle. 
The microscope images analysis revealed that the TiO2 sol-gels (sol-gel #1 and sol-gel #5) had a 
uniform and consistent structure, while the TiO2/ZrO2 sol-gels (sol-gel #6 and sol-gel #7) had a 
more granular texture. This difference in structure was attributed to the different coating 
materials and application techniques used. The granular structure of the TiO2/ZrO2 sol-gels 
made them more susceptible to corrosion, which led to the removal of the coating layer during 
the cleaning cycle. In contrast, the uniform structure of the TiO2 sol-gels made them more 
resilient to corrosion, which helped maintain the ceramic discs' mechanical strength. 
Overall, the results of this study suggest that TiO2 sol-gels are a promising material for enhancing 
the mechanical strength and surface protection of Al2O3 ceramic membrane discs. Further 
research is needed to optimize the sol-gel synthesis and coating processes, as well as to 
investigate the long-term durability of the coated discs.

1. Introduction 
The field of membrane filtration technology has been subject to significant advancements and 
developments, offering innovative and efficient solutions to the separation challenges in many 
different industries, such as the food industry, biotechnology, water treatment, desalination, gas 
separation, petrochemicals and pharmaceuticals and many others.  
This development has been driven by the many advantages of the membrane technology over 
conventional separation methods such as distillation, adsorption, and absorption, mainly their 
cost efficiency, energy saving, simplicity of operating and scaling up, and the ability to perform 
under mild conditions and continuous operation mode. [1] 
Membranes are broadly categorized into two types, either composed of organic (polymeric) 
membranes or inorganic (ceramic or carbon) membranes. For many years, the commercialized 
application of membranes has been dominated by organic membranes.  
But recently, inorganic membranes have witnessed a considerable increase in research and 
development, leading to a massive emergence in the market for some existing applications or to 
find new potential ones, with expectations of a higher share in the market for the inorganic 
membranes with time.  
That is due to the advantages of the ceramic membranes over the polymeric type, represented 
in exceptional thermal and chemical stability, broader pH ranges, higher efficiency and 
robustness with higher fouling resistance, and the ability to withstand aggressive cleaning 
processes. [2] 
Historically, the development of ceramic membranes started within the nuclear plants in the 
sixties and eighties of the past century. The goal was to replace the organic microfiltration (pore 
diameter is larger than 50 nm) and ultrafiltration (pore diameter 2 – 50 nm) units in the uranium 
enrichment process, that the polymeric (organic) MF and UF have been suffering from limited 
durability, pressure, and temperature mitigation and periodical replacement.  
Later, the manufacturers developed membranes with a pore size of 1 nm and less 
(nanofiltration).  
==PAGE:11==
2 | P a g e  
 
The research field of inorganic membranes has been rapidly increasing, where intense 
investigations on various materials, pore size, and physiochemical properties have taken place, 
creating many different ceramic membrane models, including tubular, flat sheet, and disc 
membranes.  
The ceramic membranes are primarily composed of metal oxides, such as alumina (Al2O3), 
zirconia (ZrO2), silica (SiO2), and titania (TiO2). [3] 
Filtration systems and operation modes have seen changes and improvements over the years to 
establish a continuous and efficient process in the industry. These goals required a separation 
system where the formation of the layer cake is minimized, and high throughput is achieved. 
Dead-end Filtration or static filtration is a system where the liquid feed flows perpendicular to 
the membrane, and the pressure on the receiving end of the membrane is higher than the 
pressure on the effluent end. The drawbacks of such a filtration system are its high clogging ability 
and its unsuitable for separating small particles. 
 
 
Figure 1: illustration of Dead-end filtration vs crossflow filtration. [4] 
 
==PAGE:12==
3 | P a g e  
 
Crossflow filtration (CFF) has reduced the effect of clogging issues because the flow of the liquid 
feed is tangential across the membrane surface, leading to the creation of a shear force that will 
prevent the particles from forming a layer cake on the surface.  
Crossflow filtration also offers a higher efficiency and flux rate, with a drawback represented in 
the complexity of the system and the increased operating cost.[4] 
Both systems use conventional configurations of membranes, such as tubular tubes or flat sheet 
membranes. In both scenarios, the backflush frequently recurs to remove the clogged layer; even 
though it is at a higher rate with the dead-end filtration, it is still required in CFF.[5] 
Dynamic crossflow filtration (DCF) doesn’t use the pump to create the flow and the conventional 
type shapes of membranes; this system uses stacks of disc membranes (ceramic membranes) 
mounted on a hollow shaft, where a motor is responsible for the rotation of the discs.  
The feed flows into a chamber of the discs, then permeates through the surface of the discs into 
the pores, then into the hollow shafts via the center of the discs. At the same time, the retentate 
is continuously concentrated in the chamber and then can be discharged from an exit in the 
chamber. 
 
Figure 2: comparison of the filtration performance between dead-end, crossflow, and dynamic crossflow 
filtration.[5] 
==PAGE:13==
4 | P a g e  
 
The principle behind the DCF is that the rotating discs will create a turbulent crossflow of the 
liquid in the chamber, with high shear force on the membrane surface, preventing the particle 
build-up and creating the filtration cake. [5] 
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the DCF with rotating ceramic discs mechanism. [5] 
 
DCF provides a lower energy consumption than dead-end filtration and CFF, with a higher 
throughput, more straightforward maintenance and cleaning procedure, and the ability to scale 
up to meet the industry’s demand.[5] 
Still, even in the DCF, the retentate can get high in concentration in the chamber and can build 
up particles on the surface of the membranes, so a backflush is needed in some situations. [5] 
 
1.1. 
Problem Statement 
In the pharma industry, DCF is used for efficient separation following fermentation. DCF utilizes 
ceramic membrane discs in the micropore region made from aluminum oxide (Al2O3). The vendor 
claims the disc's lifetime is ten years, or the exposure to caustic cleaning cycles up to 250 hours.  
Due to the pharma industry highly regulated procedures and the requirement of rigorous 
cleaning imposed by repeated cycles under high-temperature conditions, the ceramic discs 
==PAGE:14==
5 | P a g e  
 
experience a huge dramatic reduction in their lifespan and a high degradation in performance 
and efficiency. The discs sometimes become so brittle and fragile that they might break. 
This premature degradation of the disc’s nature is translated into significant challenges in the 
industry, starting with economic implications due to the extra expenses of purchasing discs, the 
degradation of performance, and the increased workload for maintenance teams and 
technicians.  
Also, it raises environmental concerns due to the unrecycled nature of the discs after utilizing 
them, which will end up as landfill waste.  
 
1.2. 
Objective 
This master's thesis project is dedicated to identifying practical tools and methods to enhance 
the durability and mechanical strength of ceramic membrane discs utilized in dynamic crossflow 
(DCF) within the industrial sector. 
Recently, advancements in coating technology have paved the way for numerous enhancement 
opportunities to elevate ceramic membrane discs' functional capabilities and longevity. [6] 
Surface coatings have the potential to fine-tune fouling resistance, maintain chemical stability, 
enhance selectivity, and counteract the impacts of aggressive cleaning agents.  
Sol-gel coatings, harnessed from metal oxides like zirconium and titanium, stand out as promising 
solutions for enhancing the operational lifespan of ceramic membranes. The approach involves 
applying a thin layer of sol-gel coating onto the membrane surface. [6] 
This strategic application mimics and establishes a protective barrier, effectively preventing 
direct contact between the media and the membrane surface. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:15==
6 | P a g e  
 
The key points that outline the sequence of events in this study: 
• Conduct a series of filtration experiments on a replicable medium utilizing a laboratory-
scale DCF setup equipped with newly unutilized membrane discs, then again using coated 
discs. 
• Implemented a continuous caustic cleaning regimen using NaOH at 80°C for 300 hours on 
the DCF system two times. First using uncoated discs and second, using coated discs to 
study the corrosion in alkaline conditions. 
• Depending on information from the project owner, the study will focus on corrosion in 
alkaline conditions only. 
• Investigate and synthesize multiple coating materials suitable for membrane surface 
enhancement. 
• Apply synthesized coatings onto the new membrane discs. 
• The filtration analysis aims to ensure that the application of coating materials does not 
adversely affect the disc's filtration performance. 
• It's important to note that this project is not focused on dissecting the filtration 
mechanisms or the advantages of the DCF (dynamic crossflow) system. 
• The primary objective of this project is to synthesize an innovative metal oxide coating, 
distinct from Al2O3, which can effectively reduce the corrosive impact of cleaning 
materials on the disc. This coating is also intended to contribute to the preservation of 
the membrane's mechanical robustness. 
• To assess the mechanical durability of the discs, a hydraulic press will be utilized. This 
method will involve comparing breaking points between the coated and uncoated discs. 
• Besides the hydraulic press analysis, a microscopic examination will be performed on the 
discs. This microscopic evaluation will provide a visual representation of the structural 
composition of the coating materials.

1.3. 
Hypothesis 
• Metal oxide presents a suitable solution for creating fitting coating materials tailored to 
ceramic membranes. 
• Titania, zirconia, and their mixtures are highly promising choices for coating materials, 
effectively maintaining the mechanical robustness of the discs while reducing surface 
corrosion while contacting caustic cleaning materials. 
• The sol-gel process offers an appropriate methodology for synthesizing titania and 
zirconia coatings. 
• Provided accurate synthesis is achieved, the coating materials exert no impact on the 
filtration attributes of the discs, including selectivity, flux, and pore size.

2. Theory and Literature Review 
2.1. 
Fabrication 
Porous ceramic membranes are favored in highly harsh environments due to their inherent ability 
to withstand thermal and chemical stresses, setting them apart from their organic counterparts. 
They exhibit strong resilience against various cleaning agents utilized to combat fouling. 
Nevertheless, it's essential to acknowledge that while these qualities are advantageous, they are 
not immune to alteration over time, potentially leading to thermal and chemical instabilities 
under prolonged harsh conditions. [2] [3]  
Ceramic porous membranes exhibit an asymmetrical configuration, originating from commercial 
sources or being synthesized in a laboratory.  
This asymmetry is guided by the objective of attaining the desired permeability and selectivity 
characteristics. [3] 
An asymmetric membrane is characterized by its composition, featuring a support layer with an 
open porous structure.  
Positioned atop this support layer are multiple thin layers distinguished by smaller pores 
arranged sequentially. These upper layers progressively diminish in pore size, as illustrated in 
Figure 5. [3] 
Creating a layer with progressively diminishing pore sizes is crucial due to the risk that a layer 
with uniformly small particle sizes could penetrate the larger pores.  
Consequently, obstruct the pores leading to a decrease in permeability.  
Furthermore, placing such a delicate thin layer directly atop larger pores can lead to mechanical 
instability, potentially causing issues like cracking or peeling. [2] 
 
==PAGE:18==
9 | P a g e  
 
 
Figure 4: Multilayer alumina ceramic membrane pore size distribution. [2] 
 
The primary role of the support layer is to enhance the overall durability of the disc. In contrast, 
the upper layers manage filtration processes (permeation and selectivity functions). Additionally, 
these upper layers play a part in refining the surface's texture, contributing to its smoothness. [3] 
 
Figure 5: Ceramic membrane multilayer structure (SEM Picture). [7] 
 
 
 
==PAGE:19==
10 | P a g e  
 
Support layers are commonly made using extrusion and slip-casting techniques.  
The most common method is sol-gel synthesis when producing the intermediate and top layers 
from metal oxides.  
This approach is preferred as it enables the creation of membranes with precise pore sizes and 
uniform size distribution. The sol-gel process can be carried out through two routes: colloidal and 
polymeric methods. [3] , [8] 
The colloidal approach enables the creation of mesoporous intermediate layers. This involves 
dissolving metal alkoxide in alcohol and introducing excess water for hydrolysis.  
By keeping the resultant solution at a high temperature, a stable colloidal particle solution of 
concentrated oxide forms.  
As the solution cools, metal oxide is generated. This oxide is subsequently applied onto the 
support layer and subjected to high-temperature calcination (ranging from 500°C to 800°C). [3] , 
[8] 
The polymeric sol-gel route creates small pore-size layers (including microporous, ultra, and 
nanoporous). This method uses a significantly lesser amount of water for hydrolysis compared to 
the colloidal sol approach.  
The alkoxide's active hydroxyl group reacts to produce an inorganic polymer, which is then 
applied onto the membrane's surface as a coating. Drying and calcination of this coating lead to 
a thin layer of metal oxide. [3] , [8] 
==PAGE:20==
11 | P a g e  
 
 
Figure 6: A diagram describing the sol-gel colloidal and polymeric routes. [9] 
 
The process of calcination plays a crucial role in maintaining the stability of the crystallographic 
structure and shaping its overall morphology. The temperature at which calcination is carried out 
can impact the ultimate pore size of the dried layer. [3] 
 
2.2. 
Sol-gel Coating  
Implementing surface modifications can offer a more cost-effective approach than the complex 
process of developing, researching, and manufacturing new materials.  
Simultaneously, these modifications retain the original properties of the base materials while 
addressing their inherent limitations. [6] 
While coatings might not achieve absolute perfection, their drawbacks often stem from 
discrepancies in surface chemistry and physics to the original subject, such as surface 
==PAGE:21==
12 | P a g e  
 
smoothness, compatibility with active materials upon contact, and susceptibility to thermal 
fluctuations can arise.  
However, these challenges can be mitigated through well-designed coating synthesis and 
meticulous application to the surface. [6] 
Inorganic sol-gel coatings bring several advantages, notably enhancing thermal, mechanical, and 
chemical stability, as well as enabling the creation of precise pore sizes.  
Sol-gel coatings offer a straightforward, economical, and efficient means of boosting membrane 
performance, increasing stability, and enhancing resistance to wear. [6] 
The high volatility of alkoxides makes them particularly well-suited precursors for Sol-gel 
synthesis, with tetraethoxysilane (TEOS) being a widely employed alkoxide derived from silicon. 
Among metal alkoxides, titanium tetraisopropoxide, aluminum isopropoxide, and zirconium (IV) 
propoxide are commonly utilized. Alternatively, metal salts can substitute for metal alkoxides in 
certain instances. 
In the commercial context, the precursor of metal alkoxides M(OR)n is typically dissolved in an 
alcohol solution. In this formula, 'M' denotes the network-forming element, such as Al, Ti, or Zr, 
while 'R' represents the alkyl group (CxH2x+1). 
The Sol-gel synthesis process is influenced by various factors, including pH, reaction temperature, 
types of reactants and their ratios, as well as the choice of solvent. [6] 
The Sol-gel coating can be employed through various application methods, such as dip coating, 
spray coating, spin coating, and roll coating. [6]

Among these methods, dip coating is the most commonly utilized technique, involving immersing 
the substrate into a suspension, which adheres to the surface of the intended substrate. [8]

2.3. 
Alumina Al2O3. 
The predominant composition for many commercially available ceramic membranes is alumina 
(Al2O3), a naturally abundant material renowned for its good chemical stability, impressive 
mechanical strength, and high thermal resilience. [11] 
Alumina membranes can be tailored with diverse pore sizes, spanning hundreds to a few 
nanometers, rendering them well-suited as supportive layers in composite ceramic membranes. 
Alumina comes in various allotropes, including alpha alumina (α Al2O3) and gamma alumina (γ 
Al2O3), which are applied in ceramic membrane production, typically through a sintering process 
at temperatures exceeding 1300 °C. [11] 
 
2.4. 
Titania TiO2 
Titanium dioxide (titania TiO2) is a prevalent choice for coating ceramic membranes because it 
can endure harsh chemical surroundings, including strong acids and bases. This compound is 
naturally present as ilmenite FeO—TiO2 ore, containing 15 % and up to 40% iron.  
Or it exists as rutile ore, which has only up to 15% iron alongside other impurities, comprising 
85% TiO2. [11] 
Titania exists in three distinct crystalline configurations: a stable rutile form and two metastable 
forms, anatase, and brookite. Upon exposure to heat, these two unstable forms transform into 
the stable rutile structure, which stands as the sole commercially utilized form. [11] 
 
2.5. 
Chemical stability 
Chemical stability refers to a material's capacity to endure the corrosive assaults of aggressive 
liquids. The corrosive reaction is influenced by many factors, rendering the study of a material's 
chemical resistance a complex endeavor. [3] 
From a thermodynamic perspective, materials exhibiting significantly negative free energy 
values are expected to possess high chemical stability.  
==PAGE:24==
15 | P a g e  
 
Additionally, kinetic factors come into play, including the duration of contact, environmental 
temperature and pressure, and concentration of substances. [3] 
The morphology of a membrane emerges as a pivotal determinant in gauging chemical stability. 
In membranes, the presence of granular materials on the surface exposes them to initial 
contact with harsh liquids, often leading to the formation of favorable dissolution spots.  
This, coupled with the existence of impurities and defects, accelerates corrosion on the surface, 
particularly outpacing other layers. [3] 
Surface texture, whether smooth or rough, holds significance in assessing chemical stability. 
Rougher surfaces tend to experience greater levels of corrosion, as the presence of granular 
cover promotes this process. [3] 
Among ceramic membranes, alumina membranes exhibit higher susceptibility to corrosion within 
challenging chemical surroundings, thereby impacting their mechanical stability.  
In contrast, titania and zirconia membranes demonstrate enhanced chemical resistance. 
Nonetheless, these materials encounter their own set of challenges, including diminished 
thermal stability, larger pore size, lower selectivity, and the propensity for film cracking when 
employed exclusively. [12]

Subsequent experiments adopted a dynamic approach to corrosive testing, simulating industrial 
conditions by inducing permeation through the membrane over a designated period. [3]

4. Methodology 
4.1. 
Yeast Suspension Filtration 
Before each filtration process, the suspension was freshly prepared to serve as the filtration 
medium. For each filtration run, an 8-liter yeast suspension was prepared using baker's yeast 
"Saccharomyces cerevisiae" (25 g/L) and YPT media "Yeast extract peptone dextrose" (50 g/L). 
This suspension was vigorously mixed utilizing a magnetic stirrer within an 8-liter flask over a 
span of 2 hours. 
The assembly of the DCF system adhered to the manufacturer's guidelines, involving the 
utilization of appropriate tubing and connectors. The feed was accumulated within a pressure 
hold tank, set at 0.5 bar, before being introduced into the feed chamber of the DCF system. 
Once the chamber reached its capacity, the rotation of the discs initiated, commencing the 
filtration process which lasted approximately 1 hour. 
This process yielded distinct permeate and retentate, each collected in separate vessels. The 
subsequent analysis involved quantifying the yeast concentration (biomass) using a 
spectrophotometer set at 600 nm wavelength.  
This procedural iteration was undertaken four times, employing new discs for each run and 
subjecting the discs to a cleaning cycle. Additionally, when utilizing coated discs in the DCF 
system, these were subjected to the cleaning process. 
A standard curve was established for calibration using a sequence of solutions characterized by 
differing yeast suspension concentrations (50, 25, 12.5, 5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.25, 0.1 [g/L]). 
 
4.2. 
Cleaning 
The cleaning solution was formulated by blending pure NaOH pellets (100%) with RO water 
((purified using Reverse Osmosis process), resulting in a concentration of 0.1 M (0.4% w/w) and 
a pH of 13. A total of 10 liters of this cleaning solution were prepared and stored in a holding 
tank. 
 
==PAGE:33==
24 | P a g e  
 
Subsequent to the filtration procedure, both the retentate and permeate were collected. The 
system underwent a thorough cleaning process, employing RO water to eliminate all traces of 
yeast suspension. This process was repeated three times to eradicate any traces of the yeast 
suspension medium completely. 
The system then underwent a caustic cleaning phase. In this stage, a closed system was created 
by redirecting the permeate flow into the NaOH solution holding tank. The system operated in 
this closed operating mode for 13 days (approximately 300 hours), simulating the exposure to 
repeated cleaning cycles with caustic materials over a 250-hour duration, maintaining a 
consistent flow rate of 100 ml/min. 
Throughout this process, a temperature of 80°C was maintained using an external heating liquid 
coursing through the DCF's heating jacket. This specific temperature choice aimed to replicate 
an extreme condition of cleaning temperature typically encountered during an industrial clean-
in-place procedure. 
Following the caustic cleaning, a thorough rinsing using RO water was conducted to rinse the 
system of the caustic solution.  
This comprehensive rinsing process was replicated to ensure the effective cleaning of the coated 
discs.

wearing eyewear protection and gloves, along with the precise use of pipettes.

blockage at the center of the disc during the coating process and minimize hand contact with the 
discs. A specialized tool was designed and produced using a 3D printer, as depicted in Figure 18

4.4. 
Coating of the sol-gels 
Unfortunately, the attempts to synthesize Sol-gel #2, Sol-gel #3, and Sol-gel #4 were 
unsuccessful, and obtaining any sol-gels from those trials was impossible. 
However, four different Sol-gels were successfully obtained after the Sol-gel synthesis 
experiment. These Sol-gels were subsequently coated onto various ceramic membrane discs for 
further testing and analysis. 
4.4.1. Spray Coating 
Sol-gel #6 underwent an aging process for three days at room temperature [12], after which it 
was diluted three times volume-wise using ethanol. The gel solution was then used to coat a new 
ceramic membrane disc through a spray coating technique, employing an air paintbrush for 10 
minutes. This coating process was repeated three times, and the coated disc was left to dry at 
room temperature. 
To remove any remaining water content, the disc was dried in an oven at 110 °C for 30 minutes. 
Subsequently, the disc was subjected to calcination in a muffle furnace at 300 °C for 1 hour. 
It's important to note that spray coating is only applied for sol-gel #6. When exposed to air 
pressure, the other sol-gels tend to dry quickly inside the air paintbrush, making them unsuitable 
for spray coating. Therefore, a dip coating method was chosen for the other sol-gels. 
 
4.4.2. Dip Coating 
After aging the Sol-gel for three days at room temperature [12], it was diluted three times 
volume-wise using ethanol. A new ceramic membrane disc was coated with the Sol-gel solution 
using a dip coating method, where the disc was immersed in the solution for 30 minutes and then 
allowed to dry at room temperature. The dip coating process was repeated three times to ensure 
a uniform coating. 
==PAGE:39==
30 | P a g e  
 
Next, the coated disc was dried for 30 minutes in an oven at 110 °C to remove any remaining 
water content. Finally, the calcination process occurred in a muffle furnace at 300 °C, with the 
disc being heated for 1 hour to complete the synthesis. 
4.5. 
Pressure-Breakage Test. 
To prepare the discs for the hydraulic press experiment, each disc was divided into four pieces 
using a rotating head angle grinder, as there were no other suitable tools available in the 
laboratory for this task without causing damage. 
Two reasons stand behind the dividing of the discs, the first is to get multiple data points to be 
statistically accepted, and the second is that the area to place the studied subject was smaller 
than to fit a whole disc. 
The hydraulic press experiment involved four readings for each disc. The discs used in the 
experiment were as follows: 
1. A new, undamaged disc. 
2. A disc that underwent cleaning with a caustic solution at 80 °C for 300 hours. 
3. Four discs coated with different sol-gel coatings, specifically sol-gel #1, sol-gel #5, sol-gel 
#6, and sol-gel #7. After the coating process, each of these discs was also cleaned with 
the caustic solution for 300 hours at 80 °C. 
These discs were then subjected to pressure-breakage tests to study their structural integrity and 
resistance to break.

5. Results 
5.1. 
Yeast Suspension Filtration Analysis 
A comprehensive overview of the yeast suspension filtration analysis results and the subsequent 
comparisons of each filtration process has been made. The aim is to understand the impact of 
caustic cleaning on the ceramic membrane discs and assess if the metal oxide coatings can 
mitigate this effect. 
The calibration curve data in Appendix 1: Yeast suspension filtration analysis and Figure 20 
demonstrate a clear relationship between the absorbance and yeast cell concentration when 
using a spectrometer to determine the yeast (biomass) concentrations from samples obtained 
during the different filtration experiments.  
It shows that the absorbance increases linearly with the concentration up to 5 g/L, at which point 
it reaches the limit of detection (LOD). The curve maintains linearity within the concentration 
range of 0.1 to 5 g/L with a high R-squared value (R2 = 0.98), indicating a reliable correlation for 
concentration calculations in this range. 
However, it is essential to note that concentrations exceeding 5 g/L or falling below 0.1 g/L result 
in absorbance values that surpass the LOD. Therefore, in such cases, the calibration curve 
becomes invalid for calculating the retentate or permeate concentration.

After multiple attempts to replicate the procedure of sol-gel #4 for forming a TiO2/ ZrO2 sol-gel, 
an interesting observation was made. Adding water to the beaker before adding zirconium led to 
a rapid reaction and the formation of solid structures. To address this issue, adjustments were 
made in the process. 
The modified approach involved mixing zirconium, ethanol, and acetic acid without water 
initially. Then, the titanium mixture was added to the zirconium mixture, and water was 
subsequently added dropwise but in smaller quantities compared to the procedures used in Sol-
gel #1, Sol-gel #4, and Sol-gel #5. This change successfully formed a TiO2/ ZrO2 Sol-gel derived 
from mixing titanium butoxide and zirconium butoxide in ethanol and acetic acid. The Sol-gel 
obtained displayed a consistent white color and structure. 
Following the aging of the Sol-gel and dilution, successful coating on the ceramic membrane was 
achieved via spray coating using an air paintbrush. After calcination, a thin layer of the coated 
material was successfully deposited on the disc's surface, maintaining a consistent white color 
without any color variations, as shown in Figure 25.

5.3. 
Hydraulic Press Breaking Test. 
The observation that Al2O3 ceramic membrane discs became fragile and brittle after 300 hours 
of cleaning cycles led to conducting a breaking point test. The primary objective of this test was 
to visually assess the damage caused by the cleaning procedure on the discs' mechanical strength 
and overall durability. 
Simultaneously, the breaking point test offered an opportunity to investigate the effectiveness 
of the coated materials in protecting the disc's surface from corrosion. 
To perform the test, a hydraulic press was utilized to apply pressure to the discs until they 
reached their breaking points. The force required to break the discs was measured and recorded 
in kilonewtons (where 1 kN = 102 kg * 9.81 m/s2) as a single value for each breaking test. 
Unfortunately, the experiment faced a limitation because the hydraulic press available in the 
laboratory had a defect that prevented it from connecting to computer software. As a result, 
pressure increase graphs against time were not obtainable for this experiment, and only the 
hydraulic breakpoint was recorded. 
Figure 27  shows that the mechanical strength of the Al2O3 ceramic membrane disc is dramatically 
decreasing after prolonged contact with caustic cleaning materials at high temperatures, the new 
unutilized disc breakpoint is registered at 0.8 kN, but it drops down to 0.2 kN after the cleaning 
procedure, meaning that the discs are losing approximately 75% of their mechanical strength and 
structure integrity.

6. Discussion 
Utilizing new ceramic membrane discs (DCF) in the filtration process proved effective in visually 
separating yeast cells from the liquid feed. The resulting permeate contained a transparent liquid 
with a minimal concentration of cells, while the retentate exhibited haziness and contained a 
much higher concentration of yeast cells.  
Despite challenges in obtaining an accurate calculation for the retentate concentration, the 
overall success of the separation process is evident, providing valuable insights for further 
applications in yeast suspension filtration. 
Subjecting the discs to harsh cleaning conditions affected the filtration process, as indicated by 
the changes in absorbance readings. Nevertheless, further investigations and comprehensive 
analyses are required to fully assess the extent of the impact caused by the cleaning procedure 
on the efficiency of yeast suspension filtration using these ceramic membrane discs. 
Dilution and Concentration Requirements: A potential limitation in the experimental approach 
was noted, as the calibration curve was not established at the beginning of the experiment. To 
fit the spectrophotometer's detection limits, the retentate samples should have been diluted, 
and the permeate samples concentrated during analysis. 
In conclusion, while the visual results provided some insights, the lack of accurate concentration 
quantification limited a comprehensive evaluation of the cleaning procedure's impact and the 
efficacy of metal oxide coatings.  
Further experiments with proper dilution and concentration adjustments, along with additional 
analyses, are necessary to fully understand the effects of cleaning procedures and coating 
technologies on the filtration efficiency of the ceramic membrane discs.  
These improvements will enable researchers to draw more precise conclusions and gain a deeper 
understanding of the filtration process for yeast suspension in future studies.

The experiments conducted to synthesize various TiO2 and TiO2/ZrO2 sol-gels revealed valuable 
insights and essential conclusions regarding the feasibility and challenges of the sol-gel synthesis 
process. 
The initial experiments with TiO2 sol-gel (Sol-Gel #1) demonstrated promising results, yielding a 
semi-white Sol-gel with a consistent formation. This successful outcome validated the 
effectiveness of the hydrolysis of titanium butoxide in ethanol as a viable approach to produce 
the desired sol-gel material.  
The appearance of a consistent Sol-gel structure indicated a controlled reaction and highlighted 
the potential for various applications in areas such as thin film coatings. 
However, the attempts to form ZrO2 and TiO2/ ZrO2 sol-gel (Sol-Gel #2, Sol-gel #3, and Sol-gel #4) 
encountered significant obstacles, despite multiple adjustments inspired by previous research. 
The challenges faced in obtaining the desired sol-gel product using zirconium propoxide 
hydrolysis in propanol and adding DEA as a chelating agent underscored the complexity and 
sensitivity of the sol-gel synthesis process.  
These adverse outcomes may be attributed to zirconium compounds' specific properties and 
reactivity, necessitating further exploration and optimization for successful sol-gel formation. 
The experiment involving the TiO2/ ZrO2 sol-gel (Sol-Gel #4) was a significant breakthrough for 
mixing TiO2 and ZrO2 conditions. That led to successful results in the cases of Sol-gel #6 and Sol-
gel #7. This achievement broadens the potential for hybrid sol-gel materials, which could exhibit 
unique properties and applications compared to their individual counterparts. 
The coating and calcination processes proved effective in depositing thin layers of the sol-gels 
onto the surfaces of ceramic membrane discs.  
The dip coating method allowed for controlled and uniform application, while the subsequent 
calcination step facilitated the conversion of the gel into stable, functional coatings. The 
successful deposition of the thin layers demonstrates the feasibility of using these sol-gels for 
membrane modification and enhancement.

One notable observation during the experiments was the slight color variations observed in some 
thin layers on the membrane surfaces. While specific explanations were not provided for these 
variations, it underscores the need to control reaction parameters and optimize the coating 
process and calcination process parameters carefully. Further investigations are warranted to 
understand and mitigate potential factors contributing to these color variations.  
More efforts should have been made to explore further adjustments to fine-tune the synthesis 
procedures. And additionally, investigating the influence of different calcination temperatures 
and processing times.  
Overall, these experiments on synthesizing TiO2 and TiO2/ ZrO2 sol-gels have yielded valuable 
results and essential insights. The successes and challenges encountered in the experiments 
highlight the complexities involved in sol-gel synthesis and underscore the significance of 
carefully controlling the reaction conditions.  
These findings lay the foundation for future research, fine-tuning experimental parameters and 
exploring novel approaches to unlock the full potential of these sol-gels for various practical 
applications, especially in membrane technology.

potential protective effect of the coated materials against corrosion. 
In conclusion, using coated thin layers of sol-gel proved beneficial in enhancing the mechanical 
strength of the ceramic discs across different levels. The results indicated that the TiO2 sol-gels, 
specifically sol-gel #1 and sol-gel #5, exhibited higher stability and achieved better mechanical 
strength in the breaking point test compared to the TiO2/ ZrO2 sol-gels (sol-gel #6 and sol-gel #7). 
Furthermore, the TiO2 sol-gels demonstrated superior surface protection capabilities, indicating 
that they were more effective in safeguarding the ceramic discs from damage and wear during 
the cleaning cycle. This can be attributed to the high ability of titanium to withstand harsh pH 
environments. [3]

7. Conclusion 
Sol-gel #1 and Sol-gel #5, both derived from titanium oxide (TiO2), offer promising opportunities 
as coating materials for Al2O3 ceramic disc membranes. These coatings can potentially mitigate 
the detrimental effects of caustic cleaning materials and exceptionally high pH "NaOH" 
solutions used in repetitive cleaning in-place cycles over extended periods. 
However, to fully validate their effectiveness, further analysis is required. It is essential to 
ensure that these coatings maintain the filtration characteristics of the disc, including pore size 
and interactions at the surface, and achieve similar permeation flux with stability. Additionally, 
verifying that they do not react with the intended filtration solutions is crucial, especially 
considering their applications in the pharmaceutical industry. 
Sol-gels derived from a mixture of titanium oxide (TiO2) and zirconium oxide (ZrO2) did not 
demonstrate a high ability to counter the effect of cleaning materials, suggesting the need for 
alternative coating options. 
The synthesis of Sol-gels from metal oxides is a delicate procedure that demands careful 
execution under specific conditions to ensure successful and reliable outcomes. Precise control 
and adherence to the synthesis process are crucial for achieving desirable properties in the 
coatings. 
The challenges encountered during the synthesis of ZrO2 and TiO2/ZrO2 sol-gels underscore 
the complexity inherent in the process. This complexity necessitates a continued exploration of 
optimization strategies to harness the potential of these coatings effectively. The successful 
deposition of these sol-gels onto ceramic membrane discs offers an encouraging glimpse into 
their potential for membrane enhancement, though precise control of reaction parameters 
remains pivotal to achieve consistent and reliable results. 
In essence, this study has revealed a delicate balance between material properties, synthesis 
methodologies, and operational conditions. By probing into the complex relation between 
ceramic membrane disc performance, sol-gel synthesis, and coating dynamics, this research 
sets the stage for future investigations. The insights gained offer a roadmap to unlock the full 
==PAGE:63==

potential of ceramic membrane discs and pave the way for enhanced filtration efficiency, 
prolonged mechanical integrity, and innovative applications across diverse industries.
