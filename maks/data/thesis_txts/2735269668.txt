Preface
The current thesis is prepared in the autumn and winter of 2023/2024 and serves as the
completion of the education Master in Fire Safety at the Technical University of Denmark
- DTU.
The thesis is written by Morten Lunden Svensgaard and Tobias Vestergaard Brøndum in
a collaborative effort, and has been conducted with the assistance of supervisor Anne S.
Dederichs, associate professor at DTU. The thesis represents the equivalent of 2×15 ECTS
points.
The objective of the thesis is to investigate the effect of wind in relation to the efficiency
of natural smoke and heat exhaust ventilation systems, commonly known as thermal fire
ventilation systems. The investigation is based on simulations performed in FDS, with
temperature and smoke layer height being the main parameters to document the efficiency.

Abstract
Thermal fire ventilation, or natural smoke and heat exhaust ventilation systems (NSHEVS)
as the technical term, is a widespread example of a effective fire technical installation, which
both limits fire spread and smoke dispersion, while also ensuring enough time for evacuation
in buildings. The design of NSHEVS are often based on prescriptive requirements, which
ensures a relatively simple and quick design, while the method being standardized thereby
not taking the design of the specific building or location into account. In this project the
efficiency of NSHEVS is investigated if affected by an exterior factor as wind, which is
partially neglected when designing based only on prescriptive requirements.
The efficiency of NSHEVS when affected by wind is investigated based on the temperature
and smoke layer height inside a building, which is affected by different scenarios of wind,
varying the wind speed and wind direction. The investigations are based on simulations
performed in FDS, with two main models being used. One model is made with realistic
exhaust-openings in the roof, while the other model is made with standard holes in the roof,
typical for simulations related to NSHEVS. Both models are investigated with different
wind speeds of either 2, 5 or 10 m/s in combination with the four main geographical
orientations, while also varying the placement of the air-inlets in the building in order to
document a possibly effect of this.
Through the study it is indicated, that wind in terms of both speed and direction can have
a large impact in regards to the the efficiency of NSHEVS, when the results are compared
with the efficiency of NSHEVS not affected by wind. In the investigations it was found,
that the wind lead to turbulence and the creation of vortexes inside the building affecting
the movement of the smoke and the hot gasses.

Resumé
Termisk brandventilation, med den tekniske betegnelse natural smoke and heat exhaust
ventilation systems (NSHEVS), er i dag et udbredt eksempel på en effektiv brandteknisk
installation, som både begrænser brand- og røgspredning samt sikrer evakuering af personer
i forbindelse med projektering af bygninger.
NSHEVS projekteres tit på baggrund
af præskriptive standarder og vejledninger, som sikrer en forholdsvis enkel og hurtig
projektering, men som samtidigt er standardiseret, og derved ikke tager udgangspunkt
i den enkelte bygning. I dette projekt er effektiviteten af NSHEVS undersøgt, hvis denne
påvirkes af en udefrakommende faktor som vind, der ikke direkte medtages i projektering
ud fra præskriptive standarder.
Effektiviteten for NSHEVS ved påvirkning af vind er undersøgt på baggrund af
temperaturer samt højden på røggaslaget inde i en bygning,
som udsættes for
vindpåvirkning med forskellige vindhastigheder samt vindretninger. Undersøgelsen udføres
ved brug af simuleringer i FDS, hvor der opsættes 2 modeltyper.
Den ene model er
opsat med korrekt modellerede brandventilationsåbninger i tagfladen, mens den anden
model er opsat med standardiseret huller i tagfladen, som er normalt for simulering af
brandventilation.
Begge modeltyper udsættes for vindhastigheder mellem 2, 5 og 10
m/s fra de fire geografiske orienteringer, hvor der samtidigt veksles mellem placering af
erstatningsluftåbninger i bygningen for også at dokumentere en eventuel effekt heraf.
Ud fra de gennemførte analyser indikeres det, at vinden i form af både hastighed og retning
kan have stor betydning for effektiviteten af NSHEVS, når resultaterne sammenlignes med
effektiviteten af anlægget uden vindpåvirkning. Det fremgår af undersøgelserne, at vinden
leder til turbulens og hvirvelstrømme i bygningen, som påvirker den termiske opdrift og
derved ventileringen af røg og varme.

1.1
Motivation
The fire safety in buildings is an important factor and fire technical installations are praised
and widely considered to have a positive impact regarding the fire safety. Natural smoke
and heat exhaust ventilation systems (NSHEVS) are among these technical installations,
and have been deemed effective in different types of buildings especially where large
compartments occur. NSHEVS is intented to have a positive impact on different aspects
of the fire safety in buildings [4]. But the design of the installations are often based on
prescriptive requirements, hereby not necessarily taking all exterior factors into account.
One of the exterior factors which is partially neglected is wind.
The partial neglect can be derived by The European Standard “EN 12101-2 Smoke and
heat control systems - Part 2: Natural smoke and heat exhaust ventilators” [5], which
applies to heat exhaust ventilators operating as part of a NSHEVS. In this standard,
test methods and requirements for wind are only specified for heat exhaust ventilators
as isolated components, thereby not taking the effect of neither the placement nor the
influence of the wind regarding the air-inlets into account. Whether or not this omission
could result in a negative effect to the efficiency of a NSHEVS when affected by wind is
difficult to state. But the air-inlet’s being a crucial part of an effective NSHEVS makes it
a possible scenario.
1.2
Objective
The current thesis’ main objective is to investigate the effect of wind in relation to
the efficiency of the NSHEVS, and what the consequences of a fire occurring in windy
conditions would be. This will be investigated by simulating different scenarios of the
design of the NSHEVS in FDS, with the temperature and smoke layer height being the
main parameters to document the effect on the efficiency of the NSHEVS.
The main objectives in the thesis is summarized in the following aspects:
• How does wind affect the efficiency of NSHEVS with respect to heat and smoke?
• What effect has the location of air-inlets in regards to the efficiency of NSHEVS,
when affected by wind?
The aspects of the objective in the thesis are to be commented in chapter 4 based on the
results found throughout the thesis, with a summarisation presented in the conclusion.

Article - Influence of wind on natural smoke and heat exhaust system performance in fire

Article - Fire smoke dispersion inside and outside of a warehouse building in moderate

In the article [6] the authors are making simulations were wind speed and direction is
implemented, through their analysis they examine how the wind are affecting the effiency
of the NSHEVS. In the study a reduction in the efficiency is seen up to 23,6 % at the
roof ventilators and up to 37 % reduction at the wall-mounted ventilators. Through their
simulations they are also facing negativ flow discharge, as an result of the winds impact
on the placement of the ventilators. The article conclude that there should be carried out
further investigations with wind and ventilators, the importancy of the geometry of the
compartment, the wind conditions, and the location should still be kept in mind.
The article [12] is focused on 25 CFD simulations with the fire and smoke dispersion as
the main point of the analysis. In the CFD models the NSHEVS system was implemented
in order to remove the heat and smoke from the building.
The study was looking at
how the surrounding buildings was affecting the NSHEVS in the analysised compartment,
compared with simulation were there was no buildings arround the compartment. The final
performance of the NSHEVS was measured in % and with the approach of: "How much
smoke was removed from the building?" The results showed that in 25% of the simulations
the flow was better than the estimated flow. They saw a variation in the flow related
to the wind speed, with moderate flow 78%-114% and with strong wind 78%-158%, this
indicates that the actually flow when there is wind involved can vary both to the positive
and negative side.
The literature shows that a NSHEVS in combination with wind is a section of fire safety
engineering, were there is done different studies, with different focuses. Furthermore, the
previous studies shows how the NSHEVS is affected by the wind, and thereby this can’t
be excluded, but should be taken into considaration when designing a NSHEVS.
The fact that literature with a focus on the placement of air-inlets or comparison of realistic
roof openings and a standard hole in the roof, when affected by exterior wind, haven’t been
found, makes the objective of the current thesis relevant and interesting.

Theory 2
2.1
Natural smoke and heat exhaust ventilation systems
The basic principle of natural smoke and heat exhaust ventilation systems (NSHEVS),
commonly known as thermal fire ventilation, is to release heat and smoke from a room in
order to change the conditions inside of a burning building. The release of heat and smoke
is done by an attempt to controle the flow path. The flow path being the route which air
takes, when entering the building in order to feed the fire, after which the air flows towards
the exhaust openings bringing heat and smoke with it. [14]
Fire ventilation is usable for a great number of purposes, some of them being [4]:
• To ensure occupants are able to evacuate by themselves without encountering critical
conditions.
• To ensure the capacity of the load bearing structures in a building.
• To ensure no risk of flashovers in a compartment.
• To ensure primary escape routes and access paths for the firefighting crew are clear
of heat and smoke.
Furthermore, fire ventilation brings advantages to the firefigting crew by [14]:
• Reducing the impact of heat and smoke to the fire and rescue service.
• Reducing the generel thermal load ensuring the possibility of rescue operations for
the fire and rescue service.
• Reducing the smoke inside the building improving the visibility for the fire and rescue
service.
• Preventing and/or containing fire spread.
In generel two types of fire ventilation exist, these being mechanical- and thermal fire
ventilation.
Mechanical fire ventilation is based on fans creating a pressure inside the
building in order to release smoke and heat.
Thermal fire ventilation is based on
thermal buoyancy, and can be performed both horizontally and vertically.
Horizontal
fire ventilation is performed when the exhaust openings are placed in the same height as
the fire, resulting in a sideways flow path. Vertical fire ventilation is performed, when the
exhaust openings are placed in the top of the building, resulting in an upwards flow path.
The higher the exhaust openings are placed in the building, the better utilization of the
thermal buoyancy is reached. For both types of fire ventilation different types of air-inlets
are available, since the air-inlets in both situations can be performed as mechanical or
thermal. [4] [14]

2.1.1
The stack effect
In a burning building, where exhaust openings are placed in the top, and the air-inlets are
placed at the bottom, a stack effect can occur. The "stack effect" is also known as the
"chimney effect", because it has similarities with the effect happening inside a chimney,
with air intake at the bottom, which afterwards is heated up by the fire, and finally rises
upwards to be released at the top. The stack effect is based on thermal buoyancy, and
is developed because of differences in the air density. Hot air’s density is lower than cold
air’s density, and flows upwards as a result of this. In generel terms the hotter the air, the
lower the density.
During a fire the combustion products form a plume above the fire consisting of gas and
particles. Fresh air from the air-inlets in the bottom of the building feeds the fire and
becomes a part of the combustion, where the air is heated and mixed into the plume. The
gases, particles and hot air in the plume form the fire gases with a low density, which
causes them to rise upwards and be ventilated by the exhaust openings in the roof. [14]

the subjects described below. [3]

2.2
Wind
In general the wind can be described as pressure differences in the atmosphere, resulting
in wind conditions to occur. When the air is heated the buoyancy will move it against
the atmosphere, and oppesite when it is cooled down it will move against the surface of
the earth, where it will create a high pressure. As a result of the air moving against the
atmosphere there will be created a low pressure near the surface. The actual wind speed is
a result of the differences between high and low pressure in the atmosphere[15]. Changes
in the topography will likewise have an impact in the wind behaviour together with the
temperature of the air.
2.2.1
Wind turbulence
Wind flow on a compartment will produce turbulence around the compartment, the extent
of the turbulence will depend on several factors such as wind speed, wind direction, the
topography and the design of the compartment. At figure 2.1 it is seen how the wind is

mentioned factors is not taken into consideration in the figure.
In general the turbulence and the wind distrubution in cities or were buildings is placed
closer together will be more complex, compared with a single building placed in an open

2.3
Computational Fluid Dynamics
The scientific computer tool: Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a tool to predict the
fluid flow through quantitative predictions, where the computer tool bases the predictions
on conversion of mass, momentum, and energy, also know as the conversation laws. CFD is
discretizing the chosen domain into a smaller mesh were the numerical solutions is working
there way through in time steps [16].
Over time CFD has developed its accuracy and can therefore quickly give the user a great
knowledge and data of the analyzed domain. CFD takes a lot of information on the fluid
flow into consideration, which makes it more accurate. The tool is converting a lot of
different partial equations into a system with algebraic equations. The approach in CFD
to discretize the Navier-Stokes equations into a system of algebraic equations, is not the
only method, but other methods share the same approa ch. It is important to be aware
of the results from CFD is depending on the input parameters, so the participants in the
analysis shoud have a satisfying level of knowledge [17].
2.4
Fire Dynamic Simulator
The computer program Fire Dynamic Simulator (FDS) is a tool that can solve equations
regarding the evolution of fire. FDS reads its inputs parameters from a textfile, and works
as an command prompt program, where the calculation(simulation) of the fire is made
according to the input textfile. The output files from FDS gives the results which can be
read in the spreadsheet program Excel, the results from the simulation is related to the
input and the desired output [18].
Navier-Stokes equations are solved by FDS and is appropriate for low-speed flow, the
program has a belonging program called Smokeview(SMV). The belonging program
generates visualizations of the results in form of 3D animations and similar, and
can be quite realistic when the correct input data are implemented.
FDS operates
with turbulences according to Large Eddy Simulations (LES) and Direct Eddy
Simulations(DNS), where LES is the default mode in FDS. In the program there is a big
variation of possibilties when modelling the domain, with respect to the surfaces, openings,
HRR, devices, etc. [19].
As a graphical interface for FDS, the program PyroSim can be used, due to it having FDS
closely integrated. PyroSim gives the user instant feedback of the implemented aspects
in the domain, and it can be seen visual concurrently as its incorporated in the model.
When using PyroSim there is a large variation of features which gives the user a greater
experience such as; 2D and 3D geometry, import CAD files, import existing FDS models,
tools to manage multiple meshes etc. In PyroSim the file can be exported as a FDS file
[20].

Wind-parameters

the NSHEVS, and how the air-flow inside and outside the compartment will react in the

implemented with the largest heat release rate found to be 10 MW, and the stack of wood
pallets is dimensioned accordingly.

3.8.2
The growth phase
In generel every ordinary fire has a growth phase from the point of ignition until it gets to an
fully developed fire, where the maxumum HRR is reached. The growth phase furthermore,
depends on the material, which is ignited. The purpose of the thesis however, is to analyze
how the wind is affecting the NSHEVS based on steady-state conditions. Therefore the
normal growth phase of a fire is irrelevant, and as a result of this, won’t be implemented
in the model. In the model the design fire will be set as fully developed from the start
of the simulation, in order to reach steady-state conditions as early in the simulation as
possible.

experiment conducted in the validation guide it is concluded, that the program is suitable
for simulating models with the geometry given for the compartment in the thesis, as the
geometry in general matches the experiment.
3.9.7
Verification of FDS regarding wind
In the FDS User Guide [18] various ways to implement wind in FDS are specified. It is
possible to model the wind conditions in a range of complexities. The complexities range
from a basic level with implementation of an initial wind condition with a specific wind
direction and wind speed, to a wind condition which can vary in both direction and speed
over time. Furthermore, the wind condition can be altered to also vary over altitude.
The different wind methods specified in the FDS User Guide is shown below:
• Wind Method 1: Specified Wind Speed and Direction
• Wind Method 2: Monin-Obukhov Similarity
• Wind Method 3: Advanced Meteorological Concepts
• Wind Method 4: The "Wall of Wind"
Based on the various ways to implement wind in FDS it is concluded, that the program is
suitable for simulating models with the conditions chosen in the thesis.
3.10
Limitations
Due to the limited timeframe in the completion of the current thesis, limitations towards
the objective have to be made. Besides the generel limitation presented in section 1.4,
further limitations with respect to the performed simulations have been set. The main
part of the following limitations have been made with the purpose of narrowing down the
results, and hereby the amount of data to be processed.
3.10.1
Wind-parameters
FDS has various ways to implement wind as mentioned in section 3.9.6, where the current
thesis only deals with wind method 1. This method does not take changes in the wind into
consideration, nor other buildings or surroundings in relation to the analysed compartment.
Another praised method to implement wind according the FDS User Guide [18] is
Monin-Obukhov Similarity, which takes the stability of the surrounding atmosphere into
consideration.
In cases with a know specific building or generel surrounding, Monin-
Obukhov Similarity would be preferable to use.
Implementing generel surroundings, typography and different wind profiles would enlarge
the parameters of the simulations thereby making the results and dataoutput considerably
bigger. In order to simplify the simulations only wind method 1 have been used in the
thesis, with no other wind-parameters taken into account, except the wind speed and wind
direction.

3.10.2
The growth phase of the fire
A standard design fire has a growth phase where the fire developes from ignition until a
fully developed fire has occured. This growth phase has not been taken into consideration
in the thesis, because the phase is deemed not relevant in the purpose of the simulations.
In the simulations a steady state is desired to occur as quickly as possible in order to keep
the simulation time to a minimum.
A consequence of not implementing a growth phase of the fire in the simulations is depicted
in the outputdata. It is possible that the ignition phase of the fire can be unstabil because
of a large smokecloud, which results in quite extensive fluctuations in the dataoutput. The
outputdata however calms down in a relatively short period of time, and reaches a steady
state at some point.
3.10.3
Surroundings and distances in relation to the size of model
When implementing wind in FDS an important factor is the area around the compartment,
and the surrounding buildings, while these factors have an impact on the turbulence of the
wind. In the thesis the surrounding buildings are not implemented, due to the fact that it
would make the FDS model considerably bigger and thereby extend the simulation time.
The distances around the compartment have a similar impact, and are implemented as large
as possible with the meshsize in the simulations taken into consideration. Simulations in
the thesis have had a limited simulation time to a maximum of 72 hours, which did not
allow the domain of the simulations to be extended further, nor an implementation of
surrounding buildings, as seen implemented in some of the litterature in appendix A.
3.10.4
Measuring points
In the thesis the temperature and smoke layer height is measured at the points stated
in section 3.7. The amount of measuring devices in the thesis have been reduced to a
minimum, while still receiving enough output data in order to perform an andequate
analysis.
The reason for this is, that implementation of further devices would enlarge the amount
of data needed to be processed in the analysis.
Further measuring points could have
been implemented in order to investigate the temperature in different heights in the
compartment as well, where only the temperature just below the ceiling is part of the
current thesis.
3.10.5
Placement of fire
In the thesis the fire is placed in the middle of the compartment, and no other variations of
the placement is performed. The approach is done in order to narrow the data amount that
needs to be processed in the analysis. Another placement of the fire in the compartment
would most likely lead to different results from the FDS simulations. In the thesis one
placement of the fire is concluded to be sufficient in order to investigate winds effect on
the efficiency of NSHEVS.

4.3
Comparison of model types
A comparison of the two main models in the thesis are performed in order to investigate a
potentiale effect of the exhaust-openings in the roof, whether the openings are modelled as
holes or as realistic as possible. When comparing different models an important factor is
the models being as similar as possible, so the foundation of the comparison is acceptable.
In the thesis two different mesh-size setup has been used, due to the modelling of the
realistic exhaust-openings in the roof. As a consequence of this, the comparison carried
out should be seen as initial and would need to be investigated further in order to make
an adequate conclusion.
The initial comparison showed large deviations between the model types, with some of
the deviations calculated to upwards 148,0 %, and therefore an indication of the modeled
geometry of exhaust-openings having a very large effect on the efficiency of the NSHEVS
when affected by wind. In appendix B the initial comparison is presented in tables, where
the mean values of temperature and smoke layer height are compared and a difference
(deviation) between the corresponding simulations from model type 1 and model type 2
are calculated.
The comparison could serve as the basic for further analysis of the effect of realistic modeled
geometry of exhaust-openings, which is also mentioned in the outlook in section 6.

Conclusion 5
The main objective of the thesis was to investigate the effect of wind in relation to the
efficiency of NSHEVS, and to document possible consequences of a fire occurring in windy
conditions. Preliminary studies were made in order to concretize the subject and to gain
relevant knowledge found in other analysis made in regards to the subject.
The investigations in the thesis were based on simulations performed in FDS, which made
it possible to analyze a building containing a NSHEVS affected by different scenarios
with varying wind speeds, wind direction, placement of air-inlets and design of exhaust-
openings in the roof. The study was carried out using two main models related to the
type of exaust-openings, each containing two designs of air-inlets with the NSHEVS being
affected by exterior wind from either north, south, east or west. Furthermore, the models
were investigated with different wind speeds of either 2, 5 or 10 m/s in combination with
the aforementioned wind directions resulting in a total of 52 simulations through the thesis.
Through the simulations it was concluded, that wind does have an effect on the efficiency
of NSHEVS in regards to heat and smoke, and that the effect varies depending on the
wind conditions. The wind was discovered to mainly affect the smoke layer height inside
a building, hence the results showing a generel tendency of aggravation in regards to the
smoke layer height, when the wind speed was increased no matter the wind direction or
design of air-inlets. The wind was discovered to have less of an affect on the heat inside
a building, hence the deviations found was relatively small when compared to the heat in
a building affected by no wind. Furthermore, no generel tendency could be documented
based on the results in regards to either the wind speed or wind direction when investigating
the efficiency of NSHEVS in regards to the heat in a building.
The placement of air-inlets in the facade of a building however, proved to have an impact
on the efficiency of NSHEVS with respect to the heat. The amount of impact was shown
to be related to the given wind speed and wind direction. With design 1 (parallel) of
the air-inlets, a wind speed of 5 m/s was discovered to be critical with a wind direction

A thesis needs to be specific and concretize the main objective, and to ensure that the
thesis did not get to comprehensive, this section describes different aspects, approaches and
information which wasn’t possible to implement in the current thesis. Multiple limitations
made through the thesis is seen in section 3.10. Most of the limitations could be of interest
to analyze separately or in combination with some of the following subjects.
In the following, aspects which could be interesting for authors or others to process in

BR18

Comparison between realistic roof-openings and holes

6.5
More wind-speeds
In the thesis there is only made simulations with three different wind speeds, due to the
limitations of the amount of simulations. In a further process more wind speeds could be
implemented, in order to analyze more accurate at which wind speeds the efficiency of the
NSHEVS is affected most. The original approach in the thesis was to simulate the wind
speed in steps of 1 m/s, by doing this there would have been generated much more data to
be analyzed. Likewise was the intention to use the maximum wind speed of 27 m/s. The
wind speed of 27 m/s is specified in Eurocode 1 [24], which is related to the loadbearing
constructions affected by wind. In the Eurocode both wind speeds of 24 m/s and 27 m/s
are stated, with 27 m/s being used in worstcase scenarios. A test simulation with the wind
speed of 27 m/s showed that the run time for the simulation would exceed the max of
72h at DTU-cluster, and the result after 72h wasn´t wide enough to make any conclusion.
Making a small model, splitting it, or reducing the simulation time could be an approach
in further studies.
6.6
Wind direction
As described in section 3.5 the thesis is limited to analyze four wind directions. Following
analysis could be carried out with other directions, such as south-west, south-east and
similar. These directions will give a numerous of new simulations to be carried out, were
the wind would be applied to the compartment with angles of 45 degrees from the facades.
The wind would therefore "hit" two facades at the same time, resulting in new scenarios
of the pressure zones.

It could be
implemented in some further work, were the surrounding topography and buildings in
combination with the main objective in the thesis, could be analyzed.
No doubt that
the surrounding buildings would have an impact on the efficiency of the NSHEVS, the
interesting information would be if the impact would be positive or negative. An analyze
like this would give the best results and be mostly useful, if the analyzed compartment,
was placed in a real location, were the topography and buildings around the compartment
was known.

and increase to its maximum at a given height above the ground. Different simulations
and analysis could be carried out, were the wind-profiles was implemented, to document
what impact on the NSHEVS it would have.
