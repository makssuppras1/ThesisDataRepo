Abstract
Bottom contacting ﬁshing gears pose a threat to the ecological status of the seabed and
the magnitude of damage is often habitat-speciﬁc.
The sensitivity of mussel Mytilus
edulis (Linnaeus, 1758) beds to dredging has not been extensively studied. Here, we
conducted an investigation of the eﬀects of mussel dredging in the Limfjord, Denmark,
which occurs in Natura 2000 areas.
Using a Before-After-Control-Impact design, we
showed that the eﬀects of dredging on soft sediment mussel beds and the associated
fauna were restricted to within the dredge track. In the dredge track the density and
diversity of fauna decreased, and the community composition changed as 83% of the
families decreased after dredging, whilst 89% of families increased in the control site. In
an area exposed to dredging, mussel density was lower 16 weeks after the impact, but
concomitant changes were not observed in the faunal density, diversity and community
structure.
Thus, the level of dredging used within may not be unfavourable for the
ecological status of the mussel habitat in the investigated Natura 2000 site.
Mussel
dredging in this study was carried out in spring, rather than in autumn, which may aid
the recovery of the fauna and explain the lack of short-term eﬀects that have otherwise
been documented. These results could be important for guiding ecosystem-based ﬁsheries
management (EBFM) in the Limfjord, and contribute to setting adequate objectives for
ecological status.

Introduction
It
is
widely
accepted
that
mobile
bottom-contacting ﬁshing gear cause dam-
age to the seaﬂoor, but the severity and
extent of damage is dependent on the gear
type used, as well as habitat resilience and
ecosystem stability ((Collie et al. (2000),
Kaiser et al. (2006) Lambert et al. (2014)).
Understanding the sensitivity of habitats to
diﬀerent stressors, such as ﬁshing, is cru-
cial in implementing eﬀective management
strategies. The consequence of inappropri-
ate management measures can be economic
loss and/or severe reductions in ecosystem
functioning.
The coastal water body of the Limfjord
in Northern Denmark supports the largest
blue mussel Mytilus edulis (Linnaeus, 1758)
(hereafter ‘mussels’) ﬁshery in the country,
and thereby provides an important source
of income to the rural communities (Hoﬀ-
mann & Dolmer 2000).
Mussels form a
biogenic reef, which is a habitat type pro-
tected under Annex II of the Natura 2000
Habitats Directive. However, are not desig-
nated as protected in the Limfjord. Mus-
sels have a multitude of ecosystem ser-
vices and are described as ecosystem engi-
neers, as their three dimensional structure
creates an environment quite diﬀerent to
that of the surrounding seabed. The struc-
ture forms a habitat that supports a more
abundant and diverse community of organ-
isms compared to surrounding areas (As-
mus 1987). Bio-deposition, through excre-
tion of pseudo-faeces, is important for com-
munity metabolism, providing up to 31% of
the energy demand for the associated fauna
(Norling & Kautsky 2007). The mussel and
associated fauna are rich feeding grounds
for higher trophic levels (e.g.
birds and
ﬁsh), highlighting their role in the food-
web (Nehls et al, 1997). Furthermore, mus-
4
==PAGE:5==
sels help to improve water clarity in nutri-
ent rich systems, like the Limfjord, by ﬁlter
feeding on plankton.
Once an oligohaline system, the Lim-
fjord now has two openings; eastwards to
the Kattegat and westward to the North
Sea.
Saline water from the two seas mix
with fresh water from streams and ground
water diﬀusion, creating a salinity gradi-
ent throughout the Limfjord system. The
relatively shallow water depths (on aver-
age 5.8m(Poulsen et al. 2010)) and absent
tidal ﬂow results in thermal stratiﬁcation in
summer months. The lack of mixing leads
to oxygen depletion that eﬀects 15% of the
seaﬂoor area annually Hoﬀmann & Dolmer
(2000). The combination of these charac-
teristics creates a high level of seasonal dis-
turbance in the Limfjord, which ultimately
shapes the community of biota, and ulti-
mately determines the ecosystem services
from the system.
Ecosystem based ﬁsheries management
(EBFM) aims to support a prosperous
ﬁshery whilst maintaining ecosystem ser-
vices.
In European coastal waters, such
as the Limfjord, this is guided by legis-
lation in the European Union’s Common
Fisheries Policy (https://ec.europa.eu/
fisheries/reform),
the Water Frame-
work
Directive
(http://ec.europa.eu/
environment/water/water-framework),
the Habitats Directive http://ec.europa.
eu/environment/nature/legislation/
habitatsdirective), and the Birds Direc-
tive http://ec.europa.eu/environment/
nature/legislation
/birdsdirective).
Although the func-
tion of these policies are underpinned by
the same notion to promote both economic
growth and environmental protection, the
fundamental purposes diﬀer. Therefore, in
practice there can be conﬂicts. For exam-
ple, when aiming for a sustainable ﬁshery,
Good Ecological Status (GES) of the en-
vironment and protection of habitats and
species, at the same time.
The aim of this study was to assess
the eﬀect of mussel dredging in a Lim-
fjord sub-basin in terms of changes to the
mussel matrix (density and biomass) and
the associated invertebrate fauna.
The
goal was to better understand the ramiﬁ-
cation of the ﬁshery on the Natura 2000
protected habitat.
To meet these objec-
tives changes in faunal composition were
analysed in a Before-After-Control-Impact
(BACI) design after experimental dredging.

Materials Methods
Study site and experimental de-
sign. Løgstør Broad, a central sub basin of
the Limfjord, was selected for a compara-
tive analysis of the mussel matrix and asso-
ciated benthic fauna (hereafter collectively
termed ‘mussel habitat’) in response to ex-
perimental mussel dredging.
Løgstør has
a ﬁshable area of 163km2 with an average
depth of 5.8m (Poulsen et al. 2010). It has
an intermediate level of eutrophication after
decades of elevated nutrients (Christiansen
et al. 2006) and supports an estimated ﬁsh-
able mussel population of 368,000 tonnes
(water depth of >3m;Poulsen et al. (2010)).
To test the hypothesis that mussel dredging
would result in a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the
mussel matrix (density and biomass) and
the associated faunal community, a Before-
After-Control-Impact (BACI) experimental
design was used.
Using data from vessel
monitoring systems (VMS) (Supplementary
Figure 1) an area with low exposure to ﬁsh-
ing was identiﬁed. In Løgstør, two adjacent
100m2 sites were selectedFigure 1. The im-
pact site would be subjected to experimen-
tal dredging, whilst the control site would
5
==PAGE:6==
remain unﬁshed, (from here on the sites are
referred impact and control). The BACI ex-
perimental design enables an analysis that
isolates the eﬀect of the impact from natural
variability (Underwood 1992). An impact is
shown if the response variable in the impact
site, after impact, diﬀers from the control
(representing natural variability).
To de-
tect whether mussel dredging had a direct
eﬀect (within the dredge track) and/or an
eﬀect on the dredged area, two experimen-

Faunal sampling. The benthic faunal
community was ﬁrst sampled before dredg-
ing (T0) on 10th May 2017 in both the
impact and control sites.
Between 15th-
23rd May 2017 experimental dredging took
place in the impact site removing a to-
tal of 180 tonnes of mussels. After dredg-
ing (T1d) on the 23rd May 2017 benthic
faunal core samples were taken again in
both sites. Four core samples were taken
from mussel patches in the control site, and
eight cores were taken from mussel patches
in the impact site (four were taken within
dredge tracks and four samples were taken
from the outside of the dredge tracks). On
the 4th September 2017, 16 weeks after
dredging (T16wkss), four more core samples
were taken from each site.
Following re-
trieval, the faunal core samples were sieved
through a 20mm mesh size, followed by siev-
ing through a 1mm mesh. The fraction re-
tained on the 20mm mesh sieve was stored
in a plastic bag and immediately frozen (-
18◦C). The fraction retained on the 1mm
mesh sieve size was preserved in an 80%
Ethanol (EtOH) solution mixed with am-
bient seawater.
Faunal processing.
The two faunal
fractions were transported to the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) for analysis
and identiﬁcation. Benthic faunal samples
were processed in accordance with Hansen
& Josefson (2014) and Worsfold & Hall
(2001). In brief, the fraction retained on the
1mm sieve was systematically sifted for or-
ganisms with the aid of a compound micro-
scope (Leica M165 C X120). The fractions
retained on the 20mm sieve were sorted
in a plastic tray, using a hand-lens (X10).

Statistics. The comparison of sediment
characteristics was carried out using the
statistical software R studio version 1.1.4.3
www.rstudio.org assisted with the package
Rysgran version 2.10 (Gilbert et al. 2014).
A linear model used to test organic carbon
content had two factors: treatment; 2 lev-
els (control and impact) and layer; 3 levels
(top, middle, deep). An analysis of variance
(ANOVA) was used to determine whether
the organic carbon content between sedi-
ment layers diﬀered, and whether the ob-
served diﬀerences in the layers was found in
both sites. Sediment grain characterisation
was determined by cumulative frequency,
where the sediment grain size of the 50th
percentile is used to categorise the sample.
The linear models used for testing uni-
and multivariate faunal hypotheses con-
tained two factors: time (maximum three
levels; T0, T1d, T16wks) and treatment (max-
imum two levels; control and impact). A
signiﬁcant interaction of time and treat-
ment would indicate an eﬀect of dredg-
ing. The interaction term identiﬁes that the
change (before and after) in the each site is
diﬀerent to one another. Subsequent pair-
wise test should be as described in follow
the conceptual diagram (Figure 2). To test
for absolute changes in fauna rather than
relative change, the analysis was carried
out on non-transformed data. Mussel den-
sity and biomass were analysed separately
from the other fauna, as the high density
and biomass concealed underlying patterns
of the associated fauna.
Crabs, Carcinus
maenus (Linnaeus 1758) were removed from
the data.
As large mobile epifauna they
distorted the biomass dataset.
They are
also likely to be under-represented using the
box-core sampling method. Although three
individuals were sampled, they were all
gravid malting females, which do not rep-
resent normal C. maenus behaviour. Bar-
nacles, Semibalanus balanoides (Linnaeus
1767) were also removed from the data, as
they are correlated with mussel abundance
as they are permanently attached to mus-
sels. To validate that removing these organ-
isms had the desired eﬀect on the dataset
the statistical analysis initially carried out
with and without them.
The statistical software R studio and
add on package Car 3.2.0 (Fox & Weisberg
2011) was used to test univariate faunal hy-
potheses. A generalised linear model (glm)
was used to routinely test the best predictor
variable for the univariate analysis. Model
selection was based on the lowest Akaike in-
formation criterion (AIC). Univariate anal-
ysis of variance (ANOVA) was carried out
to test immediate, direct eﬀects of dredging
in a dredge track (Figure 2: Exp I) and im-
mediate and short-term eﬀects of dredging
in a dredged area (Figure 2: Exp II). Dif-
ferences in mussel density and biomass, to-
tal density, family-level diversity (hereafter
diversity) and total biomass were tested in

The glm distribution family
‘poisson’ was used for mussel density, to-

of

Discussion
Mussel matrix. Fisheries impacts of bot-
tom contacting gears are considered to be
most severe for stable biogenic habitats
(Collie et al. (2000), Kaiser et al. (2006)),
and reduction in habitat complexity has se-
vere implications for a number of other nat-
ural systems; coral reefs, rain forests, man-
grove forests, seagrass meadows.
In the
present study the impact of mussel dredging
(hereafter “dredging”) on mussels (Mytilus
edulis) seemed to be less deleterious.
As
the impact of dredging was only evident
immediately within a dredge track, and no
immediate eﬀect was apparent on the sur-
rounding mussel beds.
The decrease in
mussel density and biomass in the dredge
track may not be surprising, as mussels are
the target species of the ﬁshery.
Yet, it
was unexpected that the mussel density and
biomass increased in the boundary areas af-
ter dredging ??. One explanation might be
based on the mussels species’ ability to relo-
cate (Tan (1975), Cˆot´e & Jelnikar (1999)).
As a result of mussel dredging some mus-
sels would get dislodged from the matrix,
as mussels gain stability from attaching
to one another and hard surfaces on the
seabed.
The dislodged mussels may have
sought security from drifting unattached
over the sandy seabed and crawled to mus-
sels nearby and reattached (Cˆot´e & Jelnikar
1999), which resulted in the observed in-
crease in density and biomass.
The following, contrary, shift between
T1d and T16wks of mussel density in each
treatment; decreasing in the impact and in-
creasing in the control Figure 9, could be
deduced as a negative eﬀect of dredging on
the density of mussels. Particularly, if it is
coupled with the apparent recruitment suc-
cess in the control T16wks. Recruitment is
seen as two bell-shaped curves in the control
T16wks Figure 10, which divides the popula-
tion into two diﬀerent size structures. The
same is not evident in the impact T16wks
(Figure 10; one bell-shaped curve). How-
ever, on closer inspection, the recruitment
of juveniles only occurs in one replicate in
the control.
Recruitment in one replicate
is not enough evidence to conclude that re-
cruitment success was apparent in the con-
trol site and not in the impact site and thus
draw conclusions pertaining to negatives ef-
fects of dredging. Instead, it highlights the
patchy nature of recruitment and indicates
more replicates would be necessary.
Density of fauna.
The density of
the associated fauna in the dredge track
was signiﬁcantly diﬀerent to the control.
In accordance, Dolmer et al. (2001) found
that the density of the polychaete fam-
ily Capitellidae decreased signiﬁcantly in
a commercial dredge track.
Although no
signiﬁcant diﬀerence at the 95% conﬁdence
level were evident in the present study, the
SIMPER analysis showed that average den-
sity of a number of polychaete, amphipod
and bivalve families decreased in the im-
pact site, whereas they increased in the con-
trol (Table 5). Decreased faunal density in
the dredge track did not lead to increased
density in the boundary area, making dis-
placement of organisms to adjacent mussel
patches unlikely Dolmer et al. (2001). In-
stead, the decreased faunal density in the
dredge track Figure 13 may be assumed
to be as a result of removal and possibly
in situ mortality in the dredge track. Di-
rect moralities in the path of a beam trawl
in the North Sea was highest for bivalves
which reached up to 65%.
Mortality was
attributed to either, direct impact with the
ﬁshing gear, or due to exposure from the
ﬁshing gear contact and subsequent pre-
dation Bergman & Van Santbrink (2000).
It may then be hypothesised that scaveng-
ing organisms would increase in the dredge
track as a result. This could therefore ex-
plain the observed increase in the carnivo-
33
==PAGE:34==
rous polychaete, Polynoides (Table 5). Al-
though it has been shown that moribund
benthos is consumed within a few days of
impact Groenewold & Fonds (2000), thus,
a more pronounced increase of scavengers
density might occur later, as sampling oc-
curred on the same day as dredging occured.
The immediate and localised spike in food
availability could give a competitive advan-
tage to opportunistic scavengers, although
it is not likely to inﬂuence trophic interac-
tions to a large extent Groenewold & Fonds
(2000). To better understand the inﬂuence
of dredging on trophic interactions, sam-
pling additional times closer to the dredging
event might be useful. The application and
analysis of biological traits could be used
to determine changes in feeding types over
time in response to the impact.
Family level diversity. In contrast to
total density at the family level, the diver-
sity in the dredged area showed no impact
of dredging, or impact of time. Instead fam-
ily diversity in the impacted area remained
somewhat constant between times, and did
not signiﬁcantly diﬀer from the control (Im-
pact, Figure 20; Exp II, Table 3). This re-
sult is contradictory to the results of Rag-
narsson & Lindegarth (2009) who demon-
strated that diversity was more sensitive to
trawling than density, in an Icelandic fjord.
There, decreased diversity was attributed
to the reduction of rarer and more sensi-
tive species post ﬁshing Ragnarsson & Lin-
degarth (2009).
Alternatively, species di-
versity might be expected to be higher in
the impact site at T16wks as opposed to the
control, due to an intermediate disturbance
caused by dredging, as was described after
the closure of a scallop ﬁshery in the North
West Atlantic (Blyth et al. 2004). The long
term eﬀects of disturbance are thought to
lead to an overall loss of species density
(Gray et al. 2006), whether this is the case
for the current study would require further
sampling.
An explanation for the lack of
change in diversity over time in the present
study, may be the low species diversity in
the area; characteristic of high levels of dis-
turbance. Persistently disturbed habitats,
whether anthropogenic or natural, are typi-
cally colonised by resilient community (Col-
lie et al. 2000), made up of specialists and
opportunists. Specialists are successful in
their habitat as they have evolved to beneﬁt
from the ‘stressful’ conditions. For example
an amphipod found in this study, Caprella
linearis, has a specialised body plan which
is laterally ﬂattened to allow it to with-
stand fast ﬂowing water (Peattie & Hoare
1981).
Opportunists do well due to their
ability to rapidly re-colonise through high
reproductive outputs and often fast growth
rates (e.g. Capitellia capitata). The com-
position of organisms in a disturbed habi-
tat is dictated by whether they can survive
in the environmental conditions. Therefore
in semi-enclosed systems such as Løgstør
Broads, when recruitment occurs, it is of-
ten these same organisms that are able to
recruit, resulting in their increase in abun-
dance Figure 13, whilst the diversity re-
mains the same Figure 18.
Temporal pattern.
On the tempo-
ral scale of a few months, natural seasonal
variability appears to have a larger inﬂu-
ence on the mussel matrix and associated
fauna in the boundary of a dredge track
than mussel dredging.
Similar observa-
tions were described after scallop dredging
on unconsolidated sediment (Lambert et al.
(2017),Sciberras et al. (2013) (LeBlanc
et al. 2015)).
The density of organisms
increased after the spring/summer recruit-
ment (Lambert et al. (2017), Sciberras et al.
(2013)), in accordance with the present
study Figure 16.
Although a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence in the total density occurred in
the impact site between T1d and T16wks,
the pattern (increase) and extent (amount)
34
==PAGE:35==
was synonymous with the control. The lack
of diﬀerence in the community composition
and total density at T16wks between treat-
ments (control and impact) suggests that
removing the mussel matrix in the area
had minimal/no eﬀect on recruitment to the
boundary mussel matrix habitat. The use of
underwater markers to re-locate the dredge
track after 16 weeks would have allowed for
an interesting analysis of track community
recovery.
Fisheries eﬀect. The lack of conclu-
sive evidence showing an eﬀect of dredg-
ing on benthic fauna in the present study,
is not an unusual ﬁnding (Lambert et al.
(2017),van Denderen et al. (2015),Linde-
garth et al. (2000)).
In fact, it’s indi-
cated that the negative eﬀects of ﬁsheries
on benthic fauna are reduced with increased
natural habitat disturbance (Hiddink et al.
2006). Furthermore, the outcome of using
certain ﬁshing gears may be similar to that
inﬂicted by natural disturbance in speciﬁc
habitats (van Denderen et al. 2015).
For
example, in temperate soft sediment habi-
tats that has high shear stress, trawling was
not detected.
Moreover, the faunal com-
munity in areas of lower shear stress con-
formed to one similar to that of high shear
stress as a result of trawling (van Denderen
et al. 2015). Additionally natural seasonal
variability was more prominent on a sub-
tidal sandbank and cobble reef habitat in
the Irish sea than changes inﬂicted by the
highest ﬁshing intensity (>6 times per year)
(Lambert et al. 2017). These results could
have huge implications for ﬁsheries manage-
ment, in setting appropriate levels of ﬁshing
intensity.
Thus, intensive ﬁshing in areas
where there is naturally high levels of dis-
turbance may be able to alleviate stress on
sensitive areas that are prone to damage.
Methods evaluation.
In determin-
ing environmental regulations and man-
agement plans, the practice of precaution-
ary principle is needed, as stated in the
(http://ec.europa.eu/environment/
water/water-framework).
Experimental
results that are used in advising manage-
ment of a potentially detrimental activity
should be met with a certain level of cau-
tion. This is because there could be large
implications of advising ﬁsheries managers
in the Limfjord that there is no negative
impact of mussel dredging on the mussel
habitat, if there is indeed one. Thus, a re-
ﬂection on the methodology and sampling
design is aimed to elucidate the level to
which the results can be relied on, and ideas
for improvements in any future studies.
In the present study, a singular con-
trol and impact site was used, in a classic
Before- After-Control-Impact (BACI) de-
sign.
This basic design has been criti-
cized in the literature (e.g.
Underwood
(1992), Lindegarth et al. (2000)).Linde-
garth et al. (2000) argues that a singu-
lar control lacks independent replication,
and risks confounding the results.
The
BACI theory is based on the assumption
that what happens in the control would
also happen in the impacted site, if the im-
pact had not occurred. This is a substan-
tial assumption, as we know that in nature
vast amounts of variation occurs between
locations (spatial variation), which is de-
termined by a number of diﬀerent factors
(e.g. temperature, sediment grain size, nu-
trients). Temporal variation, such as sea-
sonal patterns, are expected, but are dif-
ﬁcult to predict precisely, and often vary
between locations as evidenced in Linde-
garth et al. (2000) when comparing three
pairs of control and impact sites. In their
study, non-consistent diﬀerences were ob-
served in the response variable for three spa-
tially separate control sites. Secondly, when
separately comparing the three pairs of sites
(control/impact) diﬀerent conclusions could
be drawn; both positive and negative ef-
35
==PAGE:36==
fects of dredging on infaunal communities.
This highlights the necessity of using mul-
tiple control sites. Having large variation
within a set of samples makes it diﬃcult
to detect any true signal from an impact
(Gray et al. 2006). Hence, to minimise sam-
ple variation caused by spatial variation,
the present study used sites that were ad-
jacent to one another to ensure temporal
changes in temperature, oxygen concentra-
tion etc. were consistent across sites. Short-
term pulse disturbancesm, such as analysed
in Figure 2: Exp I, are thought to be less af-
fected by confounding variables as changes
are expected to occur over a longer tem-
poral period (Lindegarth et al. 2000). Fi-
nally, obtaining information on abiotic pa-
rameters (temperature, oxygen concentra-
tion, sediment grain size, organic carbon
content), at each sampling time, can elu-
cidate whether changes in abiotic parame-
ters are correlated with an observed change
in community structure.
Sediment grain
size was for example shown to be the most
important variable for structuring infaunal
communities (Reiss et al. 2009).
Here, the large variation in averaged val-
ues between replicates gave ambiguous re-
sults that could have led to false conclu-
sions, exempliﬁed in the analysis of commu-
nity biomass in dredged area Figure 2:Exp
II. Firstly, the biomass in the control in-
creased between T0 and T1d, with mini-
mal diﬀerence in the impact site, possi-
bly indicating a negative eﬀect of dredging
in the dredged area Figure 23A. However,
on closer inspection, the large increase in
biomass in control T1d was as a result of
three large individuals being sampled (from
two replicates).
When comparing faunal
biomass, the occurrence of a few large indi-
viduals (their individual weight far exceed-
ing that of the total sample weight) can
swamp the underlying signal of the data,
as also described by Sk¨old et al. (2018) and
highlighted in Figure 23B. From the results
shown in Figure 23A, one conclusion may
be that dredging had negatively aﬀected
the biomass in the impact site.
Speciﬁ-
cally, having a detrimental eﬀect on large,
hard-bodied organisms, consistent with the
results of van Denderen et al. (2015) and
Sciberras et al. (2018). The subsequent re-
duction in biomass at T16wks poses question
to this conclusion.
It is more likely that
the total population was under-sampled and
the occurrence of these organisms was by
chance in control T1d.
More numerous
replicates (and/or larger samples) should be
used to ensure representation of a larger
proportion of the species occurring in an
area.
With extensive resources, one should
consider having multiple sites for both im-
pact and control to account for spatial vari-
ability (3 of each; total 6 sites Lindegarth
et al. (2000)). Sampling should occur multi-
ple times before and after the impact to ac-
count for temporal variability. Within each
site at each sampling time suﬃcient repli-
cates of 0.1m2 should be taken. A design
such as this was carried out by Lindegarth
et al. (2000) and resulted in 480 samples,
which equated to 36 tonnes of sediment to
be processed. Nonetheless, other studies on
ﬁsheries impact have used less comprehen-
sive sampling eﬀorts and produced statisti-
cally reliable results. Often one impact site
(or impact intensity) is used, with multiple
controls (between one and four) (Lambert
et al. (2017), Dolmer et al. (2001), LeBlanc
et al. (2015), Kaiser & Spencer (1996),
Goldberg et al. (2012)). The use of more
than one sampling time before impact is less
common Don´azar-Aramend´ıa et al. (2018),
but multiple sampling times (two or three)
after impact has more commonly been prac-
ticed (Lambert et al. (2017), Dolmer et al.
(2001), Kaiser & Spencer (1996)).
The
number of replicate samples varied between

studies, usually based on sample size. Large
samples (0.1m2) had generally fewer repli-
cates (3 replicates,Dolmer et al. (2001); 4
replicates, Kaiser & Spencer (1996)) and
smaller samples (0.02m2) had more numer-
ous replicates (8 replicates, LeBlanc et al.
(2015)). However, some studies combined
both, and had numerous larger replicates
(5-9 replicates,Lambert et al. (2017)).
Improvements for the present study,
based on similar studies mentioned above,
would be to include a second control site,
to make more reliable conclusions about
the observed diﬀerences in the dredge track
community composition, density, and diver-
sity from the control. Sampling twice be-
fore, and four times after dredging, would
be suitable to address the immediate and
short-term eﬀects of ﬁshing. For example,
samples could be taken on day 2 and day
7 after impact for determining the immedi-
ate eﬀects, and at week 16 and week 20 for
short-term seasonal eﬀects. The small sam-
ple size would also require more replicates,
to be more representative of the whole com-
munity (10-12 replicates). A ﬁnal improve-
ment to this study would be to mark the
dredge track such than it is detectable at
the later sampling times. However, Dolmer
et al. (2001) formed a grid reference sys-
tem on the study site such that divers could
mark where dredge tracks had been.
Al-
ternatively, Kaiser & Spencer (1996) used
side-scan sonar to identify dredge tracks
and recorded the GPS points for subsequent
data collection. The absence of any visable
sign of the dredge track may appear that
the mussel habitat had recovered. Dolmer
et al. (2001) found that the negative eﬀect
of dredging in the dredge track was still ap-
parent 40 days (6weeks) post dredging, pro-
viding evidence of the importance of moni-
toring the dredge track speciﬁcally.
Eﬀects
of
manipulating
the
dataset.
The results indicated that sig-
niﬁcant changes occurred in the fauna com-
munity composition of the dredge track, but
these were only apparent at the family level.
Many species were sparsely occurring in the
dataset, which could be indicative of too low
sampling eﬀort, although Gray et al. (2006)
argues that regardless of spatial scale, 70%
of the species sampled will be represented
by one or two individuals.
However, de-
tection of a signal from faunal community
data with few and small replicates might
beneﬁt from reducing the taxonomic res-
olution.
There is wide speculation in the
literature as to whether species or a higher
taxonomic level should be used, the con-
clusion is largely dependent on the purpose
of the study Jones (2008). Using ants and
beetles to detect environmental changes,
higher taxonomic levels were equally as ca-
pable as species level at detecting change
(Rosser 2017). The ﬁnding was ascribed to
the highly correlated relationship between
species and higher taxa (Rosser 2017). The
correlation between species and higher taxa
increased when communities were less in-
tensively sampled (Rosser 2017). This pro-
vides further support for the suitability of
using family instead of species level in the
current study. The advantage here of us-
ing family over species was the ability to
more clearly detect signals in the commu-
nity data related to the impact investigated.
The lack of a clear signal using the species
level is likely due to the high frequency of
sparsely occurring single species across all
samples.
In a community analysis, com-
mon species will have a higher weighting
than rarer species, especially when com-
paring absolute change (no transformation)
rather than relative change (e.g.
square
root-transformation).
Thus, having many
sparsely occurring species in a set of sam-
ples essentially takes a large proportion of
the community out of the analysis at the
species level, while grouping them by family

would allow more of the data to ‘contribute’
to the analysis.
An alternative method to amplify weak
signals in a data analysis is to exclude
data-elements that disproportionately inﬂu-
ence, or ‘muddy’ underlying patterns. For
example, Sk¨old et al. (2018) removed all
‘larger bodied organisms’ from their data
analyses because they ‘strongly inﬂuenced’
the response exhibited by the majority of
species. Alternatively, LeBlanc et al. (2015)
removed all taxa that did not contribute
more than 1% to the overall total abun-
dance, or was not found in 50% of the sam-
ples.
Further, Lambert et al. (2017) re-
moved all organisms that were not present
in >25% of the samples.
To investigate
whether clearer signals were evident all fam-
ilies that were not present in more than
two samples were removed from the data

The removal of the less com-
mon organisms gave a non-signiﬁcant result
in the dredge track (no change in commu-
nity) as opposed to a signiﬁcant result in the
full data set, highlighting the importance of
these sparsely occurring species in detect-
ing community changes in response to mus-
sel dredging. This result is in accordance
with Kaiser & Spencer (1996) who found
beam trawling to signiﬁcantly alter the in-
fauna community in sandy coastal habi-
tats in British waters, caused by a reduc-
tion in the number of less common or rare
species. Therefore, an alternative explana-
tion to the frequent occurrence of species
with just a few individuals in the data is
that these organisms may just be rarer, k-
selected species which exhibit low reproduc-
tive output, slow growth rates, and thus are
found in lower abundance. Furthermore, in
the terrestrial environment, habitat special-
ists have been shown to be most sensitive to
habitat fragmentation Keinath et al. (2017).
Fisheries Management.The suitabil-
ity of ﬁshing with bottom-contacting gear
in an area protected under Natura 2000
Habitats Directive is somewhat controver-
sial, and the occurence of which has posed
some question. The results presented here,
indicate that if management sets the ﬁshing
intensity to be similar to what was carried
out in the present study, the achievement of
a sustainable mussel ﬁshery may possible.
The surrounding mussel habitat appears to
be unaﬀected by dredging; shown by a lack
of a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in faunal density,
diversity, biomass, or community structure.
In accordance, Hoﬀmann & Dolmer (2000)
showed that mussel ﬁshing with diﬀerent
types of ﬁshing gear, had no long term ef-
fect on the epibenthic fauna. Thus, the only
eﬀect of dredging on the mussel habitat oc-
curs immediately in the dredge track.
The health status of the benthic envi-
ronment is measured by its macroinverte-
brate quality in the WFD, where the abun-
dance and diversity of organisms not devi-
ating from a non-impacted reference site is
recognised as high ecological status. Thus,
maintaining the same community of inver-
tebrates is an indication that the ﬁshery is
not having a negative eﬀect on the benthic
environment in the Natura 2000 site. Al-
though, there is evidence that the mussel
beds are ‘thinned’ as a result of dredging,
shown as the signiﬁcant reduction in abun-
dance in the impact site T16wks.
The re-
duced mussel density didn’t eﬀect the fau-
nal abundance and diversity (Figure 16 &
20).
The reduction of hard surfaces in the
soft sediment habitats (removing mussel
shell and pebbles) has been suggested
to be detrimental to recruitment (Dolmer
& Frandsen 2002).
It was inconclusive
whether the amount of ‘thinning’ that oc-
curred as a result of dredging negatively
impacted mussel recruitment. However, it
didn’t appear to have aﬀected the recruit-
ment of the associated community, as there
38
==PAGE:39==
was no diﬀerence in faunal community den-
sity in the impact and control site Fig-
ure 15.
It may be argued that ‘thinning’
the mussel beds, may beneﬁt the mussel
habitat by reducing the chances of anoxia.
Dense mussel beds have higher metabolic
rate than thinned beds (Jørgensen 1980).
Therefore, as oxygen concentrations dimin-
ish in summer months, the oxygen demand
of a dense mussel oxygen may tip the bal-
ance into anoxia resulting in mass mortality
(Jørgensen 1980). Therefore, the intermit-
tent thinning of mussels (dredging) every 3
years Dolmer & Frandsen (2002), may help
to reduce this risk.
Previously, the eﬀect of mussel dredging
persisted in the dredge track and bound-
ary area up to 6 weeks after impact when
dredging was carried out in the September,
after the summer recruitment Dolmer et al.
(2001). Whereas, in the current study no ef-
fect in the boundary area was evident even
after 16 weeks.
This might suggest that
mussel dredging earlier in the year (May),
could facilitate a more rapid recovery, as re-
covery might be enhanced by the summer
recruitment (Lambert et al. 2017).

Conclusions
In view of the observed results, and the
adoption of the precautionary principle in
environmental legislation, it would be con-
servative to highlight the result that there is
a negative eﬀect of dredging directly in the
dredge track, and the short-term eﬀects are
unknown until further studies can be car-
ried out. However, overall it appears from
this small study that at this level, mussel
dredging is able to sustain a similar com-
munity of fauna, with minimal detriment to
the mussel density and biomass in the area.
