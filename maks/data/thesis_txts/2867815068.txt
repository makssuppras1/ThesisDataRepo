Abstract 
This master’s thesis examines the challenges in the Mastertool for calculating Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) on urban districts through the framework of the DGNB certification 
system. The study emphasises the importance of LCA for quantifying environmental 
impacts and analysing urban development to identify sustainable practices that can 
effectively mitigate carbon emissions and resource depletion. 
 
The first part of the project maps the growing pressure of urbanisation and its role in climate 
change. It explores Denmark's regulations and the DGNB certification system for Urban 
Districts, using LCA Mastertool to measure and reduce carbon emissions. The project 
highlights the role of LCA in assessing environmental impacts, particularly Global Warming 
Potential (GWP). It discusses the influence of the functional unit in interpreting results and 
the underlying incentives that may arise. 
 
The second part of the project presents a case study concerning the project Fælledby in 
Copenhagen Municipality. Based on the knowledge foundation, the study identifies four 
attention points analysed through the Mastertool. It explores how LCA on an urban district 
scale may deprioritise district-level impacts in favour of building-specific assessments. It 
emphasises the importance of incorporating more comprehensive infrastructure and site 
preparation into LCA and discusses the need to consider lifespan beyond material values. 
The study also highlights the benefits of using specific Environmental Product Declarations 
(EPD), showing that replacing outdated generic values can significantly reduce carbon 
emissions by almost 90%, with reductions achieved by using best-in-class EPDs. 
 
The project concludes that current LCA methods for urban districts face significant 
challenges, including an overfocus on building impacts, inadequate infrastructure and 
material lifespan considerations, and outdated data for generic materials. To better support 
sustainability goals, a future LCA tool must be capable of handling project-specific 
modifications with greater customisation in the underlying data framework.

1.1 Population and Urbanization 
Population refers to the total number of people living in a particular district, whether it’s a 
city, country, or the entire planet (Cambridge Dictionary, 2024). The human population is 
dynamic and constantly changing due to births, deaths, and migration (Magawa et al., 2023). 
Over the past 50 years, the global population has more than doubled, surpassing 8 billion 
people (Ritchie, Rodés-Guirao, et al., 2024). This rapid growth has brought significant 
changes in where and how people live. Today, more than half of the world's population 
resides in cities, and it is predicted that by 2050, more than two-thirds of all people will live 
in urban districts or other densely populated centres (United Nations, 2018b). This trend, 
known as urbanisation, refers to the increase in population concentration in urban districts 
and is often caused by various factors like economic opportunities, higher living standards, 
and improved infrastructure and services (FAO et al., 2023). 
 
 
 
Figure 1: People living in urban and rural districts worldwide (Ritchie, Samborska, et al., 2024) 
  
Urbanisation leads to the growth of existing cities, making them more extensive and densely 
populated. As cities expand, they often absorb surrounding towns and rural districts, 
transforming them into part of the urban landscape. Additionally, entirely new cities emerge 
to accommodate the increasing population. As a result, urbanisation contributes to the 
development of larger and more complex urban networks, reshaping the geographic, 
economic, and social structure of entire regions (United Nations, 2018a).

1.2 Climate Change 
Climate change represents one of our planet's most significant and urgent challenges. It 
refers to long-term alterations in temperature, precipitation, and other elements of Earth’s 
climate system, driven primarily by the increase in human activity (Forster, 2014). Fossil 
fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes have increased concentrations of greenhouse 
gases, such as carbon dioxide, in the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide, commonly referred to as 
CO2, traps heat, leading to a rise in global temperatures, widely known as global warming 
(NASA Climate, 2023). 
 
Understanding the relationship between human activity and climate change, it becomes 
clear that the growth of urban districts plays a pivotal role in climate change (Seto et al., 
2016). 
 
 
Figure 2: Growth and Carbon Dioxide Emissions (Statista & O’Neill, 2024) (Ritchie & Roser, 2020) 
 
 
Urbanisation significantly impacts climate change through various interconnected factors 
contributing to global warming. Urban expansion frequently involves the conversion of 
natural landscapes, such as forests and wetlands, into developed districts. This 
deforestation reduces the Earth's capacity to absorb CO2 from the atmosphere. Additionally, 
the loss of these natural habitats negatively affects biodiversity and disrupts ecosystems 
essential for regulating local and global climates (Royal Society, 2024).

As cities develop and expand, the demand for transportation and infrastructure grows. 
Districts require extensive networks of roads, bridges, and public transit systems. The 
construction and maintenance of these infrastructures not only consume substantial energy 
but also result in the emission of greenhouse gases. Furthermore, poorly planned areas can 
lead to a greater reliance on cars, which increases overall emissions (Masson et al., 2020). 
 
Energy consumption in cities is another major factor influenced by population growth and 
urbanisation. Areas with dense populations and extensive commercial and industrial 
activities have a high energy need. The demand for electricity to power lighting, heating, 
cooling, and other systems is substantial and often sourced from fossil fuels (Energy 
Agency, 2020).  
 
In urban settings, waste production typically increases. Much of this waste is disposed of in 
landfills, decomposing and emitting methane, an influential greenhouse gas (The World 
Bank, 2024). Building emissions are also a critical concern. Urban infrastructure construction 
requires materials like cement, steel, and asphalt. Cement production alone accounts for 
approximately 8% of global carbon emissions, primarily due to the energy-intensive process 
of heating limestone and the associated chemical reactions (International Energy Agency 
(IEA), 2018).

1.3 Sustainability 
Sustainability is a comprehensive approach to managing and conserving resources. A 
holistic concept that aims to balance environmental, social, and economic factors to ensure 
a long-term healthy planet and society. Sustainability seeks to harmonise human activity 
with Earth’s natural systems, ensuring that our actions today do not jeopardise the health 
and prosperity of future generations (Damsgaard, 2023). 
 
The importance of sustainability has grown exponentially in recent decades, mainly due to 
the rise in population and, as a result, climate change. However, sustainability is not just 
about environmental preservation. Social sustainability involves ensuring communities are 
inclusive and resilient, with access to basic needs and equal opportunities for all. Economic 
sustainability focuses on creating systems that support growth and stability without 
depleting natural resources or harming the environment. By integrating these three 
considerations - environmental, social, and economic - sustainability aims to create a 
balanced and fair approach to development that benefits both people and the planet 
(Damsgaard, 2023). 
 
 
 
“Sustainable development is a development that meets the 
needs of the present without compromising the ability 
of future generations to meet their own needs.” 
(United Nations, 1987) 
 
 
 
Influencing a society to become more sustainable can be achieved through various 
initiatives and can often lead to companies and individuals moving towards more 
sustainable conduct. Economic incentives also play a crucial role in accelerating societal 
change. Governments can introduce legislation that mandates sustainable behaviour 
through carbon taxes, incentives for renewable energy, or bans on environmentally harmful 
practices, such as excessive plastic use or deforestation (Det Økologiske Råd, 2015).

Raising public awareness about the importance of sustainability can create a cultural shift. 
When citizens are informed and engaged, they are more likely to support green policies, 
adjust their consumption habits, and hold businesses accountable. Alongside public 
awareness, accountability is transparency. Establishing agencies responsible for monitoring 
compliance with sustainability regulations ensures that progress is measurable and 
continuous. Regular audits and transparency reports keep businesses and governments 
accountable for their sustainability commitments (Landbrug & Fødevarer, 2023).  
 
Another effective approach is establishing clear sustainability benchmarks with minimum 
performance standards for carbon emissions, either by law or agencies. By implementing 
such standards, industries are encouraged to innovate and adopt practices that reduce 
environmental impact.  
 
The building industry is one of the sectors where sustainability is increasingly integrated 
(Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2024c). Buildings are a significant part of our daily lives - they 
provide shelter, workplaces, and spaces for recreation. However, building construction, 
operation, and maintenance are responsible for a large portion of global resource 
consumption and environmental impact. The building industry accounts for nearly 40% of 
global energy use and over 30% of energy-related carbon dioxide emissions (World Green 
Building Council, 2024). 
 
The concept of sustainable building - often called “green building” - has emerged as a critical 
strategy for reducing the environmental impact of the construction industry. Sustainable 
building practices involve designing and constructing energy-efficient, environmentally 
responsible, and resource-efficient buildings throughout their life cycle, from site selection 
to design, construction, operation, and eventual demolition. This includes using renewable 
energy sources, such as solar or wind power, incorporating energy-efficient systems and 
appliances, and selecting sustainable materials that minimise environmental harm (Green 
Building CA, 2024). 
 
Moreover, building sustainably extends beyond environmental concerns. It also focuses on 
creating healthy indoor environments that enhance the well-being of occupants. This can 
include improving indoor air quality, maximising natural light, and using non-toxic materials. 
Social and economic factors are also considered, such as ensuring that buildings are 
affordable, accessible, and contribute positively to the community (Green Building Council 
US, 2016).

In 1955, the first version of the building regulations, as we know them today, was published. 
Since then, these regulations have governed how construction is carried out in Denmark 
(Dansk Byggeskik, 2024). As society became more aware of the importance of sustainability 
and the impact of construction on the climate, the Green Building Council Denmark, now 
called “Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri”, was formed in 2010. In 2012, their first Manual was 
launched, introducing stricter sustainability requirements than the building regulations 
(Genvex, 2024). Den Frivillige Bæredygtighedsklasse (FBK) was introduced in 2020 as a 
further step towards a greener transition, developed by the Ministry of Social Affairs and 
Housing (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2024a). The latest development came in 2022 when Real 
Dania and the Villum Foundation funded the Reduction Roadmap tool through the "4 to 1 
Planet" project, developed by EFFEKT, Artelia, CEBRA, and Denmark’s leading universities. 
The project aims to reduce the carbon footprint of residential construction by 75%. 
According to Earth Overshoot Day, Denmark consumes four times more than its share of the 
Earth's total resources, with the goal being to reduce this to one planet's worth of resources 
(4 til 1 planet, 2023). 
 
 
Figure 4: Reduction Roadmap (4 til 1 planet, 2022) 
 
 
Progress with LCA has advanced less on the urban scale level, which also applies to 
regulations. In recent years, the building level has focused on material sustainability and 
Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs). As previously mentioned, the Reduction 
Roadmap is one of the initiatives to reduce carbon emissions at the building level. However, 
at the urban district level, this development has yet to be as advanced, and this also applies 
to the tools used to calculate the climate impact of urban districts.

Generally, tools can be seen as incentive structures to encourage choices beyond standard 
practices, a form of soft law that drives change. These tools also give decision-makers an 
understanding of how the world is structured, allowing them to "play" with the numbers and 
optimise the significant impact factors, helping push the industry in the right direction. When 
decision-makers demand more sustainable materials, it pushes the industry toward a more 
sustainable direction. 
 
The need to measure and evaluate progress becomes essential. Quantifying sustainability 
allows us to assess our actions' environmental, social, and economic impacts, helping to 
guide decision-making and policy development. By turning abstract concepts into 
measurable data, we can better understand how our practices align with sustainability goals, 
identify districts for improvement, and track the effectiveness of interventions over time. 
Quantifying sustainability involves a multi-dimensional approach that considers a wide range 
of factors and various tools, metrics, and frameworks that have been developed to capture 
these aspects (Klorane Botanical Foundation, 2024).

1.4 Integrated Design 
Contrary to common belief, building sustainably is not necessarily more expensive than 
conventional construction, and in many cases, it can lead to significant cost savings over 
time (Finans.dk, 2020). Sustainable building practices optimise resource efficiency, reduce 
energy consumption, and minimise waste, which can offset initial costs and provide long-
term financial benefits. 
 
In any project, timing is crucial, and initiating various processes at the appropriate stages 
can lead to significant time and cost savings. Integrating sustainability early in the project is 
particularly advantageous, as it allows for more influence on the design without substantially 
increasing costs. When sustainability is considered from the outset, it becomes easier to 
seamlessly incorporate eco-friendly materials, energy-efficient systems, and other 
sustainable features into the design. This proactive approach minimises the need for costly 
changes later, as core elements become more expensive to alter in the advanced phases of 
the project. In contrast, adjustments to lighter elements can be made and optimised in the 
later stages without incurring substantial additional costs, ensuring the project remains 
sustainable and financially efficient (Region Midtjylland, 2023).

1.5 Objectives and Approach 
Based on an initial mapping of rising population, urbanisation, and the challenges it entails 
regarding climate change and sustainable city development, this master’s thesis aims to 
apply theoretical knowledge in practice. The focus is on urban development, limited to four 
attention points, with an investigation of the last aspect through a case study. The motivation 
behind the project stems from the increasing pressure on sustainable urban development 
and how we quantify it. The project examines the DGNB’s method for quantifying 
sustainability in urban districts while being critical and collecting knowledge for a future 
method.  
 
The connection between theoretical and practical applications within the focus districts will 
be continuously discussed. The integrated design process is essential for the project's 
conclusion and recurring focus. This leads to the formulation of the project's problem 
statement. 
 
What are the challenges in the current method of calculating LCA for urban districts, 
and what considerations should be applied to a future method? 
How can this be concretised in a case study?

further. The first two sub-questions will be addressed in the Knowledge Foundation’s partial

1.6 Delimitation 
Delimitation defines the scope and boundaries of the study, outlining its specific focus while 
clarifying what lies outside its purview to ensure a clear and precise research framework. 
 
1. This project is based on the requirements in the DGNB Urban Districts Manual 2020 
version 2.0.1 and will not consider other versions of the Manual. 
 
2. The LCA Mastertool "21. BYO_LCA-LCC_2020-01-17 Fælledby - version 1.1" will be the 
baseline in this project. It stems from the generic DGNB 2020 Urban Districts Manual 
Mastertool, available for versions 2.0.1 and 2.0.0. 
 
3. The LCA analysis includes only phases A1 - A3, consistent with the EPDs and what is used 
in the Mastertool. Phases A1 - A3, or "Cradle to Gate", represents a partial product life cycle 
assessment. This encompasses the processes from resource extraction (cradle) to the 
factory (gate) before the product's transportation to the consumer. 
 
4. In this case study, only the GWP has been calculated and compared, primarily due to 
regulatory requirements and comparability constraints. According to building regulations, 
the focus is solely on climate impact, measured in CO₂ equivalents, without covering other 
environmental indicators. Additionally, GWP is the only metric that remains directly 
comparable between the Mastertool and the latest EPD. 
 
5. In the fourth attention point of the case study, “3.3.4 Materials in the LCA Calculations,” on 
page 56, optimisation is performed solely based on materials used in public areas, excluding 
buildings and private areas from the LCA calculation since this thesis is focused on urban 
areas that planners can influence. 
 
6. Natural areas such as meadows, trees, and water bodies not intended for direct use or 
foot traffic are excluded from the calculation since they are either already established areas 
or below the insignificant threshold for carbon emission. 
 
7. In the LCA calculation, the reference lifespan is set to 50 years, accounting for the 
durability of materials and the frequency of replacements over this period, similar to the 
building level.

1.8 Method 
The assignment's problem formulation and sub-questions have been addressed through 
theoretical literature studies and practical validations on a specific case. The results from 
the case study have been processed iteratively and continuously assessed with a critical 
approach. 
 
Part 2: Knowledge Foundation 
 
The Knowledge Foundation is established through a thorough literature review of relevant 
research articles, textbooks, and applicable legislation, serving as the basis for the case 
study in Part 3 with continuous connections between these two sections. This section 
introduces the Danish Green Building Council, its history, and the DGNB sustainability system 
while exploring the Urban Districts Manual, its criteria, and how they are weighted. 
Differences between the project's Manual and the latest version are discussed, alongside a 
general overview of LCA and the evaluation parameter, GWP. The LCA Mastertool, used to 
calculate LCA in relation to DGNB certification, is described. Finally, the main points of the 
Knowledge Foundation are summarised in the partial conclusion. 
 
Part 3: Case Study 
 
The Case Study examines four attention points, with an in-depth exploration of the last one 
through a specific case. Building LCA, infrastructure, lifespan and materials. Fælledby, the 
case study, is a new urban district under development by PensionDanmark, with ambitious 
goals to achieve Platinum, the highest possible rating in the DGNB system. The case study 
Fælledby serves as a baseline for the analysis and is compared with various hypothetical 
scenarios to explore optimisation opportunities. The Danish Green Building Council has 
provided all materials related to the case. The main objective of the case study was to 
evaluate the Mastertool while being critical of its derivative effects. 
 
In the final part of the project, the results from the case study are concluded, and the project's 
limitations and opportunities for further studies are discussed. 
 
The Mastertool, other tools, and the Adobe Creative Cloud (Photoshop, Illustrator, and 
InDesign) have been utilised during the project writing to support points through 
visualisations.

2.1 Danish Green Building Council 
In 2010, Denmark’s construction industry, led by the Danish Energy Agency, formed an 
independent sustainability council to establish a standardised framework for measurable 
sustainability in Danish buildings and urban districts (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2024). 
 
On April 12, 2010, the Green Building Council Denmark (DK-GBC) was founded as a non-profit 
organisation for stakeholders focused on sustainable construction, later rebranding in 2023 
as the Council for Sustainable Building (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri). A study by the 
Danish Building Research Institute (BUILD) and the Building Evaluation Centre found that the 
German DGNB certification was best suited for Denmark based on three critical factors 
illustrated in Figure 7 (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2024). 
 
The Council for Sustainable Building in Denmark now manages the DGNB certification 
system, which includes five Manuals: DGNB Renovation and New Build, DGNB Service, DGNB 
Room, DGNB Villa, and DGNB Urban Districts. DGNB certification has been widely adopted 
in Denmark, with 256 projects certified in 2023 (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2024). 
 
 
Figure 7: Three reasons why DGNB was chosen (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020)

2.2 DGNB Manuals 
The release of DGNB Urban Districts from 2014 was produced through collaboration with 
the Ministry of Environment, Realdania, SBi, DTU, and over 60 national experts. It has had 
many revisions; the latest version was released in 2020. It evaluates the environmental, 
social, and economic aspects while considering an urban development district's process and 
technical aspects. The five qualities contribute equally to 20% of the final evaluation, also 
shown in Figure 8 (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2024).  
 
 
 
Figure 8: Sustainability contributions in the DGNB Manual, DGNB Manual for Urban Districts 2.0. 
 (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020) 
 
 
The five qualities are divided into 27 criteria and sub-criteria. Based on the final evaluation, 
the overall degree of fulfilment for the urban district is calculated and awarded either silver, 
gold, or platinum (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020).A minimum score in each quality 
must also be met to achieve a well-balanced distribution of points throughout the five 
qualities. Each criterion in every quality is weighed differently to emphasise the importance 
of certain aspects of sustainability. Additionally, the urban district must comply with 
minimum requirements and knock-out criteria.  
 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
32     l   2 Knowledge Foundation 
 
 
Figure 9: Certification medals (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020) 
 
In the DGNB system, individuals can train to become consultants or advance to the role of 
auditors. To register a project for DGNB certification, one must be an auditor or obtain 
auditor status for the specific project. The title of consultant and auditor is earned through 
training, and an exam in the chosen Manual, and both roles are protected titles requiring 
further education. The certification system is a method that quantifies sustainability, making 
it measurable. It allows comparison of different urban districts using a standardised set of 
sustainability benchmarks (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020). 
 
As described in the scope, this thesis is based on the DGNB Manual for Urban Districts 
version 2.0.1. However, the last version of the Manual is version 3.0.0. There are two reasons 
why the thesis is based on an earlier version. The main reason is that version 2.0.1 uses the 
Mastertool for LCA evaluation, while version 3.0.0 allows for freedom of method instead. 
Second, the case study considered in this thesis was evaluated based on version 2.0.1. The 
two significant differences between Manual 2020 versions 2.0.1 and 3.0.0 are described 
below, focusing on LCA. 
 
Version 3.0.0 thoroughly revises the 'ENV1.1 Life Cycle Assessment'. Inspired by the Building 
Manual for DGNB Renovation and New Build, points are now deducted for demolition, and 
the calculation of buildings' climate impacts is carried out according to the method in the 
current building regulations. The method for calculating the climate impact from other 
elements of the urban district is flexible. In addition, the project must now address how it 
relates to the municipal climate action plan. 
 
The Excel sheet (the Mastertool), previously used for LCA and LCC calculations in version 
2.0.1, has been discontinued. In ENV1.1, various industry tools are used to calculate climate 
impact. For total economic calculation in ECO1.1, LCCbyg must be used.

2.3 The importance of LCA 
LCA provides a comprehensive evaluation of the environmental impacts on various 
indicators associated with buildings, infrastructure, and urban systems throughout their 
entire life cycle, from raw material extraction to construction, operation, and disposal (Rådet 
for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020). Using LCA calculations early in the design process helps 
identify the most sustainable practices and materials, reducing the overall environmental 
footprint. The method can assess long-term environmental costs and allows for more 
proactive and informed decision-making, prioritising options with lower carbon emissions, 
reducing waste, and conserving resources (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2020). 
 
 
 
Figure 10: Life Cycle Assessment (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2022) 
 
 
To fulfil the requirements for calculating a building’s climate impact according to building 
regulations, the effect must be assessed over a calculation period of 50 years, beginning 
from the date of the building's final approval.  
 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
34     l   2 Knowledge Foundation 
 
This specified period does not necessarily represent the expected lifespan of the building 
itself but is set by regulations to enable comparability across different building climate 
impact assessments (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2022). 
 
 
Figure 11: Life Cycle Assessment phases (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2022) 
 
 
LCA for buildings is divided into phases and modules defining each stage of the building’s 
lifespan, from raw material extraction to disposal and beyond. The standard EN15978 
divides these phases into specific modules (A1-D), each representing distinct life cycle 
processes that impact the building’s overall environmental footprint (Social- og 
Boligstyrelsen, 2022). The five main stages are the Product Stage, where raw materials are 
sourced and processed; the Construction Process Stage, encompassing the transportation 
and assembly of materials; the Use Stage, covering ongoing operational impacts like 
maintenance and energy use; the End-of-Life Stage, involving deconstruction and waste 
management; and Beyond Building Life Cycle impacts, which account for recycling and 
reuse potential after demolition. Each phase and its constituent modules offer a clear 
breakdown of all stages where environmental impacts occur, enabling an accurate cradle-
to-grave assessment of a building’s ecological footprint. This holistic approach is 
instrumental in aligning building design and construction practices with sustainability goals 
and regulatory standards (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2022).

2022).

LCA is a technical tool and a strategic approach to sustainable urban development. It fosters 
a culture of sustainability that extends beyond individual projects and can influence entire 
metropolitan regions. Sustainability suddenly becomes measurable, and it helps meet 
sustainability goals and regulatory requirements, making it invaluable for developers, 
architects, and policymakers.

2.4 Global Warming Potential 
GWP stands for Global Warming potential and is a crucial metric for comparing the relative 
impact of different greenhouse gases on global warming. As greenhouse gases accumulate 
in the Earth's atmosphere, they trap heat, leading to the warming of the planet - a process 
commonly referred to as the greenhouse effect. While carbon dioxide (CO2) is the most well-
known and abundant greenhouse gas, other gases such as methane (CH4), nitrous oxide 
(N2O), and various fluorinated gases also contribute to global warming. However, these 
gases differ significantly in their ability to trap heat and their persistence in the atmosphere 
(US EPA, 2024). 
 
GWP provides a standardised measure, CO2-eq, to assess how much heat a greenhouse gas 
traps in the atmosphere over a specific period, usually 20, 100, or 500 years, relative to 
carbon dioxide. By assigning carbon dioxide a GWP of 1, other gases can be evaluated 
regarding their warming effects. For example, methane has a GWP of approximately 25 over 
100 years, meaning it is 25 times more effective at trapping heat than carbon dioxide over 
that time frame (Klimaleksikon, 2024). Quantifying the GWP of all emissions associated with 
an LCA can help achieve a more accurate and comprehensive understanding of the 
environmental footprint.

2.6 Materials in LCA 
Materials are one of the main factors that significantly influence the LCA result, as they 
directly impact the environmental footprint. The choice of materials influences several vital 
factors, which are described below (One Click LCA, 2024). 
 
1. Embodied 
Energy 
and 
Emissions: 
Construction 
materials' 
production, 
transportation, and installation contribute significantly to a district's embodied 
energy and carbon emissions. Material choices can drastically reduce these 
environmental impacts (Teknologisk Institut, 2024). 
 
2. Lifespan and Durability: Materials with longer lifespans reduce the need for 
replacements, lowering the overall resource consumption and waste generation 
throughout the life cycle of the urban district (Social- og Boligstyrelsen, 2024b). 
 
3. Resource Use and Depletion: The extraction and manufacturing of materials 
deplete natural resources, such as water, minerals, and fossil fuels. Selecting 
materials that are renewable, recycled, or locally sourced can minimise resource 
depletion (Miljøstyrelsen, 2024b). 
 
4. End-of-Life Impact: Materials' recyclability, reuse potential, and disposal methods 
affect the environmental burden at the end of a district’s life cycle. Materials that 
can be reused or recycled reduce landfill waste and the need for new resources 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2024a). 
 
5. Operational Efficiency: Some materials enhance the energy efficiency of buildings 
and public spaces by providing better insulation, thermal mass, or renewable energy 
potential, contributing to a lower operational energy demand over time 
(Energistyrelsen, 2024). 
 
Incorporating sustainable, low-impact materials is essential for reducing the overall 
environmental footprint and achieving long-term sustainability in urban districts.

2.7 Functional Unit 
A functional unit is a quantified function description that serves as a reference point in an 
LCA. It allows for evaluating environmental impacts in relation to a specific output or 
outcome, enabling meaningful comparisons across different scenarios.  
 
The choice of functional unit in an LCA can significantly influence the results and 
interpretations of the assessment. For instance, when results are expressed in terms of 
square meters (m²) rather than per person, the perception of the result can vary widely. 
 
When, for example, the functional unit is defined as “CO2-eq per m²”, it may inadvertently 
encourage the construction of more significant buildings or more extensive urban areas. 
This approach can lead to a project seeking to maximise its floor area to dilute the overall 
environmental impact per square meter. It may also incentivise developers to focus on 
quantity rather than the quality or efficiency of the building. For example, suppose a 
developer knows their environmental impact is assessed per m². In that case, they might 
prioritise increasing the total floor area rather than investing in sustainable materials or 
energy-efficient designs. This could lead to more significant buildings that contribute more 
to urban sprawl without necessarily providing proportional benefits in terms of functionality 
or sustainability (Weidema et al., 2004). 
 
Such a metric can also influence urban planning decisions, where planners may feel 
pressured to approve more significant developments to achieve favourable LCA results. This 
could undermine the broader goals of creating compact, sustainable communities 
prioritising walkability, green spaces, and efficient resource use. 
 
To avoid these unintended consequences, reassessing and redefining the functional unit 
may better reflect the urban district's intended outcomes. For example, using a functional 
unit based on “CO2-eq per person” might encourage design that optimises resource use and 
social equity, promoting a balance between density and liability, but that also has its 
loopholes (Weidema et al., 2004).

3.2 Objective of the Case Study 
The primary objective of this case study is to critically evaluate the LCA Mastertool for DGNB 
Urban Districts version 2.0.1. When conducting an LCA for urban districts, several key factors 
must be carefully considered to ensure the accuracy and comprehensiveness of the 
assessment. Understanding the environmental impact of an urban district requires more 
than just analysing the buildings; it involves a holistic view of the entire district’s 
infrastructure, materials, and systems. This case study highlights four critical aspects that 
significantly influence the accuracy and reliability of LCA for urban districts. 
 
 
First, distinguishing building-level LCA from district-level LCA ensures that the assessments 
focus on urban sustainability factors rather than being overly influenced by individual 
building calculations. Second, including infrastructure such as sewer systems, energy grids, 
and transportation networks is essential, as these elements play a significant role in the 
district's environmental performance and are not included in the building LCA. Third, the 
lifespan of materials is crucial to capture long-term environmental impacts associated with 
maintenance, replacement, and resource use. Finally, selecting specific EPD for materials, 
rather than relying on generic data, improves the accuracy of LCA by reflecting the actual 
environmental impacts of chosen products and incentivises product manufacturers to 
develop sustainable products. 
 
 
A perfunctory analysis of the three first attention points and a more in-depth analysis of the 
fourth attention point will be done to analyse how these factors might affect the assessment. 
Furthermore, the attention points will be assessed regarding incorporating these features in 
a future tool for LCA. This case study, therefore, aims to provide a detailed analysis of 
Fælledby’s environmental impacts, using these four attention points as a framework for 
evaluating the DGNB Mastertool for LCA. This is not a complete LCA calculation but a partial 
LCA assessment.

3.3.2 Infrastructure 
Infrastructure, such as sewer systems, water supply networks, and energy grids, represents 
a significant share of a district's overall environmental impact. Sewer systems, for example, 
involve extensive excavation, use of concrete and steel, and ongoing energy demands for 
pumping and treatment. Similarly, roads, bridges, and public transportation systems have 
high material and energy demands during construction and ongoing environmental impacts 
throughout their operational life.  
 
Realdania has published a report on the environmental and climate impact of on-site 
development on urban planning. Their key takeaways emphasise that climate-friendly site 
development requires a solid commitment to minimising carbon emissions at every stage 
of urban development, from the initial selection of the district’s location to the practical 
aspects of construction. The report highlights the potential for reducing carbon emissions 
during the early planning stages. Decisions made here, such as the choice of energy supply 
and strategies to shape transportation needs and mobility patterns, are crucial for 
influencing the district’s overall carbon footprint. Additionally, the report underscores the 
importance of a thoroughly developed urban plan, including careful planning of excavation 
work and soil management (Realdania By & Byg, 2023). 
 
 
 
“Roads and public spaces, parking, sewage, utilities, and climate resilience - developing a 
new urban area requires far more than just constructing buildings.” 
(Realdania By & Byg, 2023) 
 
 
In Fælledby, subsurface infrastructure is not included in the project’s assessments; only 
roads and other surface-level elements are considered. It is estimated that building 
maturation accounts for 15-20% of the total CO2 impact of urban districts, with 70% of all 
materials below ground being concrete in the form of sewage systems and other 
infrastructure (IPCC, 2023) (Eea, 2023). Concrete production alone accounts for 8% of the 
global total (Damsø Pedersen, 2021). 
 
 
The DGNB certification system for urban districts encompasses a range of activities 
involved in site development, covering both the planning and construction phases. 
 
==PAGE:51==
 
51 
 
 
However, site development and associated carbon emissions are not specific focal points 
within this system.  
 
The InfraLCA tool, developed by Vejdirektoratet (The Danish Road Directorate), is a shared 
LCA tool for Danish stakeholders, focusing primarily on the planning, operation, and 
construction of infrastructure. InfraLCA ensures that a standardised method is used across 
the sector to quantify the environmental impacts of infrastructure projects (Vejdirektoratet, 
2024). Appendix A contains a table comparing the input categories of the two tools. The 
table shows that the InfraLCA tool includes twice as many categories, confirming that 
InfraLCA provides a more detailed and comprehensive approach to LCA for infrastructure 
than the DGNB Mastertool. 
 
Vejdirektoratet and Banedanmark convened an expert panel on October 9, 2023, consisting 
of Niels Buus Kristensen, Harpa Birgisdottir, and Henrik Gudmundsson to conduct an 
independent evaluation of the InfraLCA tool. This evaluation assessed the methodological 
foundation, data quality, functional scope, and usability of InfraLCA. The review concluded 
that InfraLCA can broadly be interpreted as a reliable representation of a construction 
project’s CO₂ footprint, albeit with three considerations (Vejdirektoratet, 2024): 
 
- 
Not all LCA phases and modules are included. Specifically, impacts in the 
operational phase (such as traffic changes) are critical for a comprehensive 
footprint. Energy consumption during operations and land-use modifications should 
also be factored in for some project types. 
- 
For aggregated uses, it is essential to clarify the tool’s application boundaries and 
establish guidelines for its use, particularly regarding detailed specification levels. 
- 
The more diverse the infrastructure projects being compared (e.g., road vs. rail), the 
greater the need to consider functional scaling with a unified functional unit. 
 
These considerations are worth noticing when looking at the tool for inspiration. Regardless, 
several advantages can be seen by incorporating InfraLCA into the DGNB LCA Urban District 
toolset. InfraLCA is a tool designed to address the complexities of assessing urban districts, 
primarily focusing on infrastructure. InfraLCA includes features that allow for the detailed 
analysis of material flows, energy consumption, and carbon emissions throughout the 
lifecycle of infrastructure components. 
==PAGE:52==
 
52     l   3 Case Study 
 
The evaluation process becomes more robust, covering the urban district and the essential 
infrastructure that supports them. This ensures that the urban district's LCA reflects the total 
environmental impact, providing a more accurate and actionable framework for improving 
sustainability at the district level. This holistic approach is crucial for creating urban 
environments that are truly sustainable, resilient, and aligned with long-term climate goals. 
 
A significant yet often overlooked aspect of urban development is the CO₂ impact from the 
external infrastructure required to support new areas, which current tools cannot assess. 
Large-scale urban projects like Fælledby require extensive connections to the existing city 
network to facilitate access and integration, such as expanded roadways, bridges, or transit 
lines. These external infrastructures bear substantial CO₂ footprints, highlighting the 
importance of incorporating their impacts into holistic urban sustainability assessments. 
Ignoring these factors could lead to underestimating the environmental costs of new urban 
district developments. 
 
Urban districts should be at the forefront of sustainable development, promoting low-carbon 
lifestyles by emphasising sustainable transportation, such as cycling infrastructure and 
public transit, over car-dependent infrastructure like parking lots and major roadways. Urban 
districts can reduce emissions and encourage more sustainable urban living by prioritising 
green transportation options. Another parameter not included in the LCA is traffic, which 
presents challenges regarding the calculation method. The environmental impact of traffic 
involves complex variables such as traffic volume, vehicle types, and travel distances, which 
require careful consideration to ensure accurate assessment. These factors add a layer of 
complexity to the LCA process, making it challenging to integrate traffic-related emissions 
effectively into the overall analysis. 
 
 
Infrastructure elements like roads fundamentally differ from buildings' operational energy 
consumption during the use phase. While buildings typically incur ongoing energy expenses 
for heating, cooling, and maintenance, the energy demands of infrastructure tend to be 
minimal after construction. Consequently, it may be prudent to focus on "upfront carbon 
emissions" when assessing the environmental impact of infrastructure projects. This 
approach emphasises the carbon footprint associated with the materials and construction 
processes rather than ongoing operational impacts.

In addition, urban districts must be designed with resilience in mind, given their typical 20 - 
30-year development horizon. Future urban environments may face unforeseen changes in 
climate, transportation needs, and technological requirements. By integrating adaptable 
infrastructure and prioritising sustainable urban design principles, urban districts can remain 
resilient and relevant in the face of evolving environmental and social conditions.

3.3.3 Material Lifespan 
Incorporating material lifespan into LCA for urban districts is essential to assess 
environmental impacts and sustainability over time. Material lifespan indicates how long a 
component can fulfil its function before needing replacement or significant maintenance. It 
also affects production, transport, and disposal frequency, each contributing significantly to 
environmental impact.  
 
For instance, a material with lower initial carbon emissions but requiring replacement every 
25 years may not outperform one with a higher upfront carbon impact yet a 50-year lifespan, 
as replacements demand additional resources, including production, transport, and 
machinery for installation. By selecting materials with extended lifespans, especially in long-
term infrastructure such as roads or water systems, the need for frequent replacements is 
minimised, reducing resource use and emissions associated with repeated production and 
installation. 
 
 
Figure 16: CO2 impact vs lifespan

3.3.4 Materials in the LCA Calculations 
Rather than relying on generic values, choosing specific EPDs for materials is essential for 
creating accurate and reliable LCA data for urban districts. EPDs provide detailed, product-
specific data on a material's environmental impacts throughout its entire lifecycle, from raw 
material extraction and manufacturing to transportation, use, maintenance, and disposal. 
These declarations are based on third-party rigorous and scientifically validated 
assessments of a material’s carbon footprint, energy consumption, water use, and other 
critical sustainability metrics. 
 
While useful as a starting point, generic values often need to be more generalised to reflect 
the true environmental impact of the materials chosen for construction. These values are 
typically based on industry-wide averages, which do not account for variations in production 
processes, supply chains, or regional differences. For instance, two manufacturers 
producing the same material might have vastly different environmental footprints. One 
manufacturer may rely on renewable energy sources in production, while another might use 
fossil fuels. Similarly, the transportation distances for materials could vary widely, depending 
on the location of the material source, which would lead to differences in associated 
emissions. By relying on generic data, these critical variations are often ignored, which can 
lead to significant discrepancies in the results of an LCA. This, in turn, can result in either 
underestimations or overestimations of the actual environmental impact of the urban 
district, potentially compromising the effectiveness of sustainability efforts. 
 
 
By selecting a specific EPD, it is possible to make more informed decisions about material 
selection, ensuring that the chosen materials align with the district's sustainability goals. 
This approach also identifies materials with lower embodied carbon or reduced resource 
consumption, facilitating more sustainable choices during the planning and design phases. 
It also encourages manufacturers to develop more sustainable materials with a third-party 
verified EPD. 
 
Using specific EPDs also fosters greater transparency and accountability throughout the 
LCA process. Unlike generic data, which can often be opaque or subject to interpretation, 
specific EPDs are based on accurate, verifiable data, making the assessment more reliable 
and trustworthy. This transparency allows for a clearer understanding of where the 
environmental impacts are coming from and how they are distributed across a material's 
lifecycle. 
==PAGE:57==
 
57 
 
 
Using specific EPDs encourages a more systematic and scientifically rigorous approach to 
sustainability assessment. It promotes a detailed, comprehensive understanding of the 
environmental trade-offs between different materials, helping to identify the most 
sustainable options and areas where further improvements can be made. This level of 
granularity in data ensures that every choice made during the planning, design, and 
construction phases is supported by evidence, reducing the likelihood of poor decision-
making that could compromise the district's overall sustainability performance. 
 
 
Takeaways from materials: 
• 
Materials with an EPD are more accurate and reliable. 
• 
Project-specific data on materials help identify high CO2 pitfalls. 
• 
Incentive for product manufacturers to make better sustainable products.

3.5 Partial Conclusion 
The case study analyses four attention points of the Mastertool, drawing on numerical 
examples from the Fælledby project to address critical challenges in the LCA methodology 
and explore optimisation strategies. This analysis establishes a foundation to answer the 
following two sub-questions. 
 
 
3. What are the challenges in the Mastertool’s method of calculating LCA? 
4. What key considerations are important when optimising the calculation method? 
 
 
The current tool faces several challenges, as highlighted by the four attention points in the 
case study. Including buildings’ LCA introduces a factor of 10 difference compared to the 
LCA for the urban area, which can potentially deprioritise the sustainability of urban district 
elements. Another challenge lies in the limited inclusion of infrastructure, resulting in 
incomplete assessments where essential infrastructure elements are often omitted. 
Additionally, site development and external infrastructure required to support the urban area 
are not incorporated in the tool, leading to a potentially understated CO₂ footprint.  
 
While the tool does account for material lifespan, in a perfect world, the tool should go 
beyond mere durability to include considerations of future societal needs, technological 
advancements, and evolving sustainability standards. A final issue raised in the case study 
is the reliance on outdated generic values for materials sourced from a 2012 German 
database that no longer meets current standards. Consequently, projects using these 
generic values may not gain an accurate representation of the CO₂ impact of their urban 
areas, leading to assessments that do not truly reflect the environmental footprint. 
 
The tool is outdated and lacks sufficient detail. LCA was still a relatively new concept when 
it was initially developed, necessitating a user-friendly approach. However, the industry now 
requires a tool that allows users to make project-specific modifications. 
 
The current tool's area types are predefined, and it is impossible to alter their composition—
for example, the specific road construction. The four attention points of concern all point to 
the need for a greater detail and flexibility tool, enabling users to adjust underlying data as 
needed. 
==PAGE:67==
 
67 
 
 
Based on the challenges described, it can be concluded that the Mastertool is unsuitable for 
conducting LCA in urban districts. To improve its accuracy and relevance, a future tool 
should be refined to address the following considerations: 
 
1. Exclusion of Building LCAs from Urban Area LCA Calculations 
 
Building LCAs can completely overshadow the LCA's impact on urban areas, so they 
should be excluded from the urban area LCA calculation to ensure a focus on urban 
district sustainability. 
2. Comprehensive Inclusion of Infrastructure 
 
Ideally, the tool should account for indirect CO₂ impacts from surrounding 
infrastructure to support the area and include provisions for sustainable mobility, 
such as public transport and cycling networks. These factors align with the 
"mobility" criterion in the Manual, enhancing urban connectivity and sustainability 
evaluation. 
3. Rethinking the lifespan  
Lifespan is more than just a material's durability; it is also reflected by other factors 
like social needs, technological advancements, and evolving sustainability 
standards. 
4. Elimination of Generic Material Values 
 
Using generic material values from the Mastertool database leads to inaccurate 
results. While a future tool should still offer these values, as EPDs are yet to be 
available for all materials, they remain valuable, especially in the early design phases 
when material choices still need to be finalised. These values help highlight general 
differences in climate impact between materials. However, the data for generic 
materials must be updated to reflect current materials accurately. The generic 
values should remain conservative, representing the worst-performing segment of 
available EPDs, thus incentivising EPDs and encouraging the industry to develop 
more of them. 
 
Addressing these LCA challenges and refining methodologies for urban applications will 
significantly enhance the development of sustainable urban environments like Fælledby, 
ensuring that assessments reflect the actual environmental impact of all urban elements.

4.1 Conclusion 
As urbanisation increases, the demand for new districts grows, driven by population growth 
and climate change pressures. The construction industry, responsible for nearly 40% of 
global energy use and over 30% of carbon emissions, must adopt sustainability to address 
these challenges. Regulations and performance-based certifications in Denmark encourage 
sustainable construction beyond the minimum requirements. The integrated design 
emphasises sustainability from the outset and shows that sustainable buildings can be 
achieved without significantly raising costs. 
 
LCA is a good tool for reducing the CO₂ impact of urban districts, as it provides insights into 
how each material and type affects the overall consumption. It assesses the environmental 
impact across various stages, from production to end-of-life and potential recycling. The 
results reveal several climate impacts, with GWP being a primary focus of this project.  
 
Findings from the Fælledby case study indicate that incorporating LCAs on an urban district 
scale can inadvertently deprioritise district-level sustainability considerations in favour of 
building impacts. This is because buildings often dominate the environmental footprint, 
typically accounting for ten times the CO₂ impact of outdoor spaces. For instance, in 
Fælledby, the outdoor areas are 3.5 times the size of the building footprint, yet the CO₂ 
impact of the buildings is ten times greater than that of the outdoor spaces. This is the case 
even though most urban districts seeking certification ensure their buildings are individually 
certified. 
To address this disparity, separating building LCAs from urban district LCAs in urban area 
certification processes would shift the focus towards the environmental impact of urban 
areas themselves. This distinction ensures that the sustainability of outdoor spaces and 
urban infrastructure is appropriately evaluated and prioritised rather than being 
overshadowed by the environmental impact of buildings. 
 
Infrastructure plays a pivotal role in achieving representative and comprehensive LCA 
results. However, this aspect is largely overlooked in the current methodology of the 
Mastertool. Greater attention to infrastructure is essential for improving the tool's accuracy 
and relevance. Inspiration can be drawn from more robust models, such as the InfraLCA tool, 
which offers a comprehensive approach to assessing infrastructure impacts. 
 
==PAGE:71==
 
71 
 
Moreover, site preparation activities below ground level, which significantly contribute to 
environmental impact, must be better integrated into future LCA tools. Additionally, the 
supporting infrastructure surrounding a new district - such as roads, utilities, and 
connections to public transport - should be considered, even at a preliminary level. 
 
Lifespan is influenced by more than just a material’s durability. It also depends on changing 
social needs, technological advancements, and sustainability standards. As society’s 
preferences shift, materials may become more or less relevant, while new technologies can 
improve or replace them. 
 
An important area of focus is using EPD values specific to materials. Fælledby's initial 
analysis relied on generic values derived from an outdated 2012 German source. However, 
replacing these generic values with best-in-class EPDs for the same material types and 
structures dramatically improved, reducing the total CO₂ impact by nearly 90%.

What are the challenges in the current method of calculating LCA for urban districts, and

1. Why is LCA important in fighting climate change? 
2. How can current digital tools be used to calculate LCA? 
 
 
Integrating sustainability into urban development through frameworks like DGNB 
certification and LCA helps quantify and reduce urbanisation’s environmental impacts. LCA 
is crucial for combating climate change, offering insights across a project’s lifecycle to 
identify practices that reduce carbon emissions, energy use, and resource depletion. Digital 
tools, such as the DGNB Mastertool, streamline LCA calculations by automating data input, 
using standardised benchmarks, enabling project-specific adjustments, and facilitating the 
early implementation of sustainable measures. 
 
 
3. What are the challenges in the Mastertool’s method of calculating LCA? 
4. What key considerations are important when optimising the calculation method? 
 
 
The current Mastertool has notable challenges, as shown in the case study's four attention 
points, including buildings’ LCA, which results in a factor of 10 difference with urban areas, 
potentially deprioritising district-level sustainability. The limited infrastructure and external 
site development inclusion also omits essential elements, leading to an understated CO₂ 
footprint. Outdated material values from a 2012 database further distort results, failing to 
reflect environmental impacts accurately. 
 
Optimising the LCA method requires accuracy, transparency, and adaptability. Accurate 
assessments depend on updated generic values, EPD-specific data, and building and urban 
LCA transparency. Adaptability is also crucial since the current tool is outdated and lacks the

challenges will enhance LCA methodologies for sustainable urban developments like

Throughout the project, various limitations emerged, as outlined in the delimitation section. 
The case study centred on the entire Fælledby area but excluded LCA for buildings, private 
spaces, and the natural regions within public spaces. While additional parameters, such as 
infrastructure, would have been valuable to analyse, Fælledby did not include this impact in 
its LCA, resulting in a lack of comparison data for site development and surrounding 
infrastructure. 
 
As Fælledby is already under construction, with the first phase scheduled for completion in 
2025, the case study’s scope was confined mainly to available areas. This restriction turned 
the analysis into a desk-based exercise to test the Mastertool’s limitations and examine the 
parameters it could optimise. Implementing LCA improvements from the early design phase 
would have allowed for a more substantial influence on project planning and cost-efficiency, 
highlighting the potential benefits of early integration. 
 
Several relevant discussion points have emerged, including the “functional unit” parameter.  
The functional unit that has traditionally been, and still is, the most used is CO₂-eq/m²/year. 
However, a drawback of this unit is that some urban areas might choose to expand their 
project boundaries by incorporating nearby natural areas - often protected or off-limits for 
construction - to divide the total CO₂ impact across a larger area, thereby achieving a lower 
value. An alternative functional unit could be CO₂-q/person/year, which theoretically seems 
more equitable but is challenging to implement in practice. This approach also opens the 
door to loopholes, such as constructing public buildings theoretically designed to serve a 
certain number of people but failing to meet this capacity in practice. This allows the CO₂ 
impact to be divided among more people, artificially lowering the calculated result. 
Regardless of the chosen functional unit, there will always be potential loopholes. A related 
concern is that developers might undertake additional construction after the LCA 
calculations are completed, meaning these subsequent developments are not included in 
the final CO₂ accounting for the urban district. 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:77==
 
77 
 
The case study also addresses lifespan considerations other than the material's durability. 
Extending the lifespan of materials used in infrastructure can be positive, aligning with their 
typically more extended durability compared to buildings. However, dividing emissions by a 
more extended lifespan might reduce the apparent CO₂ impact, creating an unrealistic 
impression of sustainability. 
 
Another topic for discussion is whether all LCA phases are necessary for infrastructure 
elements. While all phases are essential for buildings due to their operational energy needs, 
infrastructure components like asphalt roads do not necessarily have ongoing energy 
demands. One could argue that snow removal on roads and sweeping autumn leaves from 
bike paths using machinery fall under operations or maintenance. However, the energy 
consumption required for these tasks is likely significantly lower than the operational energy 
demands of a building. Therefore, focusing solely on “upfront” carbon emissions (phase A) 
for infrastructure to reflect its true environmental impact may be more relevant. 
 
LCAs for urban districts currently cover limited infrastructure, focusing primarily on roads 
while omitting indirect impacts associated with the broader network. For example, large-
scale projects like Fælledby exclude the CO₂ footprint of connecting infrastructure. Future 
assessments should integrate such elements to provide a realistic view of urban 
sustainability. 
 
The findings raise a fundamental question about sustainable urban planning: Are we asking 
the right questions? Instead of merely seeking the most sustainable material for a road, it 
may be more effective to question the necessity of building the road. Emphasising 
alternatives like cycling infrastructure and public transit could encourage greener transport 
habits and help shape a sustainable future. 
 
Finally, LCA optimisation should consider social and economic factors beyond 
environmental metrics. For instance, while asphalt may have a higher CO₂ footprint, it could 
significantly reduce road noise compared to a gravel road, contributing to social well-being. 
An overemphasis on environmental metrics alone risks creating unsustainable urban spaces 
in practice, ultimately failing to meet the needs of their users.

materials are provided in 1-ton units, and units are converted below for all three materials. 
 
Slag aggregate

2. Mass of slag aggregate (average density of 2.650 kg/m3 stated in the EPD)

3. CO2 impact 
CO2 impact = 0,1325 tons * (-9,2 kg CO2-eq/ton) = -1,219 kg CO2-eq/m2 
 
Bitumen
