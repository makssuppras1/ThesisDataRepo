Summary 
Fucoidans are complex, sulphated fucose-rich polysaccharides, that are mainly found in brown 
macroalgae. Different fucoidan backbone structures are observed in different types of 
macroalgae. Fucoidans are generally regarded as heterogenic, as different species of 
macroalgae produce different fucoidan structures. Additionally, the fine structure of fucoidans 
depend on many factors, such as the season of harvest and the extraction method. Different 
structures of fucoidan molecules have been shown to exhibit different bioactive properties. To 
be able to make use of these bioactivities in e.g., biomedicine, the fucoidan must be 
depolymerized to obtain homogenous fucoidan products.  
Fucoidanases are a relatively unexplored type of enzyme, that are capable of depolymerizing 
fucoidan molecules in a controlled manner, achieving homogenous, low molecular weight 
fucoidan products.  
In this study one fucoidanase, Fim28, has been analyzed and the characterization process 
initiated. Fim28 originates from a metagenome from an enrichment in an Icelandic hot spring. 
The characterization of Fim28 was carried out on fucoidan extracted from the brown 
macroalgae Laminaria digitata. Fim28 has optimal activity at pH 6-7.4, 35-40 °C, and at 700-900 
mM NaCl. Fim28 furthermore exhibits a dependency of NaCl, as no significant activity is 
observed at NaCl concentrations below 100 mM. The halophilic characteristic of Fim28 is a 
unique characteristic, that no other currently published fucoidanase exhibit.

1. Introduction 
Sustainable and bio-based products are of increasing interest and demand, especially regarding 
the discovery of renewable resources capable of fulfilling needs in food, feed, and medicines. 
One renewable resource, that is available in abundance, is macroalgae. Even though 
macroalgae have been used as food, feed, and dietary supplements, the full potential is yet to 
be explored [1]. In this study, the potential use of macroalgae for pharmaceuticals is in focus. Of 
particular interest is the sulphated polysaccharide, fucoidan, found primarily in brown 
macroalgae, as it has been shown to possess interesting bioactivities [2]. The work carried out 
in this study, is part of the BlueBio project, MARIKAT.  
 
1.1 Macroalgae 
Macroalgae, or seaweed, has been used as food, flavoring agents, dietary supplements and 
feed for many years [1]. Macroalgae have gained increasing attention in recent years, as their 
fast growth rates and large biomass yields, make them a great alternative renewable feedstock 
[3]. Macroalgae do not require any terrestrial land for cultivation, which in the perspective of 
food versus feed areas on land, makes their cultivation favorable [3]. Another very important 
factor to consider regarding macroalgae cultivation, is the opportunities the cultivation offers 
concerning mitigation and adaptation to climate change. Macroalgae consume carbon dioxide, 
nitrogen and nutrients in their local environment, which mitigates greenhouse gas emissions, as 
well as ocean acidification and eutrophication [4]. Eutrophic ocean areas, which can occur as a 
result of warming, are particularly prone to hypoxia. To combat this, cultivation of macroalgae is 
advantageous. Macroalgae are autotrophs, and release oxygen through their photosynthesis, 
however they consume oxygen when they are remineralized. Cultivation of macroalgae 
prevents this, as the biomass is harvested, and thereby removed prior to the remineralization 
[4]. The harvested macroalgae biomass has several possible uses, as they contain both high 
value components, e.g., polysaccharides and bioactive molecules, and compounds that are 
considered platform chemicals for the bio-based economy [3].  
Generally, macroalgae are divided into three categories, namely brown, green and red, which 
are three independently evolved linages of macroalgae [5]. Currently, only 10 species of 
macroalgae are extensively cultivated, out of nearly 300 species of interest [1]. Of these 
cultivated macroalgae, brown and green macroalgae are predominantly used in food, where 
they are a source of protein, fiber and minerals. Cultivated red macroalgae are used as food, 
and as a source of agars and carrageenan used in e.g., food and cosmetics [1].  
One particular macroalgae of interest in this study, is the brown macroalgae Laminaria digitata.  
  
1.1.1 Laminaria digitata 
Laminaria digitata is one of the largest growing species of brown macroalgae within the brown 
taxonomic group [3]. L. digitata is native to Ireland but is cultivated and harvested globally, 
where it for instance is used in the alginate industry [1].  
A high value carbohydrate of special interest in this study is fucoidan. The fucoidan used for the 
majority of the characterization assays in this study was purified from the brown macroalgae L. 
digitata. Bruhn et al. (2017) [6] found that the concentration of crude fucoidan in L. digitata 
ranged from approximately 2.8% to 7.4% of the dry matter, depending on the time of year the 
harvest was carried out.

1.2 Fucoidans 
Fucoidans are a family of complex, heterogenous, sulphated fucose-rich polysaccharides 
commonly produced in brown marine macroalgae, and certain marine invertebrates (such as 
sea cucumbers) [6, 7, 8]. Various bioactivities, of pharmaceutical interest, has been suggested, 
which has increased the interest in studying fucoidan in recent years [3, 6].  
 
1.2.1 Occurrence of fucoidans 
The main components of the brown macroalgae cell wall, are anionic polysaccharides, more 
specifically alginates and fucoidans (Figure 1) [1].

Alginate is partly responsible for the flexibility of the macroalgae, meaning that species growing 
in turbulent areas usually have a higher alginate content [1]. While fucoidans appear to stabilize 
and strengthen the cell wall by crosslinking between matrix cellulose microfibrils [6]. 
Correlations between the mechanical stress, osmotic stress, and fucoidan content in the cell 
wall of brown macroalgae has been discovered. For macroalgae experiencing higher levels of 
mechanical stress, e.g., residing in turbulent areas, a lower tissue content of fucoidan has been 
observed compared to that of the same species residing in protected areas. Regarding osmotic 
stress, it has been shown that the same species of macroalgae contains a higher level of 
fucoidan when in higher salinity [6]. 
Additionally, the location and season of harvest, as well as the extraction method, has been 
observed to affect the fucoidan structure [9, 10].   
 
1.2.2 Extraction of fucoidan 
Several methods for the extraction of fucoidan have been developed. The oldest known 
fucoidan extraction method is acid extraction [8]. Here, fucoidan is solubilized in aqueous acid 
at high temperature, followed by precipitation with ethanol. The principle of this type of 
extraction is based on the fact that fucoidan is soluble in polar liquids at low pH and high

temperature, and insoluble in less polar solvents [8]. However, this is a time-consuming process 
with low efficiency, that may affect the fine structure of the fucoidan polymers [11].  
A the main goal of extracting fucoidan is to extract the polymers as intact as possible, since 
changes in the fine structure may have adverse effects on the bioactivity exhibited from the 
fucoidan; using an acid-based extraction method is hence unsuitable [11].  
A recently developed, and greener, fucoidan extraction method is an enzymatic extraction. The 
process of which basically consists of enzymatically degrading the surrounding cell wall 
polysaccharides, removing the residual seaweed material followed by precipitation of leftover 
alginate with CaCl2, and then precipitating the crude fucoidan with alcohol [8, 11]. After the 
crude fucoidan has been extracted, an ion-exchange chromatography-based fractionation is 
required. The fractionation separates the non-fucoidan components from the fucoidans and 
divides the different fucoidans according to size and charge. This results in more homogenous 
fucoidan substrates in terms of chain length and fine structures, that possibly possess similar 
bioactivities [8].  
 
1.2.3 Structure of fucoidans  
Fucoidan molecules are described as being heterogenic, with a backbone primarily comprising 
of glycosidically linked α-L-fucose, alongside other sugars (often in minor levels), and with 
varying levels of branching [12]. Typically, the α-L-fucose units in fucoidan polymers are mono- 
or disulphated, and may be acetylated, these groups are responsible for the anionic 
characteristics of fucoidan [13]. 
Fucoidan originating from macroalgae is commonly branched, more sulphated, and 
encompassing several sugar monomers apart from α-L-fucose, such as β-D-galactose, β-D-
mannose and α-D-glucose [13]. Fucoidan from marine invertebrates (such as sea cucumbers) 
are less complex, mainly consisting of linear and regular chains of repeating α-L-fucose units 
[13].  
Currently, no generally accepted fucoidan nomenclature exist, however Zvyagintseva et al. 
(2021) [10], suggests dividing fucoidans into 5 different groups depending on their structure, 
more specifically their monosaccharide composition and the type of main chain. The 5 groups 
suggested are: (1) α-(1,3)-L-fucans, (2) α-(1,3)/α-(1,4)-L-fucans, (3) galactofucans, (4) 
fucoglucuronomannans, and (5) more complex heteropolysaccharides [10]. However, 
Zvyagintseva et al. (2021) [10], disclaims that due to the heterogeneity of fucoidan, some 
fucoidans may not be dividable by this classification system. Macroalgae within the same 
genus, and even species, might share several characteristics, but the structure of fucoidans is 
neither genus-specific nor species-specific [10]. Additionally, mixtures of different classes of 
fucoidan oligomers may be found in the same organism extracts or fractions, once again 
highlighting the high heterogenic properties of fucoidans [14].  
For the work carried out in this study, groups (1), (2) and (3) are the most relevant. Group (1) 
fucoidans (Figure 2 A), consisting of α-L-fucans with a main chain of (1,3)-linked residues, are 
mostly synthesized by algae of the Order Laminariales (e.g., Genera Laminaria, Ecklonia, 
Alaria, and Saccharina).  Group (2) fucoidans (Figure 2 B), consisting of α-L-fucans with a main 
chain of alternating (1,3)-linked and (1,4)-linked residues, are mostly synthesized from the algae 
of Order Fucales (e.g., Genera Fucus, Sargassum, Turbinaria, and Ascophyllum).

Group (3) fucoidans, galactofucans, consists of fucose, galactose residues and sulphate. 
Members of the Laminariales and Fucales Orders most often synthesize galactofucans, and to 
a lesser extent α-L-fucans [10]. Galactofucans have an extremely diverse structure, e.g., in the 
ratio of fucose and galactose residues, branches, sulphation degree and positions. The main 
chain of many known galactofucans consists of α-(1,3)-linked fucose residues, where galactose 
residues can be included not only in the branches, but also in the main chain [10]. Future 
studies of the seasonal variations and structural differences of galactofucans are required to 
elucidate structure-activity relationships [14].   
 
The fucoidan from L. digitata has thus far not been fully characterized, however the backbone 
has been found to consist of α-(1,3)-linked fucose residues, which is in agreeance with fucoidan 
structures synthesized by other species of macroalgae within the Laminariales Order [1,15]. 
Furthermore, the monosaccharide profile of L. digitata fucoidan has been analyzed by Kostas et 
al. (2017) [3], where it was found to contain approximately 42.5% fucose, 30.9% glucose, 17% 
galactose, 4.6% arabinose, and 2.6% xylose. The fine structure of L. digitata fucoidan is 
currently not known and will have to be elucidated at a later point in time 
 
1.2.4 Bioactivity of fucoidans and their future use  
Fucoidan-containing products that currently are commercially available, include dietary 
supplements, food additives, and cosmetology products [10]. Fucoidans are practically non-
toxic and have been shown to possess a range of bioactive properties, making them very 
promising for future use in pharmaceutical, food, and cosmetic applications [8, 10]. 
Consequently, fucoidans have the potential to have a significant and economic impact [8].

Fucoidans are currently relatively unexploited as a biomedical source, because of their high 
heterogeneity, viscosity, and their size, that is too large to be applied as a drug as is [7, 15]. The 
characterization of the complex fucoidan polymers is a requirement for their future application in 
e.g., biomedicines, but the process is intricate. The bioactive properties of fucoidans are not 
only based on the degree of sulphation, the charge density, and the size of the fucoidan 
molecule, but also other fine chemical features [2, 13]. Consequently, the characterization of the 
complex fucoidan polymers is considered a bottleneck [13]. Accordingly, a need for smaller, 
depolymerized, homogenous fucoidan molecules arise, to investigate fucoidan for the possible 
use in biomedicines, as well as enlightening the fine chemical structure of fucoidan from 
different sources. Several methods exist, that possibly could depolymerize fucoidan, an 
example of this could be via acid treatment. However, with acid treatment random 
hydrolyzations will occur, resulting in an uncontrolled depolymerization and thereby 
heterogeneity. Furthermore, acid-hydrolysis of fucoidans is suspected to cause a loss of 
sulphates, which is unfavorable, as sulphation of the fucoidan molecules is a major factor in the 
various biological activities they exhibit [15, 16]. 
A more ideal method, that enables a controlled and site-specific depolymerization of the 
fucoidan polymer is enzymatic degradation. Fucoidan-degrading enzymes, fucoidanases, can 
be applied for the production of chemically defined bioactive and homogenous 
oligosaccharides, as they catalyze hydrolyzation of specific linkages in the fucoidan polymers 
[8, 13]. Fucoidanases additionally preserve the sulphation pattern of fucoidans [15]. This makes 
fucoidanases very promising tools for production of bioactive fucoidan oligosaccharides for the 
application in biomedicines [17].  
Examples of currently published bioactivities exhibited from fucoidans include antitumor, anti-
inflammatory, anticoagulant and antivirus abilities [2].  
 
Silchenko et al. (2017) [18] investigated the antitumor activity of fucoidans extracted from 
Sargassum horneri. Here, they depolymerized the native S. horneri fucoidans with the 
fucoidanase, FFA1 (GenBank accession number: WP_057784217.1), obtaining low molecular 
weight (LMW) fucoidans. They concluded, that fucoidan polysaccharides showed anticancer 
properties, while LMW fucoidans (with a polymerization degree of 4-10) were non-effective on 
the inhibition of cancer cells colony formation [18]. Effectively showing how different structures 
of fucoidans, from the same origin, may exhibit different bioactivities.  
 
Ohmes et al. (2020) [19] investigated the effect of enzymatically extracted high molecular 
weight (HMW) fucoidans from Fucus evanescens on angiogenesis (formation of new blood 
vessels from existing ones). Angiogenesis is an essential process for successful bone 
regeneration, but also a key factor in the development of bone-related disorders, such as bone 
tumor formation and inflammation [19]. The HMW fucoidans exhibited anti-angiogenesis 
properties. Later, Ohmes et al. (2022) [20] investigated the possible change in bioactivity when 
the HMW F. evanescens fucoidans were depolymerized with the fucoidanase Fhf1 (GenBank 
accession number: WP_066217780.1). The depolymerization yielded both medium and low 
molecular weight products (MMW and LMW). The MMW and LMW fucoidans were observed to 
have lost their anti-angiogenic properties, which indicates that enzymatic processing holds the 
potential to tailor the bioactivity of fucoidan extracts to effects on angiogenic processes [20].

1.3 Fucoidanases  
Fucoidan-depolymerizing enzymes, fucoidanases, have been found in both marine 
invertebrates and fungi, but are mainly found in marine bacteria, where they are produced 
extracellularly [9, 21]. 
Based on their activity, fucoidanases can be divided into two groups, exo-acting and endo-
acting. Exo-acting fucoidanases are capable of cleaving fucoidans from their non-reducing end. 
Endo-acting fucoidanases, which are the most common type of fucoidanase, are capable of 
cleaving glycoside bonds within a molecule of fucoidan and produce fucoidan oligosaccharides 
with different degrees of polymerization [15]. Endo-acting fucoidanases are classified with two 
different EC numbers: 3.2.1.211 and 3.2.1.212, based on their activity. EC 3.2.1.211 are the 
endo-α-1,4-L-fucanases, and EC 3.2.1.212 are the endo-α-1,3-L-fucanases.  
Fucoidanases are defined in two glycoside hydrolase families in the Carbohydrate Active 
Enzymes database (CAZy, http://www.cazy.org/) [22] namely GH107 and GH168, the two 
families do not share any sequence similarity [9]. 
Currently, 34 fucoidanases are found in the GH107 family, of which 12 are characterized. The 
currently characterized endo-α-(1,4)-L-fucanases within the GH107 family, are Fhf1, FFA2 
(GenBank accession number: WP_057784219.1), Fhf2 (GenBank accession number: 
WP_066217784.1), MfFcnA (GenBank accession number: CAI47003.1), Fp273 (GenBank 
accession number: AYC81238.1), Fp277 (GenBank accession number: AYC81239.1), Fp279 
(GenBank accession number: AYC81240.1), FWf1 (GenBank accession number: 
ANW96097.1), and FWf2 (GenBank accession number: ANW96098.1). The only endo-α-(1,3)-
L-fucanase that currently is characterized in the GH107 family is Mef2 (GenBank accession 
number: WP_055392200.1). Two additional fucoidanases are characterized in the GH107 
family, however these have not had their activity specified, these are P5AFcnA (GenBank 
accession number: AYF59291.1) and P19DFcnA (GenBank accession number: AYF59292.1). 
In the GH168 family, 47 fucoidanases are found, of which only one have been characterized, 
namely the endo-α-(1,3)-L-fucoidanase, FunA (GenBank accession number: ANW96599.1).  
 
The fucoidanase, from this study, belongs to the GH107 family. The sequence and structural 
work was carried out in the context of other GH107 fucoidanases. The catalytic machinery of 
the fucoidanases, is conserved throughout the GH107 family, where the structure of the 
catalytic domain, denominated the D1 domain, has been found to consist of a (β/α)8-barrel [12, 
17]. The structure furthermore reveals active sites that are contoured as grooves on the surface 
of the enzyme, which are consistent with recognition and internal cleavage on fucoidan chains 
[12]. Within the D1 domain of fucoidanases, only a few amino acids have been found to be 
conserved. These include the proposed catalytic amino acids, aspartic acid (D) and histidine 
(H), and four additional residues found in the -1 subsite [12]. The hydrolytic mechanism of the 
proposed catalytic amino acids is consistent with the sidechain of D acting as the nucleophile, 
while the sidechain of H acts as the acid/base [12]. The -1 subsite in the D1 domain likely 
constitutes a basic pocket, that can accommodate and complement the charge of the fucoidan 
molecule [12]. The sulphation pattern of fucoidan may play an important role in its recognition 
by the fucoidanase’s -1 subsite [12].  
Structural data point towards the non-conserved subsites, outside of the primary -1 subsite, as 
possible key contributors to determining the substrate specificity of the fucoidanase, as this lack 
of conservation likely reflects the different subsite architectures that allow the fucoidanases to 
recognize different fucoidan structures [12]. Additionally, substrate specificity of fucoidanases 
have been directly linked to the size and structure of their active site [23].

This implies that different fucoidanases exhibit different fucoidan substrate specificities, likely 
resulting in different fucoidan oligosaccharides. Different fucoidan oligosaccharides may exhibit 
different bioactivities, which is of special interest.  
An additional characteristic of fucoidanases within the GH107 is their possible calcium-
dependency, as Ca2+ binding sites have been found within the D1 domain of several 
fucoidanases, which likely plays a role in their catalytic activity [17, 12].  
 
Discovery and characterization of new fucoidanases are a necessity required for the deeper 
understanding of the function of fucoidanases and their catalytic domain [9]. However, no 
simple and sensitive quantitative method for the detection of the catalytic activity of 
fucoidanases exist, making this process slow and complex [15]. Although, only a few 
fucoidanases have been characterized to date, fucoidanases are believed to be used in the 
biomedical industry for the production of fucoidan oligosaccharides in the near future [15].  
 
1.3.1 Fucoidanase Fim28 
The fucoidanase characterized in this study, by the name Fim28, originates from an Icelandic 
metagenome from a geothermal hot spring, namely Skarðshver, where temperatures range 
from at least 14 °C to 34 °C, and the salinity is in the vicinity of 34.5 ppt, which is approximately 
590 mM NaCl [24, 25]. Four enrichments consisting of Fucus vesiculosus were placed in 
Skarðshver, which later had one metagenome prepared for each of the enrichments.  
Subsequently, a screening of the metagenomes predicted 10 putative fucoidanase genes. After 
initial characterization assays, the 10 putative fucoidanase genes were narrowed down to one, 
namely Fim28. The name “Fim28” stems from an abbreviation of Fucoidanase Icelandic 
Metagenome, while the number “28” stems from the plasmid number of the received plasmid 
from Iceland.

2.2 Fucoidan recovery  
The fucoidan used in this study was either purified from macroalgae in-house or purchased from 
either Glycomix (Berkshire, England) or Sigma-Aldrich (Steinheim, Germany) (Table 3). In either 
case the fucoidan was procured prior to the start of this study. Each type of fucoidan was given 
an abbreviation specifying the source (Table 3). The method used to recovery and purify the 
fucoidan from L. digitata was an enzyme-assisted purification method, which is described in 
Nguyen et al. (2020) [11]. The enzyme-assisted purification method consisted of drying and 
grinding the seaweed to produce a powder, which then was enzymatically treated with cellulase 
and alginate lyase. Following this, the alginate was precipitated with CaCl2, and removed via 
centrifugation. The crude fucoidan, present in the supernatant, was precipitated with ethanol 
and recovered as the pellet via centrifugation. Subsequently, the crude fucoidan was

2.5 Fucoidanase recovery and purification  
The purification process was initiated by thawing the harvested biomass on ice and 
resuspending it in cold extraction buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, 0.2 
mg/mL Lysozyme from chicken egg white (Sigma-Aldrich), pH 7.4). Generally 3 mL of extraction 
buffer was added per gram of harvested biomass. The thawed and resuspended biomass was 
sonicated, to lyse the cells, on ice using a UP 400S Ultrasonic processer (Hielscher Ultrasonics, 
Teltow, Germany) equipped with a Sonotrode H3 (Hielscher Ultrasonics) for a total of 8 minutes 
pulse, 10 seconds on, 25 seconds off, at 25% amplitude.  
Post-sonication, the crude solution was centrifuged, to precipitate cells and cell debris, at 
19.000 x g and 4 °C for 15 minutes.  The centrifuged crude enzyme solution was filtered 
through a 0.45 µm filter onto an appropriate amount of pre-equilibrated HisPur™ Ni-NTA Resin 
(Thermo Fisher). The resin was pre-equilibrated in a buffer consisting of 20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 
mM NaCl, pH 7.4. The crude enzyme in resin was incubated on ice, with slow agitation, for a 
minimum of one hour.  
After the incubation the resin was washed by centrifuging the resin for 2 minutes at 2000 rpm 
and 4 °C, discarding the supernatant and resuspending the resin in 10 mL cold wash buffer (20 
mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, pH 7.4) and incubating on ice with slow agitation 
for 10 minutes. The wash was performed twice, to minimize the impurities present in the eluted 
enzyme. Following the second wash, the resin (suspended in wash buffer) was transferred to an 
empty column. In order to ensure the enzyme was kept cold, this was performed either in a 
fridge or cold room (approximately 5 °C). The enzyme was eluted using five different elution 
buffers with increasing concentrations of imidazole (20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, 100-300 
mM imidazole, pH 7.4).

2.5.1 Bradford assay 
In order to determine the protein concentration in the elutions from the purification, a Bradford 
assay was performed using bovine serum albumin (BSA) as the standard [28]. The BSA 
standard was prepared by mixing 190 µL Bradford reagent with 10 µL of each BSA solutions at 
seven different protein concentrations (0, 0.125, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1 and 2 mg/mL). 
To determine the protein concentration of the different protein elutions, 190 µL Bradford reagent 
was mixed with 2-10 µL of the elution of interest. The actual volume of protein elution that was 
added to the Bradford reagent, was determined by the color change of the reagent, which must 
be within the color range exhibited in the BSA standard. The protein and Bradford reagent 
mixes were incubated at room temperature for approximately 10 minutes to ensure full color 
change, before measuring the absorbance at 595 nm. From this data the protein concentration 
in each of the elutions was determined. Where possible, the enzyme elutions were diluted with 
wash buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, pH 7.4) to reach a final 
concentration of 1 mg/mL, as precipitation has been observed when freezing higher protein 
concentrations. Following the dilution, the enzyme elutions were placed at -80 °C until use.

3. Results  
3.1 Construct design 
The fucoidanase, Fim28, which is analyzed in this study stems from a metagenomic analysis of 
an enrichment placed in a hot spring called Skarðshver in Iceland. A total of four enrichments 
were placed in the hot spring, the enrichments were named SH1L, SH2L, SH1S, and SH2S, 
which describes Skarðshver, the number of enrichment, and the size of the enrichment 
(small/large). Fim28 stems from the metagenome of the enrichment SH2L. 
In order to predict putative fucoidanases, the metagenomes of the four enrichments were 
analyzed by the dbCAN server (https://bcb.unl.edu/dbCAN/). dbCAN is a web resource that is 
capable of automatically annotating carbohydrate-active enzyme signature domains in any 
given protein dataset [31]. From this analysis 10 putative fucoidanases were predicted, one of 
which was chosen to be studied in this project, namely Fim28. A construct containing the fim28 
gene was prepared and inserted into a plasmid (Figure 3), prior to the beginning of this study.

The construct was designed with a 6xHis-tag in the C-terminal, enabling His-tag-based protein 
purification and size-based visualization via Western blotting. To aid in the proper folding of 
Fim28 post transcription, an MBP-Smt3 fusion was placed in the N-terminal of the fim28 
construct. MBP, a maltose-binding protein encoded by the gene malE, enhances both the 
production and solubility of the final recombinant protein [32]. Studies suggest that MBP 
furthermore acts as a “chaperone magnet”, recruiting chaperones that normally associate with 
maltose-binding proteins to the vicinity of the target protein [32]. Smt3, a ubiquitin-like protein, 
enables the removal of the fusion post transcription [33]. In the duration of this study, the MBP-
Smt3 fusion continues being fused to Fim28.  
The malE-smt3-fim28 construct was then cloned into a pHWG1106 vector (Figure 3), opened 
with the restriction enzymes NdeI and BamHI, which enables high fidelity assembly. The 
pHWG1106 vector contains an L-rhamnose inducible promoter, facilitating controlled expression 
of Fim28. The vector furthermore contains ampicillin as its resistance marker, allowing correct 
selection of transformed cells and furthermore putting selection pressure on the cells, enticing 
the cells to keep the plasmid when growing in cultures.  
 
3.2 Sequence work  
Before initiating the sequence analysis of the fim28 gene, sequencing was performed to ensure 
that the correct sequence was used. As fim28 stems from a metagenomic analysis, there was a 
possibility that the provided sequence and the actual sequence received were dissimilar, 
although the majority of the sequence likely will be identical, because of the biological variance 
between the bacteria, that provided the DNA in the metagenome 
Two different forward primers were used to complete the sequencing of fim28 (Table 1). The 
original forward primer was located too close to the beginning of fim28, resulting in low quality 
sequencing results of the very beginning of the gene (Figure 3). The new forward primer was 
designed to enable high quality sequencing of the beginning of fim28, as it is located further 
back into the malE-smt3 genes (Figure 3). 
The sequencing results were prepared for further analysis by trimming the sequences to only 
contain the part with high quality sequence, i.e., adequate nucleotide signals. Reverse primer 
sequences were furthermore transformed to their reverse compliment. Subsequently, the 
sequences were aligned with the template sequence from Iceland, using the multiple sequence 
alignment tool MUSCLE provided by EMBL-EBI. A mismatch between the sequencing results 
and the template sequence, was only accepted and adapted into the validated sequence if two 
or more sequencing results were in agreeance. The validated sequence of fim28, compared to 
the originally received fim28 sequence (Appendix 2), had a total of 61 base pair changes and 21 
amino acid changes. The validated protein sequence of Fim28, without the MBP-Smt3 fusion, 
was estimated to be approximately 42.6 kD by Benchling (https://www.benchling.com/, San 
Francisco, California, USA). However, for the experimental work performed in this study, Fim28 
was expressed with the MBP-Smt3 fusion, which resulted in an estimated size of 97.2 kD.  
Continuing the sequence work, the validated sequence was then analyzed, starting with the 
prediction of the presence of a signal peptide, as well as intermembrane regions. No signal 
peptide was predicted, and no intermembrane sections were predicted. The lack of 
intermembrane sections is ideal, as this otherwise could interfere with the expression of Fim28 
in the expression strain. Following this, a domain search using EMBL-EBI’s InterProScan tool 
was carried out. No domains or important sites were predicted using InterProScan. All 
prediction results can be seen in Appendix 3.

The catalytic domain (D1) does not automatically show up in the InterProScan analysis of the 
sequence. This can be reasoned with the fact that fucoidanases are a lesser-known type of 
enzyme, as well as the low sequence identity that has been observed between fucoidanases 
[15]. Consequently, a manual prediction of the location of the D1 domain must be performed. To 
determine this, sequences of currently known fucoidanases, along with the sequence of Fim28, 
were aligned using EMBL-EBI’s multiple sequence alignment tool, MUSCLE.  
The location of the catalytic D1 domain was predicted from the alignment, by using one of the 
two fucoidanases from Vickers et al. (2018) [12], P5aFcnA, as template, as it previously had its 
protein structure determined and analyzed.  
As a further study regarding the similarities and differences between the D1 domains of the 
currently known fucoidanases, the sequences were trimmed to only contain the D1 domains of 
the fucoidanases. Following this, the now trimmed sequences were run through an additional 
multiple sequence alignment using EMBL-EBI’s multiple sequence alignment tool, MUSCLE 
(Appendix 4). The reason for aligning the sequences twice, originates from the possibility of 
other conserved domains affecting the alignment. Although Fim28 was not predicted to contain 
other conserved domains, the other fucoidanases in the alignment may. Fhf1 is an example, as 
it contains a cadherin-like superfamily domain next to the D1 domain [9].

3.2.1 Predicted 3D structure of Fim28 
As a final part of the sequence analysis of Fim28, the three-dimensional (3D) structure of the 
validated sequence of Fim28 (without the MBP-Smt3-fusion) was predicted using AlphaFold. 
The output from AlphaFold, was 5 ranked structures, which were manually inspected for 
defects. All structures furthermore received a pLDDT value, indicating the accuracy of the 
model. A high pLDDT value indicates that the structure is accurately modelled and 
physiologically likely. This does, however, not necessarily mean that the predicted structure is 
the actual 3D structure of the protein in nature. The scale of which the confidence (pLDDT) is 
measured is on a scale from 0-100, the higher the number, the higher the confidence. 
Generally, a pLDDT score above 90 is regarded as having high accuracy in the modelling [34]. 
The highest scoring structure (ranked_0.pdb, pLDDT = 94.22) was chosen for further analysis. 
The 3D structure of ranked_0 was visualized in PyMOL (Figure 6 A). The surface charge of the 
predicted structure of Fim28 was likewise visualized (Figure 6 B).

The two catalytic amino acids, D and H, are located in a β-barrel consisting of 7 β-sheets, 
containing the active site (Figure 6 A). The D1 domain has been described as an (α/β)8-barrel, 
meaning Fim28 is predicted to contain one less β-sheet. This prediction, however, is not 
unheard of, as Vickers et al. (2018) [12], found a similar defect in the secondary structure of 
P5AFcnA. They found that the polypeptide regions corresponding to one α-helix and one β-
strand did not form regular secondary structures and were better described as random coil [12]. 
This is in agreeance with the Fim28 prediction. It is, however, important to note that the 
predicted 3D structure of Fim28 is based on alignments and similarities in the sequence 
compared to the available structures and sequences in the library of AlphaFold. The top three 
scoring proteins were P5AFcnA (RCSB accession number: 6M8N), MfFcnA4 (RCSB accession 
number: 6DLH), and MfFcnA9 (RCSB accession number: 6DNS), all three structures were 
described in Vickers et al. (2018) [12]. A list of the top 100 scoring proteins, from the output file 
pdb_hits.hhr, can be found in Appendix 5. 
Regarding the estimated surface charge (Figure 6 B), the active site is the only area in which 
the surface charge is solely positively charged. Other areas of Fim28 are almost exclusively 
negatively charged. From a biological point of view, this makes sense, as fucoidan polymers are 
negatively charged and for that reason would be repulsed from all areas of the surface of 
Fim28, except for the active site.  
 
The 3D structure of a fucoidanase, that at the time of the AlphaFold analysis, was not available, 
is that of Mef1 (which later has been determined via crystallization). Mef1 (GenBank accession 
number: WP_055392206.1) is found in the same phylogenetic cluster as Fim28 (Figure 4), 
meaning that Mef1 and Fim28 share some sequence similarities. Aligning the sequences and 
secondary structures of Fim28, Mef1, and P5AFcnA using ESPript 3.0 
(https://espript.ibcp.fr/ESPript/ESPript/, [35]) enlightens the similarities in the structures 
(Appendix 6). A general agreement of secondary structure is observed in this alignment.

3.4 Substrate specificity assay 
As an initial activity screen, to enlighten whether Fim28 possessed any fucoidan-depolymerizing 
catalytic abilities, as well as testing which linkage specificity Fim28 might have, an overnight 
substrate specificity assay was carried out. Fim28 was screened on 16 different types of 
fucoidans, from 14 different sources (Table 3) and separated on C-PAGE (Figure 11). A 
fucoidanase is active on substrates in which several oligosaccharide bands appear in the C-
PAGE gel, in contrast to intact substrate, which does not migrate in the gel, but is retained in the 
top of the gel. The substrate control is a negative control for this reaction, if the reaction and 
control of the same substrate look identical, no activity is observed.  
A standard is also added to the C-PAGE gels, enabling the estimation of the smallest 
oligosaccharide bands’ sizes. The standard is a reaction consisting of the fucoidanase Fhf1 on 
Fe fucoidans. The smallest visible band in the standard is estimated to be a tetramer (four sugar 
monomers) in size, every band above this is assumed to be 2 sugars in size larger than the 
previous one. This assumption is made because the backbone of the fucoidan from Fe consists 
of a repeating pattern of alternating α-(1,3) and α-(1,4) linkages, and Fhf1 has been found to be 
an α-(1,4) specific fucoidanase. 
Fim28 exhibited activity on fucoidans purified from: Sl, Ld, An, Er, and Lh. This suggests that 
Fim28 is likely an α-(1,3)-linkage specific fucoidanase since the backbone linkage in these 
fucoidans are believed to be primarily α-(1,3).  
One type of fucoidans, namely Ld fucoidans, was chosen for the remaining characterization 
assays. This decision was based on the purified fucoidan available in stock, where Sl and Ld 
fucoidans were available at the largest quantities.  
The choice between Sl and Ld fucoidan was based on the requirement for few, distinct low 
molecular weight fucoidan fragments produced in the depolymerization process. This is 
necessary to even consider the fucoidan oligosaccharides’ use in biomedicine, as the native 
sizes of fucoidans are too large. The fucoidan oligosaccharides must furthermore be able to be

3.7 pH assay  
With a reaction time of 30 minutes, and the reaction temperature of 35 °C, the pH assay was 
performed, in order to determine the pH optimum for reactions with Fim28. Two different pH 
buffers were used in the pH assay, in order to cover the pH values ranging from pH 4 to 11.  
As Fim28 was eluted in a Tris-HCl buffer, a buffering agent, the amount of enzyme added to the 
reaction had to be minimal as to not disturb the wanted pH value, for that reason half the normal 
volume (and thereby amount) of Fim28 was added to the reaction mixes. With half the enzyme, 
the reaction time was doubled, assuming Fim28 was operating at a reaction rate close to the 
maximal in the prior assays and will continue to do so with half the amount and double the 
reaction time, which presumably would end up reaching the same level of fucoidan 
depolymerization. This method of changing the pH in the respective pH assay reaction mixes, 
would have to do, as Fim28 was shown to not be able to have its buffer changed. Alternatively, 
Fim28 could be purified using the respective pH buffers, however this would realistically not be 
possible. The optimal binding range for proteins to the Ni-NTA resin is pH 7.2-7.8 [38]. This 
would not be able to cover the pH range desired in this assay and was therefore not carried out. 
Unfortunately, no activity was observed (data not shown). Other characterized fucoidanases 
were shown to have pH optimum at approximately pH 7-8 [9, 21], for that reason the 
characterization work on Fim28 was continued at pH 7.4.

3.8 Minimization assays  
With the aim of preparing the enzymatically digested fucoidan oligosaccharides for Nuclear 
Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, to determine the chemical structure of the low 
molecular weight fucoidan oligosaccharides, two minimization assays were carried out.  
Samples of fucoidan oligosaccharides cannot contain high concentrations of salts and buffers, if 
their structure is to be determined via NMR. If the digestion of fucoidans by fucoidanases 
cannot be performed in low salt and buffer concentrations, the sample purification will be 
difficult, as the small fucoidan oligosaccharides are of such a small size that they will be lost 
with the removal of the salts and buffer [16]. Thus far, the CaCl2 concentration in the reaction 
mixes has been 10 mM. Different concentrations from 0 mM to 10 mM of CaCl2 were tested in 
this minimization assay and visualized on a C-PAGE gel (Figure 14 A). The activity is 
significantly affected by decreasing the CaCl2 concentration, consequently the CaCl2 
concentration was kept at 10 mM for the remaining assays. The NaCl concentration used 
previously was 112.5 mM. Concentrations of NaCl ranging from 0 mM to 700 mM were tested in 
the minimization assay and visualized on a C-PAGE gel (Figure 14 B). The original minimization 
assay had NaCl concentrations ranging from 0 mM to 112.5 mM, but this however was 
increased to 700 mM NaCl, as no significant activity was observed at NaCl concentrations 
below 100 mM (Figure 14 B). Based on the NaCl minimization assay, it was observed that 
Fim28 is highly NaCl dependent. An initial optimal NaCl concentration was predicted to be 600 
mM. These results could explain the lack of activity in the previous pH assay, where the NaCl 
concentration was 56.25 mM. This could also explain the results of the CaCl2 minimization 
assay, in which the highest tested concentration (10 mM) exhibited the highest activity.

4. Discussion  
The main focus for the MARIKAT project, of which this study is part of, is to find new, robust 
fucoidanases that have the potential to be used in industrial settings. This implies that new 
fucoidanases must be discovered and screened. Several options for discovering new enzymes 
exist, ranging from simple, practical screenings to more complex screening such as 
metagenomics and bioinformatics. 
A simple screening method is described in Silchenko et al. (2015) [41], where fucoidanase 
producing bacterial strains were plated on fucoidan-agarose plates. Subsequently, the bacterial 
colonies were removed, and the plates were then treated with the cationic detergent, Cetavlon. 
Cetavlon forms a water-insoluble salt with a white color in the presence of fucoidan. Fucoidan 
that is enzymatically depolymerized has a low charge density and is not precipitated with 
Cetavlon. Consequently, a transparent halo can be seen in the location of colonies with 
fucoidan-degrading abilities. This allowed for a simple detection of fucoidan-degrading catalytic 
activity in bacterial colonies [41].  
However, a major fallback in this method is the need for culturable bacteria, which limits the 
method substantially, as not many bacteria are culturable in the laboratory. In contrast, 
metagenomic libraries can be made from any environmental sample, giving a total view of the

genetic material within a given sample at a given time. Schultz-Johansen et al. (2018) [42] 
surveyed bacteria associated with brown macroalgae, in a combined enrichment culturing and 
metagenome sequencing. Here, they discovered three fucoidanases, which are also used in the 
phylogenetic analysis in this study, namely Fp273, Fp277, and Fp279 [42]. Another sequence-
based method of discovering new fucoidanases, is the screening of genomes using BLAST. 
However, it is important to note, that these genomes likely will have been isolated and 
sequences previous to this. Nevertheless, these genomes can be prepared for many other 
reasons than the discovery of fucoidanases, leaving a potential of discovering new 
fucoidanases by analyzing the available genomes. This method was used to discover three 
putative fucoidanase genes, encoded in an operon, in the genome of the marine bacterium 
Formosa haliotis [17]. Two of the three fucoidanases have been published and showed 
fucoidan-depolymerizing catalytic abilities, namely Fhf1 [9] and Fhf2 [17]. 
 
In this study, the discovery is based on metagenomes of enrichments placed in a location where 
some enzymatic fucoidan-degrading activity is expected. In doing this, all DNA present in the 
sample is sequenced, resulting in a great amount of data. The analyzation method is crucial to 
extract all putative fucoidanase genes from the metagenome, however, this can prove tricky as 
fucoidanases are relatively unknown. From the analysis of the metagenomes, 10 putative 
fucoidanase genes were proposed. However, from these sequences the original host for each 
of the fucoidanases remain unknown for the time being. Further investigation into which 
microorganisms the proposed fucoidanase genes stem from should be performed, as other 
studies have found that some microorganisms encode several different fucoidanases in their 
genome, e.g., F. haliotis [9].  
The investigated sequence of Fim28, was truncated in its C-terminal, which likely could explain 
the lack of other domains than the catalytic N-terminal domain, D1. Vickers et al. (2018) [12] 
determined that the fucoidanase MfFcnA4 has an additional 3 domains, R1, R2, and R3, that 
wrap around the D1 domain. The three domains belong in the immunoglobin-like super family, 
as each domain binds a calcium atom, and are believed to facilitate structural rigidity in order to 
adopt the extended conformation in solution [12]. In terms of Fim28, it could be interesting to 
investigate whether these domains can be found in the C-terminal of the full sequence. 
Vuillemin et al. (2020) [9] truncated the C-terminal of Fhf1, the structure of the truncated 
fucoidanase was found to be more stable compared to that of Fhf1. This could also be the case 
for Fim28, which will have to be elucidated at a later point in time.  
 
The prediction, carried out by AlphaFold, predicted the most physiologically likely 3D structure 
of Fim28. The prediction was based on the known 3D structures of other proteins with similar 
sequences to Fim28, the list of the top 100 proteins from the AlphaFold library, used for the 
prediction can be found in Appendix 5. The enzyme with the highest similarity to Fim28, is 
P5AFcnA described by Vickers et al. (2018) [12]. The 3D structure of P5AFcnA must 
consequently affect the predicted 3D structure of Fim28, which is important to keep in mind 
when analyzing the resulting structure. Two other fucoidanases, namely MfFcnA4 and 
MfFcnA9, also shared a high similarity with Fim28. The remaining enzymes in the alignment 
study performed by AlphaFold, had a high but not as high similarity to Fim28.  
The sequence analysis revealed that Fim28 is almost exclusively consisting of the catalytic D1 
domain, when disregarding the MBP-Smt3 fusion. The structure of the D1 domain in other 
fucoidanases have been described as consisting of a characteristic (β/α)8-barrel domain [12, 
17].

Similar to Fhf2, the active site of Fim28 seems to be situated in a groove. This groove could 
potentially accommodate the positioning of the fucoidan molecule [17]. Based on the estimated 
surface charge of Fim28, the active site groove is the only surface area that is exclusively 
positively charged. A positively charged surface area in the active site groove, further 
accommodates the potential binding of the negatively charged fucoidan molecule [17].  
 
The end goal of the characterization of Fim28 in this study, was to prepare the low molecular 
weight fucoidan digests for NMR analysis, to determine their chemical structure, the choice of 
substrate was of great importance. Previously, Fim28 was in the characterization process using 
Sl fucoidans. However, several smaller bands were observed right next to each other, which 
was the determining factor in discontinuing the characterization work on Sl fucoidans. This was 
based on the requirement of dividing all bands of low molecular weight fucoidan products, to 
achieve adequate NMR results. With many fucoidan products (bands) having a very similar size 
and almost appearing as a smear, the task of dividing them was impossible.  
Instead, another fucoidan was utilized, namely Ld fucoidan. From the substrate specificity 
assay, it was observed that only a few low molecular weight fucoidan products resulted from the 
enzymatic treatment with Fim28. Another option was the Er fucoidan, however the available 
fucoidan was very scarce, which was a major reason for the choice of substrate.  
Even though the available Ld fucoidan was higher than that of Er, the total amount of purified 
fucoidan amounted to approximately 800 mg at the beginning of this study. The purification of 
the Ld fucoidan was performed in-house, and with time being a limiting factor, an additional 
purification from fresh frozen macroalgae was not possible. For that reason, it was estimated 
that the available amount simply was not enough to ensure an adequate yield of the fucoidan 
digest to be analyzed via NMR, as a significant amount is lost in the purification process. If an 
NMR analysis of the low molecular weight fucoidan products is to be performed, a new recovery 
and purification of fucoidan from Ld must be performed.  
 
The smallest oligosaccharide fragment observed of Ld fucoidan digested by Fim28, was 
estimated to be an 8-mer. However, when further investigating the time-scale assay, an even 
smaller fragment can be observed as a faint band, that starts to appear at a reaction time of 
approximately one hour (Figure 12 A). This band is lower in the gel than the smallest fragment 
in the standard (tetramer), which indicates that these released oligosaccharides are either 
smaller in size or more charged (i.e., more sulphated) [7]. Similarly, Cao et al. (2018) [7] 
observed that such a small band appeared when depolymerizing fucoidan from Sm with 
fucoidanases Fda1 and Fda2, that have been determined to be α-(1,3)-linkage specific. This 
could point towards α-(1,3)-linkage specific fucoidanases sharing similar substrate attack 
preferences. However, in order to investigate this further the structure of Ld fucoidan must be 
elucidated.  
 
During the course of the experiments, it was discovered that Fim28 was dependent of relatively 
high concentrations of NaCl and that Fim28 likely is a halophilic enzyme. The halophilicity of 
Fim28 likely affected several of the experiments carried out prior to this discovery. These 
experiments included, the initial pH assay, where no activity was observed, as well as the CaCl2 
minimization, where the activity was significantly affected by decreasing concentration of CaCl2.  
The pH assay used half the volume, and thereby concentration, of the purified Fim28 while 
doubling the reaction time. However, no significant activity was observed.

At this time, it was speculated that Fim28 might have a sensitivity towards Tris-HCl, both in 
regards of too high and too low concentrations. Another purification of Fim28 was prepared and 
the elutions were diluted with the respective pH buffers, however this resulted in an even lower 
concentration of NaCl in the reaction mix. The reaction mixes were also mixed in such a manner 
that the final concentration of each pH buffer was approximately half of the previous 
concentrations. No activity was observed in the assay. To continue the characterization process 
of Fim28, a pH value that is commonly used in fucoidanase assays for other fucoidanases, 
namely pH 7.4, was chosen for the subsequent assays. This was also the pH of which the 
previous assays in the study had been carried out. Following the pH assay, a CaCl2 
minimization assay was carried out, from which it was concluded that the highest tested 
concentration exhibited the highest level of activity. At the time, minimization was in focus, 
however it could have been interesting to investigate whether increasing the CaCl2 
concentration further, resulted in an even higher level of activity. Both experiments reduced the 
amount of Cl- in the reaction mixes, which could explain the loss or partial loss of activity. 
However, based on the experiments carried out, it cannot be concluded whether its specifically 
Cl- that affects the activity of Fim28. An initial assay to investigate this could be a KCl assay, 
where KCl substitutes NaCl  in the reaction mix. If activity is observed in the KCl assay, it would 
point towards Cl- affecting the activity of Fim28. 
 
Following the CaCl2 minimization assay, the NaCl minimization assay was carried out.  
To perform the NaCl minimization assay the purified Fim28 was required to be in a buffer 
containing 0 mM NaCl. This can normally be achieved by desalting the purified enzyme, using a 
PD10 column and an elution buffer without NaCl. However, Fim28 lost its activity when desalted 
in this manner. Another method tested was a buffer change using Vivaspin® columns, 
unfortunately Fim28 also lost its activity with this method.  
Circumventing the need for PD10 for the desalting, or Vivaspin® buffer change, of Fim28, a set 
of new elution buffers with 0 mM NaCl was prepared. From the NaCl minimization assay, and 
the following expanded NaCl assay, it was observed that Fim28 seemed to have a NaCl 
dependency, i.e., Fim28 could possibly be a halophilic enzyme.  
Fim28 was found to exhibit the highest activity at NaCl concentrations ranging from 600 mM to 
900 mM. In comparison, sea salinity is approximately 600 mM NaCl [43]. The environmental 
sample for the metagenome in which Fim28 was discovered, was taken from the hot spring 
Skarðshver, where the NaCl concentration is approximately 600 mM [25]. Fim28 most likely 
originates from a marine bacterium, as it shares high identity with P5AFcnA from the marine 
bacterium Psychromonas species SW5A. Bacteria that grow best at 500-2500 mM NaCl are 
denominated moderate halophiles [44]. Fucoidanases, which are produced extracellularly, will 
be exposed to the same environment and must be able to withstand and optimally thrive in it.  
All this points towards Fim28 being a halophilic enzyme. Halophilic enzymes are similar in 
characteristics to other enzymes but differ in their structural properties, such as excess negative 
surface charge [43, 44]. The estimated surface charge of Fim28 is also mainly negative, except 
for the active site, that is located in a groove in the enzyme. Additional characteristics for 
halophilic enzymes include a requirement for salt for biological functions, and to remain soluble 
[44]. Regarding Fim28, a NaCl concentration of approximately 300 mM was required for 
significant activity to be observed (Figure 16). Additionally, concerning the storage of purified 
Fim28, at -80 °C, precipitation was observed at protein concentrations higher than 1 mg/mL 
when stored in 250 mM NaCl, but when stored in 600 mM NaCl no precipitation was observed

even at a protein concentration of 3 mg/mL. This could also point towards Fim28 being 
stabilized at the elevated NaCl concentration.  
A final characteristic of halophilic enzymes is their extraordinary stability, allowing them to 
function at conditions where conventional enzymes cease to function, e.g., high temperatures 
[43, 44]. In regard to Fim28, assays concerning the stability must be performed in order to be 
able to comment on this characteristic. One assay to be performed is a more detailed Tm assay, 
in which the melting point of Fim28 could be determined in the presence of varying 
concentrations of NaCl. Additionally, a thermostability assay, investigating the thermostability of 
Fim28 in different concentrations of NaCl could be performed. This could enlighten whether 
Fim28 indeed is stabilized by high concentrations of salt.  
To investigate the effect of salts on the structure of Fim28, in regard to possible Cl- or other 
monovalent binding sites, as well as possible Ca2+ or other divalent binding sites, a 
crystallization of Fim28 must be carried out. Fim28 has several characteristics, that thus far 
have not been reported in the context of fucoidanases. With a crystallization of Fim28, new 
knowledge regarding the stability of fucoidanases could be obtained.  
 
No significant activity was observed at NaCl concentrations below 100 mM, which is different 
from other fucoidanases, such as Fhf2 that is only slightly affected by changes in the NaCl 
concentration [17]. Additionally, Fhf1 showed no NaCl dependency and seemed to tolerate all 
NaCl concentrations within the tested range, 15 mM to 500 mM, with no apparent changes in 
activity [9]. The fucoidanase Mef2, was also not influenced by varying concentrations of NaCl 
(at tested concentrations ranging 25 mM to 400 mM NaCl) [21]. However, Fim28 did not share 
high sequence identity with either of these fucoidanases. It would be interesting to investigate in 
the NaCl requirement and tolerance of the fucoidanases, P5AFcnA and P19DFcnA, which both 
share high sequence identity with Fim28.  
 
During the purification process it was observed that a significant amount of Fim28 was lost, 
which is unfavorable for protein purifications in general. To bypass this loss, the construct 
design could be reconsidered, especially the length of the His-tag. By adding several more 
histidine to the His-tag, Fim28 is likely to bind stronger to the resin. This could result in the need 
for elution buffers with higher concentrations of imidazole, in order to be able to elude Fim28 
from the resin, but could then simultaneously result in a less contaminated enzyme stock. It is 
not likely, that the resin is to blame for the poor binding capacity, as the resin is able to bind 5-
10 mg protein per mL resin solution [38]. In one of the performed purifications of Fim28, 2 mL 
resin was used to bind the enzyme. After the protein concentration of each elution had been 
determined, it was calculated that the total amount of enzyme purified was 5.93 mg, which only 
1 mL of resin should be capable of binding.  
 
5. Conclusion and future perspectives 
The sequence of Fim28 was validated and analyzed in this study, where it was predicted to only 
contain the catalytic D1 domain, as no domains were predicted by the online tool InterProScan. 
This could possibly be explained by the C-terminal truncation of Fim28, which should be further 
investigate in the future.

The smallest observed fucoidan oligosaccharide band from the Ld fucoidan depolymerization, 
was estimated to consist of an 8-mer (8 sugar monomers), while the second smallest fragment 
was estimated to be a 14-mer. Additionally, a distinct laddering pattern is observed in the C-

No other currently published fucoidanase exhibit the halophilic characteristics that has been 
observed for Fim28. Many possible assays could be interesting to perform in order to elucidate 
these characteristics further. The thermodynamics of Fim28 in different concentrations of NaCl 
could be performed, both in regard of a possible change in melting temperature, but also the 
thermostability. Additionally, as the discovery of the high NaCl requirement of Fim28 happened 
quite late in the lab work process, many assays were not repeated at a higher NaCl 
concentration. The pH assay was carried out at 600 mM NaCl and must be repeated at a 
concentration of 900 mM NaCl. Furthermore, the different results in the two substrate specificity 
assays must be investigated, in order to determine whether the optimized conditions possibly 
could be the cause.   
 
With future industrial use in mind, Fim28 could be applied in situations where a high NaCl 
concentration is present, such as directly on fresh macroalgae. A specific example could be the 
pre-treatment of macroalgae as a dietary supplement for animals, such as pigs or cattle, where 
desired bioactivities could be exhibited. The dietary supplement could possibly exhibit naturally 
derived anti-inflammatory properties, aiding in good animal health. This, however, is just one 
example, the possibilities are vast and would be interesting to investigate further in the future.
