Abstract  
In Denmark the main drinking water source is groundwater. The herbicides bentazone and 
chloridazone and its metabolites (desphenyl chloridazone (DPC) and methyl-desphenyl chloridazone 
(MDPC) are these years of huge concern due to contamination of the groundwater used for drinking 
water supply in Denmark. Therefore, it is important to find a sustainable and cost-effective solution 
to remove the pesticides from the groundwater. The current treatment of groundwater in Denmark 
does not include removal of pesticides. However, investigations have clarified that methanotrophic 
bacteria (MOBs) in the treatment processes at Drinking Water Treatment Plants (DWTPs) are able to 
co-metabolic degrade micropollutants such as herbicides. In order to maintain and enhance this 
microbial potential at DWTPs, it is necessary to understand the kinetics and parameters of the 
metabolism behind the degradation of pesticides.  
The purpose of this master thesis was to investigate the potential of the microbes to degrade the 
herbicides by continuous addition of methane. Moreover, the influence of copper was investigated 
since copper is important for the enzyme methane monooxygenase (MMO) involved in the metabolic 
pathway of methanotrophs to degrade micropollutants such as pesticides. As the last, it was 
investigated if the microbial removal already occurred at a DWTP in Denmark. 
The experiments include either biomass or filter sand from the two DWTPs, Stenholt or Borre. The 
experiments were setup with microcosms consisting of either biomass or filter sand, outlet water and 
100 µg/l of either bentazone, Chloridazone, DPC or MDPC and 1 µg/L of 14C-bentazone.  The content 
of methane and oxygen in the microcosms were controlled during the experiment.  
Removal of the compounds was found in all of the microcosms along with removal of methane. The 
microcosms from Stenholt DWTP and Borre DWTP consumed between 5.63-7.91 mg and 0.88-4.41 
mg/day/microcosm of methane, respectively.  Oxygen was consumed as well in all the microcosms.  
The removal rate of bentazone with addition of methane was 0.42 ng/h/g-biomass (day 0-13) and 
1.91 ng/h/g-biomass (day 21-33), Stenholt DWTP. In the microcosms with both addition og methane 
and Cu the removal rate was 0.36 ng/h/g-biomass (day 0-13) and 0.73 ng/h/g-biomass (day 21-33), 
Stenholt DWTP. It was not possible to observe any influence of the Cu due to release of Cu from the 
tap at Stenholt DWTP. The removal rate was 0.60 ng/h/g-biomass, 1.20 ng/h/g-biomass, 4.35 ng/h/g-
biomass in the microcosms with 14C-bentazone (day 0-13), chloridazone and DPC (day 0-33), 
respectively. In microcosms with filter sand from one of the three sand filters, the removal rate was 
in the range 0.02-0.10 ng/h/g-sand for the primary filter. For microcosms with sand from the tertiary 
filter, the removal was in the range 0.01-0.06 ng/h/g-sand. However, the transformation yields of the 
chlorinated compounds were in the range 10-4-10-2 mole compound/mole CH4 which complies with 
reported values of chlorinated compounds. Furthermore, the microbial degradation of DPC and 
MDPC was demonstrated at Borre DWTP. Overall, this study demonstrated a potential for microbial 
degradation of bentazone, chloridazone, DPC and MDPC at the two DWTPs. The relation between 
the methane consumption might be due to methane oxidation by the microbes when pesticides were 
degraded. This study then demonstrated the potential of microbial removal in both aeration tank and 
sand filters at DWTPs in Denmark.

1. Introduction 
 In Europe, the water supply is based on 70 percent on groundwater (Navarrete et al., 2008). 
However, in Denmark the groundwater is the most important source since 99% of the drinking water 
supply is based on groundwater (Danish Nature Agency, 2014). The existing and simple groundwater 
treatment in Denmark only involves chemical and biological treatment such as aeration and biological 
sand filtration of the groundwater. The most common process for biological filtration is rapid sand 
filtration (Bækgaard, 1997). Several trace contaminants including pesticides have been a huge 
challenge for water supply over the last 35 years (GEUS, 2009). In 2016, pesticides and their 
metabolites were detected in 34 % and 25 % of all monitoring wells and active abstraction wells 
(AWs), respectively. The concentration of pesticides and their metabolites were exceeded in 8.6 % 
and 2.9 % of the monitoring wells and active AWs, respectively (Thorling, et al, 2018).  
The occurrence of pesticides in the environment including groundwater and drinking water are of 
concern since some pesticides, a group of Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) are defined as 
Persistent, Bioaccumulative and Toxic (PBT) (Ritter, et al, 1995). The toxicity to humans can be both 
acute- and long-term effects including harm to the central and peripheral nervous system and cancer 
(Younes, and Gorchev-Galal, 2000).  
According to the European Directive of the quality of water intended for human consumption 
directive (98/83/EC) and the protection of groundwater against pollution and deterioration 
(2006/118/EC), the guidance value of a single pesticide and its metabolites should not exceed 0.1 
μg/l or in total a concentration of 0.5 μg/l in groundwater and drinking water (Miljø- og 
Fødevareministeriet, 2017b; European Parliament and Council, 1998; European Parliament and 
Council, 2006).  
The Danish water works organization (Danva) indicates that it is increasingly difficult to find 
abstraction wells without pesticides. This includes both legal and illegal pesticides (Bredsdorff, M, 
2011). The existing treatment process at Danish drinking water treatment plants (DWTP) is not 
designed to remove pesticides. However, recommended processes to remove the pesticides from 
drinking water includes activated carbon, membrane filtration and distillation in which activated 
carbon is the most common (Christensen, et al. 2006).  
The implementation of other treatment processes to remove the pesticides from groundwater has 
been prioritized in order to obtain a more cost effective and sustainable solution. For instance, this 
could be bioaugmentation. In which single strains or microbial consortia are used to degrade 
micropollutants such as pesticides (Benner, et al. 2013). According to this Hedegaard and 
Albrechtsen, (2014) found that the methanotrophic bacteria in rapid sand filters were able to co-
metabolically degrade pesticides along with a growth substrate such as methane (CH4).  
Some of the current and most critical herbicides are Bentazone and Chloridazone and its metabolites 
Desphenyl chloridazone (DPC) and Methyl-desphenyl chloridazone (MDPC). The herbicide Bentazone 
is still legal and able for use in Europe. Bentazone is of concern since it still has the potential to pollute 
the groundwater now and in future years (Bolvin, et al., 2004). In 1995, the Ministry of Environment 
and Food in Denmark decided that the use of bentazone should be limited. Bentazone is very soluble 
in water and mobile, which means that it can easily be transported through the soil. The 
contamination of the groundwater depends on the amount of bentazone used in agriculture 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2013). Bentazone was in 2016 one of the most common pesticides in the drillings for 
groundwater (Thorling, et al, 2018).

The herbicide Chloridazone was used in Denmark until 1996. Currently, the metabolite DPC exceeded 
the guideline value in Danish drinking water. The Ministry of Environment and Food in Denmark then 
decided that all DWTPs should test for the two metabolites DPC and MDPC of chloridazone (Miljø- 
og Fødevareministeriet, 2017c). Chloridazone and its metabolites have high mobility in soil which 
contribute to the potential of pollution in the groundwater (Pérez- Fernández, et al, 2009). Moreover, 
Bentazone, Chloridazone, DPC and MDPC have all been found in AWs in Denmark, in some of the 
AWs the concentration of each pesticide exceeded the guideline value of 0.1 µg/l (Thorling, et al, 
2018). 
Therefore, there might be a need for new sustainable solutions in the future to remove the pesticides. 
One promising solution could be microbial degradation in the sand filters (Hedegaard and 
Albrechtsen, 2014). This was later improved by evidence that the microbial composition includes 
methanotrophic bacteria (MOB) which are able to degrade bentazone and other pesticides in 
presence of methane as growth substrate (Hedegaard, et al. 2017). The enzyme methane 
monooxygenase (MMO) involved in the co-metabolic pathway is divided into particulate 
monooxygenase (pMMO) or soluble monooxygenase (sMMO). In which most of the methanotrophs 
contains pMMO and only some sMMO. The concentration of methane (CH4) is important in the 
expression of either pMMO or sMMO (Semrau, et al. 2010).  However, it was found that copper (Cu) 
is important in the regulation of the gene expression encoding pMMO and sMMO as well as the 
activity of the two genes for methanotrophs which contains both forms of MMO (Semrau, et al, 
2010).  
The importance and relevance to apply and improve the technical solution as biological removal of 
pesticides is of interest, as it might be more efficient, sustainable and cost effective. To apply and 
improve this biological solution, more investigations are needed for the evidence of methanotrophic 
degradation such as the presence of the methanotrophs and how to understand the co-metabolic 
process. According to that Bentazone, Chloridazone and its metabolites DPC and MDPC are of huge 
concern in Denmark, the co-metabolic potential of the microbial community at DWTPs in Denmark 
are both relevant and important to investigate. Therefore, the aims of this study are to investigate: 
 
1) What is the microbial (co-metabolic) potential of the microbial composition, as pure biomass 
and in the filter sand, at two Danish waterworks to degrade the pesticides? 
2) How methane influences the stimulation of the microbial (co-metabolic) potential- is it 
possible to observe a difference with or without addition of methane?  
3) How does copper influence the expression and activation of the MMOs (pMMO and sMMO) 
involved in the methanotrophic degradation of pesticides?  
4) If the microbial removal already occurs at the DWTPs in Denmark? 
 
This study will investigate the microbial removal potential of bentazone, chloridazone and its 
metabolites DPC and MDPC by the present biomass (MOBs)from the aeration tank (Stenholt DWTP) 
or in filter sand (Borre DWTP). Furthermore, the influence of the (co-) metabolic process by addition 
of methane and copper to activate or inhibit the responsible genes pMMO and sMMO for the 
degradation by the MOBs. At last if the microbial removal is already taking place at DWTPs in 
Denmark.

2. Background 
This section covers the most important background knowledge of drinking water treatment, the 
pesticides as well as the properties and metabolic pathway of the microbes (MOBs) to understand 
the results of this study.  
 
2.1 Drinking Water Treatment in Denmark 
In Denmark, the drinking water supply is only based on groundwater, since the overall quality of the 
groundwater in Denmark is good.  The existing treatment of groundwater used for drinking water is 
only based on chemical and biological treatment such as aeration and filtration before it reaches the 
consumers (GEUS, 2009; Bækgaard, 1997). If the abstracted groundwater complies with the drinking 
water quality demands, the process can be minimized, which will reduce the costs of treatment (Gray, 
1994). Some substances within the water need to be reduced such as iron and carbon dioxide (CO2). 
However, anthropogenic pollution of the drinking water has increased due to increased agriculture 
production. In this case, treatment then needs to include removal of nitrates, pathogens and organic 
compounds (pesticides) (Gray, 1994).  
 
2.1.1 Aeration 
Most commonly, the abstracted groundwater only contains low or limit amounts of dissolved oxygen. 
In this case, the water is aerated to strip off CH4, hydrogensulphide (H2S) and to add oxygen for the 
oxidation of ferrous iron (Fe2+), manganese (Mn2+) and ammonium (NH4+). The simplest processes of 
aeration are cascade or fountain system (Gray, 1994). Moreover, the aeration reduces the odors, 
corrosiveness by stripping of CO2 gas. Since the stripping of CO2 will decrease the pH, iron and 
manganese will no longer be soluble in the water, if the pH >6.5 (Gray, 1994).  
In the case of high CH4 concentrations in the groundwater, an INKA-aerator is very efficient and often 
used to remove CH4 in some regions of Denmark. The INKA-aerator is sufficient to remove around 
95-99 % of the methane (Brinck, et al. 2007). It operates by leading the water in a 2-3 cm layer over 
a plate of corrosion-resistant material. The air arrives from an aerator below the plate. This results in 
a 10-20 cm layer of bubbles with a huge contact area between air and water. The INKA-aerator is 
only sufficient if the concentration of methane in the raw water is around 30 mg/L (Winther, et al. 
2010). According to the Danish guidelines, the concentration of methane in the outlet water from 
the DWTP should not exceed 0.01 mg/L (Miljøministeriet, 2007).  
 
2.1.2 Filtration 
The purpose of the filtration process is to remove the residual material within the water.  Moreover, 
to retain the compounds of iron and manganese (Fe(OH)3, MnO2)  from the aeration process 
(Winther, et al. 2017). The efficiency of the filtration process includes suspended solids (SS) 
concentration < 10 mg/l. If the content of suspended solids is above this, storage is needed for the 
SS to settle (Gray, 1994).  
The filters consist of sand and gravel in which the water passes through. The particles that are 
removed by the sand filters will at some point clog the filters, and thereby reduce the flow of water. 
In order, to prevent this the filters are backwashed (Gray, 1994).  
The filtration process can include either slow-, or rapid sand filtration, whereby the last one is the 
most common in Denmark (Bækgaard, 1997). The rapid sand filtration consists of coarse grades of 
quartz sand (1 mm diameter), to ensure that the water can easily pass through (between 5 to 10

m3/m2/h). The rapid sand filters have reduced capacity to remove small particles such as bacteria and 
odors. The design of rapid sand filters variate between open-topped gravity-fed systems or more

disadvantage of the rapid sand filters is, that biological activity might be reduced compared to the 
slow sand filters due to the larger size of the sand particles. However, the slow sand filters cannot be 
backwashed, which raises the costs (Gray, 1994). 
 
2.1.3 Degradation of pesticides at DWTPs 
Since a lot of pesticides are found in the AWs, the Danish water works organization (Danva) indicates 
that it is increasingly difficult to find any AWs without pesticides. This includes both legal and illegal 
pesticides (Bredsdorff, 2011). Since existing treatment process at DWTPs does not include the 
removal of pesticides. There might be a need for this in the future. The current recommended 
processes to remove pesticides are, so far, activated carbon, membrane filtration and distillation in 
which activated carbon is the most common. The application of activated carbon is a costly process 
(Christensen, et al. 2006). Therefore, implementation of other treatment processes to remove 
pesticides at DWTP has been prioritized to obtain a more cost effective and sustainable solution.  
One promising and more sustainable solution to remove the pesticides is bioaugmentation of 
biologically active processes in DWTPs. The principle is to use single strains or microbial consortia 
with the ability to degrade micropollutants such as pesticides. However, the biological active sand 
filters in DWTPs already contains these bacteria (Benner, et al. 2013). A study by Hedegaard and 
Albrechtsen, (2014) found that the methanotrophic bacteria in the rapid sand filters were able to 
degrade pesticides. The degradation was based on the co-metabolic properties of the MOBs, which 
means that these bacteria are able to degrade the pesticides a long with a growth substrate as 
methane. In a later study by Hedegaard et al. (2017) found that the largest biological removal was 
found with the highest concentration of methane. 
 
2.2 The pesticides 
Contamination of groundwater has been a huge challenge in water supply for the last 35 years. This 
is mainly due to pollution of nitrate from farming, chemicals, toxic materials and pesticides from 
urban areas and farmland (GEUS, 2009).  The loss of pesticides is depending on factors such as rainfall 
after application, the slope and soil type of usage and the physical-chemical properties of the 
pesticides (Buttiglieri, et al. 2009).  
 
2.2.1 Bentazone  
Bentazone is an herbicide used primary in agriculture with maize, peas, rice and soy beans. It is mainly 
used in winter and spring (World Health Organization, 2004). Bentazone was approved in Denmark 
in 1974 and is still legal in Europe including Denmark (Miljøstyrelsen, 2013). The previous application 
and continual use of bentazone is of concern due to current and future contamination of 
groundwater (Bolvin, et al. 2004).  The sale and use of bentazone has variated a lot from 1988 until 
2016. Moreover, from 1995 to 2016, the amount of bentazone has been decreasing.  In 1995 the 
amount of used bentazone in Denmark was approximately 93 ton (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet 
1996) compared to the amount in 2016 of approximately 22 ton (Miljøministeriet, 2016).  The 
decrease in use is mainly due to the restrictions in 1995. The restrictions included limited application 
of bentazone only to be legal in spring and summer (Miljøstyrelsen, 2013).

Bentazone has high water solubility, high mobility in soil (Koc) and low degradability (Table  1). 
Therefore, Bentazone can easily be transported through the soil (Miljøstyrelsen, 2013). Furthermore, 
bentazone can easily be transported in the aquifers to the abstraction wells (Boivin, et al. 2004). The 
biological degradation of bentazone in soil is affected by pH and soil organic matter. Moreover, in 
high concentrations of bentazone, the bacteria are negatively affected which increase the 
persistence of bentazone in soil (Boivin, et al. 2004). 
The concentration of bentazone in groundwater covers concentrations between <0.1 to 6 μg/l (World 
Health Organization, 2004). In 2016 Bentazone was one of the most frequently found herbicides in 
both monitoring wells and active AWs (Thorling, et al. 2018).

 2.2.2 Chloridazone, Desphenyl-Chloridazone (DPC) and Methyl-Desphenyl Chloridazone (MDPC) 
Chloridazone is an herbicide used to inhibit photosynthesis, for pre-plant, pre-emergence and early 
post-emergence to control annual broad-leaved weeds specially applied for sugar beets and red table 
beets (Pérez- Fernández, et al. 2009). Chloridazone was approved in Denmark in 1964 and was band 
in 1996 due to the threat of contaminated groundwater (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2018). The 
last legal application of chloridazone in 1996 was approximately 11 tons in Denmark (Miljø- og 
Fødevareministeriet, 1996). Chloridazone has high mobility and low sorption in soil (Pérez- 
Fernández, et al, 2009) and a moderate solubility in water ( Table  2). Due to the high mobility in soil, 
chloridazone can easily be transported and contaminate surface- and groundwater (Pérez- 
Fernández, et al. 2009; Buttiglieri, et al. 2009). The metabolites DPC and MDPC have the same 
properties such as high mobility, low sorption and classified as persistent.  
Chloridazone is degraded by microbes in soil to its metabolites DPC and MDPC. The first step involves 
dephenylated degradation by splitting of the phenyl group to DPC which is found to be a common 
metabolite of aerobic soil metabolism (Buttiglieri, et al. 2009). The second step involves, if relevant, 
methylation to the metabolite MDPC. The metabolites are not active as chloridazone itself. 
Moreover, in Germany DPC and MDPC are classified as non-relevant even though they might be both 
mobile and persistent in soil (Dechene, et al. 2014).

2.3 The microbial degradation strategies of pesticides 
Two main metabolic degradation pathways have been observed for microorganisms in environments 
with micropollutants (pesticides) (Figure 1). The first pathway, the metabolic strategy, in which 
microorganisms are able to use specific micropollutants in a growth-linked process and thereby 
mineralization of micropollutants (Benner, et al. 2013). The second pathway, the co-metabolic 
strategy, where microorganisms interact with micropollutants such as pesticides.  In this process 
compared to the metabolic pathway, the micropollutants cannot be used as growth substrates. 
Instead, the microorganism contains enzymes able to co-metabolically degrade micropollutants 
(Benner, et al. 2013).

2.4 Methanotrophic bacteria  
The methane oxidizing bacteria (MOB) are chemoheterothrophic (Pepper, et al. 2009) and parts of 
metyhylotrophs which in aerobic environments are able to catabolize CH4 and other one-carbon 
compounds as electron donors and carbon sources (Madigan, et al. 2012).  This catabolic process 
only occurs in aerobic environments because the methanotrophs need Oxygen (O2) in the first step 
(Figure 2) (Madigan, et al. 2012).  The aerobic catabolism of hydrocarbons is rapid due to the 
metabolic advantage of available O2 as electron acceptor compared to others such as nitrate (NO3-), 
Iron III (Fe3+), sulfate (SO42-). Furthermore, the MOBs need aerobic conditions for growth and are 
found in both soil and waters (Madigan, et al. 2012; Pepper, et al. 2009). The best growth conditions 
for methanotrophs includes a pH between 5-8 and temperature of 20-35° C (Semrau, et al. 2010). 
The MOBs have been observed to have co-metabolic properties, therefore able to degrade 
micropollutants such as pesticides in presence of the primary growth substrate as CH4 (Benner, et al. 
2013). Moreover, a study found evidence for the co-metabolic properties of MOBs in the degradation 
of bentazone (Hedegaard, et al. 2017).  
The evolution of MOBs might arise from ammonia-oxidizing bacteria (AOB) by selection for 
conversion of ammonia monooxygenase (AMO) into methane monooxygenase (MMO). Even though 
MOBs are able to oxidize ammonia (NH3) as well as CH4, they cannot grow by using the substrate of 
AOBs as electron donor (Madigan, et al. 2012). Since cultures of methanotrophs are found in aquifers, 
wastewater, etc. they can enhance the degradation of pollutants as chlorinated hydrocarbons 
(Semrau, et al. 2010).

2.4.1 Methane-monooxygenase (MMO) 
The oxidation of CH4 (Figure 2), is based on the enzyme methane monooxygenase (MMO). The 
enzyme MMO catalyze the incorporation of the oxygen atoms of O2 into carbon compounds such as 
CH4, which result in methanol (CH3OH) used for the further catabolism (Madigan, et al. 2012). 
 
Figure 2 The oxidation of methane by methanotrophic bacteria. First, CH4 is oxidized to CH3OH by the enzyme methane 
monooxygenase (MMO). A proton motive force is established from electron flow in the membrane which fuels ATPase. FP, 
flavoprotein; cyt, cytochrome; Q, quinone; MP, methanopterin. (Madigan, et al. 2012) 
Two different types of MMO exists, the particulate methane monooxygenase (pMMO) and soluble 
methane monooxygenase (sMMO) (Benner, et al. 2014; Semrau, et al. 2010). Common for both 
MMOs is that both use NADH as electron donor. Several types of MOBs exist, and some contain both 
types of MMO. In this case copper (Cu) is important in regulating the expression of the genes that 
codes for pMMO and sMMO (Semrau, et al. 2010). At very low copper:biomass ratio the sMMO is 
expressed while pMMO is expressed when the ratio increase (Semrau, et al. 2013). The Cu-specific 
binding compound, methanobactin, is important in how the cells coordinate the regulation of pMMO 
and sMMO (Semrau, et al. 2010).  
 
2.4.2 Particulate Methane monooxygenase (pMMO) 
The pMMO is found in most types of MOBs and located in the cytoplasmic membrane. Even though, 
only little is known for the pMMO. It was found that the enzyme pMMO contain Cu. The oxidation 
potential of pMMO is a bit limit compared to sMMO (Semrau, et al. 2010). It is only able to oxidize 
alkanes up to 5 carbon atoms, and no oxidation of cyclic alkanes or aromatic compounds. The 
methanotrophs that express the pMMO are more competent to survive if presence of chlorinated 
ethenes because they are able to degrade more of these in high concentrations due to the specifity 
of pMMO for CH4 and slower rates for transformation of the more toxic products (Benner, et al. 2014; 
Semrau, et al. 2010). One of the advantages for pMMO is that even though it is a slow enzyme, the 
pMMO is able to degrade pollutants over longer time than sMMO. Another advantage is the cells, 
which express pMMO are more competitive to turnover CH4 at low concentrations (Semrau, et al. 
2010). The methanotrophs that express pMMO have high affinity for Cu and might therefore be 
effective in the uptake mechanism of Cu. To inhibit pMMO, high phenylacetylene: biomass ratios can 
be used (Semrau, et al. 2010).

2.4.3 Soluble Methane monooxygenase (sMMO) 
The sMMO is only found in some methanotrophs and located in the cytoplasm and consist of 
hydroxylase, a reductase and a protein for regulation. The expression of sMMO only occur at low 
copper: biomass ratio (Semrau, et al. 2013) and is found to cluster at this ratio (Semrau, et al. 2010). 
However, sMMO is a Fe-containing enzyme and is only expressed at Cu-deficiency by a few types of 
the MOBs (Semrau, et al. 2013; Binnerup, et al. 2005).  
The sMMO is able to oxidize alkanes up to 8 carbon atoms, as well as ethers, cyclic alkanes, and 
aromatic hydrocarbons. One of the advantages for cells that express sMMO, is that they are more 
competitive in the turnover of CH4 at high concentrations. Furthermore, this should be more 
preferable since this process is faster, which might allow these cells to growth more rapidly (Benner, 
et al. 2014; Semrau, et al. 2010). To inhibit the sMMO phenylacetylene can be used as inhibitor 
(Semrau, et al. 2010). 
 
The microbial degradation is a promising sustainable solution to remove the pesticides from 
groundwater at the DWTP. Hence, it is important to understand the kinetics and parameters behind 
to optimize the conditions for the microbes (MOBs) to remove the pesticides. A better understanding 
and more evidence for the process would make this solution both relevant and interesting in the 
future.

3. Method and Materials

experiments. First, an introduction of the two DWTPs used for the experimental setup. Followed by

3.1 Drinking Water Treatment Plants 
In 2017 the number of DWTPs in Denmark was approximately 2700 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2017).  
This study only includes the two DWTPs Stenholt and Borre DWTP. The first DWTP, Stenholt DWTP, 
was chosen since previous studies by Hedegaard and Albrechtsen, (2014; 2017) and Sykyta and 
Milanovic (2017) showed a potential of biological pesticide degradation in both the aeration tank and 
in the sand filters.  
The experimental setup aims to investigate the microbial potential to degrade the compounds 
bentazone (incl. 14C-bentazone), chloridazone and DPC according to the influence of methane and 
copper. However, this setup only investigates the potential of the pure biomass in the aeration tank 
from Stenholt DWTP since the methanotrophs were expected to be part of the biomass in the 
aeration tank responsible for the degradation of the pesticides. Furthermore, the water from two out 
of three AWs, Stenholt DWTP contained CH4 (Thomsen, et al. u.å, c,d).  
  The second DWTP, Borre DWTP, was interesting since the two abstraction wells have different 
groundwater chemistry. One AW with low and the other with high methane concentration (Thomsen, 
et al. u.å, f,g).  Furthermore, DCP and MDCP have been observed in one of the AWs. Therefore, the 
experimental setup for Borre investigate the influence of methane for the microbial degradation of 
the compounds bentazone, chloridazone, DPC and MDPC. The investigations will include filter sand 
from the three sand filters at Borre DWTP. Furthermore, the study will investigate if the microbial 
removal of pesticides already occurs and if it is possible to relate to the concentration of CH4. 
 
3.2 Stenholt Drinking Water Treatment Plant  
Stenholt DWTP is located in Fredensborg, Denmark. It was built in 1972 and consist today only of one 
out of the two originally treatment lines due to problems with groundwater abstraction (Cold, 2015). 
Stenholt DWTP consist of aeration, open rapid sand filtration and disinfection before the clean water 
is stored in a tank (Figure 3). 
The extraction license is of 500,000 m3/year which is abstracted from the three AWs (DGU nr.

4. Uncertainties of the method validation at LC-MS/MS 
As it was the first time that the compounds chloridazone, DPC and MDPC have been measured in 
drinking water samples by LC-MS/MS at the laboratory for DTU Environment. Therefore, the method 
validation (described in previous section) was used at the first time to measure the concentrations 
of all compounds and in this study. It was observed that the expected (injected) and measured 
concentration for the samples from Borre DWTP (Table  3) were probably of low accuracy. The same 
tendency of the low accuracy was also observed for the autoclaved controls and samples from 
Stenholt DWTP. However, since the samples was analyzed in the same order as given in the table, 
the accuracy tends to increase with the number of measurements. Since the measured concentration 
of the samples with MDPC were closer to the expected concentration. Moreover, the procedure of 
the preparation of the samples for the analysis at the LC-MS/MS variated from Stenholt and Borre 
DWTP as described in the section of the method. This was probably because that the method 
validation was not finally developed. For further measurements, the method validation needs to be 
improved and developed to ensure a higher accuracy from through the analysis. The difference 
between the expected (injected) and measured concentration could be explained by the handling of 
the samples during sampling. However, since all samples were handled with the same procedure and 
even the measured concentration in some of the samples (Table  3) were close to the expected, this 
reason might be rejected. Therefore, the low accuracy might be explained by the low accuracy or a 
third unknown reason.

5. Results and discussion 
In this section, the results of the experiments are presented and discussed. First the results from the 
experiment with biomass, Stenholt DWTP followed by the experiment with filter sand, Borre DWTP. 
At the end a discussion of the results.   
 
5.1 Stenholt DWTP 
Stenholt DWTP was chosen to follow up on previous investigations by Hedegaard et al (2014, 2017) 
and Sykyta and Milanovic (2017). Several parameters were analyzed to understand how these 
parameters influenced the microbial degradation of the pesticides. For Stenholt DWTP, the 
parameters included concentration of copper, methane and level of oxygen in the microcosms.

concentration (300- 335 µg/L) of Cu were found

5.3 Concentration of chloridazone, DPC and MDPC through the treatment steps at Borre 
DWTP 
 The concentrations of chloridazone, DPC and MDPC were measured at several treatment steps  
at Borre DWTP by LC-MS/MS at ALS laboratory. Chloridazone was not found at any treatment step. 
However, both DPC and MDPC were measured in the highest concentration at AW 227.159, as 
expected, which also was registered in the Jupiter Database (appendix 7). The concentration of DPC 
and MDPC in the Jupiter Database was 0.15 µg/L and 0.037 µg/L, respectively (appendix 7). Both DPC 
and MDPC were not registered after the primary sand filter as the water from AW 227.159 was lead 
directly to the tertiary filter (Figure 4). The lower concentration of both DPC and MDPC from AW 
227.159 (0.22 µg/L and 0.072 µg/L, respectively) to before the tertiary filter (0.090 µg/L and 0.033 
µg/L, respectively) can be explained by that the water from AW 227.160 was mixed before the 
tertiary filter with water from AW 227.160. This complies with the amount of abstracted water for 
each of the AWs since 35 % of groundwater was abstracted from the AW 227.159 and 65 % of 
groundwater from AW 227.160. However, since the water was already mixed before the tertiary sand 
filter further degradation occurred between after the tertiary filter and after clean water storage. An 
explanation for this could be that the remaining methanotrophs and methane from the primary filter 
contribute to a continuously microbial degradation throughout the treatment at the DWTP and 
possible also in the clean water storage tank.

5.4 Relation between pesticide removal and addition of copper  
The addition of copper to the microcosms were expected to reduce the removal of bentazone. 
However, since the outlet water used from Stenholt DWTP already contained a relative high 
concentration of Cu (app. 6 to 18 µg/L) it was difficult to comply that Cu regulate the expression of 
pMMO and sMMO important for the microbial degradation of pesticides.  However, it was found in 
a previous study by Wagner, et al. 2017 that an additional concentration of Cu (<5 µg/L) enhanced 
the activity of AMO responsible for the first step in nitrification and similar to MMO involved in the 
first step of methane oxidation (Figure 2). Therefore, if comparing the results of this study with the 
ones by Wagner et al. 2017 it was expected that the activity of sMMO would be reduced by adding 
copper (20 µg/L) since the expression of sMMO only occur at low Cu:biomass ratio.  It is therefore 
difficult to conclude that the additional Cu concentration contributed to further reduction of sMMO 
activity. However, it was found by Hedegaard, (2018) that a higher concentration of copper probably 
reduced the removal of bentazone and it was suggested that the higher concentration of Cu would 
express pMMO instead of sMMO. Hence, the inhibited expression of sMMO, therefore reduced the 
removal of bentazone. However, it was not possible to confirm this observation in this study caused 
by a possible release of Cu from the outlet tap (brass) at Stenholt DWTP to the outlet water. 
Therefore, further investigations would be suggested to demonstrate that Cu actually might influence 
the expression of the MMOs. Hence, the removal of Cu could be explained by microbial consumption 
since copper is expected to be a key parameter in the expression of pMMO and sMMO but not to 
conclude that either pMMO or sMMO was expressed.  
  
5.5 Relation between pesticide removal and stimulation with methane 
It was expected that the stimulation with CH4 would enhance the activity of the microbes (MOBs) 
leading to a larger removal of the compounds.  The results in this study, the largest removal of the 
compounds was found in microcosms with addition of CH4. In general, the highest removal was found 
in microcosms with filter sand from primary filter (Borre DWTP) which also showed the highest 
consumption of CH4. However, a larger consumption was found for chloridazone in microcosms with 
sand from the tertiary filter but without addition of methane. This observation might be explained by 
difference in the composition of the biomass in the primary and tertiary sand filter. A suggestion 
might be that the presence of non-methanotrophs in the tertiary filter were able to degrade the 
compounds. According to the concentrations of methane found at Borre DWTP, a much smaller 
amount of methane was found at the tertiary filter than the primary filter. This could explain a lower 
presence of MOBs in competition with other bacteria. Otherwise, the microbes in the tertiary filter 
was more efficient without presence of CH4. To confirm this the composition of the biomass in each 
filter should be investigated (e.g. by quantitative polymerase chain reaction, qPCR).  
The relation between the microbial degradation and expected co-metabolic properties of the MOBs 
to remove the compounds in the microcosms was confirmed by comparing with the autoclaved 
controls (abiotic controls). Since the relative concentration (C/C0) for the autoclaved controls were 
lower for all microcosms except the for the ones including chloridazone with CH4 with filter sand from 
the primary filter. The decrease in C/C0 for the autoclaved controls were unexpected and probably 
caused by uncertainty of the analysis or matrix effect in the microcosms.

5.6 Relation between methane concentration and pesticide removal at Borre DWTP  
Both the concentration of DPC and MDPC decreased from the AW 227.159 to before the tertiary 
filter. Explained by that the water from AW 227.159 and AW 227.160 were mixed before the tertiary 
filter in the ratio 35 % and 65 %, respectively. However, the results (Table 5) showed that a further 
decrease in the concentration of both DPC and MDPC occurred in the step between after the tertiary 
filter and after clean water storage tank. Suggested by that some MOBs or other microbes remained 
in the water which were able to continue the degradation of DPC and MDPC. It should be noticed 
that the concentration of DPC at AW 227.159 did not comply with the guideline values of pesticides 
for drinking water (0.1 µg/L) but complied after the clean water storage tank. Hence, even though 
the contact time in this study spend over several days compared to the contact time at Borre DWTP. 
These results demonstrate that the treatment at Borre DWTP was sufficient to reduce the 
concentration of DPC to comply with the guideline value of pesticides in drinking water.  
 
5.7 Removal rates and transformation yield 
The removal rates of bentazone varied from 0.02 to 0.08 nmol/h/microcosm in microcosms with 
addition of methane from Stenholt DWTP. In microcosms with both addition of methane and Cu, the 
removal rates varied from 0.02 to 0.03 nmol/h/microcosm (table 4). It was only possible to observe 
an influence of the methane in the time period of day 21 to 33. A previous study found removal rates 
10 times higher (Hedegaard, 2018) than this study. However, the methane removal was higher in this 
study compared to the one with biomass by Hedegaard, (2018) from Stenholt DWTP. This could be 
due to that the mass of biomass in this study was twice the mass in the study by Hedegaard, (2018). 
Another suggestion would be competitive inhibition since the concentration used in this study was 
lower. Further investigations need to confirm this explanation.  
The removal rate of 14C-bentazone was 0.35, 0.13, 0.24 nmol/h/microcosm in microcosms with either 
primary, secondary or tertiary filter sand, respectively from Borre DWTP. The corresponding removal 
of methane was 11.00, 3.55, 2.33 µmol/h/microcosm. These removal rates corresponded to the ones 
found in sand filters at other DWTP with addition of acetylene (known to inhibit both the methane 
and bentazone removal) (Hedegaard, 2018). Moreover, the removal rate of 0.35 nmol/h/microcosm 
correspond to the removal rate of 20 g of enrichment culture. This is interesting and further 
investigations should analyze the composition of the biomass in the filters at Borre DWTP.  
  The transformation yield (10-6 mole compound/mole methane) was in the same range for all 
compounds except DPC which had the highest transformation yield (10-5 mole compound/mole 
methane) in the experiment with biomass from Stenholt DWTP (Table 4). The values were in the low 
range of transformation yields compared to reported values of chlorinated aliphatic hydrocarbons 
(2.2*10-4 to 6.3*10-1 moleTC/moleCH4) (Hedegaard, et al. 2017). Due to that the transformation yields 
in the experiment with Stenholt DWTP were all in the low range indicate that a large consumption of 
the primary substrate is needed to remove the secondary substrate.  
The transformation yields in the experiment with filter sand from Borre DWTP varied in range (10-5 
to 10-2 mole compound/mole methane) (Table 5). The transformation yield (10-4 to 10-2 mole 
compound/mole methane) for the compounds chloridazone, DPC and MDPC were found in the same 
range as reported values of chlorinated compounds.  However, the transformation yield of 14C-
bentazone was in the lowest range (10-5 mole compound/mole methane) which also was found in a 
low range (10-6 mole compound/mole methane) in the experiment with Stenholt DWTP. Hence, the 
transformation yields in each of the experiments were more less in the same range and indicate that 
the removal of the compounds might be explained by the same mechanism.

6. Perspectives:  
This study demonstrated that by stimulate the environment of the sand filters (Borre DWTP) or 
biomass (Stenholt DWTP) with CH4 enhanced the removal of the compounds. Except for the tertiary 
filter (Borre DWTP) which showed the opposite relation. Hence, the occurrence of methanotrophic 
bacteria in the microbial community of the sand filters were able to (co-metabolic) degrade the 
compounds. The co-metabolic degradation was further demonstrated as the decrease in 
concentrations of the compounds and at the same time methane oxidation (methane consumption). 
Moreover, this study was set up with a much longer contact time compared to the normal contact 
time of a DWTP in Denmark. The contact time in rapid sand filters for a DWTP in Denmark is between 
7.5 to 12 minutes (Winther, et al. 2010). In this study, the contact time was either 13/14 or 33 days 
for the two DWTPs. However, this study demonstrated that the treatment at Borre DWTP was 
actually able to reduce the concentration of DPC to comply with the guideline values of pesticides in 
drinking water.  Moreover, the concentrations used in the microcosms were much higher (100 times) 
than registered and found in real life. However, the filter sand of Borre DWTP was only collected at 
the top of the filter sand and the removal potential throughout each of the three sand filters may 
variate from the top to the bottom. The influence of adding Cu to the microcosms include 
uncertainties as the outlet water of Stenholt DWTP already contained copper which might have been 
in such a high concentration that it was able to inhibit the sMMO that was expected to be important 
in the co-metabolism of the methanotrophs. 
As the method of the analysis to measure the concentrations of pesticides with LC-MS/MS was newly 
developed, the method needs more development to increase the accuracy. The expected 
concentrations at the start of the experiments did not comply with the measured ones. This could be 
due to uncertainties of the method analysis as if comparing the LC-MS/MS results with the ones of 
the analysis for the 14C-labelling pesticides, these concentrations are much closer to the expected 
concentrations. Furthermore, the linear regression showed a better correlation.    
The results of this study create the basics for further investigations of the ability of the microbial 
community at DWTPs to degrade the pesticides. The suggestions for further research investigations 
if the results of this study should be applied in situ could be  
 
1) evidence for the presence of methanotrophic bacteria including the types of the biomass in 
the aeration tank and sand filters by quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) 
2) to estimate the exact level of methane to enhance the removal by the methanotrophs 
 
Moreover, a better understanding and knowledge of which pesticides are able to be removed at the 
DWTPs. The ability of other microbes in the sand filters to degrade pesticides e.g. AOBs as the AMO 
is comparable to MMO for the MOBs.  
However, if further investigations, as recommended above, contribute to the evidence of microbial 
degradation of pesticides at DWTPs. This solution has the potential to be a much more sustainable 
and economic efficient instead of the current solution by closing the contaminated AWs.

7. Conclusion 
The removal of the herbicides bentazone, chloridazone, DPC and MDPC was examined of two DWTPs, 
Stenholt and Borre, in Denmark. It was investigated how methane and copper influence the co-
metabolic removal of the compounds by microbial removal assumed by the methanotrophic bacteria. 
Moreover, investigated if the microbial removal of pesticides already occurs at a DWTP (Borre). For 
both DWTPs removal of all compounds occur. The removal of methane was in the range 5.63-7.91 
mg/day/microcosm and 0.88-4.41 mg/day/microcosm of methane for Stenholt and Borre DWTP, 
respectively.  Oxygen was consumed as well in all the microcosms with addition of methane. At 
Stenholt DWTP, the removal rate of bentazone in the microcosms with addition of methane was 0.42 
ng/h/g-biomass (day 0-13) and 1.91 ng/h/g-biomass (day 21-33), Stenholt DWTP. For the microcosms 
with addition of both methane and copper the removal rate was 0.36 ng/h/g-biomass (day 0-13) and 
0.73 ng/h/g-biomass (day 21-33), Stenholt DWTP. Furthermore, it was not possible to observe any 
influence of the copper due to release of copper from the tap at Stenholt DWTP. The removal rate 
was 0.60 ng/h/g-biomass, 1.20 ng/h/g-biomass, 4.35 ng/h/g-biomass in the microcosms with 14C-
bentazone (day 0-13), chloridazone and DPC (day 0-33), respectively for Stenholt DWTP.  
At Borre DWTP, the highest removal of the compounds chloridazone, DPC and MDPC was found In 
microcosms with filter sand from the primary filter of 0.02-0.10 ng/h/g-sand. For microcosms with 
sand from the tertiary filter, the removal rate was a bit lower (0.01-0.06 ng/h/g-sand). However, the 
transformation yields of the chlorinated compounds were in the range 10-4-10-2 mole 
compound/mole CH4 which complies with reported values of chlorinated compounds in microcosms 
with filter sand from Borre DWTP. Furthermore, the microbial degradation of DPC and MDPC was 
demonstrated at Borre DWTP. Overall, this study demonstrated a potential for microbial degradation 
of bentazone, chloridazone, DPC and MDPC at the two DWTPs. The relation between the methane 
consumption might be due to methane oxidation by the microbes when pesticides were degraded. 
This study then demonstrated that methane is able to enhance the potential of microbial removal in 
both aeration tank and sand filters at DWTPs in Denmark.
