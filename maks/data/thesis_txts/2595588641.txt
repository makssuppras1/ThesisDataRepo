ABSTRACT 
As the floating offshore wind industry prepares for utility-scale commercialisation, efforts to 
develop performance- and cost-efficient mooring systems have begun to accelerate. Currently 
deployed solutions have been adopted directly from the petroleum industry and have not been 
optimised for the wind sector. The aim of this project was to design and evaluate mooring 
configurations for floating wind turbines in shallow waters. This work was carried out in 
collaboration with Stiesdal Offshore Technologies A/S for their TetraSub® floater concept. 
To serve as a foundation for the design process, a review of existing and proposed mooring 
systems was undertaken to identify current trends and promising solutions. Consequently, 
three mooring candidates were selected: a conventional chain catenary and two synthetic fibre 
systems consisting of taut anchor legs and submerged buoys, with one being an improvement 
over the other through the addition of a clump weight. These moorings were designed for the 
DTU 10 MW reference wind turbine, such that for a driving load of twice the rated thrust, the 
excursion was limited to half the site depth. Each candidate was characterised statically, 
forming the basis of design optimization codes. 
The mooring designs were first optimized for radial footprint and evaluated quasi-statically in 
HAWC2®. The performance and its sensitivity to the direction of load application was 
investigated, and it was found that the catenary exhibited the highest compliance but for the 
largest footprint. The other two systems were significantly stiffer for much smaller footprint 
radii, though the clump weighted version exhibited slightly increased compliance due to the 
favourable interaction between buoy and clump mass. Moreover, the effect of the main design 
parameters on their respective mooring systems was studied by mapping the parameter space 
and exploring the feasible regions bounded by the physical and design constraints.  
A costing model was also developed based on material and acquisition considerations, and thus 
used to optimize the mooring designs for cost. The resulting configurations were evaluated for 
standard design load cases representing normal operation and extreme conditions. For this 
dynamic analysis the LIFES50+ OO-Star Wind Floater Semi 10MW concept was considered 
instead of the TetraSub® due to model availabilities. The moorings performed well under 
operation conditions, but for the extreme state the line tensions developed were excessive. 
Diagnostics indicated that the moorings were inadequate for the effective load applied due to 
wave forcing and tidal current. Moreover, possible resonance effects were identified. Design 
modifications to both platform and moorings were considered as a means of improving the 
system performance, though time constraints meant that a viable solution could not be found.  
The project thus delivered a design cost-optimization model that is sufficient for quasi-static 
analysis, but exhibits breaking loads under extreme dynamic conditions. Though the diagnostic 
studies did not identify a functional system, they provide insight and preview the effect of 
several design modifications. This should serve as a starting point for further development.

INTRODUCTION 
As the urgency brought about by climate change mounts, Europe is increasingly looking 
towards achieving carbon neutrality within the next decades. A cornerstone of this green 
transition is the growing penetration of sustainable energy sources. By the end of 2020, it has 
been estimated that renewable energy will account for 35% of the European Union’s electricity 
mix with around 16% coming from the wind [1]. This evolution is detailed in Figure 1 broken 
down by source, and it is evident that wind energy has become the fastest growing segment of 
Europe’s renewable energy production, supplying a total of 417 TWh in 2019 from 205 GW of 
cumulative installed capacity [2]. Moreover, according to estimates presented by WindEurope 
in [3], this cumulative number could grow to anywhere between 250 and 280 GW by 2023 
when considering realistic economic and political scenarios.   
 
1.1 
OFFSHORE WIND ENERGY 
To date, onshore installations account for the vast proportion of wind energy production, as 
land sites are more accessible and cheaper to develop. In fact, only 11% of Europe’s installed 
capacity is located offshore, though this accounts for 16% of production as of 2019 due to the 
substantially higher capacity factors off the coast [2]. In fact, there are a host of advantages 
associated with offshore installations: primarily the stronger and steadier wind resource, 
greater political and social acceptance due to reduced visual and acoustic pollution, and the 
vastly larger developable area. Nevertheless, three quarters of new installations were onshore 
in 2019 and this is not projected to change going forward in the immediate future.  
However, in order to meet the European Union’s sustainability goals and carbon-neutral target 
by 2050, the European Commission has estimated that up to 450 GW of offshore capacity could 
be required [4]. This would make offshore wind a crucial energy pillar, potentially covering 30% 
of European demand. Though this vision is feasible due to the continent having one of the best 
offshore wind resources globally, a host of challenges exist, amongst them being the allocation 
of this capacity. According to a report prepared by BVG Associates [4], the optimal placement 
is illustrated in Figure 2, with the necessary targets for each of the sea basins.

1.1.1 PLATFORM STABILITY 
On the subject of stability, much work has been done, with a vast number of potential platforms 
investigated. Consequently, a number of floating foundations now possess a high technology 
readiness level. Such platforms can be categorised into the three main substructure designs 
shown in Figure 5, each characterised by its own stability mechanism.  
 
The semi-submersible type is stabilised through a large waterplane area restoring moment, and 
is generally comprised of a number of large interconnected vertical columns that can be 
ballasted to achieve the required draught. Wind turbines can be installed from the quayside, 
and subsequently towed out to a site of potentially intermediate depth.  One prime example 
of this concept is the WindFloat® [7] platform which has been deployed in pilot farms off the 
coasts of both Portugal and Scotland. The second design type is the spar and it achieves stability 
through a large ballast placed at a significant depth below the water line. In fact, these 
structures tend to be slender and cylindrical, as in the example of the Hywind® [8] concept 
presently installed in Scotland and Norway. Though simple in design, spar-buoys are only 
applicable for deep waters. Finally, the tension-leg platform (TLP) is mooring stabilised, 
whereby a lightweight structure is moored in place by taut lines that restrict motion 
significantly. However, these platforms are difficult and expensive to install, and consequently 
no TLP has been deployed to date at utility scale. Nevertheless, some concepts are coming to 
fruition and will be deployed at the Provence Grand Large [9] and Eoliennes Flottantes de 
Groix [10] farms. There does, however, also exist a fourth substructure type referred to as the 
barge. This has the same restoring mechanism as the semi-submersible, but as it utilises a 
significantly larger waterplane area, the barge is characterised by a very low draught. An 
example is the Damping Pool® [11] concept deployed off the coast of France.

1.1.2 LEVELISED COST OF ENERGY 
The other major barrier to the large scale utilisation of floating wind turbines has been cost.  
Due to the novelty of the technology, its increased complexity relative to bottom fixed 
foundations, and the inherently higher risk, floating offshore concepts have been characterised 
by high levelised costs of energy. The levelised cost of energy (LCOE) is a measure of capital and 
operational expenditure normalised against the energy that the system is estimated to produce 
over the course of its lifetime. In 2013, Bjerkseter and Ågotnes [5] estimated the LCOE ranges 
for various floating and fixed bottom foundations, summarising their results in Figure 6. By 
considering the floating concepts alone, an approximate LCOE of 145 €/MWh can be estimated. 
Comparing this value to that for monopile foundations, it is clear that floating wind is 
significantly more expensive. Moreover, when compared against the costs of other types of 
renewable energy technologies as collected by Thomsen [12] in Figure 7, floating wind power 
is also one of the more costly sectors. Consequently, there have been substantially cheaper 
resources for developers to exploit up until now. 
 
 
However, in recent years there have been major cost reductions in both onshore and bottom 
fixed offshore wind energy sectors, and it is reasonable to assume that floating systems will 
follow the same trend. In fact, by 2050 the cost of floating wind is expected to decrease by

1.2 
MOORING SYSTEMS 
A mooring system is the means by which a floating structure, be it a ship or a turbine platform, 
is confined to a specific location. Historically, moorings were necessary for short periods of time 
in order to anchor sea-going vessels. However with the advent of the petroleum industry, 
permanent mooring became a necessity. There are various mooring systems operating on 
different mechanics, but ultimately all satisfy the same requirements. These are discussed by 
Harris et al. [15] in the context of energy production systems and can be summarised as follows:  
 The floating structure must be kept at its station, to within specified excursion limits, under 
all metocean conditions including extreme scenarios and tidal variation if applicable. 
 The allowable motion of the platform must not permit tension loads to be developed in the 
electrical transmission cable. 
 Sufficient compliance is necessary to ensure that mooring and anchor loads do not exceed 
breaking and holding limits respectively.  
 All components should exhibit adequate strength and durability to last over the operational 
lifetime of the system and must also allow for inspection and maintenance. 
 The mooring system should not adversely affect the efficiency of the generator. 
1.2.1 MAIN CATEGORIES 
As will be discussed in greater depth in Chapter 2, there are a vast number of different mooring 
systems. However, they can be broadly divided into three main categories, shown in Figure 8: 
catenaries, taut spread systems, and tension legs or tendons [16]. A fourth category, not shown, 
is single point moorings (SPM) whereby a platform is attached at a single point, thus allowing it

to weathervane into incident waves, wind, etc. [17]. However, the point of attachment is itself 
a floating structure and this is moored via a system that falls within the main categories. 
 
The catenary consists of a long slack line hanging freely from the point of connection (fairlead) 
down to the seabed. From touchdown to the anchor, the line rests along the seabed. The line 
is usually heavy chain with significant weight, and once the platform moves, the chain is lifted 
up thus increasing the suspended mass. This in turn increases the tension at the fairlead, with 
the horizontal component providing a restoring force that returns the platform to equilibrium 
condition. This is referred to as the catenary effect and is an example of geometric stiffness 
(how much force is required to change the line geometry).  The taut spread system on the other 
hand, touches down at an angle. This reduces footprint, though anchors must be capable of 
resisting vertical loads. In this case the restoring force is elasticity of the line. The tension leg is 
a special case of the taut spread whereby the lines are vertical.  
1.2.2 MOORING COMPONENTS 
Typically, the mooring line is comprised of a number of components, these being the mooring 
line itself, the anchor and the connectors. In more complex systems, there are also buoyancy 
elements (buoys) and clump weights. The choice of components and their combination 
ultimately defines the mooring system and is based on the requirements and behaviour sought. 
Mooring Line 
 
There are three types of line available for use in mooring 
systems: chain, wire rope and synthetic fibre rope. Chain is 
by far the most common, and comes in different diameters 
and grades. The diameter defines the thickness of the links 
and hence the weight of the chain. Whereas the grade 
specifies the proof strength and stiffness of the line, and can 
range from grade R3 to R5 for marine applications [18]. Overall, chains exhibit good resistance 
to seabed abrasion and bending properties [15]. Additionally, chain links can be stud or studless 
as illustrated in Figure 9. Stud link chain is utilised in moorings that must be reset numerous 
times, since the link’s range of motion is restricted by the stud hence avoiding chain 
entanglement. However, this stud also increases surface contact between the links resulting in 
higher abrasion which could potentially lead to failure. Consequently, studless link chain is used

for permanent moorings [16]. The second product type is steel or wire rope, which has a lower

it tends to suffer more from damage, material fatigue and corrosion due to strand abrasion. 
Moreover, extreme bending should be avoided. 
The most recent category is the synthetic fibre ropes which have become increasingly popular 
due to their light weight and superior elasticity. On the other hand, they have relatively little 
resistance to wear and tear without coatings and their structural performance deteriorates 
over time [5]. They are characterised by significant change in axial stiffness post-installation; 
thus requiring re-tensioning [15], whilst the lack of long term experience using these ropes 
means higher safety factors are applied in the range of up to 25% more. Moreover, fish-bites 
and marine growth can be an issue and it is important to ensure that lines are kept well above 
the seabed to avoid particulate intrusion between the fibres which introduce stress points and 
sources of abrasion [19].  
Common fibre materials are high modulus polyethylene (e.g. Dyneema®), aramid, polyester, 
and nylon. High modulus polyethylene (HMPE) and aramid is very strong and stiff with 
properties comparable to chain. Polyester is more elastic though also less durable, whereas 
nylon is the most compliant due its lower stiffness. However, the latter is still subject to 
investigation as a viable fibre as wet nylon rope has relatively short cyclic fatigue. This being 
said, modifications to nylon strand construction have yielded an impressive increase in fatigue 
performance, though not yet on par with polyester [20]. Moreover, nylon also suffers from 
internal abrasion due to strand rubbing [21]. Overall, polyester is the most widely used as its 
properties are close to nylon whilst exhibiting better abrasion resistance and fatigue 
performance. It is also cheaper than HMPE and aramid.  
Line Accessories 
In order to connect the mooring line to both the anchor and platform fairlead, as well as to 
other segments within the same line, connectors are required. Various products exist serving 
different purposes and line types. The most common are shackles consisting of a bow closed 
by a pin, and typically used with chains. Swivel shackles are more complex since they allow the 
two connection points to twist counter to each other thus relieving line torque. This contrasts 
with rigid connections such as H-links or tri-plates. Unlike chain, wire and synthetic ropes 
cannot be locked onto connectors directly and consequently are spliced into eyelets which can 
then be looped around or through a thimble (spool) which acts as the intermediary [16].  
Although not necessarily required, other elements are commonly included along the line such 
as buoys and clump weights. The first serves to introduce buoyancy at the connection point to 
the line, thus lifting it upwards. Whereas the clump mass introduces weight, pulling the line 
down. These elements can be employed alone or in combination to modify line geometry and 
stiffness. They are also used to relieve tension along the line or route it such as to avoid 
obstacles or the seabed. With regards to material, at the scales relevant here the buoy tends 
to be constructed from steel whilst the clump weight can be concrete, steel or cast iron.

Anchors 
The final component of any mooring system is the anchor at the end of the line. These products 
serve to fix the end point to a specific location on the seabed and under no circumstance can 
they be dislodged. Otherwise the moored structure could undergo a permanent displacement 
at best or at worst capsize in the case of a tension leg moored platform. Consequently, anchor 
design and selection is critical to the robustness of the mooring system.  
The choice of anchor is primarily dependant on the depth, soil condition and load to be 
withstood. The main types are shown in Figure 10 and the following discussion is based on 
information contained in the Vryhof Anchoring Manual [16]. 
 
The dead weight or gravity anchor is the simplest and oldest form, and relies on its weight and 
to a lesser degree friction with the seabed. Such anchors are commonly constructed from 
concrete blocks or steel, and are deployed on harder soil types such as sand and hard clays. 
The drag embedment anchor (DEA) and vertical load anchor (VLA) are both installed in the 
same way: by being dragged along the seabed causing them to penetrate and become 
embedded thus accumulating a certain volume of aggregate around them. In this way, the 
holding capacity is achieved through the resistance of the soil in front or above the DEA and 
VLA respectively. However, the DEA can only resist horizontal loads in the direction that it has 
been dragged, though it can be installed in any soil type. The VLA on the other hand can 
withstand omnidirectional load, though to do this it must penetrate much deeper and thus 
requires only the softest of soils (mud).  
Finally, the pile and suction anchor are vertically forced into the seabed either by mechanical 
or gravitational means for the former, or a pressure difference for the latter. Holding capacity 
is thus achieved by friction with the seabed and lateral soil resistance. Both are capable of 
withstanding omnidirectional loads. However, whereas the suction anchor requires fairly soft 
soil, the pile can be installed in harder seabeds, though this entails pile driving.

1.2.3 DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
When designing a mooring system there are some considerations that must be made, especially 
when catering to shallow water sites. The following discussion primarily concerns compliance 
and snap loads.  
Consider a platform subject to environmental loading such that it is driven to move from its 
equilibrium position. This motion is referred to as the excursion, and its amplitude is a function 
of the mooring system employed. Compliance is the degree to which said mooring system 
allows this motion to occur and a sufficient level is important in order to keep line tensions and 
hence anchor loads to a minimum, as severely restricting the excursion requires high restoring 
loads to be developed in the mooring. The necessary compliance can be provided in two ways: 
through variation in the geometry of the mooring configuration and through the elasticity of 
the lines themselves [20].  In the case of a catenary, this change in geometry is the lifting up 
and straightening of the line until the catenary is diagonal between anchor and fairlead. The 
weight of the increase in suspended chain is the mooring load that develops and which the 
platform experiences as a restoring force. The load developed as a consequence of the 
excursion yielded is referred to as the geometrical stiffness of the mooring. Therefore, the 
higher the overall system stiffness, the lower the compliance. 
Moreover, in the case of wave-induced excursion, the motion results in alternating slack-taut 
conditions in the catenary, with a large horizontal tension component at the fairlead followed 
by a mainly vertical component once the platform is brought back to the equilibrium position 
in time for the next incident wave. This cycle causes the platform to experience snap-type 
impact loads as the tension in the mooring line spikes as it re-engages from equilibrium. This 
results in a discontinuity in the mooring stiffness, during which the shift to taut condition can 
cause shock loads up to thrice the cyclic tension [22]. Such impact loading can be detrimental 
to the lifetime of the mooring system and the operation of wind turbines due to the resulting 
acceleration at the hub. Consequently, slack is ideally avoided by pre-tensioning the lines. 
Continuing with the example of the standard chain catenary, the implications of the above are 
discussed as follows. In deep waters, the main concern is the weight of the suspended chain, 
which results in a significant vertical load on the platform at equilibrium, thus requiring over-
engineering for no benefit as the suspended mass provides no restoring force. Thus, synthetic 
line can be used instead to transverse the water column as it is lighter, with chain reserved just 
for the resting segment in order to provide the catenary effect and exploit the superior abrasion 
qualities of steel. This results in the same geometric stiffness but higher line elasticity due to 
the synthetic fibre rope.  
However, in the case of a shallow water site, the suspended length of the catenary line is much 
shorter and hence becomes taut relatively quickly, beyond which the line tension increases 
substantially for small excursion increments [23]. That is, the compliance is reduced. As a result, 
an extraordinarily long resting segment relative to the depth is necessitated, with the footprint-
to-depth ratio being significantly higher than that for a deeper water site for the same excursion

requirement [24].  Moreover, such a length of chain would be both expensive and also subject 
to higher rates of wear as a result of friction. The latter is a consequence of the greater 
incidence of segment lift-up resulting from the reduced curvature of the suspended line [19]. 
That is, lift-up will occur for smaller excursions and resulting in more frequent snap loads for 
less severe environmental conditions. Some mitigation can be achieved by using synthetic fibre 
rope in the water column to provide lower stiffness and hence increase the system compliance 
and the line’s ability to absorb dynamic motions through axial extension without causing 
excessive tension [25]. However, this would have only a limited improvement given the shallow 
depth and the footprint would be reduced only marginally.   
The above considerations imply that there exist serious issues with the use of catenaries for 
shallow water mooring and thus, in order to avoid snap loads, maintain compliance and achieve 
a reasonable footprint, alternative configurations must instead be deployed.

1.4 
PROJECT OVERVIEW 
In the context of the nascent floating offshore wind industry, currently deployed mooring 
systems are a product of the marine and petroleum sectors and have not been optimised for 
floating turbines. The development of performance- and cost-efficient mooring configurations 
has been a serious challenge and is becoming increasingly important as the industry gears up 
for large-scale commercial projects. This is especially critical for shallow water sites, where the 
issues exhibited by conventional catenary lines are magnified. 
The scope of this project is thus to design and evaluate efficient mooring configurations suitable 
for deployment in shallow water for floating offshore wind turbines. This work was carried out 
in collaboration with Stiesdal Offshore Technologies A/S, and is driven by their interest in 
shallow water sites for their TetraSub® floater concept. 
1.4.1 OBJECTIVES 
To achieve the scope, a design process was undertaken involving the conceptualisation of 
several mooring candidates, the development of quasi-static design-optimizing models and the 
subsequent simulation and evaluation of the resulting systems through both quasi-static and 
dynamic analyses. The mooring configurations were designed for maximum excursion, 
footprint and cost. The primary objectives of the project are thus: 
 Review existing and proposed mooring systems and current research in the field, to serve 
as a basis for the design process. 
 Characterise several mooring candidates for the TetraSub® floater and model them 
numerically from a static perspective. 
 Perform a quasi-static analysis of the mooring performance and explore the design 
parameter space. 
 Develop a cost model based on the main drivers in order to generate cost-efficient systems.  
 Evaluate the resulting mooring designs through the analysis of their dynamic response to 
standard and design-driving loadcases. 
1.4.2 REPORT STRUCTURE 
This project report is structured as follows. Chapter 2 presents a review of the current mooring 
context. Chapter 3 identifies and describes the shallow water site considered. Chapter 4 details 
the design criteria and derives size-optimization models for the mooring candidate systems.

 2.2 MOORING CONFIGURATIONS 
In order to design improved mooring systems, the current state of the research and knowledge 
base will first be reviewed. In this way, promising proposals can be identified and taken as the 
basis for further development. The following discussion mainly centres on selected proposed 
mooring configurations found in literature, though already deployed systems and traditional 
types are also presented to give context and for the sake of completeness. 
Although, some work has been to date carried out on mooring development in relation to 
floating wind turbines, the industry has focussed largely on the platform. On the other hand, 
much work has been carried out to optimise mooring systems for wave energy convertors 
(WEC) over the past decade. Without going into the physics or merits of this technology, such 
convertors exhibit lower energy yields and hence cost savings in construction, installation and 
mooring have been the primary areas of focus in order to achieve economic viability by 
lowering an otherwise excessive LCOE [12] illustrated in Figure 7. In fact, Martinelli et al. [38] 
estimate that the mooring cost amounts to 20-30% for the total WEC cost. Consequently a large 
body of research exists studying mooring alternatives in shallow water, where these devices 
are usually deployed. In most cases, such configurations are single point moorings as the WEC 
is allowed to vane into the wave direction. However, the higher energy yield of floating wind 
turbines may well warrant a triplication of the mooring system to achieve a fixed position.  
2.2.1 LINE TYPE MOORINGS 
The concepts discussed here have been grouped by similarity to allow the reader a better 
understanding of progression. Consider those illustrated in Figure 16, where the restoring 
mechanism is dependent on the line material and geometry.

Type (a) is the standard chain catenary whereby the restoring is provided by the lift-up of the 
drag chain and hence the increase in suspended mass. This is a commonly used and mature 
method of mooring. It is relatively easy to install. However it is heavy, material intensive and in 
shallow water exhibits low compliance [24]. Hence, higher footprint-to-depth ratios are 
required to avoid anchor lift. An improvement to this is Type (b) which makes use of synthetic 
rope in the water column. The line at equilibrium that hangs suspended has no function other 
than to connect the platform to the drag length, and hence provides no restoring load. Thus, 
the use of synthetic fibres instead of chain allows for a reduction in vertical loads on the 
platform. This is increasingly important for deeper waters. Moreover, such ropes are more 
flexible and can absorb dynamic motion by extending for lower tensions [25]. Snap loads are 
also reduced due to higher line strain prior to drag chain lift-up, hence smoothening the 
transition between restoration mechanisms. However, as discussed by Fonseca and Pascoal 
[19], synthetic lines are sensitive to UV exposure and marine growth and consequently, it is 
common practise to use a short length of chain to transverse the splash zone. Moreover, such 
ropes are also much more prone to wear from abrasion and particulate intrusion, and so the 
inclusion of buoyancy elements at the connection point between synthetic and chain are 
deployed to ensure that the terminal remains adequately clear of the seabed.  
Regardless of the line material used, Types (c) and (d) present the concept of introducing 
intermediate buoys along the water column to modify line geometry. As can be noted, line 
segments closer to the device have lower gradients, resulting in reduced vertical loads and 
higher compliance. Consequently, as presented by Vicente et al. [39], lower tensions are 
developed for the same excursion, decreasing with increasing number of buoys. Alternatively, 
the buoyancy component can be distributed over a number of smaller consecutive buoys as 
illustrated in Type (e). This results in the geometry of (c) being modified to a lazy wave 
configuration, though a steep wave can also be achieved whereby the line rises directly from 
the anchor attachment. As discussed by Barltrop [17], this format reduces both tension and 
local bending in the line. 
Numerous other configurations have been considered in [40], where Ringsberg et al. propose 
mooring systems for WEC arrays. Type (f) consists of a taut synthetic fibre line, thus introducing 
vertical loads on the anchor. The primary restoring mechanism here is the elasticity of the line. 
However, in order to reduce shock loads in an efficient way, the line is weighted along its length. 
Consequently, this format approximates to a synthetic catenary where geometric stiffness also 
contributes. Type (g) on the other hand is purely a taut line where the elastic stiffness is the 
only restoring mechanism. Thus, the authors indicate that Type (f) is an improvement over 
Type (g) except for the increased vertical load that may negatively impact the efficiency of the 
oscillating column WEC under their consideration. Both systems proposed above are examples 
of taut leg moorings.  
Finally, Type (h) represents a tension leg mooring and has been included here for the sake of 
completeness. The taut lines are vertical and provide restoration through their stiffness.

2.2.2 MULTI-LINE TYPE MOORINGS 
The second group of systems focuses more on the restorative effects introduced by non-line 
components, primarily buoys and clump weights (masses). Though line mass and elasticity are 
still important aspects, the vertical loads of these bulk components increase the efficiency of 
the mooring system and allow for reduced footprints and improved characteristics. Figure 17 
illustrates the selected configurations which are discussed below.  
 
Type (i) is commonly referred to as a Single Anchor Leg Mooring (SALM) [15], whereby a large 
submerged or surface buoy is tethered by a taut line to a vertically loaded anchor. The platform 
is connected to the buoy by means of a hawser. Restoring motion is provided by the elastic 
stiffness of the hawser and the variation in the tether tension due to the change in the buoy 
depth. As the platform undergoes excursion, the buoy is dragged downwards and outwards, 
resulting in an increase of the buoyancy force component perpendicular to the tether. This acts 
as a restoring load by driving a return of the tether to its relatively vertical position at 
equilibrium. In order to increase compliance at small excursions, softer synthetic fibre ropes 
are used in the hawser (typically polyester or nylon), whereas the overall stiffness of the system 
is increased in step with the volume of the buoy. The line material of the tether is dependent 
on the mooring loads and other cost and stiffness considerations.

2.2.3 CONCLUDING REMARKS 
This concludes the review of existing mooring configurations. However, it is important to note 
that the discussion presented is not exhaustive, as only systems with the potential to be utilised 
for a platform restricted from weathervaning were selected for inclusion. Additional mooring 
types can be found in the discussions in both [38] and [15], and include turret moorings and 
piles. The former involves fixing a small buoyant platform (turret) and connecting the energy 
generating platform to it by means of a hawser setup. Alternatively, said turret could also be 
integrated directly into the platform itself as has been proposed for the X1 Wind PivotBuoy 
concept [48]. On the other hand, piles are rigid-type moorings driven into the seabed and also 
allow for weathervaning. This is however limited to shallow waters only. Moreover, dynamic 
positioning systems are another possibility and include the use of servo-controlled winches and 
even thrusters. 
A final remark concerns the use of anchors. These have been omitted from the above discussion 
even though they are critical components of the mooring system. The reason for this is that the 
number of options are limited to those presented in Section 1.2 and their choice is driven by 
the mooring configuration selected. Generally, catenary type systems make use of gravity or 
drag anchors as vertical loads are not an issue. For mooring types with vertical loads on the 
anchor such as Types (h, i and m), VLAs or piles becomes necessary.

WAVE HUB TEST SITE 
For the purpose of this work, a shallow water site with publicly available metocean data was 
required to provide an environmental context for the design and evaluation process of the 
mooring systems. To this end, the Wave Hub test site off the northern coast of Cornwall, UK 
was selected. This site is characterised by a depth and tidal range that are similar to 
commercially sensitive areas under consideration by Stiesdal Offshore Technologies for the first 
deployment of the TetraSub® floater concept. 
 
Wave Hub was developed to provide a technologically advanced open access site for the testing 
of floating renewable energy technologies with the opportunity of exploiting the strong ocean 
resource of the eastern Atlantic. It was designed as a plug-in facility with four test berths and a 
power grid connection with export capacity of 48 MW [49]. The site plan is shown in Figure 22 
along with the cable route and locations of the measurement buoys. As of March 2020, 
New Power reports [50] that the site and its grid connection point have been put up for sale 
with the preferred option of being developed into a floating wind farm.  
3.1 
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
By virtue of its location, the site is directly exposed to the Atlantic Ocean from the west and 
south west and consequently the environmental conditions are dominated by these directions, 
as will be seen in subsequent sections. The other directions are relatively sheltered due to the 
Cornish and Welsh coasts. Moreover, the site is subject to tides and hence large mean water 
level variation. The Wave Hub is located a distance of 16 km offshore and is characterised by

CHAPTER 4 
MOORING DESIGN 
In order to develop efficient mooring systems for the TetraSub® floater concept, a design 
process was undertaken involving conceptualisation, quasi-static characterisation and analysis, 
cost optimization and finally dynamic evaluation. The quasi-static component deals only with 
line geometries, tensions and mean environmental loads, whereas the dynamic part also 
considers frequency loads and inertias. In this chapter the concepts selected will be modelled 
and discussed. Moreover, the solutions presented here are designed for footprint efficiency 
with no regard to cost. The latter will be considered in a later chapter. 
Three mooring candidates were considered. The first is a simple catenary and was chosen to 
provide a base case to be used as a reference for comparison. The second concept introduced 
a buoy component along the line, whilst the third iteration involved the addition of a clump 
weight element. These three systems correspond to Type (a), (i) and (m) in Section 2.2. 
4.1 
DESIGN CRITERIA 
To provide a framework for the process, a set of design conditions was formulated. These 
criteria were driven by considerations based on the TetraSub®, SOT requirements and the 
environmental context. 
The main design condition is that for a steady horizontal load applied to the platform, the 
mooring system should not allow said platform to exceed a set amount. This design load 𝐹𝑑𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑔𝑛 
was taken to be twice the rated thrust of the DTU 10MW RWT, which according to the turbine 
documentation [30] is 1560 kN. On the other hand, the maximum resulting excursion Δ𝑋𝑚𝑎𝑥 
was limited to half the depth of the site considered, that is 52 m for the Wave Hub.

4.1.1 OTHER CONDITIONS 
As with the minimum depth for buoys described above, clump weights were subjected to a 
maximum depth requirement, in order to ensure that they do not touch down on the seabed. 
This would result in de-tensioning of lines and possible embedment in the sediment, leading to 
severe snap loads during re-engagement. Similar design considerations in [44] were used as 
inspiration to apply a minimum clearance ℎ𝑥 of 10% of the clump mass length, between its base 
and the seabed.  
An additional limit was placed on the design of both buoys and clump weights. Since these 
components are here implemented as cylindrical, their diameters were constrained by SOT. 
The company’s policy is that no component should have a larger diameter that is greater than 
that of the turbine tower, since if this can be manufactured and transported to site, then all 
other components can be done so in the same way. According to the DTU 10MW RWT 
documentation [30] the tower base diameter is envisioned to be 8.3 m. Consequently, this 
value was taken to be the upper limit applied to both the diameters of buoys and clump masses.  
Finally, a condition on line tension was imposed. This is relevant to Candidates 2 and 3, and 
involves the requirement that all lines other than the tether be at least 10% of their tension at 
equilibrium. This is applied to avoid slack occurring in the lines under retraction conditions 
(when rear mooring folds up in the face of the advancing platform). As the tethers are taut legs, 
this criteria is deemed unnecessary in their regard. 
4.1.2 DESIGN ASSUMPTIONS 
In order to simplify the design and subsequent analysis, a number of assumptions were made 
throughout, at different stages of the process. They are gathered together here for clarity. 
 The design load is applied such that no moments develop on the platform 
 All other environmental loads are ignored 
 Mooring lines connect at the still water line 
 The platform is fixed along the vertical axis and thus the draught cannot change due to 
variation in the vertical mooring loads 
 Mooring lines have constant weight per unit length and are inelastic elements 
 Line dynamics are ignored 
 Connectors between lines, components and the platform are ignored both in terms of 
weight and dimension 
 The seabed is horizontal with no friction 
 Anchors are fixed points located on the seabed surface

4.4 
CANDIDATE 1: CATENARY 
To provide a reference case for the mooring systems developed, a simple catenary was devised. 
As highlighted in Chapter 2, the catenary mooring is the most widely used form of station 
keeping and has been employed on most floating wind turbine platforms. It will also be used to 
moor the TetraSpar® prototype. This system of mooring is widely understood, with standard 
formulations detailing its characterisation. A comprehensive review of the mathematics can be 
found in Barltrop [17] which forms the basis of the catenary model developed below. Note that 
henceforth the catenary mooring candidate may be referred to as the CATN system. 
4.4.1 OPTIMUM SIZING MODEL 
Consider a platform floating in still water of depth ℎ, with fairleads located at the still water line 
(SWL). A catenary line of length 𝐿 hangs freely from the fairlead down to the seabed, where it 
lies resting until the anchor. The horizontal distance between fairlead and anchor at equilibrium 
is 𝑋𝑜 and the mooring system is comprised of a total of three lines with equiangular (120°) 
spread. The setup is illustrated in Figure 29 (top row). Note that the model has been formulated 
such that the dimensions of the platform need not be considered.  
When an external load 𝐹𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑 is applied, the mooring system must counteract through the 
development of a restoring force 𝑅𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑. This is generated through the modification of the 
geometry of the catenary line, whereby resting line is lifted off the seabed, increasing the 
suspended weight, and thus the fairlead tension. In the context of this work, the external force 
is the design load 𝐹𝑑𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑔𝑛 given in Section 4.1.  
Although the assumed horizontal force can be applied from any direction, for this and all 
subsequent models, it was taken to bisect two mooring lines as shown in Figure 29 (bottom 
right). This will be referred to as bisectional loading, whereas application in the opposite 
direction is aligned loading. In the latter case, 𝐹𝑑𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑔𝑛 is applied along a mooring line, and the 
excursion is yielded by the geometric and axial extension of that line, which thus develops a 
restoring force.  When loaded bisectionally, the excursion is yielded by two lines and is now 
also a function of the change in the angle of orientation of the moorings. That is, the bisected 
angle 𝛾 < 60° under the design load. As this angle narrows, a portion of the excursion is provided 
by a component of 𝑋𝑜 and hence the lines need to yield less load-inducing extension than the 
aligned line, for the same excursion.  Consequently, the same restoring load is achieved for a 
greater displacement under bisectional loading, and thus it is this case that must be designed 
for to ensure the excursion limit is respected.

4.5 
CANDIDATE 2: SINGLE ANCHOR LEG MOORING 
Numerous issues have been identified with the catenary mooring system. As discussed in 
previous Sections 1.2.3 and 2.2.1, when deployed in shallow water, catenary lines exhibit low 
compliance, high snap loads and require large footprints.  
In order to develop more efficient mooring systems, inspiration was drawn from current 
research within the field of station keeping. Based on the high interest shown in the literature 
reviewed, it was decided to investigate a single anchor leg mooring system as a means of 
mooring a floating wind platform. This type of mooring has shown potential for load and cost 
reduction, and overall efficiency, especially when used in conjunction with synthetic fibre lines 
[12] [58]. Since, in this case the floater cannot be allowed to weathervane, a tri-moor setup was 
considered, similar to the three line system deployed for the WaveEL demonstrator 
documented in [41]. In this way, it was hoped to achieve a significantly reduced footprint 
relative to the catenary.  Note that henceforth, this mooring candidate may be referred to as 
the SALM system. 
4.5.1 OPTIMUM SIZING MODEL 
Unlike the catenary system, there does not exist a standard governing set of equations for the 
single anchor leg mooring. However, various quasi-static investigations, such as those by Pecher 
et al. [59] and Lou et al. [47], have described similar setups mathematically. Their same 
approach will be employed here in order to characterise the physics and geometry of the SALM 
candidate, and thus develop a size optimizer.  Note that the model described below is the result 
of an iterative process, and only the final version is presented. However, commentary is 
provided alongside in order to discuss key aspects of the evolution of the model.

In a similar fashion to the CATN candidate, consider three line conditions: initial, design and 
retraction. By deriving their static force equilibriums, the geometric relations, and the 
interaction between the different line scenarios, the mooring can be adequately characterised. 
Consider the same dimensionless floater moored in still water of depth ℎ by a tri-moor setup, 
with a horizontal anchor-fairlead distance (AFD) of 𝑋𝑜 at equilibrium. As illustrated in Figure 30, 
the mooring line consists of two taut segments: a relatively horizontal hawser of length 𝐿ℎ and 
an inclined tether of length 𝐿𝑡. The hawser connects the fairlead to a submerged buoy, whilst 
the tether moors said buoy to the vertically loaded anchor. In contrast to previous work where 
the buoy was considered to be a point buoyancy element, here its dimensions are taken into 
account. Given the function of the buoy, it has the potential to be a significantly large 
component and so has substantial influence on the overall line geometry. Consequently, the 
hawser was assumed to attach at buoy mid-height, whilst the tether at its base. To simplify the 
sizing of the buoy, its height was taken to be equal to its diameter 𝐷𝑏.  
The displaced volume of the buoy 𝑉𝑏 results in an upthrust force 𝐹𝑏 which dominates the 
restoring mechanism of the system. As explained in Section 2.2.2 for mooring Type (i), platform 
motion results in a change of the line geometry and subsequently a displacement of the buoy 
farther away from a vertical position. This leads to an increased force component perpendicular 
to the tether and in the restorative direction.  
 
When an external load 𝐹𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑 drives an excursion Δ𝑋, the displacement is governed by the 
stiffness of the mooring. As shown in Figure 30 (bottom left), the loaded line segments 
straighten out relative to each other with increasing angles of inclination. The buoy is dragged 
diagonally downwards and consequently provides the restoring load alongside the line material 
elasticity. The resulting geometric configuration yields an overall horizontal extension of Δ𝑥. 
The relation between the excursion and the line extension for a bisectionally loaded mooring 
system was describe qualitatively for the CATN model and reproduced schematically here.  
In retraction, the rear line folds up such that the tether and buoy tend towards a vertical 
alignment. The buoy reaches its shallowest depth and the tether tension is almost entirely due 
to upthrust. However, the hawser should remain under tension to ensure that the line does not 
go slack. Otherwise, snap loads would occur upon re-tensioning of the line. To achieve this, the 
mooring system is pretensioned at the initial condition, resulting in tether inclination. The angle 
is such that during retraction under the design conditions, the tether does not completely reach 
vertical alignment, ensuring continued tension in the rear hawser. A system without inclined 
tethers is also presented below for the sake of comparison.  
As with the catenary, the scope of the following optimization model is to size the SALM based 
on the design conditions. In this case, for a minimization of the radial footprint 𝑋𝑜, the main 
parameters to be sized are the line lengths, buoy diameter, and angles of inclination. However 
it is important to ensure that line tensions do not exceed the minimum breaking load of their 
material type.

An interesting discussion concerns the buoy size. The SALM(S) has a larger buoy, and this runs 
counter to intuition. At the design condition, a larger buoy should imply a higher restoring load

stiffnesses are similar, it is the SALM(T) that is characterised by the smaller buoy.

hawser tension is small with very low fairlead loads. When an external force is applied to the 
floater, the buoy is displaced from its vertical orientation, resulting in the development of 
restoring forces. This mechanism thus engages only after the load is applied, and hence the 
buoy volume is responsible for almost the entire restoration.  
On the other hand, the SALM(T) is initialised with inclined tethers. This is a result of 
pretensioning done by the installation vessels during deployment. The inclination implies that 
the mooring system already exhibits line-specific restoring loads at equilibrium, which are 
however balanced by the other lines. This is similar to the catenary system. Upon the 
application of external load, the buoy is thus required to ‘merely’ increase the already existing 
restorative load within the bisected mooring lines. It is therefore the augmenting effect 
provided by the pretension to the buoy driven mechanism that allows the buoy volume to be 
sized smaller than that for SALM(S) for the same design condition.

4.6 
CANDIDATE 3: CLUMP WEIGHTED ANCHOR LEG MOORING 
One potential issue with the single anchor leg mooring candidate is the slacking of rear lines for 
more demanding environmental conditions than designed for. The taut variant SALM(T) 
described previously, was devised with an inclined tether at equilibrium in order to avert a loss 
of tension when loaded. However, this inclination and the line pretension can only be a function 
of the expected design load and the resulting excursion. Any load or condition that results in 
an exceedance of this design state, would result in the rear tethers achieving vertical alignment, 
leading to the de-tensioning of the hawsers as in the slack variant. Such a scenario is possible 
not only for larger than expected loadings, but also for variation in the environment that is not 
considered in the quasi-static model. One example is a drop in surface elevation as a 
consequence of long period waves, which would result in premature slacking even before the 
design excursion is reached.  
In order to cater for such eventualities, and possibly lower the mooring footprint further, an 
option is to introduce a clump mass on the hawser line. This would ensure a minimum tension 
at the fairlead. A similar system has already been discussed in Section 2.2.2 as Type (m), where 
it was described as a steep wave mooring configuration by Harris et al. [15]. The application of 
this clump weight was expected to require a smaller tether inclination, as the hawser is 
effectively split into two segments that can fold up in retraction. The resulting system can thus 
be viewed as a SALM system with the addition of a clump mass, here called the clump weighted 
anchor leg mooring and henceforth referred to as the CLMP candidate. 
4.6.1 OPTIMUM SIZING MODEL 
As with Candidate 2, standard governing equations are not given in literature for this mooring 
configuration. Moreover, similar systems to the CLMP have been investigated to a lesser 
degree. However, the approach when formulating a quasi-static model remains the same as 
discussed in [47] and [59], whereby the physics and geometry of the mooring is characterised. 
Consequently, a size optimizer can be developed and is presented below. The following is the 
final version, and the reader may observe close relations with the SALM model, as it is 
ultimately an evolution of the latter.  
The CLMP mooring system is illustrated in Figure 33. The setup is similar to the other two 
candidates, with three line conditions: initial, design and retraction. For the initial case, again 
consider a platform floating in water of depth ℎ with a horizontal AFD of 𝑋𝑂. The SALM 
geometry has now been modified with a clump mass placed at mid-hawser. Consequently, the 
line is divided into two segments: a hawser from the fairlead to the clump mass, denoted by 
subscript ℎ, and an intermediary connecting the latter to the buoy, denoted by subscript 𝑖. The 
orientation of this second line segment is defined by an angle 𝜓 to the vertical at equilibrium. 
For this project, the clump mass was assumed to have the same cylindrical form as the buoy 
such that its diameter 𝐷𝑐 is equal to its height, and a characteristic mass 𝑀𝑐 that results in a 
gravity load 𝐹𝑐 at the point of connection to the lines.

The gravity load introduced by the clump mass forms the basis of the third restoring mechanism 
present in the system, alongside that from the buoy and line elasticity. As discussed in 
Section 2.2.2 for mooring Type (o), the mass can be considered to exhibit similar yet inverted 
physics to the buoy. Upward motion of the clump, as the hawser tends to a horizontal 
alignment, alters the tension components relative to the line, thus creating a restoring load 
back towards the equilibrium position. This gravity load on the hawser hence parallels the 
buoyancy force from the buoy on the tether. For a discussion of the buoy-induced restoring 
mechanism, see Section 4.5.1. Consequently, the CLMP system ensures that the line segments 
are taut under all static scenarios.  
With the above mind, when an external load 𝐹𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑 drives a horizontal excursion Δ𝑋, the loaded 
lines straighten out as shown in Figure 33 (bottom left), resulting in the clump mass being 
dragged diagonally upwards, and buoy diagonally downwards. The change in geometric

balances the design load 𝐹𝑑𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑔𝑛 applied in the bisectional direction as shown in the schematic. 
When the rear line retracts, the system folds up with the line segments tending towards 
vertical. The buoy and clump mass reach their shallowest and deepest depths respectively. 
Furthermore, due to the clump, the hawser and intermediary remain under tension, with the 
downward force ensuring that the tether does not achieve complete vertical alignment.

5.1.2 COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS 
In order to evaluate the mooring candidates effectively, consider their quasi-static performance 
summarised together in Figure 40. Apart from the force-displacement contours, the behaviour 
presented is that for the bisectional loadcase, as this is the design setup in the optimisation 
models. The displacement curves for the slack SALM variant are also included for a more 
comprehensive comparison.  
The main difference in performance between the systems is their stiffness, its development, 
and its dependency on direction. The catenary mooring exhibits the lowest stiffness overall and 
hence the largest excursion, though it is also the most affected by load directionality. 
Conversely, the SALM(T) variant is the stiffest and yields a more rounded displacement contour. 
The reason for this variation stems from systemic mechanics and geometric setup.  
Consider the stiffness-displacement curves. The CATN mooring exhibits the lowest rate of 
change, as it stiffens geometrically due to the increasing length of suspended mass.  On the 
other hand, the taut SALM is governed by the position of the buoy. As it is displaced down along 
a circular orbit about its anchor, the buoy induces an increasing horizontal load in the inclined 
tether for a decreasing horizontal motion component. This drives the geometric stiffness, with 
pre-tension in the system ensuring that the restoring mechanism is active at equilibrium. The 
latter accounts for the steady initialisation of the curve, whilst its absence explains the sudden 
and rapid build-up in stiffness for the SALM(S) variant, which starts from a vertical position with 
a slack hawser. Lastly, the CLMP is a function of both this mechanism and similar physics for 
the clump mass. As the clump provides a smaller vertical load relative to the buoy, the initial 
geometric stiffness is lower until the intermediary and hawser align.  For all systems, the final 
restoring mechanism to engage is the line axial stiffness, though since both chain and 
Dyneema® are extremely stiff, this would result in sharp spikes in the curve gradients. 
Thus, the difference in excursion yielded by the mooring candidates is a result of their 
respective line stiffnesses. Moreover, as illustrated in Figure 37 through Figure 39, the width of 
the force-displacement envelope is more limited for stiffer systems and manifests as a reduced 
dependence on load orientation. This directionality is due to two main reasons. Firstly, a 
mooring that develops geometric stiffness at an increased rate will limit the excursion to a 
greater extent, and when the load is misaligned, the system will develop the necessary 
restoring tension component over a relatively shorter distance. Secondly, as discussed in 
Section 4.4.1 and illustrated in Figure 29 (bottom right), as the spread angle 𝛾 narrows, the 
component required to counter a misaligned load is provided by a larger portion of the line 
tension. Furthermore, a system with a smaller footprint exhibits a relatively larger reduction in 
this spread angle, hence inducing a smaller restoring load in the line and thus excursion. 
Moreover, the catenary is characterised by extensive lengths of resting chain and this requires 
additional load to drag its mass along when the line follows the fairlead. Overall, these 
mechanisms account for the 45% directional difference observed for the CATN in contrast to 
the approximately 28% for the other candidates.

The latter is due entirely to the pre-tension in the hawser. With the addition of the clump

though time constraints on the project limited the possibility of doing so. This being said an 
essential conclusion can be drawn here in that when performing quasi-static modelling and 
designing for maximum excursion, then the bisectional loadcase should be considered. But

5.2 
DESIGN PARAMETER SPACE MAPPING 
To better understand how the different elements of the mooring system affect its overall 
performance, one approach is to map out the space in which they exist and form potential 
mooring solutions. By identifying the feasible regions of such maps and understanding the 
physical and design-imposed bounds, the optimization process and its compromises can be 
explored by both author and reader. 
This exercise was carried out for the SALM and CLMP mooring candidates and was based on 
their footprint-optimized designs presented in Table 9. The catenary was excluded as the 
characterising equations are well understood and given in terms of a very limited number of 
parameters. The slack SALM variant was only presented above for comparison purposes.  
The approach employed to populate the space maps was as follows. First the design parameters 
to be investigated were selected. It is was decided to focus on the primary elements of the 
mooring system which could be represented by a single key parameter and varied 
independently of the others. Though the range of variation must respect physical constraints, 
it was chosen to relax the design limits such that possible solutions outside the feasible regions 
could be explored hence yielding a better understanding of the trends.  
With the axes of the space maps thus defined, the mooring setup was modified to generate the 
map grid. It is important to note that the modifications were not carried out by the optimizer. 
A ‘brute-force’ approach was employed whereby the parameters were altered manually and 
the effect on the geometric setup was driven by simple relations based on the initial line 
condition for the corresponding model. The optimized solution was retained unchanged in the 
grid to illustrate its spatial location. Each design point on the grid was then simulated quasi-
statically in HAWC2® as described in Section 5.1. The space maps could thus be populated with 
the resulting performance.  Note that all maps presented below are valid only for the design 
condition where the mooring system is driven by the design load applied in the bisectional 
direction.

With reference to the footprint-optimized design presented in Table 9 for the SALM(T) variant,

To vary the selected parameters independently and in a consistent manner, some aspect of the 
design had to be fixed. Considering the initial line condition illustrated in Figure 30 (top left), it 
was decided to maintain the position of the geometric centre of the buoy. In this way, only the

is enough line to vertically reach between the anchor/fairlead and the buoy. Meanwhile the 
buoy volume is limited to a maximum of around 449 m3 as previously discussed. Finally, the 
tether must also respect its upper bound defined by Eq.(37) which ensures that the buoy 
remains submerged for the maximum wave in a 3 hour sea state with 50 year return. However, 
the optimized design length already just satisfies this limit. So to explore a wider range of 
variation in the tether, it was decided to relax the constraint such that the buoy is maintained 
below the SWL only. This has the added advantage of allowing the effect of the assumed trough 
depth on the design to be investigated,

CHAPTER 6 
COST MODEL 
The focus till now has been on the technical performance of footprint-optimised mooring 
systems. That is, the minimum footprint required by the candidates considered to satisfy the 
design constraints. However, in practise one of the main factors influencing mooring design 
and eventual adoption is the cost of the system. As discussed in Section 1.1.2, the cost of 
floating wind remains significant, even though it has seen substantial reductions in recent years. 
Thus, ensuring cost-efficiency in mooring systems is becoming increasingly important. In this 
Chapter the attention will shift to the formulation of a cost model with the aim of sizing the 
mooring candidates to the same design constraints but now for minimum cost.  
6.1 
MOORING COSTS 
They are many economic aspects of a mooring system that need to be taken into account when 
costing. As such, these can be organised into four main cost categories:  
 Material 
 Installation  
 Inspection 
 Decommissioning 
Material costs are those directly involved with the manufacture or purchase of the system 
components, i.e. the line, anchors, etc. Apart from acquisition, the cost also includes delivery 
especially for components sourced from outside the immediate area, thus requiring shipping 
or import. Overall, the material costs constitute the bulk of capital expenditure.  
On the other hand, the cost of installation (defined separately from that for the floater) covers 
transport logistics to site and the deployment process. It can be viewed as a function of vessel 
size, vessel fees and time required to install. The size of the installation vessel is the major 
consideration as it defines the deck area and weight capacity available to carry components to 
site, as well as the bollard pull that can be provided during drag embedded anchor installation. 
Though additional specialised equipment may be required for more complex anchorage, such 
as piles or suction anchors. The deployment of such vessels is subject to mobilisation fees as 
well as a day rate, so the ability to transport the entire system in one trip and the ease of 
installation is an important factor, as larger ships cost more but finish the job faster.  
Inspection is an operational cost and involves line maintenance. This includes monitoring the 
integrity of the mooring, inspection regimes and any potential repair required in the event or 
onset of damage. The associated costs with transport to site, service and monitoring 
infrastructure are also taken into account here. 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
Cost Model 
 
68 
 
Lastly there is a cost of decommissioning the mooring system, whereby it is removed at the end 
of the project lifetime. This requires a reversal of the installation process and thus necessitates 
vessels and time. What do with the components and where to dispose of them if they cannot 
be repurposed or recycled must also be considered. 
Another cost worth mentioning here is that as the offshore energy market moves towards 
competitive auctions, the footprint of the turbine mooring system could become increasingly 
important due to seabed leases. In this type of auction bid, potential developers compete on 
the rent per unit acreage for a farm, and thus the area of seabed required per turbine becomes 
a determining factor for the feasibility of a project. One example where this lease type will be 
awarded is the ScotWind Leasing programme [62], only just launched by Crown Estate Scotland 
at the time of writing.  
The scope of the cost model developed for this project was to provide indicative costings for 
each of the mooring system candidates considered, thus allowing for a degree of cost-
optimisation and comparison. A detailed costing exercise was not required. Consequently, 
consideration was limited to material costs only in order to understand how the main line 
elements drive the overall investment. Decommissioning and inspection were deemed to be 
too uncertain and case-specific, whilst the installation cost was assumed to be somewhat 
similar for all systems given the sole use of drag embedded anchors. 
6.2 
LINE COMPONENT COSTINGS 
Each of the mooring candidates considered is comprised of a number of different components, 
but all include: the line, an anchor and appropriate connectors. The line material and 
characteristics has been previously discussed for each candidate and remains unchanged 
throughout the project. The line connectors are material type and dimension specific. Hence, 
apart from an initial selection, these too are unaffected by any changes to the mooring systems.  
The anchor on the other hand will vary according to the estimated design tensions. Moreover, 
though anchors are largely independent of the line material, their application requires 
appropriate seabed conditions as discussed in Section 1.2.2. However, in order to simplify the 
anchor selection process, after deliberation with SOT it was assumed that the sediment type at 
the selected site was suitable for all anchors, consequently allowing a drag embedment anchor 
(DEA) for the catenary and vertical load anchors (VLA) for the SALM and CLMP systems. The 
benefit being that both anchors are sized in the same way. In reality, the VLA requires only the 
softest of soils, i.e. soft clay, and a very deep sediment layer to allow it to penetrate sufficiently 
to develop the required capacity [63]. Suitable seabeds are typically found in much deeper 
waters. This is therefore a significant assumption given the sediment type documented at the 
Wave Hub site, and so in practise a different anchor mechanism would be required, thus 
introducing an additional degree of uncertainty in the investment costs presented here.

6.2.1 ANCHOR SIZING MODELS 
The loads in a mooring system can be identified as quasi-static or dynamic, where the former 
are due to platform displacements induced by the mean environment at low frequency, whilst 
the latter are due to momentum and inertia effects from the platform and mooring motion at 
high frequency. Combined, these loads are referred to as the total dynamic load. To size the 
anchor, the required holding capacity must be determined by applying a factor of safety to the 
load. This is known as a partial load factor [16] and depends on the load type, as it is significantly 
higher for quasi-statics to account for the dynamic effects as well. 
The partial load factor for an ultimate limit state analysis depends on the consequence class 
chosen, here taken as defined by the DNVGL-RP-E301 recommended practice [72]. Given the 
potential for unacceptable consequences due to the loss of a mooring line such as collision or 
umbilical rapture, Class 2 was selected. Since the line tensions determined in the optimisation 
model are quasi-static, then a partial load factor of 2.5 was hence applied. 
As mentioned above, the Stevpris® Mk6 DEA and Stevmanta® VLA were selected for the CATN 
and SALM/CLMP systems respectively. Their holding capacity is each referred to as the ultimate 
holding capacity (UHC) and ultimate pull-out capacity (UPC). These values can be used to 
characterise the anchors by means of the sizing charts given in the Vryhof Anchoring 
Manual [16], and reproduced in Figure 49 assuming the most suitable sediment.

6.4.1 TOTAL COST SENSITIVITY TO PRICE VARIATION 
Consider the total cost of the mooring systems presented in Table 11. A basic approach to 
testing for sensitivity is to vary the price in question and note how this affects the cost of the 
system. This was done for the cost-optimized designs, whereby the selected prices were varied 
in increments of 25% from their reference value. The resulting variation in total system cost is 
presented normalised in Figure 50 for the SALM and CLMP candidates.  
 
It is clear that the price of manufactured steel has by far the largest effect on the cost, largely

though the CLMP is slightly less sensitive to common prices as they affect a lower portion of

upwards variation of 25% in the DEA supply cost yields an increase of 2% in the total CATN cost.

7.1.2 DESIGN LOAD CASES 
The various situations which an offshore wind turbine must endure can be described by a set 
of design-driving loadcases representing a range of typical and extreme scenarios. These are 
defined in the IEC 61400-3-1:2019 and IEC TS 61400-3-2:2019 standards [73] [53]. For the 
dynamic analysis presented here, DLCs 1.3 and 6.1 were considered. The first describes normal 
operation in extreme turbulence and was selected based on [74] where it was found to drive 
the largest surge motion due to the rotor thrust. The second loadcase represents an extreme 
50 year return metocean state for a parked turbine.  
For either DLC, the wind and wave conditions were simulated both codirectional and 
misaligned. With reference to Figure 35, the wind direction was fixed at 0° (positive surge), 
whilst the wave was aligned with the wind or misaligned perpendicularly.  Moreover, for the 
codirectional condition, the floater system was thus subjected to aligned loading in order to 
monitor the maximum line tensions.   
The characteristic parameters for the selected load cases are presented in Table 13. For DLC 1.3 
the rated wind speed of the DTU 10MW RWT was considered as this would produce the 
greatest thrust load. The turbulence intensity was also defined as the reference value for the 
turbine in the IEC 61400-1:2019 standard [75]. The shear exponent is described in Section 3.3, 
together with the joint distributions used to derive the normal sea state for the rated wind 
speed. For this loadcase the current is specified in the IEC 61400-3:2009 standard [76] as being 
wind generated and thus left aligned with the wind even for a misaligned wave condition. 
For DLC 6.1 an extreme wind model is required, whereby the wind speed at the hub is the 
turbine class reference value and is defined together with the corresponding turbulence 
intensity in the IEC 61400-1:2019. As presented in Section 3.2, directional data for the 50 year 
return extreme sea states was available. Consequently, given the assumed south-westerly 
dominance of the wind, the sea state (both wave and current) was based on the westerly and 
north-westerly directions to yield the most severe codirectional and misaligned cases

7.3 
RESULT DIAGNOSTICS 
The issues observed with the mooring dynamics indicate that elements of the systems may 
need to be modified. However, before this is done it is important to understand the cause of 
the adverse behaviour and what mitigations can be applied.  
One may first comment on the sizing model. The mooring optimizer derived in previous 
chapters is based on a static representation of the mooring and loading regimes. There is no 
consideration for inertias or time scales. This is especially critical given that the natural 
frequencies of the system are generally lower than those of the wave spectrum (see Figure 56), 
and thus the driving loads force the system at a higher rate than the stiffness can absorb. 
Hence, the assumption of quasi-statics does not hold and inertia terms are important. 
Moreover, note that the natural frequency in surge that is sized to in the model (0.04 Hz) is 
incomparable to those presented in Table 14. This highlights the need for decay tests to 
determine the natural frequency of systems with complex restoring mechanics. 
7.3.1 WAVE FORCING 
As a starting point, consider the wave forcing experienced by the system in the ECOD case. The 
tensions are clearly dominated by this load, as they are a function of the platform motion. 
Figure 58 presents the surge and heave motion in relation to the wave elevation and loaded 
line tension for the CLMP mooring. Note the maximum wave at 840 s; an event that will form 
the focus of all forthcoming investigations. The surge motion follows the wave but with a phase 
lag due to the acceleration required by the large mass of the floater. On the other hand, the 
heave reacts almost immediately as it relies primarily on buoyancy which changes instantly for

into a trough. By the time the buoy has adjusted, the platform is moving downstream again so 
the hawser and intermediary are jolted whilst the evidently too-light clump mass simply 
bounces in between. The tension thus redevelops due to the surge and the cycle repeats. 
In conclusion, the maximum wave introduces a significant impulse load that causes a delayed 
motion. The tension is a function of both heave and surge, though the latter is more dominant 
due to its relative magnitude and scope. However, further analysis is required as amplification 
around the heave natural frequency is clear in Figure 56. Given the mooring setup, heave 
translates as surge at the buoy, and hence the two modes are tied and mutually driven. Finally, 
the motion is too aggressive for the mooring, so augmentation of the mooring may be required.

7.3.3 WIND FORCING 
The third environmental load is the wind. As specified in Table 15, drag on the tower is a 
significant component of the forcing in DLC 6.1. Moreover, although the turbine is parked with 
feathered blades, loads continue to develop along the blade length, characterised by variation 
due to the turbulence. The result is a small rotor thrust and torque. The thrust contributes to 
surge, but the torque causes the platform to roll. In fact, the roll response in Figure 87 is driven 
by the wind, and will in turn cause sway and yaw due to line asymmetry in the sway direction. 
Furthermore, the sway response is almost entirely dominated by its natural frequency. 
As the mooring system governs motion in 
sway, then resonance in this degree of 
freedom will lead to an amplification of the 
platform dynamics in the lines, potentially 
aggravating excessive loads. Consider the 
motion of the intermediary and hawser 
segments of the loaded line for the CLMP 
system. The sway response for selected 
nodes are presented in Figure 61 around the 
maximum wave event. The fairlead sway is 
that seen for the platform, but the severity 
grows until reaching the buoy where the side-

be seen for the SALM in Figure 95. This lends support to the argument that resonance may be 
occurring in the mooring system. Such amplification would further drive line dynamics and 
place a higher effective excursive demand on the system and thus increase tensions. 
To investigate, decay tests were performed for the buoys and clump masses constituting the 
SALM and CLMP systems. These are presented in Figure 96 to Figure 98, and indicate that the 
natural frequency of their motion in surge and heave is equal to that of the overall system in 
surge. This is expected, since it is primarily their motion that governs platform surge and sway. 
Although the natural frequency of the heave mode should be driven by the tether elasticity, 
given the aforementioned coupling between surge and heave, the line setup dictates that buoy 
heave is governed by buoy surge. These tests confirm that the line components govern the 
natural frequency in surge and sway. Thus excitation at this frequency, e.g. from the turbulent 
wind, can cause resonance, and hence increased loads, in the mooring system. One may thus 
postulate that if the components were to be sized larger, then the system would stiffen and 
consequently the natural frequency would move out of the wind spectrum. This may help 
reduce the overall tensions observed.

CHAPTER 8 
CONCLUSION 
The aim of this project was to design and evaluate mooring solutions for floating offshore wind 
turbines in shallow waters, given that the development of performance- and cost-efficient 
systems has been a serious challenge to date. This work was carried out in collaboration with 
Stiesdal Offshore Technologies A/S, primarily for their TetraSub® floater concept, but adaptable 
to most platforms. The Wave Hub test site in the UK was chosen for metocean considerations. 
To serve as a foundation for the design process, a review of existing and proposed mooring 
systems was undertaken to identify current trends and promising solutions. Thus, a catenary 
system (CATN) was selected to serve as a baseline candidate, and a second mooring 
configuration was modelled on single anchor leg mooring (SALM) systems. A third candidate 
(CLMP) involved the addition of a clump weight to the SALM to reduce footprint and line slack. 
These systems were designed for the DTU 10MW reference wind turbine, such that for a driving 
load of twice the rated thrust, a maximum excursion of half the site depth was allowed. Each 
candidate was characterised statically, forming the basis of design optimization codes. 
Footprint-optimized mooring designs were evaluated quasi-statically in HAWC2® to investigate 
performance and load direction dependency. The CATN was shown to be the most compliant 
but also the most directional, whereas the SALM stiffest due to the buoy mechanics. The CLMP 
achieved a lower footprint and stiffness than the SALM by relieving load on the buoy through 
energy absorption at the clump weight. Analysis also indicated that performance directionality 
is reduced for smaller footprints and stiffer systems. Overall, the results confirmed that the 
largest excursion is yielded when the load is applied bisecting two mooring lines. However, it 
was also shown that the highest surge stiffness occurs when a single line is loaded, highlighting 
an oversight in the models where only bisectional loads were considered. Consequently, the 
optimized designs may not satisfy the frequency limit for all directions. Therefore, when 
performing quasi-static modelling, the bisectional loadcase should be considered for maximum 
excursion, but the aligned case for maximum stiffness. Finally, to investigate the effect of the 
main design parameters on the mooring systems, the solution space was mapped and feasible 
regions explored. The resulting design parameter space maps indicated that for both SALM and 
CLMP systems, excursion is reduced and systemic stiffness thus increased, for shorter hawsers, 
longer tethers, larger buoy volumes, and smaller clump masses where applicable. 
To optimize for cost, a costing model was developed involving material and component 
acquisition, whereby costs were derived from commercial data, quotations and estimations. 
Following cost-optimization of the designs, the CATN was shown to be cheapest, though the 
connection cost plays a significant role, accounting for 43% of the CLMP total cost relative to 
just 8% for the catenary. Prices were also subjected to a sensitivity study, where it was shown 
that uncertainty in the cost of manufacturing the buoy has the greatest impact. Finally, for a 
large enough design load, the SALM becomes more expensive than the CLMP due to favourable 
interaction between the buoy and clump mass for the latter.

Conclusion

The cost-optimized moorings were then evaluated for design load cases 1.3 (power production) 
and 6.1 (extreme conditions) as specified in the IEC 61400-3-1:2019. Due to issues with model 
availability, the LIFES50+ OO-Star Wind Floater Semi was modelled in HAWC2® instead of the 
TetraSub® for this dynamic analysis. The moorings performed well under operating conditions, 
but for the extreme codirectional state, the line tensions were excessive with snap load / slack 
cycles developing throughout the simulation. Analysis indicated that these mooring loads were 
driven by downstream surge coupled with the extreme tidal current for the site. Amplification 
of motion around the heave and sway natural frequencies of the floater and mooring 
respectively were also observed. Design modifications to both platform and mooring were 
studied, with changes in floater mass and mooring footprint having the largest effect. Though 
a functional system was not achieved, this diagnostic exercise highlighted trends towards an 
adequate solution. It is hoped that this work will thus serve as a starting point for future work. 
Overall, it can be concluded that over the course of the project, a design optimization model 
was developed that is sufficient for quasi-static analysis. However, in fully dynamic scenarios 
the resulting designs suffer issues under extreme conditions. Nevertheless, the studies in 
Section 7.3 present good insight into potential design modifications and preview the promising 
degree of improvement attainable. With regards to mooring cost, the catenary is cheaper, 
though there is scope for cost reductions for the other systems through the use of cheaper 
synthetic fibres, commercial scale acquisition, and increasing industrialisation of anchors. 
Finally, as footprint increasingly becomes a metric for auction bidding, efficiency in this regard 
will gain importance. In fact, the CLMP system exhibits the better footprint-to-depth ratio even 
after mitigating for excessive peak tensions through increased footprint-driven compliance. 
8.1 
SUGGESTIONS FOR FURTHER WORK 
In order to improve on and develop the work accomplished in this project, a number of areas 
can be addressed. To begin with, the quasi-static model should be expanded to include the load 
direction dependency of the stiffness, as well as coupled motion in heave and surge to mimic 
wave forcing. Tidal ranges can also be accounted for through variation in water level. It is 
suggested that line elasticity is included in the static setup, especially if less stiff materials were 
to be selected. Moreover, as the effect of the intermediary line on CLMP behaviour was noted 
to be significant, then its length should be included in future parameter space maps.  
With regards to dynamic simulations, the diagnostic studies could be expanded into a full 
parametric analysis to quantify the measures required to achieve a viable solution. This could 
potentially form the basis of a revised dynamic load correction for the quasi-static model. 
Lastly, a more robust characterisation of the performance would be achieved by considering a 
wider range of misalignment cases, whilst full three hour simulations would bolster the 
statistical analysis. Experimental tank testing might also be utilised to validate viable solutions. 
Another area worth delving into is alternative line materials such as nylon or polyester. Though 
less strong, they are also more compliant and significantly cheaper. This could aid the mitigation 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
Conclusion 
 
93 
 
of the excessive tensions observed as well as reduce the total cost of the system. A similar 
argument can also be made for a lighter chain with higher grade for the catenary. However, 
especially for the nylon rope, a fatigue analysis of the mooring system would be required as 
there are unresolved issues concerning its viability for permanent moorings. 
Finally, the cost model employed is simplistic, and would need to be improved to provide 
realistic cost comparisons. To begin with, the price estimations for steel products are inherently 
uncertain but better values could be obtained through a more comprehensive review of 
available product data. Moreover, the effect on cost due to industrialisation trends, learning 
curves and acquisition scales would also be important to consider. Apart from material costs, 
installation needs to be investigated as each system will ultimately vary in terms of component 
size, pre-tensioning, etc. This would require a robust study of the logistics, installation practises 
and vessel rates. Lastly, in order to reduce cost further, one option may be to consider shared 
anchor solutions for wind farms, though this would in turn necessitate layout optimizations.

A.1 BUOY CHARACTERISTICS 
An integral component of the mooring system candidates considered in this project is the buoy. 
The physical characteristics of these buoyancy elements must be defined in order to accurately 
taken them into account in the sizing and cost optimizations. Moreover, it is also necessary 
when modelling the mooring in HAWC2®. The following section is a description of how the 
structural and hydrodynamic properties of the buoy was characterised.  
A.1.1 MASS MODEL 
The net upthrust of the buoy is a function of its volume 𝑉𝑏, the density of sea water 𝜌𝑤 and a 
term here referred to as the buoy density 𝑚𝑏.The latter is the mass of the buoy per unit volume 
and depends on the plate thickness, material, loads present and the shape of the vessel. These 
factors will be discussed below with the aim of deriving a characterising definition.

Thickness Sizing 
The wall thicknesses were determined via a structural rigidity analysis based on the buckling 
resistance of cylindrical shells as treated in DNV-RP-C202  Sections 3.1 - 3.4 [80]. The approach 
centres around the comparison between the stress induced and the buckling strength 
associated with different load types: axial compression, bending moment, circumferential 
pressure and shear force. Their respective elastic buckling strengths can be determined based 
on physical dimensions of the buoy and the buckling coefficients presented in Table 3-2 in [80], 
whilst the induced stresses are calculated by standard equations. The combined stress and 
buckling strength can then be derived. The former by means of the von Mises yield criterion 
and the latter as a function of the individual stresses, strengths and a material factor. 
Ultimately, the design stress developed cannot exceed the design buckling strength. 
In the context of this work, the buoy experiences different loads depending on the section. The 
central cylinder was assumed to sustain hydrostatic pressure on its surface and axial tension 
longitudinally. The latter is due to the upthrust of the buoy and the resulting tether tension 
acting on the bottom end cap and thus manifesting as a downward force distributed over the 
cross-section of the cylinder wall 𝐶𝑆𝐴𝑤. Thus, the tension-induced axial stress 𝜎𝐴 is given by

A.1.2 HAWC2 MODEL 
In contrast to the above, when modelling the buoy in HAWC2®, the form assumed was that 
with flat end caps shown in Figure 73. This was done in order to replicate the setup in the 
optimization models as far as possible. Consequently, the buoy was defined as a segmented 
cylindrical shell, where physical properties such as the second moments of area and radii of 
gyration were determined by standard relations. Moreover, the shear factor required for 
principle bending was taken to be 2 as given for hollow cylindrical cross-sections in [82].  
With regards to the hydrodynamic characterisation, apart from the physical dimensionality of 
the component, the coefficients of added mass and drag are required. These properties were 
determined from DNV-OS-J101 Section 4.5.4 [54]. Assuming a smooth roughness for the buoy 
and steady state flow under a quasi-static regime, the drag coefficient 𝐶𝑑 was taken to be 0.65. 
Whereas, the inertia coefficient 𝐶𝑚 was given as 2 for shallow water with significant current, 
as is the case at the site considered for this project. 
A.2 CLUMP MASS CHARACTERISTICS 
Unlike the buoy, the clump mass is solid and thus external 
pressure loading is of no concern. Consequently, its simple form 
shown in Figure 77 was considered throughout the project, 
assuming it could be cast from materials such as concrete, steel, 
etc. Thus, when modelling in HAWC2® the clump mass was 
defined as a segmented cylinder with physical characteristics 
defined by standard relations in terms of its diameter. However, 
due to the solid cross-section, the shear factors were now taken 
as 1.11 as specified in [82] for the corresponding cross-section. 
On the other hand, the hydrodynamic properties were assumed 
to be identical to those of the buoy as the model form remains 
the same. Consequently, the drag and inertia coefficients were 
taken to be 0.65 and 2 respectively from the DNV-OS-J101 [54].
