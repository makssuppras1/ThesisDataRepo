Summary
Within the frame of sound field reconstruction, this Master’s thesis addresses the
development of a model that attempts to characterise the sound field of a loudspeaker
reproduction system such as the one in the Audio Visual Immersion Lab (AVIL) at
DTU.
The focus is on investigating the approach by statistical methods, specially Bayes the-
ory, to solve acoustic inverse problems in real applications. The existing experiments
of applying this approach in acoustic experiments seems to suggest better results than
others more classical methods, at least in some scenarios. Frequently in cases where
there is a significant presence of uncertainties.
The project collects the basis and shows the model development from theoretical
concepts to a final implementation of a sound radiation model using spherical har-
monics. The Bayesian inference approach is compared with an approach using ridge
regularisation for solving the acoustic inverse problem.
The estimated values obtained using a basic implementation of Bayes theory show
slightly better results. However they differ about 20 dB from the observed data.

Introduction
1.1
Context
The objective of this project is to develop a model from experimental data in order
to characterise the sound field from a loudspeaker system. The characterisation is
given by means of the reconstruction of the acoustic transfer function between a set
of sources and a set of measurement points. The transfer functions are modelled with
spherical harmonics.
Consequently, the model attends to find a solution for the implicit inverse problem.
The acoustic properties of the radiation of the loudspeakers are unknown so, by using
the transfer functions, the problem is to reconstruct them. This inference process is
addressed to be investigated in different ways with gradual complexity.
As it is a specialised topic, it has been required to acquire a significant amount of
knowledge about sound field reconstruction, statistical concepts, Bayes theory, basis
functions, inverse problems, computational model development and error measures.
Besides, the fact that the model is based on experimental data, has involved a series
of measurements to create the model.
With all that, this chapter is supposed to collect all the elements involved in the
development of a model for this type of application and introduce the related concepts.
This report follows the scientific writing structure IMRaD (Introduction, Methods,
Results, and Discussion), so the content is distributed in the remaining chapters
following this structure.

1.2
Problems in science
In any branch of science, when a phenomenon is intended to be understood, the most
logical approach to solve the problem is to formulate a direct problem. In a direct
(or forward) problem, the effects of the phenomenon are determined from the causes.
However, in many cases the causes cannot be known or are very complicated to be
completely characterised. These scenarios require the approach to be the opposite:
to determine the causes from the observation of the effects. This is known as inverse
problem. This contrary approach introduces more complexity in order to solve the
problem.

In turn, inverse problems are most used for investigation purposes. The development
of models helps to support the explanation of physical processes where is not possible
to obtain direct information from the object of interest, and it is required to make
inferences about physical parameters from the observational data.
Each literature selects the nomenclature regarding to the field of expertise and the
traditional conventions.
Before going into more details, let establish some letters.
The primary elements involved in an inverse problem are three:
• the observable data ‘y’ (effect) acquired by means of measurements,
• the unknowns ‘x’ (cause) which are called parameters,
• and a mapping operator ‘O’, also called measurement operator, mapping or
sometimes literally “forward problem”.
The latter maps the parameters x in a functional space X, typically a Banach or
Hilbert space, to the space of data Y, another Banach or Hilbert space [5].
O : X −→Y
x −→y = O(x) .
(1.1)
The solution of the direct problem defines this mapping operator O, which transforms
any object of the space X into a noise-free image of the space Y [6].
The next step is to introduce some elements to constrain the general definition of a
model to the problem of concern. Firstly, assume O is a linear operator. Secondly,
the observable data is obtained from measurements, so obligatorily involves some
deviations in the data respect with respect to the reality.

measurement error. Thirdly, consider the spaces are 3-dimensional Euclidean spaces.

model because as long as the phenomenon possess some complexity, it is considerably
difficult to develop a model that perfectly resembles the physical process and show
the exactly same results. This fact implies two tasks: to quantify the deviation model-
to-reality and to evaluate the conditions under that deviation is small enough to be
considered as negligible. Thus, the model will be valid.

1.3.1
Ill-posedness
Jacques Hadamard [7] [8] defined that well-posed problem must fulfil 3 conditions:
1. the problem has a solution (existence),
2. the solution must be unique (uniqueness), and
3. depends continuously on initial data (continuity).
If the measurement data makes the problem to fail in any of these requirements, the
problem is considered as ill-posed. Generally, inverse problems are ill-posed. So it
has to be reformulated by adopting, for example, adopting assumptions, providing
more data, introducing bounds, etc.
For the existence condition, technically as the data is observed , not observed is ,
not y, it could be an issue. However, the existence of the solution is assumed to be
fulfilled by the addition of the error term.
The uniqueness is forced to be fulfilled because the goal of investigating the problem
is to determine the cause. If there is no a unique solution, it is necessary either to
observe more effects or restrict the set of possible causes to restore uniqueness [9].
Continuity is related to stability, but is not sufficient condition for stability [10]. The
continuity dependence does not mean robustness against noise [11].
As the theo-
retically continuous model is discretised to get a computational model, there is yet
another terminology which accounts for the stability of the solution. A numerical
problem is then said to be well- or ill-conditioned whether the numerical solution
behaves well or not. A well-posed problem can be ill-conditioned [12]. An inverse
problem solved with a computational model is usually ill-conditioned. And this nu-
merical stability condition is harder to fix as small arbitrary perturbations in the
initial can produce large arbitrary perturbations in the solution.
For example1, a sinusoid term with small amplitude apparently is could not been
seen as problematic at first sight, but using differential equations imply to operate in
terms of the derivative of the sinusoid, and consequently, the resulting amplitude can
be large particularly in case of high frequencies. Linking this to that a perturbation
of noise in data can be viewed a small randomly oscillating function, small oscillating
data produce large oscillating solutions [6].

1.3.2
Solving inverse problems
According to Argoul [13], the approach of finding a solution to the inverse problem,
ill-posed by nature, can be classified into three categories:
• Functional analysis: introduce physical constraints and change the spaces to
turn it into a well-posed problem.
• Regularized inversion: based on rewriting the problem. Some techniques are:
Tikhonov’s regularisation, reduction of the number of parameters, introduction
of constraints mathematically, filtering of trial data, quasireversibility method,
etc.
• Stochastic Inversion: work with the statistic properties of the variables consid-
ering the deviations as independent normal distributions (this approach also
called Bayesian inversion).
1.3.3
Overdetermined systems and optimisation
The data from measurements is always finite and discrete. When the number of pa-
rameters in a model is smaller than the number of data points from the measurements,
the problem is overdetermined, [13]. The matrix O of the computational model (from
this point forward, referred just as model), which is assumed as to be a linear system,
has the dimensions N by L and the vector of data y is of dimension N, then
• if N < L the system is underdetermined,
• if N = L has a unique solution,
• if N > L is overdetermined.
Often the case is the latter, and most common estimation method for linear models
is the ordinary least squares (OLS). It squares each of the errors, sum all of them
and take the smallest. The error, or residual, is defined as the difference between
predicted and actual values (Ox - y). Least squares search the parameters x that
minimise the squared error. Thus, it can be formulated as an optimisation problem
described as arguments of the minimum, arg minx f(x), that is the value of x for

1.4
Sound field reconstruction
Sound field reconstruction accounts for the reconstruction of acoustic sources using a
series of discrete measurements in the sound field in order to perform an extrapolation
to estimate the sound field at another positions. Most existing methods utilised to
solve the inverse acoustic problem share common grounds [3]:
• Consider the sound field as a linear combination of spatial basis functions.
• Infer the coefficients of the basis functions to reassemble the sound field mea-
sured at a set of positions.
The assumptions about the source distribution, geometry, propagation and the fre-
quency range of interest permits different approaches. Some of the approaches are:
• Near-field Acoustical Holography (NAH)
• Statistically-Optimized NAH (SONAH)
• Equivalent Sources Method (ESM)
• Beamforming
• Inverse Boundary Element Method (IBEM)
• Wave superposition algorithm (WSA)
• Helmholtz Equation Least Squares (HELS).
1.4.1
The model
The model developed in this project is a source radiation model is for an spatial sound
application. It is experimental based as the parameters of the model are fitted by data
from measurements, which are in a loud-speaker system with special characteristic.
The distribution of the 64 loudspeakers form a sphere.
The data introduced in the model are the transfer functions between the loudspeakers
and a microphone at different positions, in an anechoic environment. All the sources
are considered equal, as are the same model of loudspeaker, excited with the same
signal and the same power. So they are assumed to have the same directivity.
The transfer functions provide complex values, therefore the of the equations and
mathematical definitions are applied in the complex domain.

Once the measurements were done, with all the bulk experimental data, the develop-
ment of the model focused on incorporate the real data and finish the development of
the two experimental models, ’Ridge’ and ’Bayesian’, named after their background.
In terms of the development, they structure is the same. In first place it is necessary
to collect the data from the measurements: positions and transfer functions.
Then, Also is shared the process to obtain the mapping operator. As it consist in
Spherical Harmonics, the variables are product kr (wavenumber times distance), angu-
lar coordinates and number of modes. The mapping operator for each fixed frequency
is a matrix of N measurements and L modes (corresponding to the truncation of eigen-
functions in spherical harmonics). There will be two operators: one for building the
model (obtain the coefficients of the model) and another for the model validation
(generate the simulation from the model). Once having the transfer functions and
the operators, the next step is to perform the inverse problem itself. ’Ridge’ model
uses ridge regression which in MATLAB is given by a function with the same name
’ridge’ 2, which return the vector with the coefficients:
x = ridge(y, O, k, scale)
where ’y’ is the transfer functions from the set of transfer functions for the model
building, ’O’ is the mapping operator created with the positions corresponding to the
same set of measurements, scale is set as 0 for not to scale the coefficients, and ’k’ is
the parameter that controls the regularisation strength. It is actually the Lagrange
multiplier α2. This factor penalises large values in the coefficients in the minimisation
problem.
After this regression is performed, the model is run with the obtained
parameters x and the mapping operator of the positions measurement set for the
model validation. Finally, the estimated ’y’ can be compared with the set of transfer
functions reserved for the validation.
The code is attached in the Appendix. It is divided in two parts. The first part which
involves the common parts for both models, and the second solves the minimisation
problem using this type of regularisation. It has been chosen as it is the most extended
method obtain a solution for this kind of simple linear inverse ill-posed problems.
For the Bayesian model, the MATLAB implementation has more complexity. There
are few and different approaches to address the implementation of the Bayes theory
in MATLAB. The likelihod and prior(s) have been implemented as function handle
type. Thus, it is possible to define the posterior as the combination of other functions.
The posterior is actually the probability density function of the wanted coefficients.
Therefore, the extraction of samples from that distribution and the selection of the
Maximum-A-Posteriori coefficients is the last step in this approach. After this, the
model is run with these coefficients to compare with the validation set of transfer
functions.

2.2.3
Position measurements
In the model, for each combination of loudspeaker and microphone, it is required to
specify the relative spherical coordinates of each pair. Given the architectonic features
of AVIL chamber, and the limitation of time and additional equipment, the method
to determine the positions was not trivial. It was not feasible to take references from
the walls or others absolute positions to use as global references and neither to take
directly the spherical coordinates from microphone to loudspeaker. The loudspeaker
positions are known as it is documentation from the AVIL. So the plan turned into
determine the centre of the system, measure the coordinates of each element from the
centre and later compute the loudspeaker-to-mic relative coordinates by the combi-
nation of loudspeaker-to-centre and centre-to-loudspeaker coordinates.
Two cross-line lasers were attached to the structure of the main ring, the one that
supports the loudspeakers that are in the horizontal plane at the centre of the system.
The lasers allowed to check the alignment of the centres of the loudspeakers of that
ring and also it was used to locate the global centre of the loudspeaker system. The
intersection of the laser with a thread hanging from the top of the spherical-shaped
structure, with a metal nut attached to its end, provided the centre of the coordinate
system.

4.1
Evaluating the model
In first place, the theoretical model was not conclusive. It only provided an simplis-
tic approach to the concepts and calculations with spherical harmonics and inverse
problems.
The insights it provided could be summarised as:
• The order of magnitude of the parameters x and the coefficients of the mapping
operator.
• The value of the parameter corresponding to the first spherical harmonic (l=0)
is significantly higher than the others for the case of exciting with an ideal
monopole (which is expected).
• The value of the second and following are supposed to decrease to zero. The
fact that using a ideal-theoretical situation seems be accomplish for low number
of modes but increasing them make the some aberrant values.
For the experimental-based model, in the case of the results for the ridge regression the
Figure 3.1 shows the deviation between transfer functions of the set of measurements
for validation and the estimated of the model for those positions. It is shown the
average for the 512 values. The parameter α of the ridge regression was adjusted at
0.1. However there was no significant variations in the results with different values of
α.
Figure 3.2 shows the transfer functions (measured and estimated) for 4 randomly-
chosen positions for the set of validation. It outlines there is no a shared tendency in
frequency for the deviations. The same is shown for Bayes (Figure 3.4) therefore there
is no apparently relation with the frequency within the selected frequency range.
Processing the average of the 512 differences for the bayesian model illustrated in
Figures 3.3, shows an improvement with respect to the ridge model. Furthermore,
the dynamic range of the difference (maximum - minimum) remains very similar. The
improvement can be seen summarised in Figure 4.1.

4 Discussion
Figure 4.1. Average of the difference between the observed transfer functions in the measure-
ments and the estimates from the model.
Although there is an improvement and it seems to roughly be noticeable along fre-
quency, the estimates are still far from observed values to be considered as acceptable.
On the bright side, it is encouraging that, whereas the ridge model has only the
factor α to control the smoothness of the regression to try to improve the subsequent
estimation, the bayesian model has more possibilities to be optimised and improved.
The results shown where calculated with a truncation of modes up to the degree of
spherical harmonics l=5. Looking only up to 500 Hz, it has been demonstrated [1]
that using small number of modes improves the reconstruction. So for applications
with more limited frequency range it, the model at this point can provide better
results.
Furthermore, due to high computational costs and issues in the final part of the
project, the bayesian model estimated are based in a posterior distributions that does
not includes error in the position measurements. Further research can be done by
including the prior information about the accuracy of the distances an angles, so that
the model could differentiate that error component and improve the estimates.
==PAGE:35==
Conclusion
There are many different methods to apply sound field reconstruct. A good choice
will depend first on the interest of the study, and secondly of the conditions. The
spherical characteristics of this project led to use spherical harmonics. A model based
on experimental data has been developed to reconstruct the sound field of a spherical
distribution of 64 loudspeakers. An implementation based in Tikhonov regularization
and another in statistical approach (bayesian framework) provide an estimation, but
differs from the observed data about 22 and 20 dB respectively.
There are still
capacity to feed the bayesian model with more information, therefore there is space
for better estimates.
Apart from the results of the model, the conceptual part paves the way the background
to an acoustic application of inverse problems by providing a big picture of all concepts
involved.
