Abstract 
Increasing near and offshore construction is putting the marine environment under pressure. The 
blossoming of these constructions including wave breaks, bridges, energy installation foundations, 
seawalls, ship docks is denoted ocean sprawling. Ocean sprawling introduces hard structure to the 
marine environment, following a suede of physically alterations to the natural habitat with 
consequences of changes in the local marine ecosystem. One of the most applied materials in 
maritime construction is concrete. Earlier work stats that manmade concrete constructions are poor 
surrogates for natural stone reefs in relation to reef related organisms. Biodiversity and biomass are 
reduced while non-native opportunistic species seems to be better with colonizing concrete 
surfaces. This may be due to the low complexity of concrete structures as well internal chemistry of 
the material. Concrete solutions favouring biological settlement without reducing structure 
durability is much needed to mitigate these impacts. 
 
This thesis sat out to investigate interactions between newly introduced concrete cylinders and the 
colonizing biology during a 6 weeks’ exposal period in the late fall in the strait of Øresund, 
Denmark. Three concrete mixes were designed with varying binder compositions. Ordinary 
Portland Cement (OPC) was substituted with, the low carbon footprint, supplementary cementitious 
materials (SMCs): calcined clay and a grey microfiller (LC3) in 3 different ratios (OPC100, LC3-65 
and LC3-56 denoting percentage OPC in the binder mix). Half of the samples had biofouling 
removed mechanically once a week. 
Concrete cylinders were harvested after 2, 4 and 6 weeks and analysed for presence of biofouling 
by photography. Chloride profiles were conducted by dissolving concrete powder in HNO3 prior to 
titration, and dilute was sent for total cation analysis with ICP-OES in an external lab. Samples 
harvested after 6 weeks was analysed under microscope to determine the biological community. 
Samples harvested after 4 weeks was plane polished and studied under SEM-EDX to analyse 
elemental zonation and phase alterations. Non-exposed concrete elements were tested for elemental 
composition (ICP, XRD and TGA), compressive strength, water content, porosity and surface 
roughness to determine initial physical and chemical conditions.   
 
All untreated samples showed presence of biofouling, while samples of more coarse surface 
structure revealed a higher presence of diatoms. TGA and XRD data provided detailed information 
of the elemental structure of all samples. ICP-OES data and SEM-EDX images revealed an altered 
elemental relationship in the outermost millimetre of all samples, while also revealing the loss of 
the outermost concrete layer due to treatment – these findings were supported by microscopy 
images. Apparent diffusion rates for chloride (Da) was comparable to those found in existing 
literature. Sample specimens with increased SMCs levels of LC3 showed lower Da compared to the 
ones without. No consistent differences in chloride intrusion were found between the weekly 
treatments removing biological appendage. An effect was found from the weekly treatments on the 
elemental compositions of the outermost layers of the concrete samples, weather the effect was 
caused by biological activity or from the treatment itself could not be determined. 
The lowered chloride penetration found for LC3 concrete is promising for developing a cheap 
available low pH concrete with a lowered carbon footprint. However, experiments on rebar 
protection in terms of calcification, and long-term studies of biological colonization needs to be 
addressed.

1 Motivation 
 
With a growing global population, a trend of urbanization (United Nations, World Urbanization 
Prospects 2018), and an increase in the human settlement at coastal zones (Small & Nicholls, 2003), 
near shore marine areas are exposed to ever growing human activity. About 40% of global 
population are estimated to live within 100 km from the coastline, and >60% of the world largest 
cities (>5 million inhabitants) are placed along the coasts (Martínez et al., 2007). 
Urban infrastructure (boating, bridges, harbour peers, artificial islands etc.), coastal protection 
(seawalls, breakwaters, groynes, hardening etc.), aquaculture constructions and marine energy 
installations (windmills, wave energy, oil platforms) all contribute to human construction elements 
in contact with the marine environment and most are made of hard materials like concrete and metal 
(Firth et al., 2016).  
 
Structures introduced by humans are not only altering the natural habitat, but are also changing 
local natural physical characteristics including bathymetry, hydrodynamics, sedimentation patterns 
(e.g. organic sedimentation and sand movement) and light regime important for biological 
development. In all cases of human construction an alteration of the natural marine habitat is taking 
place, leading to a gradual change (i.e. homogenization) of the ecological niches available for the 
marine organisms (Firth et al., 2016). The European Water Framework Directive (WFD) requires 
that heavily modified water bodies should fulfil its maximum ecological potential by using all 
possible mitigations measures, without compromising with the initial performance of the 
modification purpose. Thereby ecological assessments of the effects and means of mitigation must 
be done prior to construction. (European Commision, 2000) 
 
Introduction of hard structures in the marine environment is often used as an act of conservation 
with the primary objective of establishing complex hard bottom structure and giving reef associated 
fish species much needed habitats for growing and spawning (Jensen, 2002; Schroeter et al., 2015; 
Selfati et al., 2018; Sherman et al., 2002).  Artificial reefs, in terms of conservation, may be used to 
improve fish stocks, reduce fishing activity or for recreational purposes as diving and angling 
(Baine, 2001). Reefs may also provide eco-system services as excellent natural wave breaks, 
improvement of regional industrial fishing and increasing water quality by sequestering carbon and 
nutrients 
After placement of any submerged structure an invasion of surface area takes place. The earliest 
invaders being a mixed community of bacteria, fungus and microalgae, referred to as biofilm, 
followed by larger and more complex organisms including mussels, crustaceans and macro algae all 
dependent on hard structure to settle on (Jenkins & Martins, 2010). The formation of healthy 
biological reef communities can have positive cascade effects through up all levels of the food 
chain. 
 
Hard structures have a diversity of effects on marine biology and ecology dependent on purpose, 
location and design. While construction engineers main task is to increase the service life (period of 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
use) of marine constructions by understanding the interactions between the harsh marine 
environment and the concrete used (Barberon et al., 2005; Costa & Appleton, 1999; Jakobsen et al., 
2016), conservation biologist and ecologists focus on describing and reducing any negative effects 
as a result by introduction of hard structures in marine environments (S. D. Connell & Glasby, 
2006; Kerckhof et al., 2010; Naylor et al., 2012; Ostalé-valriberas et al., 2018; Petersen & Malm, 
2006).   
 
For this thesis, focus is placed on the mechanisms controlling the transport of chloride ions into the 
concrete in the marine environment, with special attention on any effects caused by the early 
biological film growing on concrete exposed to the marine environment.   
Reinforcement rust formation, caused by chloride interaction, is proposed to be one of the main 
factors decreasing concrete service life due to its high presence in the marine environment and use 
in de-icing salt on roads (Angst et al., 2009; Marchand & Samson, 2009; Obla et al., 2016). 
Interactions between biological communities and marine concrete must be mapped, in order to 
construct marine concrete infrastructure (MCI) serving both satisfying strength and durability 
properties while enhancing marine wildlife and biodiversity. Finding a sweet spot between all of 
these factors will favor a “one solution fits all”, and may help construction engineers to use 
materials that include conservation of marine ecology in future MCI without compromising with 
product quality, lifetime and price.  
 
2 Objective  
 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the interactions between the early settling organisms on 
introduced concrete elements – with a focus on the settling dynamics of pioneer species, mineralogy 
of the concrete elements and the chlorine intrusion. By understanding fundamental interactions 
between the applied materials for marine construction, and the marine biology it may become 
possible to support the marine ecology as a result from construction activates, while maintaining 
durability of the structure. Specifically, this study investigates if the early forming biofilm on 
concrete elements (after 2, 4 and 6 weeks) have an effect on;  
 
(i) 
the transport kinetics of chloride ions into the concrete,  
(ii) 
the mineralogical composition of the concrete. 
And  
(iii) 
 if the initial mineralogical composition of concrete has an effect on the formation of the 
colonizing biological community after 6 weeks.

4. Theoretical background 
 
The following sections are split into two with the first describing relevant concrete technology and 
implications for marine constructions and the second describing relevant biological considerations 
for studying processes of biological colonization on hard substrata. The information is provided in 
order to reach out to both scientific fields to provide a common understanding of the possible 
interactions between the lithological system, that concrete structures represents, and the biological 
community inhibiting its surfaces in submerged marine systems.  
 
 
4.1 Concrete and marine ecology 
 
4.1.1 Manmade hard structure and marine ecology 
 
An artificial reef is defined as a purposely submerged structure, with the aim of mimicking the 
functions of a natural reef. Within the preparation of placing artificial reefs, managers and 
stakeholder have to define the scope of the project. A clear definition of the ultimate goals of the 
reef makes it possible to validate the success rate of the reef (Baine, 2001).   
Detailed planning before submerging a reef is crucial in reaching the goals and may involve design 
complexity, size in terms of volume and area, shelter, crack and crevice sizes, surface texture, 
stability of the hard structure. Also physical characteristics as distance to other reefs in term of 
species recruitment and colonisation, sediment type, currents and wave actions alongside with 
possible negative environmental effects on surrounding habitats must be included in the preparation 
process. (Baine, 2001; Sherman et al., 2002) 
 
Even though the science revolving around artificial reef is traditionally linked to the 
abovementioned criteria, marine biologists and marine managers shows interest in the reef effects 
of new construction at coastal and offshore zones. Biological settlement on these structures is both 
inevitable and unintended. This is referred to as the artificial reef effect (Hammar et al., 2010; 
Kerckhof et al., 2010; Sella & Perkol-finkel, 2015; Strain et al., 2018). As placement of these 
structures is not initiated as an act of biological conservation their appearance may draw both 
positive and negative side effects on the marine fauna besides their primary goal as foundation for 
construction. Some of the primary considerations are that man-made structures may serve as 
“stepping stone” - corridors acting as bridges between hard-substrate that natural biological 
spreading cant not gap (Glasby & Connell, 1999), this may be relevant for both native and non-
native species. Man-made constructions can facilitate the loss of heterogeneous native habitat 
replaced by homogenous alien habitats  (Sanabria-fernandez et al., 2018) and even provide 
unsuitable substrate for natural biofouling, promoting the settlement of non-native species (Ashley 
et al., 2014; Petersen & Malm, 2006; Vaselli et al., 2008).

(Bowman et al., 2017) argues for embedding hard-structure engineering into conservation biology, 
as a tool for artificial habitat construction, as further ocean sprawling is inevitable. In order to do so 
strong collaboration between ecologists and engineers are crucial. Several authors describe the

al., 2012; Ostalé-valriberas et al., 2018; Pioch et al., 2018; Strain et al., 2018)

4.1.2 Hard substrate succession 
 
Any blossoming of a new ecological system follows a certain sequence of development called 
succession (Jenkins & Martins, 2010). In the simplest explanation organisms tend to have different 
requirements in order to settle in a novel unoccupied habitat (primary succession). Many organisms 
life strategies are interlinked and interdependent thus providing a cascade of species replacements. 
This can be interfered (delayed) by the presence of physical or chemical disturbance, but also by 
biological interference (secondary succession) 
The early species will be colonists adapted to habitats of low biological complexity, often with 
traits of high reproduction and growth rate. These species are also referred to as opportunistic 
species or r-selected species (after the r/K selection theory distinguishing between organisms with 
different life strategies). As succession continues introduction of more species will be present, as 
colonists “prepare” the habitat, and species of later successional stages replace the early colonists 
by having a longer life span and more tenacious traits (K-selection species). The habitat will, by 
theory, then have a more diverse composition of organisms, organisms with larger body size and 
larger competitive ability at a later successional stage (Jenkins & Martins, 2010). A simplified 
version of marine hard substrate colonization and succession is shown graphically in Figure 1 
borrowed from Grzegorczyk & Pospiech (2018). 
 
 
Figure 1 Theoretical successional sequence as presented by Grzegorczyk & Pospiech (2018). An exposed novel 
substrate will first be covered by a conditioning film of organic molecules floating in the water column. These are 
serving as substrate and feeding ground for bacterial and fungal growth. Once bacteria are settled, biofilm is formed 
by excretion of extracellular polymeric matrix excreted. Photosynthetic microalgae and cyanobacteria are among the 
first organisms to colonize the conditioning biofilm.

4.1.3 Fundamental mechanisms of marine succession 
 
The nature of settling organisms can roughly be divided 
into three groups as proposed by Connell and Slatyer 
(1977). The first group are inhibiters, which life 
strategy is to cover free substrata, actively inhibiting the 
growth of other organisms. This is either passively via 
high growth rates or by active inhibition tactics. The 
second group are tolerateors, of which the next 
occurring species are unaffected. The last group are 
facilitators. This group provide basis for the growth of 
other species, this can be by altering the habitat 
physically. E.g. by colonization of mussels preventing 
certain predators from entering the habitat, and 
providing structural complexity. 
 
These features may determine the developmental pace 
of the biological community. Farrell et. Al 1991 showed 
further adjustment of the model by introducing grazers 
to the model (Figure 2), by having a specific effect on 
the abovementioned mechanisms proposed by Connell 
and Slatyer (1977). Grazing activity on early inhibitors 
may increase the rate of succession, while grazing on later occurring species will indicate a 
drawback in the successional sequence, due to the simple removal of a non-pioneering species.  
 
The theories proposed can be used to explain succession on an experimental level, where many 
factors may influence the successional sequence in a natural environment, dependent on the level of 
succession studied: Season, climatic variations, recruitment variation, growth and mortality 
(including predation, diseases, physical damage), hydrology, chemical variations, pollution, light 
levels, patch characteristics (substrata type, surface nature, geographic placement, orientation etc) 
are among the factors affecting the successional trajectories (Dobretsov, 2010; Grzegorczyk & 
Pospiech, 2018; Hutchinson et al., 2006; Jenkins & Martins, 2010; Lotze, 1999; Salta et al., 2013). 
For a comprehensive review of papers dealing with physio-chemical factors affecting single species 
settlement the reader is referred to Prendergast (2009). 
Even though succession is a settled concept in biology, detailed investigations has to be given each 
system when defining the trajectory of species occurring. Precautions and reservations must be 
made when estimating future stages in detail and a stable equilibrium is seldom the case (Sousa, 
1984). In case of periodically stable equilibrium of biofilm, macro algae and/or epifauna (fauna 
settled on substrate), correlations with strong environmental factors, as nutrient enrichment, can be 
made (Grzegorczyk & Pospiech, 2018).

4.1.4 Early settlement 
 
For a biological community to develop, early colonists have to be introduced by natural dispersal or 
anthropogenic introduction (invasive species) (Carlton & Geller, 1993). 
For marine species, distances between suitable habitats may be large – therefore most marine 
organisms have a planktonic propagule (A life stage serving propagation and dispersal) in order to 
increase dispersal distance with ocean currents or waves.  
Distance to similar habitats and transport possibilities defines the recruitment pool and thereby the 
following species composition of a novel habitat. As an early introduced species is settled, it may 
be a strong competitor for new colonists if all vacancy is taken and the first introduced species can 
control the pace and trajectory for the following succession sequence.  
 
General trends the development of biological communities on novel habitat are described by 
Dobretsov (2010) and divided into 4 phases. Figure3 (a) presents a schematic overview of the 4 
phases. 
 
(i) 
adsorption of dissolved organic molecules (molecular biofouling / conditioning film),  
(ii) 
colonization of prokaryotes. Heterotrophic prokaryotes may feed on the molecular 
biofouling. An extracellular matrix (ECM) is formed by the prokaryotic 
microorganisms.  
(iii) 
Settlement of unicellular photosynthetic eukaryotes, often dominated by diatoms.  
(iv) 
invertebrate larvae, and microalgae spores colonize the substrate. The phase description 
following a certain pattern may be seen as a simplified model, while more complex 
models including interactions between all phases is also proposed (Figure 3 (b)). 
 
Figure 3 a): general and simple model for biofouling of preceding phases. (b): A more complex model, including 
interactions between all fouling organisms. All three life strategies (inhibition, tolerance and facilitation) and grazing 
may influence to structure of the system. Solid lines indicate the inevitable entry  of various  propagules to a substrate, 
while the dotted lines indicate interactions of various character.   Figure borrowed from Clare et al., 1992 in Terlizzi & 
Faimali, 2009.

4.2 Introduction to concrete technology.  
 
Concrete is reported to be the most applied material on Earth after water with an annual use of 10 
km3 year-1  (Gartner, 2011). Concrete is applied for multiple purposes in the building industry 
covering housing, infrastructure, fundaments for various platforms and armoring. In its simplest 
form concrete consists of cement, water and aggregates in the form of rocks and sand.  Concrete has 
major advantages compared to other construction materials, including its low costs, ability to 
withstand high compressive strength, high availability and the ease of handling and casting due to 
its fluid nature prior to hardening (Flatt et al., 2012). To cope with the lower flexible strength, 
expansion and contraction, concrete elements are reinforced with metal bars, referred to as 
reinforced concrete.  
Several factors interplay, determining the final properties of the concrete. Initially, the interaction 
between the cement and aggregates used, thermodynamic reactions when water is added to the 
cement, casting practice, the concretes microstructure and curing method. Secondly, to the ongoing 
chemical and mineralogical processes altering the durability of the concrete after application. 
Concrete science and technologies are a major topic that must not be underestimated for its high 
level of complexity both in terms of physics and chemistry.  
 
4.2.1 Ordinary Portland Cement 
 
The most common type of cement used in the building industry is Ordinary Portland Cement, 
normally denoted OPC, developed by and patented by William Aspdin in 1843.  
OPC clinker is produced from heating limestone (CaCO3) and clay (aluminosilicate) in a kiln to 
1450° C. The heating process causes calcium from the limestone and silicon from the clay minerals 
to form clinker under the process of sintering. The clinker contains calcium silicates (C3S and C2S) 
along with aluminate (C3A) and ferrite minerals (C4AF). The four cement-products’ chemical name 
and notation are stated in Table 1 along with the special cement chemist notation commonly used 
within concrete science and technology.  Other chemical species are also found in OPC, as calcium 
sulphates, calcite, calcium oxide, magnesium oxide, Na- and K-sulphates, even though these 
compounds are contributing to a much smaller part of the cement (Lothenbach & Winnefeld, 2006).

4.2.3 Hydration 
 
Once water is added to the cement, the cement is hydrated and a dissolution of the compounds in 
the cement takes place. Over time the end products from the thermodynamic reactions precipitates 
as crystals defined as hydration products. The main hydration mineral products are C-S-H gel 
(calcium silicate hydrates), ettringite, portlandite, calcium monosulphoaluminate and/or calcium 
monocaarboaluminate (Lothenbach & Winnefeld, 2006), where C-S-H are the most dominant and 
important hydration products responsible for strength development in concrete. The formation of 
portlandite (Ca(OH)2) and its equilibrium products in presence of water in the concrete, provides a 
high pH around 13. See equation (1)

In concrete mixtures, the hydration products bind to the surface of sand and gravel (aggregates) by 
forming a crystal matrix, resulting in the formation of solid durable concrete. The final strength is 
primarily defined by the water/cement ratio (w/c) with increasing strength at lover ratios, even 
though, temperature, loss of water (change in w/c over time) and curing time may alter the final 
strength as well. When hardening, strength in concrete develops over time with a standard curing 
period of 28 days at 23 ° C in moist conditions (Mehta, 1991). The final compressive strength of 
concrete may be empirically derived in a lab and is measured in the units of mega pascal (MPA).  
 
Strength and durability of concrete are the most important factors for construction work, and 
therefor scientists and engineers are putting much effort in understanding and describing the 
chemical and crystal nature of the hydration products and their relevance for the concretes physical 
properties of concrete (Lothenbach & Winnefeld, 2006; Ludwig & Zhang, 2015).  
 
4.2.4 Service life, types and causes of concrete deterioration.  
 
Concrete elements may be exposed to unfavorable conditions deteriorating the material or reducing 
its strength. During construction planning, environmental influence has to be considered before 
designing the concrete mix, casting and curing practice. Standards for concrete properties are 
defined for different environmental aggressive levels (presence of water/moist, chloride etc; Danish 
Standard (DS) 411) Environmental influence can be of both physical and chemical character, as 
freezing/thawing or ionic intrusion through the permeable concrete, due to the porosity of the 
cement.  
Even though concrete elements may reach stable physical properties, in terms of strength over time, 
the many chemical equilibriums between hydration products and pore solutions may alter the 
mineralogical composition and physical properties concrete (Lothenbach and Winnefeld 2006.) 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
Different types of concrete deterioration mechanisms are often referred to as concrete diseases as 
presented by Poulsen, 1985 (e.g. Rust formation, freezing/thawing damage, alkali reaction etc.), 
where water, as dissolvent, is the key component to almost all types of deterioration except for heat 
damages.  
 
4.2.5 Reinforced concrete  
 
Concrete structures are reinforced with steel bars to cope with tensile stress. In applied engineering, 
one challenge is to avoid the corrosion of steel bars as this causes a volumetric expansion leading to 
crack formation in the concrete, thus weakening of the structure and leading to further exposal of 
the embedded steel to the surrounding environment. This will cause further corrosion and 
introduction of pathways into the concrete for chemical agents, see Figure 6. 
The concrete itself offers a protective coating to the steel, caused by a dense physical layer of the 
cements hydrates and aggregates reducing exposure to the surrounding environment, as well as 
maintaining a high pH around the steel bar supporting the presence of a thin layer of iron and 
calcium oxides (Fe(OH)2 and Ca(OH)2). Due to the high concentration of hydroxides the formation 
of iron ions is reduced and the iron-ions present will create a thin layer of Fe3O4, Fe2O3 and FeOOH 
interrupting the electrical current causing the corrosion process to stop. (Montemor et al., 2003; 
Page, 1975; Poulsen, 1985; Rahner, 1996), see Parboux diagram of iron species ranging with pH 
(Figure 5). 
Two mentionable factors known to weaken passive film surrounding the reinforcement rebars, 
includes chloride and carbonation.  
While chloride is reported to weaken the passive film around the steel bars and induce corrosion, 
the exact chemistry of the breakdown of the film remains uncertain (Montemor et al., 2003), and 
will therefore not be treated further in this report. Details about the proposed processes have 
previously been described by Montemor et al., 2003. Once the protective film is weakened, 
dissolved chloride ions will work as an electrolyte increasing the speed of rust formation.  
 
Another factor weakening the protective film are the process of carbonation due to the presence of 
inorganic carbon species present in water. Once carbonate (CO3
-2) reaches the calcium rich cement, 
it reacts with available calcium ions from Ca(OH)2, precipitating as CaCO3 – this process is called 
carbonation (Poulsen, 1985). Carbonation of the concrete reduces the pH of the pore fluid to around 
9, thus altering the appearance of the ionic species of iron and the steel will be vulnerable to 
corrosion if carbonation reaches depths of reinforcement steel cf. the Parboux diagram. This is also 
referred to as CIC, carbonation induced corrosion.

4.2.6 Ionic intrusion 
 
The mechanisms of ionic transport in concrete is traditionally explained in terms of diffusion 
(Marchand & Samson, 2009) even though diffusion is a simplified description for the ionic 
transport through concrete. Ions are electrically charged molecules, therefore interactions with 
surrounding electrical charged surfaces and coupled ions are inevitable as well as microstructure, 
sorption and capillary suction. These factors may retard or increase the transport kinetics, thus 
emphasizing that simple diffusion is not the only mechanism controlling transport (Marchand & 
Samson, 2009).  
 
Transport kinetics are crucial for the time passed before reaching critical chloride levels (Ccrit) 
causing the steel protective film to passivate (see section above). European standards (EN 206-1) 
are defining Ccrit between 0.2-0.4 % chloride by mass of binder for reinforced concrete. The critical 
threshold for reaching steel depassivation are though reported to be affected by other factors than 
Ccrit including degree of hydration, hydration products and oxygen availability (Angst et al., 2009).  
 
Despite chloride penetration and carbonation being of major concern in terms of steel corrosion, 
alternative elemental intrusion may also alter the concrete composition itself. Cl, Na, Mg, S, Ca and 
K are the main elements found in sea water and may cause phase changes in the concrete. Mg2+ can 
interact with both portlandite (Ca(OH)2) forming brucite (Mg(OH)2) and C-S-H forming M-S-H 
(Magnesium-Silicate-Hydrate)). SO4
- can react with portlandite forming gypsum or AFm phases, 
such as monosulfate, forming ettringite. Cl- can be bound in AFm phases forming Kuzel’s and 
Friedel’s salt. Jakobsen et al. (2016) presented a thorough description of the elemental zonation of 
both long- and short-term exposed concrete in seawater, concluding minor effects on total durability 
on the tested concrete, but suspected it might have an effect on the chloride intrusion.  
 
4.2.7 Environmental footprint.  
 
Due to the enormous amount of cement produced annually, and an estimated increase in global 
demand (Tam et al., 2006), the cement industry is believed to be the third largest source of 
anthropogenic CO2 emission, after fossil fuels and land-use changes (deforestation, agriculture etc.) 
(Andrew, 2018).  The two main contributors of carbon dioxide release are the chemical release of 
CO2 from the from limestone once heated above 600 ° C, and the energy used in the heating process 
of the clinker production. Even though exact estimates are difficult to obtain, the cement industry is 
reported to be responsible for 4-8 % of anthropogenic CO2 emissions (Andrew, 2018; Flatt et al., 
2012). Simultaneously non-renewable resources of natural aggregates (sand, gravel, rocks) are in 
many countries in short supply for future demand and news solutions for these problems need to be 
addressed. Six main points have previously been described by Flatt et. al (2011) as possible 
solutions to overcome the environmental challenges the cement industry faces and reduce future 
impact.  
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
1. Replacement of cement with supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs). (fly 
ash, slag, silica fume, calcined clay, pozzoloanic materials)  
2. Development of alternative binders. 
3. Development of mix designs with decreased use of cement, without reduction of 
concrete durability. 
4. Recycling of old concrete. 
5. Enhance durability of new concrete structures. 
6. Extend the service life of already existing concrete constructions.  
 
2.3.1 Limestone Calcined Clay as SCMs. 
 
Due to the overall scarcity in presently applied SCMs (blastfurnace slags, fly ash and other 
industrial waste/residues with pozzolanic properties) compared to the cement demand, there is 
increasing interest in finding sufficient and economically viable alternatives to reduce the impact on 
natural resources and carbon footprint.  
 
The blend of ordinary Portland cement (OPC) and calcined clay and limestone (LC3, Limestone 
Calcined Clay Cement) is a promising material to accommodate the demand for SCMs due to the 
high availability of both clays, containing sufficient amounts of kaolinit and limestone in the earth 
crust layers.   
 Life cycle assessments have showed that carbon reductions up to 30% are possible by using LC3 
compared to OPC (Sanchez et al., 2016; Scrivener et al., 2018). The reductions are especially high 
in tropical and subtropical regions were kaolinite clay are highly available, and the largest increase 
in building activity is expected (Scrivener et al., 2018).  
For the work of this report LC3 concrete was applied as SCMs. 
 
Kaolinite (Al2Si2O5(OH)4) can be calcined and dehydoxilated at 700-850° C, being significantly 
lower than temperatures needed for the production of traditional cement clinker. A lower 
temperature regime in the production cause severe reduction in energy consumption for the heating 
process. The dehydroxilation of kaolinite exposes aluminum and silicate groups, which are highly 
reactive in the presence of portlandite, thus serving as pozzoloans. Simultaneously the high 
aluminum content in the calcined clay can react with limestone to fill out voids, without strength 
reduction (Fernandez et al., 2011). These mechanisms provide basis for the substitution of OPC 
without strength loss, thereby reducing the total carbon footprint of concrete structures. 
 
Dhandapani et al., (2018) provided detailed information on LC3 concrete properties compared to 
traditional OPC. The results showed similar performances in terms of strength and elastic modulus 
while LC3concrete showed higher performance in terms of ion-intrusion due to a finer pore 
structure. As there is a reduction of OPC in the LC3 concrete, pH values are expected to be lower, 
thus more susceptible to carbonation induced corrosion of embedded steel (Rathinarajan & Pillai, 
2018).

5.10 Grinding practice  
 
The cylindrical concrete elements were mounted on a mechanical metal lathe, with an attached 
diamond blade. The fine adjustment of the lathe allowed to grind of 0.5 and 1 mm layers of the 
concrete cylinders for further analysis with a tolerance level down to ±0.1 mm. Turning speed was 
adjusted to allow grinding dust to be collected in a container below the blade. Prior to grinding all 
cylinder ends, of approx. 15 mm, were cut off with a stone saw thus only grinding the centred 
section of the cylinders. 
 
Layers of 0-0.5, 0.5-1, 1-2, 2-3 and 3-4 mm (±0.1 mm) were grinded and concrete dust was 
collected for analysis of chloride content. A reference point was marked in the area not grinded, 
while adjustment was done in reference to this for every grinded layer. All layers in the result 
sections are presented as mean values (e.g. 0-0.5 mm = 0.25 mm) 
 
5.11 Chloride analysis and ICP-OES 
 
Chloride analysis was performed according to standard NT 208.  
Approx. 5.00 g of concrete dust from each layer of all grinded samples were weighed of on a scale 
(0.01 g accuracy), dissolved in 65% nitric acid (HNO3) and further diluted with demineralised water 
(H2O). Granite residuals were filtered off through a filter (VWR filter paper 413, particle retention 
5-13 µm), and the dilute were titrated with AgNO3 to precipitate chloride ions, while measuring 
potentiometric potential for determining total chloride content. Calculations are available in the 
digital supplementary material. Non-exposed concrete elements of each recipe were tested for Cl- 
content to account for background concentrations.  
 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
A sample of the dilute, from each layer, was send to an external lab for analysis for cations with 
ICP-OES (ICP Optical Emission Spectometrer, Varian 72-ES) to determine calcium content as a 
mean of the cement concentration, as well as other cations.  
 
Theoretical calculations of calcium content were conducted to produce apparent diffusion rates for 
Cl/Ca and Cl/cement. Calculations are added in the digital supplementary material. 
 
5.12 Calculations

ASTM 1556-03 (Nordtest, 2003), by using the solution to Fick’s second law of diffusion (equation 
7, Cs = initial chloride content at x=0, Ci = background chloride content, x = depth, t = time).  
Calculations were performed in Microsoft Excel and can be found in the digital supplementary 
material.

5.13 Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) 
 
TGA was conducted on pure mortar cast powder (no aggregates) of each binder recipe, of similar 
water/binder ratio (0.5) to determine and quantify initial mineral composition.   
The simultaneous thermal analyser NETZSCH STA 449F3 was setup with approx. 50 mg hydrated 
mortar powder. For the analysis mass detection was initiated at 25°C with a temperature increase 
10°C /min until reaching 850 °C.  
 
Mass loss between 370 and 500 °C is used for dihydroxylation of portlandite (Ca(OH)2). Portlandite 
decomposes to CaO and H2O in that temperature range, and water evaporation is used to calculate 
the portlandite content of the cement paste.  
To account for the back ground mass loss of C-S-H during this temperature regime, the stepwise 
method presented in Lothenbach et al., (2017) was used by assuming the percentage weight loss 
==PAGE:33==
 
33 
prior to 370 °C represents the mass loss of C-S-H from 370 – 500 °C. This value is extrapolated, by 
using the tangential slope, over the mass loss of portlandite and subtracted the total mass loss. 
 
Mass loss between 600 and 800 °C was treated as loss of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) , as calcium 
carbonate decomposes to CaO and CO2. The release of CO2 into the carrier gas results in a mass 
loss and used to calculate the calcium carbonate quantity for the cement paste. Reference for 
calculations and mass loss characteristics can be found in Lothenbach et al., 2017. 
All calculations and graphs were performed in R Studio. Codes are added in the digital 
supplementary material.  
 
The calculated mineral quantities were normalised to cement paste by accounting for the water in 
the mix design and there by dividing the quantity with 2/3 due to the presence of 1/3 H2O in the 
concrete (w/b = 0.5) 
 
5.14 X-Ray powder Diffraction (XRD) 
 
XRD was conducted on cement paste (no aggregates) of each binder recipe with a water/binder 
ratio of 0.5 to qualitatively determine the differences in initial crystalline phase composition 
between the concrete mixes.  
A PANalytical X’Pert Pro instrument was used, operating at 45 mA and 40 kW with Cu Ka 
radiation and a 2Q X’Celerator detector. The samples were schannes in the range of 3-65 2Θ within 
45 minutes in steps of 0.1 2Θ. The main peaks of the diffractograms were identified utilizing 
references from the ICDD PDF-4 database.  
 
5.15 Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) and energy-dispersive X-ray 
spectroscopy (EDX) 
 
SEM micrographs were obtained on concrete recipes with both treatments after 4 weeks’ exposal. 
Unfortunately, not all samples could be measured due to technical issues of the electron

Concrete discs were cut, stored in isopropanol alcohol, dried at 40 °C, impregnated with low 
viscosity epoxy resin, polished and carbon coated. 
The instrument used was an environmental scanning electron microscopy FEI Quanta 200 (ESEM) 
equipped with a large field detector and an Oxford X MaxN EDX detector that was operated with an

6. Results and Discussion 
 
6.1 Porosity, water content, strength and surface roughness.  
 
The tested concrete properties are presented in Table 6. Open porosity and water content were 
tested and compared, in order to out rule large out dissimilarities when comparison the general 
performance of the concrete types. Compressive strength was compared to validate the actual 
usefulness of calcined clay and grey microfiller (LC3) as SMCs. Surface roughness was compared 
to be able to evaluate on the biological settlement.  
 
 Porosity, water content and strength displayed similar characteristics for all 3 concrete mixtures, 
demonstrating the usefulness of LC3 mixtures in terms of the tested characteristics. For strength, all 
mixtures revolved just below 40 MPA, as suggested for marine concrete structures by Aalborg 
Portland(AalborgPortland.dk, n.d.), while w/c ratio was purposely too high to increase chloride 
penetration for the short-term experiment. Average compressive strength for concrete containing 
LC3 was slightly higher after 15 days of curing in well accordance with results obtained by Antoni, 
2013. Average surface roughness (Ra) revealed to be higher for OPC100 compared to both LC3 
concretes. This may be due to the use of super plasticizer in the LC3 concrete (see Table 3). The 
granite cylinder showed an even higher surface roughness 2,5-7 x higher than the concrete 
cylinders. 
 
As seen from Figure 18 and Figure 17 some samples were split (prior to exposal). Whether this was 
due to different operator using the stone saw on different days (different cutting speed and a more 
doll blade) or due to a more fragile structure of the concrete is uncertain. Nevertheless it can be seen 
that the OPC100 samples are all intact, while 5 / 28 of the OPC65 LC3 26:9 were split, 23/30 of the 
OPC56 LC3 26:17 were split. When cutting the cylinders, they were still embedded in the PP-pipes, 
and splitting are suspected to have happened as the cutting saw have reached the centered brass pipe 
causing heavy vibrations in the concrete, although explanation remains uncertain. For the further 
analyses of Cl- and Ca2+ content, only cylinders in intact condition were used, to avoid irregular 
chloride intrusion from the crack formations, while the broken specimens were used for microscopy 
surveys of the formed biofilm.

6.4 Images and microscopy 
 
Most overgrown surface of each exposed sample is presented in Figure 15, 17 & 18. Image 
brightness has been increased 30% for all samples to improve visualization of the algae cover.  
A selection of representative microscopy images is presented in Figure 16, Figure 20 and Figure 19 
along with a superficial species identification of microalgae, noted in the comments of the figure 
down to genera.  
 
All samples seem to have some degree of biofouling, based on color change, in well accordance 
with the microscopy images. No sessile invertebrate species or larvae where found during 
inspection, and the successional stage is determined as phase III determined by the presence of 
several diatom species. Due to the season and duration of the experiment (late fall: six weeks) 
invertebrate colonization was not expected. A selection of representative images is presented in the 
following section.  
 
6.4.1 OPC100 
 
The surface nature of OPC100 samples has clearly undergone a color change towards more 
greenish colors on the surface images (Figure 15) with an increase from 2 to 6 weeks.  
Patches of green are already present on samples exposed for two weeks, and all treated samples also 
demonstrate greenish coloring, indicating that biofilm formation is happening faster than the weekly 
treatments, which is in good accordance with theoretical biofilm formation presented by 
Grzegorczyk & Pospiech (2018). 
Microscopy images (Figure 16) demonstrated a well-developed community of diatoms covering 
almost all surface area of the untreated samples while the treated samples had almost no presence of 
diatoms or other algae species. The green coloring may be related to early fouling phases (phase 1-
2) according to Dobretsov (2010) while the presence of these fouling types could not be recognized 
in the microscope. These findings may be caused by either a high colonization rates of organic 
molecules and/or bacteria or that some fouling organisms may have survived the weekly treatment.

rate. If leaching of Ca2+ is significant, it may also reduce the precision of Da for Cl- / Ca2+.

and suggested this would affect and reduce capillary suction, resulting in lower ionic penetration

samples with treatment. S/Ca profiles showed a lowered relationship compared to OPC100, except 
for LC3-65 no treatment, 4 weeks), which indicate a lower penetration rate found in the chloride 
profiles, assuming that the sulfate content originates from the seawater. A buildup in Mg/Ca was 
seen in the outermost layer similar to OPC100.  
 
LC3-56 showed overall similar ionic relationships to calcium as the LC3-65 samples, and was also 
demonstrating decreasing Al/Ca in the outermost layers with time. S/Ca was generally found to be 
higher for the samples exposed for 2 weeks for all samples (except LC3-56, 4 weeks), which is 
counter intuitive as sulfate intrusion would be expected to increase the sulfur content (Jakobsen et 
al., 2016). Results from chloride penetration revealed altered diffusion rates for chloride, 
throughout the experimental period (see Table 8) and this may also case for sulfur.  
The high Mg/Ca in the outermost layer was validated by SEM-EDX images.

6.7 SEM-EDX 
 
Due to constrict use of SEM, only images from OPC100 WT, NO and LC3-56 NT (NT=no 
treatment, WT= with treatment), were gained. Only samples exposed for 4 weeks for plain polished 
and used.   
 
SEM images of OPC100 WT and NT revealed detailed information about the degree of mechanical 
weathering from the treatment. From Figure 27 it can be seen that OPC100 WT demonstrates 
exposed aggregates to the outer surface, while the untreated sample has a cementitious outer layer 
in the outer surface, including a thin dense crust. These findings correlate well with the microscopy 
images for the treated and untreated samples (Figure 16). A dense crust is further demonstrated in 
Figure 27 and in Figure 28 with detailed element mapping of both magnesium (Mg) and calcium 
(Ca) on OPC100 NT and WT samples. Less clear accumulation of both Mg and Ca is observed for 
OPC100 WT. These findings demonstrate that the elemental accumulating (as calcite and bructite) 
in the exposed concrete elements may be altered by the presence of marine biofouling, or more 
likely that the weekly treatment by removing the fragile outer surface, resulting in less significant 
zonation patterns.  
The most profound difference for Da between the treatments was found for OPC100, where the 
sample without treatment had the lowest chloride content and Da.. Jakobsen et al. (2016) suggests 
that a dense calcite crust might have a sealing effect on the cover of marine exposed concrete.

silicate hydrate) phases – conclusive verification of this requires quantities comparison between Ca, 
Mg and Si ratios (K. De Weerdt & Justnes, 2015). If M-S-H is present this would explain why 
surface layer was more fragile to treatment as M-S-H is more fragile than C-S-H.

7. Conclusion and outlook.  
 
During the work of the thesis following conclusions were drawn to the initially raised questions.  
 
i) 
Mean apparent diffusion rates (Da) was comparable between treatments. The most 
profound differences both in terms of Da and initial chloride content (Cs) was found for 
the concrete type OPC100.  No constant pattern in Da indicated altered chloride 
penetration as a function of the treatment of biological growth.  
   
ii) 
Elemental ratios obtained from IPC data revealed a more profound difference between 
the periodic S/Ca profiles in the samples without treatment for OPC100 and LC3-65, 
even though there was a general tendency of decreasing S/Ca content in all samples.   
SEM-EDX imaging revealed increased accumulation of Ca and Mg on the outer surface 
of the non-treated OPC100 compared to the treated sample. Whether these altered 
relationships were due to the presence of biofouling or the mechanical weathering from 
the treatment of the samples in unknown.  
 
iii) 
Concrete composition did seem to have an effect on the biological community. Biofilm 
was present on all untreated samples after two weeks and biofilm of succession phase III 
was determined after 6 weeks, by the presence of diatoms. OPC100 had a more severe 
cover of diatoms compared to LC3. The coverage of diatoms on OPC100 was more 
similar to that found of the granite blocks.  
 
 
==PAGE:55==
 
55 
The conclusions drawn for the experiment represents only a short glimpse of the lifespan of 
submerged concrete structures and the marine biological community present on these. Despite the 
short period of the study, conditioning organic film and unicellular biofouling, seen as various 
diatom species, was observed already from 2 weeks, and documented via microscopy after 6 weeks. 
These patterns of colonization were in well accordance to the inspected literature. Non-the less the 
fast colonization of newly casted concrete in relative cold and low light conditions was surprising.  
The higher presence of diatoms on OPC100 and granite samples may be due to a coarser surface 
texture promoting adhesion of fouling particles and organisms, as shown for barnacle settlement in 
Coombes et al., (2015). This would indicate that the early fouling in this study are controlled by 
basic settlement strategies whereof substrate surface nature are the confining factor. (Prendergast, 
2009) 
 
In relevance to chloride or ion intrusion in concrete, no arguments were found in terms of 
promoting or regulating early biofilm formation due to bio-protecteion or erosion. The use of low 
emission SCMs LC3 in marine concrete revealed promising results in term of chloride intrusion, 
probably due to an altered pore structure by void filling mechanisms (Fernandez et al., 2011) 
compared to concrete based on pure OPC.  
IPC data revealed varying elemental relationships within the first millimeter of all samples, while 
deeper layers showed similar ratios for each respective sample. A stable relationship for Al/Ca 
through all measured depths was found for OPC100, both treatments, after 6 weeks. SEM-EDX 
images did not reveal particular leaching of aluminum. In general IPC measurements revealed 
generally lower stoichiometric relationships (in relation to Mg) than was expected from theoretical 
calculations and results may be misleading. This could be due to an altered phase composition, with 
time, in the concrete. Dhandapani et al. (2018) shows how both LC3 and pure OPC based concrete 
expose increased strength far after 50 days of curing, indicating that phase equilibrium is not 
reached for the exposed samples. Some phases may be more soluble in HNO3 than others, and the 
phase alteration may be responsible for some of the variation seen in the samples. This would also 
point out a week spot in chloride measurement standards, as there is no certainty that all phases are 
dissolved. The lack of especially dissolved silicate and aluminum in the control experiment in 
section 6.6 stresses this.    
 
  XRD, TGA and SEM data provided detailed and useful information of the mineralogical 
compositions of all concrete types, which allowed a more detailed perspective of the concrete. 
Future work will provide SEM-EDX images for all concrete types, and may reveal more details.  
 
Generally using replicates when documenting these effects would be beneficial, but the time-
consuming processing of each concrete samples, may limit the overall work during a short-term 
experiment.  
 
Biological surveys were downsized due to the extensive work of harvesting and prepping samples, 
even though algae identification was performed no quantitative measurements were made. None-
invasive biological surveys of epifauna are normally performed on species larger than microalgae. 
Quantitative analysis of biofilm requires advanced equipment, as confocal reflection microscopy 
(Grzegorczyk & Pospiech, 2018) or fluorescence measurement techniques, inaccessible  for this 
project. The simple method of organic carbon measurements (burning organic carbon at 
temperatures at 550 °C) was originally planned to be executed, but the fact that the cured concrete 
lost up to 5 % of its initial weight at between 200 and 500 °C, made this method highly imprecise, 
when taking the relative mass relationship between microalga and concrete into account. Image 
==PAGE:56==
 
56 
analyzing codes was produced in Matlab, to register and calculate color ratio of the concretes 
surface area as a mean of biofilm, but failed to produce valid data, due to the complex coloring of 
the concrete, biofouling and the reduction of colors (to RGB) when using digital image capturing. 
Matlab code for image color analysis are provided in the appendix. 
 
The presence of grazing gastropods was an interesting input to the experiment as grazing organisms 
has been shown to control the early marine benthic community (Hillebrand et al., 2000; Hutchinson 
et al., 2006; Lotze, 1999). Microscope analysis revealed severely higher presence of diatoms on the 
OPC100, compared to the two LC3 concrete types, whether this was due to a higher surface 
roughness or grazing pressure is unknown.  Granite cylinders was used as a control as a natural 
material to validate on the stage of succession. These were mounted above OPC100 cylinders (see 
Figure 7) and if an effect in grazing activity, due to e.g. difference in concrete pH is present, the 
control is useless. The above are exclusively speculative but may be important to investigate in 
order to draw final conclusions on the concretes effect on the early marine biological community.  
 
A study of similar concept has been performed over a prolonged exposal period up to 10 years by 
Kawabata et al. (2012). During the 10-year period, biological community build up had been allowed 
on concrete elements to a later successional sequence. The presence of larger encrusting biota 
(oysters and barnacle) significantly reduced chloride intrusion into the tested concrete elements. 
Coombes et al. (2017) provided similar results from an indirect study by the presence of barnacles 
in the tidal zone. The study showed that the presence of barnacles reduced the variation in the 
temperate regime, thus reducing crystal formation from evaporating salt water and chloride 
intrusion. Coombes et al. (2013) found a similar relationship between seaweed cover and reduced 
thermal variations by 22 % limiting wetting-drying cycles and salt crystallization on concrete. Lv et 
al., 2015 obtained reduced chloride penetration in vitro by introducing bacteria strains, isolated 
from marine exposed concrete elements, on mortar samples.  
Actively incorporating designs into marine concrete structures promoting growth of sessile 
organisms, may be an effective tool for coastal managers to mitigate the impact from marine 
sprawling and urbanization causing pollution and habitat fragmentation (Strain et al., 2018). In 
order to incorporate conservation biology into marine infrastructure, without reducing structural 
performance, detailed mapping must be made of the possible interactions between the biological 
community and the materials applied in marine construction. Interactive mechanisms experiments 
between material and biota must be done both for single species community and for more complex 
communities (Coombes et al., 2013). Borrowing practicing guideline from artificial reef 
management should be implemented, in order to set realistic targets and validate on results (Baine, 
2001), especially as a tool to determine site specific interventions from marine structures (Strain et 
al., 2018). Primary goals, from a conservation perspective, may be of ecosystem service character 
as increasing abundance of fish or filtering organisms for, much needed, better water quality in 
coastal waters. These goals would be in well accordance with the goals of the European Water 
Framework Directive. At present ECOncrete® provides patented solutions for concrete promoting 
settlement of marine organisms, by a e.g. lowering pH of the concrete and by providing complex 
structure while documenting increased abundance of all taxa, biomass and a decreased ratio of 
native to exotic species (Sella & Perkol-finkel, 2015). These solutions are primarily used for 
breakwaters while similar considerations should be incorporated in other marine construction types 
as well.   
 
Further research on LC3 concrete as marine concrete is needed to validate on its usage as marine 
exposed concrete. The overall reduction in portlandite reduces the pH of the concrete, consequently 
==PAGE:57==
 
57 
reducing protective properties of the embedded steel. Scrivener et al. (2018) provided data on 
increased carbonation rates of concrete containing LC3 compared to OPC based concretes, while 
Rathinarajan & Pillai (2018) showed similar  increased carbonation rates leading to increased steel 
corrosion under an accelerated carbon dioxide environment, no details on binder composition were 
added. As steel corrosion is induced by the presence of both water and oxygen and accelerated by 
the presence of chloride, the total effect of carbonation along with transport processes in LC3 
concretes should be investigated.  Despite the lower abundance of diatoms on the LC3-concrete 
samples in this experiment, a reduction of pH is generally believed to promote biological settlement 
on concrete  (Manso, 2014; Perkol-Finkel et al., 2018, ), and future research for a prolonged time-
scale is advised to reveal the long-term effects of calcined clay and limestone in cement on the 
settlement of marine organisms.

Ved dyk medbringes en bøje med line, hæftet til dykkeren med påmonteret det dykkerflag flag. 
Bøjen er en torpedobøje indeholdende ca. 55 liter luft. Bøjen kan udover blikfang for både bruges 
som redningsbøje i tilfælde af en nødsituation.  
Ved dyk medbringes altid en kniv der hæftes i en skede på underbenet hvor dykkeren altid har fri 
adgang til den. Kniven kan bruges i tilfælde af at dykkeren kommer i karambolage med 
omkringliggende eller fritsvævende fiskegarn og liner. 
Vægtvest og bælge er fastmonteret ved hjælp af to quick release mekanismer på brystet og over 
skridtet. Disse kan begge administreres med handsker, ligesom kniven kan skære begge dele over – 
i tilfælde af nødsituation kan bæltet aftages op dragtens opdrift vil på de 2 meters dybde bringe 
dykkeren til overfladen.  
 
Planlagte dyk fremgår af vedlagte dykkerplan, og det er dykkerens ansvar enten at medbringe en 
person eller have telefonisk kontakt med en kontaktperson før og efter estimeret eller aftalt 
tidspunkt for dykket. Kan telefonisk kontakt ikke oprettes kender kontaktpersonen placeringen af 
rammen og rette henvendelse til rette myndighed værende Søværnet Operative Kommando 
(SOK) på telefon 4590 6000.  
 
For at undgå afkøling inden dykket, foretages skift til våddragt hjemmefra, og dykkeren kan derfor 
sidde varmt i bilen inden dykket – ligeledes kan telefonen sidde i opladning da det er muligt at 
parkere helt op til kysten hvor rammerne ligger placeret.  
 
Risici:  
Afkøling. 
 
 
Forbehold: Dykkene holdes korte, gerne under 30 minutter, prøverne er placeret på relative lavt. 
Føler dykkeren tegn på nedkøling eller ubehag skal dykkes afbrydes.  
 
Risici: 
Dykkeren driver væk fra kysten. 
Forbehold:  Opstillingen er placeret få meter fa kysten, på relativt lav dybde. Opstillingen er sat på 
en sydøst vendt kyststrækning – de dominerende vinde kommer som regel fra vest. Dykkeren 
medbringer en bøje der kan bære dykkeren. I tilfælde af kraftig strøm eller kraftige bølger, kan dyk 
udskydes. I tilfælde af at dykkeren føres væk med strømmen, skal dykkeren hæfte sig til bøjen og 
lade sig drive med strømmen indtil strømmen aftager og dykkeren kan svømme imod land. Er vejret 
ugunstigt bør dyk ikke gennemføres.  
 
Risici: 
Vand i lungerne 
==PAGE:70==
 
70 
Forbehold: Dyk holdes kortvarige, max 30 sekunder, for at undgå ildebefindende under vand. Ved 
dyk fjernes snorklen fra munden for at undgå at vand i snorklen inhaleres når dykkeren atter er i 
kontakt med overfladen.  
 
Risici:  
Iltmangel 
Forbehold:  Pauserne imellem dykkene skal være min. 30 sekunder, og gerne længere, for at 
optage tilstrækkeligt ilt inden næste dyk 
 
Risici: 
Kollision 
Forbehold: Rammerne er placeret tæt ved en stenmole, på et aflåst område. Dykkeren bør til hver 
en tid orientere sig om maritim trafik af enhver art inden dyk påbegyndes. Ligeledes medbringes en 
bøje med påmonteret dykkerflag.  
 
Risici: 
mistet orientering 
Forbehold: Dyk bør altid foretages i dagslys, og i dage med klart vejr.
