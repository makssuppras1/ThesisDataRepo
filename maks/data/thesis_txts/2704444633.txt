Abstract 
Aspergillus fumigatus, a predominant pathogen is estimated to affect more than 300,000 
patients annually. Antifungal agents are used in clinical treatment as both frontline and 
salvage therapy. Due to the rise in acquired resistance to antifungals, patients with antifungal-
resistant Aspergillus disease may fail to respond to therapy, which has been associated with 
extremely high mortality rates. Previously characterized resistance mechanisms, alone or 
even in combination poorly explains the majority of the observed antifungal resistance in A. 
fumigatus clinical isolates. Since A. fumigatus is always present in our environment and also 
in close proximity to patients at risk for aspergillosis, there is an urgent need to investigate 
the mechanisms leading to the increased antifungal resistance.  
The project investigated raw sequence files of three antifungal resistant clinical isolates of A. 
fumigatus (SSI-2716, SSI-6207 and SSI-6703) with the ultimate goal of identifying and 
assessing the genetic determinants contributing to resistance using genome-based approach. 
QC analysis, de novo assembly and non-synonymous mutations in the isolates were identified 
utilizing bioinformatic tools. It appeared that previously described clinical resistance 
mechanisms explained only a fraction of resistance in isolates studied through in-silico 
analysis. All isolates lacked mutations in cyp51A, a gene widely known to contribute to 
antifungal resistance in A. fumigatus. Non-synonymous mutations in hp (L446F) and hmg 
(Y564) was seen in all isolates whereas, mutations in alpha-amino (A1038G, K1075N) were 
seen only in SSI-2716 and SSI-6703. Therefore, the gene editing experiments conducted 
examined both previously characterised and uncharacterised resistance mechanism present 
in all isolates.  
tRNA mediated CRISPR-Cas9 system was used in gene editing experiments to correct the 
identified non-synonymous mutations. The CRISPR-tRNA plasmid were made using USER 
fusion employing PCR fragments with PacI/Nt.BbvCI digested empty CRISPR vector having hph 
(pFC332) as the fungal marker. GE-oligonucleotides were designed with changed codon to 
correct the mutation that served as the repair templates for specific Cas9/sgRNA induced DNA 
DSBs. Purified CRISPR-tRNA vectors were sent for sequencing and restriction analysis was also 
performed with BspEI. Similarity between the obtained agarose gel and in-silico gel pattern 
with BspEI indicated proper insertion of the sgRNA tRNA-splicing cassette. A series of 
transformation experiments were carried out following protoplastation with 150-250 mg/mL 
hygromycin as the selectable marker.  Isolates cultivated upon correction were checked for 
antifungal susceptibility for azoles to verify if the identified non-synonymous mutation was 
the factor contributing in inducing resistance according to EUCAST-AFST screening method 
and also with itraconazole susceptibility test designed. The results of this project would allow 
further exploration of the resistance mechanism in the identified genetic determinants that 
could potentially be used to enhance the efficacy of antifungal agents and improve the quality 
of life of immunocompromised patients. For future work, identification and correction of 
additional new targets followed by antifungal susceptibility test would be recommended, 
thereby aiding in elucidating new resistance mechanisms.

Project description and scope 
Background 
A. fumigatus, a filamentous fungus commonly found on almost all surface of the earth and in 
air, is the major causative agent of aspergillosis. Its conidia dispersed in air have a diameter 
of 2 to 3 µm, and can reach lung alveoli when inhaled. In contrast to immunocompromised 
individuals who are susceptible to severe and potentially fatal invasive infections caused by 
inhaling conidia, an immunocompetent individual is capable of eliminating the adverse effects 
efficiently through their innate immune mechanism. In immunocompromised individuals or 
individuals with local immunosuppression, A. fumigatus colonizes and invades the lung 
tissues leading to the development of aspergillosis, making it one of the most prevalent 
opportunistic fungal pathogens. The incidence of A. fumigatus-related invasive infections 
reported are increasing annually, causing 50% mortality if diagnosed and treated and 100% 
fatal when undiagnosed or delayed. Although antifungal therapy is commonly employed in 
clinical treatment, there is a growing resistance to antifungals among A. fumigatus isolates 
globally in recent years, making treatment less effective. According to reports, isolates 
acquire resistance to antifungals after its prolonged use in clinical treatment or exposure in 
the environment through agriculture. Among the various molecular pathways implicated in 
conferring antifungal resistance in fumigatus, mutations in the gene encoding sterol 14α-
demethylase, cyp51A, are the most common. Additionally, overexpression of sterol-
demethylase genes, overexpression of efflux pump-encoding gene abcC, mutations in HMG-
CoA reductase-encoding gene hmg and other genes that results in increased level of 
ergosterol production and/ or accumulation may also result in resistance.  
However, these mechanisms alone do not explain the existence of antifungal resistance in all 
clinical isolates of A. fumigatus, and potentially there may be other mechanisms causing 
resistance. Thus, identification and characterization of novel genetic determinants of 
antifungal resistance will offer insight into ways to reduce resistance development. This thesis 
will explore the opportunity of using genome driven identification of novel drug resistance 
mechanisms in clinical isolates of A. fumigatus, using in-silico tools to analyse sequence reads 
and identify potential resistance inducing targets. Specifically, this project will employ CRISPR-
tRNA mediated gene editing to correct the target mutations using GE-oligonucleotides as 
repair templates in all the clinical isolates of A. fumigatus. The focus of this project is to correct 
non-cyp51A mutations in clinical isolates and later check their antifungal susceptibility, 
thereby devising a strategy to overcome antifungal resistance and improve the quality of life 
of the immunocompromised individuals affected by A. fumigatus. 
Goal 
Therefore, the following goals will be the focus of this project: 
 
Screening and analysis of the raw sequence files of the obtained A. fumigatus clinical 
isolates. 
 
Identification of the genetic determinants leading to antifungal resistance in the 
obtained A. fumigatus clinical isolates.  
 
Design and construction of CRISPR-tRNA for the correction of identified mutation 
using GE-oligonucleotides via gene editing experiments.  
 
Transformation and antifungal susceptibility testing to assess the mutation-mediated 
resistance within the clinical isolates.

Bases for this project 
In this project, genome driven approach was used to identify novel modes of drug resistance 
in three clinically isolated A. fumigatus strains to test the genetic determinants (i.e., non-
cyp51A mutations) hypothesized to induce antifungal resistance. The project is divided into 
two phases: 
 
Phase 1: In-silico analysis of A. fumigatus clinical isolates 
 
Phase 2: Analysis of target mutations among the antifungal-resistant A. fumigatus 
clinical isolates 
Phase 1: In-silico analysis of A. fumigatus clinical isolates 
Initially, adapters in the raw sequence file of A. fumigatus clinical isolates were removed using 
AdapterRemoval (Version: 2.3.0) program then the quality assessment of the read data was 
performed using FastQC (version: 0.11.5). Following de novo assembly of the cleaned files, 
the target mutations conferring antifungal resistance were identified in all the three clinical 
isolates with the aid of CLC Genomics Workbench (CLC Bio-Qiagen, Aarhus, Denmark). Prior 
to identifying mutation targets, the gene prediction tool Augustus was used to annotate the 
assembled sequence files and predict the computational molecular function of the genes 
predicted. 
Phase 2: Analysis of target mutations among the antifungal-resistant A. fumigatus clinical 
isolates 
The sequence files of all three antifungal-resistant A. fumigatus clinical isolates revealed only 
non-cyp51A mutations, which were further assessed in this project using the below described 
stages.  
 
Stage A: Design and construction of CRISPR-tRNA for gene editing experiments 
Primers were designed to insert protospacers, through primer tails, between promoter 
and sgRNA-terminator fragments. USER fusion was performed using PCR fragments i.e., 
Up- and Dw-sgRNA cassette of CRISPR plasmids with empty CRISPR vector (pFC332) with 
hygromycin as the fungal marker and then transformed into E. coli DH5α. Following 
plasmid purification from three independent colonies of each transformant, restriction 
analysis with BspEI was performed and purified vectors were also sent for sequencing. GE-
oligonucleotides were designed with codon change to be used in transformation 
experiment for mutation correction induced by CRISPR-tRNA.   
 
Stage B: GE-oligonucleotide mediated CRISPR-tRNA gene-editing 
Following protoplastation, transformation were performed with hygromycin as the 
selectable marker for CRISPR-tRNA mediated target-mutation correction. GE-
oligonucleotides were used as repair templates along with their respective purified 
vectors having accurate sgRNA tRNA-splicing cassette.  
 
Stage C: Antifungal susceptibility analysis 
Both antifungal susceptibility test based on ESCMID-EFISG aspergillosis guidelines and a 
designed itraconazole assay was conducted on transformants resistant to hygromycin. 
This approach was intended for the phenotypic detection of isolates resistant to 
itraconazole, voriconazole, or posaconazole to determine whether CRISPR-mediated 
mutation correction reduced resistance in the transformants.

1. Literature Study  
 1.1 Introduction 
Kingdom fungi is regarded as one of the largest and oldest clades of living organisms on earth. 
It is a monophyletic group that diverged from a common ancestor with that of the animals 
about 800 to 900 million ago comprising ten phyla: Cryptomycota, Microsporidia, 
Chytridiomycota, Monoblepharidomycota, Neocallimastigomycota, Blastocladiomycota, 
Zoopagomycota, Mucoromycota, Ascomycota and Basidiomycota [1].  Fungi are the second 
largest group of eukaryotes, with estimates stretching between 1.5 to 5.1 million species [2]. 
They possess the capability to digest food externally by secreting enzymes into the 
environment and then the dissolved organic matter is absorbed back into their cells. Their cell 
walls are primarily composed of chitin (a substance also found in animal kingdom) and they 
store food as reserves as glycogen and lipids. The plasma membrane of fungi is similar to that 
of the other eukaryotes but their structure is stabilized by a steroid molecule, ergosterol. They 
form an independent kingdom in spite of having superficial resemblance to plants (for 
instance having rooted, stalked structures) owing to their cell membrane chemistry, food 
storage methods, non-photosynthetic, absorptive feeding method and DNA. Fungi and 
Animalia are regarded to be closely related to each other  among the kingdom of life based 
of evidence gathered through evolutionary studies [3].  
 
Figure 1: Tree of life- schematic representation of kingdom fungi. Well known fungi examples 
from Ascomycota and Basidiomycota are shown. (Note. Adapted from [4]) 
Members of the fungal kingdom have complex cell structures and abilities to make tissues 
and organs as expected in higher organisms and impact significantly on the environment, 
industrial processes, and human health. Pertaining to the ecological aspect, fungi act as the 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    2 
 
‘primary decomposers’ of organic matter thereby playing a crucial role in the biogeochemical 
cycling of carbon in nature. In present day agricultural industry, fungi play key roles as 
symbionts, mutualistic, pathogens and saprophytes by mobilizing nutrients and affecting the 
physiochemical environment [5]. Additionally in agricultural operations they extend many 
established valuable roles, in particular as biofertilizers, mycorrhizae, and biocontrol agents 
of pests, pathogens and weeds. Fungal metabolism for instance, degradative activities has 
been instrumental in bioremediation of contaminated land, biotransformation of specific 
compounds and industrial waste treatment [6]. Exploitation of yeast and fungi metabolism 
has led to production of numerous industrially important economical commodities which 
includes diverse range of products as fermented beverages, whole foods, food additives, 
antibiotics, enzymes, biofuels, organic and fatty acids, pigments, pharmaceuticals, and 
sterols. Secondary metabolites with biological activities are produced by fungi as they are 
well-known to produce numerous bioactive molecules playing a key role in drug discovery. 
Some notable secondary metabolites from fungi in current pharmaceutical includes penicillin, 
those used as cholesterol lowering, antibiotics, or immunomodulatory agents [7].  
However, the same abilities of the fungi can also infect and decay of important artefacts, 
buildings, commodities, materials and food and fodder or cause fatal disease in living 
organisms, including humans. With regard to human health, yeasts and fungi constitute major 
opportunistic pathogens causing life threatening diseases, whereas others are life-savers by 
being the source of antimicrobial and chemotherapeutic agents. Fungi are also competent to 
produce mycotoxins which pose a serious concern as it risks he safety of livestock and humans 
worldwide [8]. 
1.2 Fungal morphology and growth  
Diverse range of fungal morphologies exist i.e. most higher fungi are filamentous, yeasts 
(unicellular), and some primitive fungi such as Chytridiomycota which  grow as rounded 
individual cells or dichotomous branched chains of cells with root-like rhizoids.  Yeasts are 
unicellular fungi and divide asexually by budding or fission [5]. Examples of yeasts include 
Saacharomyces cerevisiae commonly known as baker’s yeast and Candida species capable of 
causing both superficial and serious systemic disease [9].  
With most fungi being multicellular organisms, they display two distinct morphological stages 
i.e. vegetative and reproductive stage. Filamentous fungi are multicellular and grow as 
multinuclear network of filament shaped cells named as hyphae[10]. The long, slender 
thread-like structure of a fungus is termed as hyphae (singular, hypha) which is the main mode 
of vegetative growth. Individual hypha shows growth through hyphal tip extension and 
branching. A network of interconnected hyphae, or mycelium is resulted as the filamentous 
fungi grow owing to the continuous formation of fusions between the hyphae by a process 
called anastomosis. Different nuclei can coexist in the cytoplasm by forming a heterokaryon 
through hyphal fusion occurring between different fungal individuals [10]. This 
interconnected mycelial network from multiple vegetative hyphal fusion events is important 
for homeostasis, translocation of nutrients and water, and intra-hyphal communications  [11].   
Yeast and filamentous fungi are merely different manner of fungal growth suited for 
occupying different habitats and produced by varying cell growth polarities. The vegetative 
body of the fungus is unicellular or multicellular thallus whereas, dimorphic fungi can change 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    3 
 
from unicellular to multicellular state, in other words, adopt either unicellular yeast or hyphal 
form depending on environmental conditions. For example, certain fungal pathogens of 
humans and animals exist at ambient temperature as moulds in soil but are able to convert 
to yeast upon inhalation of the infectious spores into the lungs of a host. Such temperature-
induced phase transition is observed as a signature feature of systemic dimorphic fungi [12].  
1.3 Fungal life cycle 
Fungi have complex and diverse life cycles and are capable to reproduce asexually, sexually 
and/ or parasexually. Parasexual cycle in fungi refers to the recombination of the hereditary 
determinants outside that of the sexual cycle, in other words, genes from different individuals 
combine without formation of sexual cells and structures [13]. This event occurs through 
either fragmenting hyphae or production of different kinds of spores. Fungi reproduce 
asexually by budding, fragmentation, or spore production and sexually with homothallic or 
heterothallic mycelia.  Asexual cycle is mitotic whereas the sexual cycle comprises of two 
events for variation generation: fertilization and meiosis. Genetically different individuals are 
generated from sexual process as long as the mates assembling at fertilization are different 
genetically from one another while uniform progeny are resulted from asexual reproduction. 
In both asexual and sexual reproduction, most of the fungal spores germinate giving rise to 
vegetatively growing mat with each of the hyphal cell further divide mitotically to produce 
mycelium [14].  
1.3.1 Asexual reproduction 
Asexual reproduction in fungi occurs by budding, fragmentation, or production spores. Buds 
are formed by the somatic yeast cells. In the course of budding (a type of cytokinesis), a bulge 
is formed on the side of the cells with nucleus dividing mitotically, and the bud finally detaches 
from the mother cell. New colonies can grow from fragments of the hyphae. Fungal mycelium 
separates into pieces through mycelial fragmentation resulting in each component to grow 
into a separate mycelium.  Formation of asexual spores is the most common mode of asexual 
reproduction. Such spores are genetically identical to that of the parent as they are produced 
by only one parent through mitosis. After being released from the parent thallus they allow 
the fungi to stretch their distribution and colonize new environment. Asexual spores can be 
released either outside or within sporangium, a special reproductive sac where 
sporangiospores are produced. Many types of asexual spores can be produced such as 
conidiospores are unicellular or multicellular spores are released from the side or tip of the 
hypha directly while others originate from hyphae fragmentation to form single cells that are 
released as spores.  
1.3.2 Sexual reproduction  
Sexual reproduction is resulted in response to adverse environmental condition along with 
introducing genetic variation in the fungi population. Homothallic species refers to those 
having both the mating type in the same mycelium. It is also termed as self-fertile as they can 
complete the sexual cycle on their own with sexual reproduction being triggered without 
fertilization between two different strains. In heterothallic species, interaction between two 
different strains is required to complete the life cycle with initiation of sexual reproduction 
being dependent on the availability of compatible nuclei. Even though there are many 
variations in sexual reproduction of fungi, all comprise the following stages. First, two haploid 
cell fuse during plasmogamy stage, directing to the dikaryotic stage where in a single cell two 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    4 
 
haploid nuclei coexist. Further, diploid zygote nucleus is formed by the fusion of haploid nuclei 
during karyogamy. Finally, gametes of different mating types are generated through meiosis 
in gametangia (singular, gametangium). Ascospores, or sexual spores are resulted following 
meiotic division within the developing asci in closed spherical ascocarps [14].

1.4 Aspergillus 
Aspergillus species constitute a diverse fungal group worldwide amidst the most abundant 
fungi.  Spanning over 200 million years of evolution over 185 aspergilli are known to have an 
impact on society and human health, comprising 40 human pathogens in addition to the 
beneficial species used in food preparation, fermentation of beverages, production of food 
additives and industrial enzymes [15]. Aspergillus is an anamorph genus with a total number 
of 837 species names introduced under it [16]. Sexual stages associated with them are 
approximately classified into ten different genera (i.e. teleomorph genera) indicating they a 
diverse group of fungi.  To cite an instance, A. fumigatus is a part of teleomorph genus 
Neosartorya, while A. nidulans and A. flavus belong to the genera Emericella and Petromyces, 
respectively [17]. Despite considerable difference genome sequence and size in the same 
genus, the three aspergilli i.e. A. nidulans, A. fumigatus and A. oryzae have protein identity 
comparable to that between fish and mammals with these animals being separated around 
450 million years ago [18]. Aspergillus species are not very specific for growth with respect to 
abiotic conditions (Table 2) and are capable of growing over a wide range of temperature (6-
55 °C) and at relatively low humidity [17]. They grow abundantly on decaying vegetation as

Aspergillus species exhibit fascinating biotechnological potential by producing numerous 
industrial enzymes (e.g. amylases), organic acids (e.g. citric acid), food stuffs (e.g. soy sauce)

combination with molecular biology and established fermentation technology [27].

1.5.1 Environmental niches 
Owing to its thermotolerant nature, A. fumigatus grow and survive at a wide range of 
temperature (optimally at 37°C) and pH (optimum 5.5-6.5) as listed in Table 2. They are 
distributed worldwide and can be isolated throughout the year from numerous 
environments, for instance soil, air, and organic debris. They can also be isolated from 
decaying vegetation and soil at a temperature reach range between 12°C and 65°C [47] and 
pH range between 2.1 - 8.8. A. fumigatus comprises 35 to 70 percent of the total numbers of 
colony forming fungi making them the predominant fungus in greenhouse and garden soil 
[46].  They are capable of causing disease in humans however they do not typically colonize 
the human respiratory tract [48]. 
1.5.2 Life cycle 
A. fumigatus are reported to produce conidia (asexual spores) upon starvation on 
conidiophores which are specialized hyphal structures. Conidia are successively produced on 
these characteristic conidial heads and are then dispersed in the environment. A. fumigatus 
conidia are hydrophobic in nature which allows them to disperse easily and efficiently when 
compared with other Aspergillus spp., and the melanin in their cell wall protects them from 
ultraviolet irradiation (UV) [46]. Around 100-1,000 conidia are estimated to be inhaled by 
humans every day and owing to their small size 2-3 µm they can reach the lung alveoli. Inhaled 
conidia are removed by the airway epithelial cells cilia and resident alveolar macrophages. 
The conidia can germinated to cause invasive infection upon remaining in the lungs when the 
inhaled conidia are not cleared by the individuals. The conidia can germinate into germ tubes 
(short hyphae) within 4-9 hours owing to the fact that the physiological temperature of 
human body is 37°C which is optimum for their growth and germination [48].  
 
Despite A. fumigatus being first described 158 years ago [49], it was under the impression to 
exclusively reproduce by asexual means not so long ago. One possible explanation for this 
could be that the environmental parameters required to trigger sex are rarely encountered in 
nature. Cross set between two strains of opposite ‘mating-type’ (MAT) resulted in

1.5.3 Origin of virulence  
The evolutionary origin pertaining to the mechanism responsible for determining the 
virulence of A. fumigatus, an environmental fungi, is unclear.  A. fumigatus come across 
numerous abiotic and biotic hazards in its natural ecological niche. It has been speculated that 
the ‘virulence school’ of A. fumigatus may have advanced due to their ability to survive and 
grow in competitive environment. In such competitive environment the fungus is confronted 
with various stresses, for instance, high temperatures, pH changes, limited nutrients and 
fluctuating oxygen and carbon dioxide levels [52]. This indicates that A. fumigatus obtained 
traits that could translate them to be virulent in humans by surviving in such environment as

such stresses are present within the host. Additionally, some have argued that the natural 
environment external to the human body is distinctive that it is unlikely that the conditions 
required to survive in the external environment will be comparable to those necessary for 
virulence. The fact that A. fumigatus, an opportunistic pathogen of environmental origin, may 
have previously encountered other hosts and microorganisms in the natural ecological niche 
prior to causing infections in humans is an important consideration. Aspergillus spp., compete 
with all the prokaryotic and eukaryotic species in the soil.  This hostile environment could 
cause competition for nutrients and contribute to virulence as a result of mechanisms likely 
evolved in such conditions [53].

1.5.4 Survival and propagation under variety of environmental conditions 
A. fumigatus can be readily isolated worldwide from different types of substrate all year 
round. Mycotoxins production by A. fumigatus aids them to survive in the highly toxic natural 
environment and compete with microflora. The same secondary metabolites in mammalian 
host exhibit pathogenic and virulent effects, including immunosuppression, cytotoxicity, 
carcinogenicity and mutagenicity. Conidial inhalation leads to hyphae growth causing 
infectious disease in the lungs. The hyphae grow and embed themselves forming a dense 
extracellular matrix (ECM) in the lungs during the development of the mycelium. The function 
of ECM, is to provide protection to the fungus from the hostile environment by tightly gluing 
the hyphae together. Despite no studies have been conducted in A. fumigatus, ECM consists 
of proteins, polysaccharides, hexoses and pigments which may hinder drug penetration [57]. 
In addition to their remarkable thermotolerance, A. fumigatus ascospores have an 
extraordinarily thick wall unlike other aspergilli, like A. nidulans for protection. These 
ascospores germinated after being heated to 70°C for 30 minutes and sustain in compost piles 
with a core temperature exceeding 70°C [41]. Despite the inability of A. fumigatus to grow 
below 12°C, its conidia can survive stresses imposed by prolonged freezing. It is possible for 
the conidia to survive up to 18 years in liquid, depending on the strain [47].   Few genes known 
to be associated with fungal growth at a temperature of 48°C are characterized, whereas the 
genetic systems responsible for their survival and growth are unidentified [58].  
The widespread of A. fumigatus in nature is a result of successful defence systems like efflux 
pumps and potent secondary metabolites. A. fumigatus has 383 genes in 22 gene clusters 
corresponding to secondary metabolites [57] and a total of 16 different secondary 
metabolites have been discovered [59], including gliotoxin, a toxic antimicrobial agent. A. 
fumigatus encodes enzymes involved in secondary metabolites production and also ABC in 
transporters in higher number than A. fisherianus, its close genetic relative [58]. A. fumigatus 
has specific enzymes, such as 5 catalases, 4 superoxide dismutases [60] and glutathione 
transferases [61] for detoxification of reactive oxygen species, ROS. Extracellular enzymes, 
primarily proteases, are secreted by A. fumigatus, which degrades and recycles organic 
matter in their environment, but they break down the host’s structural barrier to gain access 
to nutrients upon onset of infection. Nutrient deprivation is host’s one of the antimicrobial 
mechanism, and the amount of hydrolases secreted may assist A. fumigatus in obtaining the 
nutrients from mammalian tissues [60]. All these features collectively equips A. fumigatus in 
their survival and propagation under a range of conditions that would be detrimental to a 
variety environmental organisms.  
1.5.5 Characteristics of A. fumigatus conidia  
Melanins are negatively charged, hydrophobic macromolecules produced through oxidative 
polymerization of indolic or phenolic compounds. Usually they result in brown or black colour 
pigments, but other colours are also seen. 1,8-dihydroxynaphthalene-like melanin (DHN-
melanin) found in A. fumigatus conidia contributes to their bluish green colour, along with 
DHN-melanin pathway by-products exhibiting immunosuppressive or antibacterial properties 
[62]. DHN-melanin is known to contribute in protecting A. fumigatus against environmental 
conditions and is a key factor for its virulence [63]. The melanin protects the conidia from UV 
irradiation and the incredible hydrophobicity causes the conidia to disperse even to the 
slightest air current. It scavenges ROS generated by neutrophils and alveolar macrophages to 
protect A. fumigatus against the host immune effector cells [62]. The spore dispersal 
efficiency is impacted by the degree of hydrophobicity and conidia that are wetter easily don’t

disperse in air currents readily [64]. In comparison to other aspergilli like A. nidulans, conidia 
of A. fumigatus show greater hydrophobicity. A. fumigatus conidia reach the lower airways 
causing systemic infection by overcoming mucociliary clearance due to their size (2 to 3 µm) 
and melanin in their cell wall provides resistance to lysis mechanism by the host cells. 
Negatively charged sialic acid residues on the surface of A. fumigatus conidia are exposed

adheres to the airway epithelium and alveoli than those fungal species with similar airborne 
spore sizes.  
1.6 Aspergillosis 
The increase in the incidence of Aspergillus-related diseases remains a growing problem as it 
is an outcome of increase in the number of patients developing specific immune defects. The 
immunosuppressive therapy used for treatment of malignancies, autoimmune diseases or 
during hematopoietic stem cell transplantations (HSCTs) or solid organ transplantations 
(SOTs) contribute to such defects. The disease caused by Aspergillus spp. Is termed as 
aspergillosis and it includes a range of infection manifestations i.e. invasive aspergillosis (IA); 
pulmonary aspergilloma; and allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA). The number of 
life-threating infections documented that are attributable to aspergillosis are 300,000 
annually [66] although preventive measures like isolation of the patient or prophylactic 
antifungal therapy are employed [48]. A. fumigatus is regarded as the most common 
Aspergillus species frequently isolated, with A. flavus being the second [29]. A. fumigatus are 
exceptional in having primary and opportunistic pathogen role as well as being a significant 
allergen and are involved causing these serious diseases. Humans are constantly exposed to 
its conidia as they produce them abundantly and their thermotolerant trail plays a crucial role 
by allowing them to thrive in mammalian infections. Individuals under sustained 
immunosuppression are at an increased risk to such infections caused by the mold pathogens. 
Invasive aspergillosis (IA) is a life-threatening infectious disease in those who have received 
immunosuppressive treatment, such as SOTs or HSCT, intensive chemotherapy, or 
corticosteroid therapy, neutropenia and/ or macrophage dysfunction, and acquired or 
congenital immunodeficiency[67][29]. Invasive infections are difficult to diagnose and treat 
besides being most prevalent primarily in lungs and followed by CNS. The number of IA cases 
annually estimated among individuals with impaired immune function is over 300,000 [68]. 
Nevertheless, due to under diagnosis, this number likely reflect only 50 to 60 percent of the 
actual cases that exists. IA has an overall mortality rate of 50% when diagnosed and treated 
also almost 100% fatal when diagnosed late or missed. Certain group of immunocompromised 
individuals, especially those in intensive care units (ICUs), those with chronic obstructive 
pulmonary disease (COPD), brain infections and mismatched HSCT recipient perform poorly 
with a mortality rate of nearly 75% [66].   
Pulmonary aspergilloma refers to conglomeration of Aspergillus hyphae intertwined with 
mucus, fibrin and cellular debris in the pulmonary cavity or ectatic bronchus. Aspergillomas 
are usually described as benign saprophytic colonization of Aspergillus within the lungs, 
producing mycetoma or fungus ball. The progression of Aspergillus lung disease are actually 
a continuum between tissue invasion and colonization. Aspergilloma can further develop into 
invasive pulmonary aspergillosis and is generally fatal. It is difficult to control the fungus ball 
==PAGE:25==

formed in the pre-existing cavities employing antifungal drugs. The permanent cure for 
pulmonary aspergilloma is surgical resection, however, it remains controversial due to the 
frequent postoperative complications [69]. A. fumigatus is responsible almost always for 
causing allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis. ABPA is defined as an immunologic 
pulmonary disorder where the patient present chronic asthma, bronchiectasis and recurrent 
pulmonary infiltrates. It is a hypersensitivity disease of lungs occurring in absence of IA of 
nonimmunocompromised individuals although the factors associated with development of 
ABPA is unclear [70].

1.7.1 Allylamines 
Terbinafine is the only allylamines currently in clinical use and was discovered as a derivative 
of antifungal naftifine (original allylamines compound). It is used to treat dermatophyte 
infections, such as onychomycosis, but has limited effectiveness in treating invasive fungal 
inefctions. Despite this limitation, the activity of terbinafine can be improved by combining it 
with other antifungal drugs, for example voriconazole, in treating invasive infections [72].  
1.7.2 Pyrimidine analogues 
As a pyrimidine analogue, flucytosine does not have intrinsic anti-neoplastic activity but is 
effective against yeasts such as Candida spp. and Cryptococcus spp. that are medically 
important. Flucytosine has limited activity against Aspergillus spp. and is necessary to further 
investigate their use in preclinical studies since there is an increased interest in combination 
therapy for IA [74]. 
1.7.3 Echinocandins  
Caspofungin, micafungin and anidulafungin belong to echinocandins class of antifungal drugs 
having in vitro and in vivo activity against Candida spp., as well as Aspergillus spp. The action 
mechanism by which echinocandins act is to inhibit the cell wall protein 1,3-β-D-glucan 
synthase in a non-competitive manner. 1,3-β-D-glucan plays a crucial role in osmotic stability 
of fungi and essential for cell growth and replication. They do not kill Aspergillus spp., but 
they cause morphological changes to the fungi and lessen their propensity to cause 
pulmonary infection [75]. Caspofungin is approved for the treatment of IA in patients who are 
unresponsive to conventional therapy, but there is limited data on their usage for IA 
treatment in patients who are persistently neutropenic. Although caspofungin has been 
shown to have efficacy as salvage therapy for the treatment of IA, data are lacking on its use 
as a primary therapy [76].

1.7.5 Azoles 
Ketoconazole was the first bioavailable imidazole for oral consumption active against 
endemic mycoses, oral candidiasis, dermatophyte infection, coccidioidomycosis, and 
veterinary fungal infections, despite its toxicity and variety of drug infections. Subsequently, 
triazoles were created through modifications of the active ring, and currently in clinical use 
are fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole and posaconazole. The enzyme 14-α demethylase 
is the potential antifungal target for the azoles. The synthesis of ergosterol that are essential 
for normal membrane function in fungi is disrupted by inhibition of fungal cytochrome P450-
dependent enzyme lanosterol 14-α demethylase [72]. In A. fumigatus, sterol demethylase is 
encoded by the genes cyp51A (Afu4g06890) and cyp51B (Afu7g03740) and are competitively 
inhibited by triazole class of antifungal drugs to prevent ergosterol synthesis [45].  
Fluconazole, water-soluble triazole is extensively used in treatment of mucosal candidiasis 
[77] and ineffective against Aspergillus spp. or other moulds of medical importance. 
Itraconazole is the first orally bioavailable agent with significant inhibitory and fungicidal 
activity against Aspergillus spp. [78]. Itraconazole is available as an oral capsule formulation, 
as well as cyclodextrin formulation for oral and intravenous (IV) administration. When 
metabolized, it produces hydroxyitraconazole, which has antifungal activity in humans. The 
major drawback of the capsule formulation is its acid dependent oral bioavailability, resulting 
in subtherapeutic concentrations in critically ill patients suffering from hypochlorhydria 
(elevated gastric pH). Itraconazole solution enhances oral bioavailability, but gastrointestinal 
intolerance is often associated with it. It is used in treating chronic pulmonary aspergillosis, 
allergic reactions associated with Aspergillus spp., blastomycosis, sporotrichosis, 
histoplasmosis and onychomycosis. Prophylaxis with itraconazole effectively protects 
patients from invasive fungal infections and reduces mortality as well as incidence of 
Aspergillus infections in neutropenic patients with hematologic malignancies. This effect 
requires dosages of oral cyclodextrin (400 mg/d) or IV formulations (200 mg/d) of itraconazole 
[79].  
Voriconazole, structurally related to fluconazole, is the first second-generation triazole 
effective against Aspergillus spp., Candida spp. and Fusarium spp. [72]. Voriconazole is 
available as tablets or as cyclodextrin solution for IV use.  It is used as a first-line antifungal 
drug in treating IA and offers superior results over D-AMD in initial treatment [73]. Several 
drug interactions and highly variable pharmacokinetics complicate the use of voriconazole in 
clinical settings. It also has more side effects than fluconazole, for instance, visual effects, skin 
reactions, elevation in hepatic enzymes and other safety issues [80].  Posaconazole, a 
structural congener of itraconazole, has broad spectrum antifungal activity against Aspergillus 
spp., Candida spp. and the Mucorales. Posaconazole is approved as a second-line therapy for 
treating IA and its oral bioavailability is improved by food especially fatty foods. Compared to 
fluconazole or itraconazole, mortality rates was significantly lower indicating that antifungal

prophylaxis improved the survival of patients with acute leukemia during remission induction 
chemotherapy [81]. Posaconazole exhibited activity in treatment of IA in patients intolerant 
of and/ or resistant to AMB or itraconazole when 800 mg/day of it was given in divided doses 
orally [82]. This severe patient population tolerate posaconazole well and is a promising 
treatment option for patients with limited therapeutic options.  
1.8 Drug-resistance 
Therapy for fungal diseases caused by A. fumigatus are restricted to a few drug classes, such 
as azoles, polyenes and echinocandians [83]. IA is commonly treated clinically with antifungal 
therapy using azole class of antifungal drugs, as both frontline therapy (voriconazole and 
isavuconazole) and salvage therapy (posaconazole and itraconazole). In recent years, 
resistance to triazole has increased among the A. fumigatus isolates globally, and the cause 
for this resistance remains largely unexplained. The likelihood of treatment failure associated 
with infections induced by azole-resistant A. fumigatus isolates are higher than that with a 
wild-type susceptibility [84]. Studies suggest that the incidence of azole resistance varies 
widely across countries, and that environmental exposure to azoles may contribute to 
development of resistance in addition to patient therapy. Summarized in Figure 6, the routes 
through which azole resistance is acquired: (a) in vivo, as a result of azole treatment and; (b) 
ex vivo, due to use of azole fungicides.

1.8.1 Drug resistance: Patient exposure  
The use of azoles in treatment of patients with cavity lung disease and aspergilloma have 
resulted in resistance development. The development of the disease involves A. fumigatus 
isolate that is susceptible to azole acquiring resistance under azole pressure. The adaptation 
of A. fumigatus to the selective azole pressure is facilitated by the formation of multiple 
generation by asexual reproduction within the lung cavity in patients with aspergilloma [85]. 
In case of IA, hyphal elongation enables infection progression and mutations are expected to 
occur during infection as the human body is a hostile environment for A. fumigatus because 
of host defences and antifungal drugs [86].  
1.8.2 Drug resistance: Environmental exposure  
A. fumigatus is hypothesized to develop resistance through exposure to the azole compounds 
in the environment. Azole compounds could be exposed to the fungi through agriculture, 
where such compounds are often to protect plants. Persistent azole pressure is applied on 
the fungi through repeated application of the fungicide over a long period of time. 
Immunocompromised individual would inhale both azole-resistant and susceptible conidia, 
but only the resistant conidia would germinate in lungs and cause invasive diseases due to its 
selective advantage upon azole therapy [86].   
1.9 Drug resistance mechanism 
In the last few years, there has been a reported increase in acquired resistance to azole 
antifungals in A. fumigatus that were previously susceptible. Additionally, evidence shows 
that patients with azole-resistant Aspergillus disease may not respond well to therapy. In A. 
fumigatus, three distinct molecular pathways for resistance to azoles in clinical isolates has 
been identified. These mechanisms of azole-resistance are similar to the paths previously 
reported among pathogenic fungi, like Candida albicans: (a) mutations in sterol-demethylase-
encoding gene cyp51A, (b) overexpression of sterol-demethylase genes, and (c) 
overexpression of the efflux pump-encoding gene abcC. According to the recent findings, 
none of the above-mentioned paths account for the degree of azole resistance observed in a 
large collection of A. fumigatus clinical isolates [45]. 
The most commonly identified mechanism of azole resistance is mutations in the gene 
encoding sterol 14α-demethylase, cyp51. Azole inhibits sterol 14α-demethylase, thereby 
targeting its function in the ergosterol biosynthesis pathway, leading to ergosterol depletion 
and toxic sterols accumulation. There are two cyp51 (erg11) genes in the A. fumigatus 
genome, namely cyp51A and cyp51B, each encoding proteins that belong to the same family 
of Cyp51 proteins [87]. Research has shown that cyp51B gene mutation does not play a role 
in azole resistance [88]. As opposed to this, mutations in the cyp51A gene cause resistance to 
azoles. Non-synonymous point mutations at the coding region of cyp51A gene was spotted 
among many azole resistant isolates. Such point mutations, for instance at positions G54, 
G138 and M220 was found among those patients treated for a long period with azoles [89]. 
Point mutations alter channels that gives the azole access to a heme molecule and this change 
hinders binding the azole to heme molecule.  Furthermore, the A. fumigatus strains from the 
environment, as opposed to those from the in-host environment, also carry a tandom repeat 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    18 
 
(TR) duplication in the promoter region as well as mutations in the cyp51A gene. As a result 
of the presence of the TR repeat, overexpression of cyp51A is driven by increased binding 
activity of the sterol regulatory element binding protein, resulting in enhanced resistance to 
azoles. Promoter duplications that has been implicated in resistance mechanisms are 
TR34/L98H, TR53, TR46/Y121F/T289A [90]. About 10% of natures resistant strains do not show 
any mutations in the cyp51A gene, and the induced azole efflux mechanism is highly likely is 
responsible for non-cyp51A mediated resistance. Furthermore, it was observed that the 
mutations in 3-hydroxy-3 methyl-glutaryl-coenzyme A (HMG-CoA) reductase-encoding gene 
hmg1 increased the resistance in azole-resistant clinical A. fumigatus isolates to all the 
clinically available azole agents. It has been demonstrated that mutation hmg1 resulted in 
sustained and even increased levels of ergosterol production in addition to accumulation of 
multiple ergosterol precursors, such as eburicol, a substrate for the sterol-demethylase 
enzyme, which is competitively inhibited by the azoles [45].  Additionally, HapE P88L 
substitution is also capable of inducing azole resistance; however, this mechanism is not 
frequent [91].         ` 
These mechanisms alone do not adequately explain the existence of azole resistance in all the 
clinical isolates of A. fumigatus, and there are potentially many other mechanisms that lead 
to azole resistance. Therefore, there is a need for identification and characterization of novel 
genetic determinants of drug resistance that could provide insight to ways to reduce the rate 
of resistance development. An understanding of novel resistance mechanisms will have an 
impact on the invasive strains resistant to drugs and improve the quality of life of the 
immunocompromised individuals affected by A. fumigatus. The present work focuses on 
discovering such new target for antifungal drugs through genome driven identification 
resulting in new therapeutic strategies that can be devised to overcome the drug-resistant A. 
fumigatus isolates.  
1.10 CRISPR-Cas9 system for genetic engineering 
Researchers have recently developed a powerful gene editing system using the bacterial and 
archaeal immune mechanism CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic 
Repeats)/ CRISPR-associated (Cas) system. There are three types of CRISPR-Cas system, 
namely type I, type II and type III. Both type I and III are found in bacteria and archaea, but 
type II is found only in bacteria. Cas9 protein, which is the critical component of bacterial type 
II CRISPR/Cas, is the best studied and characterized of the three types [92].  
The CRISPR-Cas9 system consists of only two components, a single chimeric guide RNA 
(sgRNA) and the Cas9 nuclease. These two components aids in introduction of specific DNA 
double strand breaks (DSBs), which are then utilized to efficiently stimulate gene 
targeting [93]. The protospacer sequence of the sgRNA provides accurate targeting of RNA-
guided Cas9 nuclease to the specific DNA sequence. The protospacer is made up of only 20 
nucleotides that identifies the target site through base pairing. The ability with which 
synthetic oligonucleotide accommodates a sequence corresponding to the protospacer 
simplifies the construction and expression of genes encoding novel sgRNA for Cas9. At the 
target site, CRISPR-Cas9 system requires short Protospacer Adjacent Motifs (PAM) present 
adjacent to the binding site of the guiding protospacer sequence. The PAM sequence differ 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    19 
 
among Cas9 proteins. In Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9, which is most often used in CRISPR-
Cas9 gene editing, the most frequently occurring NGG sequences are regarded as 
PAMs. Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 system has been adapted to allow efficient fungal CRISPR 
mediated gene editing by employing a copy of Cas9 and adding an extending sequence of 
SV40-NLS to the C-terminus of the resulting enzyme. Gene encoding sgRNA is expressed under 
the control of RNA polymerase II promoters. As a result, sgRNA is an integral part of the 
splicing cassette, as it is flanked by two ribozymes: hammerhead (HH) and hepatitis delta virus 
(HDV). Therefore, these two ribozymes release the mature sgRNA from the transcript. This 
CRISPR-Cas9 system has a limitation for delivering multiple sgRNAs into a single strain via one 
vector for multiplex experiments.  In other organisms multiple sgRNA expression was 
achieved successfully by employing tRNA-spacer based system and specifically in filamentous 
fungi the tRNA splicing machinery processes the pre-tRNAs containing internal 5’- and 3’ 
splice sites. This results in release of mature tRNAs from the larger pre-tRNA transcript by 
RNase P and RNase Z [94]. This machinery is exploited in CRISPR experiments for the release 
of sgRNA species. Multiplexing was successfully achieved by the construction of genes that 
encoded transcripts having multiple sgRNAs species spaced by tRNA entities as multiple 
sgRNAs was released by RNase P and RNase Z from the common transcript (Figure 7) [95].

1.11 USER fusion  
Uracil-specific excision reagent, USER fusion is an efficient and fast technology to fuse and 
clone PCR fragments into a vector of interest. This fusion technique is based on excision of a 
single uracil (U) base from either end of the insert generated through PCR. The primers 
includes uracil bases as a part of the 9 bp long 5’ extension. Upon PCR amplification using the 
USER primers, excision of the uracil bases results in producing 9 bp long 3’ PCR product 
overhangs which is complementary to the 3’ overhangs present in the vector fragments. The 
3’ overhangs in the PCR fragments is generated as a result of treatment with the USER enzyme 
mix (Uracil DNA glycosylase and DNA glycosylase-lyase Endo VIII), PacI (standard restriction 
enzyme) and Nt.Bbc.CI (nicking enzyme) are used in combination to cut the vector DNA to 
generate the 3’ overhangs. Annealing the USER-treated PCR fragments and the digested 
vector allows the formation of a stable recombinant molecule that can be directly employed 
in the chemical transformation [96].

In contrast to overlapping PCR, USER fusion requires shorter primers and can fuse and clone 
the product into the desired vector in a single step along with eliminating the need for 
secondary PCR amplification steps [97]. These advantages tempted us to employ USER fusion 
in this project and the primers for the construction of the CRISPR-tRNA vectors to be used in 
gene editing experiments were designed as seen in Figure 9. The protospacer can be inserted 
between the promoter and the sgRNA-terminator fragments through primer tails. As 
indicated, the 20 bp arbitrary protospacer sequence has to contain an A and T that are spaced 
by 6-15 nucleotides for the USER fusion ensuring matching overhangs for in vivo ligation of 
the two PCR fragments [95].  The PCR fusion fragments generated using the primers 
containing tails with uracil residue facilitates USER fusion. The correct orientation of the 
CRISPR-tRNA vector obtained via USER fusion can be assessed by restriction analysis and 
sequencing.

1.12 Bioinformatic tools: AdapterRemoval, FastQC and CLC Genomics Workbench 
As new sequencing approaches are constantly being developed, high-throughput sequencing 
is routinely being used to detect cancerous copy number variations, assemble microbial 
genomes, quantify gene expression, and monitor epigenetic changes in diseases, among 
many other applications. A high-throughput sequencing data analysis pipeline begins with 
quality control (QC). QC analyses a set of statistics within a sequenced read file to check if the 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    22 
 
content aligns with experiment expectations and whether the data can be used for 
downstream analysis. For example, QC test typically includes counting of relative nucleotides 
frequency at each positions in a set of reads to detect the potential deviations from expected 
frequencies, identify low quality base positions based on the summary distribution of Phred 
quality scores, and testing frequency of sequencing adapters and contaminants in the sample 
that shouldn’t be considered to be biological DNA. Those data that pass specific QC tests will 
then be subjected to downstream analysis, such as include adapter trimming, filtering low-
quality reads and contaminants, and mapping the resulted reads to a reference genome or 
transcriptome [98]. AdapterRemoval is a bioinformatic tool which is used for searching and 
removing remnant adapter sequences from the sequence data along with optional trimming 
of the low quality bases from the 3’ end of the reads following adapter removal.  
One of the most widely used tools for QC is FastQC, which includes a broad range of QC tests 
to cover multiple scenarios since its release. This tool provides QC report that identify 
problems caused by the sequencer or at the beginning of library material [99]. Analysis 
reports generated by FastQC have been used as standards for various QC tools, and 
automated analysis pipelines often utilize its evaluation as a safety criteria to determine 
whether to proceed with downstream steps or, to trim, filter or eventually discard the data. 
FastQC was followed by introduction of several other QC software tools that aimed at speed 
improvement, flexible module visualization, addition of paired-end reads and filtering the 
sequences that failed QC check. Despite offering different options for calculating and 
presenting QC results, these tools' modules are mostly the same as FastQC. Furthermore, 
FastQC's analytical results are already a component of many standard pipelines for initial 
analysis. A new QC tool would need to remain consistent with FastQC's output formats and 
results, if it were incorporated into these pipelines [98]. 
The sequenced data analysis in this project was performed with an easy-to-use program 
called "CLC Genomics Workbench" that identifies genes using a step-by-step methodology. 
CLC genomics workbench is a powerful customizable bioinformatics software for genomics, 
epigenomics, transcriptomics and metagenomics.  It is a reliable tool that enables basic 
sequence analysis such as BLAST (basic local alignment search tool) search and high-
throughput sequencing which involves preparation of sequencing data, read mapping and De 
novo sequencing [100].  Workbench allows comprehensive analysis of NGS data through 
methods such as whole genome assembly, transcriptome assembly, targeted resequencing, 
variant identification, ChIP-seq, and DNA methylation [101]. Data analysis processes are 
accelerated and simplified with custom workflows that combines quality control (QC) steps, 
adapter trimming, read mapping, variant detection, and filtering and annotation steps into a 
pipeline [102].  In this project, the sequence files of the A. fumigatus clinical isolates were 
analysed using the bioinformatics tools: FastQC and CLC Genomics Workbench to perform 
quality control checks, assemble the genome and identify the point mutations inducing 
antifungal resistance.

2.7 Fungal isolate protoplastation and transformation 
Protoplastation and transformations were performed using ‘Genetic transformation of 
filamentous fungi’, (Author: JBN, Ver 5) protocol. As initiation step, A. fumigatus clinical 
isolates, SSI-2716 and SSI-6703 were grown individually as three-point stabs on the solid MM 
media at 37°C for 5-6 days. The conidia were collected by firmly rubbing the each isolate 
plates using drigalski spatula with 5 mL liquid MM media. The obtained conidial suspension 
were transferred into separate shake flask with a total of 100 mL liquid MM media and 
cultivated overnight at 37°C with shaking at 150 rpm. Mycelia were harvested from each 
cultivated isolate by filtration using sterile Mira-cloth and the retained biomass were washed 
with milli-Q followed by Aspergillus protoplastation buffer (APB). Each mycelium were re-
suspended in 10 mL filter- sterilized (0.45 µm filter) APB containing 40 mg of Glucanex/ mL in 
a sterile falcon tube and incubated on the shaker for 2-3 hours at 37°C and 150 rpm until 
protoplasts were produced. The enzyme-mycelium mix were filtered again using sterile Mira-
cloth to a new falcon tube and the protoplast solution were diluted by adding APB to a total 
volume of 40 mL. An overlay of 5 mL Aspergillus transformation buffer (ATB) in milli-Q (1:1) 
was carefully placed on top of the APB. The tubes were centrifuged at 3000 RCF; Temperature:  
4°C; acceleration: soft and deceleration: soft for 13 minutes and interphase of two liquids 
were obtained. The halo of the white slurry obtained contained the concentrated protoplast 
which was carefully separated from the remaining cell wall debris. 40 mL ATB was added to 
the separated white slurry followed by centrifugation at 3000 RCF; Temperature:  4°C; 
acceleration: soft and deceleration: soft for 13 minutes. Further the supernatant was 
discarded and each protoplast pellet was re-suspended in 1 mL ATB and stored at -80°C for 
future use.

For the CRISPR experiment, a tRNA-based system for the delivery of sgRNA was built which 
contained -sgRNA flanked on both sides by identical glycine tRNA gene sequences under the 
control of Af_U3p to provide a tRNA splicing cassette. The sgRNA sequences were released 
by endogenous RNase P and RNase Z activities. Consequently, CRISPR-tRNA vectors were 
produced by inserting the tRNA splicing cassette containing a single sgRNA controlled by U3 
promoter and U3 terminator into an empty CRISPR vector (pFC332). With DNA DSBs bordering 
the sequence to be corrected, a specific mutation correction could be performed. In such a 
scenario, the mutation would be corrected if the DNA ends upstream and downstream of the 
DNA DSBs were joined in a repair process guided by GE-oligonucleotides. Specifically, four 
designed hph-based CRISPR-tRNA vectors pU1, pU2, pU3 and pU4 expressing hp-sgRNA, hmg-
sgRNA, alpha-sgRNA1, and alpha-sgRNA2, respectively, each containing AMA1 and cas9 were 
used for transformation. Each of the protospacers targets Cas9 to the mutation within the hp, 
hmg, and alpha genes that are hypothesised to induce antifungal resistance in SSI-6703 and 
SSI-2716.   
Each isolate was co-transformed with the pU1 vector for L446F mutation correction in hp 
gene and either of the two different 60 bp GE-oligonucleotides; one with an asymmetric 
design (GE-Oligo1-hp) and, one with a symmetric design where the two homology sections of 
the GE-oligonucleotides were of the same length, (GE-Oligo2-hp). In two independent 
transformation experiments for SSI-6703, the number of transformants obtained using the 
symmetric GE-oligonucleotides were higher than the asymmetric GE-oligonucleotides, which 
was used as mutation correction repair template (Figure 22A). For SSI-2716, there was no 
notable difference in the number of transformants obtained with either of the GE-

oligonucleotides (Figure A4.1). For Y564H mutation correction in hmg gene, both isolates 
were co-transformed with pU2 and a symmetric GE-oligonucleotide, GE-Oligo-hmg, and

by Cas9/ -sgRNA.  
The development of a technique to assess the protospacer efficiency (TAPE) was made as a

sgRNA, produced in more than 100 colonies in both isolates. Growth selection control plates

3.7 Antifungal susceptibility analysis 
Four-well plates having agar-media containing azole and antifungal-free agar were observed 
for the growth of the selected transformants one and two days after inoculation.  The EUCAST 
azole agar screening method was implemented to validate the mutations corrected using 
CRISPR-tRNA based gene editing with itraconazole (4 mg/L), voriconazole (2 mg/L), 
posaconazole (0.5 mg/L), and azole-free agar control in wells 1, 2, 3, and 4 respectively. Due 
to research constraints, only SSI-6703 was tested for antifungal susceptibility using colonies 
from transformation plates 2, 4, 6, 8, and 12.  By incubating them on separate plates, it was 
observed whether the obtained transformants became azole susceptible or were still 
potentially non-susceptible to the azole by visually checking for the growth in each well after 
incubation (Figure 25). 
In the assay, phenotypic evidence of growth was not observed in azole containing wells 1, 2, 
and 3 when the colonies from mutation corrected transformants via gene editing (GE-) 
oligonucleotides targeting hp, hmg and alpha1 were plated, even after 48 hours of incubation. 
For hp, transformants obtained with both GE-oligo1-hp and GE-oligo2-hp in two independent 
plates showed no growth in azole containing wells (Figure 25A). Likewise, transformants 
obtained with GE-oligo-hmg and GE-oligo1-alpha targeting hmg (Figure 25B) and alpha1 
(Figure 25C) did not result in growth in wells 1, 2, and 3. The mutation correction for alpha1 
with gDNA as the repair template also failed to grow with azoles. Additionally, the incubation 
of the colonies from the five described SSI-6703 transformation plates showed growth only 
in the wells free of azole (well 4), indicating normal growth of the transformants. As a control 
for antifungal susceptibility test, colonies from the wild isolate SSI-6703 were also plated to 
determine whether or not it is resistant to azoles. The obtained SSI-6703 isolate had small 
green conidia visible in well 1 with itraconazole and normal growth in well 4 without azole 
(Figure 25D). Even though there was not much distinctive colonies formed on well 1 as 
compared to well 4 with no azole it should be taken into account and substantiated with a 
confirmatory MIC testing. The results of this assay signified that CRISPR-tRNA-based gene

editing was effective in correcting the identified non-synonymous mutations in hp, alpha1, 
and hmg to render the isolate azole susceptible.

4. Conclusions 
This project investigated the raw sequence files of three A. fumigatus clinical isolates (SSI-
2716, SSI-6207 and SSI-6703) and de novo assembly generated contig sequences for the 
cleaned reads using CLC Genomics Workbench.  A total of 8778, 11850, and 11413 genes were 
also annotated for SSI-2716, SSI-6207, and SSI-6703 assembled reads, respectively. By using 
genome-based approach, genetic determinants contributing to the development of 
antifungal resistant in the sequenced A. fumigatus clinical isolates was identified. CLC analysis 
revealed that no isolated showed mutations of the cyp51A gene, therefore the other genetic 
determinants manually identified was hypothesised to contribute to antifungal resistance. As 
a result of in silico analysis, non-synonymous mutations in hp, hmg, and alpha have been 
found in all three obtained clinical A. fumigatus isolates. This presented the previously 
uncharacterized genetic determinants in the obtained isolates:  
 
Non-synonymous mutations in hp (L446F) and hmg (Y564) was seen in all isolates. 
 
Mutations in alpha (A1038G, K1075N) were seen only in SSI-2716 and SSI-6703. 
  
hph-based CRISPR- tRNA vectors expressing hp-sgRNA, hmg-sgRNA, alpha-sgRNA1, and 
alpha-sgRNA2 as well as GE-oligonucleotides serving as the repair template was generated 
for target-mutation mediated gene editing in SSI-6703 and SSI-2716. L446F mutation in hp 
gene was corrected using an asymmetric and symmetric 60 bp GE-oligonucleotides 
individually. The number of SSI-6703 transformants obtained using the symmetric GE-
oligonucleotides (GE-Oligo2-hp) were higher than the asymmetric GE-oligonucleotides (GE-
Oligo1-hp). Colonies were successfully obtained for both SSI-6703 and SSI-2716 when 
symmetric GE-Oligo-hmg was employed for Y564H mutation correction in hmg gene.  Co-
transformation of both isolates with the CRISPR-tRNA vectors expressing the alpha-sgRNA1 
and alpha-sgRNA2 tRNA-based splicing cassette and the symmetric GE-oligonucleotides (GE-
Oligo1-alpha and GE-Oligo2-alpha) resulted in colonies. TAPE identification of the 
protospacers that efficiently targets the antifungal resistance inducing non-synonymous 
mutations was investigated. The severely reduced number of obtained transformants for 
both isolates using vector encoding alpha-sgRNA1 and alpha-sgRNA2 indicated efficient 
protospacer efficiency.  
  
Successfully obtained SSI-6703 and SSI-2716 transformants were subsequently tested for 
mutation correction mediated resistance in antifungal susceptibility test. Due to research 
limitations, antifungal susceptibility for azoles according to EUCAST-AFST screening method 
using four-well plate was performed with only SSI-6703. Visual observation for the presence 
or absence of colonies revealed that there were no potentially azole non-susceptible 
transformants among the inoculated colonies. This indicated that the transformants were 
azole susceptible, as evidenced by their growth only in azole free wells. In the control plate, 
SSI-6703 showed small green conidia visible in well 1 with itraconazole and normal growth in 
well 4 without azole suggesting that it should be taken into account and substantiated with a 
confirmatory MIC testing. Additionally, itraconazole susceptibility testing performed for both 
SSI-6703 and SSI-2716 had no growth for all the transformants inoculated in the itraconazole 
test plates. This suggested that all the transformants obtained are susceptible to itraconazole. 
Presence of small conidia in the itraconazole test plate at wild isolates inoculation point for 
both isolates indicated they could potentially be itraconazole non-susceptible isolate. 
==PAGE:61==
 
 
 
 
 
 
    48 
 
Whereas, all transformants along with the wild isolate inoculated showed normal growth on 
MM plate with no itraconazole.   
 
This report adds another piece to the complex picture of emerging antifungal-resistant A. 
fumigatus and might serve as a catalyst to stimulate further research. For future work, it is 
required to address the uncharacterized resistance mechanisms potentially contributing to 
resistance emergence.  
 
It is required to thoroughly analyse the sequence reads to identify all the genetic 
determinants conferring antifungal resistance. In this way, the non-synonymous 
mutations in the resistant isolates that have yet to be characterized could be 
determined. 
 
Based on the identified mutations, it could be proposed that mutation correction 
could restore antifungal susceptibility in resistant strains employing CRISPR-tRNA 
mediated gene editing. To substantiate the hypothesis proposed, antifungal 
susceptibility testing should be conducted.
