Abstract
Automating the grading of student reports is a challenging yet promising application of arti­
ficial intelligence (AI), particularly with recent advancements in multimodal large language
models (LLMs). This thesis explores the feasibility of using multimodal deep learning mod­
els to predict rubric­specific assessments of student reports by integrating text and image
embeddings, as well as attributes qualifying the characteristics of the reports. This study
utilizes the embedding models RoBERTa and CLIP to extract text and image represen­
tations, which are then processed through an attention­based pooling mechanism and a
feed­forward neural network, trained using an ordinal regression loss function.
A dataset of past graded reports from the course “02450 Introduction to Machine Learn­
ing and Data Mining” was used to evaluate different architectures, including unimodal
(Text­Only, Image­Only) and multimodal models (text, images, and report attributes). The
results showed that the Text­Only model achieved the highest accuracy, outperforming
both the Image­Only and multimodal models. Transformer­based architectures did not
demonstrate significant improvements, and the multimodal integration introduced noise
rather than enhancing prediction accuracy. A synthetic study further revealed that the
models struggled with fully random noise but were nonetheless more robust when noise
maintained some structure.
The findings of this project highlight key challenges in automated grading, including data
variability, grading subjectivity, and embedding noise. While the proposed models demon­
strate moderate success in predicting individual rubric grades, their performance were not
significantly better than the defined baselines, for reliable automated grading. Future work
should focus on fine­tuning pre­trained embeddings, exploring alternative model architec­
tures, and addressing ethical considerations related to the fairness and bias of automatic
grading.

Introduction
1.1
Context and problem
Grading student reports can be quite time­ and resource­consuming, especially in the con­
text of large university courses, where teaching assistants (TAs) are usually employed to
assist the teacher in grading these reports. Moreover, the group of TAs regularly changes
over the years. This can also be challenging when reports vary in structure and content.
Traditionally, the course “02450 Introduction to Machine Learning and Data Mining” has
hundreds of reports per semester graded by several TAs. The TAs not only have to grade
a large number of reports each, but also have to do it twice per semester and within short
periods of time. Moreover, since there is a rotation of TAs almost every semester, the
grades are frequently given by different people. Because of these factors, and the fact
that the reports cover a great variety of topics, grading reports can become stressful and
overwhelming and can introduce a certain amount of variability in the way reports are
evaluated.
Due to these constraints, one can imagine that automating the grading process could be
beneficial, as it would alleviate the stress put on teachers and TAs, allowing them to focus
more on helping students. It could also reduce the variability that could exist in the report
evaluation process.
1.2
Introducing approach
Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) and multimodal artificial intelligence
(AI) have transformed the integration of textual and visual data. LLMs such as BERT
[1] and GPT­3 [2], have pushed the boundaries of natural language understanding by
using transformer architectures [3], massive training datasets, and innovative learning
techniques. These developments set new benchmarks in AI performance and versatility,
as evidenced by improved results across tasks like translation [4], summarization [5], and
reasoning [6].
In our research project, which predicts student grades from PDF reports, we chose to use
RoBERTa and CLIP for their state­of­the­art capabilities. RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized
BERT Pretraining Approach) [7], a refined version of BERT, excels at extracting rich, con­
textual text embeddings (a vector that represents the meaning of a word or text) due to
its optimized training regime, making it ideal for interpreting complex student arguments
and written content. CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre­training) [8] complements
this by generating image embeddings, crucial for understanding diagrams, charts, and
illustrations embedded in reports.
1.3
Related work and State­of­the­Art
Many studies have explored ways to automate grading, offering valuable insights. For ex­
ample, a system called GradeOpt [9] makes use of LLMs to make grading more consistent
and reliable. Another study by Baniata et al. [10], focused on predicting student progress
over time, emphasizing the importance of accurate and dependable models. Research by
Duong and Meng [11], looking into grading short answers, showed how embeddings can
help evaluate responses and achieve results comparable to human grading. These stud­
ies highlight that, while automated grading systems can be effective, challenges remain

==PAGE:7==
in handling complex data and providing meaningful feedback. For example, Xie et al. [12]
proposed a system that combines rubric creation, grading, and post­grading review, but
also pointed to the need for further innovation in managing student work.
A critical part of automated grading is how text is represented numerically, which is where
embeddings come in, where RoBERTa has been shown to perform exceptionally well. In
a comparative analysis [13], RoBERTa outperformed other models in understanding com­
plex text. Similarly, Meijer et al. [14] found that embeddings work well for shorter texts,
making them useful for analyzing student reports. However, another study [15] highlighted
a concern: pre­trained embeddings can sometimes unfairly favor well­written responses,
showing the importance of adjusting models to ensure fairness. A study that compared
Transformer­based models [16] showed how RoBERTa achieved top performance in un­
derstanding how similar two pieces of text are, proving its relevance for educational tasks.
Practical applications of embeddings show their potential for tasks like analyzing course
content and assessments [17].
The potential of multimodal systems, i.e., those that use both text and images, adds an­
other layer of possibility. Researchers showed how combining image and text embeddings
can enhance LLMs’ ability to understand [18], which is particularly relevant for grading
student reports that include diagrams or figures. Similarly Katuka et al. [19] expanded
the scope of grading by incorporating systems that provide both scores and feedback, an
approach that could improve learning outcomes.
1.4
Research questions
With the recent development of LLMs, including in the field of multimodal prediction, it
is legitimate to wonder if a system could be developed to automate the grading of stu­
dents’ reports. This project therefore aims to explore the feasibility and effectiveness of
automated grading systems by addressing three key research questions:
1– To what extent can an AI system accurately predict ordinal grades in student
reports? This question is addressed by developing a deep learning model trained on
a dataset of past graded reports, using text and image embeddings to generate predic­
tions. The model’s performance are evaluated using exact and within­one accuracy met­
rics, which measure how often the model assigns the correct grade and how frequently
its predictions are within one grade level of the true score. A comparative analysis of
different model architectures is done, including attention­based and transformer­based
approaches, providing insights into the model’s capabilities to predict.
2– What are the advantages and challenges of utilizing text and image modalities in
the automated grading process? To investigate this, separate models are trained using
different input modalities: a text­only model, an image­only model, a multimodal model
combining text, images, and attributes, and a transformer­based model. The impact of
each modality on grading accuracy is analyzed to determine whether integrating multiple
sources of information improves predictions or introduces unnecessary complexity. Addi­
tionally, a synthetic study is conducted to assess how the model responds to structured
noise, providing further insights into how text and image embeddings influence perfor­
mance.
3– What are the limitations and ethical considerations of implementing automated
grading systems? This question is explored by identifying challenges related to data
quality, variability in report formats, and possible inconsistencies in human grading. The
project also considers the potential risks of bias in AI­generated grades, particularly if the

model learns from historical grading patterns that may be inconsistent or unfair.
This report first dives into the data curation and pre­processing as well as the creation

including the comparative study of the synthetic data and different models with real data.
Moreover, the obtained results are presented, and compared to the defined baselines. Fi­

and suggestions for future work.

2.1.1
Report representation
The aim of the data pre­processing is to obtain an accurate representation of the reports.
This was done by extracting the text, images, and tables separately from each report,
to which a grade vector was associated. Additionally, a vector of attributes qualifying the
characteristics of the report, was created for each of the reports.

The extraction of the text was done automatically with the help of a custom function and
the PyMuPDF Python library. First, the first page was ignored if it contained less than 50
words; this was done to easily eliminate text that was not related to the report (assuming
that the first page was for the report cover). Then, the bounding boxes of tables were
detected and the words inside those were excluded from the extracted text. This was
necessary for differentiating any text that was part of the tables and removing it from the
report content. For each report, the text was extracted from the PDF and stored in their
respective semester folder.
A filter type was passed as an argument to the text extraction function to determine how
the text is chunked: i) “size” when the font size of a new line is bigger than the previous by
4 pt, usually indicating a new section title, ii) “paragraph” when the spacing between two
lines is greater than certain values, and finally iii) “None” when the text should be extracted
as a single block. The “paragraph” argument was kept for this study as it proved to provide
the most consistent chunking, and the arbitrary spacing of 22 pt was chosen for the same
reason.
The images were extracted as PIL (Python Imaging Library) object and converted to py­
torch tensors before being saved as a list of image tensors for each report.
Finally, since report attributes are hypothesized to play a role in the grading process,
an attribute vector of size 13 was created automatically for each report, containing the
following information:
• Number of students/group members
• Number of pages
• Number of images
• Number of tables
• Number of external links (“http”)
• Number occurrences of 8 relevant key words

Target grades
The target grade vector was created by pulling the grades from the Excel sheet and as­
sociating them to the student IDs given in the same column. Each grade from the vector
is attributed to an individual grading criterion, for example Is the problem clearly defined?.
Their grade takes a natural value from 0 to 4 and the weighted sum of all the individual
grades gives the overall report grade. Grades were automatically retrieved from the Excel
sheets simultaneously with the textual reports, as a vector. Project 1 had 22 individual
grading criteria, with only 19 counting towards the overall grade. Since there were 3 grad­
ing criteria that did not count in the weighted sum that made up the overall grade, and
some reports did not have these criteria graded, we decided to entirely discard these 3
criteria. As for project 2, there were 17 grading criteria.
When looking at the distribution of grades for each criterion, we noticed that a vast majority
of criteria across both projects and all the previous semesters had the grade 4 as their
most common grade. We also noticed that some semesters had a higher proportion of

2.2.1
Data pre­processing
In order to create the clean dataset, a list of “valid” reports had to be constructed. The
following criteria yielded non­valid reports:
• Reports with a file type different from “.pdf”, or PDFs that were unreadable.
• Reports that, when parsed, contained less than 10 paragraphs or more than 200
paragraphs.
• Reports with a faulty or missing grade in the Excel file.
• Reports that did not contain any images.
The requirement for the number of paragraphs was chosen with the embedding generation
in mind, where reports with few paragraphs were hypothesized to have exceedingly long
sections and would likely have a lot of content removed due to the strict limit of tokens.
Reports with too many paragraphs had a lot of paragraphs carrying too little information.

2.3
Synthetic datasets
To evaluate the performance and robustness of the proposed grading model, a series
of synthetic studies were conducted. These studies were designed to simulate various
conditions and challenges that the model might encounter in real­world applications. By
generating controlled datasets, we assessed the model’s ability to predict grades accu­
rately under different scenarios. Each synthetic dataset used 768­dimensional vectors
Using Multimodal LLMs to Predict Rubric Specific and Overall Assessments of Students’ Reports
7
==PAGE:13==
representing report embeddings. This vector size was chosen to reflect the size of em­
beddings produced by the text and image embedding generators used with the real data:
RoBERTa and CLIP (detailed in subsection 3.2.1).
Reports were modeled as containing three sub­vectors (study 1) or sub­lists (study 2 and
3), each corresponding to a distinct section of the report, with their corresponding grades.
The synthetic datasets were generated by first determining the target grade vector and
then creating input vectors based on these targets, ensuring a direct correlation between
the input data and the expected outputs. Each synthetic dataset consisted of 1000 reports,
with 800 used for training and 200 reserved for testing. The model was trained for 2000
epochs on the training set before being evaluated on the unseen test set.
2.3.1
Study 1
The first study aimed to validate the effectiveness of the custom ordinal regression loss
module integrated into the model. In this simplified scenario, each report was represented
by a single 768­dimensional vector, constructed by concatenating three 256­dimensional
vectors. Each of these sub­vectors was sampled from a specific Gaussian distribution
corresponding to one of the grades (0, 1, 2, 3, or 4). This controlled setup ensured clear
separation between different grades, with embedding vectors pulled from the normal dis­
tribution of their unique centroid with variation 1.
2.3.2
Study 2
The second study explored the ability of the model to handle true random noise within the
embeddings. In this experiment, each report was represented by three sub­lists of varying
sizes, each corresponding to a report section and containing 4 to 10 vectors. Similarly to
the first study, the target vector is generated first, then a centroid, unique to each section
and grade, is created, with values ranging from ­5 to 5. Noise was introduced by altering
a random portion of each vector. The noisy segment was consistent across all vectors
within the same section but differed between sections. This means that within a given
section, the same portion of each vector was replaced with random values, while different
sections had different portions affected. The noise levels were systematically varied and
the model’s performance was evaluated at each noise level.
2.3.3
Study 3
The third study is an extension of the second study with a key difference being the number
of vectors in each section, ranging this time from 2 to 6. In this study, the noisy part of the
embedding vectors were drawn from a larger distribution of the centroid vector instead
of being truly random. This aimed to reflect closer the real data since the reports were
assumed to not include truly random data. The subspace size and variation amount were
increased to try to reach the point were the model was not performing well anymore. The
portions of noise were incremented by 12.5% and the standard deviation of the distribution
from which the noise was drawn was incremented by 5 each.

Methods
In this section we will introduce the baselines, which will help us measure the quality and
compare the accuracy of our models. We will also describe the architecture of the model
and the metrics used to evaluate and quantify its abilities. Finally we will walk through the
training procedure and hyper­parameter tuning.
3.1
Baseline
Baselines were set up in order to determine how adequate a certain model was. For this
project, our baselines were a combined set of three baselines. These simple baselines
tested how the model performed compared to just guessing each report’s grade vector
based on i) the mean grade, ii) the median grade, or iii) the most common (mode) grade
across all reports. The detailed description of how the accuracy of a model is measured
can be found in subsection 3.3.1 and the performance of the model compared to the
baselines can be found in Table 4.1.
3.2
Model architecture
3.2.1
Embeddings generation
The predictor model was trained using static embeddings; however, it also included a
module for generating embeddings dynamically. This module allows the gradients to flow
through the pre­trained models, specifically RoBERTa for text, and CLIP for images.
RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining Approach) is a transformer­based
language model developed by Facebook AI [7] that builds upon BERT’s architecture [1] to
enhance language understanding. In our model, RoBERTa processed the raw text para­
graphs and converted them into numerical representations, known as embeddings, which
captured the semantic meaning of the text. RoBERTa first tokenizes (splits) the text into
smaller units and maps them into simple embeddings, which are then processed through
multiple transformer encoder layers. These layers apply self­attention to understand the
relationships between words in the entire paragraph, allowing the model to generate con­
textualized embeddings that capture the meaning of the text. The resulting paragraph
embeddings can be represented as:
Et = RoBERTa(Xt),
where Xt represents the text input and Et denotes the resulting text embeddings. The
embeddings were 768­dimensional because RoBERTa’s hidden layers use this fixed size
for all token representations.
For image processing, we used the CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre­training)
model developed by OpenAI [8]. CLIP is trained on paired image­text data, allowing it to
extract embeddings that capture both the visual content and its semantic associations.
Specifically, we employed the ViT­L/14 variant of CLIP to transform input images into
dense vector embeddings. The process involved converting raw image tensors to PIL im­
ages, applying CLIP’s pre­processing steps, and then passing the preprocessed images
through the CLIP image encoder to obtain normalized embeddings:
Ei = CLIP(Xi),

where Xi and Ei denote input images and their resulting embeddings, respectively. The
embeddings also were 768­dimensional because of the ViT­L/14 model’s final transformer
layer outputs vector size.
3.2.2
Embedding pooling
The architecture includes an AttentionPooling module designed to aggregate multiple
embeddings from text or images into a single fixed­size vector for each report. Initially,
the project aimed to utilize a transformer­based architecture to make use of the power­
ful sequence modeling capabilities of transformers. However, through experimentation, it
was found that the current attention pooling approach performed comparably without the
added complexity.
The AttentionPooling module employed a self­attention mechanism, where input embed­
dings were passed through linear layers to compute attention scores, which were then
used to create a weighted sum of the embeddings. This resulted in an aggregated embed­
ding that effectively captured the most relevant information from the input data. Formally,
attention scores are computed as:

• E: Input embeddings,
• Wa and wc: Learnable parameters.

Models using the AttentionPooling module will be called Attention Models henceforth.
And while the majority of models were trained with this AttentionPooling, other models,
Transformer Models, used an alternative pooling method, the TransformerPooler, which

3.2.3
Neural networks
The architecture includes two parallel Feed­Forward neural networks, the NeuralNetwork
and the SigmaNetwork, both designed to process the same input features to optimize pre­
diction accuracy while managing model complexity. Each begins with a projection layer
that reduces the input dimension (e.g., from 768) down to 20 dimensions, effectively pre­
venting overparameterization and reducing the risk of overfitting. The NeuralNetwork then
passes the projected features through a fully connected layer with a hidden dimension,
followed by batch normalization, a ReLU activation, and dropout for regularization, before
producing a final output vector. This can be expressed as:
f(x) = ReLU(W2 ·BatchNorm(W1 ·C+b1)+b2),
where f(x) represents the grade logits (i.e. prediction).
The SigmaNetwork follows a similar structure but omits batch normalization; instead, after
the dropout layer, it applies a softplus activation to ensure that the predicted sigma values
are non­negative:
f(σ) = Softplus(W2 ·(W1 ·C+b1)).
These sigma values represent the uncertainty associated with each grade prediction, pro­
viding a measure of confidence in the model’s outputs. Both networks are optimized si­
multaneously using the Adam optimizer to achieve the highest possible accuracy in grade
predictions.

3.3
Training the neural network model
Given the substantial differences in content and grading criteria between Report 1 and Re­
port 2, two separate models were developed and trained to ensure optimal performance
for each report type. The training process began by initializing the embedding pooling
module, which aggregated the variable­length textual and image embeddings into fixed­
size vectors. Each model incorporated both the NeuralNetwork and SigmaNetwork to
predict the grade vectors and estimate the associated uncertainty (sigma values), with
the optimizer used to adjust all model parameters, with a learning rate of 10−4 and a
weight decay of 10−5 to prevent overfitting. The loss was computed using the OrdinalRe­
gressionLoss function. Every 50 epochs (i.e. training cycles), the models were evaluated
to monitor loss reduction and accuracy improvements. This iterative training approach
allowed for continuous refinement of the models.
In addition to training separate models for Report 1 and Report 2, several variations of the
model were trained to evaluate the effectiveness of different input modalities and pooling
mechanisms. Specifically, Attention Models were trained with the following modalities:
• Text­only inputs
• Image­only inputs
• A combination of text, images, and attributes
To explore the impact of the pooling mechanisms, a Transformer Model was trained with
text, images, and attributes as inputs. This configuration allowed for a direct comparison
between the AttentionPooling and TransformerPooler approaches in aggregating multi­
modal information.
Finally, an additional Multimodal Classification Model, using the AttentionPooling mod­
ule and was trained using to classify reports as either Report 1 or Report 2. This classifica­
tion model was designed to assess the feasibility of automatically distinguishing between
the two types of reports based on their textual, visual, and structural features.
3.3.1
Evaluation setup and performance metrics
To evaluate the performance of the grade prediction models, we used two primary metrics
which were the Exact Match Accuracy and the Within­One Accuracy which, for each
individual grade, measure the percentage of predictions that exactly match the true grade
labels and those that are within one grade level (either one grade above or below) of the
true labels, respectively.
These metrics were chosen because they provide a more intuitive understanding of the
model’s performance in the context of grading. Exact Match Accuracy directly indicates
how often the model predicts the grades perfectly, while Within­One Accuracy captures
cases where the predictions are slightly off by just one grade level, reflecting a practical
measure of near­correct predictions. In contrast, metrics such as Mean Absolute Error
(MAE) or Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) were not prioritized because they aggre­
gate errors into a single average value, which, while informative, does not convey the
distribution of prediction errors or the frequency of nearly correct predictions. By focusing
on Exact Match and Within­One Accuracy, we ensure that the evaluation highlights the
model’s grading accuracy and its ability to stay close to the ground truth grades.
The evaluation process involved converting the continuous predictions f(x) from the neu­
ral networks into discrete ordinal labels based on the learned thresholds from the Ordinal

Regression module. Specifically, each predicted value was compared against the cumu­

For the evaluation setup, the dataset was divided into training and testing subsets using an

3.3.2
Training Procedure
The training of the grade prediction models was conducted using an iterative approach
that involved careful tuning of hyper­parameters and thoughtful design choices to optimize
performance while managing computational constraints.
Initially, the models were configured with semi­random values for key hyper­parameters,
including the learning rate, batch size, projection dimension, and hidden layer size. These
parameters were manually tuned based on validation performance to identify the optimal
settings that yielded the highest accuracy. Regularization techniques, such as dropout
and weight decay, were incorporated when over­parameterization issues arose, helping
to prevent overfitting and enhance the models’ generalization capabilities.
During the early stages of training, each model was run for up to 10,000 epochs with
evaluations conducted only at the conclusion of training. However, it was observed that
the models’ performance peaked after a certain number of epochs and subsequently de­
clined, likely due to overfitting, as the training accuracy continued to improve while the
validation accuracy did not. To address this, the training duration was reduced to 2,000
epochs, which proved sufficient for the models to achieve their maximum performance. At
this point, the evaluation metrics were monitored every 50 epochs to track progress and
ensure consistent improvements in grade prediction accuracy.
Throughout the training process, hardware constraints played a significant role in shaping
the model design and training strategy. Utilizing a setup with two GPUs and four CPUs,
memory limitations became particularly pronounced when integrating both text and image
modalities into the models, needing up to 200GB of memory. These constraints influenced
decisions regarding model size and complexity, leading to the adoption of the projection
layer that reduced the input dimensions and minimized the risk of overfitting without com­
promising performance. Although cross­validation techniques were considered, they were
not implemented since the chosen train/test split approach provided a balanced and effi­
cient means to evaluate model performance within the available computational resources.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of results
The results provided insights on how different neural network architectures and data
modalities contributed to the automated process of grading. Both the synthetic and real­
world experiments highlight important factors such as embedding quality, the impact of
noise, and the choice of pooling mechanisms.
5.1.1
Insights from the synthetic study
The synthetic experiments helped evaluate the core components of the model in a con­
trolled setting. The first study demonstrated that the ordinal regression loss function per­
formed well when the data was well­structured, with the model achieving 100% Exact
Accuracy. This confirmed that the model could correctly learn the grading structure under
ideal conditions. The second study introduced truly random noise, which led to a notice­
able decline in accuracy as the noise increased. This suggests that the model is sensitive
to irrelevant information and benefits from embeddings that clearly represent the grading
criteria. The third study, where noise was structured rather than fully random, showed
that the model could tolerate a certain level of variation while still maintaining reasonable
accuracy. This further indicates that, in practice, ensuring high­quality embeddings that
preserve relevant report characteristics is important for maintaining performance.
5.1.2
Performance on real data
The experiments on real data revealed clear differences in performance between different
models:
The Text­Only Attention Model achieved the highest Exact Accuracy, suggesting that
textual features are the most important factor in grading reports. This result suggests that
written content carries the most direct information about report quality.
The Image­Only Attention Model performed worse than the text­based model but still
showed reasonable accuracy. This suggests that while images may contain some infor­
mation, they are not as reliable for grading as text, in the scope of this project.
The Multimodal Attention Model, which combined text, images, and attributes, did not
improve accuracy compared to the text­only model. This could indicate that the additional
modalities introduced more complexity without adding much additional value to the pre­
dictions.
The Multimodal Transformer Model performed worse than the Multimodal Attention
Model and showed instability during training. While its within­one accuracy reached 98%
for Project 2, exact accuracy remained lower. This behavior could suggest that the hyper­
parameters were not tuned enough as the model was still able to learn the ordered nature
of the grades. The lower Exact Accuracy may indicate overfitting or difficulties in effectively
integrating multiple modalities.
We initially expected stacked transformers to be able to perform well, identifying the non­
noisy parts of the vectors while ignoring irrelevant information. However, results indicate
that the model struggled with this task, suggesting that further architectural modifica­
tions may be required. Transformers may need better regularization or alternative training
strategies to focus on grading­relevant features while ignoring noisy elements.

The Multimodal Classification Model performed exceptionally well, achieving over 99.8%
accuracy. This confirms that Report 1 and Report 2 have distinct characteristics that make
them easy to differentiate. This result also validates the use of text and image embeddings,
specifically with RoBERTa and CLIP, for representing reports.
Findings from the synthetic study, combined with results from real­world data, suggest that

they may also include irrelevant information that prevents the model from learning mean­

and images did not outperform the text­only approach.
Another important factor influencing model performance may be the grading criteria them­
selves. Each report is graded on multiple aspects, and the model may struggle to capture
the specific features that differentiate one grade from another. Some criteria, such as plot
quality, may be particularly difficult for an embedding­based model to assess. The current
embeddings, while capturing general report structure and content, may not sufficiently
represent these finer details, which could be limiting the model’s predictive power.
When it became clear that we could not accurately predict the rubric specific grades (grade
vector), we considered trying to predict directly the overall grades for the reports. However,
this value is normally computed as a weighted sum of all rubric specific grades. So, by
trying to predict the overall grade instead of accurately predicting the individual grades
and performing their weighted sum, it could lead to a loss of information. Indeed, if the
model were to output one single grade, it would not be possible to determine the individual
factors that make up that grade.
5.1.3
Achieved performance
The Text­Only Attention Model was the only model performing better than the highest
baseline: the mode baseline. The other models performed similarly or worse than the
median baseline. These results show the struggle of the model to predict accurately the
individual grades. This could be due to multiple reasons: 1– Even though the embeddings
carry significant information, as demonstrated by the report classification, there might be
too much noise within those embedding vectors. This is supported by synthetic study 3
which showed that the accuracy drops as the noise increases. 2– There could be a slight
mislabeling of the target data, and with only 5 ordinal categories, this could lead to poor
predictions. 3– There might not be enough training data for a model with such complex
architecture, resulting in premature overfitting.
5.2
Limitations
5.2.1
Nature of the data
One challenge faced in this study was the variability in report structure and content. Since
the students write reports on different topics, with different structure and layout, etc., the
model had to generalize across a wide range of writing styles, which may have affected the
obtained accuracy. Additionally, extracting text and images from PDFs introduced some
inconsistencies, as incorrect parsing could impact the quality of embeddings.
Another challenge was that grading involves multiple aspects, such as clarity, structure,
and presence of specific figures. The model needed to learn all these aspects simultane­
ously, which may explain why multimodal models struggled making predictions of a grade
vector.

5.2.2
Computational constraints
Training multimodal models required substantial computational resources, particularly
when handling both text and image embeddings. Although the study was conducted on a
powerful system, training was still time­intensive. Additionally, privacy regulations required
all the processing to be done locally, limiting the possibility of using external cloud­based
resources.
5.2.3
Human error
The ground truth labels used for training were based on grades assigned by TAs. Since
grading can be somewhat subjective, inconsistencies in how different TAs assessed re­
ports may have introduced some noise into the training data. This could have impacted
the model’s ability to learn an idealized grading standard. Additionally, some non­relevant
files (e.g., PDF files of appendices, additional code) had to be manually removed, and
any errors in this process could have influenced the results.
5.3
Future work
While this project demonstrated the potential of automated grading systems, several areas
require further exploration and improvement.
5.3.1
Addressing data challenges
One key area for future work is the standardization of report submissions. The results
indicate that inconsistency in report formatting affects model performance. If reports were
submitted in a structured format such as LaTeX or Markdown, with predefined sectioning,
font sizes, and layout specifications, the quality of extracted embeddings could improve
significantly. Additionally, moving away from PDFs to more structured text formats could
reduce parsing errors and ensure that all content is properly processed.
5.3.2
Fine tuning the pre­trained embedding models
A critical improvement would be the development of a course­specific embedding gen­
erator. Currently, the model relies on pre­trained embeddings from RoBERTa and CLIP,
which are not specifically trained for student reports. Fine tuning the pre­trained to pro­
duce custom embeddings using past course submissions could significantly enhance the
model’s ability to capture grading­relevant features. This approach could lead to more
accurate predictions and better generalization across different writing styles.
5.3.3
Exploring alternative model architectures
Since the transformer­based model showed instability, alternative architectures could be
explored: Contrastive learning techniques, where the model is trained to explicitly distin­
guish between high­ and low­quality reports could improve its grading accuracy.
5.3.4
Ethical considerations
Another consideration is the ethical implications of automated grading. Since the model
learns from past grading decisions, it could unintentionally reinforce grading biases or mis­
interpret incorrect patterns as valid. Future research should explore techniques to prevent
systematic biases and incorporate mechanisms for detecting and correcting potential er­
rors in grading predictions.

6
Conclusion
This project investigated the feasibility of using deep learning models to automate the
grading of student reports, focusing on the integration of text and image embeddings,
and the impact of different model architectures. The research aimed to answer three key
questions, each of which was addressed through experimental evaluation and analysis.
The first research question asked: To what extent can an AI system accurately predict or­
dinal grades in student reports?. The results showed that while the AI models were able to
capture some aspects of grading, replicating human evaluation fully remains challenging.
The best­performing model, the text­only attention­based model, achieved an exact ac­
curacy of approximately 56% and a within­one accuracy of over 85%. This suggests that
the model is capable of making reasonably accurate predictions but still struggles with
exact grade prediction. The multimodal models incorporating text, images, and attributes
did not significantly outperform the text­only model, indicating that text embeddings carry
the most relevant grading information.
The second research question explored: What are the advantages and challenges of uti­
lizing text and image modalities in the automated grading process?. The findings suggest
that while text embeddings provide a strong foundation for predicting grades, the inclu­
sion of image embeddings did not yield substantial improvements. The image­only model
performed worse than the text­only model, highlighting the fact that the embeddings of
visual content in reports do not always contain grading­relevant information. The multi­
modal models, including the transformer­based approach, faced challenges in effectively
integrating multiple modalities, often introducing noise rather than improving predictive
performance. Additionally, the synthetic study demonstrated that noise in embeddings
could significantly impact accuracy, emphasizing the importance of high­quality feature
extraction.
The final research question considered: What are the limitations and ethical considera­
tions of implementing automated grading systems?. One major limitation identified was
the variability in report formatting and content structure, which affected the consistency of
the extracted embeddings. The reliance on human­graded training data also introduced
subjectivity, meaning that the model could learn inconsistencies from previous grading de­
cisions. Ethically, automated grading raises concerns about fairness and bias, as models
may reinforce grading patterns that are not entirely objective.
Overall, while this project provides a foundation for automated grading, further improve­
ments in data quality, embedding methods, and model architecture are necessary before
such systems can be deployed for report grading tasks as intended for the course “02450
Introduction to Machine Learning and Data Mining”. With continued refinement, AI­based
grading could be a valuable tool for assisting teachers and TAs, reducing grading workload
and variability.
