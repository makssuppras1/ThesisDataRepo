Abstract  
 
The world is being impacted by a series of trends, known as megatrends, and by the 4th Industrial 
Revolution. This is disrupting politics, economy, technology, and even what it means to be a 
human being. These challenges are explored and studied within this thesis, proposing the Smart 
City as the element that is going to be one of the most impacted by these trends, but also the 
one that has the capacity of answering back to the new challenges proactively and 
constructively.  
 
Hence, the Smart City and its pillars (Government, Mobility, Environmental Sustainability, 
Economic Development, Life Quality, and Intellectual Capital) are analyzed and studied, as well 
as their relationship with the Sustainable Development Goals. 
 
The goal of this thesis is to build a Smart City conceptual model that reflects the role of the Smart 
City in the current disruptive global context. The figure of a new citizen, the i-Learner, is 
proposed as the only profile that will be able to adapt and successfully overcome disruptive 
changes, co-create the Smart City, and take advantage of its benefits. For this purpose, high-
quality and strong education is crucial.  
 
The thesis also includes guidelines in which different measures and initiatives are proposed for 
building the Smart City and its elements.  
 
The development of both the model and the guidelines is based on the scientific knowledge 
from the literature review and Danish municipalities’ webpages dedicated to Smart Cities’ 
initiatives. Moreover, experts from the Danish municipalities of Lyngby-Taarbæk and Holbæk 
were interviewed, as well as experts from Copenhagen municipality were surveyed. This 
information was also used for the development of the model and the guidelines.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Introduction to the project 
 
The global context is being disrupted. Technological, environmental, political, and economic 
changes are challenging world nowadays, making it an unstable place, uncertain about its future. 
These unprecedented changes are known as megatrends, which are unstoppable tendencies 
that are already impacting the world, and thus society. These megatrends have caused the 4th 
Industrial Revolution to emerge, a global situation that is disrupting the world of work, 
productivity, skills, and even what means to be a human being.    
 
All these disruptive situations are going to hardly impact the world, which will have direct 
consequences on cities. Trends in the global population indicate that the world population is 
going to increase from 7.6 billion to 8.6 billion by 2050, and by 11.2 billion by 2100 (DESA/UN-
WUP, 2018). Moreover, it is expected a displacement from people living in rural areas to cities. 
Nowadays, 54% of the global population lives in urban areas. However, these new trends are 
going to make this number rise to 66% by 2050 (United Nations, 2014).  
 
Hence, urban areas must establish strategies for facing and successfully adapting to these 
changes. Cities need the attention of governments, the private sector, and civil society for 
initiating transformational processes and new solutions for facing these new challenges. 
 
Cities must develop strategies and plans for responding to the challenges derivate from 
population growth, demographic change, urban growth, as well as from climate change and 
resource scarcity. All this forces cities to convert into Smart Cities and thus, to adopt adaptation 
mechanisms for ensuring their sustainability. 
 
This new city context and the need to advance to smart urban systems remark the urgency for 
developing strategies and measures consistent with the reality. There are different models and 
indicator guides that are aimed to contextualize and give solutions to city's problems. Some of 
them focus on the city's stakeholders, governance, sustainability, technology…However, there 
is a lack of a model that studies the city in the new global context, taking into consideration 
megatrends and the 4th Industrial Revolution. Only by having a holistic vision of the context and 
its consequences, cities will be able to react to contemporary challenges.  
 
Therefore, this project reflects the holistic character of the Smart City concept and the pillars 
that constitute it: Government, Mobility, Environmental Sustainability, Economic Development, 
Life Quality, and Intellectual Capital. Besides, the model also considers the global and complex 
context created by megatrends and the 4th Industrial revolution. It is also proposed a new citizen 
profile: the figure of the i-Learner, who is considered the only one who is going to be able to 
adapt to disruptive changes and co-create the Smart City. 
 
The proposed model has a series of initiatives contained in a guide. These initiatives are framed 
by the Sustainable Development Goals, which will lead the cities to perform sustainable 
practices that respect and protect citizens and the environment. 
 
The whole project is human-centered, and the use of technology is considered fundamental but 
not primary. ICT and new technologies will be used as tools for developing the Smart City; always 
as a mean, never as an end.

1.2. Motivation 
 
The concept of the city might seem small, only related to urban spaces where people live and 
work. It is only when this concept is deeper analyzed when it is realized everything that it covers. 
From buildings, technological infrastructure, public institutions, private companies, to educative 
and culture institutions, cities shape people lives more than they might think. Therefore, the in-
depth study of the ecosystem formed by cities is complex, but at the same time fascinating and 
revealing. 
 
Furthermore, if it is added to it the study of the global trends context and the 4th Industrial 
Revolution, not only the complexity increase but also the excitement. Only when cities are 
studied in this extent global context, is when it is realized how significant is the role of cities as 
transformative elements. 
 
Therefore, this project motivation is to get a deep understanding of what is a Smart City, what 
are its components, as well as how it should react to global and disruptive changes. 
 
1.3. Problem statements and research questions 
 
The world is being impacted by several disruptive situations that are generating difficulties and 
challenges, which affects all the aspects that form the society. The main cause of this are the 
megatrends, that have made the 4th Industrial Revolution to explode, which in turn is causing 
several side effects that are profoundly impacting society. Cities are one of the primary 
recipients of this impact, and therefore, they have to face the several problems that are arising 
from this new situation. One of these problems is the need of knowing how to respond to them 
and transform these challenges into growth opportunities for the cities and their components. 
For a better understanding of this need, two problem statement has been formulated:   
 
1. Nowadays, the world is being impacted by several disruptive changes that are going to 
affect all aspect of society like business, governments, and citizens. 
2. Cities are going to be significantly impacted by these disruptive changes. If cities fail to 
respond to these new challenges successfully, several economic and societal crisis will 
arise. 
 
For correctly answering to these problem formulations, research questions are needed. The first 
set of research questions encompasses the identification of the unique and disruptive situation 
of nowadays world and its impact on society: 
 
RQ 1. What are the megatrends that society is nowadays facing?

      RQ 1.2.  How are megatrends impacting society?

RQ 2.1. What are the consequences of the 4th Industrial Revolution? 
RQ 2.2. How are these consequences going to impact the world?

2. Methodology and theory 
 
2.1. Method and structure of the project 
 
 
Figure 4 The Triple Diamond Method 
The development method followed by this project is the so-called Triple Diamond.  This method 
is a derivative of the Double Diamond. This design method describes the creative process as a 
series of processes in which ideas are created (divergent thinking) before refining and narrowing 
down (convergent thinking). This process is iterative since ideas are developed, tested, and 
refined several times, dropping weak concepts (Design Council, n.d.).  
 
The development of this project started due to intrinsic and personal motivation of the author. 
This led to an initial informal research based on the reading of some papers and national 
strategies about the Smart City. The prelaminar research made arisen a concern for the moment 
of continues and disruptive changes that the world is living.  From this concern, the problem 
statements were made, followed by the research questions. Nevertheless, both the problem 
statements and the research questions were refined and updated during the development of 
the project, since new information of interest made them evolve and change, but always  
following the same premises. 
 
Moreover, the global context was explored and studied for having a deep understanding of the 
current world situation and about what challenges are impacting it. This included the study of 
the 21st Century challenges, the megatrends, and the 4th Industrial Revolution. Hence, the study 
and discussion of this part of the project answer the first research question: what are the 
megatrends that society is nowadays facing? This first parts of the research is the foundation for 
a more in-depth analysis in regards to Smart Cities development.  
 
Then, the Smart City working concept of the project was discussed. Furthermore, an in-depth 
analysis of the Smart City pillars (defined during the literature review search) was conducted 
and related to the Sustainable Development Goals. 
 
This step was followed by field research. Danish municipalities and other international cities' 
webpages were scanned in order to find if they have any Smart City initiatives, as well as two 
workers of two different Danish Kommunes (Lyngby-Taarbæk and Holbæk),  were interviewed. 
Moreover, two works of the Copenhagen Kommune working in the Smart City department were 
contacted and surveyed. This information was used for designing the Smart City model and 
guidelines. These parts of the project answer the second set of research questions: what are the 
pillars of a Smart City? 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
The objective of this project is the developing of a humanistic and holistic model for assessing 
and helping cities for becoming smart. In this part of the thesis, a new profile of citizen will be 
proposed, based on education, which will be the foundation of the last part of the project 
process: the development of the model. This is an iterative process since it required to go back 
and do additional research about the initiatives that international cities and Danish's 
municipalities are conducting for completing existing gaps in the model.  
 
The development of this model have a bottom-up approach, meaning that the foundation of the 
whole process is on the human being. The technological improvements that are now available 
as a consequence of the technological revolution will be taken into consideration as part of the 
possible solutions only if they fit with the Sustainable Development Goals. The final result must 
be a model for developing a Smart City that corresponds to a world in continues transformation. 
This model must be focused on the people and the environment, always considering education 
as a fundamental key to change and evolve. 
 
Due to the scope and time restriction of this project, the last part of the process, referring to 
validation and testing of the models in Danish’s municipalities, remains pending of future 
research projects. 
 
2.2. Complexity theory 
 
The disruptive changes and trends that this project is going to discuss later are incredibly 
complex. They demand the mobilization of all actors (such as governments, businesses, 
academic experts, citizens, etc.) in order to confront them successfully, as well as the 
transformation of these actors, which is even more complicated. Thus, their analysis must be 
done from the perspective of complexity and with an ecosystem vision. 
 
A cell, a brain, an organism, a computer, an immunologic system, and a market economy have 
a thing in common: they form a complex system. A system can be considered complex when 
several interconnected and interdependent parts compose it. These parts dynamically interact 
among them, which generates difficulties for decision-making processes, as well as uncertainty 
about what strategies to follow (Clemens, 2014). Complex systems must be studied as a whole 
and not as the sum of their parts. This is because of the difficulty or even impossibility of studying 
the general behavior of the system by inferring information from the properties of its different 
parts (Bar-Yam, 1997).  
 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
 
Figure 5 Characteristics of complex systems. Source: New England Complex Systems Institute 
Besides, complex systems(Bar-Yam, 2011)(Bar-Yam, 2011) are self-organized, which means that 
the order and structure of the system and sub-systems emerge from local interactions 
(Heylighen, 2009). Complex systems also have a hierarchical organization since a small group of 
parts can organize themselves hierarchically into increasingly larger groups (Ravasz & Barabási, 
2003).  
 
Complex systems present different aspects that give them the complexity attribute, which are 
grouped as follow (Dodder & Dare, 2000): 
 
1. Static complexity: structural aspects of system complexity (connectivity, detail, variety, 
etc.). 
2. Informational complexity: complexity involved in describing or evaluating complex 
systems.  
3. Dynamic complexity: complexity related to behavior, cause and effect, feedback, 
fluctuations and stability, cycles, and time scales. 
 
2.2.1. Complex adaptive systems 
 
Complex adaptive systems are a particular case of complex systems, but they still share general 
complexity aspects, even if they are less integrated or organized than the other types (Chan, 
2001). Industries, ecosystems, social networks, governments, power grids, etc. are good 
examples of complex adaptive systems. Besides, they form dynamic networks of interactions 
and relations with the capacity of modifying their individual and collective behavior, adapting 
and evolving in a changing environment. According to Neirotti et al., cities are also complex 
adaptive systems. Cities have large amounts of interconnected citizens, modalities, businesses, 
communication networks, and services, which confer to cities the complexity attribute (Neirotti, 
De Marco, Cagliano, Mangano, & Scorrano, 2014)(Van Der Hoeven, 2017). Hernández Muñoz 
sustains that a Smart City is a system of significant complexity, a system of systems, or an 
ecosystem in where multiple processes (which are intricately linked) co-exist, making very 
difficult to approach them individually (Hernández Muñoz, 2011).   
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
 
Complex adaptive systems have the following attributes or characteristics (Chan, 2001): 
 
• 
Connectivity: an action by one part of the system will influence in a non-uniform way 
the other related parts. 
• 
Co-evolution: elements of a system can change because of their interactions among 
them or with the environment. 
• 
Sensitive dependence of initial conditions: small disruptions might have a profound 
impact on the overall behavior of the whole system. 
• 
Emergent order: the inability of predicting results from the study of the individual parts.  
• 
Far from equilibrium: new inputs might provide unexpected outcomes, which might 
lead to a significant impact on the system.    
• 
State of paradox: the dynamics underlying complex adaptive systems combine both 
order and chaos (stability and instability, competition and cooperation, order and 
disorder).  
  
This project will use the complexity theory because it allows to explain the behavior of this world 
in continuous transformation, hyperconnected and interdependent and so contributing to a 
new vision and approach, which will be reflected in a model.

3.3. The difficulties of defining the Smart City concept 
 
Smart, intelligent, digital, technological, creative, innovative, cultural, etc. are just some of the 
adjectives that are being used for describing the present and future cities. Therefore, the 
concept of what is a Smart City is ambiguous and questions whether all the cities that are self-
proclaimed as "smart" actually possess that label (Hollands, 2008).  
 
To begin with, Smart Cities are often confused by the use of terms such as wired, digital, 
knowledge cities, etc. Even if these terms might be related between them to some degree, they 
have different meanings (Hollands, 2008)(Nam & Pardo, 2011b). The wired city (Dutton, 1995) 
is a city with a laying down of cable and connectivity, which does not have to imply "smartness." 
Digital cities frequently refer to the building of digital platforms to support community 
networking (Ishida, 2000), as well as digital policies for supplying e-services to the citizens 
through the use of technologies (Cocchia, 2014). Knowledge-based cities are commonly 
described as those whose primary focus is on their relationship with universities and academic 
knowledge, as well as on their links with the businesses world (Rhoades & Slaughter, 2004). 
 
All these terms imply, one way or another, the use and implementation of ICT (Information and 
Communication Technologies) systems for improving economic and political efficiency, as well 
as for enabling social, cultural and urban development (Nicos Komninos, 2002). The Smart City

dependency on the use of ICT is something in which everyone agrees on. Therefore, ICT is one 
of the main economic driving forces of cities, producing numerous social and spatial effects 
(Graham, 2002).  
 
For Mahizhnan, “Information technologies represent the key concept. The vision of an intelligent 
city is not confined to economic excellence that can be led by information technologies, but an 
integral part of this vision is its concern for the quality of life for the ordinary citizen.” 
(Mahizhnan, 1998). 
 
Moreover, significant research and effort have been carried out by academics for exploring the 
IoT (Internet of the Things) role in the Smart City context. The primary reason is the exponential 
growth of smart objects that can be connected to an IoT network and participate in data 
exchange processes. Another factor is the increasing number of population that possess or have 
access to IoT devices (Theodoridis, Mylonas, & Chatzigiannakis, 2013). Some examples of areas 
in which IoT can be implemented are public transport (traffic control and monitoring), electric 
energy provision (energy peak control, fault alerts), water and gas distribution system (leak and 
fault alerts), waste management (smart bins with filled up sensors), etc. (Theodoridis et al., 
2013). IoT systems can also be implemented in other areas, like at the social and economic 
dimensions of the city. Furthermore, IoT can also be used for managing schools buildings, 
historical and touristic places, commerce, etc. (Theodoridis et al., 2013). Therefore, the ability 
to automatically share data and interact with different devices and services whenever and 
wherever is required is an essential characteristic of a Smart City. 
 
Other cities and authors emphasize the business and creativity feature of a Smart City as one of 
its main components: 
 
“Smart is used as a synonym of creative. “[…] aimed at nurturing a creative economy through 
investment in quality of life, which in turn attracts knowledge workers to live and work in smart 
cities." “The nexus of competitive advantage has […] shifted to those regions that can generate, 
retain, and attract the best talent.” (Thite, 2011). 
 
Furthermore, some definitions stress the link between business-driven urban development, 
technology, and governance. These definitions refer to the creation of a unified interface 
between the city, authorities, commercial organizations, and citizens (Hollands, 2008). They also 
refer to governments capacity for innovating in service delivery (Rossi & González, 2011).  
 
"[…] two main streams of research ideas: 1) smart cities should do everything related to 
governance and economy using new thinking paradigms and 2) smart cities are all about 
networks of sensors, smart devices, real-time data and ICT integration in every aspect of 
human life." (Cretu, 2012). 
 
There is also a more humanistic stream of authors that stress the role of human capital and 
education in the development of the Smart City. These authors talk about terms such as smart 
communities and the importance of social learning, education, and social capital (Eger, 2003). 
Besides, Berry and Glaeser show that the most rapid urban growth rates have been achieved in 
those cities where the labor force is well educated (Berry & Glaeser, 2005) (Edward L Glaeser & 
Berry, 2006).  
 
Finally, there is a third group of authors that defines the Smart City in terms of social and 
environmental sustainability. For Carley, social sustainability implies social cohesion and sense 
of belonging (Carley & Smith, 2001), while, for Gleeson and Low, environmental sustainability 
implies an ecological and “green” urban growth and city development (Low, Gleeson, Elander,

& Lidskog, 2005).  Moreover, the department of Businesses, Innovation, and Skills (United 
Kingdom) defines the term as “the effective integration of physical, digital, and human systems 
in the built environment to deliver sustainable, prosperous and inclusive future for its 
citizens”(Department for Business Innovation & Skills, 2013). Furthermore, Cocchia sustains that 
a Smart City is “[…] when the city follows sustainable strategies through the innovative and 
sustainable use of own natural resources.”(Cocchia, 2014). 
 
3.4. A holistic concept of Smart City 
 
All these different definitions reveal how broad is the Smart City concept and its continuous 
evolution. Nevertheless, there is a common consensus among academics that ICT is an essential 
feature of a Smart City. As stated by Neirotti, (Neirotti et al., 2014): “Smart Cities are 
characterized by a pervasive use of Information and Communication Technologies (ICT), which, 
in various urban domains, help cities make better use of their resources.” Nevertheless, several 
authors recognize the holistic dimension of Smart Cities and describe them in more board terms 
that just a digital network. Chris Wilson (the University of Ottawa Centre on Governance) states 
that "being connected is no guarantee of being smart” (Evans, 2002). Paquet shares this same 
thinking and suggests that technology should be an enabler and not necessarily the most 
defining aspect of the Smart City (Paquet, 2001).  
 
Townsend also sustains and defends that Smart Cities requires and holistic approach for 
understanding its complexity and solving its derivate problems. For him, ICT should be a 
connector to other Smart City aspects: 
 
“The broad view is important, since cities must be viewed holistically. Simply installing 
some new technology, no matter how elegant or powerful, cannot solve a city’s 
problems in isolation. But there really is something going on here—information 
technology is clearly going to be a big part of the solution."[…] places where information 
technology is combined with infrastructure, architecture, everyday objects, and even our bodies 
to address social, economic, and environmental problems” (Townsend, 2013). 
 
Therefore, ICT is an essential element of the Smart City that should be present, but it should not 
be taken as its primary and more critical component. Instead, ICT should be considered as an 
enabler for connecting the different city's features, which is a vision that Townsend shares with 
more authors (Neirotti et al., 2014)(Nam & Pardo, 2011b)(Cocchia, 2014). 
 
3.5. The three core components of a Smart City 
 
Cocchia and Nam and Pardo discuss the fundamental factors of a Smart City based on technology 
(infrastructure of hardware and software), people (creativity, diversity, and education), and 
institutions (governance and policy) (Nam & Pardo, 2011a) (Cocchia, 2014). These three factors 
are highly interconnected. Thus, a city will become truly “smart” when investments in human 
and social capital, as well as in IT infrastructure, enhance sustainable growth and the life quality 
of the citizens, through participatory governance (Caragliu, del Bo, & Nijkamp, 2011).  
 
Nam and Pardo remark again that any Smart City initiative should extend beyond technology, 
but still value its essential role in facilitating the creation of an innovative environment 
(Komninos, 2009).  
 
The human factors must emphasize the importance of social learning, education, and training 
actions. Nam and Pardo sustain that the critical factor for the successful development of a city 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
is its citizens and how they interact among them, with the city, and its institutions (Nam & Pardo, 
2011a). Awareness, education, and leadership services should be available and accessible to 
citizens, as well as cultural, language, disabilities, and education barriers should be avoided (Coe, 
Paquet, & Roy, 2001).  
 
Coe, Paquet, and Roy also discuss about the role of governance, which encapsulates 
collaboration, cooperation, partnership, citizen engagement, and participation (Coe et al., 
2001). They, among other authors, stress the importance of governments collaboration with 
other stakeholder's, such as businesses, academics, non-profit and voluntary organizations, etc. 
(G. Anderson & Tregoniong, 1998)(Ingram, Carbonell, Hong, & Flint, 2009)(Lindskog, 
2004)(Paskaleva, 2009). Moreover, Coe, Paquet, and Roy highlight the critical role of leadership, 
both within the government and for its relationships with the citizens (Coe et al., 2001).

3.6. The pillars of a Smart City 
 
The concept of the Smart City has evolved from the initial definition based on digital and more 
technological features to a holistic concept, focused on the people. This brings new variables 
related to the improvement of citizen's life, economic development, and innovation. Technology 
and ICT are still a vital feature of the Smart City, but only as a mean for achieving the Smart City. 
 
According to the literature, there are six relevant characteristics (also known as pillars or axis) 
of the Smart City that are generally accepted by the academic community. The goal of this 
classification is to facilitate a system for ordering and categorizing the different projects and 
initiatives developed in the Smart City field, as well as for determining whether a city is “smart” 
or not (Manville et al., 2014): 
 
1. Smart Economy – economic development. 
2. Smart People – intellectual capital.

3. Smart Governance – government. 
4. Smart Mobility – mobility. 
5. Smart Environment – environmental sustainability. 
6. Smart Living – life quality.

3.9. Identification of global megatrends  
 
3.9.1. Definitions 
 
Nowadays, the world is being impacted by various disruptive changes that are shaping 
economic, social, and environmental reality. These changes are known as megatrends. Retief, 
Bond, Pope, Morrison-Saunders, and King refers to megatrends as “trends that are global and 
call for strategies for adoption, rather than strategies for affecting change to the trends 
themselves”(Retief, Bond, Pope, Morrison-Saunders, King, 2016). PwC (Price Waterhouse 
Coopers) defines megatrends as “macroeconomic and geostrategic forces that are shaping the 
world. They are factual and often backed by verifiable data. By definition, they are big and 
include some of society’s biggest challenges— and opportunities.”(Price Waterhouse Coopers, 
2016), while Hajkowicz, Cook and, Littleboy describe them as “a significant shift in 
environmental, economic and social conditions that will play out over the coming 
decades.”(Stefan Hajkowicz, Hannah Cook, 2012).

3.10. Exploring the 4th Industrial Revolution concept 
 
As sustained by Klaus Schwab5, the megatrends are the drivers of a new industrial revolution 
(Schwab, 2016). Science and technology advancements are supporting the development of 
manufacturing, internationalization, and industrialization all around the world (Belvedere, 
Grando, & Bielli, 2013).  This has led to the emergence of the 4th Industrial Revolution, also 
known as Industry 4.0, which is preceded by three previous industrial revolutions. According to 
McKinsey Global Institute, the 4th Industrial Revolution is the age of cyber-physical systems, 
which are systems that integrate computation, networking, and physical processes. They also 
include several technologies, as the Internet of the Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), 
smartphones, robots, cybersecurity, and 3D printing (Manyika et al., 2013).  
 
3.10.1. Definitions and approaches 
 
According to the available literature, there are different categories for classifying 4th Industrial 
Revolution’s definitions depending on their main domain and context (based on Piccarozzi, 
Aquilani, & Gatti, 2018): 
 
1. An in-depth examination of the components of Industry 4.0, like IoT, ICT, 3D printing, 
etc.: 
 
“Industry 4.0 is the totality of the spheres of economy in which the fully automatic 
production processes are based on the artiﬁcial intellect and the Internet create new 
machines without human participation.” (Majeed & Rupasinghe, 2017). 
 
“The essence of Industry 4.0 conception is the introduction of network-linked intelligent 
systems, which realize self-regulating production: people, machines, equipment and 
products will communicate to one another.” (Kovacs & Kot, 2016).  
 
“Industry 4.0 represents nothing less than the fourth industrial revolution, comprising 
3D printing, Big data, Internet of Things, and Internet of Services, i.e., all the ingredients 
needed to facilitate smart manufacturing and logistics processes.” (Kagermann, 
Wahlster, & Helbig, 2013). 
 
“Industry 4.0 represents the ability of industrial components to communicate each 
other’s.” (Pan et al., 2015). 
 
2. Value creation and value chain6: how innovations from the Industry 4.0 influence 
business processes. 
 
“Industry 4.0 is the new buzzword in the manufacturing industry. It comprises the 
entire value chain process in manufacturing goods and providing services.” (Kinzel, 
2016).

3.10.2. A social innovation perspective 
 
The different definitions for the 4th Industrial Revolution might differ in their element of study, 
but most of them share the same dominant characteristic: the technology focus. These 
definitions show how Industry 4.0 is highly represented by the augmented growth in technology-
enabled platforms and how this has disrupted the existing industry structure. This is going to 
cause profound changes across all industries and create new business models. It will also disrupt 
and reshape production and consumption patterns (Schwab, 2016).  
 
Klaus Schwab declared during the World Economic Forum, conducted in 2016, that the world 
should be aware and have a comprehensive and globally shared understanding about how these 
disruptive technological advancements will change social, economic, ecological, and, cultural 
lives (Morrar & Arman, 2017) dramatically. Therefore, it is of crucial importance to shape the 4th 
Industrial Revolution in a way that it ensures a human-centered approach and empower people, 
rather than taking a divisive and dehumanizing approach (Schwab, 2016).  
 
Brynjolfsson and McAfee, as well as Rifkin, shares Schwab’s vision and emphasizes the 
importance of the recognition of the Industry 4.0 impact on the whole society, as well as the 
opportunities that it will bring (Brynjolfsson & Mcafee, 2014) (Rifkin, 2014). Therefore, the focus 
can not only be in technological development. Having in high consideration social innovation is 
also critical. Thus, the focus must be on the alignment between technological development and

3.11. Smart City models  
 
Many scholars analyzed how the Smart City is structured and organized. With that 
understanding, they have tried to build different conceptual models, guidelines, and 
frameworks. These modeling processes have been carried out from different perspectives.  
 
Several authors use as the base for their models the triple helix conceptual model for gaining 
some understanding of the role of stakeholders in the Smart City. For example, Deakin focuses 
on studying the network formed by university, industry, and government (Deakin, 2014), while 
Lombardi et al. add to this network the civil society indicator group (Lombardi, Giordano, 
Farouh, et al., 2012). Later, Lombardi et al. expands its scope and includes five city clusters 
(Smart Governance, Smart Economy, Smart Human Capital, Smart Living, Smart Environment) 
(Lombardi, Giordano, Caragliu, et al., 2012), which is based on Giffinger’s (2007) research, but 
excluding Smart Mobility. Lombardi, Giordano, Caragliu et al. use these pillars for analyzing what 
interrelations occur within the Smart Cities, from which a set of guidelines is proposed. 
 
Other studies go beyond the triple helix model by proposing a model based on a quintuple helix, 
which includes civil society and the natural environment (respectively) (Carayannis, Barth, & 
Campbell, 2012). Anthopoulos and Tougountzoglou agree with this model and sustain that only 
by integrating all domains of interventions, helped by ICT tools, cities will be able to achieve 
long-lasting and sustainable economic growth and better life quality for citizens (Anthopoulos & 
Tougountzoglou, 2012).  
 
Nam and Pardo conceptualized a compressive view of Smart City innovation from the 
technological, organizational, and policy perspective. From it, they created a multidimensional 
framework for Smart City innovation, placing the same value in the three perspectives (Nam & 
Pardo, 2011b), which will help to the success of Smart Cities initiatives. Chourabi et al. built on 
Nam and Pardo’s proposal by creating a more complex framework. They propose an integrative 
framework, which is aimed to explain the relationships and influences between what they 
identify as external factors (Governance, People and Communities, Economy, Natural 
Environment and Infrastructure) and Smart Cities initiatives. Chourabi et al. state that their 
framework provides a basis for assessing how cities are envisioning and implementing Smart 
Cities initiatives. Their framework can also be used as a tool for a better understanding of the 
success of the implemented Smart Cities initiatives. Moreover, it can be used for measuring 
what is the actual impact of the internal factors (organizational, technical and contextual) (also 
discussed by Nam and Pardo) on the success of Smart Cities initiatives (Chourabi et al., 2012).  
Fernández-Güel et al. take another approach and propose a methodological framework based 
on systems theory. It aims to provide conceptual support for those Smart City initiatives that 
might be struggling to take a holistic approach of the city structure and operations (Fernández-
Güell, Collado-Lara, Guzmán-Araña, & Fernández-Añez, 2016). 
 
Other authors base their conceptual models on governance, which they consider the most 
critical element for the success of Smart Cities’ initiatives. Indeed, Castelnovo et al. understand 
that governance must be the starting point for creating a holistic framework for assessing Smart 
Cities and their capability to generate public value, which is the goal of good governance 
(Castelnovo, Misuraca, & Savoldelli, 2016). Therefore, Castelnovo et al. take a holistic approach 
of the city's environment and consider that its capability to progress and survive depends on its 
stakeholders and policymakers. They propose a citizen-centered framework in which 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
governance is the main actor, placing "Community Building and Management" as the center of 
the model. Nevertheless, the framework also includes four more dimensions (Vision and 
Strategy Formulation, Public Value Generation, Asset Management, and Economic and Financial 
Sustainability) (Castelnovo et al., 2016).  
 
Some companies have also published their own Smart City's conceptual frameworks. Deloitte 
published the so-called "Smart City Capability Framework." Deloitte states that for successfully 
building a Smart City, there must be a clear vision of what it has to be achieved, as well as 
maturity and a strategy to realize this ambition. This company sustains that there are the six 
capability dimensions (public-private ecosystems, data, technology, skills and competencies, 
innovation culture, and attractiveness) that form an inner circle, and four outer dimensions, 
which are the city’s stakeholder (citizens and other users, government, businesses, knowledge 
partners) (Dubbeldeman & Ward, 2015).  
 
Other models developed a series of indicators. For example, IESE Cities in Motion has developed 
a conceptual model composed by nine dimensions (human capital, social cohesion, economy, 
governance, environment, mobility and transportation, technology, urban planning, and 
international outreach) and a total of 96 indicators. All these elements are aimed to reflect both 
objective and subjective data. Moreover, they offer a comprehensive view of the city that is 
being studied (Berrone et al., 2019). Deloitte’s conceptual model is based on an in-depth study 
of several cities labeled as "smart," as well as on city leaders, entrepreneurs, academics, and 
experts working on the Smart City study field. Moreover, Deloitte also proposes a set of steps 
(diagnosis of the situation, the development of a strategy, and its implementation) for 
conducting the study of cities (Berrone et al., 2019). 
 
These types of frameworks are mainly used for classifying and ranking cities performance in 
different categories. Boyd Cohen is famous for the elaboration of various Smart City ranking. For 
this purpose, he has invented a conceptual model called "The Smart City Wheel." This 
framework's primary purpose is to get a better understanding of the six dimensions of the Smart 
City, which are again those proposed by Giffinger (2007). Nevertheless, Cohen gives particular 
importance to the Smart Economy, Smart Government, and Smart Mobility dimensions. Each of 
these six components has a set of indicators for measuring the eighteen sub-components of the 
wheel (for example, Smart Buildings, productivity, online services, education, safety, integrated 
ICT, etc.) (Cohen, 2014). Cohen uses this framework for analyzing the data from each studied 
city. Then, he applies a mathematical formula (z-scored), which permits the comparison of data 
in different units and therefore, the creation of an index and the subsequent city's ranking 
(Cohen, 2014). 
 
Azamat Abdoullaev took a sustainable approach for the creation of his conceptual model. He 
sustains that "the world is requested to synergistically driven by natural capital, social capital, 
technological and digital capital, like as the Global Internet/Web of Things, Knowledge, and 
Social Intelligence and Renewable Energy Sources. Such a New World is emerging as a Smart 
World, converging the Cyber, Social, and Physical Domains”(Abdoullaev, 2011). From this 
thought, he creates the World Trinity Diagram. He proposes this diagram as a foundational 
framework for the European Community and its EU 2020 Strategy for smart, sustainable, and 
inclusive growth, as well as for global industries initiatives from IBM and CISCO. He also discusses 
that the relevance of the Smart World concept consists in its capacity to merge and integrate as 
a whole the Ecological World, the Digital World, and the Social World. Accordingly, he states 
that a genuinely sustainable community must be digitally smart, socially intelligent, and 
ecologically sustainable (Abdoullaev, 2011).

4. Global context 
 
This chapter is aimed to describe world nowadays: its challenges and trends that are leading to 
a new revolution that significantly differs from the previous ones, which is called the 4th 
Industrial Revolution. This new situation affects society, economy, political powers, etc. and 
presents features of complexity as uncertainty and interconnectivity.  
 
4.1. The 21st Century Challenges 
 
Nowadays, humanity lives in a disruptive world where the future is a paramount concern due to 
the moment of continues changes in which society is living in, which are: 
 
• 
Technological changes: currently, technology is advancing fast, and it becomes 
quickly obsolete, or it is easily renewed. Artificial Intelligence, robotics, etc. are not 
only allowing to make significant advances in fields such as medicine or electronics, 
but they are also causing disruptive situations, especially in the world of work. 
Furthermore, technology is an integral part of nowadays world since it is 
omnipresent in all fields and areas of society. 
• 
Environmental changes: climate change, global warming, air and water pollution, 
acid rain, toxic waste, and shrinking energy supplies are challenging and threatening 
the world. If this is not faced and big measurements are not taken, consequences 
could be catastrophic. 
• 
Political changes: there is a general disaffection among people concerning political 
affairs and politicians. Democracy is based on political trust; therefore, the 
increasing lack of it could challenge democratic governments (van der Meer, 2017). 
The leading cause of this problem is corruption as well as new parties and new 
political leaders whose discourse and politics are not only belligerent but also 
inconceivable 50 years ago. An excellent example of these disruptive situations is 
the ascension of extremist and populist political parties that are calling into question 
democracy and the European Union.  
• 
Economic changes: the Internet revolution and data flows are encouraging new 
economies, like the digital economy. This will change not only the way of selling but 
also the way of working, which, in turn, will alter the labor market needs (Guajardo 
Villarreal, 2016). 
 
All these changes are challenging the world and making it an unstable place with an uncertain 
future. Not only governments but also society, and therefore, citizens, have to face these 
problems. From this certain responsibility, the 21st Century Challenges arose.    
 
4.1.1. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 
 
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are a series of 17 challenges adopted by all United 
Nations Member States that the world and the society should achieve by 2030 for a better and 
more sustainable future. SDGs represent an urgent action call not only for developed countries 
but also for those that are still in the developing process by forming a global partnership (United 
Nations, 2015d).

4.1.2. New jobs and new skills for the future of work: six disruptive situations that will 
change the work world 
 
New challenges are also emerging in the work world as a consequence of the current 
technological world. Various disruptive situations are breaking the traditional ways of working: 
a person study, works in that field and finish his or her working life in the same job position. 
Almost anyone was able to predict his or her life itinerary. 
 
However, due to the technology irruption, this is not possible anymore. This change will require 
the creation of new jobs and the learning and training of the new skills necessaries for the future 
of the work world. 
 
The Institute of the Future for the University of Phoenix Research Institute identifies six drivers 
or disruptive situations that are going to reshape the future of jobs. These drivers are going to 
change the work perception, what is a job as well as the skills that future workers will need in 
order to be able to contribute to the society (A. Davies, Fidler, & Gorbis, 2011): 
 
1. Extreme longevity: the increase in global life expectancy, as well as the delay in the age 
of retirement, will demand that workers receive continuous training and education, and 
therefore, educational institutions must adapt to this. Moreover, the willingness for 
accepting new working challenges (as taking a new job), and therefore having to acquire 
new knowledge over the upcoming professional years, will be a critical skill for future 
and current workers. 
==PAGE:49==
 
49 
2. Rise of smart machines and systems: the technological world and more specifically, 
robotics and programmable systems, are going or are already impacting those jobs that 
are focused on repetitive tasks susceptible to be automatized. These kinds of jobs are 
going to disappear, which means that their workers will have to be re-educated and re-
trained for new jobs that require to perform higher thinking and non-repetitive tasks.   
3. New media ecology: new communication tools and multimedia technologies will 
require new alphabetization and a transformation in the way of communicating, which 
goes beyond the mere lecture of a text. This driver is vital for understanding the 
technological, cultural, and cognitive transformation that has been occurring in the past 
two decades. 
4. Computational world: the massive increase of sensors and processing powers are 
transforming the world in a highly programmable place. This driver connects with the 
rise of smart machines and systems since all tasks susceptible of being programmed will 
experiment the needless of a worker who performs the task manually.  
5. Superstructured organizations: new social technologies will drive new production and 
value creation ways. Thanks to this, society will be able to connect with other people or 
organizations in a much easier way as never before.  
6. Globally connected world: Society is living in a hyper-connected world in where, mostly 
thanks to the Internet, connecting and performing globally is easier and more accessible 
for everyone. This increase in global interconnectivity is placing diversity and 
adaptability at the center of the organization's operation. This requires new 
competencies and skills not only for businesses but also for citizens. Therefore, new 
ways of relating, interacting, operating, selling, etc. will be needed, being this a change 
from the previous linear way of performing.

4.1.3. Redefining economic and social models: a framework for equity 
 
The challenges that the Sustainable Development Goals are addressing, as well as the disruptive 
situations in the work world and the technological, environmental, and political changes,  are 
causing that society is in crisis. All these disruptive changes that break with the old and create a 
new era also generate uncertainty. As a consequence, they call into question the current 
economic models. Therefore, there is a need for rethinking these models in order to offer more 
significant access to necessary resources for all the citizens. Nowadays, there are significant 
inequities as SDG number 1 (No Poverty) and number 2 (Zero Hunger) present. Even in 
developed cities, it can be easily found how resources are not equitably distributed among 
citizens, which is caused by a very ruthless capitalist model. 
 
Therefore, economic models have to change for providing access for everyone to primary 
resources.  For this matter, The Institute of the Future for the University of Phoenix Research 
Institute has created a framework for equity formed by three strategic points (Alto, 2010):

person in order to achieve economic and social equity. 
2. Reinventing public assets: it refers to collective assets that are held and collectively 
owned in the public trust by non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and public entities 
(governments, communities, town halls…). An example of these assets is public schools, 
natural and cultural resources, legal and social services, etc. For fostering access to 
them, bureaucratic processes should not be an obstacle in order to allow people to

4.2. Megatrends 
 
Megatrends are global macroeconomic forces that are impacting businesses, economy, society, 
culture, and individual lives and therefore, shaping the world and defining its future (Singh, 
2012). Megatrends will change how people communicate, how daily activities are conducted, 
production, education, businesses, and how governmental decisions are taken. 
 
From all the disruptive situations that are occurring right now, only those that have a sustained 
impact on the world and are stable over time are considered megatrends. Besides, megatrends 
are certain aspects which can hardly be altered or even changed. Thus, they will instead become 
obstacles to humanity's progress or opportunities. Therefore, for driving megatrends and 
successfully surpassing their challenges, society must react proactively and be the agent of the 
change. 
 
Megatrends can be classified in different ways, which gives a sense of structure of the drivers of 
the future (Klein, Bansal, & Wohlers, 2017). This project will discuss the following megatrends 
and their related sub-trends:   
 
1. Rapid urbanization 
2. Demographic and social change 
3. Shift in global economic power 
4. Technological breakthrough 
5. Climate change 
 
4.2.1. Rapid urbanization 
 
The global population living in urban areas is rapidly increasing. Right now, 55% of the world's 
population lives in urban areas. Moreover, it is expected that by 2050, this percentage increase 
to 68% (DESA/UN-WUP, 2018). Two trends mostly cause this: 
 
• 
Rural-urban migration: the global rural population is now close to 3.4 billion, but it is 
likely to decline to around 3.1 billion by 2050 (DESA/UN-WUP, 2018).  
• 
Global rise population: the current world population is around 7.6 billion, but it is 
expected to increase to 8.6 billion by 2050 and 11.2 billion by 2100 (DESA/UN-WUP, 
2018).  
 
These phenomena will mostly impact on developing countries as China, other Asians' countries, 
and Latino America. One of its leading causes is the lack of working opportunities in rural areas 
due to the over-consumption of land and a surplus in the rural workforce (Wong, 2015). This 
creates high poverty levels and the desire of migrating to more prominent urban communities, 
where more chances of improving life quality might be waiting. 
 
These massive migratory movements are going to constrain the capacity of the cities, creating 
mega-cities and mega-regions. This will result in a massive demand for infrastructure, service 
provision, transport, government, planning, etc. Moreover, it will cause growing unemployment, 
urban insecurity, and environmental damage. All these challenges are going to impact cities, 
which will have to respond powerfully and inclusively if they want to successfully avoid the rise 
of inequality levels, pollution, and development costs. Therefore, governments and economic 
powers must work together in the developing of new businesses models, planning strategies, 
use of new technologies, new economic models, empowering citizen participation, inclusive 
==PAGE:53==
 
53 
infrastructure, etc. In turn, this will improve the livability, sustainability, and productivity of the 
city (Price Waterhouse Cooper, n.d.-b).  
 
Furthermore, the population growth of cities and economic development are linked. In 2015, 
85% of the global GDP was generated in cities (New Climate Economy, 2015).  Cities are one of 
the most productive economic powers. They can attract firms, talent, as well as sharing 
knowledge and ideas (Price Waterhouse Cooper, n.d.-b). Therefore, the competition among 
cities for these resources is going to increase. This again highlights how important it is for cities 
to work in building livable places where people want and chose to live. They must also fill urban 
areas with attractive and equally distributed services, jobs, and high-security levels.  
 
Another obvious problem is going to be the need of providing affordable housing for all the new 
dwellers. Therefore, new ways of land administration will be required. Innovative solutions as 
modular housing, which can be extended as the family grows, will be needed. 
 
Moreover, cities consume over two-thirds of the world’s energy and account for more than 70% 
of global CO2 emissions (C40 Cities, n.d.). This means that an increasement of the urban 
population will most likely lead to an even more significant deterioration of climate change due 
to, for example, traffic congestion. Therefore, aspects of transport, infrastructure, and land use 
are going to be determinant for the sustainability of the city. Sharing economy, and so the 
enhancing of collaborative consumption, will be an economic model that might also help to 
solve these problems (shared cars, co-working spaces, shared houses…). 
 
In brief, the sustainable development of the city is going to depend on how urban growth is 
managed. Infrastructure, social services, education, housing, jobs, and health care, with a 
particular focus on the most vulnerable parts of the population, are going to be needed for 
creating a safe and inclusive community.

4.2.2. Demographic and social change 
 
Globally, the elderly population shows the fastest growing: by 2030, there will be 390 millions 
of population over 65 years old more than in 2015 (United Nations Department of Economic and 
Social Affairs, 2015). Moreover, the global population will increase by 2050. Nevertheless, this 
phenomena is mostly attributed to just nine countries with high fertility rates or with large 
populations (India, Nigeria, Democratic Republic of the Congo, Pakistan, Ethiopia, the United 
Republic of Tanzania, the United States of America, Uganda and Indonesia) (United Nations 
Department of Economic and Social Affairs, 2017). On the other hand, the population of more 
than fifty countries of the world is expected to decrease by more than 15% by 2050. These 
countries are mostly from Europe, where the current fertility rates required for replacement of 
the population in the future is below the needed level, which has reminded the same for 
decades. Thus, global fertility is expected to fall from 2.5 births per woman in 2010-2015 to 2.4 
in 2025-2030 and 2 in 2095-2100 (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs, 
2017). 
==PAGE:54==

Besides, the largest generation in history is the Millennials (1980-2000) with a population of 91 
million, which has even surpassed the Baby Boomers (1946-19649) with "only" 77 million 
(Goldman Sachs, n.d.). Nevertheless, since 2008, there has been a slowdown in birth rates. 
Besides, demographics shows that the spending peak happens when the population reaches the 
age of forty-six years old. So, what will happen when the largest generation in history reach this 
peak, followed by a generation way smaller? Moreover, what will happen when most of the 
population retires, and just a few percent enter the workforce? (S. Dent, 2014). Furthermore, 
life expectancy is increasing, which means that people will be living longer in retirement.  
 
In brief, due to low fertility rates, more people are retiring than entering the workforce, which 
will directly affect economic growth. This could trigger a global crisis that could have severe 
economic and societal consequences. 
 
Therefore, solutions must be found and implemented. People living longer and the technological 
disruption of the work world is going to demand continuous education and training. This means 
that, in order to overcome the workforce shrinkage, workers will have to keep working for longer 
and therefore, retirement age must be delayed. Furthermore, thanks to technological advances 
in bio and nanotechnology, as well as in robotics and in cleaner energy sources, life expectancy 
is not only going to increase, but also life quality is going to be improved. This reinforces the 
possibility of implementing late retirement measures. Therefore, business models will need to 
change and adapt to the fact that workers might live beyond a hundred years old. Measures as 
reforming pension rules, outlawed age discrimination, re-training, and development of new 
skills for older workers (e.g. digital skills), as well as the use of robots for repetitive tasks, must 
be fostered. 
 
Nevertheless, an aging population will also present some threats that could increase inequality 
levels. A large population of elderlies will require a considerable investment in healthcare 
service. If the economic system is not strong enough to sustain them, this will affect their life 
quality and therefore, equity. Political institutions should be prepared for these challenges and 
take care of older people by, for example, the use of medical technological advances and 
inclusive health care and nursing policies. 
 
Moreover, more women should join the workforce to compensate for the low amount of 
workers. Indeed, mothers having fewer children or having them at a later age could help the 
number of working women to increase substantially. Nevertheless, this could have a negative 
effect and even lowering more fertility rates. Therefore, measures must be taken for providing 
childcare services so women can both work and begin a family (kindergarten at work, 
government support, shared paternity leave, pay equity and transparency, more flexible 
working options, etc.). 
 
Besides, the current generation of workers, the Millennials, are aware of the world globalization 
and global connectivity, and therefore, they are willing to look for jobs internationally. Actually, 
in the last decade, the number of mobile employees increased by 25%, and it will probably 
increase to 50% by 2020 (Price Waterhouse Cooper, USC University of Southern California, & 
London Business School, 2013). Therefore, the globally interconnected market will allow young 
workers to move around the world. This will create new ways of working as freelancers, virtual 
workers, etc. as well as enhance entrepreneurship. These phenomena will increase migration 
levels, which might contribute to develop countries population growth. In fact, 85% of the 
population growth in the G7 economies8 might come from migration. This could benefit the

4.2.3. Shift in global economic power 
 
It is expected that the world economy doubled by 2050 (Price Waterhouse Coopers, 2017). 
Moreover, even if nowadays' advanced economies will continue having a higher average 
income, emerging economies will be the growing power of the global economy. Therefore, as 
the following data shows, there is being a power shift from west to east (Price Waterhouse 
Coopers, 2017): 
 
• 
In 2014, China became the largest economy in purchasing power parity, overtaking the 
United States. 
• 
By 2028, China is expected to overtake the United States in the market exchange rate. 
• 
By 2025, India and Indonesia could respectively place second and fourth in the group of 
the largest world's economies (based on purchasing power parity). 
• 
By 2025, Mexico and Indonesia's economy could be more extensive than the United 
Kingdom and France's economy. Moreover, Pakistan and Egypt could surpass Italy and 
Canada in purchasing power parity. 
 
In any case, the economic growth potential of developing economies will highly depend on the 
willingness of governments and economic powers to implement new and modern measures 
(investment in infrastructure, more effective political and legal institutions, improve 
macroeconomic stability, etc.) (Price Waterhouse Coopers, 2017). Moreover, the investment in 
education, the openness to new technologies, new and more flexible and inclusive business 
models, as well as the attraction and caption of talented people, will be fundamental for these 
economies to enhance their economic power. 
 
The irruption as economic powers of developing countries is going to challenge already 
developed economies. Therefore, in order to successfully adapt to these changes, dynamic and 
flexible business strategies will be needed. Furthermore, it will be necessary to understand new 
markets and consumers, which will need close collaboration with local partners. Besides, for the 
achievement of global sustainable development, western economic powers must get involved 
with developing economies by investing in education, infrastructure, etc. Moreover, it is crucial 
never to forget that already existing western economic powers (as for Europe and North 
America) have lower risks when doing businesses because of their much stable political

Besides, trade patterns have changed and are impacting the economy. This is happening 
because of the integration of national economies in a globally interconnected market, which is 
generating substantial monetary benefits. This phenomena, also known as globalization, has 
changed existing business models, creating new competitors and lower prices all around the 
world. This will be enhanced by the arrival of the Internet of the Thing (IoT), artificial intelligence, 
robots, etc. (Schreiber & Forer, n.d.).  
 
Globalization will accelerate productivity growth, foster free trade, competitiveness, and 
increase labor mobility. Nevertheless, it will also generate some difficulties and challenges, both 
for economic powers and civil society. A globally interconnected market will negatively impact 
on small industries and businesses that are not capable of performing free trade, nor compete 
globally. For consumers, it will mean more buying choices and more competitive prices. Besides, 
globalization will impact on society. A globally connected world can transform attitudes and 
ideas about society, religion, and culture because of its immense diffusion power. On the other 
hand, a global market also means a higher production, which will impact the environment by 
increasing resource scarcity and the use of non-renewable sources (Pettinger, 2017).  
 
Furthermore, globalization is impacting changing consumption patterns and fotering the rise of 
middle classes. This phenomenon is mostly happening in developing economies, where the 
population now can participate in the global production network. Therefore, they will 
experiment a substantial economic growth, a rise in purchasing power, and higher living 
standards. Thanks to this, the global middle class will expand dramatically from half a million in 
1960 to four billion in 2030, being this more notorious in developing economies like China and 
India (Pezzini, 2012).  
 
Therefore levels of production and consumption will rise as well. This will directly affect the 
environment because of the increasement in the use of water and energy, food, waste 
generation, etc. (United Nations, 2010). The overuse of resources will enhance the need for 
urgent measures to palliate the damaging of the environment. Examples of that are the use of 
energy-efficient technologies, greener business models, and a change in consumption and 
production patterns. 
 
Lastly but not less necessary, it is the volatility of commodity prices. The fluctuation of 
commodity prices is just a response to the hyperconnected and uncertain world where 
disruptive and unprecedented changes hit the economy and society. One of the reasons why 
commodity prices' value might go down is the production increment caused by globalization and 
the rising of the middle class. If current supplies exceed demand, prices will be lower. However, 
if the demand is higher than supplies (grow in the global population), prices will go up (Kowalski, 
2019). These contradictions highlight how volatile and unpredictable is the behavior of 
commodity prices. Moreover, demanding economies like China and the global stock market are 
making commodity prices to fall (Hammond, 2009).

4.2.4. Technological breakthrough 
 
The world is living an unprecedented digital revolution, where technological advancements are 
disrupting every single aspect of society. Technology is cheaper, faster, more abundant, 
multidimensional, and more able to deliver new experiences and services. Technology is going 
to affect the whole world's aspects, including governments, businesses, and society. This means 
that this phenomenon must be studied as an ecosystem. Therefore, it is needed a deep 
understanding of its interconnections, implications, and consequences. 
 
The dimension that is going to be most directly impacted will be the production sector. 
Manufacturing produces the double of what it used to do in the past but with notably fewer 
employees (Blanchette, 2004). Besides, a study from Oxford University shows that 46% of the 
jobs at the United States are in risk of automation (Frey & Osborne, 2017), which results in the 
believing that an automated job is a lost job. This disruptive situation will be caused or is already 
caused by the development of artificial intelligence and robotics. Examples of this are machinery 
that can learn from its experience and optimize its own performance, as well as robots that can 
work more precisely than humans and for a lower cost. 
 
Nevertheless, the reality is that machines do not replace jobs so much as they replace tasks: 
automatized jobs will not disappear; they will change. Indeed, those jobs more susceptible to 
being automatized are high routine occupations, simple physical or manual work, and repetitive 
tasks (Wisskirchen et al., 2017).

agriculture sector. Nowadays, only 2% works in it (Autor, 2015). With all the rapid and 
unprecedented technological and digital revolution, it is impossible to predict the future of work 
as well as what kind of jobs are going to be created. Therefore, it is essential to prepare future 
workers with skills and knowledge that machines are not able to perform (as complex critical 
thinking, creative problem solving, and social intelligence). Indeed, the most demanded worker 
profiles will be those related to teaching, sciences, humanistic, social sciences, media science, 
artistic professions, as well as lawyers, doctors, and nurses (Wisskirchen et al., 2017).  
 
It is also essential not to forget about those workers that inevitably are going to lose their jobs. 
Governments, businesses, and educational institutions must respond to this situation and help 
these workers by re-training them in new skills, as in digital skills.

4.2.5. Climate change 
 
As previously discussed, the global rise population, rapid urbanization, and an emerging middle 
class are rising consumption levels. Therefore, there is an increase in the demand for resources 
like energy, food, and water. However, the earth has a finite amount of resources. Moreover, it 
is unable to support the enormous demand for goods that the increasing population needs. This 
means that if production and consumption patterns keep increasing, a shortage of resources 
will most likely happen that will affect the global population. Indeed, humanity is using resources 
at a rate 50% faster than nature can regenerate them. This data projects that by 2030, society 
is going to need more than two planets for meeting goods demand (Weizsäcker et al., 2014).  
 
Moreover, global warming has been increasing since the end of the 19th Century, and it is 
expected to continue the same trend.  This has been majorly caused by an increasement in CO2 
emissions and other gases due to the burning of fossil fuels. In turn, this is causing an average 
increase in temperatures, which are expected to rise by more than 2 degrees before 2100 (NASA, 
2019). The rise in temperatures will directly impact in the rise of sea levels, which will propitiate 
the increasement of storms and flooding. This will be mostly caused by the melting of glaciers, 
thermal expansion, and the loss of Greenland and Antarctica's ice sheets (Nunez, 2019). All of 
this will have as a direct consequence the loss of lands due to floods. The most affected will be 
those in not so wealthy countries as the Caribbean Islands, Ecuador, Vietnam, Bangladesh, etc. 
(Kasyoka Musili, 2017).

4.3. The Fourth Industrial Revolution 
 
4.3.1. The context of an unprecedented revolution  
 
The 21st Century Challenges and megatrends are the catalysts of the 4th Industrial Revolution 
that profoundly differ from previous ones. Throughout history, different revolutions have 
happened in which disruptive changes have not only changed the industry, but also society and 
the human's way of living.  
 
The first industrial revolution occurred at the end of the 18th century when mechanization (by 
the use of water and steam power) first appeared. This invention fostered the emergence of the 
steam engine and, therefore, of new transport means, as the railway and the steamboat. One 
century later, the second industrial revolution happened. Mass production and assembly lines 
impacted the old manufacturing ways, thanks to the use of electric power. Moreover, new 
communication tools appeared, like the telegraph, the automobile, and the plane. The third 
industrial revolution came in the second part of the 20th century, with the emergence of nuclear 
energy, electronics, automated production, computers, and the Internet.  
==PAGE:61==
 
61 
 
From the development of this third revolution, a fourth industrial revolution is taking place right 
now (also known as Industry 4.0). The 4th Industrial Revolution is impacting on economy, 
industry, and society, like have never happened before. It constitutes a whole new era that 
completely differs from past revolutions (Schwab, 2016).  The speed at which the changes are 
happening is unprecedented. These changes are occurring in an exponential way rather than in 
a linear pace. This is a consequence of the globally interconnected world, and the technology's 
ability to engender new and more powerful technologies. 
 
Moreover, the combination of multiple technologies and the digital revolution are impacting in 
a disruptive way not only the economy, business, and society. Moreover,  culture and the 
individual are being reached by this impact. Lastly, the 4th Industrial Revolution is going to 
disturb almost all the industries globally, as well as transform production, management, and 
governance systems.

In brief, this new era is marked by a high technological impact, which is going to have a global 
effect on every single aspect, including economic systems and social structures, that shape the 
world. New risks and challenges will arise that can seriously damage society. Therefore, it is 
essential to deeply study and reflect on how all these disruptive situations are going to affect 
the industry,  public field, people, and cities. Moreover, it is essential to discover new ways of 
challenging all these issues and transform them into opportunities and growth. 
 
One of the main aspects that characterize the 4th Industrial Revolution is the uncertainty of a 
hyperconnected world that is continuously changing. It means that no one knows how this 
revolution is going to evolve and what are going to be its consequences. Moreover, due to the 
hyper-connection across the different sectors, global collaboration among the different 
stakeholders (governments, business, educational institutions, and civil society) will be required 
in order to drive this new and unprecedented situation. Therefore, the public and private sector

4.3.2. Global transformations of the 4th Industrial Revolution 
 
The 4th Industrial Revolution is transforming the world. It has been driven by fascinating new 
technologies that are fusing together across different domains in ways that could have not being 
imaged ten years ago. This is producing huge innovations that are bringing several benefits to 
the increasement of productivity, and thus of the economy. Moreover, these innovations are 
stressing industries, organizations, businesses, and individuals, causing massive changes and 
disruption.  
 
This is generating a growing concern about not just the future of businesses, but also about the 
future of jobs. This is probably the biggest question and issue that is most challenging: what kind 
of jobs are going to be needed? Moreover, given the new powerful technologies and their 
possible applications, how is going to be the labor market in the future? There are several critical 
challenges in assuring that people get the same opportunities and benefits in the new labor 
world, given the skills that they will need to acquire.  
 
At the same time, the disruption of jobs might inevitably lead to an increasement in forms of 
discrimination, leaving people out and removing opportunities for some. Thus, the question of 
inequality is critical. Security, international security, and individual security are other critical 
issues of significant concern. New technologies are being used for military purposes, which will 
threaten the security of countries and people. 
 
It is also essential to think about new ways of governing. It is needed to find new ways of 
governance that approach agile governance models. These new models must not only look at 
the rules that should frame and restrict technology and the 4th Industrial Revolution. Moreover, 
governments, businesses, and civil society must be able to keep up with the fast pace of 
technological change. 
 
Ethics and identity are another issue of concern. Despite what people might think, technology 
is not a neutral element, and it is not just a tool: technology has the power of shaping our lives 
and the world's context.

Furthermore, global competition is becoming more and more innovation-driven. This is

global economy. Furthermore, circular economic models are going to be needed for a wise 
use of the available resources. 
 
Moreover, there is a shift from current economic models that work under the premise of 
buying a product, using it, and later dispose it. Resource scarcity and social awareness are 
fostering new economic models based on more circular approaches that involve the 
regenerative use of materials by remanufacturing products or components, recycling

advanced products. Furthermore, the Internet of the Things is increasing emphasis on 
human-machine collaboration.

5. The Smart City 
 
5.1. Working definition 
 
Of all the Smart City concepts and models that have been studied and described in the literature 
review, this project identified itself with these holistic and humanistic models and definitions in 
which technology is a mean and not an end in itself. 
 
This project ‘s proposal admits the importance of the following determinant factors of a Smart 
City: 
 
1. Human capital 
2. The environment, its improvement and maintenance. 
3. The predominant role of education to achieve the profile of an intelligent citizen. 
 
These aspects are the foundation of the proposal for this master thesis. 
 
In order to achieve the predominant role of the person, within the framework of the Smart City, 
and to arrive to the concept of intelligent citizen, it will be proposed the sociotechnical model 
of the i-Learner, which will be discussed later. 
 
The role of the environment is crucial in this proposal because the concept of the Smart City can 
not be achieved without taking it into high consideration. A healthy natural environment 
determines the quality of life of the city and the people who live in it. Again, at this point, 
education appears as one of the most relevant tools of awareness and action for the 
environment integral improvement.  
 
This project places Sustainable Development Goals as tools for transforming the world, and thus 
cities. The Sustainable Development Goals are a guide towards transformation in all its 
dimensions. For achieving all this, the necessary alliances of society, institutions, governments, 
and the promotion of collaboration at all levels must be made.  
 
By achieving this, the Smart City will be able to guarantee the access of the whole society to all 
the basic and necessary resources for the integral and dignified development of the people. 
 
This is the Smart City proposal of this project. 
 
It is important to realize that the catalyst for everything has been the internet. Internet has 
democratized society in the sense of its empowerment, since it allows access to all kind of 
knowledge, as well as disintermediation, in the sense that the mean people need to get 
information and knowledge is also the Internet (Rubio Royo, 2011). 
 
From all the above, the working Smart City definition of this project is as follows: 
 
"The Smart City is that city centered on the person, the environment, and education. Its 
purpose is the integral development of each of its members through education. An education

5.2. The importance of cities 
 
Significant challenges are going to impact, without a doubt and very notably, big cities. Three 
reasons justify this statement. The first, because in these large cities the majority of the world's 
population will be concentrated; the second, because technology is going to be the key to its 
sustainable management; and the third, because cities are going to be the key for the 
transformation of the world. 
 
Regarding the first reason, 55% of the world's population lives in urban areas, and it is expected 
to increase to 68% by 2050. If at the moment this percentage represents more than 7.7 billion 
people (Worldmeters, 2019), it is easy to visualize how crowded cities are going to be.  
 
It is true that this proportion depends very much on the regions. For example, and according to 
data from 2018, in North America the proportion of people living in cities increased to 80%, 
while in Africa only to 43% and in Asia to 50 % (United Nations Department of Economic and 
Social Affairs, 2018a), while in Europe the percentage is 75.5% (Worldmeters, 2019). This trend, 
which is economic in nature, does not need justification, since it is evident that the population 
moves where it finds opportunities. Moreover, one in five people in the world lives in a city of 
more than one million inhabitants (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs, 
2018b)(UN-Habitat, 2012)(Caragliu et al., 2011), known as “megacities”14. 
 
To manage cities’ population (the second reason to develop), it will be necessary a lot of 
innovation and creativity, and a technological adaptation that may not be easy, but which is 
essential (Lim & Mack, 2017).  
 
The risks have already been exposed: marginality, insufficient supply, dehumanization, climate 
change, natural disasters, insecurity, terrorism, economic crisis, and a long etcetera. 
Thus, the development of the Sustainable Development Goals in these big cities will be essential. 
This, together with awareness of the need for their application, will be the key to success and 
make cities a place in where people can find the opportunities they need to live: opportunities 
to access the so-called "Private assets"; the importance of collaboration ("collaborative assets"); 
and the importance of participating in the public decisions of the institutions ("public assets"). 
 
Finally, the third reason. Cities will transform the world. The logic of this last reasoning is linked 
to the previous two. The very concept of Smart City that this project develops, focused on the 
person, the environment and education, and on the basis of the application of the Sustainable 
Development Goals, should be the engine of change of this world towards a more sustainable, 
more human, more critical and ultimately more fair one. Again, at this point, the role of 
education and a new citizen profile, for what this project advocates, arise.

5.3.2. The six Smart City Pillars 
 
The next six pillars of a Smart City are going to be studied: 
 
1. Government: services and operations for enhancing citizen participation in the political 
decision making processes, optimizing governmental digital services, promoting 
transparency, etc. The representatives of this pillar are mainly the governing authorities, 
city managers, policy makers… 
2. Mobility: ICT systems for improving mobility, as well as other measures that promotes 
a cleaner use of energy. The representatives of this pillar are workers performing in this 
sector, as car companies, public transport responsible, etc. 
3. Environmental sustainability: this pillar is aimed to safeguard the natural environment 
of a Smart City. It concerns different measures related to clean and renewable energy, 
re-use of wastewater, and an efficient management of waste. The representatives of 
this pillar are those workers related to environmental sciences, urban planning, 
environmental protection, waste management companies, energy and water suppliers, 
etc.  
4. Citizens-Intellectual capital: this pillar is related to the citizen’s capacities to adapt and 
progress in the Smart City context. The representatives of this pillar are teachers, 
scientists, researchers, entrepreneurs, and even students. 
5. Economic development: new economic models and more sustainable ways of economic 
development are studied in this pillar. The representatives of this pillar are those related 
to economic power, as companies, investors, entrepreneurs, banks… 
6. Life quality: this pillar is aimed to increase the life quality of the inhabitants of the Smart 
City, by building a city in which humans come first than technological advances, and in 
which the same opportunities are available for everyone. The representative of this 
pillars are doctors, nurses, teachers, polices, artists… 
 
These six pillars are highly interconnected between each other. This means that it might happen 
that the Smart City will require coordinate efforts among its six pillars, which sometimes will 
overlap in their functions.  
 
5.3.2.1. 
Government 
 
A good government must be centered on the citizen. Moreover, it must create laws,

the appropriated environment for profit and sustainable development to flourish. Indeed, the 
2030 Agenda conceive a world in which “democracy, good governance and the rule of law, as

(health, infrastructure, education, water, and sanitation, etc.), protect the natural environment, 
and build a resilient society that can adapt to difficulties and achieve the Sustainable 
Development Goals.

Digital services 
 
Digital technologies are already changing the way citizens interact with each other and their 
environment. Therefore, it is not difficult to deduce that people expect to be able to 
communicate and perform digitally with governments and public services and get more effective 
results. 
 
E-Government means "Electronic Government" and involves everything from online public 
services to both directional communication channels with business, citizens, and its employees. 
It also involves innovation in the use of ICTs for more effective and efficient service delivery. 
Moreover, it includes a better answer to citizens and businesses' demand, an improvement in 
its performance by reducing monetary and time costs, and therefore, the better well-being of 
its citizens. This will result in more sustainable development,  and better use of the resources 
(United Nations, 2018a). Nevertheless, these improvements will add extra complexity to the 
government's tasks since the digitalization of its services must promote accountability, 
inclusivity, and transparency in public services. Therefore, governments must lead part of their 
efforts in restoring the trust of citizens in their performance. 
 
According to a survey conducted by the United Nations in 2018 about e-Government, the three 
most commonly used online services in that year were utility payment, submission of income 
taxes, and registration of new businesses. Furthermore, the use of e-mail, apps, feed updates, 
and SMS has doubled globally, especially in the education and health sectors (Department of 
Economic and Social Affairs, 2018). This remarks the importance to have a well-developed and 
continuously updated electronic headquarters, in where citizens and businesses can easily 
access to electronic services, administrative procedures, and public information. Denmark, 
Australia, and the Republic of Korea lead the e-Government rankings, followed by the United 
Kingdom, Sweden, Finland, Singapore, New Zealand, France, and Japan (United Nations, 2018c). 
 
As defined by the Governance Institute of Australia, the term governance “encompasses the 
system by which an organisation is controlled and operates, and the mechanisms by which it, 
and its people, are held to account. Ethics, risk management, compliance, and administration 
are all elements of governance" (Governance Institute of Australia, n.d.). Governance expands 
to e-Governance when it makes use of ICT for its different processes. E-Governance aims to 
enhance the quality of information and services delivered as well as improving transparency, 
ethics, citizen's participation, and reduce corruption levels in an economical, easy, and effective 
way. This will require the proper use of ICT, as well as re-thinking and re-engineering all the 
processes for better service delivery (Haque, Pathrannarakul, & Boonan, 2014).  
 
The concepts of e-Government and e-Governance are interrelated and share similar goals, as 
the achievement of administrative efficiency and good public services. Nevertheless, they are 
aimed for different purposes: e-Governance is focused on the improvement of the efficiency of 
affordable public services, as well as better communication and response to businesses and 
citizens inquiries. On the other hand, e-Government stresses the application of ICT for 
supporting these tasks (Marche & McNiven, 2009). Therefore, e-Government can be considered 
as a tool for good e-Governance. Thus, the use of the interconnection of both concepts will 
enhance governments' transparency and responsiveness to citizen's needs. Singapore is a good 
example of a country that is enhancing e-Government and e-Governance services. It has created 
the eCitizen Portal, from which citizens can access government information and services15.

Cybersecurity and data security 
 
Despite the several advantages of the implementation of ICT in public institutions, this also 
supposes different security threats. Online services are susceptible to suffer from cyber-attacks, 
which reveal the importance of protecting state security, citizens, and businesses data from 
malware. Therefore, governments should implement cybersecurity measures to ensure high-
security standards in all online public services and data storage centers. 
 
Moreover, the interconnections among different sensors and IoT devices are also susceptible to 
suffer cyber-attacks. IoT is especially sensitive to cybercrime due to the sometimes lack of a 
security system when they are manufactured. This could highly jeopardize governments and 
citizen's security if measures are not taken and implemented, as for example (Alcatel-Lucent, 
2018):  
 
• 
Authentication and authorization of users. 
• 
All the implemented devices must be authenticated and compliant with established 
security rules. 
• 
Blocking, limiting bandwidth, and controlling access to networks' applications. 
• 
Deep packet inspection and other technologies to detect the type of data and 
applications moving through the network.

empowered. For example, the Milton Keynes Council (United Kingdom) consults its citizens on 
what the town should look and feel like over the next thirty years. All citizens are welcomed to

disable and vulnerable people. Thus, an open government will help to restore the trust in public 
administrations and increase their credibility, which will, in turn, combat and mitigates 
corruption.

government does not only depend on the use of digital technologies, but also requires high 
levels of accountability, transparency, and the fostering of innovation and content creation. An 
good example of the provision of open data by governments is found in Denmark. The Danish

citizens.

mobility will foster the use of new energy sources for transport means.

their implementation presents some difficulties, such as an insufficient recharge network, long 
recharging times, and high costs in comparison with conventional vehicles. Therefore, in the

(especially relevant for taxis, buses, shared vehicles, as well as institutional cars).

5.3.2.3. 
Environmental sustainability 
 
The rapid urbanization of the urban areas and its unavoidable effects on climate change is 
defying cities with significant environmental challenges. Cities are considered as physical 
elements capable of transforming and modeling the natural environment in order to achieve 
humans settlement. This transformation sometimes implies the edification of invasive 
infrastructures, which generates a significant environmental impact (Fernández Güell & Collado 
Lara, 2014).  
 
The consumption of natural resources and energy, as well as emissions generated by the cities 
and their processes, are profoundly impacting the natural environment. It is estimated that cities 
consume more than two-thirds of the world's energy, and account for more than 70% percent 
of global CO2 emissions (C40 Cities, n.d.). This negatively impacts, not only on climate change, 
but also on air, water, soil and, green areas, etc., and thus to the livability of the city. 
With global urban population unstoppable increasement, these numbers are also expected to 
rise substantially. Furthermore, devasting consequences caused by climate change, as storms, 
flooding, droughts, etc., will cause unexpected expenditures, and therefore it will have 
economic effects on business and city budget (C40 Cities, n.d.). 
 
All these damaging problems must be addressed by those that are causing it: cities. This must 
be done not only for palliating the effects on global climate change, but also for improving cities 
livability and thus becoming a better place for citizens to live and prosper. Cities are potent 
entities capable of generating wealth and innovation; therefore, they also can work in 
environmental solutions that lower their carbon footprint, and thus for a better life quality for 
their citizens. 
 
For achieving these purposes, cities must focus on promoting urban environmental 
sustainability, which includes:  
 
• 
Adoption of measures for preventing environmental impact, including: 
 
- 
Energy efficiency for buildings and urban services. 
- 
Control and reduction of harmful emissions. 
 
• 
Management of services and environmental infrastructures, including: 
 
- 
Water management, in urban processes and services. 
- 
Urban waste management.

Building Management System (BMS): management and monitorization in real time of 
buildings, which allows storing data, management of alarms, visualization of the devices 
of each building, etc. 
• 
Energy Management System (EMS): a system for managing and analyzing the 
installation behavior that proposes new ways of performing more efficiently. It also 
storages and processes data from measurements and actions of the different devices 
and sensors from the installations.   
 
A sector that could benefit from these new ways of managing energy is public street lighting. 
The digitalization of street lights is about the adjustment of light intensity based on different 
parameters (presence, daylight intensity, etc.). They make use of multifunctional interactive 
systems that are capable of communicating with their surrounding environment. Therefore, 
these systems must be connected to an IoT network and the cloud for data storage.   
 
The street lighting of a Smart City must be integrated with smart sensors, cameras, etc. This will 
allow to report malfunctions automatically, and thus a dynamic, immediate, and interactive 
respond. Furthermore, the digitalization of streets lights will help to decrease energy and 
maintenance expenditures, lower error rates, control light levels remotely, etc. In turn, this will 
help to the reduction of light pollution, as well as it will provide with more efficient power 
consumption and generation, creating a  positive impact in the natural environment (Agoria 
Smart Cities, 2018). For example, the city of Tilburg (The Netherlands) has installed interactive 
street lamps, created by Philips. This light system is able of providing light on demand, based on 
the activity registered on the road. Thanks to it, energy savings up to 80% have been registered 
(Amsterdam Smart City, n.d.). Moreover, Copenhagen is already replacing its high-pressure 
sodium lamps with highly efficient LEDs. This will not only provide better light quality but also 
energy savings up to 65% (Itron, 2018). 
 
Buildings also have an improvement opportunity in the Smart City. Smart Buildings are defined 
as those buildings that deliver useful building services by the use of automated systems that 
control different functions (lighting, security, indoor climate monitoring, and other devices 
related to IoT)(Hussey, 2019). Those devices allow the sharing of information and data to 
optimize the building’s performance. This interaction between the building and the different 
operators, as well as with its inhabitants, will help the occupants to make more efficient and 
productive use of the consumption needs of their homes. In turn, this will lower the cost and 
the environmental impact of the building during its lifespan. 
 
Another initiative for fostering more sustainable use of the energy in the city is the 
implementation of Smart Grids. A Smart Grid is defined as an electricity network that efficiently 
integrates the behavior and actions of all users connected to it (generators,  and consumers).  It 
ensures an economically efficient and sustainable power system with low losses and high levels 
of quality, supply security,  and safety (EU Commission Task Force For Smart Grids Expert Group, 
2010).

Some of the several benefits and features of Smart Grids are (Shabanzadeh & Moghaddam, 
2013): 
 
• 
Collecting, measurement, and analysis of energy usage by enabling the sending of data 
back and forward over both directional communication network between smart meters. 
•

•

This technology is still being improved and developed. When it is fully implemented, it will 
deliver several benefits to homes, cities, and nations by reducing energy cost and the use of 
resources. Besides, Smart Grids will have a positive impact on the environment. They will foster 
the use of renewable energy sources and clean energy, which in turn will reduce pollution and 
help to mitigate climate change. 
 
One of the most significant issues that Smart Cities will face is how to power their communities 
and buildings more efficiently and effectively, without contributing to increase harmful 
emissions. Thanks to the lower cost of renewable technologies, and according to a UN report, 
the global investment in renewable energies has surpassed the investment in fossil fuels 
(Molloy, 2018).  
 
Solar, wind, hydraulic, and bioenergy power (among others) have a low contribution to global 
warming. Moreover, these new ways of generating energy will create economic opportunities 
for businesses and citizens. 
 
Rooftop solar energy can help households to become self-sustaining. Besides, the super plus of 
energy generated can be stored and used later or sold back to the grid (Molloy, 2018). Smart 
solar parks distributed across the city are another green energy initiative, which can help to 
power solar street lighting, water heaters, pumps, traffic signals, etc. (Sarawgi, 2018).  
 
The implementation and integration of renewable energies and energy conservation systems in 
buildings can help the Smart City to become greener and more energy efficient. Moreover,  it 
will help to reduce some monetary and environmental costs derivate from the use of fossil-fuel 
energies. 
 
 
Control and reduction of harmful emissions 
 
Smart Cities must contribute to the reduction of atmospheric emissions, such as greenhouse 
gases. Traffic is one of the leading causes of these phenomena, which negatively impacts on 
human health and well-being, as well as it contributes to global CO2 emissions. Therefore, it is 
very linked to the mobility pillar, since transportation accounts for 15% of the greenhouse gases, 
being the second most impactful factor after electricity and heat (31%). This percentage is 
expected to dramatically rise due to the rapid urbanization of the cities (WRI, 2017). Besides, 
pollution caused by the emission of harmful gasses is causing that about 95% of the world 
==PAGE:81==
 
81 
population is breathing dirty air (The Guardian, 2018), which has devasting consequences as 
respiratory illness, cancer, etc. 
 
The burning of solid fuel or biomass as coal in homes for cooking and heating is also contributing 
to the production of harmful emissions, as well as the use of non-renewable energies by 
factories.  
 
For Smart Cities to palliate the emissions of greenhouse gases, and therefore to contribute to a 
more sustainable and livable city, there are some factors that they must have in consideration. 
Again, IoT can be used as a tool for monitoring and controlling these emissions. For example, 
Siemens has developed a smart software that can predict the air pollution level five days in 
advance in cities by connecting to the IoT. This will allow cities to take preventive measures for 
reducing harmful emissions and atmospheric particulate matters (Smart City Press, 2018). 
Moreover, cities as Chicago, Oklahoma, and Barcelona are using sensors connected to the IoT 
network, which provide data and information on air quality and pollutants in real time (Smart 
City Press, 2018). 
 
Another good initiative related to IoT is the implementation of an urban management platform 
where data can be centralized and distributed for its later use. These type of software are 
beneficial for determining the correlation between the different factors that cause air pollution 
(as vehicle traffic increase and a higher quantity of fine suspended particles). As the solution 
created by Siemens, urban management platforms also can predict the frequency in which 
emissions are going to be generated. Air purifier devices and mobile apps for providing real-time 
data are also good solutions to these problems (Ikusi Spider, n.d.).  
 
Solutions regarding the reduction of harmful emissions do not have to necessary means the use 
of technology. Education plays an important role as well, since raising awareness about how 
citizen's action impact on pollution and climate change is crucial for addressing and solving these 
problems. 
 
Urban waste management 
 
Solid waste landfills in cities are contributing to air, water, and soil pollution, which in turn 
deteriorates human health. Thus, one of the main challenges that the cities are going to face is 
the increasement of waste derivate from overpopulation. Therefore, urban waste management 
is going to become an essential city service, which entails the collection, transport, and 
treatment of residues. Thanks to technological advances, there are several new ways of 
improving the quality of waste collection services. The implementation of tools that make use 
of IoT systems for managing and controlling waste generated in cities is going to contribute to 
fostering sustainability and reducing costs. A good example of this is the installation of Smart 
Bins, which some countries, like Singapore and Seoul, are already implementing (Boh, 2016). 
These smart bins are solar powered and equipped with internet hotspots and fill-level sensors. 
The sensors ensure that the waste is only collected when the bin is completely filled. This will 
highly contribute to the reduction of traffic congestion caused by waste recollection trucks, 
resulting in cleaner and safer cities. It will also help to reduce the emission of CO2 and other 
harmful gases (Gelsin, 2017). 
 
Other solutions do not require the help of technology. Cities as Barcelona are directing their 
effort to foster home-based recycling, which encourages citizens to take part in the recycling 
processes through educational activities (Ajuntament de Barcelona, n.d.). Moreover, Seattle is 
implementing mandatory commercial recycling rules for achieving the recycled of 70% of 
==PAGE:82==
 
82 
municipal solid waste by 2022. Also, Seattle is conducting several educational programs for 
teaching about the importance of reducing waste production (US EPA, 2018). 
 
Another initiative that must be taken into consideration is the implementation of circular 
economy models, which will be explorer later in this chapter. 
 
In brief, the implementation of measures and initiatives for palliating the increase in waste and 
its proper management will not only solve logistic problems. Moreover, it will decrease cost 
expenditures, reduce the emissions of damaging gases, and the contamination of water and soil. 
 
Water management  
 
The UN has predicted that the world could face a 40% shortfall in water availability by 2030 
(United Nations, 2016). In addition to this circumstance, cities are facing a growing demand for 
water and sanitation services caused by rapid urbanization (WWAP, 2015). Incorrect 
management of this situation could lead to the over-exploitation of water resources. Therefore, 
cities must work on providing a sustainable management of water and sanitation, as well as they 
must ensure the availability of clean water for everyone. 
 
More specifically, Smart Cities must start working with the following points: 
 
• 
Smart tele-management of parks and garden irrigation systems: smart tele-
management systems uses IoT to automate water management. This results in better 
use of the water resources and a reduction of environmental impact. Smart tele-
management systems are formed by different interconnected sensors that can 
exchange data and remotely communicate with irrigation systems. This will help 
municipalities and businesses to make better use and control of the water systems, as 
well as to reduce unnecessary water waste (Cisco Jasper, n.d.).  
The city of Barcelona is already implementing smart tele-management watering systems 
that allow the control of irrigation. The sensors can gather information about humidity, 
salinity, temperature, wind, etc., and automatically regulate the amount of water. This 
can be controlled by using a computer, a smartphone, or a tablet (Logitek, 2014).  
• 
Smart networks (smart meters): the utilization of smart meters give institutions and 
citizens the chance of accessing to real-time information consumption patterns (stored 
in the cloud). This will allow them to make more straightforward information and data 
analysis, which will, in turn, result in better water management and resource allocation. 
Smart water meters, powered by IoT systems, also provide information about leakages 
and breakdowns. This is very beneficial since it allows to prevent problems or to fix them 
before they become more extensive and expensive (Sigfox, n.d.). San Francisco has 
already implemented smart water meters in more than 96% of the 178.000 water 
accounts. Information and data related to water consumption are stored in a cloud and 
accessible through the web for further monitoring and leakage control (Brears, 2018). 
• 
Use of reclaimed water: the recycled and the reuse of wastewater might be a good 
option for increasing the availability of water, preventing water pollution, and thus 
contributing to a more sustainable water system. Water reuse consists of the 
reclamation, treatment and, recycling of waste-water or rainwater collected from 
homes, commercial buildings, and industries (LeChevallier, n.d.).  
A possible solution for treating residual water is the use of decentralized wastewater 
treatment systems. This technology can treat domestic and industrial sewage water. 
The collection and treatment of wastewater by using this system will allow its later 
redistribution to homes and industries, which will reduce transportation cost and help 
==PAGE:83==
 
83 
to decrease water wastage (Lenger, 2019). Moreover, educational programs and 
environmental awareness are vital for reducing water consumption in homes. Citizens 
must understand the economic, societal, and environmental problems that 
overconsumption of water cause and be part of the solution by controlling their use of 
the water. 
• 
Energy generation from wastewater: related to the use of reclaimed water is the 
wastewater treatment plants. These industrial plants need the use of substantial energy 
amounts for performing, which generates greenhouse emissions. However, they also 
have the potential to be sources of renewable energy. For example, the city of 
Vancouver (Canada) use sewage water to generate energy on-site, producing both heat 
and electricity. This energy is lately used for helping to operate the wastewater 
treatment plant. Such an innovative measure can reduce operating cost, energy 
consumption, and greenhouse gas emissions (MetroVancouver, n.d.).  
 
In brief, proper urban water management might be a complicated task that requires, besides 
smart meters and water management, the reduction and control of wastewater. For achieving 
this, it is needed the coordination across the different city stakeholders (governments, 
industries, and citizens) for more equitable, efficient, and sustainable use of the water 
resources. 
 
Sustainable management and planning 
 
Rapid urbanization, resource scarcity, high pollution levels, etc. are seriously harassing the 
environment. Therefore, Smart Cities must respond to these challenges for achieving 
sustainable development.  One possible approach is sustainable resource management policies 
that foster the use of available resources without depleting the  source, and thus preserving 
them for future generations. This is especially relevant for the development of the agriculture 
sector, which have a particular interest in overcoming the current resource scarcity. Another 
essential aspect to take into consideration is sustainable urban planning. It involves different 
disciplines such as architecture, material sciences, technology, transportation, etc. (University 
of Texas, 2016). Due to rapid urbanization, addressing sustainable urban planning is crucial for 
creating a sustainable community. Some of the solutions that Smart Cities must undertake 
include green and smart buildings, open spaces, promoting biking, new and smart 
transportation means, and the use of alternative energy sources. An excellent example of 
efficient urban planning is found in Quarter Vauban, a former French military base placed in 
Freiburg (Germany). This base was completely renewed and converted into a residential area, 
built-on sustainable and eco-friendly principles23.  
 
Moreover, Smart Cities must foster the development and implementation of low carbon 
economies (economies which a minimal output of greenhouse gas emissions)24. This type of 
economies will not only reduce pollution levels and the use of non-renewable resources. If the 
right policies are appropriately implemented, they could maximize economic profit due to the 
significant reduction of operational costs associated with extracting and transporting coal and 
gas (Nelson, Zuckerman, Hervé-Mignucci, Goggins, & Szambelan, 2014).  
 
The development of these strategies by governments and businesses will ensure the livability 
and sustainability of the cities in the long term.

5.3.2.4. 
Life quality 
 
The transformation process of the Smart City must be focused on the increment of the life 
quality of the citizens, on better access to services, education, and new opportunities for 
economic development. Therefore, for achieving life quality, based on a holistic conception of 
the city, the next requirements are needed (Fernández Güell & Collado Lara, 2014): 
 
• 
To balance the interest of the operators of technological and digital platforms with the 
real necessities of the citizens. 
• 
To orientate smart initiatives to people, having citizen well-being as the main objective, 
and use technology as a mean for achieving it. 
• 
Smart initiatives must be based on a vision of the future, shared with public institutions 
and citizens. 
• 
A Smart City must not only be innovative, technological, and sustainable; the 
intelligence of a city also concerns inclusiveness, participation, and interconnectedness. 
 
In brief, the life quality pillar of a Smart City is the one concerning the improvement of the living 
conditions of the citizens and the development of the services that the city offers to them. The 
main challenges that this pillar will face and solve are housing, health, safeness,  and social 
cohesion. For successfully surpassing these challenges, the life quality pillar must be closely 
related to the rest of the pillars, since they also contribute to the well-being and comfort of the 
citizens. Therefore, the investment in the development of Smart City's pillars will help to 
increase the satisfaction of the citizens. 
 
Universal accessibility 
 
An essential factor for the proper development of the life quality pillar is the so-called universal 
accessibility. This is the capacity of a city of providing efficient services to the citizens without 
any distinction, despite their capacities (Fernández Vidal & Muñoz del Castillo, 2015). By 
accessibility, it is mean not only physical accessibility for elderlies or people with any disability, 
but also to all services and facilities that technology is bringing to cities. As an example of this, 
Singapore’s government has created the Enabling Village25, an entire community designed for 
disabled people, which include accessible residence, accessible shopping, etc. 
 
Universal accessibility must follow the philosophy of the Design for All strategy. Design for All 
has the goal of designing products and services that can be used for the biggest number of user 
possible. It considers the full range of human abilities, and work for making a better and more 
comfortable life for everyone, independently of their age, gender, incomes, or capacities 
(Stephanidis, n.d.).

5.3.2.5. 
Intellectual capital 
 
The capacity of the inhabitants of a city of adapting to continuous changes and to the urban 
transformation of a Smart City is an essential and critical pillar of it. The competitive advantages 
of the city progress depend on the development of learning, researching, and innovation skills, 
even more than access to infrastructures and raw materials (Palacios & Galvan, 2008).  
 
The intellectual capital of a Smart City is based on the educational level of the citizens and their 
creative and innovative skills. This includes their capacities for using technological tools, as well 
as their flexibility for adapting to rapid and disruptive changes produced by the exponential 
development of technologies. These skills are also needed for having the necessary digital and 
knowledge competencies for accessing the services offered by the Smart City, and for 
participating in its development. 
 
The development of intellectual capital is the driver of the creation of Smart Citizens. The 
following skills characterize them: 
 
• 
Multiculturalism and cosmopolitanism: citizens must be open-minded and promote an 
immigration-friendly environment. Without cultural diversity, some of the catalysts of 
economic growth that make a city competitive and advanced (innovation, 
entrepreneurship, or technological advancements) would not be possible. Moreover, 
the attraction and retention of international talent and investments will, in turn, 
positively impact economic growth. 
• 
Political empowerment: the intellectual capital pillar must empower citizens to raise 
their voices and take part in political processes that concern the development of the city 
where they are living. This, alongside responsible decision making, will engage citizens, 
resulting in a more open, transparent, and participatory urban system (Ferrer, 2017).  
• 
Complex critical thinking: analytical, open-minded, communication, creativity and 
problem-solving skills (Doyle, 2019).  
• 
Creative problem solving: solving problems and identifying opportunities by using 
innovative and creative perspectives (Mind Tools, n.d.).  
• 
Social intelligence: effective listening skills, knowledge of social rules, verbal fluency, 
and conversational skills (Riggio, 2014). 
• 
Emotional intelligence: the ability to understand personal and others emotions, as well 
as controlling personal ones (Song et al., 2010). 
 
The intellectual capital and the education level of the citizens are a sign of the level of smartness 
of a city. Indeed, more significant rates of urban development are achieved by cities with large 
percentages of qualified workers (Berry & Glaeser, 2005). Furthermore, the citizens capacity for 
learning, adapting, and innovating is related to their ability to obtain benefit from technologies 
used by the Smart City (Coe et al., 2001), since they are familiar with the use of  ICT and new 
technologies. For this matter, a quality, life-long education is especially relevant, which is going 
to be the tool that allows citizens transformation to be achievable.

Circular economy 
 
These new ways of doing businesses must take into consideration the implementation of circular 
economy principles into their policies. Thus, businesses must re-think how they manage 
resources. In turn, this will create economic, environmental, and social benefits. As defined by 
the Ellen Macarthur Foundation, “a circular economy is characterized as an economy that is 
restorative and regenerative by design and aims to keep products, components, and materials 
at their highest utility and value at all times, whilst distinguishing between technical and 
biological cycles” (Ashima, Julia, Kate Brandt, & robin Yeoman, 2017). Circular economy provides 
to businesses and policymakers an actuation framework for re-considering their use and 
managing of natural resources for creating value, as well as the after-life of the product. Thus, 
the economic development pillar of a Smart City must work on generating economic growth and 
prosperity based on a low carbon economy. Value creation must be decoupled from the 
consumption of finite resources, which will, in turn, contribute to the reduction of waste 
(Ashima et al., 2017), and empowered by governments.

A good example of businesses applying this economic model is VIGGA29, a Danish company who

Entrepreneurship and innovation  
 
A Smart City identifies itself with the city of the future and as an innovation environment. 
Therefore, the creation of a Smart City will require a robust ecosystem of entrepreneurs based 
on innovation. It must also take advantage of all the information generated by the massive 
deployment of technology in the urban space, and by the city itself, its processes, services, and 
people. 
 
Governments and businesses should work for making available an entrepreneurship ecosystem, 
involving infrastructures, funding, services, formation, etc. Open data is one of the critical 
resources for fostering innovative and entrepreneurial initiatives, which in turn will create social 
value. 
 
Moreover, hackathons, partnership with governments, smart procurement policies, or public 
funds are needed for supporting and improving Smart Cities initiatives (Eden Strategy Institute, 
2018). This attracts star-ups and citizens to participate in the creation process of the future of 
their city, as well as it contributes to the creation of innovative ideas and proposals. 
 
Smart City Hubs are also a useful tool for achieving Smart Cities' goals. Smart hubs will support 
networking and collaboration in specific cases related to the sustainable development of the 
city, which will require the collaboration of citizens, businesses and governments (Lyngby-
Taarbaek City of Knowledge, n.d.). Lyngby-Taarbaek is a good example for illustrating this. They, 
in collaboration with the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and several Danish companies 
and international companies, have created a Smart City Hub for making this municipality as 
“smart” as possible (Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge, n.d.). 
 
In brief, the economic development pillar must contribute to the Smart City by: 
 
• 
The use of network infrastructure (ICT), not only for better economic 
development but also for achieving political efficiency and cultural, social, and

• 
Use of new economic models for urban development that promotes a low

Engagement of businesses with the Sustainable Development Goals 
 
To the use of low carbon and circular economic models, it must be added the engage of 
businesses with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Engaging with SDGs represent 
growth and wealth opportunities for businesses, as well as some significant challenges. The 
agenda 2030 concerns a wide variety of social, environmental, and economic topics. Therefore, 
how to start implementing SDGs in businesses strategies and how to use them as an actuation 
framework might not be easy at the beginning. In any case, the effective integration of SDGs 
into the businesses' backbone will report several benefits to companies. Indeed, by 
incorporating SDGs into their businesses strategy, the private sector could achieve globally to 
unlock 12 trillion of US dollars by 2030, and create 380 millions of new job positions (about 90%

between aspiration and action, which is locking several economic and social benefits.  
 
Businesses must start considering SDGs as a business's philosophy and working framework. This 
can generate a positive impact. Consumers today expect companies to do more than only 
delivering a product or a service; they want them to create social value. Therefore, businesses 
need to re-think about their supply chain and their strategy.

5.3.3. The Smart City pillars and the Sustainable Development Goals 
 
The development of the Smart City must be guided and based on the premises covered by the 
Agenda 2030 and the Sustainable Development Goals. These goals were made to assure a more 
sustainable world for future generations, in where everyone has access to primary resources, 
same opportunities despite their capacities, humans right are fulfilled, and climate change is 
fought. 
 
Therefore, the six pillars of the Smart City must be oriented to the achievement of Sustainable 
Development Goals. 
 
Government 
 
As stated in The 2030 Agenda, Sustainable Development Goals are aspirational and global. 
Therefore, each government must decide how to implement SDGS in their national strategies. 
They must also establish their own targets, guided by the level of ambition set by the United

Nations, but always in concordance with their national circumstances and human rights (United 
Nations, 2018b). Thus, national policies and plans must lead their effort to express SDGs 
objectives coherently at the national level (United Nations, 2018c).  
 
A good government is mostly linked to Goal 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions) and Goal 
17 (Partnerships for the Goals).

Goal 16 stress the role of governments of fostering peaceful and inclusive societies, provide 
access to justice for all and build effective, inclusive, and accountable institutions at all levels. 
Furthermore, this goal stands out for reducing corruption levels, ensure public access to 
information, and protect fundamental freedoms. According to Goal 16, governments should 
work to strengthen the participation of developing countries in the institutions of global 
governance, as well as promoting policies for enhancing sustainable development (United

For achieving not only all the points of Goal 16, but also for fulfilling the objectives of the other

In any case, Goal 16 and Goal 17 underpin the achievement of all the other Sustainable

6. A conceptual model for the Smart City 
 
6.1. A human-centered smart city 
 
This section will address what the role of people in the framework formed by Smart Cities, the 
megatrends, and the 4th Industrial Revolution as well as their consequent challenges and 
difficulties is.   
 
The answer might seems logic: the center. However, why? This other answer seems to be very 
logic still: because the risk of dehumanization is exceptionally high, because it is an agreed 
principle from the World Summit on the Information Society in the section A.1 (World Summit 
on the Information Society, 2003), and finally because technology is simply a mean for the 
development of the society.  
 
Nevertheless, if technology (in the framework of the 4th Industrial Revolution) is what is going 
to characterize Smart Cities principally, then it must be used as a tool through which society will 
develop all the aspects of personal and social life: work, leisure, family, learning, health, etc.   
 
The risk of humans ending up merging with the machines is real. For limiting this risk, it is crucial 
that cities develop themselves as "humanizer" places that counteract the technology invasion. 
A right way of achieving this is by making nature burst in cities. For example, there is a movement 
that advocates for, among other actions, covering the facades of the buildings, rooftops, and 
balconies, with plants that cover all these spaces (Vilches, Toscano, Pérez Gil, & Macías, 2014). 
This will have multiple benefits as well as it is aligned with the Sustainable Development Goal nº 
11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities)(United Nations, 2015a). More specifically, with the 
target 11.3, which talks about the need for achieving urbanization processes of the cities that 
are inclusive and sustainable, and ultimately, more human. 
 
Hence, the center must always be on the people and the environment, as the primary tool for 
helping the maintenance of a sustainable city in optimal conditions. 
 
Disruptive technology can not only cause dehumanization but also, as one of its more dangerous 
consequences, it can facilitate the interested control of people (manipulation): those are the 
risks of the Big Data (Olier, 2019), artificial intelligence (Belfield, n.d.), fake news, etc.  
 
For palliating these dangers, there is no other choice but to empower people by creating a new 
citizen profile: the i-Learner, which is advocated in this project. The i-Learner is a connected 
person, politically participative and socially engaged, critic, and aware of the risks and 
opportunities of the world around him/her, and to which him/her must adapt. This profile of 
the i-Learner must be the engine of change towards a more human world. A world where society 
know how to take advantage of the several benefits offered by the technology and know how 
to protect themselves from the dangers that it also entails.    
 
The tool for achieving this new profile can only be an adapted education to the technological 
times that society is facing. Education must also be of high quality, inclusive, personalized, critic, 
and that develop those people's capabilities that are very difficult to emulate with a machine. 
 
Therefore, in the center, together with the person, it also has to be the education, which must 
adapt to nowadays uncertainty and changing world.

    "The i-Learner is defined as 'Basic Social System', with capacity for self-organization, which 
contemplates and assumes personal learning 'in situ' (self-managed, social, and collaborative), 
as a permanent strategy of adaptation, and personal and citizen development, to a changing 
environment, which is interdependent, diverse and unpredictable." (Rubio Royo, 2011) 
 
The i-Learner involves a series of characteristics and requirements, of which the following are 
highlighted (Rubio Royo, 2011): 
 
- All human beings, individually or in the different social forms in which they are organized 
(group, community, organization, society) constitute complex adaptive systems, 
interdependent and "apprentice". 
 
- Adoption of new approaches and attitudes for knowing and being in the uncertain and 
complex nowadays world. 
 
- Almost all knowledge is on the Internet network, within which people are linked with 
ideas, concepts, objects, sources of information, etc. 
==PAGE:103==
 
103 
 
- Permanent self-learning is absolutely necessary for this profile of citizen to arise, and it is 
also the strategy of adaptation to nowadays world.  
 
- Social and collaborative learning emerges as exponential learning, and therefore, it is 
much more than the sum of individual learning (collective intelligence). 
 
- This whole system leads to personal and collective empowerment. 
 
- The need for new network structures that allow the creation of new spaces of knowledge 
and learning for generating shared value. 
 
The aim of these characteristics is the integral development of the person, who learns to be in 
this world in transformation. This is a bottom-up approach, which assumes that this integral 
development will not only change the person, but the group, and society in general, and 
therefore, the cities (organic development)32. 
 
This proposal has several components to analyze (Rubio Royo, 2011): 
 
1. The context of the proposal. 
2. The profile of the i-Learner. 
3. The methodology to be used to achieve proposal 
 
6.3.1. The context of the proposal: a new vital space, expanded and complex 
 
This space is the Internet, which is called the new vital space, expanded and complex, and all 
the associated technologies (mobile phones, for example) that it entails, which turns cities into 
connected and Smart Cities. 
 
The Internet is the context that allows the i-Learner to integrally develop. The Internet is 
immense in information, content, etc., which properly selected suppose an unprecedented 
wealth of knowledge. 
 
However, for "learning to know and to be" in this environment, there is a series of competencies 
that the i-Learner must necessarily acquire, which are explained in the following section.

6.3.3. Methodology to be used to achieve the objectives of the proposal 
 
This methodology is composed of the competences that have been analyzed before, the 
tripolarity of knowledge, the development of learning on the Internet, and the development of 
digital identity. 
 
6.3.3.1. 
The tripolarity of knowledge 
 
The i-Learner starts with data and information, etc., (K-Knowledge), and through the 
competencies acquired, learning is produced (L-Learning). Innovation occurs after a process of 
reflection and internalization, based on the understanding of how the person has learned: to 
understand personal learning processes (I-Innovation).

6.3.3.2. 
Development of learning on the Internet 
 
The way to deal with the immense information that exists on the Internet is reproduced in the 
image that describes the process called KMP: Knowledge Management Process. 
 
This management is carried out through the following tools: select the information, validate it, 
filter it, and classify it.

Once this is done, this information is shared with groups of people who constitute a thematic 
network, which validates the information again, giving it an added value that enriches it. 
 
This is a cyclic process which generates cognitive learning in the i-Learner and, at the same time, 
generates social learning. 
 
6.3.3.3. 
Digital Identity 
 
The development of the digital identity of the i-Learner is the fruit of the previous steps. From 
being an anonymous i-Learner (the result of obtaining competencies) to gaining visibility (the 
result of actively participating in thematic networks, which allows others to know about it). The 
next steps are recognition (the result of the evaluation of previous participation), and credibility 
and trust (when its contribution is recognized and respected by the community) and finally 
leadership (Rubio Royo, 2011).

7.3. The role of the Smart City’s pillars in responding to the global 
context 
 
The five megatrends are going to be the drivers of all the changes and challenges that the 4th  
Industrial Revolution is going to bring or is already bringing. All these aspects of the new era are 
going to profoundly and dynamically interact together, which will generate several disruptive 
situations that are going to challenge cities, their rulers, and their citizens.   
 
Due to its adaptiveness and hyper-connectivity, this whole ecosystem must be treated and study 
together. As a dynamic complex system, the capacity of the different cities' pillars of adapting, 
modifying their individual and collective behavior, and evolving is going to be very important for 
cities to successfully overcome social, economic, and governmental difficulties in such a 
changing environment, from where they must emerge stronger and reinforced.    
 
The parameters of this new situation might be unstable, unpredictable, and relatively unique, 
which is why there is not a single solution. Therefore a deep reflection about what is happening 
and its consequences are highly needed as well as big doses of innovation, creativity, and design 
thinking. 
 
This section aims to explain some of the measures that Smart City must conduct in order to 
react to these disruptive changes and create growth opportunities. 
 
For a visual representation of the global context and its related challenges, see Appendixes 
13.3 and 13.4. 
 
Innovation and Productivity 
 
Resource scarcity and social awareness about climate change are challenging businesses. They 
will have to re-think and re-structure the life cycle assessment of their products or services for 
being more sustainable. From the environmental sustainability pillar of the Smart City and in 
relation with the economic development pillar, measures should be taken for implementing 
business practices that take into consideration the re-manufacturing of products or their 
components after their life use, as well as the recycling 
 of materials.  
 
Moreover, global competition is creating an entrepreneurship ecosystem driven by innovation 
and creativity. This is, in turn, creating new markets and new ways of doing businesses, which 
will need not only investment but also the right skills for achieving successful results.  
 
Therefore, the economic development and the intellectual capital pillars must act together for 
converting this new business trend into a growth opportunity for the city. For doing this, 
universities and research labs should foster idea generation and support entrepreneurs with 
guidance (during the development of their business and also through licensing, etc.) and 
==PAGE:112==
 
112 
economic support. Regarding economic support, angel investors, large companies, etc. have to 
be willing to collaborate with these new business models by providing funding. This will also 
return to them with economic benefits since they will become part of the new business. 
 
Moreover, policies that help to protect intellectual property are needed in order to protect 
entrepreneurship. The government must be in charge of this; plus, they must take part in co-
funding initiatives for helping investors to engage with start-ups economically, as well as create 
Smart Hubs, incubators, and accelerator programs 
 
Regarding the role of intellectual capital in this matter, a new set of skills is going to be needed 
in order to prepare citizens to become entrepreneurs. Pitching, inspirational leadership, 
complex problem solving, etc. are going to be critical skills for becoming an entrepreneur and 
creating a successful business. Not less important is the creation of a culture that tolerates 
failure. Creating a star-up is not only difficult and exhausting; it might happen that despite all 
the efforts, the businesses do not succeed. Therefore, being prepared for possible failure and 
yet take the experience and the process as a positive outcome is another needed skill. 
 
Furthermore, IoT and mobile devices have made people lives more comfortable, bringing several 
opportunities and economic growth, but at the same time it has brought some significant 
threats. Hyperconnectivity has made personal identity susceptible of being robbed, and thus 
personal data. Therefore, it is governments tasks to train their employees in how to spot 
potential threats and security failures so action can be taken immediately. Back-up files must 
also be kept and regularly updated, as well as high-security standards for protecting online 
services a data storages centers (encryption of the webpage, multifactor identification, own 
server, blockchain, biometrics…) 
 
These threats also concern the economic development sector. Besides training employees, 
biometric information (e.g. fingerprints) for identification and authentication are useful tools for 
securing data. 
 
Citizens must also be aware of the digital threats that they must also suffer. For avoiding identity 
and personal data theft, citizens must have the capacity of identifying potential threats. Another 
challenge related to the cyberspace is the proliferation of the so-called "Fake News". This 
propaganda consists of delivering misinformation and hoaxes spread by digital media to mislead 
public opinion or damage an entity, company, or a person. Therefore, it is part of the intellectual 
capital pillar to train citizens to have the capacity of discerning between what is real news and 
what is fake, as well as knowing how to distinguish reliable sources and double check 
information that they receive. 
 
Another concerning issue is advanced weaponry powered by Artificial Intelligence. It is not 
entirely developed yet, but its consequences could be immensely destructive. Therefore, 
governments must be flexible and be prepared for any unexpected situation that could 
potentially or directly threat national and international security. Strong leaders and complex 
problem-solving skills will be highly needed for developing solutions and strategies for facing 
new conflicts. 
 
Inequality 
 
New digital markets, income disparity, and unemployment rates are making an inequality feeling 
to grow. This is causing growing concern about fairness and thus, political and social tension.  
The Smart City must react to these challenges proactively and inclusively by creating solutions 
that foster inclusiveness, equity, and fairness. 
==PAGE:113==
 
113 
 
From the Life Quality pillar, there are various tasks that must be done for ensuring that the 
inequality challenge is adequately addressed and positively overcame. The Smart City must 
ensure the personal and economic safeness of all its citizens. It must have effective, accountable 
and inclusive institutions that protect and ensure citizen's rights. Social protection is another 
critical factor. Retirement security for the elderlies, work opportunities for the youth, and 
broader financial well-being for everyone must be fostered. Besides, for reducing inequality, a 
better use of land and more accessible housing is needed, as well as better distribution and 
more equative access to resources (education, legal and social services…). Moreover, the digital 
era and the digitalization of procedures and services by the use of new technologies are a very 
useful tool for democratizing the access to resources and services (online education, online 
municipal services…). 
 
Reducing inequalities is directly linked to increase equity. A non-discriminatory society is crucial 
for achieving it. This means that the Smart City must promote a society in which everyone, no 
matter race, gender, sexual orientation, ethnicity, age, disabilities etc. have equal opportunities 
and rights.  
 
The economic development and government pillar also have an essential role in the inequality 
issue. These pillars must understand the demographic change, business disruption, and the new 
skills needed. They must work on adequate approaches for fostering job creation, and thus, 
economic growth. Fostering collaborative business and economic models, Smart Hubs, start-ups 
incubators, partnerships with innovators, and encouraging work scalability are some of the 
initiatives that the economic development and the government pillar must implement, as well 
as policies that ensure greater protection for employees. Besides, in order to restore political 
trust and calm down social and political tension, governments must be open and transparent. 
This can be achieved by allowing the access to governmental data through digital services 
(municipal budget, contracts, debts, official documents...), promoting citizen participation, and 
having a clear and public ethics code. 
 
Agile Technological Governance 
 
Agile technological governance implies the use of new technologies for delivering public services 
and achieving better governance. For this purpose, governments should acquire the needed 
digital tools. This is going to suppose an initial investment in modern ICT infrastructure for the 
development of high quality and secure online services. This mainly includes the digitalization 
of the government's headquarters, and public administration services (utility payment, 
submission of income taxes, registration of new businesses...).  
 
The use of new digital technologies will enhance accessibility and transparency levels of public 
institutions. Nevertheless, the use of online services always implies cyber-risks. Thus 
cybersecurity measures should be taken by governments (multifactor authentication, own 
server, blockchain, biometrics…) in order to ensure the safety of digital services and the personal 
data of citizen. Moreover, governments should develop innovative governance protocols to 
keep pace with technological development. 
 
Business disruption 
 
Digital communication tools are fostering new ways of doing businesses. This is disrupting 
businesses that are anchored in old ways of delivering value. This challenge could either be an 
opportunity for evolving or a threat. 
==PAGE:114==
 
114 
 
Therefore, the economic development pillar must foster new ways of making business through 
the use of ICT and Internet platforms. For achieving this, businesses that want to be part of the 
digital economy must develop their own e-business infrastructure (hardware, software, digital 
networks…) and e-commerce (digital transfer of goods). 
 
New ways or relating to customers are also needed for overcoming these challenges. Online 
communication channels, as the use of social media (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter…) are being 
demanded by clients. Moreover, it is a more natural way of getting feedback about products 
and services. 
 
Also, the capability of companies to adapt to unexpected and disruptive changes and turn them 
into growth opportunities is going to be critical for succeeding. Cross-functional teams, 
experiment with alternative solutions, strong leadership,  open communication philosophy, 
transparency, use of digital tools, etc. are some examples of what measures the economic 
development pillar should start implementing. Besides, the collaboration within business and 
government could foster new ways of financing the digital tools needed for leaping to the digital 
economy. 
 
Disruption of jobs and skills 
 
Rapid technological development is not only disrupting jobs but also the skills needed for 
performing them. This situation, if it is not properly managed, could lead to growing 
unemployment rates, which in turn will also put pensions and healthcare systems under 
pressure. 
 
Therefore, workers need to be repurposed and retrained in new skills, which will be mostly 
technological and digital skills. The representatives of the economic development pillar should 
be in charge of conducting workshops, ICT formation course, etc. in order to retrain workers 
with the needed skills for performing. 
 
From the intellectual capital pillar, new intellectual skills that robots and automated systems are 
not able to perform should be taught and encouraged. Complex critical thinking, creative 
problem solving, social and emotional intelligence, entrepreneurial spirit, responsible decision 
making, etc. should be fostered from schools, universities, and research centers. A good way of 
doing this is by conducting courses, talks, hackathons, idea competitions, the inclusion of these 
skills in schools' curriculums, etc. 
 
Another needed skill is the capability of being flexible in regards of changing job position, even 
if it is in a different field or abroad to work. For encouraging this, economic security during labor 
stoppage must be ensured, as well as the availability of several job pools. Representatives from 
the mobility pillar also have an essential role in this matter. A well-connected transport system, 
accessible and affordable to all, will help to the displacement of workers looking for new 
opportunities. 
 
Ethics and Identity  
 
Robotics and artificial intelligence are creating machines that have human-like capacities. This 
is creating moral and ethical issues that are harassing human identity.  
Representatives from the intellectual capital and the life quality pillar will play an essential role 
in overcoming these challenges. 
==PAGE:115==
 
115 
 
Art and culture (theaters, music halls, art schools, artistic university studies…) accessible for all 
must be fostered in order to enhance these social skills that so far can not be imitated by 
machines.  
 
Moreover, the life quality pillar must ensure personal and economic safeness in order to fight 
the fear of substitution of workers for robots and machines. Since this fear is real, it must be 
ensured social protection for all citizens. This must include retirement security for the elderly, 
work opportunities for the youth, broader financial well-being for everyone, and teaching 
students and future workers, as well as retaining current workers, with new technological skills.  
 
Fusing Technologies 
 
New digital systems and processing powers, sensors, etc. are bringing several benefits to 
industries, and thus economic growth. These technological advancements would not have been 
possible without the integration of research disciplines (e.g.: mechatronics is a technology that 
combines electronic and mechanical engineering).  
 
This is going to challenge educational institutions and jobs.  The intellectual capital pillar must 
foster broader knowledge about different study disciplines that are interlinked between them. 
Besides, high-level research centers must be created and enhanced for promoting knowledge 
creation. For achieving this, it will be needed the use of ICT tools and hyper-connected spaces 
with high technological deployment for fostering education institutions, highly qualified 
students, and workforce. 
 
Besides, the willingness of continuously learning, including informal education ways, must also 
be encouraged for being able to contribute to discipline linkage. Therefore, it is essential to 
facilitate access to education for adults and elderlies, ICT formation courses, as well as language 
courses for being able to communicate with international professionals performing in similar or 
different research fields.

8. Conclusions 
 
The conclusions derived from the development of the project are the following: 
 
• 
Technological, environmental, political, and economic changes are disrupting reality 
nowadays, challenging cities and their citizens. 
 
• 
Megatrends and the 4th Industrial revolutions are changing the global context and 
shaping society. Cities, and more specifically, Smart Cities, must face these disruptive 
situations and create a balanced ecosystem from which they can generate growth 
opportunities.    
 
• 
The Smart City must be identified with the city of the future, and it is perceived as an 
innovation environment.  
 
• 
A range of technological, institutional, and human factors must shape the Smart City 
and its pillars. 
 
• 
The Smart City pillars (Government, Mobility, Environmental Sustainability, Economic 
Development, Life Quality, and Intellectual Capital) are interlinked. The Smart City can 
not be fully developed if significant differences exist in the level of development of each 
pillar.  
 
• 
Technological and social innovation should be the driver that provides sustainable 
solutions for the creation of the Smart City. 
 
• 
The Smart City requires the participation of multiple agents, both public and private. 
 
• 
Smart City initiatives must be oriented to people, having citizen well-being as the 
primary objective. 
 
• 
The use of ICT and new technologies for the city development must be used as a mean 
for achieving the Smart City, but never as an end or as the main factor.  
 
• 
There is no unique model for the Smart City: 
 
- 
Each city that conducts urban transformation projects emphasizes different pillars 
(depending on their necessities) and with different level of development. 
 
- 
Context conditions influence the level of implementation of the Smart City. 
 
• 
Sustainable Development Goals must act as a restrictive framework for forcing the 
Smart City to perform sustainably.  
 
• 
Education is one of the most important factors for the creation of the Smart City. 
 
• 
The development of the Smart City will require significant monetary investments that 
some developing country can not afford yet. In any case, these countries can also find 
opportunities in new technologies used in Smart Cities. They could have the possibility 
==PAGE:117==
 
117 
of leapfrogging initial development steps that developed countries had to make and 
directly leap to more advanced stages.   
 
• 
For the sustainable development of the Smart City, the i-Learner profile is crucial. Only 
someone with digital and social skills, lifelong trained, and politically active and 
empowered, will be able to lead Smart City’s efforts towards a more sustainable future.

13.5. Interview with Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune 
 
Date: 07.05.2019 
Location: Lyngby-Taarbæk headquarters 
Conducted with: Jakob Jespersen, climate coordinator of Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune. 
Goal: gathering of information related to what measures and initiatives are Danish 
municipalities already implementing or planning to, in Smart City terms. 
Key learnings:  
 
- 
Creation of special groups for the digitalization and optimization of online 
services. 
- 
Use of multi-layer street maps not only for guidance through the city but also for 
locating climate action initiatives, and waterflows in case of heavy rain. 
- 
Availability of notes for politic meetings, streamed and recorded board 
meetings.  
- 
Municipal app as a tool for citizens for communicating about problems that they 
find in the city. 
- 
Use of figures for better explaining municipal data to citizens. 
- 
Formation of politicians in topics of interest (digitalization, climate change, 
providing jobs, how to work with the youth…). 
- 
Use of municipal’s car for helping the elderlies.  
- 
Help and welcome of foreigner new comers to the city. 
- 
A city with high knowledge businesses will attract talent and people.  
- 
CTS for controlling installations, ventilation, heating, and refrigeration systems 
(Central Tilstandskontrol og Styring in Danish, meaning Central Condition Control 
and Management). 
- 
Flooding prevention. 
- 
Lyngby-Taarbæk 
also 
acknowledge 
the importance 
of 
public-private 
collaboration. They are right now involved in a project named Knowledge City, 
alongside DTU and private companies (including international), for fostering the 
development of Smart City initiatives in their Kommune. 
- 
This Kommune is mostly investment in solutions regarding traffic management, 
smart networks for water and electricity, and mobility. They are little investing 
innovation and entrepreneurship, and culture. 
 
Government: 
 
• Electronic headquarters: 
 
- 
What kind of administrative procedures can the citizens access through the 
Kommune Web? 
 
Mostly of the services are national. The web page is of really easy access, 
highly digitalize. From it you can access NetBank, databases, your personal 
healthcare records, statistics, local politics… 
 
==PAGE:152==
 
152 
We are also working in the creation of a new group for improving the digital 
services that the Kommune offer, how to run a better services for locals 
through digitalization. 
 
- 
Is it easy for citizens to access the webpage and official documents? How is 
the level of the digitalization of the web page? 
 
Citizens just need to use their NemID in order to access online services and 
personal data. 
 
We have developed a new webpage, which is easier to access and to navigate 
than the previous one, as well as easy to find in Google when you are 
searching for the Kommune. We also use social media (Facebook, 
newsletters, LinkedIn…). 
 
• Transparency: 
 
- 
Updated information: Is the information on the website updated 
regularly? 
 
Some sections are updated regularly, others do not need it since they have 
static information 
 
- 
Data access:  
 
Do the citizens have access to municipal data? 
 
The national system allows the access to personal health records. We also 
have street maps, in which you can see where are schools, where climate 
action is being taken, waterflows in case of heavy rain… Citizens can also 
access municipal strategies, notes for politic meetings…When the board 
and the subcommittees meet, these meetings are streamed as well as 
recorded. 
 
- 
Web:  
 
Is there an specific channel for citizens’ complaints on the web? 
 
Yes. It is call Lyngby-Taarbæk (this app is used for citizens to complain 
about problems that they might find in the Kommune, as garbage, pot 
holes…). This app is aimed to increase citizens participation, include them 
more, and so they are more informed. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:153==
 
153 
Is there a web section for citizen’s opinions and proposal for political 
parties?  
 
Citizens can join discussions through local media. Also, they can e-mail the 
mayor, with suggestions and complains. Moreover, sometimes citizens are 
inviting to access meetings and join the discussion. 
 
Are they any neighborhood association and do they have an space 
reserved for them?  
 
Yes, they are still a few. They usually meet in public places, like schools. 
 
How do you use social media in the commune?  
 
Social media is used for communicating to citizens what is going on at the 
Kommune. There is a good dialogue, we get suggestions from citizens. 
 
How would you rate the level of transparency of the Kommune? 
 
Yes. It is a high level of transparency in Denmark in general terms. You can 
get information about cases, personal data…The legislation of Denmark 
force to do it. 
 
Is information about budget/municipal incomes and expenses and debts 
available for free access?  
 
Yes, with overall figures. You can also ask for access to small budget from 
departments. Also you can see if we have made any contract with a 
company. 
 
Ethics: Is there any set of values, norms and standards of conduct for the 
Kommune and its employees that is used as a guidance for taking 
decisions and making actions? Is it public available? 
 
Yes. It has four topics: service to the citizen, organization, working 
environment… 
 
In your opinion, what is the level of willingness of political leaders of the 
Kommune for addressing the present and future challenges that society 
faces? 
 
They are very involve in dialogue with citizens. We also conduct some 
workshops in topic that politicians need to know about, like digitalization, 
climate change, how to work with the youth, providing jobs… 
 
 
==PAGE:154==
 
154 
• Voter participation: How high is the willingness of the citizens to participate 
in political decisions?
