Abstract 
Oxford Nanopore Technologies presents a different approach to DNA sequencing, which allows for the 
generation and analysis of sequencing data in real-time, but with the caveat of a relatively high error rate 
compared to standard high-throughput sequencing methods. In this study the effect of the high error rate on 
the ability to detect genotypic antimicrobial resistance determinants in Nanopore sequencing data is 
evaluated, through a comparison of results between Nanopore data, Illumina data and hybrid assembled draft 
genomes, for 24 Escherichia coli isolates. The genotypic resistance profiles used for the comparison are 
generated by using alignment and mapping tools in combination with a database of antimicrobial resistance 
genes, and only cover acquired antimicrobial resistance genes. The comparison is purely genotypic, and does 
not include an evaluation of the agreement between the genotypic resistance profiles and growth-based 
phenotypic resistance profiles. To reduce the effect of sequencing errors in the Nanopore data, a method of 
filtration based on the alignment depth of genes from the resistance database is applied, resulting in a clear 
improvement to the accuracy of the genotypic resistance profiles. Following the data filtration, it was found 
to be possible to correctly identify genotypic resistance profiles at predicted phenotypic level. There was 
found to be some disagreement between the Nanopore results and the hybrid assemblies used as references, 
on which specific gene-variants were present in the isolates. The effect of the amount of Nanopore 
sequencing data used to generate the genotypic resistance profiles was also tested, to evaluate the potential 
for rapid and precise genotypic resistance profiling done in real-time. It was found that resistance profiles at 
the level of antimicrobial classes could be correctly produced after ~1 hour and 15 minutes of sequencing. 
Resistance profiles at the level of predicted phenotypes could be correctly produced after ~5 hours and 34 
minutes of sequencing. These sequencing times are based on average sequencing runs of the Nanopore 
MinION with 12 multiplexed samples, and as such the sequencing times can be reduced by sequencing of 
fewer samples per run. In conclusion, the ability to rapidly and accurately produce genotypic resistance 
profiles based on acquired resistance genes appears to be possible with Nanopore sequencing data. The study 
also includes discussions on possible future improvements to the technology and subsequent analysis, which 
could allow for the production of complete genotypic resistance profiles also including resistance as a result 
of point mutations in chromosomal genes.

1 – Introduction 
The discovery of compounds exhibiting antimicrobial properties was one of the biggest breakthroughs in 
modern medicine, revolutionizing the treatment and management of infectious diseases. Prior to the 
discovery of antimicrobials, infectious diseases brought on by pathogenic microorganisms, such as smallpox, 
cholera, diphtheria and typhoid fever, were all untreatable, and infectious diseases were the leading cause of 
death worldwide. This all changed in the middle of the 20th century, with the emergence of the antimicrobial 
era, which helped eradicate many previously deadly diseases, and offered a way of treatment for those that 
remained. Unfortunately, the effects of antimicrobial drugs are diminishing, as the ability of microorganisms 
to resist their effect is on the rise, a phenomenon termed antimicrobial resistance (AMR). Significant AMR is 
already displayed in many pathogenic microorganisms, such as Carbapenemase-Producing Organisms 
(CPO), Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci 
(VRE). If the spread of AMR is allowed to continue, it threatens to revert one of the major accomplishments 
of modern medicine and again making infectious diseases a leading cause of mortality worldwide. One way 
to combat AMR is to limit the misuse of antimicrobial drugs, by only using them against infections where 
they are effective. In order to do this, the resistance profile of the infection has to be defined. Traditionally 
this has been achieved by in vitro antimicrobial susceptibility testing, in which the growth of a microbe is 
measured in the presence of antimicrobial drugs. The improvements seen in DNA sequencing in recent years 
has provided an alternative method for AMR detection, wherein the resistance profile is determined based on 
the presence of genotypic resistance determinants.  
Oxford Nanopore Technologies presents a new approach to DNA sequencing that enables the generation and 
analysis of sequencing data in real-time, which has the potential to reduce the time required to identify the 
resistance profiles of pathogenic microorganisms. A drawback of the technology is the relatively high error 
rate, which can lead to incorrect detection of gene-variants, potentially resulting in erroneous resistance 
profiles.

2 – Aim and scope 
2.1 – Aim 
The aim of this study is to compare the genotypic resistance profiles predicted by analysis of Nanopore 
sequencing-data with those predicted from analysis of hybrid assemblies and Illumina sequencing-data. The 
hybrid assemblies were generated by a combination of Nanopore and Illumina sequencing-data, and are 
considered the gold standard in genotypic AMR detection. The analysis is twofold, with the initial part being 
centered on the noise generated by sequencing errors in Nanopore data. Specifically how the sequencing 
errors impact the predicted genotypic resistance profiles of 24 Escherichia coli isolates, and if this impact 
can be reduced. Following this, the potential for rapid and accurate prediction of genotypic resistance 
profiles using Nanopore sequencing is evaluated. This is achieved by simulating real-time data generation 
and comparing the predicted genotypic resistance profiles at varying amounts of data. To facilitate the 
comparisons of the genotypic resistance profiles and automate the process of simulating real-time data 
generation, a Python pipeline has been constructed. 
2.2 – Scope 
The focus of this study is a bioinformatics comparison of genotypically predicted resistance profiles. The 
genotypically predicted resistance profiles have not been phenotypically confirmed, such as through 
comparisons with in vitro antimicrobial susceptibility tests, as this information was not available. The 
analysis was conducted on 24 Escherichia coli isolates which were already available at Statens Serum 
Institut, and as a result of this no laboratory work was conducted as part of the study. The hybrid assemblies 
that are used as references for the 24 isolates were also constructed prior to this study. For three of the 24 
isolates, the hybrid assemblies included clear signs of misassembly, and were therefore removed from the 
further analysis. For the remaining 21 isolates, the gene-variants detected in the hybrid assemblies were 
assumed to be correct, and was used as reference points when comparing with the Nanopore results. No 
additional tests, such as Sanger Sequencing, have been done to verify the accuracy of the gene-variants 
detected in the hybrid assemblies.  
The study evaluates the ability to generate rapid and precise genotypic resistance profiles using Nanopore 
data, with the caveat that only acquired resistance genes are included in the analysis, meaning that the effect 
of point mutations and intrinsic resistance is not considered in the results. The price of doing resistance 
profiling based on Nanopore data as opposed to conventional methods has not been evaluated either. 
The coding aspect of the Python pipeline created in connection with this study, named 
NanoporeFiltrationCompare (https://github.com/CWestergaard/NanoporeFiltrationCompare), is not touched 
upon, the focus is instead on the aspects of the analysis that it assists with.

3 – Antimicrobials 
The association between microbes and disease was established by Robert Koch and Louis Pasteur in the late 
1800s. This discovery kick-started the search for new substances capable of inhibiting the growth of 
microbes. Naturally available treatments such as herbs and mold had previously been used to treat infections, 
but with varying results and no clear understanding of how they worked. Penicillin, probably the most 
widely recognized antimicrobial, was discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928, and became available for 
treatments in 19451. In the years that followed, many new antimicrobial compounds were discovered. 
Initially most new antimicrobial compounds hailed from naturally occurring microorganisms, but as time 
passed and researchers’ understanding of the mechanisms of antimicrobial compounds improved, synthetic 
antimicrobial compounds began to emerge. Compounds derived from microorganisms, which are capable of 
killing or inhibiting the growth of other microorganisms in concentrations that are safe to humans, are often 
referred to as antibiotics. Antimicrobial is a broader term for compounds with the same properties, which are 
not necessarily derived from microorganisms, but can be of natural, semisynthetic or synthetic origin. Today 
more than 100 different antimicrobial compounds exist. These compounds are generally grouped by their 
chemical structure, as similar chemical structural units often share patterns of toxicity, effectiveness and 
other important properties. Most compounds belong to one of the following classifications, based on 
chemical structure: beta-lactams, macrolides, quinolones, tetracyclines or aminoglycosides, but even more 
exist (Figure 1).

Various important properties of antimicrobial compounds, such as their spectrum of activity and toxicity in 
the human body, cannot be deduced simply based on their chemical structure. Especially the classes 
containing many compounds will have discrepancies in these properties.  Other properties worth considering 
are the compounds’ rate of clearance from the blood, mode of administration, ability to cross the blood-brain 
barrier and its susceptibility to being degraded by enzymes present in the target cell.  
3.1 – Spectrum of activity 
Antimicrobial compounds differ in their spectrum of activity, with some compounds having a narrow 
spectrum of activity, meaning they can only act against a limited range of microorganisms. Other compounds 
display a broad spectrum of activity, affecting a wide range of microorganisms. A broad spectrum compound 
can be advantageous in some cases, especially if the microbe causing disease is unknown, but generally the 
narrow spectrum antimicrobials are preferred. This is due to the fact that narrow spectrum antimicrobials can 
act specifically on the pathogenic microbe of interest, applying selective pressure towards a narrower range 
of microorganisms. 
3.1.1 – Toxicity 
The sole purpose of antimicrobial compounds is to disrupt their target cells, so these compounds are toxic by 
definition. A wide range of compounds with antimicrobial activity have been isolated over the years, but 
only a few are deemed fit for clinical use. What separates clinically useful antimicrobials from the rest is 
their selective toxicity, meaning their ability to be highly effective against a target microbe but with minimal 
or no toxicity to humans. This ratio of microbe toxicity to human toxicity is denoted as the therapeutic index 
of a compound. Selective toxicity is achieved by antimicrobial compounds that act on vulnerable targets that 
are unique to the target microbe. For instance, beta-lactam antimicrobials inhibit synthesis of the bacterial 
cell wall, which is not present in eukaryotic cells, giving them selective toxicity against bacteria.  
3.2 – Type of action 
Antimicrobial compounds can be classified based on whether they are bacteriostatic or bactericidal. 
Compounds that destroy their target, often by targeting the cell wall or cell membrane, are classified as 
bactericidal. Compounds that inhibit or slow the growth of their target, often by inhibition of protein 
synthesis or disruption of metabolic pathways, are classified as bacteriostatic. Here, the immune system will 
then have to clear the infection of the bacteria, who are arrested in their growth. 
3.2.1 – Mode of action 
A more specific description of the function of an antimicrobial compound than the type of action is the mode 
of action. While the type of action describes the effect of a compound, the mode of action describes how that 
effect is achieved, the actual function of the compound. There are five main modes of action, each targeting a 
major process or function responsible for the growth of a microorganism, these are: cell wall synthesis, cell

membrane functions, protein synthesis, nucleic acid synthesis and metabolic pathways. Examples of some of 
the antimicrobial classes and drugs that target each of these processes can be seen in Figure 2.

3.2.1.1 - Inhibition of membrane functions 
The cytoplasm of a cell is covered by the cytoplasmic membrane, which acts as a selective barrier that aids 
in controlling the internal composition of a cell. Antimicrobial compounds such as polymyxins can disrupt 
the cytoplasmic membrane of a cell, increasing the permeability of the membrane and disrupting the osmotic 
balance. This results in an outflow of macromolecules and ions, as well as an intake of water, resulting in the 
death of the cell.  
3.2.1.2 - Inhibition of protein synthesis 
Protein synthesis is integral to the survival of any cell, be that a prokaryote or an eukaryote. This makes the 
selectivity of antimicrobial compounds targeting this process an important factor, as the aim is to disrupt 
only the target microbe and not the normal functions in a patient’s own cells. Protein synthesis is a multi-
stage process, allowing for a wide range of antimicrobial compounds to target the process at different stages. 
Aminoglycosides, macrolides and tetracyclines are just some of the antimicrobial classes that act on and 
disrupt the protein synthesis of their target. Tetracycline for example, achieves selective toxicity by 
inhibiting the formation of the 70S bacterial ribosome, through binding to the 30S subunit, which is not 
present in eukaryotic organisms. 
3.2.1.3 - Inhibition of nucleic acid synthesis 
Differences in the enzymes involved in DNA and RNA synthesis between eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells 
allow for selectivity of antimicrobial compounds that target these processes. Compounds with this mode of 
==PAGE:11==
7 
 
action can be divided into DNA and RNA inhibitors. Fluoroquinolones are DNA inhibitors that act by 
binding to the enzyme DNA gyrase, resulting in inhibition of DNA replication. Rifampin is an example of an 
RNA inhibitor, which binds to the DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, inhibiting RNA elongation. This 
prevents gene transcription and leads to cell death. 
3.2.1.4 - Inhibition of metabolic pathways 
Some synthetic antimicrobial compounds act as antimetabolites, competitive inhibitors for bacterial 
metabolic enzymes. Sulfonamides are examples of such compounds, which structurally resemble para-
aminobenzoic acid (PABA), an early intermediate in folic acid synthesis3. Sulfonamides work by inhibiting 
the enzymes involved in dihydrofolic acid production, blocking the synthesis of folic acid, which is required 
for nucleic acid synthesis. The drugs are generally broad-spectrum, and they achieve selective toxicity 
against bacteria, due to the fact that humans do not synthesize folic acid, but instead obtain it through food. 
3.2.1.5 - Inhibition of cell wall synthesis 
The cell wall of an organism is integral to its survival and growth. Bacterial cell walls are structurally 
different from any other organism, due to the presence of a peptidoglycan polysaccharide backbone. 
Antimicrobial compounds that target the cell wall synthesis, such as beta-lactams, inhibit the peptidoglycan 
synthesis required for growing the bacterial cell wall, making the bacteria unable to multiply. These 
compounds are bactericidal and require the target cells to be actively growing in order to exert their toxicity. 
They achieve their selective toxicity against bacteria because the cell wall of human cells does not contain 
peptidoglycan. Gram-negative bacteria are generally less susceptible to cell wall synthesis inhibitors, as the 
compounds can be blocked from reaching the cell wall by the outer membrane. Additionally, some bacteria 
can produce enzymes known as beta-lactamases, which are capable of inactivating beta-lactam compounds, 
rendering the bacteria resistant. 
3.3 – Beta-lactams 
Antimicrobial compounds from the class beta-lactam deserve a special mention, as they are by far the most 
commonly used antimicrobial compounds4. The effectiveness of beta-lactam compounds has led to the 
production of a multitude of different beta-lactam drug, which can be further divided into subgroups based 
on their chemical structure. All beta-lactam compounds share the same chemical backbone, a four-membered 
ring named the beta-lactam ring5 (Figure 3). The beta-lactam ring resembles the D-alanyl-D-alanine peptide6 
(Figure 3), which is an essential part of the bacterial cell wall. By irreversible binding to the D-alanyl-D-
alanine transpeptidase, beta-lactam compounds inhibit the extension of the peptidoglycan layer, which leads 
to a weakened cell wall in growing bacteria and resulting in the cell bursting.

3.3.1 – Penams  
Penams contain a beta-lactam ring fused to a 5-membered thiazolidine ring (Figure 3). The penam group 
contains natural penicillin’s, such as penicillin G and penicillin V, produced by fermentation of Penicillium 
chrysogenum. These compounds are narrow spectrum, as they are not effective against Gram-negative 
bacteria, and they are typically given by parenteral administration, due to their instability in the presence of 
stomach acid. 
Penams also include semisynthetic penicillin’s such as amoxicillin and ampicillin, which are modified 
chemically by the addition of side chains. These semisynthetic compounds display distinct advantages over 
the natural penicillin’s, such as increased spectrum of activity due to their ability to target Gram-negative 
bacteria, ability to be administered orally and reduced susceptibility towards the beta-lactam degrading group 
of enzymes known as beta-lactamases. 
3.3.2 – Cephems 
Cephems contain a 6-membered dihydrothiazine ring fused to the beta-lactam ring (Figure 3). Cephems are 
produced by species of Cephalosporium molds. They display low toxicity and a broader spectrum than 
penams, and are often used for treatment of Gram-negative bacteria. They are subject to degradation by some 
bacterial beta-lactamases, but show resistance towards others. Cephems are further grouped into generations 
(Figure 4), based on their spectrum of activity and resistance towards beta-lactamases. Each new generation 
show greater antimicrobial activity towards Gram-negative bacteria than the one preceding it, often with

decreased activity against Gram-positive bacteria7. Fourth generation cephems show a broad spectrum of 
activity.

3.3.3 – Carbapenems 
Carbapenems contain the same 5-membered ring as the penams, except that the sulfur atom is replaced by a 
carbon (Figure 3). Carbapenems have the broadest spectrum of activity of all beta-lactam drugs, and only a 
smaller subset of beta-lactamase enzymes is capable of hydrolysis of carbapenems, termed carbapenemases. 
The most effective carbapenemases are the KPC, VIM, IMP, NDM and Oxa-48 types8. Carbapenems are 
considered last-resort antimicrobials, meaning that they should only be used when no other treatment options 
are left, such as for multidrug resistant infections. 
3.3.4 – Monobactams 
Monobactams have no ring attached to the beta-lactam ring (Figure 3). Monobactams are particularly useful 
for treatment of allergic individuals, as they are structurally different enough from the other beta-lactam 
compounds, as to not induce allergy. Aztreonam is an example of a monobactam compound, originally 
isolated from the Gram-negative bacteria Chromobacterium violaceum. Aztreonam shows no activity 
towards Gram-positive bacteria, but is highly effective against a wide range of Gram-negative bacteria. 
3.4 – Antimicrobial resistance 
With the discovery of antimicrobials it was believed that humans had won the war against microorganisms, 
and the diseases that they cause. Unfortunately, this was not the case, as it was soon discovered that 
microorganisms were adept survivors, capable of developing resistance towards these newly discovered 
wonder-drugs. AMR in pathogenic organisms is on the rise, and it has added significantly to the impact of 
infectious diseases, in the number of infections, as well as the added healthcare cost for treating resistant 
infections. Studies have shown that patients with antimicrobial-resistant infections have significantly higher 
costs than those that are infected with susceptible organisms, with an added cost of $6,000-$30,000 US 
dollars9 per patient. AMR has become one of the biggest threats to global health, with current estimates 
putting the death toll as a result of antimicrobial-resistant infections at 700.000 a year worldwide. This figure 
is estimated to rise as high as 10 million by 2050, if appropriate steps are not taken to mitigate the threat10.  
==PAGE:14==
10 
 
Unfortunately, the economic viability of researching and producing new antimicrobial compounds is no 
longer what it once was, as companies have to weigh the cost against profits that are potentially time-limited, 
due to the risk of quick emergence of new resistance mechanisms towards the newly developed drugs. Other 
factors that can affect the profitability of new drugs are the facts that antimicrobial compounds are typically 
only used in treatment for short periods of time, and that new compounds are often subject to restrictions by 
national drug authorities, to safeguard against resistance. Identifying new antimicrobial targets has also 
proven to be difficult, resulting in many new compounds being chemical variants of older compounds. This 
means that resistance mechanisms towards similar compounds already exist, making the risk of resistance 
towards the new compound even greater11. 
From 2010-2016, eight new antimicrobial drugs were given FDA approval, with an average time for 
approval of 6.2 years, and with a cost per dose of these new drugs ranging from $2,000-$4,200 dollars. Of 
the eight new drugs, only one was a novel antimicrobial12. This lack of new antimicrobial drugs entering the 
market makes it all the more important, to make the best use of the compounds that are currently available. 
In order to optimize the use of antimicrobial compounds, and limit the emergence and spread of resistance, 
clinicians should strive to only treat infections with antimicrobials that are effective. To assist with this, the 
antimicrobial resistance profile of the infection should be characterized. Use of ineffective compounds 
against an infection leads to the proliferation of resistant organisms, which are then able to spread that 
resistance to other organisms, furthering the resistance in the population towards that compound.  
Traditionally, AMR has been identified through phenotypic assays, where the growth of a microbe is 
measured in the presence of antimicrobial compounds. The gold standard for measuring antimicrobial 
susceptibility is based on standardized dilution- or diffusion-based in vitro antimicrobial susceptibility 
testing methods13. Culture-based methods have the advantage of determining the true in vitro relationship 
between the antimicrobial compound and the strain tested, and will be able to detect any new emerging 
resistance mechanisms. These methods require the microorganism to grow in order to determine the 
phenotype, so the time from sampling to answer is solely dependent on the growth rate of the 
microorganism. A pure culture sample of the microorganism is required, so the microorganism needs to be 
grown twice before a phenotype can be determined, first to obtain a pure culture, and then to determine the 
phenotype. In most cases this will take between 1-2 days, but for some microorganisms such as 
Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which can take weeks to grow, it becomes an unfeasible way of detecting 
resistance, when the aim is to inform a treatment plan. 
3.4.1 – Antimicrobial resistance mechanisms 
Resistance to antimicrobial compounds is correlated with a wide range of molecular mechanisms, depending 
on both the organism and the compound. These mechanisms are highly diverse, and can arise as a result of 
very different genetic events. Examples of such events are up-regulated expression of resistance genes due to

Intrinsic resistance is to be expected, as antimicrobials are often designed to exert their effect on specific 
targets, as a way to focus their effect and at the same time limit their toxicity. This can be countered by the 
development of antimicrobials with different spectrums of activity, designed to combat specific types of 
organisms. This is one of the reasons why so many antimicrobials exist, and if intrinsic resistance was the 
only type of resistance, then at this point in time there exists more than enough different antimicrobials to 
combat it. Unfortunately this is not the case, and just as humans are able to develop new antimicrobials, 
microorganisms are able to develop new resistance mechanisms towards those antimicrobials. This is called 
acquired resistance, and it occurs when an intrinsically susceptible microorganism adapts and becomes 
resistant to an antimicrobial. This adaptation can result from changes in genes involved in normal 
physiological processes and cellular structures, large genomic changes such as insertions and deletions, 
chromosomal rearrangements or gene duplications. It can also result from the acquisition of foreign 
resistance genes, such as resistant genes carried on plasmids and other mobile genetic elements, such as 
integrons and transposons15.  
While chromosomal changes can be just as effective as acquired foreign resistance genes, these changes 
usually come with an increased fitness cost, meaning that organisms are only at an advantage when there is a 
selective pressure for resistance. Uptake of mobile genetic elements also comes at an increased fitness cost, 
but the chances of the organism ejecting said mobile genetic element is much larger than the chance of a 
chromosomal change in a household gene to be reversed, once selective pressure is no longer applied. The 
amount of chromosomal changes able to confer resistance will also vary depending on the microorganism. 
The ability to spread resistance in a population is also much higher when genes are located on mobile genetic 
elements as opposed to chromosomal changes in individual organisms. While chromosomal changes are only 
passed on to the progeny of the mutated microorganism, mobile genetic elements can be passed on to other 
nearby microorganisms, furthering the spread of resistance. Foreign resistance genes can be acquired by

bacteria through multiple mechanisms for uptake of genetic material: transformation, transduction and 
conjugation, generally referred to as horizontal gene transfer. Probably the most common transmission of 
resistance genes between bacteria is through the uptake of plasmids, circular genomic elements that often 
contain many different resistance genes. Plasmids conferring multidrug-resistance (MDR) is one of the main 
causes for the spread of AMR in bacteria, and uptake of such a plasmid can severely limit therapeutic 
options. In the worst cases the acquisition of a MDR plasmid can transform an easily treatable infection into 
one untreatable by the current antimicrobial arsenal16. The rise in carbapenem-resistant infections is 
particularly worrisome, as it is considered one of the last lines of defense against hard-to-treat infections. The 
presence of carbapenemases on mobile genetic elements, such as plasmids that harbor MDR, can result in 
selective pressure for carbapenem resistance, even when another antimicrobial is used for treatment, a 
process termed co-resistance. This selectivity for AMR mechanisms against drugs that are not used for 
treatment, due to their presence on MDR plasmids, plays a major role in the spread of AMR, especially 
against the compounds meant as treatments of last resort17. 
A major problem with using antimicrobial compounds in treatment of infections is that the use of these drugs 
leads to increased resistance, through selection of already resistant bacteria or the selection of strains that are 
adept at acquiring resistance, such as hyper-mutable strains or strains with an increased affinity for the 
uptake of foreign DNA. While uptake of foreign DNA, such as plasmids, is primarily responsible for the 
spread of antimicrobial resistance, it is through mutations that new resistance mechanisms are developed18. 
Bacteria have an average mutation rate of 1 for every 106-109 cell divisions19, depending on the bacterial 
species, but most of these mutations have no benefit to the bacteria. Mutations that lead to antimicrobial 
resistance mainly occur in a few types of genes, those involved in the four main mechanisms of antimicrobial 
resistance (Figure 5): i) Limiting uptake of an antimicrobial compound; ii) Modification of the antimicrobial 
compounds’ target; iii) Inactivation of the antimicrobial compound; and iv) Efflux of the antimicrobial 
compound. Similarly, bacteria can take up a lot of genetic material that will have no effect on antimicrobial 
resistance, only genes that are related to one of these four mechanisms will lead to acquired resistance 
towards antimicrobial drugs.

3.4.1.1 - Limiting drug uptake 
In bacteria with large outer membranes, such as Gram-negatives, the transfer of large and/or hydrophilic 
molecules into the cell is often facilitated by porin channels. As a result, porin channels are instrumental in 
the uptake of antimicrobial drugs in many bacteria. The bacteria can alter and limit the uptake of specific 
drugs through either a reduction in the number of porin channels, or by modifications to the selectivity of 
them. Another mechanism for limiting drug uptake is the formation of biofilm, where the thick and sticky 
consistency of the biofilm matrix makes it difficult for the drug to reach the target bacteria. As a side note, 
the formation of biofilm requires bacteria to be in close proximity, which also facilitates the transfer of genes 
through horizontal gene transfer, potentially increasing the spread of resistance genes in the bacterial 
community that makes up the biofilm19.  
3.4.1.2 - Modification of drug target 
Many different targets for antimicrobial drugs exist in a cell, but whatever the target may be, the effect on an 
antimicrobial can be greatly reduced or completely eliminated by modifications to the target, if those 
modifications interfere with the ability of the drug to bind to its target. An example of this is acquired 
resistance towards the drug vancomycin, through the acquisition of the van gene, which results in a change in 
structure of peptidoglycan precursors, the target of vancomycin19.  
==PAGE:18==
14 
 
3.4.1.3 - Drug efflux  
Bacteria contain efflux pumps, which primary function is to pump out substances toxic to the cell. There are 
five different types of efflux pumps, classified based on chemical structure and energy source. Many bacteria 
contain multiple of these types of efflux pumps, which are chromosomally encoded, and can be expressed 
both constitutively or induced under certain environmental stimuli, such as when a specific substrate is 
present. Depending on the type of efflux pump, substrates can be either pumped into the cytoplasm or across 
the cell-membrane and into the surrounding environment. Resistance towards antimicrobial drugs can be 
obtained through mutations in the chromosomally encoded genes that code for the efflux pumps, either by 
up-regulating pumps that are already capable of transporting out the drug, or by altering the specificity of a 
pump to also efflux the drug. Resistance can also be acquired by obtaining foreign genes that code for efflux 
pumps that are effective against the drug. The specificity of the different types of efflux pumps vary, with 
some having a very narrow substrate range and others, such as the AcrAB-TolC pump in Escherichia coli, 
being capable of transporting a wide range of antimicrobials including beta-lactams, chloramphenicol, 
macrolides, fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines19.  
3.4.1.4 - Drug inactivation 
Bacteria can inactivate drugs through two different mechanisms. The first is through degradation of the drug, 
a mechanism often used by microorganisms against beta-lactam drugs, through enzymes known as beta-
lactamases. The second way to inactivate an antimicrobial drug is by transferring a chemical group to it, 
which interferes with its binding to its target. This transfer often involves an acetyl-, phosphoryl- or adenyl-
group21.  
3.4.1.4.1 - Beta-lactamases 
Beta-lactamases are bacterial enzymes capable of degrading beta-lactam antimicrobial compounds, 
potentially rendering the bacteria resistant towards that form of treatment. Beta-lactamase enzymes 
hydrolyze a specific site in the beta-lactam ring of beta-lactam drugs, which causes the ring to open, making 
the drugs unable to bind to their target. The enzymes are secreted through the cytoplasmic membrane. In 
Gram-negative bacteria the beta-lactamase enzymes are captured in the periplasm, located between the inner 
cytoplasmic membrane and the bacterial outer membrane, resulting in a high concentration inside the 
bacteria. In Gram-positive bacteria the enzymes diffuse to the surrounding environment, resulting in a lower 
concentration of beta-lactamase enzymes in the bacteria, leading to less degradation of beta-lactam 
compounds inside the bacteria. The genes coding for these enzymes can be located both on the chromosome 
or acquired via plasmids. Many members of the Enterobacteriaceae family of Gram-negative bacteria, as 
well as some Pseudomonas spp., Acinetobacter spp. and Aeromonas spp., possess chromosomal beta-
lactamase genes19. Beta-lactamase genes carried on plasmids are most often found in Enterobacteriaceae, 
but can also be seen in some Gram-positive bacterial species, such as Staphylococcus aureus, Enterococcus 
faecalis, and Enterococcus faecium22.

3.4.1.4.2 - Beta-lactamase inhibitors 
Beta-lactamase inhibitors are structurally similar to beta-lactam 
compounds, but have little to no antimicrobial activity of itself; instead it 
works by inhibiting the enzymatic degradation of beta-lactams by beta-
lactamase enzymes. By combining a beta-lactam compound with a beta-
lactamase inhibitor, called combination therapy, the inhibitor can hamper 
the inactivation of the beta-lactam compound, making the compound 
effective against bacteria that would otherwise be resistant to it.

4 – DNA Sequencing 
Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is one of the building blocks of life, containing the instructions needed for an 
organism to develop, survive and reproduce. DNA sequencing is the process of determining the order of 
nucleotides within a DNA molecule. A cell’s DNA sequence is essentially an instruction manual for that cell 
on how to behave, and DNA sequencing opened up the possibility for researchers to decipher that manual, in 
a more detailed fashion than ever before. In 1977 Fred Sanger developed the first sequencing method to gain 
widespread use. Sanger sequencing was the sequencing method of choice for many years to come, and 
improvements to the method made it possible for a computer to sequentially read the nucleotides. This 
facilitated an automation of the sequencing process, and the first commercial DNA sequencing machines 
became available in 198623,24. These machines were able to produce reads of a length close to 1 kilo base 
(kb). To analyze longer fragments of DNA, researchers had to make use of additional techniques, such as 
Shotgun sequencing. This involved shearing longer fragments, and sequencing and realigning the resulting 
smaller fragments into one long continuous sequence, known as a contig. The ability to generate high 
concentrations of pure DNA through PCR amplification helped further the effectiveness of DNA sequencing. 
In 1998 Applied Biosystems released the ABI PRISM 3700 DNA Analyzer25. This machine allowed 
simultaneous sequencing of hundreds of samples, and was part of the reason that the first draft of the human 
genome could be completed in 2003, years ahead of schedule26.  
This, however, was just the beginning of high throughput sequencing, and not long after, technologies known 
as Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) entered the market. These technologies differ from Sanger sequencing 
in their capability to produce sequences at higher throughput, at a much lower cost per base and without the 
need for electrophoresis. NGS technologies can sequence in parallel, sequencing millions to billions of reads 
in a single run. Using the sequencing of the human genome for comparison, took almost 13 years to 
sequence using Sanger sequencing and required the cooperation of multiple laboratories across the world, 
costing approximately $2.7 billion dollars27. In contrast, the HiSeq X Ten sequencer, released by Illumina in 
2014, is capable of sequencing 45 human genomes in a single day, at a cost of approximately $1000 dollars 
per genome28. The capability of DNA sequencers is currently growing at a rate even faster than that in the 
computing revolution described by Moore’s law, stating that computer chip performance will roughly double 
every two years. From 2004 to 2010, sequencing capabilities doubled every five months29. The decreased 
cost of sequencing a human genome as a result of improvements in sequencing throughput is showcased in 
Figure 7. 
==PAGE:21==

The high throughput sequencing technologies are divided into two groups. Second-generation sequencing, 
also often referred to as NGS technologies, require PCR amplification of DNA in order to generate the high 
throughput of sequences, and generate DNA sequences of 100-400 base pairs in length. In contrast, Third-
generation sequencing, also referred to as Single Molecule Real-Time (SMRT) technologies, do not require 
amplification of DNA, and are capable of generating longer reads than the NGS technologies.  
4.1 – Second-generation sequencing – Illumina 
Illumina is a NGS technology, and is due to its low error rate at ~0.1%31 currently the gold standard, by 
which other technologies are compared. Illumina utilizes the sequencing by synthesis approach, which 
involves a step by step incorporation of fluorescent nucleotides. This approach is also used by other 
technologies such as Ion Torrent32 and 454. The SOLiD technology is the only second-generation sequencing 
technology to do sequencing by ligation instead of synthesis33, in which short oligonucleotide probes are 
ligated together.  
The short-read technologies are capable of producing billions of nucleotide sequences during each run, 
where each genome is sequenced multiple times in small random pieces, generating very large datasets. High 
throughput technologies differ in their biochemistry and arrays, but their workflows include some common 
steps: i) DNA extraction; ii) Library preparation; iii) Template preparation; and iv) Automated sequencing34.

The NGS technologies have revolutionized the analysis of DNA, but they are not perfect. Their requirement 
for PCR amplification introduces an extra step in which errors can occur, and the short read-lengths limit 
their ability to reconstruct repetitive sequences and detect structural variations, such as gene copy numbers, 
which can lead to fragmented genome assemblies.  
Illumina offers an impressive line of instruments that differ in their productivity, speed and price. From small 
benchtop sequencers able to produce 1.65-7.5 Gb of data per run, to production scale systems capable of 
producing thousands of Gb of data per run35. While the instruments vary in price, all of them require a 
significant initial investment, ranging up to several million dollars for the larger intruments. All of the 
instruments implement a similar chemistry using modified dNTPS, containing a reversible terminator, which 
blocks further polymerization, allowing only a single base to be added by the polymerase enzyme to each 
growing DNA strand. The terminator contains a fluorescent label, allowing a camera to detect it. The colored 
dNTPS are added to the templates and images are recorded after each incorporation-event. The terminators 
are then removed, and another cycle is begun. This result in a single base being added for each cycle, 
resulting in DNA sequence reads of uniform length. This process is extended across millions of fragments in 
a massively parallel fashion, leading to the large amounts of data that the high throughput technologies are 
known for.  
4.2 – Third-generation sequencing – Oxford Nanopore Technologies 
SMRT technologies is a relatively new approach to DNA sequencing, which are able to generate significantly 
longer reads than NGS technologies, resulting in less fragmented genome assemblies. The longer reads are 
particularly useful when sequencing repetitive regions of complex genomes, as well as phages and plasmids. 
==PAGE:23==
19 
 
In addition, SMRT technologies have a lower sequencing cost per base and simpler sample preparation 
without the need for PCR amplification, making them capable of generating data at a significantly faster rate 
than NGS technologies. The two most well-known SMRT technologies are developed by Pacific Biosciences 
(PacBio) and Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT). Both technologies are capable of producing very long 
reads, of 10k base pairs and longer, but with significantly higher error rates in the raw data than the NGS 
technologies. Error rates are 11-15% for PacBio and 5-15% for ONT36, but these error rates were initially 
much higher, and continue to drop. PacBio owns a patented circular-template approach, allowing them to 
sequence each template multiple times, resulting in a consensus sequences with much lower error rates 
(~0.001%)37. The circular-template consensus approach is suboptimal for DNA templates longer than ~3 kb, 
so in order to achieve the low error rate some of the advantages of the longer read lengths are lost37. PacBio 
uses a form of sequencing by synthesis, similar to Illumina, but it is capable of sequencing single molecules, 
removing the need for amplification of DNA.  
In contrast, ONT presents an entirely new approach to sequencing, one that does not rely on the synthesis of 
DNA, and is achievable with an initial investment of only ~$1000 dollars. By passing long stretches of single 
stranded DNA through a protein nanopore that is stabilized in an electrically resistant polymer membrane, 
single-molecule sequencing of DNA without synthesis or amplification is achieved (Figure 9). The sequence 
of nucleotides is inferred from changes in the ionic current across the synthetic membrane, as a single DNA 
molecule passes through the protein nanopore. The change in conductance is interpreted by a base-calling 
algorithm to identify pentamer sequences within the pore during transit, allowing for the reconstruction of 
sequences of long individual molecules of DNA in real-time.

The red box indicates the size of the R9.4 nanopore reading-head, capable of reading pentamer sequences.

Currently there exist two main sources of systematic errors in Nanopore sequencing: detection of 
homopolymers and proper detection of base-modifications, such as methylation. The detection of 
homopolymers is currently limited to stretches of five nucleotides, due to the size of the reading-head of the 
current R9.4 nanopore. In March 2019 ONT announced the early access release of a new nanopore R1039. 
The R10 nanopore has a dual reader-head allowing for the analysis of longer stretches of DNA, ranging up to 
8-10 nucleotides, which should improve the base-calling accuracy of homopolymer stretches. ONT has also 
recently released a more precise, albeit much slower, base-calling algorithm. This new high-accuracy base-
calling algorithm is optimized for methylation base-calling, specifically DCM, DAM and CpG-island 
methylation-sites40. Many more methylation-sites exist, but the fact that ONT is actively working on 
improving their base-calling algorithms to better handle them is a step in the right direction, and should help 
reduce systematic errors. The new algorithm is ~8 times slower than the old one, meaning that real-time data 
generation is not achievable with the more precise base-calling algorithm, unless run on a system that utilizes 
powerful GPUs to speed up the process. 
Like any other sequencing technology, Nanopore sequencing requires DNA extraction (~1-4 hours) and a 
library preparation step before sequencing can commence. During library preparation a sequencing adaptor 
with a motor protein is added to the end of a fragment. This motor protein is what controls the translocation-
speed of the fragment through the nanopore. ONT currently offers two different library preparation kits 
(Figure 10).

The Ligation Sequencing Kit offers the highest throughput, and allows the user to control the DNA fragment 
length, and as such the read lengths as well, but the library preparation takes several hours. In contrast, the 
Rapid Sequencing Kit can be completed in as little as half an hour, at the cost of lower throughput and loss 
of control of fragment lengths. The Rapid Sequencing Kit can be preferential in some circumstances, where a 
large amount of data is not required. This pairs well with clinical tests designed to provide a quick answer, 
utilizing the real-time data generation capabilities of the ONT devices. Barcoding of samples is also possible, 
allowing for multiplexed sequencing runs where up to 24 samples41 are sequenced in parallel, which can help 
keep the cost per sample down. When running 12 multiplexed samples per sequencing run, the cost per 
sample is similar to those seen in Illumina sequencing.  
4.2.1 – Advantages and limitations 
Nanopore sequencing has several advantages over traditional NGS platforms, the most obvious one being the 
ability to generate long reads, a property shared by PacBio, but due to the fact that Nanopore sequencing, 
directly sequences the input molecule without amplification or synthesis, it means that there is potentially no 
limit to how long the obtained reads can be. Sequencing reads exceeding two megabases have been reported 
using the Nanopore approach, showcasing its exceptional ability to sequence long stretches of DNA42. Reads 
of this length is not the norm, but the fact that it is even possible is a great achievement. A read of that size is 
comparable to the size of some smaller bacterial chromosomes, showcasing the potential to sequence entire 
chromosomes in one read. The only limiting factor to the length of DNA being sequenced is the initial 
library preparation, which requires a different approach than that used for short-read technologies, in order to 
minimize DNA shearing and maximize the fragment lengths of the input DNA43. 
The requirement of the motor protein to translocate the DNA, combined with the real-time data generation of 
Nanopore sequencing, enables a rather unique feature of this technology. It allows the user to predetermine 
DNA sequences of interest, and to only sequence DNA with a similar voltage signature. Once the first bases 
of a DNA molecule pass through the nanopore, if the voltage signature does not fit with the signature of 
interest, the nanopore can eject the DNA molecule, freeing up the nanopore for new reads. The motor protein 
is cleaved off during this, ensuring that the DNA molecule cannot be sequenced again. This method of target 
enrichment, termed Read Until44, could significantly reduce the time required from biological sampling to 
data inference, an extremely suitable feature for both in-field testing and point-of-care clinical applications. 
While this is a very interesting feature it is currently not feasible in practice, due to the extremely high 
requirements for computational-power. In the future this is likely to change. 
There are other advantages native to the new Nanopore approach, such as the fact that it detects nucleotides 
without the use of imaging equipment. This facilitates a downscaling in equipment size, with ONT’s 
MinION device weighing only 90g and with the size of a thumb drive. Making sequencing devices portable 
is potentially a great advantage for field researchers. MinION devices are not only small and portable, but 
==PAGE:26==
22 
 
they can also be powered through the USB port of a laptop computer, meaning that sequencing can be 
conducted at any location. The cost is also much lower compared to the NGS platforms, with the cost of the 
MinION device and initial set of reagents being around $100045. Other sequencing technologies require a 
substantial frontloaded investment to acquire the often large sequencing machines. ONT also offers more 
expensive and larger machines than the MinION, such as the GridION and PromethION, which are capable 
of higher throughput, comparable to that of other sequencing technologies such as Illumina. Recently ONT 
also released the Flongle, their smallest sequencing machine to date, specifically built to sequence a single 
isolate. The Flongle is built for single-use and is capable of producing 1GB of data in approximately 24 
hours, and at a cost of 100$ per Flongle. 
The ability to base-call reads as they are generated, and subsequently start the data analysis, facilitates the 
use of DNA sequencing in clinical testing that were previously unfeasible, with Nanopore sequencing 
potentially becoming a rapid and robust alternative to conventional clinical testing methods. Multiple 
examples of clinical tests that capitalize on this feature already exists, such as the online tool MetaPORE, 
which has been shown to be able to detect Chikungunya virus, Ebola virus and Hepatitis C virus directly 
from clinical samples with an unprecedented sample-to-answer turnaround time of less than 6 hours46. 
Another example is the use of the MinION sequencer to target 16S rDNA to identify the bacterial 
composition of a sample, shown to be possible within 2 hours of obtaining a DNA sample47. In a 2015 study 
of a hospital outbreak of Salmonella it was also shown that MinION data was able to acquire clinically 
relevant information within minutes of loading the DNA library, and could properly determine if a sample 
was part of the outbreak in less than 2 hours48. The combination of the MinION devices portability and real-
time data generation capabilities has also been utilized in surveillance and monitoring of viral outbreaks, 
such as the Ebola virus49, and is especially effective in resource-limited areas. Sequencing after receiving a 
blood sample could be done in less than an hour, and without the need for transportation of hazardous 
material, as the MinION sequencing can be done on site, as long as a laptop PC and the laboratory equipment 
required for DNA extraction and library preparation are available. In a 2018 study of real-time AMR 
detection50 in 40 Klebsiella pneumoniae,  it was found that AMR profiles could be generated from raw reads 
within 8 hours of receiving sub-cultured isolates, with a 77% agreement between the genotypic result and 
anticipated antimicrobial susceptibility results. After 14 hours, including an assembly step, the agreement 
rose to 92%, due to an increase in the ability to detect allelic variants and chromosomal point mutations. The 
authors claim that if these methods were implemented in real-time on a patient isolate, they would 
hypothetically shorten the median time for effective antimicrobial therapy by 26 and 20 hours respectively.

The high error rate of the individual reads can be mitigated by achieving a high read coverage and using 
consensus-based methods. Consensus sequences can reduce random errors, but will have no effect on the 
systematic errors which exist in the technology. The human genome has been assembled with an accuracy of 
99.44% using only Nanopore reads, followed by signal-based polishing, utilizing the underlying additional 
information in the ionic raw currents used for base-calling43. While this is indeed a big improvement over the 
error rate in the raw reads, an accuracy of 99.44% still results in 16.800.000 errors in a human genome 
(3Gbp), and 28.000 errors in a Gram-negative bacterial genome (5Mbp). The impact of these errors will be 
dependent on both the conducted analysis and whether or not the errors occur in stretches of sequence that 
are relevant to that analysis. 
Removal of systematic errors typically requires polishing with short read sequence data, a method known as 
hybrid assembly (Figure 11). Hybrid assemblies exploit the inherent strengths of the long reads, such as their 
ability to handle structural variants and repetitive regions, and combine it with the precision of short reads 
(e.g. from Illumina sequencing), generally resulting in highly accurate assemblies. Some errors still occur in 
hybrid assemblies, but they can be hard to characterize. In some cases there is evidence of a clear 
misassembly, in which case the errors can sometimes be resolved by hand-polishing conducted by an expert.

5 – Detecting AMR through sequencing 
The recent advancements in rapid and cost-effective DNA sequencing technologies have revolutionized 
diagnostic microbiology and surveillance of pathogens, with genotypic detection of AMR determinants being 
a prime example. An important part of any strategy to tackle AMR is to have rapid and accurate methods for 
identification of resistance markers, and whole genome sequencing (WGS) of bacterial strains have proven 
to be just that. Traditional antimicrobial susceptibility methods determine the “true” in vitro phenotype of a 
microorganism based on its growth in the presence of antimicrobials, subject to the interpretation of the 
laboratory conducting the test. In contrast, the WGS method relies on a translation from genotype to 
phenotype, where antimicrobial susceptibility and resistance is inferred based on the presence of resistance 
genes in an isolate. As such, determining the predicted phenotypic resistance profile from a genotype 
requires a comprehensive database of genes conferring AMR, and a method for accurately identifying those 
genes in sequencing data. Additional requirements are detailed information about the intrinsic resistance, and 
potential resistance as a result from chromosomal changes, for the species in question.  
Sequence-based predictions of susceptibility should be made with caution, as new resistance mechanisms or 
gene-variants not in the database can incorrectly give the impression of susceptibility. While all mechanisms 
of resistance might not currently be included in the resistance databases, WGS of pathogenic organisms also 
allow for backward screenings, where genomes of already sequenced pathogens are reanalyzed, as new 
information is added to the databases. This is a useful feature for determining how widespread newly 
identified resistance mechanisms are, without the need to redo expensive screenings. Despite these caveats, 
several studies have proven that it is possible to predict AMR from sequencing data, with both high 
sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (true negative rate)51. While reduced antimicrobial 
susceptibility can in many cases be inferred from the genotype, it is important to remember that some 
organisms are intrinsically resistant. In other cases chromosomal changes such as point mutations, which 
effect can be hard to characterize, can lead to reduced susceptibility. This leads to a requirement for priori 
knowledge about the organism when outlining its phenotypic resistance profile.  
5.1 – Database of resistance genes 
The in silico translation from genotype to phenotype relies on the quality and exhaustiveness of the currently 
available knowledge about antimicrobial resistance determinants (genes as well as point mutations). The 
database of resistance genes must be kept up to date with the newest information about resistance genes, so 
that all resistance genes in an isolate can be identified and used to infer the correct phenotype. Additionally, 
all entries in the database must contain quality annotations about what antimicrobials they confer resistance 
against, the predicted phenotype. This is not an easy task, as new variants of genes often emerge, and should 
ideally be experimentally tested to determine the phenotypic significance of the mutations in the new variant.  
==PAGE:29==
25 
 
For genes conferring resistance towards beta-lactam antimicrobials, it is especially important to properly test 
new variants phenotypically, as it is an area of extensive study, and new variants, which may not always 
share a phenotype with the most closely related variant, are often discovered. The emergence of new beta-
lactamase variants is a result of the widespread use of beta-lactam antimicrobials, where the selective 
pressure results in a high degree of variation within the class. This can be seen in Table 3, which shows a 
count of gene-variants and unique phenotypes for some of the most common antimicrobial classes, based on 
the ResFinder 4.0 database52. The high number of unique phenotypes for the beta-lactam class can be 
explained by the high number of different antimicrobial compounds in the class, leading to many different 
possible combinations of phenotypic resistance profiles. 
Table 3: A count of gene-variants and unique phenotypes for different antimicrobial classes, based on the information in the 
ResFinder 4.0 database. 
The link between antimicrobial resistance genes and phenotype is generally well understood for genes which 
only function is to confer resistance, such as beta-lactamase genes. The understanding of AMR as a result of 
mutational events in household genes, often point mutations, is much less complete. Mutations in many 
genes are known to result in resistance towards specific antimicrobial compounds, such as mutations in 
topoisomerase-genes which can reduce affinity for fluoroquinolones53, and mutations leading to a decrease in 
expression, such as the oprD-gene in Pseudomonas aeruginosa, which can result in resistance towards 
carbapenem beta-lactams54. This becomes even harder to track in cases where specific combinations of gene-
variants result in a phenotypic change. Databases need to be continuously updated with information about 
the effect of such mutational events, as well as newly emerged gene-variants, to correctly infer the phenotype 
based on the genotype. There currently exists at least 47 different databases for in silico AMR prediction51, 
ranging from simple reference databases which need to be employed in conjunction with the users own 
bioinformatics pipeline, to tools capable of performing the complete analysis. The databases vary in 
complexity to use, accepted input, database comprehensiveness, curation and many other properties, making 
it difficult to compare results across different databases. 
==PAGE:30==
26 
 
5.1.1 – ResFinder 
ResFinder 4.0 is a software and a database that provides exhaustive information on AMR genes. For this 
study only the database is used. The database contains sequence data on both AMR genes and mutational 
variants of household genes conferring resistance. The database also contains a comprehensive phenotypic 
translation table that lists the predicted phenotypic effect of all gene-variants included in the database. This is 
a key component for the analysis conducted in this study, as it allows for additional comparisons of predicted 
phenotypic resistance profiles, instead of only comparing the detected gene-variants. The software does not 
accept protein sequences as input, but can be used with both nucleotide raw reads, contigs and assembled 
genomes. Publication is not a strict requirement for inclusion in the ResFinder database, but gene-variants 
must have a GenBank number, and expert review of the GenBank entry is required. The database is 
periodically updated and curated.  
The European Food and Safety Authority (EFSA) has in an attempt to harmonize the monitoring of 
resistance in zoonotic and indicator bacteria from food and food-producing animals, suggested the 
implementation of WGS in combination with the ResFinder software, to replace current phenotypic methods. 
In 2025 this approach will become mandatory for the monitoring of specific cases, such as AmpC- and 
carbapenemase-producing Salmonella51.This showcases the evolution currently happening in WGS, as well 
as the confidence in the curation of the ResFinder database. 
5.2 – Identifying resistance genes in sequencing data 
The resistance gene database contains sequence information for all the relevant genes. The next step is to 
identify which of the genes in the database are present in a sample. The two most common bioinformatics 
approaches used when searching for specific genes in a sample are mapping of raw reads and alignment of 
de novo assembled contigs. Both methods compare sequencing data to template DNA sequences with known 
and annotated properties, such as the contents of the ResFinder database. Assembly-based methods might 
discard reads due to low coverage, or due to the presence of very similar genes, which can be troublesome 
for assembly algorithms to handle correctly14. This is not a problem in methods based on mapping of raw 
reads, as all reads are included.  Reads that result from contamination of the sample will generally not be 
included in a de novo assembly, but they will be included in the mapping of raw reads, which is a caveat that 
one should be aware of. Assembly-based methods might also have the added information of knowing if a 
gene is chromosomally-located or on a plasmid, which can be helpful in some cases. 
5.2.1 – KMA 
KMA55,56 is a newly developed tool designed to map and align raw reads directly against redundant 
databases, specifically designed with rapid and accurate bacterial genome analysis in mind. Other mapping 
tools, such as the often used Burrows-Wheeler Aligner, were designed to map to large reference genomes, 
==PAGE:31==
27 
 
and later on applied to microbiology51. AMR gene-variants are smaller fragments of DNA, and often many 
highly similar variants exist, making them an ideal target for KMA.  
KMA is in principle capable of detecting both acquired resistance genes and point mutations that lead to 
resistance, as long as the chosen database includes both. The tool uses k-mer seeding to speed up mapping, 
and applies the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm for accurate alignment of k-mer seed extensions. Other tools 
for mapping raw reads suffer from random selection when there is a tie between templates for best match, a 
problem that is exacerbated when using redundant databases. KMA overcomes this by using a novel scoring 
scheme called ConClave, which sums together alignment scores for all reads mapping to a template, and uses 
this score to determine the most probable correct template, in the event of a read mapping equally well to 
multiple templates. 
5.2.2 – ABRicate 
ABRicate57 is a de novo assembly-based method, based on the popular alignment tool BLAST, for mass 
screening of contigs for AMR or virulence genes. It only supports contigs as input, which means that 
assembly of raw reads is required prior to running the tool. In some cases the read lengths generated by 
SMRT technologies will be long enough to not require assembly. Within this study, ABRicate was used to 
detect AMR genes in hybrid assemblies, which were then used as reference points when comparing with raw 
read mappings. ABRicate can only detect resistance as a result of acquired resistance genes and not 
chromosomal point mutations.

6.1.2.1 – Quality filtration 
Reads generated by Nanopore sequencing are assigned an average read phred quality score (Table 2) during 
base-calling. The quality score is a measure of the expected amount of errors in the read. To remove the most 
error-prone reads, a quality-score cut-off set at q8 is applied, corresponding to a maximum error rate of 
~15.85%. All reads with an average read quality below this is discarded from the further analysis. As the 
technology becomes more precise and sequencing errors more rare, this cut-off may be increased.  
Figure 12 shows the distribution of reads (left), as well as the read quality distribution over time (right) for a 
single isolate, CPO20180005.  As can be seen in the figure, most reads are above the q8 threshold, and a 
large portion of the removed reads are those with relatively low read length. The proportion of reads 
removed by the q8 filtration of the 24 isolates ranged from ~10%-15%. The violin plot on the right shows 
that read quality scores are highest at the start of the sequencing run, with a gradual drop-off as time 
progresses. An interesting observation with rapid real-time analyses in mind.

6.2.1 – Simulating real-time analysis 
Part of the aim of this study is to answer how the amount of sequencing data, which can be translated into 
sequencing time, influences the capability to correctly identify AMR determinants in Nanopore data. 
Running new sequencing runs to test the NanoporeFiltrationCompare pipeline is unfeasible in terms of cost 
and time. Fortunately every read generated by Nanopore’s MinION contain a timestamp and by sorting reads

timeframes. By running the analysis on these subsets of reads, it is possible to analyze the change in results 
as data is generated. This information can be used to guide the analysis of future sequencing runs, where 
real-time answers are required.

6.2.3 – Filtration of results 
The output from KMA contains a list of resistance gene-variants found in the sequencing data, with 
information about the identity, coverage and depth. Coverage is the portion of the template gene that is 
covered by the query gene found in the sequencing data. Low coverage can be indicative of a truncated gene, 
==PAGE:37==
33 
 
which will most likely have lost its function. The identity represents how alike the template and query 
sequence is, based on stretches of sequence covered by both, meaning that truncated genes can still have a 
high identity. The lower the identity, the more nucleotide differences there are between the template and 
query, and as a result the probability of them having the same function is also lower. Minimum thresholds 
for including genes in the further analysis were chosen to be 90% coverage and 90% identity, as a way of 
only including presumably functioning genes. The thresholds were chosen based on those used in the article 
describing KMA, and mirrors the thresholds used by the researchers at Statens Serum Institut. The hybrid 
assembly results generated by ABRicate also contains information on coverage and identity, and the same 
thresholds were applied there. 
Depth is a measure of the amount of reads that is aligned to a template. Due to the relatively high error rate 
in the Nanopore raw reads, and the sometimes small differences between gene-variants in the database, it is 
to be expected that some reads will be mapped to an incorrect but related gene-template. Incorrect in the 
sense that it is not the gene-variant present in the sample, but the mapping will see it as such due to 
sequencing errors. When running multiple samples on the same Nanopore sequencing run there is a small 
risk of barcode contamination, which occurs when sequencing errors in the barcode result in a read from one 
sample being miss-assigned to a different sample. Contamination can also occur during the growth of the 
microorganism, as well as during the DNA extraction. By filtrating gene-variants based on their depth, it is 
possible to limit the effect of both contamination and wrong variant-calls due to sequencing errors. The depth 
filtration is only applied to the Nanopore data. 
The filtration is done in two steps both including the grouping of genes in gene-groups, in which the first 3 
letters (6 in the case of beta-lactamase genes) of gene-names are used to group them. Genes within the same 
gene-group have similar sequences, so while sequencing errors might result in calling the wrong variant, the 
risk of the variant called not being in the same gene-group as the correct variant is extremely low. By 
grouping the gene-variants in gene-groups, a representation of the amount of reads containing similar gene-
variants is obtained, regardless of how sequencing errors have affected the alignment.  
Depth thresholds were chosen based on manual observations and have been optimized on the sample set of 
21 MDR Escherichia coli isolates. If this method were to be tested on a wider range of isolates, a more 
stringent method for determining the optimum thresholds should be introduced. 
The first filtration is a minimum depth requirement for a gene-group to be included in the further analysis, 
intended as a way of eliminating reads resulting from contamination events. A similar filtration is used in the 
paper describing and testing the alignment performance of the tool KMA56 on Illumina reads, where a 
minimum gene-depth threshold of five is applied. For this study, as the filtration is applied to Nanopore 
reads, which are more error-prone than Illumina reads, the filtration is applied to the gene-group instead of 
==PAGE:38==
34 
 
the individual gene-variants. The threshold is relative to the average genome coverage, as the depth should 
increase as more data is included in the analysis. The minimum depth for gene-groups was set at 20% of the 
average genome coverage. The filtration is meant to discard low depth gene-groups that are most likely a 
result of contamination, the gene-group tet in Figure 14 would be an example of this. 
 
Figure 14: Example of depth filtration for an isolate with an average genome coverage of 10. Minimum gene-group depth is 
set at 20% of the average genome coverage, corresponding to a minimum depth of two. This removes the gene-group tet from 
the analysis. Individual gene-variants are discarded based on their depth relative to the highest depth gene-variant in the 
same gene-group, with the minimum depth set at 45%. This discards the gene-variants blaTEM-77_1 and blaOXA-372_1. 
The second filtration is of incorrectly called gene-variants, based on the depth of the gene with the highest 
depth within a gene-group. While sequencing errors will result in some reads being assigned to incorrect 
gene-variants, the correct variant should in most cases be the variant with the highest depth, unless incorrect 
calls are due to systematic errors. The reason for not simply choosing the highest depth gene is that it is 
possible for multiple genes from the same gene-group to be present in a sample. The depth threshold for 
individual gene-variants was set at 45% of the depth of the highest depth gene from that gene-group. Using 
the blaTEM-group from Figure 14 as an example, where blaTEM-1B_1 is the correct gene-variant but 
sequencing errors have led to reads being aligned to two additional gene-variants, blaTEM-76_1 and 
blaTEM-77_1. The depth filtration correctly removes blaTEM-77_1, but includes blaTEM-76_1 in the 
further analysis. In this specific case, the two included blaTEM-variants exhibit different phenotypes, 
making it highly important that only the correct gene-variant is included. Correctly removing blaTEM-76_1 
at low amounts of data is problematic in multiple isolates of this study, but the filtration improves as more 
data is included in the analysis, as will be shown in the results section. Most highly-similar gene-variants 
exhibit the same phenotype, meaning that the inclusion of an additional gene-variant will not always result in 
a clinical effect, such as the case of blaNDM in Figure 14, where both blaNDM-10_1 and blaNDM-22_1 
share the same phenotype. As a result, the amount of data required to correctly identify an isolate’s genotypic 
==PAGE:39==
35 
 
resistance profile depends on the presence of gene-variants such as blaTEM-1B_1 or blaTEM-76_1, which 
are highly-similar but phenotypically different gene-variants 
6.2.4 – Comparison with reference 
Gene-variants called by ABRicate on the hybrid assembly generated by Nanopore- and Illumina reads are 
used as the reference points. The KMA results on reads from Illumina and Nanopore, where Nanopore data 
have been split into smaller files to simulate real-time analysis, is compared with the assembled reference. 
Ideally it should be possible to identify the correct gene-variants in all the isolates, but realistically this 
requires more precision than currently available, due to sequencing errors in Nanopore data. This however 
does not mean that relevant and important information cannot be garnered by the analysis of Nanopore data 
and to demonstrate this, a set of analyses requiring increasing precision was constructed. From the 
phenotypic translation of gene-variants, based on the information in the ResFinder database, gene-variants 
have been grouped based on four different properties. Groupings are: i) Antimicrobial class; ii) Phenotype; 
iii) Gene-group; and iv) Gene-variant. Using the simulation of real-time data generation, the amount of data 
required to correctly identify the contents of a sample at these four different levels of precision can then be 
obtained. Results generated from the raw reads and those generated from the hybrid assembly are compared 
across these four groupings. An example of grouping of five gene-variants is shown in Table 6.  
Table 6: Example of information available for each gene-variant. The color coding visualizes whether a gene-variant is

To explain how this works in practice, imagine that blaTEM-1B_1 was detected in the reference hybrid 
assembly. When comparing at the level of antimicrobial class, a correct hit in the Nanopore reads would then 
be any of the four beta-lactam gene-variants. At phenotype-level blaTEM-1B_1, blaTEM-2_2 and blaSHV-
1_1 all share the same predicted phenotype, so the presence of any of these three gene-variants in the reads 
would be considered a correct hit. At gene-group level only genes from the same gene-group and with the 
same predicted phenotype are grouped. So while blaTEM-76_1 is in the same gene-group as blaTEM-1B_1 
and blaTEM-2_2, it is not grouped with them due a difference in predicted phenotype. At the gene-variant 
level only the correct variant, blaTEM-1B_1, is considered a correct hit.

7.2 – Predicted phenotypes 
While identifying resistance at antimicrobial class-level can in some cases be considered too imprecise for 
clinical use, predicting the resistance at phenotypic-level should in most cases be precise enough to guide a 
treatment plan. Any information beyond this, such as the gene-group or specific gene-variant conferring said 
phenotype, can be considered an added bonus, but will in most cases not have any significant effect on the 
chosen treatment. Though being able to correctly identify resistance at these more precise levels increases 
the trust in the result. Figure 18 shows the results of the comparison of predicted phenotypes, with the

8 – Discussion 
The SMRT sequencing technology developed by ONT is radically different from any other sequencing 
technology available today, with its ability to generate sequencing data in real-time without the need for 
synthesis. The advantages of Nanopore sequencing over short-read sequencing technologies are plentiful, 
prime examples being the ability to generate reads with potentially no limit to the length, the ability to detect 
base-modifications and the ability to generate sequencing data in real-time. The only real limitation of the 
technology is the error rate, which currently is much higher than error rates seen in Illumina and other short-
read technologies. Precision is an extremely important property of any sequencing technology, and for many 
applications, especially those centered on research, the advantages of Nanopore sequencing will not be 
enough to warrant the decrease in precision. Outbreak detection and tracking the evolution of pathogens for 
example requires highly accurate draft genomes, but even in these cases where the low error rate of Illumina 
is paramount, the advantages of long reads are being incorporated into hybrid assemblies, to create more 
accurate and contiguous genome assemblies. In clinical settings the importance of time-to-answer is more 
apparent than for research purposes, and in this case Nanopore sequencing is uniquely positioned to provide 
rapid answers. While precision is still of vital importance in clinical settings, where inaccurate results can 
have a direct effect on patients, the definition of what can be considered accurate enough can be slightly 
different than for research purposes.  
8.1 – Precision in Nanopore AMR detection 
Detection of AMR determinants is a prime example of a case where reduced precision can be acceptable if 
the corresponding reduction in time-to-answer is considered to be more important. Correctly detecting 
resistance towards antimicrobial classes for example does not require complete accuracy in sequencing, as 
sequencing errors at most lead to detection of incorrect variants that closely resemble the correct variant. 
These incorrect variant-calls might result in a different phenotype, but the amount of errors that would be 
required to result in a change at antimicrobial class level is highly unlikely and even if this should occur in a 
single read, it would be removed during the depth filtration. As mentioned, the error rates in Nanopore 
sequencing can lead to calling the incorrect gene-variant, but as long as the called variant shares the same 
phenotype as the correct variant, the clinical effect of this is minimal.  
Based on the analysis of 21 Escherichia coli isolates, detection of resistance towards antimicrobial classes is 
possible at an average genome coverage of nine (Figure 17), corresponding to a sequencing time of around 
one hour and 15 minutes (Table 5), for a multiplexed sequencing run of 12 isolates. Detection of the correct 
predicted phenotypic resistance profile for all 21 isolates is possible at coverage of 40 (Figure 20), 
corresponding to a sequencing time of around five and a half hours (Table 5). Conversely, only three of the 
21 isolates correctly identified all gene-variants with this amount of data (Figure 21). This showcases the fact 
that highly-similar gene-variants often share the same phenotype, which reduces the clinical effect of minor 
==PAGE:54==
50 
 
sequencing errors. In some special cases highly-similar gene-variants exhibit different predicted phenotypes, 
such as blaTEM-1B_1 and blaTEM-76_1 (Table 12), where different predicted phenotypes are seen despite 
only a single nucleotide difference between the two gene-variants. If not for the incorrect calls of these two 
variants in two of the isolates, identifying the correct phenotypic resistance profile for all isolates would have 
been possible at coverage 30. This illustrates the ability to weed out random sequencing-errors by generating 
more data. On the other hand, increasing the amount of data has no effect in cases where systematic errors 
lead to an incorrect call of a variant. ONT is currently working hard on reducing the systematic errors of the 
technology, with the introduction of the R10 nanopore, thought to reduce the systematic errors in 
homopolymer sequences, as well as the ongoing improvements to their base-calling software, to reduce 
sequencing errors and improve detection of base-modifications. 
While somewhat expected, that it would be difficult to identify the correct gene-variants in the Nanopore 
data, due to the known high error rate, it came as a surprise that the same problem was evident in the 
Illumina data. At coverage 20 and above Nanopore performed better than Illumina, identifying the correct 
gene-variants in three isolates as opposed to the two isolates in Illumina. A potential explanation for this 
could be that the draft genome hybrid assemblies put too much emphasis on the Nanopore reads in areas 
where there is no need to correct the Illumina results, instead of only using the long-reads in areas where the 
short-reads are lacking, such as repetitive regions and structural variations. Whether trusting the hybrid 
assemblies or the Illumina reads when it comes to correctly identifying gene-variants, the conclusion remains 
the same; Nanopore raw reads are, at least with the current 9.4 nanopore and standard base-calling algorithm, 
too error-prone to correctly identify all gene-variants. 
Nanopore outperformed Illumina at all other levels of precision, due to wrong variant-calls as a result of the 
short read-lengths of Illumina, such as aligning reads to two different variants resulting in low coverage for 
both, and not detecting that a gene was truncated. These types of errors would most likely be removed if the 
Illumina reads were assembled into a draft genome, but nonetheless it displays one of the advantages of the 
long reads from Nanopore. 
8.1.1 – Effect of depth filtration 
The relatively high error rate in Nanopore sequencing will undeniably lead to incorrect variant-calls, 
especially for genes where multiple highly-similar variants exist in the database, which is often the case for 
especially the beta-lactamase genes. In addition to this, there is a potential problem of contamination events. 
Correctly dealing with contamination events is an important part of the analysis of raw reads, as there is no 
previous filtration of these, as opposed to analysis of draft genomes, where reads that results from 
contamination are not included in the assembly. The problem of contamination events is exacerbated when 
running multiplexed sequencing runs, which is often the case for Nanopore sequencing, due to potential 
barcode misclassifications. The effect of barcode-contamination can be completely removed by running a 
==PAGE:55==
51 
 
single isolate per sequencing run, which the Flongle machine from ONT is specifically built to do. The 
amount of barcode contamination is proportional to the amount of sequencing errors, so the problem should 
be reduced as the precision of Nanopore sequencing increases, leaving only contamination that occur during 
growth and DNA extraction of the microorganism. 
By imposing a minimum depth requirement for gene-groups, the effect of contamination is minimized, as 
can clearly be seen by comparing Figure 16 and Figure 17. Choosing an absolute value for the minimum 
depth is imprecise when dealing with different amounts of data, and as such the minimum depth was set to 
be relative to the total amount of data, at 20% of the average genome coverage. After removal of 
contaminants, only gene-groups actually present in the sample should remain, but due to sequencing errors, 
often with read mappings to multiple closely-related variants within each gene-group. Distinguishing 
between these variants can be difficult at low coverages, but as more data is generated the distribution of 
random errors should result in the correct variant having the highest depth. This is not always the case, as is 
evident by Figure 21, but due to the fact that closely-related variants often share the same predicted 
phenotype, it can still be possible to correctly identify the predicted phenotypic resistance profile. The real 
problem occurs with gene-variants like blaTEM-1B_1 and blaTEM-76_1, where a single nucleotide 
variation can result in a different predicted phenotype, but this can also be resolved if enough data is 
included in the analysis.  
To remove incorrect gene-variants such as blaTEM-76_1, a depth filtration relative to the amount of reads 
mapped to closely-related variants was applied, as opposed to the total amount of data (average genome 
coverage) used in the previous filtration. The reason for not applying an absolute minimum-depth threshold, 
like the minimum-depth of five used in the performance evaluation of KMA56 on Illumina reads, is due to the 
sequencing errors expected from Nanopore sequencing. Some gene-groups contain many highly-similar 
gene-variants, and the risk of Nanopore reads being mapped to a wrong variant is high. This could 
potentially lead to all the gene-variants being discarded, due to none of the variants being above the absolute 
minimum-depth threshold, despite the total amount of reads mapped to these highly-similar variants being 
above the threshold. As such, a filtration relative to the depth of the gene-group was found to be the best 
option, with a depth threshold for variants belonging to a specific gene-group set at 45% of the depth of the 
highest depth gene in that gene-group. One obvious problem with this approach would be the presence of 
two gene-variants in a sample from the same gene-group, in which one of them is present on a high copy 
number plasmid. This would most likely lead to the low copy number variant being discarded. Fortunately, 
not many AMR genes are present on high copy number plasmids, and as sequencing errors are reduced, the 
filtration threshold should be able to be reduced as well, which should minimize the effect of this.

8.1.3 – Optimizing thresholds through the use of machine learning 
The thresholds chosen for the depth filtrations were based on manual analysis, and could very well be over-
fitted to the isolates of this study. Chances are that the optimal thresholds would change as more isolates 
were included in the analysis, especially when including isolates from different species. Improvements made 
to reduce sequencing errors, such as the introduction of the R10 nanopore and implementation of the new 
high-accuracy base-calling algorithm could likewise result in changes to the optimal thresholds. The optimal 
phred quality score cut-off might also be different than the chosen q8 cut-off, and could similarly change as 
the technology improves. Ideally the optimal values for all these thresholds would be decided by rigorous 
analysis of each of their individual effects as well as in combination. If this method were to be used in a 
clinical setting, it would make sense to implement an artificial neural network to continually optimize the 
threshold values, as more data becomes available and the general sequencing technology improves. 
Similarly the thresholds to discard gene-variants based on coverage and identity could potentially be 
optimized, but this would require results from phenotypic susceptibility testing for comparison. Perhaps a 
single value for these cut-offs is also too generalizing, and individual cut-offs depending on the gene-variants 
would yield better results, albeit the amount of data required to generate these values might be unfeasible.  
==PAGE:58==
54 
 
The required amount of data to correctly produce the genotypic resistance profiles is also most likely over-
fitted to the isolates of this study. The amount of data required may change as more isolates are included, or 
if some of the previously mentioned improvements are implemented. A similar machine-learning approach 
to determining the optimal amount of data could be implemented, where information such as the bacterial 
species and the presence of problematic genes could be used as parameters. 
The amount of data required to properly implement and train this type of artificial neural network far exceeds 
the 21 isolates used in this study, but if it were to be implemented it would allow for continual improvements 
to the method, potentially resulting in both faster and more precise results.

8.2.1 – High-accuracy base-calling in real-time 
While the actual effect of high-accuracy base-calling cannot be deduced based on only a subset of reads from 
two isolates, a clear reduction in wrong variant-calls was evident from the high-accuracy base-calling on the 
blaTEM-1B_1 reads in CPO20180005 and CPO20180062. The reason it was only possible to test on such a 
small subset of the total dataset was due to the time required to run the high-accuracy algorithm. The 
algorithm has a high computational requirement, which the current system at Statens Serum Institute is not 
able to handle in a manner fast enough for real-time base-calling. The high-accuracy base-calling algorithm 
should be able to run in real-time on systems utilizing powerful GPUs. Running the analysis on such a 
system would potentially allow for more precise results, meaning that depth thresholds could potentially be 
lowered, the amount of wrong variant-calls should be reduced resulting in a lower requirement on the 
amount of data, and it might even increase the precision in such a way that chromosomal point mutations can 
be accurately detected, and all of these improvements would be possible while still conducting the analysis in 
real-time. 
An alternative to this would be to run the standard base-calling algorithm first, extract all reads containing 
resistance genes using the tool ReadExtractor, and then run high-accuracy base-calling only on these reads. 
The analysis could then be performed only on the high-accuracy reads containing resistance genes. This 
==PAGE:60==
56 
 
would reduce the amount of reads to run high-accuracy base-calling on extensively and would significantly 
reduce the additional time required to run the algorithm. Exactly how much additional time is required to run 
the analysis like this, compared to the standard base-calling approach, is hard to predict. But if the increase in 
precision from the high-accuracy algorithm is significant, it should produce the correct result in a quicker 
fashion, as it should result in a lower requirement on the amount of data needed to correctly create the 
genotypic resistance profiles. 
8.3 – Choice of database 
The ResFinder 4.0 database was chosen for this study due to its comprehensive phenotypic information on 
acquired resistance genes, allowing for comparison between a hybrid assembled reference genome and the 
raw Nanopore data at different levels of precision in regards to antimicrobial resistance. The aim of this 
study was to identify the effect of the noise generated by Nanopores sequencing errors, and at the same try to 
reduce it through data filtration. For this particular objective, seeing as we are comparing two sets of results 
both generated by the same database, the comprehensiveness of the database is of less importance, and no 
other databases were considered. If the goal on the other hand was to compare the genotypic resistance 
profiles generated by the Nanopore data with phenotypic resistance profiles generated by in vitro 
antimicrobial susceptibility testing, which would most likely be required before the method could be used in 
a clinical setting, the choice of database would be an important parameter. In this case, a more thorough 
examination of the effect of different databases on the final resistance profile should be conducted. 
8.3.1 – Chromosomal point mutations 
If the genotypic resistance profiles were to be compared with those generated by in vitro antimicrobial 
susceptibility testing, it would be important that the genotypic method was able to detect resistance due to 
point mutations in household-genes. Unfortunately due to time restraints, this was not tested in this study, but 
the ResFinder 4.0 database contains information on the effect of many of these mutational events known to 
confer resistance. For clinical use, any choice of database would have to include this type of information and 
would require continuous updating as any new resistance determinants were discovered. 
A previous study of real-time AMR profiling in Klebsiella pneumonia found that doing assembly of 
Nanopore data increased the agreement with phenotypic antimicrobial susceptibility tests from 77% to 
92%50, due to the increased ability to detect allelic variants and point mutations. This exemplifies the 
importance of being able to detect these single nucleotide differences that can result in a change of the 
resistance profile. The accuracy required to detect these types of point mutations would most likely be 
similar to that required to detect the correct gene-variants, which was not possible for all gene-variants for 
the majority of isolates in this study. If Nanopore sequencing can reach a point where correct identification 
of point mutations is possible using real-time analysis, the potential for rapid and accurate genotypic 
==PAGE:61==
57 
 
resistance profiling is starting to look extremely promising. Based on the results of this study, detection of 
point mutations in real-time analysis is most likely not possible yet, but it might be in the near future. 
Potential optimizations that could be made to improve the detection of point mutations are the 
implementation of high-accuracy base-calling, the new R10 nanopore, changes to the phred quality score 
filtration or by including more data in the analysis. The only way to know for sure would be to conduct the 
analysis. 
As the genotypic resistance profiles generated in the Klebsiella pneumoniae study were tested against 
phenotypic resistance profiles, which are not available for the Escherichia coli isolates tested in this study, a 
direct comparison of the results cannot be made. But it is still interesting to compare the sequencing times 
found to be optimal for genotypic resistance profiling. In this study, the sequencing time required to correctly 
identify the predicted phenotypic resistance profiles in all 21 isolates, excluding point mutations, is ~5 hours 
and 34 minutes (coverage 40). Add to this the DNA extraction and library preparation steps and the time 
requirement looks similar to the 8 hours for real-time genotypic AMR profiling, excluding point mutations, 
deemed optimal in the study of Klebsiella pneumoniae. An interesting observation though, is that while the 
sequencing times used in this study are based on multiplexed sequencing runs of 12 isolates per run, the 
Klebsiella pneumonia study sequenced a single isolate per run. Based on the workflow of the Klebsiella 
pneumoniae study, the sequencing aspect was 2 hours out of the total 8 hours used in the real-time method. 
This amounts to ~1/3 of the sequencing time found optimal in this study. But due to no multiplexing, the 
result is that ~4 times the amount sequencing data was used to construct the genotypic resistance profiles of 
the Klebsialla pneumonias, compared to the data amounts used in this study. As the real-time approach in the 
Klebsiella pneumoniae study was unable to detect resistance as a result of point mutations, the genotypic 
resistance profiles showing 77% agreement with the phenotypic resistance profiles, must be similar to those 
generated in this study. It is then interesting why they would require such a large amount of data compared to 
those found optimal in this study. Some differences between the approach used in the Klebsialla pneumoniae 
study and this one are: A slightly newer version of the R9.4 nanopore was used in this study (R9.4.1), the 
analysis is of two different bacterial species, and perhaps most importantly, the real-time analysis was 
simulated in this study, whereas it was actually tested in real-time in the Klebsialla pneumoniae study. To 
achieve this real-time analysis, the Antimicrobial Resistance Mapping Application (ARMA) from ONT was 
used. As mentioned, a direct comparison with the results of their study is difficult, due to differences in the 
methods used and the bacterial species tested, but potential explanations for the lower requirement on the 
data amount seen in this study, is the use of KMA for rapid mapping to resistance genes, and the subsequent 
data filtration. Potential explanations as to why additional data was required in the Klebsiella pneumoniae 
study is the fact that their analysis included ~twice the amount of isolates, and that they ran the analysis in 
real-time, which might add additional challenges not considered in the simulation of real-time analysis used 
in this study.

9 – Conclusion 
By comparing the genotypic antimicrobial resistance profiles generated by analysis on Nanopore sequencing 
data with those generated by draft genome hybrid assemblies, achieved using the Python pipeline 
NanoporeFiltrationCompare, it has been shown that at sufficient amounts of data and with relevant data 
filtration, the predicted phenotypic resistance profile can be correctly identified for all 21 Escherichia coli 
isolates in this study, with some disagreement on the presence of specific gene-variants. By simulating the 
real-time data-generation of Nanopore sequencing, the effect of the amount of data included in the analysis 
has also been evaluated. It was found that resistance towards antimicrobial classes could be correctly 
identified in all 21 isolates at an average genome coverage of 9, corresponding to a sequencing time of ~1 
hour and 15 minutes, for isolates run on multiplexed sequencing run of 12 isolates. Resistance at predicted 
phenotypic level, with additional information about the gene-groups conferring said resistance, could 
correctly be identified for all 21 isolates at an average genome coverage of 40, corresponding to a 
sequencing time of ~5 hours and 34 minutes. A problematic gene-variant was detected in multiple isolates, 
where a highly-similar gene-variant with a single nucleotide difference exhibited a different phenotype, 
resulting in the need for additional data to identify the correct phenotype. After running high-accuracy base-
calling on this problematic gene-variant the predicted phenotypic resistance profile could be correctly 
identified for all isolates except one, at an average genome coverage of 20, corresponding to a sequencing 
time of ~2 hours and 45 minutes. At coverage 20, a single isolate incorrectly discarded a gene-variant due to 
depth filtration. Reducing sequencing errors, such as through the use of the new R10 nanopore, or by 
implementing high-accuracy base-calling, could lower the threshold for depth filtration, potentially resolving 
the issue of incorrectly discarding gene-variants at low coverages. 
Detection of AMR determinants through KMA and subsequent filtration of the results can be done in a 
matter of seconds to minutes, facilitating the creation of a tool capable of real-time analysis as sequencing 
data is generated. Genotypic antimicrobial resistance profiles could be updated as the data is generated, with 
the amount of data included in the analysis used as guideposts for the accuracy of the results. Before this 
could be implemented, further study of the effect of data-amount should be conducted, and the analysis 
should include a wider range of bacterial species. The optimal filtration thresholds should be revisited as 
more species are included in the analysis, and the effect of high-accuracy base-calling should be tested. The 
required precision will also depend on the presence of potentially problematic gene-variants, in which 
highly-similar gene-variants exhibit different phenotypes. A database of these gene-variants could be 
constructed based on sequence homology, and the presence of these could be used to indicate if a larger 
amount of data is required to accurately determine the resistance profile. Additionally, a study of the ability 
to detect resistance as a result of chromosomal point mutations should be conducted, and the agreement 
between genotypic resistance profiles and phenotypic resistance profiles generated by in vitro antimicrobial 
susceptibility testing should be studied comprehensively. When comparing the resistance profiles, the choice 
==PAGE:63==
59 
 
of database should also be considered, as different databases might lead to different genotypic resistance 
profiles, depending on how well curated they are. 
To summarize, while a lot of work still remains to be done to prove the efficacy of Nanopore sequencing at 
the level required for clinical use, it appears to be possible to rapidly and accurately predict genotypic 
resistance profiles from acquired resistance genes using Nanopore sequencing. Continued improvements to 
the technology and the subsequent data-analysis could permit real-time genotypic AMR profiling from 
clinical isolates in the not too distant future.

10 – Future perspectives 
The results of this study exemplifies the ability of depth filtration to reduce the noise generated by 
sequencing-errors in Nanopore raw reads, allowing for precise and rapid determination of bacterial genotypic 
resistance profiles from acquired resistance genes, at both antimicrobial class and predicted phenotypic level. 
But while the results are promising, a lot of work still remains to sufficiently prove the effectiveness of the 
method for clinical use. In this section I will outline the steps that should have been taken, to improve the 
robustness and precision of the method, if more time had been available. 
The first step would be to re-run the base-calling of all isolates using the high-accuracy algorithm. Statens 
Serum Institut is currently in the process of acquiring a more powerful computational system utilizing a 
powerful GPU, which should allow for high-accuracy base-calling in a similar timeframe as the one 
currently achieved using the standard base-calling algorithm. Alternatively, all reads containing resistance 
genes could be extracted using the tool ReadExtractor, and high-accuracy base-calling could be run only on 
these reads. The effect of the more precise algorithm should be evaluated by re-running the analysis and 
comparing the results with those generated from the standard base-calling algorithm. If a significant increase 
in precision is seen, specifically at gene-variant level, then it would make sense to expand the analysis to also 
cover resistance as a result of point mutations in chromosomal genes. This information is already available in 
the ResFinder database, so the implementation should be relatively straightforward. As ABRicate is not 
capable of detecting resistance as a result of these point mutations, a different method for creating the 
genotypic resistance profiles for the hybrid assemblies would have to be found. The analysis should then be 
extended to also include additional bacterial species other than just Escherichia coli. At this point the optimal 
values for the various filtration thresholds and requirements regarding data amounts would most likely have 
to be re-evaluated. This would be a fitting time to consider an automation of that process. The next step 
would be to compare the genotypically predicted resistance profiles with growth-based phenotypic resistance 
profiles, and in connection with this the choice of database would have to be re-evaluated. ResFinder is a 
popular and well-curated database, so it might be found to be the ideal choice, but it would require further 
research to be certain of this. Assuming all this goes well, and a high agreement with the phenotypic 
resistance profiles is seen, the last step would be to develop a tool capable of running the real-time analysis 
and producing a meaningful output-format for clinical use.
