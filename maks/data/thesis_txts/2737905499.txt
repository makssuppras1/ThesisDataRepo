Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to explore the effects of leeway and travel speed which
is induced by wind assisted propulsion, on increased resistance of the KVLCC2 ship.
The implementation of a leeboard and its benefits to reducing the experienced resistance
is subsequently examined. To achieve this, theoretical solutions in combination with a
potential-flow and Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes solver (Shipflow) have been used. A
model of the KVLCC2 was developed and validated which was then used for subsequent
calculations. The findings show that adding a leeboard to a ship experiencing leeway,
can influence the location of the center of hydrodynamic pressure, moving it closer to
midship. The findings also suggest that implementing a leeboard has positive effects on
the rudder action and WASP induced leeway, but a negative effect on the heeling stability.
The results provide a new and currently unexplored solution to the drift caused by wind
assisted propulsion systems which will no doubt become more popular as further inroads
are made into the reduction of greenhouse gasses.

Introduction
In recent years there has been a growing awareness towards sustainability in day to day
activities. The world has never before emitted as high a volume of greenhouse gasses
to the atmosphere and a large contributor to this is the shipping industry. As of 2018 the
industry is responsible for 1076 million tons of global CO2 equivalent emissions, which
represents around 2.9% of emissions from human activities [1]. According to projections,
by 2050 these emissions could increase by 90% - 130% compared to 2008. It therefore
stands to reason that some actions must be taken to make the industry more sustainable.
Some manufacturers and operators have opted for retrofitting. This change enables their
engines to utilize greener fuels like biofuel or LNG in place of the traditionally used Heavy
fuel oil (HFO) or Marine diesel oil (MDO) [2]. Another alternative is to minimize the emis-
sisons of the traditional diesel engines, which can be done by utilizing so called Wind
Assisted Ship Propulsion (WASP) which will be the focus of this thesis. These systems
for example consist of sails or Flettner rotors which when applicable will provide thrust.
This results in less power being needed by the engine, reducing its emissions.
Historically, wind power has been utilized since man first traversed the seas and was used
up until the late 1800’s when steam engines replaced them. It is still used today but mainly
on smaller boats as opposed to large commercial vessels. The concept of harnessing the
wind has however not been erased, and when crisis occurs ship makers have reverted
to the tried and tested methods. The energy crisis of the 1970’s caused by conflict in
the middle east [3] saw the re-emergence of wind powered ships. One notable example
is the ”Shin Aitoku Maru”, a Japanese sail-assisted tanker built in 1980. This ship had
collapsible, rigid sails that in favourable weather conditions could extend, and the ship
operating on wind power alone could reach speeds up to 12kn [4]. More recently another
Japanese vessel, the ”Shofu Maru” which was built in 2022 by MOL is currently utilizing a
rigid sail which has reduced their GHG emissions by 5%-8% depending on their route [5].
As uncertainty in energy related matters increases, wind related solutions could regain
popularity.
Wind assisted vessels are though not without drawbacks. WASP can take up valuable
cargo space while contributing to effects which have a negative impact on the sailing
efficiency. The leeway angle is one those effects and it cannot be understated when
discussing the drawbacks and benefits of WASP. The contributing propulsion gained from
the installation of WASP introduces an additional side force component which has to be
balanced out by hydrodynamic forces acting on the hull and the rudder [6]. The forces
reduce the efficiency of the wind assisted propulsion and undermine the potential fuel
savings gained. Learning how to mitigate this side effect is essential to the viability of
wind assisted solutions for commercial sailing vessels.

==PAGE:12==
This thesis aims to establish an empirical relationship for the increased resistance due
to WASP as a function of ship speed and leeway angle [β] to . This relationship will be
established based on calculations conducted in the CFD software Shipflow and will ini-
tially be made for the hull type KVLCC2. This hull was designed by the Korean Institute
of Ships and Ocean Engineering, or KRISO for short, with the objective to provide data
for tanker ships with a bulbous flow. No full scale ships have been made to date [7].
The KVLCC2 is a large ship with a cargo-carrying capacity ranging up to 250,000 tons
[8]. This thesis aims to create and validate a Shipflow model by using experimental data.
Subsequently the calculations are expanded to a wider range of speeds and finally, the
design and implementation of a Leeboard is conducted. Traditionally, a leeboard is a
large wooden plank or frame which can be lowered into the water with the aim of reduc-
ing leeway. Leeboards have been traditionally employed by Dutch flat bottomed sailing
vessels in shallow waters. The purpose of the leeboard is to improve the performance of
the ship by balancing the side force and yaw moment produced by the WASP system.

Literature review
The literature relating to the subject explores the connection between the induced resis-
tances associated with wind assisted ship propulsion in relation to leeway angles, heel
angles, keel and rudder resistance. Most of the literature relates to the operational con-
ditions of the ship where the action of increased side forces leads to leeway and heel
angles. The literature is often more concerned with the change in performance under
these new conditions rather than on how to improve upon them. That is not to say that no
attempts have been made. The literature often relates the WASP related side forces to
the rudder angles and forces. There have also been tests of additional appendages such
as keels, where a dynamic keel proved to be the best to improve upon or reduce these
effects[6]. The scientific literature has however not broached the subject of leeboards or
fins for stabilization purposes on commercial ships. Leeboards have an advantage over
keels as they can be designed and deployed asymmetrically. Since they are typically
attached to the sides of the ship, they also do not reduce cargo space when retracted.
The leeboard is thus a new and untested method for stabilization of modern commercial
ships.
For the purpose of this project, the implementation of leeboards will be explored. Histori-
cally the leeboards originated in Asia as early as the 8th century and were used on the east
coast of China and the Coromnandel coast of India. These leeboards were introduced
to the Dutch around the 16th century as European merchants were taking more frequent
trips to Asia [9]. They were then utilized in Dutch waters and on the British and Danish
east coasts. These were in fact imitations of the Chinese boards which the merchants en-
countered. Today the leeboards are mainly used amongst hobbyists, with the commercial
shipping industry opting for centerboards or daggerboards on smaller ships/yachts, and
bilge keels for larger ships.
Due to the performance based nature of the research, there is a good amount of infor-
mation available regarding tests focusing on performance under leeway. The Delft Wind
Assist series holds a catalogue of over 65 hulls [10] which has been used for publication of
research by van der Kolk, mainly in open access articles. This source of information has
been valuable, as it acted as a baseline for evaluation of the results from Shipflow sim-
ulations. Kramer and Steen have also published results based on OpenFoam modelling
which again serves as a baseline for comparison [6]. This research publishes results for
hydrodynamic models with an externally applied side force for two different test cases. In
the first test. the side force is changed while the center of effort is fixed at the location of
midship, which in their case is roughly the aerodynamic center of effort. For the second
test, the side force remains constant but the location of the center of effort varies. Their
research has been informative in highlighting the challenges faced when attempting CFD
modelling of commercial ships. Their findings highlight the benefits and potential gains
3
==PAGE:14==
of WASP, while also focusing on factors that should be considered when calculating the
center of effort and induced side force.
Performance under the sailing condition has been the subject of quite a few articles, mainly
focused on the steady leeway and heel angles under which the ship sails [11]. The sailing
conditions become especially apparent with the addition of sails or Flettner rotors. The in-
creased resistance in sailing condition is mainly caused by leeway (or drift) induced rudder
forces, with some contributions from hydrodynamic forces acting on the hull. Research by
Kramer and Steen also shows that the heel angle plays a minor part but mostly does so in
combination with large leeway angles [6]. The forces associated with the sailing condition
also tend to cause moments around the ship. The moment around the z-axis especially,
that is the vertical axis through the ship, tends to be a subject of great interest. The reason
for this interest is that the moment around the vertical axis tends to be offset by the keel,
causing a massive increase in the ship resistance. This moment is primarily caused by
the side forces acting on the ship from the increased hydrodynamic side force induced
by WASP. The center of effort of these side forces is often referred to as the center of
lateral resistance. As bare hulls tend to have the center of lateral resistance ahead of the
bow [12] appendages are used to bring it closer to midship with the aim of minimizing the
moment and thus reducing drag caused by the rudder. This is a major concern for this
project as the stabilization of these moments is a role of the leeboard implementation.
Concerning numerical simulations, the literature suggests that model scales smaller than
1:4 could present inaccurate results caused by flow separation at the bottom of the ship
hull due to lower Reynolds numbers in smaller scales in the flow [13]. To emphasise that
point, they provide examples of numerical experiments for model scales between 1:1 up
to 1:225 to investigate the static drift and pure sway for a KVLCC2 hull. The yaw moment
isn’t affected much when the scale is decreased, while the sway is over predicted by
roughly 20% in the sway test and 27% in the yaw test. Other examples with different
geometries for the ship hull provide rudder and leeway angles which are over predicted
by 5% and 13% respectively. The same condition, a vessel sailing with a leeway angle to
produce a hydrodynamic side force leaving a wake of large vortices and separation, was
described by van der Kolk as the greatest computational challenge during simulation [14].
The scaling however is double edged, while an increase in scaling can produce more
accurate results the increase in computational effort is quite significant. A decision must
thus be taken whether the sacrifice in computational accuracy is worth the time saved
with the reduction of computational effort.
Though it is not delved into in detail in this project, there is readily available information on
the implementation and the optimal use of the WASP systems. Information on both sails
and Flettner rotors could prove useful to the understanding of the operational conditions
of ships sailing under WASP induced conditions. The main focus regarding the scope
of this project concerns the induced side forces and their effects on sailing performance,
both on leeway and heeling of which the WASP is the primary contributor. Knowing the
4
==PAGE:15==
lift and drag ratio and vertical center of effort of the WASP system provides useful infor-
mation regarding the forces generated and the aerodynamic center of effort. The sailing
performance of the ship is heavily influenced by these factors since the hydrodynamic
forces acting on the ship are generated as a response to the aerodynamic forces.
The subject of leeboards is rarely studied by the scientific literature in any sort of mod-
ern usage in English. There are however a few written in dutch. ”Het ontwerpen van
zwaarden” by André Hoek and ”Zijzwaarden” by IR. H. Vreedenburgh provided some in-
formation on the traditional ways a leeboard is designed showing excellent schematics
for the traditional designs, such as the shapes and NACA profiles the leeboard resem-
bles [15]. ”Het ontwerpen van zwaarden” has schematics for both the traditional fin-like
construction of the tjalkenzwaard, a low aspect ratio leeboard and the higher aspect ratio
and the more sword like zeezward, both shown here in fig. 2.1a and fig. 2.1b. The articles
also mention practical considerations such as the camber angle and the vertical angle of
the leeboard relative to the ship. One of the few instances a leeboard was mentioned in
the literature review was in relation to Edwin Doran Jr.’s ”The History of the Leeboard”
which is however more concerned with tracing the origin of the leeboard to its roots and
following the European adaption of this novel concept then the explanation of its inner
workings and effects on sailing conditions.

3
Methodology
3.1
Objective
The objective of this thesis is firstly to create a Shipflow model which can be used to
validate future calculations. This will be done by comparison with the measurement results
from towing tank experiments provided by Kume et al [16]. This data will be used as the
backbone of an analysis of the effects of wind assisted ship propulsion. The analysis will
include an overview of the added resistance along the principal axes of x and y, along with
considerations for the position of the center of pressure along the x-axis. In the context
of this paper, added resistance refers to the increased resistance, caused by the WASP
system and will be referred to as such. Next, an experimental design for a leeboard will be
added to this configuration in an attempt to produce more hydrodynamic side force while
moving the center of pressure closer to the midship. The performance of the leeboard
will be evaluated on the basis of increased hydrodynamic side force and the effects it
has on the center of pressure along the x-axis. Furthermore, its influence on generated
propulsion and rudder performance will be explored.
3.2
Scope
The scope of the project will be primarily focused on the hull of the ship, meaning that for
the purposes of simplicity the forces related to the propeller and rudder will be neglected
for numerical simulations. The effects of the rudder will be evaluated by theoretical calcu-
lations. The main parameters of consideration are the leeway angle and its influence on
the sailing conditions with a focus on increased resistance and possible means to coun-
teract it. The forward speed of the ship is also a concern with numerical simulations using
Shipflow being performed at the design speed of the ship. Calculations for further speed
ranges will consequently be performed once validation has been completed. The influ-
ence of heeling angles will be investigated and explored though they are not considered
a major contributing factor.
3.3
Value ranges
Before any calculation can be made the value ranges for the study need to be established.
These value ranges include the speed in knots and the leeway angle in degrees. And
if it turns out to be relevant to the result, a value range for the heel angle will also be
established.
3.3.1
Speed
As of 2022, laden (ships carrying cargo) VLCC ships logged an average travel speed of
just over 12kn [17], while ballast (any substance, other than cargo, which is usually placed
in the inner compartment of a vessel to produce a desired draft or trim) [18] ships were just
7
==PAGE:18==
below 12kn. 12kn should therefore be within the chosen range. Some documentation for
the tanker also exists in the form of model data. [19]. According to the documentation the
service speed can reach 15.5kn which is considerably higher than the number presented
above.
If the literature is consulted, Kramer and Steen design their experiments with the speed
ranging between 8kn - 16kn [6], providing a benchmark for the ranges used in the sub-
sequent calculations. In this paper there is however the assumption that ”each design
speed is assumed to represent a different ship where the engine size is adjusted to the
required power”, which should be considered when deciding the value range.
It should furthermore be noted that since the ships considered in this study are regarded
as environmentally friendly (due to wind assisted ship propulsion), the speed targets will
be lower than those of the conventional fleet.
Based on the aforementioned sources, consultation with experts and considerations as
to what most applicable is to this study, a range of 6kn - 15.5kn was deemed appropriate.
It is recognized that the ships will not always be travelling in ideal weather conditions.
Factors like draft, wind direction and force, sea conditions etc. all have an effect on how
fast the ship can travel. It is therefore assumed that when sailing in poor conditions the
ship will slow down considerably. It is also recognized that the weather conditions can be
calm and allow the ships to sail faster than their average speed. It’s therefore assumed
that the upper speed value will be higher than the average speed, and 15.5kn deemed to
be an appropriate increase over the average.
3.3.2
Leeway angle
According to van der Kolk [20] the definition of the leeway angle is ”The angle between the
direction of vessel motion and the longitudinal axis of the vessel. The leeway angle places
the vessel hull in an oblique flow, generating a hydrodynamic side force and moment” [20].
This in turn influences the sailing conditions of the ship.
Commercial ships equipped with WASP will experience more side forces than their non
wind assisted counterparts. The aerodynamic side forces induced by the WASP causes
the ship to drift at a leeway angle β. This induced drift produces an equal and opposite
hydrodynamic side force which acts on the hull. The relationship between the leeway
and added resistance is therefore a major concern when considering wind assisted ship
propulsion.
When selecting the range for leeway angles there are three things to consider, firstly the
increased resistance, secondly the extra computational time and thirdly experimental in-
formation available. Research conducted on the subject shows that as the leeway angle
increases so does the resistance and the side forces acting on the vessel. This increase
is due to the gathering of a large wake of vortices behind the vessel which is the primary
8
==PAGE:19==
challenge in the numerical simulations [14]. The range of leeway angles considered for
this project will be 0°- 9°. This range is based on experimental data available from re-
search done by Kume et al. [16], which conducted test for the aforementioned range. To
minimize computational effort the leeway angle should also be kept within a moderate
range. Furthermore, in the first two stages of the project the vessels will be considered to
be symmetrically designed. Thus, only positive leeway angles will be considered.
3.3.3
Heel angle
Again using van der Kolk’s description, the heel angle can be described as ”An inclination
of the vessel about its longitudinal axis. Vessel heel changes the distribution of displaced
water volume and leads to a hydro-static restoring moment” [20]. The heel angle will
be considered if it is deemed a significant factor during simulation. The heel angle is
sometimes neglected as it mainly affects drift induced forces where large cross-flow drag
is experienced, which happens when the drift angle is large[6]. The range considered
for heeling angles will be at most up 10°. This is due to the design of modern ships as
they have to comply with stability regulations which state that they can’t heel more than
10°[21].

3.5
Shipflow
The simulation tool used for the calculations is Flowtech’s software1, which is developed
”by naval architects, physicists and numerical analysts and is optimized for hydrodynamic
designs for ships with top shipyards and universities being among users” [22]. The soft-
ware is designed with ship designers in mind, and uses efficient potential flow methods
to reduce lead time, making it possible to run comprehensive and complex calculations
with a relatively short wait time. This is in part due to the fact that the software is made up
of modules which all serve different functions within the system. This creates the whole
range required to create a complete picture of the ship and it’s surroundings.
The most notable modules are XPAN, a potential flow solver, XCHAP, a CFD Reynolds
Averaged Navier Stokes solver for wake and boundary layers and other supporting mod-
ules surrounding ship movement and mesh generation. More details on the modules can
be found in section 3.5.3 - section 3.5.5
3.5.1
XFLOW
XFLOW is the central command of Shipflow. In XFLOW all offset files are inserted, pa-
rameters such as length, speed, hull type etc. are defined as well as deviations from de-
fault calculations such as enforcing asymmetrical calculations and specifying onset flow
direction. Finally, all modules which should be run for the subsequent calculations are
activated in XFLOW.

To create the free surface and body grid XMESH requires that the body elements from

The mesh grid refinement is based on results from convergence for the vessel. The mesh
generation requires a balance between computational time and precision as the number of
elements can greatly increase computational effort in XPAN and XBOUND. The process
to determine the point of convergence will be done by hand and down to limit where the
difference in the quantity is deemed negligible, or if the increased computational effort
outweighs the increase in accuracy. This issue also presents itself in the selection of
mesh area size. This issue can be seen in direct correlation with the speed of the vessel,
the faster the speed the greater the area needed.
3.5.3
XPAN
According to Flowtech, the developers of Shipflow, XPAN is the ”World’s most powerful
free surface potential flow panel method”. It can account for linear as well as non-linear
boundary conditions and perform first or higher order panels and singularity distributions.
It can compute waves, wave resistance, lift if the conditions in section 3.6 are considered,
induced resistance, sinkage & trim and pressure distributions to name a few. For calcula-
tion speed it has been threaded to speed up computations on multi core machines, which
means that many calculations can be run concurrently instead of sequentially. [23]
As mentioned before, XPAN uses the mesh generated by XMESH to calculate the free
surface flow surrounding the vessel. In practical terms this means that XPAN is able to
calculate the wave resistance. The controls specified for XPAN are minimal, the model
type used is free, meaning that the vessel can sink and trim according to speed & the sur-
rounding conditions and the boundary conditions are non-linear. The maximum number
of iterations is usually specified but to get accurate results the solution should converge
thus leaving the maximum iteration command high enough in order to reach convergence.
3.5.4
XGRID
As XMESH generates the mesh for XPAN, XGRID generates the grid for XCHAP. Like
XMESH the input for XGRID is an offset file, which requires the specification of where
on the hull an offset section is located, whether it is the frontal bulb or the overhang for
example. Unlike XMESH the grid generation is based not user specified but rather based
on automatic generation from Shipflow, with the only user input being the the selection of
the density of the grid. XGRID has the limitation that it cannot consider appendages, which
12
==PAGE:23==
means that lifting surfaces cannot be calculated in XCHAP. Since the lift is calculated in
the potential flow solver this isn’t too much of an issue but means that more manual labour
is required to extract the lifting forces and connect to the integrated forces.
3.5.5
XCHAP
CFD (RANS, Reynolds average Navier Stokes) solver for wake and boundary layer. XCHAP
incorporates the solutions from the potential flow along with the generated grid from
XCHAP to solve for the viscous part of the solution. When Lpp, the draught T, the beam
B and a computational origin are defined, the solution is presented as integrated forces
in the x, y and z direction. This coordinate system is flipped 180° around x compared to
the coordinate system defined in fig. 4.1 which needs to be accounted for. The moments
around each axis is also calculated.

4.3
Discussion of results
The validation results for 0° and 9° were gathered by averaging 8 sets of data while 1°-6°
were individual runs. Thus the comparison of 0° and 9° should be of special interest. For
0° there is a difference of 3.97% in CX, that is CX,Val = −0.0176 to CX,Mod = −0.0169. As
CY ≈0 at 0° of leeway it will not be covered. At 9° the error is larger with an approximate
error of 6.94% where Shipflow overshoots the Validation material, CX,Val = −0.0173 to
CX,Mod = −0.0185 . For the Y the error is CY,Val = 0.0462 to CX,Mod = 0.0482 which is a
difference of 4.33%. Three possible reasons for the difference between the calculations
and the validation material should be mentioned specifically.
1. The comparisons are based on a computational model and an experiment, con-
ducted in a towing tank. The computational model operates under ideal conditions
and should have minor numerical uncertainties as each of the runs reached conver-
gence, while the towing tank operates at near-ideal conditions. In the towing tank
there could be some disturbance which could cause some unexpected forces to
present which won’t show up in the computations. It should be noted that the con-
ditions in the towing tank are very good so that the expected error is minimal, but
it is however something to be aware of when comparing the two results. Kume et
al. [16] presented averages and uncertanties for the calculated values which were
considered during the calcualtions and are included in fig. 4.6
2. The discrepancy for the lower leeway angles in center of pressure could be caused
by the fact that the N/Y calculation is a subject of two low magnitude variables
which increase both in magnitude and accuracy with increasing leeway.
3. Finally there are some minor differences between the model and the experiment,
with regards to the principal dimensions. The Lpp and T for example are defined to
be the same in Shipflow but the program calculates some of the other values to be
different to the experiment. In particular the wetted surface area could be the cause
of some minor differences in results.
The comparison presented in figs. 4.6 and 4.8 does show great agreement, but based
on the points mentioned above it is not expected that they will be completely identical.
Comparing the 0° and 9° shows noticeable difference in both the wave contours seen on
the free surface as well as the pressure contours on the hull. The wave plot and pressure
contours for both 0° and 9° from Shipflow can be seen in fig. 4.10a - fig. 4.13b.
Summarizing everything discussed in this chapter the model and the validation material
show good agreement. Overall the comparison provides a good result based both on the
variation in the 9° experiments, and the magnitude of the forces in the 1° validation and
model calculations. The model is therefore considered validated and accurate enough to
move on to further calculations which will include more hull forms and a range of Froude
numbers for the same leeway angles as in the validation tests.

5.1
Establish baseline effect from heel angle
Having validated the Shipflow configuration for leeway angles 0°to 6° and 9° a baseline
response has been established for the increased resistance under leeway. Ships sailing
with WASP experience constant but ever changing side forces due to the lift and drag
generated by the WASP. This side force leads to a moment around the ship which can
cause it to heel. The effects of the generated heel angle may cause additional resistance
on the ship, but in the prior calculations the effect of heel angle was neglected. The
literature review suggests that the effects of heel angles are minimal, but it has the greatest
influence while paired with high leeway angles due to changes in crossflow drag [6]. As
the range considered for both leeway and heel angles in this project is not large, the
influence of heel should be small but needs to be verified.
To confirm this the configuration was run for heel angles, -3°, -6° and -9° for leeway angles
0°, 3°, 6° and 9°. The heel angle direction is defined according to the coordinate system
in fig. 4.1. The heel angle is added to the Shipflow configuration by using the command
Xrot. Xrot rotates the body mesh selected by the degree specified in the input. Xrot is
added to all the body meshes and the same rotation is applied to them all corresponding
to the calculations being made. The resulting wave profiles can be seen in
The results shown in figures fig. 5.3 and fig. 5.4 show that the influence of heel angle
is negligible. There is almost no perceivable difference between the calculations for the
coefficient CX and CY . The heel angle is therefore not seen to have a noticeable increase
on the bare hull resistance. The difference in magnitude is so minute, that the initial
assumption to exclude the effects of heel is deemed to be valid.
5.1.1
Heel balance
The heeling moment from the WASP is counteracted by the restoring heeling moment
working on the hull. The moment from the WASP is heavily depended on the choice of
WASP, whether it is a sail or a Flettner rotor for example and the number of sails or rotors
implemented. The chosen WASP depends of course on the desired output and available
space on board the ship. The potential thrust is limited by a need for heel and yaw balance
which can be upset if the developed side forces upset the heeling balance and cause the
heel angle to exceed safe limits [28].
Osflow simulates the conditions produced by the aerodynamic moment and forces from
WASP under leeway. The aerodynamic moment and the hydrodynamic restoring heeling
moment acting on the hull should offset each other and produce a constant heeling angle
as a part of the ”sailing condition” for wind powered vessels. As the aerodynamic and
hydrodynamic forces must achieve an equilibrium, it is possible to use the calculated

6
Leeboards
As mentioned in chapter 2 the leeboard is not a new solution, though it perhaps hasn’t
been implemented in quite the manner suggested in this paper. They do however provide
some benefits over conventional appendages which are currently implemented. Since
the leeboards haven’t been implemented on commercial ships the literature available is
essentially non-existent. The best reference for their implementation would be literature
on conventional sailing yachts, which have been known to use asymmetrical appendages
such as leeboards, in tandem with literature on large commercial ships. Together, they
can shed light on both benefits as well as drawbacks related to the implementation of
leeboards.
Some of the benefits the leeboard offer include:
• The leeboards can be implemented asymmetrically, meaning that we can deploy a
leeboard on either side of the ship depending on the direction of the leeway angle.
This means that based on conditions, we can get a more effective countermeasure
to drift.
• Centerboards have the benefit of being retractable when the ship does not expe-
rience drift. This would however require a compartment of sorts which would take
up valuable ballast space. The leeboards share the benefit of being retractable, but
without the downside since they are mounted directly on the side of the hull.
• Since the centerboards need to be mounted centrally they can’t be designed asym-
metrically, meaning that they cannot provide lift unless the ship is drifting. That
suggests that a cambered profile like a leeboard would be superior, providing more
lift than a centerboard.
Based on the aforementioned points, it could be argued that the leeboards could provide
an exciting solution to the problem of WASP induced leeway. To further illustrate how the
implementation of the leeboard might work, a quick sketch, fig. 6.1, was made. Notice
that the leeboard in fig. 6.1 is oriented closer to aft than in the actual computation, but
this is purely for illustration purposes. In the computed case it’s location would lie close
to midship.
From fig. 6.1 it can be seen that the lifting force generated perpendicular to the onset flow
can be broken down into two components, its x and y components. While the leeboard’s
main function is to introduce a side force to counteract the drift, there is an auxiliary benefit,
in that it generates a positive force in the x-direction. The leeboard should therefore also
reduce the experienced resistance along the x-axis. The performance criteria for the
leeboard’s implementation is to see a noticeable increase in the side force generated as

Discussion
A Shipflow model was created to replicate effects that a ship with wind assisted propulsion
experiences. The KVLCC2 was chosen for validation as there was satisfactory informa-
tion available about the model as well as experimental towing tank data. The configuration
of the experimental data was used to create a model which proved to be the backbone
of this thesis. The model shows good agreement with the towing tank test for the forces
along the x and y axis and in terms of moment around the z-axis. There was however,
slight discrepancy in the comparison for the center of lateral resistance (CLR) calculation
for lower leeway angles. With increasing leeway the agreement between the model and
the experimental results improved.
The validation model was expanded upon by studying the effects of heel, both in relation
to sailing resistance and as a means of balancing against the side forces. The heeling
angle proved to have minimal effect on the increased resistance and was thus deemed
negligible. The restoring heel angle showed that the moment around the x-axis proved
to have a minimal effect, with all leeway angles resulting in less than 1° of heel while
experiencing side forces. A second order regression model was created to predict the
increased resistance coefficients based on leeway angle and speed. This was done for
both the primary resistance coefficients for the x and y axis and for the moment around z.
All the regression models were found to be excellent predictors for the force coefficients
for ships under leeway. The regression models were all non-dimensionalized with the
ship speed, Lpp and T in the hope that this would make the predictions accurate for other
ships as well. No model calculations were however run for other ship geometries, so this
cannot be verified.
Having modelled the bare hull forces and moments for the hull, an attempt was made to
improve upon them. To do this a leeboard was designed based on traditional dutch design
and a 2410 NACA airfoil profile. The leeboard was designed to have a high aspect ratio
as the area along side of the ships hull should not produce any lift due to the interaction
with the hull. The leeboard calculations were based on theoretical calculations as the
Shipflow modelling proved unreliable. Modelling attempts in Shipflow proved unsuccess-
ful in creating a model which could produce consistent results for multiple leeway angles.
The models were too reliant on the Kutta factor which was not fully understood or defined
in its application of providing lift. The theoretical approach was therefore preferred over
the Shipflow modelling approach.
For the theoretical approach the 2D flow over the airfoil was compared to its 3D flow coun-
terpart where effects such as the downwash on the wingtips were considered. Potential
effects such as the ground effect between the hull and the leeboard and the image ef-
fect were neglected as it was unclear how the interaction would present itself. With and
53
==PAGE:64==
without the 3D effects, the implementation of the leeboard proved to be a good method to
produce hydrodynamic side force to counteract the aerodynamic forces from the WASP.
The leeboard was positioned at midship so the leeboard forces were applied near the
center of effort of the aerodynamic forces. This moves the center of lateral resistance
backwards closer to midship, and as it has been mentioned previously the distance be-
tween the aerodynamic center of effort and the center of lateral resistance should be close
to each other. This helps to reduce the moment action on the hull which has to be cor-
rected by the rudder [21]. Furthermore, the addition of the leeboard means that the model
is able to produce more side forces at lower leeway angles, even providing lift while un-
der no leeway. This improves upon the ”sailing condition” which reduces the resistance
experienced by the hull.
Lastly the model was finalized by the addition of the aerodynamic forces. This was done
by using the aerodynamic efficiency of a Flettner rotor, where the generated side forces
of the ship are matched to the side forces of the Flettner rotor to estimate the potential
generated thrust. Lastly the increased moments around the x-axis were taken and the
restoring heeling moment calculated again to validate that the heeling experienced by
the ship was within safe limits. The results showed that the ship experienced only 3° of
heeling which is well within the safety limit of 10°. That means that if more propulsion
is needed, a bigger leeboard could be implemented to counteract more of the generated
side force.
7.1
Limitations and simplifications
Due to practical concerns regarding calculation and modeling a number of assumption
and limitations were made. The most prevalent have been listed here below
• One of the most prevalent limitation of this study is that it does not include the rudder
forces, as the rudder is a significant contributor in the force and moment balance
around the ship. The omission of it garnered the leeboard effect more prevalence
but it neglects all interplay between the two. This area could be a feature for future
studies.
• The another aspect neglected in this study is the modeling of the interaction be-
tween the leeboard and the hull. This study was unable to captured this interplay
through Shipflow modeling, thus further CFD analysis in this area is required to bet-
ter understand how the lift and drag coefficients change with leeway and positioning
relative to the ship.
• The leeboard design could be optimized further in terms of rounding the edges and
shaping the profile. The design used was chosen to simplify calculations and to
ease Shipflow modeling.
• Traditionally leeboards lie at an angle along the side of the ship and have a slight
camber angle relative to the hull of the ship. This again was neglected to simplify
54
==PAGE:65==
modelling and calculations.
• All calculations were kept in model scale, which might reduce the accuracy of the
computation when compared to its full size counterpart. This was mainly done for
two reason, firstly there is a direct comparison available for the model scale and
secondly this was done to save computational time and effort.
7.2
Future application
Hopefully with time as WASP become more common place in the maritime industry, so-
lutions such as the leeboard will gain increased interest. The implementation of the lee-
board is still a novel idea but there is a case to be made for their practical use. Before
their implementation becomes widespread, further research and analysis should be con-
ducted, perhaps in the form of physical model scale tests to verify the results presented in
this thesis. The congregated effort of further research could result in the leeboard being
implemented on wind assisted ships worldwide.

Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to explore the increased resistance induced by wind as-
sisted ship propulsion. Numerical calculations and detailed simulations were performed
to validate the model against experimental data. The study found that a medium grid
refinement worked best as a combination of accuracy and computational effort. When
compared to their experimental counterparts, the resistance coefficients CX, CY and CN
showed good agreement for the range of leeway angles considered in the study.
The theoretical implementation of a leeboard was consequently explored, after numeri-
cal simulations were deemed to be inaccurate. The addition of a leeboard was shown
to have a positive effect on the resistance coefficients, reducing the resistance in the x-
direction and y-direction respectively, while moving the center of lateral resistance back-
wards closer to midship. This resulted in lower induced rudder angles and less drag
generated by the rudder.
The insights which are gained from this study could be applied to commercial ships which
utilize wind assisted propulsion. Further research into the application of leeboards is rec-
ommended, with more complex situations and experimental models. Overall, this study
provides a solid foundation for further research into the implementation of leeboards for
practical applications in the maritime industry.
