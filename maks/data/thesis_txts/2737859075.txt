Abstract
Cognitive impairment after critical illness is frequent but poorly understood and its trajecôÙ
tory is difficult to predict. Alterations in Neurovascular Coupling (NVC), the interaction
between neuronal activity and regional cerebral blood flow, might be used as a biomarker
for cognitive function after critical illness. This study used integrated ElectroencephalogôÙ
raphy (EEG) and Functional NearôÙInfrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS) to capture neural activity
and hemodynamic responses during an nôÙback task paradigm. Theta power in the frontal
midline and Oxygenated Hemoglobin (HbO) concentration in the frontal cortex were anôÙ
alyzed in both healthy controls and critically ill patients across the nôÙback tasks. The
analysis revealed higher Theta power in patients compared to controls, with a notable deôÙ
crease during the 3ôÙback task, indicating difficulties with higher task complexity. Healthy
controls exhibited a greater increase in HbO concentration, suggesting better hemodyôÙ
namic response under cognitive load. Despite the lack of statistically significant results,
the study provided insights into NVC and cognitive function in critically ill patients.
Key words: cognitive impairment, functional nearôÙinfrared spectroscopy, encephalograôÙ
phy, neurovascular coupling

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The investigation of human brain functions has greatly interested the scientific community.
Understanding the human brain is a significant challenge many researchers are addressôÙ
ing. The brain is arguably the most complex organ in the human body, which performs
a wide range of cognitive functions: from visual recognition to language understanding,
speech, and executive control. It comprises billions of neurons, forming synapses and
establishing a complicated network with connections enabling the brain to function in an
adaptive matter. Research has been conducted on neurons on a microscopic scale and
how these huge numbers of neurons and synapses work collectively to generate macroôÙ
scopic brain signals and human behaviours. It is believed that brain functions and human
behaviours are mediated by complex neural activations and networks. These internal acôÙ
tivities result in electrical activity (direct effects) that are accompanied by hemodynamic
and metabolic responses (indirect effects), which are the fundamental sources for all nonôÙ
invasive neuroimaging techniques. Brain imaging techniques can be categorized into two
main groups: those that capture neural electrical activities by detecting induced elecôÙ
trical or magnetic fluctuations in the scalp, such as Electroencephalography (EEG) and
Magnetoencephalography (MEG)ëƒ and those that rely on hemodynamic (cerebral blood
flow, cerebral blood volume) and metabolic (glucose and oxygen utilization) responses to
neural activity, including Functional NearôÙInfrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS), Functional MagôÙ
netic Resonance Imaging (fMRI), and Positron Emission Tomography (PET) [1]. Due to
the absence of a specific technology that can record the whole spectrum of the information
generated by brain activity, simultaneous multiôÙmodal monitoring of brain state states has
become increasingly common in the last decade. Among these multiôÙmodal approaches,
the integration of fNIRS and EEG has gained significant interest.
fNIRS is a relatively new neuroimaging technique that has become a useful tool for monôÙ
itoring brain activity due to its portability, lightweight properties, and limited costs. fNIRS
uses light injection and detection points to measure hemodynamic fluctuations in the brain
tissues. EEG is a wellôÙestablished technique in neuroimaging settings suitable for capôÙ
turing the macroscopic temporal dynamics of brain electrical activity through the passive
measurements of scalpôÙlocated voltages. EEG systems are widely used for clinical and
nonclinical purposes to diagnose and monitor brain functions and dysfunctions. ElectriôÙ
cal brain activity and its hemodynamic counterpart interaction are mediated through the
Neurovascular Coupling (NVC) mechanism that can be studied using the combined techôÙ
nologies.
This thesis aims to evaluate cognitive outcomes in survivors of critical illness by investiôÙ
gating NVC.
Survivors of critical illness often experience prolonged and disabling cognitive impairôÙ
ments, which remain inadequately characterized. These impairments are marked by new
deficits or exacerbations of preexisting mild deficits in global cognitive or execution funcôÙ
tions. Such cognitive dysfunction is very common and in patients can persist for at least
1
==PAGE:16==
1 year. Characterizing this longôÙterm cognitive impairment after critical illness is becomôÙ
ing increasingly relevant due to the large number of acutely ill patients being treated in
Intensive Care Units (ICUs). [2]
Previous studies have evaluated brain functional connectivity in both healthy controls and
patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) using fNIRS. fNIRS is used to measure
hemodynamic responses in the subjectsã Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) showing that healthy
controls present significantly stronger connectivity, while no difference could be seen in
the MCI group. This study showed the potential of fNIRS to study brain functional conôÙ
nectivity in neurodegenerative diseases [3]. Another study used fNIRS to obtain reliable
biomarkers for diagnosing MCI by performing different mental tasks. Nonetheless, fNIRS
biomarkers were not reliable enough to distinguish MCI patients from healthy controls with
an accuracy over 60% [4]. A recent study [5] used fNIRS neural biomarkers to discrimiôÙ
nate mild cognitive impairment. This study obtained promising results showing significant
reductions in oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO) concentration in the PFC in the MCI group.
Although research has been conducted using fNIRS to evaluate cognitive impairment,
still there is no neural biomarker and good characterization of this impairment, suggesting
more research needs to be conducted in this field.
EEG is used extensively in clinical and research settings to assess and monitor cognitive
functions being used to detect cognitive impairment. It is a nonôÙinvasive method, which
provides high temporal resolution allowing for detailed analysis of brain activity and conôÙ
nectivity. Previous research has highlighted the potential of EEG as a potential biomarker
for early detection and monitoring of cognitive decline. A study conducted by Meghadadi
et al. [6] analyzed restingôÙstate EEG to identify biomarkers associated with Alzheimerãs
disease (AD) and cognitive impairment. EEG allowed researchers to identify characterisôÙ
tic changes in brain wave patterns associated with cognitive impairment, highlighting that
increased Delta and Theta power and decreased Alpha and Beta power, indicate a slowôÙ
ing of brain activity in MCI and AD patients. Functional connectivity analysis using EEG
has been used to understand how cognitive impairment affects brain network dynamics.
A study conducted by EchevarriôÙOcampo et al. [7] used graph theory and the visual oddôÙ
ball paradigm to evaluate the functional connectivity of neuronal networks in brain lobes.
The study demonstrated the efficacy of combining functional connectivity measurements
derived from EEG with machine learning for cognitive impairment classification. Other
studies such as Clark et al. [8] have used advanced methods, such as the Imaginary Part
of Coherency (iCOH) and eigenvector alignment to better evaluate brain connectivity beôÙ
tween healthy controls and MCI patients. Recent research also explores the integration
of machine learning with EEG data to enhance diagnostic accuracy. A study carried out
by Tahani Jaser Alahmadi et al. [9] used EEG data and a Bidirectional Long ShortôÙTerm
Memory (BiLSTM) network to provide a nonôÙinvasive system for the early identification
and diagnosis of cognitive diseases. The BiLSTM model was trained to classify the data
gained from EEG tests of various patients including both normal individuals and those
diagnosed with MCI and AD achieving promising results suggesting that this system can
potentially be utilized as a diagnostic tool for the early diagnosis of MCI. An additional
study by [10] built a deep learningôÙbased framework for the effective identification of MCI
individuals from healthy volunteers achieving 96.41% accuracy. These advancements
show the promise of EEG as a practical and costôÙeffective approach to diagnose and
monitor cognitive impairment, complementing traditional methods like neuroimaging and
cerebrospinal fluid analysis.
Several studies have integrated EEG and fNIRS to evaluate cognitive impairment, particôÙ
ularly in AD and MCI. Using both modalities gives a more detailed understanding of the
2
==PAGE:17==
underlying neural mechanisms of cognitive decline. For example, a study combining EEG
and fNIRS improved the accuracy of classifying motor imagery [11]. Moreover, a study
conducted by Minhee Kim et al. [12] described the development of a realôÙtime monitoring
platform for AD using EEG and fNIRS. The platform aimed to effectively detect various
domains affected by AD, the preliminary results suggest that this multiôÙmodal approach
can be used as a screening tool for AD. Also, EEG and fNIRS have been used to explore
dynamic, regional alteration in the ADôÙlinked brain network, showing that patients with
mild AD revealed weaker and suppressed cortical connectivity. These results validated
the feasibility of using integrated EEGôÙfNIRS to better understand the spatiotemporal dyôÙ

that the fusion of EEG and fNIRS features has more accuracy in detecting a cognitive
task than EEG and fNIRS alone [14]. The fusion of EEG and fNIRS provides a powerful
tool offering detailed insights into electrophysiological and hemodynamic changes.
This study focuses on assessing NVC differences in critical illness survivors and their
cognitive function. NVC refers to the increased blood flow resulting from neural activation.
fNIRS and EEG neuroimaging techniques are used to study this phenomenon, leveraging
their distinct nonôÙinvasive, mobile and flexible nature. Both fNIRS and EEG are robust
against motion artifacts and do not impose significant physical constraints, making them
more feasible for natural cognitive tasks and applicable to a wide range of populations.
Additionally, unlike fMRI, PET, or MEG, fNIRS and EEG do not involve exposure to high
magnetic fields or ionizing radiation. Finally, the hardware costs for these modalities are
significantly lower.
1.2
Research questions
Cognitive impairment in critical illness survivors is not yet fully characterized, necessitating
further research to improve patient outcomes. This study focuses on evaluating cognitive
function using EEG and fNIRS signals recorded during an nôÙback task paradigm. The
nôÙback task is a widely psychological test designed to induce cognitive workload. ParticiôÙ
pants must identify whether the current stimulus matches the one presented ãnã positions
back in the sequence, with ãnã varying between 0ôÙback, 1ôÙback, 2ôÙback, and 3ôÙback.
The objective of this study is to compare NVC differences between critical illness surôÙ
vivors and healthy controls at time of hospital discharge. Additionally, this study aims to
evaluate cognitive differences between these groups during the nôÙback task paradigm.
By employing a multimodal approach using EEG and fNIRS, we aim to capture both neuôÙ
ral and hemodynamic responses. This comprehensive analysis will provide insights into
how critical illness impacts brain function and cognitive performance, potentially identifyôÙ
ing biomarkers for cognitive impairment in this population.
The two hypotheses postulated for this study are:
ãÂ Critical illness survivors are expected to exhibit higher theta power than healthy conôÙ
trols, indicative of cognitive impairment. Specifically, theta power will be elevated
during the 0ôÙback, 1ôÙback, and 2ôÙback tasks compared to healthy controls. A sigôÙ
nificant decrease in theta power is anticipated during the 3ôÙback task, reflecting the
increased cognitive effort required, which these individuals may struggle to achieve
due to their impairments.
ãÂ NVC is impaired in survivors of critical illness who perform poorly during cognitive
testing at ICU discharge compared to controls

Background Theory
This thesis covers the fields of neuroimaging, health, and computer science. It focuses
on EEG and fNIRS, two methods for recording brain activity. It delves into their coupling
to evaluate cognitive function in healthy subjects and critically ill patients.
This chapter provides an overview of both neuroimaging modalities and presents their
core concepts and analysis which is crucial for understanding the thesisãs scope and deôÙ
velopment. Moreover, it presents the concept of NVC, the process by which increased
neuronal activity is linked to the local regulation of Cerebral Blood Flow (CBF).
2.1
Recording modalities
2.1.1
The fundamental basis of fNIRS
fNIRS is an optical imaging technique for the nonôÙinvasive investigation of hemodynamic
responses in the brain. fNIRS usually utilizes lights with distinct wavelengths (between
600 and 1000 nm) that can penetrate the scalp and reach the cortical surface to measure
the changes of HbO and HbR that are coupled with the metabolic activity of neurons in
the outer layer of the cortex. This technique is particularly useful for studying the funcôÙ
tional activation within the brain due to the inherent relationship between neural activity
and hemodynamic responses in the brain [15]. fNIRS measures the regional changes
of HbO and HbR concentration, which are reported to have served as an indicator of
hemodynamic changes associated with neural activity in the brain [15].
The use of fNIRS in clinical settings has increased due to its low cost and simplicity. The
measurement of the HbO concentration relies on the physical basis that chromophores
inside the brain, particularly HbO and HbR, have specific and sensitive absorption characôÙ
teristics in the nearôÙinfrared range (between 600 and 1000 nm). Lights at different waveôÙ
lengths are injected into the brain through sources (illuminators) placed on the scalp, and
the attenuated lights are detected by the optical detectors placed near the illuminators,
from which concentration changes of HbO and HbR can be computed based on the ModôÙ
ified BeerôÙLambert Law [16].
fNIRS devices have another important feature, which are shortôÙdistance detectors. These
detectors are placed very close to the source to reduce the depth of the measurement.
The signal from short channels does not contain neuronal activity, instead, it contains
most extracranial activity which is highly sensitive to systemic noise (scalp perfusion, resôÙ
piration, Mayer waves and cardiac pulsation). The measurements from the shortôÙdistance
detectors will be used to filter out the nonôÙneuronal activity from the channels which record

2.1.2
The fundamental basis of EEG
EEG is a technique for recording brain activity by measuring electrical potential differôÙ
ences between electrodes placed on the scalp, generated by neurons in the cerebral
cortex. This method nonôÙinvasively captures weak electromagnetic signals induced by
neural currents. EEG, compared to other neuroimaging methods, is known for its exôÙ
cellent temporal resolution, on the order of milliseconds, allowing it to detect both slowly
and rapidly changing brain dynamics. Furthermore, EEG is advantageous due to its costôÙ
effectiveness, mobility, ease of implementation, and strong clinical practicality. EEG is
often employed during taskôÙspecific schemes, visual or auditory tasks, mental arithmetics
or restingôÙstate. In this thesis, datasets included resting state and mental arithmetic.
It is also important to be aware of the EEG limitations. These include relatively poor spaôÙ
tial resolution. This is because scalp electrodes which record brain electrical activity are
generated several centimetres below the recording electrodes. As a consequence, corôÙ
tical current must go through different resistive layers, which provide at the scalp level a
distorted view of the brain activities, inducing a blurring effect at the scalp level. At evôÙ
ery spatial scalp position, the recorded activity is a weighted sum of the underlying brain
sources. Thus, this volumeôÙconductionôÙinduced mixture is the main cause of the poor
spatial resolution of the scalp EEG. Also, the use of the reference electrode to measure
the difference in potential contributes to this spatial smearing [17]. While volume conducôÙ
tion introduces complexities, several approaches have been developed to minimize these
limitations. These include choosing recording configurations wisely and using advanced
modelling techniques such as simulating the conductivity of different tissues and their imôÙ
pact on EEG signals. Furthermore, signal processing methods can be implemented such
as source localization to separate neural signals from volume condition effects, and comôÙ
bine EEG with other neuroimaging techniques such as fMRI which have a higher spatial
resolution or MEG to provide a more comprehensive understanding of brain activity [18].
EEG signals are measured through EEG electrodes including a reference and ground
electrode placed over a subjectãs scalp. Voltage differences between the electrodes and
the reference electrode are measured and amplified. The recorded EEG signals represent
the largeôÙscale neural oscillatory activity. This neural activity can be divided into various
rhythms depending on characteristic frequency bands, which include Theta (4ôÙ7 Hz), AlôÙ
pha (8ôÙ14 Hz), Beta (15ôÙ25 Hz), and Gamma (> 25 Hz) [19]. These brain rhythms contain
information related to neuronal processing at specific brain areas, which allows EEG to
be used as a nonôÙinvasive method for the characterization of cortical reorganization.

2.4
Cognitive Function
This study assesses cognitive function in healthy controls and critically ill patients by meaôÙ
suring their working memory (WM), given its centrality in human cognitive processing. WM
is a theoretical concept which refers to a cognitive network that enables to temporarily hold
a limited amount of information for immediate processing. The amount of information that
needs to be stored in WM to meet task requirements at any given time, is referred to
as WM load which increases with task difficulty and is associated with mental effort. InôÙ
dividual differences in WM capacity have been shown to correlate with performance in
a variety of tasks including learning, planning, comprehension and problem solving as
well as with general fluid intelligence [21]. In healthy individuals, activity in a variety of
brain regions and cortical oscillations were found to be consistently modulated by WM
load. Such correlations, make WM load biomarkers a valuable tool for the detection of
cognitive impairment [22].
The nôÙback task is a popular paradigm for measuring performance under varying WM
loads. The NôÙback task requires subjects to indicate whether the current stimulus is idenôÙ
tical to the stimulus shown in ãNã presentations before. In a given block the value of N
remains constant and blocks are more difficult the higher the value of N. In the 1ôÙback conôÙ
dition, subjects need only to hold the last item in WM, while the 2ôÙback condition requires
subjects to update two items to be held in WM as well as decide whether item 2ôÙback
9
==PAGE:24==
matches the current one. The reason for its popularity is that the input and output aspects
of the task do not vary with increased WM load. The visual input and the nature of the
response remain constant across values of N. Therefore, differences in performance or
neuroimaging measures across values of N are attributed to differences in WM demands
[21].
Various studies used the nôÙback task to manipulate WM load during the recording of brain
activity with EEG in the healthy population. They found that increasing WM load is associôÙ
ated with ThetaôÙband power increase in the frontal regions [23], [24], [25] and alphaôÙband
power decrease in the parietoôÙoccipital midline regions. [26]
Moreover, ERPs and EventôÙRelated Synchronization/Desynchronization (ERS/ERD) can
be used to measure taskôÙrelated changes. The high temporal resolution of ERP enables
to examine the time course of cognitive operations, and the spectral EEG oscillation (repôÙ
resented by ERS/ERD) can provide information about the dynamic of functional network
formation. The study conducted [27] showed that P300 amplitudes are positively correôÙ
lated with subjectsã WM capacities and general cognitive abilities.
Additionally, [28] shows that the workload induced by different nôÙback conditions results
in hemodynamic responses. In functional imaging studies, the PFC has been identified to
be among the relevant areas for memoryôÙrelated tasks. Several studies suggest that both
measures are correlated with attentional resource allocation, WM capacity, and general
cognitive abilities. Furthermore, several studies present that increased working memory
load leads to significant activation of blood flow in the frontal and parietal cortices. SpecifôÙ
ically, the Bilateral Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (dlPFC) and parietal regions show inôÙ
creased blood flow and functional connectivity, emphasizing their role in cognitive load
management and memory processing [29], [30], [31].
2.5
Neurovascular Coupling
In this thesis, both fNIRS and EEG neuroimaging modalities have been used. As menôÙ
tioned, the main advantages of fNIRS are good portability, lower cost, good resistance
to motion artifacts and applicability to various measurement scenarios providing a Blood
Oxygen LevelôÙDependent (BOLD) response similar to the one obtained by fMRI. MoreôÙ
over, its temporal resolution is higher than fMRIãs, reaching approximately 10 Hz [32].
Nonetheless, it presents several limitations due to its limited penetration depth and low
signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio. On the other hand, EEG presents advantages for exploring dynamic
brain activity. It is portable, inexpensive and presents high temporal resolution (millisecôÙ
onds). However, EEG is highly vulnerable to motion artifacts inhibiting the EEG meaôÙ
surement in natural settings and has a poor spatial resolution (centimetres) [33]. Having
exposed the advantages and disadvantages, the possibility of integrating both recording
modalities makes it a good choice to comprehensively explore the functional activity of
the brain.
The rationale behind the combination of EEG and fNIRS relies on the physiological pheôÙ
nomenon called NVC within the brain. NVC reflects the close temporal and regional conôÙ
nection between neuronal activity and cerebral blood flow [34]. The brain is critically deôÙ
pendent on a continuous supply of blood to function. Therefore, the cerebral vasculature
is endowed with NVC control mechanisms which ensure blood supply compensates for
the energy needs of its cellular constituents. This regulation of CBF during brain activôÙ
ity involves the coordinated interaction within the ãneurovascular unitã, which consists of
neurons, astrocytes, and vascular cells, including the bloodôÙbrain barrier [35].

NVC arises from the basis that neural activity is inherently accompanied by an increase in
CBF that carries vital oxygen and nutrients to neurons. When neurons are activated within
a specific brain region, blood will flow to that brain region to meet the increased demand
for glucose and oxygen, resulting in fluctuations of HbO and HbR which can be detected
by functional brain imaging techniques such as fMRI and fNIRS. NVC mechanisms are
disrupted in pathological conditions, such as hypertension, Alzheimerãs disease, and isôÙ
chemic stroke. Consequently, CBF is no longer matched to the metabolic requirements
of the tissue causing homeostatic unbalance may contribute to brain dysfunction.
NVC forms the theoretical basis for integrated fNIRSôÙEEG imaging of brain activity. This
study aims to use NVC to predict cognitive impairment in critically ill patients. The idea
behind using NVC is to provide a more comprehensive view of both neural activity and
vascular responses, unlike individual neuroimaging techniques that typically focus sepaôÙ
rately on either neural (EEG) or vascular (fNIRS, fMRI) mechanisms.
It has been studied that NVC is impaired in subjects with pathologies and could serve as
a sign for several neurological diseases such as AD and stroke [36], [13]. [37], shows
altered cerebrovascular function in AD. Resting CBF is reduced and the increase in CBF
produced by activation is attenuated. The cerebrovascular dysfunction often precedes the
onset of cognitive impairment, suggesting a role in the mechanisms of dementia. MoreôÙ
over, the study [38] showed that CBF in posterior parietal and thalamic areas produced
by cognitive tasks is reduced in patients with chronic untreated hypertension relative to
normotensive individuals. The attenuated CBF response was associated with a lower
cognitive performance. Additionally, in ischemic patients, it has been reported that there
is a decrease in CBF produced by functional activation [39].
The integration of fNIRS and EEG provides complementary information about the brainãs
electrical and metabolicôÙhemodynamic activity, leading to increased investigations into the
benefits of this combined approach.

3
State of the Art
NVC represents the causal relationship between neural activities and the subsequent
changes in cerebral blood flow through cerebrovascular reactivity [40]. Neuroimaging
modalities such as fMRI, fNIRS, and EEG are powerful tools that are usually employed to
investigate the underlying NVC process of the human brain [41], [42].
In the last decade, numerous studies utilizing integrated fNIRSôÙEEG systems have been
reported on both nonclinical and clinical topics [43]. Several studies have evaluated NVC
through EEGôÙinformed fNIRS analyses relying on the principle that regional neural acôÙ
tivity is accompanied by the generation of electrical activity and the resulting metabolic
variation. Therefore, EEG signal characteristics are used to enhance fNIRS analysis.
Conversely, some studies rely on fNIRS to enhance the processing of EEG signals due
to its robust spatial information of fNIRS compared to EEG.
Although several papers describe directional integration analysis of EEG and fNIRS, the
majority of studies use a parallel analysis/integration of the two complementary neuôÙ
roimaging techniques. These parallel analyses of concurrent fNIRS and EEG data inôÙ
vestigate the interaction between EEG and fNIRS signals through featureôÙbased fusion
analysis or correlation analyses [1] .
Hybrid fNIRSôÙEEG classificationôÙbased studies account for a significant portion of featureôÙ
based fusion analyses of concurrent fNIRSôÙEEG data. By fusing features derived from
both modalities, hybrid fNIRSôÙEEG studies have shown enhanced classification and deôÙ
coding accuracy over a single modality in various tasks, such as motor imagery and exeôÙ
cution. This approach has been used for the development of BrainôÙComputer Interfaces
(BCIs). On the other hand, the complementary properties of fNIRS and EEG have led
to investigations of spatiotemporal hemodynamic and electrical patterns of brain activity
associated with a variety of functions, such as workload, affective state, and intellectual
functions. Similar analysis pipelines have also been used to identify atypical brain patterns
associated with different brain disorders, such as Alzheimerãs and Parkinsonãs diseases.
These studies follow similar steps when processing concurrent fNIRS and EEG data conôÙ
sisting of feature extraction, feature fusion and classification. The most common derived
features from fNIRS are HbO and HbR concentration changes, including the mean, slope,
skewness, kurtosis, peak value, variance, and median of HbO/HbR. For EEG signals, the
features used depend largely on the experimental task, being the band power features for
studies involving cognitive tasks. In terms of classification, most studies use traditional
machine learning techniques such as decisionôÙtree, Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA),
Support Vector Machine (SVM) and KôÙNearest Neighbors (KNN).
As has been defined, NVC is a phenomenon in which neural activity is accompanied by
electrical activity generation and concurrent metabolic variation. Correlational analysis
between concurrent fNIRSôÙEEG recordings has been conducted to explore the spatiotemôÙ
poral association between hemodynamic and electrical patterns of various brain functions.
Pearson correlation, partial correlation, and simple linear regression are measures that
have been used for assessing the relationship between eventôÙrelated potential patterns in
EEG and hemodynamic changes in fNIRS. Several studies assessed the relationship beôÙ

tween EEG and fNIRS signals through crossôÙcorrelation and CanonicalôÙCorrelation AnalôÙ

Compared to Pearsonãs correlation method, crossôÙcorrelation can capture the delayed

is a statistical method to identify a linear relationship between the two modality data sets
by determining the interôÙsubject coôÙvariances. Other studies have adopted frequency and
phase coupling evaluating the interaction between electrical activation and hemodynamic

to assess the NVC. General Linear Model (GLM) based analysis was utilized to model

It has been studied that the timeôÙ or frequencyôÙspecific regressors derived from EEG can

Reading EEG data
The data utilized in this study was available in multiple XDF (Extensible Data Format) files.
XDF is a simple extensive storage format for time series and associated data, capable of
containing multiple multiôÙchannel streams. Each stream includes an XMLôÙbased header
that specifies both the format and metadata, along with the actual data samples.
MNEôÙPython does not support XDF files due to their inherent flexibility, which makes it
challenging to provide a oneôÙsizeôÙfitsôÙall function. XDF supports signals from various
modalities recorded at different sampling rates. There are two methods to import XDF
files and convert them to Raw objects, which are supported by the MNE package. The
first method involves importing a specific stream (such as EEG signals) using the pyxdf

As mentioned in 2.1.2, EEG signals are not robust against artifacts. Artifacts are parts of
the recorded signal that arise from sources different than the source of interest which is the
neuronal activity in the brain. As such, artifacts are a form of interference or noise relative
to the signal of interest. There are different possible causes for signal interference in
EEG, however, the most common ones are environmental, instrumentation and biological
artefacts.
The basic options we have considered to evaluate artifacts in the EEG signal are to exôÙ

such can be done using interpolation, which will be explained later.

ãÂ ReôÙreferencing
As mentioned in 2.1.2 EEG measure a voltage difference in electric potential between
each electrode and a reference electrode. This means that the signal at the reference
electrode is subtracted from all the measurement electrodes. The ideal approach would
be that the reference electrode is one which records all the interfering noise from the
environment but does not pick up any fluctuating signals related to brain activity.
We opted to apply the Common Average Reference, which compares all of the electrodes
to a single reference point. This reference point commonly is the average electrical acôÙ
tivity measured across all scalp channels. This method is called reôÙreferencing which is

achieved by creating an average of all scalp channels and subtracting the resulting signal
from each channel. The main advantage of using this method is that the amplitudes of all
electrodes will be reduced, but each channel will contribute equally to the new reference.

ãÂ Repairing artifacts with ICA
EEG data has artifacts which can be eye blinks, eye movements, and muscle contractions.
It is important to remove these artifacts to increase the reliability of the obtained results,
attributed to brain activity.
ICA can be used to remove artifacts. It can take a complex signal and separate it into
mathematically independent components. In EEG, each electrode records brain activity
and noise which includes physiological and nonôÙphysiological artifacts. Although nonôÙ
physiological artifacts can be removed with filtering and handling of bad channels and
epochs, physiological noise requires ICA algorithm. ICA is capable of separating EEG
from artifacts due to the different spatialôÙtemporal properties.
In EEG, ICA has become widely used for artifact identification and removal. This is beôÙ
cause of its ability to identify ocular artifacts (blinks and eye movements), and usually
muscle artifact. The different ICA components are mathematically independent of each
other, thus, having identified which components do not correspond to neural activity (such
as blinks), allows to remove these components from the data without affecting the other
components. Meaning that we can effectively remove the effects of ocular artifacts from
the data while preserving EEG signals.
ICA is very good at capturing features of the data that explain the most variance. Blinks
and eye movements are wellôÙcaptured because they are so much larger than EEG, and
they contain a lot of variance. Therefore, it is fundamental to lowôÙfilter the data to avoid
capturing lowôÙfrequency drift.
ICA works best with segments of data, rather than the continuous raw data [54]. For that
26
==PAGE:41==
reason, the entire raw data recording is segmented into a series of consecutive 1ôÙsecond
segments ignoring event markers. This is done to ensure the proper functioning of the
ICA algorithm.
Additionally, to the segmentation of the raw data, we ran the AutoReject function from
the AutoReject package to remove sections of data which are excessively noisy. This
is done to avoid large amplitudes of data due to these noises which generate high variôÙ
ance. AutoReject is an automated manner to determine a threshold to distinguish beôÙ
tween ãnoisyã and ãcleanã channels and epochs in the data. It is an iterative process that
repeatedly takes subôÙsamples of the data set and computes the variance of each channel
and epoch. It computes a threshold for each channel and epoch, based on the distribution
of variances across the entire data set. It rejects any channels or epochs that exceed the
threshold [55].
ICA can clean some of the artifacts identified with AutoReject. AutoReject threshold is
computed and used to mark but not reject major artifacts. Then, ICA is run on the clean
subset of data, and ICA correction is applied to remove artifacts. After that, AutoReject is
run again to correct the remaining artifacts that ICA did not fix.
Bad epochs have been marked and excluded from ICA analysis. To run ICA, we create
an ICA object which is separate from EEG data itself and then we fit the ICA object to
the data. To run ICA, the number of independent components needs to be specified. It is
possible to specify which is the desired number of components or compute which is the
optimum number of components to obtain for each subject. This is done by specifying a
floating number ãÊ1, which represents the percentage of data that the ICA components
should explain. We will define n_components = .99 which defines as many independent
components as needed to explain the 99% of the variance in the data. When running
the ica.fit(), we pass the epochs excluding the epochs that were marked as bad by
AutoReject. Each IC component can be visualized as a scalp topography map, showing
where on the scalp the IC is largest.

ãÂ SCI and Interpolation
To remove instrumental noise, noisy channels were detected using the scalp coupling
index and rejected. Then, adjacent channels were used to interpolate them. SCI was
a term introduced in [57]. The term is used to quantify the contact quality between the
optical probes and the scalp. It relies on detecting strong cardiac oscillation in both phoôÙ
todetected fNIRS raw signals with different wavelengths, 760 nm and 850nm. Although
cardiac oscillation is an unwanted physiological noise in the hemodynamic response, it is
used to evaluate the contact between the optode and the scalp since it records intracraôÙ
nial physiological activity. The definition of SCI arises from filtering both photodetected
signals between 0.5 and 2.5 Hz preserving only the cardiac component and normalizing
the resulting signals to balance any difference between their amplitude. Then, the zeroôÙ
lag crossôÙcorrelation is computed to quantify the similarity between both photodetected
signals of the same channel and be used as a quantitative measure for the signalôÙtoôÙnoise
ratio of the channel. Based on [57] we rejected channels with a scalp coupling index less
than 0.8.
After rejecting the channels with an SCI lower than 0.8, these channels were interpoôÙ
lated using the information from the closest channel. The benefit of interpolating relies
on keeping the highest amount of data and preserving the structure of the data for further
analysis.

The decomposition of the EEG signal into frequency components is commonly achieved
through Fourier transforms. The almost invariably used algorithm to compute the Fourier
transform (and arguably the most important signal processing algorithm) is the Fast Fourier
Transform (FFT), which returns, for each frequency bin, a complex number from which one

spectral analysis, it is then common to take the magnitudeôÙsquared of the FFT to obtain
an estimate of the power spectral density (PSD) (or power spectrum, or periodogram),
expressed in ôçV2/Hz in the case of EEG data.

classic periodogram method. This is because EEG data is timeôÙvarying and the spectral
content changes over time due to the neuronal activity under the scalp. The Welch alôÙ
gorithm averages the periodograms obtained over short window segments, reducing the
variance. However, this method has the cost of a lower frequency resolution.

As defined in Section 2.5, NVC refers to the neuronal activation accompanied by an inôÙ

response is delayed compared to neural activity.
In most studies, EEG band powers are convolved with a canonical HRF. These studies
consider that the neurally modulated part of the hemodynamics is the convolution of the
EEG power with the HRF. Complexity arises from the variability in methodologies used to
assess NVC and its relationship to cognitive function. An inherent delay exists between
neural activation and the increase in blood flow, but this delay has not yet been precisely
determined.
Given our projectãs aim to evaluate cognitive function, we decided to focus on assessing
the correlation between Theta power in the frontal midline and the concentration of HbO
in the frontal cortex by generating a delay between both signals. Therefore, we decided
to evaluate NVC by introducing a delay between both signals and assessing when the
two signals exhibit the highest correlation. Although various methodologies could capture
more robustly the dynamics of NVC, we decided to follow this approach due to the specific
question of determining the delay between neural and hemodynamic responses and the
limitation of time.
Moreover, by evaluating this delay and identifying the point of highest correlation, we
could detect differences in the patterns of NVC between healthy controls and subjects.
We expect patientsã NVC would exhibit a different pattern compared to healthy controls.

6
Discussion
The discussion is organized to first provide a brief description of the final results, highôÙ
lighting the differences in EEG and fNIRS responses related to cognitive function. This is
followed by a literature review, which compares the obtained results with previous studôÙ
ies. Next, the challenges encountered during the study are presented. Furthermore, it
discusses the limitations of the study. Finally, the discussion identifies areas for improveôÙ
ment to enhance the assessment of NVC in critically ill patients, emphasizing the need
for refined methodologies that better capture the spatiotemporal resolution of neural and
hemodynamic signals.
The objective of this study was to scout the differences in the EEG and fNIRS responses
related to cognitive function and to assess the strength of NVC. The study relied on a
multiôÙmodal paradigm developed to capture both electrophysiological and hemodynamic
responses during the nôÙback task.
Although the results were not statistically significant after performing Bonferroni correcôÙ
tion, we evaluated the behaviour of both EEG Theta power in the frontal midline and
fNIRS HbO concentration in the frontal cortex. Regarding the EEG signal, we observed
that the Theta power in the frontal midline was higher in patients compared to healthy
controls. This validated that patients require more neurological effort to perform cognitive
tasks compared to healthy controls. Moreover, the behaviour of healthy controls matched
expectations, with Theta power increasing linearly as the difficulty of the nôÙback tasks inôÙ
creased. In contrast, patientsã behaviour also aligned with expectation: Theta power for
the 1ôÙback and 2ôÙback tasks was greater than that of healthy controls, but in the 3ôÙback
task, Theta power decreased, indicating that patients may struggle or give up due to the
increased task complexity.
The higher relative Theta power observed in critically ill patients compared to healthy
controls aligns with the literature, as higher baseline Theta power has been found to be
indicative of subsequent cognitive decline [64], [65].
The increased relative Theta power in critically ill patients is consistent with the findings
of Moretti et al. [66], who reported increased Theta frequency power in MCI adults, which
was also associated with hippocampal atrophy. Jelic et al. found cognitive impairment asôÙ
sociated with increased Theta power in adults with Alzheimerãs disease. Increased Theta
power was also reported in a caseôÙcontrol study comparing 43 patients with pneumonia
to 30 controls [67]. This study reported higher Theta values both in patientsã acute phase
of pneumonia and after recovery. Additionally, a study comparing quantitative EEG meaôÙ
sures found that both the spectral EEG ratio (the power content in the alpha and beta
bands divided by the power content in the delta and Theta bands) were lower in patients
with sepsis compared to normal values, indicating a lower Theta power which aligns with
our results [68]. Furthermore, in other critical illness contexts, such as postôÙCOVIDôÙ19
cognitive dysfunction, Theta power was also higher in both central and parietal areas in
patients than in the controls.
For fNIRS signals, we obtained higher tôÙstatistic values for healthy controls compared to
patients, indicating a higher increase in HbO concentration in healthy controls. The HbO
concentration increased during the 1ôÙback, 2ôÙback and 3ôÙback tasks compared to 0ôÙback,
validating the higher cognitive load necessary when performing tasks with more working
memory. Although patients also showed an increase in HbO concentration for the 1ôÙback,
51
==PAGE:66==
2ôÙback, and 3ôÙback tasks compared to the 0ôÙback task, the increase was lower than that
observed in healthy controls. The study [28] shows that extracting the whole relaxation
period, 15 seconds long, directly after the beginning of the trial yields the worst results
when classifying nôÙback tasks to baseline. This suggests that the approach we followed
of subtracting the mean of the baseline from the whole duration of the nôÙback task is
not the best to evaluate the hemodynamic response. This can be explained also by the
fact that subjects are only beginning to memorise the stimuli and are not experiencing
workload yet.
Moreover, a significant limitation of this study regarding the evaluation of the hemodyôÙ
namic response is that we only assessed the frontal cortex. Future studies should evalôÙ
uate the connectivity in hemodynamic concentration between the frontal and parietal reôÙ
gions. Functional connectivity between these regions has been reported in fMRI studies
to be influenced by memory load, highlighting their role in working memory and cognitive
processes. By expanding the scope to include these additional regions, future research
can provide a more comprehensive understanding of cognitive impairment in critically ill
patients.
Throughout the study, the heterogeneity there is in the literature on what methodology to
follow when assessing NVC has been highlighted. Although NVC is a wellôÙestablished
concept, there is no consensus on the best methodology to capture this phenomenon.
Given this challenge, we aimed to evaluate NVC by analyzing the correlation between
both neuroimaging modalities and additionally evaluating the delay between the signals
that would yield the highest correlation. This methodology relies on the premise that reôÙ
gional neural activity is accompanied by both electrical activity generation and concurrent
metabolic variation. Therefore, a correlational analysis was conducted to investigate the
spatiotemporal association between hemodynamic and electrical patterns of brain funcôÙ
tion and relate these findings to cognitive function in the brain. However, after impleôÙ
menting the presented methodology and introducing a delay between both signals, no
significant results could be extracted. This is likely due to the high complexity of evaluatôÙ
ing NVC in such a large dataset and attempting to correlate it with cognitive function.
As has been mentioned, the duration of the nôÙback task is quite long, and the phenomenon
of NVC requires high spatiotemporal resolution. The approach we followed of correlating
the full recording including the four nôÙback tasks for both EEG and fNIRS, may not capture
the phenomenon with precision. One of the reasons for that is likely because we do not
have information on when the subject is actively engaging with the task. Consequently,
we cannot accurately evaluate when the NVC occurs during the full recording. FurtherôÙ
more, the extended duration of the task may dilute periods of high engagement, making
it challenging to detect the subtle changes in NVC.
Furthermore, NVC is a dynamic process, thus to be able to capture it we must ensure neuôÙ
ral activity and subsequent hemodynamic response are closely aligned to be accurately
captured. Studies have shown that focusing on more specific and shorter periods where
the subject is known to be highly engaged can give more accurate results. By isolating
shorter, more relevant time frames where subjects are actively engaged, these methodôÙ
ologies improve the temporal and spatial resolution necessary to observe the relationship
between neural activity and hemodynamic responses. For instance, the study conducted
by Rosengarten et al. [67] evaluated NVC in patients suffering from communityôÙacquired
pneumonia, showing more promising results for early microcirculatory dysfunction in inôÙ
flammatory syndromes. This study evaluates brain activity during Visually Evoked PotenôÙ
tials (VEP), which generated a much more clear neurological response in the subjects.
52
==PAGE:67==
Another study [69] also evaluated NVC using visual stimulation, obtaining promising reôÙ
sults regarding NVC in young and older adults. Future research should consider adopting
such refined approaches to better capture and understand NVC.
The temporal resolution of EEG and fNIRS is critical to ensure that the measured signals
accurately capture the rapid neural and vascular responses that take place during NVC.
To accurately capture NVC, many studies have convolved EEG band powers with an
HRF. This approach is done to align EEG data with the slower hemodynamic changes
measured by fNIRS, allowing for a more precise assessment of the correlation between
neural activity and blood flow. However, in our study, due to time limitations, we did
not employ an HRF. This decision represents a significant limitation, as convoluting the
EEG band powers with an HRF would have detected more accurately the changes in
neural activity and hemodynamic response. The use of HRFs is a common practice in
neuroimaging studies to model the expected hemodynamic response to neural activity,
providing a more refined temporal alignment between EEG and fNIRS signals. Studies
such as [70] and [71] have emphasized the importance of incorporating this approach. The
study [71] further detailed the physiological responses of neural activity and hemodynamic
response, highlighting the need for accurate temporal alignment to capture NVC. Future
studies should incorporate an HRF to better capture the spatiotemporal dynamics of NVC
and ensure that the measured signals accurately reflect the rapid neural and vascular
responses.
Other studies found in the literature, propose an integrated EEGôÙfNIRS system and exôÙ
tract entropy measures instead of doing a timeôÙfrequency analysis of the EEG signals.
The study [46] employs the PTE method and GRE measurement to quantify the broken
detailed balance of the human brain related to various working memory loads. These
features provide an advanced classification framework of working memory loads via asôÙ
sessing the broken detailed balance of NVC measures. This approach is the only one
found in literature attempting to assess working memory load using the nôÙback task.
Despite the previously mentioned limitations, I would like to highlight several challenges
encountered during the project. The heterogeneity in assessing NVC complicated the
selection of an appropriate methodology for this study. Moreover, the limitations due to
the artifacts in the data also did not allow to capture changes in alpha band which has
shown to be a relevant feature in cognitive function. Lastly, the long length of the nôÙback
task has also complicated the assessment of which periods is the subject more engaged
in the task, which are the ones that would show more significance. Nonetheless, this
project has helped to see the limitations in the project design, which will be considered to
follow a better methodology to assess NVC in critical illness.

7
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study aimed to explore the differences in EEG and fNIRS responses reôÙ
lated to cognitive function and to assess the strength of NVC using a multiôÙmodal paradigm
during the nôÙback task. Despite the lack of statistically significant results, the analysis
provided insights into the behaviour of Theta power in the frontal midline and HbO conôÙ
centration in the frontal cortex. Our findings indicated that patients exhibited higher Theta
power compared to controls, with a notable decrease during the 3ôÙback task, suggesting
difficulties with task complexity. Additionally, healthy controls showed a greater increase
in HbO concentration compared to patients, reflecting higher cognitive load handling.
The study faced several limitations due to the high complexity involved in feature exôÙ
traction during the nôÙback paradigm and the evaluation of NVC. The absence of a clear
consensus in the literature on the best methodology to assess NVC further complicated
the project.
Future research should focus on refining methodologies, including the use of HRFs to betôÙ
ter capture the intricate dynamics of NVC. Additionally, focusing on specific brain regions
and handling shorter time paradigms could enhance the precision of NVC assessments
in critically ill patients.
7.1
Future Work
The neuroimaging approaches presented in this work, which combine EEG and fNIRS
modalities to record brain activity, hold strong potential due to the multivariate data analyôÙ
sis that can be performed with these signals. This combined approach enables the evaluaôÙ
tion of cognitive function and the assessment of NVC differences between healthy controls
and critically ill patients.
Future directions for this project include incorporating convolution with an HRF to betôÙ
ter capture the temporal resolution of NVC. Additionally, further research could include
removing the artifacts in the EEG data for a more accurate assessment of cognitive funcôÙ
tion changes between both groups across the nôÙback tasks, particularly in the Alpha and
Beta frequency bands, in addition to Theta. Further studies could also explore the correôÙ
lation between both signals using entropy measures derived from EEG. Future research
could also include the assessment of hemodynamic changes in both critically ill patients
and healthy controls to evaluate neural connectivity between the frontal and parietal lobes,
as well as the changes in HbO concentration in these two brain regions. This approach
would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the neural networks involved and
the impact of critical illness on brain function.
To enhance the assessment of NVC in critically ill patients, the same study could be conôÙ
ducted using an experimental paradigm which ensures greater engagement from the subôÙ
jects and focuses on smaller brain regions. Moreover, a paradigm which created a higher
neural response would allow for a more precise analysis of the phenomenon.
