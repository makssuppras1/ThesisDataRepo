Abstract
The subject in this master thesis is thermo-mechanical topology optimization. Optimizing
structures towards thermal neutrality, by constraining the allowed displacement in points of
interest while minimizing the material used. Here the main task is to optimize a structure
with the purpose of holding an optical mirror, a housing for the mirror. In the problem case
the housing is exposed to thermal forces which can cause warping of the mirror. To limit the
eﬀect of the thermal forces on the mirror topology optimization is used.
The topology optimization code is extended from the basic 88-line handling simple minimize
compliance problems to also include thermo-neutrality problems. The thermo-neutrality
problem is deﬁned as minimizing the volume subject to a displacement constraint in some
speciﬁed degrees of freedom. In this case the interest will be to constrain the displacement on
the boundary of the mirror.
The ﬁrst part of the thesis covers a parameter study using thermal actuators of one and
two materials to validate the extensions of the code. The extensions used for the actuators
include the density ﬁlter with the Heaviside projection, the robust formulation and material
interpolation schemes.
The extensions used for the thermo neutrality problem covers introduction of passive elements
(deﬁning the mirror), a coating scheme, and a problem deﬁnition to constraint multiple degree
of freedoms. For numerical tests a bridge and a simple beam are used.
Results obtained from the optimization of the mirror holder shows that it is possible to reduce
the total material volume fraction by more than 6 [%] when using two materials with diﬀerent
thermal expansion (compared to using one material with low thermal expansion).
The viability of using coated structures for solving problems of thermal neutrality is explored,
using material soft material with high thermal expansion coeﬃcient as inﬁll and stiﬀmaterial
with low thermal expansion coeﬃcient as coating.

Introduction
The growth in applications within topology optimization have been driven by new production
methods such as additive manufacturing (3D printing). These have opened up a ﬁeld in
topology optimization catering to improve material distribution in 3D printing and made
designs, that earlier would be cumbersome to produce, relevant.
Most 3D printers use a solid outer layer and a inner lattice structure to save material. This
combination of a stiﬀer outer shell and a softer inner, gives good mechanical load properties.
This is also widely used in the industry where coatings and surface treatments have been
used to improve the mechanical properties of parts. Topology optimization of solid structures
with coating has been explored [1] and shown good properties for mechanical load problems.
Coating of plastics can increase its physical performance under mechanical loads but can
also have interesting properties when subjected to thermal loads. In this project coating will
be used to simulate a thin outer layer corresponding to electroplating a surface. Research
on optimizing structures like this in 2D and 3D has already been done [1] [? ], but with the
object of minimizing compliance. In this project we will explore if its possible to achieve good
performing designs that can constrain displacement in a certain area of a design space while
being subjected to thermal load and minimizing the material used. The design should be a
solid structure of a softer material with relatively large thermal expansion coeﬃcient coated
with a stiﬀer material with a lower thermal expansion. Two good candidates for the materials
could be ABS and cobber or nickel. In order to see how well our model can perform on a
real world problem a thermo-mechanical system which can be used to hold an optical mirror
has been used. An English university has expressed interest in this problem with regard to
mirrors in optical equipment, where small strains can course warping and distorted optical
results. Topology optimization has been used for shape preservation in the paper "Shape
preserving design with structural topology optimization" [? ], but here for a single material.
If a good design is achieved, a 3D printed model of it will be made and test done on it. In
order to achieve producible designs, a robust formulation [4] and ﬁlter scheme projecting and
sharpening the structure to deﬁne material will be used [1].

Project plan
2.1
Objective of the project
The objective of the project is to optimize structures exposed to thermo-mechanical loads.
The model will be able to create a structure which is not expanding at certain boundaries
when exposed to large temperature diﬀerences. Throughout the rapport this is referred to
as thermo-neutrality. Simple thermo-mechanical actuators are used to establish conﬁdence
in the model. Neutral thermal expansion of bi-material structures have already been made
using topology optimization, but a common problem with these structures is that they are
hard to produce, due to the two materials being entwined in each other. In order to make
the structures feasible to produce one material is restricted to lay on the surface of the other
material, acting as a coating. To achieve the optimized structure a topology optimization
scheme is used. The project was introduced due to the interest from a university in England
in neutral thermal expansion in order to hold optical mirrors, which will act as one of the
design problems in the report.

Introduction to Topology Optimization routine
The general formulation of the topology optimization problem can be deﬁned having an
objective which is sought to be minimized or maximized. This is done by distributing a design
parameter (e.g. density) within a given design space subject to prescribed constraints (e.g.
the allowed volume fraction in the design space). Furthermore a set of loads and boundary
conditions are applied to the design space. The design space is discretized using ﬁnite element
modelling (FEM) where each element is assigned a value between 0 and 1 using the SIMP
approach (see Section 8) to deﬁne material and void elements. The density ﬁlter is used to
avoid mesh-dependent designs with the Heaviside projection (see Section 10) to avoid gray
scale due to intermediate densities. In order to ﬁnd the optimal solution the gradient based
solver Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) [? ] is used (see Section 5).
An overview of the Matlab program can be seen in Figure 1 with description below.
(M):
Choose the size of the design space, discretization, the magnitude and number of loads,
the boundary conditions, and passive elements (elements with predeﬁned material and
not a part of the optimization). An initial density guess (starting point of the MMA) of
elements is made, and the allowed volume fraction is deﬁned. The ﬁlter radius (Rmin)
and the projection parameters (β, η, βFactor, IterStart,β,IterInter,β, βMax) are deﬁned.
(A1):
The initialization block is described in Appendix B.
(B1):
The ﬁltering and projection process is done in (B11) and is described in Section 10.1.
In (B12) the E and α interpolations are calculated (Section 8), the global K and F are
assembled and the displacements, U, are calculated (Section 6). In (B131) the derivatives
of K and F are calculated which is described in Section 8.1. In (B132) the displacements,
which can be used as either constraints (Section 12) or objective (Section 11.1) in the
optimization problem, are calculated. The sensitivities of the constraints or objective
using the adjoint method are calculated in (B1321), described in appendix 7. In the same
manner the constraints (Section 11.1) or objective (Section 12) of the volume and the
sensitivities are calculated in (B133). In (B134) the sensitivities of the passive elements
are put to zero and in (B1341) the sensitivities are ﬁltered as described in Section 10.2.
In (B14) the robust factor from eq. (43) is calculated.
(C1):
It is deﬁned what the objective (f0val, df0/dx) and constraints (fval, df/dx) are, which
depends on the problem.

FEM Analysis
The ﬁrst step in the Matlab code is to formulate the Finite Element equations. In order to
simplify the equations the assumption of plane stress and small deformations (linear elasticity)
have been made. The plane stress assumption is typically used for thin plates where stress in
the thickness direction can be neglected. The assumption of linear elasticity implies that the
stiﬀness of the structure does not change due to displacements. However the structure will
change during the topology optimization in which the stiﬀness needs to be updated. A study
of linear vs non-linear modelling can be viewed in the article by Sigmund [6] though it will
not be described in detail in this report.

Interpolation schemes
The most popular interpolation scheme is the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisation (SIMP)
approach proposed by Bendsøe and Sigmund (2003). The reason for using the SIMP approach
is that it makes intermediate densities unfavorable to choose due to the penalization factor.
The SIMP model using one material interpolates between solid and void elements and can be
seen in eq. (21).
E(ρ) = Emin + ρp(E0 −Emin)
(21)
Where E0 is the physical Young’s modulus of the material, Emin is the minimum stiﬀness
equal to 10−9 · E0 to avoid singularity, p is the penalization factor, and ρ is the vector of
densities assigned to each element. The Poisson’s ratio is held constant to 1/3 which makes a
penalization factor of p = 3 to be admissible according to [7].

to interpolate between material 1, 2 and voids. In eq. (22) and eq. (23) two interpolations are

Throughout the project, designs which demands for passive elements, will occur. Passive ele-
ments are deﬁned as elements which are not considered in the MMA during the optimization
process. To handle designs with passive elements a scheme with the ability to handle void
and passive materials of diﬀerent types has been implemented.
The scheme works by picking the elements of interest, which can be done by looping over the
elements in x and y direction to form a given geometry. In algorithm 1 the boundaries are
deﬁned by xvalup/down and yvalup/down.
A passive element matrix is constructed with zeros in the active elements and numbers to
denote voids or diﬀerent kinds of material in the passive elements. The passive element

passive materials are deﬁned as {1, 2.., m} ∈Z, where m is the number of passive materials.

If an additional passive material is needed x is set to one and a vector with the element
numbers corresponding to the additional passive materials, is used to set the E modulus and
thermal expansion coeﬃcient to the values of the additional material.

11.9
Part conclusion
In Sigmund’s article from 2001 [6] generally higher eﬃciency’s are obtained compared to the
results obtained here. The model used in the article has inherent diﬀerences compared to the
model used in this rapport.
In the article a sensitivity ﬁlter is used, we use a density ﬁlter and Heaviside projection.
In order to improve the non-convex problem that topology optimization is a continuation
method is used in the article. This is done by starting with a large rmin value (0.1 x length of
design space) and letting it gradually decrease to a low value (1.2 x element). In the model
used throughout this report this is done by gradually increasing beta.
From the β study it was found that initializing with a β value of 1 and increasing it by a factor
of 2 at iteration 150 and then every 100 iterations or when change < minchange gave good
and stable result, why it has been chosen to be the default setup.
The initial guess of material did increase the eﬃciency’s signiﬁcantly. This show that the
initial guess can have a large impact on the topology the model converges too. By having a
guess closer to viable solution the model will have a better change at converging towards a
eﬃcient optimum.
Since all parameters and the full setup used in the model from the article is unknown and we
use a diﬀerent ﬁltering scheme diﬀerence in performance of designs can be expected.
In section 11.2 we conﬁrm that the model does indeed converge to optimum solutions and
only have small discrepancies in results compared with analytically solutions, see table 2.

Discussion and conclusion
The conclusion of the parameter study of the thermal actuators seems to be that the parameters
can be tweaked to obtain good eﬃciencies. However it is rather diﬃcult to deﬁne global
optimal values of the parameters since it depends on the problem to be solved.
We have seen that it is possible to limit the material usage of one material by adding a material
with a larger thermal expansion.
Thermal mechanical topology optimization shows promising results for problems where
thermal neutrality is vital. Here in the report for optical mirror housings. Other applications
could be thermal sensitive measuring equipment, large structures where expanding material
could cause deformation and stresses in structures or in general when designing components
that are subject to thermal loads. Using a coated structure with a cheap soft material with
high thermal expansion coeﬃcient as inﬁll and an expensive stiﬀer material with a lower
thermal expansion coeﬃcient as coating opposed to make the entire structure of the expensive
material seems to be able to achieve similar thermal neutral performances.
This can save material resources. An inexpensive thermal neutral housing can be made in
either small numbers with additive manufacturing or in high volume with injection molding.
The housing can then be electroplated to add the coating.

Future work
Future implementations and studies have been considered and can be seen as bullet points
below.
• Additive manufacturing and coating of 2D design to do physical test.
• Adding moving boundary conditions to simulate the outer structures thermal expansion.
• Using the robust formulation for the coated and un-coated optimization problems. Eg
the mirror housing.
• Applying the optimization to other cases of thermal neutrality.
• Extend the optimization code to handle 3D cases in e.g. PETx.
