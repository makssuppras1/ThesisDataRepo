Abstract
In this project the concept of post combustion carbon capture and utilization is
investigated. First of all is the carbon capture process using monoethanolamine
optimized by lowering the concentration of the resulting carbon dioxide stream
from 99.2 % to 97.2 % on molar basis. Through a simulation of the process it
is concluded that this optimization saves 87.7 % of the utility cost while also de-
creasing the equipment purchase cost with 29.0 % as a side eﬀect.
The less pure carbon dioxide stream is then utilized to form methanol. This is
done in order to evaluate whether this process can be operated with the impuri-
ties, mostly water, in the reactor. Through simulations it is established that the
process is able to operate while still being feasible.
Lastly is a novel process design constructed for utilization of carbon dioxide. This
is done to form formic acid which shows great potential as a possible product from
carbon dioxide utilization. The process is evaluated and optimized to improve both
feasibility and sustainability. The ﬁnal process annually produces around 7.46·108
kg from the carbon dioxide formed by a 150 MW power plant. The pay back period
is 0.99 years for construction of the plant and the net return is 117,376,288 $. The
carbon footprint is calculated to be 1.74·106 kg equivalent CO2. This means that
the total process emits more carbon dioxide than what is captured.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In a world with an increasing energy consumption, problems with climate changes
resulting from the release of green house gasses is of increasing concern. One of the
most abundant green house gasses is carbon dioxide primarily released by com-
bustion of fossil fuels as oil and coal. While the carbon dioxide concentration in
the atmosphere is increasing there are no signs of the global energy demand de-
creasing. Especially developing countries are expanding their energy infrastructure
with more power plants and an increasing number of cars, and since the majority
of these run on fossil fuels, more and more carbon dioxide is emitted. Currently
is 75 % of the greenhouse gas emissions caused by combustion of fossil fuels (with
a small contribution from cement manufacture) while the rest is mostly caused by
land use change and chemical processing [11].
According to forecasts the global energy demand will have been increased with 53
% by 2030 leading to a 55 % increase of carbon dioxide emissions [12]. Figure 1.2
shows the global carbon dioxide emissions from the diﬀerent sources for the years
1990 to 2004.
The increasing amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere is believed to cause
a global temperature rise which will aﬀect the climate by, among other things,
melting the ice on the poles [11]. It is believed that the carbon dioxide levels in
the atmosphere will have doubled before 2050, and that the global temperature
increase caused by the carbon dioxide (among other causes) will be up to 4◦C by
the year 2100 [13].

The implications from the use of fossil fuels and the resulting emissions have led

One of these proposals is the post-combustion carbon capture technology which is
thought to be easily implemented since it is an end-of-pipe technology.
After capture of the carbon dioxide it could either be stored or utilized as a raw
material in the chemical industry. Storage is done by pumping the carbon dioxide
down into for example depleted oil reservoirs or other reservoirs several kilometres

1.1. THESIS OBJECTIVE
1.1
Thesis Objective
Given the environmental concerns about rising concentration of carbon dioxide
in the atmosphere sustainable solutions to the problem are needed. Part of the
solutions is to focus on renewable energy sources but changing from fossil fuels will
take decades thus other solutions are needed.
In this project it is desired to investigate post combustion carbon capture using
monoethanolamine as a solvent. It is wanted to verify whether lowering the exit
concentration of carbon dioxide from the capture process will decrease the energy
needed for the process enough to make it sustainable.
Subsequently it is desired to investigate whether the impurities introduced to the
stream of puriﬁed carbon dioxide complicates the utilization. This should be done
through two case studies.
The ﬁrst case is proof-of-concept by investigating a
methanol synthesis process utilizing the carbon dioxide where it is investigated
whether the process is able to operate while being feasible with the low purity
carbon dioxide.
The second case is to set up a novel process utilizing low-purity carbon dioxide.
The process should be analysed and subsequently optimized to improve both fea-
sibility and sustainability.

Chapter 2
Background Theory
This chapter gives an introduction to the theories behind the purpose of this
project and presents some of the concepts used later on as well as justiﬁes some
of the objectives.
2.1
Carbon Capture
The concept of capturing carbon dioxide has been investigated for many years
and several approaches to fulﬁlling the task has been proposed.
Among these
approaches are three main routes which are [14]:
• Pre combustion This methods converts the fuel into syngas which through
the water-gas-shift reaction forms a mixture of hydrogen and carbon dioxide.
Combustion of the hydrogen then leaves a stream of carbon dioxide and
water.
• Oxyfuel combustion By burning the fossil fuel under pure oxygen instead
of air the ﬂue gas will only contain easily separated water and carbon dioxide.
The cost of producing pure oxygen is the main downside for this process.
• Post combustion Capture of the carbon dioxide from the ﬂue gas after the
combustion of the fossil fuels.
Post combustion carbon capture is the route chosen for this project and it will
therefore be further described.
7
==PAGE:22==
2.1. CARBON CAPTURE
Post Combustion carbon Capture (hereafter PCC) is an end-of-pipe technology
which could limit the carbon dioxide emissions from stationary sources, for ex-
ample power plants and steel mills. The technology is intended to be added to
already existing carbon dioxide emitting facilities, and thereby decrease the carbon
footprint of these plants, as well as new plants.
Various diﬀerent approaches for capturing the carbon dioxide have been proposed,
including membrane separation, while the technology further described in this
project is a solvent based capture and release technology. This concept was devel-
oped for gas sweetening and has been used industrially for decades. The concept
is to feed the gas stream to an absorption unit where the carbon dioxide (and H2S)
is absorbed by a solvent through a chemical reaction. One of the most used and
best described solvents is monoethanolamine (hereafter MEA) diluted with water,
while other amines have also been used. Typical MEA concentrations lie between
20 and 30 weight percent since higher concentrations give problems with increased
corrosion; however, these problems can be reduced by additives.
After the absorption of the carbon dioxide, the carbon dioxide containing solvent
stream needs to be regenerated which is done by heating the stream in a desorption
column. While the absorption process is exothermic, the reversed process, the
desorption, is endothermic and therefore requires an energy input. Typically the
temperature in the desorption unit is around 130 ◦C. This allows a satisfying level
of regeneration while higher temperatures would lead to degradation of the MEA.
The utility needed for the regeneration of the solvent is the main downside for
the solvent based carbon capture process and is what most research is focused on
decreasing.

2.2. UTILIZATION
2.2
Utilization
Several possible routes exists for what to do with the captured carbon dioxide.
One possibility is to store it underground in depleted oil reservoirs. This solution
has the advantage that it will enhance the oil recovery from the reservoir while
diminishing the carbon dioxide emissions. According to the US Department of
Energy [16] using enhanced oil recovery (hereafter EOR) techniques can allow 30
to 60 % of the oil in the reservoirs to be utilized while without EOR this number
lies between 20 and 40 %.
The success of carbon dioxide storage in oil reservoirs depends on the safety of the
technology, while enhanced oil recovery will lead to more oil burning where car-
bon dioxide needs to be captured. The risk of earthquakes releasing the captured
carbon dioxide from the reservoirs might be concerning, while an earthquake in
Japan measuring 6.8 on the Richter-scale did not release any carbon dioxide from
the Nagaoka project with carbon dioxide stored in the underground [17].
Another interesting possibility is to utilize the captured carbon dioxide as a raw
material in the chemical industry. Here the carbon dioxide would substitute other
carbon sources as a building block for forming larger organic compounds. Depend-
ing on the original carbon source this might be a more sustainable way to form
organic compounds. Several diﬀerent compounds are subject to possible produc-
tion from captured carbon dioxide. Among these are [18]:
• Methanol
• Formaldehyde
• Acetic acid
• DME
• Formic acid
These are just a very few of the possibilities. A lot of other compounds are theo-
retically possible to produce from carbon dioxide. All of the above compounds are
proposed to be produced through a reaction between carbon dioxide and hydrogen,
which might implicate the carbon dioxide balance badly. Methanol is for example
produced industrially by ﬁrst production of synthesis gas from natural gas. This

catalyst. If the hydrogen used for the utilization of carbon dioxide still comes from
synthesis gas, spare carbon monoxide will be present which needs to be used for
something. Therefore, is the use of carbon dioxide as a raw material only beneﬁcial
if there is a sustainable method for producing the co-reactants, often hydrogen.

Formic Acid
After the investigation of the methanol synthesis, a short literature survey was
done in order to ﬁnd another pathway for utilization of carbon dioxide worth
investigating. Several possible products were considered, including formaldehyde
and urea, but in the end formic acid was chosen as the product for the third case.
In this section formic acid is described and the current and possible uses as well

3.3.1
Uses
Formic acid is industrially produced by reacting methanol with carbon monoxide
in the presence of a strong base, and this is annually done to produce around
800,000 tonnes [10]. Formic acid is used for a wide variety of purposes, including:
• in the textile industry
• for cleaning
• as a preservative
The amount of formic acid is not impressive if compared to the worldwide annual
human emission of carbon dioxide around 35 GT, but the interest in formic acid
lies in a potential use.
Formic acid has a hydrogen density of 53 g H2 per liter and is therefore a possible
and safe storage material for hydrogen. Hydrogen as a fuel is very interesting but
has several diﬃculties. Especially the storage and distribution of hydrogen will re-
quire a totally new network and technology. Formic acid is, at standard conditions,
a non-toxic liquid and could therefore be a solution to the above problems. The
de-hydrogenation of formic acid to release the hydrogen has been done both with
homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts and recently also with cheap materials
as iron [10].
This potential use is the reason for why formic acid is an interesting chemical to
produce from carbon dioxide and why it has been chosen for this third case study.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In this chapter the results and the assumptions taken on the road to these results
are discussed. This is done in order to give a clariﬁcation of the validity of the
results. The discussion is conducted for each of the three cases separately starting
with the carbon capture case.
Carbon Capture Optimization
The carbon capture process was optimized by decreasing the requirement for the
exit concentration of carbon dioxide to 97 % on molar basis. The process was not
optimized by other means and it should therefore be possible to further optimize
the process. The optimized process uses 102.2 MW for the heating. As the process
is scaled to ﬁt a 150 MW combustion unit, around 68 % of the power generated is
used for the capture process. This gives a very ineﬀective power plant and would
mean that around three times the amount of combustion units are needed to gen-
erate the same energy output. SaskPower’s carbon capture facilities were ﬁtted to
a combustion unit which without the capture unit generated 139 MW and with
the capture unit activated generated 110 MW. That means they are loosing 21 %
of the generated power to the capture process. It should however be noted that
SaskPower’s capture facility only captures 90 % of the carbon dioxide while not
reporting on the purity of the carbon dioxide exit stream.
All of this shows that the capture process optimized in this project is not the op-
timal design, but that was not the objective of the project. The objective was to
show the eﬀect of decreasing the concentration and the eﬀect proved to be signiﬁ-
69
==PAGE:84==
cant with an utility saving of 87.6 % on the reboiler and 37.2 % on heat exchanger
1 which heats the solvent before the desorption column. Thereby is the optimized
process showing that decreasing the concentration is a very promising option for
lowering the utility need for the capture process while comparing the process with
the SaskPower capture process shows that lowering the concentration can not be
the only parameter that needs to be optimized.
The way the simulations are designed means that one of the parameters changed
for the simulation to converge is the concentration as well as amount of MEA
compared to water in the feed to the absorption column. Thereby is the ratio
between ﬂue gas and solvent not the same for the reference and optimized designs.
For the reference case the concentration of MEA is 13 % on a molar basis while it
is around 30 % on molar basis for the optimized case.
Lawal et al. [29] simulated the eﬀect of operating with 20, 30 and 40 % MEA
and reports that an increase in the concentration will ease the capture and re-
lease process and thereby decrease the utility needed. Changing from 20 to 40
% increases the net power output from 437 MW to 467 MW while it is 500 MW
without carbon capture included in the simulation. This change is a decrease in
utility for the capture process on around 50 % thus a part of the saving determined
in this project is coming from the increase in concentration of MEA and not from
the decrease in concentration of carbon dioxide in the product stream. The total
decrease in utility determined in this project is, however, still of a size that can
not be explained solely by the increase of MEA concentration and lowering the
concentration of carbon dioxide is thus still showing the potential of being a good
way to improve the process.
When this diﬀerence in operating concentration of MEA was discovered a quick
study was done to investigate the impact. This was done by changing the opti-
mized process so that the concentration changed from 30 % to 20 %. This changed
the reboiler duty to 0.1665 GJ/s from 0.0945 GJ/s as reported in section 5.1. This
gives a duty saving on the reboiler of 77.6 % instead of the 87.6 %, proving the
point above that some of the saving determined in this project comes from the
change in concentration, but this only accounts for a part of the saving and the
change in carbon dioxide concentration is therefore still a good design change.
Operating with a higher concentration of carbon dioxide gives some complications
70

with regards to corrosion in the equipment. This can, however, be controlled by
additives which will inhibit corrosion.
Combining the low purity design with other optimizations as adding more trays
to the desorption column, improved column designs, mixed solvents or additives
could give a process which would be a lot more sustainable and feasible than the
designs currently in operation. Further simulations would be necessary to prove
this postulate but this is outside the scope of this project.
Methanol Synthesis
The simulation of a methanol synthesis process utilizing carbon dioxide from the
capture process was conducted in order to assess whether the process could op-
erate with the less pure feed of carbon dioxide. The process is only a base case
design and it is therefore not an optimized process. Based on this, the process
design conducted in this project should not lead to the construction of the plant,
however, the simulations serve their purpose. The process is able to operate and
are being feasible with the less pure feed of carbon dioxide.
The process could be further optimized by constructing a heat exchanger network
or greater recycling of purges as possible ways. This means that the process can
become even more feasible and sustainable than the design included in this project.
Formic Acid Synthesis
The last case serves as a novel design for utilization of the captured carbon dioxide
for the formation of formic acid while again showing that a process design can be
made where the impurities in the carbon dioxide feed is not a problem. For the
formic acid synthesis the water is actually an advantage since the reaction, accord-
ing to the patent and the article the reaction conditions are based on, occurs in
the presence of water, and because water is needed to keep the produced formic
acid stable under storage. If the process could operate with only the water in the
carbon dioxide feed stream the cost of operation would decrease since the recycle
streams would be smaller and the feed into the distillation column would be on
the right side of the azeotrope which could possibly ease the separation.

==PAGE:86==
As mentioned previously are the reaction conditions based on an article and a
patent with limited data. Therefore has the ﬁnal reaction conditions been based
on more or less justiﬁed assumptions. The validity of these assumptions highly
questions the accuracy of the results for this case. The degree of conversion has
no veriﬁcation from the neither the article nor the patent and is therefore highly
questionable. The temperature and pressure are chosen based on data and are
probably good estimates for the process. Both are though based on data from
batch experiments.
Because of these inaccuracies it can not be recommended to construct the plant
based on this project. This project, however, shows that there is a big potential
in the process as a path to utilize captured carbon dioxide but further studies are
needed. First of all is it necessary to acquire better data on the reaction and the
optimum conditions. Further experiments are needed, especially for continuous
operation - ﬁrstly in laboratory scale and later in pilot plant scale. This will en-
able a more precise process to be designed and the following economical evaluation
would give a more realistic result. This does not mean that the results achieved in
this project are not realistic. They are uncertain because of the uncertain reaction
conditions.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This project has had three main objectives all with the same cause: to decrease
the emission of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.
The ﬁrst objective was to investigate whether the post combustion carbon capture
process using solvent based capture technology could be optimized with regards to
utility need by decreasing the requirement for the concentration of carbon dioxide
in the exit stream. This investigation was conducted through simulations in the
steady state process simulation software Pro/II for a reference case designed by
Godfroy [19] and an optimized case where the speciﬁcations for the desorption
column were altered. Through these simulations it can be concluded that the al-
terations to the desorption column yielded a 87.7 % saving on the utility cost and
as a side eﬀect from the optimization the total equipment cost was also decreased
by 29.0 %. The saving on utility cost is mainly achieved by a 87.6 % saving on
energy needed for the reboiler and 37.2 % on the heat exchanger heating the feed
going into the desorption column. The energy need for the condenser is decreased
by 92.3 % while the heat exchanger cooling the lean loaded solvent being recycled
to the absorption column increases by 20.3 %. Based on these numbers it can be
concluded that the desired energy saving was achieved by lowering the exit con-
centration of carbon dioxide from 99.2 % to 97.2 % on molar basis while the total
carbon dioxide removal does not change signiﬁcantly. It can also be concluded
that the carbon footprint for the capture process changes from 4.9 kg CO2 equiv-
alent/kWh to -0.26 kg CO2 equivalent/kWh. This means that the process goes
from emitting more carbon dioxide than it captures to actually capture more than
73
==PAGE:88==
is being emitted.
In the discussion (chapter 6) it was concluded that the capture process uses 102.2
MW on heating utilities, and thereby is the net power output from the power plant
only 47.8 MW. It is concluded that this energy consumption still is too high for
the carbon capture process, but since the objective was to investigate the eﬀect of
lowering the concentration of carbon dioxide and not to design the optimal process
the objective has still been fulﬁlled.
It is discussed whether the change in MEA concentration is accounting for some
of the energy savings and it is concluded that this is the case, while it does not
account for the majority of the saving.
The second objective of the project is to evaluate whether the less pure carbon
dioxide would give any problems with regards to a following utilization process.
It is concluded that the methanol synthesis is feasible while operating with the
less pure carbon dioxide even though the introduction of water as an impurity
does decrease the conversion of carbon dioxide in the reactor. It is also concluded
that since the process design is only a base case design, further optimizations are
deﬁnitely possible and the process should therefore become even more proﬁtable.
As a feed of hydrogen is needed for the synthesis of methanol the sustainability
highly depends on the source of hydrogen.
The third objective was to design a novel process for utilization of carbon dioxide.
It was chosen to design a process forming formic acid. Among the reasons for
choosing this substance was that it according to literature should be a reaction
happening in the presence of water while water plays a role in keeping the substance
stable at storage conditions.
Through the design of the process and optimization of it, it can be concluded that
capturing carbon dioxide from ﬂue gas and utilizing it for the formation of formic
acid is a feasible operation under the assumptions taken in this project. It is,
however, concluded that the assumptions adds a big uncertainty to the process
and the economical analysis and that further work are needed before constructing
a plant for utilizing carbon dioxide to produce formic acid.
Initially the hydrogen was assumed to be acquired for free but by varying the price
it was concluded that the process is very sensitive to these changes. If the hydrogen
source is hydrolysis of water the process would no longer be feasible, while steam

7.1
Future Work
Based on the results of this project, proposals for future work is presented for the
further investigation and improvement of carbon capture and utilization.
Further work is needed on optimization of the carbon capture process. This project
has assessed the gaining by decreasing the concentration of carbon dioxide but this
does not give a fully optimized process. Changing the concentration of carbon diox-
ide is one of the many routes for decreasing the utility need for the process and
further work, combining low purity with other of the routes is needed in order to
evaluate the full potential of the capture process.
As the process for methanol synthesis investigated in this project is not a fully op-
timized design, further work is needed on the process before actually constructing
a plant for operation.
As discussed previously especially the reaction conditions for the formic acid syn-
thesis are very uncertain and future work is needed in order to set up a certain
simulation and analysis of the process before constructing an actual plant running
the process. More data is needed about continuous operation and the dependence
of controllable parameters as pressure, temperature and amount of water.
