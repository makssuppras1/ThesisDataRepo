Abstract 
Iron zeolites were prepared by ion exchanging parent zeolites BEA and ZSM-5. This resulted in 
catalysts with iron contents of <3 wt.% as determined by XRF and quantitative EPR. These catalyst, 
were tested under SCR conditions and monitored with EPR. Results showed that only peaks at high 
g values (>4) were affected by the feed. Here ZSM-5 and BEA both had a peak at g = 4.3, while 
ZSM-5, in oxidative conditions, had an extra peak at g = 6. Simulations of these peaks revealed that 
isolated iron species in axial (g = 6) and distorted tetrahedra (g = 4.3) were present in the zeolite 
structure. For ZSM-5 it was seen that lowering the salt concentration during ion exchange led to the 
disappearance of the g = 6 peak. Both structure types were not irreversible affected by the addition 
of water to the feed. It was attempted to run NH3TPD but EPR did not reveal much information about 
the desorption of NH3, as the peaks did not change during heating from 100 °C to 300 °C. Along with 
this heating, a cooling to 77 K was also performed ex situ. This provided information on the 
temperature dependence of all the observed peaks revealing that the peak at g = 4.3 increased upon 
cooling while the broad peak at g = 2 decreased upon cooling. The iron content of proton zeolites was 
also investigated, and both structures returned positive results, however at significantly lower 
concentration than in the ion exchanged samples.

Introduction 
Nitrogen oxides or NOx (x=1,2) gasses are responsible for several respiratory diseases [1], smog 
formation and acid rain [2]. The main source or NOx is from the road transport sector which is 
accountable for 39 % of the total NOx emissions in 2015 [3]. To combat this the EU have made 
regulations on how much NOx is allowed to be discharged from heavy duty diesel engines [4]. This 
has forced the automotive industry to come up with solutions as to how NOx emissions should be 
reduced. 
The selective catalytic reduction (SCR) of NOx with heterogenous catalysts is one of the top 
contenders in NOx emission reduction, and has been under development for several decades. It is still 
undergoing development to produce better and more stable catalysts for various applications in both 
mobile and stationary exhausts. The SCR reaction consists of NOx which is reduced by typically 
ammonia to produce nitrogen, which is much more environmentally friendly than NOx. Zeolites are 
useful supports for various transition metal catalysts as their porous structure only allows small 
molecules such as NOx, CO2, NH3, H2O to pass through, and therefore limits side reactions which 
might occur at the active sites of the catalysts. Commercially, zeolites Beta and ZSM-5, are cheap 
and industrially available, unlike the more expensive MOR or CHA zeolites [5; 6], while still 
maintaining sufficient catalytic activity. As the active site in the catalyst both copper, iron, manganese 
and vanadium are good candidates along with other redox active metals.  
While the various catalytic reactions contain many steps, three main reactions are important to be 
familiar with when discussing SCR: 
Standard SCR, Eq. 1, is the overall reaction for the reduction of NO with ammonia. 
4NO + 4NH3 + O2 →4N2 + 6H2O 
Eq. 1 
Fast SCR, Eq. 2, is an alternative version of SCR which takes place when NO2 is present, and reduces 
both NO and NO2 to N2. As the name suggests, this reaction is usually significantly faster than 
standard SCR [7].  
2NO + 2NO2 + 4NH3 →4N2 + 6H2O 
Eq. 2 
NO oxidation, Eq. 3, is a reaction that oxidizes NO to NO2, which makes fast SCR possible. 
2NO + O2 →2NO2 
Eq. 3 
The challenge in developing good SCR catalysts is to make them active at relatively low 
temperatures, as the DeNOx catalyst is placed far from the engine, and the exhaust gas goes through 
multiple other purification catalysts before arriving at the DeNOx catalyst. To successfully do this it 
is helpful to know the nature of the active sites in the catalyst. A method successfully used for 
investigation of active sites is electron paramagnetic spectroscopy (EPR) which only focuses on the 
unpaired electrons in the samples. For a zeolite catalyst containing transition metal ions such as 
copper or iron, this technique allows zooming in on just the active site to study its behaviour during 
exposure to a various SCR active gasses.

Theory 
A brief theory section is presented to provide the reader with an idea of the basic concepts surrounding 
this report. It will include the general subjects relevant to DeNOx SCR along with an introduction to 
Electron paramagnetic Resonance (EPR) and X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) which are the measurement 
techniques applied in this study. The sources given in headlines refers to a literature work relevant to 
the entire section, and is referred to if the reader wishes for further information on the subject. 
Zeolites [8] 
Zeolites are porous crystal structures with the general formula [Mx (SiO2)y (AlO2)z]. Where M is a 
cation balancing the negative charge introduced by the [AlO2]-. Typically, either protons, alkali or 
alkaline earth metals are replacing M, but small molecules such as ammonium can also be balancing 
the charge. The backbone of the zeolites is made up of interconnected [SiO4]-4 and [AlO4]-5 tetrahedra, 
also referred to as the primary building blocks of zeolites. These primary building blocks can be put 
together to make secondary building blocks, which in turn can be put together forming a unit cell. 
This unit cell can be repeated to form the final zeolite structure, and the ideal zeolite structure is 
therefore typically well described. Besides the unit cell, the Si/Al ratio (sometimes SiO2/Al2O3 which 
is the same as Si/Al except its twice as big) is important when describing a given zeolite. This ratio 
dictates the amount of charge balancing cations in the structure, and furthermore the stability of the 
structure, as the introduction of aluminium decreases the stability. Typical Si/Al ratios are 10-infinite, 
but this is also dependent on the zeolite type. The highest theoretically obtainable Si/Al ratio is 1, as 
structures with two aluminium tetrahedra connected (Al – O – Al) aren’t stable as dictated by 
Löwensteins rule [9]. This makes it harder for multiple valency cations to bind to the structure, as the 
probability of two Al atoms in near vicinity of the cation is lower than for cations only relying on a 
single Al atom. This problem can be solved by binding to other anions inside the structure such as 
OH- such that Fe3+ doesn’t require three Al atoms, but can instead make due with one or two along 
with balance OH- ions.   
Zeolites contain several features which makes them attractive when discussing heterogeneous 
catalysis, and DeNOx SCR. They are found naturally, but they can also be synthesized in the lab with 
few defects. The cation balance ions can be easily adjusted with for example ion exchange or 
impregnation to produce the desired zeolite catalyst. Their structure is well defined, which makes 
catalytic active sites easier to be identified, whether it is a metal or a proton. The skeleton of the 
zeolites works as a sieve which induces shape selectivity. This prevents molecules larger than the 
zeolite channels to enter, it prevents intermediates too large for the zeolite structure to be formed, 
effectively excluding some reactions, and lastly it prevents the formation, of products too large for 
the zeolite cavities. It should be noted that products small enough to be formed, but too large to diffuse 
through the zeolite can still be formed, but inactivates the site of formation due to steric hindrance. 
The zeolite structure is generally thermally stable at temperatures up to 600 °C, where further heating 
can lead to dealumination. Finally their porous structure leaves a large surface for active sites making 
it able to obtain many active sites per volume and therefore high reaction rates. 
ZSM-5 
ZSM-5 is a zeolite with MFI structure (Figure 1). It has both linear and sinusoidal channels where 
spherical particles with a diameter of ~4.4 Å can diffuse along. It is widely used in industry, and is 
considered a cheap zeolite, like BEA, compared to other DeNOx zeolites like SSZ-13, which become 
expensive upon large scale production [6].

Catalysis with zeolites [11]  
Zeolite used in catalysis belongs to the category of heterogeneous catalysis, in which two different 
phases interact, namely the gas and solid phase. The solid phase consists of the transition metal on 
the zeolite support, while the gas phase is the various SCR gasses. The requirement for the catalyst 
thus include the ability to adsorb SCR gasses onto the active site, along with the ability to catalyse 
the SCR reaction and return to the initial state, completing the catalytic cycle.

SCR 
The NH3 used for reduction of NOx as stated in Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 needs to be supplied from an external 
source as this isn’t part of the engine exhaust. In stationary plants this can be gaseous NH3, but in 
mobile applications urea/water solutions are used as a source of NH3. This is done in a two step 
process, where in the first step, water is evaporated from the mixture, and the urea molecule 
decomposes into one part NH3 and one part isocyanic acid (see Eq. 4).  
(NH2)2CO →NH3 + HNCO 
Eq. 4 
Next is the reaction of isocyanic acid with water, which converts the isocyanic acid into CO2 and NH3 
(see Eq. 5). 
HNCO + H2O →NH3 + CO2 
Eq. 5 
DeNOx catalysts are typically either vanadia, copper or iron based where the latter two are 
incorporated into zeolites. For catalysis at 200 °C both iron and vanadia, conversion depends greatly 
on the NO2/NOx ratio (having an optimum at 0.5), while copper catalysts are less dependent on the 
NO2/NOx ratio. All three types decreases in conversion when NO2/NOx approaches one, but vanadia 
is more strongly affected, where the other two are only affected to a lesser extent. A problem with 
copper catalysts though is that they are very susceptible to sulfur poisoning [11; 12]. The sulfur 
content in diesel has decreased dramatically since 1965 where the sulfur content was close to 1 wt. 
%, to 2005 where the number is lowered to less than 10 ppm [13]. Even though this is a great reduction 
in sulfur content, sulfur is still a consideration to take into account when designing catalyst for use in 
diesel engines. 
Another important aspect of the catalyst is its low temperature performance, as the catalyst should be 
able to perform at temperatures as low as 200 °C. Simultaneously the catalyst should retain high 
performance and stability at higher temperatures. Comparing copper and iron zeolite catalysts here, 
it is generally found that copper has the better low temperature activity, while iron has the better high 
temperature activity [14]. Along with high temperatures and water present in the exhaust comes also 
the problem of hydrothermal aging. This describes another requirement of the catalyst. It should retain 
high performance across multiple catalytic cycles where water is present in the exhaust gas without 
destroying the zeolite structure. 
NOx formation 
NOx is generated when mixtures of oxygen and nitrogen reacts at elevated temperatures. This is the 
case in combustion engines such as the ones found in diesel motors operating under lean burn 
conditions (excess oxygen compared to fuel). The mechanism for NO formation in a combustion 
engine is called the Zeldovich mechanism and uses three equations (Eq. 6 - Eq. 8) to describe NO 
formation [15; 16].  
O + N2 →NO + N 
Eq. 6 
O2 + N →NO + O

The lean burn engine leads to a decrease in hydrocarbon emissions, but with the unfortunate side 
effect of an increase in NOx formation. This includes both NO and NO2, but typically NO2 is formed 
by oxidation of NO in oxygen rich environments [17]. The amount of NO2 compared to the total 
amount of nitrous oxides can be up to 10 – 30 % [16]. The NO oxidation to NO2 is suggested to be 
the rate determining step [18] for some Cu and Fe ZSM-5 catalysts, but this is up to debate as other 
groups [19; 20] find this impossible due to the low rate of NO oxidation compared to the rate of 
standard SCR. 
EPR [22; 23; 21] 
Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) is a spectroscopic method that utilizes the spin of an unpaired 
electron to describe the environment around the parent atom or molecule. This limits the technique 
to systems containing unpaired electrons, as the pairing of electrons cancels the observed effect 
produced by the electron. A system with unpaired electrons is characterized by the spin quantum 
number S, and the secondary spin quantum number ms which is the projection of S onto an arbitrarily 
chosen z-axis (typically parallel to the magnetic field). ms is takes values from -S to S with steps of 
one, and the number of allowed ms values can be summarized as 2S + 1. For the simple system S = 
½, ms is allowed to be either +½ or –½ allowing observation of the transition between these two 
states. 
Without a magnetic field acting upon the system, these two energy levels will be degenerate and 
therefore equal in energy. However, once a magnetic field is applied a splitting of the energy levels 
occurs based on whether the electron spin is parallel (β, ms = –½) or antiparallel (α, ms = +½) to the 
applied field This is known as the Zeeman effect and is illustrated in Figure 3. The energy of the 
system with a magnetic field acting upon it is given in Eq. 9. 
E = g µB msB  
Eq. 9 
Where g is a dimensionless factor correcting for the magnetic moment of the electron, µB is the Bohr 
magneton and B is the strength of the magnetic field. As g, µB and ms are constants it can be seen that 
there is a linear correlation between the applied magnetic field and the energy of the system. 
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the Zeeman effect. 
In continuous wave EPR (CW EPR), which is the most simple approach, the system is irradiated with 
a continuous wave of energy typically around 9 GHz corresponding to the X-band. While this energy 
==PAGE:12==

is applied to the system, the magnetic field is swept. When the magnetic field is increased enough 
that the energy difference is matched, an absorption of energy occurs, which is then detected by the 
detector in the EPR instrument. This results in a spectrum with absorption on the ordinate axis against 
magnetic field strength on the abscissa. As a way to reduce the signal to noise ratio during an EPR 
measurement, the magnetic field is equipped with a lesser oscillating field. This oscillation is matched 
with the receiver and then signal obtained corresponds to the gradient of the absorption curve. In 
practice this means the presented EPR spectrum is the first derivative of the absorption spectrum is 
shown against the magnetic field. 
Quantification with EPR 
The intensity of the signal obtained in the EPR measurement is proportional to the amount of centres 
absorbing at a given magnetic field. This makes EPR able to quantify the amount of EPR active 
species in a given sample by comparing the unknown sample intensity with the intensity of a series 
with known amounts of EPR active material. As mentioned, the EPR spectrum is given as the first 
derivative of the absorption spectrum. Thus, to obtain the total intensity, the spectrum  has to be 
integrated twice across the entire field. In Figure 4, an EPR spectrum of Cu2+ with S = ½ is displayed 
along with both the integral (absorbance spectrum) and double integral (intensity spectrum). 
 
Figure 4: EPR spectrum of Cu2+. The EPR spectrum is red, while the green and blue lines are the first and second integrals 
respectively.  
Typically, multiple sources of signal exist, coming from unpaired electrons in slightly varying 
environments, and the observed EPR spectrum is thus the summation of all the contributions. To find 
the impact of a single species, a recreation of the observed spectrum is made from simulations, where 
each simulated specie can be weighted against each other until a simulated spectrum matching the 
observed spectrum is obtained.  
It is important to keep the temperature constant as this will change the recorded intensity according 
to a variety of factors. The Curie law for magnetic susceptibility is influenced by the temperature, the 
Boltzmann distribution is influenced by temperature along with material specific parameters such as 
ferromagnetism/antiferromagnetism.

EPR on Iron 
When using EPR on iron catalyst, iron is expected to be present as either Fe2+ or Fe3+. For an 
octahedral geometry this gives a total of four different scenarios depending on whether the system is 
high or low spin. The energy diagram for each of these scenarios is presented in Figure 5. It is seen 
that all systems except low spin Fe2+ contains unpaired electrons and therefore should be applicable 
to EPR. However, since the zero field splitting (see next section), is greater than what can be observed 
with X-band EPR, high spin Fe2+ is also silent. Furthermore, when iron coordinates to oxygen it is in 
its high spin state, and the scenario with low spin Fe3+ will not be seen. The observed EPR spectrum 
will this be based on a S = 5/2 system. However, iron can also have and intermediate spin 
corresponding to S = 3/2. This can be explained by iron being in an environment where the energy 
diagram is not octahedral, but for example square pyramidal instead [24]. 
As stated earlier the ms states takes values from +S to -S in steps of one. For a S = 5/2 system this is 
visualised by having all electrons pointing in the same direction for ms = 5/2, then flipping the spin 
of one electron causes ms to be 3/2. Changing the spin of another electron causes the spin to be 1/2 
etc.Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Visualization of degenerate d-orbitals for various ms values when S = 5/2 for Fe3+.

XRF [25] 
X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) is a method used in analytical chemistry which utilizes the fluorescence 
produced by an exited atom returning to a relaxed state by emission of a photon. The sample is 
radiated with an X-ray tube producing X-rays with enough energy that one of the inner shell electrons 
are knocked out. To avoid confusion between X-rays going in and out of the sample, X-rays from the 
X-ray tube are labelled primary radiation, while the photons emitted by the sample are labelled 
secondary radiation. The secondary radiation is much weaker than the primary radiation, and for small 
nuclei, it can be difficult to detect. This means that the element examined should have at least three 
shells, corresponding to Na and elements greater than Na. The iron in the sample will have electrons 
in K, L and M shells, and the characteristic wavelengths are thus found in Kα for relaxations L  K, 
Kβ for M  K relaxations and Lα for M  L relaxations. Depending on the spin of the electron each 
wavelength is a doublet with close to, identical energies. A typical instrument operates only on a few 
select lines chosen to represent each element.  
A typical problem with XRF is the secondary radiation being absorbed by nearby atoms, which is 
called “matrix effects”. To avoid this it is recommended by the International Zeolite Association 
(IZA) that the sample is melted into a glass disc and making references in similar discs in order to 
obtain precise (relative standard deviation of -0.1 - 0.2 %) XRF results on zeolite structures [10].

Catalyst synthesis 
The samples were prepared using a LIE (Liquid Ion Exchange) method inspired by the works of other 
groups [29; 28; 30; 31; 27; 6]. Parent zeolites BEA (Si/Al = 19) and ZSM-5 (Si/Al = 11.5) were 
obtained from Zeolyst in their ammonium forms and were calcinated at 580 °C for 4 hours with a 
heating rate of 7 °C/min in ambient air to convert them into their protonated forms. 2 g of calcinated 
zeolite was mixed with 200 mL of either 1.5, 3 or 4 mM Fe(NO3)3 solution at 80 °C for 24 hours at 
pH ~ 2-3 (actual concentrations and zeolite weight listed in appendix). Produced iron zeolites were 
then filtered and washed with 1 L demineralised water, and finally calcinated again. For use in EPR 
experiment, samples were fractionated to a particle size of 150-300 µm. The final samples were 
named after zeolite type and concentration of ion exchange solution such that the BEA zeolite 
exchanged in 1.5 mM Fe(NO3)3 is referred to as Fe-BEA_15, the BEA exchanged in 3 mM Fe(NO3)3 
is referred to as Fe-BEA_3 and the BEA exchanged in 4 mM Fe(NO3)3 is referred to as Fe-BEA_4. 
Similarly, the ZSM-5 catalyst are referred to as Fe-ZSM-5_15, Fe-ZSM-5_3 and Fe-ZSM-5_4.

Results 
The results of the experimental work are presented in this section along with a description of the 
observations relevant to the discussion.  
Unless otherwise noted, all EPR spectra have been normalized to 1 mg sample, 9.45 GHz frequency, 
and a background consisting of an empty tube with glass wool have been subtracted. Every EPR 
spectrum is also presented with a y-axis, which is arbitrary in itself, but provides a picture of the 
relative intensities between samples, as all results are normalized to identical conditions with respect 
to microwave frequency and sample size. 
For EPR spectra of ZSM-5 the peaks are named according to the names used in literature [32]. The 
three peaks dominating the discussion are the peaks at g = 2, 4.3 and 6, labelled α, γ and β respectively. 
It is believed that these three peaks correspond to isolated [Fe3+] sites in either distorted octahedral 
(β), distorted tetrahedral (γ) or highly symmetric (α) coordination. The peak at g = 2 is a summation 
of both the α peak along with a broad peak arising from non-isolated iron species, and iron outside 
ion exchange positions. For Fe-ZSM-5, it has been suggested that these signals correspond to specific 
sites within the MFI structure corresponding to within the 5 member ring (γ), within the 6 member 
ring (β) and lastly within the channel system (α). It should however be noted that these iron sites are 
suggested based on a study on Co2+ [33], and the preferences for Fe3+ might be different. Nevertheless, 
throughout this study, the same labelling will be used for these three sites/peaks. While BEA have a 
different structure, the sites cannot be translated directly into this structure, but the same names will 
still be used when peaks arise at these positions in the Fe-BEA EPR spectrum.

On Figure 7 the EPR spectra of the samples used for quantification are presented. It is noted that the

Quantification of samples using XRF 
As XRF does not include oxygen in the measurement, this element must be factored in manually. To 
do this, it was assumed that the all oxygen is present as either SiO2 or Al2O3, and based on the Si and 
Al results, oxygen was factored in corresponding to just above 50 wt. %. From Figure 9 it is seen that 
the iron content determination methods are in relative good agreement with a few exceptions in the 
proton samples, and the low concentration BEA sample. The high iron content in the proton samples 
determined by EPR can be attributed to a broad feature as mentioned earlier, and not a specific EPR 
signal as seen on Figure 7. For these samples the XRF results are therefore considered to be the more 
reliable ones as these results also are reminiscent of what other groups have found using commercial 
zeolites [28], and Zeolyst zeolites[35].  
The XRF samples are completely dry as the samples were fused into a solid glass disc at 1040 °C 
prior to measurement. This means the water present in the zeolite prior to fusing has left the sample 
and therefore the actual sample mass is slightly lower than the one used by XRF software to calculate 
the weight percentages. The EPR samples were measured without pretreatment, and therefore 
contained an unknown amount of water due to storage in ambient conditions. This is also true for the 
exicator samples, but to a lesser extent. However, this water present in the sample means that the 
actual wt. % of iron in a dry sample is slightly higher.

NH3TPD 
The Ammonia Temperature Programmed Desorption (NH3TPD) experiment is performed in order to 
determine how NH3 adsorbs onto the catalyst. After the dehydration procedure, the sample 
temperature is lowered to 100 °C and 1200 ppm NH3 is added to the helium feed. The sample is 
assumed saturated after an hour and NH3 is removed from the feed such that only helium runs through 
the sample. After 45 minutes of helium purging, the temperature is set to ramp to 300 °C at a slow 
rate (2 °C/min). 
Fe-ZSM-5 
In Figure 16, left, it can be seen that the three spectra at 100 °C are identical at g = 2. Only the initial 
spectrum (black) shows signs of a β peak. When comparing the intensity of the γ peak it can be seen 
that the peak before and after TPD (Red and Pink respectively) is identical although having an 
increase in intensity during He flushing (Blue). It should however be noted that the pink spectrum is 
recorded at a different temperature, and as seen during cooling in the rehydration experiment, is 
therefore likely to be negatively affected due to this high temperature (the γ peak increased in intensity 
upon cooling of a dehydrated sample during the rehydration experiment). This means that in reality 
the γ peak might have increased after TPD, but without a proper measurement at 100 °C this can’t be 
said for certain.

SCR gasses 
To investigate the catalyst behaviour under SCR conditions a protocol (Table 1) was setup as follows: 
After dehydration, the sample was oxidized with feeds containing NO and NO + O2 to determine the 
properties of an oxidized sample which have not been subjected to NH3. Afterwards a feed with NO 
+ NH3 was led over the sample to bring the sample into a reduced state. The reduced sample is then 
oxidized with first NO and afterwards NO + O2 to see the oxidative properties of NO, and to see the 
oxidized state of a sample which have previously been subjected to NH3. This oxidized sample is 
then subjected to a feed containing NH3 and afterwards NO + NH3 to see the reductive properties of 
NH3 and to bring it into a reduced state. To reoxidize the sample first O2 and then NO + O2 were led 
over the sample to see the oxidative properties of O2 and to bring the sample to an oxidized state. 
Then NH3 was added to the NO + O2 feed to make a complete SCR feed on an oxidized sample. O2 
was then removed from the feed to produce a reduced sample, which then in turn was subjected to 
SCR feed. 
Fe-ZSM-5 
The effect of different oxidizing feeds is compared in Figure 23. It is evident that there is no change 
in either α, nor the broader peak around 2. The differences are therefore all found in the γ and β peaks 
(Figure 23, right). While the differences are small, it is seen that the β peak is at its largest after 
exposure to NO + O2, but also after having been reduced at least once with NH3. The reverse scenario 
is observed for the γ peak, which is at its greatest in its oxidized form before exposure to NH3.

Discussion 
Simulation of EPR spectra 
To qualitatively describe the iron sites, it was attempted to model the experimental spectra with 
simulated iron species. Oxidized spectra of the Fe-ZSM-5_4 and Fe-BEA_4 were chosen as these 
show the most characteristic peaks. It will only be attempted to model single iron sites, as these are 
the most easily described, and do not take into account the interaction between several iron centres. 
This however excludes the iron clusters from the model, and a perfect fit is not expected, but instead 
some distinct features of each spectra is sought to be resolved. As the entire sample is treated in an 
oxidizing atmosphere, all iron is expected to be Fe3+ and with a spin S = 5/2.  
ZSM-5 – 200 C° 
From the experimental spectrum, features to be resolved are located at g = 4.3, g = 5.7, g = 6.3, while 
the broad peak at g = 2 will not be considered as this is not arising from an isolated Fe3+ ion, but from 
iron oxide clusters in different sizes [32]. As it is observed experimentally that g = 5.7 and 6.3 always 
occurs together and have identical behaviour they are assumed to belong to the same iron species, 
while the g = 4.3 belongs to another species. This can be simulated with models containing a large 
zero field splitting, and therefore large D values are used while varying the rhombicity, E. The peak 
at g = 4.3 can be simulated by a maximal rhombicity, E/D = 0.33 around the iron centre, which leads 
to the only observable line at g = 4.3. To match the experimental spectrum a peak to peak line width 
of 8 mT was used in a fully Lorentzian fitting. The other species is simulated with a very small 
rhombicity. Not a complete axial system (E/D = 0), as this would only give rise to one peak at g = 6. 
A small deviation from the ideal axial system however, splits the peak at g = 6 into two peaks at 
slightly higher and lower g values depending on the amount of deviation. To match the observed 
values of g = 5.7 and g = 6.3, a E/D = 0.02 was used along with a peak to peak linewidth of 5 used 
with a fully Gaussian fitting. It was attempted to assign a relative weight to the spectra. The most 
optimal weighting of the two species to make the relative intensity match the experimental one was 
0.2 of the species associated with β and 0.8 of the species associated with γ. The simulated spectrum 
can be seen in Figure 34 together with the experimental counterpart and the relevant simulation 
parameters can be found in Table 6. To fit the peaks of the simulation with the experimental peak, 
the experimental spectrum was subtracted an arbitrarily chosen baseline.

Preparation method 
Multiple methods for iron introduction into zeolite structures exist, and they more or less all produce 
the same sites, but in varying ratios. It is therefore desirable to find the method which produces the 
largest amount of SCR active iron. In this study, a simple liquid ion exchange is used, but some 
noteworthy contenders to this method includes the chemical vapour deposition (CVD) and the 
incipient wetness impregnation (IWI). Other groups which have compared the preparation methods 
have found that iron oligomers are more likely to form when using impregnation, than ion exchange, 
and even less likely to appear using CVD [39]. It was therefore chosen to do ion exchange over 
impregnation (CVD was ruled out for practical reasons). 
As zeolites are crystalline structures, X-ray diffraction (XRD) would have been a powerful tool to 
determine the stability of the zeolite during ion exchange, and also the to determine the amount of 
iron oxide clusters, as these also shows up on XRD when larger than 2 nm [28].  
The iron source used for the catalyst synthesis was chosen to be ferric nitrate. Both Fe2+ and Fe3+ can 
be used for the ion exchange, and while Fe2+ is argued to have better ion exchange properties [31], 
Fe3+ is chosen as this is more convenient when the ion exchange is done in ambient air instead of in 
an inert gas. As an Fe3+ source, FeCl3 could also have been used, but risk of chloride ions in the final 
product is undesired. FeCl3 has been successfully used as an iron source in the literature, where the 
product was washed with water until no AgCl was formed in the wash water [30]. Here, the nitrate 
anion was chosen to accompany iron, as any nitrate left in the structure after ion exchange would 
decompose during calcination. 
Behaviour of peaks in various feeds 
The EPR spectrum of the dehydrated Fe-BEA_4 sample looks similar to what have been reported 
elsewhere, although these were obtained under vacuum dehydration conditions and at RT [36]. The 
shoulder and the γ peak are both believed to be isolated Fe3+ in strongly distorted tetrahedral 
symmetry. There is however very scarce literature on the behaviour at high temperatures and with 
SCR gasses running over the sample for the Fe-BEA catalyst. 
From the oxidations with either NO or O2 alone on both zeolites it was showed that either gas had 
low capabilities to oxidize EPR observable iron sites. An interesting study of mechanism would be 
to have a feed with NO saturating the sample proceeded by an O2 only feed and then see if the 
oxidation will occur. Some caution is in order with respect to temperature as NO physorbed on Fe-
ZSM-5 is reported to desorb at 126 °C [40]. From the NH3 reduction, it was seen that NH3 alone was 
enough to reduce the iron at 200 °C without the need of NO. From the NH3 TPD experiment it was 
shown that even 100 °C was enough to reduce the γ and β peak, to the same state as at 200 °C for 
both samples.

For a catalyst being active in SCR it is assumed that it can be both reduced and oxidized under the 
conditions present in the exhaust. This means that an isolated iron site should enter its reduced state 
Fe2+, when presented with NO + NH3 while retaining an oxidized state, Fe3+, when presented with 
NO + O2. On the basis of this it is suggested that both γ and β sites are involved in SCR in ZSM-5 
(Figure 23 and Figure 24) while the α site is inactive. The observation that the reduced Fe-ZSM-5_4 
sample exhibits identical EPR spectra in NO + NH3 as it does in NO + NH3 + O2 is an indication of 
the reduction being significantly faster than the oxidation. This can be said as the mechanism behind 
the SCR reaction is a catalytic cycle, and for every oxidation, a reduction must also occur resulting 
in an even number of oxidations and reductions. The same observations are made for Fe-BEA_4 
(Figure 26) with the exception that the β peak is not observed, and it is thus only the γ site which is 
involved in SCR.  
When observing the temperature dependence of the EPR signals, it is seen in Figure 8 for Fe-ZSM-
5_4 that the γ peak increases upon cooling while the broad peak around g = 2 decreases. This is 
expected as the broad peak have been attributed to small iron oxide clusters, or dimers which at low 
temperatures are anti-ferromagnetic, and therefore able to increase in intensity when temperature is 
increased [41]. The γ peak, however is an isolated iron specie, and reacts to temperature change 
through the curie law, which states that magnetic susceptibility is proportional to T-1 [36]. This is also 
seen in Figure 12 where the stepwise temperature reduction of a dehydrated sample increases the 
broad peak around g = 2 while slightly decreasing the γ peak. It was attempted to integrate the γ peak 
(integrating from 110-190 mT) for the O2 dehydration to prove the validity of the Curie law on this 
peak. It is expected that the intensity of the γ decreases when the temperature is increased, and when 
multiplied with the recorded temperature (in Kelvin) it should give a constant value. This was not 
observed, but instead a decrease in intensity was observed from 50 °C to 150 °C while increasing 
between 150 °C and 250 °C (Figure 36). A steady increase of temperature multiplied with intensity 
was also observed being most prominent at higher temperatures. with temperature was observed. This 
suggests that the broad peak at g = 2 is broad enough to interfere with the γ peak (especially at 
temperature 200 °C and 250 °C), while γ follows the Curie law. To obtain ideal results it is suggested 
here that the sample is further cooled as this decreases the impact of the peak at g = 2 on the overall 
EPR spectrum.

The effect of water in the system should also be addressed as water is present in realistic exhausts. 
Partly from the atmospheric air used to burn the fuel, partly from the urea/water solution added, partly, 
from water formed as a product during catalysis, but most importantly from the combustion of diesel 
fuel. From the rehydration experiment it is seen that a sample dehydrated in wet and dry feeds 
produced slightly different spectra. For the Fe-BEA_4 sample a reduction in γ was seen along with 
an increase in the broad peak around g = 2. This may be because some of the iron, in the absence of 
water, is forced into a coordination which is not preferred (γ) compared to one where iron is able to 
coordinate to water (g = 2). This explained by iron either coordinating to oxygen from water which 
is very mobile, or framework oxygen which is very rigid. During dehydration water is removed as a 
possible ligand, and framework oxygen will thus take its place by reworking the geometry around 
iron. With water in the feed iron is allowed to be less tightly coordinated and therefore producing a 
signal at g = 2 instead of the γ peak. This is not the case for Fe-ZSM-5_4 (Figure 13) as this sample 
shows a decrease in both γ and g = 2 peaks while increasing the intensity of the β peak at 200 °C. 
This might suggest that the presence of water in the feed favours iron located in β sites. However, 
this is only at 200 °C as the β is not observed at RT. This shows that hydrated iron species at RT 
prefers to coordinate different from that of the β peak (axial with a slight rhombic distortion).

Comparison of Fe-ZSM-5 and Fe-BEA 
The main difference between BEA and ZSM-5 samples observed in this study is the ability to 
consistently follow the development of reduced and oxidized states in ZSM-5 while not being able to 
in BEA. This indicates that quantitative in-situ EPR is useful on ZSM-5 while being less informative 
on BEA samples. Both zeolite types have however shown SCR activity with iron loading similar to 
the one used herein [42; 36], which implies that there is SCR activity in the catalyst caused by sites 
which are not detected by EPR. One reason for this might be the observed β peak on ZSM-5 which 
was not observed on BEA. This peak completely disappeared and reappeared when influenced by 
reductive and oxidative feeds respectively.  
The same procedure was used to prepare both samples, but as XRF and EPR results showed, BEA 
contained more iron in the most concentrated sample, while the lower concentration samples gave 
similar XRF results. EPR generally estimated that there was more iron in BEA than in ZSM-5.  
Comparing the NH3TPD of the two zeolites there is an interesting observation to be made before the 
heating step of the TPD. It is seen that the broad peak around g = 2 along with the α peak is very 
much affected by NH3 adsorption in the Fe-BEA_4 sample, and not at all affected in the Fe-ZSM-
5_4 sample. This could be related to an easier adsorption of NH3 on iron clusters and iron in the sites 
responsible for the peak around g = 2.  
Critique of methods 
For the NH3TPD to give a lot of information, it would require the NH3 to adsorb on to the isolated 
Fe3+ ions without reducing them, as a reduction would mean they would not show up in EPR. 
Furthermore, the adsorption should cause the coordination sphere of iron to change, which is easier 
to observe when hyperfine (coupling of unpaired electrons with nucleus spin) or superhyperfine 
(coupling of unpaired electrons with ligand nucleus spin) structure is detectable. Ideally the 
superhyperfine coupling, as this is the unpaired electrons interaction with the nitrogen atom in the 
adsorped NH3. By having a detector analyzing the feed after having been run through the sample this 
would allow for detection of the desorption of NH3. Furthermore, this would allow the conversion to 
be determined, and thus assessing the ability of each sample to convert NO. 
XRF was used herein instead of Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectroscopy (ICP-MS), which is 
another method for elemental analysis. Compared to XRF this method also gives information on 
lighter elements such as oxygen, such that the oxygen content of the sample is not determined by 
assumptions of ideal zeolites. Determining the iron content by EPR was possible for ion-exchanged 
samples, but it was seen that the iron content was significantly overestimated on proton zeolite 
samples.

Conclusion 
The incorporation of iron into zeolites types ZSM-5 and BEA was successfully done using a liquid 
ion exchange method. It was shown that an increase in iron concentration was obtained when the 
concentration of the ion exchange solution was increased, with the exception of a single sample which 
disobeyed this trend. This was determined by both quantitative EPR, and XRF. Fe-BEA zeolites were 
shown to have peaks at g = 4.3, g = 2 along with a shoulder at g = 8. Fe-ZSM-5 contained the same 
peaks, except the shoulder was replaced with a signal at g = 6. Using an in-situ EPR setup, oxidative, 
reductive and SCR feeds were applied to the samples, and the development of the peaks could be 
followed. It was seen that for the BEA sample, only the g = 4.3 peak varied with feed, while both g 
= 4.3 and g = 6 varied in the Fe-ZSM-5 zeolite. Oxidized and reduced states were observed on the 
total intensity development on Fe-ZSM-5, while these states could not be distinguished on the total 
intensity development of Fe-BEA. It was showed that oxidation required both O2 and NO to be 
present on the same time as either gas alone could change the EPR spectrum of a reduced sample. 
For the reduction, NH3 was able to reduce the iron without NO being present. A temperature 
dependence between peaks and intensity was also shown. It was seen that an increase in temperature 
caused the peak around 2 to increase while the peak at 4.3 decreased. The opposite was also shown 
when cooling to 77 °K, where the g = 4.3 grew, while g = 2 decreased. This was explained with the 
Curie law and antiferromagnetic iron dimers.
