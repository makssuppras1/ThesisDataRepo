Abstract
Excavations commonly constitutes an essential part in construction projects. For
prediction of wall and soil interaction due to excavating, FEM-calculation are often
utilized in which diﬀerent constitutive soil models are available.
In this thesis,
the prediction of wall displacements and settlements have been evaluated, using
the Hardening Soil model (HS-model) in a FEM-based calculation.
Predictions
have been performed for two cases where wall displacements and settlements were
measured. Case 1 is the Karlsruhe Project, where the predictions are evaluated for
normally consolidated sand in a 5 m deep excavation using sheet piles with one strut
level. Case 2 is a 15.2 m deep excavation in overconsolidated clay, constructed with
secant piles and three anchor levels.
A literature study have been conducted in order to evaluate expected shortages of
the prediction. Furthermore, the literature study have been utilized for comparison
and validation of the predicted soil behaviour.
For case 1, a large amount of soil tests have been conducted and interpreted.
For case 2, only the primary present layer have been interpreted whereas the rest
of the soil proﬁle has been externally provided. Measurements for both cases have
been evaluated to achieve a proper validation of predictions.
Multiple calculations have been conducted in order to investigate impacts in
predictions. The results have been evaluated in order to estimate the reliability of
the numerically applied method by the HS-model that we use today.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Excavations are a common subject within the ﬁeld of construction work and has been
for many years. Recently, the development of urban areas require that excavations
are performed near existing constructions, and this puts pressure on the engineers.
On top of that, demands for a cost-eﬃcient design increases without compromising
the safety. FEM-codes are often used for solving complex designs both inside the
ﬁeld of structural engineering and soil mechanics.
However, as results based on
FEM-codes are products mainly of the inputs, a valid result requires inputs that
reﬂect reality to an extent of acceptance. A proper input demands a suﬃciently
realistic model behaviour, which may not always be the case, and this may induce
errors that can be hard to quantify. For any given model, the magnitude of error
should be quantiﬁed and considered before a ﬁnal design can be determined.
This thesis strives to deﬁne the magnitude of errors that can occur during a
FEM-based prediction of wall and settlement behaviour when performing an exca-
vation. The research is based on the FEM-code, PLAXIS, where predictions of wall
displacements and settlements are compared with measurements from two diﬀerent
cases:
The ﬁrst case is the Karlsruhe Project, that is a simple full scale experiment
conducted in 1993. The project involves an excavation behind a sheet pile wall with
one strut level executed in normally consolidated sandy conditions. A combination
of comprehensive investigation of soil characteristics and careful study of wall/soil
deformation makes the project ideal for testing the prediction of wall and soil be-
haviour during excavation. The second case is Nordhavnsvejen, that is a project
from 2011 where a deep excavation has been conducted in relation to tunneling con-
struction. The project involves a secant pile wall supported by 3 ground anchors
executed in soil conditions of primarily overconsolidated clay. The project was car-
ried out close to existing buildings, where wall displacements and settlements were
measured.
The structure of the thesis is described as follows. Based on a literature review,
the previous ﬁndings with regards to the subject of excavation are presented. The
literature review contributes to a clariﬁcation of the potential errors that may be
involved in prediction of an excavation. Additionally, previous research is utilized
to evaluate predictions based on expectations and observations. The constitutive
Hardening Soil Model, that is considered proper for description of soil behaviour

Chapter 2
State of the Art
Despite that excavations have been carried out for centuries, their complex behaviour
is hard to predict, even today. Rankine (1857) presented an early estimation of
lateral soil pressure on a smooth wall for a homogeneous soil layer. Reality however,
shows that both the assumptions of smooth wall and a homogeneous soil layer
is hardly fulﬁlled.
As this fact increases the complexity of the scenario, it sets
high requirements for mathematically based prediction methods. Therefore when
Peck (1969) proposed the ﬁrst practical approach of determining settlements behind
an excavation, it was on an empirical basis. A generalized settlement proﬁle was
based on the wall excavation depth and basal heave stability. It was proposed that
the maximum wall displacement under normal circumstances would be up to 1 %
excavation depth.
Due to improved construction technique Clough and O’Rourke (1990) proposed
an improved empirical method of predicting wall displacement and settlement be-
haviours. Still basal heave stability was considered, however now wall deﬂection was
accounted for as well. Clough and O’Rourke (1990) also proposed settlements caused
by sheet pile wall installations and noted diaphragm wall installation eﬀects. Hsieh
and Ou (1998) reviewed the method proposed by Clough and O’Rourke (1990) and
proposed a new and more advanced settlement proﬁle contour, where the maximum
depends on the maximum wall displacement. The overall geometry was close to
Clough and O’Rourke (1990), however the settlement proﬁle matched the 9 recent
performed case studies better.
Today a more theoretical approach, through FEM, is often applied for estima-
tions of wall and settlement behaviour. FEM has the advantage of implementing
a lot of factors such as particular soil behaviour, wall stiﬀness, surface loads etc.
Research today are often focused on improving FEM-based methods, such as uti-
lizing advanced soil models to display a certain soil behaviour, modelling of wall
installation process or estimation of shortcomings in the current modelling.
The literature review have been focused on information contributing to the FEM-
based prediction of wall and soil behaviour.
The review have been divided into
general wall/settlements behaviour followed by a review of previous investigations
of soil behaviour behind excavation.

2.1.1
Empirical Methods
Following tendencies are found for sand and stiﬀclay. Clough and O’Rourke (1990)
proposes a general maximum wall displacement of 0.2 % of excavation depth. Fur-
ther it was proposed that the maximum settlement was equal to 75 % of the max-
imum wall displacement.
This was conﬁrmed by Mana and Clough (1981) that
proposed a common maximum settlement of 50-75 % of the maximum wall displace-
ment, with an uncommonly seen maximum of 100 %.
Two types of settlement proﬁles are observed, relating to wall displacement be-
haviour. A cantilever wall displacement provokes a sprandel settlement proﬁle. An
inward wall displacement provokes a concave settlement proﬁle. Often a cantilever
wall displacement is observed initially, followed by a inward due to stiﬀsupports.
Clough and O’Rourke (1990) proposes a resulting settlement proﬁle equal to the
sum of incremental settlement proﬁles through the stages.
For a cantilever wall displacement, Clough and O’Rourke (1990) proposed a
triangular settlement proﬁle with maximum settlement next to the wall, decreasing
away from it. For sand, a slope of 0.5 was suggested and for stiﬀclay, a slope of 0.33
was suggested. Ou et al. (1993) proposed a general settlement proﬁle with similar
tendency. The slope however, was only 0.67 and lower close to the surface.
For an inward wall displacement, Ou et al. (1993) a observed a tendency of
maximum wall displacement located around the excavation level. The maximum
settlement proﬁle was observed behind the wall at a distance of 50 % excavation
depth. Further it was found that settlement proﬁle close to the wall was equal to
50 % of the maximum settlement. The slope behind the maximum settlement was
still deﬁned as 0.67 with a lower slope close to the surface.
2.1.2
Wall/Soil Interface
A concrete wall exposed to an earth pressure may result in cracks on the tension side
of the wall. Cracking leads to a reduced cross section which would eventually lower
the walls moment of resistance. Comodromos and Papadopoulou (2015) investigated
the impact of cracking on diaphragm walls. As a rule of thumb, it was proposed to
reduce EI by 70 % in the construction phase and up to 50 % afterwards.
Forum (2007) propose diﬀerent values of interface shearing between various ma-
terials, which correlates well with studies by Tiwari et al. (2010); Potyondy (1961).
Forum (2007) propose a maximum steel/soil interface friction of 0.67 and 1.00 for
bored piles.
Tiwari et al. (2010) and Potyondy (1961) investigated the friction
between several soil types and steel, wood and concrete. The interface friction re-
duction were given as a reduced angle of friction. For an angle of friction of 32◦,
the average interface shearing reduction were 0.58 for Potyondy (1961) and 0.79 for
Tiwari et al. (2010). Considering the divergence between the two investigations, a
shear reudction of 0.67, proposed by Forum (2007) seems as an appropriate value.

2.1.3
Wall Installation
In general, the in situ stress conditions form the basis for calculations of an exca-
vation surrounded by a wall. However, Forum (2007) states that the in situ soil
conditions are easily disturbed. Even by gathering soil samples, the in situ states
may change. Wall installation may have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the in situ stresses
and should be considered when designing an excavation. Unfortunely, often no in-
formation is collected when installing structural elements, which limits the research
within the ﬁeld, Potts and Zdravkovi´c (2001). However, investigations have been
conducted, and in the following results of some are presented.
Under construction of Bell Common Tunnel secant piles were utilized and in-
stalled in overconsolidated London Clay using betonite slurry. Symons and Carder
(1992) notes that settlements occured under wall construction accompanied with
a signiﬁcant reduction of horizontal stresses. These were accompanied with large
settlements Gunn and Clayton (1992); Tedd et al. (1984) investigated the same
case and veriﬁed the observations. It was added that the initially higher horizontal
stresses caused by overconsolidation may induce a passive earth-pressure along the
wall over time, despite deﬂecting towards the excavation.
Kutmen (1986) did a numerical model of a bored pile installation process in soil
with K0,insitu of 2. The installation process caused an immediate reduction of K0 to
a level of 0.7-1.3, hence a reduction of 50 %.
Symons and Carder (1992); Clough and O’Rourke (1990) relates the vibration
level to compression and settlements.
It is proposes that overconsolidated sedi-
ments may cause an expanded area of eﬀect, thus leading movements of potential
tieback anchorages. The vibrations causes compaction of the soil to a degree de-
gree dependent of the soil type and in situ conditions. Massarsch (2002) proposes a
classiﬁcation for compaction potential on behalf of CPT measurement results, and
the compaction potential is higher for looser and coarser soil conditions. It is pro-
posed that heavily consolidated clay may not have a good compaction potential.
This correlates well with Finno and Nerby (1989), that relates sheet pile vibrations
with building of an excessive pore pressure in the adjacent soils and adds that an
excessive pore pressure may cause an undrained behaviour for clay conditions.
2.1.4
Constitutive Modelling
Whenever scenarios inside the ﬁeld of statics becomes complex, calculations tend to
beneﬁt from usage of FEM. However, an essential and very important aspect of using
FEM is to consider the possibility of errors that it may imply. Bathe (1996) and
Cook (1995) outline the potential errors well. If a proper system discretization has
been implemented through adequate boundary conditions, elements and meshing,
the potential errors of signiﬁcance are deﬁned by a lack of model inputs which
may cause deviation between reality and the FEM-model. This may include proper
deﬁnition of structural elements, constitutive soil model, installation process and
soil/structure interaction.
Modelling of an excavation using plate elements may be faster due to fewer nodes
and illustrating the stresses through the plate is also eased due to fewer nodes.
According to Zdravkovic et al. (2011), the plate thickness however disregards the

Whether the moment contribution
increases or decreases the wall displacement depends on the deﬂection mode. For
a sheetpile wall, the contribution of shearing is zero, as the shear stresses have a
centerline at the center of the sheet piles. However, for secant pile walls the shearing
may have an impact as it will create a moment due to distance between wall edge
and centerline.
An essential part of the FEM-model in a geotechnical scenario is the soil model.
Duncan and Chang (1970) proposed a model using a hyperbolic deviatoric stiﬀness
softening and a hardening dependent on third principal stress level. On basis of
this, the Hardening Soil model was developed by Schanz et al. (1999). The model
was calibrated against loose Hostun sand in drained and undrained behaviour and
veriﬁed ﬁrst by Schanz et al. (1999) and recently by Zhaohua and Yongxing (2013).
The model have been compared with other applicable constitutive models on pre-
dicting wall and settlements for a deep excavation in undrained clay conditions by
Lim et al. (2010) and showed good results.
Commonly a wall installation in a FEM-program is performed by a WIP (Wished
in place). This does not expose the soil to any stresses changes during installation.
Installation process may be hard to model, and PLAXIS does not have a function
of doing so. Schaefer and Triantafyllidis (2006) modelled the installation of a di-
aphragm wall in a soft normal- slightly overconsolidated clay using the FEM-code
ABAQUS. The result was 15 % larger wall displacements and 20 % larger settle-
ments in comparison to a similar model conducted with a WIP wall installation.
Finno et al. (1991) conducted a sheet pile installation in a soft-medium silty clay
and found that the wall displacements increased by 50 % and settlements by 70 %.
The two references display a wide spread between the installation impacts, however
the respective soil conditions and construction processes were diﬀerent. According to
Massarsch (2002), the vibration shouldn’t cause much compaction in a soft-medium
silty clay. Indeed, there are uncertainties regarding the impact by wall installation
and how it should be implemented in a FEM-model.
Grammatikopoulou et al. (2015) did a study on stiﬀness anisotropy on overcon-
solidated London Clay, and found diﬀerence between long and short term behaviour,
whereas on the short term, dependent on prop syste, the movements were 9-15 %
smaller with stiﬀness included.
Ou et al. (1996) investigated the inﬂuence of corner sections in a ﬁnite element
analysis of a deep excavation. The corner sections were found to have a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the wall displacement, dependent on the distance to and the length of
the complementary wall.

2.2.2
Taipei National Enterprising Center
The Taipei National Entreprising Center in Taipei was constructed close to sev-
eral adjacent buildings. Ou et al. (2000) covered the soil behaviour with respect to
strains, wall behaviour, settlements and adjacent building response. The construc-

Chapter 3
Constitutive Soil Model
Due to the complexity of soil stress/strain behaviour, a simple Mohr-Coulomb model
may have a hard time presenting a realistic ﬁeld of strains over a varying stress ﬁeld.
A constant stiﬀness until failure state, as seen in the Mohr-Coulomb model, may
underestimate the strains at a low stress range and/or overestimate the strains at
a high stress range. The hardening soil model considers an adaptive stiﬀness due
to both stresses and strains. This allow the model to consider diﬀerent stiﬀness
moduli for an element in diﬀerent stages of construction. Being able to capture a
proper stress/strain relationship of soil, the HS model should be a good alternative
to the Mohr-Coulomb model.
Disadvantages of the HS-model are that the soil
model requires more CPU power and additional parameters for calculation of soil
behaviour. Additional parameters require advanced soil tests, which may not always
be available.
The strain contribution deﬁned by the HS-model can be divided into a deviatoric
and a volumetric part of which the total strains can be deﬁned as a sum of the two.
For both contributions, there exists an elastic and plastic state. The elastic state
always relates to the current stress state whereas the plastic state requires knowledge
of the strain history, as the yield surface depends on the strains. For the HS-model,
the yield surface is deﬁned by two equations, one for deviatoric stresses and one
for volumetric stresses. As the yield surfaces crosses, these creates a closed shape
deﬁning the complete yield surface of the HS-model.
In the following, deviatoric and volumetric stress/strain relations of the HS-
model are presented. The theory chapter have been based on Schanz et al. (1999);
PLAXIS (2016, 2015); Zhaohua and Yongxing (2013).
For both deviatoric and volumetric stress/strain relationships, the HS-model
utilize reference stiﬀness parameters that are deﬁned at a certain reference stress
level, σref. Hence, for deﬁnition of model parameters, the stiﬀness moduli should
be known at the deﬁned reference stress level. The changes of stiﬀnesses in the HS-
model depends on the third principal stress according to the HS hardening function,
fHS, deﬁned by equation 3.1. As the third principal stress increases, the value of
fHS increases as well according to the soil characteristics, c’, ϕ and m. The stiﬀness
moduli of the HS-model is related proportionally with the hardening function. The
function displays that an increased cohesion decreases the stress dependency of the
hardening, whereas an increased angle of friction and/or m increases the stress

Chapter 4
The Karlsruhe Project
In the year of 1993 at Hochstestetten, approximately 15 kilometer north of Karl-
sruhe in Germany, a full scale experiment was performed. The experiment regarded
a simple 5 meter excavation in sand. A small construction pit was created using
sheet piles with one strut level. The experiment was a part of a geotechnical chal-
lenge held by University of Karlsruhe. The challenge involved predictions of wall
displacements and strut forces as well as settlements of soil surface behind the wall.
43 geotechnicians from around the world were attending the challenge. After the
participants had submitted their predictions, the experiment was performed. Re-
sults were gathered and discussed, leaving the predictors anonymous.
The Karlsruhe project involved a simple construction pit in a sandlayer. With
a comprehensive collection of structural, soil and measurement data, the project is
well suited for evaluation of prediction methods of wall displacements, settlements
and strut forces. The Karlsruhe case contains 6 parts. First part gives an overview
of the project. Second part provides relevant data for the construction pit. Third
part regards soil parameter interpretation. Fourth part concerns measures includes
a discussion of their reliability. Fifth part presents the numerical model, results and
discussion of these. The discussion incorporates results of parametric studies that
have been performed in regard to the case. Sixth part sums up the ﬁndings of the
case.

CPT’s
The relative density is estimated by applying Baldi et al. (1986)’s ﬁndings of rela-
tionship between cone resistance, vertical eﬀective stress and relative density. The
ﬁndings were performed on normally consolidated Ticino sand which have an angle
of friction around 33◦. The relationship is seen in ﬁgure 4.7. It is noted, that the
similar ﬁndings were found by others, Lunne et al. (1997). Figure 4.8 shows the
interpreted relative density through the soil proﬁle.
An investigation of the relationship was also conducted for overconsolidated Ti-
cino sand i.e. sand with a higher value of K0. By comparing the two investigations,
is observed that at a deﬁned cone resistance and vertical stress, the overconsolidated
state lead to lower relative densities. An overconsolidated state represents a higher
lateral stresses, i.e. higher level of K0 for a normal consolidated sand, which may be
caused by a lower angle of friction. Physically, it does make sense that at a certain
vertical stress level, high horizontal stresses (high K0) at a low relative density would
cause similar cone resistance as low horizontal stresses (low K0) and high relative
density. Thus, using Baldi et al. (1986)’s relationship for a normally consolidated
sand with an angle of friction above 33◦, the relative density is expected to be higher
than the estimation.

HS Calibration program
1. Interval and reﬁnement of parameters for calibration are inserted.
2. Loop starts.
3. One parameter set is applied according to interval and reﬁnements.
4. Soil test results added by a textﬁle. Strains are calculated up to
maximum soil test stresses.
5. Calculation of strains by the HS-model within the stress interval.
6. Calculation of dispersion between strains for current parameter
set and soil test results. If calculated dispersion is lower than the
previous lowest dispersion, then store parameter set.
7. Loop ends.
8. The stored parameter set is applied for last strain calculation. Re-
sult is plotted.

Plate Loading Tests
Diﬀerent stress/strain relationships are observed between the two tests. This indi-
cates high deviation between characteristics of the top soil level.
Stiﬀness moduli evaluated by test 2 is approximately the twice of test 1.
A
softening happens for both tests around 0.7 MPa and both tests seem to fail around
1.05 MPa. The loading prior to 0.7 MPa follows a stiﬀness response corresponding
to approx. 50 % of the reload response shown in the tests. This indicates that
plastic strains occur in the loading prior to 0.7 MPa, hence following a primary
stiﬀness modulus. A very sudden and rapid strain softening is observed for both
samples after a stress level of 0.7 MPa has been reached. Diﬀerent stiﬀness moduli
and strain softening can be modelled by the HS model, however the softening cannot
be modelled as rapid as observed in the plate loading tests.
Additional strains are observed due to multiple unload/reloading, which also
can not be imitated by the HS-model.
However multiple unloading/reloading is
not expected to occur under the excavation, therefore this lack of response is not
considered being an issue.
An estimate of HS-model stiﬀness parameters has been made by performing a
numerical model of the plate loading test. The soil has been deﬁned as the ﬁnalized
selection of parameters. The plate is displaced 2 mm and the stress/displacement is
observed. The results shows that 2 mm displacement can be achieved by applying
a stress of 0.056 MPa on the plate. This shows that the deﬁned stiﬀness parameter
is a factor 4-6 too low.
Multiple PLAXIS test was conducted, and the results
showed that the stress/displacement relationship changed by changing the forced
size of displacements. The tests are therefore not considered reliable and will not
be utilized. Further hand approximations for conducted for estimating the stiﬀness
moduli by using Terzaghi’s bearing capacity formula. However due to uncertainties
of the third principal stress range, the calculations have been disregarded7.

Validity of Measurements
Validity of the results are discussed in the following.
Phase 1, Excavation to -1 m
Despite consistency between measurements, the
wall displacement measurements for phase 1 may be overestimated. The measured
settlement does not indicate any impact from the excavation, however it was noted
that small settlements wouldn’t be observed by the vertical geodetics. The published
results indicates a very large settlement, which also seems unlikely. The measure-
ments match the relation between excavation depth and wall displacement deﬁned
by Clough and O’Rourke (1990) which increase their reliability, however it should
be considered that Clough and O’Rourke (1990) may not be valid for a very low
excavation depth. The reliability of the measurements is low.

In phase 3 the struts are installed. The measure-

unreliable. The deviation between measured and published strut forces are due to
average night and day measurements.
Phase 4, Excavation to -3 m
Measurements of phase 4 indicates increased wall
displacements along the excavation depth. The wall displacements along the top
are approximately constant. The settlements increased as expected. As mentioned
in section , the measurements of strut forces are lower for CS2 than CS3. However,
with an increased wall displacement, the strut force would be expected to increase as
(due to additional straining). As the strut force measurements are considered more
reliable, it may either be a result of unreliable wall displacement measurements of
CS2 or due to a drop of prestress, which was mentioned in section 4.1.3. As the
settlements matches the displacement, the latter is expected. The wall displacements
and settlements are considered reliable.
The plane conditioned strut forces are

Remarks
• Due to the lack of plane strain conditions for the measurements, the predictions
are expected to be larger than the measurements. The diﬀerence is expected
to be more pronounced after the strut has been installed, due to 3D eﬀects on
the retaining wall for the measurements.
• The lack of wall installation modelling is not expected to have a signiﬁcant
inﬂuence as the measurements have been initialized after wall installation. The
soil adjacent to the wall may have experienced compaction which could cause
increased density and stiﬀness. Hence, by modelling installation process, it
is expected that the predicted wall displacements and settlements could be
reduced marginally.
• The deviatoric stress range is low inside the excavation. This may cause a HS
stiﬀness calibration against a deviatoric stress level that was too high. This
may have resulted in overestimated predictions.
• The predictions of wall displacements are considered good for the cantilever
wall displacement despite a potential marginal underestimation. As the wall
displacement shape turns inward, the predictions start overestimating. The
overestimation ratio seems to increase by excavation depth. Hence, the pre-
dictions of wall displacements for the last phases are not well.
• The settlement proﬁle for the cantilever wall displacement shape is good. The
extension may be marginally too short. The predictions of the concave set-
tlement proﬁle are overestimated. The overestimation seems to be caused by
the overestimation of wall displacements. As the overestimation ratio of wall
displacements increases by excavation depth, the overestimation ratio of the
settlements increases as well. Furthermore, the extension of the concave set-
tlement proﬁle is too short. It seems that the area of settlements are correct
in relation to the wall displacements. Therefore, if a proper wall displacement
was calculated, and with an extension of the settlement proﬁle, the maximum
settlements would likely have a magnitude similar to measurements.
• The strut forces are well calculated for the cantilever wall displacement pro-
ﬁle. For the inward wall displacement proﬁle, the strut forces are marginally
overestimated.
• The error of wall displacements seems to be caused by the level of initial
rotation along the wall. For the early phases the rotation is located at a level
too high, causing underestimated predictions. For the last phases, the rotation
is located at a level too low, causing overestimated predictions. It is believed
that the level of rotation is based on mobilization of the passive pressure within
the excavation. Hence, the calculation of this is expected to be insuﬃcient.
According to the parametric studies, no parameters have a signiﬁcant impact
on the level of rotation.

function of the constitutive model is insuﬃcient. As the stresses within the
excavation decreases due to excavating, the soil softens. This causes an almost

calibration have been performed by performing a general 3D stress test in PLAXIS,

the triaxial test results and parameters have been adjusted to achieve best ﬁt. The

Phase 2, Excavation to +4.5 m
No wall displacement measurements are col-

data collections. The ﬁrst ﬂoor prisms indicate much larger displacements than the
third ﬂoor prisms and levellings. As the third ﬂoor prisms and do have a descent
correlation along the area where both have been collected, these are the best in-
dication of settlements for phase 2. It is noted that the settlements show heaving
approx. 16 m from the wall. Ou et al. (2000) notes that a construction would settle
linearly, however that tendency was observed for a rectangular construction. The
measured settlements for phase 2 are considered unreliable.

and prestressed. According to the prisms on ﬁrst and third ﬂoor, this cause a heaving

6.1
Conclusions
Multiple soil tests have been conducted for the Karlsruhe Project. Interpretation of
triaxial tests shows large discrepancies between stiﬀness calibrations and inconsis-
tencies between the tests. Some tests did show consistency, however it shows that
triaxial tests should be considered with cautious. Comparison of stiﬀness interpre-
tations between CPT’s and triaxial tests indicated that stiﬀness interpretations by
CPT’s were either remarkably under- or overestimated, dependent on the method
applied.
Soil testing by triaxial tests have shown that the HS-model is incapable of mod-
elling a proper stiﬀness modulus of a large deviatoric stress interval. In order to
achieve a proper calibration, a considered stress interval should be selected and
calibrated against.
Multiple triaxial tests have shown that the deviatoric strain
softening may be insuﬃcient as a more sudden softening is demanded for a proper
calibration. The stress-dependency in the hardening function of the HS-model is
decreased signiﬁcantly for a high cohesion soil. This is considered as a constrain
within the model.
Both cases show signs of non-plane strain conditions. It does seem that a plane
strain condition may be hardly exist. However, calculations using a plane strain
model is considered conservative.
Measurements conducted for both cases did not show a proper reliability. Espe-
cially for Nordhavnsvejen, the measurements of settlements were very poor and too
unreliable to be compared with predictions.
Predictions of wall displacements and settlements in normally consolidated sand
seems proper as long as the wall displacement proﬁle is cantilever. Predictions of
incremental wall displacements and settlements under a inward bending wall dis-
placement proﬁle shows an overestimation. In general the predictions show that the
extension of settlements are too short, however this observation is more distinctive
for the concave settlement proﬁle.
Predictions of wall displacements in overconsolidated clay indicates very large
overestimations. By performing additional calculations, it has been shown that the

6.2
Recommendations for Future Work
The high discrepancy between soil test results conducted in relation to the Karlsruhe
Project indicates a lack of reliability. According to triaxial test results in relation
to Karlsruhe Project, the interpreted parameters may be either safe or unsafe. Def-
inition of the potential errors is considered high priority and should be quantiﬁed
separately for diﬀerent soils and soil tests.
As the HS-model seems to estimate proper stress/strain relations only for ﬁnite
stress intervals, execution of triaxial tests should be conducted within a stress in-
terval that is expected for the respective soil layers. Hence, for an excavation, the
upper layers should be executed with a lower conﬁnement pressure than the deeper
layers.
In order to achieve a proper calibration by the HS-model, the constrains within
the model could be removed. Allowing a hardening parameter, m, larger than 1
would allow the model to consider more hardening. This would be particularly rele-
vant for high cohesion material, where the hardening is diminished by the cohesion
part. Further, changing the allowable relation between Ei and E50 in the HS-model,
may increase the application of the hyperbolic deviatoric softening.
A careful investigation of the soil behaviour behind an excavation should be
conducted in order to deﬁne the cause for an overestimated inward bending wall
displacement proﬁle and lack of soil settlement proﬁle extension. The investigation
could be conducted by experimental comparison with a numerical model.
The predictions performed for Nordhavnsvejen has shown that the lateral stress
proﬁle estimated by PLAXIS recommendation results in too large deformations.
This indicates a need for an improved deﬁnition of lateral insitu stresses in overcon-
solidated clays.
For both the Karlsruhe Project and Nordhavnsvejen, ﬁeld measurements have
shown a lack of reliability. In general measurements should gather with care and
evaluated before use. As it generally seems that settlement measurements may be
unreliable, these should be gathered with more reliable measurements, for instance
strut forces and compared for veriﬁcation before use.
