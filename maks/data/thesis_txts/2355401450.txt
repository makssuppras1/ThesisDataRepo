Abstract 
 
English 
 
A prosthesis socket must be moulded specifically to fit the individual user, 
a manual and labour intensive process. Additive manufacturing could 
alleviate the man hours needed for production while maintaining the 
requirement for a unique shape of each prosthesis socket. But anisotropy of 
the produced parts and poor control of the fiber direction stand in the way of 
producing sockets that are sufficiently stiff, strong and lightweight.  
Therefore, a method of using Additive Manufacturing to produce a fiber 
reinforced polymer prosthesis socket with advantageous fiber orientation is 
proposed.  
This is done by studying and analysing the current possibilities within both 
prosthesis manufacture and Additive Manufacturing and using this 
knowledge as a basis for developing and detailing new solutions.  
A functional prototype capable of demonstrating the viability of FDM 
printing with continuous carbon fiber is built, and it is shown that this system 
can be used for printing continuous fiber reinforced thermoplastic composites 
with load path optimisation.  
It is found that the proposed concept for using Additive Manufacturing for 
prosthesis printing is likely to be viable and that there is basis for further 
developing and testing the solution.

Introduction 
Fiber reinforced polymers are used to create materials that are stiff and 
strong, but at the same time lighter than metals [1]. This is relevant for a 
range of application areas, including lower limb prostheses: To ensure 
optimal mobility, the socket of a prosthetic leg should not weigh down the 
user, but should at the same time not carry the risk of buckling or bending 
under the different loading types that happen during use [2].  
The fabrication of fiber reinforced polymers can be done in several 
ways, but in prosthesis production it is commonly done by hand lay-up, as 
each socket must be moulded to fit the individual user. This is extremely 
labour intensive, and additive manufacturing could alleviate the man hours 
needed for production while maintaining the requirement for a unique 
shape of each prosthesis socket [2].  
Solutions using additive manufacturing in combination with fiber 
reinforcement and prostheses have been suggested, but common 
characteristics are anisotropy of the produced parts and poor control of the 
fiber direction [3] [4] [5]. In order to use additive manufacturing in 
prosthesis manufacturing, a different approach would be required in order 
to get the fiber orientation needed for adequate material properties of the 
socket.  
 
Objective 
The question that was sought answered during the project is: 
 
Can additive manufacturing methods be used to produce a fiber 
reinforced polymer prosthetic leg socket with advantageous fiber 
orientation?  
 
The aim of the project was to explore different possibilities for using 
fiber reinforced additive manufacturing in prosthesis manufacturing and 
perform well founded evaluation and comparison in order to choose one 
method for test and validation. The end goal of the project was to build a 
prototype for composite manufacturing that could elucidate the feasibility 
of the method and allow for evaluation of the resulting material. 
 
During the course of the project, as more knowledge was gathered and 
new insights gained, the research question was further defined and split 
into two strands: 
 
 
Is it possible to print thermoplastic with embedded continuous 
carbon fibres? 
 
Is it possible to conceive a method for FDM printing that arranges 
layers in a cylindrical shape rather than the characteristic layer 
structure of xy-planes? 
 
The project would have relevance for lightweight products with strict 
requirements to mechanical strength in more than one direction, 
specifically for predictable loading patterns. While this applies to a long 
range of products, specific focus will be put on the applicability for 
prostheses.

1.1 Prostheses and orthoses 
A prosthesis is an artificial replacement of a missing body part. The 
prosthesis can have either a cosmetic or a functional purpose – or both.  
The socket of a prosthesis is the contact point between patient and 
prosthesis, and it is therefore essential for the comfort of the patient that 
the fit is optimal, or appropriate movement will be hindered.  
 
An orthosis is an external device that is worn by the patient to support, 
re-align or redistribute pressure across the patient’s musculoskeletal 
system [6]. This serves to relieve pain or improve function for the patient. 
An example of a common reason for wearing a lower limb orthosis is drop 
foot, where the patient is unable to lift his foot dorsally while walking. A 
brace supporting the foot and holding it straight can therefore be of help. 
Another scenario where orthoses can be applied is for arthritis where the 
orthoses limit e.g. fingers from moving to end positions, hereby relieving 
pain.  
 
Prostheses and orthoses are used for all body parts, but this thesis 
primarily considers lower limb prostheses and orthoses of mobile patients, 
as these are subject to loads that require more extensive strength 
considerations for the used material. 
 
To identify the aspects of prostheses that influence the perception and 
acceptance from the users, a product analysis is carried out. This will cover 
three dimensions: Function, manufacturing and use.  
The analysis of prostheses and orthoses is based on literature studies 
and two non-consecutive days spent at the prosthesis manufacturer Ortos 
in Odense. During those field-studies, passive observations were 
performed, documented by note-taking [Appendix E], both in the 
workshop and during the visitations of patients by prosthetists. Informal 
interviews and conversations were conducted with both employees and 
patients. 
The analysis and the report in general will take prostheses as the 
starting point. But due to an assessment that the similarities between 
manufacturing of prostheses and orthoses could lead to them both 
benefitting from the later AM solution, orthoses will be included whenever 
relevant.   
 
1.1.1 Functional analysis 
The overall function of a prosthetic leg is to allow the patient to take a 
step forward as he normally would have if he had two legs. This is 
facilitated by the four organs in the prosthesis, i.e.: 
 
 
Stump-connection organ (socket) 
 
Joint organ (knee) 
 
Shock absorption organ (foot) 
 
Aesthetics organ (cosmesis) 
 
As is seen in the following, multiple choices for alternative 
technologies for each organ exist, from e.g. mechanical knee joint to an 
electric one with microchip.

The process flow of taking a step with a prosthetic leg can be seen in 
Figure 4, where it is clear that three of the four organs play vital roles in 
performing a step, and that the interplay between them is crucial for the 
successful execution.  
However, the aesthetics organ is not found in this process chain, as it 
does not actually contribute to this function in any way. This organ consists 
of a decorative structure placed on the outside of the prosthesis to give it a 
closer resemblance to a real leg. As such, this organ fulfils a sub-function 
of giving the right appearance to the leg. It is significant to note that while 
the aesthetics organ plays the most apparent role in this function, the other 
organs also affect it greatly by controlling how natural the gait cycle looks. 
For example, proper timing in terms of locking/unlocking of the knee joint 
is key in walking naturally.    
 
The four organs are examined below through describing their function, 
mode of action and interactions [7]. 
 
Socket  
The most important functions of the socket are to: 
 
 
Keep the prosthesis in place on the amputated stump  
 
Prevent the prosthesis from falling off, get loose or twist 
around 
 
Allow for comfortable wear  
 
Be simple to get off and on.  
 
If the socket fails to comply with these points, the patient will be in 
greater risk of sustaining damage and pain from falls, skewed walking 
pattern or dislocated pressure points. Additionally, increased immobility, 
insecurity and dependency can be consequences of an ill-fitting prosthetic 
leg [8].  
Different principles for constructing the socket to comply with the 
above exist, the most commonly used being Total Surface Bearing (TSB) 
and Specific Surface Bearing (SSB). TSB design entails spreading the load 
evenly across the stump so as to have no areas carry extra load. SSB design 
seeks to focus the load on pressure points that are better able to carry the 
load – hereby taking load off e.g. nerve endings and bony areas [9]. Both

approaches rely entirely on the socket being custom made to the individual 
patient, as it must fit the stump exactly.  
The approach often used at Ortos is a mix of the two principles: The 
sockets are initially based on total surface bearing (TSB), i.e. the inside of 
the surface should correspond as well as possible to the patient’s stump. 
However, sensitive areas of the stump are then taken into consideration 
during manufacture, and it is common for the prosthetist to manually add 
more room in places where e.g. the bone end is located.    
 
To fulfil the function of keeping the prosthesis in place on the stump, 
the socket needs a suspension system. While the most basic approach is to 
simply tie the prosthesis on with laces, there are more comfortable and 
practical options: The two most commonly used solutions are shuttle lock 
and vacuum seal.  
The shuttle lock works by rolling a silicone liner with a pin-attachment 
onto the stump. The pin then locks in place in the prosthesis and keeps it 
in place until it is released mechanically by pressing a button on the socket.  
The other method, the vacuum seal, works by securing the socket to the 
liner through vacuum instead. Again, a liner is rolled onto the stump, but 
the liner type for this type of suspension instead relies on a sealing ring 
that form an airtight barrier for the vacuum to form between the socket and 
liner. The vacuum can either be created passively by the patient pressing 
the stump down in the socket, forcing air out of a one-way valve, or 
alternatively, air is actively pumped out from the cavity. The patient can 
remove the prosthesis again by releasing the vacuum by the press of a 
button on the socket.    
The vacuum suspension tends to keep the leg better in place, allowing 
better sense of control and less friction. However, this type of suspension 
requires a TSB socket and is more time consuming when taking on and 
off.  
The stump of an amputee tends to change over time, and it is therefore 
required to have a new socket made from time to time even though they 
are rarely worn out. Even on a daily basis the stump can swell or shrink 
according to e.g. activity level and time of day. The vacuum seal largely 
compensates for this effect, ensuring that no further measures need to be 
taken for a proper fit. The shuttle lock mechanism does not, however, and 
with this solution the patient must compensate for swelling by varying the 
thickness of the sock worn under the prosthesis.  
 
The interfaces of the socket consist of the contact to the stump’s skin 
surface and the connection to the knee joint of the prosthesis (or directly 
to the pylon if the amputation is below the knee). Here, proper and secure 
connection is key to have the prosthesis stay in place. Most prostheses use 
a modular approach where the socket is the only custom-made component 
and is mounted on standard components that can be interchanged to match 
the needs. This is possible through standardisation of the connection types 
between the components.  
 In addition to secure and reliable fastening, it is also important that the 
interface is designed to take alignment into account. While most prostheses 
can be adjusted for alignment after production has finished, the design of 
the socket also plays an important role in this. Misalignment can cause 
discomfort when wearing the prosthesis and over time joint damage in the 
joint above the prosthesis [10].

Prosthetic knee 
The prosthetic knee is needed for transfemoral amputation patients, as 
they need the bending of the knee to obtain a normal gait cycle. For 
patients with amputations below the knee, this function is unneeded in the 
prosthesis as it lies in their own knee.  
The functions the knee organ serves are:  
 
 
Support the weight of the body during stance phase 
 
Swing back when taking a step forward in a speed matching 
the walking speed 
 
Allow for bending (when sitting, kneeling etc.) [11] 
 
Since the amputee has no means of controlling the knee joint through 
his muscles, solutions for imitating properly timed bending of the knee to 
facilitate walking have been developed. This is either achieved 
mechanically or by microprocessor control.  
A mechanical knee relies on different load directions to control the 
locking and unlocking of the knee: If the patient puts the weight on the 
heel, the knee will lock and when he puts the weight more forward it will 
allow for bending. This is often achieved by polycentric kinematics, e.g. 
by using a four-bar mechanism that locks in place when loaded in certain 
directions but swings freely when loaded in others. [12] To obtain a 
controlled swing of the leg during walking, a hydraulic or pneumatic 
dampener is often included. 
For persons with lower activity levels, an option is to use knees that 
remain extended during walking. It is then only during sitting that the knee 
is bent, and this is controlled manually by the patient by e.g. pulling on a 
cable.  
 For more precise movement control, an electrical knee joint reacts to 
sensors in the lower part of the prosthesis in order to lock and unlock the 
knee appropriately according to where in the step process the patient is.  
The microprocessor allows for more sophisticated adjustments of the 
knee when changing walking patterns – e.g. when walking backwards or 
running. 
 
The interface between the knee join organ and the foot organ is the 
pylon – this is considered a part of the knee-organ in this case since the 
function of transferring the rotation around the joint lies in this. The 
interfaces are standardised so that they mostly use a pyramid adapter as the 
connection system. This allows for choosing the organs independently to 
best match the individual needs of the patients.  
 
Prosthetic foot 
The function of the foot organ is to mimic the workings of a normal 
foot and ankle. This, however, is no simple feat, as this function can be 
split into a series of sub-functions that are each needed to create a 
convincing imitation of a foot’s function. The four that seems the most 
critical are: 
 
 
Shock absorption 
 
Energy storage and release 
 
Natural gait cycle 
 
Adjustment to unlevelled surfaces

But in addition to those, functions such as filling out the shoe, adjusting 
heel height to match high heels and giving the right sense of weight adds 
to the patient’s perception and use of the foot.   
Similar to the knee joints, a broad range of different ankles exist, and 
not all available prosthetic feet possess all of the above functions. Studies 
show no clear answer to which choices of technology lead to the highest 
perceived gain in quality [11]. And as the price (and in some cases other 
trade-offs such as weight or fragility) increases with complexity, the needs 
of the individual patient are usually matched closely with the product 
choice. As an example, the prostheses used for professional runners are 
built specifically to store energy in the ankle joint for better performance.  
 
The mode of action for accommodating each of the needed functions 
vary with the chosen technology for the foot.  
For shock absorption, the means can be elasticity in the heel material 
or structure (Figure 13 and 14) or it can be the implementation of hydraulic 
or pneumatic shock absorbers (Figure 12 and 15). 
 
For prosthetic feet with energy storage and return (ESAR) principles, 
carbon fiber springs are often used (Figure 12, 13 and 15). It is common 
that several springs are implemented to better mimic the many joints of a 
regular foot, i.e. energy storage happens both in the forefoot and ankle.  A 
more advanced option is the micro-processor controlled ankle (Figure 12) 
that, among other benefits, has the advantage of active push-off rather than 
passive. The option of leaving out the function for energy storage means 
that the patient must compensate by higher energy input. 
 
A natural gait cycle is facilitated by the foot by allowing flexion and 
extension. The simple way to obtain this is by a single-axis joint at the 
ankle with rubber bumpers. Or, in the case of powered or hydraulic angles, 
the flexion and extension is incorporated in these components.  
Leaving out the option for ankle movement (Figure 14) gives a less 
even gait cycle and is not commonly used for patients with normal activity 
levels.  
 
 Adjustment to uneven ground is relevant for patients walking outdoors, 
where some multi-axial movement is needed to adapt to the non-planar 
surface. This can be incorporated in the foot as elasticity in the material 
(Figure 13 and 15), or by incorporating a multi-axial joint with a rubber

1.1.2 Production analysis 
From the previous section, it is clear that each organ of prostheses 
consists of several smaller components. Neither knees, feet or cosmeses 
are manufactured at Ortos, and these are instead purchased from OEMs 
and adjusted and assembled into finished prostheses. The sockets however, 
are handcrafted at Ortos’ production facilities, meeting the need for each 
to uniquely match the patient’s stump shape.  
 
This section describes the production methods that Ortos follow at their 
workshop. While there are small variations between manufacturers, the 
general procedures followed are similar. As such, it is assumed that the 
findings from Ortos are sufficient to make general conclusions in terms of 
production of prostheses and orthoses.  
While the production methods for prostheses and orthoses share many 
commonalities, special considerations must be made for both: The 
orthoses are generally subject to larger forces, as they function to force 
limbs into correct positions – but without restricting movements that are 
desirable. The prosthesis sockets suffer much lower forces, especially due 
to the fact that they are commonly built based on TSB, meaning that the 
pressure is spread evenly on the surface.   
Both processes rely on a cast of the patient’s leg or stump where the 
prosthesis/orthosis is to be attached. This step is handled by the prosthetist, 
who manages the contact with the patient.

modifications are common in this case, but are limited to e.g. fixing 
another strap on the orthosis or narrowing the width of the foot plate.  
In some cases, Ortos produces an orthosis individually made for a 
specific patient, this is the case if there are more specific or unusual 
requirements to the function or if the patient does not fit the standard ones. 
Their handcrafted orthoses will generally be of larger dimensions (with the 
same strength level upheld) than the mass-produced ones, as Ortos does 
not use an autoclave in the production, and therefore cannot reach the same 
compression of the fibers in the composite. 
Orthoses are for the larger part made of carbon fiber, but can, if the

reinforcement in the form of particles. For different orthoses for other body

for finger orthoses.

Manufacturing prostheses 
For socket production, a test socket is commonly produced to check the 
fit before initiating the production of the actual socket. This test socket is 
made in clear thermoplastic, allowing for easy inspection of compressed 
skin areas and air pockets on the stump when the patient tries it on. 
Based on the observations with the test socket, the plaster model is 
modified, and when this is satisfactory, the manufacture of the actual 
socket can be initiated. This is done by a lamination process similar to the 
one of orthoses.  
Instead of cut pre-preg pieces, cylindrically woven tubes of fibers are 
pulled over the mould. Most commonly glass fiber or carbon fiber is used, 
and tubes are added on top of each other until a satisfactory thickness is 
reached.  
Similar to the orthosis production, two plastic socks – one pulled over 
the cast and one pulled over the fiber layers – make up a cavity to apply 
vacuum to.  
Since untreated fiber material is used, the matrix is added through a 
funnel to be led in between the two plastic layers. The seeping in of the 
matrix into the fibre material takes several hours, during this time the 
socket is occasionally smoothened and straightened manually to ensure 
even distribution.  
After the matrix has hardened, the shell can be taken off the mould and 
postprocessed by adding appropriate cut-outs and cutting and grinding the 
edges. The silicone insert is made in a similar manner as the test socket, 
i.e. a plate of the material is heated in an oven and pulled over the mould 
while applying suction as seen in Figure 25.  
Valves and connectors are attached to the socket after the hardening, 
the mounting of those is enabled through dummy inserts that were placed 
on the cast during the lamination process.  
The socket is then ready to be assembled with the remaining parts of 
the prosthesis and put into use. The remaining components are screwed on

Material 
The material used for the prostheses socket is commonly thermosetting 
glass or carbon fibre composite with a flexible inner socket of silicone. 
Thermoplastics can be used in cases where the prosthetist assesses that the 
loads the socket will be subjected to are small enough for this material to 
withstand it. This would be the case for less active patients.  
Choosing thermoplastics when possible is both due to the lower price 
and easier processability of this material, but also for the reason of it being 
possible to after-form the socket: If a poor fit is discovered after 
production, or occurs due to stump changes over time, the socket can be 
adjusted for a better fit. If thermosetting composites are used, a new socket 
has to be made in these cases if the poor fit cannot be compensated for by 
adding extra padding.   
For orthoses, carbon fiber is the common choice, more rarely kevlar is 
chosen instead. Similarly to the socket considerations, a simple 
thermoplastic is chosen whenever possible.  
While a full material evaluation is above the scope of this thesis, the 
choice of this material can be superficially assessed. 
As can be seen from the two CES charts in Figure 26 andFigure 27, 
composites outperform other material classes in terms of minimising 
weight while maximising strength and stiffness. The guidelines of 
relevance are the one with index E/ρ and σ/ρ respectively, as the loading 
situation on a prosthesis socket resembles that of internal pressure in a 
cylinder. [1]

be able to pass this knowledge onto the patient in order to include him in

Perception and acquisition 
The script of the prosthesis has a big say in the possibilities of its use, 
but is highly dependent on the patient’s acceptance and understanding of 
the script. E.g. if a microprocessor controlled prosthesis is described by the 
patient in the framework understanding of a mechanical prosthesis, it will 
lead to not only unutilised potential, but also possibly lower trust in the 
prosthesis from the patient. This would stem from a feeling of not being 
able to predict the leg’s responses and could ultimately lead to a lower 
mobility level than if a simpler prosthesis had been chosen. 
This emphasizes the role of the prosthetist in communicating with the 
patient so that a mutual understanding of needs and use patterns is 
established. While some of these needs can be clarified through the 
disability type and the physical limitations, the variations in use within 
each patient group are important too: The perception a new amputee has 
of his prosthesis can be drastically different from that of an amputee who 
has been used to wearing a prosthesis his whole life. 
Even for a single patient, the perception might change over a lifetime – 
during the appropriation phase it can be a challenge to simply trust that the 
prosthesis will carry the body weight, while in later phases focus can move 
to facilitating e.g. climbing as the patient becomes capable of incorporating 
the prosthesis in his normal behaviour.  
To determine the individual patient’s needs, it is therefore important to 
consider that the length of each phase of domestication of the prosthesis 
can be very unpredictable, and some patients never reach acceptance of the 
technology. I.e. some patients see the prosthesis as part of their body, some 
see it as a tool and some see it as foreign body that refuses to cooperate 
[13]

1.2 Additive Manufacturing 
While often perceived as a new technology by the public, 
additive manufacturing (AM) has been around since the 1980s, 
where the first commercial AM system was patented and made 
available [15].  
Leading up to this, a series of patents and technologies 
regarding topology and photosculpture formed the basis for the 
AM-thinking of building 3D structures using layered material. 
The first patents in these fields were seen in 1860 and 1890, and 
a continuous fine-tuning and further development since then lead 
to Munz patenting a technology resembling stereolithography in 
1951 [16].   
In the following years, technologies bearing closer 
resemblance to today’s AM emerged, but it was not until 
computer power was more developed that AM as we know it 
today was seen.   This step into modern AM is usually attributed 
to the patent by Charles Hull, with the Stereolitography 
Apparatus in 1986. Through the 1990s new AM technologies 
followed, the first ones being Fused Deposition Modelling 
(1991), Laminated Object Manufacturing (1991) and Selective 
Laser Sintering (1992) [16] [17] [18].  
Since 2010, a significant increase in development of low-cost 
3D printers happened with the expiration of the patent on Fused 
Deposition Modelling. With the patents on other AM 
technologies gradually expiring, it is reasonable to expect that 
this increase in development will continue [15]. 
 
1.2.1 General definition of AM 
Additive manufacturing was previously known as rapid 
prototyping, due to the fact that the technology was originally 
primarily used for creating prototypes, i.e. to get a physical feel 
of the form, fit and function of the model [15]. However, during 
the last years the AM technology has been gaining ground in 
being used for end use, and the term ‘rapid prototyping’ was no 
longer sufficient and exclusive to describe AM. As a response to 
this, ‘additive manufacturing’ has taken over as the generally

accepted term. However, the popular name commonly used is ‘3D

The mode of operation of AM systems is to build the model layer by 
layer from a 3-dimensional CAD drawing. The mode of which the layers 
are created and bonded together varies between the different AM 
technologies, but common to them all is the aspect of addition of material. 
The contrast to this additive process is subtractive and formative 
fabrication processes [18]. Formative processes shape the material into 
form by applying mechanical forces, i.e. forging and injection moulding. 
Subtractive processes remove material from a solid block until the desired 
shape is obtained, i.e. by CNC or milling.  
While all current AM technologies use the layer-wise addition, the 
definition of AM does not limit the mode of operation to this, and a 
production method that does not add material in a layer-wise fashion might 
still fall under the AM definition [15]. 
The process of AM can be described by eight steps which are applicable 
across the different AM technologies – although the weight of each step 
might vary between them. The steps, as described by Gibson [15], are as 
follows: 
 
 
Creation of CAD file 
 
CAD to STL conversion 
 
Transfer model to AM machine and modify position, shape etc. 
 
Set machine parameters  
 
Build  
 
Part removal and cleanup of e.g. supports  
 
Post-processing  
 
Application (e.g. assembly). 
 
The first three steps are relatively independent of the AM technology 
used, although there are different approaches to those steps as described in 
section 1.2.2. The subsequent steps are sensitive to the choice of 
technology however, as the different AM systems have different strengths 
and limitations. An obvious influence on the workflow is whether support 
structures are present with the chosen AM method: How the step of part- 
and support removal plays out is largely dependent on how the part is 
supported during build and which material is used for support.  
Another factor that can affect several of the steps is the layer height that 
the technology in question is capable of. Larger layer height generally 
means reduced build time, however it can also lead to increased need for 
post-processing to even out rough surfaces or reach desired tolerances. or 
it can render finer details impossible altogether. Of course, the material 
available for a given AM technology also has a large effect, as most of the 
AM technologies have few material choices available. This puts strict 
limitations on the material properties that can be chosen for the part, as 
well as influencing all of the aforementioned steps in terms of 
incorporating special considerations in both design, build and further 
handling of the part to make sure it is suitable in terms of material [15].

1.2.2 The 3D model 
Generally, the process of submitting usable input to the AM system 
falls in three steps as listed in the above section. First, the part is modelled 
using 3D drawing software, CAD. This file is then converted to the STL 
(Stereolithography) file format, which is the input file used by practically 
all AM methods. STL describes the CAD model by its geometry alone, i.e. 
it does not contain any info about e.g. modelling history, and approximates 
the surfaces by splitting them up into triangular facets. The triangle size 
defines how well approximated the surface is, and to maximise the part 
quality, the triangle size should be set below the resolution of the AM 
machine. 
The last step of readying the file for print is to create the cross sections 
in the model that is to be printed. This task is carried out by appropriate 
software capable of analysing the STL-file and slicing it up corresponding 
to how the AM system is to print the part.  
While STL is by far the most commonly used file format, a newer file 
format, AMF (Additive Manufacturing File Format), has been developed. 
It is based on the same principle as STL, but includes info about material, 
units, colour etc. [19] 
 
As an alternative to the first step of the process, the 3D model can be 
created by use of a 3D scanner: Instead of drawing the model from scratch 
in CAD software, a physical object is scanned to make a virtual model that 
can then be converted to an STL file.   
Recently, the better price and availability of a good 3D scan has made 
this much more applicable, and where an effective 3D scanner cost 
$100,000 some years ago, the same results can now be obtained for a few 
hundred dollars with a phone camera [15] [20]. 
 
The process of using a 3D scanner to create the model for the prosthetic 
leg based on scans of the stump will not be explored further in this report. 
It is sufficient to note that the technology to do so is ready and available 
for purchase, and it will hereafter be treated as such. Further exploration 
of the different possibilities of 3D scans and its concrete application in 
prosthesis manufacture is left for further studies.  
 
1.2.3 Overview of process types  
While many different classification methods for grouping AM 
technologies exist, a standard terminology was developed in ASTM F42 
and ISO TC 261 [15]. This classification will be used to cover the AM 
technologies in this section.  
 
Vat photopolymerization 
The process of vat photopolymerization (VP) functions by hardening 
resins through exposure to irradiation. This happens by placing the 
photopolymer in a vat and focusing light on the parts that are sought 
solidified to create the 3D model. This can either be done in a point-wise 
fashion, as seen on  Figure 34, or by irradiating an entire layer at once by 
the use of Digital Light Processing technology (DLP). A third option is the 
two-photon approach where two laser beams intersect to cause 
photopolymerization [15] [17].  
After a layer has been cured, a new layer of resin must be applied to 
continue the process. There are two approaches to this: First is a top-down

The primary material for the technology is photopolymers, i.e. resins 
that harden upon exposure to irradiation. The wavelength that is needed to 
cure the liquid resin varies with the material used, and as such the type of 
radiation used includes gamma rays, X-rays, UV, electron beams and 
visible light. However, only UV and visible light is used in commercial 
systems, the others have only been applied in research projects [15]. 
Photopolymers harden by cross-linking, i.e. parts made by VP exhibit 
less creep and stress relaxation compared to parts of e.g. thermoplasts. But 
this crosslinking also means the parts cannot be reshaped after 
manufacture.  
The first resins used for VP were acrylate based. They had high 
reactivity, but higher degree of shrinkage and warp (5-20%), which lead 
to more inaccurate parts. Additionally, with these materials curing had to 
be done in two rounds, increasing production time and complexity.  
An alternative to the acrylate based resins was found in epoxy based 
resins. Epoxies cure by ring-opening, resulting in less shrinkage (1-2%) 
and hereby stronger, harder, more accurate parts. However, epoxy resins 
have slower photospeed and higher brittleness than acrylates.  
The resins used today are epoxy with some acrylate content, as this 
takes the advantages from both types.   
Current VP-materials have low impact strength and durability 
compared to injection moulded parts. They also have degrading 
mechanical properties over time. The main strength in VP is its ability to 
create small parts with high accuracy and surface finish [15] [18]. 
 
Powder bed fusion 
Powder bed fusion (PBF) is characterised by using thermal energy to 
fuse a powdered material one layer at a time. In accordance to this, 
thermoplasts and weldable metals are generally suitable for PBF, while 
e.g. thermosets are not.  
Selective laser sintering (SLS) is the most widespread form of PBF, and 
this principle can be applied to both polymers (pLS) and metals (mLS). 
The process of pLS happens by placing a powdered polymer in an enclosed 
chamber filled with nitrogen to prevent degradation. The material is heated 
just below its Tm, and as such lower laser power is required. A CO2 laser 
is focused using galvanometers onto the powder bed, thermally fusing the 
layer. A new layer of powder is then put on top with a counter-rotating 
roller, and the process is repeated. 
Besides from the material requirement of being susceptible to melting 
under increased temperature, additional considerations have to be made: 
Crystalline polymers have a more defined melting point, and as such they 
act more reliable than amorphous polymers during the PBF process and 
result in more accurately shaped parts with less porosity. However, 
crystalline polymers have higher shrinkage and warp.  
The mechanical properties of polymer parts made using PBF are not far 
from those of injection moulded parts, and in addition to this, small beads 
of glass or carbon can be added to the powder for better mechanical 
properties.

In addition to fusing by melting, the powder in PBF can be fused by

LPS refers to partial melting of the powder that acts as a binder between 
the particles while portions of the material stay solid.  
 
The principle of using powder as base material offers both benefits and 
difficulties: On one hand it gives a broad range of possibilities in material 
choice, and the powder functions as support while the part is being built, 
meaning no post-removal of support structures and a wider range of 
possible geometries. On the other hand, the handling of the powder leads 
to challenges such as density variations due to higher compression of the 
powder at the bottom, special considerations in design to handle small 
particles, and even and smooth distribution of the powder [15].   
 
Material extrusion 
Material extrusion is one of the more tangible AM processes, as it 
simply consists of pushing molten material through a layer and distributing 
it in layers forming the desired 3D shape. In order to avoid deformations 
and weaknesses, the material must fully solidify in the shape it has been 
placed in, while still bonding to the below and above layers that are placed.   
The most wide-spread form of extrusion technology within AM is 
Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM), which leads a polymer filament 
through a heater to melt it, and pushes it out through the nozzle by rollers 
acting on the filament. In contrast to PBF, FDM works better with 
amorphous polymers, as the process is easier to control with a more widely 
defined melting zone. Short fibres can be mixed in the filament to improve 
mechanical properties, and new advances in FDM have led to the 
possibility of printing with continuous fibre as well, this will be covered 
in section 1.2.5. While thermoplastics, e.g. ABS, are the most obvious 
choice of material for FDM, the principle can also be used for viscous 
materials that can be dispensed via syringe, or allow for chemical reaction 
for curing upon exiting the nozzle. 
The main advantage of FDM is its simplicity, meaning that desktop 
FDM printers are available at low cost. This also makes it viable to install 
multiple print heads, allowing for e.g. multi-material printing. However, 
the technology also has a series of drawbacks:  
The mechanical properties are generally poor in FDM printed parts, and 
anisotropic properties are significant, as the strength in the z-direction is 
lower than in the xy-plane.  In addition to this, the print speed is low and 
the accuracy and surface quality is limited. Supports will be needed for 
some geometries, and while there are technologies allowing for e.g. water 
soluble supports, it is generally necessary to consider the design in terms 
of supports carefully to avoid surface defects or unreachable internal 
supports [15] [19].  
 
Material jetting 
Material jetting is usually based on systems with multi-nozzle print 
heads, each nozzle forming droplets that are deposited on the build plate. 
The technology bears close resemblance to common inkjet printers in the 
sense that it has a wide print head moving back and forth over the build 
plate.  
Waxy polymers and photopolymers are so far the only materials used 
for material jetting in the industry, but research is being done to explore 
the possibilities of using the technology with e.g. polymers, ceramics and

material enough to allow for droplet formation. 
Material jetting is still facing significant technical challenges to 
overcome, including optimal material compositions for low viscosity, 
better control of droplet formation behaviour and the deposition of those, 
and in the end solidification.  
Different droplet formation technologies have been explored in 
attempts to best solve these issues, those can be roughly categorised into 
the way they are expulsed from the print head: Continuous stream or DOD 
mode. The former is based on a column of fluid being ejected from the 
nozzle, of which droplets break off – possibly facilitated by vibrating the 
jet. In contrast to this, DOD is based on the droplets forming directly from 
the nozzle when an actuator facilitates it.  These droplets will have smaller 
diameter than those created by continuous mode, but the frequency of the 
droplets will be lower [15].   
  
Binder jetting  
Binder jetting is comparable to PBF on many points, as it is also based 
on a vat of material powder that is fused layer by layer. However, instead 
of melting the powder together, it is joined by adding a binder before a 
new layer of powder is spread out. Similar to material jetting, the printer 
head has multiple ejection nozzles. 
The technology was originally invented at MIT in 1995 and named 3D 
printing (3DP). Since then it has been licensed to several companies with 
slightly different takes on the commercialization of it. The most significant 
difference is found in the materials available, as the first materials were 
starch based and mostly suitable for visual prototypes. However, 
infiltration with other materials and new advances in which materials are 
applicable have resulted in wider variety of mechanical properties.  
Similar to PBF, binder jetting requires no creation of support structures, 
as the surrounding powder functions as such. Also, the process is often 
faster than other methods, as only the binder and not the full material 
volume is to be ejected from the print head. This increased speed is further 
accentuated by a new approach by Voxeljet, where the build plate it 
inclined, allowing for continuous printing and very long prints.    
 
Sheet lamination 
Laminated Object Manufacturing (LOM) was, in its first form, based 
on paper sheets being cut into shape for each layer, and glued together. 
Material surrounding the part was cut out for easy subsequent manual 
removal. This procedure results in low shrinkage, warpage and internal 
stresses in the parts – however swelling and inconsistent layer thickness 
can be seen in the z-axis for materials such as paper.  
A much wider selection has since become available with LOM, and 
now includes plastics, metals and ceramics. Variations are seen in the 
technology depending on the material used, e.g. the time in the process that 
the material is cut: For metal layering the sheet is cut before addition to the 
part (form-then-bond), while for paper materials the sheet is often cut into 
shape after having been glued to the part (bond-then-form).  
As well as a series of materials, several bonding methods exist for the 
technology. In addition to gluing, methods include thermal bonding, 
diffusion bonding and sheet metal clamping

Ultrasonic Additive Manufacturing (UAM) is a more recent form of

welding. The advantage of this technology is the possibility of embedding 
intricate internal structures such as cavities, fibres or sensors.  
 
Directed energy deposition 
Directed Energy Deposition (DED) is a technology mainly used for 
metal. Its mode of function is to melt the material simultaneously with its 
deposition. For the melting process, a laser or an electron beam is focused 
on the material in the exact region where the deposition happens, meaning 
the DED head leaves behind a track of material.  
The technology is almost exclusively used for metal printing. While the 
technology could be applied to other materials such as polymers, it has yet 
to be commercialised. Ceramics and metals with high reflectivity and 
thermal conductivity are not easy to process by use of DED, but any 
materials that melts with applied heat are generally applicable to the 
process. 
The feedstock material come in either powder- or wire form, with 
powder being most common. In both scenarios, the material is fed from a 
nozzle into the small melt pool that is present under the beam, and as the 
beam moves forward, the material solidifies and slowly builds up a 
structure in a similar manner to FDM.   
To keep the gas around the melt pool inert, oxygen concentration is 
kept low by a gas recirculation system in an enclosed gas chamber, as is 
e.g. seen in the DED machine LENS.  
DED is most commonly used for repair and feature addition, and is 
capable of producing parts with satisfying material properties that can be 
planned to fulfil specified requirement in the x- y- and z-axis. This is 
possible due to the option to control the microstructure through deposition 
in several directions via a 4- or 5-axis motion system. The main limitations 
of DED is the poor accuracy and surface quality caused by the size of the 
melt pool [15] [19].

AM has been gaining ground as a viable production method during the 
last years, and it is seen that it has gone from being seen as mainly a means 
of producing prototypes to be used primarily for functional parts [19], cf.

This shift is likely attributed both to the fact that AM technologies are 
evolving and improving, and that the industry is starting to gain a better 
understanding of its benefits and limitations – and hereby a higher trust in 
its capabilities of performing as expected when applied to suitable 
applications.  
While low production speed, limited choice in materials, compromised 
mechanical properties and tolerance challenges are ultimate barriers for 
some products in terms of being produced by AM, other types of products

1.2.5 State of the art in fibre reinforced AM  
It has long been possible to print composites with the available AM 
technology. However, when this is mentioned, it is practically always short 
fibre fillers that are referred to. While this does indeed increase e.g. 
strength, hardness and rigidity of a material a great deal, the benefits from 
the fillers are limited to the x-y-direction as the fibres do not cross over 
layers [19].  
Composites are most commonly used in relation to power bed fusion, 
where fillers such as glass, carbon or aluminium is added to the base 
material (often nylon). Also for FDM and VP is the addition of short fibres 
common [19] [15]. 
However, as was mentioned in section 1.1.4, a requirement for 
manufacturing prostheses with AM is the possibility of printing with 
continuous fiber. The current options for purchasing 3D printers with 
technology to print continuous fiber is limited to say the least, but within 
the last couple of years, systems making it possible have started to emerge.

Markforged 
The Markforged printer is the only mention of a printer able to embed 
continuous fibre in the Wohlers 2016 report [19]. It is a relatively new 
system with the first version, Mark One, introduced in 2014 [21]. It adds 
continuous fiber to a nylon model by using two print heads: One extruding 
the molten nylon and one extruding the reinforcement, which can be in the 
form of either glass fiber, carbon fiber or kevlar.  
While the system does indeed increase mechanical properties (such as 
strength) greatly in the x-y-direction, a number of limitations were 
observed during the use of it. The printer that was tried out is bought 
recently in relation to a Ph.D.-project at DTU and is located at FabLab. 
The findings during the try-out was that the principle in adding continuous 
fiber is viable and useful: Compared to a pure nylon model it performs 
significantly better in tests. This is in line with the company’s claim that 
the printed material is 20 times stiffer than ABS parts and has a better 
strength-to-weight ratio than the aluminium alloy 6061-T6 [21] [19]. But 
the way the machine is made, it only allows for fiber addition to a part if it 
can place 40mm of continuous fibre, as this is the length it is cut off at. 
This means that e.g. smaller features cannot have fibre addition. 
Additionally, the closeness of the fibres is limited to layer thickness in the 
z-direction and to nozzle width in the x-y-direction. Very close packing of 
the fibres is therefore not possible.  
While the principle of Markforged is relevant and interesting to the 
subject of this thesis, the system is not fit to be used directly to produce 
prosthesis sockets. This is due, on a very basic level, to its part-size 
limitation that does not allow for printing of large enough parts. But, a 
greater hindrance is seen in the layering of the fibres: The produced parts 
are very anisotropic when comparing x-y and z-direction, and the load 
application to a prosthesis socket means that the produced parts from 
Markforged would not be applicable with that system.    
 
Orbital Composites 
While still in the early phases and with pending patents, Orbital 
Composites is a start-up company offering a print head capable of coaxial 
extrusion, i.e. it incorporates a continuous fibre inside the extruded 
polymer. In contrast to the Mark One, it allows for use of off-the-shelf 
filaments and fibers rather than needing specially produced material. This 
significantly increases the amount of available materials for printing, 
making it easier to get a proper fit between needs and material properties. 
The claim is that when printing with carbon fiber, only 1-2 shells will be 
needed for an object which would normally require 5-10 on normal FDM-
based 3D printers [22] [23], something that both targets the weight-
strength ratio for the prosthesis issue, but can also generally increase print 
speeds of components as fewer layer will be required.

Besides mounting on a 3D printer, it can be fixed to e.g. a lathe to create

approaches to controlling fibre direction in the print compared to the Mark 
One. 
In addition to coaxial extrusion, the print head also has an active mold 
roller that is to remove voids from the extruded fiber by applying heat and 
pressure while passing over it. Another interesting functionality

Arevo has developed an additive manufacturing software that allows

Impossible Objects 
Composite-based additive manufacturing method (CBAM) is an AM 
technology in development that aims to place itself between conventional 
3D printing and manual carbon fibre layup.  
The method is to print a solution onto a sheet of e.g. carbon fibre. The 
sheet is then covered with a thermoplastic powder that adheres to the 
regions that have been printed with solution. A sheet is printed for each 
layer in the part, and the sheets are subsequently stacked, compressed and 
heated to fuse the layers. The surrounding, excess fibres are then removed, 
leaving the desired 3D shape [25].  
The produced parts are stronger than the ones seen by e.g. FDM 
methods, but the problem of anisotropy would be expected to affect this 
technology as well.  
 
EnvisionTec 
EnvisionTEC named their new patent-pending process Selective 
Lamination Composite Object Manufacturing (SLCOM). It works much 
like LOM, fusing large sheets of woven composites, such as carbon fiber, 
Kevlar or fiberglass together. The sheets are pre-impregnated with 
thermoplastics that melts when a heated roll passes over the sheets. An 
inkjet prints wax and binding agent on the sheets, with the wax marking 
the areas that are to be cut away.  
Apart from printing in composites, the notable thing about the SLCOM 
machine is the upper limit to the size of the parts it can produce: 
Components of over 200kg are possible and the machine itself is estimated 
to be priced at $1 million [26]. 
This capacity far overshoots the demands of this project, but the basis 
of the technology could prove relevant to a solution.  
 
Carbon3D 
While not printing with continuous fibres, Carbon3D’s M1 printer is 
interesting in the way that it claims to create isotropic properties and a non-
layered surface through faster-than-normal DLP-print. This is made 
possible by an oxygen-permeable membrane, allowing for faster build 
speed and hereby no recognisable layer-structure [27] [19] [28].

1.3 Benefits and barriers for 
applying AM to prosthesis 
manufacture 
The idea of applying AM to the prosthesis manufacturing process is not 
a new one, and attempts to utilise it in the fabrication of lower limb sockets 
have been made. While these first steps towards proving the possibilities 
for 3D printed sockets show good potential, the focus is only on them being 
a replacement for sockets that are currently made of thermoformed 
polymer. These sockets are mainly used for low-activity patients and in 
developing countries: They are cheaper and simpler to produce, but are 
less resistant to high forces and need a higher shell thickness to withstand 
normal activities.  
For the high-activity sockets made of composites with long carbon or 
glass fibers, the material of choice is rarely challenged. This is with good 
reason, as other materials cannot reach the same strength-to-weight ratio 
and stiffness. The fact that no AM technologies exist that are capable of 
printing carbon fiber composites comparable to those produced by the 
current manual lay-up process, means that the possibilities for applying 
AM to this type of sockets are fairly unexplored. 
This is despite the good fit of the prosthesis case in the product category 
that, according to Wohler’s report [19], should be considered candidates 
for AM production. These products are generally characterised by:  
   
1. Small batch sizes 
2. Custom parts 
3. Lightweighting 
4. Complex internal structures 
5. Consolidation of many parts into one 
 
With prosthesis sockets readily being able to check off the first three 
points, there should be reasonable potential in looking into its application 
possibilities. Point four and five are relevant as well, although the reasons 
might be less apparent – those will be clarified later. 
 
1.3.1 Benefits 
The benefits that could be achieved by using AM in prosthesis 
production would be tangible and profitable: Cutting down manual labour, 
saving material and a chance of increasing fit and decreasing weight.

The reduction in needed man hours lies in the automatization of the

that a switch to AM would cut down the total production hours needed. 
But since the AM process is largely unattended and requires interaction 
only during the first and last steps, it would reduce the man hours needed.  
Additionally, experiences with 3D printed sockets show that the fit 
might be better with this technology than with production with a casted 
mould [30]. This leads to a shorter fitting period after production, as the 
patient does not have to come in repeatedly for testing and subsequent re-
adjustments. This could potentially shorten the time interval between 
requisition and delivery of the socket, something that is a great benefit to 
the patient.  This is illustrated in the process flows in Figure 51, where it 
is also shown how the material usage can be reduced by AM usage, as the 
test socket and cast/mould can be saved. 
To further reduce the fitting time and material waste, the possibilities 
of using a reinforced thermoplastic rather than a thermoset as is currently 
the case for the high-activity sockets, could be explored. This would allow 
the prosthetist to make faster and more immediate adjustments, lowering 
the time consumption on the test-adjustment-cycles. It would also lead to 
a higher average use time per socket, as more comprehensive adjustments 
could be made – increasing the lifetime despite stump changes and 
avoiding the reproduction from scratch of a newly made ill-fitting socket.  
 
As for the potential for decreasing weight and shell thickness of the 
socket, it relates to the possibility of full fiber direction control. Whereas 
the orthotist currently controls the fiber direction in sockets only by 
varying the relative rotation of the sheets, an AM approach could permit 
control of the direction of each thin fiber bundle. This would allow for 
optimising strength and stiffness of the part by analysis of the expected 
loads on the digital model and corresponding arranging of the fiber 
orientations. 
It should be noted, however, that it is uncertain which material 
properties a new AM technology would be able to produce, as factors such 
as layer adherence and limitations in available materials could decrease the 
strength of a produced part. As such, the optimisation of strength through 
defining fiber direction is not considered as a value to be added to the 
existing socket properties.   
For orthoses, however, the defining of the fiber direction has a more 
tangible influence on product quality: There is no doubt that the stiffness 
in different areas of the product can be varied with this approach, 
something that influences the function of the orthosis greatly. For example,

accentuated. While the five points for AM-applicability are relevant for a 
very long range of medical products, prosthesis sockets have the advantage 
of being categorised as an aid rather than medical technology. This is very 
significant in terms of dodging a barrier that is commonly present when 
considering AM technology in relation to this type of products: The highly 
regulated standards (e.g. ISO) that are required for product approval within 
the medico industry. These strict regulations are generally poorly 
compatible with the shortcomings in process monitoring and control that 
characterises AM due to it still being at an early stage of maturing.  
But for prosthesis manufacture it is sufficient that the prosthesis fits and 
is accepted by the patient – an approach that is closer to craftsmanship than 
guaranteeing and documenting tolerance intervals.   
 
1.3.2 Barriers 
While there clearly is a series of benefits for applying AM to prosthesis 
manufacture, challenges also lie at the ready – the main one being that 
none of the currently available AM processes are suited for printing 
sockets for active amputees.

1. The limited possibilities for printing with continuous fibers 
2. The anisotropy characterising 3D printed parts 
 
The first point is a barrier in the sense that choosing another socket 
material than continuous fiber composites increases the weight and shell 
thickness of the socket, hereby decreasing comfort and level of function 
for the patient.  
Currently, the only available AM technology capable of printing with 
continuous fiber is Markforged, and its principle is not directly applicable 
to socket manufacture: Firstly, the fiber/matrix-ratio is low compared to 
that of carbon fiber sockets. In the manual lay-up process, excess matrix is 
removed during the vacuum process, resulting in a high fiber content. For 
the Markforged process, the fiber content is limited by the fact that fiber 
has to be sandwiched in between polymer layers. Secondly, there are 
significant limitations in how the fibers can be laid out.  
It would therefore be beneficial to rethink the method of embedding the 
fibers during print in order to increase the fiber/matrix ratio.  
 
The second point, the issue of the anisotropy, stems from the planar 
layering method used in AM. Since the socket is not loaded in just one 
direction, the anisotropy cannot be aligned with the load pattern, and 
therefore would have to be compensated for by increasing dimensions of 
the part.   
Two approaches exist for tackling the anisotropy challenge: Either the 
layer bonding is increased in order to approach isotropic conditions, or 
alternatively the directionally dependent strength properties are arranged 
to match the load pattern. 
 
Another barrier worth considering is the investment cost, which is a 
common matter of importance for companies for acquiring new production 
technologies. While desktop 3D printers have reached a level where their 
cost is no longer a real barrier to most industries, a more significant 
introduction cost than the price of the printer itself, is the challenge of 
starting up the use of CAD software.  
Even though the assumption is that the CAD model of the socket would 
be simple to create via a 3D scan, it should be expected that the prosthetist 
would need to make modifications to this model based on his experience 
and expertise. To do this, training in CAD software would be required, 
which, combined with the acquisition of CAD-licenses, would incur 
significant costs compared to the price of the AM system itself.  
So, while it is of course sought to keep the price of the proposed concept 
low, it is seen as more significant that the system facilitates easy use and 
introduces other advantages than employee efficiency, as this might take a 
while to manifest itself.  
In relation to this, Ortos expresses unwillingness to invest in 
intermediate or short lived solutions: Unless a solution is proposed that 
lightens the production steps that are truly labour intensive and material 
consuming, they would be unlikely to be willing to depart from their 
current production methods. This ambition is backed up in this project, as 
methods for printing moulds for manual fibre layup already exist and 
therefore not of much interest to this report.

1.4.1 Requirements for production 
A requirement specification for an AM production system for 
prosthesis sockets was set up based on the work done throughout this 
chapter. This can be found in Appendix D.  
The process of uncovering the requirements happened through the 
literature study and analysis, where the demands and goals were identified 
continuously, and hereafter gradually detailed and decomposed. A tool 
used for this was the objectives tree [7], which can be seen in Appendix H.  
 
The demands that were found to be the most defining for the initial 
ideation phase were: 
 
Ability for post-production adjustment 
If the shape of the socket can be modified, the lifetime of a prosthesis 
potentially increases. This is due to the possibility of making more 
substantial adjustments to accommodate a stump changes over time than 
simply varying the thickness of the padding. Additionally, the production 
process would be simplified by allowing for continuous adjustments 
through thermoforming.  
For this to be possible, a thermoplastic must be used as the socket 
material.  
This excludes AM technologies that are based on thermosetting 
polymers, and as a consequence vat polymerisation and material jetting are 
removed from the list of production principles for inspiration.  
 
Embedding of continuous fibers 
The socket material must have high stiffness and strength/weight-ratio 
in order to compare to existing sockets for active patients. This points to 
composites with continuous fibers being the appropriate material choice. 
This material choice is consistent with the orthotists experiences of it 
performing well for this type of socket. 
None of the currently available AM technologies are capable of 
producing CFRTs that are appropriate for the use in high-activity sockets. 
As such, none of the technologies are excluded from the idea generation 
process based on this. However, it is assessed that binder jetting has 
insufficient material properties to function as a satisfactory matrix in 
CFRTs, and this technology is therefore not considered in the following.  
 
Regards for anisotropy 
As mentioned in the previous section, the solution must be able to either 
produce parts with close to isotropic properties, or alternatively arrange 
the anisotropic properties in such a way that they don’t compromise the 
part strength in the load directions.  
Considering that all successful attempts on avoiding anisotropy in 3D 
print have been reached with thermosetting polymers [19], it is reasonable 
to withdraw from the challenge of finding a viable solution for printing 
isotropic thermoplastic, and rather look at a more favourable way of 
avoiding the planar layering. 
As a consequence of this, LOM takes a questionable status in terms of 
possible applicability. It seems unlikely that the sheet placement would 
allow for adequately free layer arrangement for load path optimisation.

1.4.3 Utility goals 
Mission statement 
Prosthetists seek to be able to promise the patient that his new 
prosthesis will last and be comfortable, i.e. feel and function naturally. 
They need to produce it as quickly as possible, as cheaply as possible and 
with as few iterations as possible.  
The key means for satisfying this are automatization in production, 
digitalised shaping, minimal weight and thickness of the socket, freedom 
in determining design and fiber direction and the possibility for 
adjustments after production.  
 
Target group 
Prosthetists and orthotists dealing with production of prosthetic legs 
and orthoses. 
 
The idea with the product: 
Prosthesis socket production is made cheaper and simpler, with shorter 
delivery time while increasing the possibilities for creating a more 
comfortable socket for the patient.  
 
The idea in the product: 
Automatisation of the socket production through an additive 
manufacturing technology capable of printing with continuous fibers. The 
fiber direction is customisable through a digital model and can be coupled 
with mathematical calculations for local strength and stiffness 
manipulation.

2.1 Idea generation 
Different methods for brainstorming were used during this process in 
order to facilitate viewing the problem and solution space from more sides 
and generate more varied concept ideas.  
As described by Osborn, brainstorming is a group activity for idea-
production and, to be successful, should build on four rules [31]: 
 
1) Criticism of ideas must be withheld until later 
2) The wilder idea, the better 
3) Quantity is wanted 
4) Combination and improvement is sought  
 
Since group brainstorming was not available during this project as the 
work was carried out by a single person, adjustments were made to 
some of the approaches to still make them fruitful. The four basics 
suggested by Osborn were adhered to, as these are not group dependent. 
 
2.1.1 Brainsketching 
Brainsketching [32] is a brainstorming method where ideas are 
gathered by sketching them on paper with short accompanying notes. After 
a short while of sketching, the participants relate to the others’ drawings 
by discussing or exchanging them. They then continue the silent sketching 
to develop ideas or create new ones.  
To circumvent the fact that sketches could not be exchanged between 
different group members, the effect of circling and continuing ideas of 
other was sought mimicked by bringing previous stashes of ideas back out 
to review them at a later point with fresh eyes. This resulted in being able 
to see new possibilities within the idea, and additional notes were made on 
the sketch or a new idea sprung from it. 
  
2.1.2 Random stimulation 
Random stimulation [33] is a technique used to introduce more 
diversity into the type of ideas created if these become too homogenous. 
The idea is to present items that are not immediately related to the topic of 
the brainstorm in order to divert the minds of the participants from the 
solutions that are most readily associated to the topic. The presented items 
can be of very different types and have the possibility to inspire in different 
aspects, e.g. material, shape, use etc.   
While no dedicated sessions of this method were planned, random 
stimulation was used throughout the idea generation phase. This was done 
by carrying a notepad during breaks, such that ideas that were sprung from

Double head 
A carbon fiber sock, i.e. a cylindrically woven tube is suspended from 
a plate by leading hooks through the fabric at the top of the tube. An FDM 
nozzle touches the fabric from the outer side while a small, heated head 
matches the position of the nozzle at the inside of the fabric. By moving 
the double head across the fabric while extruding molten polymer, the tube 
will stiffen in the position that the path of the head dictates. This allows 
for forming of the soft sock into a stiff shape of the desired geometry, 
without requiring mould or support structures.  
The concept relies on a coaxial coordinate system, where the double 
head can rotate around the center line to follow the cylindrical shape of the 
sock. By adjusting the radius of the rotation, the definition of the shape of 
the printed part can be created.

Spider weave, elastic frame 
Three elastic bands are strapped up, constituting a triangular shape. The 
print head then manipulates the relative placement and shape of the bands 
by distending thread between the bands and glueing them onto those. As 
such, the triangular starting shape is modified and adjusted thread by 
thread. The process is similar to that of a spider weaving its net: connecting 
two individual threads with a transverse thread in tension will pull the 
threads together. Laying down thread without tension allows the printhead 
to lay down surfaces on the created shape.  
 
  Spider weave, fixed frame 
Similar to the spider weave concept with the elastic frame, the fixed 
frame weaving spindles fiber onto the frame to create a woven structure 
that can subsequently be treated with a matrix to obtain a fiber composite 
with fibers that have been laid down in predetermined directions.  
The fixed frame concept relies on a 3D printed mould for support and 
attachment on the fibers when weaving. This can be removed after the 
matrix addition, but will leave holes in the socket where the attachment 
hooks were placed.  
 
Coaxial 
The coaxial concept is based on an FDM process where fiber is inlaid 
during the extrusion. The coaxial approach is used to be able to lay down 
the fibers and extrudate lines in direction that are beneficial to the part in 
terms of resisting predicted loads. The fiber direction is not completely 
customisable for this solution, but it is suitable for the conical shapes of 
sockets: for this geometry, the fiber direction can be said to be laid down 
to match all the possible loads of a prosthesis socket.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
External supports 
For the concept ‘external supports’, the approach is, as for the coaxial 
solution, to incorporate fiber lay down in the extrusion process of an FDM 
printer. However, instead of using a coaxial coordinate system as above, 
this concepts uses a regular FDM printer with Cartesian coordinates. In 
order to obtain further control over the fiber direction than limiting it to 
the xy-plane, the concept relies on interruptions during the print process in 
order to turn the part to accommodate to the, at that printing stage, 
specified print direction. To allow for the rotation of the part while it 
should still function as print surface, external supports are printed in the 
part to give it sufficient stability when placed at angles.   
The need for turning the print during the process, slows the production 
speed and introduces a higher risk for faults.

2.2.2 Evaluation 
In order to weigh the five concepts against each other and get further 
input to the selection considerations, a Pugh matrix [36] was set up. The 
reason for choosing this approach for evaluation was that the need was for 
a screening method to assist in the subjective assessments of the concepts. 
Had the goal been to analyse the concepts’ compliance with the 
requirement specification, the Weighted Objectives Method [37] could 
have been carried out instead. However, due to the five concepts still being 
at a very early stage, evaluating them through a full Weighted Objectives 
Methods analysis would be excessive, and might even inaccurate: Due to 
the low detailing of each of the concepts at this point, they might not be 
fully comparable in how they score in a WOM. 
As such, a short list of requirements is chosen, including the criteria 
that are deemed to both be important and to be possible to asses for each 
concept at this stage. The reason for only including few and select criteria 
is that the Pugh matrix rating has no nuance for the relative importance of 
the objectives. Therefore, including too many less important criteria could 
skew the results of the evaluation.

2.4.2 Concept 2: Simple 
Like the ‘Woven’ concept, ‘Simple’ also has a coaxial movement 
system rather than Cartesian. Its extrusion technology is FDM, but it 
should embed a continuous fiber into the filament it is laying down. This 
means that the coaxial system has a double purpose: It both helps avoid 
that the anisotropy of FDM prints does not compromise strength in any 
critical directions on the socket, and also means that the fibers can be laid 
down in a pattern that further increases the socket strength in the direction 
of its load.  
Since the prosthesis socket is hollow and must have an inner surface 
that is both smooth and customised to each patient, there is no readily 
available starting point for initiating the print of the socket. This requires 
an inner shell to be printed up by a traditional Cartesian FDM printer. 
Mounting this shell on the rotating axis then allows for the ‘Simple’-printer 
to start  extruding fiber embedded in thermoplastic directly onto the shell.  
The inner shell stays a part of the socket after production, and should 
therefore be sought to be kept as thin as possible as to not increase shell 
thickness. 
Disadvantages: 
• 
Risk of low fiber/matrix ratio due to large amount of extruded 
material around each fiber.  
• 
Thicker shell dimensions due to the need for printing the inner 
shell in standard Cartesian way.  
Benefits: 
• 
Simple to achieve a fiber pattern that corresponds exactly to 
requirements.  
• 
Good realisability of the prototype. 
 
2.5 Selection of final concept 
Careful considerations about the listed pros and cons of the two 
concepts, as well as evaluation based on the previous review points used 
in Pugh and graphs led to the choice of “Simple” as the most appropriate 
solution.  
However, another round of rework introduced a few changes, these are 
marked on the morphology, and the concept is renamed to DOCPSP 
(Directionally Optimised Composite Prosthesis Socket Printing) 
 
2.5.1 Description of the concept 
The selected concept is a method for prosthesis socket production that 
allows for printing thermoplastic reinforced with continuous carbon fibers. 
The fibers can be printed into the socket in the direction and pattern that 
best facilitates optimal strength properties compared to the expected load.  
The alignment of the fibers to load paths allow for a socket that is 
lighter and thinner while being able to withstand the same loads as current 
sockets. This increases comfort for the patient, as the prosthesis weight is 
reduced and the socket dimensions are closer to those of the other leg.  
Using thermoplastic as matrix material means that the socket can be 
thermoformed when the need for adjustments occur. This benefits the 
patient, as he can keep the prosthesis for a longer time with a better fit. 
And when he does need a new socket, the process of fitting and adjusting 
is less time consuming for him and he gets his new prosthesis faster.

The production method is based on additive manufacturing, making it 
an automatic, unattended process. This puts the previously very manual 
socket production into a new context where the chance for a well-fitting 
socket becomes more dependent on knowledge than on craftsmanship, and 
where the work hours and time frame needed to produce a prosthesis are 
reduced. 
 
The production method works by printing with a fiber tow that is lead 
through the nozzle of an FDM printer and hereby embedded in the 
extruded melt. There is no other driving force on the fiber than the pull 
from moving the nozzle while the end of the fiber is stuck of the end that 
is already laid down on the print surface.  
By implementing a coaxial movement system rather than a Cartesian 
one, the print path can be designed to optimise the fiber direction in a 
beneficial way compared to how the load distribution on the socket is. The 
coaxial coordinates are better fitting to socket production due to the 
elliptical, approximated rotational symmetric geometry: With this 
movement system, it becomes possible to print on the curvilinear surface 
plane of the socket.

2.5.3 Mode of function: 
The method relies on a CAD model of the socket to be produced. This 
is obtained by a 3D scan of the patient’s stump, that is then modified 
according to the prosthetists expertise in how the socket should be shaped. 
In addition to this, the desired fiber directions should be defined in the 
model to let the slicing software plan the print path accordingly.  
These initial software preparations are not within the scope of this 
project however, and will not be detailed further. 
    
Step 1: 
The first step is to produce the surface that the print can be initiated on. 
Since most sockets have a flexible inner socket inside the composite shell, 
this is taken advantage of and used for the print surface to save material.  
This requires multimaterial print due to the insert being flexible and 
therefore not stiff enough to act as support for the print. As such, the insert 
is printed in silicone and a non-flexible, cheap polymer (e.g. PLA) is 
printed inside of this as support structure.  
This step is done on a regular, Cartesian FDM printer.  
 
Step 2:  
The printed silicone liner is mounted on the rotating axis of the printer.  
The fastening to the axis is made possible by printing the support 
structure within the silicone to support this assembly.  
The fiber is led to the print head where it is fed through melted polymer 
under conditions that ensure adequate wetting.  
 
Step 3: 
When pulled through the nozzle, the fiber is laid out in the extruded 
path.  
The fiber lay-down happens in a process comparable to pull-winding, 
i.e. the pull of the fiber through the nozzle happens by the movement of 
the nozzle away from the point where fiber is embedded in hardened 
plastic. As such, no extrusion process is incorporated for the fiber.  
The filament is fed through the nozzle by the force of a regular extruder 
– but at a lower extruder force than normal due to the fiber taking up some 
of the extrudate volume.  
The build process from there is comparable to common FDM prints: It 
happens layer-wise, just with an approximated cylindrical layer 
arrangement rather than the planar.  
 
The print speed is decreased during directional changes to ensure 
sufficient fixation in the hardened plastic to resist the pull force.

2.5.4 Focus points for further detailing: 
Hot end design 
The specific workings of how the hot end leads fiber and molten 
filament together must be established. The hot end capability of printing 
with fiber is the most critical point of the concept, and a hot end for testing 
this principle should therefore be produced to examine the feasibility of 
the concept. 
 
Matrix deposition 
The behaviour of the extrudate when subjected to fiber printing must 
be evaluated in order to design the process well. There are questions such 
as how well it adheres to the fibers, how the extruded amount relates to 
layer bonding and whether it will be possible to reach a balance of making 
the melt contained to the hot end, but at the same time be able to flow with 
the fiber.  
 
Print surface 
The applicability of using the silicone insert as the print surface requires 
further investigation in order to uncover whether this is a viable approach.  
It is not clear that the adherence of the printed material will stick to the 
silicone if it is a different type of polymer. And there is also the issue of 
silicone being pliable, and there could therefore be doubts as to whether 
the material is too soft to print on for a process that requires that thread is 
pulled by the nozzle by force.  
 
Material choices 
Since an important requirement to the prosthesis socket is the high 
strength and stiffness, material considerations should be made to find 
suitable materials for the process. The right material must be able to both 
adhere well to the fiber and the silicone insert – but it must also have 
inherent good material properties and be accepted for constant skin 
exposure.  
 
Movement system 
The printer requires a different setup of the movement system than the 
regular AM technologies relying on the deposition of planar layers. While 
the question of changing one of the linear movements to a rotational one 
is very straightforward, it would be interesting to see how the software 
handled it in terms of precision and options for customisation of print 
paths.

3.1 Tooling, hot end 
It was decided to purchase an E3D V6 hot end [see Appendix M for 
documentation] capable of printing with available polymer filaments to 
use this as a basis for the hot end design.  
The reasons for choosing this hot end over any alternatives were: 
 
1) It is all metal (as opposed to being made of e.g. PEEK or being 
PTFE lined). This allows for using high melt temperature polymers 
– e.g. nylon – without the risk of melting or damaging parts of the 
hot end itself.  
2) The behaviour of the hot end has a high degree of predictability due 
to its design. For example, the transition zone is clearly defined, 
which means that the physical point at which the melt starts flowing 
can be identified more reliably.  
3) It has a relatively long melt zone. This means that a less drastic 
temperature can be used, which is relevant for avoiding the risk of 
the polymer degrading at the higher temperatures. Also, the longer 
melt zones opens up for the use of larger nozzle sizes and faster 
printing speed, since the melting of the polymer can “keep up” with 
the increased flow rate. In addition to this, the longer melt zone 
might also play a role in securing that the fiber is properly 
immersed and wetted in the melt while passing through the hot end.

As seen from the equations, the flow rate has a significant influence on 
the pressure drop stemming from the melt channel but not on the pressure 
drop from the nozzle contraction. Seeing that the melt flow is very low, 
i.e. around 10-6 m3/s, it is clear that the pressure drop in the hot end can be 
attributed to the nozzle contraction rather than the course of the melt 
channel.  
While it should be noted that the given equations are not entirely 
applicable and descriptive for the melt flow in the hot end, they illustrate 
the relation between the two pressure drops to a sufficient extent. 
As a result, considerations about how the channel design affects 
pressure drop, can to a large extent be neglected (apart from point B on the 
list below), and the attention can instead be moved to other crucial 
constraints and considerations for the hot end.  
The considerations that were embedded in the detail design of the hot 
end channels are described in the following list:  
 
A) Straight fiber path. 
The fiber channel must run in a straight line from entry point to nozzle, 
i.e. it must enter vertically. This is due to the properties of the carbon fiber 
and would be required regardless of the choice of fiber type: Precoated 
carbon fiber (such as the one used by the Markforged printer) cannot, if at 
room temperature, be bent more than a few degrees before breaking. As 
for the untreated fiber, its softness and flexibility meant that leading it 
through the heater block to prepare for print would be near impossible if 
the channels.had corners. (It was assumed at the time that the flow of the 
melt alone would not be likely to carry the fiber through the nozzle. This 
was later found to be a correct assumption, and the fiber would therefore 
have to be inserted through the channel manually). 
 
B) Homogenous melt channel. 
To avoid dead zones and uneven melt flow that could cause turbulence 
and degeneration of the polymer due to being heated for excessive amounts 
of time, attention should be put on the relations between different parts of 
the melt channels. 
Expansions should be avoided, and contractions should happen 
gradually without drastic diameter changes. In general, the guidelines for 
injection mould design could serve as inspiration - even though the 
pressures and flows used are very different under these circumstances, the 
behaviour of the melt in the hot end would be comparable. 
 
This, however, only applies to the melt channels, not the entry channel 
for the filament as there is no viscous flow there. Rather, abrupt diameter 
changes might be used intentionally at this point to prevent leakage of melt 
into the fiber channel. 
 
==PAGE:65==

C) No melt backflow through the fiber entrance channel. 
Due to the pressure inside the hot end channels, any empty space within 
the hot end will be filled with melt. From this follows that the entry hole 
for the fiber would suffer melt leakage if no preventive measures are taken. 
This could be either in the form of fitting a valve to the hole or designing 
the entry hole to let the fiber fit snugly. The latter seems simpler and less 
likely to fail. The drawback would be a higher difficulty of inserting the 
fiber through the entrance channel, but since this is not expected to be 
needed at short intervals, this would be a tolerable trade off.  
For preventing that the melt enters the fiber channel, it would be 
required that the pressure in the melt channels did not surpass that needed 
to push the melt into the fiber channel. As was seen from the 
abovementioned formulas about pressure drop, this depends largely on the 
diameter change from melt channel to fiber channel. An exact calculation 
of the pressure needed to push the melt into the fiber channel would be 
comprehensive – and actually unneeded: It would be sufficient to ensure 
that the value was higher than the pressure required to push the melt 
through the nozzle. 
Again, as per the above equations, this would be achieved by having a 
larger contraction at the fiber entry than at the nozzle. (Note here that the 
actual size of the orifice that the melt can pass through is smaller than that 
defined by the diameter of the hole, as part of the hole is obstructed by the 
volume of the fiber. However, the diameter of the fiber is the same upon 
exit as upon entry, and differentiating the contraction diameters therefore 
still gives effect.) 
It is estimated that a rather small difference in diameters is required, as 
the pressure in the melt channels will not need to be as large as in common 
FDM prints: To a large extent the fiber should drag out the melt with it 
through the nozzle rather than having it pushed out by force. The filament 
feed pressure will simply have to be large enough to keep the internal 
channels filled.  
 
The diameter of the fiber channel is therefore set to 0.5mm. This is 
based on the Markforged fiber being 0.4mm, and to allow for free 
movement of it in the channel, 15% is added to this dimension, as this 
seems to be the extra space granted to common filament channels: 
0.4mm∙1.15=0.46mm. This is rounded up to 0.5mm due to PTFE tubing 
being available as standard with this inner diameter.  
As per the above points, the nozzle size would therefore need to be 
larger than 0.5mm. 
 
D) Tight contact at potential leak points between components 
constituting the melt channel 
It is of course to be avoided that there is a risk of leakage in the hot 
area. This requires that there are no gaps between interfaces that the melt 
can be forced out of. There are two approaches to this: Either the channels 
are drilled directly in the heater block itself, which would lead to assembly 
interfaces near the surface of the block where heat sinks had to be mounted. 
This is seen e.g. in the Cyclops hot end. Alternatively, an approach like 
that seen in the E3D v6 could be used, where the heat break and nozzle 
meet inside the heater block and forms the melt channel without letting the 
melt come into contact with the heater block. However, both require an 
exact fit and high degree of tightening of the components.

The benefit of the first option would be that all channels could be

getting 3 channels to line up by screwing them into the heater block would 
require very tight tolerances, both in production and assembly, and pose a 
risk of damage on the parts when tightening.  
Instead, the second approach is used, where the channels merge directly 
in the heater block itself and the heat breaks are inserted with threaded 
interfaces. This is easier to manufacture and less sensitive to variations in 
production and assembly. A tight and precise fit is ensured by assigning 
tight tolerances to the non-threaded surfaces of the parts. This is to avoid 
the thread controlling the alignment of the canal, which would likely be 
rather imprecise.  
The downside of the chosen approach is that the nozzle and heat break 
from the E3D v6 cannot be used as components for the modified hot end. 
This is due to the full threading on these parts, and as the new hot end relies 
on those components being screwed into shallow holes in the heater block, 
the fact that the thread extends all the way to the flat bottom means that 
the part cannot be screwed into the hole fully. 
Therefore, new heat breaks where designed to fit the new heater block. 
They were made to fit the E3D heat sinks however, to cut down the number 
of customised parts.  
As for the nozzle, the Cyclops hot end uses a unique nozzle, and this 
does not have full thread. This matches the need for having the end surface 
line up with the bottom of the hole in the heater block, and the heater block 
was therefore designed to fit the Cyclops nozzle. 
 
Production process limitations 
The design of the channels in the heater block should take into 
consideration that these do not complicate the manufacturing steps 
unnecessarily. The hot end parts, including the channels in the heater 
block, should preferably be made with common machining operations, e.g. 
milling and drilling. 
Therefore, if the heater block is to consist of one piece, the channels 
must be drilled in straight lines from the surface. If curvy channels are 
wished, the heater block would have to be assembled from components. 
Since this assembly would cause a risk of both misaligned channel walls 
and leaks into the assembly interfaces, the design was instead 
accommodated to drilling.  
 
The channel diameters are bound by the Cyclops nozzle dimensions, 
the filament size and the earlier specified 0.5mm fiber entry. 
The confluence of the fiber channel and the filament channel is set as 
high as possible to obtain a higher distance for the fiber to be wetted. The 
filament channel is angled relative to horizontal both to allow for the heat 
sink to be further from the print surface and to get a melt inflow angle of 
less than 90○. A cut-off of the corner of the heater block is added to allow 
for drilling the channel from a perpendicular surface. 
Chamfers are added to channel contractions in the direction of the melt 
flow to allow for less turbulence and to allow the use of standard drills in 
production.

A challenge for the heat break design was the decision that the entry 
hole for the fiber should be 0.5mm (see point C). While drilling a hole of 
this size is not a problem if the hole depth is less than ten times the 
diameter, a drill that thin cannot make deeper holes without being damaged 
or compromising tolerances. So while the 0.5mm hole in the heater block 
was unproblematic in terms of manufacturing as the depth would not 
exceed 5mm, the fiber hole in the heat break would need to be longer to 
allow for a large cooling area. Therefore, two choices for the fiber heat 
break existed: Either design it with only a short 0.5mm hole or 
alternatively accept the need for less common methods for making holes, 
significantly increasing production cost and time. 
Since it was at this point not clear whether a shorter heat break or 
contractions in its fiber channel would affect the functionality of the fiber 
printing, it was decided to opt for the safer solution: A 0.5mm hole in the 
full extent of a regular-sized heat break.  
It was, however, found during the later testing of the hot end that this 
was not influential on the performance of the hot end (see Chapter 5), and 
deep hole drilling would therefore not be a requirement for producing the 
needed parts.   
 
3.1.2 Adequate wetting of the fiber 
Two different approaches were considered that could ensure adequate 
wetting:  
1) Pre-wetted fiber is used. If the fiber is properly wetted before being 
fed into the hot end, this will facilitate low occurrence of voids in the 
printed material. However, thermoplastic preimpregnated carbon fiber is 
most commonly commercially available as sheets or tapes. The only tow 
found for purchase is the one produced by Markforged [21], and this has a 
high price and only one available choice of diameter. Therefore, pre-
wetted fiber would likely have to be produced specifically for the 
application. This is assumed to not be overly complicated though, in 
literature it is described as a matter of simply leading the fibers through 
the plastic while ensuring that it enters in between each fiber in the tow – 
e.g. by spreading the fibers out [40]. One principle that could be interesting 
for this procedure is the siphon principle [41] where the tow is lead through 
a curved channel containing the matrix. Through pressure areas the matrix  
is forced in between the fibers, leading to wetting of the fiber.

itself.  
It was therefore decided to leave out any special measures for wetting 
in the hot end, and instead see if the later testing showed that the simple 
contact between melt and fiber in the nozzle would result in sufficient 
wetting. If this would prove not to be the case, a method of pre-wetting 
would be applied before the fiber entered the hot end.  
 
3.1.3 Method of starting or changing a print 
path 
Having the fiber grab on to the surface is essential when starting a print: 
If the fiber end is simply dragged across the surface, the pull required to 
drag it through the nozzle will never initiate. 
Similarly, changing the direction of a path drastically during a print 
requires the path leading up to the direction change to withstand the pull 
of the fiber in the new direction. This is assumed to only be a problem at 
corners, since if the path continues in the same direction, the drag load will 
be distributed over a longer area of the fiber, allowing the cooled part to 
carry the load.  
Two options are seen for ensuring sufficient adherence:  
1) The adherence can be made sufficient by manipulating the factors 
contributing to coalescence and cooling. E.g. pausing the print to allow for 
hardening of the polymer before continuing or decreasing layer height for 
better adherence to the print surface.  
2) Having a mechanical fixation, e.g. a compacting roller, to hold the 
fibre in place during start or direction change.  
 
It was decided to leave out the mechanical fixation of the design until 
it was tested whether the bonding of the extrudate would be enough to keep 
the pull on the fiber. This was both due to a wish to not over complicate 
the design, but also due to a mechanical fixation mechanism introducing 
new issues about taking print direction into account – this would be very 
preferable to avoid. 
 
3.1.4 Cutting of the fiber 
The cutting of the fiber would be required when ending a print, when 
moving the nozzle to a different position and possibly also when changing 
layers. 
Cutting very near to the extrusion site is desired, both to avoid problems 
with refeeding the fiber through the nozzle and to avoid the issue observed 
in the Markforged printer: That short fiber paths are not allowed due to the 
need for the full length of the cut-off to be placed.  
Also, a method should be used where either no melt was deposited on 
the cutting mechanism, or where residue would not impact the 
functionality of it. Additionally, a method that does not damage or weaken 
the area around the cut should be chosen.  
Two solutions that would fulfil these critera would be: 
1) Cutting the fiber inside the nozzle, near the exit. This could for 
example happen by controlling a rod’s linear movement with an 
electromagnet and a spring, cutting the fiber when the rod was moved into 
its path.

2) A cutting blade that could move under the nozzle to cut the fiber 
between the nozzle and the print surface.  
The mode of cutting would be to have the hot end move upwards circa 
1mm when cutting was needed, hereby having the thread form a 90○ angle 
with the print surface. The cutter would then swipe above the surface, 
cutting the thread at the point of discontinuance, leaving a 1mm thread 
dangling from the nozzle. This end would then serve as fixture for the next 
initiation of printing. 
 
Solution 2 seems to be the by far least complex solution and the one 
intruding the least with the printing process itself. Additionally, the fact 
that the cut would happen on a cooled extrudate would reduce or eliminate 
the risk for melt residue on the cutting mechanism.  
The movement of the cutting blade under the nozzle could be made 
with a rotary movement with a fixed center point above the nozzle. 
It was decided that this mechanism should not be fixed to the heater 
block, as there is no requirement for it to be heated - in fact heating would 
be likely to complicate the function. Besides, the movement requires a 
motor which would put wires in risk of coming in contact with the heater 
block.  
Instead, the cutting mechanism would be fixed to the mount of the hot 
end as it would be out of the way when not required and the extra distance 
that it would need to move would not be of functional importance.  
 
3.1.5 Extrusion system 
It was decided to use a Bowden extruder as the space for placing an 
extruder at the hot end is quite limited due to the two material entries. A 
standard extrusion system can be used, as there are no special requirements 
to the filament feed, and the hot end is designed to receive standard 
filament. 
For the fiber, no extrusion system is needed, as the feeding force of this 
would be the pull from the nozzle as it drags the adhered fiber along the 
path.  
 
3.2  Motion system 
The components needed to create a motion system that allows for 
rotation and two directions of linear translation would be straightforward 
to acquire and assemble. For the most part, the existing motion system 
from a common table top printer could be used, as it would only require 
few modifications to have one of the motors control the rotatory motion.  
A bigger concern would be found in the control system’s capability for 
handling the switch from Cartesian coordinates to coaxial. Not because it 
would take significant changes in the code to make this switch happen, but 
because it would be uncertain how the need for truer 3-dimensional 
movement would be tackled: Cartesian AM technologies only use the Z-
axis to change layers, which is why they can be heard referred to as 2.5D-
printers [4]. For the suggested concept, it would need to move truly in 3D 
to comply with the requirement for free choice of fiber direction.  
But since the motion system would be easy to construct and there is 
new software such as Arevo [24]  concerned with defining real 3D printing 
paths, this concern will not be seen at determining for the product viability.

A more tangible design decision for the motion system is its size. It 
follows naturally from the aim of this thesis, that the range of the motion 
system should be big enough to accommodate the size of a prosthesis 
socket.  
The sockets vary significantly in size, but through anthropological data 
it is found that by setting the max dimensions to 70cm x 25cm x25 cm, the 
95th percentile of men will be included in this for transfemoral prostheses. 
And since other types of sockets are smaller, this seems like a reasonable 
number for inclusion.  
 
As for the interface between hot end and motion system, it is clear that 
the motion system should not be mounted on the sideways heatsink: It will 
by too bulky, lead to too much torque, and the movement would unscrew 
the heatsink from the heater block. Instead, the movement should be fixed 
at the top, at the fiber connection. 
 
3.3 Print surface 
The print surface is defined in the concept to consist of the cylindrical 
silicone inner socket – stiffened out with supports. 
While this would be an elegant solution as no material loss would occur 
from mould production, it is also unclear whether this in applicable 
solution: There could be problems with lack of adhesion and lack of 
stiffness of the surface. It has not been possible to test the potential of this 
subsolution within the scope of this project. Therefore, this aspect of the 
concept is left as is until the answer about its applicability can be answered. 
The reason that it is possible to leave the point unanswered is that it is not 
critical for the function of the solution that this aspect works – it is simply 
a nice to have.  
The alternative, if the silicone printing turns out not to be applicable, is 
to print up an inner shell on a regular FDM printer that can function as the 
cylindrical build plate. If this material was chosen to be the same as the 
used matrix material, chances are high that there would not be adhesion 
problems with this method.    
 
3.4 Filament material 
The focus when choosing filament material is of course that the 
material properties of the polymer are suitable to act as matrix in highly 
stressed products. But equally important is the material interactions: If the 
plastic of the chosen filament type does not adhere properly to the fibers 
or if the chosen plastic is not appropriate for prolonged skin contact, the 
product has failed.  
As such, the full picture should be known when deciding on the 
material choices. Due to this, there is some unwillingness to settle on a 
belief about which materials would be most suitable before testing of the 
technology has happened.  
At this stage in the process the focus is on carbon as the main fiber 
material. Due to the uncertainty about whether treated or untreated carbon 
fibers would be more suitable, it was decided to create the prototype to 
allow for testing of both types. Since the only available pre-wetted fibers 
for AM are the ones from Markforged, nylon has been a strong candidate 
for the filament of choice since this is known to be compatible with the 
==PAGE:71==

markforged fiber. However, nylon absorbs water to a high extent and is 
generally one of the more difficult materials to manage in FDM 
technology. And the details about the specific materials used to create the 
Markforged fiber are not disclosed, leading to considerations about the 
right fiber/filament match being mainly speculation. 
The focus is therefore kept on getting the principle of the function 
working, then material tests and more evidently backed up findings could 
help clarify the most appropriate material choice. Due to this, there is 
reason to believe that any preliminarily produced materials samples would 
have good basis for improvement of their properties with further 
development and detailing of the concept.    
 
3.5 Software 
Due to the move from the cartesian coordinate system as seen in most 
3D printers to a cylindrical coordinate system, the existing software for 
slicing cannot be applied. Additionally, more customisation of the print 
pattern would be required in order to incorporate load path optimisation.  
The change of coordinate system in the software should be a fairly 
simple process, whereas the implementation of a system for freely 
dictating the print pattern would require more work. However, since 
software for defining fiber paths in 3D has been developed [24], it could 
be plausible that the principles from this could be applied.

4.1 Requirements to the prototype 
Following the detailing of the concept as described in the previous 
chapter, it was sought to demonstrate that the proposed solution for 
prosthesis manufacture would be likely to be a working solution in practice 
as well as on paper.  
Therefore, a prototype demonstrating the functionality of the concept 
was to be built. Since the scope of this project did not allow for a prototype 
incorporating all functionalities of the concept (i.e. a prototype of the 
production apparatus capable of producing a full size, usable prosthesis), 
it was decided to test the functionality of the aspects that were not clear if 
would work.  
It was judged that the movement system of the concept was not likely 
to contain many surprises in functionality: Combining a rotating axis with 
linear movement is not a new concept and is seen e.g. in lathes and ink 
printers capable of applying images to e.g. bottles. Another example is the 
EggBot, a machine drawing on e.g. eggs, also via a rotary and linear 
movement system. 
It was therefore assessed that if the prototype could be made to print on 
a flat surface, the principle could be assumed to be transferable to rounded 
surfaces with implementation of the suggested movement system. 
 
Rather, the principle of printing with continuous fiber was deemed 
more critical to examine, as existing literature and experiences within this 
area is so limited: Should this prove to not be accomplishable with 
satisfactory results, the whole concept would fall to the ground.  
Due to this, a prototype for printing continuous carbon fiber embedded 
in a thermoplastic was built. The print would happen on a horizontal 
surface, but was to have free customisable movement within this xy-plane 
– i.e. the paths could be laid down in a pattern most suitable for resisting 
the predicted load.  
The process of building this prototype will be documented in this 
chapter.

4.5.1 PID tuning 
After this, the thermistor needed calibrating, i.e. defining the value of 
the constants P, I and D in order to control the system’s predicting of how 
long to power the heater in order to obtain the desired temperature.  
Without the use of PID control, the hot end will heat up in bang-bang 
mode, meaning the heater cartridge will shut off if the target temperature 
is exceeded and turn on if below the target temperature. While this method 
will eventually reach the set temperature, it will happen with repeated 
under- and overshoots due to the delay associated with the heat travelling 
the distance from heater cartridge to thermistor.  
With the PID control, an algorithm is applied to predict when to turn 
off the heater cartridge in advance such that the target temperature is 
reached without overshoot, but instead rises at a slower pace to meet it 
more exact. The three parameters used in the calculations are KP, KI and 
KD, respectively proportional, integral and derivative.  
Without proper definition of these three values, the temperature control 
would be characterised by large overshoots and ripples, failure to reach the 
exact target temperature or slower heating. 
Marlin has an inbuilt PID autotune which was attempted (using 
pronterface), however, this failed as the temperature kept overshooting by 
too extreme values. Two approaches were thought to solve this: One would 
be to define different (“incorrect”, but lower) values of PID in order to get 
closer to the actual behaviour of the thermistor. However, instead it was 
chosen to limit the current to the nozzle to half, as it was seen that the 
heating up of the hot end was happening extremely fast during the PID 
autotune – indicating that going on full current might be excessive. 
This was changed by correcting the value PID_MAX in the 
configuration file of Marlin to 128 instead of the max of 255. 
 
This change resulted in the PID autotuned to succeed, and the last set  
of PID values obtained from the autotuned were used as the defining 
parameters:   
Kp: 6.51 
Ki: 0.37 
Kd: 28.80 
 
The default values in the Marlin configuration file were changed to 
these values.  
 
 
Upon testing the heating with the newly defined PID values, it was seen 
that the target temperature was now achieved with insignificant initial 
overshoot, whereas the overshoot had been 30-40○C prior to the tuning.

4.5.5 Shifted layers and shape irregularities 
While the Ultimaker was now able to lay down satisfactory lines 
without extrusion failures, it was noticed that the geometrical and 
dimensional accuracy of the shapes it was set to print was very poor.  
This was at first thought to be a problem with the mechanical parts of 
the movement system, as this was slightly difficult to move around 
manually and made more noise during print than usual. The axes were 
therefore oiled with sewing machine oil and the pulleys and belts were 
tightened and repositioned to make sure there was no interference with the 
frame.   
The effect of this, however, was minimal, and other causes for the 
problem were therefore sought. The movement of the motors during print 
was observed, and it was noticed that they were skipping steps, causing the 
misalignment of the print. This indicated that the problem lay in 
electronics. It was found that the cooling fan for the circuit board was 
dysfunctional. This would be very likely to cause the stepper drivers for 
the motors to overheat and hereby failing to activate the motor at short 
durations.  
A working cooler fan was mounted on the PCB, and the problem 
ceased. The printed shapes were now as specified.

The new hot end for fiber print was mounted to the movement system 
by modifying the existing mount slightly by cutting off excessive features 
that were in the way and securing proper fastening with screws and strips.  
While this was not the most elegant solution, it fastened the hot end 
sufficiently and no need for printing a new mount was seen as this was 
working as intended.  
 
The limit switches where again adjusted slightly to fit the new 
dimensions and the bed was re-levelled to fit the new height.

5.1 Expectations for functionality 
During the building of the prototype, it was continuously tested that the 
printer could function as a regular 3D printer. However, apart from the 
initial tests with feeding fiber into the unmounted E3D hot end manually, 
it remained unanswered whether the printer would at all be able to cope 
with continuous fiber printing.  
Even though careful consideration had been put into the design of the 
new hot end, the design decisions were, to a high extent, based on 
theoretical and practical experience with simple filament printing and dual 
extrusion due to lack of literature describing continuous fiber printing. 
Therefore, it was not until the redesigned hot end was produced and 
mounted on the printer that it was clarified whether these assumptions 
would apply to printing Carbon Fiber Reinforced Thermoplastic (CFRTP) 
There was, however, an indication of the plausibility of it working 
during the course of the prototype building: While no descriptions of FDM 
printing with CFRTP was available prior to designing the new hot end, 
three recent studies on the topic [46] [47] [48] were published during the 
course of this project. These used an approach similar to the one described 
in this report, and all three were successful in printing with CFRTP.  
In two of the studies the entrance for the filament and fiber is shared, 
whereas they are separate in the third study – similar to the concept 
presented in this report. While the studies are characterised by still being 
in an initial stage of testing prints with CFRTP, their findings indicate that 
it would be reasonable to believe that the prototype setup described in this 
report would have a chance of functioning. 
 
5.1.1 Critical points 
To reasonably evaluate whether printing CFRTP is a viable method for 
prosthesis socket production, a list of critical points was formulated. This 
was to judge whether the fiber printing seems to be valid in terms of 
translating it to the presented concept, or if there can be expected to the 
critical errors that would call for a change of concept due to inoperability. 
It is reasoned that if each of the critical points is fulfilled, it is reasonable 
to expect that printing CFRTP is a viable solution to use in the full concept.   
 
Critical points: 
A. The fiber is pulled out from the nozzle without requiring further 
assistance than the pull from the previously printed path and the 
melt feed.  
B. The fiber does not fracture during print. 
C. The adherence of the extrudate to the print plate is sufficient to 
start the print from an unattached fiber. 
a. Temporary adjustment of print speed, melt flow and 
nozzle/bed distance can be of influence. 
D. The adherence of the extrudate to the print plate is sufficient to 
allow for a change in direction (angling) of the print path. 
a. Temporary adjustment of print speed, melt flow and 
nozzle/bed distance can be of influence. 
b. Full 180○ path change is not necessarily required. As long 
as some change in direction is possible without slipping, 
load path optimisation can be incorporated.

5.2 Qualitative observations 
5.2.1 Initial try-outs 
After having mounted, connected and calibrated the redesigned hot end 
on the printer, the functionality of the principle was first tested by 
threading the head with Markforged carbon fiber (data sheet in Appendix 
M) and feeding in the filament by hand while moving the head along the 
axes manually and via Pronterface controls. 
As seen in Figure 94, this initial try-out was able to produce a very basic 
confirmation that the presented principle could produce actual material 
samples. 
While the manual controlling of the head meant that the process could 
not yet be referred to as real 3D printing, a series of helpful observations 
were still made at this stage. This was both in terms of which printing 
variables would be reasonable to use in Cura, and possible challenges that 
needed to be accounted for in the setup. 
 
The following findings in terms of functionality were made. Letters 
refer to the above list of critical points:  
 
A) Fiber traction 
By moving the printhead after the extrudate had adhered to the print 
surface, it was seen that the principle of laying down the fiber through the 
use of pull force was possible, and the force required was insignificant 
compared to the friction from e.g. the bearings on the printer’s movement 
system. 
 
K) Filament pressure 
It was possible to vary the amount of plastic to be extruded with the 
fiber by applying a higher feeding force (equivalent to higher E speed) to

There is a lower barrier for how low the filament feedrate can go in 
relation to a set print speed, as the fiber will pull out melt from the heater 
block channel – if the feed rate is too low, the melt in this channel will be 
depleted and the result is extrusion of uncoated fiber. As such, the 
minimum feed rate should be the one that allows for a constant full 
channel.  
The upper barrier is less well defined, and is similar to the one set for 
common FDM printing: It depends on e.g. the nozzle size and the traction 
force the extruder motor is capable of providing. However, as a reasonably 
high fiber to melt ratio is desired, the upper limit for relative extrusion 
speed is of less interest. 
It was found during those tests that a slightly larger nozzle size gave 
better results, as the filament pressure could be lowered while obtaining a 
more reasonable and steady amount of melt extruded with the fiber. 
Therefore, the nozzle size was drilled bigger, from 0.4 to 0.6mm. 
 
H) Tilted Z 
Printing “uphill” was tested briefly by letting the head print on the 
surface of a bottle. This was without problems, both uphill and downhill 
printing seemed to offer no further complications compared to printing in 
a horizontal xy plane. Therefore, the only possible cause for concern about 
this point would be if uphill printing results in significant extra wear on 
the fiber: The testing of this was not long enough to safely say that the 
fiber would not at any point over longer time have a higher chance of 
fracture during print. However, nothing indicated this, and it is deemed fair 
to assume that if the risk was slightly increased, this could be countered by 
making the sharp edges of the nozzle rounder during processing – this had 
an immense effect at 90-degree traction and could easily be improved 
much more by simply applying another chamfer.  
 
G) Fiber insertion 
Inserting the Markforged fiber through a cleared-out heater block 
channel and nozzle was rather easy due to the stiffness of this fiber.  
For the untreated fiber, the process of threading the hot end was a bit 
more delicate, comparable to threading a needle. But by wetting the end of 
the fiber, hereby making it stiffer, it was possible to get it through the hot 
end without requiring unreasonable amounts of time. By removing the 
nozzle and threading this while unscrewed, the process was made simpler 
and faster. 
For both fiber types it was required that the hot end had cooled down 
below 40 degrees to permit the threading. For the Markforged fiber this 
was due to the fiber coating becoming soft at higher temperatures and the 
loss of stiffness meant more difficult threading. The same issue was 
present for the uncoated fiber, here it stemmed from the wetting 
evaporating at higher temperature.  
Different methods for clearing the path of the hot end was tried. The 
most effective was found to be to heat the hot end to around 200 degrees, 
unscrewing the nozzle and pushing up PLA filament from the bottom. 
After this, the hot end was set to cool to 100 degrees, where the PLA has 
a “stringy” behaviour that allows for pulling it out of the channels in one 
peace, hereby clearing out the channels in the heater block.

behaviour, and so this replacement of the polymer type in the channel

While this method is not as thorough, and therefore can make the threading

As will be noted later, fiber re-insertion should generally not be

5.2.2 Automatic printing 
Following the initial try-outs, actual prints were initiated in Cura 
15.04.6 (an earlier version of Cura was used as this allows for a freer 
tweaking of parameters) to see how the prototype would handle printing at 
longer time frames and at more consistent conditions. 
First, simple one-layer rectangles were printed with a concentric print 
pattern as seen in Figure 96. Markforged fiber was used, and during these 
prints the bed levelling and the variables in Cura were adjusted until the 
print was running in reasonable quality. It was seen that layer height and 
print speed influenced the adherence of the fiber to the surface very 
significantly, and therefore those values were set low to ensure successful 
prints - and would later be sought increased to the point of failure to find 
their limits.   
From the printed samples was seen that there was no difference in how the 
paths were laid down in relation to the directional movement of the print 
head. I.e. it is concluded that the mode of printing is accepted as being 
multidirectional.  
After this, the printed geometry was changed to a dogbone model. 
Using the more fitting printing parameters (see section 5.2.3), the platform 
adhesion brim was printed successfully and seemed to adhere well to the 
print surface and the neighbouring paths. However, a rectilinear infill 
pattern was used, and printing this shed light on the printer’s ability to do 
sharp direction changes after very short distances. 
Apart from very small part details, the printing with the Markforged 
fiber proved successful and capable of producing parts. So the fiber input 
was switched to untreated carbon fiber (datasheet in Apendix M). The 
dogbone shape was printed, and similarly to above, the printing parameters 
were adjusted during the print: As such, large differences in print quality 
were seen in this part, the most obvious being too large fiber content that 
did not allow for proper adhesion of the extrudate. But after this adjustment 
of parameters, a print quality close to that of the parts made with 
Markforged fiber was achieved.  
An overview of the used printing parameters can be found in the 
following section.

Letters refer to aforementioned list of critical points:

A chamfer (59 degrees) was therefore applied to the nozzle exit. After

completely eradicating any fractures during the testing, it would therefore

E.g. if further testing showed there would be higher wear on the fiber when 
printing uphill on a curved surface. 
 
C) Adherence to print plate 
While the fiber does not adhere to the print plate at very first contact, it 
will attach after a few milimeters without manual assistance. This 
requirement for a short “starting path” would easily be incorporated in 
software for the printer, and be manually cut off after the print had 
completed.  
The adherence to the surface is very much dependent on print speed 
and layer height, and to a lesser extent also seems dependent on melt 
temperature and fiber content. Both PLA and ABS has been tested as the 
matrix, and both successfully adhered to the surface without problems.  
While this point proved rather unproblematic during testing, it is worth 
noting that the presented concept builds on a drastically different print 
surface than the one tested: While there is reason to believe that the 
rounding of the print plate will not prove to be a problem in terms of 
adherence, it is very likely that coating the silicone liner with an 
appropriate binder could prove problematic: This has not been tested and 
therefore only builds on assumptions. However, as previously mentioned, 
the concept can accommodate adjustments in this aspect: If it should prove 
to cause too poor adherence, a thin printed shell would instead be used as 
the print surface.  
 
D) Direction change 
It was feared that a change in the direction of the printed path would 
cause the fiber to be pulled excessively in the new direction if the adhesion 
to the surface had not had time to happen sufficiently.  
This phenomenon was indeed observed to a small degree, but 
fortunately it seemed very predictable when it would happen: 
It is seen that angles of 90 degrees and above are handled well in terms 
of the part being printed to the desired dimensions, i.e. no path overlap or 
skewed paths. Some rounding of the corners can be seen though, roughly 
2mm for the untreated fiber and around half that for the Markforged fiber. 
While this is indeed a deviation from the specified geometry, it also seems 
to be rather consistent and varies predictably with e.g. print speed and melt 
content. However, it is clear from observation that the factor most deciding 
of this, is the path length of the path leading up to the corner. I.e. how much 
solidified path length is there to keep the corner from being pulled to the 
side by the traction force of the fiber.

smaller details. However, decreasing the speed much lower becomes 
impractical as the prints take too long to complete. But, if the speed was 
decreased only at the corners, a higher overall print speed could be 
maintained while achieving a better detailing.  
 
E) Path coalescence 
While more formal material strength testing is required to make 
definitive statements about whether the interlaminar bonding is sufficient, 
it can be seen during the prints that bonding between layers happens in a 
similar fashion as in common FDM prints: A thin rim of melt is seen at the 
brink of each path, allowing for merging.  
 
F) Floss 
A flossed fiber could potentially affect the print in several ways: It 
could cause the nozzle or the channel to clog by build-up of fiber residue, 
or it could cause the fiber to vary in thickness due to wear on it before its 
exit through the nozzle.  
It was expected that no floss would be seen with a precoated fiber, and 
this was indeed the case: At no point during the testing was the Markforged 
fiber in any way flossed.  
The degree of flossing of the untreated fiber was more unknown – it 
had flossed a great deal during the first tryouts of the E3D hot end, but it 
was difficult to say whether this was due to the need for the later 
modifications or if it was inherent in the principle.  
During the printing tests, it showed that it would be justified to not 
consider floss a problem, even for the uncoated fiber: The full diameter of 
the fiber entered the top of the heater block channel, and also exited the 
nozzle.  
Occasionally, a small amount of fraying could be seen at the entrance 
in the heater block, but this would be pulled with the fiber into the channel 
shortly after. It is worth nothing that the fiber used was split into half of its 
original diameter, and therefore was actually already quite uneven and

flossed when inserted. It therefore seems reasonable to assume that a fiber 
purchased with the correct diameter would show even less flossing.  
One clog due to floss was removed during all the printing done, and 
this was after experimenting with inserting the full diameter of the fiber.  
The floss clog was very easy to spot and remove, and the problem of 
floss clogs is therefore deemed of little risk and influence to the printing 
process.  
 
I) Melt leak 
The question of whether the fiber entrance channel would be cause for 
melt leak remained unanswered until the test. While it had been assumed 
that the small diameter of the channel and the opposite drag of the fiber 
would prevent the leakage, it was not possible to calculate for sure, since 
the filament pressure needed in combination with the fiber was unknown.  
It was quickly seen during the testing, however, that this leakage did 
not pose a problem: The setup was initially tested without the heatbreak 
for the fiber, and the fiber entrance was therefore directly in the heater 
block. Here it was seen that if the filament pressure increased to higher 
values than normal, a small melt droplet would form at the fiber entrance. 
This was an insignificant amount however, and as the fiber started moving, 
the melt would be dragged back into the heater block with it.  
While not tested, it would be fair to assume that the leak issue would 
vanish with mounting of the heatbreak for the fiber: During the test, the 
melt had a temperature of 210 degrees all the way to the fiber entrance and 
therefore a very low viscosity. Upon installing the heatbreak and heatsink 
with cooling on this entrance, a temperature gradient would be found in 
the fiber channel. This would cause the upper end of the “leak” to have 
higher viscosity and therefore be even more likely to be pulled back down 
with the moving fiber. 
 
J) Fiber wetting 
The squishing of the fiber on the surface when laid down seems fine – with 
the naked eye it seems like well distributed and wetted. A more accurate 
evaluation requires a microscopy however (see section 5.2.4). 
 
K) Filament pressure/fiber content 
The filament extrusion rate was set to 30% in Cura, meaning that a 
rough estimate of the fiber content of the material can be made: Since this 
value was set to 85/90% when printing with pure PLA; it is assumed that 
the fiber content in the printed carbon fiber parts is roughly 65%, which is 
comparable to existing prepreg products. 
 
L) Prevention of dead zones 
It is difficult to make definitive statements about potential dead zones 
in the melt channels, but no burned/degenerated plastic was observed 
during extrusion or pull-out cleaning of the channels. But this is by no 
means a guarantee, as it would be very difficult to recognise in this way. 
A throrough flow analysis would instead be required to judge whether this 
was a risk to note. Should it prove to be the case, it is assumed that small 
adjustments to the channel design could alleviate the problems found in 
the flow analysis.   
In terms of potential heat damage to the plastic used to coat the 
Markforged fiber by printing at the temperatures needed for the matrix, 
this is not a cause for concern: While Markforged does not disclose the

In order to investigate the fact that the paths tend to round at corners 
due to pull back of the fiber, a test was set up to measure the magnitude of 
the problem and get an idea of the factors influencing it.  
The method was to print an outline containing different angle sizes, as 
seen in Figure 109 and measure how far the actual corner was from the 
intended corner position.  
The test was performed at different print speeds with several runs for 
each speed. The data sets for each speed were averaged and set up as the 
graph seen in Figure 110 andFigure 111. The most noticeable is that two 
of the graphs behave significantly differently than the others: Above print 
speed 3 they show highly decreased quality of corners, to the point where 
they are more likely to fail than succeed. Since the two graphs in question 
are sharp angle and wide angle respectively, it would be unreasonable to 
connect this observation to angle size. Instead, it has probably rather to do 
with those two shapes being located on the left side of the track that was 
printed, whereas the other three angles were on the right side. The probable 
reasons for this difference are: 
 
1) Dependence on print direction. Since the print head is travelling in 
positive y-direction when printing the three other angles and

It is a peculiar observation that the three graphs seem to have 
differences in coefficient and cross eachother around 3-4 mm/s, i.e. they 
have different sensitivities to print speed changes. It is not immediately 
clear why printing speed has a more drastic effect on larger angles – it was 
assumed to be the other way around, that sharper angles would suffer more 
from high printing speed. As this is not the case, however, two plausible 
explanations can be thought of: 
 
1) If the angle is sharper, the nozzle is closer to the path that needs to 
have adhered sufficiently to resist the pull. As such, if the nozzle is 
closer to this path, it could heat it up, meaning it would be more 
susceptible to pull. 
2) The direction of the pull actually has different mechanics behind it 
depending on the angle: For a 90 degrees angle, the path is pulled 
sideways, i.e. if one bit loosens it is more likely that the whole path 
comes off. For sharp angles, the pull happens more in the 
longitudinal direction, i.e. the pull works against a higher force to 
pull it loose. 
 
Overall, it would seem like a print speed of 3mm/s would be reasonable 
to use with the used setup around smaller details as there is not much gain 
from lowering the speed further than that and higher speeds lower the 
corner quality. The speed could be increased on linear parts of the prints 
without affecting quality negatively.  
 
5.3.3 Design of Experiments 
During the qualitative observations, it was found that a series of 
variables affect the quality of the print. These variables are the values that 
are defined in e.g. Cura, the geometry of the printed part and the print path 
chosen for it, and the material characteristics for both fiber and polymer. 
While it is clear that some of these factors influence the outcome greatly 
and others less, it is less clear exactly how each of them rank in terms of 
importance. Even less clear are the interaction effects of the different 
factors – but it is certainly hinted that there could be some, e.g. as seen 
from the effect of the print speed vs. angle of a direction change on the 
pull-back of the path.  
In order to get a clearer idea of the influencing factors and their 
magnitudes, a Design of Experiment (DoE) is proposed. While the scope 
of this project does not allow for the experiment to be carried out, it could 
be an interesting future study.

6.1 Viability 
The question that was sought answered during the work of this project, 
was whether it was possible to develop an AM technology that would be 
capable of producing carbon reinforced prosthesis sockets where the fibers 
could be arranged to match the strength and stiffness requirements locally.  
 
As was covered in the introduction, this goal was dependent on two 
challenges to be realised in order to claim its feasibility: 
 
 
Is it possible to print thermoplastic with embedded continuous 
carbon fibres? 
 
Is it possible to conceive a method for FDM printing that arranges 
layers in a cylindrical shape rather than the characteristic layer 
structure of xy-planes? 
 
The first question was examined through the development and testing 
of a prototype attempting this procedure. By the end of the project, material 
samples were successfully created by this prototype, indicating a clear 
proof of concept for this part.  
The second question has been examined theoretically, and a suggestion 
for a concept based on coaxial coordinates rather than Cartesian has been 
presented in this report. The evaluation and assessments done before, 
during and after the concept development points to it being a solution 
capable of solving the presented challenge.  
 
With the positive answers to both research questions, it is deemed likely 
that a viable solution has been found. 
 
6.2 The goodness of the concept 
By comparing the concept characteristics with the requirement 
specification that was formulated as a result of the product analysis, it is 
found that the proposed solution complies with all the requirements. 
Additionally, the majority of the criteria are fulfilled, and an additional 
number of them has the potential of fulfilment through further 
development.  
Comparing with the existing production method for prosthesis sockets, 
the presented concept offers solutions to demands that were not previously 
fulfilled. This trend can be seen both when assessing the production 
methods with the prosthesis socket requirements and with the concept 
requirements. This indicates that the concept meets the needs of different 
user groups and offers benefits to others than the target group. This likens 
the possibility of successful implementation of the solution, as more actors 
will have personal benefits of accepting the technology rather than having 
acquisition forced upon them to fulfil needs of other actors.

6.3 Evaluation of the relevance of 
the concept 
The concept solves the very concrete needs formulated by the 
prosthesis industry, and as such meets the business owner’s demands for 
cheaper and faster production. The relevance in this area is mainly 
economic, but also serves to potentially increase the product range that a 
single business is capable of offering, as the production via AM is more 
versatile than the currently used procedures and equipment  
But in addition to the relevance for the business owners, the concept 
meets demands that were voiced by the employed prosthetists and 
orthotists or uncovered through observation and analysis. These demands 
were largely concerned with the quality offerings of the prostheses and 
their possibilities for improving comfort for the patient: Either by offering 
a better fit of the socket or by taking away discomforts for the patient such 
as long waiting time for prosthesis finalisation or the need for the many 
trips to prosthetists. The relevance of the concept in relation to this, is that 
the proposed concept has the potential to offer a better fit for the patient 
with fewer adjustment iterations required from the prosthetist. And 
through data analysis and digital storage of stump changes over time, it 
introduces the option for implementing correct adjustments without the 
patient needing to be present.  
There is also the possibility that the technology will be able to offer 
product quality improvements rather than just meeting the material 
properties of regular sockets. While this would require further 
development and material testing to establish, the option of fiber direction 
optimisation opens up for opportunities for being able to offer patients 
sockets that are better accustomed to their exact needs.  
 
These are problem definitions of the western world, and here the 
concept has the opportunity to bring higher comfort to the patients.  
But in addition to this field of relevance, the solution has potential to 
offer a better accessibility to the higher-performing sockets in regions of 
the world where the prosthesis accessibility is low. In third-world countries 
the focus of the patient is often on getting a prosthesis at all rather than the 
quality of it. Therefore, the production method used is commonly the 
cheapest available, e.g. thermoforming the socket from a heated plastic 
plate. But with initiatives to introduce AM as a production method for 
prostheses in developing countries [49], the technology proposed in this 
thesis offers the opportunity for better differentiation in patient needs in 
those cases: without any other cost increase than the higher material cost, 
the performance of the socket could be increased for patients with a need 
or wish to perform physical activities that require better socket quality.   
 
It is also of relevance to mention that the possibility of printing with 
continuous carbon fiber could be relevant to a range of other industries and 
applications. The concept would need rework to fit other purposes, as the 
coaxial coordinate system is specifically appropriate for geometries like 
prosthesis sockets that are approximated rotational symmetrical.  
For products of other geometries, a different coordinate system could 
be adopted. The application of the technology to Cartesian printers could

6.4 Disadvantages 
The disadvantages identified in the proposed concept were largely 
connected to the limitations that the general lay-up of carbon fiber 
composites face: 
The technology performs poorly when it comes to smaller details and sharp 
directional changes. It is, however, a general characteristic of carbon fiber 
that it cannot be bent excessively, and it is therefore to be expected that 
geometries that force this upon it are less likely to succeed.  
But it would still be beneficial to find workarounds for this issue – e.g. by 
having the printer plan its travel paths to incorporate loops around smaller 
details that allowed for sufficient adherence but could be subsequently cut 
off as part of post processing.  
Similarly, the fact that the printer lays down continuous fiber means 
that the hot end cannot travel over geometries that are e.g. supposed to be 
empty without leaving a fiber track. The option of omission of material in 
regions of the component is therefore only possible if the fiber is laid down 
in a pattern around it.  
 
While significant problems with shrinkage and warp have not been 
observed during the prototype testing, it is likely that these factors would 
affect the parts when printing larger geometries, since it is an inherent 
characteristic of the FDM process. Due to the high need for exact fit of the 
socket to the patient’s stump, these effects could pose a relevant problem 
to the technology. While some correction and compensation could be done 
by subsequent after forming, it would be inconvenient and suggestions for 
mitigating the problem should be sought.  
 
6.5 Suggestions for changes and 
improvements 
The issue that caused the most difficulties when printing with carbon 
fiber was the splitting of the fiber to get the right fiber diameter. The reason 
for this split to be needed was that fiber tows of very low K-values (and 
hereby low diameter) and harder to come by and more expensive in price.   
Therefore, dimensional changes to allow for a more available standard 
fiber thickness would be reasonable to increase both reliability during 
printing and decrease the material price. Printing with a thicker fiber would 
compromise the detailing of the parts, as this would be equivalent to 
printing with larger nozzle size and therefore also higher extrusion path 
width. However, this would not be an issue for prosthesis printing, the

6.5.1 The cutting mechanism 
An automatic method of cutting off the fiber was initially thought to be 
a part of the concept and the prototype, but was not incorporated into the 
prototype due to the wish to keep this simple and keep the focus on 
experimenting with getting the printer to print with fiber in a reasonable 
way.  
During the building and testing it was found that the printer did not 
really need a new initiation of fiber path to commence a new direction or 
layer, and could keep printing with the same strand of fiber in theory from 
the start of the print to the end. This made the need for a cutting mechanism 
rather needless, as there would then only be a need for cutting the fiber 
when human interaction was required anyway: For initiating and removing 
a print and in case of printer failure or fiber spool runout. It therefore seems 
like it would not only be redundant in the early prototyping stages, but 
possibly also in the final model. The possibilities for the cutting of the fiber 
impacting the ability to print smaller details should be investigated though, 
as this would create a motive for incorporating a cutting mechanism after 
all.    
 
6.5.2 Wetting 
A method for pre-wetting could be considered implemented prior to the 
fiber’s entrance into the hot end. This would be relevant in case optimal 
adjustment of printing parameters would not be sufficient to optimise the 
wetting that is happening in the heater block. 
 
6.5.3 Inner shell printing 
The possibilities for printing up the inner shell to function as the socket 
mould should be investigated, as an alternative method for obtaining a 
print surface should be incorporated if the silicone print is not fitting for it.  
If this would be the case, it would be interesting to examine the 
possibilities for adding multimaterial print to the concept: In this scenario, 
the function of the flexible insert could be incorporated into the socket by 
printing in softer areas directly.   
Additionally, the option for multimaterial printing would add more 
room for customised decoration of the sockets, as patterns and aesthetic 
effects could be printed directly during the production.  
Also, If the technology was to be applied to other business areas, details 
of smaller size and higher complexity would often be a requirement for 
applicability. Since such small details are not handled well by the carbon 
fiber, it would make sense to print details with a pure plastic and let the 
fiber reinforce the rest of the part where it could be appropriately placed. 
Alternatively, the carbon fibers tendency to drag when doing small details 
could be counteracted by assistance from the other material: If the outline 
of the detail was printed first in regular thermoplastic, this would allow the 
fiber to be wrapped around this and hereby be placed in smaller roundings 
than otherwise possible. The same effect was seen while printing the 
reinforced dogbone: As soon as one circle at the end was placed, the bigger 
ones would be prevented from being dragged in.

6.5.4 Software 
The introduction of software fitting the production method would be 
required for its entry into service. The changes that would be required to 
implement in the currently used software would be: 
 
Path definition in order to dictate fiber direction. 
 
Omission of travel paths that leave a fiber track across the 
model.  
 
Different filling patterns for structures between the shell, as the 
currently used have a tendency to pull back too much.  
 
Algorithms to counter predictable pull-back of fiber at corners.

Conclusion 
An AM method for prosthesis socket manufacturing was suggested based 
on literature studies, field work and analysis.  
The key functions in the suggested concept were the ability to 3D print 
long fiber CFRT and to be able to place these fibers in the direction and 
location they were needed in. 
The realizability of the first part was tested by building a proof-of-concept 
prototype. This prototype succeeded in performing its task, showing that 
the first key function is an achievable technology.  
The second part was not tested practically, but was investigated 
theoretically and found to be feasible.  
It is therefore concluded that it is believable that the suggested technology 
for prosthesis socket production is likely to work in practise and be of 
benefit to prosthesis users and manufacturers.
