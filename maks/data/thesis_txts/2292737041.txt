Abstract
Background:
The point spread function (PSF) is an essential aspect of any magnetic resonance
imaging experiment. In most studies, the PSF is merely displayed as a by-product
of the experiment without much thought about why it appears so or if it could be
improved. Especially in rapid magnetic resonance spectroscopic imaging, good point
spread functions are required to accomplish proper representation of the underly-
ing distributions. Trajectories with non-constant maximal k-space coverage could
improve the PSF as the spectral information is more accurately represented.
Objective:
To design a model capable of accepting data reconstructed o of a Cartesian grid
and reconstructing spectroscopic images. Furthermore, to shape the point spread
function by minimizing the dierence between an ideal PSF and an approximated
one.
Methods:
A modied gridding algorithm suited for reconstruction of spectroscopic imaging
data have been implemented in MATLAB ®. The algorithm was thoroughly tested
with several dierent parameter settings and objects. Furthermore, the algorithm
was challenged with added noise, and compared to a reconstruction with a classical
trajectory shape. The gridding algorithm was used in the design of a model for
optimization.
Four optimization techniques were tested with four dierent cost
functions to attempt to locate global minima.
Results:
16 optimization experiments were performed that all ran to convergence. The re-
sulting point spread functions spanned multiple shapes and amplitudes, despite very
comparable cost function values. One PSF that matched the desired PSF in both
shape and amplitude was used to for testing. The reconstruction with these parame-
ters showed a clear improvement in the reconstruction when compared to a classical
trajectory.
Conclusion:
The diversity in the optimization results indicate that the techniques used for op-
timization are not ideal for this experiment.
Point spread functions that better
resemble the desired can probably be attained with more advanced methods. How-
ever, the immediate advantages of an alternative approach to spectroscopic imaging
is obvious and should be investigated in depth by future works.

Resume
Baggrund:
Punktspredningsfunktionen (PSF) er et essentielt aspekt af ethvert magnetisk res-
onans billeddannelseseksperiment. I de este studier, bliver PSF blot vist som et
sideprodukt uden yderligere tanker om hvorfor den ser ud som den gør eller om den
kunne forbedres. Specielt i hurtig magnetisk resonans spektroskopibilleddannelse er
gode punktspredningsfunktioner vigtige for at opnå en god repræsentation af den
underliggende fordeling. Trajektorier med ikke-konstant maksimal k-rums dækning
kan muligvis forbedre PSF fordi spektral information bliver mere nøjagtigt gengivet.
Formål:
At designe en model der er i stand til at modtage data optaget udenfor et Kartesisk
grid og rekonstruere spektroskopiske billeder. Endvidere at forme punktsprednings-
funktionen ved at minimere forskellen mellem en ønsket PSF og en tilnærmet en.
Metoder:
En modiceret gridding algoritme, tilpasset rekonstruktion af spektroskopiske billed-
data er blevet implementeret i MATLAB ®. Algoritmen er blevet grundigt testet
med adskillige forskellige parameterindstillinger og objekter. Derudover er algorit-
men blevet udfordret ved at tilføje støj og sammenlignet med en rekonstruktion
med en klassisk trajektorieform. Gridding algoritmen blev brugt til at designe en
optimeringsmodel. Fire optimeringsteknikker blev anvendt med re forskellige tab-
sfunktioner for at forsøge at lokalisere globale minima.
Resultater:
16 optimeringseksperimenter blev udført som alle endte i konvergens.
De resul-
terende punktspredningsfunktioner dækkede over mange forme og amplituder, på
trods af sammenlignelige tabsfunktionsværdier. En PSF der passede godt til den
ønskede PSF, både i form og amplitude blev brugt til afprøvning. Rekonstruktionen
med disse parametre viste en klar forbedring når den blev sammenlignet med en
rekonstruktion med et klassisk trajektorie.
Konklusion:
Diversiteten i optimeringsresultaterne indikerer at teknikkerne der er brugt til opti-
mering ikke er ideelle til dette eksperiment. Punktspredningsfunktioner som bedre
gengiver den ønskede kan formentlig opnås ved at anvende mere avancerede metoder.
Alligevel kan de øjeblikkelige fordele ved en alternativ tilgang til spektroskopisk
billeddannelse ses klart og bør undersøges til bunds i fremtidigt arbejde.

Introduction
In this introductory chapter, a brief history of the origin of magnetic resonance
(MR) and spectroscopy in MR will be given, followed by fundamental MR concepts
to ensure that the rest of the thesis can be understood properly. To justify the
usability of the investigations, a perspective to application in the medical eld will
be given as well. Furthermore, a thorough coverage of studies related to all aspects
of the thesis will be presented to account for the motivation and thought process
behind the project. The overall ideas of the thesis will be presented along with an
outline at the end of the chapter.
1.1
Historical overview
The phenomenon of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) relies on the observation
that certain nuclei possess nuclear spin angular momentum. In 1938, Rabi [1] be-
came the rst to describe and measure this for molecular beams. Bloch & Purcell [2,
3] expanded this observation to liquids and solids in 1946 thus laying the foundation
for modern magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Only nuclei with an odd number of
protons and/or neutrons exhibit this trait, making them magnetic and hence usable
in MR experiments. These nuclei will, if placed in a magnetic eld, precess around
the direction of the eld. Lauterbur [4] created the rst NMR image in 1973 by
recoding one-dimensional projections of an object and applying back-projection to
reconstruct the object. The same year, Manseld [5] demonstrated the application

Magnetic resonance spectroscopy has several uses in modern medicine, including aid
in diagnosing of a large number of cardiac diseases [7] (including heart failure and
ischaemic heart disease), measuring of hepatic lipid accumulation for assessment of
intestinal-failure-associated liver disease [8], to classify epilepsy in mesial temporal
sclerosis [9] as well as characterize cell membrane turnover, metabolism, cancer, is-
chaemia, and macrophage activity [10, 11] among many others.
In traditional spectroscopy, a single voxel is excited with gradients and the transver-
sal magnetization is recorded for that area as it decays over time. This technique
allows for obtaining frequency information about the chemical environment within
the voxel. By demodulating the frequency spectrum with respect to the precession

1.2
Central concepts
1.2.1
Precession
Magnetic nuclei placed in an external magnetic eld will precess around the direction
of the eld with a precession frequency given by
f = γ · B,
(1.2.1)
where B is the total magnetic eld (in tesla) experienced by the individual nucleus
and γ is the gyromagnetic ratio, approximately 42.6 MHz/T for protons. The orien-
tations of the nuclei are still in all directions, but because of the magnetic eld, there
is a macroscopic net magnetization in the direction of the magnetic eld which is the
basis for obtaining signals. The net magnetization is however small, only a few ppm
compared to full alignment. To obtain a measurable signal from this magnetization,
large quantities of the chosen nuclei must be present; as the human body contains
large amounts of water and lipids, 1H is usually chosen.
Three types of magnetic elds are usually distinguished between: the main eld, the
gradient eld, and the radio frequency (RF) eld, orthogonal to the main eld.
1.2.2
Main eld
The main eld, termed B0, is responsible for creating the net magnetization, of
strength M0, by partially aligning the nuclei, by denition along the z-direction
called the longitudinal direction. The main eld is created by a powerful magnet
that induces a homogeneous (ideally) eld across the entire object thereby making
all nuclei precess at the same frequency, given by Eq. 1.2.1. However, if the mag-
netic eld is not homogeneous throughout the object of interest, identical nuclei at
dierent positions will precess at dierent frequencies. Hence, by selectively chang-
ing the magnetic eld in certain areas, a more specic region of interest (ROI) can
be dened.
1.2.3
Gradient eld
Gradients, rst introduced in NMR by Manseld [5], are caused by coils capable of
creating small (relative to the main magnetic eld) changes in the total magnetic
eld experienced by the nuclei in the z-direction. By applying a linearly increasing
gradient in e.g.
the z-direction, the nuclei will precess at a slightly higher fre-
quency at increased z-positions.
Only nuclei on resonance during application of
radio frequency (RF) pulses will have their longitudinal magnetization transferred

to transversal magnetization that can be recorded. In the presence of linear gradient
elds, the individual nuclei will precess with a frequency given by
Bz = B0 + G · r,
(1.2.2)
where G = Gxi + Gyj + Gzk, · terms the inner product, and r = [x, y, z]. i, j and
k are the unit vectors in the x, y and z direction, respectively. Hence, if gradients
are active in all three directions, the total magnetic eld in the z-direction can be
expressed as

Gradients are used in conventional Fourier transform MRI to excite slices in various
directions (slice-selection gradients), to prepare a given k-space line for read-out
(phase-encoding gradients), and to move along this line during data acquisition
(read-out gradients) [12]. This allows for sampling one line in k-space per excitation
which is then replicated at dierent k-space positions until k-space is suciently
sampled. In rapid MR imaging, however, gradients are applied in a slightly dierent
manner; here, several gradients can be active during acquisition of data. In two-
dimensional spiral trajectory MRI for instance, both the x and y gradients are
applied during read-out leading to a faster acquisition as compared to a single line
[13]. Three-dimensional trajectories with all gradients active during read-out has
been proposed by several authors [5, 13, 14, 15] to increase speed in 3D imaging and
spectroscopy.
1.2.4
Radio-frequency eld
The net magnetization vector, M, in the direction of the main eld (and gradient
elds) is, however, almost impossible to measure directly. This is mainly because
the strength of the magnetization of the nuclei is several orders of magnitude smaller
than the B eld and thus the recordable signal is as well. However, as the B eld is
only non-zero in the longitudinal direction, a signal can be recorded by rotating the
nuclei orientations to a plane perpendicular to the B eld and measuring the signal
in this plane, the transversal plane. To transfer the magnetization of the nuclei of
interest, a radio-frequency eld, B1, is applied, tuned to the resonant frequency of
the specic nuclei. This pulse rotates the magnetization of the nuclei at interest by
an angle determined by the strength and duration of B1. After the excitation, the
B1 eld is turned o and the excited nuclei again precess around the B0 eld. These
rotating magnetization vectors induce an electromotive force (EMF), as described
by Faraday's law of induction. The recorded time signals are called free induction
decays (FIDs) and the amplitude of the recorded FID contains information of the
amount of nuclei at the spatial positions located by the gradients.

1.2.6
Chemical shift
Dierent substances placed in the same magnetic eld will precess at slightly dier-
ent frequencies. This phenomenon is referred to as the chemical shift and is caused
by shielding of the magnetic eld by the orbiting electrons. The eective eld felt

eld. Usually, the chemical shift is dened in parts per million (ppm) with respect

1.2.8
Point spread function
The point spread function (PSF) is an essential aspect of magnetic resonance ex-
periments where linear reconstruction is applied. It basically describes the response
of the system to a point source (the impulse response). If, for instance, an object
consisting of only a point source is scanned uniformly in a 2D magnetic resonance
experiment, the resulting reconstructed image will consist of this source, convolved
with the Fourier transform (FT) of the continuous sampling density. In the uniform,
rectangular case, the point will thus be convolved with a sinc function in each dimen-
sion (the Fourier transform of a boxcar function is a sinc function). The width of the
sinc function is inversely proportional to the width of the boxcar function, hence an
innitely large k-space coverage (which is not possible) will yield the original point
source without blurring as the innitely narrow sinc function is simply an impulse
response. This implies that a nite sampling will result in a contribution from one
pixel (or voxel) to neighbouring pixels (or voxels), called bleeding. Naturally, this is
an undesired phenomenon in MRI and should be sought minimized through proper
design of dierent elements in the experiment. The choice of trajectory and sam-
pling density during acquisition directly inuence the resulting PSF of the object
recorded; the density-weighting intuitively as it describes the sampling pattern and
the trajectory design as it aects the sampling possibilities in k-space.
1.2.9
Filtering
To enhance the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and thus improve image quality, ltering
is often applied.
Filtering in MR is usually performed in the sampling domain,
6
==PAGE:20==
(kx, ky) in traditional MRI, (k, t) in chemical shift imaging (CSI). In general, the
lter should be adapted to the sampling parameters to optimize the improvement.
Mathematically, it can be shown that in the presence of additive stochastic noise, as
is usually the assumption in MR experiments, a matched lter is the choice of linear
lter that maximizes the SNR. If for instance, the desired PSF is a Gaussian in
the x-dimension, the required sampling density in the k-dimension is a Gaussian as
well (see Section 2.1) and the optimal lter to implement this should be a Gaussian
function. The Gaussian lter is a low-pass lter, hence the high spatial frequencies
toward ±kmax are attenuated while SNR is improved as the relative noise amplitude
to signal amplitude is higher here.
1.3
Origin of magnetic resonance spectroscopy
Magnetic resonance spectroscopic and simultaneous spatial recordings are not a
new technique. It was described in 1973 by Manseld [5] and further explored by
many others, among others, Maudsley et al. [16]. In that paper, an experiment was
performed where a 31phosphorus-containing phantom was volumetrically scanned for
spectroscopy purposes using a slice selective pulse and recording the spectrum for
all points in each slice, one at a time. in 1984, Manseld [17] presented a method for
more rapid acquisition of spatial-spectral information, utilizing a technique normally
referred to as echo-planar spectroscopic imaging (EPSI). The technique was further
advanced by Twieg [18] among other authors in 1989. Twieg suggested recording
information along one spatial and the spectral dimension simultaneously by applying
an oscillating, trapezoidal gradient during readout.
This method also focused on the reduced requirements placed on the hardware
specications: maximum gradient amplitude and slew rate. Additionally, non-linear
spatial sampling was introduced at this stage, severely complicating the reconstruc-
tion procedure as simple Fourier transform will give rise to spectral and spatial
distortion because of this characteristic.
1.4
Spectroscopic imaging advances
Since the rst proposed methods, numerous advances have been discovered in mag-
netic resonance spectroscopy (MRS). A few of these will be discussed here for a
proper understanding of the thought process behind this thesis.
A sequence was implemented by Adalsteinsson et al. [19] in which a slab was excited
and an oscillating gradient in the slab-selection direction was used throughout the
readout period. This allowed for simultaneous recordings in both the kx, kz and kf
dimensions in the same time as a single kz slice in traditional single-slice excitation.
The loss in SNR was reported to be approximately 10 % compared to that method.
One issue created by this method is that data samples do not fall on a Cartesian
grid, thus creating the need for gridding which will be discussed in Chapter 2.
Posse et al. [20] presented a fast echo-planar spectroscopic imaging (EPSI) method
using surface coils, capable of recording two-dimensional (2D) spectroscopy data in
64 seconds as compared to approximately 30 minutes for traditional CSI. The draw-
7
==PAGE:21==
back were a spatial SNR loss of 10-20 % for a phantom but a decrease of a factor
5-6 for an in vitro experiment. The decrease is, however, a result of the shorter
acquisition time. Despite this, the same spectral resolution and artifacts, or lack
thereof, was attained in either experiment, allowing for rapid acquisition if only the
spectra are required or a decreased SNR in the image is acceptable.
A comparison study of fast spectroscopic imaging methods was performed by Pohmann
et al. [21]. The sensitivity - the ratio of SNR and the square root of the acquisi-
tion time - was used as the performance measure for the dierent sequences. In
summary, a lot of factors inuences this factor and no method attained the same
sensitivity as the conventional CSI. However, fast methods can still be advantageous
as they provide possibilities not present in CSI, i.e. fast imaging to prevent blurring
from motion artifacts but also give rise to new challenges; for instance demands on
hardware and possible artifacts due to eddy currents.
Adalsteinsson et al. [22] presented a sequence, commonly known as the "stack of
spirals", where the spatial frequency components are sampled by slice selection in
one dimension and a spiral-based trajectory in the two other dimensions. The spiral
trajectories were designed as a function of the position in the third dimension allow-
ing for faster acquisitions, eectively resulting in a elliptical or spherical sampling
distribution in the spatial domain. This method also allowed for suppression of wa-
ter and lipids which is advantageous in spectroscopic imaging, but again oering a
trade-o between acquisition time and SNR.
Several other sequence designs have been proposed, all with various advantages and
disadvantages; Cunningham et al. [23] designed a variant of EPSI, yback EPSI,
where data were only acquired during movement in increasing k-space direction,
unlike acquisition in both directions in normal EPSI. This method attained large
k-space coverage with short acquisition (compared to phase-encoding techniques)
on the cost of an SNR penalty. The study did not compare to normal EPSI, but
expected a loss in SNR because of time spent not sampling but instead simplifying
the reconstruction process; Gurney et al. [24] suggested using multiple cone-shaped
trajectories to cover k-space, all starting at the origin of k-space. Point spread func-
tions and reconstructions were compared with 3D projection reconstruction (PR),
the new method providing better SNR and better PSF performance; Uribe et al. [15]
suggested a sequence where concentric spheres are used to sample k-space, uniformly,
in all three spatial dimensions. Additionally, k-space was intentionally undersam-
pled to speed up acquisition in some experiments. The method was compared to
stack of spirals (SOS), with the same number of interleaves, yielding a narrower
spectroscopic PSF, even when undersampling was applied. The two latter methods
also rely on gridding for reconstruction.
These methods all use manually designed trajectory shapes with no guarantee of
optimality with respect to acquisition time, SNR or point spread function.
1.5
Computer-derived trajectory design
More recently, several techniques have been proposed where the trajectory through
k-space is designed by algorithms rather than intuition and (competent) guesses.
These does, intuitively, not follow a Cartesian grid throughout k-space and hence,

Sabat et al. [14] proposed a technique where generic algorithms were used to op-
timize a number of trajectories covering a sphere in k-space, subject to gradient
hardware restrictions. For each trajectory, a new excitation is required thus induc-
ing limitations to acquisition time, 100 and 200 trajectories was presented as those
yielded optimal results. The point spread function during ideal conditions and sub-
ject to o-resonance eect was compared to that of SOS, yielding a narrower main
lobe.
Pipe & Zwart [13] presented a numerical algorithm for automatically designing spiral
trajectories in both two and three dimensions, limited by hardware characteristics.
These analytically best utilized the hardware constraints to create the fastest k-
space coverage.
An algorithm for automatically constructing the time-optimal gradient waveform
for a given k-space trajectory was presented by Lustig et al. [25], once again sub-
ject to constraints. This methods allows for easy conversion between theoretically
designed desired sampling trajectories to specic gradient characteristics a scanner
can utilize.
Recently, Chauert et al. [26] proposed an algorithm capable of constructing gradi-
ent waveforms and trajectories dierent from earlier described techniques. The main
dierence in this method is that it focuses on the sampling density. Earlier methods
simply designed the fastest trajectory through k-space with no regards to sampling
density. Chauert argues that this will lead to heavier sampling in areas with high
curvature of the k-space path due to time-constant sampling. The parametrization
suggested, creates trajectories with less curvature but does not utilize the maximal
characteristics of the hardware.
1.6
Density-weighted acquisition
An important parameter during data acquisition is the choice of sample function.
This function is commonly designed as a function of time or location in k-space;
the earlier mentioned studies (save [25]) all used a time-constant sampling. How-
ever, as the PSF is directly coupled with the sampling distribution, via the Fourier
transform, designing this in a specialized way can inuence the resulting spectrum
or image. Tsai & Nishimura [27] implemented a variable-density sampling for both
a 2D spiral trajectory and a conventional 2D phase-encoding trajectory. The rea-
soning was that most of the energy is stored close to the origin of k-space, hence
by sampling more densely here, a better reconstruction could be obtained. Both of
these sampling densities reduced aliasing in the reconstruction despite violating the
Nyquist sample criterion in the outer parts of k-space.
Another algorithm was developed by Greiser & von Kienlin [28] where three sam-
pling methods of a 2D k-space were compared: uniform sampling, (Hanning) density
weighting (DW), and acquisition weighting (AW), all with an equal number of ac-
quisitions; the latter sampling some points multiple times. Both DW and AW were
more densely sampled in the center of k-space, following the same logic as [27]. The
density-weighted sampling provided the PSF with least contamination to neighbour-

ing areas without reducing the SNR. The DW and AW methods were compared on
an in vivo experiment providing similar results, although DW is capable of increas-
ing the FOV without aliasing, unlike the AW.
Geier et al. [29] demonstrated a technique where DW was used in combination with
parallel acquisition which was tested on both a phantom and an in vivo recording.
The DW attained improved SNR of 17-34 % and reduced Gibbs ringing compared to
uniform sampling. Furthermore, a PSF with smaller sidelobes was calculated from
the phantom object.
An approach was suggested where DW was used in a turbo spin-echo (TSE) exper-

that method the DW attained an average SNR increase of 43 %. Furthermore, the
relative side lobe amplitude was reduced from 40.3 % to 6.4 % but the width of
the main lobe was increased by a factor 1.4 thus creating the need for an increased
k-space coverage of 1.4 times in the readout direction, compared to what is required
by uniform sampling to obtain identical spatial resolution.
1.7
Point spread function considerations
The studies described previously all discuss new or improved techniques in various
parameters. However, these focus on the resulting reconstructed images or signals
and merely visually display the point spread function (PSF) of the techniques, if at
all. The PSF notably eects the reconstruction and is thus an important parameter
to consider in every MRI experiment. One study by Tan & Zheng, [31] optimized the
PSF using linear matrix inequality (LMI) constraints. The optimization criterion
was a trade-o between width of main lobe and amplitude of side lobes for the PSF.
The resulting reconstructed phantom clearly showed improvements both in terms of
visual inspections and was also numerically closer to the original object. The point
spread function in this study, and traditionally, is described in the spatial domain,
henceforth referred to as the spatial point spread function. This determines how
the reconstructed image will be distorted compared to the original object. In this
paper however, the distortion of the chemical shift spectrum is of higher interest.
This measure will be termed the spectral point spread function (SPSF) and the
combination of the two, the spectral-spatial PSF.
1.8
Outline
From the study of related works within (or closely related to) this eld, it should
be obvious that focus on shaping the point spread function is an area only vaguely
described and explored. Multiple factors inuence the PSF, including the trajec-
tory, the sampling function, the ltering applied, and the gridding parameters, to
mention a few. The aim of this paper is rst and foremost to shape the point spread
function by optimizing a model that includes these parameters to yield the desired
sampling distribution and PSF, independent of metabolite type. The model will use
the recorded samples at arbitrary locations, grid these and compute the reconstruc-
tion of the objects.

2.5
Trajectory design
The trajectory describes the manner k-t-space is traversed during sampling of an MR
signal in spectroscopic imaging. Unlike traditional MRI, the trajectory is less exible
due to the fact that time moves forward continuously during sampling, hence limit-
ing the possibilities of properly sampling the domain. For instance, if the recorded
object is a 2D boxcar function, the Fourier components that are sampled, constitute
a two-dimensional sinc function. To properly reconstruct this object, the compo-
nents, mainly located about the origin of k-t-space, need to be sampled adequately.
This issue puts some demands on the hardware and design of trajectory, among
other factors. Additionally, the sinc function is located at both positive and nega-
tive values, thus for the rectangular example, negative t-values must be sampled as
16
==PAGE:30==
well.
The trajectory type used in this thesis was limited to consist of sinusoidal waveforms
in the k-dimension, either with constant amplitude (meaning maximal k-space cov-
erage), or with decreasing amplitude, implying that smaller areas of k-space are
covered as more time passes. This limitation was included to simplify the model as
an innite number of possible trajectories exists. The sinusoidal trajectory form is a
commonly used type of trajectory as it has some desirable properties: the demands
on gradient hardware (maximum amplitude and slew rate) are low compared to
other types and optimization-wise it is characterized by few parameters: oscillation
frequency, amplitude (often 1) and phase factor (often 0).
The motivation for decaying the trajectory arises from knowledge of the behaviour
of the tissue recorded. In a magnetic eld, the amplitude of the metabolites is known
to decrease at at rate of e−t/T ∗
2 depending on magnetic eld variation and chemi-
cal environment. Typical T2 values for relevant metabolites (N-acetyle aspartate,
choline, creatine) is in the range of 200-450 ms, with T ∗
2 in the vicinity of 100 ms.
However, T2 for lipids is around 85 ms with T ∗
2 distinguishably shorter. The extremes
of k-space (±kmax) are useful for reconstructing fast changing objects as ±kmax
covers the maximal spatial frequencies recorded. Lipids are often a major challenge
in spectroscopy as extra cranial lipids will be present in the eld of view. These
typically display a small extent in space but a large extent in frequency whereas the
metabolites are well-dened in frequency but more diversely spread in space. To be
able to properly image both the spatial extent of the lipids and the frequency extent
of the metabolites, large k-space coverage is required when lipids are present, but
good low-frequency coverage is required to localize the metabolites. This behaviour
justies sampling widely in k-space initially, while lipids are present, and sampling
near the center after the lipid signals have decayed away. Furthermore, sampling
extremes of k-space after long time has passed is known to be non-ideal in terms of
SNR as close to no information is present here. An example of a sinusoidal trajec-
tory multiplied with a decaying factor in the k-dimension of e−10·t can be seen in
Figure 2.1.

2.10
Optimization
The model explained thus far depends on certain parameters (convolution width,
sampling frequency, oscillation frequency etc.). Instead of assigning arbitrary val-
ues (as is done in many studies), optimization algorithms were implemented to nd
better parameters for these. Three optimization algorithms were implemented: Ge-
netic algorithms, pattern search and constrained non-linear optimization. The basic
principles of these algorithm will be elaborated upon below.
2.10.1
Genetic algorithms
Genetic algorithms (GA) are search heuristics that imitate the process of natural
selection. They are suitable for nding a local or global minimum, both for smooth
and non-smooth cost function and model parameters. An advantage with using ge-
netic algorithms is that they can account for some of the variables being discrete
(i.e. number of interleaves as integers or window type as if-then-else reasoning).
As initiation, a large population of random variable values (within specied bound-
aries) is dened from a stochastic uniform distribution. These points constitute the
rst generation, and each parameter set is called a parent. This population should
be large enough to roughly cover the solution space given by the boundaries. For
this generation, a partition is chosen based on the cost function to breed the next
generation. The next generation is then created from this group of well-tting par-
ents using three dierent methods: crossover, mutation, and elite selection.
Crossover mimics the breeding of children from its parents and as such, children
are created by mixing a pair of random parents and randomly choosing each vari-
able from one of the parents.
Mutation mimics the natural evolution by applying changes to a single parent to
create a new child. The changes are drawn randomly from a (scaled) normal distri-
bution and the parent is chosen at random.
Elite selection consists of allowing the best parents, i.e. those with lowest cost func-
tion, to survive unto the next generation. This is an essential part of the process as
20
==PAGE:34==
a minimum or near-minimum will not be removed if already found.
The children created from these three methods then constitute the parents of the
next generation and the process starts over. The population remains constant over
all generations.
2.10.2
Pattern search
Pattern search (PS) is a type of numerical optimization techniques that can be used
to locate a single local or global minimum if such exists. Pattern search does not
require gradients and can be applied to non-dierentiable and non-continuous func-
tions and is suitable for nding solutions to non-smooth functions. The technique
applied in this study is generalized pattern search (GPS) with the poll method pos-
itive basis 2N.
Pattern search starts at an initial position which can either be user specied or
chosen at random. From this point it evaluates a mesh of 2N locations, N being
the number of variables, around the point corresponding to the unit vectors for each
variable and the negative of these, multiplied with a gain factor which is usually set
to 1 at initiation. Two dierent evaluation techniques can then be used: rst poll
or complete poll. In rst poll, the function value at each new location (unit vector
added to the current point) is evaluated in turn; the rst point that reduces the cost
function is chosen as the new point. In complete poll, all new 2N cost functions are
evaluated and the lowest is chosen as the new point.
In both methods, if a new location is found that lowers the cost function, the gain
factor is doubled and the search is repeated at the new location. However, if none of
the 2N points would reduce the cost function, the current location is kept and the
gain factor halved. If then a new point is found, the gain factor is again doubled.
This up-scaling and down-scaling of the search area makes the algorithm able to
both move past local minima but also locate the minimum within a region of close
to evenly good parameter values.
2.10.3
Constrained non-linear optimization
Constrained non-linear optimization (CNLO) is a general technique to solve a large
number of optimization problems. It is suited for nding a number of local minima
as well as a single global minimum.
The technique implemented here basically
implements an algorithm to nd the local minimum using gradients from a random
starting point and then repeats the experiment with multiple starting points to nd
the best solution.
The technique utilizes the Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno
(BFGS) algorithm, a common technique in the family of Quasi-Newton approaches,
to estimate the Hessian matrix (second-order partial derivative) of the Lagrangian
and nds the local minimum within a the basin of attraction of the starting point.
The gradients are calculated according to the method of steepest descent, given by

3.4
Reconstruction of spectroscopic image data
From the demanding aspects of reconstructing squares in CSI, focus is now moved
to more realistic cases, such as those that would be recorded in actual spectroscopy
experiments. Again, the area of attention is to validate that the model reconstructs
the correct distributions under various situations. Typically in CSI, the pixel (or
voxel) sizes are larger in the spatial dimensions corresponding to fewer pixels (or
voxels) and has more samples in the time dimension. This is done to provide a
sucient bandwidth to represent the spectra after reconstruction.
In the following, the sinusoidal decay corresponds to the constant chosen for the
narrowing trajectory to decrease by, i.e. the c in e−c·t. Unless otherwise stated, the
T ∗
2 for the metabolites reconstructed were set as 100 ms. The full reconstruction
of Lorentzian and Gaussian-shaped distributions are dicult to interpret visually
which is why lines through the objects are displayed for most of the validation ex-
periments.
The rst experiment consisted of attempting to reconstruct a Lorentzian-distributed
metabolite signal present at a single x-location, x = 0, and shifted 20 f-values. The
objective was to investigate the eect of bleeding and ltering when using narrowing
trajectories. Ideally, signal should only be present in one x-location, but as the grid-
ding algorithm spreads out information when convolving the samples with the kernel,
some bleeding was expected. The parameters for recording and reconstruction can
be seen in Table 3.2. Inspecting Figure 3.7, the imminent eect of ltering is not
clear; it appears that amplitude is simply slightly decreased when ltering is used.
This was intuitively expected as the ltering operation attenuates some information
to improve SNR. From Figure 3.8 however, the result of ltering is more clearly
seen: frequencies far from the desired band are attenuated, an important property
as aliasing can occur in these regions if sampling is not performed suciently. Addi-
tionally, it can be seen that some information has bled to the neighbouring region.
The average signal amplitude in the neighbouring region was 33.45 % of what was in
the signal region (33.85 % at the peak). This is obviously undesirable, but can likely
be reduced by optimizing the parameters for the model or by using some threshold
to distinguish signal from bleeding.

Noise addition
To challenge the model and make the case more realistic, an experiment was per-
formed where the recorded signal was added noise. In this test, a square object of
5 pixels with signal was created in the x-direction, all with a Lorentzian distribu-
tion in the f-direction, shifted 20 f-values. The noise were drawn randomly from a
standard normal distribution and two samples from this distribution was added to
each recorded signal sample; one was added to the real part, one was added to the
imaginary. The reconstruction and sampling parameters used were identical to those
in Section 3.5 (Table 3.3) although the object was slightly dierent. The mean of
the absolute amplitude of the recorded signal was 0.49, the same value for noise was
1.24. The horizontal lines with signal of the reconstructed object with and without
noise can be seen in Figure 3.10. As can be seen from the un-zoomed image, Figure

amplitude of the noise components is approximately 2.5 times that of the signal.

reconstruct the metabolite signal, almost as well as without noise. This indicates
that if the frequency spectrum of the signal is known, the model reconstructs as
expected, even when contaminated with noise. When challenged with a lesser noise
component, drawn from the same distribution but multiplied with a factor of 0.2
(mean absolute amplitude of 0.25), the reconstructed spectrum closely resembles
the reconstruction without noise, see Figure A.3. The mean amplitude of the noise
was approximately half of the recorded signal, and almost no visible eect on the
reconstruction was seen.

at each Cartesian point, the window should be sampled densely enough that the
curve between any two adjacent sample points is roughly linear. A metabolite with
a signal value at a single x-location was reconstructed using pre-sampled window of
length 8, 16, 32, 64, and 128. The reconstruction and sampling parameters are listed

windows of increasing length only have minor inuence on the reconstruction. As
the number of kernel samples is not a limiting parameter in the computational speed,

Choosing proper termination criteria for the optimization algorithms is a trade-
o. Fundamentally, the algorithms should run long enough to reach convergence
and diverse enough to roughly cover the solution space. Concurrently, they should
be delimited to reduce the load on software and hardware.
The choices for the
algorithms were made according to these aspects. For genetic algorithms, it was
chosen to use an initial population of 200. In each new generation, 10 children are
chosen from elite selection. Of the remaining 190, 80 % (152) are bred from crossover,
20 % (38) are created from mutations. The algorithm was set to terminate when the
change in minimum cost function was lower than a threshold, chosen as 10−6, or a
maximum number of generations was reached, here set to 100. For pattern search,
the termination criteria were set as mesh size lower than 10−6 or a maximum of 700
iterations. Both rst poll and complete poll was attempted in this study, with initial
gain of 1 and initial conditions specied in Table 4.1. The constrained non-linear
optimization was run 50 times with uniformly stochastic initial points.

5
Discussion
In this chapter, the previously obtained results will be analysed and assessed in
relation to the impact on CSI experiments. The decisions and discoveries of certain
impacting factors in the models will also be discussed. Furthermore, suggestions for
improvements of model specications and future studies in this eld will be given.
5.1
Convolution kernel
The reconstruction with both Gaussian and Kaiser-Bessel windows show that choos-
ing either of these only has marginal eect on the nal result. This implies that the
choice of window function (between these two) is not of great importance, as long as
the relevant parameters are set appropriately. These are found during optimization,
hence either can be chosen if optimization is run properly. Several optimization
results were produced with both windows and while the Gaussian window showed
slight improvements for two cost functions, the Kaiser-Bessel window showed an
overall (minor) improvement when compared to the Gaussian. Therefore, the Kaiser-
Bessel window was applied in the displayed optimization in this thesis which also
agrees with what is commonly discovered in the relevant literature as covered pre-
viously. However, the Gaussian window is capable of providing good results as well
and could be considered an alternative to the commonly used Kaiser-Bessel window.
5.2
Reconstruction of squares
To validate the model, squares of dierent locations and sizes were initially re-
constructed. While squares are not representative of what is typically imaged in
CSI, they provide conceptual insight in the mechanisms of the gridding algorithm.
Furthermore, squares are burdensome to reconstruct due to the presence of many
Fourier components, also at negative t-values. Because of these factors, extremely
high sampling frequencies are required to record all relevant Fourier components.
A sampling frequency of 300 kHz might not be feasible in experiments, but neither
is reconstruction of square objects in CSI. These were mainly included to provide
intuitive understanding of the reconstruction and eectiveness of the model as the
amplitude across each square is constant and the eect of attenuation correction is
evident.
56
==PAGE:70==
5.3
Reconstruction of spectroscopic imaging data
The eect of applying a Gaussian image lter in the k-t domain has been demon-
strated. Intuitively, this low pass-lter attenuates high frequencies thus lowering
the eective spatial resolution as described by Skoch et al. [36]. However, as the
relevant information is centered around the origin of k-space, the SNR is improved
by this operation. Additionally, the ltering has an eect on the eective PSF, as
the resulting sample density is altered by this procedure.
As described, one of the main reasons for deciding upon a narrowing trajectory
is to be able to localize both metabolites in the frequency domain. From the com-
parison of a constant amplitude trajectory and a decreasing amplitude one, the
reconstructed spectrum is visibly better with a narrowing before the parameters are
optimized, indicating the attractiveness of this type.
Furthermore, the model has proven very robust when adding noise to the recordings.
When the noise amplitude was more than twice the average value of the recorded sig-
nal, the spectrum was still clearly drawn; with noise of amplitude half of the signal,
the eect was barely visible. The model was also able to distinguish between signal
from two dierent metabolites, even when the relative frequency shift between those
became minimal and noise was added. As the frequency changes between metabo-
lites in CSI are of small magnitude, this suggests that the model will perform well
with in vivo data.
One experiment that could give insight in the reconstruction of realistic data would
be to model actual distributions of lipids and metabolites from in vivo data and use
as input parameters for the model. This experiment would very closely resemble
what is actually recorded and might contribute with knowledge about the subject.
This is beyond the time frame of this thesis, though.
5.4
Optimization results
In general, the optimization algorithms arrived at very dierent results. This phe-
nomenon is probably caused by one of two issues or a combination hereof: either the
cost function may not have a clear global minimum within the boundaries or the
optimization algorithms were unable to nd such minimum. As the solution space
for the cost function is seven-dimensional, it can not easily be visualized to conrm
or disprove this possibility. The alternative, that the optimization algorithms are
unsuited for this specic problem is another option.
The pattern search heuristic clearly had some diculties with changing the pa-
rameters. In several of the optimal results, the technique simply kept initial values
or added/subtracted a small number from this and converged rapidly. This im-
plies that the solution space is not explored in depth and better solutions could be
missed. The diculties observed could arise from the dierent scaling of the pa-
rameters. With a large gain factor, for instance 64, only two of the parameters can
57
==PAGE:71==
be evaluated upon as the remaining parameters does not cover a range this broad.
Adjusting the parameters to the same order of magnitude by introducing scaling
factors in the model might aid the pattern search to better explore the solution
space.
The CNLO algorithm generally attained low cost function values (second-lowest
for both SD optimizations, lowest for both PSF optimizations).
However, upon
inspection of the computed PSF's, three of the four optimal parameter sets result
in point spread functions with very large contribution across the spatial locations.
This will cause bleeding and lead to a point source in space being reconstructed as
nearly constant across the spatial domain. Especially for the mean squared error
this phenomenon is experienced. This intuitively harmonizes for mean squared error
optimization as the number of samples in the spatial dimension is lower than in the
spectral dimension. The CNLO algorithm was run 50 times; expanding this num-
ber might accomplish better results, but as this algorithm is computationally heavy,
it was not performed. More powerful computers should be used for such experiment.
The GA generally arrived at solutions with roughly the same shape and ampli-
tude as the desired PSF, indicating that this method is suited for the experiment.
The reconstruction of metabolites with dierent T ∗
2 times and added noise clearly
shows that the reconstruction in both spectral and spatial dimensions resemble the
reference signal. The GA was run with a population of 200 for 100 generations. Like
the CNLO algorithm, these numbers could be expanded but was not performed here
due to the computational burden it leads to.
Many of the reconstructed sampling densities show discrepancies with the ideal one
at or around t = 0. This is not unexpected as the sampling distribution in the time
domain essentially goes from 0 (at negative t-values) to a certain value at t = 0,
comparable to a step function. This discontinuity is dicult to approximate and is
thus an area where attention could be put. One way of overcoming the discontinuity
could be to sample negative t-values, using a sequence similar to the spin-echo ap-
proach, which would likely aid the sampling density approximation. The trajectory
could then go from narrow at -tmax, to wide at t = 0, to narrow again at tmax
to be able to properly reconstruct both spectral distributions and spatial locations.
This is, however, merely an idea and has not been pursued further.
In general, the optimal parameter sets exhibit a relationship between decay factor
and convolution width. Both of these factors are either relatively high or relatively
low. This makes perfect sense as a high decay factor causes the trajectory to narrow
rapidly and only sample high k-values initially. To compensate for this, the con-
volution width is enlarged to give information in these areas and vice versa. This
property aids the believe that the optimization is running accordingly. A set of
parameters that resembled the desired PSF was chosen for comparison with a nor-
mal non-decaying trajectory. The performance in the experiments clearly improved
when using the narrowing trajectory for the chosen parameters. This suggests that
decaying trajectories in CSI experiments have practical value and could be applied
58
==PAGE:72==
for better results.
5.5
Reections on model design
In the process of designing the model, simplicity was a key factor. This was pri-
oritized in order to make physical interpretation of the parameters settings as well
as the optimization results easier. Furthermore, the computational burden of nd-
ing the optimal parameters is lighter when fewer parameters are used. Expressing a
model with only 7 variables obviously limits the obtainable results as the complexity
is low. However, the experiments have shown that the optimization algorithms were
able to nd combinations of parameters that gave better results. Two extensions
of the model could be to put fewer restrictions on the trajectories and sampling
densities.
5.5.1
Trajectory design
The trajectory type was limited to a sinusoidal function of time with a decay factor.
The advantage of this function type is that the slew rate of the gradients can be
kept low, which ensures that eddy currents will be minimized. This reasoning was
also used when choosing upper boundaries for oscillation frequency; the higher the
frequency, the more eect eddy currents will have on the recordings.
The chosen narrowing function was exponentially decaying. There is no guaran-
tee that the exponentially decaying trajectory is the best function to narrow the
k-space coverage. Other types of decaying functions might provide a sample density
closer to the desired, such as one side of a Gaussian distribution. This investigation
is not performed in this study but would be an interesting addition to future explo-
ration of the eld. Even if decaying the trajectory is not advantageous, as this study
suggests, a normal trajectory traversing k-t-space can still benet from optimizing
the reconstruction parameters instead of arbitrarily assigning these.
Obviously, the sinusoidal trajectories can be replaced by more advanced types, but
not only will this complicate the optimization, it will also limit the understanding of
the optimal parameters. One option to expand the possible trajectories is to allow
more interleaves. This was originally implemented in the model but left out due to
the indisputable advantages of interleaves: more signal is present at the beginning
and total sampling time is usually longer due to time used for exciting the tissue,
spoiling, and suppression among other factors. Longer sampling time leads to better
SNR, hence for the experiments to be comparable, the sampling time should be kept
constant. One possibility for improving the reconstruction would be to keep the to-
tal acquisition time constant but allow more interleaves. A discussion of this aspect
is given by Hanson [37]. In this, Hanson argues that the sampling time should be
of the same order of magnitude as T ∗
2 to optimize SNR for a given sampling time.
Thus, if the metabolites to record are known (as well as their T ∗
2 ), the model can be
designed to allow several interleaves for a gain in SNR.
59
==PAGE:73==
5.5.2
Sampling function
The sampling function could also be designed to a more unrestricted form. The
time-constant sampling was chosen for the simple noise distribution that follows as
well as being dened by a single variable, ∆t. More advanced sampling methods
might attain even better results. For instance, the sampling could be a function of
both k-position and time such that lower absolute k-positions are sampled heavier
as is commonly applied in DW acquisition. This might achieve the same as decaying
the trajectory, that the center of k-space is denser sampled to better depict the low
spatial frequencies but could be explored in depth.
In this study, the sampling time for each optimization experiment was set to the
next power of 2 of Fs/2. The next power was chosen for the computationally ef-
cient Fourier transform it provides and the value was chosen to ensure that all
signals were recorded for at least half a seconds. This, of course, leads to dierent
number of samples for dierent sampling frequencies and dierent sampling times.
However, when including Fs as an optimization parameter, restricting the number of
samples to a single value severely limits the changeability of the sampling frequency.
For more comparative experiments where the reconstruction time is not of major
importance, constant sampling time could be implemented. This can complicate
visualizations however, as peaks of e.g. Lorentzian distributions may not be located
at a single value.
5.5.3
Other parameters
Some of the model parameters were kept constant during optimization for dierent
reasons. The eect of changing these within reasonable limits should not eect the
model greatly. These parameters are listed in Table 4.2 and include the size of the
reconstruction grid, the kernel type and number of kernel samples, and the decay of
the signal, T ∗
2 . Each of these have been tested at dierent values during the study
without major impact on the reconstruction for reasonable levels. Hence, it can
be justied to keep these at a certain level without invalidating the results. For
instance, the T ∗
2 was kept constant at 100 ms during optimization but investigated
at 50, 100, 120 and 500 ms with the optimal parameter set to test the robustness
of the model. Regardless of T ∗
2 -values and frequency displacement, the model was
able to adequately reconstruct the spectra.
Additionally, normalizing the ideal and approximated sampling densities was at-
tempted to investigate if any benet could come from this. The cost function values
(after accounting for alterations in magnitude) and the reconstructions showed no
improvement after this normalization (results not included).
The relative convolution width in t-direction as compared to k was included be-
cause of the dierence in grid size in each dimension. The decision to include the
factor was intuitive to allow the model to maintain proper sampling in areas with
little curvature and hence large distance between samples. This adjustment to the
traditional gridding algorithm was justied by allowing the lower boundary of the
60
==PAGE:74==
convolution factor to include 1, i.e. same convolution width in both dimensions. If
adding the factor was disadvantageous, the models should simply nd an optimal
value of 1. 15 of the 16 optimization algorithms used a relative convolution fac-
tor larger than 1, thus indicating that this parameter is a useful extension to the
gridding algorithm in CSI experiments.
5.6
Reections on cost function design
The specication of the cost function is an essential parameter in any optimiza-
tion experiment. Very dierent results can arise from changing this function, even
slightly. Therefore, the reasoning behind choosing a cost function should be thought
through. In this thesis, two dierent types of cost functions were used, the maxi-
mum squared error and the mean squared error between a desired function and an
approximated one. The motivation from deciding on this particular type originates
from the focus on shaping the resulting point spread function. Squaring the error
penalizes large dierences which is desirable as major deviations in the SD or PSF
lead to unwanted signal in areas where none is expected. Minimizing either the max-
imum or mean squared dierence ensures the optimization algorithms approximate
the desired signal.
In CSI experiments, several performance measures are commonly evaluated upon
and could thus be used as cost functions or as part hereof. Some of these are spa-
tial PSF, spectral PSF, SNR (or CNR), hardware limitations, and reconstruction
artifacts. Artifacts can, for example, arise from rapid switching of the gradients
causing eddy currents that distort the signal or aliasing from inadequate k-t-space
sampling. The chosen cost functions combine spatial and spectral PSF, as the entire
spectral-spatial PSF is sought approximated. As the size of the reconstruction is
larger in the spectral dimension than the spatial, the mean squared error focusses
on minimizing deviations in the spectral PSF; the maximum squared error present a
more abstract weighting between the two point spread functions. Depending on the
experiment success criterion, weighting factors can be included to alter this prop-
erty. If, for example, exact localization of the metabolites, lipids, etc. is of essence,
the deviations in spatial PSF can be penalized.
In this study, the mentioned artifacts are not directly used in the cost function, but
instead treated by setting realistic boundaries for the parameters, to minimize the
eect of these artifacts as well as not to put major demands on hardware. This is
carried out by limiting the oscillation frequency of the gradients, limiting the sam-
pling frequency and performing low-pass ltering in the k-t-space.
Extending the cost function to include other parameters or a dierent weighting of
these is a natural next step but is not investigated further this study. However, it
is an important quality of the model that it can be personalized to accommodate
dierent situations, depending on what is of interest by adjusting the cost function
accordingly.

5.7
Extension to more dimensions
A model capable of reconstructing arbitrarily sampled data unto a Cartesian grid
without severe distortions of the underlying distributions has been implemented.
The model has been validated with multiple settings and parameter combinations
to ensure that data are reconstructed properly. The model was limited to one spa-
tial and one spectral dimension for two main reasons: computational demands and
visualization purposes.
Mathematically and programming-wise, the extension to more dimensions is straight-
forward and could easily be implemented; from the description of gridding and
density compensation this should be obvious. However, the time required for recon-
struction and optimization increases proportionally with the number of dimensions
and three or four dimensions would put serious demands on the hardware and soft-
ware utilized. Furthermore, displaying e.g. the point spread functions or sampling
densities in more than two dimensions is non-trivial and dicult to interpret from
visual inspections. As the purpose of this study was to bring attention to the eld
of shaping the PSF to specic requirements, the conceptual implementation in two
dimensions suce to show this point. The same types of trajectory and sampling
function can easily be implemented in more dimensions, though a gradient in each
spatial dimension is required to traverse that direction.
5.8
Combination with other techniques and
physical application
One of the main challenges with recording volumetric spectroscopic imaging data
over large areas is the need for homogeneity of the main magnetic eld, B0. This
is attained primarily by shimming the magnetic eld. As a large area of tissue is
recorded in a single excitation, greater demands are put on the quality of the shim-
ming. Insucient shimming can lead to incomplete water or lipid suppression or as
de-phasing of signal because of the variability of the local eld [19]. This can again
result in spatially-dependent distortions in the reconstructed object.
Another issue origins from the large water signal present in all tissue. As the con-
centration of the water is several orders of magnitude larger than the metabolite
signals, the issue of suppressing or otherwise limit the eect of the water should be
considered. The resonance frequency of the water diers slightly from that of the
metabolites. Therefore, sequences for water saturation such as frequency-selective
RF pulses to saturate the water protons or likewise could aid in eliminating the
water signal for better metabolite spectra.
The proposed model expresses the ability to rapidly collect CSI data and still pro-
vide good reconstruction of the data. The model should not necessarily stand alone
but can be combined with other well-established methods. As the model can provide
accurate spectral results with good sets of parameters, combining the shaping of the
PSF with general linear models (GLM) or similar techniques for estimating metabo-
lite concentrations is an obvious development path in fast spectroscopic imaging.
62
==PAGE:76==
Furthermore, as hyper-polarized MRI is a rapidly emerging eld, techniques to best
cover all relevant Fourier domain components are of high interest. Implementing
optimized methods like the one described in this thesis can possibly assist the cre-
ation of even better CSI than with hyper-polarization by itself.
In brain CSI, the undesired lipid signal mainly origins from extra cranial lipids.
Instead of attempting to properly locate these in both location and frequency con-
tent, suppressing the lipids by other means might improve the reconstruction of
metabolites and make other dierent trajectory types attractive. Boer et al. [38]
recently published an article with a novel approach to suppress these lipids. In this
approach, the lipids are suppressed with an outer volume crusher coil generating a
highly inhomogeneous local magnetic eld in the outer layer of the head. If this
technique can be combined with shaping the point spread function, the proposed
method has the potential to obtain better reconstructions.

6
Conclusion
Two main objectives were sought solved in this study: to implement a gridding algo-
rithm capable of correctly reconstructing CSI data from arbitrary sampling methods,
and to build an optimizable model with a set of parameters and optimize this model
to locate the best set of parameters.
The gridding algorithm was successfully built in two dimensions and validated thor-
oughly in Chapter 3 by a number of objects with analytical Fourier transforms and
hence known distributions. Furthermore, several parameters were varied and their
inuence on the algorithm tested in depth. The model appears sturdy and is able to
properly reconstruct even dicult objects, also when the recorded signal is distorted
with noise. The model diers slightly from the common gridding algorithm as it is
focused on reconstructing spectroscopic imaging data where dierent challenges are
encountered: time continuously moves forward thus limiting the possible trajectory
types and the number of pixels in the spectral dimension is usually notably larger
than the number of pixels in the spatial dimension(s). These challenges have been
recognised and solved accordingly.
The model to optimize was built with 7 parameters. This number was intentionally
kept low to minimize the complexity of the model and thereby make optimization
lighter. Some parameters inuencing the model was kept constant after validating
that varying these would not greatly eect the model, thus setting the parameters
to the chosen levels was justied. The cost function was dened as either the maxi-
mum squared error or the mean squared error between a desired sampling density or
point spread function and the approximated one, generating 4 total cost functions.
The model was optimized with three dierent optimization techniques, with two
search heuristics for one of the techniques, totalling 4 optimization methods. All
16 optimization experiments were run to convergence, with very dierent results.
Some optimal parameter sets lead to point spread functions quite similar to the
ideal; some lead to point spread function very dierent from the ideal, even when
the cost function value was low. This could imply that the experiments have not
located a global minimum or the global minimum does not dier greatly from other
local minima. The solution space is seven-dimensional and thus can not easily be vi-
sualized. Alternatively, the cost function might be too simple to properly represent
the desired distributions. A parameter set that produces a point spread function of
similar shape and amplitude as the desired have been tested. With these param-
eters, the decaying trajectory outperformed the normal trajectory in 10 of 12 test
64
==PAGE:78==
cases with an average SNRdB increase of 3.08.
Suggestions have been made in the discussion for areas where improvements may
occur for interested parties or future works. Some of the most prominent improve-
ments include more complicated optimization methods and advanced design of cost
function, trajectory, and sampling function. Experimenting on these settings might
provide even better results.
Shaping the point spread function remains a highly interesting eld, especially in
combination with other well-established or emerging elds such as hyper-polarized
magnetic resonance imaging. The practical implementation on hardware and soft-
ware is straightforward and has the potential to provide even better results than
already seen in this thesis. This study attempted to bring focus to an important
aspect of magnetic resonance experiments only rarely accounted for. More studies
should be performed in this eld before any denite conclusions are drawn, nonethe-
less, the concept of shaping the point spread function remain an interesting and
promising eld in modern time fast magnetic resonance spectroscopic imaging.
