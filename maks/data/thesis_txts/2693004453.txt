Abstract
CNC operation presents a user with a barrier between idea to physical part by requiring
knowledge within 3D modelling and manufacturing processes. In this thesis a Computer
Vision based CNC control paradigm is proposed and developed in a proof of concept
state, called VisualCNC. This system is meant to prove extended possibilities in using
image analysis and computer vision methodology in manufacturing processes using CNC
machines. The focus of this project is to let a user skip CAD (Computer Aided Design)
and CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing) steps, allowing for a more analog way of
working with a CNC machine. The features implemented in the system proposed in
this thesis covers simple cutting and engraving operations, where the user is able to
draw a contour on a piece of material, to then let VisualCNC analyse and perform cuts
from this drawing. The system is implemented in Python and tested on a open source
CNC router, build as a development platform for this project.
The tests suggests that this system has potential to support ultra rapid prototyping
in a reliable way and presents a basis for further investigations within using Computer
Vision methods in CNC control. The system achieved dimensional accuracy of ±0.5mm,
corresponding to two pixels. It is theorised that this error can be minimised further.
VisualCNC operates with high robustness to choice of material and marker under the
assumption that the marker is signiﬁcantly darker than the material. This system is
tested to be easily approachable for new users with no background within prototyping
or manufacturing.
The system presents a solid basis for further implementation of
computer vision based CNC features and possibilities for automating manufacturing
processes.

Introduction

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Production techniques utilising CNC-based systems have been used since the 1950’s
to produce high-quality parts with high precision beyond what conventional methods
by hand previously allowed. Since then, CNC machines have been almost exclusively
used by machinists, engineers, and some aspects of woodworking, only to recently be
introduced to the homes of hobbyists with 3D Printers. [1]
The CNC production methods utilize the high precision of computer-calculated opera-
tions to create physical parts that would be impossible or impractical to manufacture
by hand. This can also be used to scale up productions as a CNC machine never gets
tired, and always perform the same operations.
Figure 1.1: CNC milling machine in operation [2]
CNC milling and routing is in most cases exclusively used in the production industry,
such as carpentry, machining, tool making, etc. Most CNC systems require education
and training for operation, and the process of making parts on a CNC system can be
slow and intimidating for new users.
The process of using a CNC router or CNC mill usually is conducted as follows: a
CAD (Computer-Aided Design) part is created, a CAM (Computer-Aided Machining)
program will calculate and program the processes included for the part to be produced,
and then an operator will start the CNC for production. The last step might sound
simple, but choosing and changing bits, setting machine coordinate systems, and pre-
cisely determining the material placement relative to the operations calculated by the
CAM program requires skill and knowledge.
1
==PAGE:11==

Figure 1.2: Flow from idea to part with CAM/CAD [3]
The CAD/CAM workﬂow might pose a barrier for users who could otherwise beneﬁt
from the features a CNC Router, or Laser cutter could oﬀer. A study by the Nielsen-
Norman Group [4] shows that only about 5% of persons of age 16 to 65 are suﬃciently
adept with a computer to learn new programs and systems without supervision (What
the study calls level 3 proﬁciency). Assuming that CAD/CAM operation requires at
least the computer proﬁciency, referred to in the report, as level 2 proﬁciency ", means
CAD/CAM is available to about 31% of adults, according to the study. If the CAD/-
CAM step could be bypassed so that a user could, in an analog fashion, present the
machine with the desired part, this could open the availability of CNC systems to a
large number of potential users.
The main problem to solve to allow more users to get into CNC machining is to induce
some intelligence and machine awareness to CNC machines. CNC machines are tra-
ditionally not intelligent systems, and have no understanding or relation to the stock
material, as no inormation about what is being processed is contained within the sys-
tem. CNC machines only execute commands given to them through a set of G-codes.
This makes for a robot system with no conversion from idea to item, ie. non-intelligent.
1.2
Hypothesis
In this project, the possibility for visual systems to enhance the way a user operates
a CNC machine will be explored in the context of a basic cutting operation. Such
a system could simplify the workﬂow for an operator by removing the need for both
CAD and CAM, saving hours of work, and introducing some degree of intelligence in
the form of workpiece analysis to the machine. The aim of this project is to prove by
2

example that CNC systems have room for further development by introducing methods
from computer vision and image analysis. The system proposed will be for all future
references in this report be called "VisualCNC". The speciﬁc idea here is to allow a
user to draw a closed contour freely on the workpiece that are to be processed, and
place the workpiece in the CNC machine. The VisualCNC system will then analyse
the workpiece and extract the required information of the contour to perform a cut
in the shape of the contour into the material. A secondary goal for this system is to
make this adaptable to any CNC machine with a cutting operation feature, Ie. laser
cutter, plasma cutter, water-jet cutter, etc. This feature is meant to oﬀer an example
of how a production technique can be made accessible to more users through methods
of computer vision.

• Provide theoretical understanding - The theory and background of CNC
machines in general and computer vision will be clearly explained to give the
reader the basis for understanding a proposed implementation of a Visual CNC
system.
• Construct CNC router - For the project, a DIY CNC machine will be devel-

open-source mechanical plans, the hardware will be chosen to be similar to a 3D

in general. The choices of hardware and ﬁrmware are to be documented and con-

and convert a drawing into a cut will be proposed and developed to a proof of

of this will be included.
• Evaluate performance - The results will be presented for the CNC machine
and the Visual CNC system individually, in relation to a possible use case. For
the CNC machine, this will be evaluated on behalf of the speciﬁcations and the

can be done. The visual CNC system will be tested on behalf of precision and
robustness where all subsystems and methods used are tested to determine possi-

Theory
2.1
Computer Numeric Control (CNC)
Computer Numeric Control (CNC) systems are not intelligent or autonomous systems
that can carry out tasks themselves. They are simply machines with motors. The
ﬁrmware contains a library of commands the CNC can perform with these motors, and
these commands in succession are what makes a useful CNC program. The general
structure of a CNC system can be seen in ﬁgure 2.1
Figure 2.1: Overview diagram of the control structure of a CNC [5]
The commands themselves are programmed onto the control board (MCU) of the CNC,
which serves as the brain of the system. The instructions are fed through the input
device. This is typically either a serial connection, USB, or SD card in a ﬁle called a G-
code instruction. Often some instructions are stored as G-code macros in the memory
of the MCU, these can be accessed using the Display unit. Such instructions could be
homing the machine, acquiring bed height mesh or preparing the peripherals. As the
instructions are being performed, they have to be propagated into actual movement,
this is done through the motor drivers, which turns motion instructions into electrical
signals making the motors turn. For stepper motors, the signals are PWM signals for
5
==PAGE:15==
Theory
each of the phase-windings of the motors, this will be further described in section 2.3.
As the machine is working, the feedback devices will provide information to the MCU,
to assure correct performance. This is however not necessary for the system and is often
not used in cheap CNC routers and 3D printers. Also, the CNC build can be chosen
with only the most critical feedback sensors. Any such system with direct numerical
control is considered a CNC system, but the most common examples of CNC machines
are CNC Mill, CNC Router, CNC Lathe, Laser Cutters, and 3D Printers [6]. The
technique of removing material to create a product is called subtractive manufacturing.
Techniques where material is added to make a part is called additive manufacturing.
All of these CNC machines, but the CNC Lathe, belong to a category called Cartesian
Robots, meaning they manipulate their end eﬀector by moving linearly in 3D cartesian
space.
CNC systems are a big part of manufacturing processes as they increase the eﬃciency
in a production cycle. Some of the advantages to using CNC Systems are [6]:
• Repeatability - Compared to manual machining, the quality and precision is much
more consistent.
• Better production planning - Manufacturing processes can be streamline in an
autonomous way to increase productivity.
• Complexity - The control that is presented in CNC systems can allow for produc-
tions not possible, or impractical manually.
• Waste Reduction - Full automation control makes optimising material use easier.
The simplest CNC routing and milling principle works by removing material in layers,
where the removal of layers is planned to create parts, this approach is called 2.5D
carving. Methods exist where 3D shapes are carved out by following the vertical contour
instead, as well as more complicated machining strategies. For CNC routing/milling,
there are some fundamental technical terms that should be understood in order to work
with the machine, some of which are presented here.
• Home - This refers to the origin of a reference frame, the machine home is usually
found using endstops.
• Endmill / Tool - This is the tool that chips away the material. These come in
many sizes and shapes.
• Feeds - refers to which at speed the Endmill is pulled through the material in
each direction.
• Speed - refers to the rotational speed of the spindle.
6
==PAGE:16==
Theory
• Depth of cut - The maximum depth of which the endmill is removing material at
each layer.
• Roughing and Finishing pass - For ﬁne parts, roughening is used to remove most
material, and ﬁnishing is a more delicate run to achieve ﬁne details and good
surface quality.
• Stepover - Refers to the percentage of the endmill engaged in the cut.
The list here could be expanded almost indeﬁnitely, an extended dictionary are available
at CNCcookbook [7]
2.2
G-Code script Language
As brieﬂy mentioned in section 2.1, the instruction language that CNC systems derive
their instructions from is called G-code. G-code instruction language was developed by
engineers at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), in the late 1950’s [8].
This became the industry standard in the 1960’s with the rise of early CNC routing
units. Today G-code is still the absolute standard, but the amount of G-code commands
since then has been increased greatly. Also, diﬀerent machines and ﬁrmware have some
deviations in G-code commands, and so compilation of G-code has to be mostly machine
speciﬁc. The G-code to task ﬂow is presented in ﬁgure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: MCU Workﬂow in reading G-Code
G-code instructions are divided into purposes. The most used command codes are as
follows
• G - General preparatory commands, used for controlling movements, linear ad-
vancements, coordinate reference choice etc.
• L - Loop, deﬁnes a loop of a speciﬁc command cycle with a ﬁxed counter.
• M - Miscellaneous/Machine functions, used for handling variables, data struc-
tures, and general purpose functions
7
==PAGE:17==
Theory
• S - Speed settings, these are a part of the setup for diﬀerent subtasks in a set of
instructions
• T - Tool selection, this is a library of makros for tool changing.
• X, Y and Z - Positional arguments, Refers to a position in the chosen reference
frame.
Many more exists and the G-code library can be extended in the ﬁrmware. This also
means that it is ﬁrmware dependent, for each ﬁrmware the list of codes can be found in
the respective documentation. For machining parts on a CNC, there are some important
G-codes to understand speciﬁcally when building CNC programs and debugging them.

2.3
Stepper Motors
Stepper motors are a category of motors that is used for high precision, low-speed pur-
poses. The main feature of a stepper motor making them ideal for automation purposes,
is that it can very accurately perform movements without any feedback loop. They are
composed of winded stator teeth which are used to induce a controlled magnetic ﬁeld
in the motor, and sets of rotor teeth connected to the shaft of the motor. The rotor
teeth directly exerts a torque on the shaft under operation, a simple stepper motor can
be seen in ﬁgure 2.3.

• step 2: Match Features
• step 3: Align images
• step 4: Project images

Literature Study
To get a clear understanding of the work already done in the ﬁeld of using Computer

methods relevant for the VisualCNC system is presented here.
3.1
Computer vision and CNC
Few projects have previously used computer vision in relation to automating tasks in
a CNC machine.
Computer Vision Haimer Probe - One of these is a LinuxCNC based project by
Kent Vander Velden, called Haimer Probe [21]. In this project, the system succeeded
in using a Camera attached to the chassis of a CNC milling machine, to estimate where
to probe holes with a Haimer 3D sensor. The goal here is to achieve an automated
probing operation, although it is usually done by jogging the CNC with the Haimer
probe. The computer vision involved in the project is a simple blob detector, which
only accepts low eccentricity shapes, thus ﬁnding round holes speciﬁcally. This project
achieved a relative accuracy on the camera measurement relative to the Haimer sensor
measurement of 97.3%.
PacCAM - In 2013, a research group from JST ERATO Igarashi Design Interface
Project developed a software system that uses an overhead camera, for analysis in a
CNC laser cutter for simulating cut placement. This is called PacCAM: Material cap-
ture and interactive 2D packing for eﬃcient material usage on CNC cutting machines
[22]. The point of the project is to reduce waste and allow laser cutter users to optimally
place contours to be cut, as to reduce the amount of material that goes to waste. Here
a simple segmentation algorithm is used to determine the placement of the existing cuts
in a sheet and placement relative to the CNC laser cutters coordinate frame. This is
veriﬁed before cuts by using an IR proximity sensor.
Wiremold - An idea distantly related to VisualCNC has been proposed by Japanese
students in a project called WireMolding [23]. In the mentioned project, a 3D modeling
workﬂow using deformable wire, and analysis of the resulting shape to convert to ex-
truded shapes on a 3D printer is proposed. The system is not automated. It works by
manually taking a picture of the wire on a sheet of paper and using a simple threshold
operation to construct an image importable in Fusion360, to be extruded and printed
on a 3D printer, see ﬁgure 3.1. No systematic evaluation of the results was performed
on the proposed method, other than qualitative evaluation of the 3D prints.

State of the art: Text segmentation in images
One of the key elements which cannot be avoided in a computer vision based system is
segmentation. Software capable of segmenting text from camera-captured images is of
interest within the ﬁelds of machine intelligence and data science. Some of the powerful
frameworks for achieving text segmentation might be useful for the VisualCNC system
as well. So here is a couple of relevant methods.
Otsu Threshold - In this method proposed by Nobuyuki Otsu [25], an image is as-
sumed to contain a foreground and a background. The distinction between the two is
attempted to be established by grayscale histogram analysis of the image in question.
This is very robust if the foreground and background are divided into two separated
peaks in the histogram, and the foreground is of homogeneous intensity.
Sauvola Adaptive Thresholding - In this thresholding approach, a complex work-
ﬂow for doing adaptive threshold of speciﬁcally text documents are proposed [26]. An
image is scanned in regions like adaptive threshold, but for each region choose which
adaptive method is used through analysis of the peak intensity in the intensity his-
togram of the given region. See the ﬂow diagram from the paper in ﬁgure 3.2.

4.1
Mechanical build
The mechanical build process for the MPCNC machine itself was followed as instructed
in V1 Engineerings guide [28]. The considerations and process for choosing the elements
and components in the ﬁnal build are as follows.
Tubes - Reportedly on the MPCNC forum and guide, the most imperative aspect of
an MPCNC build is the choice of tubes. For larger builds even more so as the torque
exerted from the middle assembly onto the gantries will create oscillations if not stiﬀ
enough. At the same time, heavy gantry rails will introduce greater forces for the step-
per motors to overcome in motion. For this build, both aluminum tubes and steel tubes
were considered. As aluminum was available from another project, this was tested to
be insuﬃcient in stiﬀness, with the gantry visibly bending during operation, and so
steel tubes were installed for the gantry for the ﬁnal build.
3D Prints - For this build, 2.8 kg ﬁlament is required. What is the most important
regarding the prints is their mechanical accuracy as the MPCNC is designed such that
many links are incredibly tight, tolerance at ±0.005mm, in order to assure rigidity.
This is easiest to achieve when using PLA ﬁlament, the main downside to PLA is, that
it has a glass transition temperature at between 50◦C and 80◦C, depending on brand
and mixture. The alternative is PETG, which is better for mechanical parts in general
as it is known to last longer under exposure to heat, UV light, and humid conditions.
This has the downside of being harder to print with good tolerances and is more ex-
pensive. Under these considerations and the fact that the CNC will be placed at room
temperature for all of its life span, PLA was the choice, with some of the parts close to
the motors being printed in PETG. The prints were made on a TronXY X5SA and an
Ender 3 printer.
Table - For the base of the CNC, a table is constructed. The requirements for this
table is to provide a rigid base for the machine, as well as keeping the top surface level.
For the table top, 2 layers of 13mm MDF is used. MDF does not warp unless exposed
to high humidity and is one of the cheapest materials available. These were acquired
for free. For the legs, a frame is constructed from pine two-by-four, fastened with angle
brackets and screws of the sizes 3.5x25 mm and 6x80 mm. To attain stiﬀness, perfo-
rated tape was tightened hard on the diagonals of the back and the sides. This table
could be constructed in a lot of ways, but this was very quick and cheap to acquire and
assemble.
Enclosure - The enclosure has to be able to keep dust inside. For the construction, the
same two-by-fours were used. To be able to observe the machine in operation Plexiglas
is chosen for the sides.

4.2.3
Stepper Drivers
Stepper motors are not worth much without a way to drive them, in order to control
the current going into each coil of the motor and producing the alternating signal to
make them run, a designated stepper driver circuitry is used. For comparison, three
stepper drivers are to be considered here, these are TMC2208, TMC2209, and TMC5160
[33] [34] [35]. Other stepper drivers are available, and most cheap desktop size CNC
machines use the A4988 stepper drivers [36], as these are reliable for their low price at
around 7 DKK / 1 USD per unit. For the MPCNC it is expected that the motors run
under continuous currents at around 1A to 2A per phase, which excludes the A4988
from the comparison as this is rated for 0.7A continuous use. For comparison of the
TMC stepper drivers, see the table 2.
For the MPCNC build, 5 stepper drivers are needed, so the price scales up to 720
DKK with the TMC5160, for this reason TMC5160 is excluded. Trinamic’s stallGuard
functionality serves as a closed-loop controller, where the driver reads the Back-EMF in
the motor to determine the critical load and deviation from the wanted position. This
allows the stepper driver to control the current in such a way that the motor is driven
at a higher current at higher loads, and lower current at low loads. This will help avoid
some otherwise critical loads to induce lost steps. The continuous driving current is
valued highly in a CNC router, as the loads from hard materials provide high loads for
long durations of time. The motors used in this build is only rated for 2.5 A current, so
anything above this might damage the motor through heat dissipation. As a reference
for what is contained in this stepper driver, the block diagram is seen on ﬁgure 4.3.

Power control
The power supply for the MCU and stepper motors, and the spindle cannot be the
same. The spindle is a brushless DC motor that runs at DC voltages from 35 to 100 V.
Where the mainboard uses 12 or 24 V. But the mainboard should still be able to control
the feeds and speeds of the spindle. The power control to the stepper motors will not
be considered here, as this has an integrated solution on the SKR 1.4 mainboard. For
the initial build of the MPCNC, the power of the spindle is connected such that the
spindle rotates clockwise, as this is the standard for most end bits. For controlling the
spindle on/oﬀa solid-state relay is used. For controlling the spindle rotation in the
future an H-bridge could be utilized. Such a setup should be decoupled, as the voltage
diﬀerence between the MCU ground, and the spindle control reference is measured to
about 43 V. Speed control is, for the ﬁrst iteration of the build, controlled manually
with a potentiometer. For future iterations, this could be improved upon by either using
a servo to control the potentiometer, using a digital potentiometer, or using some kind
of feedback system with speed reading through a servo controller. If another spindle
will be installed some day, a VFD (Variable Frequency Drive) will be used to control

4.2.6
Calibration
In the ﬁrmware, a default set of steps pr mm. for each axis is speciﬁed. These are a
guess, to later be tuned through calibration. Creating a precise CNC platform relies
heavily on these settings being calibrated correctly. This can be done on the machine
in the ﬁrmware. The machine motion is the ﬁrst thing to calibrate. Due to the double
gantry system, the most common problem here lies in possible skewed motion, of which,
the types of skew are illustrated in ﬁgure 4.5.
Figure 4.5: Illustration of skew in the construction of the MPCNC.
The side skew is not possible to correct by calibration. This would have to be dealt
with through a coordinate transformation in the ﬁrmware or by rebuilding the MPCNC
if it is not square. In the case of this MPCNC build, the diagonals are measured to be
the same length within 1 mm accuracy, so this has carefully been mitigated in the build
process. Gantry skew can be the result of uneven tightening of the bearings, slight
deviations in the 3D print, or simply bad assembly. But here gantry skew can be ﬁxed
by using dual end-stops. This allows for calibration of the relative positioning of each
side of the gantry and setting the zero position of each stepper motor accordingly. This
is done by adjusting end-stop placements.

Implementation: Visual CNC
With the MPCNC build ready and working, the rest of the project focuses on imple-
menting a computer-vision / image analysis based system for using a CNC for simple
cutting operations. This system will be called VisualCNC. The system has the purpose
of bypassing the workﬂow of creating CAD and CAM models before performing an
operation, thereby providing a easily approachable CNC system for unfamiliar opera-
tors. This system will be expected to have less versatility in the amount of problems
that can be solved with this system, but in return allow anyone with a pen to do CNC
operations. In this section, such a system will be proposed and developed through a
Python implementation as a proof of concept. The system will be documented with
considerations about hardware, methods and practical implementations included.

5.1
Assumptions and problem speciﬁcation
Establishing the link from handmade contours and the cutting operation of the CNC
machine will be a process of multiple steps, in this subsection, the required input will
be precisely deﬁned along with the desired capabilities, and the expected output. The
proposition for a system, that is able to accommodate as many of these speciﬁcations as
possible, will be described in the upcoming sections. The system should be operational
under the following assumptions and input speciﬁcations as a minimum:
• The hardware has been properly installed, to fulﬁll the scene presented in 5.2
• The user has drawn a series of closed contours with a signiﬁcantly darker color
than the background, the development assumption will be a black ball pen on
plywood as a ground reference.
• The material to be cut is within the limits of the CNC machine, the development
assumption is plywood.
• Contours drawn don’t intersect other contours or themselves.
• Contours is drawn with a thin, but distinct marker.
• The user does not interact with the system during analysis and calculations unless
prompted.
• Information about the machine operation, such as bit radius, material depth,
clearance gap, feeds, etc. is provided by the user.
• The machine is externally placed at the work space home position at the start of
cut and start of analysis. Work space reference frame is not necessarily equal to
machine reference frame.
• The machine to be used with the system, is the MPCNC that has been build.
• Fiducial markers is placed in top left and bottom right corner of the workpiece,
with edges pointing along the X- and Y-axis of the workspace. The top left corner
of the top left ﬁducial marker deﬁnes the origin of the workspace. This has to be
visible in the camera frame when starting the system.
These assumptions provide the basis for the minimal working conditions, the extent
to which the Visual CNC system should work beyond these will be discussed and
considered through test results in the following sections. The image in ﬁgure 5.1 shows
the pen and wood used as a reference for development. The output of the system is
a G-code instruction ﬁle, which should resemble a cut of the input contour as close as

5.6.2
Stitching
When the dataset is acquired, the images need to be stitched together to represent
the entire workspace. This is either done through the GhettoStitch() method, or by
using a separate stitcher. The separate stitcher used in this project is the AutoStitch
algorithm [19]. AutoStitch will be used in all tests and results. AutoStitch is a stitching
algorithm based on SIFT and RANSAC, see appendix A.2 and section 2.9. Applying
these feature extraction algorithms ensures very robust stitching when strong features
are present, but can cause issues if large areas lack strong features. Which might be
the case if multiple contours are processed at once with a distance between them. In
the case that not enough good features are found, the solution could be to put some
objects onto the material to introduce guiding features. The AutoStitch algorithm has
no implementation in python for the time being, but will still be considered the stitching
method used in VisualCNC. The GhettoStitch() method works by simply placing
each image with an oﬀset distance equivalent to the displacement of the stepsize_x
and stepsize_y in pixels. This is only valid if the camera is in a known position in
each picture captured relative to the rest, and only if the camera pixel grid is oriented
exactly along the machine axis. Additionally, this requires hand-tuning to correlate
for the height above the work space.
The images will have seams where they are
stitched, if performed on grayscale or RGB images. Before being placed on the resulting
image, thresholding and closing operations will therefore be performed, this is why the
boolean valued ghettoStitched ﬂag will carry the information into the image analysis
part if this method is used.
Finally, when stitched by ghetto stitching, noise will
be removed using the blobFilter() method. The GhettoStitch() method is only
included to illustrate a possibility for a complete end-to-end python implementation.
OpenCV’s built-in stitcher was also tested, but performed worse than both AutoStitch
and GhettoStitch while requiring unfair amount of memory and time. In short, not
worth considering for VisualCNC.

5.7.1

The detection of the ﬁducial tags is done using OpenCV’s built in methods [43]. In the
VisualCNC system, this is implemented in the function analyzeMarkers(), here the
corners are found in pairs of four. This is used to determine the scaling from image

The approach is only accurate if the ﬁducial is completely square with the camera and
workspace. Small deviations in ﬁducial alignment should not be critical as per the small

cases, the use of trigonometry can solve the issue in later implementations of Visual-
CNC. With the information of the ﬁducial markers extracted, the initial binarisation is

5.7.2
Image segmentation
Segmentation is done through multiple operations. First, a blurring operation is per-
formed using the OpenCV function cv2.medianBlur(), with a unit kernel of size 5x5
on the image to smear out problematic features of the wood. The kernel was tuned
through trial and error. The kernel is small enough that the pen lines are intact, but the
wooden features become less distinct. The blurred image is converted into to grayscale,
which will then be used to threshold the image. As the image is not homogeneously
lit for various reasons, this will be done using an adaptive threshold operation, as de-
scribed in the theory section 2.7 [15].
The outermost corners from analyzeMarkers() are passed onto the next part of ﬁdu-

5.9
Path planning and G-code generation
Generating G-code is principally simple. You write the G-code commands sequentially
to a ﬁle to be read by the CNC as commands. The problem to solve in this section
is then; Which commands should be written to that ﬁle? The contour processing has
left the system with a set of coordinates that indicate the wanted cut. Here are some
important problems to consider before constructing the G-code script:
• Is the wanted cut meant for a part, a hole, or engraving? If the cut is meant
to resemble a part, the resulting cut must not go inside of the contour bound-
ary. Oppositely, if the hole is wanted, the cut must not go outside the contour
boundary. And for engraving, the general contour is to be directly outlined on
the material.
• How do we calculate the cut while taking the bit size into account? In the previous
point, it is mentioned that for parts and holes, the cut must stay on the inside or
outside of the boundary. For this to be done, the cut must be oﬀset with some
distance to make sure the bit does not cut beyond the boundary. Additionally,
heat and friction will cause cuts to fail if only one bit diameter is cut around the
contour, the cut needs to be expanded for more than one bit’s width.
• How do we keep the cut from falling out during machining? When cutting through
the material, if the inner piece is left to move freely, it will move around in the
hole created. This can lead to material or machine damage, which is not wanted.
• How fast can we cut? Parameters for speeds, feeds, step-over, and depth of cut
need to be provided to the G-code. This information has already been chosen by
the user as variables, see table 6.
In the implementation presented here, these issues and problems will be dealt with to
achieve an executable G-Code. An illustration showing the desired result can be seen
in ﬁgure 5.16

5.9.1
Preparation and end script
The start and end sequence that the machine has to use for cutting can be chosen
in many diﬀerent ways, and is often deﬁned in CAM programs by the user. For this
system, the following sequences for setting up and ending the cut was chosen.
Preparation G-Code sequence:
Require: CNC has been homed and jogged to the upper left corner of the workpiece.
G92 X0 Y0 Z0 ;
G01 Z(CLEARANCE) F(FEED RATE) ;
M03 S(SPINDLE SPEED) ;
Ending G-Code sequence:
Require: The CNC has performed the wanted operations.
G00 Z(CLEARANCE) ;
G00 X0 Y0 ;
M05 ;
The startup sequence starts by setting the coordinate system origin, then raises the
endmill to a clearance height and starts the spindle.
The end sequence raises the
endmill to the clearance height, moves to the origin in the XY plane, and stops the
spindle.

6.4
VisualCNC - Robustness
In this section, a set of tests determining the capabilities and robustness to changes in
extrinsic parameters will be conducted. The parameters to be changed and tested here
are
• Camera - The system’s capability of generating G-code with other cameras that
that which the camera mounted.
• Defects - The response to defects in the contour in terms of small branching
lines, broken line sections, too thin or too thick lines should be tested.
• Noise - Physical artifacts such as knobs and lighting conditions might pose prob-
lems to the system and should be tested.
• Marker and material match - The tests made thus far have all been done with

Conclusion and Dicussion
7
Conclusion and Discussion
The VisualCNC system is developed as a proof of concept that manufacturing pro-
cesses can be simpliﬁed in order to make simple processes faster and available to more
users. This system achieves the goal of simplifying the technical prerequisites to being
able to handle a pen and being able to prepare a CNC. This means that neither CAD
modeling, nor CAM programming is needed for the operations included in VisualCNC.
The VisualCNC system was tested to be reliable and perform as expected under the
assumptions made in section 5.1 and with the setup presented in section 5.2. From
the presented VisualCNC setup, a tolerance of ±0.5mm was achieved, as shown in the
accuracy tests in section 6.3. To put this in perspective, this corresponds to the contour
consistently being correctly established within two pixels precision of the camera used
in the tests. The main issues regarding precision during operation are the precision
of the user and the width of the contour. Internally in the system, the morphology
and analysis of ﬁducial markers can introduce some loss in accuracy and precision. In
the "big complex shape" test, it was seen that the stitching did not introduce sig-
niﬁcant distortions aﬀecting precision and accuracy. And considering that no camera
calibration and un-distortion is performed. The AutoStitch algorithm might even have
un-distorted the images a bit. The VisualCNC system’s capabilities besides precision
are highly related to the speciﬁc case. It is demonstrated in the robustness tests, that
the system as presented has its limitations when it comes to extracting contours from
some combination of materials and markers. This is not regarded as a problem, since
any contour that can be correctly analysed will be able to be projected onto any other
surface. Drawing a contour on paper might sometimes be the most practical way of
using this system. VisualCNC’s capabilities was found to have two critical steps, being
the stitching step and the segmentation step. If, for a given contour, the output of
these two steps does not break the integrity of the contour, the rest of the system is
guaranteed not to introduce any fatal errors if used correctly.
The user experience, as implemented, is not ready for a commercial product. But the
system may be if some parts of the application, where the user will need to perform
an action to use the system, can be automated in a future version. The goal for a
future version is to implement the above mentioned improvements to a degree where
user interactions are reduced to choosing critical user variables, insert a drawn contour
into the work space and then press a button to execute all steps up from acquisition
to G-code. The VisualCNC was partially implemented on a Raspberry Pi 4, but was
ultimately tested on a Windows 10 Computer since no Linux implementation of Au-
toStitch is available. if a Linux based implementation of AutoStitch become available
for the Raspberry Pi 4, this would serve as a plug-and-play compatible way to use the
system, which could be added to any CNC machine.
VisualCNC is also developed with the possibility of expanding compatible in mind. In
the future, it can easily be expanded to work with Laser cutters, 3D Printers, and other
Cartesian robot based CNC systems. For cutters (laser cutters, plasma cutters etc.),
the VisualCNC system would be especially useful, as cutting is what both VisualCNC
78
==PAGE:88==
Conclusion and Dicussion
and cutters are developed for.
The MPCNC that was developed in this project does cover most needs of a hobbyist.
But performance is not similar to that of industrial machines. As with most things,
there is a trade-oﬀbetween speed and accuracy, and the MPCNC lacks the stiﬀness
and the rigidity to operate at speeds that would be considered usual for industrial CNC
routers, while still having good accuracy and precision. The electronics and ﬁrmware did
not prove to be restrictive from the tests conducted in this project, and the mainboard
chosen for the machine has a lot of possibilities to expand upon the MPCNC’s features
in the future.
7.1
Use cases
The VisualCNC system oﬀers possibilities within rapid prototyping and productions,
some areas and speciﬁc cases where the system will be able to improve the workﬂow
for using a CNC, in terms of user experience or practical aspect is suggested here:
• Copying existing ﬂat shapes - To copy a ﬂat object and cutting it only requires
tracing the border with a pen and then use VisualCNC.
• Engraving handmade drawings - Engraving handmade drawings can be done for
many reasons. VisualCNC can make the link between the drawing and the en-
graving faster that usual CAD and CAM software.
• Create complex stencils - Stencils could be easily created with this system, which
would be useful for many hobby projects, marking items or painting.
• CNC Sewing - For the production of textiles, CNC sewing is used for some stan-
dardised operations [46]. To be able to use the rapid and exact control from CNC
on textile translated through VisualCNC could be very useful.
• Construction Work - Using VisualCNC in construction work can help create com-
plex shapes without the use of a band saw, also potentially with features beyond
what a conventional band saw might oﬀer.
• Woodworking - woodworkers in general may beneﬁt from a workﬂow that allows
to use their skills in new ways. For example to create special shapes for furniture
or installations.
In general, VisualCNC is developed for use cases where rapid prototyping or mock ups
of products is in focus. For complex shapes which are hard or impractical to draw in
CAD as compared to by hand, the system might oﬀer the best possible solution. In
use cases where the hand drawn aesthetic is desired, VisualCNC oﬀer an easy solution.
Most importantly VisualCNC is developed for all users that would otherwise not be
able to beneﬁt from CNC operations.

8
Future work
As for future work, the MPCNC and the VisualCNC system will be considered seper-
ately, as they are independent systems.
8.1
Future work related to VisualCNC
The features that has been implemented in the VisualCNC as presented in this system
performs as expected under the assumptions made. Some critical points of the system
has been discussed, and for those are some suggestions of methods to examine. The
points worth analysing deeper to optimise the performance of the VisualCNC system
is:
• Segmentation - Adaptive thresholding is the step that is most critical point. It
would be interesting conduct a thorough analysis of the adaptive thresholding
methods available, and try to ﬁnd an optimised method.
• Stitching - The stitching step is currently the only step where a user must use
a seperate system. To implement the stitching in Python and make it a part
of the system internally would make it a smoother experience to use.
If this
are to be done, maybe also consider optimisations for the stitching algorithm to
accommodate the VisualCNC systems needs.
Beyond the optimisation of the VisualCNC system itself, some features could be added
to expand upon the uses of the system. Some suggestions are:
• User interface - A user interface where a user can interact with the system to
choose things such as, which contours are supposed to be holes and which is
parts. A possibility to set seperate depths for diﬀerent contours, and maybe a
G-code simulation to let the user check the G-code within VisualCNC before
executing.
• More post processors - The ﬂow of "Writing G-code" is within CAM programming
called a post processor. The G-code language of each speciﬁc CNC machine is
added here. To use VisualCNC in other CNC systems than the MPCNC Marlin
based system, other post processors should be added.
• Add slots carving - Creating slots for inserts, or connection can be very useful, the
only diﬀerence here is that all material within the material need to be removed
to a certain depth instead of only removing the edge.
• Add 3D methods - Adding features to carve chamfers ﬁllets, or other 3D features
could be interesting. Using perspective view to derive complex 3D shapes could
also be interesting. These features would take a lot of time to develop though.
80
==PAGE:90==
Future work
• Material detection - From images, humans are capable of determining the type of
stock material. Maybe this detection could be implemented in VisualCNC and
used to load the variables for the material. Some of the methods from S. Klancnik,
J. Šenveter’s system could be used [24].
• Grayscale Engraving - Lithophane images could be engraved from grayscale rep-
resentation. Automating this process could add another feature.
• Automate the material homing sequence - After analysis of the workspace, the
ﬁducial could be used to also determine correct positioning of the CNC with
respect to the material.
• Implement a Raster Vectorization algorithm - Vectorization of the binary images
could lead to smoother cuts.
• Topological cut optimisation could be added in the style of PacCAM [47], to
reduce waste when using VisualCNC.
These features would build towards a more polished product which could become com-
mercialised some time in the future.
8.2
Future work related to MPCNC
The MPCNC machine is a wonderful machine, and oﬀers a lot of CNC machine for the
money. But as discussed, the machine has issues regarding the precision and structural
stiﬀness. This is the most pressing issue. Which should be solved by either construct-
ing a support structure on the gantry, or possibly completely rebuilding the mechanical
structure. Rebuilding the mechanical structure is in fact what will be done to the ma-
chine in this project. The dual gantry system will be changed to a overhead carriage
system. An example of this style of system is the Piranha CNC system by MakerSup-
plies [48], see ﬁgure 8.1, although the exact build for next iteration are to be determined
some time in the future.
The CNC could also beneﬁt from some added features. Here are a couple of features
that will be a priority to add in the future.
• ATC Spindle - The most important future feature for further automisation of the
CNC operation is a Automatic Tool Change (ATC) spindle. This will without a
doubt push the price well over the scope of this project as the spindle itself would
range between 2000 and 5000 dollars, or 14000 to 35000 dkk. But this would
be an incredibly useful addition to the machine as it would allow for completely
automatised procedures.

• Z probe sensor - For more consistently being able to produce parts, a Z probe
would be beneﬁcial. This way the ﬁrmware can store a grid representation of
the workpiece top face, and use that to more precisely perform operations. This
might be done using a capacitive touch sensor and sending the trigger signal to
Marlin along with the trigger height to store in a grid. The ﬁrmware already have
methods for using this to perform height correction in the G-code G29.
• Automatic camera attachment - For use with the Visual CNC machine, a G-
Code macro could be used to attatch the camera from 5.3b, to the machine
automatically oﬀering even more ease for a user of the visual CNC system. This
would likely need a rework of the camera mount itself.
• Vacuum - As the machine stands, the only way to remove chips and debris from
the routing operation is to vacuum clean it manually. A mount where the vacuum
head is in close range with the CNC spindle would make it a lot easier, and such
system is pretty standard on CNC routers.
• Laser engraving - As discussed in section 7, the Visual CNC would be just as useful
if not more so in laser cutting or engraving systems. So it would be interesting
to add this to the CNC machine as well.
• Mach4 - The Marlin ﬁrmware is primarily meant for 3D printers, so a possible
upgrade could be to change the ﬁrmware to Mach4, which is closer to a industry
standard ﬁrmware. As 3D printers have had some signiﬁcant momentum in de-
velopment in the last couple of years, Marlin may oﬀer more for a CNC router as
well, so a thorough investigation would be necessary before changing the ﬁrmware.

A.1
Bézier curves
Discrete representation of image data is the standard in most purposes, but for universal
scaleability this gives problems as pixels will have to be interpolated or extrapolated to
maintain the resolution. This problem can be fully disregarded in the realm of vectors.
For transforming image data into vector format, some math is needed in addition to the
basic image analysis tools. The problem statement that we are trying to solve here is
to ﬁt a group of discrete points to a parametric curve. This will allow us to interpolate
indeﬁnitely using the parametric curve. Assume the simplest such problem, where two
points P1 and P2 has to be ﬁtted as to be able to extract any point in between them.
This can be done using a line, which has the vectorfunction representation
l(t) = ⃗P + t⃗R
(33)
Where P is any point on the line and R is a direction vector. Now if we have t to be
the percentage of distance that the two points should be from the two points P1 and
P2. The parameterization can be written as
l(t) = (t −1)P1 + tP2
(34)
Extending this to more points get somewhat less intuitive. Imagine 3 points P1, P2 and
P3, where P2 is some midpoint to P1 and P3. How should we create a curve that outline
a smooth curvature from these points? This is where Bézier curves helps. The Bézier
curve interpolates the two curves
l1(t) = (t −1)P1 + tP2
(35)
l2(t) = (t −1)P2 + tP3
(36)
Through the same procedure of matching each point on the curve to their respective
partner on the other, and parameterizing a line in between.

A.2
SIFT
From 3D modelling with photogrammetry, by Aleksander Lund, Emma Borre
and Søren Engell. Formulated as a part of a project report for the DTU
course 30330
Scale-invariant feature transform, or SIFT, is an algorithm used for feature detection
to both detect and describe features in an image, and was invented and patented by
Canadian computer scientist David Lowe in 1999 [49]. The algorithm can be used for
many applications including object recognition, image stitching, 3D modeling and so
on.
Figure A.3: Illustration of feature detection using SIFT [50].
SIFT is a robust feature extraction method, that can identify objects even among clutter
or partial occlusion, the advantages using the SIFT algorithm can be listed [51]:
• Locality: The features found with SIFT are local, and thus robust to occlusion
and clutter.
• Distinctiveness: The individual features that are found, can be matched to objects
in a large amount of data.
• Quantity: Many features can be found, and also for very small objects.
Appendix - 4
==PAGE:101==
Appendix A
• Eﬃciency: SIFT can be performed close to a real-time performance.
• Extensibility: The algorithm can easily be extended to detect many diﬀerent types
of features, that also adds robustness.
The algorithm consists ﬁve main stages, which are listed below:
• Scale-space peak selection
• Keypoint Localization
• Orientation Assignment
• Keypoint descriptor
• Keypoint matching
These stages are described in detail in the following sections.
A.2.1
Scale-space peak selection
In the scale-space peak selection phase, an appropriate scale-space is determined. The
scale-space of an image is deﬁned as the convolution of a Gaussian kernel with the input
image, where the Gaussian kernel is constructed at diﬀerent scales. This convolution is
also referred to as the blurring (see section 2.5), where the blurred image is described
by eq. (41) [51].
L (x, y, σ) = G (x, y, σ) ∗I (x, y)
(41)
Where G (x, y, σ) is the Gaussian Blur operator as a function of the local coordinates
x and y and the scale parameter σ, and I (x, y) is an image as a function of local
coordinates x and y.
The Gaussian blur operator is given by eq. (42) [51].
G (x, y, σ) =
1
2πσ2 · e−(x2+y2)/2σ2
(42)
The blurred images can then be used to generate Diﬀerence of Gaussians (DoG), which
can be used to ﬁnd keypoints in the given image. The DoG is the diﬀerence between
Gaussian blurrings with two diﬀerent scale parameters, and then for diﬀerent octaves
of the image in the corresponding Gaussian Pyramid as illustrated in ﬁgure A.4.

A.2.3
Orientation Assignment
After the processing in the previous sections A.2.1 and A.2.2, the keypoints are tested
to be stable, and they are scale invariant. In order to make them rotation invariant, the
orientation should be assigned. To do this, a Histogram of Oriented Gradient (HOG)
is used.

(see ﬁg. A.6), in which the gradient magnitude and direction is calculated for each pixel.

The assigned orientation for the keypoint is computed from the highest peak in the
HOG, where other peaks above 80% also is considered. The rotation invariance achieved
from this process, creates keypoints that has the same location and scale, and help to
ensure stability of matching later on.
A.2.4
Keypoint descriptor
Now that the keypoints has a location, scale and orientation, a descriptor should be
computed for the 16 by 16 window around keypoint. The descriptor should be distinc-
tive and invariant for robustness. In the 16 by 16 window, each pixel is divided into 4
by 4 spatial grid as in ﬁg. A.8, where the dimensions of the grid are dependent of the
keypoint scale.
Figure A.8: Illustration of the subdivision of pixels in the neighbourhood of the keypoint
[51].
Appendix - 9
==PAGE:106==
Appendix A
The orientation histogram of 8 bins for each of the sub-blocks is then computed (see
ﬁg. A.9), by adding the gradient angle at each pixel to the respective spatial bin in
the grid. Each pixel is then weighted by the gradient magnitude and a scale-dependent
Gaussian, which is centered on the keypoint. Thereby the small changes far from the
keypoint in the window, will have smaller inﬂuence, and the result will be more robust
to sudden changes. When creating the histogram, a trilinear interpolation in x, y and θ
is used to avoid boundary eﬀect and to distribute the gradient values of the subsample
in the 4 by 4 grid into adjacent histogram bins.
Figure A.9: Illustration of orientation of sub-blocks and their orientation histogram
[51].
Since there are 16 blocks with 8 bins of a histogram each, there will be 16 · 8 = 128
bins in total, which results in a 128 dimensional vector called the keypoint descriptor.
The descriptor is made from gradient orientations, it is therefore rotation dependent.
To compensate for this, the rotation of the keypoint is subtracted from each of the ori-
entations, then orientation Independence is achieved. Besides the rotation dependency,
the descriptor can also be illumination dependent. To compensate for the illumination
dependency, the values in the descriptor are thresholded at 0.2, so the values above 0.2
is changed to 0.2. The descriptor will then also be illumination independent.
A.2.5
Keypoint matching
Lastly, the keypoints found with the tools in the previous sections A.2.1, A.2.2 and
A.2.3 can be used to match with keypoints from another image of the same scene. The
matching is done by identifying their nearest neighbours. If the second closest match
is very near to the ﬁrst match, the ratio of the two is taken and thresholded by a value
of 0.8. If the value is higher than 0.8, they are rejected. According to David Lowe’s
paper, this procedure eliminates ≈90% of false matches, and only ≈5% of the correct
matches.
An illustration of keypoint matching is displayed on ﬁgure A.10, with coloured lines
connecting and matching the keypoints of two images of the scene.
