Abstract 
Strategical planning is an important step in the successful development of offshore wind farms and 
requires a thorough screening of the Sea for the identification of potential areas. This is a very time and 
cost consuming process since it depends on a high number of restricting parameters related to 
geographical and geological conditions, land use interests and nature. The objective of this study is to 
optimize the process of screening potential areas in order to improve the decision making-process (in 
terms of time, money spent and quality of results) and to find the most suitable locations with highest 
potential for energy harvesting.  
To achieve this objective an exemplary screening process applied on the Danish North Sea is 
documented. Furthermore, seven selected areas are benchmarked in order to rank their suitability for an 
offshore wind farm. For each area, three offshore wind farms are designed with different capacities i.e. 
200, 300 and 500 MW. The calculations are conducted with the help of EERA DTOC (Design Tools for 
Offshore Wind farm Clusters [1]) which uses the AEP model WAsP/PARK. In this step the Annual 
Energy Production and levelized cost of energy (LCoE) are calculated. To assess the improvement using 
the results of this study a Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is carried out based on experts’ 
opinion.  
The benchmark gives an indication of the impact of different site-specific parameters (i.e. wind resource, 
water depth, distance to coast) on the LCoE. The LCoE ranges between 92.2 €/MWh and 98.0 €/MWh 
for a 300 MW wind farm. These values seem to be representative regarding the goal of eleven European 
companies to achieve LCoE for offshore wind of 80 €/MWh and below by 2025 [2].  
The FMEA shows that the documentation of the exemplary screening process in this study (including 
GIS maps, legal framework analysis, criteria catalogue of all restricting parameters and list of 
responsible agencies) as well as the benchmark results will expectedly have a positive impact on the 
decision-making process when potential areas for offshore wind energy projects are being screened. It 
is expected that this study can be used as a guide for wind project developers and strategic planners to 
deploy offshore wind projects not just in Denmark but also in different countries where this technology 
has not been exploited yet.

1. Introduction 
With increasing energy demand induced by the growth of population and economy, a reliable power 
production, which is able to cover those demands is needed. Regarding problems of non -renewable 
energy supplies – noticeable by i.e. the nuclear disaster in Fukushima, the oil crisis in 1973 or the 
contamination of the sea and climate change – sustainable energy technologies have high potential for 
the future [1].  
One important part of the sustainable energies is the steadily growing wind energy industry. Denmark 
as important leader of the wind energy industry has a wind energy production covering close to 35 % of 
the total electricity consumption [2] with a total capacity of almost 5000 MW in 2015 of which 26 % 
are offshore wind [3]. Denmark’s success with the development of its offshore wind industry can be 
related to its beneficial natural resources such as long coastline, excellent wind resources and shallow 
waters as well as the political support and stable policy framework [4]. Denmark aims to reach a 50 % 
wind energy share of electricity consumption by 2020. Therefore, according to the 2012 Energy 
Agreement, additional 2 GW wind power should be realized before 2020 [5]. 
This requires strategical planning of new offshore wind farms including a screening of the Danish Seas 
for suitable areas. This is an important step that has to be optimized in order to improve the decision 
making-process and to find the most suitable locations with highest potential for energy harvesting. 
Allocating potential areas for future wind farms depends on a high number of parameters such as wind 
resource, social and environmental impacts, seabed conditions, cables, lighthouses and other restricting 
factors. The method that is used in this study to address this problem is outlined in the next chapter.

2. Problem statement  
In this chapter the status quo of the current practice of allocating potential areas for future wind farms 
is briefly reviewed. Afterwards, the objective and added value of this thesis is pointed out. Finally, the 
methodology and expected results of this thesis are presented. 
Motivation & Status quo 
Although the allocation of potential areas for future offshore wind farms has already been performed 
many times by strategical wind farm planners, there is only little documentation on the procedure. Parts 
of the process have been documented though e.g. a report published by the Crown Estate [6] and one by 
the Danish Energy Agency [7].  
The common approach to screening potential sites for offshore wind energy includes collecting 
information about various parameters related to geographical and geological conditions, other land use 
interests and nature which can have an effect on the suitability of an area for offshore wind farms. Also 
the permitting process of the concerning country and the legal framework including regulations and 
guidelines such as noise regulations, distance to airports, archaeological objects etc. play an important 
role. The gathered information is then analysed using Geographical Information Systems (GIS) in order 
to map areas having potential for offshore wind energy.  
However, an openly available documentation of an exemplary screening process of potential areas for 
offshore wind farms would help both strategical planners as well as wind farm developers to improve 
the decision-making process of allocating potential areas in terms of time, money spent and quality of 
results. 
Objective 
The main objective of this study is to achieve an improvement of the process of screening potential 
areas. To accomplish this objective an exemplary screening process is documented and further 
investigations including a benchmark of selected areas are conducted. To assess the improvement with 
the results of this study a Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is carried out based on experts’ 
opinion. 
Hypothesis 
The study includes two hypotheses. It should be noted that the first hypothesis presents the main 
objective of this study while the second hypothesis can be regarded as a plus. 
1. Using the same data and conditions the process of allocating potential areas for offshore wind 
farms can be improved (in terms of time, money spent and quality of results) by an open 
available documentation. The improvement of the process can be measured by assessing it with 
a FMEA analysis with the help of experts’ opinions. The experts are stakeholder which are 
involved in the screening process of potential areas. A list of addressed experts is shown in 
Appendix C. Therefore, a FMEA is carried out to assess both the current procedure for site 
selection and the procedure using the results of this study. 
 
2. Additionally – provided that a documentation is available – ranking the suitability of selected 
areas for offshore wind energy can improve the process of allocating areas for offshore wind 
farms. Again the improvement of the screening procedure can be assessed with the help of a 
FMEA based on experts’ opinion before and after the above mentioned investigations. 
Scope 
The scope of this study involves the documentation of the screening process for the allocation of new 
locations for offshore wind energy in the Danish Seas.

After the planning procedure and permits of offshore wind farms in Denmark are described and 
compared with the ones in Germany and the UK, the procedure of screening potential areas in the Danish 
North Sea is documented. To assess the screening procedure without and with having an exemplary 
documentation a FMEA is carried out for both cases based on experts’ opinion.  
Further investigations are then carried out using EERA DTOC (Design Tools for Offshore Wind farm 
Clusters [8]), which is a software for planning of offshore wind farm clusters in order to gather additional 
knowledge that can help to improve the screening process of potential areas. This includes the 
benchmarking of selected potential areas by using the same wind farm design in order to conclude the 
area with highest potential. 
In the end, a conclusion of the improvement of the screening procedure after this study is assessed for 
Denmark as well as for other countries. 
Methodology 
 
 
The procedure of screening potential areas for offshore wind farms using GIS has to be 
described and applied on the Danish North Sea. Therefore, the necessary up-to-date data 
representing the legal framework and parameters influencing the selection of a location has to 
be collected.  
 
Further investigations using DTOC: A benchmarking process of selected sites from the screened 
areas has to be carried out using DTOC. A prediction of costs and energy output of the wind 
farms on the selected areas has to be conducted. Therefore, a suggested wind farm design has 
to be made for each area using the same type of turbines, foundations, cables and layout. The 
calculations are made using fixed costs for the turbine components and cables and using the 
same wake and cost model. 
 
To finally test the hypothesis of the thesis a FMEA is carried out in order to evaluate the current 
procedure of allocating new areas for offshore wind farms as well as the procedure when the 
results of this thesis are available. For this assessment opinions of experts in the field of 
screening potential areas (i.e. planning agencies, wind farm developers) are collected. 
 
Expected results 
 
At the end of this thesis:  
 
A documentation of the procedure for screening potential areas for offshore wind farms in the 
Danish North Sea is presented. The process before and after having an open available 
documentation is assessed and presented in a FMEA matrix. 
 
Additional investigations using DTOC delivers a benchmark of selected sites of the screened 
area in the Danish North Sea indicating the suitability of the sites for wind farms. The 
improvement of the procedure for allocating potential areas for future wind farms using the 
above mentioned results is assessed and presented in a FMEA matrix. 
 
A discussion and conclusion including the relevance of this study for other countries is 
presented

3. Planning procedure and permits for OWF 
A wind farm project undergoes several individual work phases which can be divided into: preliminary 
and project planning, construction, operation and decommissioning. The planning phase of an offshore 
wind farm (OWF) includes legal aspects of the approval process, economic aspects and technical aspects 
such as estimation of the wind regime, expected energy yield, site selection, grid connection etc. [1] 
The identification of potential areas for OWF involves consideration of many restricting parameters 
related to geography and geology, land use interest and nature. Although, there can be differences in the 
consent regime and spatial planning strategies, the methodology for filtering potential areas to a smaller 
core is in general the same in each country (described in detail in Chapter 4.2) 
Because of the comparatively new offshore wind energy sector that is still under development, the 
approval procedure of OWFs presents several challenges and has less or no experience concerning the 
marine spatial planning for OWF. Regarding these challenges the governments of the European 
countries developed different solution strategies concerning the regulatory framework in terms of 
support strategies, approval procedures and grid connection. The amount of permits required and 
authorities directly involved in the permitting process differs considerably in the different countries [9].

3.1. Denmark 
As mentioned before stable and supportive policy framework and the long term planning have led to a 
successful wind energy development in Denmark. The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) under the Ministry 
of Climate, Energy and Building is responsible for planning and commissioning of new OWFs [10]. 
There are two ways of how wind farm planning can be conducted, either by a governmental tender 
process or by an open-door procedure. 
Governmental call for tenders 
One advantage of the tender process in Denmark for wind energy developers is that the DEA serves as 
a ‘one-stop-shop’ which means that all licenses that are needed for developing a wind farm are granted 
by it. In total there are four licenses that have to be achieved during a tender bidding. In the following 
paragraphs the ‘ideal’ screening process is described. It should be noted though that in reality some steps 
are taking place in different order or simultaneously because of the number of authorities involved in 
such a tender process. 
A tender process starts with the screening process of allocating new potential areas in the Danish Seas 
where future wind farms can be built. This process is carried out by the DEA. From these allocated areas 
DEA chooses one area that corresponds best to the tender process. 
In case of a governmental tender Energinet.dk, the national transmission system operator (TSO), 
constructs, owns and maintains the transformer station and underwater cable carrying the electricity 
from the OWF to the land [11]. Energinet.dk is responsible for the electricity infrastructure in Denmark. 
Therefore pre-investigations i.e. concerning the seabed condition and an Environmental Impact 
Assessment (EIA) have to be carried out by Energinet.dk for the area where the future wind farm, 
transformer stations and submarine cables will be placed. 
==PAGE:18==
Planning procedure and permits for OWF 
 
7 
 
Using the EIA as a base the DEA then carries out a hearing of other governmental bodies to clarify if 
there are other public interests in the chosen area. If there are no other interests, a public hearing will 
take place to assure the project will be accepted by the public (this step is more important for nearshore 
tenders since there is a higher visual impact). In case the governmental bodies or the public disagrees, 
changes have to be made according to the complaints and another hearing has to be carried out. This is 
iterated until the project gets accepted.  
After successful hearings DEA announces the tender for an OWF project of a certain size in a specific 
geographical area [12]. Due to competition in this fully transparent tender process the objective is to 
achieve the most-cost effective project by selecting the bidder offering the lowest price per energy 
production [12]. 
Once the owner of the future wind farm is selected the wind farm owner has to make sure to obtain the 
four licenses. The first one granted by the DEA is the license to carry out preliminary investigations. 
The second license is granted to establish offshore wind turbines. The third license is given to exploit 
wind power for a given number of years; in cases the wind farms capacity is higher than 25 MW an 
additional “approval for electricity production in compliance with the electricity legislation” [12] has to 
be made. And, to finalize the tender process a license for the grid connection is needed.  
Open-door-procedure 
In the open-door procedure on the other hand, the initiative for establishing an OWF is taken by the 
project developer. The project developer decides the location and size of the planned wind farm. 
Locations that are already designated for OWFs in the report “Future Offshore Wind Power Sites – 
2025” are reserved for governmental tenders and cannot be chosen by project developers [11]. As in the 
governmental tender procedure the four licenses mentioned above have to be obtained. Apart from the 
pre-investigations the developer additionally has to carry out an EIA in order to get the second license 
for establishing offshore wind turbines. Another difference is that the developer has to pay for the 
transmission of the produced electricity to land [11]. So compared to the governmental tender process, 
an open-door process is more complicated and costly for the wind farm developer. 
In both approaches the support scheme is based on Feed-in premiums (FiP) which means that an 
additional amount is paid for each unit of energy produced on top of the electricity market price [13]. 
The tender scheme is paid by a so called Contract for Difference (CfD) which is a guaranteed payment 
that the developer receives on top of the electricity market price in order to reach the fixed bid price of 
the project. This support scheme applies for each project between 11 and 12 years of normal operations, 
for the first 50000 full load hours (FLH) of operation [13]. OWF projects that are commissioned under 
the open-door procedure receive a fixed premium of 30 €/MWh for equivalent 22000 FLH plus 
3 €/MWh for covering balancing cost. Coastal projects where a 30 % share of the project ownership has 
to be offered to local residents or companies, can receive an extra bonus of 1.3 €/MWh [13].  
3.2. Germany 
There is currently no tender procedure in Germany concerning the permit of OWFs as it exists in 
Denmark or other European countries such as Sweden, the Netherlands or the UK [14]. Instead the 
permitting procedure for OWF’s in Germany is based on the general proceeding on the granting of 
permission (Baugenehmigungsverfahren) and other regulations for offshore wind energy development, 
which is described in the following paragraphs. 
The Offshore Installation Ordinance from January 2012, regulating the permitting procedure of offshore 
wind turbines in the Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) of Germany, aims to simplify the permitting 
procedure. Accordingly, permissions for OWFs in the German EEZ are granted by the Federal Maritime 
and Hydrographic Agency of Germany (Bundesamt für Seeschifffahrt und Hydrographie, BSH). OWFs 
located within the 12 NM zone on the other hand have to be permitted by the corresponding state 
authority. 
==PAGE:19==

The BSH is responsible for identifying potential areas for future OWFs in agreement with the ministry 
for environment, nature protection and reactor safety (Bundesministerium für Umwelt, Naturschutz, Bau 
und Reaktorsicherheit, BMUB), other ministries and the public. This step requires a hearing of the 
federal states. Also grid connection parts passing the EEZ have to be permitted by the BSH.  
The establishment of an OWF project is only permitted when the project ensures the security of 
navigation, does not endanger the environment and when requirements of spatial planning as well as 
other public interests (i.e. material extraction, military, fishing) are secured. In order to retain an obtained 
permit the construction of the OWF at the specific site should begin at least 2.5 years after the granting. 
To avoid the reservation of areas the right to establish and operate wind turbines at the site is limited to 
25 years. The approval procedure for permitting OFW by BSH passes through several phases: 
1. After the wind farm developer submits his proposal it has to be tested if it is detailed enough. If 
it is not the case the wind farm developer gets the opportunity to revise the proposal. At the 
same time affected public stakeholder (e.g. Directorate for waterways and shipping, State 
Mining Authority, Federal Environment Agency, Agency for Nature Conservation) can be 
informed and asked to give statements concerning the plans. The wind farm developer has the 
chance then to review and revise his proposal accordingly. 
 
2. In the second phase also other interest groups (e.g. nature protection, shipping, fishing and wind 
energy organisations) additional to the public stakeholder are included in the procedure. At the 
same time the public gets the chance to participate by publishing the proposal documents. 
Another essential part of the approval procedure is the early involvement of the federal states 
who are concerned with the coastal waters and the corresponding TSO’s being responsible for 
the planning and construction of grid connection. Following the second phase of the approval 
procedure an application conference, in which the wind farm developer has the chance to present 
his project, takes place. This conference serves for the discussion about necessary investigations 
for assessing possible impacts on the marine environment and other competing land use 
interests. 
 
3. Based on the results of the application conference the scope of investigations is determined. The 
wind farm developer has to create a plan which should include a demonstration of safety 
measures, a survey from an approved expert about the quality and safety of the wind turbines, 
a collision risk assessment of ships with the installed wind turbines and an EIA. For OWFs with 
more than 20 turbines an EIA has to be carried out. While the Federal Agency for Nature 
Conversation (Bundesamt für Naturschutz, BfN) is responsible for assessing the impacts of the 
OWF on the environment, the wind farm owner has to finance the investigations of the 
environment at the planned site and also possible mitigation measures that might arise for the 
project. The planning documents created by the wind farm developer will be then sent to involve 
public stakeholder for giving a statement. At the same time the planning documents are made 
public so that anybody whose interests interfere with the project is able to raise objections within 
two weeks. 
 
4. The objections will be discussed with the wind farm developer, authorities and affected people. 
Finally, after possible compensatory measures are being defined and the Directorate for 
waterways and shipping has agreed to the plan, the wind farm project will successfully pass the 
approval procedure. 
Since December 2006 the TSO’s are obliged to ensure the grid connection of offshore wind energy, 
therefore it is not anymore the task of the wind farm developers. There are two TSOs that are responsible 
for the grid connection of the German OWF’s: The Dutch company Tennet TSO GmbH being 
responsible for the North Sea and 50 Hertz Transmission GmbH being responsible for the Baltic Sea. 
The development of the grid connection of German OWFs is currently a rather slow and costly process

since the OWFs are located relatively far away from the shore as compared to the ones in other European 
countries. The reason for the high distances to coast is to protect the UNESCO world heritage Wadden 
Sea. In order to restore the project security for wind farm developers and investors, the grid connection 
and its upcoming questions of liability have been defined in the Energy Industry Act 
(Energiewirtschaftsgesetz, EnWG). 
Another design element that supports the development of offshore wind energy is the German 
Renewable Energy Act (EEG) which obliges TSO’s to give priority to transmit electricity from 
renewable energies. According to this law the offshore wind energy producers are granted a feed-in 
compensation of 184€/MWh during 8 years and 154€/MWh for the following 12 years of support [15]. 
A new reform of the EEG (EEG 2017) introduces public tender procedures for offshore wind energy 
starting in 2017 [16]. Therefore, instead of receiving statutory feed-in tariffs the project developers will 
have to bid for tariffs in public tender procedures which will be run by the German energy regulator 
(Bundesnetzagentur). The new feed-in tariff will be decreased to 154€/MWh for the first 12 years of 
support with a possible extension of 20 years [15]. 
3.3. The UK 
The UK marine areas are divided into marine planning regions by the Marine and Coastal Access Act 
2009 with an associated planning authority that prepares a marine plan for the area [13]. Decisions for 
the siting of OWFs are conducted in two stages: 
The first stage is the national (or strategic) selection of large areas performed by the Department for 
Energy and Climate Change (DECC) in terms of Offshore Energy Strategic Environmental 
Assessments. In the second stage the Crown Estate, which grants leases for the use of UK seabed, 
designs the suitable zones [13] [6]. Under the program of round 3 these zones are awarded to different 
developers in a competitive process [13]. 
In England and Wales different planning authorities are involved depending of the project size [13]: 
Wind farm projects with capacities lower than 100MW are granted by the Marine Management 
Organisation which is responsible for marine licensing for sea activities. The development consent 
application for projects above 100MW is assessed by the Planning Inspectorate, while the Secretary of 
State for Energy and Climate Change makes the final decision. Finally, the consent for any associated 
development (e.g. onshore substation) situated in Wales is given by the corresponding Welsh planning 
authority [13]. 
In Scotland licensing organized by a ‘one-stop-shop’ principle is administered by the Marine Scotland 
Licensing Operations Team [13]. 
Since there is no responsible TSO for the connection of offshore wind energy, the owner and operator 
for a new transmission link is selected by a tender procedure: As compared to other countries the grid 
connection to shore in the UK is generally built by the developer and then outsourced to other entities 
receiving a transmission fee [13]. This outsourcing is done through a tender process and can occur at 
different stages of the construction process. Most commonly, the plant developer builds the connection 
system while the TSO has the responsibility for its operation and ownership [13].  
OWFs in the UK are supported by tradable green certificates (TGC) where an OWF project receives 
two certificates per MWh. The price of the certificates which are “tradable financial assets sold on a 
specific market” is market-based since it is defined by market equilibrium between supply and demand 
for certificates [13]. In March 2017 the support scheme using TGC will be replaced by the auction-based 
feed in tariff structure Contract for Difference [13].

4.2. Methodology for screening process 
As described in Chapter 3 the permitting procedures vary in different countries which also leads to 
differences in the allocation process of potential areas for OWFs. The differences of the legal 
frameworks (regulations, requirements, strategical spatial planning etc.) consequently lead to different 
definition of exclusion areas for offshore wind energy. Also collecting the necessary GIS maps can be 
more difficult in some countries (e.g. concerning military activities) than in other countries. 
Nevertheless, apart from the varying legal framework leading to diverse definition of exclusion areas, 
the general method for finding potential areas is the same in every country. 
The procedure for screening potential areas for future OWFs is done by gathering all the information of 
restricting parameters and defining the resulting excluded areas. Afterwards, the potential of the final 
areas that are left is analysed.  
Mostly GIS is used for such a screening process. The number of parameters that have to be considered 
have to be displayed by GIS layers laid on each other. The information of the GIS-layers belongs to 
different areas of responsibility. A list of the sources where GIS-maps with the necessary information 
can be found for Denmark can be found in the Appendix A. 
Figure 4 shows a suggested procedure for defining the exclusion areas for each parameter. 
Firstly, information about each parameter including regulations and guidelines concerning wind farm 
siting has to be collected. The information can be acquired by addressing the responsible authorities. A 
list of the responsible authorities for each restricting parameter in Denmark can be found in the Appendix 
A. In case information is not found or no clear regulations are existing, the definition of exclusion areas 
can be based on other sources. Other sources can be responsible authorities from other countries (e.g. 
Crown Estate), impact assessments from already existing wind farms etc.  
At the same time, GIS maps showing the spatial distribution of each parameter has to be collected. 
Again, the responsible authorities have to be addressed. In case a GIS map is not available it has to be 
created using available information such as maps of other format, surveys etc. It has to be taken care 
that updated maps of the exclusion zones are used due to the constantly changing parameters.  
Then, the collected information has to be used to define the excluded areas and safety distances where 
offshore wind energy is not possible. 
In the optimal case the defined exclusion areas are discussed with involved stakeholder in order to avoid 
conflicts of interest at later planning stages of a wind farm project. 
When the stakeholder have agreed, the exclusion area is mapped on a GIS layer for each parameter. In 
the end the sum of exclusion areas is subtracted from the area of interest in order to map the final areas 
which have potential for future offshore wind energy. 
It should be noted that the screening process described in this study is a screening of large areas which 
are generally suitable for offshore wind development. Therefore, datasets at a national level are more 
suitable for the analysis than data with highly detailed information. For the planning of individual 
projects at specific sites further more detailed information needs to be collected and investigations need 
to be conducted in order to assess the opportunity within the potential area. 
After the final areas are identified the suitable size, layout, foundations and cost of the future wind farm 
has to be estimated in order to give a preliminary indication about the OWF. For this purpose other 
software like WAsP, FUGA or WindPro can be used.

4.2.1. Geography and geology 
Seabed conditions 
Information of the seabed condition is important when screening suitable areas for OWFs. Also for the 
design of a wind farm i.e. choice of foundation type and wind farm layout as well as risk minimisation 
during the installation phase seabed conditions play an important role. 
Generally, when screening a big area a rather rough mapping of the seabed is used in order to exclude 
areas which are obviously not suitable for installing OWFs. When later on a site has been proposed for 
a planned wind farm seabed surveys have to be undertaken for that specific area using specialized 
vessels. The seabed survey consists of geophysical surveys of bathymetry and seabed features and 
geotechnical surveys of seabed characteristics [18]. This includes amongst others acoustic imaging of 
the seabed (side scan sonar), vertical layering of the seabed (sub-bottom profiles) and detection of 
possible metallic obstruction on the seabed (magnetometer) [19].  
The turbines foundations are designed for each wind farm individually dependent on the soil condition 
at the specific site. There are currently four main foundation types used for seabed conditions ranging 
from soft soil to hard soil or boulders. Monopiles can be used for a wide range of soil types from soft 
clays to even harder soil or boulders [20]. For comparatively soft soil conditions suction bucket and 
jacket foundations can be used. Especially for the suction bucket rather homogeneous soil without hard 
layers or boulders is optimal. For firm seabed conditions on the other hand a gravity base structure can 
be used.

As already mentioned when looking at a specific site, geophysical and geotechnical surveys have to be 
conducted analysing locations of possible seabed depressions or objects such as boulders, archaeological 
objects and unexploded ordnances (UXOs). Archaeological objects, wrecks and UXOs are described in 
the following two paragraphs. 
Archaeological objects 
It is possible to find many ancient monuments and wrecks in the Danish Seas. All monuments and 
wrecks that have been lying over 100 years on the seabed are protected even if not registered in the data 
base of the Danish Agency for Culture (see [22]) There are safety regulations concerning cultural and 
historical interests in the Danish Seas: Cultural and historical interests in form of Stone Age settlements, 
high concentration of artefacts, cultural and discarded battle shipwrecks older than 100 years and their 
cargoes, ballast bunker, harbours and other objects are secured under § 29g paragraph 1 and 2 of the 
Museum Act and may not be removed or destroyed without permission of the Danish Agency for 
Culture.  
Therefore, in the detailed project planning phase of a wind farm investigations for possible 
archaeological remains have to be carried out since shipwrecks, Stone Age settlements and other 
artefacts can be found in marine areas. This investigation may include both a review of archaeological 
archive as well as diving inspections for analysing the specific area in order to determine the cultural 
heritage potential of the site [7].

UXO 
Sea mines, so called unexploded ordnance (UXO) which were deployed in the Danish Seas during WWI 
and WWII still represent a risk for marine work [23].  
When planning a wind farm at a specific site it is therefore necessary to carry out a risk assessment 
based on “DS/ISO 31000:2009 Risk management – Principles and guidelines”. If a risk due to UXOs at 
the site exists the wind farm developer is responsible for risk mitigation and is recommended to get aid 
from a professional UXO consultant for that decision [23]. Risk can be mitigated by relocating wind 
turbine foundations or rerouting cables [24].  
Specific locations of remaining UXOs are not accurate enough since not all UXOs have been detected 
yet and wartime records regarding mine laying are not accurate. Also the fact that UXOs can migrate 
hundreds of meters makes it difficult to map the exact positions of possible UXOs. Therefore in this 
study, for screening the North Sea, no area is excluded due to possible UXO presence. Nevertheless, in 
detailed project planning it is important to conduct a risk assessment concerning UXOs.

Generally wind farms are placed at distances of 5 to 50 km distance from shore [26]. In order to make a

Distance to other countries 
Placing wind farms close to the territory of a neighbouring country makes cross-border wind farm 
projects in collaboration with the neighbouring countries possible. This ensures strengthening of the 
regional energy market and increases the energy supply security due to higher capacity.  
Nevertheless, when planning a wind farm close to the territory of a neighbouring country it has to be 
considered that different regulations and restrictions might apply there. 
In the example of the planned combined grid project Kriegers Flak the wind turbines have to be placed 
with a minimum distance of 500 m to the Swedish and German EEZ borders. While this minimum 
distance has to be measured from the centre of the turbine foundations, the blades can enter the 500 
meter zone [30]. 
Therefore, in this study a 500 m minimum distance to the EEZ border of neighbouring countries is 
considered. 
4.2.2. Land use 
According to the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (1982) (UNCLOS) coastal states have the right 
to exploit natural resources in their own Economic Exclusive Zone (EEZ). The EEZ of a coastal country 
can stretch out to 200 NM from its coast. Therefore, the only owner of the Danish sea territory is the 
Danish state. The Ministry of Transport is the responsible authority for regulating the control of the 
Danish sea territory if no other sector ministry is assigned by law like e.g. the authority for granting 
permission for spatial planning [31]. Additionally, several ministries have the responsibility for different 
sectors (i.e. fishery, raw material extraction, energy production etc.). Areas in the Danish Seas can be 
==PAGE:31==
Screening potential areas in the Danish North Sea 
 
20 
 
licensed for sea-based activities (e.g. for wind energy, material extraction, oil and gas activities etc.) to 
concessionaires for a certain amount of time. 
Denmark currently does not have any specific maritime spatial plan for regulating the use of marine 
space [31]. Instead the use of the Danish Sea is regulated by various sectoral laws (e.g. Raw Materials 
Act, Subsoil Act, Electricity Supply Act, Fishery Act etc.) which makes it more complicated to plan sea-
based activities.  
The planning process of new sea-based activities therefore requires consideration of the number of 
sectoral laws regarding the use of the Danish Seas and discussion with stakeholder which might be 
impacted by the planned project. 
To help balancing out the different stakeholder interests and to support a coordinated development of 
the use of sea space Denmark is preparing a maritime spatial plan which will be issued latest by 31 
March 2021. 
Navigation conditions 
Regarding conflicts of interests in the screening process of OWFs, maritime traffic is one of the most 
important issues [32]. Especially in the North and Baltic Sea where the sea traffic is very dense special 
care has to be taken when siting wind farms in order not to compromise the safety of passing ships. 
As mentioned before Denmark does not have a maritime spatial plan for regulating the use of sea space. 
Nevertheless, the Danish Maritime Authority (DMA) is the primary authority to lay down requirements 
in connection with OWFs as in the Danish law no. 72 on maritime safety from 17 January 2014. The 
legal authority to demand and accept marking is stated in order no. 45 from 22 January 2015. 
An agreed planning guideline defines shipping lanes as the “basic structure of any marine spatial plan” 
[32] meaning that maritime traffic should be guaranteed “unobstructed non-harmful transit” through 
Danish waters [33]. Therefore as a main principle OWFs cannot be placed in immediate vicinity of 
agreed national and international shipping routes including traffic separation schemes in connection with 
those routes [33]. The same applies to highly frequented waters such as entrances to harbour and fjords 
[33]. Building wind farms near to the common shipping routes can be dangerous since ships might 
collide with the structures.  
In comparison to travel on land maritime travel routes have to be considered with a large margin of error 
since ships are affected by wave movements which can lead to deviations of the planned course [34]. 
Further investigations such as an assessment for the ship collision risk has to be conducted and risk 
reduction measures (e.g. marking the wind farm as warning for ships to prevent collisions with turbines) 
have to be implemented. In order to avoid collision with the turbine, ships generally navigate around the 
turbine structures with a minimum distance of 500 m according to [35].

Air traffic 
Also airspace has to be considered when planning a wind farm. Placing wind turbines close to an airport 
can influence systems related to aviation e.g. radar systems, communication systems and radio-
navigation systems and thus flight safety. Therefore, there are several regulations and guidelines 
regarding safety distances and markings which are described in this section. 
Wind turbines have to be sited with a distance of 15 km to an airport. When planning a wind farm within 
the safety distance of 15 km NAVIAR/NUAC (Nordic Unified Air Traffic Control) has to be contacted 
which is the license holder and is responsible for en-route air traffic management.  
To assure that wind turbine installations do not interfere with the safety of air traffic wind turbines with 
a total height of more than 100 m have to be approved by Civil Aviation Administration-Denmark 
(CAA-Denmark) [39]. Wind turbines taller than 150 m have to be equipped with high-intensity, white 
flashing lights. For turbines with a total height of 100-150 m a specific assessment for the need of 
marking will be carried out by CAA-Denmark amongst others taking into account Danish Defence’s 
assessments of military flights in the area [39]. 
==PAGE:34==

Normally a marking with low-intensity fixed red obstruction lights on the nacelle and a white painting 
of the wind turbine is sufficient [39]. For areas where special air safety is needed, the turbines need to 
be marked additionally with medium-intensity flashing obstruction lights. Those regulations ensure the 
visibility of the OWF at a suitable distance in order to allow the pilot to take necessary operational 
actions in time [39] and are stated in the BL 3-10 Regulations for Civil Aviation [40]. 
Ideally, the requirements for air traffic markings should be defined with CAA-Denmark before an EIA 
(if required) is conducted. In some cases where new wind farms are constructed the minimum flight 
altitude has to be increased and for certain turbine heights the visual navigation for the airspace might 
be limited.

Radars & Radio transmission networks 
Large OWFs can also have an impact on radio transmission networks, likewise coastal radars with 
different type of activities such as related to military, marine, civilian and meteorological operators [42].  
Radio transmission networks 
Radio communication is the transmission and reception of electromagnetic waves with frequencies 
lower than 3000 GHz [42]. Radio communication can be used for a wide range of purposes such as air 
traffic navigation, private maritime, telephone, mobile, remote or survey data.  
Concerning radio-and telecommunications the radio relay operator determines the safety distance 
between a certain project or radio channel type [43]. Generally, when siting a wind farm the general 
guidelines of the Danish Business Authority on distance to radio links have to be considered [33]. The 
recommended minimum distance a wind farm should have to the line of sight of a radio relay link is 
200 m [7].

Radar  
Radars normally operate by transmitting high powered radio pulses and detecting the signal that is 
reflected on objects (such as aircrafts and ships) within range and line of sight [42]. Depending on the 
type of radar this provides information about the target range, height, bearing and direction of travel 
[42]. In case of a wind farm situated in the range and line of sight of a radar, the spinning blades of the 
wind turbines will falsely be detected as a target [29]. This radar interference is also called clutter and 
reduces the radar’s ability to detect targets close to a wind farm [47].  
Although there might be more radars affected by wind farms only meteorological, military, civil 
aeronautical and marine radars are described in the following paragraphs. 
Meteorological radars 
Meteorological radars predict weather patterns by tracking rainfall and precipitation. Since turbines 
reach significant heights they can appear in the line-of-sights of weather radars [48]. Wind farms can 
generate significant clutter at distances of 100 km and more which without mitigation falsifies the rain 
fall estimates in the order of tens of mm making them useless for most purposes [48]. Therefore “relative 
altitudes of wind turbines with respect to the radar sites” are relevant parameter for the approval process 
of wind farm sites [48].  
Currently, there is no guidance given by the Danish meteorological organisations regarding the 
installation of structures close to the weather radar stations, but guidelines regarding radar technology 
are expected to be provided in the future [29]. Nevertheless, a guidance which is provided by the British 
Meteorological Office has been used in this study. It recommends to avoid placing turbines within 5 km 
of a radar installation since there will be unavoidable adverse impacts, additionally within a range of 20 
km of a radar, an impact assessment must be conducted [42].

Military and air defence radars  
Military radars and air defence radars are used to control the airspace around military installation [51] 
and can detect the position and speed of an aircraft at 80-400 km distance. The network of air defence 
radars under the responsibility of the Royal Danish Air Force (RDAF) provides early warning of an 
“impending air attack”, “missile defences” and coordinates land, sea and air defence assets [42]. It 
includes i.e. stationary and transportable long range surveillance radars and command and control 
facilities [52]. 
Regarding the impact of OWFs on military radars systems, there is currently no known guidance [42]. 
Nevertheless, in case a wind farm causes any unacceptable impacts on the air defence radar systems, the 
Danish military may require the introduction of mitigation measures. Therefore when planning a wind 
farm in line of sight of a military radar it is recommended to carry out an impact assessment regarding 
the position and specific type of military radars. 
Since there are no regulations regarding the safety distance between OWFs and military radar 
installations, studies assessing the impact of the existing wind farms Horns Rev 1 to 3 and Versterhav 
Syd have been used as a guidance in this study. All wind farms Horns Rev 1, Horns Rev 2 and Horns

Civil aeronautical radars 
Civil aeronautical radars are used for monitoring the air traffic [42]. There are two different types of 
radars, primary surveillance radar and secondary surveillance radar. A primary surveillance radar is a 
conventional radar having a range of 60 NM (111 km). It can determine the azimuth and range of an 
aircraft but not its height [42]. A secondary surveillance radar on the other hand has a significantly 
longer range of 250 NM and is able to provide more information about the aircraft. It can additionally 
receive information about the aircrafts height from the transponder which is an on-board equipment 
fitted to all larger aircrafts [42]. 
Since secondary surveillance radars operate at bigger ranges they are more impacted by wind farms 
[54]. Wind turbines situated within line of sight can generate clutter effects resulting in increased number 
of unwanted detected objects and hide the presence of aircrafts close by [47]. These impacts can be a 
problem especially for Civil Air traffic Control (ATC) for en-route and terminal manoeuvring areas 
reducing the air traffic safety. Also Air Defence (AD) communities might be concerned about the impact 
of wind farms since it can reduce their ability to detect clandestine approach to the country [54]. 
Currently, there is no guidance related to the impact of OWF installations on civil aeronautical radars 
provided by the Danish authorities [47]. However useful guidance can be found in CAP764 ‘CAA Policy 
and Guidelines on Wind Turbines’ provided by the Civil Aviation Authority (CAA) which is the British 
aviation regulator. It states that most impacts are expected at a distance of 24 km between the wind 
turbine and the radar receiver and therefore a discussion with the respective radar operator has to be 
conducted when planning turbines in that specific range [47]. 
In Denmark the governmental air navigation provider Naviair analyses the consequences of wind farms 
related to radar sites and other Air navigation equipment using EUR region ICAO documents (EUR 
DOC 015, published on the ICAO website) as guidelines. When any plans are under processing, Naviair 
will be informed in a hearing, by either the local authorities, or by the planning company used by the 
owner of the planned wind farms. In such a hearing Naviair will give an official answer on the 
consequences for Naviair [55]. 
The scope of those impacts are dependent on the spacing within the wind farm, the type of radar and 
also the air traffic environment at the location of the wind farm which means whether it is located at 
arrival or departure flight paths of an airport [47]. When a wind farm is located in the range of a civil 
aeronautical radar several mitigation measures can be used such as rerouting aircrafts around the wind 
farm in order to avoid its signal being lost due to clutter effect, the use of in-fill radars which detect 
aircrafts around the wind farm, or adjusting the elevation of the radar antenna [47].  
For the screening of potential areas in this study a safety distance of 15 km towards airports shown in 
Figure 13 has already been considered.

Lighthouses 
Lighthouses are designed for navigation aid and mark dangerous coastlines, shallow water, nautical 
channels and entries to harbours. Due to the usage of electronic navigation systems, the number of 
lighthouses has decreased. However, in case of failure of electronic navigation systems lighthouses play 
an important role and thus placing a wind farm in its light range can cause adverse effects on the marine 
safety. 
The North Sea includes 69 lights [59]. The lighthouses have ranges between 1.5 nm and 23.2 nm, which 
corresponds to 2.8 km to 43.0 km. A map of the lighthouses in the Danish Seas can be found in official 
nautical sea charts such as the OpenSeaMap [60]. 
There are no regulations concerning a safety distance towards existing lighthouses in Denmark. 
Nevertheless, in case a planned wind farm impacts an already existing lighthouse or any other 
navigational aid, action must be taken to mitigate the impact by constructing additional lighthouses or 
changing the existing lighthouse. Expenses that arise hereby must be paid by the OWF constructor [61]. 
Since in this study a distance to the coast of 5 km is already applied in the screening process of potential 
areas, no exclusions are made due to lighthouses. Nevertheless, at a distance of up to 43.0 km (dependent 
on the lighthouse range) a wind farm can already compromise the light beam of a lighthouse and an EIA 
might be necessary after choosing a site close to a lighthouse.

Grid connection to land 
With the increasing share of wind energy, integrating the electricity of wind farms into the transmission 
grid became a big technological and economical challenge. Compared to large-scale conventional power 
plants wind power shows a higher imbalance between production and consumption due to the variations 
in the wind [1]. 
Figure 24 shows the transmission network in Denmark which is connected to Germany, Norway and 
Sweden including the location of substations, power stations and different voltage lines. Denmark’s 
transmission grid is divided into a western part and an eastern part. The western part is connected to the 
European continental grid while the eastern part is connected to the Nordic grid, both being connected 
via a 600 MW DC connection [29]. 
As mentioned before when planning an OWF, the task of the Danish TSO Energinet.dk is to build and 
operate the technical installations for the grid connection of the wind farm to the electricity grid onshore 
==PAGE:44==
Screening potential areas in the Danish North Sea 
 
33 
 
[64]. This includes the installation of offshore transformer substations, cabling as well as all necessary 
reinforcement onshore. The costs occurring herewith are paid by the electricity consumer directly. The 
wind farm owner on the other hand is responsible for the internal grid in the wind farm from the wind 
turbines to the transformer substation. 
There are three basic options for connecting OWFs with the common grid onshore: 
The first one is to connect at the wind farms internal voltage. Due to the high losses in low voltage lines, 
each wind turbine has its own transformer which increases the voltage of the wind turbine to the medium 
voltage line which is then the internal voltage of the wind farm and can be chosen to be between 20-24 
kV and 30-34 kV [26]. One example for this type of connection is the OWF Middelgrunden which is 
located at 3 km distance to the coast of Copenhagen with a total capacity of 40MW and a connection 
voltage of 30 kV which is transmitted via medium voltage AC lines.  
The second option is to step up the voltage to a higher voltage level before transmitting the power to 
land. This requires the installation of an offshore transformer substation which is more expensive but at 
the same time reduces the cable losses. One example for this type of grid connection is Horns Rev I 
which has a capacity of 160 MW and is connected to land via 150 kV high voltage AC cables over the 
distance of 15 km. While most of the already existing and planned nearshore wind farms (e.g. Kriegers 
Flak, Horns Rev, Anholt) are connected at low voltages with a total output of 33 kV the near future 
solution for nearshore wind farms will be to use 66 kV cables as it is already planned for the coming 
nearshore wind farm tenders. 
The third type of grid connection is generally used for long distances to the coast where a high voltage 
DC cable is used. This requires even more expensive equipment because an AC-DC converter is needed 
additionally and therefore is only cost effective for high distances of more than 80 km to coast [65]. An 
example therefore is Borkum2/BARD with a capacity of 400 MW, a connection distance of 203 km and 
a connection voltage of 150 kV.

To conclude, when siting an OWF several aspects have to be considered regarding the grid connection. 
The distance to the closest point of common coupling (PCC) plays an important role and also if the PCC 
is strong enough for the integration of the wind farm or if alternatively an enhancement of the existing 
electricity grid is needed, which would result in higher costs. The selection of the cables’ voltage level 
and the choice of AC or DC transmission depend on the connection distance. As it can be seen in Figure

North Sea can be fed. Taking a look into the future where new optimized technologies are likely to be 
available, which will make the installation of transformer substations and submarine cables less 
expensive and thus enable the siting of OWFs further away from the shore. Therefore in this study, no 
area is excluded from the potential areas. The effect of the distance to the next PCC, however, will be 
analysed later on.

thus allows navigation and fishing within the wind farm [68]. Nevertheless, protection zones with 
prohibition against anchoring and fishing with trawl are established due to the cables and a wind farm 
can apply for a 50 m safety zone around each structure as it is done the case of the OWF Anholt [68]. 
Compared to other land use interests such as material extraction the siting of new OWFs requires a 
higher distance to existing wind farms due to wake. Studies have been conducted to analyse the wake 
effect on a future cluster of wind farms with 531 turbines. Results show that at a distance of 15 km 
between the large wind farms the wake will not be noticeable anymore [8]. 
In order to avoid high wake effects that could decrease the energy output of a wind farm significantly a 
distance of 5 km to current wind farms in the Danish North Sea (Horns Rev 1, Horns Rev 2, Rønland) 
is excluded from the potential areas. Also the OWFs in the German Seas close to the border of the 
Danish EEZ have to be considered (i.e. DanTysk). There is also a number of planned OWFs in the 
German Seas directly at the border to Denmark but a safety distance to those are not considered in this 
study. 
Fishing 
OWFs can have a significant impact on commercial fishery activities in the area where the wind farm is 
placed. If an OWF interferes with the interests of fisheries, the fishermen have to be compensated for 
their losses. The costs regarding the compensation for fisheries are calculated based on official catch 
figures for the specific area and on assessments of the type of fisheries important for the site. The loss 
is calculated for the area of the wind farm including a 500 m protection zone around it [7].  
Therefore, the wind farm developer has to negotiate a compensation agreement with the affected 
fisheries according to sections 76-80 of Fiskeriloven (the Danish Fisheries Act). In case of Horns Rev 
OWF the negotiated compensation to fisheries cost almost DKK 3 million [7]. 
Since the favourable fishing places in Denmark are generally not published by fisheries due to the 
existing competition, the interest of local commercial fishing will be taken into account when planning 
the design of an individual wind farm in detail and not for screening a big area as the North Sea [7]. 
Therefore, in this study no area is excluded due to fishery interests. 
4.2.3. Nature 
When planning new OWFs it is important to site them at locations where they do not cause any 
unnecessary impacts on the environment [10]. Environmental impacts that wind farms can cause have 
been investigated in a detailed environmental monitoring program carried out for the two large OWFs 
Horns Rev and Nysted in the period 1999 to 2006. The outcome of this investigation shows that there 
are no significant consequences for nature emerging from the OWFs. Nevertheless the impacts of each 
planned wind farm have to be specified and mitigated and regarding the growing offshore wind energy 
sector more research is needed concerning the cumulative effect of wind farms. 
Apart from Natura 2000 which will be described in the next paragraph no area has to be excluded for 
reason of nature and animal. Nevertheless, guidelines and regulations for the construction and operation 
of wind farms are described in this chapter. 
Natura 2000 
The nature protection areas are specified by Natura 2000. A map of Natura 2000 areas in the Danish 
Seas is shown in Figure 27. Those areas are designated to protect habitat types, wild animals and plants 
which are rare, endangered or characteristic for EU countries [69]. There are exceptions where the 
construction of wind farms is interfering with Natura 2000 areas. One example is the Kriegers Flak wind 
farm which is planned to be built in the Baltic Sea. Its cable installation onshore is planned to cross the

Marine mammals 
The noise produced during the construction of a wind farm can have a significant impact on the sea 
mammals particularly on porpoises. Wind farms both in construction but also in operation can cause the 
animals to avoid the area. The noise produced during the construction phase can affect marine mammals 
close by in the way of permanent and temporary hearing loss [71]. During the operation phase the 
emitted noise is only audible for animals in direct vicinity of the wind turbines. 
To mitigate this impact the animals have to be scared away from the area before the pile driving starts 
and additional measures (e.g. usage of bubble curtain system) can be taken to reduce the noise impact 
[71]. Also the maximum noise level of the wind farm should not exceed the maximum noise limit 
according to the noise regulations for wind turbines which are described in Order No. 1284 of 
15/12/2011 in the EIA regulations. 
Generally, seals are unaffected by the construction and operation of a wind farm as experience from the 
Horns Rev and Nysted OWFs has shown. For both wind farms the amount of porpoises in that area 
dropped. After Horns Rev started to operate the number of porpoises recovered, while in Nysted the sea 
mammals were slowly coming back after an operation time of two years [7]. 
Birds  
The construction of wind farms in the sea can have different effects on birds. First of all wind turbines 
represent an obstacle for migrating birds and birds foraging in the area (e.g. velvet scoter, black scoter, 
long-tailed duck). Therefore those birds might change their migration local route in the feeding area [7]. 
This could affect their breeding and survival [7]. Also due to the installation of wind turbines habitats 
may be lost due to a risk of collision for bird flying above the wind farm. Nevertheless, observations of 
Horns Rev and Nysted have shown that those effects on birds are small.

Fish 
Fish can be affected by OWFs both negatively but also positively: 
As for the sea mammals also fish are affected and scared away by the noise during the construction 
phase of a wind farm. Also studies at the OWF Nysted have shown that the fish changed their behavior 
around the 132 kV sea cables. It has not yet been confirmed though if the electromagnetic field caused 
by the sea cables can be related to the change of the fish’s behavior [7].  
On the other hand the turbine’s foundation and the surrounding erosion protection functions as artificial 
habitats for animals and plants. This can attract fish to the wind farm site and therefore change the 
existing fish communities [7].  
Benthic fauna and vegetation 
Other environmental impacts such as on the benthic fauna and vegetation also have to be included in the 
EIA when planning an OWF.  
The foundation is surrounded by a scour protection which aims to decrease the risk of scouring due to 
the wave and current action. The protection mostly consists of stones set around the foundation at 
distances of several meters [7]. Studies have shown that this increases habitat heterogeneity and the hard 
bottom communities. Therefore at the area where the wind farm is located biomass is increased which 
serves as food for fish and seabirds which might increase the number of fish as well as the range of fish 
species [72]. 
Since observations at Horns Rev and Nysted have shown rather positive local effects of offshore wind 
turbines on benthic fauna and vegetation, no area is excluded in this study regarding this aspect.

5.5. DTOC software 
The DTOC software is a result of the EERA DTOC (European Energy Research Alliance - Design Tool 
for Offshore Wind farm Clusters) project which aimed to deliver a robust and efficient software for the 
planning of OWF clusters [8]. The project was funded by the EU and ran from January 2012 to June 
2015 under the coordination of the Technical University of Denmark – DTU Wind Energy [8].  
The software is composed of existing models for the design of OWFs focusing on wind farm wake losses 
for spatial modelling and electrical cabling for interconnection optimisation. Also a precise energy yield 
prediction for the wind farms or windfarm clusters is included in order to make investment decisions 
and benchmarking of different wind farm designs easier (see Figure 32) [8]. 
 
Figure 32: Concept of the DTOC software [8] 
Tools embedded in the software are meteorological mesoscale models, wake models, wind resource 
assessment tools, models for calculation of electrical flow and networks and other specialized models. 
The tools which are used in this study for assessing the different sites are described in detail in the 
following chapters. 
 
Figure 33: Overview of the DTOC design tool structure [8]

DTOC is developed by a cooperation of researchers and directed at wind farm developer and strategic 
planners for OWFs. The end-users can be consultants, research institutions, manufacturers and system 
operators. The decision of the models being integrated is based on the user requirements from industrial 
partners [78]. 
The structure of the software is illustrated in Figure 33. The DTOC platform on the central server can 
be accessed by a web interface via a standard internet browser on a local computer. For changing 
geometrical properties such as turbine position, cabling layout etc. the current wind farm design project 
can be opened via QGIS.  
The DTOC software has potential to be a very helpful tool for benchmarking selected areas. For the 
preliminary design of the OWF at one area, different scenarios can be created. An advantage of the 
software is the optimized workflow which combines models for calculating the wind resource, wind 
farm effects, cable losses and costs including uncertainty modelling and risk management. The software 
gives an estimation of the net AEP and LCoE of each scenario which makes it easy to compare the 
different scenarios and to identify how changing different parameters (e.g. layout, foundation) optimizes 
the wind farm design.  
5.5.1. WAsP model 
The Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program (WAsP) is an industry-standard software package 
for bankable wind resource assessment and siting of wind turbines and wind farms [8]. It is used 
worldwide by many companies for wind resource and energy yield assessments and characterisation of 
wind conditions and site suitability [79].  
WAsP is able to perform vertical and horizontal extrapolation of wind climate statistics and to describe 
the wind flow over different terrains (offshore, simple terrain, complex terrain) and close to sheltering 
obstacles with the help of several physical models [8]. 
The wind resource assessment and wind farm calculations are based on measured data at or close to the 
site of the wind farm [80]. The implementation follows the so-called wind atlas methodology as shown 
in Figure 34.  
 
Figure 34: Wind atlas methodology of WAsP [80] 
==PAGE:57==
Benchmark of selected areas 
 
46 
 
In the analysis part the generalized wind climatology is calculated from measured data with the help of 
meteorological models. This generalized wind climatology is then used for calculating the wind climate 
at any specific site in the reverse process (application of wind atlas data) [80]. With the specific wind 
farm data (hub height, power curve and layout) and the terrain effects, the Total Gross AEP of the wind 
farm is calculated using a flow model that predicts the wind climates at the wind turbine sites of the 
wind farms. The Total Farm AEP is then calculated by taking into account the wake losses which are 
estimated at each turbine site using a wake model. In order to calculate the Net AEP value at the PCC 
the technical losses are estimated and subtracted from the Total Farm AEP [80]. Finally, using an 
estimate for the uncertainty of the whole energy yield process, the net AEP with a certain probability of 
exceedance, e.g. Total Net AEP P75, is calculated.

5.6.1. Detailed O&M Costs 
The operational phase is essential for the economic success of an OWF as compared to other life-cycle 
phases [85]. The O&M costs of an OWF project represent about a quarter of its total life-time cost [86] 
and its estimation is often associated with high uncertainties. Therefore, when planning a wind farm a 
detailed O&M cost estimation is necessary.  
O&M Costs include expenses for crew, vessels and parts for operation and maintenance services for the 
wind turbine generator, support structure, electrical infrastructure and the transformer station [87]. 
Additionally, operational expenses also include transmission costs, insurances and for some cases land 
lease [87].  
The maintenance service can be divided into preventive maintenance and corrective maintenance.

be changed by the software user accordingly to the wind farm project. The model does not explicitly 
take into account the wind farm’s distance to the next O&M port and weather conditions at the site. 
Nevertheless, O&M costs mainly depend on the distance to the next O&M port [84].

5.7. Summary and discussion 
The benchmark results of the seven sites in the Danish North Sea show that site 3 (see Figure 40) has 
the highest potential for offshore wind energy with an estimated LCoE of 92.2 €/MWh for a 300 MW 
wind farm, while site 1 is the most costly site with LCoE of 98.0 €/MWh. On the first view the 
comparatively low costs for the far-shore sites are surprising but can be explained with the good wind 
resource at site with average wind speeds of higher than 10 m/s. 
 
Figure 40: Seven sites selected for benchmarking inside potential areas for offshore wind energy  
The LCoE which ranges between 92.2 €/MWh and 98.0 €/MWh for a 300 MW wind farm seem to be 
representative regarding the goal of eleven European companies who have declared to achieve LCoE 
for offshore wind of 80 €/MWh and below by 2025 [90].  
Parameters that have an influence on the cost calculation in the DTOC model are distance to coast, water 
depth and wind resource and lead to different foundation and cable costs as well as different AEP thus 
O&M costs added per AEP and revenue cost. 
As mentioned earlier, the calculations made with the cost model implemented in DTOC do not take into 
account different logistical concepts used for O&M activities. Also different design strategies in order 
to optimize the performance of the wind farm are not taken into account in the benchmark analysis. 
Furthermore, the benchmarking should only indicate a rough estimation of LCoE for each site. When 
planning a wind farm at a specific site further aspects such as the seabed conditions have to be precisely 
investigated since it can change the costs significantly. 
The DTOC calculations contain several uncertainties. These include uncertainties regarding the wind 
resource, water depth and distance to the coast. As mentioned before, especially for the sites very close 
to the coast, the WRF simulation from which the wind resource is obtained, can have higher uncertainty

different sub aspects in the design of a wind farm (layout, foundation, turbine size, wind resource etc.)

6. FMEA analysis 
As mentioned in the problem statement, a FMEA is carried out in order to measure the improvement of 
the procedure for screening potential areas. A FMEA is used for assessing the reliability of a process 
[91]. Firstly, the process is subdivided into different functions. For each function all possible failure 
modes and its effects are identified. Following, all possible causes for each failure mode are listed. These 
three aspects are presented as different column in a table (see Appendix C). In order to assess the process, 
the severity S of each failure mode has to be rated with a number on a scale from 1 (= insignificant) to 
10 (= catastrophical). Also the occurrence O for each cause of failure is rated with a number from 1 (= 
extremely unlikely) to 10 (= inevitable). The multiplication of severity S and occurrence O results in a 
number called criticality which ranks the cause of failure modes: 
𝐶𝑟𝑖𝑡= 𝑆 ∙𝑂 
(3) 
 
Less sever failure modes give a smaller criticality than more severe failure modes. Therefore, the 
criticality can give guidance for the order the failure modes of the process should be addressed [91]. By 
summing up all criticalities one gets a number assessing the whole process. 
First, a FMEA is carried out to assess the state of the art of the screening process for potential areas. 
Then, a FMEA is carried out again to assess the screening process with having the results of this thesis 
available. The difference of the criticalities indicates the improvement of the screening process. 
Therefore, two FMEA tables are prepared:  
 
FMEA 1 for assessing the improvement with the documentation the screening process applied 
in the Danish North Sea 
 
FMEA 2 for assessing the improvement with additional information gained from the DTOC 
model 
In FMEA 1 the occurrence of each cause of failure has to be rated for three cases: 
1. Current practice of screening process 
2. Screening potential areas in Denmark with having documentation for Danish North Sea 
3. Screening potential areas in another country with having documentation for Danish North Sea 
By having a criticality of the screening process without and with having a documentation the 
improvement of the process can simply be measured with the difference of criticality.  
In FMEA 2 the screening process is assessed for three cases: 
1. Screening potential areas with having documentation 
2. Screening potential areas with having documentation + benchmark of selected areas 
3. Screening potential areas with having documentation + sensitivity analysis on LCoE (wind 
speed, turbine size, layout etc.) 
Here again, that improvement of the screening process can be measured with the difference of criticality 
between case 1 and case 2. Furthermore, case 3 is added in order to assess the improvement if a 
sensitivity analysis on the LCoE was conducted which can be part of the future work. 
The FMEA tables filled out by the experts can be found in the Appendix C. For each FMEA table filled 
out by an expert the percentage of improvement is calculated using the criticality values for the different 
cases. The average percentage of improvement for the four cases are shown in Table 30.

6.1. Discussion 
Concerning the improvement of the screening process in other countries, the collection of data and GIS 
maps specific to the Danish North Sea is not expected to be of significant value for the allocation of 
potential areas in other countries. Nevertheless, the method applied, the list of different agencies to 
contact and criteria catalogue of all parameters that have to be considered can be of value beyond the 
Danish borders, especially in places where offshore wind energy is less developed yet. It has to be noted 
though that the criteria catalogue cannot be completely adapted to other regions but has to be adjusted 
to the characteristics of nature and administration in the specific region. 
Benchmarking selected sites on the other hand provides additional information which can be of value 
for the screening of potential areas. It indicates the impact of different site-specific parameters (e.g. wind 
speed, water depth and distance to coast) on the LCoE of a wind farm. This additional information could 
prove considerably valuable in the decision-making process when screening potential areas. 
In order to receive a FMEA result which is representative for all relevant participants in the market, the 
number of experts participating ought to be higher. It should be stated that a considerable number of 
wind farm developers, wind farm planners and consultant companies have been contacted in this study. 
However, many of the contacted people did not regard themselves as experts in the field of selecting 
sites for offshore wind energy or refused to pass on information for reasons of confidentiality. 
==PAGE:71==

7. Conclusion 
This study presents a systematic collection of requirements for the screening process i.e. GIS maps, 
involved governmental agencies, legal framework analysis and technical criteria catalogue as a guide 
for wind farm developers and strategical planners to deploy offshore wind projects. Although this study 
concentrates on the Danish North Sea, the methodology can be applied not only in Denmark but also in 
other countries where offshore wind technology has not been exploited yet. This collection implies the 
adoption of requirements given by governmental institutions in neighbouring countries e.g. Great Britain 
and Germany. 
The benchmark of selected sites on the other hand delivers additional information, which can be of 
considerable value for the screening of potential areas. From the total of 21 OWF designed (with three 
different capacities 200, 300 and 500 MW in 7 selected areas) the calculations made in EERA DTOC 
show as expected that different site-specific parameters affect the LCoE (i.e. wind resource, water depth, 
distance to coast, O&M). The LCoE ranges between 92.2 €/MWh and 98.0 €/MWh for a 300 MW wind 
farm. These values seem to be representative regarding the goal of eleven European companies to 
achieve LCoE for offshore wind of 80 €/MWh and below by 2025 [2]. However, the cost calculation in 
DTOC only gives a rough estimation of expected costs for a wind farm. Therefore, when planning a 
single OWF at a chosen site, further investigations at site and a more detailed cost calculation has to be 
conducted.  
The experts interviewed for this study say that the collection of data and GIS maps specific to the Danish 
North Sea is not expected to be of significant value for the allocation of potential areas in other countries. 
Nevertheless, there is an agreement among the experts that the material provided about the method 
applied, the list of different agencies to contact and criteria catalogue of all restricting parameters can 
be of considerable value for other countries, especially in places where offshore wind energy is less 
developed yet.  
The general aim in the identification process of potential areas is to achieve highest energy harvesting 
at lowest cost while at the same time reducing the impact on society and environment as much as 
possible. The results of this study are a first step to optimize this process. With an optimized process, 
additional suitable sites can be identified. Furthermore, a more efficient procedure can be achieved, 
saving time and unexpected problems at late planning stage e.g. due to not considering certain restricting 
parameters. Therefore, optimizing the process of allocating potential areas can reduce the cost of 
offshore wind energy and push forward the development.

8. Future work 
The DTOC software gives a representative estimation of the LCoE of a wind farm compared to current 
costs of existing OWFs and the declared goal to achieve energy costs for OWF of 80€/MWh by 2025. 
However, it can be further improved by implementing a more detailed O&M cost calculation which 
includes the different logistical concepts for O&M operations. 
Future work can be conducted by creating a software with a GIS interface that automates the process of 
filtering out parts from the potential area that are not suitable for offshore wind energy. It should consider 
all the parameters from the criteria catalogue presented in this study and suggest areas which have 
potential for offshore wind energy. Therefore, collected GIS-maps and information about regulations 
and guidelines found in this study can be implemented in the software. Such a tool could then be 
connected with the DTOC software in order to benchmark the potential areas. 
Furthermore, more detailed investigations can be conducted using the DTOC software. A sensitivity 
analysis on the LCoE can be performed to measure the impact of varying parameters: 
 
Wind speed 
 
Water depth 
 
Distance to coast 
 
Turbine size 
 
Turbine Spacing 
 
Foundation type 
 
Costs 
 
Wake model (WAsP vs. FUGA) 
 
Cable & substation layout 
 
Cable rating 
 
Layout 
 
Distance to neighbouring OWF (cluster effect) 
Results from the FMEA analysis show that the definition of potential areas for OWE can be improved 
by having an estimation of the importance of the key parameters. Therefore, with the help of a sensitivity 
analysis the decision-process for defining suitable areas as well as the design of OWFs can be improved.
