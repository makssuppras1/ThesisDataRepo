Abstract
In nature proteins and peptides consist of the 20 natural amino acids which are the
backbone of many functions in living organisms. The key to function of proteins and
peptides is their three-dimensional conformation, if a protein or peptide loses this; it
is likely to lose its function. However, due to low bioavailability, proteolytic degrada-
tion and expensive syntheses, peptides and proteins are most oﬀen not considered good
pharmaceutical candidates. In order to overcome these challenges, a ﬁeld of the so-called
peptidomimetic compounds has emerged, peptidomimetic means compounds which mimic
the peptides, and the challenges of the three-dimensional conformation have given rise to
the peptidomimetic foldamers, compounds that fold as peptides, types of compounds are
N-alkyl-β-alanines or β-peptoids. In this project a helix of hexamers of β-peptoid with
naphtyl ethyl side chains was tried functionalized by replacing the fourth residue side
chain with triazolium. The triazolium was selected because of their high preference for
cis conformation which is thought to be important for peptoid to take a helical confor-
mation. The hexamer was synthesized applying a submonomer synthesis trimer that was
coupled by amide bond formation, and the triazole was synthesized by CuAAC ”click”
chemistry.
The hexamer with triazolium was proven with CD and ﬂuorescence spec-
troscopy not to take a helical conformation. A hexamer with an α diﬂuorinated fourth
residue was attempted, however, the coupling of trimers were unsuccessful, most likely
because of reduced reactivity due to the inductive eﬀect of the ﬂuorines.

Introduction
In nature proteins and peptides composed of the 20 natural amino acids have a vital
role, performing a variety of functions in living organisms.
Their function is closely
related to the three-dimensional structure, which are controlled by non-covalent inter-
actions: van der waals-, hydrophobic and electrostatic interaction, hydrogen bonds and
disulphurbridges[1]. In regard to applying peptides and proteins as pharmaceutical agents,
it has proved to be challenging due to low bioavailability, they are subject to prote-
olytic degradation, and they are diﬃcult to synthesize, therefore production costs are
high[2]. To overcome these challenges, research in unnatural analogues of peptides has
been performed, the so-called peptidomimetics[3]. Foldamer is a term which describe com-
pounds that ”adopt a speciﬁc compacted conformation”[4], a subgroup is peptidomimetic
foldamers which mimic the folding of proteins and peptides. Research in this area has
resulted in many peptidomimetic foldameric structures, including the helical β-peptoid
hexamer[5].

System, Schr¨odinger, LLC
The aim of this Master thesis has been to investigate methods to functionalize the β-
peptoid helix synthesized in the Olsen Lab, UCPH[5] (Figure 1.1). To investigate the
eﬀect on the folding pattens of the β-peptoid helix, the fourth β-peptoid residue was

1.1
Peptidomimetics
Figure 1.2: Structure of selected peptidomimetic monomers.
The goal of peptidomimetics is to synthesize compounds that are capable of mimick-
ing the function of peptides, which includes keeping the three-dimensional structure and
solving the problem in regard to bioavailability and proteolytic degradation[2]. Another
advantage of unnatural peptide analogues is the possibility to improve the selectivity and
potency over their natural counterparts, which is ideal for drug development[3]. Some
classes of peptidomimetic compounds, e.g. peptoid, also have the advantage of reduc-
ing the complexity of synthesis[7]. Research into peptidomimetics has yielded diﬀerent
compounds, e.g. β-Peptides where the backbone has an additional carbon, where the
side chain can be on either the α or the β-carbon[8], azatide where the α-carbon has
been replaced with a nitrogen[9], and peptoid or α-peptoid where the side chain is shifted
from the α-carbon to the amino group[10]. Other peptidomimetic compounds ”combine”
the changes from the above mentioned, e.g. are aza-β-peptide where the charges of aza-
tide and β-peptide are combined[11], or β-peptoid where the charges of α-peptoid and
β-peptide are combined[12]. The selected peptidomimetic is shown in Figure 1.2. These
compounds are applied in combinations of pure oligomers[13] or in hybrid backbone[14].

Foldamers
Figure 1.3: The common secondary structures A: Alanine α-helix[15] B: β-sheet C: β-turn.
Three-

Foldamer is a term used to describe an oligomer with a speciﬁc compact conformation,
and was coined by Gellman in 1998 who describes ”any polymer with a strong tendency to
adopt a speciﬁc compact conformation.”[4]. Foldamers can also be described as oligomers
because of non-covalent interactions of non-adjacent residues[16], these interactions are
π-interactions, van der Waals, hydrophobe and electrostatic interactions[4]. The following
will concentrate on peptidomimetic foldamers, i.e. foldamers which mimic the folding of
proteins and peptides[17]. Peptide and protein structure are described in terms of primary,
secondary, tertiary and quaternary structure[18]. The Primary structure is the sequence of
amino acid, this sequence folds into speciﬁc structural elements, which are the secondary
structure. These structural elements are divided into helix (mostly α-helix), β-sheet, and
turns (Figure 1.3).
A last structural element, loop, which is a structure without any
internal hydrogen bonds to stabilize them, and it is therefore the most ﬂexible motif[19].
Figure 1.4: The levels of Protein structures[20].

The tertiary structure is the three-dimensional structure or overall folding of the secondary
structure elements into a protein or a subunit of one[18]. Quaternary structure describes
the three-dimensional structure of multi-subunit protein[18]. An overview of protein and
peptide structure is shown in Figure 1.4. The folding into this structure is due to non-
covalent interaction and covalent disulﬁde bonds in cysteine-cysteine bridges[1].
Pep-
tidomimetic foldamers are oligomers that resemble the three-dimensional structure, and
because most of these compounds do not fold in the same way as proteins and peptides,
they are designed with focus on mimicking the structure rather than the mimicking of in-
dividual side chain sequences[21]. In the following sections three relevant peptidomimetic
compounds, which act as foldamers, are described β-peptoid and its parent compounds
β-peptide and α-peptoid.
1.2
β-Peptide
Figure 1.5: The structure of β2-, β3- and β2, 3-peptide
β-Peptides has been on the fore front of research in peptidomimetic foldamers, and are
therefore the most studied[8]. β-Amino acid can, unlike many other unnatural analogues,
also be found in nature, where β-alanine is the most common[22], however, in nature β-
amino acids in hybrid backbone with natural amino acids is never found. The ﬁrst study
of unnatural oligomers of β-peptides was published in 1996[23][24]. The three types of
β-peptides, β2 where the side chain is on the α-carbon, β3 where the side chain is on the
β-carbon and β2, 3, which has side chains on both the α- and β-carbons[8] (Figure 1.5).
Consequently, introduction of a methylene into the backbone may at ﬁrst glance cause
minor changes, however, it has high impact on the characteristics of the compounds[25].

β-Peptides has been observed taking both helical and sheet conformation[27]. The sheet of

1.3
α-Peptoid
Figure 1.7: A: Solid-phase synthesis of α-peptoid. B: Solution-phase of α-peptoid.
The ﬁrst research into α-peptoid or N-substituted glycine was published in 1992[10] in
attempt to develop a new class for drug development. The shift of the side chain from the
α-carbon to the amino group results in that these compounds are quite easy to synthesize.
A submonomeric strategy consisting of an acylation most often with bromoacetic acid and
thereafter amination, this synthesis can be performed both as solid-phase synthesis[30]
and solution-phase[31] (Figure 1.7). In the amination step, many diﬀerent primary amines
can be introduced into the peptoid, thereby, it is possible to synthesize a great variety of
α-peptoid[7] and compared to the synthesis of natural peptides, the α-peptoid synthesis
is simple and inexpensive[32].
Figure 1.8: Cis and trans isomerizetion
Because of the shift in α-peptoid of the side chain to the amino group, α-peptoid is de-
prived the ability to be stabilized by backbone hydrogen bond between the carbonyl group
and the amino group[8]. The shift also results in the amide bond being tertiary, which
is more willing to isomerize in cis or trans[7] (Figure 1.8), making α-peptoids highly
ﬂexible oligomers.
However, α-peptoid oligomers are still peptidomimetic foldamers,
because of their ability to form well-deﬁned secondary structures[33]. The most well-
known is the helix and loop conformations[7], but also turn has been shown to be
possible[34]. In general cis/trans isomerism has been shown to be important for fold-
ing patterns of peptoids[35][36]. The peptoid residues forming helical conformation are
often cis isomers[37]. Other forces as steric hindrance for bulky side chain[38], charged
side chains[39] and hydrophobic[34] interactions are factors in the peptoid folding pattern.

1.3. α-Peptoid
7
Figure 1.9: The N-(S)-(1cyclohexylethyl)glycine helix[33]. Three-dimentional structures were prepared
using the PyMOL Molecular Graphics System, Schr¨odinger, LLC
An α-peptoid helix of N-(S)-(1cyclohexylethyl)glycine, which is a cis inducing group, was
successfully studied by x-ray crystallography[33], shown in Figure 1.9. Study of these
foldamers has yielded that helix and loop give distinct CD signals[40]. A study on thion-
ated and ﬂuorinated α-peptoid compounds has indicated that these change the backbone
so the π∗interaction is possible, which can help stabilizing the backbone conformation[41].
α-peptoids have been shown to mimic peptides as antimicrobial peptides (AMP)[42], lung
surfactant[43] and amylin[44]. In 2003 a helical α-peptoid foldamer that mimics an AMP
which is closely related to the helical structure of the compound was synthesized[13]. α-
peptoids also showed complete resistance to proteolytic degradation[45] and higher cell
permeability[46]. α-peptoid oligomers have also been the subject of study into their ap-
plication in nano materials[47].

1.4
β-Peptoid
Figure 1.10: Kcis/trans for selected β-peptoid side chain in acetonitrile. a[48] b[6].
β-Peptoids or N-alkyl-β-alanines are less investigated peptidomimetics and can be de-
scribed with the changes of both β-peptide and peptoid, so both having the extract methy-
lene unit in the backbone and the side chain on the amino group[12]. The ﬁrst β-peptoid
described in literature was described in 1998 and was a library of di- and trimers[12]. The
synthesis method was similar to those of α-peptoid, the ﬁrst step is the acylation with
bromopropanoic acid instead of bromoacetic acid followed by the amination, however,

2.4
Copper(I)-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition
Figure 2.4: Mechanism for CuAAC reaction
The azide-Alkyne Huisgen Cycloaddition was ﬁrst described in publication in 1961[69].
The Copper-catalyzed version of the azide-alkyne Huisgen Cycloaddition (CuAAC) was
described in 2002 in two publications: one by Morten Meldal from Carlsberg Laboratory[70]
and another by Valery Fokin and K. Barry Sharpless from the Scripps Research Institute[71].
Figure 2.5: Selected ”click” chemistry reactions
The CuAAC is a so-called click chemistry reaction[72].
In order for the reaction to
be a click chemistry reaction, the following characteristics are required: stereospeciﬁc,
modular, give high yields, no or easily removed side products, strong thermodynamic
driving force and a broad scope of employment, the reaction should preferably also have
simple conditions, simple puriﬁcation steps, water as solvent or other benign solvent,
and simple reagents and starting materials[72].
Other click chemistry reactions than
the CuAAC reaction are for example the Diels-Alder reaction[73], the thiol-ene click
reaction[74] and thiol-yne reaction[75] (Figure 2.5). A proposed mechanism[76] for the
reaction begins with the coordination of the copper(I) species coordinated to alkyne,
whereafter the copper displaces the terminal hydrogen on the alkyne. In the next step
the azide binds to the copper, before the azide binds to alkyne forming a six-membered
ring with copper now double bonded to carbon, then the copper is excluded, when the
ring is reduced to a ﬁve-membered ring, then the copper is released from the ring, and the
Copper(I) species is regenerated (Figure 2.4). Copper(I) species are not stable in aqueous
solvents, ligands as TBTA can be added to stabilized the copper(I) species, and thereby
improving the yield[77].

2.8.3
Protection groups
Protection groups in chemistry are groups which are added to a functional group to mask
it, so when chemistry is conducted on the rest of the molecule, it will not be aﬀected. The
functional group can be deprotected to the original form[100]. In order for the protection
group to perform its function, it has to be selected with care, and which types of reaction
that else will be performed have to be taken into account[100].
Protection groups in
amide bond formation are masked by other functional groups like amine, carboxylic acid,
alcohols and other that can react in coupling reactions[101]. Amide bond formation is
applied for synthesizing oligomers of units connected with a amide bond, the synthesis
is performed from C-terminal to the N-terminal[101], in order to do so, monomers have
the amine protected, the main strategy is called the Boc or Fmoc strategy[101], where
the named protection group is applied to protect the amine in the backbone, which is
then removed, so the coupling amine can couple to the next unit[101]. In the following
paragraph, the protection group applied in this synthesis will be explained.
Acetyl
Acetyl can as a protecting group protect the functional groups alcohol and
amine[100]. It can be introduced by acylation and is removed most often with base[100].

acid[100]. In can be introduced by esteriﬁcation step and removed by acid, base or a

nucleophilic on the carbonyl carbon, whereafter deprotonated tert-Butanol is eliminated
resulting in the free carboxylic acid (Figure 2.14).

2.8.4
Solid-phase synthesis
Figure 2.17: Concept of solid-phase synthesis
Another approach to produce oligomers by amide bond formation is solid-phase synthe-
sis, this technique is often applied to synthesize peptide, the so-called SPPS[102], but
have been applied to unnatural oligomers, such as α-peptoid[30] and β-peptoid[12]. This
technique is not chosen in this project; therefore it will only be described brieﬂy. The
concept in solid-phase synthesis is resin, a polymer that are non-solvable in the chosen
solvent, it is stable to reaction conditions and has to swell in chosen solvent, for example
PEG or polyacrylamide, where a linker binds the C-terminal end to resin[101]. The most
important with the linker is that it has to be stable to conditions applied to remove the
amine protection group and that it can be removed under mild conditions, not to damage
the end product[103]. When the ﬁrst unit is bound to the resin, then the amine group in
the back bone is deprotected before performing the amide bond formation with the unit
in which amine is protected, after this deprotection and coupling again, the oligomer syn-
thesized is cleaved from resin and puriﬁed[103] (Figure 2.17). One main advantage over
solution-phase synthesis is that between each coupling it requires no other puriﬁcation
than washing and only one puriﬁcation step in solid-phase synthesis[101]. The coupling
reaction is performed by a coupling reagent, commonly HATU.

3.1
Synthesis strategy
Figure 3.1: The model compounds
To functionalize the helical hexamer from the Olsen lab[5], a side chain with triazolium
was selected due to its high preference for the cis conformation, indicated by a high
Kcis/trans value[6] as well as its charged nature. This should give better solubility in polar
solvent, while the hexamer still adopts the helical conformation. Another advantage of
triazolium groups is the possibility of introducing a broad range of functional groups on
them, which gives good opportunities for further functionalization of the hexamer. Two
model compounds were designed, both with the new side chain in the fourth residue and
both with a benzyl group on the triazolium.
The benzyl group was chosen for these
model compounds for its low reactivity, its ability to act as chromophor and its simple
synthesis. In study of Kcis/trans of β-peptoid side chain from the Olsen lab has shown
that for naphthyl the Kcis/trans is higher for the ethylnaphtalene side chain than for the
methylnaphthanlene. So the two models were designed; one methyl-1,2,3-triazole, the
other ethyl-1,2-3-triazole to investigate the eﬀect of the branch methyl group.
Based on studies on the eﬀect of ﬂuorinated α and β-peptoid in the Olsen lab[41] [48][104]
an additional oligomer with a diﬂuorinated residue was designed. This was chosen because

3.3
Secondary Structure of the Synthesized Hexamers
The ﬁve synthesized model hexamer systems, namely compounds (20-24), were investi-
gated by CD and ﬂuorescence spectroscopy in order to elucidate their secondary structure
and compare with the data for the β-peptoid helix from the Olsen lab[5].
3.3.1
CD and Fluorescence spectroscopy
Figure 3.19: CD spectra 190-260 nm of the compounds (20-24) and the β-peptoid helix
CD spectra of the ﬁve model systems were measured at 190-260 nm (Figure 3.19), and
all the spectra had the maximum and minimum at 216-220 nm and 226-230 nm that
compares with the peaks at 215-218 nm and 224-228 nm from the CD-spectra of the
known β-peptoid helix[5]. Since these peaks are present in the monomer, they are not
related to secondary structure. However, in the CD spectrum of β-peptoid helix there is
a local maximum at 232 nm, which is not present in the spectrum of any of the ﬁve model
compounds, indicating that these do not fold into a helical conformation.
==PAGE:48==
3.3. Secondary Structure of the Synthesized Hexamers
38
Figure 3.20: CD spectra 188-500 of the compounds (20-24) and the β-peptoid helix
In a published research article from 2013, it was found that the aggregation of naphthyl
groups can result in correlated CD signals and ﬂuorescence signals[56]. In order to in-
vestigate whether any aggregation such as naphthyl interacting with each other, in the
so-called π-stacking, CD-spectra for the ﬁve model compounds and the β-peptoid helix at
188-500 nm were measured (Figure 3.20). The signal at 190-260 nm resembles the spec-
tra described above; the diﬀerences in compounds (21) and (22) are most likely due to
problems with saturation of the detector. In all six compounds there were no signal from
260-500, which indicates that there are no π-stacking interaction in the compounds. To
support this a ﬂuorescence spectrum of the ﬁve compounds was recorded (Figure 3.21);
the spectra of compounds (20), (22), (23) and (24) all show a maximum emission around
330 nm which corresponds with the signal of non-aggregated naphthyls[56]. The spec-
trum of compound (21) shows a maximum emission around 320 nm, but otherwise the
signal generally resembles the others, so it can be concluded that for all the compounds
there were non-aggregated naphthyls present. This indicates that the compounds do not
have conformation which allows for the naphthyls to π-stack. As none of the compounds
(20-25) seem to take the helical conformation, however the lack of π-stacking interaction
in the compounds indicates that their are in some sort of extended conformation. This
is consistent with molecular dynamics simulations of β-peptoid helix[5], which show the
helix unfolded and folded through an extended stage. In a study published in 2009, an
interaction between tryptophans in folded structures was found; this interaction gives rise
to CD signals with a minimum and a maximum at 212 nm and 225 nm, respectively[108],
which corresponds well with the signals seen in the CD spectra of compounds (20-24).
However, because the signal in the CD spectrum of tryptophan’s structure[108] decrease
upon heating, the signal at 215-218 nm and 224-228 nm in β-peptoid helix[5] do not de-
crease upon heating which indicates that the signal in β-peptoid helix and in compound
(20-24) are not interactions between naphthyl groups.

3.3.2
Alternative Ways to Functionalize the β-peptoid Helix
The attempts to functionalize the β-peptoid helix by introducing triazolium into the
compound were unsuccessful, since the folded state of the naphthylethyl counter part was
not maintained. This means that new approaches to functionalizing the β-peptoid helix
are required. In the following three suggestions on other methods to functionalize the
helix will be presented.
Backbone Modiﬁcation: α-carbon Substitution
In this project diﬂuorinated α-
carbon was tried unsuccessfully, due to challenges with the coupling reaction, however, as
suggested above the coupling issue can be solved by coupling through a dimer strategy
rather than the trimer strategy applied. However, this rises the issue of how to synthesize
the diﬂuorinated dimer.
The possible advantage of the α-carbon substitution is that
maybe the tight packing of the naphthyl group around the backbone has inﬂuence on
the helical conformation, so substitution of the α-carbon might result in a packing which

helps keep the helical conformation. Consequently, since the substituents on the α-carbon
are to ”ﬁll” the space around the backbone, instead of ﬂuorine, two methyl groups could
be introduced, which might remove the coupling challenges, since the ﬂuorine eﬀects are
removed.
Side Chain Functionalize of the Naphthyl Ethyl
Another approach to functional-
ize the β-peptoid helix could be changing the ethyl part of the naphthyl ethyl side chain.
In the x-ray crystal structure of helix[5], the ethyl part of the side chain sticks out from the
helix core, indicating that it could be possible to extend it with functionality. This means
that a new amine with the desired functionality is required; an approach to synthesize
this can be a Copper Bromide- and PINAP ligand-Catalyzed three-component coupling
reaction[109]. This reaction could enable to synthesize an amine with naphthyl and TMS
protected alkyne, which can incorporated in the helix, deprotected and functionalized with
CuAAC. A suggested synthesis of the the amine based on the three-component coupling

The angle
between the α and the β carbon in a β-peptoid unit in the helix[5] is highly similar to
that of an alkene bond, consequently, by introducing an E-alkene in the backbone, it
is possible to constrain the structure, and it will remain in the helical conformation
even without the naphthyl group. In order to produce these monomers with the alkene
group, a new synthesis approach have to be planned. (E)-3-aminoacrylic acid is available
commercially, and this compound can be protected and then converted into a secondary
amine by reacting with a aldehyde in the presence of base and tetrahydroborate[110]

Conclusion
Peptidomimetic foldamers could play an important role in order to produce eﬃcient phar-
maceuticals with peptide like function, since they in theory can solved challenges that pep-
tides are facing such as low bioavailability and prolytic degradation. Therefore, discover-
ing new compounds that take well deﬁned conformations that resample protein/peptide’s
three-dimensional structure is an important tool to further understanding within this ﬁeld.
In this thesis the synthesis of the model compounds, the β-peptoid hexamer with tri-
azole and triazolium as the fourth residue side chain was successful.
The strategy of
synthesizing the hexamer by couple two trimers by submonomer synthesis was proven
to be an eﬀective way of synthesizing these types of compounds. However, the CD and
ﬂuorescence spectroscopy indicated that synthesized hexamers are not in a helixcal con-
formation. Attempts to synthesize a β-peptoid hexamer with a α diﬂuorinated fourth
β-peptoid was unsuccessful most likely due to the decreased reactivity because of the
ﬂuorine. Another interesting ﬁnding was that it is possible by CuAAC reaction to func-
tionalize directly on the propargyl side chain, adjacent to bulky naphtyl etyhyl side chain
Another approach to functionalize the β-peptoid helices could be by introducing a con-
strain into the backbone. One way of preforming this would be to introduce, as fourth
unit in hexamer, a β-peptoid with α-β-carbon alkene and changing the side chain. This
constrain might force this hexamer to keep the helical conformation without the naphtyl
ethyl side chain, since the angle of the alkene and the angle between the α and β-carbon
are similar.
