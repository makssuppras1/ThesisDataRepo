3 Abstract 
 
The objective of this research paper lies within the examination of the impact of intrinsic 
motivational factors on early-age start-ups by integrating several theory perspectives such 
as the Maslow’s Hierarchy of needs, Self-Determination Theory (SDT), the Expectancy 
Theory and the Person-Organization-Fit theory. The study aims towards identifying the key 
motivational factors that contribute to job satisfaction, commitment to the organization 
and ultimately the success of the start-ups. As for the choice of methods that would directly 
contribute to the identification of motivational aspects within these companies, it was 
decided to take a mixed method approach, that would incorporate both quantitative data 
from surveys, and qualitative data from interviews. As it was discovered throughout the 
process, start-up enthusiasts, which include both the founders and joiners are not the 
keenest to participate in such inquiries, therefore the research stumbled across various 
limitations. Nonetheless, the obtained data allowed for a descriptive analysis to be 
conducted which, in turn, acknowledged the established hypothesis. 
 
4 Introduction 
 
4.1 Background 
 
Early-age start-up success is pivotal in fostering innovation and economic growth as they 
create new job opportunities, seek to disrupt well-established markets with novel solutions 
and are key players in driving technology advancements. Although, even if these factors 
are well-established aspects of the start-up ecosystem, one critical component that 
influences the above-mentioned factors is the motivation of the individuals that work 
within these organizations. Therefore, understanding the motivational factors, that drive, 
both start-up founders as well as start-up joiners can help the managers and the founders 
themselves create a supportive environment that encourages commitment, innovation, 
and ambition, which in turn would lead to an overall success.  Moreover, considering that 
90% of start-ups end in a failure, with 70% failing during year two up to year five (Embroker, 
2023) where 23% of the failing rates are caused by a weak founding team, it can be safely 
assumed that a part of these rates can be directly related to the motivation of the early 
joiners and the founders themselves. Additionally, by further exploring the statistics behind 
==PAGE:5==
 
4 
start-up failure rates, it can be observed that several factors were explored thoroughly. 
Some of these factors include the misreading of the market demand which is currently the 
number 1 reason why start-ups fail with a staggering 42%, 19% fail because of being 
beaten by competition and 10% include other factors such as pricing issues, poor 
marketing, and products. On the other hand, by looking at the success rates of the start-
ups, 82% of business owners claim that a vital factor in their success rate was due to 
having right qualifications and being backed up by experience (Embroker, 2023). 
Considering the statistics, it can be safely assumed that the success rates of the start-ups 
are directly influenced by having the right people on board. Nonetheless, the following 
statistics doesn’t don’t elucidate how start-ups can keep the qualified and experienced 
employees on board by intrinsically motivating them. Furthermore, by exploring literature 
related to start-up success, it can be observed that a wide range of factors have been 
examined, including the role of an entrepreneurship education (Bruce C. Martin, 2013), 
the influence of the entrepreneurial ecosystem (Stam, 2015) and the availability of venture 
capital (Paul Gompers, 2008). However, the role of human factor, in particular the 
motivational factors have received less attention given that startups are primarily driven 
by human capital, and the motivation of the individuals involved plays a pivotal role in the 
commitment, job satisfaction, and overall performance of the start-up. 
 
4.2 Objectives 
 
A set of objectives were established in order to explore the correlation between motivation 
factors and the overall success of the start-ups: 
• To examine the relationship between motivational factors and early-age start-up 
success through the lens of multiple theoretical perspectives. 
• To identify key motivational factors that contribute to job satisfaction and 
organizational commitment in early-age start-ups. 
• To provide insights and recommendations for start-up founders and managers on 
how to harness the power of motivation to increase the likelihood of start-up 
success. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:6==
 
5 
4.3 Research questions 
 
Furthermore, a set of research questions was developed in order to back-up the above-
mentioned objectives and dive deeper into understanding the motivational factors within 
these organizations and guide the investigation: 
• How do motivational factors, as explained by Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, Person 
Organization Fit Theory, Self-Determination Theory, and Expectancy Theory, 
influence the start-up success? 
• What is the relationship between job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and 
early-age start-up success in the context of these motivational theories? 
• How can start-up founders and managers leverage these theoretical perspectives 
to create a motivation work environment and increase the likelihood of start-up 
success? 
 
4.4 Hypothesis 
 
Lastly, to provide a clear statement of the relationship of the key variables in this research, 
namely intrinsic motivation, personal values alignment, job satisfaction and organization 
commitment as well as a basis for the data analysis, the following hypothesis was 
developed: 
• “Individuals who are intrinsically motivated to be part of a startup and whose 
personal values align with the values of the startup organization will have higher 
levels of job satisfaction and organizational commitment.”

5 Methodology 
 
5.1 Research Design 
 
This section provides a comprehensive understanding of the methodologies used in this 
study. The following research was conducted through an adoption of a mixed-method 
approach, meaning that a combination of qualitative and quantitative data collection 
techniques was used to provide a better insight into the Danish start-up ecosystem and 
the motivational factors involved in the success of early age start-ups.   
By adopting the following approach, it was possible to conduct a more detailed overview 
of the subject by providing means to validate the results from one method with another. 
Moreover, it is worth mentioning that the research was conducted mainly at DTU, 
specifically at DTU Skylab, and included some of the most remarkable start-ups from the 
DTU Pilots programme in addition to shoutouts towards the personal network.  
 
5.1.1 Quantitative research 
 
As a tool for conducting quantitative data research, a survey was used (Survey). The main 
purpose of it was to gather information regarding intrinsic motivation, job satisfaction and 
organizational commitment among start-up employees. Participants were asked to answer 
a series of questions based on the Self Determination Theory, Expectancy Theory, and the 
Person-Organization Fit Theory. In turn, the responses obtained were then subjected to a 
descriptive analysis (reasoning can be found in section Limitations and Future Research). 
Moreover, the Likert-scale was used during the survey for several reasons. Firstly, it 
allowed to transform qualitative data from the responses into quantitative data. Secondly, 
it provides the respondents with more straightforward answers leaving no space for 
misinterpretation. Lastly, the choice of Likert scale would allow the conduction of a more 
advanced statistical analysis in the future (Limitations and Future Research). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:8==
 
7 
5.1.2 Qualitative research 
 
The primary method used for the qualitative research were semi-structured 
interviews(Interviews) which were conducted with participants from 2 different start-ups.  
By applying this method to gather qualitative data, it was possible to gain valuable insights 
regarding the participants experiences. In return, the following research has allowed to 
capture the depth of personal experiences therefore complimenting the quantitative data 
by providing context and clarity. 
 
5.1.3 Theoretical analysis 
 
Due to the exploratory nature of the research, it was deemed relevant to conduct a 
Literature review in order to understand the frameworks within which these motivational 
factors operate. The four theories which were subjected to the research were the Maslow's 
Hierarchy of Needs, Self-Determination Theory, Expectancy Theory, and the Person-
Organization Fit Theory. Each of them was studied in detail, including their relevance to 
the start-up ecosystem. Even though, four theories were selected to back up the findings 
of this research, by diving deeper into the concept behind Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs 
throughout the process, it was concluded that the other 3 theories would be more 
appropriate to be used in the quantitative research as the Maslow’s theory explores more 
the general levels of human motivation and extrinsic factors.

6 Literature review 
 
The following section provides a descriptive analysis of the four theories which would serve 
as the basis for this research. 
 
6.1 Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs 
 
The theory behind the Hierarchy of needs which was firstly introduced by Abraham 
Maslow in the paper “A Theory of Human Motivation” (Maslow, 1943)  serves as the 
foundation of the understanding of human motivation. The complex structure of the 
pyramid (Figure 1. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs) allows a clear division of the categories 
in which various human needs might fall, where each category is intended to motivate 
humans at different stages of their lives. 
 
 
Figure 1. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs 
 
 
6.1.1 Theory Overview and application in a start-up context. 
 
As visualized in Figure 1. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, at the first level of the pyramid lie 
the basic psychological needs, which essentially represent the basic survival needs, such 
as the need for water, air, food, clothing, and shelter. According to Maslow, an individual 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
would always strive to accomplish these needs firstly, therefore neglecting other levels of 
the pyramid. (Maslow, 1943). In a start-up context, the application of Maslow’s Hierarchy 
is crucial for fostering a positive work environment and drive motivation. Addressing the 
different level of these needs, start-ups can better engage and retain their workforce, 
consequently contributing to the organization’s success and growth. Therefore, if we were 
to translate the first level of the pyramid in order to be able to apply it in a start-up context, 
then having job satisfaction and organizational values would be the most relevant factors 
at this stage. Considering the nature of start-ups, especially the ones who have acquired 
little to no funding, offering competitive salaries which in turn help individuals to fulfill their 
basic needs such as food, shelter, clothing, etc... is not always an option. Therefore, start-
ups must strive to fulfill these needs in a different way. An example would be providing 
perks like free meals, flexible working hours, and remote work options to fulfill the 
physiological needs of their employees by offering a comfortable work environment and 
basic benefits (Figure 2. Maslow's Hierarchy in a Startup Context). 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Maslow's Hierarchy in a Startup Context 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
By fulfilling the first level of needs, individuals would move towards seeking the next 
motivational aspects from the second level of the pyramid, specifically the safety needs 
which involve security and stability. At this stage, Maslow has adopted a broader view, by 
incorporating not only physical safety, but also job security, financial resources, health and 
well-being. In a start-up context, we can safely affirm that the environment is less stable. 
Therefore, it is paramount for founders to focus on enhancing job security, providing clear 
communication and options for growth and development in order to fulfill employee needs 
at this stage. The transparency of communication can directly influence the level of 
concerns about job stability and contribute to a sense of safety for employees (Vecchio-
Sadus, 2007)  
 
Moving up the pyramid, towards the third level, Love and Belonging, Maslow has described 
one’s desire for interpersonal relationships, such as camaraderie, family, and an overall 
sense of connection with others (Maslow, 1943). Translating this into a start-up context, 
this would incorporate the overall team culture. Social events, team bonding exercises with 
would positively influence the team dynamics and encouraging interpersonal relationships 
among employees. By addressing all this factors, a start-up would directly assist the build-
up of a strong sense of community, therefore addressing the third level of needs of the 
pyramid. Additionally, start-ups can encourage mentoring and peer support to foster a 
sense of belonging and connection within the company.  
 
At the fourth level of the pyramid, Maslow has incorporated the Esteem needs, which 
describe factors such as self-esteem, respect from peers, and an overall sense of 
accomplishment. This level of the pyramid can be easily fulfilled in a start-up context by 
employees as well as founders, by achieving challenging goals, receiving, and giving 
positive feedback, praise for contributions and rewards for personal and team 
achievements. One way that start-ups could address this level of needs would be by 
implementing a reward system that acknowledges the achievements and contribution of 
the employees. The recognition itself can be done in forms of verbal praise and team 
celebrations. Additionally, more formal recognition can be achieved through awards and 
bonuses if the start-up has the financial stability to offer these.  
 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
Lastly, at the top of the pyramid, Maslow has positioned the self-actualization needs, which 
is related to the fulfillment of one’s potential and desire to become the most that one can 
be. By relating this to a start-up context, it can be safely assumed that the last level of the 
pyramid is responsible for personal growth, contribution, and involvement in the decision-
making process, as well as overall development.  
 
Maslow’s theory incorporates a somewhat rigid structure, which adds to the difficulty of 
being empirically tested through survey, therefore it will not be used in the quantitative 
data gathering on this research. With that being said, it is important to accentuate that 
Maslow’s hierarchy of needs provides a useful framework for understanding the various 
aspects of human motivation and serves as a foundation for many other motivational 
theories including the ones used in this research.  
 
6.2 Person Organization Fit Theory. 
 
The Person Organization Fit theory (P-O fit) accentuates the importance of the compatibility 
between one’s values, goals, and personality and those of the company they work for 
(Kristof, 1996). In order to fully grasp its relevance in a start-up context it is essential to 
dive deeper into the theoretical construct and its application. 
 
6.2.1 Theory Overview and application in a start-up context. 
 
P-O fit was initially developed as a way to assess how compatible are an individual’s values 
and goals with those of an organization (Cable, 2002). The principle behind it is that 
employees who find themselves aligned with an organization’s values, become more 
comfortable and committed and experience a higher level of job satisfaction. Therefore, 
these factors, directly influence their performance. Jennifer Chatman has developed the 
person-organization fit model in 1989 with a focus on value congruence and the alignment 
of individual and an organizational value as the primary criteria of fit (Figure 3. Model of 
Person Organization Fit).  
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
 
Figure 3. Model of Person Organization Fit (Jolita Vveinhardt, 2017) 
 
Here, Chatman starts by measuring an organization’s characteristics based on values and 
norms. Initially, individuals within an organization are given a score in order to create an 
organizational profile. Next, the scores are used to find 3 things: 
• Crystallization – how widely shared these values are. 
• Intensity – how strongly held values are relative to the others. 
• Content – which values are important to the organization. 
Lastly, individual scores are compared to the organization’s profile to see how well the 
individuals match the organization. (Jolita Vveinhardt, 2017) 
 
 
Kristof-Brown has further developed the concept behind the Person-Organization Fit 
Theory. They have separated the fit in 2 categories, the supplementary fit and the 
complimentary fit. Where the supplementary fit describes the situation where both the 
individual and the organization have similar characteristics, while the complimentary fit 
describes the scenario where an employee’s characteristics complete the organization. 
(Kristof-Brown, 2005) 
 
 
This section aims to illustrate how this theory fits in the start-up context which is 
characterized by complexity, uncertainty, and volatileness. Besides the previous-
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
mentioned characteristics of the start-up nature, the start-up ecosystem spotlight lies on 
factors such as innovation, dynamism, and rapid growth (Stam, 2015). This type of 
environment can be very demanding in terms of a skilled and experienced workforce that 
is able to prosper and succeed in such ecosystem and are ready to take risks. Therefore, 
it is safe to assume that not every individual is able to withstand such conditions. The 
importance of securing the right fit for the organization from the early stages cannot be 
overlooked, and as means of addressing this aspect, start-ups can rely on the P-O Fit 
theory. 
 
The Person Organization Fit theory can serve as a matching mechanism between 
individuals and start-ups where they can fit and thrive. Taking into consideration the fast-
paced and dynamic environment of start-ups, every contribution from employees can have 
a critical impact on the success of failure of the said start-up. Compared to larger 
organizations, start-ups often have a more unique culture, which allows the fit between 
individuals and the organization to be more evident.  
 
Taking into consideration the fact that a higher level of fit between an employee and 
organization can drive a higher level of satisfaction and commitment among individuals, it 
is safe to assume that these feelings can become strong motivators, and raise employees 
will to perform at their best. Consequently, these factors would lead to a strong intrinsic 
motivation where the job itself becomes rewarding. 
 
 
The following concept is crucial in a start-up context, where it is difficult to compete with 
already established organizations which can offer greater extrinsic motivation such as 
financial compensations and other benefits. Thus, focusing on the intrinsic motivators for 
their employees, start-ups can compete with already established organizations in terms of 
attracting and retaining talents.  
 
From the founder’s perspective, a high level of person-organization fit can be equally 
motivating. When seeing that their employees share the same values and goals as them 
and are committed towards the success of the organization, it can lead to a sense of 
validation when seeing that their vision for the company is also shared among the team 
members. Therefore, this shared commitment can become a strong motivator for the 
founders, which in turn would lead their start-up towards success. 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
Assuming these factors, it is safe to declare that the focus on the alignment between 
founders and joiners as well as organizational values as described by the person 
organization fit theory, possesses a critical role within the start-up ecosystem. It can lead 
to a higher job satisfaction, commitment, and sooner or later to the organization’s success. 
Considering these factors, it is also important to notice that reaching a high level of P-O fit 
in a start-up context can be an extremely complex process which would involve a 
meticulous selection process and an extended cultivation of a shared culture. 
 
6.3 Self-Determination Theory 
 
The Self Determination theory focuses on the extent to which human behaviour is self-
motivated and self-determined, and it was firstly proposed in 1985 by Deci and Ryan. The 
theory accentuates the importance of intrinsic motivational factors that shape the human 
behaviour. It suggests that activities are usually the most engaging and bring a higher level 
of satisfaction whenever they fulfill some basic psychological needs (Richard M. Ryan, 
2000). Moreover, Deci and Ryan introduce three vital aspects of the theory: autonomy, 
competence, and relatedness. (Figure 4. Self-Determination Theory (University of 
Rochester, p. 2023)) 
 
Therefore, it is safe to declare that this theory is complimentary of the Maslow’s Hierarchy 
of needs which was described earlier in this research (Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs). Both 
theories share some similarities in terms of the focus on intrinsic needs and both suggest 
a form of progression. While these theories are quite similar, the Self-Determination theory 
is a more up-to-date framework and possess some differences from Maslow’s proposed 
structure. One easily observed difference is that the Self-Determination theory doesn’t 
possess any hierarchy compared to Maslow’s framework. Moreover, while Maslow 
proposes a variety of needs including ones such as the basic survival needs and social 
needs, the Self-Determination Theory focuses solely on psychological needs which are 
related to personal growth and psychological well-being through the 3 components 
mentioned earlier. Thus, it is worthwhile to dive deeper into the details of each one of them 
as well as their application in a start-up context. 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
Figure 4. Self-Determination Theory (University of Rochester, p. 2023) 
6.3.1 Theory Overview and application in early-age start-ups. 
 
One of the components of the Self-Determination Theory is the Autonomy aspect, which 
refers to the desire to be self-directing and to have ownership over one’s behaviour 
(Richard M. Ryan, 2000). The authors emphasize that autonomy does not imply 
independence or individualism as the name suggests, rather it embodies an individual’s 
desire to feel volitional and have the experience of choice. In a start-up context the 
importance of autonomy cannot be neglected as it multiples given the VUCA - volatile, 
uncertain, complex, and ambiguous environment (Mind Tools Content Team, 2023) that 
the start-ups usually operate in. In the following eco-system, autonomy becomes a by-
product of the work environment. Therefore, employees often that have the autonomy for 
the decision-making process, can take ownership of significant roles and responsibility. 
Thus, it can be assumed that such a work environment can develop intrinsic motivations 
as the employees may feel a much greater sense of purpose and control in their work 
compared to big organizations. However, the aspect of autonomy should not neglect the 
presence of direction and support. Therefore, a sense of balance must be present, where 
founders leave enough room for their employees to perform their tasks independently, but 
still offer the necessary guidance and support when needed. By addressing the autonomy 
aspect in such way, founders can nurture an environment where this sense of autonomy 
provides a higher level of job satisfaction, commitment, and motivation. 
 
 
Another key figure in the Self-Determination Theory is Competence. The authors of the 
theory imply that individuals are driven to interact effectively with their work environment 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
and have a need to experience a sense of mastery (Richard M. Ryan, 2000). In a start-up 
environment this can be translated as the possibility of providing employees with 
challenging opportunities which would help them build and showcase their skills. Due to 
the ever-changing environment these opportunities there is an abundance of such 
opportunities, which in turn fulfill this psychological need. Although, it is important to 
notice, that by just providing challenging tasks it is not enough to fulfill this need. As it is 
equally important to provide recognition and appreciation for the efforts of the individuals. 
One way through which this can be achieved is through constructive feedback and 
acknowledgment of their work. By doing so, founders can ensure that they foster a sense 
of competence among the joiners. In turn, through these actions, a substantial and positive 
impact can be developed which would directly influence the motivational levels of the 
employees and their commitment towards the organization’s goals. 
 
Lastly, Ryan and Deci in the Self-Determination Theory present the third aspect of it which 
is Relatedness. The authors refer to it as the universal urge to establish close emotional 
bonds and attachments with other individuals. It is about feeling connected to others 
without having to forfeit one’s sense of autonomy (Richard M. Ryan, 2000). In a start-up 
context united teams and a collaborative work environment can provide multiple 
opportunities for the fulfilment of this need. Moreover, a culture of transparent and open 
communication can directly influence the sense of community within the team. 
Additionally, providing opportunities for social interactions through team events and 
activities can help individuals feel more connected. Therefore, it is safe to declare that a 
strong sense of relatedness is crucial for fostering commitment towards the start-up’s 
goals, increase morale, and boost motivation. 
 
In conclusion, the Self-Determination Theory offers a strong framework for understanding 
motivation in a start-up context, but it is important to note that it does not come without 
challenges. As the SDT framework provides a structural approach towards developing high 
levels of intrinsic motivation, it can also be one of its potential drawbacks. As mentioned 
earlier, start-ups operate in a VUCA environment, therefore high levels of uncertainty and 
constraints related to resources cannot be avoided.  
 
 
Thus, founders must also pay close attention to factors such as financial incentives and 
job security which fall in the category of extrinsic motivation. Overall, the Self-
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
Determination Theory is able to provide start-ups with a working framework which can drive 
motivation by cultivating an environment which fulfills the psychological needs of 
autonomy, competence and relatedness, which in turn would contribute to the start-up’s 
success. 
 
6.4 Expectancy Theory 
 
The expectancy theory was developed by Victor H. Vroom in 1964. The foundation of the 
theory lays on the fact that an individual will act in a certain way based on the expectation 
that the act itself will be followed by a desired outcome (Vroom, 1964). Similarly, to the 
self-determination theory, the theory proposed by Vroom accentuates that an individual’s 
motivation is directly related to three distinctive factors: expectancy, instrumentality, and 
valence (). These three aspects of the theory drive the motivational force an individual can 
experience at their workplace. 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5. Expectancy Theory Model (iEduNote, 2023) 
 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
 
6.4.1 Theory Overview and application in Early-Age start-ups 
 
As showcased in , the expectancy represents the first stage of the model. It refers to the 
conviction that an increased effort would lead to a higher job performance. This connection 
between effort and performance is directly influenced by aspects such as the possession 
of the necessary skills for completing the job, the available resources, and the constant 
support from peers.  
 
In a start-up context, where the environment is very dynamic and very limited in resources, 
this can be a challenging process to overcome. Being part of a start-up means that you get 
to wear a multitude of hats and go beyond your role. Therefore, as a way to ensure the 
individuals that their efforts are observed and appreciated, continuous support from 
managers and founders would be required. One way to approach this would be by providing 
the necessary resources in the boundaries of the available budget, encourage upskilling 
and developing an environment that advocates creativity and problem-solving. Moreover, 
founders themselves tend to showcase high level of expectancy through their passion for 
the vision of the organization and their belief in the idea, thus, motivating others. 
 
 
At the next stage of the model, Vroom has positioned the Instrumentality, which describes 
one’s belief that if they excel in their performance than they would receive great outcomes. 
This aspect is directly influenced by the trust in the people who make the decisions 
regarding the distribution of the rewards. In a start-up context, usually, that would be the 
founders 
 
 
Moreover, in the start-up ecosystem, it is crucial that there is a rewarding system in place. 
As start-ups cannot compete with large organizations in terms of financial compensations, 
they must ensure that they provide a clear and transparent path towards defined rewards 
based on performance. These rewards do not have to necessarily be financial, instead, 
they could be presented in form of growth opportunity and recognition, as well as a sense 
of contribution. In case of founders, in this scenario, they would be motivated by the 
preeminent reward, the success of the start-up itself. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
19 
At the last stage of the model, Valence is found which invokes that every individual places 
a certain value on the offered rewards. This aspect of the model is deeply personal and 
can be extremely different from one individual to another, as something that can be highly 
valued by one employee might not be that much important to another.  
 
 
Therefore, it is mandatory to understand what employees value the most in the start-up 
ecosystem. Because a monetary reward system would be out of context in most scenarios, 
start-ups could focus on various intrinsic motivators instead, such as providing flexible 
working hours, a sense of purpose, and opportunity for rapid growth. On the other hand, 
founders are driven by creating an impact in their chosen field, having their vision come to 
life and the overall success of their venture. 
 
 
The Expectancy Theory provides a valuable model through which founders can understand 
and enhance motivation for others that are contributing to their vision. Developing an 
environment where an increased effort is directly linked to a better performance, where 
good performance levels is rewarded and where these rewards are valuable for the 
individuals can help start-ups improve the motivation of both employees and founders. 
 
With that being said, it is also crucial to note that following the Expectancy Theory Model 
in a start-up context can be challenging. The dynamic environment which characterizes the 
start-up ecosystem in a combination with limited resources can make it very difficult for 
start-ups to set-up clear links between expectancy and instrumentality. Furthermore, 
because of constrained resources, shaping the reward system in a way to fit all the 
employees can prove to be difficult. Nonetheless, the Expectancy Theory provides a great 
framework to address these aspects in an organized manner which would help to enhance 
motivation.

6.5 Synthesis of Theoretical Perspectives 
 
In order to fully understand the four theories presented earlier in the Literature Review 
section (6) the synthesis of these theories is necessary. As these theories bring their 
unique view on the factors of human motivation, a synthesis would allow these aspects to 
come together and form an integrated view of the subject. The following section will be 
divided in 5 different segments, a comprehensive understanding of the theories, a 
contextual understanding, the identification of gaps, enhanced relevance and 
applications, and finally, supporting evidence and justification.  
 
6.5.1 Comprehensive understanding 
 
Maslow’s Hierarchy of needs presents a structured framework, which divides the factors 
that drive human motivation in 5 different levels, the physiological, safety, love and 
belonging, esteem, and self-esteem. This detailed progression which can also be seen in 
Figure 1. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, offers a simplified version of the essential human 
needs. Compared to it, the Self-Determination Theory (Figure 4. Self-Determination Theory 
(University of Rochester, p. 2023)), presents these needs as being equally important, and 
eliminates the hierarchical factor. Instead, it focuses on the psychological needs of 
autonomy, competence, and relatedness. The Expectancy Theory, on the other hand, 
introduces a new concept in the realm of human motivation. It states that there is a direct 
influence between the efforts, performance, and outcomes at the workplace. Lastly, the 
Person-organization Fit theory analyzes the congruence between the values of an 
organization and the ones of an individual, therefore accentuating the significance of a 
cultural fit in the workplace.  
 
6.5.2 Contextual understanding 
 
The synthesis of these theories allows a better understanding of these theories in the 
context of the start-up environment. As mentioned earlier, there are a couple of traits which 
are common in most start-ups, these are high degrees of uncertainty, limited resources, 
innovation and dynamism. Therefore, a highly motivated team, that is committed to the 
cause, is a must to a achieve success in the long run.  
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
When applied in the start-up environment, Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs model implies that 
it is necessary to develop a supportive and fulfilling work environment. The physiological 
needs can be engaged through the insurance of a balance between work and life, as well 
as through catering a comfortable workplace. Safety needs can be addressed through 
ensuring job stability in such uncertain environment. The love and belonging needs, can 
be met through developing a healthy team culture, whereas esteem and self-actualization 
needs can be engaged in by offering recognition for one’s accomplishments as well as 
offering opportunities for growth. 
 
 
The Self-Determination Theory would be a great tool for fostering motivation in the start-
up environment by putting an emphasis on the autonomy, competence, and relatedness. 
In order to address the autonomy section of this model, start-ups could provide joiners with 
decision-making power and responsibility for their actions, competence by offering 
opportunities for growth and skill development, and lastly relatedness, by creating a sense 
of camaraderie and community through various activities, and team bonding events.  
 
Moving towards the Expectancy Theory, start-ups could create a reward system in order to 
drive motivation by adequately associating efforts to desirable outcomes. By doing so, 
start-ups would ensure that they provide clearly established performance standards, link 
performance to rewards and adjust these rewards in accordance with the employees 
desired goals.  
 
Finally, the person-organization Fit theory, could be an useful guidance tool in the start-
ups’ hiring processes as well as the build-up of an organizational culture. By doing so, start-
ups can ensure that they hire employees whose values align with those of the organization 
and that they maintain a culture that is already aligned with the existing employees, 
therefore improving the levels of job satisfaction, commitment and performance. 
 
6.5.3 Identification of Gaps 
 
Through the synthesized view of these theories, it is also possible to understand the gaps 
that lie within these theories, specifically in a start-up context. Thus, even though Maslow 
through his model provides a deep understanding of the human needs, it does not provide 
a concrete explanation of how these needs can be met in a start-up environment. Moving 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
to the self-determination theory, similarly to the Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, it does not 
provide an explanation of how these needs can be met in the ever-changing start-up 
environment, therefore, one must base their approach towards the self-determination 
theory on assumptions. Next, in regard to the Expectancy Theory, even though it is a great 
tool to grasp an understanding of how an individual’s motivation is affected by their 
performance, efforts and rewards that they might get, it almost completely neglects the 
intrinsic motivational factors, which is vital in a start-up context, where extrinsic rewards, 
such as financial compensation, are usually absent, or heavily delayed. Lastly, the Person-
Organization Fit Theory, focuses mostly on the compatibility between an individual and an 
organization, but it does not concretely explain how it can have an influence on the 
motivational aspects. Therefore, by examining the gaps in these theories, it is safe to 
assume that the start-up ecosystem is in need for a thorough motivational model, which 
can include all the positive aspects from these models, with a detailed guidance of how to 
address both intrinsic and extrinsic aspects in a start-up context. 
 
6.5.4 Enhanced Relevance and Applications 
 
In the start-up ecosystem, the understanding and application in practice of these theories 
can be highly relevant. As Maslow’s Hierarchy Of Needs can help the start-ups develop 
more supportive work conditions. The Self-Determination Theory could allow start-ups to 
compose jobs which promote autonomy, competence, and relatedness, while the 
Expectancy Theory allows the establishment of clear performance specifications and 
reward systems. Finally, the Person-Organization Fit can help start-ups with hiring practices 
as well as the development of a tailored culture. Therefore, by creating this synthesis, it is 
possible to create an overview of the potential combination of these theories and the effect 
that these might have on generating a clear approach towards motivating both founders 
and employees in the start-up context. Additionally, through a synthesis of these theories, 
it is possible to create an understanding of the stages of when these theories might be 
useful to be put in practice. For instance, a start-up in its very early stage, could focus 
primarily on autonomy, competence, and relatedness, while one in a later stage might 
focus more on person-organization fit, and expectancy. 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 
6.5.5 Supporting evidence and justification 
 
Lastly, by examining the literature and empirical evidence, it is possible to confirm the 
relevance of these theories in a start-up context. The study of “The nature and experience 
of Entrepreneurial Passion” showcases that both, start-up founders, and joiners retrieve 
motivation from both extrinsic and intrinsic factors (MELISSA S. CARDON, 2009). The study 
claims that entrepreneurs are usually motivated by intrinsic factors such as satisfaction, 
sense of accomplishment and the overall enjoyment of the tasks related to their field of 
work. Similarly, extrinsic motivators such as financial compensations, recognition and 
status are also sought by entrepreneurs. But while these types of rewards can be strong 
motivators in the start-up context, the intrinsic aspects prove to be more complex, 
therefore, the physical rewards are often amplifiers of the passion that drive the 
entrepreneurs. Thus, it can be assumed that theories such as Maslow’s Hierarchy, and 
Self-Determination which focus on intrinsic motivators, as well as the Expectancy theory 
which emphasizes the need for extrinsic motivation are all relevant in a start-up context. 
Moreover, research also shows, that the Person-Organization theory in the context of start-
ups is as relevant as the others. The compatibility between an individual and an 
organization is critical in developing a high level of commitment, job satisfaction and turn-
overs in organizations (Cable, 2002). 
 
 
6.5.6 Conclusion 
 
The synthesis of the following theories creates a better understanding of motivators in a 
start-up context as it addresses the relevant gaps and points out the relevance and 
application of these theories. Moreover, it is supported by empirical evidence. Therefore, 
it is safe to assume, that this synthesis can be beneficial for creating a more efficient 
model for motivating individuals in a start-up context, which, in the long run would 
contribute to the organizational success.

7 Data Collection and Results. 
 
7.1 Surveys 
 
The Survey was created with using the Likert scale in mind and was distributed to 
participants through social media posts, and physical copies in form of a QR code. The 
questionnaire itself was divided in 4 different sections, where one section was reserved 
for gathering demographics data, and the rest were reserved for addressing three out of 
four theoretical perspectives: The Person-Organization Fit Theory was used in order to 
evaluate the alignment of participants values and the ones of the start-ups that they are a 
part of. The Self-Determination Theory was used in order to assess the participants’ sense 
of autonomy, competence, and relatedness. And an Expectancy theory section in order to 
gain an understanding of the participants perception of the relationship between effort, 
performance and rewards. The decision to eliminate the Maslow’s Hierarchy of needs 
theory from this survey was due to the fact that most elements from the theory itself are 
already implicitly covered by the other three, therefore it was decided to eliminate it from 
the survey, as its presence could drive a set of drawbacks, for example, participants might 
get bored filling the survey because of the redundancy in the questionnaire, therefore, 
stopping midway.

start-up ecosystem.

8 Discussion 
 
8.1 Summary of findings 
 
The following study aimed towards exploring the motivational factors within a start-up 
context and how these can be related to the success of early-age start-ups. Through 
surveys and interviews and their correlation with the explored theories, it was determined 
that employee motivation is severely affected by a supportive and inclusive work 
environment. Moreover, it was discovered that a culture that promotes a sense of 
belonging and rewards employee’s contribution through recognition can lead to an overall 
job satisfaction. This was evident through the responses to the interview, where 
participants felt that they were part of a larger mission and that their work was appreciated.
 
 
 
Furthermore, it was discovered that transparency and openness from the leadership 
severely affects the sense of belonging and purpose among employees, as employees who 
experienced involvement in the decision-making process and were always up to date about 
the progress of the organization reported high levels of commitment and satisfaction.  
 
Additionally, the link between the values of the organization and the one of the employees 
proved to be a critical aspect that drives motivation. This was confirmed through the 
interviews where employees who stated that they felt aligned with the values of the 
organization also felt an overall sense of satisfaction and commitment to the organization. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
Moreover, a repeatable aspect that was present throughout the study was that individuals 
experience high level of appreciation towards the aspects of autonomy, competence and 
relatedness. By analysing the interviews, it became clear that employees appreciate being 
trusted to do their work and take decisions, as well as the overall feeling of camaraderie 
at the workplace. 
 
 
Finally, it was determined that employees who developed a clear understanding of the 
connection between performance and rewards felt more motivated. This was primarily due 
to transparent communication within the organization, which would directly assist to the 
acknowledgment of the link between efforts and desired outcomes. 
 
 
By concluding these findings, it is clear that the acquired quantitative and qualitative data 
directly support the proposed Hypothesis, which states that the factor of intrinsic 
motivation and the alignment of personal goals with the ones of the start-up plays a crucial 
role in the overall job satisfaction and commitment.  
 
8.2 Implication For Early-Age Start-ups 
 
The Summary of findings allows to create a coherent implication for early age start-ups. A 
supportive and inclusive work environment, supported by a transparent communication 
culture can enhance employee motivation, job satisfaction, and commitment in the start-
up ecosystem, which in turn would lead to the organizational success.  
 
The findings provide concrete evidence that organizations, specifically start-ups, which 
ensure a good fit between the individual and the organization, promote autonomy, 
relatedness, and competence, as well as establish a clear connection between efforts and 
rewards, directly influence the overall motivation of all the individuals involved, and 
increase their job satisfaction and commitment.

8.3 Limitations and Future Research 
 
This study of intrinsic motivation provides valuable observations and understanding of 
various motivational factors within the start-up ecosystem. However, there are several 
limitations and areas of improvement in the future, which could provide supplementary 
insights. 
 
One of the most important aspects that was limited during this research falls within the 
sample size for both survey and interviews. The collected data was limited by personal 
network, and unwillingness of multiple start-ups to cooperate, due to time constraints, as 
a common response to the inquiry of filling out the survey and participate in the interview 
was “Sorry we do not have time right now, maybe next week”, or a general lack of interest 
in this topic. Thus, the collected data might not be able to fully represent the diversity of 
different start-ups and their applied practices. Therefore, it can be asserted that the 
obtained data does not apply to a broader start-up context. Future research could attempt 
to eliminate these constraints by collaborating with various organizations, such as 
accelerators and incubators, which in turn would arrange a connection to a more diverse 
participant pool. 
 
Moreover, this study took a descriptive approach and relies on qualitative data and 
personal perceptions of founders and joiners. Thus, even if this method can provide in-
depth insights, it is susceptible to biases. Therefore, this descriptive approach of the study 
limits the capability to the make conclusive declarations.  
 
Therefore, future studies could employ broader quantitative methods, such as metrics for 
employee performance and start-up success rates in order to provide a more coherent 
analysis of the link between motivation and start-up success. Moreover, the addition of a 
larger dataset would allow to conduct a statistical analysis in order to understand the 
connection between variables. Such statistical analyses might include a regression 
analysis, t-tests, or ANOVA, which would allow a more comprehensive understanding of the 
connection between motivation, commitment, satisfaction, and overall success of the 
start-up. 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
35 
Additionally, future research could include additional areas that influence motivation. 
These can consist of the exploration of leadership styles, team dynamics and 
organizational structures in the start-up environment. Furthermore, a longitudinal study 
which would track motivation and its impact on success could offer greater insights.  
 
Finally, by engaging in cross-cultural studies, future research could prove whether the 
findings of this study maintain authenticity across different cultural ambiances. This could 
prove beneficial, as start-ups are a global phenomenon, therefore understanding cultural 
differences in terms of motivation could assist founders in establishing compatible work 
environments. 
 
 
The findings of this study, in combination with the recommendations for future research 
can develop a greater understanding of the motivation within early age start-ups. 
9 Conclusion 
 
The ever-changing start-up environment, which is characterized by dynamism, high stakes, 
risks, and often high rewards can be challenging in terms of developing intrinsic 
motivation. Therefore, creating a comprehensive understanding of the motivational factors 
which assist in organizational commitment, job satisfaction, and lastly, start-up success is 
of great importance. 
 
Through the perspective of the four theories: Maslow’s Hierarchy Of Needs, Self-
Determination Theory, Person-Organization Fit, and Expectancy Theory, this study aimed 
to elucidate the motivational factors present in the start-up environment. 
 
The Maslow Model proclaims that human beings are motivated by a series of needs, which 
can be structured in a hierarchical manner, starting from the basic physiological needs at 
the foundation of the model, and finishing with the need for self-actualization at the peak 
of the pyramid. Through the data analysis, it was established that start-ups do not provide 
extrinsic motivators such as financial compensation, and overall offer less stability than 
established organization. However, the finding suggest that start-ups often address higher-
level needs, such as needs for belongingness, esteem, and self-actualization.

36 
Moreover, the aspect of alignment between an individual values and the ones of the 
organization as described by the Person-Organization Fit Theory are vital in creating a 
sense of job satisfaction and enhancing organizational commitment. It was found to be a 
reoccurring subject across the survey and interviews, as many participants expressed their 
alignment with the culture of their organizations, which in turn provides them with a sense 
of fulfilment and job satisfaction. 
 
Furthermore, the study identified autonomy, competence and relatedness as presented 
by the Self-Determination Theory to be key motivational factors. Through interviews and 
surveys, it can be observed that these aspects greatly contribute to the overall satisfaction 
of individuals, which in turn lead to higher levels of motivation.  
 
Lastly, in terms of theory alignment, the study found that individuals are more prone to 
showcase effort when they acknowledge that it would lead them to the desired outcomes, 
as also explained by the Expectancy Theory. In accordance with this, participants 
expressed that transparency in communicating organizational goals, and recognition of 
their contributions to those goals have a significance influence towards their motivational 
levels. 
 
Moreover, in regard to the proposed hypothesis “Individuals who are intrinsically motivated 
to be part of a startup and whose personal values align with the values of the startup 
organization will have higher levels of job satisfaction and organizational commitment.” 
the study has been able to identify concrete evidence that supports it. Both the qualitative 
and quantitative data implies that there is a direct connection between the alignment of 
personal and organizational values, intrinsic motivation, job satisfaction and commitment 
in the context of early-age start-ups. 
 
 
Additionally, the study applied qualitative and quantitative methods of data collection. 
Although due to a relatively small sample size, a descriptive approach was chosen, 
therefore limiting the possibility of a definitive assertion. This can be tackled in the future, 
in order to get more insight on the matter, through collaborations with organizations such 
as accelerators and incubators. Besides, future research would benefit from a larger data 
sample, as it would allow for the application of a statistical analysis in order to determine 
the correlation between variables.   
==PAGE:38==
 
37 
In conclusion, the study has been able to gain valuable data regarding the motivation in 
early age start-ups. It has put in the spotlight the importance of aspects such as shared 
values, autonomy, openness and transparency in communication, autonomy, 
camaraderie, and skill development. Even though, future research is needed in order to 
gain an even deeper understanding of these aspects, this study highlights the vital role 
motivation plays in the context of early-age start-ups. Consequently, providing start-up 
founders with useful insights on how to engage in the development of these aspects in 
order to foster a motivating environment.
