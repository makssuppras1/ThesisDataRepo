Executive summary 
This report presents modelling and experimental analysis for the effectiveness of MDEA/PZ solvent 
blends for carbon capture in the absorber section of a biogas upgrading process that was tested in 
DTU’s pilot absorber column. 
Section 1: Introduction overviews the necessity of replacing traditional fossil fuels with green energy 
sources, such as biogas. A brief review of the mature biogas upgrading technologies of physical 
scrubbing, pressure swing adsorption, membrane technology, and chemical scrubbing are discussed 
with the objectives of this study. 
Section 2: Methods and experimental work discusses methods used to construct the rate-based 
Aspen Plus model for simulating the absorber column, preparation of DTU’s absorber column, the 
plan for the experimental campaigns, and model-fitting with fine-tuned simulations for the 
experimental steady-state regions. 
Section 3: Results and discussion concerns the results from the modelling work together with the 
experimental results. Firstly, the study of mass transfer rate limitations within the absorber column is 
discussed to determine an appropriate residence time limitation for the experimental campaigns. 
Then, the effect of increasing PZ content within the solvent blend on the CO2 capture efficiency is 
discussed with an overview and results of the experimental campaigns utilizing various PZ 
concentrations within the solvent blend. Moreover, the effect of operating at various liquid-to-gas 
ratios and lean solvent temperature values is discussed through a combination of modelling and 
experimental analysis. Additionally, the absorber liquid temperature and gas phase CO2 concentration 
profiles are analyzed over modelling, experimental, and fine-tuned model-validation specific to the 
experimental steady-state regions are analyzed for possible process optimization. 
Section 4: Conclusion deducts that increasing PZ content within the MDEA/PZ solvent blend 
significantly enhances the CO2 capture efficiency while the achievement of the target CO2 slip of 2.5 
vol% is facilitated with increased lean-to-gas flow rates and low lean solvent inlet temperatures. 
Moreover, the cost of operation without increasing significantly increasing capital expenses can be 
reduced by optimizing the absorber liquid temperature gas phase CO2 concentration profiles. 
Section 5: Future work addresses the possibility of optimizing the complete biogas upgrading process 
with the improvement of the fine-tuned model, analysis of the samples taken during the experimental 
campaigns, and potential exploration of additional formulations of the solvent blend.

Problem statement 
The dependence on fossil fuels is a big threat to all countries not only because there is a limited 
amount of fossil fuels, but also because the ultimate transition to green energy sources will be vital. 
Biogas is a promising alternative energy source; however, it typically contains around 40 vol% carbon 
dioxide that should be removed through biogas upgrading technologies. Typically, only the upgraded 
biogas is allowed into the natural gas grid, providing a 65 % higher burning value compared to raw 
biogas. The upgraded biogas to biomethane can be considered a green energy source with its near-
zero carbon emissions if the CO2 in raw biogas can be effectively captured and stored. 
Developing robust biogas upgrading technologies for CO2 capture and storage is necessary for the 
green energy transition. Among various methods to remove CO2 from raw biogas, chemical scrubbing 
of the raw biogas with amines in an absorption column is a mature method with high efficiency and 
low cost. 
This work investigates the performance of the MDEA/PZ solvent blend through a combination of 
modelling with Aspen Plus® V11 simulation work and pilot-scale absorption column experiments. 
First, an initial modelling work will be provided to develop a proof-of-concept for capturing a minimum 
of 98 vol% CO2 in raw biogas, corresponding to a maximum of 2.5 vol% CO2 slip in supplied 
biomethane with reasonably low energy demand. Then, a pilot-scale absorption column will be 
prepared to test different with different MDEA/PZ solvent blend formulations and various process 
specifications based on the initial modelling work. The results of the experiments will be discussed 
through an analysis of the online measurements made with the instruments of the absorber column 
and characterization of the samples taken during the experimental campaigns. Based on the results 
of the experimental campaigns considered together with the initial simulations, a fine-tuned modelling 
will be provided to further validate the correlation between the pilot-scale experiments and Aspen 
Plus® V11 simulations. 
Learning objectives: 
1. Conduct an extensive literature review on biogas upgrading technologies and chemical 
scrubbing with amines. 
2. Calibrate the vital instruments of the pilot absorption column to prepare for a reliable 
operation of the experiments. 
3. Prepare the pilot absorber column for the experimental campaigns. 
4. Plan and conduct experimental campaigns in the pilot absorption column. 
5. Model the absorber column in the chemical scrubbing process with Aspen Plus® V11 based 
on initial scenarios utilizing various process parameters and different formulations of the 
MDEA/PZ solvent blend. 
6. Fine-tune the process modelling based on the specific experimental conditions to validate the 
experimental work with optimized simulations. 
7. Summarize and discuss results obtained from experimental and modelling work.

Methodology 
The project started with a month-long literature review of the work. Sources were found using “DTU 
Findit” and “Google Scholar” to construct the literature review including the topics climate change, 
CO2 emissions, CO2 capture and storage technologies, biogas upgrading, and chemical scrubbing 
with amines in an absorption column. 
After finding the relevant literature for the project, it was understood that focusing the modelling and 
experimental work on the absorber column instead of the desorber column would provide valuable 
insights into the effectiveness and feasibility of the design for the complete biogas upgrading process. 
This is because the performance of the absorber column is influenced by both the rate at which the 
absorption reaction occurs and the attainment of equilibrium between the gas and liquid phases. 
However, the performance of the desorber column is only limited by equilibrium. 
Initially, multiple simulations were run to study the effect of the following process parameters on the 
CO2 capture performance:  
• 
Biogas flow rate, 
• 
Solvent formulation, 
• 
The ratio of lean solvent flow rate to biogas flow rate (L/G value), 
• 
Lean solvent inlet temperature, 
• 
Temperature and CO2 concentration profiles over the packing height. 
Evaluating the results of the initial simulations, experimental campaigns were planned at DTU’s pilot 
absorber column to test different formulations of the MDEA/PZ solvent blend at a constant gas flow 
rate while varying the L/G value and lean solvent inlet temperatures.  
Before beginning the experiments, the vital instruments of the pilot absorption column such as the gas 
analyzers, liquid flow meter, gas flow meters, and temperature probes were calibrated to ensure a 
reliable process operation that provides correct online measurements. 
During the experimental campaigns online measurements of the absorber column’s instruments were 
taken alongside the samples taken for each solvent formulation before the experiments and samples 
taken at steady-state regions during the experiments. After the experimental campaigns, the online 
measurements at the steady-state regions were analyzed and qualitatively compared with the findings 
of the initial simulation work. 
Finally, fine-tuned simulations were constructed based on the exact process specifications at each 
steady-state region attained to construct a model validation for the experimental campaigns and 
assess the reliability of determination for the steady-state regions based on the online measurements 
through constructing model-fitted absorber profiles. 
In the end, this report was written in Microsoft Word and submitted electronically on the 8th of July at 
CERE, Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering, DTU, Denmark.

1. Introduction 
Since the human race started burning fossil fuels with the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century, 
long-term shifts in temperature and weather patterns have become a major concern [1]. The rising 
concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere results in the trapping of heat, leading to 
climate change and ultimately contributing to global warming. The Paris Agreement was adopted in 
December 2015 to keep global warming to well below 2 °C and to “pursue efforts” to limit it to 1.5 °C 
above pre-industrial levels through 5-year review cycles of nationally determined contributions by the 
member states [2–4]. 
CO2, being the major greenhouse gas, is abundantly produced and emitted to the atmosphere by 
various human activities that can be traced in five economic sectors: energy, industry buildings, 
transport, and AFOLU (agriculture, forestry, and other land use). Among them, the energy sector has 
the largest share. It is responsible for more than one-third of the total CO2 emissions as of 2018 [5]. 
Burning of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas expands well beyond the other economic 
sectors than energy and is responsible for roughly two-thirds of the world’s greenhouse gas emissions 
[6]. To demonstrate the effect of discontinuing fossil fuels use on CO2 emissions, Davis et al. (2017) 
predicted 390 parts per million (ppm) atmospheric CO2 concentration as of 2010 would peak just 
below 430 ppm, leading to a global mean temperature increase of around only 1.3 °C if the existing 
highly carbon-emitting fossil-fuel energy infrastructure would be used for its normal life span and no 
new ones would be built [7]. Given the world's dependence on fossil fuels to meet the growing global 
energy demand, a sudden abandonment of fossil fuels without the implementation of efficient 
alternative energy resources is not currently feasible. 
To meet the Paris Agreement, it is necessary to reduce global CO2 emissions yearly by around 3 % to 
a net balance of zero in the year 2050. It means 37 Gt CO2∙yr-1 emissions reported as of 2018 require 
an additional 1.1 Gt CO2 emission reduction from the atmosphere each year until 2050. International 
Energy Agency (IEA) assesses that a 6 Gt CO2 yr-1 capture rate should be achieved by 2050 to meet 
with zero emissions target [8]. Haszeldine et al. (2018) projected just about 700 Mt CO2∙yr-1 capture 
rate could be obtained by 2050 [8]. They estimated that a 6 Gt CO2∙yr-1 capture rate could only be 
achieved in 2110, after 60 years of delay. Thus, reaching the net zero emissions target by 2050 is not 
realistic. Therefore, replacing highly carbon-emitting traditional fossil fuels with sustainable energy 
sources such as biogas, solar, and wind energy, as well as, improving the current carbon capture 
technologies are necessary to minimize carbon emissions to limit global climate change.

Among various sustainable energy generation technologies, utilization of biogas can result in 
potentially negative carbon emissions. That is because biogas is produced from a biomass source 
that participates in a stable carbon cycle such that anaerobic digestion consumes atmospheric CO2 at 
a rate similar to the combustion of biogas [10,11]. However, biogas produced by anaerobic digestion 
consists of 55-65 vol% useful methane (CH4) and 35-45 vol% CO2 with 1-2 vol% additional impurities 
of hydrogen sulfide (H2S), siloxanes, nitrogen oxides (NOx), ammonia (NH3), and halogens [12,13]. 
Most countries only allow biomethane in the natural gas grid with certain purity standards. For 
instance, in Germany and France, biomethane should contain less than 2.5 vol% CO2; in Denmark, 
Sweden, Austria, and the U.K., this number is 3 vol% CO2; while in the Netherlands biomethane can 
contain up to 6 vol% CO2 [14]. Those purity constraints are defined mainly because CO2 and also the 
other minor impurities lower the enthalpy of combustion in raw biogas; thus they should be removed 
according to the technical specifications of different countries to end up with useful biomethane for 
increased energy density [15,16]. Hence, it is crucial to strive for a target CO2 slip of no more than 2.5 
vol% in the supply chain of upgraded biogas integrated into the European Union's (EU) natural gas 
grid. 
1.1 Biogas upgrading technologies 
Biogas upgrading concerns the achievement of effective carbon capture technologies; because once 
the CO2 is separated from methane, highly pure biomethane can be supplied to natural gas facilities 
and used directly as a raw ingredient for energy generation. If the captured CO2 could be stored for a 
long term, it is also possible to end up in negative CO2 emissions since most of the CO2 utilized for 
biomass production at the beginning of the process is removed from the carbon cycle to secure the 
heat at the natural gas level [12,17]. As of 2014, there were more than 13,000 biogas plants with 260 
biogas upgrading facilities, corresponding to an upgrading capacity of more than 100,000 m3/h within 
IEA member countries, in which Denmark shares 137 of the biogas plants with only one biogas 
upgrading facility, making up 180 m3/h upgrading capacity although a political agreement was made 
to entirely replace highly carbon-emitting natural gas with green biogas by 2035 [16,18]. This

highlights the necessity of successful utilization of biogas upgrading technologies. The major mature

1.1.1 Physical scrubbing with water or organic solvents 
In water scrubbing, water is used to physically absorb CO2 from biogas in the absorber column at high 
pressure; CO2 is released from the water by using air at atmospheric pressure as the stripper medium 
in the desorber column [19]. A simplified process flow diagram of a water scrubber is given in Figure 2 
such that high-pressure biogas is injected from the bottom of the absorber column, which is usually 
filled with packing to increase the contact area of the gas-liquid interface. Water is injected from the 
top of the column with a counter-current flow of gas and liquid to minimize the reboiler duty and 
methane loss while maximizing the CO2 removal rate. Also, utilizing a recirculating water system 
reduces water demand and assures more stable operation [19]. The absorption process is very much 
selective toward CO2 due to its higher solubility in water, but a small amount of methane is absorbed 
by water as well. 
 
Figure 2. Simplified process flow diagram of a water scrubber. 
Lowering the pressure gradually first in the flash column releases the main share of absorbed 
methane with a small share of the CO2 to be recirculated to the compressor and mixed with the 
biogas entering the scrubber as recirculated gas. Then, water enters the top and air enters from the 
bottom of the desorber column to trigger CO2 desorption by lowering its partial pressure [19]. 
It is also possible to use organic solvents instead of water to optimize the physical absorption of CO2 
into the water. It has been demonstrated that the physical solubility of CO2 relies on Lewis acid-base 
interactions between the acid gas and the absorbent, thus solvents with strong electronegative groups 
are preferred such as methanol, n-formyl morpholine, n-methyl-2-pyrrolidone, propylene carbonate, 
poly(ethylene glycol) dimethyl ether, tributyl phosphate, and tetramethyl sulfone [14]. Since the 
solubility of CO2 in the organic solvents are about five times higher than in water, significantly less 
volume of solvent compared to water scrubbing is required for recirculation within the system [19]. 
Whereas, one significant drawback of employing the physical absorption process is the considerable 
energy demand associated with compressing the feed gas to high pressure [20]. In addition, the 
physical upgrading technologies, do not have the capability to generate a CO2-rich product, which is 
useful for storage and utilization purposes [21].

1.1.2 Pressure swing adsorption 
Pressure swing adsorption (PSA) consists of columns filled with a porous material having a high 
specific surface area such as activated carbon, silica gel, alumina, zeolite, or metal-organic 
frameworks, maximizing the gas-adsorbent contact to adsorb CO2 at high-pressure and then CO2 is 
desorbed at low pressure in a cyclic batch process of so-called Skarstrom cycle [14,19,22]. The 
adsorbents can either absorb a larger load of CO2 than methane or adsorb CO2 faster than methane 
with improved diffusion rates [19]. The simplified process flow diagram for a PSA unit is given in 
Figure 3 such that the four phases of pressurization, feed, blowdown, and purge make up the PSA 
column cycle [19]. 
 
Figure 3: Simplified process flow diagram of a PSA unit. 
Initially, water and H2S is removed from raw biogas since it can irreversibly damage the adsorbent 
material. Then, the column is fed with pressurized raw biogas, so CO2 is selectively adsorbed in the 
column bed while methane remains unretained by the adsorbent [19]. After that, CO2 within the 
saturated bed is desorbed by decreasing the pressure through the blowdown phase, the rich gas is 
evacuated from the column, and the upgraded gas is blown through the column through the purge 
phase. Finally, the column can be reutilized through re-pressurization with either raw biogas or 
upgraded gas [19]. 
A continuously operating PSA unit would typically have several parallel interconnected columns with 
one column always engaged in adsorption while the other columns are responsible for the different 
phases of regeneration. Therefore, the methane loss and energy consumption are reduced as the 
existing gas flow from one column during blowdown is used to pressurize another column in a 
pressure equalization phase [19]. The utilization of several columns in a PSA unit allows the 
possibility of tuning several process parameters like feed pressure, purging pressure, adsorbent type, 
cycle time, column interconnectedness, etc. to maximize the biomethane yield, reduce the methane 
loss and energy demand [19]. 
PSA offers high selectivity to efficiently remove CO2 from the biogas stream; modular design available 
at a small scale, allowing scalability and flexibility to adapt various gas flow rates and capacities; and 
minimal solvent use, unlike the solvent-based upgrading processes, resulting in reduced 
environmental impact and simplified operation [21,23,24]. Nevertheless, PSA comes with challenges

1.1.3 Membrane technology 
The separation of CO2 from methane is possible due to its relatively low molecular size within a 
porous interface of selectively permeable membranes when differential partial pressure is applied as 
the driving force [14,22]. Although there is no heating demand and an additional desorption step is not 
necessary, typically multiple stages should be implemented to deal with high methane loss through 
the membrane separation. However, more efficient polymeric membranes with less manufacturing 
cost, inorganic membranes enabling CO2 separation above 400 °C, and innovative mixed matrix 
membranes that combine the advantages of polymeric and inorganic membranes are continuously 
being developed [25]. The simplified process design for biogas upgrading with membrane technology 
is given in Figure 4 such that pressurized raw biogas could only be fed to the membrane unit once it 
has been cleaned from the membrane contaminants of water and H2S [19]. 
 
Figure 4: Simplified process design for a membrane upgrading process. 
Overall, membrane technology offers low capital costs due to the elimination of the requirement for 
large pretreatment and solvent storage units. Also, the major operational cost is the cost of membrane 
replacement after its lifespan, which is small in size and light in weight; costing significantly lower than 
the operational costs of conventional techniques utilizing large amounts of solvents or sorbents [26]. 
Whereas, there is still a methane loss of around 4-10 % after the membrane separation process [21]. 
1.1.4 Chemical scrubbing with amines 
In chemical scrubbing, a solvent is employed to selectively absorb CO2 from biogas through a 
chemical reaction. Water solutions of amines are commonly used as chemical solvents to bind to the 
CO2 molecule through an exothermic reaction, removing it from the gas, and absorbing it within the 
solution. Then, CO2 is removed from the amine solution by the addition of heat [19]. Although the 
absorption of CO2 is thermodynamically favored by low temperatures since the CO2 solubility in water 
increases with decreasing temperature, the reaction rate between CO2 and the amine increases with 
increasing temperature from a kinetic standpoint [19]. 
A simplified process flow diagram of an amine scrubber system is given in Figure 5, where CO2 is first 
removed from the raw biogas, in the absorber as it reacts with the lean amine solution forming rich 
amine. Then, CO2 is removed from the rich amine solution with heat provided by a reboiler that

generates steam, lowering the CO2 partial pressure, thus releasing CO2 from the amine with improved 
desorption kinetics [19]. 
 
Figure 5: Simplified process diagram of a chemical scrubber. 
Although chemical scrubbing with amines possesses the main disadvantage of high heat 
consumption for solvent regeneration, its high efficiency, maturity, and low cost are the obvious 
advantages over the other biogas upgrading technologies [27]. For instance, amines react selectively 
with CO2, providing near-zero CH4 loss at significantly lower operating pressures that can be achieved 
by using only a blower instead of a compressor, reducing the electric energy demand drastically, 
which is impossible to achieve with other biogas upgrading technologies [28]. In the end, it is possible 
to produce a CO2-rich product for storage or utilization purposes [21]. Besides, the highly corrosive 
minor impurity of H2S can also be completely absorbed in the amine scrubber, eliminating the need 
for a possible desulfurization step during the biogas cleaning process [28]. However, it is important to 
note that the absorption of H2S in the scrubber will only delay the need to treat the amine solvent. The 
amine solvent will eventually become saturated with H2S and need to be treated to remove the H2S to 
avoid corrosion of the scrubber and efficiency loss of the solvent." 
In amine scrubbing, physical CO2 absorption is favored at low temperatures with increasing CO2 
solubility, whereas the reaction rate between CO2 and amines increases with increasing temperature; 
allowing the possibility to optimize the absorption column temperature. Traditionally, aqueous 
solutions of single amine solvents like monoethanolamine (MEA), methyldiethanolamine (MDEA), and 
diethanolamine (DEA) are commonly used as amine solvents [19]. Whereas, recently the principle of 
the addition of either a primary or secondary amine to a tertiary amine has been studied due to the 
high reaction rate of the primary or secondary amine with CO2 combined with the low heat of reaction 
of the tertiary amine such as utilizing the solvent blend of piperazine (PZ) activated MDEA [29]. 
The use of activated aqueous solutions of MDEA with the activated PZ as a solvent in chemical 
scrubbing has become popular recently due to the possibility of achieving high reaction rates at low 
activator concentrations, lowering the energy costs, and noncorrosive properties towards the 
absorption column material of carbon steel [30]. Given their molecular structures in Figure 6, there is 
not much investigation on the exact activation mechanisms of the MDEA/PZ solvent blend and the 
optimal formulation for the lowest energy costs and highest CO2 capture fraction.

1.2 Motivation 
To completely replace the traditional natural gas with green biogas by 2035 in Denmark, the 
requirement of establishing new biogas upgrading plants is evident. However, building new biogas 
upgrading facilities will require a considerate selection and design of mature biogas upgrading 
technologies that possess advantages successfully outweigh the disadvantages. 
This project aims to investigate the possible utilization of the MDEA/PZ solvent blend in the chemical 
scrubbing process for the biogas upgrading process. Therefore, a comprehensive study of the 
absorber column on testing different formulations of MDEA/PZ solvent blend and various process 
parameters would enable us to give insights about the feasibility of utilizing this solvent blend in 
effectively removing CO2 from the raw biogas. 
Considering the limited timespan of this project, the absorber column is chosen for process 
optimization instead of the desorber column since its performance is dependent on the CO2 
absorption rate and the attainment of equilibrium between the gas and liquid phases. Whereas, the 
limiting parameter of the desorber column is only the attainment of equilibrium. Hence, the 
performance of the absorber column directly influences the design for the complete biogas upgrading 
process. 
Accordingly, DTU’s pilot absorber column was available for tests with MDEA/PZ solvent blend. First, 
the effect of the following key parameters in the process optimization for achieving a feasible CO2 
capture efficiency in DTU’s pilot absorber column with the MDEA/PZ solvent blend was investigated 
through a comprehensive initial Aspen Plus® V11 modelling work: 
• 
Biogas flowrate, 
• 
Lean solvent flow rate, 
• 
Lean solvent temperature, 
• 
Solvent formulation, 
• 
Temperature and CO2 concentration profiles over the absorber column. 
Upon developing a qualitative understanding of the effects of the key process parameters through 
modelling DTU’s pilot absorber column that aims to utilize MDEA/PZ solvent blend, the absorber 
column was prepared for the experimental campaigns. Thereby, the following process parameters 
were selected for investigation throughout the experimental campaigns considering the limited

availability of MDEA and PZ and difficulties of regenerating the solvent for reuse during the limited 
timespan of this project: 
• 
Lean solvent flow rate,

Temperature profiles over the absorber column. 
Thereafter, analyzing the online measurements of the absorber column’s instruments over the time

With the results from the online measurements of the experimental campaigns, the investigated

2. Methods and experimental work 
In this section, the methods to develop an initial Aspen Plus® V11 model to simulate an absorber 
column used for chemical scrubbing with MDEA/PZ solvent blend will be presented first. Then, the 
pilot absorber column at DTU will be described including the calibration work done with its 
instruments, the plan for experimental campaigns based on the initial modelling work, and the 
methods to analyze the data from the experimental campaigns. Finally, a fine-tuned Aspen Plus® V11 
model to simulate the specific experimental conditions at each steady-state region will be introduced. 
2.1 Process modelling  
Process modelling is a crucial step toward evaluating the comprehensive performance of the absorber 
column under various operating conditions and with different solvent blend formulations. The absorber 
column used for chemical scrubbing with MDEA/PZ solvent blend was chosen to be studied instead of 
the desorber column for the modelling and experimental work. The performance of the absorber 
column is influenced by both the rate at which the absorption reaction occurs and the attainment of 
equilibrium between the gas and liquid phases. However, the performance of the desorber column is 
only limited by equilibrium. Therefore, gaining an understanding of the performance of the absorber 
column at various process conditions would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness and 
feasibility of the design for the complete biogas upgrading process. 
By systematically varying the process parameters such as the lean solvent temperature, flow rates, 
and solvent formulation; the optimal conditions that maximize the CO2 capture efficiency could be 
figured out. The results of the initial modelling studies would provide insights into the process 
behavior and help design the experimental campaigns with an appropriate selection of operating 
conditions.

were:

•

state for vapor, 
• 
Activity-based reaction kinetics, 
• 
Electrolyte transport property models,

2.2 Experimental work 
Based on the results of the initial modelling work, experimental campaigns were planned in the pilot 
absorber column at DTU. Experiments were designed to investigate the effect of the previously 
studied process parameters of L/G and Tlean on CO2 slip using different solvent blend formulations in 
each campaign. 
2.2.1 Process description 
The P&ID of the DTU pilot absorber column that the experiments were run is given on the next page 
with Figure 10. The pilot absorber column is built of 10 glass tubes having a height of 1 m and an 
inner diameter of 0.1 m each, which are connected with flanges, enabling the sampling of the liquid 
from the valves (V01 to V10) at every meter. Four packing elements with the type Mellapak 250Y 
having a diameter of 0.084 m and a height of 0.205 m fills each 1 m glass section, thus the total 
packed height is 8.2 m and the packing volume is 0.0454 m3. 13 mm Thermaflex material insulates 
the glass sections to minimize the heat losses during the experimental campaigns. There are also 
digital temperature probes (T01 to T11) monitoring the liquid temperatures at every meter. 
The liquid inlet is possible at the heights of 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 m height with the valves (V15 to V19 
respectively). For the experimental campaigns in this project, the lean solvent enters the absorber 
column from the top with valve V19 being opened from a mobile solvent tank connected via valve V41 
and pumped with pump F44. To control the lean solvent temperature, a mobile heat exchanger was 
also installed on the liquid inlet stream just before the lean solvent inlets the column at the top. The 
liquid is distributed into the column with a round spray nozzle having 18 drip points, making up about 
1,400 drip points per square meter. The temperature probe at the top (TI01) is used to monitor the 
lean solvent inlet temperature. Since the pressure gauge PI01 cannot be monitored digitally, it was 
assumed that the lean solvent inlets the column with 1 atm head, totaling 2 atm absolute pressure. 
The lean solvent flow rate can be controlled with the valve V14 and the flow rate can be digitally 
monitored via the Coriolis flow meter (FI14). 
The gas enters the absorber column 0.25 m below the packing with an open-ended pipe. Only CO2 
was added to the column with its flow rate controlled by the valve (V21) connected to the variable 
area flow meter (FI21) and digitally monitored by the Coriolis flowmeter (FI20). The column is purged 
with N2 before the experimental campaigns with an N2 flask connected from valve (V22). Therefore, 
the total gas flow can be controlled by adjusting the fan speed and monitored via the Coriolis 
flowmeter (FI38). Also, the gas inlet pressure, temperature, and CO2 concentration can be monitored 
by the digital pressure gauge (PI38), temperature probe (TI38), and gas analyzer (CI38). There is also 
a gas analyzer (CI32) connected to the gas outlet stream that measures the CO2 concentration at the 
outlet, which was used to determine the CO2 slip. All the readings by the digital instruments were 
logged into a file within a one-second sampling interval for data analysis after the experimental 
campaigns.

•

2.2.4 Analytical methods for the experimental work 
Analysis of the transient recorded with the instruments along the column, samples taken from different 
heights of the column at the steady-state regions, and determination of the exact solvent blend 
formulation with titration after mixing its components enabled to construct the results of the 
experimental campaigns.

solvent inlet flow rate. Since the gas analyzers show the CO2 concentration at 25 °C and atmospheric

This section presents the experimental and modelling results. Firstly, an initial modelling investigation

Possible wetting issues 
Once the lean solvent travels from the top to the bottom of the column, a thin layer of liquid film is 
formed on the surface of the packing material. An increase in the flow rate of the lean solvent flow 
rate also increases the wetted surface area, providing an increased interfacial area between the liquid 
and gas phases. With the increased interfacial area, there is more opportunity for CO2 absorption by 
the lean solvent. 
However, there is a limit beyond which the benefits of utilizing a higher L/G value diminish and 
become detrimental due to flooding issues. Once the fluid motion becomes more unpredictable with a 
turbulent flow, there is a higher possibility of flooding issues since the inertial forces dominate in 
turbulent flow, forming chaotic eddies, vortices, and various flow instabilities. Therefore, at high flow 
rates, liquid tends to accumulate and flow through a limited number of preferred pathways, bypassing

larger cross-sectional area can significantly increase the manufacturing price for the absorber column

Simulations predicted the decrease in CO2 slip with increasing L/G values and the possibility of 
achieving the target CO2 slip of 2.5 mol% using lower L/G values as lower Tlean values are selected. 
This supports the CO2 slips achieved at the L/G values tested between 7.5 to 12.5 during the 
experimental campaigns. To explain the reason behind achieving the target CO2 slip with lower L/G 
values as Tlean values decrease, the inverse proportion between L and CO2 capture capacity of the 
solvent (∆α) in Equation (10) should be reminded again through Equations (7) to (9). Since the 
absorption of CO2 with amines happens through an exothermic reaction pathway, the dissipated heat 
results in a temperature increase over the column. Whereas, the absorption of CO2 is 
thermodynamically favored at low temperatures. Once higher Tlean values are selected, the 
temperature over the column would increase to higher values with the exothermic reaction happening. 
The higher extent of temperature increase should decrease the ∆α for the solvent significantly since 
CO2 absorption becomes unfavourable at higher temperatures. Therefore, the breaks in the curves as 
L/G increases shown in Figure 23 become sharper with lower Tlean values, enabling to achieve the 
target CO2 slip with lower L/G values. 
Additionally, the simulation data in Figure 23 shows lower CO2 slip values that are still well above the 
target CO2 slip value at the same L/G values could be achieved using higher Tlean values until certain 
L/G values before the curves break. To explain the reason behind being able to achieve lower CO2 
slip values with higher Tlean values at low L/G values, but achieving higher CO2 slip values with lower 
Tlean values at high L/G values, Fick’s first law of diffusion given for the CO2 absorption process 
through Equations (11) and (12) should be reminded again. At low L/G values, the lean solvent flow 
rate is not enough to completely wet the packing material in the column. However, the extent of 
thermal expansion for the solvent blend needs to be considered at higher Tlean values. Apparently, the 
reason that the higher Tlean values provide low CO2 slip at low L/G values is related to the higher 
surface area coverage of the packing material by the solvent blend at higher Tlean values. The 
increased interfacial area between the lean solvent and CO2 enhances the mass transfer between the 
two species. Moreover, the effect of the enhanced mass transfer due to thermal expansion of the 
liquid phase at higher Tlean values might become more predominant than the lower CO2 capture 
capacity of the solvent at low L/G values. 
On the other hand, once complete wetting of the packing material is achieved with higher L/G values, 
thermal expansion of the liquid becomes insignificant and CO2 absorption is limited by the expression 
for diffusion flux given in Equation (12). There, lower Tlean values would provide a temperature 
increase along the column to a lower limit, providing a higher Kov value as CO2 absorption is 
thermodynamically favoured at low temperatures. Also, the increase in partial pressure of CO2 from 
the solvent phase will decrease the driving force in the equilibrium term of Equation (12), further 
decreasing the diffusion flux of CO2. Therefore, after a certain lean solvent flow rate that enables 
complete wetting of the packing material, a lower CO2 slip can be achieved using lower Tlean values at 
the same L/G value compared to using higher L/G values. 
Furthermore, the simulation data also suggest intercooling might enhance the diffusion of CO2 from 
the gas phase to the liquid phase. The temperature decrease due to the cooling of intercoolers would

absorption process. With a lower extent of temperature increase, the partial pressure of CO2 in the 
solvent will be lower which would improve the driving force in the equilibrium term of Equation (12). 
Moreover, the solubility of CO2 in the solvent blend will increase as intercooling eliminates the 
hotspots over the column and further promote the diffusion of CO2 from the gas phase to the liquid

Firstly, the absorber profiles given in Figure 27, Figure 28, and Figure 29 data show that it is 
necessary to use higher L/G values as higher Tlean values were selected to achieve the target CO2 slip 
values of around 0.1 mol%, 1 mol%, and 10 mol%. This observation supports the previous 
discussions made on the diminishing effect of utilizing higher Tlean values on the CO2 capture capacity 
of the solvent (∆α) since CO2 absorption becomes unfavourable at higher temperatures. 
As the solvent flows from the top to the bottom of the absorber column, the CO2 loading of the solvent 
increases with the exothermic reaction of CO2 absorption. Due to the exothermic reaction with the 
solvent travelling from top to bottom and absorbing CO2 on its way, the temperature along the packing 
height increases as seen in simulations. The point where there is no mass transfer of CO2 from the 
gas phase to the liquid phase upon the exothermic absorption reaction can be defined as the pinch 
point. At the pinch point, the CO2 concentration does not change between adjacent stages over the 
packing. In the simulations, near-pinch behaviours are observed once low L/G and high Tlean values 
are selected. Although not studied with the simulations in this project, there are cases that the pinch 
point is pushed to the bottom, where the rich solvent exits the column due to insufficiently low L/G 
values providing the greatest absorption rate at the top-end of the column [40]. 
The point where the CO2 absorption rate is highest that provides the highest temperature on 
temperature profiles can be defined as the bulge point. Since the solvent blend has a higher heat 
capacity than the gas phase, increasing L/G values would provide an excessive amount of solvent to 
push the heat of the reaction more to the bottom end of the column. This results in a bulge point 
happening at a lower height of the packing as observed in simulations once the L/G values are 
increased at the same Tlean values. With a comprehensive discussion on the temperature profiles 
observed in a pilot plant study by Gunawan et al., it is evident that having the bulge point at the pinch 
point is rare, so it is not expected for the temperature bulge to affect the mass transfer performance in 
most of the cases. 
Apparently, the simulation data also shows that the maximum temperatures reached with higher L/G 
values to achieve lower CO2 slip values are lower than the maximum temperatures reached once 
lower L/G values are used to achieve higher CO2 slip values. At higher L/G values, solvent travels 
faster through the column, absorbing CO2, and releasing heat through the exothermic absorption 
process. However, as the solvent progresses down the column, the absorbed CO2 concentration 
increases, leading to a decrease in ∆α and a reduced heat generation rate. Since the maximum 
temperature is reached at a lower height when higher L/G values are used with improved CO2 
absorption rate; the solvent is saturated earlier, and further heat generation is minimized. Therefore, 
using higher L/G values lead to a lower maximum temperature observed along the column due to the 
earlier saturation of the solvent and the subsequent decrease in heat generation. 
Below the bulge point, it is observed that the liquid temperature starts to decrease and the rate of 
decrease increases until the bottom of the column. Once the cold and dry gas contacts the hot 
solvent, the liquid temperature is expected to decrease due to the sensible heat duty requirement to 
equalize the temperatures of the two phases at the bulge point. However, it is important to note that 
as the liquid temperature increases, the rate of vaporization will increase. The enthalpy of 
==PAGE:58==
Can Demir 
Experimental investigation of novel solvents for CO2 capture  
July 8, 2023

vaporization for water is way higher than its specific heat capacity. Therefore, the required heat for the 
vaporization of the aqueous solvent blend upon contact with the dry gas is expected to pull a 
significant amount of heat away from the liquid phase, resulting in a progressive decrease in liquid 
temperature towards the bottom of the column. Although the biogas temperature profiles were not 
studied with the simulations, biogas temperature is expected to increase until the bulge point, where 
the temperatures equalize each other. As biogas travels further up to the column, its temperature is 
expected to decrease.

4. Conclusion 
In this work, the absorber column within the biogas upgrading process utilizing MDEA/PZ solvent has 
been modelled with an experimental validation using DTU’s pilot absorber column. A rate-based 
Aspen Plus model was used initially to model the performance of carbon capture efficiency with DTU’s 
pilot absorber column, in which a dry gas stream consisting of 48 mol% N2, 12 mol% O2, and 40 mol% 
CO2 entered the column from the bottom and various aqueous formulations of MDEA/PZ solvent 
blend always with the same molality of 7.33 mol amine/kg water and with a lean loading of 0.1 mol 
CO2/mol amine entered the column from the top. 
Initially, an investigation for a sufficient residence time by varying gas flow rates from 1 to 14.5 kg/h 
while maintaining the same L/G value showed that a minimum residence time of around 30 seconds 
should be used in order to avoid mass-transfer rate limitations while using DTU’s pilot absorber 
column that has a height of 10.25 m and a diameter of 0.1 m. Therefore, a gas flow rate of 13.26 kg/h 
was chosen as a rule of thumb for the rest of the initial simulation work. 
Five formulations with a PZ wt% of zero, 4.91, 7.86, 9.82, and 14.73 were tested in the initial 
simulations at various lean solvent inlet temperatures of 30 °C, 35 °C, 40 °C, and 50 °C. The results 
showed that it is not possible to reach the target CO2 slip of 2.5 mol% at the given process conditions 
without having PZ in the formulation. Whereas, the increase of PZ concentration allowed to achieve 
the target CO2 slip value with a lower L/G value compared to the formulations that had lower PZ 
concentrations. Besides, the studies on the effect of the lean solvent inlet temperatures showed that 
the target CO2 slip could be achieved by using lower L/G values once lower values of the lean solvent 
temperatures are selected. It was possible to reach the target slip with L/G values of 9.05, 9.80, 
10.35, and 12.00 at lean solvent temperatures of 30 °C, 35 °C, 40 °C, and 45 °C respectively using 
the solvent blend formulation that has 7.86 wt% PZ. 
Moreover, the temperature and CO2 concentration profiles over the packing of the absorber column 
were also studied at lean solvent inlet temperatures of 30 °C, 35 °C, and 40 °C that specifically 
selected L/G values provided low, ideal, and high CO2 slip values of 0.1 mol%, 1 mol%, and 10 mol% 
at each lean solvent inlet temperature tested. The increase in lean solvent temperature also increased 
the bulge temperature and required L/G value to achieve the targeted CO2 slip values. Once lower 
L/G values that gave 10 mol% CO2 slip were used, near-pinch behaviors were observed in the CO2 
concentration profiles. 
Having developed a broad understanding of the CO2 capture performance of the MDEA/PZ solvent 
blend with various process conditions, experimental campaigns were designed to qualitatively validate 
the initial modelling work and further validate the model through fine-tuned simulations. Due to the 
limited amount of MDEA and PZ available, PZ concentrations of only 5.18 wt%, 8.29 wt%, 9.29 wt% 
with a molality of again 7.33 mol amine/kg, but a lean loading of 0.1 mol CO2/mol amine were tested. 
Qualitative comparisons with the initial modelling work validated the initial findings of the possibility to

achieve the target CO2 slip with lower L/G once lower lean solvent inlet temperatures were used, the 
increase in CO2 slip with lower L/G values, and PZ promotion in CO2 capture efficiency. 
Furthermore, model validation of the experimental campaigns was constructed with a fine-tuned 
simulation model that separated CO2 and recycle streams, took the amount of vaporized water in the 
recirculation into consideration, and utilized the same process specifications at the steady-state 
regions of the experiments. Although model-fitted temperature profiles predicted some of the 
temperature profiles of the experimental steady-state regions very precisely and most of the trends in 
the temperature profiles, some steady-state regions deviated from the model fit. Therefore, it is 
necessary to perform the mass balance calculations over the column to see the validity of the 
determination of steady-state regions based on online measurements. Hence, the fine-tuned model 
can also be further validated once the samples taken alongside the column at the steady-state 
regions would be analyzed and compared with the model CO2 concentrations of the recirculating gas. 
Overall, this work demonstrated the model validation for the evaluation of the CO2 capture 
performance of MDEA/PZ solvent blend with further experimental validation at DTU’s pilot absorber 
column utilizing various process conditions. With the results of this work, the process specifications 
utilizing MDEA/PZ solvent blend could be optimized for future absorber column designs over the next 
ten years to completely replace natural gas with environmentally friendly biomethane in Denmark to 
significantly reduce CO2 emissions due to the crucial need of energy generation.

5. Future work 
The cumulative progression of this project over a limited time span of six and half months allows the 
discussion of possible future work. Firstly, the samples taken at different heights of the absorber 
column at the steady-state regions of the experimental campaigns can be analyzed through density 
correlations for PZ/MDEA solvent blend. Therefore, the validity of steady-state regions could be 
assessed once CO2 loadings of the solvent at various heights of the column would be compared with 
the CO2 concentration profiles of the recirculating gas in fine-tuned simulations. Furthermore, the 
mass balance discussed in 3.4.2 Model validation of the experimental campaigns could be validated 
with the measurement of CO2 loading of the solvent leaving the column from the bottom at each 
steady-state region. 
It will also be worthwhile to test higher PZ/MDEA formulations and different amine molalities to 
optimize the solvent formulation that would provide the maximum CO2 capture efficiency that satisfies 
the targeted biomethane supply using the absorber column. 
It is also possible to develop more comprehensive fine-tuned simulations to successfully validate the 
model by taking the amount of water condensed due to the introduction of cold and dry CO2 in the 
system by utilizing a flash stage on the recirculation stream. 
To improve the performance of further experimental operations, the recirculation fan should be 
upgraded since its fluctuation also affects the L/G value and makes it difficult to achieve steady-state 
regions with a limited amount of solvent available. 
Furthermore, the work can be extended to the complete biogas upgrading process by introducing a 
desorber column to the simulation studies. Therefore, the reboiler duty can be decreased with further 
optimization of the complete process.
