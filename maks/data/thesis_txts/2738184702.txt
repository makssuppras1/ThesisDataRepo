Abstract 
In recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) the accumulation of ammonium-nitrogen origination 
from the feed, is often the second-limiting factor after oxygen. The biofilter is therefore a vital 
component in maintaining good water quality, as it oxidizes toxic ammonium into the non-toxic 
nitrate. However, the startup of a new biofilter is a long and costly process, primarily due to slow 
growth rate of the nitrifying bacteria. In the search for ways to shorten the start-up process, does 
this study investigate the effects that mechanical pre-treatment by abrasion and chemical 
reduction of water tension by a surfactant (ECO surf), have on the sinking rate and the bacterial 
colonization of two types of biocarriers: polypropylene (PP) RK biocarriers and polyethylene (PE) 
Mutag biocarriers, which differs in structure, specific surface area and materials composition. Both 
treatments significantly enhanced the sinking rate of the biocarriers. However, the pre-treatments 
did not significantly affect the ammonium removal rate but appeared to initially hinder the 
establishment of ammonium-oxidizing and nitrite-oxidizing bacterial communities, particularly on 
the Mutag biocarriers, though the effect on the nitrifiers lessened over time. The Respirometry test 
exposed a decline in heterotrophic bacteria activity over time, which potentially caused a failure in 
the biofilm observed at the end of the experiment. The study highlights a complex relationship 
between biocarrier properties, treatments and the bacterial community, highlighting the need for 
further research to optimize the start-up of biofilter in RAS.

Introduction 
 
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) is a technology that have seen major growth in the last 
couple of decades. Aquaculture stands for nearly half of the worlds fish production (The State of 
World Fisheries and Aquaculture 2022.  Towards Blue Transformation., 2022). To make the whole 
aquaculture industry more sustainable, RAS play a continuously more vital role, which enables the 
production to be moved from sea to land, while doing intense farming and still minimizing the 
environmental impact. In a recirculation aquaculture system only up to 10% of the water is 
exchanged daily (Keuter et al., 2017), this highly intensive recirculation leads to the build-up of 
different compounds. The most relevant of these compounds is ammonium, which exist in two from, 
the un-ionized ammonia (𝑁𝐻3) and ionized ammonium (𝑁𝐻4

The ammonium stems from nitrogen-rich food and gets excreted through the faecal matter and gills 
of the fish(Timmons et al., 2007). If not removed, the un-ionized ammonia and nitrite can become 
toxic for the fish (Jr. & Morris, 1986; Randall & Tsui, 2002). The 96-hour LC50 (LC=lethal concentration) 
varies a lot between species, pink salmon being in the lower end with and 96-hour LC50 at

However, it can also inhibit the appetite at lower levels, which leads to a lower growth rate (Zeitoun 
& Mehana, 2016). To mitigate this build-up of inorganic nitrogen compounds, the biofilter is a vital 
part of the water treatment process of any RAS operation. The biofilter is a biological filter which 
utilizes nitrifying bacteria that convert the dissolved ammonium into the non-toxic nitrate (𝑁𝑂3), 
which is called nitrification (Hüpeden et al., 2020; Timmons et al., 2007) 
Biofilters is separated in two groups, suspended growth system and fixed film, where the latter is 
the most widely used in RAS (Malone & Pfeiffer, 2006). There are many subtypes of fixed film 
biofilters, they function by having a filter media, which provide a surface for formation of microbial 
biofilm. Dissolved and colloid inorganic nitrogen and organic matter can then be transported from 
the fish tank, into the filter where it is transported into the biofilm by diffusion, in the biofilm it 
encounters the active microbes that transform the waste (Malone & Pfeiffer, 2006; Timmons et al., 
2007). The filter media comes in many different types, such as shells, sand, rocks and plastic, the 
biomedia or biocarriers, can be any material that provides surface area for the bacteria to settle on. 
The biofilm is a large bacterial community that together provides protection and ensures that the 
bacterial community can grow to a large enough size, which makes them able of converting the large 
quantity of nutrients getting excreted to the water every day.  
Nitrification is usually looked at as two-step process, where ammonium-oxidizing bacteria (AOB) or 
ammonium-oxidizing archaea (AOA) converts the dissolved ammonium into nitrite 𝑁𝑂2, which then 
can be oxidized by nitrite-oxidizing bacteria (NOB), transforming it into nitrate, a non-toxic molecule 
for the fish (Chen et al., 2006; Ebeling et al., 2006). The AOB’s kinetic reaction rate are higher than

therefore very important, as the bacteria together in the biofilm is protected from the water , helping 
them adhere to the surface, and not getting dragged on by the current that moves through the filter

layer which acts as a transport of molecules and oxygen, from the water phase and into the active 
biofilm (Prehn et al., 2012). )Prehn et al. (2012 showed that increasing the hydraulic flow over the 
biofilm resulted in a thinner diffusion layer, which increased the transport of ammonium and nitrite, 
and subsequently the nitrification process. The pores in the biofilm also effect it’s permeability, as 
larger porous allows for larger molecules to penetrate (Zhang & Bishop, 1994).    
The startup of a biofilter can be a long process, and it can take weeks up until several months before, 
before the filter can handle the full nitrogen load from the production months (Aalto et al., 2022; 
Ruiz et al., 2020). The long generation time of the nitrifiers, is the reason for the long startup time, 
and a factor that contributes to the high cost. The biofilter have been estimated to be responsible 
for 20% of the investment costs of a RAS operation (Eshchar et al., 2006), and the filter’s efficiency 
is a limiting factor in the fish production. Therefore, is the reduction of the startup time an issue with 
large economic implications, as the cost of running the biofilter is the same, whether it’s operating 
on at full efficiency or is in its start-up phase.  
A large part of the initial cost of the biofilter are the biocarriers and their effect on the startup time. 
Biocarriers can be made from a variety of materials, including organic, inorganic, and synthetic 
options. Polypropylene (PP) and polyethylene (PE) are two common material choices for synthetic 
biocarriers due to their relative ease of production and cost-effectiveness. They can be produced in 
multiple shapes that maximizes the surface area compared to the volume that they occupy. A 
biocarriers specific surface area is therefore given as its area pr. Volume (
𝑚2
𝑚3). Another advantage of 
PP or PE is its durability against mechanical and chemical degradation and fractures (Aalto et al., 
2022), caused by them bumping into each other in the filter, or by the water and particles flowing 
over the surface.  
The usage of PP and PE as biocarriers also have several disadvantages, that all can have a negative 
effect on the formation of the biofilm, and therefore adds time to the start-up phase. PP and PE are 
inherently hydrophobic due to their nonpolar molecular structure, which does not interact well with 
polar water molecules. This hydrophobic nature makes them water-repellent (Khushboo Dem, 
2023), which causes both mechanical and biological problems for the biofilm formation. Before the 
bacteria can attach to the surface, it must come into contact with the water phase. The hydrophobic 
properties cause the media to be repealed by the water, and therefore stay above the water column, 
with only a fraction of the surface area being forced down into the water, by the weight of each 
biocarrier itself, and of those laying on top of it. Aside from the repulsion by the water, the surface 
porosity can lead to formation of air bubbles, that further helps the biocarriers to be lifted out of 
the water column.

Another property of PE and PP that poses problems for the bacteria, is their position in the negative 
end of the triboelectric series, which means that they tend to gain electrons when in contact with 
most other materials, becoming more negatively charged. Adding on this is the chemical structure 
of long hydrocarbons, that lack functional groups that easily donate electrons to form a positive 
charge (MAHMUD et al., 2017). Most bacteria have molecules in their cell walls and outer 
membranes that carry a net negative charge, due to the presence of phosphate and carboxyl groups 
(Jiang et al., 2005). The negative charge in both the bacteria and the biocarrier surface causes them 
to repeal each other, creating an energy barrier that the bacteria must overcome. The negative 
charge also makes the biofilm less robust, and more prone to falling off (Al-Amshawee et al., 2020; 
Mao et al., 2017). Mao et al. (2017) showed that using electrophilic biocarriers resulted in higher 
attached biomass, and subsequently enhanced the biofilters efficiency.  
This study focuses on improving the physical and chemical conditions, during the startup phase of 
the biofilter. The study sets out to investigate the promise of potential chemical or mechanical 
treatments that biocarriers can be subjected to, specifically biocarriers made of polypropylene (PP) 
and polyethylene (PE). 
The treatments are focused on shortening the time it takes for the biocarriers to sink in the water 
column and making favourable conditions for the nitrifying bacteria, so a fully functional biofilter 
can be achieved in a shorter timeframe. A solution would benefit a RAS operation both economically 
and its sustainability.   
Biofilter evaluation 
This study uses multiple methods in the evaluation of the effect of the treatment. A common way of 
evaluating the efficiency of a biofilter, is by measuring the degradation and turnover rate of 
ammonium and nitrite into the end product nitrate (Kamstra et al., 2017; Kinyage & Pedersen, 2016; 
Mnyoro et al., 2021; Navada et al., 2021). The microbial community in the biofilter is made up of 
autotrophic and heterotrophic bacteria, which both can be found as part of the biofilm attached to 
the biocarrier surface, and as free-living bacteria in the water. As the biofilter is made up of a 
complex microbial community, where TAN can be used both by the autotrophs as an energy source 
and by heterotrophs directly for biomass formation (Ebeling et al., 2006). Will the method of 
measuring the reduction of a single substrate like ammonium or the production of nitrate, not give 
adequate information about the specific microbial activity of the biofilm. Instead, it evaluates the 
overall performance of the biofilter (Qi et al., 2022).  
For better understanding of the biofilter it is important to gain information about the efficiency of 
the individual process and the bacteria that carries them out. One way to measure this is described 
in a study by Qi et al. (2022). The study evolves a method which places biocarriers in respirometry 
chambers, where the oxygen consumption can be measured, when the biocarriers are subjected to 
specific substrate spikes. The oxygen consumption over time, that is used in the oxidation of the 
given substrate (eq. 1, 2 & 3), can then work as a proxy for activity of the specific bacterial group 
that is involved in the turnover (Ginestet et al., 1998). By using this method, we can get a better 
understanding of the effect of a given treatment on the AOB/AOA, NOB, and HB separately.

Materials and methods 
This section details the methodological approach employed to investigate the effects that exposing 
the biocarriers to different mechanical or chemical pre-treatments, will have on the microbial 
colonization speed and effectiveness of the biofilter. Here, we describe the materials, experimental 
design, data collection procedures, and statistical analyses used to address this question. 
Overview 
The experiment consisted of two different periods where data was collected. In the first part of the 
experiment a trial and error approached was used. Multiple different chemicals and mechanical 
treatments were tested on the biocarriers; to study the effect and to what extent, it had on the initial 
sinking rate of the biocarrier.  
Based on which treatment had the largest effect on the sinking rate in the first part of the 
experiment, two treatments were selected for the second part to study the effect on the bacterial 
colonization. -one mechanical and one chemical- where chosen for further analysis. Two types of

Treatment and pre-treatment 
The first phase of the experiment focused on testing different treatments, that would make the 
biocarrier overcome the surface tension of the water, and therefore have a faster sinking rate than 
the control group. The biocarrier used was the RK biocarrier. The biocarriers comes in three different 
densities: 0.95 g/cm3 which is primarily used for up flow filtration as a fixed bed, 1.00 g/cm3 which 
is used for moving bed filters, and 1.20 g/cm3 that are used for fixed bed filters that have a downward 
flow. The heavy biocarriers were selected for this trial, as the density of 1.20 g/cm3, caused the 
elements to sink when the water tension was broken, which then could be countified into an 
effectiveness of the treatment. 
Two categories of chemicals were tested on the effectiveness to enhance the sinking rate of the 
media, one group was surfactants, that works on reducing the surface tension of the water. The 
other group was industrial degreasers, where the focus was on removing any oil residue that was 
left on the surface from the production. Table 1 shows a list of the chemicals that were tested.

Pre-treatment 
A method for pre-treatment was first used, in which the biocarriers were submerged in a strong 
solution (0.5 l/l) of each of the chemicals for 48 hours, to test if any chemical pre-treatment would 
change the properties of the elements and enhance the sinking rate. After 48 hours in the chemical 
solution, was the biocarriers rinsed with water to remove any excess chemicals. The biocarriers were 
then left to airdry for at least 12 hours at room temperature and outside of direct sunlight. This was 
done to eliminate any effect that water droplet on the surface would have on the sinking rate, as 
well as changes in the polymer structure caused by heat or UV-light.  
To measure the effect of the treatment on the sinking rate, 20 biocarriers -60 in total for triplicate 
determination- from each treatment, were carefully dropped into a 1 l beaker containing 800 ml of 
tap water at 20 °C. A camera was setup to capture the specific time that each Biocarrier would sink, 
a period of 60 minutes was chosen as the duration of the trail.

Chemical reduction of water tension 
The effect of low concentration of surfactants which were added directly in the batch was tested. 
The method used 800 ml beakers filled with 500 ml of tap water at 20 °C. For each surfactant a 
solution was created in the beakers. A concentration of 1 ml/l or 1 g/l was selected as the starting 
point, in an economic and practical perspective, as a surfactant that only has a significant effect over 
1 mg/l or g/l would not be feasible in a large system. To measure the effect of the surfactants on the 
sinking rate, 20 biocarriers, -60 in total for triplicate determination- were placed in the solution and 
the sinking rate was measured, a time of 60 minutes were chosen as the measuring period. A system 
of elimination was used to find the surfactants, that needed the lowest concentration to create a 
significant effect on the sinking rate. The effect of the surfactant was tested at seven different 
concentrations, 1, 0.5, 0.25, 0.1, 0.05, 0.025 and 0.001 ml/l or g/l. Albeit not all surfactants were 
tested at all seven concentrations, as they was stopped if they showed insufficient effect on the 
sinking rate compared to the other surfactants tested.   
Mechanical pre-treatment 
Mechanical pre-treatment consisted of abrading the biocarriers 
with sand, which would create a rugged surface that could 
break the surface tension and cause the biocarrier to sink. The 
rugged surface was also thought to create a better starting 
point for bacteria to secure themselves on, as it creates slightly 
more surface area and create cavities that bacteria can settle 
in. 
For abrading the biocarrier a drum made of low-density 
polyethylene (LDPE) was used that measured 8 cm in diameter 
and 15 cm in length. A single acryl lamella for mixing the 
biocarrier was going the length of the drum, and standing on 
the drum wall with a height of 2.5 cm. The drum was placed in a V-shaped holder that kept it steady 
at the center of rotation. For rotation a DeWalt power drill was used that locked on to a bolt at the 
end drum wall (figure 3). The drum was kept at a rotating speed between 55-65 round per minute 
for 60 minutes. The rotation speed was chosen, as speeds faster than 70-75 rotations per minute, 
created a strong centrifugal force that resulted in the biocarrier staying in the same place on the 
drum wall through a hole rotation, which didn’t mix the biocarrier and sand, so no abrading of the 
biocarrier surface occurred.   
For abrading of the RK biocarrier surface, fine sand was sourced from the local beach in Hirtshals, 
and runed through a strainer with a mesh size of 0.20 µm, where any grains larger than 0.20 µm was 
discarded. Due to the Mutag biocarrier porous structure, a larger grain size was selected, to prevent 
clogging of the holes. For this gravel with a grain size between 1 and 2 mm was used. As well as sand 
water was also added to the drum to give the sand weight when running over the surface. Dry sand 
showed no abrading when checked through a microscope, so a ratio between sand and water of 1 
was chosen, as it provided both the most effective abrading (figure 4), and to preserve some 
consistency between the two types of biocarrier and grain size.

Colonization trial 
To test if the treatment and pre-treatment have any effect on the bacterial colonization of the 
biocarriers, a colonization trial was setup. Two treatments were selected, one chemical and one 
mechanical. For the chemical treatment, the surfactant ECOSURF were chosen, as it was the tested 
chemical that showed an effect on the sinking rate, at the lowest concentration. For further 
explanation see result section about treatments. For the mechanical treatment the abradsion of the 
media with sand was chosen. Aside from the two treatments, two control groups for each biocarrier 
type were used, a positive and a negative control group. The negative group consisted of untreated 
biocarrier. In the positive control group, the biocarrier were shaken and stirred to remove air bubbles 
from the surface and break the tension between the water and the polymer. The shaking and stirring 
of the biocarrier were kept going, until all elements had sufficiently mixed in the water column.  
The trail was initially planned to run for 6 weeks but were extended to 57 days.

1 𝑔 𝑀𝑔𝑆𝑂4 −𝑀𝑔, 1 𝑔 𝑁𝑎2𝐻𝑃𝑂4 −𝑃, 1 𝑔 𝐾𝐻2𝑃𝑂4 −𝐾 and 0.03 𝑔 𝐹𝑒𝐶𝑙3 −𝐹𝑒. The water was 
then aerated for 1 hour with a SCHEGO ideal 769 air pump, delivering 150 l/h.  
10 ml water samples were taken by syringe from the 25 l bucket and from each beaker. The water 
was filtered through a 0.2 µm filter into a 15 ml tube and placed in a 5 °C refrigerator until further 
analysis. The remaining water in the beakers was then poured into a 1000 ml (±5 ml) measuring 
tower, for logging the water level at the sampling time, so evaporation could be accounted for when 
analysing the sample. 800 ml of new water containing ammonium and micronutrient was then 
poured into each beaker.       
Water sample analysis 
The water samples taken each time the water was changed, were used to state the ammonium 
turnover rate, the nitrite production rate and the nitrate production rate. The concentrations for all 
three nitrogen molecules were determined using standard ISO-methods with spectrometric 
detection (International Organization for Standardization [ISO], 1996; ISO, 1988; ISO, 1984). The 
spectrophotometer used for the analysis was a Hach DR3900. 
==PAGE:14==
   
 
14 
 
Ammonium  
The ammonium concentration at the beginning and end at each water change period was used to 
calculate the turnover rate over the period. To measure the NH4 concentration, 2.5 ml of the sample 
were pipetted into a cuvette. If dilution of the sample was needed Milli-Q water very added to the 
cuvette before the sample. When the samples were ready, 300 µl reactant 1 (salicylate-catalyst) was 
added, and the liquid were mixed before 500 µl reactant 2 (alkali citrate & hypochlorite) was added 
and mixed again. The cuvettes were then incubated for one hour in darkness at room temperature. 
The samples were analysed by light absorption at 680 nm. The absorption should be between 0 and 
1, if it was measured over the interval, the ammonium concentration was too high for accurate 
determination, and it needed to be run again with a higher dilution factor.  
Nitrite 
Determining the nitrite concentration, consisted of adding 2.5 ml of sample into a cuvette, with Milli-
Q water if dilution was needed. 100 µl of reactant 1 (diazotizing reactant) were added to the 
cuvettes, then mixed and left for 4-6 minutes before 50 µl reactant 2 (N-(1-naftyl)-ethylenediamine 
dihydrochloride) was added. The cuvettes were then mixed again and incubated on the counter at 
room temperature, incubation time was between 20 and 120 minutes. The absorption was 
measured at 545nm. If the absorption was over 1, the sample had to be diluted and run again.  
Nitrate 
For determining the nitrate concentration in the samples, 2 ml reactant 1 (concentrated sulfuric and 
phosphoric acid) was added to the cuvettes, 250 µl of the sample was slowly added to the acid 
without mixing the two, a lid was lightly put on the cuvette to stop any evaporation caused by the 
combination of the strong acid and water. 250 µl reactant 2 (2,6-methylphenol in acetonitrile) were 
added and the cuvettes were mixed and incubated on the counter at room temperature for 20 
minutes. The absorption was measured at 340 nm. If the absorption was over 1 or if the nitrite 
concentration was over 1.5 𝑚𝑔−𝑁/𝑙, the sample had to be diluted.  
Calculations for water analysis  
A standard curve based on known concentration were made for each of the three nitrogen 
compounds, and a linear regression was calculated. The slope and intercept could then be used to 
calculate the concentration of ammonium, nitrite and nitrate in the samples taken, with evaporation 
in the beakers accounted for. To calculate the daily ammonium turnover rate, the measured 
concentration was subtracted from the concentration of the new water at last water change and 
corrected for the volume. The amount used was then divided by the time since last water change. 
The same approach was used for calculations for nitrite and nitrate, albeit as the concentration at 
time t0 from water change was 0 𝑚𝑔 𝑁𝑂3
−−𝑁/𝑙, the measured concentration corrected for 
evaporation and volume was the total production.  
Respiratory test 
Starting on the 22nd day of the colonization trial, a respirometry test were conducted on the 
biocarrier with 7 days interval, on day 22, 29, 36, 43 and on day 57. The respirometry test used 
==PAGE:15==
   
 
15 
 
oxygen consumption from the bacteria to quantify the activity of the biofilm to use ammonium, 
nitrite, and acetate.  
Experimental set-up 
The respiratory test was conducted in a custom-made experimental set-up, described by Wahne Qi 
et al. (2022). The set-up can be seen in figure 5. It consisted of a Plexiglas water reservoir measuring 
58 cm long x 33 cm wide x 15 cm heigh. In the reservoir were placed six cylinders made of Plexiglas 
that measured 5.5 cm heigh and 9 cm in diameter. The cylinders were fitted with an airtight lid, that 
when locked gave the cylinder a chamber with an operational volume of 350 ml. The lid was fitted 
with two ports that could connect to a 6 mm tube, one port serving as an inlet, where clean water 
could be pumped in from the reservoir via a pump (Eheim compactON 300), the other port served 
as the outlet that led to a discard bucket. An air pump (Schego® ideal – Art.-Nr. 769) delivered air via 
an airstone into the reservoir. The reservoir rested on top of six magnetic stirrers (VELP® Scientifica 
Magnetic Stirrer), that were located under each chamber. A 20 mm stirrer bar was placed in each 
chamber and a 3D printed guard were placed over it, to prevent the stirrer bar of damaging the 
biocarriers. The magnetic stirrers were set to 60% power for a sufficient mixing. In the side of the 
airtight chambers, where a small hollow tube placed with a tightening screw, a fiberoptic cable was 
secured in the tube. The fiberoptic cables were connected to a fiberoptic oxygen meter (Firesting O2, 
PyroScience GmbH, Germany), that were connected to a PC that logged the oxygen concentration 
in the champers. The water in the reservoir were kept between 14.5 and 16 °C. A thermometer 
connected to the oxygen meter were placed in the reservoir, so temperature could be accounted for 
in the measurement of oxygen.

Measurement protocol 
The respiratory test consisted of three continuous spikes with ammonium, nitrite and acetate in that 
order, and the measurement of oxygen consumption. 20 biocarriers were transported from each of 
the beakers, running the colonization trial, and put into one of the six chambers in the reservoir. The 
chambers were sealed, and the reservoir were filled with 15 °C aerated water so the airtight 
chambers were fully submerged. A pump delivered water to each of the six chambers with a flow 
rate of 2.5 l/min, and were left running for 4-5 minutes, to ensure that all water in the chamber had 
been exchanged. Stock solutions for TAN, nitrite and acetate used for spiking the biocarriers were 
prepared. The solution was prepared so a spike with 1 ml of the solution would provide a

made with ammonium chloride (𝑁𝐻4𝐶𝑙), sodium nitrite (𝑁𝑁𝑎𝑂2) and sodium acetate (𝐶2𝐻3𝑁𝑎𝑂2),

5.6 𝑔 𝐶2𝐻3𝑁𝑎𝑂2 −𝐶2𝐻3𝑂2/𝑙. The measurement period consisted of spiking the chambers with one

Analysis of data 
Standardization of data 
All data obtained were standardized to be for 1 𝑚3 of biocarriers. The specifications for RK 
biocarriers specifies that 1 𝑚3 of the biocarriers contains 255000 individual pieces. The specification 
for Mutag does not provide a count of biochips pr. 𝑚3, but does provide the weight, which is set to 
170
𝑘𝑔
𝑚3. The number of biochips were estimated by weighing 10 biochips three times with different 
biochips each time and taking the average. One biochip was estimated to 0.406 𝑔, which gives a 
total of 418719 biochip pr. 𝑚3.    
Each batch contained 45 biocarriers, and each respiratory chamber contained 20 biocarriers, the 
multiplication factor could then be calculated by dividing the number of biocarriers pr. 𝑚3, with the 
number in the batches or champers.  
 
Visualization of data  
Several bar charts were constructed from the data to visualize the removal and production rate of 
ammonium, nitrite and nitrate, and the biofilm activity that was based on the respiratory tests. The 
datapoint in the figures were derived by taking the mean of the triplicates with the corresponding 
standard error.  
Statistical analysis  
To test if the different treatment had any significant effect, several statistical models were used. The 
specific model to the specific data were first chosen based on the visualized data, and the model 
==PAGE:17==
   
 
17 
 
was thereafter checked for fit by model diagnostics. All calculation were performed in the software 
R (R Core Team., 2016), and significance levels for all values were set to 0.05. 
Ammonium removal 
A logarithmic growth model was used to describe the ammonium removal rate, where the natural 
logarithm of the sample day was used. A model was fitted to each combination of biocarrier type 
and treatment, which provided the parameters for intercept and slope, which then could be 
interpreted. A Tukey’s HSD test was used to test for significant differences between the models. 
Nitrite and nitrate production 
The data for nitrite and nitrate production was described by splitting the data in two parts, the data 
was separated between the period of day 24-28 and 28-32, the separation point was chosen, as the 
production rate started to increase in the period day 28-32 and were based on visualization of data 
and by going through the raw data. A logistic model was used on the latter part of the data, where 
the natural logarithm of the production rate was described by the time and treatment variables. The 
intercept and its significance level were used to interpret, if there had been any difference in the 
production rate between the treatments, prior to where the data had been separated. A Tukey’s HSD 
test was applied to test for significant differences between the estimated marginal means of the 
treatment groups, both for the production rate as one unit and for each separated time period’s 
production rate.  
Respiratory test 
Only the respiratory test data for RK biocarriers heterotrophic bacteria activity, were able to be 
described by a model. A linier model was fitted to this data, where the respiration rate was described 
by time of the test, the treatment and the interaction between the two. The model was used to test 
for significant differences between the intercept and slope of the treatments. The remaining data 
for Mutag HB, NOB, AOB and AOA biofilm activity, were tried analysed by several models, however, 
it wasn’t possible to fit a model that could adequately describe the data. Model diagnostics were 
performed on all steps and both QQ-plot and residual vs fitted values, showed large variation in the 
data with many outliers. Removal of the outliers would weaken the models to an extent where they 
would be unusable.   
 
Results 
Biocarrier treatment 
Chemical pre-treatment 
The RK biocarriers were subjected to 48 hours in a strong solution of different chemicals (see table 
1) None of the chemical pre-treatment showed any effect on the mixing rate, when compared to the 
control groups. No groups showed more than a 5% sinking after the 60 min had passed. Any further 
pre-treatment trials with chemicals were therefore discontinued.  
==PAGE:18==
   
 
18 
 
Chemical reduction of water tension 
For the in-batch treatment, the different emulsifying compounds were added directly to the batches, 
providing different weak solutions, to see which emulsifier had the biggest effect at to lowest 
solution. Results is shown in table 2. 
The results showed that milk had the smallest effect on the mixing effect, with only 18% of the 
biocarriers being mixed after 60 minutes, at a concentration of 1 ml/l. Both Tween 80 and Triton 
showed a large effect on the mixing rate, however both had a significantly slower mixing rate than 
ECO surf and Tergitol at 1, 0.5 and 0.25 ml/l (g/l) (p<0.001, df=19). And were therefore not tested at 
lower concentration. ECO surf and Tergitol were both tested at 0.1 and 0.05 ml/l (g/l). There was no 
significant difference on the mixing rate at 0.1 ml/l (g/l) (p=1, df=9), though at 0.05 ml/l (g/l) did ECO 
surf showed significantly higher mixing rate than the Tergitol (p<0.001, df=9). ECO surf was also 
tested at 0.025 and 0.001 ml/l. There was no significant difference between the rate in the solutions 
with 0.05 and 0.025 ml/l (p=0.3, df=9), but the mixing rate was significantly slower in the 0.001 ml/l 
solution (p<0.001, df =14).  
ECO surf was therefore chosen to be used in the colonization trial, at a concentration of 0.025 ml/l.

Discussion 
 
This study aimed to investigate the effects of mechanical pre-treatment and chemical reduction of 
water tension on the mixing of biocarriers and subsequent biofilm formation in order to improve the 
efficiency of biofilter start-up in recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS). 
The initial sinking rate of the tested RK biocarriers, was significantly enhanced by the addition of the 
surfactant ECO surf, down to a concentration of 0.025
𝑚𝑙
𝑙. Mechanical pre-treatment that consisted 
of abrading the biocarriers with a mixture of sand and water, also showed a significantly increase in 
the sinking rate. The mechanical pre-treatment also caused scratches in the surface, which were 
thought to enhance the biofilm attachment. The results suggest a nuanced relationship between the 
treatments and their impact on the different stages of the nitrification process.  
In the first 14 days of the colonization trial, when acetate was added as a carbon source, all groups 
for both RK and Mutag showed a high ammonium removal rate, together with little to no nitrite 
production. The ammonium removal rate was therefore likely driven by heterotrophic bacteria, -
which are known to grow faster than the nitrifiers- (Ebeling et al., 2006; Graham et al., 2007), that 
used the free ammonium for biomass formation.  Visual cues also indicated towards a bloom of 
heterotrophs, as all batches had cloudy water, that disappeared when addition of acetate was 
stopped.  
The ammonium removal rate in the first 14 days did not differ significantly between any of the 
treatments. However, after addition of acetate was discontinued, diverged the removal rates, with 
the negative control group exhibiting the highest growth in ammonium removal rate in the batches 
containing Mutag biocarriers. The ECO surf treatment showed the highest growth rate among the 
RK groups. The sand treatment displayed the lowest growth rate for both biocarrier types. Despite 
these variations in growth patterns, the average ammonium concentrations at specific time points 
did not differ significantly between treatments, indicating that overall, the treatments were not 
statistically distinct on the ammonium removal.  
The establishment and growth rate of AOB differed significantly between the two biocarrier types. 
As the results for Mutag biocarriers suggests that AOB and AOA takes a longer time to colonize the 
biocarriers compared to RK. Though, once established, the AOA and AOB on Mutag biocarriers 
exhibited a significantly higher growth rate compared to the RK, highlighting the importance of 
specific surface area for bacterial colonization and their activity.  The Longer time it took for the AOB 
colonization for Mutag could potentially be caused by difference in material, as polypropylene 
biocarriers (RK) have been shown to mature faster than polyethylene biocarriers (Mutag) (Aalto et 
al., 2022). However, the faster initial attachment on RK biocarrier could also be attributed to the 
different shape and specific surface area of the biocarriers, which prevents any definitive conclusions 
regarding the sole impact of material selection.  
The results showed a significant delay in both nitrite and nitrate production, with a significant 
increase observed after day 28. The delay in nitrate production suggests that the NOB community, 
==PAGE:31==
   
 
31 
 
takes considerably longer time to establish themselves, compared to the AOB that showed growth 
from day 17. This observation aligns with what have been reported in previous studies (Graham et 
al., 2007; Timmons et al., 2007). 
Interestingly for the Mutag biocarriers, did the positive control show no difference from any of the 
other groups, when the negative control did exhibit a significantly higher nitrite production rate 
between day 28 and 39, compared to the ECO surf and sand treatments (table 5). Similarly, the 
nitrate production for the negative control group for Mutag biocarriers also outperforms the two 
treatment groups up until day 43, where the positive control showed no difference from the 
treatment groups. This is different from what was expected, as the negative control groups 
biocarriers, had mixed fully in the water column later than the positive control group. The nitrite and 
nitrate results suggest, that the treatments might have initially hindered the establishment of the 
nitrifying bacteria, albeit the effect diminished over time, as eventually all groups reached a similar 
nitrite and nitrate production rate between day 43 and 54.   
For the RK biocarriers, the positive controls show a significantly higher nitrite production rate 
compared to the sand treatment, though the difference was less pronounced than the negative 
control in the Mutag biocarriers. Additionally, the nitrate production rate did not differ significantly 
between the control and treatment groups, which might suggest that the treatment’s impact on the 
biocarriers was smaller, due to the lower specific surface area. The different effects in the treatment 
of the two types of biocarriers might also be attributed to their different structures (figure 2). 
Residue from ECO surf or sand grains might get trapped in the Mutag biocarriers’ porous structure, 
which could inhibit the bacterial growth of the nitrifiers. Where the smooth surface of the RK 
biocarriers retains less residue from the treatments, explaining why there is only a small significant 
difference for nitrite production and no difference for nitrate production, between the control and 
treatment groups.  
However, it’s important to know that nitrite is the intermediate product between ammonium and 
nitrate, which in a fully functional biofilter, should be oxidized to nitrate at the same time as 
ammonium is oxidized to nitrite (Timmons et al., 2007). The measured nitrite production rate is 
based upon the concentration of nitrite in the batches at the sample time, which indicates that the 
nitrite isn’t being oxidized by NOB at the same rate as AOB oxidize ammonium, leading to the 
accumulation in the batches. This shows that the biocarriers didn’t fully mature during the length of 
the experiment. Other studies have shown a maturation of the biocarriers after 47 days, where all 
nitrite were oxidized to nitrate (Navada et al., 2021). More accurate information about nitrite 
oxidation could have been studied, if trials also were conducted with addition of nitrite as a substrate 
instead of ammonium. 
The respirometry data, intended to assess the metabolic activity of AOB, AOA and NOB, did not align 
with the ammonium substrate degradation and nitrate production results. This variation of the data 
hindered a comprehensive analysis of nitrifiers’ respiratory activity over time, despite employing a 
method previously established, that showed accordance between the oxygen consumption and the 
substrate degradation (Qi et al., 2022). The heterotrophic bacteria activity for the RK biocarriers 
showed a significant decline over the course of the experiment. The HB activity on the second last 
==PAGE:32==
   
 
32 
 
sample measurement day (day 43) was found to align with the results described in previous studies 
(Qi et al., 2022), which used mature biocarriers from a pilot scale RAS. However, it found the HB 
activity to be ten to fifteen times smaller than the AOB and NOB activity, which wasn’t seen in this 
study, where the activity didn’t differ more than a factor of two. It is therefore not safe to conclude 
that the low HB activity was due to an increase of AOB and NOB. The declining activity could be 
attributed to the discontinuing of acetate addition on day 14, which resulted in an insufficient carbon 
source, that limited the growth of the heterotrophs in the biofilm (Timmons et al., 2007). This could 
also explain why the biofilm had detached from the biocarrier between day 43 and 57, as without 
an active heterotrophic community that produced the vital extracellular polymeric substance (EPS), 
the bacteria weren’t able to adhere to the biocarriers (Tsuneda et al., 2001). Which highlight the 
importance of maintaining the right C:N ratio (Ebeling et al., 2006; Timmons et al., 2007), as a too 
high ratio will result in the overtaking of HB, and a too low will starve them instead.     
Limitation    
This study faced several limitations that might have influenced the results and the interpretation of 
them. The respirometry data showed high variability both between the time of measurement and 
between the triplicates, which can be seen by the large standard error. The method is previous tested 
on matured biocarriers, and there may be limitation when applying it to biocarriers in the startup 
phase. This caused the inability to fit models to most of the respirometry data, which limited the 
ability to draw any conclusions about the activity in the biofilm, and how the treatments may have 
affected it.  
 Additionally, is the batch experimental setup, where there wasn’t a continuous water flow, not able 
to replicate the same hydrodynamic conditions that happens in a real biofilter. The fluctuating 
concentration of inorganic nitrogen compounds over the span of the 3.5 days, -between each water 
change-, also don’t adequately reflect a real biofilter, these differences might affect the development 
of biofilm. This could be resolved in a future study where the nutrient load was added daily, and with 
a flowthrough system where water was held in a bigger tank, so the water in the biofilter batches 
would be changed continuously, this would also mitigate the potential effect of evaporation from 
the beakers, that also impacted the nutrient concentration. Further, larger batches, containing a 
larger amount of biocarriers, might resolve or lessen the large fluctuations that were measured 
between the triplicates.  
Finally, the biofilm failure observed on day 54 across both biocarrier types, raises concerns about 
the stability of the biofilm under the experimental conditions. As the course of the failure might not 
be clear, the conditions mentioned above might all had an impact on the biofilm, and the early 
discontinued addition of acetate might also play a role in the failure.

Conclusion 
 
This study demonstrated the potential that pre-treatment of biocarriers and chemical reduction of 
water tension, can enhance the sinking rate and potentially accelerate the formation of the biofilm 
formation, in the startup phase of a RAS biofilter. The use of the surfactant ECO surf significantly 
enhanced the sinking rate of RK biocarriers, even at very low concentration. Mechanical pre-
treatment with sand abrasion of the biocarriers also caused the biocarriers to initially sink faster. 
However, the pre-treatment did not show any significant effect on the overall ammonium 
degradation rate. On the contrary it seems to initially inhibit the establishment of nitrite-oxidizing 
bacteria (NOB), though the effect lessened over time.  
The results highlight a complex relationship between pre-treatment methods, biocarrier properties 
and dynamics of the bacterial colonization and the biofilm activity. The two types of biocarriers, 
showed differences in nitrification rates and biofilm development, with the polyethylene Mutag 
biocarriers showing later establishment of AOB, compared to the polypropylene RK biocarriers. 
Though once established, the Mutag biocarriers outperformed the RK, both in AOB and NOB 
performance, which underscores the importance of material and surface properties when selecting 
biocarriers.  
The respirometry test was limited by data variability, though they provided insight into the metabolic 
activity of the different bacterial groups present in the biofilm. The result showed that there was a 
declining heterotrophic activity, which could be explained by the limited availability of organic 
carbon in the latter part of the trial. The low HB activity could also be due to the increase of nitrifying 
bacteria, though it wasn’t possible to conclude. The finding suggests that monitoring the specific 
bacteria in the biofilm by their respiration rate, can be a useful tool in assessing the health of the 
biofilter and identifying instability in the microbial community.  
On the last day of measurement, the biofilm was observed to have detached from the biocarriers. 
The exact reason for the failure remains unclear, though the low HB activity might have affected the 
biofilm resilience, as the production of extracellular polymeric substance declined. The biofilm 
failure in the last period of the trial, emphasizes the need for more research in order to understand 
the resilience and stability of biofilm in a RAS biofilter and the factors affecting it.
