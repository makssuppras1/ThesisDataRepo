Abstract
Through the increasing inﬂuence of global climate change on human activities in the Arctic
such as shipping and mining, high quality ice concentration is of great value. Synthetic aperture
radar (SAR) provides great valuable dataset for analyzing the dynamic changes of ice cover. How-
ever, ice concentration maps from SAR data are still generated manually by ice experts due to
the lack of reliable estimation algorithms. There is a signiﬁcant interest in developing a robust
automatic ice concentration estimation method based on SAR data.
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) is a powerful state-of-the-art method in image process-
ing in recent years because it greatly improves the accuracy of image classiﬁcation and recognition.
Many attempts has been experienced for testing the eﬀect of application of CNNs in remote sensing
(RS) images.
In this thesis, a relatively robust CNN model is proposed, which can automatically estimate sea
ice concentration (SIC) from SAR data. The daily SIC products derived from passive microwave
radiometer are used as ground truth to train CNN model. The process of establishing CNN model
is explained and several hyperparameters are tested. The performance of selected CNN model is
illustrated in both visual and statistic evaluation. Finally, the model transferability is evaluated.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Sea ice is an important part of the cryosphere. Snow and ice reﬂect more sunlight than open
water or bare ground and protect Earth from getting too warm. Sea ice extend and duration in
the Arctic has been shown following a decreasing trend in recent years due to global warming [1].
Because of ice-albedo feedback, they interacts as positive feedback mechanisms so that the decline
of sea ice are interacting with the climate system to accelerate global warming [4]. The loss of large
areas of sea ice has an directly inﬂuence on both animals and human related activities in the Arctic
area. Such as the loss of habitat, changes of routes and options for ﬁshing and transportation.
Therefore, the changes of sea ice extent is of great interest.
Sea ice is a quite complicated terrain on earth due to its complex physical state and surface
features. It changes all the time by the inﬂuence of ocean dynamics and the changes of atmosphere
conditions, especially during the freeze-up and melting period. Sea ice is produced when seawater
freezes. This procedure requires that the layer of seawater at the air-sea interface has cooled to
about -1.8◦C (when seawater has a salinity of about 33 ppt) [2]. Basically sea ice can be divided
into ﬁrst-year ice and multi-year ice. The parameters, such as salinity, roughness and strength, are
also various in diﬀerent ice types. The form of sea ice is also eﬀect by a wide range of conditions,
such as wind, temperatures, salt content, waves etc. The complex form process of sea ice cause
some diﬃculties in observation of sea ice [5].
Lots of eﬀorts have been carried out for sea ice monitoring using aircraft and ship observations
and later also visible and infrared imagery from meteorological satellites [3]. However, the former
two methods need massive manpower with poor spatial and temporal coverage. The visible and
infrared instruments are severely interfered by cloud and illumination conditions which are not
quite useful in the Arctic region. Instead, microwave sensors can deal with these problems. The
natural microwave radiation from the surface of the Earth can penetrate almost any cloud cover
and be detected by the satellite sensors. Sea ice information has been consistently measured over
the past 40 years by passive microwave sensors. Then the sea ice concentration, which depicts the
ice cover percentage over a certain area, ice thickness and a series of sea ice information can be
1
==PAGE:10==
retrieved through signal and data processing. Passive microwave sensors cover the Arctic region
in a short time and generate continuous observations for sea ice monitoring. The mainly limit of
the passive microwave is the poor spatial resolution, which is in ten kilometers level and can not
provide much detailed information.
SAR is a type of active microwave radar which can also solve the cloud cover and illumination
issues in polar region with higher spatial resolution (most less than 100 meters) compared to
the passive microwave. Since several SAR satellites have been launched in recent years, such as
Sentinel (European Space Agency) and RADARSAT (Constellation Mission), the volume of SAR
data for monitoring sea ice grows rapidly. However, there is still no widely acceptable method to
process SAR images. Most of the existing algorithms are still needed to be tested and improved.
One commonly used reference is manually ice chart which is generated from SAR image and
interpreted by ice experts. It provides the sea ice information including sea ice thickness, ice types
and ice concentration. The disadvantages of ice chart are time consuming and subjective due to
the diﬀerent levels of expertise [7].
Normally, the desired sea ice information observed by remote sensing methods can be concluded
as follows: sea ice extent and concentration are mainly derived from passive microwave data, sea
ice types are mainly derived from active microwave and sea ice drift is mainly derived from SAR
imagery. Diﬀerent data sources are also referenced and veriﬁed between each other to test the
reliability of data.
CNNs is a specialized kind of neural network for processing data that has a grid-like topology
[6]. Convolutional networks have been tremendously successful in practical applications such as
image segmentation and classiﬁcation. The complex architecture of convolutional networks make
it possible to present the hide information of tremendous training images. Research into CNNs
image application keep proceeding. Deeper and more complicated architectures are continuously
proposed to chase higher image classiﬁcation accuracy.
CNNs has been implemented in many
image related ﬁelds to test the performance including SAR images.
The large volume of high
resolution SAR images guarantees the number of training and testing samples in convolutional
networks. SAR image contain a lot of terrain information. The various complex recognition task
of these SAR images needs powerful tools help.
This thesis is inspired by [8], which is the ﬁrst case study estimating sea ice concentration
from dual polarization SAR scenes using deep convolutional neural networks. The basic idea of
that research paper is that using a relatively simple covolutional networks processing the input
SAR image to generate the target image - sea ice concentration image. The target image is then
compare with the ice chart which is assumed to be the ground truth to train and test data. The
objective of my thesis is to extend or further test on the convolutional neural networks for sea ice
concentration retrieval from SAR data and get high spatial resolution target image. In order to
reduce the impact of human subjective factors, the sea ice concentration automatic determined

resolution, some interpolation methods should be implemented ﬁrst to the passive microwave ice

Chapter 2
Background and Contributions
The background and fundamental knowledge about sea ice monitoring by remote sensing tech-
nique are presented in this chapter. Followed by the introduction of previous methods for processing
the SAR images and the attempts based on CNNs method that have been implemented on SAR
images.
2.1
Sea Ice
Sea ice is very sensitive to climate changes especially in the polar regions because of the polar
ampliﬁcation eﬀect. Climate feedback mechanisms involving sea ice have important implications
for the future progression of climate change[18]. Arctic sea ice has thinned and summer extent
has been at or near record low levels almost every year since 2001 [1]. Analyses of 32 year sea
ice extents and areas presented a continued acceleration of the Northern Hemisphere sea ice cover
particularly during summer[9].
2.1.1
Sea Ice Types
Sea ice types are basically classiﬁed by its thickness, lasting period and some physical properties
such as salinity and surface features. It depends on the temperature, wind conditions at the time of
ice formation. Some types of ice that appear in this thesis are shown in Fig 2.1. At the beginning,
ice crystals start to accumulate into some thin and smooth ice layer if there is no wind and wave
eﬀect (Fig 2.1 a) or pancake ice with wind instead (Fig 2.1 b). When new ice is put together and
form a boundary between open water and sea ice, the transition area is the marginal ice zone (Fig
2.1 c). With the additional freezing of new ice during the frozen period, it will become ﬁrst-year
ice (FYI) as it reaches thickness between 30cm to 2.5m during the ﬁrst year (Fig 2.1 d). Sea ice
melts during summer time. If some part of FYI is survived more than one melt season, then it can
be treated as multiyear ice (MYI) which is usually 2m to 4m thickness (Fig 2.1 e). Fresh water
that melts from MYI during melt period and forms into pools above MYI are known as multiyear

2.1.2
Desired Sea Ice Information
Sea ice extent and thickness are two mainly desirable information. Other sea ice related infor-
mation, such as sea surface temperature, wind speed and drift velocity etc can also be retrieved
by remote sensing data.
In this thesis, the focus is on sea ice concentration (SIC) which is a
fundamental ice extent information. The deﬁnition of sea ice concentration is the area of water
surface covered by sea ice divided by the whole area [10]. It is usually given in a percentage from
0 to 100 or a number from 0 to 1 for a certain area. For a small enough area on Earth, the SIC
is always either 0 (open water) or 1 (ice). Therefore, the resolution and the area extent should
be predeﬁned before calculating the SIC. It should be noticed that all sea ice related variables
are interconnected, the accurate monitoring of one parameter could have a beneﬁt on determining
others.
2.2
Sea Ice Monitoring based on Remote Sensing Methods
Due to the limitation of ﬁeld work for acquiring ice data in the Arctic region. Sea ice monitoring
mainly depends on remote sensing methods. There are basically three types of satellite remote
5
==PAGE:14==
sensing technology that are used for ice monitoring: visible and infrared satellite imagery, passive
microwave radiometer (MWR) and active microwave sensors. Due to the restriction of weather
and illumination condition, the optical data is not used solely at the present. Instead, it is often
used for providing extra information and validating the corresponding MWR and active microwave
radiometer data.
2.2.1
Passive Microwave Remote Sensing
All bodies having a physical temperature higher than 0 K emits radiation. This radiation is
governed by Planck’s Law [11]. MWR antennas are designed to receive the majority of the bodies’
radiation. Passive microwave data are the most widely used data for global sea ice monitoring. Sea
ice reﬂects most of the incoming solar radiation, whereas the ocean absorbs most of the radiation
[3].
Application of Passive Microwave
The superiority of satellite passive MWR is that it can measure and collect sea ice data all
the time almost without consideration of the atmosphere. Low frequency channels of MWR can
penetrate almost all types of cloud but with large footprint. High frequency channels with better
resolution are also easier interfered by atmosphere. A near-complete MWR data has been recorded
since 1978, with the using of automatic algorithm, both monthly and daily ice concentration
products are available, which can be used to analyse long-term trend of sea ice changing pattern
[12]. Most of SIC algorithms are based on two low frequency dual polarization (H,V) channels 19
and 37 and a high frequency channel 85 H,V [16, 15]. The typically case is that the horizontal
polarization is more sensitive to the wind and clouds over open water.
However, there are still many limitations to passive microwave imagery. In order to get enough
radiation from the Earth surface, the footprint of radiometer sensor footprint is often large at
around 50km for low frequency and 5km for high frequency channels, which limits the precision of
the ice edge location and concentration at the ice edge because of inherent subpixel ambiguity [13]
and the coarse spatial resolution. In addition, sea ice concentration estimation accuracy is also
inﬂuenced by snow cover to some extent[14]. Finally, the various sea ice concentration retrieval
algorithm from brightness temperature can also result in diﬀerent level of biases in estimation.
There is a comparison of eleven sea ice concentration algorithm in [15]. Even though it quantify
the diﬀerence between the 11 sea-ice concentration estimates using daily microwave brightness
temperatures based on same factors not directly related to the algorithms basics. There is also no
outstanding algorithm that has superior performance in all cases. Then a further investigation was
experienced by the same researchers [16] and leads some conclusions. A combination of 2 algorithms
has been chosen for an overall algorithm with high accuracy and low standard deviation. Some
melt ponds and thin ice are interpreted as open water by all algorithms which lead to lower SIC in

that area. High frequency algorithms are more sensitive to atmospheric eﬀects at low SIC (around
15 percentage). In [19], analysis shows that there are signiﬁcant diﬀerences in trend between sea
ice extent and area using diﬀerent algorithms.
This indicates long-term trends in surface and
atmospheric properties inﬂuence the computed trends.
Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2 data
A speciﬁc example of a satellite passive radiometer is Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiome-
ter 2 (AMSR2) which onboard the Global Change Observation Mission - Water (GCOM-W) for
measuring weak microwave emission from the surface of the Earth. The center frequencies range
from 7-89 GHz. Only the 89 GHz is sampled for each 5 km with 3x5 km best ground resolution.
There are three level of products, the origin level 1 product is processed by atmosphere correction,
tie points generation and ice concentration calculation, then transformed to the level 2 product.
For level 3 product, the swaths from each are concatenated [21].
The frequencies that suit SIC estimate are 18.7,36.5 and 89.0 GHz as shown in Fig 2.2 [20, 17].
The SIC algorithm uses the input from brightness temperature swath data at three low frequency
channels (19V, 37H and 37V) and two high frequency channels (89H and 89V).
Figure 2.2: Channel Speciﬁcations of AMSR2
The data used in this thesis is the 25.0 km resolution SIC product based on brightness tem-
perature channels 18.7 GHz V-pol, 37 GHz V-pol and 37 GHz H-pol [23]. An example of MWR
SIC product is shown in Fig 2.3. The ice concentration is shown in percentage between 0 to 100.
There is a 15 percentage threshold which means the area with lower than 15 percentage SIC will
be treated as open water.

2.2.2
Synthetic Aperture Radar
SAR is a high resolution imaging radar. It is a type of active microwave radar that transmits
pulses and receives echoes as the measurements. The radar scattering or backscatter cross section is
an absolute measure of power reﬂected from the target. The SAR signatures of sea ice are extremely
complex, dependent not just on the physical surface of the targets but also the complicated inter
relationship with the frequency, incidence angle, polarization, noise level [2, 22]. The high spatial
resolution can be realised by using the signal processing to synthesize a aperture radar antenna
in ﬂight direction. The most common used frequency is the C-band, sometimes combination with
L or X-band depending on the requirement of the mission.
SAR sensor is capable to obtain
night imagery and detect small movement on the ground, which is quite useful for land and sea
monitoring. With resolution measured in meters, SAR can measure the details of sea ice, which
is not possible in MWR data. The recently launched SAR satellites, RADARSAR-2, Sentinel-1,
are able to measure a wide swath with small pixel spacing and work in dual or quad polarizations.
The dual polarization images HH and HV provide more diﬀerent but correlated information than
single one which can signiﬁcantly improve ice classiﬁcation and discrimination [8].

Radar Scattering
The interactions between radar waves and sea ice are mainly determined by the scattering
properties of the sea ice area. Four surface parameters aﬀect the scattering characteristics of sea
ice: surface roughness, dielectric constant of sea ice, discrete scatterers and orientation of the ice
to the radar[2]. The backscatter interactions for sea ice are shown in Fig 2.4
Figure 2.4: Backscatter interactions between radar waves and sea ice
There are two mainly scattering types: surface scattering and volume scattering. At a surface
that is smooth compared to the radar wavelength, specular reﬂection are more prone to occur. This
type of reﬂection is typically happened in open water area without strong waves caused by wind.
There is only small part of echoes are received by radar, it shows almost absolute dark in SAR
images with lowest backscatter. Surface scattering also happens in young ice areas without rafting
corresponding to the relatively dark area in SAR images. This is because the surface roughness on
ﬁrst-year level ice is often on scales of the radar wavelength [22].
Volume scattering occurs when radar wave penetrates ice surface and then reﬂects by volume
inclusions particles such as bubbles or brine pockets (high-salinity liquid trapped between the
ice crystals)[22], which are more likely formed when ice accumulation on the surface of new ice.
Volume scattering is also inﬂuenced by the salinity, snow cover situation of ice. Snow and top-layer
ice melt and refreeze during the melting period, the bubbles and salinity content vary from the
previous freezing interval. Hence volume scattering is reduced signiﬁcantly during this period.
It has been mentioned that the radar signature is mainly determined by various characteristics,
such as ice and water surface roughness, air bubbles in ice, snow cover, ice salinity and temperature.
No matter which type of scattering, when developing algorithms to retrieve geophysical information
such as sea ice concentration, the relationship between radar images and these sea ice properties
must be considered.

Sentinel-1 Data
Sentinel-1 is an imaging radar that provides monitoring imagery in all kinds of weather condi-
tion. Sentinel-1 data is widely used for land, such as agriculture classiﬁcation, and sea monitoring,
such as ice chart, ship and oil pollution monitoring. Typically the Extra Wide swath (EW) data is
used for sea ice monitoring because of its large coverage area. In EW mode, data is acquired in ﬁve
swaths using the Terrain Observation with Progressive Scans SAR (TOPSAR) technique which
can provide large swath widths and enhanced radiometric performance by reducing the scalloping
eﬀect [25]. Both Sentinel-1A and Sentinel-1B share the same orbit plane with almost less than 1
day revisit frequency at high latitudes. The range and azimuth resolutions for EW are 93m and
87m respectively. The pixel spacing is 40x40m. The Fig 2.5 show an example of Sentinel-1 level-1
Ground Range Detected (GRD) HH and HV dual polarization intensity images located at east
Greenland sea.
(a) HH with incidence angle dependence
(b) HV with banding eﬀect
Figure 2.5: An example of dual polarization SAR images.
The SAR backscatter of HH polarization image depends on incidence angle (Fig 2.5 a). The
image becomes darker with the increase of incidence angle from near range (left) to far range
(right). Under ideal condition, the backscatter of open water should be 0, absolutely dark in the
image. However, it is also inﬂuenced by wind speed and direction. In the case with low incidence
angle and strong wind, the water backscatter is even stronger than the sea ice, which makes it
diﬃcult to distinguish them. To deal with this problem, linear incidence angle correction is often
applied for HH image as pre-processing. However, previous experiences show that the dependence
of backscatter coeﬃcients on incidence angle are various from FYI, MYI and open water. The data
applied with linear normalization is not suitable for images that mixed with diﬀerent sea ice types.
Some alternatively algorithms for correcting SAR image incidence angle has been proposed[26].
However, the best ﬁt was achieved when level ice and deformed ice cases are treated separately.

During the TOPS mode SAR processing, the noise ﬂoor eﬀect on both HH and HV are in-
vestigated. The HH data generally appears brighter than the HV data because there is a higher
possibility for horizontal transmitted signal to scattered back horizontally rather than vertically.
If the signal (HV channel) that returns back to the sensor is below the noise ﬂoor of the Sentinel
satellite, no useful information is captured by the sensor. The backscatter coeﬃcients from HV
polarization are not consistent from near range to far range, which is attributed to the noise at the
border of each sub-swath known as banding eﬀect. This results in some bright stripes across the
image in HV channel (Fig 2.5 b). Even the HV data seems less robust against noise ﬂoor, but its
lesser sensitivity to surface roughness makes it a perfect indicator of the separation of the water
and ice, and this is something HH data cannot achieve. However, since the noise ﬂoor would blur
the image which could make it diﬃcult to separate diﬀerent types of ice. The high signal amplitude
of the HH data would be of great value.
During the melting season in Fig 2.5 (24th July), the open water and sea ice boarder is not
clear. First year ice and some new ice melt to lots of small pancakes mixed with open water.
The backscatter of boarders during this period are usually brighter than other area because the
deformation will cause corner reﬂection. Some brighter reﬂection spot or line in the middle of
FYI area is caused by the ice broken or ice risen above ice surface resulting from the compression
between new ice. There is also the case during the freeze-up period. Such detailed observation
can give us a ﬁner ice related information compared to MWR data. SAR data can provide high-
resolution images. The trade-oﬀis small coverage (small width) compared to the MWR data.
Existing Sea Ice Concentration Estimation Methods based on SAR data
Ice charts describe the distribution of sea ice, which are produced manually by experienced ice
experts using mainly SAR images and also other available corresponding data. The purpose of
ice chart is to provide sea ice relevant information, such as ice concentration, type and ﬂoe size
etc [18]. It is a time-consuming and labour-intensive process. The accuracy of ice chart is also
dependent on the human subjectivity and skills.
A lot of image features and algorithms have been implemented for automatic SIC estimation
based on SAR images.
Markov random ﬁelds (MRFs) and Gabor ﬁlters were used before to
classify diﬀerent sea ice types based on SAR data. The gray-level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM)
are widely used on representing sea ice features [27, 28, 30]. The basic idea is to use statistical
method to test the spatial relationship between adjacent pixels. Sea ice can be determined easier
based on some speciﬁc texture features [32, 31]. Some automatic ice water classiﬁcation methods
using backscatter and texture features based on Support Vector Machine (SVM) [31], Principle
Component Analysis (PCA), k-means cluster analysis [32] have been tested. They are not suited
for general cases, especially during the melting and freeze-up seasons. The multilayer perceptron
(Artiﬁcial neural network) regression method combined with SAR texture features improves SIC
11
==PAGE:20==
estimation accuracy to some extent. In [27], both SAR dual-polarized features and MWR features
are used as input, which shows more reliable and accurate results. However, the eﬀectiveness of
these models depends strongly on the features selected by operators.
Convolutional neural networks has been attempted to apply to estimate SIC in [8] during the
melting season. CNNs structure enables sea ice features to be learned automatically. It can also
tackle the incidence angle dependence of HH image by inputting the incidence angle as additional
features. The use of dual-polarization images show a better result than single polarization. In his
further research[33], the freeze-up period SAR images are tested.
2.3
Convolutional Neural Networks
Deep learning is a branch of machine learning, which is an algorithm that attempts to abstract
high-level information of image using multiple processing layers that contain complex structures
[6]. One simple deep learning model is multilayer perceptron which is a mathematical function
that maps a set of input values to output values. There are some hidden layers between input
and output layer which contain simple mathematical function.
Convolutional neural networks
utilize the convolutional, which is a specialized linear mathematical operation, to process network-
structure data, such as time series or images.
2.3.1
Convolutional Operation
Typically, there are some basic parameters in the terminology of convolutional networks: input,
kernel function and output (or feature map)[6]. If there is a 2D image X as input, the convolution
can be deﬁned as:
hi,j = g(W*Xi,j + b)
(2.1)
where W is a 2D kernel known as weight factors, h is output, g is activation function and b is
bias. The convolutional operation is shown in Fig 2.6. The output of each pixel value is the matrix
multiplication between input and kernel.
Each input is assigned to its own relative weight. The weight sum is transformed to a reference
output by employing a non-linear function known as activation function g. Loss function is used to
evaluate error between actual output and desired output. To minimize the error, back propogation
and optimisation function are used.
The error of one layer is typically propagated backwards
to a previous layer, where is used to modify the weights and bias that the error is minimized.
Optimisation algorithm are used to update weight and biases to reduce the error, usually calculate
the gradient - partial derivative of loss function to weights. Normally used optimisation functions
include Stochastic Gradient Decent (SGD), Adagrad and Adam. Adam is chosen in this experiment
because it works well and faster in practice.
12
==PAGE:21==
Figure 2.6: An example of 2D convolution
Convolutional operations help improve machine learning systems through three important ideas:
sparse interactions, parameter sharing and equivariant representations [6].
Sparse connectivity
can be achieved by making the size of the kernel much smaller than the size of the input. For
example, the input images might have millions of pixels, but CNN would store only thousands of
pixels by detecting the meaningful features of the input image, such as edges, which improve the
computational eﬃciency. Parameter sharing is the model using the same parameter in diﬀerent
functions. The special form of parameter sharing makes the neural network layer have the nature
of equivariant translations.
A typical convolutional network mainly contains three types of layers, convolution layer, detec-
tor layer and pooling layer. A convolution layer is the same process as shown former which is a
aﬃne transform. In detector stage, each linear activation response will pass through a non-linear
activation function, such as a rectiﬁed linear units (ReLU), deﬁned as g(z) = max(0, z). ReLU
is easy to optimize because it is similar to linear units. The only diﬀerence is that the output
of ReLU is zero when the domain is less than zero. And the constant gradient of ReLU results
in faster learning and numerical stability. In pooling layer, the pooling function uses the overall
statistical characteristics of adjacent outputs at a location instead of the network output at that
location. Max pooling is a common used pooling method which gives the maximum value in the
adjacent rectangular area. Pooling can help CNN learning the translation invariance of input data.
At the same time, pooling with larger stride can sub-sample input data.

The widespread use of CNN dates back to winning the Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge
(ILSVRC), where software programs compete to correctly classify the detect objects and scenes

2.4
Motivation and Contribution
CNNs perform extremely good in some practical applications, especially in the image object
classiﬁcation and recognition. Some CNNs application attempts have been experienced in SAR
scenes within the land cover and crop types classiﬁcation [34], terrain classiﬁcation in city area
[35, 36] and vehicle detection [37].
Some traditional methods used to estimate SIC have been mentioned in last chapter. Multi-
layer perceptron applying to SAR images got relatively accurate prediction of ice concentration.
Successful case of neural networks indicates that CNN has potential to retrieve SIC from SAR
data.
The ﬁrst successful research in estimating SIC based on CNNs method from SAR images was
shown in [8].
In his research, he explored a proper CNN model structure that estimates sea
ice concentration with lower mean squared error compared with traditional neural networks. He
demonstrated that CNNs have robust performance to the incidence angle eﬀect even without the
use of incidence angle data. Moreover, CNNs are less sensitive to the noise in SAR images such as
banding eﬀect and strong wind area. In his experiment, SIC from ice chart is used as ground truth.
The ground truth data is quite important part in training samples, the selection of high quality of
ground truth data will directly decide the quality of sea ice concentration estimation from CNN.
In this thesis, the microwave radiometer ice concentration data are used as ground truth instead
of ice chart, there are some reasons:
1. MWR data is a source for consistently processed ice extent and concentration images and
data values since 1979. With long-time explorations and experiment, several operational ice con-
centration algorithms with little principle variation were proposed. Various techniques such as
applying climatologies, atomosphere corrections, common grid reprojection, adjusting land-to-
ocean spill over are used to generate better SIC product [23]. MWR is a relatively well-developed
technique for estimating SIC.
2. The training of CNN models requires a large number of training data. The SIC products can
be generated from MWR data automatically. This automatic process will save massive amount
of time to collect training data compared with manual-interpreted ice chart.
Especially when
products are needed in the near time scale.

3.2
Preprocessing of Downloaded Data
3.2.1
Preprocessing of SAR Images
There are three steps in the preprocessing of SAR images.
A single radar image is usually displayed as a grey scale image (Fig 2.5). The intensity of
each pixel represents the proportion of microwave backscattered from that area on the ground
which depends on a variety of factors: types, sizes, shapes and orientations of the scatterers in the
target area; moisture content of the target area; frequency and polarisation of the radar pulses; as
well as the incident angles of the radar beam. The pixel intensity values are often converted to
a physical quantity called the backscattering coeﬃcient ranging from 0.1 to 0.0001 or normalised
radar cross-section measured in decibel (dB) units with values ranging from +5 dB to -40 dB for
19
==PAGE:28==
very rough ice to smooth open water.
Firstly, for converting digital pixel values to radiometrically calibrated backscatter, all intensity
values of each SAR scene are calibrated to sigma nought values. After calibration, all pixel values
are truly represent the radar backscatter of the reﬂecting surface, the incidence angle dependence
in the HH polarization can also be corrected to some extent. Unlike optical imagery, characterized
by very neat and uniform features, SAR images have inherent salt and pepper like texturing called
speckles which degrade the quality of the image and make interpretation of features more diﬃcult.
Therefore, the speckle ﬁlter operator is applied. In this study, Lee-Sigma with 7 7 window is used.
Finally, for there is no sub-sample process to the original SAR image, the input is the whole SAR
image size, the pixel number of each input SAR image is shown in table 3.1. To reduce the data
volume, the patch size of SAR image is decided according to the resolution of target image because
there is no overlapping between two adjacent patches in this study. The target pixel spacing of
estimating SIC image is 2 km. Therefore, the patch size of 50 pixels is selected. In Lei Wang’s
thesis, diﬀerent patch sizes 25, 35, 45 and 55 are tested, the root mean square (Ermse) results show
45 patch size is a turning point in both validation and testing curves. When patch seize increased
from 45 to 55, the Ermse for test and validation slightly increased, which could indicate a slightly
overﬁtting of the training data. Because the limited time in my thesis, diﬀerent patch sizes are not
compared.
In others typical image classiﬁcation problems based on CNNs, the normalization of input
image is a common step of preprocessing to improve the performances of CNNs because the pixel
values in these cases are often between 0 and 255. For SAR images, the input normalization is not
necessary because after calibration, the pixel values of both HH and HV are already in the range
0 and 1. Although most of pixel values are in low level (10−3), especially in HV channel, it did
not inﬂuence the CNNs model learning texture features and predicting reliable results.
The brightness stripe in HV polarization image (Fig 2.5 (b) left 1/4 part) should not be taken
into account as input image because this obvious bias will lead serious bias in estimate SIC. This
type of problem can be ﬁxed using thermal denoising methods to some extent [40]. However, it is
still not good enough compared with the normal part in HV channel. Additionally, the land area
in SAR images (Fig 2.5 small bright area in the middle of right side) is not been considered in
estimating SIC. The area mentioned above was cut out from the original SAR images. This is why
the ratio of fourth column pixel number in Table 3.1 is around 3:2.

Data augmentation is a way of increase the amount of training data by zooming, rotation,

sample will be doubled after 90 degree image rotation.
Dropout is a regularizing method. It will skip the update of each layer node with a given
probability. Dropout is often used when the model is large with huge parameters, especially in
a fully connected layer, which occupies most of the parameters. Normally, a large dropout rate
is applied in the last fully connected layer. In this experiment, 0.5 dropout rate is applied after
each max-pooling layer and ﬁrst fully connected layer. Without dropout, the network exhibits
signiﬁcant overﬁtting.
Batch normalization is one of the regularization methods in CNN, which renormalizes the
activation halfway through the network to zero mean and unit variance. It is important to apply
the same normalizing mean and standard deviation at every spatial location within a feature map
so that the statistics of the feature map remain the same regardless of spatial location [6]. It is
not quite reasonable to use batch normalization before each ReLU activation if the input data
is not normalized to the same range. Therefore, batch normalization is only applied to the last
convolutional layer and it actually improve the estimate SIC performance.
Various types of architecture are tested. Finally, eight convolutional layers, combined with four
pooling layers and two fully connected layers are selected based on the error analysis and visual
comparison for training and test datasets.

3.4
Training, Validation and Testing Process
Usually, datasets are divided into training set and test set. Training set contains both the input
data and expected output data. Training process is to ﬁnd the best model pairing the input with
reference output. The test process is to apply selected model on test data to estimate the error rate
of chosen model. After assessing the ﬁnal model on the test set, the model cannot be tuned any
further. However, the model that best ﬁt the training data often do not have a good performance
on test data because of the underﬁtting and overﬁtting problem. Therefore, validation dataset
is often used to evaluate a given model by calculating the loss function on the validation dataset
on the current epoch. An epoch is deﬁned as one forward pass and one backward pass of all the
training examples. Normally, the training loss will keep decrease as the number of epoch increases.
The validation loss, on the other hand, should have a lowest values through training epochs. The
datasets are splitted into eight training datasets, two validation datasets and two test datasets.

of the corresponding input patch linked with blue curved arrow connector is ampliﬁed. The green
square is a ﬁlter in convlutional layer. The common used ﬁlter size is (3,3) or (5,5). The number
of ﬁlter is often set to the power of two. There would be a convolutional operation in each ﬁlter
and the ﬁlter will move one pixel ahead and repeat the operation (stride=1). For example, the
result the green square after convolutional operation is the orange square in the next layer, the
white dotted square next to the green square can be calculated as the small white dotted square
next to the orange one. If there is no setting for padding, the size of ﬁlter output image would be
(50-3+1,50-3+1,32). Max pooling layer will perform a downsampling operation along the spatial
24

dimensions (width, height). The most common form of max pooling window size is (2,2) applied
with a stride= 2 along both width and height. Therefore, the pooling operation would take the
max over 4 values (the red square) and the output size of max pooling layer would be half of
the original input (24,24,32). Followed by two fully connected layers in which neurons have full

Many hyperparameters have an impact on network training. In Lei Wang’s paper [8], patch size

a stable patch size is selected based on prior knowledge and target MWR SIC resolution. Except

3.6.2
Batch Normalization and Dropout
Dropout is a technique of setting output of hidden neuron to zero with a certain probability.
Only the non-zero neurons contribute to the forward and back propagation. By doing this, dropout
forces CNN to learn more features in conjunction with many diﬀerent random subsets [42]. Dropout
rate with 0.5 nearly doubles the number of iterations (epochs) to get a converge result. Normally,
dropout is used after each max-pooling layer.
Batch normalization normalize the inputs to layers within the network by using the mean
and variance of the values in the current mini-batch. Batch normalization is often used before
activation function to make sure the input layer values within a reasonable range.
Activation
function ReLU that sometime does not work for a deep network refers to ’dying’ ReLU problem.
A ’dead’ ReLU always outputs or tends to output the same value for any input. Probably this is
arrived at by learning a large negative bias term for its weights, which lead to lots of zero gradient
value. And gradient of zero is also zero. A large diﬀerence in input values to each layer may cause
this problem, this is one of reasons using batch normalization. Batch normalization can provide a
bit of regularization, which helps dropout to provide a better results to some extent.
The estimate SIC results of using diﬀerent combination of dropout and batch normalization
are shown in Fig 3.16. (a) is SAR HH image. The visual evaluation of estimate SIC is based on
the results in A,B,C,D four regions marked in (b). A is located at the upper left corner, where
there is some refreezing ice and mixture of ice and water. B is located at upper right where there
is a large block of smooth ice. C is similar with B located at bottom right. D is located at middle
right area where there is a large open water area with some small ice ﬂoes. The selected model (b),
with 0.5 dropout after each max-pooling layer and one batch normalization for last convolutional
layer, shows a good consistency SIC with original SAR scene in water and new ice area (A and
D) but some underestimation in smooth ice area (B and C). Especially in B, small part of smooth

applied in other datasets, which will be illustrated in the next chapter. (c) is the model without

It cannot distinguish ice water boundary clearly, and a serious overestimation of water area in D.
(d) is the model only with dropout after each max-pooling layer. It cannot detect the new ice

of overﬁtting. The use of large dropout rate combined with appropriate batch normalization can
help to partially solve this problem.
The possible reason why batch normalization does not work very well in this case could be
that we use the original backscatter coeﬃcient values as training input data. For example, the
typical ice and water backscatter coeﬃcients in HV channel are at 0.001 and 0.0001 order of
magnitude respectively. If applying batch normalization in the former input layers, the values
input to activation function would be changed quite a lot. The original MWR SIC data values
range from 0 to 100. After divided by 100, it is used as target output data that range from 0 to 1.
Therefore, using batch nomalization in the last convolutional layer, which generate a similar range
values between input and output of fully-connected layer, provides the best estimate SIC result.

Chapter 4
Error Analysis
The error analysis mainly focus on match error of training samples and prediction error. Train-
ing sample match error includes: 1) the pixel shift for ice area between SAR scenes and corre-
sponding MWR SIC data, 2) the inconsistency between SAR images and corresponding MWR
SIC data. The prediction error is the misinterpreting in estimate SIC compared with both SAR
original image and reference SIC data.
4.1
Training Sample Match Error
4.1.1
Pixel Shift
The training sample match error can be caused by several reasons. Interpolation error and
time variation of acquired data are possible two main contributors. Interpolation error refers to
the diﬀerent interpolation methods applying to the MWR data, which would lead to some diﬀerence
on reference SIC in training samples. Time variation of acquired data error is caused by diﬀerent
acquisition time between SAR images and daily MWR SIC products.
The resolution of original MWR SIC products is 25km. Sentinel-1 data is 40m pixel spacing.
Therefore, around 600x600 pixels in SAR scenes corresponds to a 25km resolution MWR SIC
pixel. A non-overlapping 50x50 patch size is applied over whole SAR scenes, which corresponds
to 2km pixel spacing. Hence, the MWR SIC data should be interpolated to 2km pixel spacing.
The diﬀerent between scattered and gridded interpolation has been illustrated in Fig ??. The
interpolation of MWR SIC data to target SAR patch is based on longitude and latitude data.
However, after interpolation, there is also a border shift as shown in Fig 4.1. Two SIC images
have the same number of row and column pixels. In estimate SIC image (a), the ice boundary on
the top and bottom is located at around 75 and 125 column pixel. In the corresponding MWR
SIC image (b), it moves to 80 and 140 column pixel. Considering the interpolating from 25km
resolution to 2km pixel spacing, several within 10 pixels movement would happen.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Discussion
A new attempt for ice concentration retrieval from SAR data based on convolutional neural
networks has been implemented in this thesis. The new proposed CNN model can automatically
generate reasonable high resolution ice concentration from SAR scenes around Fram Strait area
during melting period. MWR data are treated as ground truth, which have been used for over 40-
years sea ice monitoring. The MWR SIC data are quite reliable even if in low ice concentration area.
In chapter 3, A complex CNN architecture has been established based on VGGNet. Dropout, batch
normalization and data augmentation (image rotation) have been applied to reduce the overﬁtting
risk. Changes of network depth, dropout and batch normalization have substantial inﬂuence on
the estimate SIC performance.
Due to the lack of high resolution ground truth, the detailed
estimate SIC can not be evaluated quantitatively. Therefore, estimate SIC results are compared
with both original SAR scenes and corresponding MWR data for visual comparison.
Average
estimate SIC are calculated to generate error statistics, scatter plot. Some typical examples are
used to illustrate the estimate SIC performance for diﬀerent input SAR scenes. Results show that
CNN have advantages compared with traditional ice concentration algorithm. Speciﬁcally:
1. CNN model can tackle the incidence angle dependence in HH polarization even if without
extra incidence angle data as input layer.
2. CNN is not sensitive to the noise in SAR backscatter. Only strong wind in HH channel and
obvious banding eﬀect in HV channel can lead to misidentiﬁcation of open water area in estimate
SIC.
3. CNN can well predict the SIC for diﬀerent ice types and water ice boundary.
In chapter 4, training sample match errors, including pixel shift and inconsistency between
SAR images and MWR SIC data, are ﬁrst explained, which may be a minor contributor to the
prediction error. Smooth ﬁrst year ice can be sometimes misidentiﬁed as open water because its
low backscatter and smooth texture. Increasing relevant training samples could improve accuracy.
Lastly, the model transferability is evaluated using SAR scenes with relatively large spatial and
temporal variation. Due to the insuﬃcient training samples especially reformed new ice with weak
53
==PAGE:62==
backscatter area, estimate SIC performance on these datasets are not as good as in the training
and test datasets. The changes of ice physical properties because of regional and time diﬀerences
would also lead to some misclassiﬁcation in estimate SIC.
6.1
Limitations and Future Work
The main limitation is the misidentiﬁcation and underestimation of low backscatter ice with
smooth texture features. One possible solution could be increasing number of training samples in
this region.
Additionally, due to lack of relevant experience in convolutional neural networks, many un-
necessary attempts were made to design and tune the CNN model, which wasted a lot of time.
The CNN architecture may not be optimal in this case. It needs further adjust and test. More
comparison results from diﬀerent hyperparameters and diﬀerent training samples should be illus-
trated. Such as, the inﬂuence of batch size, using dilated convolution, using SAR images with
normalised radar cross-section in dB units to recheck inﬂuence of batch normalization, using ﬁne
tuning technique to re-train selected model on new training samples etc. All these ideas can be
the extensions and future work of this experiment.
