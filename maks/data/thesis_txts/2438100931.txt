Abstract
Observing astrophysical phenomena in the medium energy range of the Universe is a challenging
task. At this point in time it is largely unexplored due to observational diﬃculties in this range.
It requires innovative solutions to advance current technology of detectors. At DTU Space a 3D
CZT drift strip detector has been developed allowing for sub-mm position resolution, as well as
high energy resolution. The aim of this thesis is to characterize the 3D CZT drift strip detector
prototype, determining its position and energy resolution. The position determination is expanded
to handle double site events within the detector. These will be utilized to characterize the imaging
performance of the 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype, determining its angular resolution when
operating as a Compton camera. The performance of the 3D CZT drift strip detector as a part
of a simple high energy mission concept, will be evaluated using the simulation tool MEGAlib.
Lab experiments with full, slit beam, and point source illuminations will be performed and used in
the characterization of the 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype. The ﬁndings will inﬂuence the
parameters of the mission concept simulation. The position resolution of the 3D CZT drift strip
detector prototype was found to be 0.5mm in the x-direction, 0.4mm in the y-direction, and 0.5mm
in the z-direction at 661.6keV. The energy resolution was determined to be 1.6% at 661.6keV.
Utilizing double events in the detector, the angular resolution of the single 3D CZT drift strip
detector prototype operating as a Compton camera was determined to 16◦. The simple mission
concept including the 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype was able to determine the location of
an astrophysical source in a low Earth orbit background, excluding trapped protons and electrons
with an observation time of 9300 seconds. The study will be limited by the short time span of a
master thesis project.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Astrophysical missions observing the X- and gamma-ray Universe are plentiful. Instruments ob-
serving the medium-energy range of the Universe, also known as the MeV domain, has much worse
sensitivity compared to the neighbouring energy ranges, see Figure 1.1. This is what is known as
the MeV gap. The MeV domain of the Universe is at this point in time largely unexplored due to
a number of diﬃculties associated with observing in this range [1]. The penetrating power of MeV
photons makes them diﬃcult to fully absorb in the detector material. However, this penetrating
power of the photons is also what makes them of great interest, since they are able to escape highly
obscured astrophysical sources [2]. At the same time, the instrumental background in this energy
range is huge, resulting in it being one of the most diﬃcult to study. This is why there is a need
of innovation and development of technology to take on this task to explore the MeV range of
the Universe. One of the most appropriate instruments for observing the MeV range is currently
the Compton camera, due to the a large ﬁeld of view and good signal to noise ratio compared to
other camera types [1]. However, the Compton camera is diﬃcult to design, since it has to be
large enough to completely absorb most interactions occurring in the instrument. Furthermore,
the angular resolution of the camera is aﬀected both by the energy and position resolution of the
detectors, therefore all detectors in the instrument must provide both excellent position and energy
resolution.

1.1. Project Formulation
At DTU Space, a 3D CZT (CdZnTe - Cadmium Zinc Telluride) drift strip detector prototype
has been developed[4], and DTU has ﬁled two diﬀerent patents1,2. The 3D CZT drift strip detector
not only provides a good energy resolution, but also provides sub-mm position resolution in three
dimensions. This detector could be a part of a Compton camera for observing the MeV Universe.
The current detector prototype will be investigated, characterized, and the performance of the
single detector prototype as a Compton camera will be evaluated. Furthermore, an instrument
package of a simple Compton camera concept will be investigated, including the 3D CZT detector
as a baseline design for a space mission.
This thesis is structured such that Chapter 2 will introduce the most important aspects of the
interaction and detector theory, Chapter 3 will introduce the simulation tool MEGAlib, Chapter 4
will describe the 3D CZT detector prototype, experimental setup and position algorithms. Chapters
5 and 6 will describe the characterization of the 3D CZT detector prototype, including an evaluation
of its imaging capability. Lastly, Chapter 7 will investigate a simple baseline design for a high-energy
mission concept utilizing the 3D CZT detector.
1.1
Project Formulation
The main focus of this master thesis is the characterization of the 3D CZT prototype detector
developed at DTU Space. The detector’s position and energy determination will be investigated
by experimental measurements of energy resolution and position resolution from a single 3D CZT
detector prototype. The position and energy determination of single site events will be expanded
to include double site events on multiple drift cells. Finally, the performance of the single proto-
type detector as a Compton camera will be evaluated. This will be compared to a simulation of
the experimental setup. Using MEGAlib, the performance of a simple Compton camera concept
including the 3D CZT detector will be evaluated.
Project milestones:
1. Characterization of the 3D CZT detector prototype.
(a) Gain normalization of anodes and cathodes.
(b) Position resolution determination using slit beam.
(c) Energy resolution determination using full illumination.
(d) Position and energy determination of single site events.
(e) Position and energy determination of double site events.
(f) ARM investigation using point source.
2. Implementation and simulation of a simple high-energy mission concept instrument package
in MEGAlib.
(a) Simple instrument design using 3D CZT module.
(b) Mission simulation using NuSTAR orbit environment.
3. Evaluation of experimental and simulation results.

Chapter 2
Semiconductor Radiation Detector
Theory
Observing X- and gamma-rays in space in the medium energy range (few hundred keV to MeV) is a
diﬃcult task due to three energy-loss processes, small cross-sections and the very rich instrumental
background. At the moment, the sensitivities of existing telescopes observing in this energy range
is poor compared to the neighbouring energy ranges [3]. This has resulted the MeV domain not
being widely explored.
In this chapter, the main theory concerning the semiconductor radiation detector and the X-
and gamma-ray interactions with this will be described.
2.1
Semiconductor Detectors
Any radiation detector operation requires radiation to interacts with the material of the detector
to be detected. This interaction can cause a measurable quantity, e.g. temperature change, visible
light, current, electric charge etc.
The solid state detector type is a radiation detector with a semiconductor detection medium.
The interaction between incoming radiation and detector material results in the formation of the
fundamental information carriers of the solid state detectors; electron-hole pairs.
An electron-
hole pair consists of an electron being recoiled from its atom, leaving a vacant spot called a hole.
The advantage of semiconductor material is the band-gap of the material. These materials have an
electrical conductivity between that of an insulator and a conductor. The conductivity of a material
is deﬁned by its band-gap between its valance and conduction band. A small band-gap will allow
for electrons to easily migrate from the valance band to the conduction band due to some external
energy, e.g. thermal or radiation. If the band-gap is large, the electrons will require a higher energy
to be excited and jump from the valance to the conduction band, resulting in the material being
an insulator, with band gap larger than 5eV [5]. A material like CZT has a band-gap of 1.6eV,
allowing for room temperature operation compared to Ge (Germanium) with a band-gap of 0.7eV
which requires cryogenic cooling.
The semiconductor is in-between the insulator and the conductor, and some energy must be
applied for electrons to migrate to the conduction band, crossing the band-gap. By applying an
external electric ﬁeld in the detector material, it is possible to keep the electron in the conduction
band for read-out. The hole will move in the opposite direction [5]. An illustration of the band-gap
structure of a semiconductor compared to an insulator is shown in Figure 2.1.

The advantage of a solid state detector over a gas detector, is a lower energy requirement
for creating charge carriers. A considerably smaller statistical variation of the pulse height in a
semiconductor detector, provides better energy resolution.
2.2
X- and Gamma-Ray Interactions with Matter
X- or gamma-rays interacting with matter can occur both with and without deposition of energy.
In the case of elastic scattering, the energy of the incident photon is not changed but the photon is
merely deﬂected. Each of the following three processes involves deposition of energy by the incident
photon within the detector volume.
The importance of the three interaction types depends on observation technique, detector
material and energy regime of observation. These three interaction types are:
1. Photoelectric absorption.
2. Compton scattering.
3. Pair production.
The probability of each of these interactions occurring is strongly dependent on the energy
of the interacting X- or gamma-ray and the atomic number (Z-number) of the detector material,
with which the photon interacts. In Figure 2.2 the areas where each of the three interaction types
dominates are shown.
Photoelectric absorption is most dominant at lower energies, and the probability of this in-
teraction to occur increases with the Z-number as the energy increases. Compton scattering is
dominant at a large range of energies, and the probability is even larger if the Z-number of the
material is small. The interaction dominates in general at the intermediate energy range from a
few hundred keV to several MeV. The last interaction, pair production, is the dominating process
at higher energies.
A photon interacting in a detector material will be able to cause multiple interactions, e.g.
a sequence of Compton scattering interactions ending with a photoelectric absorption. The total
amount of deposited energy will then determine the energy of the incidence photon. In the following,
characteristics of the three types of interactions will be described.

2.2.1
Photoelectric Absorption
The ﬁrst process is the photoelectric absorption. This interaction is dominating for lower energies.
The occurrence probability at higher energies increases with the Z-number of the absorber material.
The process of the photoelectric absorption occurs when an incident photon interacts with an
atom of the absorber material. This results in complete absorption of the photon, transferring the
energy of the photon to an electron of the atom, resulting in a photoelectron. The photoelectron
will be ejected from the atom, with a kinetic energy, Ep, given by the energy of the incidence
photon, hν, minus the binding energy of the electron in question, Eb,
Ep = hν −Eb,
(2.1)
where h = 6.626 · 10−34J/s is the Planck constant, and ν is the frequency of the incidence photon.
The creation of this photoelectron will result in the atom being left with a vacancy in one of its
electron shells, which is called a hole. This will be reﬁlled quickly by a free electron, if no electric
ﬁeld is applied to the material [5].
If a detector were to measure a monochromatic source, where interactions only underwent
photoelectric absorption, the energy spectrum would result in a peak around the photon energy,
see Figure 2.3. This clear photopeak at the position of the photon energy will occur since the entire
photon energy is deposited within the detector material, when undergoing photoelectric absorption.
Figure 2.3: Spectrum of photoelectric absorption of a monochromatic source. Image credit: [5] (p. 323).

The probability of Compton scattering occurring depends on both the energy of the incidence
photon, and the Z-number of the material. Compton scattering occurs more often in material with
low Z-number at all energies, as seen in Figure 2.2. As the Z-number of the material increases, the
dominant range of Compton scattering decreases.
The process of Compton scattering is when the incoming X- or gamma-ray photon interacts
with a single free electron in the material. The photon will be scattered by the electron, changing
its direction and transferring some of its original energy to the free electron. This will result in a
recoil electron. The amount of energy transferred to the free electron depends on the scattering
angle of the photon, θ, and the energy of the scattered photon, hνs,p. The subscript used in the
following will consist of two letters. The ﬁrst letter, i/s, indicates if it is before incidence (i) or
scattered (s), and the second letter, p/e, states if it is a photon (p) or an electron (e). In Figure
2.4, the Compton scattering interaction is illustrated, alongside energies of the photon and electron
before and after the scattering process.
Figure 2.4: Sketch of the Compton scattering interaction.

2.2.2.2
Concept of a Compton Camera
The process of Compton scattering, described in Section 2.2.2, can be utilized such that a detector
system becomes capable of imaging without the use of focusing optics or coded mask.
There are three basic types of Compton cameras, all with the key objective of detecting the
direction of motion of scattered X- or gamma-rays, illustrated in Figure 2.7.
The ﬁrst type, Figure 2.7a, consist of two detector systems, where the top detector system
is of a low-Z material working as the scatter layer, in which the Compton scattering interaction
takes place. A low-Z material is used here since probability of a Compton scattering process is
more likely for a large range of energies in low-Z materials, as stated in Figure 2.2. For the bottom
detector system, a high-Z material is used where the scattered photon is absorbed, also called a
calorimeter. The two layers are separated such that the time of ﬂight of the scattered photon can
be determined. This also enables the system to distinguish between the photon hitting the scatter-
or calorimeter layer ﬁrst. This system is not able to detect direction of the recoil electron therefore
the origin of the initial photon can only be reconstructed to a cone. In order to determine the
source origin, several photons must be measured, and the cones will overlap at the origin of the
source [2].
The second type of Compton camera, Figure 2.7b, does not measure the time of ﬂight but
instead has several layers in which the photons will scatter. With more than two interactions, the
redundant information will then allow for determination of the direction of the scattered photon,
and again determine the source location to a cone.
The two Compton cameras just described will only be able to determine the source direction
to a cone. Combining many reconstructed Compton cones will reveal the actual source location,
see Figure 2.8a [2].
The last type of Compton camera Figure 2.7c is capable of measuring the direction of the
recoil electron, and scattered photon, which reduces the Compton cone to a segment of the cone.
The size of the segment rely on the accuracy of the recoil electron measurement.
The image
reconstruction will therefore not consist of cones, but segments of these, see Figure 2.8b. These are
called trackers[2].
There are several advantages of Compton cameras. First of all, the source position can be
recovered with only few photons, enabling to detect low ﬂux sources. Secondly, it is possible to
do polarization measurements with Compton cameras, if large Compton scattering angles can be
measured. Additionally, a large ﬁeld of view is possible.
There are also several complications with Compton cameras. Each photon interaction will
be measured varying positions in the camera.
Each of these diﬀerent positions will introduce
a measurement error, which aﬀects the capability of determining the origin and energy of the
incidence photon. Therefore, energy and position resolutions of the detectors must be excellent.
Lastly, techniques to determine origin and energy of the incidence photon is complex [2].

Signal Formation in a Semiconductor Detector
When an X- or gamma-ray interacts with semiconductor material, it will generate a number of
electron-hole pairs. The electron charge cloud size will be proportional to the deposited energy
from the photon. An electric ﬁeld applied within the semiconductor material will cause electrons
to move towards highest potential and the holes in towards lowest. The movement of the charge
carrier is what induces a charge, Q, on the detector electrodes.
The charges are collected by a collecting electrode, and at this point the induction of charge
ends resulting in a fully developed pulse. With a pre-ampliﬁer this is converted to a voltage pulse,
which amplitude ideally is proportional to the deposited energy [11]. When collecting the electrons
and holes, these will drift along the path determined by the electric ﬁeld, E, which is perpendicular
to the equipotential surface of the collecting electrode. The task is now to predict the induced
signal.
The classical way of predicting the induced charge, Q, by a moving point charge, q, is to
calculate the instantaneous electric ﬁeld at each single point of the trajectory of the charge, and
then calculate the induced charge, by integrating the normal component of the electric ﬁeld over
the surface surrounding the electrode [11],

of E for points all along the trajectory of the point charge, q. Shockley [12] and Ramo [13] both,
independently, developed a much simpler method to predict the induced charge on an electrode,
which will be used to understand the signal formation in the 3D CZT detector throughout this
project. The trajectory of the charge, q, itself is determined by the true electric ﬁeld, E, the induced
charge, Q, can be calculated using a simpler method utilizing the weighting ﬁeld. It is much simpler

electric ﬁeld propagates instantaneously. Under these assumptions, the problem can be treated as
electrostatics at each moment of charge movement. The induced charge is independent of applied

Detector Simulation Software - The
MEGAlib Toolkit
When a photon undergoes a Compton scattering interaction in a Compton camera, followed by
subsequent Compton scattering interaction or photoelectric absorption, at least two interaction
points exist. Each hit (interaction) in the detector system will comprise a position and energy
information, and can also include time and electron track information. The direction and energy
ofthe original incidence photon must then be reconstructed from this information. To do this, the
the Compton kinematics must be utilized to reconstruct an image showing the source location.
This itself is a diﬃcult task, requiring complex algorithms. A set of software tools exists, designed
to both simulate and analyse data of Compton cameras. This tool is called ”the Medium Energy
Gamma-ray Astronomy library” (MEGAlib) [10] 1. This chapter will introduce the crucial steps in
data simulation and processing using MEGAlib for future reference.
MEGAlib is able to handle each data analysis step starting at data acquisition and throughout
event and image reconstruction. The toolkit was originally developed for the MEGA Compton and
pair telescope [16]. The library is written in C++ utilizing both ROOT [17] and Geant4 [18] for its
simulation and analysis. Not only is the tool able to simulate data, perform event reconstruction
and data analysis, it is also able to reconstruct and analyse real data acquired by the user [19]. The
library includes the entire data analysis chain which can be described by the four steps [2], [10]:
1. Acquisition of data (real data measurements / simulation data).
2. Real data calibration / introducing uncertainties of measurements to the simulated data.
3. Event reconstruction - reconstruction of the event chain of the interactions.
4. High level data analysis. This includes image reconstruction, sensitivity calculation, polar-
ization analysis etc.
All theory of reconstruction methods and algorithms in MEGAlib will not be described in
detail, but instead referring to the PhD thesis of Andreas Zoglauer [2], who is the lead developer
on the MEGAlib toolkit.
The four main programs in MEGAlib utilized in this project is, geomega [20], cosima [21],
revan [2] and mimrec [22], and the order of use will be described brieﬂy in the following.

3.1
geomega
The main purpose of the geomega (Geometry for MEGAlib) program is to describe the geometry

cosima
cosima (Cosmic Simulator of MEGAlib), is the simulator used in the MEGAlib library. It is based

the user to simulate common scenarios in X- and gamma-ray observations in space and on Earth

cosima has two main inputs, which are the geometry ﬁle deﬁned for geomega, and the descrip-
tion of the source in question. Furthermore, some general simulation inputs are given here: physics
list, output options, stop criterion etc. [23]. The output ﬁle of cosima is a sim-ﬁle containing all
information on hit positions and energy depositions of the simulated data in the detector.

revan
One of the major challenges of a Compton camera is to determine from the raw events, what
occurred in the detector and in what order. It is necessary to determine this, to acquire scientiﬁc
results. The detector provides information on number of hits, which all have position and energy
information.
The task is then to reconstruct the path of the event utilizing the knowledge of
Compton kinematics. An event can generate many diﬀerent hit patterns in the geometry, and
therefore it is important to determine the correct path of the event in the detector geometry
[2]. The MEGAlib program, revan (the Real Event Analyser) is used to reconstruct the events,
handling both single site events, Compton scattering events, and pair production events. Many
diﬀerent reconstruction algorithms are available within revan, all including and excluding electron
tracking. revan will also add noise to the simulated data. The output ﬁle from cosima, together
with the geometry ﬁle from geomega are the inputs for revan in the case of simulated data. If real
data is used, the input is an evta-ﬁle alongside the geometry ﬁle. This is so revan does not add
noise to the real data [19].
revan is the ﬁrst tool used after either simulating the data or gathering experimental data,
and the output of revan is a tra-ﬁle which can be used for further analysis with MEGAlib. Many
diﬀerent event selection thresholds can be set with revan, to further sort the data [24].
If the sequence of which the Compton interactions occurred is known, the user can bypass revan
and directly create a tra-ﬁle as input. However, this is not the case for this project, and therefore
revan will be used for the Compton sequence reconstruction of simulated and experimental data.
The Compton sequence reconstruction task has the goal to determine the true sequence which
the incidence X- or gamma-ray underwent. This is done by analysing all possible paths of the
measured interactions, and assigning each possible path a Compton quality factor. This quantiﬁes
the probability that the event actually underwent this path. The sequence which was assigned the
best Compton quality factor will be chosen.

3.4. mimrec
There is a wide range of possible energy transfers to the recoil electron, as described by
the Klein-Nishina Equation, Equation (2.4). The scattered photon from the Compton scattering
interaction, can undergo an additional Compton scattering interaction, be stopped by photoelectric
absorbtion, escape the volume or undergo pair production, if its energy is large enough.
The
reconstruction approach depends on the type of Compton event [2]:
1. If only two interactions occur, it is diﬃcult to determine the sequence due to lack of informa-
tion. Diﬀerent criteria exists which will also be used in this project, but they are weak and
can result in some events being wrongly reconstructed. This will lead to a worsened angular
resolution. Furthermore, it is not possible to evaluate whether the photon is completely ab-
sorbed from this information. In this project, data of double events are used, which will not
result in optimal performance.
2. If more than three Compton interactions occur, the information about the kinematics is
redundant, such that it is possible to determine the sequence of interactions. If at least four
interactions are measured before the photon escapes, it is possible to determine the total
energy of the incidence photon.
The Compton camera performance does not only depend on the hardware performance, but
also on the algorithms recovering the parameters of the incidence photons from measured data.
Since every missed event reduces the eﬃciency, and every wrongly reconstructed event will not
only lower the eﬃciency but also increase the background. The ﬁnal sensitivity of the camera will
strongly depend on the performance of the data analysis.

mimrec

The input ﬁle is a tra-ﬁle, most commonly provided by the revan program. This program is used
for all high level data analysis. Using this program, the performance of the Compton camera will
be evaluated. Further event selection to the data can be applied using mimrec, e.g. detector in
which the initial interaction must occur, energy range etc. Using mimrec it is possible to evaluate
the energy resolution and ARM of the instrument.

3D CZT Drift Strip Detector Concept
The 3D CZT detector is a solid state detector of the CZT material, able to determine the position
interaction in three dimensions. The CZT material has the advantage of high quantum eﬃciency
and no need for cooling to cryogenic temperatures.

4.1. 3D CZT Drift Strip Detector Concept
4.1.1
Detector Geometry
The 3D CZT detector is of size 20mm×20mm×4.7mm. On one side of the detector, 10 cathode
strips are attached held at potential -350V. Each cathode is of width 1.9mm with a gap of 0.1mm
in-between each cathode. Perpendicular to the cathodes on the oppsite side of the detector crystal
(material), are the anodes and drift strip electrodes. 12 anodes are attached, held at zero potential,
and between each of the anodes are three drift strip electrodes. Between the two edges and the
ﬁrst anode on each edge, only two drift strip electrodes are placed. The anode pitch is 1.6mm and
the width of each anode is 0.15mm. The drift strip width is also 0.15mm, with a gap of 0.25mm in
between each. The three drift strips are biased such that the center drift strip is held at potential
-120V and the two neighbouring drift strips are held at potential -60V. This will result in the
weighting potential being concentrated closer to the anodes, and concentrate the electric ﬁeld lines
towards the anodes. Thus the drift path from the cathode towards the anode being linear for the
most of the drift path, but bending towards the anodes close to collection [25].
When the electron-hole pairs are generated in the CZT material, the electrons will drift (mi-
grate) towards collection at the anodes and the holes will drift towards collection at the cathodes.
However, the drawback of the CZT material is in fact that the material contains defects resulting
in the charge to be trapped. This is especially bad for the holes as discussed in Section 2.3.1. The
outcome is signiﬁcant reduction in energy resolution [30].
Compensating the hole trapping by event rejection is not realistic for space applications, due
to the low ﬂuxes in the high-energy astronomy. The biased drift strips on the 3D CZT detector will
concentrate the weighting potential of the anodes so compact, that the hole-movement induces little
to no signal on the anodes. This will result in the induced signal on the anodes being proportional
to the electron charge cloud, and thus proportional to the deposited energy of the incidence photon.

scheme is shown in Figure 4.3. The coordinate system is given with x-direction perpendicular to the
anodes, z-direction perpendicular to the cathodes and y-direction along the depth of the detector
between the anodes and cathodes.

4.1.3
Signal Formation in the 3D CZT Detector
The signal formation within the 3D CZT detector has until this point not been described for the
speciﬁc detector in question. However, it is already stated that the induced signal in the detector
can be described by the Shockley-Ramo theorem. Furthermore, it is known that the electron charge
clouds generated in the 3D CZT detector will drift towards collection at the anode, being screened
from the hole-movement by the drift strips. In this section the fundamentals of the signal formation
in the 3D CZT detector will be described in general. Afterwards, more speciﬁc examples will be
described.
Between each anode are three biased drift strips. As previously mentioned, the anode is held
at ground potential while the cathodes are at potential -350V, which results in an electric ﬁeld
between the anodes and cathodes. The three drift strip electrodes between the anodes are biased
such that the two closest to the anodes are at -60V potential and the center drift strip electrode is
at -120V potential. This results in the electric ﬁelds being concentrated towards the anodes, and
the anodes will be screened such that they do not feel induced charge from hole movement. The
weighting potentials of the anodes will be closely concentrated to the electrode, such that charge
is only induced on the anode as it approaches collection, as seen in Figure 4.5. The color coding

anode, it passes the weighting potentials of all electrodes in the detector. Using Equation (2.14) the

Discriminating between diﬀerent interaction types in the 3D CZT detector is done by perform-
ing a pulse shape analysis. These pulse shapes depend on the interaction in the detector. In this
section the pulse shape formation of each interaction type will be described with reference to the
signal formation in the detector.
As described previously, when an interaction occurs in the 3D CZT detector material, an
electron charge cloud is created with a size proportional to the energy deposited by the photon.
Due to the presence of an E-ﬁeld, the electrons will drift along the ﬁeld towards the anode, and the
holes towards the cathode, which due to their poor mobility will most likely be trapped. Depending
on the type of interaction, the pulse shapes of the 3D CZT detector will be diﬀerent.
From the moment the electron charge cloud starts moving, it induces signals on all detector
electrodes. Therefore the anode signal will only start increasing as the electron charge cloud comes
close to collection. All cathodes will instantaneously measure the signal, as the electron charge
cloud starts moving, due to the weighting potential diﬀerences being signiﬁcant, also at larger
distances from the cathodes. As described in Section 2.2, the incidence photon can interact in a
number of ways, depending on the photon energy and detector material. If the incidence photon
is completely absorbed it underwent photoelectric absorption. This will result in one hit in the
detector material. It can also occur that the photon undergoes a Compton scattering process, and
the scattered photon escapes the detector material before undergoing another interaction. In this
case also only one hit occurred in the detector, however, not all energy of the incidence photon was
deposited. The scattered photon can also undergo several more Compton interactions and lastly
escape the detector material or end by a photoelectric absorption, thus depositing some or all of
its energy in the detector material. Lastly, the photon can undergo pair production, however this
requires a photon energy of at least 1.02MeV. Since the source used in this project is 661.6keV,
this interaction will not occur. The output signal from anodes, cathodes and drift strips will look
diﬀerent depending on what kind of interaction, and where it occurred in the detector.

4.3. Position Determination Algorithms
4.3.1
Electron Drift Time
A fundamental concept utilized in position determination of single and double events is the electron
drift time, te. The electron drift time is the time between the electron charge cloud starts moving
until it is collected by the collecting anode. The electron drift time depends mainly on the y-
position of the event. The closer to the cathode side the interaction occurred, the longer the drift
path of the electron charge cloud towards collection. Furthermore, the closer the interaction is to
a drift boundary, the longer the drift path the electron charge cloud will experience, due to the
bending of drift paths, and thus a longer electron drift time [33]. The electron drift time, described
in [33], consists of a start time, t0, and a collection time, t1, resulting in the electron drift time
te = t1 −t0.
(4.5)
The methods of determining t0 and t1 are diﬀerent from one another. Consider the example shown
in Figure 4.26. Here the anode charge signals are shown, together with the anode current signals
(Figures 4.26a and 4.26b), and the cathode charge signals in Figure 4.26c.
The start time, t0, of the drift is determined from the sum of the cathode charge signals. Since
the electron charge cloud induces charge on all cathodes from the moment it starts moving, it is
possible to determine t0 from the cathode signals. This is not possible from the anode signal, due
to the weighting potential geometry. Thus t0 is determined by scanning the cathode signal from the
top of the pulse, i.e. at sampling nr. 600. The sample number is decremented while the cathode
pulse height is above a certain threshold value equal to 10 channels. As soon as the signal amplitude
is below this threshold, the sample number is considered the start time. The sample number can
be converted to nano-seconds by multiplying it by 4, since the sample rate is 4ns.
The collection time, t1, is determined from the anode signal, however, not from the anode
charge signal, but the derivative of this, i.e. the current signal. Since the charge signal will show
a steep rise around the moment of collection the current signal will show a peak at this time. If
the electron charge cloud did not have a ﬁnite size but really was a point source, the anode charge
signal would rise momentarily resulting in a delta-function in the current signal. However, this is
not the case, and the current signal will show some broadening. The time of collection is chosen
to be at the peak position of the current signal. In reality the time of collection is when the anode
current signal has returned to zero, as the peak position is when the half of the electron charge
cloud has been collected. This is however not an issue, as long as the collection time is chosen to
be the peak position consistently for all events.

5.2
3D CZT Readout Electronics Gain Normalization
The gain of an ampliﬁer is a measure of its ability to increase the input signal magnitude. All
electrodes from the detector will have a diﬀerent gain, and therefore these must be normalized to
be comparable. To do this, the input energy must be known. For each detector electrode, the
pulse height of a given induced signal will vary slightly between electrodes. This variation must be
determined, thus the conversion from channels to keV of each anode/cathode will correspond to
the same input energy [5]. For this project, the gain normalization of the anodes and cathodes was
investigated. The method of determining which channel number corresponds to 661.6 keV diﬀers
for anodes and cathodes, and therefore these are described separately. For the gain normalization
of the anodes and cathodes, a set of full illumination data of the XZ-plane was used, with a high
hardware threshold of 3000.

5.3
3D CZT Position Resolution
When a photon interacts in the detector material, the signal is sampled with a sample time of 4ns
[33]. The position of the interaction in the detector can be determined in three dimensions x, y and
z. To evaluate the achievable position resolution of the detector, programs determining the x-, y-,
and z-positions were developed in IDL. The resolution in each direction was then evaluated using
a scan measurement of the given direction, described in Section 4.2.1. The data was sorted using
the program described in Section 5.1, so only single site events were considered.
5.3.1
X-Position Resolution
To investigate the resolution of the determined x-positions, a set of x-scan data was used. For each
scan, the x-positions of all events were calculated using the x-position algorithm from Equation (4.7)
[33]. 75 scan measurements were performed, but not all scans illuminate the detector, therefore it
was necessary to identify which of scan measurements did. For each scan data set, the number of
events was plotted, as a function of the data number, as seen in Figure 5.12. From this it is seen
that the beam starts hitting the detector from scan data ﬁle number 12, and leaves the detector
again after scan ﬁle number 60. This corresponds to a number of 50 scans hitting the detector.
The detector is 20mm along the x-direction, and each scan step is 400µm. A quick double-check
shows that 50 × 400µm = 20mm, corresponding to the detector size.
For the scan data with beam hitting the detector volume the x-positions were calculated for all
single site events. All calculated x-positions were plotted as a histogram, resulting in a peak around
the position where the beam was illuminating the detector. To determine the resolution of the x-
position, a Gaussian was ﬁtted to each individual scan histogram. The FWHM was determined
from the standard deviation, σ of the Gaussian using Equation (2.26), deﬁned in Section 4.3.

5.5.1
Energy Correction
As the electron drifts towards collection, the total pulse height generated at the anode can be lower
than the ideal pulse height due to two main contributors: the weighting potential and electron trap-
ping. The farther the electron travels before being collected, the more exposed to electron trapping
it will become. As more electrons are trapped, a smaller electron charge cloud will be collected
by the anode, resulting in the pulse height of the anode signal to become smaller. The weighting
potential eﬀect will also depend on where in the detector the interaction occurred. Consider the
illustration of the weighting potentials in Figure 5.23. The closer to the anode the interaction
occurs, the smaller the y-position, and the larger the diﬀerences in the weighting potentials. This
will result in in an attenuation of the induced charge, since the induced charge depends on the
diﬀerences in weighting potential.

Electronic Noise of the Detector
After the energy correction, an energy resolution of ∼1.6% was achieved. Previously, an energy

energy correction was done correctly. As described in Section 2.4.2, there are three contributors
to the energy resolution of the detector, the electronic noise, the statistical noise, and electron
trapping in the detector.

the energy correction to the best of capabilities, and the statistical noise is ﬁxed. Lastly was the
electronic noise of the detector setup. The current electronic setup is diﬀerent from the one used

the electronic noise. A test-pulse was used, creating pulses on the pre-ampliﬁer, and it was then

6.1
Characterization of Double Events in the 3D CZT Detector
Up until this point, only position algorithms have been developed for handling single site events in
the 3D CZT detector, as described in [33]. However, to evaluate the 3D CZT detector performance
as a Compton camera, it is essential to be able to determine the position and energy of double
events within the detector. In this section the solution to the position determination of double
events will be discussed, and the thresholds set in the initial analysis of the double events. In the
following analysis only double events will be considered, thus multiple (three or more) events will
be rejected.
6.1.1
Position Determination
As discussed previously, there exists two types of multiple events in the 3D CZT detector:
1. Single Cell Double Event.
2. Multiple Cell Double Event.
The focus of this project is the multiple cell double event. Many issues will be introduced
when double events are to be investigated. First of all, if the two events share drift strips, the drift
strip signals can be convoluted and thus, at this point, not distinguishable. Furthermore, when two
events are drifting towards collection, they will both induce signals on all ten cathodes, which as
well will be convoluted.
In this project, these issues were to be dealt with in order to determine energy and position
information of double events. In the following sections, the solutions found to the problems will be
described. The goal is not to reach a perfect solution to handle all double events, but to initialize
the process, and reach a proof of concept of the single 3D CZT detector crystal to act as a Compton
camera.
The events for the analysis were extracted using the developed algorithm described in Section
5.1.

all cathodes, due to the cathode signals being convoluted. The electron drift time will therefore

events occurred close to the anodes. Here a curvature occurs, not following the linear relationship,
which is due to the drift path not being linear close to the anodes. The linear ﬁt will therefore only
ﬁt data with drift times larger than 100 ns.

6.1.1.4
Z-Position Characterization
Similar to both x-, and y-position determination of double events, problems arise for the z-position
determination. And actually, for the z-position, not only determining the position is an issue. For x-
and y-positions, assigning the correct y-position to the correct x-position is simple due to knowledge
of the electron drift time. However, assigning the two calculated z-positions to the correct x- and
y-position is a more diﬃcult task. Z-position determination uses cathode signal pulse heights which
are all convoluted. Fitting a Gaussian proves diﬃcult for position determination.
Two diﬀerent methods to determine z-positions were investigated, both relying on the distance
between the two interactions:
1. Events where both interaction occur below same cathode. Thus less than a distance of 2mm
between the z-positions along the cathode direction.
2. Events where at least one cathode is between the interaction. Requiring a minimum distance
of 2mm between the z-positions along the cathode direction.
Both methods will be described in the following sections, but, if the distance along the z-
direction is less than 2mm, the currently applied method is uncertain due to noisy signals. There-
fore, for further analysis, only events where the distance is larger than 2mm will be used.
Short Distance
When the distance between the two interactions is short in the z-direction, they both occur
beneath the same cathode strip, or at two strips close to each other, (an example of this kind of event
is shown in Figure 6.8). The black markers in Figure 6.9 shows cathode pulse height distribution
as a function of z-position. In this case, only a single peak exist, not allowing for determination
of the two z-positions with the method described in [33]. A method utilizing the cathode current
signal, see Figure 6.8b, was investigated. Until the point where the ﬁrst electron charge cloud is
collected, both moving charges will induce signals on all cathodes. When the ﬁrst electron charge
cloud is collected, only the second electron charge cloud is inducing signal on the cathodes. An
attempt to isolate the signal of the ﬁrst collected electron charge cloud was done taking the current
signal of the summed cathodes into use. This signal should show a staircase-like shape, going down
a step as the ﬁrst charge cloud is collected. The mean value of the signal between t1 and t2 is
subtracted from the mean value of the current signal between t0 and t1. This is done for each single
cathode signal, and converted to a charge signal by multiplying with the electron drift time of the
ﬁrst collected interaction. Thus ten values has been extracted, one cathode charge amplitude for
each cathode, for the ﬁrst collected electron charge cloud. The amplitude distribution of the second
electron charge cloud is found by determining the amplitude between t1 and t2. In Figure 6.9, the
cathode charge amplitude distribution of this event is shown. The red markers are the calculated
amplitudes for the ﬁrst collected event, and the blue markers are the second collected event. A
Gaussian is then ﬁtted to each distribution to determine the z-position.
This method, however, suﬀers from noisy cathode signals, which become worse for the current
signal, introducing large uncertainties to this method.

6.2
Image Reconstruction
The goal of the characterization of double events is to investigate performance of the single detector
prototype as a Compton camera. It is expected that a single detector will not perform as ideally as
a Compton camera consisting of multiple detectors. However, the single detector prototype imaging
capability is to be investigated as a proof of concept. As already discussed, the position and energy
determination of multiple events in the single detector crystal is a diﬃcult task. This will result in
many events being rejected. Furthermore, the position determination of multiple hit events (more
than two interactions) has at this point not be evaluated. Most criteria for evaluating double site
events with no electron-track informations are fairly weak, and may result in wrongly reconstructed
events, degrading the angular resolution of the Compton camera, as described in Chapter 3. In
the following, double events from the experimental data will be included in MEGAlib, using the
toolbox to evaluate the detector performance as a Compton camera. A simple simulation of the
experimental setup will also be evaluated, and compared to the experimental results. The point
source measurement used for this experiment was introduced in Section 4.2.3. In Figure 6.13 a
sketch of the point source measurement setup is shown. In panel A the setup is seen from the
YZ-plane, and panel B shows the setup from the XZ-plane. The source is placed at a distance of
50 cm to the detector, with the collimator opening centered at the XY-plane of the detector.

6.2.2.1
Comparison of Simulation and Experimental Results
The results from the experimental setup did show that the prototype detector is able to locate the
source position, using just double-interaction events. The prototype detector achieved an angular
resolution of ∼16◦, which is worse than that of the simulation which was 12.15◦. However, the
simulation does not include all material surrounding the detector in reality.
Furthermore, the
simulation assumes the same position resolution of double events, as found for single site events,
but the position resolution of double interaction events might be worse than for single events. The
linear regression used to determine the y-position should be expanded to depend on the x-position
within the drift cell, since the drift time changes whether the events occur close or far from collecting
anode. Furthermore, energy resolutions for energies diﬀerent than 661.6keV are estimates, and not
true determined energy resolutions of the 3D CZT detector prototype. It is however proven that
the 3D CZT detector prototype of size 2cm× 2cm×0.47cm is capable of determining the direction
of a weak source, utilizing Compton interactions. By expanding the handling of multiple events
to single cell multiple events, and events with more than two interactions, this angular resolution
should in theory be able to improve below 10◦FWHM of the ARM.

High Energy Mission Concept
Finalizing this project, a simple high energy mission concept instrument package was investigated
utilizing MEGAlib. This concept was to mainly consist of the 3D CZT detector. The detector
used in the concept was changed compared to the detector in the lab setup, but builds on the
possibilities this detector entails. The scope of this analysis is to investigate the characteristics
of a simple Compton camera, and its performance with and without a LEO (Low Earth Orbit)
background.
7.1
The MEGAlib Geometry
The geometry of the instrument package must be implemented in MEGAlib in the form of a
geometry ﬁle. This geometry ﬁle must consist all materials in the instrument, and the characteristics
of the sensitive volumes i.e. the detectors. The world volume and surrounding sphere must also be
deﬁned, and packed as closely around the geometry as possible to optimize the time it takes for the
simulation to ﬁnish. The surrounding sphere in MEGAlib is used by cosima to simulate a far-ﬁeld
point source. A disc with the same radius as the sphere is placed tangentially on the surrounding
sphere, and all point source photons are initiated from this disc, to simulate plane waves. This is
due to far-ﬁeld point source being so far away, that the wave-front will be plane.
In the following each element of the geometry ﬁle will be described, resulting in the entire
instrument package being described. All materials used in the instrument package are pre-deﬁned
materials accessible in the MEGAlib toolbox.
7.1.1
The Module Design
The geometry mainly consists of replicas of the same detector module, therefore this module will
ﬁrst be described. Each detector crystal will need their own read-out electronics. This imposes
the issue that a Compton camera require minimal amount of ’dead’ material, i.e. non-sensitive
material. Therefore, the idea of this design is for two detector crystals to share one electronic read-
out plate, halving the amount of read-out electronics in the design. One module therefore consists
of two 3D CZT detectors, one on each side of an electronics plate. In Figure 7.1, a MEGAlib
illustration of a single module geometry is shown. The detector size is larger than the prototype.
The lab setup detector is 2cm × 2cm × 0.47cm, and the detector used in the instrument package
will be 4cm × 4cm × 0.5cm. The electronics plates are of the size 11cm × 11cm × 0.3cm. One
module layer consist of four of individual modules, and the entire design was chosen to have four
module layers, resulting in a total of 32 3D CZT detectors. So what are the advantages of increasing
93
==PAGE:104==
7.1. The MEGAlib Geometry
the number of layers? First of all, the quantum eﬃciency of the instrument increases, resulting
in an increased probability of a photon being completely absorbed. This is especially important
at higher energies. Second of all, it allows for the distance between two Compton interactions to
increase, which will lead to an improvement of the ARM of the instrument. The larger the distance
between the layers, the better ARM of the instrument. However, increasing layer distance will
also decrease quantum eﬃciency. This trade-oﬀmust of course be investigated to be optimized
for the scientiﬁc requirements of the mission. For the purpose of this study, a distance between
the read-out electronics of two layers was chosen to be 3cm, thus a distance of 1.7cm between the
stacked detectors. The total instrument package investigated can be seen in Figure 7.2. Initially,
a single scatter layer was included 4 cm above the top module layer of the instrument package. A
single layer like this would have little to no eﬀect on the performance of the instrument package
in the energy range in question (100keV-3MeV). In reality a full tracker layer should be included.
However, the purpose of this investigation is a simple baseline study, therefore this scatter layer
was removed.

Observing the Crab Nebula
With MEGAlib it is possible to perform simulations of realistic orbital scenarios. Lets consider the
Crab nebula. The Crab nebula is a supernova remnant with a pulsar (neutron star) at the center.
This pulsar is, in the X-and gamma-ray regime, one of the brightest sources in the sky. It has
been observed by many observatories, and is therefore often used to evaluate the performance of a
telescope [35]. MEGAlib has a built-in Crab nebula source, which will be used for this simulation.
Furthermore, we will consider the telescope in the radiation environment corresponding to that
of the NuSTAR (Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array) satellite. The background source used
in MEGAlib was obtained from the MEGAlib github 1. In LEO like this, the background will
be dominating. Since this simple baseline design does not have shielding or a heavy calorimeter
to block out some of these particles (contrary to all telescopes in this orbit), it is already at a
disadvantage. For this simulation only energies in the range of 100keV-3MeV is considered, for
the sake of the baseline investigation. From MEGAlib the NuSTAR orbit background could be

8.1
Discussion
In the following discussion, the results of the work conducted throughout the ﬁve months of this
master thesis will be reviewed, in the light of the project formulation, given in Section 1.1. Three
main project milestones were considered.
8.1.1
Characterization of the 3D CZT Detector Prototype
First of all the 3D CZT detector was to be characterized. Initially when handling the data measured
by the 3D CZT detector prototype, it was necessary to automatically sort the logged events into
diﬀerent event-classes. For this an IDL routine constructing the data structure was extended. It
was to handle event sorting, and eventually also the position calculation and energy correction.
High hardware threshold data and low hardware threshold data were investigated, to estimate the
number of wrongly assigned events. For high threshold data 200 random events were investigated.
14 events out of these 200 random sample events were assigned incorrectly. From this, three events
were assigned as single site events, but should be assigned as single cell double events, thereby 1.5%
were assigned to event-class ID 1 but should have been event-class ID 2. 11 events were assigned as
single cell double events but should have been single site events. Thus 5.5% events were assigned
to event-class ID 2 but should have been event-class ID 1. Since single cell double events were not
included in the analysis, the algorithm is considered to perform acceptable within the scope of the
project. The algorithm must be further improved to ensure event-class ID 2 to be sorted correctly.
For low threshold data, 131 random samples were investigated. From this, four events were assigned
to event-class ID 0 (undeﬁned) instead of event-class ID 3. This corresponds to 3%. This should
be improved for further study. It is noted that the algorithm performs well, largely assigning data
correctly to the event-classes considered in the project. The fallout of wrongly assigning data to
event-classes not considered in the project, is that statistics of the event-classes in question is poor
compared to an ideal algorithm performance.
Some events occurring in the detector while performing the point source measurements showed
large ﬂuctuations on several anodes. The reason has not been determined, and the events were
removed from the data set.
This issue should be investigated more thoroughly.
This sorting
algorithm can be expanded to sort the events in more classes resulting in a ﬁner catalogue. This
is necessary for handling events with more than two hits, to distinguish between single cell- and
multiple cell events. This extension can be built on the current methods examining the double
events.

8.1. Discussion
The position resolution of the detector was determined using the position algorithms derived
by Budtz-Jørgensen and Kuvvetli [33]. The algorithm for x-position determination was altered,
such that the edges of the detector was taken into account, and thereby the true positions were
determined. With this alteration, x-positions were distributed correctly within the boundaries of
the detector. The position resolutions were found to be 537µm in the x-direction, 378µm in the
y-direction, and 527µm along the z-direction. The resolutions along the x-, y-, and z-direction
were previously determined to be 410µm along the x-direction, 420µm along the y-direction, and
380µm along the z-direction [33]. The determined position resolutions can be degraded by spread
of the collimated beam. The beam is collimated to 200µm, which is not signiﬁcantly less than the
determined position resolution. Ideally, this should be much thinner. Furthermore, the distance
between the collimator and the detector will result in beam divergence. The detector will therefore
be illuminated by a thicker beam than the collimator opening. The position resolution will also be
aﬀected by the variance in the centroid of the electron charge cloud [33]. The position resolution
will also vary at diﬀerent energies, due to the size of the electron charge cloud varying at diﬀerent
incoming energies.
Ideally the detector position resolution should be characterized at varying
source energies. Comparing the newly determined prototype resolutions to the ones determined
by [33], only the y-position resolution has improved. It should be noted that the previous analysis
only investigated the four ﬁrst drift cells, anodes nr. 1-4. Furthermore, the previous analysis was
performed with diﬀerent read-out electronics than the current prototype setup. It is noteworthy,
that the determined position resolution of the detector prototype lab setup does not deviate in
order of magnitude from the previously determined position resolution.
The ﬁrst ever ﬂat-ﬁeld images of the interaction positions within the entire detector volume
were created, as done for the ﬁrst four drift cells by [33]. The image of the XY-plane resulted in
the position distribution to be as expected. The x-positions for interactions close to the anode side
showed clustering at the drift cell boundaries. This is due to the shortcoming of the x-position
algorithm determined from [33] utilizing the drift strip signal for events hitting close to the anode.
This was also found by [33], and it is stressed that events close to the anode must be handled
diﬀerently than the current method. The drift cell boundaries were also clearly visible in the XY-
image. This is due to these events currently being treated like double events. A method should
be developed for the handling of these events. Double events should be screened, to determine
if these actually are a single event suﬀering from the sharing eﬀect. By knowing the y- and z-
positions of this event, it is possible to conclude that it is a single event and not a double since the
events will share y- and z-position. The edge eﬀect was also visible in the XY-image, as expected.
Interestingly, it was much more dominating in drift cell nr. 1 compared to drift cell nr. 12. A
reason for this might be that drift cell nr. 12 has the read-out electronics beside it, where drift
cell nr. 1 has nothing beside it. If this in fact is the reason, a method of reducing the edge eﬀect
will allow for optimization of the detector volume. Indeed, a more thorough study of this should
be conducted. For the images of the XZ-plane, it was shown that the implementation of handling
determination of z-positions close to the cathode side with the correction method developed by
[33] did not work well for this implementation. The current implementation shows a tendency of
clustering for events close to the cathode side. However, comparing it to data with no z-position
correction, the clustering is improved, yet not completely accounted for. This can also aﬀect the
current position resolution in the z-direction. It might be due to the implementation in [33] being
somewhat diﬀerent than done in this implementation. This should be further investigated. Lastly,
the XZ-plane images revealed dead-zone areas. In areas where cathodes nr. 1 and 2 overlap with
anodes nr. 5, 6, and 12. Little to no signal is generated in these areas. The initial suspicion
was that the data was handled wrongly, either in the data sorting or in the position calculations.
However, this did not prove to be the case. The next suspicion was the two SIS3316 devices had

issues for the channels in question. Therefore, the channel input for cathodes nr. 1 and 2 and
anodes nr. 5, 6, and 12 was swapped with other anodes and cathodes. However, this also proved

The next task was to determine the energy resolution of the 3D CZT detector prototype. The
energy resolution is aﬀected by the quality of the detector material, the quality of the electronics
and statistical ﬂuctuations.
Furthermore, the anode gain normalization and conversion to keV
must also be performed properly, since this may aﬀect the ﬁnal result. Speciﬁcally for the 3D
CZT drift strip detector, the pulse height formation suﬀers from the weighting potential eﬀect, and
electron trapping. The longer distance the electron charge cloud must migrate before collection,
the more the signal will suﬀer from charge trapping, resulting in a dampened signal amplitude. If
an interaction occurs close to the anodes, the full signal might not be induced due to the weighting
potential geometry. This will also result in the pulse height being dampened, dominating for events
close to the anodes. With no energy correction, a clear tail was visible left of the photopeak due
to electron trapping and weighting potential eﬀect. The FWHM of this distribution was found
to be 24.7keV, corresponding to an energy resolution of 3.7%. Correcting the pulse heights for
the direction dependency along the y-direction reduced the FWHM to 13.7 keV, corresponding to
an energy resolution of 2.1%. This proves a considerable improvement in the energy resolution by
correcting the y-position dependency of the pulse heights. The y-position dependency will also prove
to be the dominant position dependence of the measured pulse heights [33] . Along the x-direction
of the detector, the pulse heights will show a dampening close to the drift cell boundaries, due to
the electron charge clouds suﬀering from more trapping, because of longer drift paths. Correcting
this, the FWHM was decreased further to 12.1keV, corresponding to a resolution of 1.8%. Lastly,
the z-position dependency was corrected. The ﬂuctuation in the pulse heights along the z-position
should ideally be uniform. However, due to varying material characteristics throughout the crystal,
this showed some structure. Correcting this, the FWHM was found to be 10.9 keV, thus an energy
resolution of 1.6%. The energy resolution of the 3D CZT detector has previously been determined to
be 1.14% by [33], but again only the ﬁrst four drift cells were considered, with diﬀerent electronics.
The change in read-out electronics might be the main contributor to the reduced energy resolution.
It has previously been shown, that it is possible to come very close to the Fano limit [28], but
to approach this, much time must be focused on electronics development. However, the current
setup is just a prototype, and therefore an energy resolution of 1.6% can be improved even further.
This resolution proves good enough for further investigation of the 3D CZT detector as a Compton
camera.
In the second part of the 3D CZT detector prototype characterization, position and energy
determination of double events were investigated.
This imposed new problems in the position
determination, due to convoluted signals. Since double events had not been treated before, it was
decided only to consider multiple cell double events in the analysis. For the x-positioning, the
convoluted drift strip signals were circumvented by introducing a time diﬀerence threshold between
collection of the two interactions. This allowed for the drift strip signals to show the amplitudes
in the inverted current signal of the drift strips. Furthermore, a threshold between the time of
interaction and time of collection of the ﬁrst event was also introduced. This is due to the x-
positioning close to the anode not providing a ﬁne position resolution with the current algorithm
[33], as shown for single site events. The two time thresholds were set to 160ns between time of
103
==PAGE:114==
8.1. Discussion
interaction and the time of collection of the ﬁrst electron charge cloud, and 100ns between the
time of collection of ﬁrst collected electron charge cloud and the second collected electron charge
cloud. If each drift strip had individual channel read-out, the signals would not be convoluted.
The y-position determination had the issue of convoluted cathode signals. Therefore, the linear
relationship between the electron drift time and y-position was utilized. This was done for all
anodes, where each y-position of a single event was plotted as a function of electron drift time.
However, this assumes that the drift path is the same length in the entire drift cell. It is known,
that the drift path is longer closer to the drift cell boundaries, than above the anodes. For further
investigation, it would be possible to divide the drift cell boundaries and investigate the linear
relationship for diﬀerent drift path lengths. Lastly, the z-positions were determined. This was
done, as for the single site events, only considering events where cathode amplitudes as a function
of z-positions showed double peaks. The result of this is a rough sorting of the data, rejecting
several events. Ideally, this should be extended to include events with only one peak along the z-
direction. It is diﬃcult to evaluate the performance of the position determination of double events,
as it can not be done similar to what was done for single site events. Using MEGAlib, the imaging
capability of the detector was investigated by simulation. Since the energy resolution had only been
determined at 661.6keV, the other energies were estimated to the best of the capability. However,
this does introduce uncertainties in the simulation. The simulation showed a FWHM of the ARM
of 12.15◦, including an Aluminium casing around the detector.
Extracting double events from
point source measurements, an image was reconstructed. Without energy corrections the source
position was located with a FWHM of the ARM of 21.58◦, which was improved with the energy
correction resulting in a FWHM of the ARM of 15.95◦, and the ARM peak position also came closer
to 0◦for the corrected energy measurements. The 3D CZT detector prototype therefore proved
its capability of determining the position of a weak point source. The ARM is aﬀected by the
energy and position resolutions, therefore, to further improve the ARM, the position and energy
resolutions should be improved. It is a proof of concept, and it successfully determined the source
position. For further investigation, the source should be placed at other locations, to ensure the
detector can robustly determine the source position. Furthermore, also performing imaging with
sources at diﬀerent positions, to investigate the performance of the 3D CZT detector as a Compton
camera.
8.1.2
Implementation and Simulation of a High-Energy Mission Concept In-
strument Package in MEGAlib
The second milestone of this project was to investigate a simple Compton camera baseline instru-
mentation design including the 3D CZT detector. The Compton camera concept performance of
an observation of the Crab nebula in a LEO background was investigated. Initially, the design
consisted of a single Silicon drift detector as scattering layer, and the four module layers consist-
ing of 32 3D CZT detectors. However, a single scattering layer will not scatter many photons,
and in this energy range, a full tracker layer will be necessary. The simulation was performed for
100keV-3MeV, beginning in the range where Compton scattering becomes more probable in the
CZT material. For the Compton camera to be optimized for lower energy ranges, the scattering
layer should also be included. Furthermore, a shield was not included in the baseline design, and
therefore trapped protons and electrons were not included in the simulation. It is important to
investigate the shielding for the LEO. Not only should the shield protect against the background,
but the secondaries created from the interactions with dead material, which will worsen the ARM of
the Compton camera signiﬁcantly. Not including the trapped protons and electrons, but including
the rest of the LEO background, it was possible to locate the Crab source after 9300s simulated

8.1.3
Evaluation of Experimental and Simulation Results
Considering the single 3D CZT detector prototype, the simulation did show better performance than
for the experimental results. This can be due to the energy resolution of the detector estimated at
other energies than 661.6keV, deﬁned in MEGAlib, are better than what they would be for the given
detector lab setup. This will result in the simulation performing better than the measurements.
Furthermore, it might also be due to the position resolution of the double event determination not
being as good as for the method for single site events [33]. However, the simulation did show that
a single detector prototype was able to determine the correct source location, only utilizing double
events in the detector, when knowing the incoming energy. If the energy is not known, photopeak
events cannot be extracted. This will be an issue for such a small detector prototype if the source
is not monochromatic, since many Compton events will scatter, escaping the material. As long as
the source energy is not too large, the photopeak will appear and can be isolated for the image
reconstruction. It would be interesting to be able to handle triple events, since this would allow for
an even better ARM of the single 3D CZT detector prototype.
8.2
Conclusion
The 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype developed at DTU Space [4] was characterized using
photons from a known radioactive source with energy of 661.6keV. The position resolution of the
3D CZT prototype detector was measured to be 537µm in the x-direction, 378µm in the y-direction,
and 527µm in the z-direction at 661.6 keV. The two main contributors to this position resolution
will be the ﬁnite beam size illuminating the detector, and the variance of the electron charge cloud
centroid. The position resolutions should vary with incoming energy since the size of the electron
charge clouds will change. The energy resolution of the 3D CZT detector prototype was determined
to be 1.6% at 661.6keV, using the method of energy correction described by [33]. It has previously
been shown that an energy of 1% could be reached [33], and the 3D CZT drift detector can come
very close to the Fano limit [28]. Since the characterized detector is a prototype, the electronics
are not optimally designed. The method of energy correction, did however improve the energy
resolution signiﬁcantly.
The position and energy determination was extended to handle multiple cell double events,
with distance between events along the z-direction exceeding 2mm, in the 3D CZT drift strip
detector. A set of thresholds were set to distinguish the drift strip signals when shared between
anodes, and to exclude events too close to the anodes. The energy correction determined for the
single site events were included to correct the energy of the double events in the detector. Utilizing
the position and energy information of the double events, it was possible to reconstruct the events
using MEGAlib, and with this, determine the source location. The resulting angular resolution of
the single 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype showed to be 15.95◦FWHM of the ARM, from
approximately 300 reconstructed events. The point source measurements were performed, and a
total of 7 hours of observation time resulted in this angular resolution. Thereby, the 3D CZT
detector was able to determine the source location, acting as a Compton camera.
Lastly, a simple baseline design including the 3D CZT detector was investigated. Only in-
cluding the 3D CZT detector, a design was created with 32 of these detectors. This small baseline
design was, in the MEGAlib simulation, placed in a LEO background observing the Crab nebula. It
was possible to reconstruct an image of the Crab nebula in a LEO background, excluding trapped

8.3. Future Work
protons and electrons, with a simulated observation time of 9300 seconds. In future investigations,
including scattering layers and shielding, this should improve signiﬁcantly.
In conclusion, work presented in this thesis did show a sub-mm position resolution for the
3D CZT drift strip detector, as previously shown by [25],[33]. The energy resolution of the cur-
rent setup did not reach the same energy resolution as determined by [33] but did show a good
energy resolution of 1.6% for the prototype, not orders of magnitude from what has previously
been found. Furthermore, it was shown, with a ﬁrst iteration analysis, that position and energy
determination of multiple cell double events in the detector can be handled. From this, the location
of a monochromatic source was successfully determined from the reconstructed double events with
an angular resolution of ∼16◦for photopeak events.
The 3D CZT drift strip detector has shown to oﬀer a unique instrumentation as part of a
Compton telescope. With no cryogenic cooling and compact size it proves appropriate for space
instrumentation and could, in combination with other technology, play a vital role in improving
the sensitivity within the MeV gap.

Future Work
There are many future paths to explore for the 3D CZT drift strip detector. Some of these are
listed below:
– Mathematical model development including non-uniformity of the detector materials.
– 3D lookup correction table using mathematical model.
– Implementing the 3D CZT algorithms in hardware using Artiﬁcial Neural Network (pulse-

– Imaging of multiple sources using the 3D CZT detector.

 Evaluation of a Compton Camera Concept Using the 3D CdZnTe Drift Strip Detectors 
S. Howalt Owea, C. Budtz-Jørgensena and I.Kuvvetlia 
a DTU Space, Technical University of Denmark , National Space Institute, Elektrovej Building 327, DK - 2800 Kgs. Lyngby, Denmark 
ABSTRACT 
At DTU Space an early version of a 3D CZT drift strip detector prototype has successfully been 
developed and fabricated [1]. The design of the prototype detector (20 mm x 20 mm x 5 mm) is 
based on the CZT Drift Strip detector principle [2]. It contains 12 drift cells, each comprising one 
collecting anode strip with 4 drift strips, biased such that the electrons are focused and collected 
by the anode strips. The electrode geometry of these prototype detectors was optimized for 
having anode pitch of 1.6 mm and cathode pitch of 2 mm. A theoretical 3D CZT detector model 
has been developed using COMSOL for electrostatic calculation and IDL for charge collection 
and signal formation to display the expected pulse shapes for given interactions position. 
Furthermore, the model also calculates the electron and hole trapping during the charge 
movements. A readout technology and algorithm were developed, resulting in excellent position 
determination in 3D, and energy determination for high energies (>50 keV - few MeV, the 
developed algorithm is tested with a narrow beam at 662 keV). However, until this point the 
algorithm only comprised position and energy determination of single hit events. 
The signal formation for all electrodes provides 3D position information of the interaction 
together with enabling a 3D correction of residual charge trapping. It also allows for detection 
and characterization of multiple interaction events occurring in the same or multiple detector 
cells, e.g. a Compton interaction with subsequent photoelectric absorption.  In this project, the 
position determination of events with multiple hits were analysed and an algorithm developed 
for multiple interaction events. Compton imaging capability and achievable angular resolution 
of the single 3D CZT detector is evaluated with a point source illumination using Cs-137 source. 
The simulation platform MEGAlib [3] is used for image reconstruction from position and energy 
information provided by the developed algorithm. Furthermore, a 3D CZT Compton camera 
instrument model is evaluated with MEGAlib simulations of realistic source illumination 
scenarios to provide spatial and spectral performance figure of merit.
