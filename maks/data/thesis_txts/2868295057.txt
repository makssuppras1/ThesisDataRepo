Abstract
This thesis investigates the potential of using hidden Markov models with time dependent
emissions for exploring patterns in sleep. The primary contribution of the project is the
implementation and exploration of training hidden Markov models with autoregressive
emissions (HMM­MARs) using stochastic gradient descent. Experiments on synthetic
time dependent data reveal challenges with consistently obtaining a good model during
training, although the training converges and does not overfit. The synthetic experiments
also show disagreement between the validation measure (negative log­likelihood) and the
ability to identify the true states. These findings are mainly attributed to to the challenge of
making a good initialization for the training of the model and to the generation of synthetic
data, which are both areas where further work can be done. Based on the findings from
the synthetic data, it is not surprising that the observational experiments on mouse sleep
data do not reveal any biological insights. Suggestions for alternative choices of model
parameters that are expected to better the performance on sleep data are given. By
addressing the presented challenges, the HMM­MAR with stochastic gradient descent is
still expected to have the potential for discovering and exploring new sleep patterns.

Introduction
Around one third of a human life is spent sleeping, and although studied for more than
2000 years, the underlying mechanisms of sleep are far from fully understood. Sleep is
essential for understanding basic human functionality, providing insights into both health
(such as memory and restitution) and sickness (such as sleep disorders and recovery).
The ongoing exploration of underlying patterns in sleep is therefore an important area
within sleep research. In the past years, modern data­driven methods have provided
powerful tools for the continuous exploration of neurological patterns, and the models
have also made their way into sleep research. Within sleep research, the main focus of
data­driven methods is prediction of established sleep stages from data, and this area is
well­explored. However, the use of data­driven methods to explore unknown patterns is
limited. This project focuses on the exploratory approach, aiming to implement a data­
driven framework which can be used to investigate sub structures of currently known sleep
patterns. The project builds upon existing methods for pattern exploration in other areas of
neuroscience, specifically hidden Markov models with continuous time­dependent emis­
sions. Hidden Markov models provide an easily­interpretable framework for exploring
underlying structures in time series data, providing a strong tool for grouping observa­
tions based on similarity. For sleep, the observations can consist of brain and muscle
activity, which is currently used to identify known sleep patterns.
The contribution of this thesis to the exploration of sleep patterns is using hidden Markov
models with time dependent emissions, allowing the models to take the time dependency
of sleep cycles into account.
1.1
Previous work and motivation
The review in [1] from 2022 provides an overview of representations of temporal sleep dy­
namics. The representations are ranged on a scale from human­defined representations
(such as standardized sleep stage labeling) to machine­learned representations (e.g. us­
ing clusters to explore model­identified patterns in the data). Somewhere in­between is
the automation of sleep stage labeling, i.e. using data­driven methods to label the sleep
stages in agreement with current human­defined representations. Both supervised and
unsupervised data­driven approaches are presented, including different clustering ap­
proaches. This review, along with other papers such as [2], supports the potential of using
data­driven methods for exploration of new sleep patterns. What is important ­ and of­
ten challenging ­ is to maintain clinical interpretability when making advanced data­driven
analyses.
The main inspiration for this project is a paper by Stevner et al. from 2019 [3], in which a
hidden Markov model with continuous emission models is used to explore sub patterns in
human sleep (fMRI data). In [1], the paper is cited and the method is categorized as ’data
driven clustering’. Although not entirely misleading, hidden Markov models are not clas­
sically described as a clustering method; they are probabilistic models, that can indeed
be used for grouping data but also have many other applications. The review does not
name hidden Markov models specifically as a method for representing sleep dynamics,
indicating that the use of hidden Markov models within sleep research is limited.
The results of Stevner et al. are promising, identifying consistent subpatterns across
subjects. The identified subpatterns also agree with established sleep stages and sleep
cycles. These are powerful insights, creating many new questions, however an examina­
tion of the +200 citations showed that no replication of the study was made. Furthermore,

none of the citing papers used any hidden Markov model framework for exploring sleep
stages in any way (although the framework is used for exploration of other neurological
patterns, such as difference between rest and task). This leaves room for not only a repli­
cation, but also an expansion of the hidden Markov model framework when used on sleep
data.
The main model choice of a hidden Markov model is the choice of emission models.
Stevner et al. [3] uses a hidden Markov model with Gaussian emissions. This works well
for data with spatial dependencies, but it does not take the possible time dependencies
within sleep into account. It is sensible to introduce a time dependent emission function
to incorporate this. A classic choice is to use an autoregression, i.e. a Gaussian where
the mean is a weighted sum over previous observations. Autoregressive emissions was
suggested already by Rabiner in 1989 [4]. A full implementation of a hidden Markov model
with (multivariate) autoregressive emissions (a so­called HMM­MAR) was released by Vi­
daurre et al. in 2015 [5] to investigate neurological signals in rest and task. The model
could identify brain states related to rest and task from recordings of brain activity, sup­
porting the usefulness of using hidden Markov models with time dependent emissions
when exploring neurological data, such as sleep data.
The implementation provided by Vidaurre et al. is in Matlab and uses variational inference
for tuning the model parameters. Whereas variational inference is a classical choice for
hidden Markov models, using direct numerical optimization (especially when implemented
well in Python) has potential in terms of efficiency and being able to handle larger amounts
of data. The literature search reveals no available HMM­MAR implementation with direct
numerical optimization. The choice was therefore to implement a HMM­MAR in Python,
using stochastic gradient descent for optimization. The starting point of the project im­
plementation was the Python code available from [6], providing a hidden Markov model
framework with continuous (but not time dependent) emission models and direct numeri­
cal optimization.
The project uses both synthetical and observational data. The synthetical data is gener­
ated from hidden Markov models and is used to test general functionality of the framework.
The observational data is sleep data from mice. Historically, mice models (and other an­
imal models) have played a crucial role in the research of sleep and sleep disorders [7],
making controlled experiments, interventions and examinations possible. Although the
sleep patterns of mice and humans are not directly translatable, there are many com­
parable features, making it possible to translate the mechanisms such as overall sleep
cycles and transition between sleep stages [8]. Using data from mice also allows for
longer recording time than what is usually possible for humans, which is beneficial as it
increases the likelihood of capturing sleep patterns. Although the data used in this project
is mice data, the model framework is independent of the data type and could be used to
investigate observations from human sleep as well.

1.2
Research questions
The following research questions are explored in the project (where HMM­MAR denotes
the hidden Markov model with autoregressive emissions):
1. What is the best possible performance of a HMM­MAR, and how do changes in
model parameters and data affect the performance?
• How well does a HMM­MAR recover states in data that was generated from
itself?
• How do changes in the time dependency structures in data affect the perfor­
mance?
• How do changes in model parameters, such as number of states, affect the
performance?
2. How does a trained HMM­MAR perform when optimized using stochastic gradient
descent?
• How can the parameters of an HMM­MAR be initialized before training, and
what is the reproducibility of the initialization?
• How does the learning rate affect the training?
• Does the training error correlate with how well the model recovers states?
• What are the challenges of working with synthetic data from HMM­MARs, and
how can they be accommodated?
3. What biological insights can be gained from using a 3­state HMM­MAR on mouse
sleep data?
• What considerations should be made in determining HMM­MAR model param­
eters when modeling mouse sleep data?
• What is the agreement between the established sleep stages and the states
discovered by the HMM­MAR?
The thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 describes the basic theory of sleep in mice,
giving the reader a foundational understanding of measuring sleep in mice and labeling
sleep stages, and also includes a description of the available mouse sleep data. Chap­
ter 3 covers the fundamental theory of hidden Markov models, including a more detailed
description of the model used for exploring sleep. Chapter 4 describes implementation
details, including numerical stability, sampling from hidden Markov models and initializ­
ing hidden Markov models for training. Chapter 5 contains the experiments and results
on synthetic data. Chapter 6 contains the experiments and results on the observational
(mouse sleep) data. Chapter 7 discusses methods and results, followed by suggestions
for further work and the conclusion.

2
Mouse Sleep Data
This chapter introduces the observational data used in the project. The data consists of
recordings of muscle and brain activity in mice during phases of sleep and wake. The
recordings stem from multiple independent experiments done by different people at Kor­
num Lab (University of Copenhagen). The recordings were assembled to a collection by
Laura Rose for her Ph.D thesis [9]. The chapter introduces the fundamental knowledge of
mouse sleep, including the established categorization of sleep stages based on record­
ings of brain and muscle activity. The laboratory setup for measuring brain and muscle
activity in mice is described, followed by a detailed description of the data available for
the project.
2.1
About mouse sleep
Mouse sleep is categorized into three sleep stages: Wake, REM sleep and non­REM
sleep (NREM). The sleep stages are characterized by different brain and muscle activity,
which is measured using an electroencephalogram (EEG) and electromyography (EMG),
respectively. In EEG, the electrical signals are separated into alpha, beta, theta and
gamma waves based on their frequencies, as depicted in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Brain wave frequencies
Overview of names and frequencies of brain waves measured by EEG.
When the mouse is awake, the EEG signal is characterized by mixed frequencies and
low amplitude, and the muscle activity is clearly present in the EMG, as the mouse moves
around. REM sleep is characterized by regular theta waves in the EEG and low muscle
activity with occasional twitches. As theta waves can also occur in wake, the EMG signal
is useful to distinguish between REM and wake. NREM is characterized by delta waves
and low muscle activity, and is thus usually the most easy to identify [10]. Figure 2.2
shows an example of an EEG and an EMG recording for each of the three sleep stages.
2.2
Experimental setup
The experimental setup for recording EEG and EMG can differ between laboratories,
however the described setup is commonly used (with a variation of number of electrodes)
and was also used to obtain the data used in this project. The mouse undergoes a surgery
where two EEG recording electrodes are implanted on top of the skull, one on the right side
and one one the left, measuring brain activity. An EMG recording electrode is implanted in
the mouse’s neck muscle, measuring the muscle activity. The electrodes are connected to

2.3
Kornum Lab data
As mentioned in the beginning of the chapter, the project data originates from experi­
ments done in Kornum Lab at University of Copenhagen. The data stems from multiple
experiments done by different people over a number of years. The experiments were not
done with this or any other data science project in mind. The different recordings were
collected by Laura Rose for her Ph.D thesis [9].
In an experiment, testing e.g. a new drug on a mouse, a reference mouse is needed to
be able to measure the effect of the drug. The reference mouse gets no drug injection
but is simply measured as is for comparison. A reference mouse is also called a baseline
mouse. All mice in the data set are baseline mice. Hence, nothing was done to affect the
sleeping patterns of the mice in the data set, and they are expected to have regular sleep
patterns.
The available data consists of 55 mouse recordings. Most recordings are of different
mice, but some mice are represented twice with different recordings. Only recordings
that lasted for 24 hours were kept, decreasing the number of recordings to 47. During
recording, the EEG and EMG measures were made every 4 seconds with a sampling rate
of 1600 (i.e. 400 Hz), resulting in 21600*1600 measurements for each mouse. Each 4­
second measure (which we shall denote a measurement epoch) was manually scored as
Wake, REM, NREM or ’artefact’ (outlier/unidentifiable). Labeling noise (i.e. difference in
manual labeling between both labs and individuals) is a well­known challenge, introducing
noise in scorings made by different people. To decrease the amount of noise introduced
in the data, the choice was to only use recordings scored by the same person. With these
restrictions, 17 recordings were available. However, due to time limits, experiments ended
up only being made based on just one recording of of the available 17. This recording
was chosen based on notes from the scorer about the noise in the EMG and EEG signals,
picking one that was considered as noise­free as possible.

Since the aim is to explore patterns within sleep, the data is limited to include the 12 hours
of light phase. Including the dark phase would create an imbalance in the data with most
measurements being while the mouse is awake, possibly leading the model to explore
patterns within wake instead of patterns within sleep. A hypnogram of the recording is

This chapter describes the basics of hidden Markov models, including model specifica­

3.1
Introduction to hidden Markov models
Consider a scenario, where T observations X = {x1, x2, . . . , xT } are generated by some
system. You are pretty sure that the system generating these observations is separated
into different states. You are also certain that the system behaves differently across states,
meaning that an observation generated while the system was in state i is different from
an observation generated while the system was in state j. The weather is an intuitive
example: A higher temperature is expected when it is sunny, and a lower temperature
is expected when it is cloudy. Looking at a thermometer, the temperature (observation)
therefore depends on the weather (state), and from the temperature, you would be able
to make a good guess about whether it is sunny or cloudy outside. For the weather, of
course, it is usually possible to look outside the window to confirm the state but in many
cases, the states cannot be observed. They are hidden. In these cases, where only
observations are available but some underlying system with states is expected, hidden
Markov models (HMMs) provide a strong tool for exploring the underlying system [4].
3.1.1
The hidden Markov model framework
The model depicted in Figure 3.1 shows a hidden Markov model unfolded over time.
At each time point t, there is an observation xt and a state zt. The observation can
be either continuous or discrete. The state can take any value in the state space, i.e.
zt ∈{S1, S2, . . . , ..., SK}, however for simplified notation, we use zt ∈{1, 2, . . . , ..., K}
instead, such that zt = k indicates that the system was in state k at time t.
In Figure 3.1, the red arrow from a state zt to an observation xt indicates the conditional
probability of seeing that observation given the state,
p(xt|zt = k, ϕ(k)).
(3.1)
This probability density is called the emission model of state k and ϕ(k) is the model
parameters. The emission probability thus describes how likely it is to the value that was
observed if the system was in state k when the observation was made. We only consider
homogeneous hidden Markov models, meaning that the emission models are the same
model type across states but have varying parameter values, with ϕ = {ϕ(1), ϕ(2), . . . , ϕ(K)}.

3.2
The three problems of hidden Markov models
Denote a sequence of observations X = {x1, x2, . . . , xT } and a sequence of states Z =
{z1, z2, . . . , zT }. The general formulation of the three questions about the weather system
are (as presented in [4]):
1. Problem 1: How do we efficiently compute p(X|θ), the probability of the observation
sequence, given the model?
2. Problem 2: Given the observations X, how do we choose a corresponding state
sequence Z which is optimal in some meaningful sense (i.e. best ”explains” the
observations?)
3. Problem 3: How do we adjust the model parameters θ = {π, A, ϕ} to maximize
p(X|θ)?
All three problems are equally important and necessary for working with hidden Markov
models on an applied level. In Problem 1, p(X|θ) provides a measure of comparison
which can be used to compare models with different model parameters. This makes
it possible to answer the question: Which of these models best describe the observed
data? Without the answer to Problem 1, there is no way of knowing if a model describes
the data well.
Problem 2 makes the exploration of underlying patterns possible. As the states are not
observed, they need to be inferred in some way ­ but how do we choose the best expla­
nation? Without the answer to Problem 2, there is simply no insights into the states. We
might know that we have a really good model but without being able to infer the states,
the hidden information stays hidden.
Problem 3 is vital to be able to develop hidden Markov models from data at all; if model
parameters cannot be determined from data, the model is useless. The answers to the
three problems are covered in the following subsections.

3.2.3
Optimizing model parameters
Problem 3 is: How do we adjust the model parameters θ = {π, A, ϕ} to maximize
p(X|θ)? In other words: How do we choose the best possible model parameters to fit
the observed data? In terms of the weather model, this corresponds to asking: How do
we best estimate the system dynamics of the weather given an observation sequence of
temperatures?
There is, unfortunately, no analytical solution to this problem, and instead, numerical op­
timization techniques must be considered. Problem 3 is classically solved by the iterative
Expectation­Maximization algorithm (EM algorithm), which is also referred to as the
Baum­Welch algorithm when working with hidden Markov models. Overall, the EM al­
gorithm works as follows [12]:
1. Choose initial values θ0
2. Expectation step Compute L = p(Z|X, θ0)
3. Maximize step Maximize L w.r.t. θ.
14
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
==PAGE:25==
In each iteration, the θ estimates from the Maximization step is used as the new estimates
in the next Expectation step, and the Expectation­Maximization steps are repeated until
the change in updates of θ is smaller than some chosen threshold. Each iteration of the
EM algorithm is guaranteed to improve the estimates, and the algorithm is guaranteed
to converge to a local maximum. There is, however, no guarantee for reaching a global
maximum (or any way of knowing if it was the case). The EM algorithm is an intuitive
choice for optimizing hidden Markov models, as it is specifically designed to work on the
standard likelihood and provides stable and interpretable updates. The EM algorithm can,
however, be computationally expensive, because it typically requires working on all data
at the same time in each iteration. Parallelizing the algorithm is also challenging, making
it hard to optimize the implementation.
A hidden Markov model can also be fitted by using direct numerical optimization, e.g.
stochastic gradient descent (SGD), to optimize the probability p(X|θ). With SGD, the
gradient of p(X|θ) is computed w.r.t all the optimizable parameters θ, and the parame­
ters are then updated in the direction of the gradient. The size of the update is determined
by the learning rate, which can be thought of as the ’size of the step’ that is taken in the
direction of the gradient. The learning rate is a hyper parameter, meaning that it is not
optimized during training, so it has to be chosen beforehand. The direct optimization up­
dates the parameters such that p(X|θ) is changed in the direction of the largest possible
increase in the likelihood. In this way, the SGD moves the parameters towards a local
maximum, however it is not guaranteed to find one. If the learning rate is large, the SGD
might ’overshoot’ the local maximum that it is moving towards, resulting in longer conver­
gence time. A small learning rate can also result in longer convergence time, since a lot
of steps are needed to get to the maximum. The optimization runs over a number of steps
called epochs until convergence. For Gaussian mixture models (which can be seen as a
special case of hidden Markov models), direct numerical optimization methods such as
SGD are found to outperform the EM algorithm on larger data sets [13]. Based on this,
and to explore the potential of a more efficient fitting process, this project implements a
direct numerical optimization approach, specifically SGD, instead of the traditional choice
of the EM algorithm for solving Problem 3. It is important to emphasize that the EM algo­
rithm is generally a good choice for the optimization of hidden Markov model parameters,
and expansions of the EM algorithm to work on large data sets is also documented in
the literature. New challenges rise when using the SGD, such as the mentioned choice of
learning rate and problems with exploding or vanishing gradients. However, direct numer­
ical optimization for hidden Markov models has potential for providing efficient solutions
in terms of speed and memory use for larger data sets, and it is therefore valuable to
explore.

4.1.1
Transformation to log domain
As shown in Equation 3.13 and 3.22, both the forward algorithm and the Viterbi algorithm
multiply probabilities a number of times proportional to the length of the observation se­
quence T. As the sequence increases, this multiplication becomes prone to underflow,
because probabilities are less than 1 and therefore always decrease when multiplied.
Therefore, both the implementation of the forward algorithm and the Viterbi algorithm are
transformed to the log domain, where the multiplications are transformed to sums. This
change improves the numerical stability of the implementation. The implication of the
change is that the implementation of the emission probabilities return log probabilities as
well. It also means that the training measure is the log likelihood.
4.1.2
Training measure
As described, we search for the maximum likelihood when optimizing parameters. How­
ever, the SGD implementation in Pytorch is made for minimizing training errors (e.g. for
neural networks), and the training therefore has to be a minimization problem. This is

4.1.3
Covariance decomposition and training updates
During training, the positive definiteness of the covariance matrices has to be ensured. A
positive definite matrix M can be decomposed into a matrix product using the Cholesky
decomposition, M = LL∗, where L is a lower triangular matrix with positive diagonal,
and L∗is the complex conjugate of L. For real numbers, L∗= LT . Ideally, the training
would update L and then construct the covariance matrix, however there is no easy fix
for constraining the update to produce a lower triangular matrix with a positive diagonal.
Instead, the following approach is used:
• A full matrix C ∈R is randomly initialized with ci,j ∼N(0, σ2).
• L is the lower triangular matrix of C with diag(L) = exp(diag(C)) to ensure diag(L) >
0. During training, both C and L will be updated, meaning that L is not guaranteed
to be lower triangular with positive diagonal after training. When L is used, it is re­
defined as described based on C. This means that it is not the direct updates on
L but the updates in C that define the covariance matrix. This works, because the
gradients of the two matrices are connected.
• Whenever the covariance is needed, it is constructed as LLT (or L is used directly,
when a method can use the decomposition instead of the full covariance).
Note that the Cholesky decomposition becomes numerically unstable for higher dimen­
sional data. For higher­dimensional data, another way of handling the covariance matrix
should therefore be implemented.

Transition matrix
The transition matrix has to fulfill the criteria that ∑K

distribution across states. However, as with the covariance matrix, this constraint is not
respected during training updates. Instead, a matrix Afull was initialized and updated dur­

4.1.5
Details on loading mouse sleep data
The following is a quite technical note on the specification of ZT time intervals. The data
loading implementation was made by Javier Ciudad for deep learning models [14]. This
is mentioned, because the implementation was not made for extracting long sequences
within specified ZT times. In the data loading, a specification of the ZT interval is possible,
e.g. from ZT0 to ZT12 (the light phase). The data loading allows specification of the
sequence length in the following way: A center epoch is chosen within the specified ZT
interval, and the number of epochs to be included right and left of the center epoch can
be specified. This means that although the center epoch is guaranteed to be within the
specified time intervals, the epochs at the ends of the sequence might go out of bounds.
This is not a problem for shorter sequences: If the center epoch is randomly chosen
close to the end of the specified ZT interval, only a few epochs from outside the interval
are included. However, for longer sequences, this is important to be aware of. If the
sequence length is specified to 12 hours, in the hope of covering the whole light phase,
and the center epoch is chosen at the end of the light phase, almost half of the data is
going to be from the dark phase. This means that, using this implementation for data
loading, it was necessary to use shorter sequence lengths than what was first anticipated
to ensure that most of the data was indeed from the light phase.

4.2
Generating synthetic data
In most applied cases, there are no true labels available for the system that the hidden
Markov model is used to model. This means that it can be hard to figure out how well the
states decoded by the model actually agree with the underlying system. It also makes it
hard to explore the general functionality of hidden Markov models or look into e.g. initial­
ization strategies. Since the exploration of the HMM­MAR framework is generally limited,
and even more with the direct numerical optimization, it is of interest to examine the func­
tionality of the framework. Synthetic data makes this possible. The idea is to create a
hidden Markov model and, instead of fitting it to data, use it to sample a sequence of both
observations and states. The general formula for sampling from a hidden Markov model
is as follows [11]:
• Sample the first state z0 from the initial distribution π.
• Sample the observation x0 from the emission probability density for state k = z0.
• Transition to the next state z1 using the transition probability density for state k = z0.
• Sample the observation x1 from the emission probability density for state k = z1.
• Repeat the two last steps, until the desired number of samples is reached.
This results in both an observation sequence and the state sequence that generated the
observations. This means that, if training a new model on the generated synthetic data
and decoding the states, the trained model can be compared to the true model by compar­
ing the decoded states with the true states. At first glance, this might look like complicating
things: If the true model is available, why not just compare the parameters of the true and
the trained model? However, the parameters in the trained model is not guaranteed to
look like the true model; in fact, permutations within parameters and across states are very
likely. Sampling methods for both the HMM­Gaussian and the HMM­MAR are described
in the following.

4.3
Quality of decoding: Normalized mutual information
Generating synthetic data creates a powerful position to evaluate the hidden Markov
model, as the decoded states can be compared to the true states. First of all, this pro­
vides a strong tool for implementation debugging. Furthermore, it enables exploration
of the best possible scenario of decoding using so­called oracle experiments, where a
model decodes states from observations that were sampled from the same model. This
answers the question: If the best model possible is obtained with training, how well does
the decoding work? By changing model parameters and sample choices, the decoding
dependency on these variables can be explored, as will be done in section 5.1.
To compare the true states and the decoded states, a measure of the agreement be­
tween the two sequences is needed. However, two state sequences cannot simply be
directly compared, as there might be permutations of the states during training: The true

measure of 100% because the patterns of the states are the exact same, although the
naming of the states are different. Such a measure is the normalized mutual information
criteria (NMI). It is a similarity measure between two sequences, invariant to permutations
in the states [16].

4.4
Initialization of the hidden Markov model
It is a well­known property of hidden Markov models that parameter estimation is highly
dependent on the initialization of the parameters, as a bad initialization might get the opti­
mization stuck around a local extremum. Although the initialization is handled in practice
in most research involving hidden Markov models, there are no policies on how to choose
the initialization, and only limited research on the effect of initialization. In [17], it is shown
(for discrete hidden Markov models) that the Euclidean distance between initialized pa­
rameters and the true parameters is strongly correlated with the distance between the
trained parameters and the true parameters, meaning that a worse initialization leads to
a worse model estimation. This underlines that initialization is important to consider, as
two models trained on the exact same data are likely to provide very different results de­
pending on their initialization. A bad initialization might result in longer convergence time,
less precise models and, as a result, less informative decoding.
In most practical cases, the true parameters that governed the data are unknown, meaning
that it cannot be directly measured how close the initialized parameters and the true pa­
rameters are. However, different initialization methods have been shown to give better re­
sults than a random initialization. In [18], eight different initialization methods are explored
for HMM­Gaussians, including variations of random initialization, clustering methods as k­
means clustering and k­medoids clustering, and Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The
experiments are made for initializations of the EM­algorithm, but the results hold for direct
numerical optimization of the likelihood, such as SGD. The initialization methods are eval­
uated on 1) how well they find the global maximum, 2) how many iterations are needed,
and 3) how well the decoded states correspond to the true states. The k­means clus­
tering initialization performs best across all measures, only with the k­medoids clustering
performing a bit better for lower data dimensions for decoding. The k­means clustering is
chosen as initialization method in this project.
4.4.1
Initialization using k­means clustering
K­means clustering groups the data into k clusters, where all observations are in the clus­
ter with the mean that is closest to the cluster centroid using the following steps (adapted
from [19]):
1. Select an initial partition with K clusters. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until observations
stay in the same cluster.
2. Generate a new cluster partition by assigning each observation to its closest cluster
center.
3. Compute new clusters.

25
==PAGE:36==
By setting the number of clusters equal to the number of states in the model, it is possible
to initialize each of the states based on observations that are in at least some aspects
similar. K­means clustering is only guaranteed to converge to a local optimum, meaning
that the initial partition in Step 1 influences the final clustering [19]. In [18], this is handled
by running k­means clustering 10 times on the data and choosing the repetetion with the
lowest within­state sum of squares, i.e. the overall lowest distance between observations
and their cluster center.
For Gaussian emission models, the usefulness of the k­means clustering as initialization
is documented, and the data can be clustered without any preprocessing. The k­means
clustering is also chosen for the HMM­MAR, as it still has a Gaussian structure. However,
the clustering should incorporate the time dependency that the emission seeks to model.
Therefore, when initializing a HMM­MAR, a feature matrix F ∈R(T−p)×d is constructed
from the observations s.t. xt is represented as the p previous observations in the feature
matrix. The k­means clustering is made on F , making it possible to cluster observations
on the temporal patterns of their predecessors.
When having clustered the data, each of the model parameters must be initialized in some
way. In the transition matrix, each element aij is initialized as the number of transitions
from cluster i to cluster j, divided by the number of observations in cluster i, resulting in
an initialized transition matrix where the rows sum to 1. For a Gaussian emission model,
each µ(k) is initialized as the mean vector of the kth cluster. For a MAR emission model,
an auto­regressive model is estimated on each of the clusters with the same order as for
the emission, and the lag matrices W (k)
l
are initialized with the estimated parameters.
Covariance matrices for each state are initialized with elements following a normal dis­
tribution with some standard deviation σ ­ they could also be initialized from data for an
even better initialization, however this was not implemented. If multiple sequences are
available, and there is an expectation that the start of the sequence has a big effect on the
development of the sequence, the initial probability could be initialized from the k­means­
clustering by counting the number of times a sequence starts in a cluster. However, in
this project, the initial probability is simply initialized with element drawn from a standard
normal. In this way, the parameters are initialized in a more optimal way than a random
initialization.

Synthetic Experiments
This chapter covers the synthetic experiments that were made to validate the models
(and the implementation). The first section covers the oracle experiments used to explore
how well the HMM­normMAR model recovers states from data that was generated from
itself, and how the recovery depends on different changeable parameters, e.g. the num­
ber of states and the order. The second section explores the reproducibility of k­means
clustering initializations for HMM­Gaussians and HMM­MARs, and furthermore looks into
the effect of initialization when training a HMM­MAR. The third section presents an ex­
periment exploring the time dependency in the synthetic data. The fourth section covers
experiments for the choice of learning rate. It is important to keep in mind that these ex­
periments are made on synthetic data that does not mimic EEG or EMG. This means that
no exact numbers can be meaningfully transferred from the synthetic experiments to the
use of HMM­MARs on sleep data, however the general tendencies for the model types
can be explored.
5.1
Oracle experiments: Best possible recovery of states
This section covers the oracle experiments. In each experiment, samples are generated
by a model, and the states are then decoded by the same model. This experiment type
gives an empirical upper bound for what can be expected when working with this kind of
model. Note that, as mentioned, the model used for generating the synthetic data is a
HMM­normMAR, and since the same model is used for decoding, the results are techni­
cally not for a HMM­MAR. It is however expected that the tendencies are transferable to
the HMM­MAR.
The exploration is split into two parts: Parameter choices related to sampling or states;
and parameter choices for the emissions. The following sampling and state related pa­
rameters are explored:
1. The data dimension, d ∈{1, 2, . . . , 6}
2. The time stayed in a state during sampling before a transition is possible, tstay ∈
{1, 5, 10, 20, 50}
3. The number of states in the model, K ∈{2, 3, . . . , 9}
The following emission model parameters are explored:
1. The order of the MAR, p ∈{5, 10, 15}
2. The standard deviation for the distribution of the covariance matrix Σ(k) ∼N(0, σ2),
σ ∈{0.1, 1, 2}
3. The distance between the scalar separating states, distc ∈{0.01, 1, 2, 3, 4}
The HMM­normMAR is initialized with standard parameters as described in section 4.5. In
each experiment, one parameter is varied, such that when exploring the effect of e.g. the
data dimension on the decoding, all parameters are chosen form the standard parameters
except the data dimension, which is varied across the values listed above. For each
variable value, 10 models are initialized, and 500 samples are drawn from each model.
The 500 samples are decoded with the Viterbi algorithm using the same model they were

Note that although the model parameters are the same, the emission model parameters
W (k)

models. Averaging over 10 different models ensures not being restricted to the results

5.2.2
Effect of initialization on trained models
The effect of the k­means­cluster initialization on training is tested as follows: 5000 training
samples and 1000 validation samples are drawn from a HMM­normMAR. For 10 repeti­
tions, a k­means initialized HMM­MAR and a randomly initialized HMM­MAR are trained
on the training data and evaluated on the validation data. The states are decoded for the
validation data. The idea of the experiment is to look into how well the models can decode
the states of unseen data, depending on whether the model was actively initialized or ran­
domly initialized. The 10 repetitions of different initializations aims to take into account
what was previously discovered: The initializations of the MAR­models on the same data
can differ a lot. The hope is that on average, the k­means initialization works better than
the random initialization.
Figure 5.5 shows the mean negative log­likelihood development over epochs for the k­
means­initialized model and the random model, respectively, with a uncertainty bound
of +/­ 1 standard deviation. For both training and validation, the k­means initialization
results in a lower starting negative log­likelihood than the random initialization, indicating
that the initialized model already fit the data better than the randomly initialized model
before training. In both training and validation, the k­means initialization has a generally
lower mean likelihood across repetitions. Furthermore, the k­means initialization has the
narrowest uncertainty bound, indicating that the k­means initialization provides a more
stable initialization across repetitions.
For both initializations, the training likelihood steeply decreases within the first epoch.
After the first epoch, the training likelihood for the k­means initialized model does not
improve, whereas the training likelihood for the randomly initialized model improves until
around the 4th epoch. This very fast convergence is unusual and could indicate that the

in an increase in the validation likelihood in the later epochs. This is not the case, and we
are left with the conclusion that the model parameters quite quickly reach a local minimum.

and the NMI was calculated. The mean NMI for all experiments for each learning rate was
calculated. This is shown in Figure 5.9. Here, it is clearly shown that the learning rate of

is almost 0.8, which was previously seen to be the best possible recovery. Using 0.1 as
learning rate, which was done in all previous experiments, generally results in a quite low
NMI.
When deciding on hyper parameters such as the learning rate, the usual procedure is
to have a validation measure (i.e. a measure that explains how well the model does on
unseen data), and the parameter combination that results in the best validation measure
is chosen. In this case, an intuitive validation measure is the validation likelihood in the
last epoch (i.e. the negative log­likelihood of the validation data in the last epoch). There­
fore, we look into the agreement between this validation measure and the mean NMI. We
look at the mean NMI across initializations but individually for each data set. The results
are shown in Figure 5.10. Ideally, the points would be scattered around a decreasing
line such that the highest NMI value corresponded to the lowest negative log­likelihood.
Unfortunately, this is not the case. With these runs, the best model is identifiable on the
lowest negative log­likelihood, however for a log­likelihood around 6000, which is not so
far away from the lowest, the NMI ranges from 0 to above 0.8. This lack of agreement
between the validation measure and the actual model performance creates a huge chal­

Mouse Experiments
This chapter contains an overview of the experiments on the mouse data. The number
of experiments are limited due to the project’s time restrictions; large fluctuation in model
performance (as shown in the synthetic experiments); and long experiment run times for
the models trained on mouse data. Some experiments, however, were made, and the
results are reported in the following.
The longer a continuous sequence that can be used as an input for a hidden Markov
model the better, as the sequence patterns are more likely to be clear to the model. For
the mouse data, this points to ultimately using the whole light phase as one sequence (i.e.
all data is in one batch during training). This, however, proved difficult for multiple reasons.
First, as described in section 4.1, the data loader did not allow specification of data within
a specific interval, making it impossible to ensure that the data was indeed covering the
light phase when loaded as one sequence. Secondly, the run time for training a model on
10800*4*64 measures was incredibly high, making experimental variations costly. And
thirdly, it turned out that sequences exceeding around 200 measurement epochs (i.e.
around a 13 minute recording) resulted in exploding gradients; the gradients became so
big that they overflowed, and the parameters were updated with NaNs. This is supposed
to be due to many updates of the gradients, increasing with the sequence length, however
it was not expected. It was attempted fixed by gradient clipping (i.e. restricting the gradient
to a certain size), however this did not work. At this time, a decision was taken to do
experiments on smaller sequences instead, and on a smaller data set. Specifically, data
was chosen from within ZT0­ZT1. It was possible to train on the whole recording, however
runtime limitations made it infeasible to make multiple experiments on so much data, due
to time limitations of the project.
The dataloader chooses sequences specified on the number of measurement epochs
right and left of the center epoch. The chosen numbers of side sequences are 10, 50 and
100, resulting in sequences of 21, 101 and 201 measurement epochs. This corresponds
to sequences of around 1.5, 7 and 13 minutes. The sequences are chosen such that they
do not overlap, resulting in the sequence length also determining the number of batches.
For simplicity (and for maximum number of updates for longer sequence lengths), each
sequence simply works as a batch within training. This means that models with shorter
sequences have more parameter updates within each training epoch than models with
longer sequences.
Two trainings ran for each sequence length; one training with learning rate 0.01 and one
with learning rate 0.1. This is chosen, as the learning rate had a significant effect on the
decoding in the synthetic experiments. Each training ran for 35 epochs and took around
3 hours. The increase in sequence length decreased the run time a bit.

learning rate 0.01) and Figure 6.2 (for learning rate 0.1). There is a clear difference be­

decreasing curves that converge within the 35 epochs. The higher learning rate results

converging as expected. For the sequence of 201, the training has the lowest likelihood

7
Discussion
The aim for this project was to implement a HMM­MAR framework in Python, using di­
rect numerical optimization (SGD) for training, and applying the implementation to mouse
sleep data. The synthetic experiments show that training is possible, but there is a lot
of uncertainty and challenges. The observational experiments are limited and show that
the HMM­MAR does not find the expected patterns. This chapter includes the discus­
sion of the findings by highlighting challenges from the results and suggesting alternate
approaches. The chapter also includes a perspective on what the next steps for HMM­
MARs used on sleep data can be. The final section of the chapter is the conclusion of the
project.
7.1
Generating synthetic data
This section describes considerations about the synthetic data. The synthetic data is sam­
pled from a HMM­normMAR instead of a HMM­MAR to take care of stationarity issues.
This fix seems like a good idea at first: It results in non­exploding samples with time de­
pendency. However, the oracle experiments show that the distinction variable c, which
is introduced to be able to investigate the impact of having very different and very similar
states, turns out to have no effect. Additionally, changing σ of the distribution that the
covariance was initialized from had a huge impact. Together, these results indicate that
samples from the HMM­normMAR are primarily defined by the state covariances which
are not time dependent. If the normalization means that the time dependency becomes
obsolete, making the states identifiable solely on their covariances, the data is not fit for
exploring the use of the HMM­MAR; it would basically be equivalent with data from a zero­
mean Gaussian. When training both a HMM­MAR and a HMM­Gaussian on the data, it is
however shown that there is indeed time dependency present, although the HMM­MAR
is not able to reach the same level of decoding as the HMM­normMAR.
The oracle experiments with the emission parameters also raise another the concern: If
variations in c do not change the ability to decode, it generally means that the distinction
variable does not work. Distinct states is a key assumption for hidden Markov models
to work well, and it turns out that the normalization removes distinction between states
made by the distinction variable. This does not mean that a distinct state HMM­normMAR
is never initialized, but merely that it is dependent on the randomness in the initialization
of the W (k)
l
s. The result is, as shown with the initialization experiments, that sometimes
a run shows the expected results, and sometimes the HMM­MAR is not able to fit any
patterns, because the states are not distinct. The fact that the distinction of the states
is not controllable from the initialization of the HMM­normMAR was discovered quite late
and had until then caused confusion, with a lot of the uncertainty in results being con­
tributed to possible implementation errors. Although it serves the purpose, this generation
of synthetic data is far from ideal, and choosing another approach is recommended for
someone doing similar experiments. Two suggestions for generating synthetic data for
testing HMM­MAR are presented in the following.
The first suggestion builds on the criteria of stationarity described in subsection 4.2.1.
The matrix indicating stationarity, C must have eigenvalues within the unit circle. This
can be obtained by constructing C using diagonalization, such that C = P DP −1 where
D is a diagonal matrix with the eigenvalues of C. In this way, the eigenvalues of C can
be chosen. Although possible to construct, the analytical solution for this becomes quite
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
45
==PAGE:56==
cumbersome, especially when the aim is a dynamical implementation where the data
dimension and order can vary. Instead, the following brute force method for lower values
of d and p is suggested:
1. Construct a matrix Cinit = P DP −1 with the elements of P drawn from a standard
normal and the diagonal D drawn from a uniform distribution (ensuring the correct
eigenvalues).
2. Extract W 1, W 2, . . . , W p from the first block­row of Cinit
3. Construct C with the correct structure using the W ls.
4. Check the eigenvalues of C. If they are not within the unit circle, start over.
For combinations of small d and small p (up to 10 and 10), this method provided a fast
and reliable initialization of a stationary autoregressive process. By initializing the Cinit,
the chance of getting the right eigenvalues increases as opposed to starting from a ran­
domly initialized matrix. With this way of initializing a stationary process, the HMM­MAR
could be sampled from directly, avoiding the HMM­normMAR. This method works for the
combinations of d and p used in this project, however increasing the order and dimen­
sions also increases the time until convergence significantly, and there is no guarantee
of reaching a stationary process. Another problem is ensuring distinct states: Preferably,
the states should have different MAR processes, however this method does not provide
any way of influencing the processes or steering them in any direction. This general prob­
lem of distinct states can be solved by simply adding some ”noise” to the observations
afterwards, based on the states. Thus, a way of generating distinct synthetic data from a
HMM­MAR is: 1) Initialize the different states with different MAR­coefficients obtained by
the brute force method 2) Sample from this HMM­MAR and 3) Add noise afterwards. This
approach, however, limits the possibility of making oracle experiments, as the difference
in the states is no longer coded within the model but added afterwards.
A better but also more complex method is presented by Vidaurre when introducing the
HMM­MAR framework. First, 1/fα noise (or colored noise) is generated with different α­
values for each state; then, oscillations of different frequencies are added to the different
states by manipulating the roots of the characteristic polynomial of each MAR process.
This way of creating synthetic data works well, creating a stationary MAR process and
distinct states. However, as manipulating the roots corresponds to analytically construct­
ing C, this method is also limiting when wanting to vary e.g. data dimension, number of
states etc. as was done in this project. All in all, there is no one­size­fits­all solution on
the sampling, and the used method very much depends on the application.
7.2
Initialization of the HMM­MAR
The results of the initialization experiments show that a k­means initialized HMM­MAR
performs better than a randomly initialized HMM­MAR. However, the experiments also
show that multiple k­means initializations on the same data set can be very different; and
the usual measure for choosing the best initialization (WCSS) does not correlate with the
ability of the trained model to decode the states (measured by the NMI). This is concern­
ing, because what good is it to know that one out of ten initializations is good if that one
initialization cannot be identified? The results also show that although the k­means ini­
tialized HMM­MAR decode the states better, the performance is not anywhere near the
performance of the true model. A part of this should rightfully be credited to the synthetic
data being generated from a HMM­normMAR, meaning that it is unlikely that a HMM­MAR
should be able to capture the dynamics, as the trained parameters cannot catch the nor­
malization. The convergence of both training and validation likelihood is fast, pointing to
46
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
==PAGE:57==
the model converging to a local minimum. The k­means initialization makes it possible to
converge to a better local minimum than a random initialization, but the general perfor­
mance of the trained models are not convincing. For future work with the HMM­MAR, a
further look into how to initialize the lag parameters better is recommended. An obvious
first is, if continuing with the k­means clustering, to also initialize the covariance matri­
ces from data. This is not done due to time limits, and it does not have an influence on
the difference in clustering of observations; however, it is likely to increase the perfor­
mance for the initializations. The initialization problem is also discussed by Vidaurre et al.
[5], who propose different methods: The first one is initializing using a Gaussian Mixture
Model, which itself is initialized by k­means clustering. This approach was also discussed
in [18], however the k­means clustering method performed better in all tests. The second
suggestion, which can be seen as an extension, is to use a MAR Mixture Model. Both
the Gaussian Mixture and the MAR Mixture need previous initialization and optimization,
leading to a longer initialization process than the k­means. The third suggestion, which
was mentioned but not explored by Vidaurre, is doing a number of short HMM­MAR fit­
tings and keeping the best one as initialization for the next round of fittings, and so on. All
of these suggestions are classical suggestions for initializing the parameters for the EM
algorithm, but they should work equally well for the SGD.
A different approach is to combine initialization and training. Instead of fitting many mod­
els and choosing the best one to initialize the next training iteration, an add­on approach
with respect to the lags could be considered: Simply start with a HMM­MAR(1) model and
train until convergence. Use the fitted weights to initialize the first lag in a HMM­MAR(2)
model, and train until convergence. Using this approach, the model does not have to fit
all lags at once. The approach is computationally expensive, since the number of models
to train is equal to the order of the model. A concern is that the fitting of the first lag deter­
mines the local minimum which the training finds, resulting in only a small improvement
from the next iterations.
7.3
Training and evaluation
The optimization choice for this project is stochastic gradient descent, a direct numerical
optimization approach. This was chosen to take advantage of the potential increase in
computational efficiency during training, improving both running time and the data size
that could be handled. Whereas the EM algorithm is tested and documented to work well
for both HMM­Gaussians and HMM­MARs, the SGD has only been tested (and found to
work well) for HMM­Gaussians. To the knowledge of this author, there is no available
studies on the use of the SGD for HMM­MARs. This project finds multiple challenges with
using SGD for fitting HMM­MARs that have to be taken into consideration if continuing
the HMM­MAR exploration.
A first point is that the implementation made for the project does not turn out to be very
efficient, mainly due to many repetitions needed both for initialization and training to get
a good model. Convergence happens relatively fast for both synthetic and observational
data; for synthetic data, there is usually convergence within the first one or two epochs
(with a batch size of 50), and for the mice data, there is convergence within the 35 epochs
for most runs (with the batch size changing based on the sequence length). This conver­
gence points to the models finding a local minimum quite early on and sticking to that.
The exceptions are two mouse experiments with learning rate 0.1; here, somehow the
training fails, resulting in a higher negative log­likelihood than what is seen earlier in the
training. This can be handled by early stopping, however a further exploration of the un­
derlying reasons is also advised. Part of the explanation might be the larger learning rate
overshooting the local minimum that it was aiming for and then getting stuck with a worse
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
47
==PAGE:58==
one.
As shown multiple times throughout the results, both synthetic and observational, there
is no guarantee of a good decoding, although the training has converged. And worse:
It turns out that the NMI and the negative log­likelihood for validation data generally do
not agree with each other. This makes it impossible to choose the best model based on
the validation measure, creating the same issue as was also found in the initialization.
It is puzzling that this happens, as this does not agree very well with the theory: The
lowest negative log­likelihood indicates the model that makes seeing the observations
most likely. This is expected to be the model which is the closest to the true model. The
NMI is measures how well the model identifies the true states. This is also expected to be
the model which is the closest to the true model. The synthetic results show that a model
that cannot identify the states at all (with an NMI of 0) can have the same likelihood value
as a model that identifies the states well (with an NMI above 0.8). How can this happen? If
the model overfits, it would be reflected in the validation data (and overfitting was generally
not found to be a problem in the synthetic data). One explanation is that training results
in similar emissions across the states, resulting in overlapping state distributions. This
would result in a good likelihood but poor state distinguishability. It could happen if the
MAR weights are modeling similar patterns across states. In late hindsight, this could
be a result of the normalization of the synthetic data, this has however not been further
explored.
One could ask: Why not use the NMI as the validation measure when that is what we
want to optimize? However, the NMI can only be calculated when true states (or a proxy
of true states, such as the sleep stages) are known. The main idea for the use of the
HMM­MAR in this project is to use it for exploration by adding more states to the model.
In this case, the decoded states can no longer be compared to the sleep stages, as there
are more states than stages, and hence the NMI cannot be used as a validation measure.
The choice of NMI as the comparison measure is partly based on the idea of comparing
the probability distributions of observations, rather than comparing sequences directly;
however, this was never followed through. This would give a more precise indication of
how the models performed against each other. For direct comparison of sequences, the
Rand index (which is used for comparing clusters) can also be used.
The synthetic learning rate experiments show that the choice of learning rate has a huge
influence on the quality of the trained models, making it important to look into this when
running on observational data. For mouse data, there was also a clear difference be­
tween the training development when using different learning rates, however there was
no clear difference in the decoding afterwards. Alongside the initialization, the hyperpa­
rameters for training such as learning rate, batch size and sequence length (the two latter
balancing each other for the observational data) should be carefully considered when us­
ing this HMM­MAR implementation. The observational results do not show any patterns
for different sequence lengths.
Part of the reason for implementing in Python was to be able to use larger data sets
than what is usually possible when using the EM algorithm. This was successful, as
it was possible to train an a full recording, although the runtime made in infeasible to
further explore with the time limits of the project. The long runtime was partly due to
not taking advantage of the Pytorch environment; the forward algorithm and the Viterbi
algorithm are implemented intuitively, following the structure of the written algorithm. This
means that the code is easily understandable, however it is also time inefficient. Rewriting
the algorithms using efficient torch notation is expected to make a huge difference for
48
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
==PAGE:59==
runtimes. Vidaurre et al. has made an expansion for the Matlab implementation of the
HMM­MAR, where multiple subjects can be taken into account [20], making it possible to
handle larger data sets. If the mouse recordings are reshaped into multiple sequences
that can work as pseudosubjects, this implementation could be used for further exploration
of the mouse sleep patterns.
7.4
Hyper parameter choices
As mentioned in the discussion of the training, hyper parameters such as the learning
rate and the batch size (which depends on the sequence length) are parameters that
have to be considered and chosen for the training. These are however not the only hyper
parameters. For the hidden Markov model framework, the number of states for the model
is a hyper parameter and for the HMM­MAR, the order is also a hyperparameter. These
are parameters of the model that cannot be trained but instead have to be chosen. In
the synthetic experiments, the trained models are chosen to have the same order and
number of states as the true model, meaning that no general exploration of the choice
of hyper parameters is made. It would be interesting to look into if the true order can be
determined by a usual hyper parameter search, i.e. training different models with different
order and seeing which models perform best. Ideally, the trained model with the same
order as the model generating the data performs the best. The number of states is more
tricky to determine, because adding more states allows the model to fit the noise in the
data (as has been documented in [3]), which generally increases the model performance.
Therefore, the number of states has to be chosen using other measures. A suggestion
made by Stevner et al. [3] is to use the median fractional occupancy (MFO) and the
average state life time (ALT). For the median fractional occupancy, the fraction of time that
was spend in each state is calculated, and the median of these fractions is chosen. For
the average state life time, the average sequence length is calculated for each state, and
these are then averaged, resulting in an average over state life times. Calculating these
two measures for a model can give insights into when added states become superfluous,
only fitting the noise and not the actual subpatterns in the data. It would be interesting
to explore if the ALT and the MFO can be used to refind the true number of states on
synthetic data. The ALT and the MFO are also important tools for an exploration of sleep
subpatterns, when more states than 3 states have to be chosen.
For the mouse experiments, the number of states is set to be the number of established
sleep stages to be able to compare the states and the sleep stages; and the order is deter­
mined by the lowest wave frequency of interest (delta wave of 1Hz) and the downsampling
rate (64Hz). To cover a full second with a delta wave, when there are 64 measures per
second, it seems logical to choose an order of 64. Training the HMM­MAR(64) however
takes a long time, and the model does not find the expected subpatterns (one state cor­
responding to one sleep stage). The exploding gradient problem is likely to stem from
having a lot of parameters to fit, as the complexity of the gradients increases with added
parameters; and the running time increases with the number of parameters as well. Over­
all, starting with a fully saturated 64 lags is not the best idea. A suggestion for choosing
the lags is to simply start with MAR(1) or MAR(2). These simpler models might be easier
to fit and can thereby potentially capture the dynamics better, although they are not ex­
pected to be able to catch the whole dynamic of the delta waves (and other waves with
lower frequencies). However, the lags do not have to be consecutive. An approach sug­
gested by Vidaurre [5] is using exponential lags with an offset instead of consecutive lags,
i.e. using L = {p0 + 1, p0 + Q, p0 + Q2, . . . , p} where p0 is an offset, and p is the order of
the model (64 would still be a good choice). To do this in the current implementations, two
approaches can be taken: Either constructing a new data matrix which fits the structure
Modeling Sleep Dynamics
49
==PAGE:60==
of the lags and using the implementation as is; or initializing all weight matrices until p but
forcing those that are not included in L to be zero, both during initialization and training.
When choosing p0 and Q, biological knowledge can be incorporated, focusing e.g. on
capturing specific wavelengths.
7.5
Future work on mouse sleep data
Although the experiments on mouse data did not reveal any patterns, a lot of questions are
still open and can meaningfully be explored with the HMM­MAR framework with the mod­
ifications previously discussed. First of all, the experiments never got to exploring more
states than 3. This is suggested as the next observational experiment on the mouse data,
including expanding the data to include the whole light phase. Secondly, the models in
this project are solely trained and evaluated on the same recording of one mouse. To
explore generalizability, it is suggested to use multiple recordings of the same mouse;
one for training and one for validation. When doing this, the sleep patterns of the mouse
during both training and validation are expected to be the same, however the validation
data is actually unseen data. To further explore generalizability, training on multiple mice
and evaluating on multiple other mice is also suggested; this makes it possible to explore
how the models capture in­mice patterns vs between­mice patterns. This could e.g. be
explored by looking into whether the states are represented in one or multiple mice, indi­
cating whether a state fits a general pattern or a mouse­specific pattern. The exploration
of substages in sleep is not limited to training one HMM­MAR model on a consecutive
recording. A suggestion for another type of experiment is splitting the sleep data based
on the current sleep stages, i.e. creating data sets only consisting of observations from
one sleep stage. Training HMM­MARs on these different data sets allow an exploration
of the subpatterns focused on each of the sleep stages. Another potential use for HMM­
MARs within the area of sleep research is fitting different models to data from e.g. healthy
mice and narcoleptic mice, and then using the models for classification; the idea is that
a new recording from a narcoleptic mouse is more likely under the model parameters of
the model trained on narcoleptic data than the model trained on healty data. In this way,
the HMM­MARs may be used for detecting sleep disorders.

7.6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the potential of using hidden Markov models with
time dependent emissions for exploring patterns in sleep. Specifically, the project con­
tributes with an implementation of hidden Markov models with autoregressive emissions
(HMM­MARs) which can be trained using stochastic gradient descent. To investigate the
model applications within the area of sleep, experiments on both synthetic and observa­
tional data are conducted. To handle stationarity, the synthetic data is generated from
a HMM­MAR with normalized mean (HMM­normMAR). This way of generating synthetic
data turns out to remove the effect of the variable that controls the distinction between
states, resulting in a lot of uncertainty when generating data. The experiments on syn­
thetic data reveal challenges with consistently obtaining a good model during training,
although the training converges and does not overfit. This points to the model quickly
identifying a local optimum and converging to that. Synthetic experiments showed that
the learning rate has a huge influence on the trained model’s ability to identify true states,
supporting the hypothesis of many local optimas. The synthetic experiments also show
disagreement between the validation measure (negative log­likelihood) and the ability to
identify the true states. As a result, the model which best identifies the system dynamics
cannot be identified based on the validation measure, which is a critical problem. Both
of these challenges are however mainly attributed to to the challenge of making a good
initialization and to the generation of synthetic data, and suggestions for alternative ap­
proaches for future work are made.
The observational data consists of EEG and EMG recordings from mice. The observa­
tional experiments explore how well a 3­state HMM­MAR can recover the established
sleep stages Wake, NREM and REM. Based on the findings from the synthetic data, it
is not surprising that the observational experiments on mouse sleep data do not reveal
any biological insights. Suggestions for alternative choices of model parameters that are
expected to better the performance on sleep data are given. By addressing the presented
challenges, the HMM­MAR with direct numerical optimization is still expected to be a valu­
able tool for exploring new sleep patterns.
